\n Perhaps it was all the turkey meat. Whatever, just around the end of November a workable format concept for writing a beginner's guide to RPG's coalesced in my mind. Its an idea I've been bouncing around for years, but the shape of the final text was always nebulous till I started scribbling a week ago. In hopes of getting some good criticism, I'm posting my initial outline. Please, feel free to tear it apart pointing out any logical fallacies or glaring omissions. The outline appears in the post following this one, but I'd like to make some commentary first.

I want the final product to be a book I can confidently hand to a person who has never played an rpg before, and know that if they give the book a fair read-through they'll be able to run a game of their own afterward (I'd also like them to enjoy reading the book, if possible).

Lots of gamers, upon hearing that, will point out that many simple and newcomer friendly game systems are already available, so such a book is redundant. I counter that all those systems are good at covering one aspect of play (the rules) but rarely even address the other, in my opinion more important, aspects of rpg's. Things like how to set up a meet socially, how to keep the group engaged, how to interact with the wider hobby or shop for other systems. I know when I rolled my first 3D6, those where the things I was most clueless about and could have used the most help with. There will be a rule system in the book, but it will be deliberately derivative and non-innovative, built only to accomodate a few sessions of play illustrating key aspects before the players move on to a more substantial system.

I'd like community support, either here or elsewhere, putting this together, but I'm not sure how to go about that. Mainly I'll probably do it like this, every time I pound out a piece of it I'll flip it into the public eye and see what the crowd shouts. Ideas for more refined approaches are appreciated, but I'm unimpressed with blogs (I think they breed insularity) and am not pursuing any sort of timetable. It gets done when it gets done. Also, this is not-for-profit; I'd like the final product offered as a PDF and at-cost hardcopy.

    \n (x-posted with the Morningstar and the Macklin)

    ET, the following post is an attack on your base idea. I'm not judging you or calling YOU out, but I don't like the idea.


    I think that there are a lot of really volatile section headings, and other outright dumb ones. I think that if you do this, you should scrap:
    Origins, Subculture, The Development of the Hobby, The State of the Hobby
    I think what this will be is an exercise in typecasting. Trying to systematically organize a diverse creative endeavour will either lead to incoherent jumping about, or centralizing a single component of the scene. D&D-centric is an example of a potential pitfall. I don't think there's a solid way to address both of those issues at the same time. Also, you run the extreme risk of telling people that something doesn't exist, in the process of telling them the scope of what DOES exist. That's potentially very insulting and marginalizing to certain groups.

    All in all, these sections seem like a bad idea, and especially a bad idea to lead a book with. I think it would be far better to say "There are like a billion different roleplaying communities. Some are very focused on rules and gaming systems, and some are all about telling freeform stories. Some are male-centric and play extended campaigns. Some are all about convention gaming. There are a thousand million different roleplaying communities." And end the entire section there. Otherwise, what you're doing is running the risk of reinforcing stereotypes, confusing someone with a boring history lesson that makes the hobby seem less immediate and now, and centralizing a certain school of roleplaying at the expense of all others.

    Running Adventures
    Um... by this point in the table of contents it strikes me how very D&D centric this thing is. It now sounds like you're writing a book about "Long form, GM-driven, adventure tabletop roleplaying games that are rules heavy".

    Most games don't follow an adventure structure, and formulizing it is a very One True Wayist approach.

    Hobby vs Industry, and going DIY
    So, you're going to take this awful dichotomy which doesn't make sense and divides good people (see 207 post monstrosity on this forum right now), and hardcode it into new people so they see it and further it? No.

    \n I most definitely agree with the suggestions to remove all the talk of "the history of gaming" and publishing. Just talk about how to play the games. Advice for players in different situations would be useful, I think--things like, "how to make your character unique/memorable" or "how to narrate interesting adversity for other players' characters". Those could potentially apply to almost any game you're playing.

    I AM curious about "why I want you to know about TTRPGs" and what you think the "Inherent Conundrum" is. Would you care to outline what will go in those sections?

    Finally, dealing with GM/player issues might be less clear-cut than you think if you're going to talk about online play. MMORPGs don't have a "GM" at all, and much of the other "online roleplaying" is based collaborative storytelling or some other form of freeform roleplay. Given the era we've entered, they're probably worth seriously discussing, particularly since both can forms can take place in tabletop roleplaying, as well.

    Finally, just because most of the roleplaying plays D&D doesn't mean they all do it the same way. In my own D&D experience (which was very "traditional", as far as we could tell) we only rarely had any prepared "adventures" and occasionally just ignored a whole bunch of rules or even totally changed the nature of the game. For instance:

    -We all played "monsters", and fought against group after group of heroes until we died. We, as the players, designed the dungeon and its traps together, without showing the GM.

    -A few times we just sat around the table pretending we were our characters in a bar (or an a long, boring space journey) and told each other stories in-character. No game mechanics involved, and the GM didn't say a word unless speaking through an NPC who was present. Everyone had a great time.

    I would imagine that most "traditional" gaming groups do stuff like this, even if they don't realize it. Even playing an "evil campaign" is a bit of a subversion of what D&D gameplay is supposed to be like.
