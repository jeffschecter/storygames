\nThis thread got me thinking... "convince the guard to let you pass" is such a major trope, what would happen if you inverted it?

The PCs are guards at the royal castle. Each encounter consists of some person they either have to let pass, or not. The problem is, some of their bullshit-sounding stories are actually true. What if that lady really *did* have an urgent message for the King's military council? Furthermore, some of the would-be entrants can offer... enticements. Money, men, women, secrets... Come on, what harm could it do to let little old Baba Yaga into the castle? She's just going to the kitchen, to add a little spice to the soup. And of course, some characters will attack if denied entry! Also for a twist, you could occasionally have an encounter where someone is trying to leave the castle who maybe shouldn't, perhaps with state secrets.

What existing game do you think would work well for this? I could see either Burning Wheel or Polaris. (Polaris would be hilarious, as the "bad" final outcome would be discovering that you yourself are an intruder into the castle! Very Kafka-esque, or least phildickian.) And actually D&D, either 3 or 4, could work well*. Taking a long rest would mean the next petitioner simply gets past, as those night-shift lads are not exactly very dependable now, are they?

*I'm trying to think of excuses to vary the battlefield, though. Drawbridge down/up? Moat filled/unfilled/full of alligators?

    \n That's a great game idea, but the way I'd like it best would be as a boardgame with light fibbing elements. A card-based thing of some sort, perhaps. Players would maintain card hands where the cards represent various people wanting to pass the checkpoint, stratagems (bribing, subterfuge, etc.) and various payloads (that is, cards with victory point values). The players would form sets out of their hands, play them face down and then try to talk themselves through the checkpoint formed by the other players. The other players would then have some mechanical means of flipping the cards over, but as long as they don't hit a payload, they're just hurting themselves. On the other hand, catching a payload card gets you the victory points. The point of the game is that any victory point cards you draw yourself can only be scored by getting them through the checkpoint manned by the other players, so you need to encourage the other players to waste their investigative capability on bum cases so that when you're actually playing those victory points the others'll just wave you through.

    Yeah, that one almost designed itself. Seems promising.
