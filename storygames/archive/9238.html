    \n I use to freeform roleplay, combat, freeform roleplay.

    But 4E combat rewards playing 3 connected encounters (due to daily powers, milestones, action points, and xp). And with combats taking 45 to 90 minutes each, freeform roleplay outside of combat felt empty. You have fun freeform roleplay for 20 minutes and then to fit 3 encounters in a session you don't have time for anymore.

    It also feels disconnected. To me, roleplaying an efficient killing machine is boring. But combat is so team focused now (which is my favorite thing about 4E) that to play inefficiently is to let your team down.

    So we play 4E now as a tactical boardgame. A few local groups feel the same way. We still describe our powers in colorful ways but having dramatic tension outside of combat tends to be very limited.

    Ultimately we have so many amazing games that support great roleplaying it feels strange tacking it on to 4E where it feels unsupported and distracting.

    That being said, I've found it actually to be very important for the GM to roleplay quite a bit in combat. Breathing life into the monsters and describing the encounters colorfully seems to add a lot to the game while not slowing things down.

    \n I started playing 4e about a month ago with my dysfunctional, DnD-only gaming group of many years. I was getting a little tired of 3.5e so was happy when we retired a stale campaign and a fellow player volunteered to run 4e, which he'd had some experience with. I had read a lot of high praise hereabouts about 4e and how it was easier to get into ruleswise, more story-gamey and had quicker combat. So far, I'm pretty underwhelmed by it as a system.

    I don't see any story game elements in the rules, which sucks, because my group is diverse and while they're not opposed to actual roleplaying, it's kind of hard to get that happening consistently. I was hoping for some more rules that would encourage drama and character building, but there's nothing like that. The crown jewel is apparently the skills challenge system, which as mentioned in a recent thread here, is nothing more, ruleswise, than getting players to go around in a circle and make simple skill rolls until the group accumulates enough successes. In said thread some peoples' suggestion was to just 'add roleplay'. The rules for this should facilitate roleplay and don't, in my opinion. During our first skill challenge, my group stared blankly at the DM when he explained it and their eyes glazed over as soon as the dice started rolling. He tried to add bonuses for roleplaying, but it felt like a tacked-on incentive and probably threw the math off and made it easier than it should have been.

    That's not my main problem with the game though. I've developed a sort of love/hate relationship with DnD. I hate that it's been the only game that I've been able to play and I hate the leveling treadmill and I hate that it doesn't have good rules support for non-combat situations. What I love about DnD is the weird kind of personality it's developed over the years, with all its crazy monsters, the bizarre alignment system, the dynamics between the core classes and its wonderful magic system, among other things. There's a lot of things that are distinctly Dungeons & Dragons.

    I think 3.5e was a revelation. It took all the idiotic, arbitrary and clumsy rules and stripped most of them away. It added new rules like skills, feats and prestige classes that helped defined ones character, even if a lot of those features were combat-related. Don't get me wrong, 3.5e is still clunky, but it's definitely still DnD. 4e feels like a dumbing down and a desperate appeal to online gamers. The thing is, games like World of Warcraft borrowed from DnD, stripped away all the roleplay elements and rendered all the abilities into predictable, easily programmed procedures. What's more, WoW and its ilk are dumb. There is little actual tactical play. It's button mashing and a monkey with a head wound could probably manage to make it to level 80. I think they made a mistake in throwing out a pretty good, mature and deep rules system, and replacing it with its simpleton mutant offspring.

    I think the clearest example of this is in the way they handled classes, which is probably the heart of DnD. The classes used to be quite distinct but now they all feel pretty much the same. They all have these wacky powers that are almost completely abstract and nonsensical, but not really distinctive (which is how it works in computer RPGS generally). It's all about the cold mechanics, it seems. The basic classes in 3.5 felt so distinctive from each other. When you were playing a wizard, you were playing a wizard, and you had all these crazy spells that required some clever resource management. You had odd spells that you could be extremely creative with during and after combat. Now it's mainly just number crunching. It just seems like they've taken away a lot of potential for creative solutions to problems all around, which is criminal.

    This isn't to say 4e is terrible, but I don't think it delivered on many of its promises. It is different, but in this case I'd lean towards bad different, and I think my gaming group would too. They definitely are not story-gamer types, but even they find 4e lacking soul and personality. That said, the combat is basically fun, if you look at nothing but the rules. It's more coherent ruleswise and probably easier to pick up than 3.5e combat. It's definitely easier to whip up characters and I do like the way the monsters work. That's probably the best change I've seen. I haven't really seen evidence that the combat is significantly shorter than in 3.5e (especially since it's encouraged to string three combats together in a row) but it seems like setup is easier for the DM.

    So, my soapbox diatribe aside, I think that yes, 4e works better as a standalone tactical combat game but 3.5e is still better for the complete campaign-style RPG package.

    \n In my experience, 4E actively sits in the way of "role-playing" as I understand it.

    I just joined a "Scales of War" campaign, which is the Adventure Path that WotC has been printing in Dungeon. The rest of the group had already finished part 1 and I entered as they were just starting part 2. The GM gave me some background information on why I was where I was, and the campaign itself came with "hooks" which are backgrounds you can pick that give you a small bonus to a skill and supposedly will become more important as the campaign goes on.

    I chose "wandering duelist" and after I found out what happened in the first part (the party had kicked the butt of a small army of monsters that were threatening a local town, returned with the townsfolk who had been kidnapped and were currently being feted by the locals), I thought I'd try playing that to the hilt. I showed up and challenged one of the PCs to a duel. After all, they were the biggest badasses I could find.

    That was about the extent of my roleplaying. It was a good place to start, certainly, but once I'd been accepted by the party, we just proceeded from fight to fight. In between you might get a skill challenge, but even there, the mechanics were what you were concentrating on. You're too busy deciding what skill to roll next and how many successes versus failures that the actual events get glossed over.

    I even tried role-playing WITHIN the combat encounters, but the rules just got in the way. I'm playing a Tempest fighter -- dual weapons, leaping about from opponent to opponent and spinning like a dervish attacking anything in sight. I started out describing things that way. By the third combat it turned into a CCG war game. "I play my 'Sweeping Blow' attack."

    Could we role-play more? Sure. But it feels like everything in the system is pulling against that. I'd be roleplaying despite the game, not because of it.

    Part of the problem, I think, is how encyclopedic the rules are. There is a rule, literally, for everything. Jumping. Jumping up. Long jumps. Jumping and missing not because you didn't jump far enough but because you ran out of 'movement' in your turn. Jumping down, i.e. a 'controlled fall' - which uses Acrobatics, not Athletics. Do you divide the result by 5 or by 10 to get squares? It got to the point that when I mentioned the possibility of jumping across a piece of tough terrain, everyone rolled their eyes.

    Don't get me started on climbing. I have to roll successfully HOW many times?

    Having that many rules really feels oppressive more than anything else. Since playing 4E, I've started to fall in love with rule-sets like microlite20. The concept of 'rulings not rules'. Any game that has a skill list with more than 4 entries makes me break out in hives.

    I'm having fun with the campaign, and I'll certainly continue to play, but only because I've learned to treat it the way I would Diablo, or more accurately, a JRPG. There's story, sure, but it's big sweeping stuff that happens in the background. I only get to really, meaningfully control my little avatar when the battle theme kicks in and my menu of options comes up. Other than that, it's watching cutscenes.

    \n I don't know that I play 4e "like a tactical boardgame", but the tactical fights are the most interesting part of the game, so I certainly look forward to them. Of course the same could be said of 3.5, Castles & Crusades, GURPS and most of the standard games out there. Most everything outside of combat was pixel-bitching investigative work or "immersive" fluff that didn't really carry any weight of its own and only felt important and interesting if it revealed a clue in the investigation or lead to a fight. Any drama was added, intentionally or not, outside of the system itself.

    Honestly, the only thing that makes 4e any less "roleplayish" than its contemporaries is that yes, the combats take a while, cutting into your investigation and fluff time. Other games often have the same problem, but since the combats are generally less "tuned" the GM is more likely to throw a bunch of underpowered or "glass cannon" encounters at the PCs, or fudge the HPs of the monsters just to move things along.

    Combat and traps and adventure are just what D&D is. Everything else is pretty much just color. I commented to a friend recently that according to the rules, defining a Warlock's pact (arguably the most important and interesting thing about the class) is ENTIRELY optional. When creating a Warlock, the game doesn't require you to write down anything about your pact other than its general source, and even that is nearly meaningless. But my friend pointed out that if you make each character too interesting, it throws off the whole "heroic adventure party vibe" which D&D REQUIRES to function. Once you split the party it's all chaos and madness from that point.

    \nI would say that the system promotes balanced classes but does not rely on it. But encounters can rely on a balance of classes. So if you customize the encounter to match your needs, the system should work fine.

    It would actually be very easy to play a group of all thieves. There are 4 different builds of rogues. The trickster rogue, brawny rogue, aerialist rogue, and the cutthroat rogue. And there are other classes that could be reskinned to work as rogues. Just replace two of their class skills with thievery and perception.

    I'd echo a few of the comments above. 3E wasn't less of a tactical boardgame but it did have many simulation elements. Suspension of disbelief mattered more. In 4E all that goes out the window. So I do feel there are huge differences between Shadowrun and Gurps vs. 4E. A warlord could smack you and then 2 of her friends heal 5 hit points and someone else is pushed back 3 squares. It's all about what is cool as a tactical game and the color is put on top of it. You are not playing a fighter, you are playing a CCG deck of Magic cards. In a movie a fight might do cool grappling moves but in 4E you might have to be a different class to do what you imagine your fighter could do. And as you level up you can reshuffle your magic card deck and all of a sudden powers you could do before you forgot and in their place you suddenly remember new powers. The fiction is very secondary.

    That being said, if you want to do more freeform roleplaying on top of 4E, I would suggest giving the players free intimidate checks against monsters that are bloodied (except for solos and elites) or cut all monster hit points in half. Also avoid monsters that have high hit points and high defenses. It's generally more fun if monsters do more damage but fall down quicker or in a more reasonable length of time.

    \n I'm in a 4e game that's been going about 16 sessions. It started with Keep on the Shadowfell, but quickly diverged. We tend to alternate between talky sessions, with little to no dice rolling and "big fight" sessions which are pure tactical encounters. I have to admit that I am less excited about the talking, and much more excited about the combat, so in that way I am treating it like a grand tactical boardgame.     \n Ultimately it's a case of proportion. Fights take longer and are more intricate, so you can do plot-fight-plot and still feel like it's plotFIIIIIIIIGHTplot. I'm okay with that, but you've gotta make sure you think about what sort of rhythm that imparts to your game. I think of combat as the bass line of the song, and D&D of any stripe, but 4E for certain, is the guy playing that song in the car with the three-foot-diameter subwoofers going BOOM BOOM BOOM BOOM BVVVVVVVVVVVVvvvv.

    If you wanna play with the graphic equalizer, though, it's an easy fiddle. The DMG even points the way some -- I'd start cutting monster hit points down to half their current levels. It'll speed up your fights (so the bass doesn't hit so hard and it'll leave more room for RP) and it means that you run less of a risk of hitting that tired "last mile" of a fight where everyone has exhausted their interesting variety (encounter and daily powers) and is just stuck with slinging around their at-wills. That last mile effect hasn't happened often for us (we're over half a dozen or a dozen sessions in, maybe more -- I haven't been counting), but it hasn't been absent either.

    I would *keep* the maps and minis no matter what. Yes, yes, you could probably figure out a way to abstract that stuff, but the sense of space and terrain and movement is a very, very important part of the 4E experience for me -- far more than any previous edition's been.

    I'd consider giving Action Points more things to do; folks can develop a surplus they can't ever use (since you can only spend one per encounter, but gain one every two scenes, until you hit a long rest & reset them). In our game we'll let folks sacrifice their surplus APs to reroll a d20 roll they don't like, but other effects work as well. You can also potentially hook aspect-like "compel-bribery" into them, to give the players a somewhat more abundant supply and to add in a little bit of story-gamey spin to things. (We touched on stuff like this briefly with the One Bad Egg product The Unbroken, which includes a feat for taking on a Code of Honor. More stuff like that would not be hard to invent.)

    I'd also consider recognizing that the out of combat stuff can be made to seem more vital by connecting it to an in-combat effect. My favorite trick in this regard is to run a skill challenge as a prelude to a combat encounter. Win the challenge, and you get to place yourselves on the map after the bad guys are placed; lose the challenge, and they place themselves after you're placed. Win the challenge by a LOT (say, double, or without any failures), and you get to place the enemy on the map; lose the challenge BADLY, and the enemy gets to place *you*. It makes ambushes have a lot more teeth, and when the players understand what's on the line (you only have to do it once or twice for them to realize what a skill challenge could mean for htem) it can compel them to get much more involved in the investigation leading up to said ambush.

    I'll do other things with my game to make sure the combats are deliberately telling a piece of the big story or develop a particular character. One of my players came from a fallen Dwarven city -- why did it fall? Well, one session started with a flash-back episode. All the players except for him played minion characters trying to escape the city as it was getting overrun with demons and undead; he played himself. The minion-PCs generally died heroically trying to help the others escape. Character backstory emerged via a map and hit points.

    The story's there. You just need to figure out how to use the tools provided to tell it.

      \n I am in the "ebb and flow" camp, or the "commingled RP and tactical boardgame" camp, as it were. Except I have no qualms about kitbashing the hell out of the game to make it more Story Gamey. In other words, I am a huge fan of the tactical boardgame 4E, as written, but I am uncomfortable running any game with such a traditional GM-is-god-of-truth setup as is D&D default, so I have worked things to have my cake and eat it too. The result is something that feels very like a video game, yes--but more like Final Fantasy Tactics meets Persona 3 than World of Warcraft, and I find that most delicious.

      The techniques I use:

      1. Personal Quests, my friends. When creating a character, everyone must define at least one personal quest for their character, and are free to introduce setting elements with only general group that's-not-cool veto to stop them. These get written down on yellow index cards. During the game, anybody can pick up a yellow index card and write a new quest down. In story game land, we call these things Flags!

      2. Warmup RP. If it's been a while since we last played, I give people a chance to get into their characters' heads at the start of the session, usually with a question-and-answer thing. My usual is a round-robin where each player reveals what their character thinks about somebody else's character.

      3. Alternate Skill Challenge rules. I use Stalker0's Skill Challenges for skill challenge encounters, and half-errataed (errataed success/fail ratio, but slightly harder than post-errata DCs) normal Skill Challenges for long-term stuff. Most notable of the latter are "faction challenges" a la Willow Palecek, which turn into more Flag/Bang stuff as people invest their downtime actions in groups/NPCs they're interested in, and cause trouble with them via their failures.

      \n More actual play would be good. So would more specificity about what "roleplaying" means to you, because I'm hearing two VERY distinct things:

      1. Mechanically supported non-combat decisions/actions with consequences, OR decisions without mechanical support, but with strong mechanical feedback
      2. Establishment of characters, relationships, and fictional elements OUTSIDE the formal confines of mechanics - which may feed back into mechanics or not

      Basically NO mainstream RPG has ever done much with #1, and I think there's a fair amount of resistance to it in the core market. To be honest, I've begun to find it a bit constraining myself in games where it occurs - "here's a cookie for making the kinds of decisions that the game thinks should be important" works great when the game is tightly focused, but loses a certain something when trying to play a looser, more player-driven game.

      AP example: a recent Bliss Stage interlude, in which my character really, really needed to pursue one mechanical outcome in the scene - I needed Trauma Relief something BAD, which meant going to this other character and dumping on her about how much failing in my last mission sucked for me. Mechanically, I needed that cookie. So I chose a scene with a character with whom I had a distant, conflicted relationship - somebody I wouldn't mind using that way. But the scene that happened went totally off in another direction, almost from the get-go. The thing that was true to the characters ended up with me being confronted with WHY I felt that way about the other character, and the result was - mechanically - obviously an Intimacy increase. Which I really, really did NOT need. Arguably, I was negatively affected for pursuing the character's truth. At the very least, I wasn't rewarded for it. The mechanically optimal thing to do would have been to be as callous as I needed to be to get the Trauma Relief - I went into the scene willing to sacrifice the relationship if I needed to in order to get that. I think the game was a lot richer for NOT following the cookie, in the end. I want to be clear: It's not that I looked at it mechanically and CHOSE a less-advantaged course of action in order to illustrate something about the character, like I might have in a D&D game - I just played the character the way I felt I needed to, and the outcome was sub-optimal.

      (Parenthetically, that's actually a cool thing about BS - I have found that characters CONSTANTLY develop that "life of their own" that demands these sub-optimal choices that don't feel like choices in the moment.)

      On the other hand, lots and lots of the RP that happens in games is type #2: portrayal of character either USING mechanical actions, but without direct feedback from that decision or without ANY mechanical relevance.

      AP example: Caravas, my Eladrin warlord, has a very heirarchal view of his companions: There's the ones who are his fated companions, the "supporting cast" in the great legend that he is building for himself as a sacrifice to the Raven Queen in order to win the freedom of the fey lady he once served; and there are the employees, who are expendable tols of necessity, certain to be replaced by his true fated companions in time. Which is which is largely determined by how they conduct themselves in battle - valor and selflessness and an intangible "favor of the gods" mark you as one of the chosen companions, whether you're being paid a retainer or not.

      It's been incredibly interesting watching his opinions of who's "in" and who isn't shift depending on the choices of my fellow players and the luck of the dice. I don't quite think the other PCs have figured out why Caravas takes crazy risks to protect Petra and Erzuli, but is curt and cooly efficient when dealing with Lia & Raime, and seems to run hot and cold toward Selina. The main way this comes out is in my tactical decisions, but it definitely plays out in other ways as well.

      \n What Ryan said.

      Also, maybe a bunch of storygamer types play 4E once or twice, never in campaign, never the same character thrice, and never really get into the groove. I know that I spent my first four games or so mastering the rules and my character. We did some rapid advancement stuff (jumping from 1st level to 4th to 8th to 10th between sessions) and I found I didn't know my character's abilities well.

      If I had played the game as intended, leveled up slowly, played each level for ten encounters or so, and slowly built up my abilities, I would have been way more comfortable in encounters.

      Relevance? Without mastery of your character, combats take a lot longer. That 4-hour session is 30 minutes of gabbing, 3 hours of fighting, and maybe 30 minutes of role-playing.

      Also, the new is shiny. You have these cool powers and want to try them out. If you're done with the game before you've mastered them, you never get to the point where you can comfortably ignore the powers a bit and lean back and enjoy the scenery.

      My point is to people who feel 4E is very much JUST a tactical game, have you played the same character for 3-4 sessions in a row? That's about the "soft spot" I found when playing.

      \n As others said upthread, I think people who hang out at Story Games are probably predisposed toward playing D&D 4e as a board game. If we were looking for something more story-oriented, we'd probably pick up a game other than D&D. That was true in the days of 3e too, incidentally.

      That said, I think there are lots and lots of people out there without our biases who happily use 4e for deep, story-oriented gaming. As an example, check out the Return to Northmoor podcast (and it's cool to boot).

      I think it's important to note that 4e is the latest in a long line of RPGs (including all previous editions of D&D) that had very little in the way of rules-based support for talking in funny voices and generating story. In many ways, 4e gives more support for that stuff than previous editions between the text dedicated to personality, mannerisms, appearance and background at the beginning of the Player's Handbook, the Skill Challenges (which at least make an attempt to create a framework for social situations beyond a simple skill check), and a fair bit of advice in the DMG dedicated to creating setting, adventures and campaigns filled with story.

      It's true that 4e can be played as a very robust tactical board game. But I think we're being myopic if we assume that's all there is to it.
