\n Games use many types incentives and rewards to guide players actions. Well designed systems can guide players to produce fiction that emulates genres or caters to certain themes. What about GMs? They too are players but often they are given a unique ways of interfacing with the game's mechanisms. In what ways can games systems incentivize or reward GMs for taking certain actions more or less often? How can systems apply pressure to a GM or punish certain actions (or lack of action)?

    \n GM incentives are hard: the go-to option for player incentives is to increase character effectiveness, but unless the GM is set up in an adversarial way the GM doesn't have much interest in making the characters comparatively less effective, so you can't just naively flip the polarity and give them more "opposition points" or whatever. In the game I'm working on, Getting There in Time, I wanted to give the GM an incentive for some behavior that I often inadvertently let slip through the cracks when I GM which is to provide rich and evocative detail during the game (left to my own devices I usually end up with bland and featureless locations and NPCs when I create them on they fly). I realized that in the game the common case would be that the GM wants a "happy" ending to the session as much as the players do, so the "reward" for doing the behavior I want to encourage in my game is that the mechanical difficulty of the obstacle that the characters have to overcome to resolve the story is higher if the GM isn't able to get enough detail into play before the "begin endgame" condition is triggered. I'm not 100% sure it works yet since I haven't seen anyone but me try to GM the game, but I think it has potential.

    \n In Dramasystem, the GM has a pool of drama tokens like everyone else (one pool for all PCs.) The drama token economy encourages the GM to grant and refuse roughly equally, just like everyone else.

    Drama tokens are useful to everyone to control the flow of the story. You get them by giving major emotional concessions to a petitioning character. Then you can spend them to get your way. Thus, the GM wants to both grant petitions some of the time and to spend their tokens and force what they want some of the time.

    \n In a strongly GM-led trad game (railroad or otherwise) where the GM authors the plot with intent to thrill the players, the GM's feedback (a.k.a. punishment/rewards) is the player response. A flat reaction says "don't do that again" while enthusiastic "Holy shit!" reaction says "do that again".

    Traditionally, the coherence of that communication is entirely up to the group, and games could certainly provide tools to formalize it. "I liked that" player-judgment fanmail and "you hit my character flag" system-mandated rewards are two examples. But most GM rewards fall flat, compared to player rewards, without a GM objective akin to the sorts of objectives many games give players.

    So perhaps the question "how can we give GMs incentives?" should start with "what can we give GMs incentives for?"

    \n Ryuutama (to be published in English sometime this year) has some interesting ideas in this vein-- there's a conceit that the party's adventures are being observed or directed by a dragon, who has its own character sheet and stats and is kind of an anthropomorphic representation of the group's evolving social contract.

    The dragon gains XP every session, and the powers you choose for it are divided between formalized applications of GM fiat (put the PCs back on the trail if they get lost in the wilderness, give a monster a sudden burst of strength, redo a combat that killed the group), flags to the players for things the GM would like to see (in-game rewards for writing IC journals, bonuses to characters that pursue love or confront sworn enemies), and rules to veer the campaign toward certain genres like intrigue or horror, or dictate certain relationships within the party (everyone's part of the army, some PCs are proteges of others, etc). It's almost like having an encounter budget for basic campaign construction and I think it'd be an interesting set of training wheels for new GMs.

    It's not that GM fiat is bad per se and I know that more experienced GMs will probably feel a bit constrained, at lest in the beginning; but this is also meant to challenge the GM to do her best with less pieces, something that has the strange side effect of boosting creativity.

    \n I note that we're mostly talking about GM resources within the game rather than incentives for the GM to play. I find both concepts interesting, but especially incentives.
    Things that reward me when I'm GM'ing:

    --players engaging with the invented content: this is a very big one for me. When I note the players speculating about, or building on, something I just threw into the description as a grace note, it makes me happy and there's a positive feedback loop where I pay more attention to the stuff that the players respond to and develop it further. I'm fairly convinced this is the secret sauce in a lot of indie games, especially Mountain Witch, Dogs, and Apocalypse World.

    --seeing players having a good time. This can also set off a feedback loop of mutual enjoyment; when it's absent, there can be a toxic feedback loop of unhappy players and dissatisfied GM rubbing up against each other.

    --Direct feedback: I always wished that Prime Time Adventures allowed for fanmail for the Producer, because there's something very powerful in praise as a game mechanic.

    \n There's a weird thing about "like my stuff!" for the GM:

    - On the one hand, it's a huge reward when it happens, and it's a great motivator -- "I came up with a cool thing I want to share!" is probably what inspires me most to GM.

    - On the other hand, every GM ever is already trying this and they don't need us to tell them to pursue it.

    Re: the AW advance, I don't think making it official that a player can want to see more of an NPC really changes anything on that front.

    I think the best tools a GM could be given for "like my stuff" are:
    - diagnosing what the players will like (thought it may be hard to beat "play with them and get to know them")
    - helping you effectively present the stuff you like

    Especially that latter. "Someone knocks out the PCs and takes them to the place I want them to be and shows them the stuff I want them to see," needs to give way to more advanced tactics of hooking curiosity and building up to reveals.

    At the moment, that seems hard to me. "Work with the players to beat obstacles provided by the dice!" might be easier.
