    \n When you look at them closely, 3:16 is a more fully developed version of Shotgun Diaries. From a design standpoint, everything about the two games is almost identical - but better done in 3:16.

    Character types in both are very simplified - with Survivor Type (plus Tools and Weapons) in SGD and Fighting Ability/Non-Fighting Ability and Ranks (which dictate weapons) in 3:16 working exactly the same - to show the advantages of a group working together.

    The Threat (zombies/aliens) is abstracted and more of a narrative hook than the point of the game - and again handled better in 3:16, both in terms of how combat is resolved and how narration is encouraged.

    SGD uses the zombie Clock and Complications while 3:16 uses Threat Tokens for exactly the same purpose.

    SGD allows players to add to the game world through the Diary; 3:16 uses Flashbacks.

    SGD uses Fear and Benefits; 3:16 uses Weaknesses, again for the same purpose.

    The games are almost identical - but 3:16's mechanisms are more cohesive and developed.

    \n If you can't separate theme, structure, and balance when examining a game design, then that probably explains why so many "story-telling" games are poorly realized. You can't rely on "and here some Narration happens," a cool concept, moody artwork, authorial "intent," or a gimmick like a Jenga Tower to solve basic game design issues.

    That is also why identifying underlying structures would be a very productive exercise for a community like this. It would help a lot of designers avoid making those same missteps all over again.

    The reason why people have a hard time seeing the structure underlying 3:16 is because it is so well integrated with the theme in its presentation - but the structure itself doesn't have anything to do with that theme. You could literally turn it into a military style zombie game just by changing the word "alien" to "zombie" and making the settings different locations in or around a large city. And it would be very easy to change into a survivor zombie game with only a little renaming and tweaking of some of the structures.

    SGD would be better if a couple of gaps in the system were filled in. Fear should be tied to failing at a Risk - which would also eliminate the really bad game effect of having the gm kill you merely for a bad die roll (something no one likes, but designers always want to do for some reason - like they are all frustrated gms). And there needs to be an explicit mechanism for dispatching zombie "threats" - the same way 3:16 has a means for dealing with alien threats (including a kill mechanism even though the actual numbers are irrelevant). Just saying "you're always killing zombies" is hoping your players stay on track - not helping them to.

    [And it's "losing," by the way.]

    \n 1. That's the definition of roleplaying - identifying with a character who has different qualities than you do (whether positive or negative).

    2. Yes. And it's exactly what Storytelling games are designed to avoid. The whole purpose of a storytelling game is to make actions and effects meaningful from the standpoint of the story - not due to the arbitrary results of a game system. Of course, even with a traditional rpg, a good gm doesn't let die rolls control the story - but it's always awkward when the gm has to overrule the system to save the story.

    3. And while Yahtzee does have strategy in one sense of the word, it only consists of managing statistical possibilities. Any action you take can always be trumped by the dice - which again means you have no ultimate control. The dice drive what you do - not the other way around. While Backgammon is similar, it adds two very important dimensions to the game through the interaction of the player's positions and the use of the doubling cube. It's not a game merely of statistics, but of variously aggressive or defensive strategies along with informed risk taking - where you are fundamentally playing against the other player's level and judgment of risk tolerance, and not just the dice.
