\n I visited a game store today, for the first time in... literally years, I think. While I was there, I cracked open a volume of "Gamemastering Secrets". It seemed well put-together, but a few things I saw straight away kind of pushed my buttons. I'm talking about things like "The GM is God" and "never let the players know you're suprised by something unexpected, that they've foiled your plans, or that you're improvising" etc.

I'd like to hear what you people think about a few of the well-known GM advice books out there. The three I'm thinking of are:

1. Gamemastering Secrets

2. Robin's Laws of Good Game Mastering

3. NPC Essentials

So, from a story game perspective, how good/suck are they? Lots of good advice? Lots of outdated advice? Unusual, innovative advice?

If you there are other books you'd like to mention in this context, that's cool, too.

    \n I have 1 & 2.

    I haven't looked into Gamemastering Secrets for a few years, but I recall it had some very good advice and some that wasn't. I'm not sure my opinions would be the same now, story game hippy that I am these days.

    Robin's Laws is more about structuring a game in order to make it fun for all the players. He breaks down player types into a few archetypes and talks about how to build your adventures to that. Again, maybe not so relevant to story game hippies but good advice for what it was aimed at.

    \n I found the GMing advice in both Spirit of the Century and Zorcerer of Zo to be excellent.

    SotC has some great advice, some is directly geared to pulp, other part work pretty much anywhere.
    2 things that really made an impact on me were the Basic Assumptions on page 290 which had the gem

    It is not necessary for you to know how anything is going to turn
    out for you to run a good session, and in fact, better stories can
    (and often do) result when you have no preconceived ideas in that
    regard....Cooperative efort will be necessary to
    make the thing work. That\u2019s good.

    and of course the dictum "Send in the Ninjas"

    Zo has a ton of Actual Play that is very much worth reading. It also has a smaller section on GMing advice that is very good.

    Oh, and PrimeTime Adventures taught me tons about how to use scene presence effectively, and how to arc a story over multiple sessions. I use that stuff in pretty much every game I run, regardless of the rule system.

    But none of these is explicitly a "How to GM" book, and every game will teach you something.

    \n I've heard interesting things about Play Dirty by John Wick. By interesting, I mean that I wouldn't necessary use the ideas, but they're extraordinary enough to spark ideas of my own.

    (I remember, in particular, something about the GM putting a black stone on the table whenever the players broke one of the social rules, such as answering a mobile phone. That black stone could be used to cause problems for the players later. I found that fascinating.)

    I'm obliged to mention my book, Play Unsafe. That's a guide for any player, including GMs: it was written, partly, as a response to guides written exclusively for GMs. That's connected to the assumption Ryan mentions, about the GM being the one who provides the fun.

    I agree that much great GM advice comes in the games themselves. I particularly like the advice sections of My Life With Master, Dogs In The Vineyard and Acts of Evil.

    So, if there's advice on (say) "How the GM should play NPCs", any player who plays NPCs could use it. If there's advice on starting scenes with a bang, anyone could use that.

    It's less easy with illusionistic techniques, and so forth, of course.

    \n Paul, I have both "Gamemastering Secrets", and "NPC Essentials". As I can't find my copy of Gamemastering Secrets, I will only talk about NPC Essentials.

    For a d20 GMing book it's not bad. It talks about NPC roles, and gives good advice on playing D&D Archetypes. It's was my go to book to help with my brainstorming of D&D NPC's. I loved the charts in the back of the book. And the advice on giving NPC's a secret was good.

    I picked it up based on both this reviews, and this one too.

    Now compared to story games advice, it does fall short. Although the price / page code was right, compared to other GM Advice books.

    Sorry to say NPC Essentials has no advice on playing with bangs, or sharing NPC narration with players. It's still very old school.

    On to Play Unsafe, I did find the advice here useful, and gratifying. I found that I was already using some of the ideas contained. It was nice to have my GMing style confirmed. But I got most of that advice from Burning Wheel.

    I found that the price / page count Play Unsafe to by way off. It's too short of a book for $20. Really this book should have been submitted to Push. I would recomend to get the PDF, and share print outs.
