\n I came up with an idea that I really like, but it has a big flaw and I wonder if there are any ways around it?

A attacks B; both are effectively fighting each other, they are going toe to toe either melee or ranged it doesn/t matter. A rolls, B counters with his own roll. If A beats B then B takes a Hit. Once he's taken a few of these, he's out. No hit points, just a small tally of Hits (though they are eminently functional, I find HP a bit dull). If B beats A then B has the advantage and A has to roll again as the situation is reversed. Here we get the ebb and flow of combat. No real need, beyond establishing who goes first (which might be represented with a simple bonus to A's initial roll), for regimented initiative/turn based systems. This even applies to gunfights: you get the ebb and flow there too, like in a John Woo movie, blam blam!

Problem is what if A attacks B, but B is intending not to duel A. This system is great for a straight duel, but if B is attacking C and C is attacking A, what happens? Does A automatically hit B who automatically hits C who hits A etc? Maybe?

    \n Imagine yourself as character B in your scenario. How would this be most interesting to you?

    Do you want to gamble on it, to see if you're quick enough to take down C before A gets to you? That's something like the Sorcerer combat system.

    Or do you want it to be a difficult choice? "Damn, do I risk exposing myself to whatever A is going to do to me, is it worth it just to nail C?" Then create some kind of powerful (but still interesting) result for when A hits B. Perhaps they can throw you into the scenery, disabling you, removing you from the fight, or applying some hefty penalties to whatever you do next (perhaps in the game they get to tag you with some kind of Condition, like "Entangled" or "Dangling from a bridge").

    I'm not a Wuxia expert, but I would guess that in this kind of genre people only get killed if they lose the fight, AND the winner decides to finish them off. Don't the fights usually end with one person on the ground, defeated, and the other standing over them, sword drawn?

    I could be wrong, but if that's how it works, it makes the mechanics easy: don't include any chance of death in the fight, just a chance of completely dominating the loser, having them at your mercy. Then it's an interesting choice: do you kill them or spare them?

    ghostwhistler, what people are implying but not really saying is this:

    You're not actually modeling physics. Give up that idea. What you're modeling is cinema, setting, and theme. Even games that seem to be complex simulations of "actual" combat are just focusing on specific details that model a specific kind of dramatic scene. Actual combat probably makes for a crappy game, and actual realistic physics are too complex for gameplay. Every game system is therefore an abstraction, and what you choose to abstract is a designer's decision, based on the elements like those I named, above.

    Wuxia should be grandiose, over the top, and it should /feel fast/ in play. If your Wuxia game's combat doesn't /feel like Wuxia/ then it fails, even if the blow-by-blow is exactly like a Wuxia movie. It's important to consider complexity and handling time and make trade-offs against the appeal to simulation in this regard.

    \n Ok, how about (hopefully this text is clear):

    Combat occurs within a limited sequence of moves, back and forth, known as exchanges. One exchange, regardless of who is participating, must resolve before another.

    The current Chi of each participant within an exchange determines how many moves they can perform. Each move is one action attempt beginning with the attacker. If a hero has no moves left he may spend Chi points directly to act, otherwise he loses the exchange. When one party loses an exchange it is over and the consequence is resolved. Combatants without Chi are defenceless.

    Starting with the attacker\u2019s first move, a roll is made. Now the defender must use his first move to roll using an appropriate defensive skill. If we've determined in the game that I've already gotten up close to the president, and you're halfway across the room, it's not believable that you'd be able to get across the room to stop me. If the president is surrounded by a crowd of people and I'm way at the back, unable even to see him, I probably won't get a shot off. In the former case, no one rolls: the president just dies. In the latter case, no one rolls: I don't even get to shoot. Why? Because the fictional circumstances have already decided the question for us.

      In other cases the answer to the question is less clear\u2014for example, if it's you and me and the president in a small room. Now, in spite of how simple or complicated the actions involved might be, there are chances for multiple outcomes. To decide those outcomes, we roll dice, or draw cards, or have a dance-off, or something. What dice we roll or how many cards we draw or which kind of dance we do, in role-playing games, is often determined by numbers and letters on a character sheet.

      You have pointed out that games don't have skills for simple actions like closing windows. That's cool, we can still roll for it, at least in most traditional games. Just like how a simple action like killing someone is now complicated and uncertain because someone opposes it, the simple action of closing a window is now complicated and uncertain because someone opposes it.

      So what do we roll to close that window? If we're playing World of Darkness we might roll opposed Strength checks, or opposed Dexterity checks, or opposed Strength + Dexterity checks, to see who can get to the window first or who can hold it open or shut. We might roll some fighting skills to see if one of us can hit the other and stun them for long enough that we can close the window unopposed. Honestly I don't even know, because I don't know what game we're playing in which we're shutting windows. I can think of ten games off the top of my head which don't even have skills. The version of D&D 5th Edition which I played, back in the early playtests, made every non-combat action an ability score roll; you could have "skills" but they just modified your die roll. So since there is no window-closing skill, like you say, it would just have been a STR or DEX roll.

      tl;dr Simple actions are no longer simple when someone's trying to stop you. That's when we go to the dice.

      EXAMPLE 1
      Five years ago, I created alternative Fu rules for Feng Shui. I wanted the player, like in fire wu movies from the 80's, to tell the name of their strike when they attacked. So I created a system where the player bought different schools and then combined ranks from different schools to create techniques.

      The Flaming Tiger Strike school gave for example a bonus to damage where fire was flaming up when hitting the target. Each rank gave a different bonus. Flaming gave +2, Tiger gave +4 and Strike gave +6. Silent Wood had only two ranks where Silent created soundless attacks and Wood controlled things in the air. When attacking, the player combined rank 1 from one school with rank 2 from another school and rank 3 from a third school. So with the two described schools, the use could create Silent Tiger (silent attack with +4 damage) or Flaming Wood (something in the air that combusted into flames with a +2 damage). My thought was that the building and telling it's name could give a nice feeling to the game.\n
      EXAMPLE 2
      There are several ways of give pointers through the rules. If there's a fictive system where you roll 1d6 against a difficulty and the difficulty of 1 says "Jumping from one skyscape to another, punch through a wall, run on water", that really sets the tone of the game.\n
      EXAMPLE 3
      From the skill set below, give one skill two dice and three other skills one dice each.

      Sword: you use your sword and your scabbard to solve problems.
      Sneak: in the shadows lures danger. Death in the night.
      Jump: you can jump as many floors as the value of your skill.
      Fire: the ability to create fire whenever you want.
      Run: at 1, you can run on any surface like walls or water. At 2, you outrun a cannon ball.
      Strength: to be able to use ridiculous oversized weapons, to punch someone through a wall or knock someone a floor up in the air.
      Apprentice: hinders are sorted out by the relationship between the master and it's apprentice. At 1, you're the apprentice. At 2, you got an apprentice.

      \n I like the Batman/Joker example. Joker's about to shoot a helpless guy, Batman wants to interfere.

      When figuring out mechanical options, here's the first thing I'd ask: what do we care about?

      Although it's technically accurate to say "who goes first?", we only care about that as a step or mechanism toward what we really care about. And that is, "Can Batman prevent the murder?"

      Given that we care about Batman's success or failure in preventing the murder, we can now move on and answer "What factors into determining that?" Again, an obvious answer is "Who goes first?" But that's not the only possibility. Depending on the style of gameplay and the type of fiction you want, there are tons of other options. I'll list 3 below. If they all sound inappropriate to you, cool, that's an important stylistic choice you're making. That said, these all do work in games with different styles.

      1. Who's more determined? This is now a game that views the conflict through the lens of psychology.

      Is the Joker able to focus on the task at hand, or does his sweaty finger slip off the gun's safety as he hears footsteps on the roof above, knowing that a muscular dude is about to come hurtling down onto him?

      Is Batman able to make himself a more tempting target, leaping into the light where the Joker can get a good shot at him, subverting the Joker's intention to shoot his prisoner by giving him something he wants more?

      2. Who's more intimately involved? This is now a game that views the conflict through the lens of relationships.

      Is the Joker moving extra quickly because he's fired up by the adrenaline of showing up his arch-nemesis? Is he performing better, or worse, than he would be if he were facing down mere policemen?

      Is he killing this prisoner to protect his organization from an incursion by the Penguin, who he disdains with a passion, causing his hands to tremble with rage?

      Is the prisoner Batman's new best friend, causing him to panic at the prospect of their loss, and botch the coordination for an early leap?

      3. Who has more points? This is now a resource-management game.

      Has Batman's player been spending turns gathering victory points that they can use to win conflicts? Maybe they do this by losing earlier conflicts, emulating the arc of stories where the hero takes some losses before rallying to win.

      Has the GM been spending his Joker budget on various crimes and capers, or have they been saving it for this showdown?

      My point here is that we don't have to worry about how someone's speed or strength either helps them close a window or doesn't help them (because anyone can close a window). Because what we really care about is whether the protagonist escapes. And to determine that we have tons of options. I am no Wuxia expert, but I might be more interested in a Wuxia game about determination, relationships, and strategic crafting of story arcs, as opposed to a Wuxia game about who's stronger or faster or goes first.

      \n That's a good solution, actually, and no need for an extra initiative roll.

      * Everyone in a fight rolls.
      * You're always either attacking someone (when you have the initiative) or defending against them (when they do).
      * If you have the initiative against your opponent and you roll better than them, you hit them.
      * If you don't have the initiative against your opponent, then if you roll better than them, you turn the tables: now you do.

      So, in the A vs. B vs. C fight:

      * A has the initiative against B (i.e. is attacking B, while B is defending).
      * C is also attacking A, and so has the initiative against A.

      Some hypothetical rolls:\n
      Example 1

      A: 10
      B: 8
      C: 6

      A hits or kills B, and turns the tables against C: now A has the initiative against them both. A is fighting them both off!

      Example 2

      A: 8
      B: 10
      C: 6

      B defends successfully against A, and will attack in the next exchange (i.e. won initiative). A defends successfully against C, and C is now on the defensive. In the next exchange, A could hurt C and B could hurt A.

      Example 3

      A: 6
      B: 10
      C: 8

      B defends successfully against A, and will attack in the next exchange. C hits or kills A. If A is still alive, s/he is in serious trouble next turn!
      \nThe only remaining design problem is "What do you do when the initiative isn't established yet?" For example, if, in the second example, B decides to attack C instead of A.

      You want to keep it simple. How about this:

      * They're both considered to have the initiative against each other (so both roll, and higher roll hits).
      * If the circumstances seem unexpected, an attacker wins initiative automatically (like if you suddenly attack an ally or you get the jump on somebody). Choosing to attack someone first qualifies: you have the initiative by default.

      To make it just a little more interesting, I would build in a little bit more complexity. For example...\n
      When you roll higher than your opponent, see how many points you beat them by:

      If you beat their roll by 5 or more, the opponent is defeated, at your mercy (and dead, if you choose to finish them off).

      If you beat their roll, but not by 5, they must choose one:

      * They suffer a wound, and lose a hit point (or a similar resource)
      * They must give ground to you, ceding their position (maybe falling off something, or on their back)
      * They are disarmed, losing a weapon they are currently using
      \nAnd certain positions give you a bonus to your roll (you have the high ground!).

      That seems like it fulfills the criteria set out so far. What do you think?

      \n Generally, game designs handle "when do you roll" in a few different ways (sometimes a mix in the same game):

      a) Roll when the GM says so. Maybe with some advice to the GM to help decide, but sometimes just left to fiat.

      b) Roll when you've touched on a specific part of the fiction. Like, if you're swinging a sword in the fiction, you use the combat system. If you're opening a window and there are no window-opening rules, you do not roll.

      c) Roll when people don't agree on the outcome. If I swing my sword at you and you and I agree that it hits you in the neck, then we don't need to roll. If I say I am opening the window and you say you want to stop me and I don't want to be stopped, then we resolve it using the mechanics.

      There are probably other general classes of solutions that I'm missing, but that's a good start, right?


      It may or may not be helpful to study what we call, IIEE, which stands for Intent, Initiation, Execution, and Effect, or the four stages everyone goes through when they add to the shared fiction game-space. Dice and mechanics can get involved at any of those stages, and you as a designer choose where you put dice and other contact with the mechanics.

      \n I like systems (like Sorcerer) where everything in a single round happens at the same time. No initiative is necessary.

      Adam: I kill Barb.
      Barb: Uh, no. I jump out the window.
      Charlie: I kill Adam before he can kill Barb.
      GM: Roll.

      If Charlie succeeds with a higher number than Adam, she kills him. Adam's action to kill Barb is interrupted.
      If Charlie succeeds but with a lower number than Adam, she kills him but doesn't stop Adam's action.
      If Charlie fails, then Adam gets to try to kill Barb.

      If Barb succeeds with a higher number than Adam, she gets out the window and Adam doesn't get a roll.
      If Barb succeeds with a lower number than Adam, she gets out the window but Adam still gets an attack roll before.
      If Barb fails, she doesn't get out the window and Adam gets his attack on her.

      If Adam gets a roll, and he succeeds, he kills Barb.
      If Adam gets a roll, and he fails, he doesn't kill Barb.
      If Adam doesn't get a roll, too bad.

      Replace "kill" with "deal damage to" or "inflict a condition on," if you insist.
