This is version 1.3 of what used to be called GDNS official, canonical theory and is now renamed RISS official, canonical theory.

Because getting on the same page about which techniques are kosher and which are iffy is SUPER IMPORTANT to us humans! It's the outside-in approach to finding common value ground!

Now what is RISS? It stands for Rails, Impro, Sandbox and Spike!

In Rails, one or more of the participants is a super talented story writer in the attic and pre-writes a very interesting story that the group can then bring to life and experience in full color at the gaming table! This could be a GM writing "First the players are gonna do X, then they're gonna do Y, then they're gonna do Z" style notes before play, this could be a player writing ten pages of backstory for their character which is going to be recounted closely during play!

In Impro, one or more of the participants is a hippie! They want to encounter choices that would be meaningful diegetically, such as "Should the hard-working cop abandon her family in order to protect them?". The most important life dream of a Impro hippie is that a very unexpected story is created during play that can then be retold to your hippie friends at the next con!

In Spike, one or more of the participants is a power-hungry munchkin! They want to encounter choices that would be meaningful mechanically, such as "Should the hard-working cop equip a +1 one beastmaster sword or a x3 mod lightning round gun in order to best protect her family?". The most important life dream of a Spike munchkin is to test their own might! To face challenges and bravely stare them down with their lifelong journey of system mastery!

In Sandbox, one or more of the participants is a curious individual! They want to examine the clockwork of the system, how will the little ants in the sandhill move around and what will happen when they collide! The most important life dream of a curious and imaginative Sandbox daydreamer is to see their doll's house come to life and see the dolls do What They Would've Done Really. If they were alive. But with spaceships.

Wait, there's more!
These four corners of the RISS quartet may sound like mortal enemies. But that's not the case, they are the best of friends!

    \n The reason I'm calling it "swerving" is because it's what's really distinguishes that sort of impro from just improvising. I.e. if there are some apples on the altar, in two game groups' SIS. In the impro group, they were just made up on the spot, in the sandbox group, the dungeon key might say "four apples in a white onyx bowl [bowl worth 280gp, weighs 1lb]". And in both groups the players ask: I grab one apple, what color is it? And in both groups the facilitator or DM (respectively) says, even though the prep in the Sandbox group doesn't have the color listed, even though the apple in the Impro group was just made up, says without shame, without batting an eyelash, without breaking poker face: "It's red, what do you do?" That's not swerving. That's "be boring", that's building platform, that's saying the obvious thing. Swerving is when that apple suddenly contains a diamond. Which is awesome in an impro group, untruthful in a sandbox group. (Unless the gloracle placed that diamond there.)

      \nFor the first "S", I'd argue that you are committing the same sin most "RPG theorists" do (and then criticize each other for ). When you're talking about "truth" in roleplaying, that's not a description of how or why we play - it's a value judgement. It's you saying, "This kind of roleplaying feels most real for me."

      I think that if you want your theory to hold water, you have to figure out how to express this idea (if it indeed exists) in a way which:

      a) Gives people who align with the idea a description they can identify with and celebrate. ("Yes! That's what we do.")
      b) Gives people who play a different way a clear sense that this is NOT what they are doing.

      Describing play as "feeling tangible" or "feeling real" is close to the idea of "immersion", and one which very, very few people would actually say they *aren't looking for*. Of course I want my gaming to feel tangible, real, true!

      It's not that far removed from saying "I like gaming which is fun for me, where the stuff that's happening feels really cool."
      \nThe sense I've gotten from the past month of posts is that what we're talking about here is "what is the causal structure we attempt to apply to find out what we agree happens next?" and the Sandbox answer is "Try to use the physics analogue the GM has created." This includes how objects react to each other. It includes the initial state of the world as populated by the GM. It includes populating parts of the world the GM forgot/neglected, by random tables, what would be there as if something reasonable was in the initial state and then interacted with the world via the physics analogue, etc. It explicitly DOES NOT take as input past or counterfactual player actions except as filtered through characters and the physics analogue - so previous low morale at the table cannot produce an easier encounter now, but previous poisoning players saying their characters poisoned a water supply definitely could. Counterfactual "players would find it tense if the villain turned out to be following them all along" cannot make it more likely the villain is currently following them, but "the villain in physics analogue would notice the characters are searching for something powerful hidden and would generate as a possible strategy 'follow them'" can.

      \n Ok, that was...a longer thread than I expected. But I did want to know what the RISS people were talking about in other threads was. A few reactions, maybe going backwards a bit:

FInally, if neither of those two provide a solid answer, you add in a Fortune card (which fundamentally means using the fortune card and the situation to produce a Karma or Drama answer; the designers have cleverly hidden the designed-in Karma clues on the cards in order to encourage Drama results here; again pointing to an Impro/Rails focus).

Fundamentally, it's Rails (and I have to wonder if you can keep the RISS name by moving Rails into Story and something else onto the R letter) if someone has a strong idea of where the story should go and the group accedes to this idea--whether this is traditional top-down rails, or a Fate compel, or people playing out their personal story as described by Lady Blackbird Keys. It's all about being a strong storyteller/plotter and deciding on story elements based on that sense of structure -- opposed to Impro, which more embodies AW's central concept of "Keep it feral" and never letting anyone decide where the story is going to go in advance.
