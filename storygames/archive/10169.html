\n The great and lovely Eric J. Boyd is in town, and we got together for an old-fashioned throwdown last night. I wanted to put something together to play with him and naturally thought of Lady Blackbird. Having recently hacked it into a historical game, I was all set to hack it into something else, and my thoughts turned to a gritty post-apocalyptic game. I quickly worked up four characters all pointed at each other and a very simple situation - a grist mill co-op, a stable community, and their dangerous gangster neighbors. I sweetened the pot by making the grist mill the target of a "hostile takeover", and threw some hostages into the mix. That was all I had going in, so I did a ton of fishing and letting the players add information and details. It went great, even though I got the die pool rules 100% backwards, which made every conflict more or less a cake-walk. We were all "Aaaaaah that makes more sense" once we realized the error.

My favorite moment was Eric, playing the lying, manipulative weasel Statesville Mike, buying off his Key of Cowardice when faced with the wrath of the warlord Clegg. Clegg's minions were about to lop off his hand and Statesville Mike pulled out a grenade he'd stolen, popped the pin, and earned two advances.

Because I had essentially no background or color going in, I surprised myself, too. They met Clegg's chief lieutenant, a ne'er-do-well named Zanni, and when they asked how old he was I said "18". From this it developed that most of Clegg's troops were dead-eyed child soldiers who had come up on unending war after the collapse.

I really like the way Lady Blackbird encourages a bit of narrative flourish as you struggle to incorporate tags. I like the buckets of dice. Everybody liked conditions, but they didn't get a lot of use (probably because they have no mechanical weight). It's a fun system that really took the best from its sources and I'd love to see it streamlined, maybe better explained, and put in a form that would allow quick pick up play.

    \n Jason: Here's my thoughts after reflecting on it for a bit:

    We only used our pool dice a few times, and most of the successes were won based on the size of our aspects alone. I just looked again at the original Blackbird files, and I think that there might have been an underlying problem: Your aspects (or whatever they're called: The descriptors we used to gain dice that are attached to the role) were *too stackable*. Looking over at Lady Blackbird, it looks like some pains were taken to make sure that they were "spread out", and not readily applicable. And while I can see that maybe one character who is crazy-good at one thing might be able to stack up a bunch of their descriptors, it seemed that a lot of us had at least one thing we could do (Gert = Crunching People, Mike = Conning People, Jake = Leading People, Joey's character = Dirty Tricks While Fighting People) to a degree where we were *easily*, without trying very hard, pulling in 5-6 dice per roll. Whereas looking at the original LB sheets, it appears to be *much harder* to do that without really stretching out the situation or description to accommodate those descriptors.

    My suggestions, either:
    1) Take a second look at the descriptors, and slim down the lists a bit.
    2) Perhaps better, do the same as 1, but create another Aspect for each character, and perhaps move some of the overlap from the other aspects into the new aspect?

    Just a thought. But still, I had a lot of fun, and honestly I don't mind the fact that we were winning a lot, it was very satisfying in contrast to many of the "boo hoo I'm all emo while failing at everything" games we often play. :-)

    -Andy
