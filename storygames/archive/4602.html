    Posted By: Peter AronsonAn Amazon search for books by Christian Aldridgereturns four different books with the same symbol on them:
    Maelstrom Storytelling;Story Engine: Universal Rules;Dacartha Prime: Maelstrom;Book of Gray.
    And a general search of the internet seems to return other books, such asTales from The Empire. So which books or books were you recommending?
    \nMaelstrom Storytelling is the core rule/setting book, the book that really makes the game. This is the only book that you really need to buy, and the only one that I personally recommend (though the Book of Grey isn't bad).

    Story Engine is a generic rule set spawned from the system that drives Maelstrom Storytelling. This standalone product is sometimes referred to as "Version 2" of the Story Engine (Version 1 being that present in MS).

    Decartha Prime is a setting book (it and Tales From the Empire were the two books that I never bought for the line).

    Book of Grey is a digest-sized supplement for Maelstrom written from the perspective of a setting resident, and also (IIRC) contains the 'Quick Play' rules known as "Story Bones".

    Tales From the Empire is a setting book (for the city of Diodet) and contains minor system revisions/additions.

    Precis Intermedia now owns the rights to the game and offers all books except the Book of Grey as PDFs or POD books.

    \n There are only two games that have ever blown my mind. Like, where I sit down, not sure what to expect, and come away thinking, "Wow, this totally changed my thinking about what a roleplaying game can be about." Not after reading the whole book, not after thinking about it for a while and coming to some realization, not even in the way that Dogs in the Vineyard made me think, "Wow, this is the tightest design I have ever read." I mean games that, after reading the first couple chapters, I had to put the book down and just think about it for a while, realizing the ramifications of what I'd read, before continuing.

    These games are Continuum and Nobilis. One is the greatest time travel game ever written (though one of the most paperwork intensive to play), the other is the crazy love child of Ars Magicka, Amber, Mage, and Kult. I thought roleplaying was, you know, this big <---------->. They showed me that roleplaying does not have borders, that roleplaying can do anything you can conceive of.
