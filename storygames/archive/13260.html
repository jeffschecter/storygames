\n Howdy Folks,

So, I have not had the pleasure of playing Lady Blackbird, but I stare at the pdf and covet it in the night.

Ahem.

At any rate, I've been looking over the mechanics and pondering running it for some friends who are more into the traditional RPGs (super original, I know), and I had a question. Has anyone had issues of people giving the most barebones possible descriptions to get their dice for actions? I'm thinking something like "Okay, so I'm athletic, and this is a fencing duel, and I'm using a rapier. I'll roll 4 dice." without any further fun or embellishment.

I've become a big fan of mechanics not only encouraging, but downright requiring engagement with the game's fiction, but considering all the positive AP I've read about this, it's making me wonder if I'm getting *too* paranoid of more freeform stuff. So any anecdotes of the traits and tags spurring on awesome fiction, or the opposite, would be really helpful to me. Thanks!

    \n Yes, this a danger in Lady Blackbird. It's also a danger in Mouse Guard particularly with helping dice.

    It's a problem that crops up in any system where you're supposed to describe what you're doing to justify using the die but in reality often the die is justified intellectually. Look at your own example. Assuming the character is an close combat situation is there any DOUBT those things relevant? So adding the description really isn't about justification. No one at the table is going to question the use of those traits. They intellectually understand why they're applicable even without description of action.

    In these kinds of situations this question has become my friend: "What does that look like?" It can get kind of exhausting to constantly have to step in and say, "What does that look like?" But some GMs don't seem to mind. I like this question for two reasons.

    1) It isn't confrontational. As I said, no one is questioning the validity of the trait use; we just want more information.

    2) It helps inform outcome description. This is most obvious in the case of failure. I've run into situations where I forgot to ask the question, the roll has failed and then I suddenly realized I had no idea what appropriate consequences were because I had no idea what was actually being done. Fortunately this is salvageable just by asking, "EXACTLY what you were trying to do?" The same is true for success.

    So those are my thoughts.

    Jesse

    \n I have had some difficulty converting my long-time D&D friends to more narrative-heavy games, though in my case it was Danger Patrol.

    With Lady Blackbird, what's important, I think, it to emphasize that their actions are not brief moments like in "other" games, where your turn is a single blow or a couple of swipes with a sword. Instead, they need to think of it as a moment in a movie or comic. What they are doing is not saying what their character is doing "right now," but what the character is trying to accomplish in the scene. Once they realize they are framing the narrative action for a relatively longer span of time, and that their descriptions help develop the fiction over time, they will typically become much more comfortable with using their tags in inventive and fun ways, and will contribute more details.

    A good comparison to give them is say, on the one hand, "I hit him with my rapier," and on the other hand, "With the footing so poor, I take advantage of the situation with my athletic prowess, advancing with my rapier darting forward to puncture this poor fellow who had the audacity to challenge me to a fencing duel."

    \n Hi Jeff,

    You should get your group together at the next possible opportunity and run the game. Seriously. You will all enjoy the hell out of it.

    As for people dice-mongering, don't give it another thought. Let them. If one player is making every effort to get dice in uninteresting ways, it's fine. He'll win his conflicts and you'll throw up a new one. If another player is being much harder on herself, she'll lose her conflict, and you'll throw an interesting twist up for them.

    Think of it not as win/loss but as different kinds of changes to the unfolding story.

    Oh, and if the first guy is really being lame, rag on him (nicely) and encourage the others to do the same. He'll probably step up.

    \n I'm with everyone else. People can be "lame" and just mention the tags they're using and roll, but you have some tools to make that a little more fun than it seems. I often let people narrate, and in my experience if they go for it with a lot of enthusiasm it either a) keys them to the fact that they can narrate their actions more or b) doesn't change a thing about their dice selection, but gets them pumped to narrate after the fact (oft times going back to the Traits/Tags/Secrets they used for inspiration).

    Other times players just won't give a shit about being particularly creative with their dice selection. That's okay! If they have more fun gaming the system and letting you describe things to them, that's their prerogative. I don't think there's anything wrong with that, unless you're super into it and no one else in your group is. That would get to me, I think.

    \n It makes me think of the way Vincent Baker advices to lead the player into describing in Apocalypse World. To begin with, an essential principle of the game is "to do it, you have to do it" which is to say that the mechanics are prescriptive and descriptive at the same time. And when a player forgets the description and is all "I Go Aggro on him. I hit 11, woohoo ! Who's next ?", all the MC (AW name for GM) has to say is "Cool ! How do you do that ?". This way, you first assert the action in the fiction, rewarding the player by complimenting him. And then, you ask him to describe what is happening in the fiction.

    Cool ! How do you do that ?
    I love this sentence, it sounds heavily studied to enhance the social cohesion by prevent the classic "No no, don't roll the dice without describing" which is discouraging.

    \n "What does that look like?" is my go-to, most-used tool in my toolkit, I think. I've used it for a decade or so, across all games. Works wonderfully.

    I am sort of boggled by what seems to be complaints of a plague of lazy descriptions in actual play elsewhere. Isn't describing things and anchoring things in the fiction fully half of the reason we play RPGs? And when we sometimes gloss over things, isn't that a form of pacing inherent in the medium?

    But whatever. Yay to "What does that look like?" and "What does that feel like?" and all of the sibling questions that help us remember to render the action in our imaginations.

    Cheers,
