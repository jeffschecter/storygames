    \n There's no such thing as badwrongfun, of course. But...

    Just to get clear here, we're talking about the kind of resolution where two or more sides eventually decide on where they'd like the story to go from here, and then mechanics decide which of the two or more stories is the one that actually happens, right?

    In PTA specifically, I dislike this because it reduces the impact of the high card player's narration. By the text, the highh card player gets to put the final stamp on what actually happens, constrained only by having to award the stakes as decided by the cards. But if the stakes included everything down to the minute detail, then that player has nothing to do.

    In the more general case, I dislike this kind of prenegotiation because game mechanics, I feel, should be more than a way to arbitrate disagreements between players about which direction the fiction should go in. I like game mechanics to take us to places which neither side wanted (or more properly, to places that neither side had yet imagined).

    Also, isn't part of the point of negotiating any stakes to get to what folks are comfortable with?
    \nNo, I don't believe so. I think that, in PTA at least, stakes are simply what the protagonist(s) in the scene wants most out of the scene (and one should be careful about generalising "stakes" across games, as I think the term is used differently in different games). Indeed, I don't think that in PTA (the way I play it) the stakes themselves ought to be "negotiated" much if at all. Indeed, reading the short section on stakes setting in the PTA rules I have here reveals that the text does not suggest that stakes be negotiated, but merely that each protagonist's player should simply state what their protagonist's stakes are.

    It's enough for each player to say what their character wants to get, and then let the cards tell them if they get it. The purpose of stakes in PTA (as I play it) is not to ensure that all players are happy with all potential story outcomes, but to ensure that all protagonists have their desires represented.

    Another point: I'm not advocating as such that people should leave open options that none of them actually want to see. there's a distinction between "an outcome nobody had pre-narrated" and "an outcome nobody wants", or even "an outcome everyone actively dislikes".

    I don't change my intent, because to my mind it's a totally suitable complication.

    Me: "Okay. Fine. So on a failure, you lose sight of your mark."

    Him: "What? Then I've just totally wasted one of my three rolls? Okay...my intent is to follow them to the right street so at least we have someplace to start."

    \n To be clear, and I'm pretty sure you know this already Bob, but I'm personally a big fan of prenegotiation. But IME, it definitely requires all the players be on the same page as to what counts as "interesting." Because otherwise, the "if you fail" exercise becomes not only meaningless, but a specific point of friction.

    In D's case above, what is "interesting" to him is him maximizing his success and minimizing his failure. The reason he's at the table is to beat the GM's challenge. So it's a conflict of interest for him to allow me to impose complications on his character's life in the event of a failure -- there is literally no reason at all for him to do so.     But in any case, this example highlights the importance of making failure fun and interesting, so that there's no result that's just plain suck or dead-end. When you don't have to fear failure as a game-breaker or adventure-killer, you can play more boldly and have more fun in general.

    My touchstone for this is Heroquest, where "dead" isn't a die result, period. "Dying" is, but you can always get rescued or healed in an appropriately dramatic manner. Thus death is a result of the players agreeing that yep, it's time for this guy to die, and if it's not that time, then failing is great! You can be beaten, captured, tortured, humiliated. . .or any number of adverse results that enrich the story instead of sucking the life from it.

    And no, HQ doesn't have "stakes," but games that do often have the same emergent feature. I'm awfully fond of Stakes in Shadow of Yesterday; very simple and direct; on each roll you've got one good thing to happen and one bad thing. The good thing cements what the character is trying to accomplish and provides a concrete metric for whether he succeeds. The bad thing ensures that the story moves forward whichever way the dice toss, and introduces fun complications. It's a robust alternative to "succeed and you accomplish your task, fail and nothing happens" type situations.

    Example: I was GMing TSoY, and my friend's Mathew's PC was trapped in the library of a burning building, having just found a rare scroll of prized information. He attempted to bash his way through the wall as the flames closed in. I set the losing stakes as, "the scroll catches fire." He failed. So the scroll caught fire, not completely burned, but part of the information lost. Thus his quest would continue, but complicated by incomplete lore. In a different system, the scene could well have fallen flat, with a failed roll either just plain killing him, or just meaning he had to roll again (and again, and again) until he made it. This way, failure cost him something, without simply bumping him off.

    Now, there was no negotiation. There could have been, like in Paul and D's case, over whether I was asking too much, and either of us could have amended our statements (in fact, Matthew was aghast at the thought of losing the scroll but only mentioned this after the roll. I, however, never had any intention of destroying the scroll; that'd be a lame and uninteresting screwjob). But in the absence of any objections, negotiation was unneeded.

    I guess what I'm trying to say is, I realized as I was reading these threads that negotiation happens in nearly all games t some extent. most of the time, you'll just be announcing actions and resolving them, but every so often you come up against a "wait, if I'd a' known that I would have done this instead" situation, or a "hmm, guys, what'd be the best way to resolve this?" question. And then people furrow their brows, consult rules, bandy options, arrive at a decision, and move on. it's not game-breaking; it's fine. In fact, TSoY addresses this specifically in the rules (hell, so does Sorcerer). It's just that negotiation is there as needed, not assumed to be the default process, and it's certainly not the same as actually playing the scene. The scene is in fact "on pause" while you hammer it out, then resumes. Which is why you can get some good negotiation without pre-playing if you stick to negotiating specific actions of immediate intent.

    \n Sigh. History Lesson. Again.

    This is my summary of Conflict Resolution as originally conceptualized by Ron Edwards on The Forge.

    "Let us step back and re-examine Conflict Resolution vs. Task Resolution. The key concept in Conflict Resolution is that it resolves the conflict of interest between two [in fiction] pro-active and motivated forces. I swing a tire iron at your head while you try to sweep kick me. That\u2019s a conflict. Just because the rules resolve these individual actions doesn\u2019t mean it\u2019s suddenly Task Resolution.

    The difference between Task and Conflict is whether or not there exists [in fiction] an opposing pro-active, motivated and intentioned force. That\u2019s why resolving climbing a fence is TASK resolution because the fence is not out to purposefully stop me, especially in light of the person whom I trying to catch fleeing on the other side. This concept is sometimes muddled by the fact that there are times when an inanimate object can be considered to be acting with motivated and intentioned force and there are times when a living agent is really just the weapon or color being used by the real pro-active, motivated and intentioned force."

    BOTH Task and Conflict Resolution as originally hashed out strictly concern the in-fiction actions of the in-fiction characters.

    Mechanics which parcel out PLAYER desires about things are NEITHER Task NOR Conflict Resolution. They're mechanics with parcel out PLAYER desires about things. They *resolve* nothing in terms of the fiction.

      \n Robert,

      In attempt to meet you half way, what you're really talking about Consequence Negotiation if we consider Outcome to be Yay or Nay on Intention.

      Because the FULL resolution of an in-fiction action involve the character taking action, the intention behind the action, and the consequences for succeeding or failing in that intention. The part I'm against is pre-negotiating those consequences.

      And then there's the whole issue of Content Authority beyond an at-hand fictional conflict. For example, someone saying, "It's raining" and a player being able to spend a resource or roll a die or whatever to say, "No, it's not" or "It's snowing instead." Universalis for example uses Tokens for this kind of thing. I'm not *fond* of those mechanics because I think they distract from the fictions momentum. I much prefer investing someone with that authority like a GM or Round-Robin (like Spione or Dirty Secrets) for that kind of thing.

      I think people tend to muddle those two things such that pre-negotiation tends to mix those two things into a frustrating mess.

      Does that break down at least help?

      I think Jesse pretty much nailed it on his Play Passionately blog, at least for me. It's about being author and audience at the same time, without neglegting one or the other. I hope it's OK to quote him here, the entire post is this: The Slippery Slope of Stakes.

      Jesse wrote: "Most often what the trip down this slope is about is protecting the player\u2019s vision of how the story \u201cshould\u201d go. The simplest form this takes concerns pre-deciding whether the character should succeed or fail at the conflict at hand and then Stakes become about negotiating what the consequences of that are.

      "Constantly coming to vigorous creative agreement about how the story \u201cshould\u201d go and always going for what the players *want* to have happen means the players never challenge themselves. They never let themselves be surprised (in the audience sense) and are never forced to re-evaluate where they want to go next (in the author sense)."

      That explains it to me, and I think this is central to this discussion. Not how many possible outcomes you care to negotiate. Not if it's "wrong" play.
