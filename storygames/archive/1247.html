THE CENTURY CLUB NEEDS YOUR HELP!


The deadly Doctor Methuselah seeks to unravel time itself with his solution to the Eternity Equation... Gorilla Khan stalks darkest Africa from conquered Atlantis... Mad scientists, strange sorcerors, and power-hungry dictators all seek to undo the fate of humanity. It's the final century of the second millennium -- and you are our last hope!

BECOME A MEMBER OF THE CENTURY CLUB!


Spirit of the Century is a complete, stand-alone pulp role-playing game based on the award-winning Fate system (Indie RPG Awards include: Best Free Game of 2003, Best Support, and Andy's Choice). Spirit features a heavily revised, revisited, and reimagined vision of Fate. Character creation can be done as quickly as a few minutes, or expand to take up an evening; adventure design is a snap with three methods for creating relevant, flavorful, player-focused stories at a moment's notice. Spirit's mission is to deliver an evening of fun, a "pick-up" game that requires little preparation, but provides hours of entertainment.

All you will need to play are some friends, some dice, and this book.

  • Rob Donoghue
    September 2006
    Ok, here's the rub. There's a decent amount of setting material, but it's probably less than 100 pages - it's a comparable volume to the amount dedicated to tips, tricks and GM tools. That may seem odd, since it suggest a kind of GURPsian density of rules which, combined with the sheer volume of the book may seem entirely old school but that is a deceptive impression.

    There is a weird focus throughout the book of turning any element in the book into an expression of how it comes out in play. Skill writeups for example, include normal things like descriptions and rules, btu they also include a lot of detail on things like how a character can be cool if _this_ is their signature skill, and as a GM, how to help make that character feel cool. This is true of every freaking skill in the book. Academics kick ass.

    Anyway, that takes up a lot of space, so that sort of thinking has made for a very thick book, but I think it's one that may defy assumptions. But then, I would think that.

    So, to get to the concrete question, releasing just the setting material isn't currently on our radar, but hell, who knows. Beyond that, I still totally dig that the price point is beyond a casual curiosity pickup. Naturally, I hope there will be some reviews and comment in the future to help move it past curiosity, and if I can answer any questions to that end, I'll be more than happy to give them a try. Barring that, once the preorder period ends we _will_ be offering the PDF (which Fred has busted hump to make sure is available in multiple presentations for ease of reading) as well for those so inclined.

    Hope that helps.

    -Rob D.

  • Rob Donoghue
    September 2006 edited September 2006
    There are two ways to do character generation.

    The first is to dedicate a session to doing so. Get everyone together and build a kind of shared history with what characters did during their childhoods, the Great War and in in their subsequent adventures, including naming their Pulp Adventure Novel and figuring out which other characters had minor roles in that book. It is powerful and awesome

    The second takes 5 minutes and does all the rest of the work during play. It is also powerful and awesome.

    Beyond that, the amount of the book she'd actually want to read is a fraction of the whole. Maybe 10 pages or less of background stuff if she's already a pulp head (bear in mind a lot of history is material that will already be familiar to Pulp fans, so it's only the differentiators, which are early and clearly laid out, that bear reading) plus about 3 pages of rules (resolution is really pretty easy), and then I'd suggest she read the entries for the three skills she's interested in just for ideas on how they can rock. She can take a quick pick for the stunts, and probably read those eventually, and voila.

    I'll not say the Gm gets off nearly as easy, but getting a player (with period knowledge) started in 25 pages or less? Totally doable. Yeah, we screw ourselves a little on the "Sell a copy to everyone at the table" demographic, but we figure once the GM has it, players will get one because they want it, not because it is so complex that they MUST.

    (That said, I think there are sections, especially in tips and tricks, that a player would _benefit_ from reading, but that's a different creature than necessity.)

    -Rob D.

    PS - Oh, and one more selling point. We had a pretty rigorous editing process, but the strongest, harshest part of it was the final round of the game being read, cover to cover, by a non-gamer and flagged anywhere it didn't make sense to her. There are a lot of assumptions that are easy for a game writer to make that translate into "What the hell are they talking about?" for a reader and it is our hope that we managed to get rid of those and produce something that makes for a decent read.

    We are dedicated to wife-friendly gaming here at Evil Hat, because if we were not, it might go very, very badly for us.

    PPS - Fred has corrected me. the 25 page assertion is based on an assumption of doing fast creation. if you are doing the more involved creation the total goes up to 40ish, provided you're still doing stunt quick picks. If you wish to fully customize your stunt choices, the door does open up a bit more there into the three digit category.
