    I like the answer above, personally, that's how we do it. You can "bank" the improvements to use that at appropriate moments in play. (This is how it works in The Shadow of Yesterday, which is where this character advancement model seems to come from.)

    As a notable exception/difference, Monsterhearts (an Apocalypse World hack) specifies that you cannot collect any more experience until you've chosen an improvement. It wouldn't surprise me all that much if I found out that some people play Apocalypse World this way too.

    Edit: My preferred way is that the improvement kicks in immediately (even in the middle of a fight, whatever) unless the group feels it would be totally jarring (for example, taking control of a gang: you can't apply that all of a sudden if the character's alone in someone's basement -- at least not usually, that is).

    I only meant my previous statement as a suggestion to solve this problem: don't level up. You are what you are, and the world is about to collide with your character is a rough and violent way and you don't have the time to learn new skills in order to deal with it.
    \nYou know, it's funny. I totally agree with this, and I think that a lot of (or most) RPGs tend to include "character advancement" simply because it was part of the standard/D&D model, which is unfortunate. I like games without this kind of character advancement, and most of my own designs have limited advancement or none at all (or sometimes allow you to reconfigure your character rather than add things).

    However, in Apocalypse World I feel like character advancement is a really powerful and interesting tool which is one of the drivers for the game's drama.

    A big part of this is how it is often qualitative rather than quantitative. For instance, I think the most significant and dramatic advance is "change to another playbook". It reshapes the story and shows how it has changed the character. Move choices similarly change the direction of the story in significant ways: consider what happens when the Angel in your game, who has a bunch of important people under his care, takes the "touched by death" move!

    This is a really powerful way for the players to communicate with each other and push the story in different directions. It forces the characters to change and for their interaction with the world to take new forms, which drives the story to develop further and in different directions. This is especially clear in the "advanced moves" (like making allies, seeing beyond the maelstrom, and so on).

    Purely mechanical improvements (like many "add to a stat" or "stat substitution" moves) are much less interesting to me (and I could even see removing them from the game as an option), but they too serve an important purpose. They allow us, as a group, to raise the stakes as we move through the story: the protagonists might not have the power to change the world at the beginning of the campaign, or they might try and fail, but when it comes down to the last straw and they confront whatever is facing them, they have a chance of prevailing.

    There is no point to try to explain this via "in-game logic", it's purely a dramatic conceit. I like to pretend that the characters have no idea that Keeler's hard went from 0 to +2: no, it's just that in this story she's finally gritting her teeth and has grown a backbone and finally things are going her way more often, because she's suffered enough and the story will end when she finally gets to do whatever it was she was too afraid to do at the beginning. The characters outgrow their humbler beginnings and eventually face those conflicts which have truly major stakes--this is how stories typically work, and in a game like AW, mechanical improvements are just a tool which helps us get there more easily as we play.

    \n Yes. *World games-style advancement works like movie logic, not real-world logic.

    \n The type of character that changes over the course of a narrative, the one with a story arc, is the dramatic character. The other type of character, the one that stays true to themselves in the face of adversity and overcomes by not changing, is the kind that isn't dramatic (it has a name that people use but I don't remember what it is). So, by definition, characters that employ advancement or improvement mechanics are dramatic characters.

    A 20th level wizard has more spells than even a well-organized person can keep track of, including spells like wish, where a 1st level wizard has one spell. And the 20th level wizard has about ten times as many hit points. So in either sense of the word, it's a more dramatic change than what you get from an AW character after 10 improvements, because those two AW character can still co-exist comfortably in a game. The 20th level wizard sees a 1st level wizard and just says "why are you even here?" But less dramatic doesn't equal not dramatic.

    The reason, I think, that Orlando wanted you to read that post of mine is because I give three good reasons for having an improvement system in a game:
    1. A long-term story arc.
    2. The desire to obtain xp and advance can be used to encourage behaviours that the game needs to function.
    3. The player can dictate changes to their character from the POV of the player, during play.

    (And the fourth reason, in the second half of the post, was specific to games we were designing at the time and not relevant here.)

    Judging from the posts you've made in this thread, it seems like none of these are your bag? I mean, the point of that post wasn't to make value judgments on any of these or to say these are necessary -- only that if you want any of these three, an advancement system is one way to go about it, and that if none of these are good enough reasons to have an advancement system... then don't have one. You don't have to like an of these things and you don't have to like advancement systems. It is okay.

    What you can't do, though, is reject these as legitimate reasons for advancement. It's good we have games where we can "start at awesome" and I like these, but I also play a lot of slow and brutal old school D&D and there is a tremendous sense of accomplishment that comes with long-term exploration and levelling-up. Provided it is also fun, and not repetitive grinding of wolf-kill quests, but if it was that I wouldn't still be playing.

    Likewise, the desire for xp in old school D&D drives the game amazingly well, but it's also a powerful incentive in AW. When a player tries their hardest to only roll their highlighted moves during a session, the people I play with call this "Johnstone's disease," but when I do this it's partly the challenge of finding ways to use these stats that appease the person who highlighted the stat but are also character-appropriate AND don't fuck up the flow of the game.

    The third one is one of the things people can mean when they say improvements can have a cinematic quality to them. You play the character as the character for a little while, and then you step back and say "you know, I want to change something so I can explore a different part of this character." The other one is the one time where improvements are technically un-dramatic, which is when they reveal an aspect of the character that we, the players and audience, have not yet seen. After three sessions, the Savvyhead could take augury for her workspace and just say it was there all this time and she just hadn't used it yet. The Brainer could take In-Brain Puppet Strings or both +1 hard advancements after barely ever rolling+hard and just say, "this was me all along, I was being soft on y'all." I don't think there have been any examples of this kind of advancement in the thread so far because the tank and lay on hands improvements were changes, so they were dramatic, but both are examples of additions to the shared narrative from a player perspective, not a character perspective. Which is something you don't get in D&D.

    The actual justification for the advancement is just that -- a justification. Finding treasure doesn't really make you a better fighter but we don't care, we want to play D&D the way we play it so we just say it's a way to measure how you get better from practicing dungeoncrawling. Advances in AW are essentially a reward for participating, and many people like that reward, whether it's a story-arc (and whether you call 10-15 sessions long- or short-term is up to you), or a feeling of accomplishment, or new toys to play with, or the ability to dictate certain aspects of the fiction. People can't always articulate why it's rewarding, either, even when it obviously is for them.

    \n On the issue of "the magic healing touch was something that was not true before, now it is, that's hella weird" (my paraphrase):

    The AW rules don't simulate a universe. I won't say they simulate a narrative, because they don't have a dramatic curve or anything built into them. But they do simulate a piece of fiction. Especially the important distinction of "this is decidedly true", "this is decidedly not true", and "this has not been determined yet".

    When a PC does not have the Healing touch move, of course he doesn't have the ability. If you'd try to lay on hands and look expectantly at the MC, they'd make a hard move. But if you don't try, and then take the advancement, it is not the case that your character suddenly (and apparently for everyone watching in-game) went from "not healing touch" to "healing touch". The fact that you could not heal with a touch was not an established fact, just like the fact that there was no hidden tank in that garage. We just didn't know what was in there.

    If you play an otherwise weird character, like a hocus or savvyhead or brainer (or any class if you play up that side and open your brain a lot), it is not hard to keep this option open. In order for the advancement to not feel out-of-the-blue when you take it, you (the player) have to keep in mind what moves from other playbooks you can see yourself taking. If you know that you're going to want Healing touch somewhere down the line, play your character as the type who could seriously, i a moment of desperation, just place their hands on a wounded comrade and pray to the maelstrom. Then when you're in that situation and have an advance, bam! You use Healing touch.

    If, on the other hand, the fiction of your game (for whatever reason, maybe your Angel player said so when asked by the MC) stated that the Healing touch is something that the weird albino tribe in the south have, you're going to have a harder time justifying it. If you want to take that advance, it's on you to make it work (but the others, including MC, are allowed to help and I know I would if I were MCing). The fiction has to acknowledge the fact that "hey, we all thought only albino southerners could do that but know this guy does. What's up with that?" And that's just the same as when, I'm pressed for a good example, General Grievous uses lightsabers despite not being a Jedi. The rules simulate a fiction, and if you acknowledge that what's established in the ficton must be respected but other stuff need not, then you're good.
