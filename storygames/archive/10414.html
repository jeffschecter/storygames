    Answering as a purchaser here...

    \n

    It's not really black or white, but I use the following criteria:

    \n
      \n
    • The more I have to go to the internet to figure out how to play the game, the less complete it is. (Yes, it could be a failure of understanding on my part, but let's assume I'm trying really hard to understand.)
      • \n
    • The more rules I have to make up to play the game, the less complete it is.
      • \n
    • The more situations I encounter while we play that the rules don't address, the less complete it is. "Wait, who narrates in this part? What? Umm..."
      • \n
    \n

    Basically, I'm not a game designer, and if I have to do a lot of "game designer" type things to be able to play the game, it's not complete for me. A game where I can sit down and follow the procedure a-b-c, and have a game happen, it's complete.

    This is a really good question, Paul.

    \n

    For me, as a purchaser, I generally just check for red-flag words (letters early in the Greek alphabet, 'ashcan', 'playtest', etc) and if I'm in doubt I ask somebody. Done for me is in the eye of the designer.

    \n

    As a designer, I ask myself, "Am I satisfied with the play experience this text provides? Have I shown it to be reproducible without my interpretation, to at least one group? Did I supply not only the procedures required for play, but the 'soft' stuff (environmental things, lists of physical objects you'll need, thought processes that are useful, an accessible way to get into the source material, etc. etc.) that make play go smoothly and enjoyably? Does it look good on the shelf and feel good in my hands? Can I make it better?"

    \n

    I like to be able to say yes to all these questions, including the last - that's because I want to know that I understand my game well enough to explain and extend it. If I don't know my work well enough to build on it, I am not prepared to send it out in the wild.

    \n I was just wondering this question myself. I'm working on a game, and I'm pretty petrified that when it's all said and done it'll be incomplete or just plain shitty. I hope it will be complete when:

    - It consistently produces the kind of play I'm going for in game sessions that I am not at.
    - It's as unambiguous as possible to readers.
    - It's passed through the hands of several people who are not me for critique and input leading to revision.
    - It is in an acceptable format in terms of how I wan to deliver the information to players (meaning pdf or book or mp3 or whatever).

    My criteria as a purchaser is a lot less strict. If I can run the game and have fun, it's good enough.

    \n\nEero_Tuovinen \n\n\n
    \n
    \n\nSeptember 2009 \n

    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n Completed games are a myth, like Santa Claus. One of these myths is used to keep children in line, the other is used to keep game designers in line. Complete games are like game line support, a psychological crutch.

    I can tell you whether a game is worthwhile or not, but I cannot tell you whether it is complete. I can redefine the concept to mean something like "the designer doesn't find it worthwhile to change anything anymore", but surely that does not equal anything like completeness in the natural meaning of the word. When people complain that a game is unfinished or claim that they only want to buy or play finished games, they invent an objective-seeming name for purely subjective criteria. When we upbraid a designer for publishing an unfinished game, we do not mean literally this: we only mean that the designer's workflow does not adhere to a set of standards we have personally internalized and accept as a valid gauge of completeness. Regardless, those standards are not universal or objective, and most importantly, they do not point directly towards the game as an object, but only towards the procedures of game creation: we criticize the lack of outside playtesting, for example, or lack of editing, or incompetence in explaining the whole game clearly, but on what grounds could we claim that the game itself is incomplete as an object? The best I've ever heard is a cry of incomprehension when the receiver has failed to understand the message; this proves only that this instance of communication has broken down, and tells us almost nothing about the game itself, lurking behind the message.

    \n There is a philosophical thing that a game is never truly finished, because (1) it's a creative thing, and (2) it's the beginning of a dialogue, even more so than something like a normal book. The real game happens at game tables, and isn't something the designer can fix into written form.

    For the more pragmatic thing, a game should work for competent players with little to no reliance on outside resources.[*] If I have to post up lots of errata, or if you have to come to me (or other people) to ask questions just to be able to play, I've fucked up as a designer.

    [*]Every relevant word in there has appropriate wiggle room depending on the particular game and its audience.
