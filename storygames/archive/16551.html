\n I wanted to post to talk about how awesome camp nerdly was this year. Camp Nerdly was awesome this year.

We had a bunch of new people, but not so many that the culture of camp nerdly was overwhelmed. Which was awesome. It was great to meet new people, or game with people who had been around a while but I had never gamed with.

The food this year was awesome. Sure I didn't have bacon every morning, but I didn't care at all. The food was good. The food went out with less effort than usual. The food got cleaned up with less effort than usual. I got to game through lunch. No one got stuck out of a game or had limited game choices because of a chore (at least it seemed that way). This was a real improvement.

The pitch worked great. It wasn't like there was a shortage of games, and it really seemed to help the newer people get involved. I hope people were not too dissapointed by not getting players. My Trouble With Demons game sadly fell short of the required number to play, but I'm not bothered by that. One of my bigger regrets was that my Monsterhearts game filled up before there was a chance to do a pitch, and I know at least one person was disappointed by that.

Several of us had a discussion and thought of one possible improvement for next year, doing a friday night welcome. The idea is to make an active effort to get people gaming with people they haven't played with before on friday night. Largely as a way of trying to reach out to the new people. Not sure how much it would help, I was happy to see at least the new people I met getting sucked into plenty of games.

I ran a game and played in games. I made new friends. Camp Nerdly rocks. Thanks guys.

    \n Trying to compress it:\n
    Kagematsu, Patty steps up to run it for me, assuring me that she can totally do it and I can play rather than "facilitate." So with Josh and Budda, we head off, and Patty totally brings it! Her Kagematsu was amnesiac at first, stumbling down the mountain, a man of means but not of clear purpose. Josh was an older (40s) woman, owner of a now run-down bath house. High Charm (6) only one Innocence. Buddah played a women in her twenties with familial responsibility. Younger brother plus an inn to try to maintain. He was 5 Charm, 2 Innocence. I was tempted to throw in with high Innocence low Charm, but it seems to me the middle road would be a better counter-example. So I did 4 Innocence, 3 Charm. She was stuck, not having yet been given an adult name (as her father is presumed dead) but trying to leave the world of children.

    Our strategies could not have been more different, and it really added depth to the game. Josh and Budda both had scenes where they easily snared two Affections, and then got hooked on the third, having to get Desperate, and the tension on those dice rolls was awesome. I have an audio recording of the entire session, but I haven't transcribed it yet.
    \n
    The Mountain Witch: Near 10 people showed, but we filtered down to 6. We had some really killer scenes, but I still struggle with how much to talk about game mechanics before we get started. So we spent nearly an hour on the first "scene" and half the game in the first "chapter." It was paced deliciously... players getting first used to the mechanic, then to one another's characters, then feeling out how to foreshadow and flirt with the fate, then commanding their fates.

    My regret here is that some players were ready and looking to crash their fates in to one another's, but some players hadn't really decided what their fate meant yet. If we had two more hours...
    BUT Oh My God! They stole narration from one another so frequently. It was amazing and I thank you all for that game. I've never seen people engage that aspect as much, and I'm grateful that you all did. One really epic scene that I was proud of was:

    Four of the ronin gathered on the edge of a long bridge to the castle. One of the Nio halted them and said that in order to pass, they must leave that which they value most. (Flagrantly stolen idea that I've been a little fearful to work in, because mechanically, the players can EASILY mash dice together and unmake that obstacle.) So Dave does just that. He steps up to the Nio and focuses his Ability to terrorize the spirit with a vision of his own death. (This means he strides towards him with his blade drawn, because the vision comes from the reflection in the blade.) Of course, he has to roll, and the Nio gets to set counter-stake. I say "He wishes to take your sword from you." Two players literally gasped at that. "Dave no!" But he did... and the dice... they didn't go in Dave's favor.

    So the Nio called to Dave's sword and it lept into the Nio's hand. The Nio rammed it downward into the offering bowl. "O-yanma has accepted your soul. You may pass." Intensity. And Dave has a talent for giving the "WTF did you just do to my character?!?" face.

    So then Willam jumps up and says he'll duel the Nio, to prove his worth. He's trying to assert that he doesn't need to pay any price now, because his intentions grant him some special exemption. (The wording was more poetic, but I didn't get an audio recording, so I don't remember the exact details.) The important thing was he wagers his life. The Nio cautions "Know this, oh man of the waves, I will not end your life if I win, I will spend it as I see fit." The duel... it does not go in William's favor. The Nio takes William's life, and pledges it gladly to the offering bowl. "You will serve O-Yanma well. You may pass."

    So then the remaining two ronin didn't want to roll dice at all. They volunteered burdens into the offering bowl. The last to cross placed his identity into the bowl, forgetting everything but his purpose. INTENSE.
    \n
    Puppetland: Had three of four returning characters. As mentioned elsewhere, I was trying to drive it to a place of darkness, and highlight that element, that naivete that Judy's purpose represents. So that when the puppets were confronted by adult ideas, they had the burden of explaining them away, or denying them. And I mean, mechanically, I would reach across and black out their characters unless they could fight of flee from the idea. Which was fantastically hard to do when we can't talk about what we are doing! We have to stay in character, at all times, but they got it. Somehow they understood that the ideas themselves were poison, the concept of suicide, the permanence of death, the fear that any puppet could be un-made. They stepped it up and swallowed that chunk of blackness, despite interruptions to the game and having to cut things short. (An hour is not Golden all the time.)

    And while we did have some really great scenes, all of which are recorded, the highlight for me is still the children next to us saying "No... keep playing." when we needed to pack up and stop.

    Thank you all for not breaking character, even when we're all dealing with the extra, out of game struggle to keep our hour of time. You were each of you amazing.

    \nSagas of the Icelanders. I ran this pretty straight up, using the situation I fleshed out with my local group but tweaking it for the character mix. Basically a Norwegian prince shows up and demands hospitality and messes things up. This session was interesting because we had a very aggressive Skjaldmey and a weak Go\xf0i. The Skjaldmey took his place in a Holmganga duel of honor, killing the second of Lambi Tonbrekka, the Norwegian asshat, in one blow. This happened to be Dave Younce's character, The Wanderer, which was pretty fun. There was a cool and tragic subplot with the Go\xf0i's wife (initially an NPC, but later a well-realized character played by someone who started the game "just watching") Fenja. She grew to hate her husband and converted to Christianity with the aid of Lambi and the "Five Christian Brothers" he traveled with. There was a fight on his longship, Fenja was struck down by Lambi (as the Sei\xf0kona had prophesied early in the game) and the Skjaldmey and her brother the Huscarl threw down with the Five Brothers, killing four of them and sealing the fate of the Go\xf0iship of Digranes. The Norwegian king had been offended, his men slaughtered. The future looked bleak. High five!

    The game went well, although there were some spotlight issues. I felt like the Sei\xf0kona and the Huscarl didn't have as much meat as the others, and I regretted letting the Skjaldmey defy her husband so easily.

    \nRyuutama was amazing. I'll remember it as Backpacking: the RPG. You travel around, and if you had an arduous trek herding your donkey up the mountain, got a little lost, ate crappy food, and slept poorly, you will be down half your hit points the next day and a giant bee or vampiric rosebush will probably kill you. On the other hand, if you made it to somewhere safe and cozy (say, a cushy bath house suite in town that you earned by beat-boxing with a harp), then look out, evil bugs and flowers!

    I also failed to stand in the way of saving Japan from the Bible in Mountain Witch, brought cannibalism to Puppetland, waffled between lying and soldierly principle in The Tribunal, got stasis-frozen right into the arms of the demonic enemy by my well-intentioned buddy in Cthulhu Dark, and briefly joined the predatory subterranean society of New York at the behest of oracular space aliens in Archipellago on the drive home.

    My playtest of Within My Clutches was extremely fun, and I think I know the few remaining changes I need to make it one-shot-compliant. The fuller version of the game may be good to go. Big props to William Nichols, Keith Stetson, William Chung and Michael Miller for deftly blending moments of poignant humanity and absurdity with the supervillainous action.

    \n My first Camp Nerdly was fun! I would do it again. I met a lot of friendly people, played some good games, and experienced honest-to-god fresh outdoors air immediately upon waking up for the first time in many years.

    Highlights: Playing several variations on D&D (even though I assumed I'd be playing nothing but hippie GMless games about elk farming or racecar drivers); meeting people in person known only to me on the internet; getting to see so many parents provide a great experience for their kids while still getting some gaming in.

    Lowlights: Nothing significant. Had I had a better handle on things, I would have been a little more involved with the pitch sessions, as well as chores and organizing, but you gotta learn the ropes. Oh, plus ticks.

    Games Played: Archipelago, 13th Age, Dungeon Squad, Lamentations of the Flame Princess, Monsterhearts
