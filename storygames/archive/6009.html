Mike's Standard Rant #13: Moving on From Party Play

\n
\n
\n
\n\nMike_Holmes \n\n\n
\n
\n\nMarch 2008 \n edited March 2008 in Play Advice
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n In a recent thread we were discussing a design that indicated that it was good for players to come up with a party concept to fit their characters into. And in my usual fasion I blurted out that I think this is a mistake. And now I have to make a thread to explain why. I've done this before, elsewhere (maybe even here) but it's not the kind of thing that's easy to look up (I shoulda made it a Standard Rant the first time). At this late date, I'm not even sure that there's much reason to recapitulate this - apologies to those who've seen it many times already - but in case it's interesting or beneficial, here goes:
\n
\nMy argument comes in two parts:
\n
\n - Party Play is Problematic -
\nNo, not for everyone. But I think it is for most "story game" play, if you will. In fact this explanation is really somewhat senseless in point of view that many (most?) story-games these days do away with the party concept well and good. But why is it a problem?
\n
\nBasically it frequently creates a conflict of interests for a player. The player is told that it's a RPG, and that he should feel free to explore the things that he finds interesting with his character. Now, of course, it's a collaborative medium, so he'll probably have to mold his inputs around those of other players (GM especially, if there is one). Otherwise it might as well be a solo activity. It's true of RPGs, however, that everyone's fun is increased by the collaboration. Or at least that's the idea. So we assume that the player will have to constrain his character's actions down from "Anything" to, "whatever is fun in response to what the other player are doing."
\n
\nNow, that said, the raison d'etra for party play is to constrain players so that they don't "split up the party." The thought being that if/when this happens, that other players are left on the sidelines uninterested. We'll get back to that in a moment. The problem, however, is that it asks the player to do this by simply asking the player to include his character in every scene. In practice, in fact, there's often no ratonale given for why a particular character is in a scene, other than that he's part of the party.
\n
\nThis, on it's own, is ridiculous, but there are easy fixes. We assume, for instance, that the party is on some sort of mission. Or that they're even a team of some sort that do things together.
\n
\nFine.
\n
\nBut... give me one example of a form of fiction in which the only scenes the main characters have are together? Oh, it might happen as the random result of the plot, but in most cases the "party" in fiction gets split up. Basically the author is exploring something about the character that requires that the character go do X. And X is simply not somthing that it makes sense for the other characters to do.
\n
\nLike going on a date with an NPC. With party play, you either simply do not have scenes like this, or you actually *do* split up the party.
\n
\nAnd while for tactical play of non-story-games, this may be fine, for some genres, for story-games it's just anathema. Oh, sure, exceptions will abound in terms of tight games like The Mountain Witch. But that's a pre-condition of play, that play is only going to be about that party's one exploit. In fact, most games in which party play is considered the norm, are not this way, and there's this theoretical notion that a player can and should go and explore things about their character.
\n
\nSo... basically the problem is that the player is being told that he should both constrain his options to always coming up with some idea of why his character is going along with the party, in whatever they're doing. And that's just going to conflict with how the player wants to explore his character sometimes.
\n
\nNo, I'm not talking about greedy play where one player wants to go off and grab all the attention for himself. Let's give an example. Jim is playing a barbarian. He's made a big deal of how his character is deathly afraid of bird magic. The party decides that they're going to go to the Temple of the Big Magic Bird. What are Jim's options?
\n
\n - Forget his character motivations, and just go along.
\n - Decide to not go along, breaking the agreement to do party play.
\n - Concoct some roleplaying moment where his character is "convinced" to go. This is work, rarely fun, and it's not really creative, because we all know what the outcome is going to be.
\n
\nNone of these are good options. Given four well-characterized characters, there quite simply are going to be moments where party play means that somebody is not getting to play their character in a way that they find fun. I think that most people have encountered this, and that it's not all that controversial. But we'll see.
\n

    \n - Another Method Exists -
    \nSo, Mike, if we get rid of party play, how do we organize play? How do we make sure that everyone stays engaged in play? If they're not getting to roll dice, they're going to go off and watch TV, right?
    \n
    \nThe alternative is what I and some others call "Scene Play." It starts with the assumption that action does not simply organically flow from one moment to the next, but that the players and GM are actively "cutting" scenes, and moving on to others. This allows you to pack a lot of drama into a relatively very short time. No, without scene play, you do need something like Party play to keep players on their toes. But doing good "scene framing"... getting to the action, and then getting out... is critical.
    \n
    \nAnd, basically, to answer the obvious question, no, not every player is in every scene (what, you were expecting some magical answer?) So, doesn't that mean that the other players will be bored?
    \n
    \nWell what a lot of people do at this point is point out other ways in which the players can actually remain involved in play (like playing NPCs). And while these are good things to mention, they're quite simply unneccessary. If you frame back and forth between players each taking their scenes, several things happen:
    \n
    \n - Players are alert because it's not going to be hours until their scene, but maybe just a few minutes.
    \n - Players find themselves actively becoming "audience." Meaning that, knowing that their character is not meant to be in this scene intentionally, they can shift gears, and take a breather. And suddenly they find themselves able to get into what's going on, as if they were watching a TV show...
    \n - But it's a TV show where they can actually be heard shouting at the screen. This is key. Whatever other form of interaction you do or do not allow, you must allow feedback. Applause or laughs are unstoppable anyhow, but you want to get players to shout things out, even interrupt, or even suggest actions for he other players. It's collaborative for them, too.
    \n
    \nI think that Party play is often combined with "Shhh, you're not there, you can't talk" limitations. Which are bunk. Players can and will separate player and character knowledge appropriately. Its not a tactical game, anyhow, so there's no "cheating" involved.
    \n
    \nI could go on and on, but this is the basic technique. Let players understand up front that they will be taking "turns" doing scenes. Expecting this, play becomes no different than any boardgame where you have to wait your turn. Better, because you actually are actively engaged in abosrbing the story created by the other players as audience.
    \n
    \nWhat ends up happening, a lot, is that players try to "horn in" on each other's scenes. Not in a malicious way, but they try to figure out motives for their characters that, in fact, *do make sense* for why they would be together. In the case of our superstitious barbarian, the barbarian player says, "How about if we do a scene before you go where you try to convince him to come." Understanding that the outcome is unclear, and it could go either way, and the players really do get to be creative in that scene.
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \nParty play is good for tactical games in which the fun is each player competing against the game to do their best, and where theme creation is not important to the players. It means maximal opportunity to make those sorts of decisions. For thematic play, however, scene play is critical, because this is what allows for appropriate player freedom of expression in creating themes.

    \n While I agree that some of the reasons you give for party play sound silly, the key reason for me is that I greatly enjoy in-character interactions with the other PCs. For me, it adds a lot to interact with the other characters rather than having the players kibbitz on an out-of-character level. So I try to find ways to keep the PCs together and interacting with each other.

    It's not like I never try splitting the party -- it happens pretty frequently, and thus I have experience trying this out, with or without aggressive scene framing. However, my experience has been that when everyone is together and can interact, it has usually been more fun. This has been just as true in indie story games as in more traditional games. For example, we recently discussed this issue in my Burning Wheel group, and we agreed that we would like to bring the PCs together more often.

    While in principle players can interact in-character through playing NPCs, the transfer is usually tricky and the NPCs are generally not well developed. For this reason, I also try to limit the number of NPCs that the PCs interact with -- i.e. preferring to have a known NPC fulfill a given function rather than making up a new NPC.

    \n In Polaris, we've been having the opposite problem - the game seems to fully support, even encourage, having each protagonist on a completely separate story with almost no interaction. We've had to take active steps to reintroduce party-play, and I think we are doing this because we WANT party-play for the reasons discussed above (the regular interaction of a small group of regular characters).
    \n
    \n(Note one way to sorta do this to have every player with multiple characters, and then try to have every scene draw upon at least one character from each player's pool. This allows every player to have a character in every scene. The drawbacks of this are it can still be one separate story for each protagonist, and that the number of characters can get very large, as many as number of players squared, giving each individual character less and less screen time. Whether this technique counts as "party play" depends, as others have said, on one's definition of party play. I think as a functional matter this method is unacceptable - there just isn't enough time to fully develop a dozen different characters)
    \n
    \nA few techniques so far:
    \n
    \n1. We've reshuffled which characters are protagonists and which are minor moon characters after the first session. Boring protagonists get turned into minor characters or dropped; the really spunky squire with a close relationship to one of the other protagonists who was merely a sidekick played by a moon gets elevated to protagonist.
    \n
    \n2. Abandon the idea that the Mistaken plays "Demons" and instead have the mistaken play characters that are highly likely to drive the heart's protagonists story arc. For example, we had two protagonists, one highly corrupt with a rough expected story arc of redemption, the other higher naive and innocent with a rough expected story arc of corruption. Have the heart of one be the mistaken for the other. Typically both characters will be in every scene of the other, as the corrupt character tempts the pure one, and the pure one reminds the corrupt one of the "Good old days"

    \n We're having a semantic problem here. I thought I cleared it up with Thor's post. But let me try again:
    \n
    \nMy perspective is that scene play is both:
    \n
    \n1. Scenes with some of the characters, and
    \n2. Scenes with all of the characters.
    \n
    \nIf you want to see scenes with some of the characters as scene play, and any scene with all characters as party play, OK, fine. Then Party Play is awesome. But it's awesome as part of an overall model where it's not made a requirement. Or even a model where undue pressure is put on players to Party Up.
    \n
    \nAgain, that might be a better perspective on all of this. Rather than talking about a certain style of play, we can talk about whether or not there are assumptions going on in play that are creating pressures that are forcing players into a conflict of interest between playing to their character's motivations, and playing to ensure some sort of ability to interact on a constant basis. Since that's not neccessary.
    \n
    \n
    \nNow, that all said, I think that my original perspective has some advantages to it in terms of the fact that it also addresses the techniques that make "split-party" play (if you will) more palatable. That is, actually doing scenes and cutting and such is important to this. For instance, if a player manages to protract his "solo" scene on by narrating, "OK, now Ragnar leaves the tavern and goes next door to the blade merchant to buy a new sword" that's a bad thing. Part of the typical mode of play that includes scene play is that there is little in the way of conscious framing, and more a sense of continuing action. Which means that player priorities are being ignored for a sense of continuity. And one that doesn't make sense, either, since the question arises: what are the other characters doing while Ragnar is getting his new sword?
    \n
    \nI knew it would be controversial to call this "moving on." But for story games, I really think that it's important to adopt scene play assumptions - or something as powerful, I don't know what - in order to organize play. Note, again, that I'm under the assumption that, in fact, many or most people here already do scene play. So, from my perspective, if Thor says, "Well our party play is good," to me he's saying, "Well our scene play is good, especially when everybody is in the same scenes over and over."
    \n
    \n
    \nAs for whether or not the game referenced creates party play through suggesting that players adopt a team paradigm, no, it doesn't. My contention was that I assumed that the design in question was doing so under the assumption that this would make party play easier to deal with (and it would.) My point is that it's unneccessarily limiting to suggest this to players, because an alternative to party play exists. Hence why I asked the designer if he thought that his design specifically was good for team play. If he has another motive for suggesting team play, then great!
    \n
    \nAnd, again, I posted this here because the author in question asked me to discuss the method that made the team concept unneccessary. As I didn't want to clutter that thread with this discussion.

    \n Following the above post of mine with this...
    \n
    \nWilliam, yes. I am as against play that creates non-interacting stories as I am against play that dilutes story in the name of forcing players to have interaction available. I assume that the players will want to have scenes where their characters interact with other players' characters. If the design disincentivizes this in any way, it's definitely bad. And it's good if the rules actually incentivize players getting together.
    \n
    \nWhat I'm against are social contract assumptions that do not really give the player the option to make that decision, but instead leave the player feeling bad no matter which decision he makes.
    \n
    \nJohn, you can deny that such exists, but then maybe you haven't experienced as much of the phenomenon as I have. For instance in my HQ IRC game, I once had a player do something very bizzare with her character, very against her character's nature. I thought she was just trying to be a bit avant guard, or, perhaps, simply hadn't realized how odd the act was to us (sometimes you miss context as a player). When I asked her, however, she said that she had done it to keep the party together. She said she assumed that I wanted her to do it, because she couldn't see how things could continue if the party got broken up over a large geographical distance.
    \n
    \nI was taken aback. I'd like to think that I go out of my way to indicate to players that it's fine to have characters go their own way. But what I had failed to convey, and this is important, is that just as easy as it is to split characters up, it's just as easy to contrive plot devices to get them back together again. No, not the player having to make some unrealistic choice about their character to get this to happen, but just some event that causes play to come together again. Sometimes this includes leaping forward considerably in time. If the players want to get back together, and this is the way to make it plausible, then that's a great way to do it.
    \n
    \nIt's just not commonly used technique. My fault for not making these things more obvious.
    \n
    \nWhat I'm talking about is empowering players to drive the story in the direction that they feel is most interesting by not falling back on traditional assumptions of how play works that would make this difficult.

    \nI absolutely agree. Turns out that I run things pretty much always in Scene Play mode, but in the initial game setup ("Situation Creation") I gravitate, like as in "the motherfucking primal force of gravity" towards situations where the players take on protagonists or anchors who:

    1) Have a tight story reason to be together more times than not.
    2) Have a reason to work together more times than not.

    This way, not everyone has to be in every scene. You can get cool character drama, talking in character, etc without having all the heads together all the time. But this method of play means that the characters are together more often than they are not (if not everyone in one scene, then at least a majority of them in one scene). It also means that there aren't often antagonists; or if they are, they are such that they can still be with the others regularly without jumping to "I kill you/you kill me" scenario.

    Examples: L5R game: 3 players: 2 are samurai in the same clan and related (one a samurai and one a samurai-wizard), and a samurai from another clan. They have been sent to guard a wall. They split up all the time, but there are also many scenes where 2 of them are together, if not all 3.

    Unknown Armies: All the 5 PCs are friends of "Erik Brassey", who has suddenly disappeared. They all have their own motivations and things they do, and yet many scenes there are 2-3 or more of them together.

    Tenra Bansho: 4 ninjas and a samurai tasked (by different agents) to kill an evil warlord: They all work for the same clan. One will betray the others, but not for some time. They all have different skills and stories, and again we see many scenes where 3 of them are in one scene, and a scant few where all 5 are in the same scene.

    I've played games where the players take characters who have little reason to be together (because of the situation), but that's not been too fun for me. In longer scenes where every character is in their own scenes by themselves, even if you bring in the other players to play NPCs, it still feels like it's too much downtime. I haven't enjoyed it in games that last more than a session or two.

    \nGiven the explanation above about the nature of the post, we're in agreement, no? That is, this thread may have been better off with the title you give it. That's certainly the idea.
    \n
    \nNote that I do think that there's a potential for dysfunction that I've identified. But it mostly has to do with players like me, running up against more traditional games. Or confusing portrayal of priorities. Nothing automatically problematic with party play itself. I'm less against party play, than I am agaisnt assuming that there are no other options.
    \n
    \nSo, if we can get past the slant, your question is how to do these things better? Well there's a whole body of technique that's involved, and each technique can be a long discussion itself. I didn't want to include all of that in the original article, but let's dig deeper here, sure.
    \n
    \nFirst, I'll address one technique that I don't think is very good:
    \n - Playing NPCs or Troup-Style Play -
    \nI agree with David that this isn't a great solution. I will always let a player play an NPC if they are interested. Yes. But what I don't do is to count on this as a way to keep players engaged with play. It'll keep them *involved* with play, sure. But if that involvement seems like work, because the character in question does not interest them (either as character or as tool), then this can even be worse than boredom. Having a player be *engaged* by what's going on in scenes in which they are not present is the key. Entertained, perhaps. The scene draws them in by being somthing that they're interested in. How do we engage?
    \n
    \n - Player Approval of Characters -
    \nMight seem contradictory to the idea of player freedom, but these are collaborative games, again, and we're accepting that we want some limits. So at the very least, before play, have the players discuss their character ideas with each other. If the character idea interests only the player proposing it, but the other players don't seem interested, then have them try again. No, the other players don't have to be as interested in the character as the player of it is. But they should potentially be interested in where such a character's story goes.
    \n
    \nFor instance, if a player makes a despicable character, another player might not really want a story that focuses on those sorts of themes. Remember, when he's not in a scene, he's audience, and we want to make a "show" that he's going to enjoy watching.
    \n
    \nTo that extent, players should be supported by the system in making interesting characters, or at the very least exhorted to come up with characters who have some real story potential to them (without being created "played out.") Methods for this could fill an entire new thread. But the very simple key is player-to-player communication during character generation. In fact, this is often how a centralized concept develops like a team. From players agreeing that this is what they want to play. It's fine for this to develop organically like this (it also doesn't mean that you have to avoid split play).
    \n
    \nUsing these methods, the selection of characters is going to inform the entire direction that play takes. Make sure everyone is happy with it before moving on.
    \n
    \n - Centralizing Concepts -
    \nOK, you've got characters. And hypothetically in this case, the players haven't come up with any team concept, or other sort of link between the characters that'll create any reason for them to have scenes together. Do we just start play, and hope that the stories will collide? No. There are a few techniques that one can use to keep a group of characters intersecting.
    \n
    \nOne popular one these days is the "Central Conflict." First named such by Chris Chinn. In this case, the group looks at the characters in question, and then at the game world, and finds some place and situation where a conflict could arise that will potentially be able to engage the players in terms of getting their characters involved. This can bring together even very disparate characters. And, yes, sometimes they'll be on the opposite side of the conflict. At least in a prima fascia way. They may switch later.
    \n
    \nThis is another reason that party play is often used, to avoid "PvP" conflict. This is yet another "problem" that's not really a problem, if you use the right techniques. I won't go into those here in detail either, but, essentially they involve making sure that players don't associate with their characters to the point where "Character vs Character" feels like "Player Vs Player." Character vs Character can be as much player team play as it is when the GM throws up adversity. Can be, when done right.
    \n
    \nBut either way, as potential allies or enemies, we come up with a central conflict into which the players want to get their characters involved. Again, come up with this as a group, and you'll be sure that they're interested. Communicate, and ask. Get exlicit buy in. These processes are actually quite easy.
    \n
    \nAnother centralizing concept is the "relationship map" as existed before Ron Edwards wrote about them, but which Ron has made into a recognizable phenomenon and tool for RPG play. This is in some ways simply a more subtle central conflict, but in the context of a network of characters. The difference in this case is that the network in question is designed to entangle the charcters in an engaging way, and perhaps "drag" the player's characters into their conflict.
    \n
    \nA good relationship map may well make use of NPCs in the PCs backgrounds. Some won't. But in any case, the key for such a map is for the NPCs to need the PCs. Some NPC has some need that drags the PC on to the map. Is this railroading?
    \n
    \n - Grabs Vs Hooks -
    \nIt's just as bad to assume that a player will have his character respond to X stimulus the way you desire, as it is to force the player to always be in the same scene with others, in a game where we want player freedom to defind their character's destinies. The traditional method is to "Hook" a PC into play, onto a relationship map, or into a centralizing conflict. Hopefully one doesn't have to do so. With a well-discussed centralizing conflict, for instance, the player has already indicated a willingness to dive in.
    \n
    \nWith a relationship map, it can be a tad trickier. In that often you'll be tempted to use the usual sort of draw, like offering gold. But the problem is that this is usually Hobson's Choice. Play along, or don't play. So how do you make a PC offer automatically drag the PC onto the map? How do you create a situation that doesn't try to "Hook" the character, but instead "Grabs" the player, and makes him want to play?
    \n

    \n - Player Controlled Plot -
    \nI maybe should have started here, because this technique may be the most important. Let me step back a moment, however, and explain something about the overall paradigm of play here.
    \n
    \nThe goal of this paradigm is to allow player creativity to define their character through play. We assume that this is an important part of why the players in question are playing the game. If that's not the case in your game, then all of this thread probably doesn't pertain to you. But, assuming that's what we want, the problem with a lot of play is that it's all about following the GM's pre-prepared plot.
    \n
    \nThe problem with this is that, in order for the plot to keep moving forward as designed, it will have to assume certain character responses. This directly eliminates the player's ability to make choices about their character. Or, if allowed, the plot is destroyed. The GM created plot evolved as a response to plotless roleplaying. That is, if you give players free-rein to do as they please, thinking that they'll create something like a plot, they won't usually (especially if you're playing with a system that doesn't incentivize it in anyway). So the response was to create scenarii in which the characters were made to jump through hoops so that something looking like a story was created.
    \n
    \nWell this allows the GM to be creative in terms of making up a plot, but the players don't get to be so. Or, if they try, and the GM allows them to head wherever, we're back to no plot at all.
    \n
    \nThe assumption is that either the GM does nothing to create plot, or he does everything to create plot.
    \n
    \nThe solution is to take a middle road. The GM doesn't create the plot himself, but collaborates with the players in creating it. They make it up together as they go.
    \n
    \nYes, this means that the GM can't be "prepared" for what may come. As I say above, however, I see this as a boon. Very little session prep.
    \n
    \nDoes that mean that the GM can't prepare at all, however? Am I saying, essentially, to "wing it" every time we play? No, there are techniques. Mainly...
    \n
    \n - Bangs -
    \nPut simply, a bang is a form of GM input into the game that gives the players a fun choice to make, but does not make any assumptions about the outcome. They are open-ended questions, presented in order to give the player a chance to be creative.
    \n
    \nIn tactical play, the GMs job is to do the same thing, to create decision-making opportunities for players. The difference is that in tactical play, the decisions are tactical. The rewards of which are the player being lauded for his own tactical prowess. In thematic play, the decisions presented are thematic. The rewards for which are accolades from the other players for the awesomeness of the plot turns that he has created.
    \n
    \nThe specifics for how to create bangs, and the many techniques for doing so, I'll leave for other threads, too. But very simply the essence is usually to put multiple things that the character values into conflict. And then let the player sort out how his character deals with that. To be clear, the things involved should be things that we identify the *player* as being interested in about his character, not neccessarily what the character is interested in. The character has no interest in finding out whether or not he values his arm more than his leg (when the loan shark comes calling), but it's potentially fascinating to the player.
    \n
    \nBut how do we know what the player is interested in? Well, for one we can ask. Nothing wrong with that method (though you can drain suspense if you telegraph). But often we don't have to, because we can look at...
    \n
    \n - Flags -
    \nAnother idea noted by Chris Chinn. The character sheet is often a good place to find Flags, which are indications of what interests the player. Things like class or race (better yet, homeland) are a good place to start as likely flags. Notes on relationships. But sometimes character sheets have lots of things on them, mandated by the chargen rules, that are not at all indicative of what the player is interested in in terms of the character. After all, he was forced to note that his character was human. It may not interest him at all. So be careful looking at the sheet. Again, ask, if you're not sure.
    \n
    \nBut better flags can be found in play. Which abilities is the character using a lot? Which ones has he raised up with his rewards? These are much better flags.
    \n
    \nAnd, if you don't have Asperger's Syndrome, simply watch the player, and monitor their responses. It usually quickly becomes apparent what it is about the character that interests them. Make bangs up that involve these things. This all works to...
    \n
    \n - Keep Play About the Characters -
    \nThis is part of the paradigm that's often really hard to get across to players coming from traditional games. It took me a loooong time for it to sink in. It's not enough to say, "Well the characters went on the adventure, so it's about the characters." No. What I'm talking about is waaay deeper than that. Basically it can be stated that, optimally, nothing should occur in the game that is not directly about exploring who the character is. For instance, combats should not happen simply for us to watch the character win or lose. They are great if in happening, they reveal something new about the character. But every scene should be devised because the situation it creates, and the potential for bangs in the scene, will potentially reveal something about the character.
    \n
    \nParty play misses this mark entirely. That is, if the reason a character is in a scene, is simply to allow character interaction... while that can, yes, potentially result in us finding out new things about the character... the percentages are way, way too low. We don't want to find out about the characters when it happens as an accident of play. That's sure to bore players who are not in the scene. Set every scene up with an understanding that it will be entertaining in that we're revealing something about the character.
    \n
    \n - Beause this is what Engages Audiences -
    \nFinding out about the characters - how they respond to crises and dilemmas, or even just every day drama - is what entertains us as audience. Make every scene about something dramatic, and everyone not in the scene will stay watching. Here's the simple rule... if you can't figure out what the scene is for, or what it's revealing (even if that's "He hasn't changed"), then cut the scene and move on. You can be in the middle of a terrible brawl with monsters, but if you can't see what this is showing us about the characters, cut right there to the scene afterwards where there aren't enough bandages to go around, and a character has to figure out who to give his last one to.
    \n
    \nAlmost done...

    \n In conclusion to this part, all these techniques together represent a paradigm change in method of play. Yes, it takes some getting used to in that it's not the "walk the dungeon" method that's very easy to use, and with which we're all comfortable.
