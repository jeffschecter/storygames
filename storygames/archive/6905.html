\n We playtested Medical Hospital today and, surprisingly, it went pretty well. The game is supposed to model episodic medical drama and I think it's going to. The thing that is most interesting to me, and is a feature of both this game and Business Solutions (which we also kicked around hard) is the blurring of the line between character and player. In both you do stuff as a player, you compete and perform, and your ability to do stuff has a direct impact on the fiction. I know some people will hate this, and that's OK. Everybody else won't, and those are the people I want to play with anyway.

My doctor went to the carnival and there was a teenaged drug addict, overdosing! It was a very television moment. Luckily there was an AED nearby and I restarted his heart after five tense tries - I got to shout "clear!" which is, of course, a primary design goal.

Joel's guy terribly botched a low tracheotomy, and as a result accrued a ton of stress (the game's currency). Apparently Joel doesn't know what "two centimeters" means.

Clinton's guy was enmeshed in a struggle over the directorship of the trauma unit, which went to the sexy new surgeon he lusted after (played by Mike), who had a great moment with a trauma patient who had been shot four times in the thorax. There was also some nonsense about a drug yet to be approved by the FDA that had caused some deaths. For a largely mechanical playtest, some good story was emerging, which is promising.

Big thanks to Mike, Joel and Clinton - you guys broke the creative logjam on these designs. Having three sets of fresh eyes questioning my assumptions and proposing zany solutions was so valuable.

Here's the promised spleen operation.

I think that is a promising sign, because I'd like players to be authoring their own procedures.

    \n Probably it would stop your heart, unless it hit at exactly the right time.

    I had a contract once doing tech writing for an electricity company. When we had the site tour I carefully memorized the longest distance that electricity could jump (10 feet, I think, and it's not for the highest voltage, either) and stayed that far away from any equipment.

    While there, I heard stories of power station workers who had not taken the precautions they were supposed to and had been thrown into walls by large zaps of electricity. Fortunately, the timing was right and their hearts didn't stop.
