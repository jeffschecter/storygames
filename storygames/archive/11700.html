\n So Paul B created a topic called "[Trail of Cthulhu] Best Practices". That took me aback a bit, because "Best Practices" is a "WORK WORD", so I had a little bit of aversion to coupling Work and Hobby. But the more I thought about it, the more I like it.

Also, Paul gave himself away as someone who works with computers (I'd wager, anyway).

So I work with enterprise-level computer storage equipment at my day job. Our official stance is, "Do whatever the hell you want with it. But please check out the Best Practices guides".

So if some client buys some $100K computer system and "blows it up" (in my world, this usually means "hundreds of misaligned VMWare luns, impacting write performance"), then that's up to them. But we have guides that show them what they should be doing to get "the most out of their system".

Applied to RPGs, this idea is very applicable. A GM might "blow it up" by ignoring or forgetting some rule about how fanmail works or how you should get into play, and the session... while not Bad, it might have been Better.

As JD is fond of saying (though with more vigor), "The designer is dead when the product is in your hands": The game has rules. Many GMs and players will forget, ignore, or willingly change some of the rules (and others will follow them to the letter). However, for each game it might be said that there are "Best Practices", discovered in the field through play, either by the author of the game (and codified in the book as Rules or Play Advice), or through players who describe their sessions online. Compiling these Best Practices (even if it's a collection of "subjective player experiences") might be really helpful to bring a game that might be "OK" to a higher level of fun.

Some "Best Practices" are more or less universal: "Listen to the players", "Pay attention to pacing", blah blah blah.

Also, sometimes Best Practice Guides will have conflicting (or "expanded") advice that will give you some advice or suggestions for running an environment (storage hardware, routers, Gaming Table) with a particular focus: Here I would bring it back to RPGs by citing JD Corley's advice of the GM running the Producer role as if it was a real, true cigar-chompin' pinstripe suit producer with incredibly aggressive, bad and conflicting ideas in order to jog the players forward. This will produce a very unique experience that you wouldn't see if you didn't adopt that advice, but that advice would steer the game experience down a particular path: Best Practices for making Game X more Realistic; Best Practices for making Game X more Dramatic; Best Practices for Streamlining Combat in Game X.

Anyway, this was something that was on the mind recently: Where the designer ends and the "Purchase Now!" weblink begins is a wall, and maybe players or fans of a game cobbling their own play methods together into "best practice guides" (basically "Player Guides", just written in the field by fans/players through play experience; and not by the game designer) can help improve play experiences without having to redesign the game from scratch.

Just a thought.

-Andy

    \n Presumably the designer has a bunch of information gleaned from playtesting that informed her design. It might be nice to have a section (probably on a website, with just a reference in the rulebook) that says "Thinking of house rules? Check this list of things we considered and how they went wrong or right in playtesting!"

    Then there's a place to say "Monopoly: Free Parking money: every time we gave out lots of money here, from the bank or from other players feeding into the pool or whatever, the game took interminably long. Lesson: don't mess with the economy dynamics just 'cause it sounds like fun." or "Monopoly: Start with less money: this seems to work all right, we chose $1500 because we liked it best but nothing seemed to break in playtesting when starting with $1000 or whatnot, just be sure you remember the auction rules. Lesson: the game's pretty robust to initial conditions, handing out properties to start might work fine too."

    This is a good idea... but only if it is then picked up by the designer and at least offered as a DL from the product site, if not totally incorporated into a Revised Edition.

    No textual product should require a supporting textual product--that's just silly.

    But if a "Best Practices to do FOO with BAR" becomes a catch phrase for forum users, that's all well and good. But if it STOPS at forum users--at that "wall" you mention--it's a broken methodology. Close the documentation loop.

    (To use an IT metaphor, that's like letting stuff accumulate in an online knowledgebase, but never folding those web articles into the new manual releases. Disclaimer: I am a Tech Writer IV for a Fortune 500 IT corporation.)\n

    So, here's something I have to deal with in editing. I'll read something and get a completely different impression than the author did, *but I won't realize that* because the impression is subtle. So, I'll edit thinking I read that part as intended. I'll get the next round back with "uh, I didn't mean that..." I see the same thing here, where players might make a BP around something they misunderstand, and maybe it's actually not a BP because the author didn't get the point across (like, stake setting in IAWA). But, what can come from the dialogue is a third thing, a bit of clarity from the designer with advice from players.

    Don't count the designer out, man. They might, you know, still have some fuckin' passion to pitch in and help. And that's what this'll take: passionate participants.
