    \n While not a game per se, you should check out the dirty dungeon technique by John Wick. Apart from the youtube video linked above, I believe he describes it in more detail in his book: Play Dirty.

    Basically, players can create dungeon elements (like rooms, monsters, traps or even treasure) by researching it in-game (rumors, libraries etc.). For each thing that would make their life more difficult, they get a die added into a bowl. During the dungeon run, they can spend those dice to boost any test.

    I also believe that labyrinths and lycanthropes lets you generate a dungeon using a deck of cards. Even that I have the game from a bundle long time ago, I have never read it, so I only have a general idea.

    \nDrowning and Falling has players create challenges for each other using playing cards. The player picks a challenge type--drowning or falling--and a base card, which determines the difficulty. Additional cards add a failure penalty or a monster, which modifies the base difficulty and drops treasure. The dungeon layout is created through play: after each challenge the party decides to go left, right, or straight, encountering a challenge prepared by the player in that direction.

    John Walton's *Dark Heart of the Dreamer* has some GM advice for coming up with dungeons on the fly by coming up with overall dangers and rolling each room to see which danger is present.

    \n Soory it took so long for me to write this but THANKS A LOT EVERYONE! I just started a 5e D&D campaign using things from this post and other places.

    Funny thing: so far I decided not to give players any incentive for adding things to the game. No extra dice for adding stuff, no xp, nothing. I'm avoiding the Czege principle by letting them enjoy the creation of a world without pushing the things they create as challenges yet (well, it's kinda easy now since we're just beggining). And it's working wonders!

    Looks like it's the first time for most of the players to start a campaign like this, we have three veteran players and four newbies, but it made no difference. A few minutes into the first session everyone was quite engaged in the creation of the world. They started by being gods creating their concept, cult and traditions. Then they become kings and shaped their countries. I added a bit of random at defining things like amount of followers, economy and population, so the newbies got easily familiarized with interpretating the dice into fiction.

    Most of my work was about connecting things. Suddenly a history of the setting started to arise while we created the world, but it's still full of mistery. An ancient continent where magic was powerful become the grave of the god of war. A legendary hero was accounted resposible for that, but the facts aren't clear. The death of the god destroyed the whole continent and refugees that barely escaped ended founding another country and creating warforged to supply their now short manpower.

    In time they started selling armies, which brought ruin to a league of feudal states whose economy was based on the exportation of mercenary armies. Fact after fact started clicking in place and now players are so invested in the setting that they are still adding things to it while waiting for the next session. They know they are going to be in there are level 1 adventurers, and yet they are already asking for Balrogs!

    The only slow part of the session was the character creation, which still hasn't finished. We haven't gone yet trhough the backgrounds process but I already explained them roughly what's that about.

    Next I'll have them create the main villains of the setting in secret. Since we have 7 players I was planning to have one of them play the villain of the day on each session. Each time a different player with roleplay his villain and try to meet an objective with a limited amount of resources, but that will be only when everyone is more accustomed to the game mechanics.

    I'm also keeping at hand a set of cards that allows players whose characters are out of the scene to intervene in the scene by adding details, roleplaying npcs or monsters or even helping any side of the conflicts with successful rolls and situational advantage. I'll let you know how it goes.

    \n Yes, my dungeon generator cards do that right now. I've got 6 different entries on each card and for each room the players get in I draw a few and take one entry of each.

    They are (in no particular order)
    -Shape of the room
    -Visible exit (if any)
    -Details noticed with a good perception roll
    -Trigger of a trap (only used when I'd like to include one)
    -Nature of the trap
    -Event in the room

    Why cards and not random tables? Cards are two seconds faster, and believe me, players really appreciate when your description is done in a fluid way without too much "err..." or uncomfortable silences. Heck, even comfortable silences kill the mood a bit, it's like waiting for a video to buffer up on the internet. It doesn't have the potential to stall the game anyway, but while not exactly annoying, whatever good emotional flow you had with the previous scene will reduce it's speed while you're cheking the tables and interpretating them. And you will lose a bit of the shock you could get with a quick response.

    The second reason is that you're not limited to a number of choices like when using dice (of course, you can roll a d100 on a table with 82 or some items, but again you will be limited to a 100 of choices and will have to ressort to a sub-table or add another dice to get 1000 options and so... but that's another roll to make and read -see my previous point about that)

    When I tested it before I got a cool scene with a door which erupted with an area fire spell when illuminated. A couple members of the party got their hair on fire until the wizard deciphered the runes. The writing went along the lines of "Let yourself be swallowed by the darkness, for it protects us from danger" and inmediatly stated thinking of the dungeon as a sort of trial for a thief guild. The next room was a spheric one with a well in the middle, except that only the wind was blowing from the well... in a rhytmic fashion. Above the well was hanging a glass orb. At that point everyone froze and looked at the party rogue.

    The whole couple of scenes went quick and the players only made rolls for saves. They were able to decipher the trap mechanic by themselves after some trial and observation.

    However what I'm looking for now is to be able to create a dungeon with a story, with some actual background that helps me add more coherent and interesting details. How to host a dungeon doesn't look bad, thoug perhaps what I'm looking for is more like what Morrius posted.

    \n Yeah, you can play Dawn of Worlds without the point mechanic and roll the dice whenever there's a disagreement about something, it will be way quicker and more fun (I'm talking from experience here)

    Also, I told the players that everything we created was "general public knowledge" but maybe NOT the actual truth, which leaves space to bring additional details and twists to the setting, allowing the players to experience the surprise of discovering things.

    Edit: I'll add something else instead of double-posting.

    Yesterday our group was missing a few players so instead of roleplaying our usual game, I convinced the players to try a random dungeon created with my deck. I hadn't tested it in this fashion so it looked like a good idea. Instead of making characters I just let players choose among 4 classes (fighter, cleric, rogue and wizard) and just roll the dice whenever they wanted to try anything. Things I limited: the amount of cure wounds spells the cleric had was limited to a 1d4 per rest. Also, PCs had only 6hp (each one was represented by a d6 on the table, in marching order)

    We were just testing the tool so the dungeon had no specific goal or theme. But then it all started working better than if I had prepared a dungeon. I still have to adjust the tool since I could only use once a "sitting bench" as a trigger for a trap before it became one of the most obvious triggers. The whole effect was somewhat old-school, quite open for players to try things and with just enough details to feed their paranoia.

    One of the best things was that though every room had a trap getting through them was more of a teamwork effort instead of just a chance for the rogue to shine. Whenever a trap made no sense as a mechanical construct, I switched it's nature into a magical one. Yet the trigger was always a different thing that could be avoided/deactivated with some careful observation and/or thinking outside the box. The trap itself could be treated this way too, so it didn't matter much if you were the fighter or the cleric, you could also give it a try at your own method of passing through traps.

    I think that all that it's missing is a few mechanics like the ones Jason Lutes included in his game aids "Perilous Journeys", like choosing a theme and builder for the dungeon, set three things related to those and mark them off your list when players have grone through that description. Also, making time and/or light sources a limited resource would do wonders. I'll try to put some of this together in english to make it playable.

    \n Hmmm ok, a "dungeon" would have as many challenges as it's level (challenges as in important rooms, traps, puzzles, monsters, natural hazards, locked doors etc).

    Optionally you could spend more features to increase the difficulty of a single feature (like a lvl 2 monster, a group of smaller ones, a false easy trap that hides another trap, etc) You start the game having a level equal to twice the addition of the levels of all the PCs that will try to steal your valuables.

    All dungeon challenges are modular, so they can be placed anywhere. You can have as many rooms, corridors, stairs, etc to connect and present those features as you want as long as they don't present a challenge. If there's more than one room/corridor/stair to connect one feature with the next, narrate how players navigate through them without problems until they reach the next

    Choose one builder, one function and two or three themes (decay, last stand, corruption, madness, etc) and use them to describe each room and challenge.

    Level up by killing adventurers (one level per each death, keep their stuff as loot) or by having them take your valuables (1d6 levels)

    \n Here's an awesome post about dungeons (and related to them being "creatures":

    Hungry Gods and Dungeons

    A lovely reframing of dungeons, gold, and their uses.

    Another one I kind of like is the idea (silly though it seems on the surface) that Dragons are magical creatures who consume minerals which turn into gold (and similar precious stones/metals) inside the furnace of their intestines.

    They build "lairs" as a mating display: the more impressive the lair, the more likely they will be able to attract a mate.

    So the typical Dragon finds a nice mountain (or an abandoned castle) and starts turning it into their lair by adding tunnels and enlarging rooms, and then "marks" their territory by leaving their feces (gold and precious metals) in most of the rooms.

    When you walk into a dragon's lair and see it sitting by a giant pile of gold... it's just the natural consequence of Dragons never having invented a proper flush toilet.

    So much about fantasy gaming makes sense when seen in this light.
