    \n If we accept any sort of relationship development during play, then this is really common. Games where you retroactively establish relationships during play (that is, we now pretend that these two characters were lovers all along) are a bit less common, but it's still entirely normal for any No Myth game. Zombie Cinema, for instance - I usually fly blind in that one to begin with, focusing on superficial actions and reactions, and then establish what's really going on about a third of the way through. Throw in some flashbacks to clarify momentous conjoined backstories, whatever.

    For some reason I'm reminded of one of my backburner projects, Valravnar for \xc1sagrimmr; that game not only establishes relationships on the fly, anew for each scene if necessary, but the establishment itself is not a consensual process, but rather a single player doles out the relationships. The game does funky things with the relationships once they're established, too.

    \n In My Life with Master, the Minions pursue relationships with the Townsfolk as an important part of gameplay and can introduce any potential new relationship in any of their scenes.
    \n

    \n There're two of my games which fit.

    One which fits better is "You are My Destiny, in which while you will usually start with relationships to some characters before the game begins (one's mandatory), you'd also certainly meet and create, and change the relationships you have with different characters as the game progresses, since relationships fuel the powers the characters use, and are what they put on the line during their conflicts.

    "The Friendship Game", sort of fits. You usually introduce characters just as play begins, and they may have relationships or not. It's not mandatory, but it usually works better if the characters know one another before game begins. Friendship and relationships are not mechanically significant, as Paul Czege put it, it's the fruitful void in the game. You can see his playtest report here.
