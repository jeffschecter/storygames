    \n I've never used DriveTHRU Cards. The way it works on GameCrafter is you create a "product" and upload images of the cards and set quantities you want for each one, and they put the product up in their storefront for people to order, at a price you set. They print the decks on demand, taking a fee for materials and manufacturing and a cut of your set price for being in their storefront.

    https://www.thegamecrafter.com/

    \nBe wary about doing things in a non-standard way with the DriveThru folks. I ran into problems when I wanted to buy a couple of copies of my own game to satisfy the domestic pre-orders. I ended up redoing the book*, taking it to another printer for printing the pre-orders. This was both costly and time consuming.

    Yet I don't want to dissuade anyone from using DT, it has been a real boost to my sales, and in retrospect worth the extra effort. Just don't expect to ever do things your way when you do business with them.

    * Different POD outfits use differing file formats and layout templates for their stuff, so there's a certain amount of tie in going on. I would like to re-release The Daugthers of Verona via DT, but they can't use the same files I used with SuperiorPOD.

    \n James and Art,

    I have done a couple of games with Lulu before, so that is my basis for comparison. For my latest game (a horror game called Okult, go check it out!) I decided to try DT.

    I submitted the files and wanted to do a proof before going live, as I had not used their process before. This was not really a supported thing to do, but their customer service rep did me "a favor" and allowed me to jump through some hoops and make a proof print.

    The proof looked good, so I wanted to order a couple of books before going live, to give to my backers who had supported the development. That was simply not possible. My request escalated through their support organization but it could not be done, and in general I should be grateful that the service rep I first communicated with had allowed me to make the proof at all.

    It left me with a sour feeling.

    In the end I had my layout guy redo the cover and we sent the game to Lulu, who did a good job at printing a stack of books that I used to satisfy the pre-orders.

    --

    Now, the question is of course, if Lulu are so good, why not use them for everything all the time?

    And my answer is:
    1. People who want to buy digital RPGs go to DriveThruRPG or RPGNow, which is just two fronts for the same company. And not Lulu.
    2. DriveThru offer a Pay What You Want option for publishers.

    1 and 2 combined let me make more money in the first month on DT than I have done in many years put together on Lulu. Even the stuff that I had as free downloads on my site have generated enough paying interest at PWYW pricing to prompt me to go back to one of the games and make a polished re-release. In one month I've had more paying downloads from DT than I've had free downloads in a year on my own site. And for every paying download I see 10-20 freeloaders.

    My stuff reaches a larger audience.

    I get enough money to finance artwork for my ongoing designs. No longer do I have to make an up front investment in my own games to release them, the game design hobby bears it's own costs. (Well, the old games gave me my money back after a while, so I'm not in the hole as they are concerned, but still.)

    That said, the interface on Lulu is so much easier to use, and their process is so much simpler to work with. If I could get Lulu service with DT market penetration I would jump ship right away.
