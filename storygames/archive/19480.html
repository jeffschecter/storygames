\n Hello I\u2019m currently working on a table top rpg that is coming to the end of the design phase, and was thing of including extra options methods for initiative, combat, character advancement, etc. For example initiative can be done a few ways chosen by the games master, super simple combatant with the highest strike rank goes right, etc, second simple combatants roll and add the result to their strike rank, third advanced combatants act according to their action type first speakers, then doers, movers, and finally fighters. These are just three picked at random from some of the methods I have listed for initiative, but the idea behind this is that the GM will have control over how they want the rules themed for their adventure, so a GM that would want a more social game would use the third method listed above in the example, or the first two if they want just plain hack, and slash. As will this in theory would allow the GM to control the level of completely, and time they want to invest in that section of rules.

I\u2019m wondering what others in the community think of this idea, have you seen this done before in a game if so I would love to know what ones, and how it worked out. I\u2019m working with a very simple stripped version of fudge, so I don\u2019t think I would have many problem adding these little GM tweak rules with worry of the system becoming bloated from the added rules.

Question to anyone responding have you played Fudge, if so did you enjoy it, and why.

    \n I think there are definitely advantages to "modular" rule design, and having options to go with a game can be a good thing, so long as it's not overwhelming. (There should be either relatively few options, or a very thorough guide to how to choose the options.)

    However, a whole slew of optional rules often means that the designer hasn't given a whole lot of thought to what their game is about, or how it does what it does. In other words, is it really possible to find four different options which are *equally well* suited to your particular game? Chances are that one or two of those are the best fit; keep those and discard the rest. It's better to have a strong, well-functioning game which is finely tuned than to offer "flexibility" at the expense of functionality.

    I used to play Fudge a lot, and enjoyed it a great deal. The way the adjectives and the Fudge dice work together make it a very fluent and fluid language. Everything is rated on the same scale and is intuitively easy to understand. You don't have to explain to a newbie what "Great Strength" means, which saves a lot of time in one-shots or any situation where you're playing with players unfamiliar with the system. That's a great feature.

    The way everything is rated on the same scale is beautiful, too. You can meaningfully compare a roll, an attribute, the outcome of a roll, a difficulty, etc. (I've never seen another system that can do this; and I think it's potentially very, very potent technology.)

    \n Concerning initiative:
    The Marvel Heroic Roleplaying system (Cortex Plus) has a wonderful initiative system and a lot of people - and even some other games - are using it as default now:
    One character starts (i.e. based on story or attribute). This character chooses who goes next, including NPCs, and so on.
    Intervention in the order is possible spending a Plot Point, Benny whatever.
    You can use two-sided, two colored coins/cards to keep track of a turn.

    Works great!
