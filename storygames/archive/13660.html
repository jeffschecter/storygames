\n Hey folks!

Finally made that last push on my RedBox Hack Hack, bringing it back into the classic D&D world. Because Lord knows the world needs another Fantasy RPG game (it does, honest!). In danger of shortselling myself I'll admit I didn't really bring a whole lot new to the table in terms of wholly original or unique mechanics, but I had a lot of fun figuring out an optimum blend of what I did use. For those in the system designy know I guess you could call it a Prime-Time Adventuresish Basic/Expert dressing over the Red Box Engine with a pepper-grinding of Fourth Edition. There's still a pretty strong DM/player line and the game's definitely more tactical compared to RBH but I think the spirit is Old School Make-it-up-as-you-go D&D emergent story from exploration.

What I'm honestly proud of is the design and presentation, I was really shooting for John Harper-ish levels of immediate approachability and if it's had playtest success, I think that's largely why.

Check it oooooouuuutt! ----> http://www.oldschoolhack.net/2011/01/basic-game/

I really wanted to give a shout-out to the Story-Games Forum because while I don't post a whole heck of a lot here I certainly have been lurking pretty consistently over the years and the enthusiasm, ideas and just generally infectious You-Can-Do-It attitude were pretty much the driving force behind turning this from a homebrew system into a distributable and pitchable game for anyone.

Happy to answer any design or process questions, of course.

    \nOriginal Redbox Hack assumes that Treasure is the de-facto reason to go adventuring, as in "You've heard rumors about this cool treasure/item, now go get it!"

    Personally for my own games I've basically allotted one piece of interesting/weird/cool magic item per session-long adventure, with occasionally extra magic item (or magic item pieces) awarded to those PCs that call out for one specifically in their adventuring goals. Many Magic Items didn't necessarily start out as such, but mundane stuff from quirky "set dressing" that players fixate on (often humorously) will sometimes get "promoted" to Magic Item status by coming up with some sort of interesting story as to why it might be.

    Most of the sample magic items in the game rules were ones that were created by either me or the players in playtesting.

    Posted By: David ArtmanThey have to hit, not just try to attack? Interesting choice. Did you try both ways in playtesting; if so, why did retaliation (rather than counter every attempt) become the rule, if I may ask?
    I think that countering every attempt is too powerful. You'd never have a reason to do anything except Defend, since you'll get to attack anyhow and you get +2 AC out of it. By requiring that you take a hit, you've introduced an element of risk to the action. You can defend an ally, but it's going to cost you. It also limits the sheer power of being defensive.

    A general comment about the rules: You use words/phrases like "usually," "should," "or so," and so on, with alarming frequency. More of the rules should be very concrete things: Freaky Big Monsters should always have a compound action, you should get to pick up to X number of powers (maybe 1 for every 5 HP of the baddie), doing these things requires that you feed the bowl precisely X awesome points, and so forth. I understand that the game is designed to play fast and loose, but you can't also have the rules be so fast and loose. Define more things and have us play fast and loose from there.

    Posted By: David ArtmanSuch vagueness will cause argument. I'd just pick a number like "one per player" or "five" to avoid such kerfuffle.
    This caught me too at first. The later section about minions and monsters provides further clarity, but as I said, this should really be very clearly spelled out.

    I think the intent for DM feeding of The Bowl is that they can pull off stuff just like the players can with Awesome Points, but the DM throws them into The Bowl. Just come out and say that directly.

    I also like the idea of having something like a "That's Bullshit!" rule, where the players can call the DM out on some particularly egregious bullshit. If the DM owns up, add one AP per player to the bowl.

    I would definitely add page directions when you talk about a concept earlier than you flesh it out. For example, on page 15, you say "when he or she does that the DM should just own up to what\u2019s going on and put a bunch more Awesome Points in The Bowl from The Stack." You should add a note at the end: "See page 18 for rules on feeding the bowl." I would also change "put a bunch more Awesome Points..." to say "Feed The Bowl with Awesome Points from The Stack." That way, you introduce the concept, the terminology, and the location for the specific rules. This also keeps the DM rules clearly separated from the player rules.

    On page 17, you start with this: "So far all this talk about 'you' has been directed at the players." That should be introduced on page 1. Introduce the Basic Rules and the Player's Section, and make the DM's section clearly separate. Put a warning in there, Dragon Strike-style, that you're about to enter the realm of actually running the game. That'd help break up the rules logically, and also feed right into the old-school feel you're going for.

    I would like to see rules for non-dungeon stuff. I sort of envision three "states" of the story of an adventuring party. You're either:

    1. Around Town - this is where you rest, investigate, interact with NPC's, buy stuff, and find out about adventures
    2. On the Road - this is where you're traveling, roleplaying and interacting with the other PC's, and possibly encountering wilderness hazards
    3. In the Dungeon - this is where you've arrived at your adventuring destination and now have to delve and explore and such

    These are really sort of broad ideas that inform the sorts of arenas that would exist at that particularly part of the story. It also helps to categorize the sorts of actions that are appropriate. I think it would really strengthen the game to have a page or two talking about how to run parts of the game that aren't just fighting at adventuring locales. Break the phases out and structure them more.

    Perhaps we have a slightly different use of the word "mechanical" then. By that logic, the trousers are "mechanical;" the confer a specific advantage (comfort) under precise conditions (wearing the pants). I refer to the latter sort of "mechanical:" a clear-cut rule which does not require additional interpretation.     Posted By: Paul T.Along the same lines, I'm curious as to why you decided to get rid of Showing Off and "danger rolls"?
    Reading the game over, the biggest mechanic at work is the economy of Awesome Points. Think about it this way. You start a session with X Awesome Points to go around. The only way to get more Awesome Points in The Bowl is for the DM to screw you with some patented egregious DM bullshit. If the DM never pulls any, no more points. So, the DM can completely control the flow of Awesome Points and thus the pace of the game.

    Meanwhile, the players are all competing over the same limited pool of Awesome Points. However, they can't compete too hard, because everyone needs everyone else to get enough AP; additionally, because the points are awarded by other players, you are encouraged to reward each other while simultaneously gunning for your own reward as hard as possible.

    This creates a semi-adversarial relationship with the DM (who is, after all, the guy in charge of monsters) and a competitive/cooperative mechanic among the players (which is totally awesome). The combination of these two factors color the story in a very particular way; they encourage all of the players to push each other to ridiculous heights, while the DM is constantly balancing the need to amp up the challenge with the desire to rein in his group's rate of advancement.

    This creates a totally awesome flow of action in the game.

    Showing Off allows you to completely bypass this economy, and turns the game into one of parallel play, rather than awesome collaborative play.
