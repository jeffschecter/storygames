    \n Did you see the thread on this topic from a few days ago?

    Dustbowl Prophets is really cool. the relationship mechanic in particular is really cool: Driving your relationships towards dramatic situations gets you rewards, as judged by the person you have a relationship with.



    For the larp I ran over the weekend, we used a dirt simple system: everyone starts with four playing cards.* In a conflict, both sides declare what they're doing and choose one of the cards in their hands to play. The higher card wins, then you give the card you played to your opponent and take the one they played. Thus, losing conflicts gets you better cards for later conflicts. After a violent conflict, the loser chose a card to discard from their hand, reducing their hand size for later conflicts.


    *My co-GM and I thought about "stacking the deck" and handing specific cards to specific characters, but ran out of prep time and just gave out cards randomly.

    Posted By: CalvinoWhat, in a political/social/intrigue LARP is better handled with rules than without them?
    \nWell, non-knowledge resources or currency is the obvious example, it's amazing how much more involved people get when resources are more than just a piece of IC chat. Anything that can be unfairly abused by alpha-players roleplaying at people until they capitulate is another, that's a particular danger in socio-political LARPs, especially in a pre-existing community.

    It is context dependent though, based on what people want out of the play, and rules can be as simple as "if he's wearing a green hat, you must follow his orders".

    \n This is probably not a good solution for a political intrigue game, but I offer these ideas as a middle ground between crunchy Minds Eye Theater rules and un-refereed free-form.
    \n
    \nI've been bouncing rules ideas around with my friend, Wil, who is running a horror-film themed LARP for a group of our friends this fall. We're taking kind of a narrativist approach: each player has authority over her character, but also receives a set number of Story tokens at the start of the game. If a player wants something to happen in the story that affects something other than her character, she spends a Story token.
    \n
    \nFor instance, Biff the football player wants to pin Jackson, the shifty loner, to a wall so he can force answers out of him. Biff's player spends a Story token, now Jackson is pinned. Nice and simple.
    \n
    \nObviously, this will only work well if you clearly define the limits of what a player can do. For instance, you can spend a Story token to knock someone out or immobilize them for a scene, but you couldn't spend a token to kill someone. Or you can spend a token to find a clue or add a useful prop to the game: "Look guys, I found a bloody knife under Biff's bed!"
    \n
    \nThe tricky bit is when two players want different things to happen. Our solution is, you can't just spend a token to simply say, "No, that doesn't happen." Spending a token gives the player a brief moment of absolute authority. To change an unfavorable situation, you have to embellish on the situation. For instance, for Jackson to escape Biff, Jackson's player might spend a token and say, "The lights suddenly go out! I escape in the confusion!" Further, you can't spend a token to do the same thing twice, so the next time Biff comes across Jackson, he'll have to think of a new approach.
