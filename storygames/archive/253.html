Spinning Wheels: how much, and why?

James_Nostack
February 2006 edited February 2006 in Story Games
Sometimes one has the urge to game! But there's no group around--so, no problem! Sit down with some books and work up a character, or a world, or (for some people on these boards) an RPG. But, like, if there's no group around to play with, that effort generally goes to waste.

How often do people do this?

If you do it, why?

What are the consequences of doing it? Is it a good or bad thing?

Are some designs more prone to this than others? Is it a desireable design feature or not?

=====
I do it too much. I generally recognize it when it's happening, and kind of resent it, but I'm really bored.

I do it mainly as a kind of doodling: at work or in class, I can't play music, paint a picture, or read novels. So, since I either know all the rules or have them as PDF's, I go from there. (On the other hand, that doesn't explain why it's gaming, as opposed to, say, writing a novel, or learning a programming language.)

I suspect it's a bad habit. You end up investing a fair amount of time in the hobby just for the hell of it, and there's never any return on that investment, which sucks. But also, I suspect that when I play, I'm always wondering: "Y'know, this is kinda fun, but does it redeem the time spent on other stuff?" Which isn't fair to the other players, the GM, or me. YMMV.

(As an aside, I think this phenomenon might be one of those factors influencing the use of Force. "I've spent a year developing this setting, I'll be damned if I'll let you guys trash it in the name of having fun!")

D&D totally facilitates this habit, perhaps to the extent that it's the majority of time spent with the books--making characters "just in case," etc. This feature is probably a strong part of the game's economic performance: you can use 'em "solitaire" and there's always a use for one more book. Primetime Adventures, in contrast, makes this kind of stuff impossible. There's probably several points in between, depending.

    On topic:

    What I find interesting about the question is that there's nothing that says that RPGs can't also be played on your own. GURPS used to have solo adventures, like choose-your-own-adventures, that used GURPS mechanics. Additionally, I spent tons of time designing vehicles and making characters and dude, that was fun. Hell yes, this helps explain some success stories, because the product had more than on entertaining use, and that's awesome.

    The thing about Lonely Fun is that it is, still, Fun. I don't think it's worthwhile comparing it to Communal Fun, because they're different animals. I do think, however, that it's worthwhile to consider how we can design game products that provide both Lonely Fun and Communal Fun.
