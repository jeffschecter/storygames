    \n It seems tome that there's a certain kind of journalistic filmmaking ("highlight reel") that amounts to jumping around confusingly to convey an overall zeitgeist of the experience, and inject a sense of excitement and energy through a rapid succession of images.

    It strikes me that filming of roleplaying experiences tends to fall into this style...presumably because even a good roleplaying session is kind of a slow-moving, tedious thing to watch, less like viewing a play and more like eavesdropping on a dinner conversation.

    The effect though, in my experience, has been to "other" the experience somewhat, to in essence say, "roleplayers, you know how to fill in the blanks, and the rest of you--don't bother yourself, this is a complicated, opaque and alien affair that you'll never understand."

    I do hope the following episodes are less like that.

    \n I think Eero is closer in successful design concept I've seen so far in filmed RPG content.

    The trick is, how do you communicate to the audience what's actually going on in play -- when so much of what is going on in play is going on in the player's heads. To atomize the choices of the players and communicate them is key. To atomize why what's happening is happening in terms of the game at hand is also key.

    When the video got to the point where all the players with PCs were sitting around trying to reach consensus, I had several quick reactions: 1) "Oh, Christ. I forgot that happened all the time in D&D." 2) "Wow, why does that happen all the time in D&D?" 3) "It'd be really cool if this show really broke out why this is happening."

    I have no idea if this show is interested in doing this, of course.

    I think another design element would be to see how much you could structure communication of what's going on without first depending on the poker game commentator. The television poker design is a brilliant construction element... and I'd steal it in a heartbeat. But it could end up being a crutch. The design under that design is, "What are the points of play you'd really want to communicate?" You'd make sure to figure out how to capture those and edit them. The commentary would be another layer of that.

    Here's the thing: There are televised games of poker where HOW someone is playing is the focus. And then there are televised games involving pornstars or whatever where the focus is just hanging out with people hanging out together.

    I'm not sure which show this show wants to be.
