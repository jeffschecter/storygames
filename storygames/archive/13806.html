\n There was a discussion about the d20 system where "Wordman" said this:\n
Posted By: Wordman9) The alignment system opens up some interesting notions. (Bear with me here.) The idea that not only does every character in the game have a definitive moral stance, but that you canactually use magic to detect what that code iscolors the game more deeply than you might think. If you ask a bunch of people "what makes this game great", pretty much no one will yell out "the alignment system", but I think it is a big part of it. I'm not entirely sure how, though
\nAnd then Jamie said:\n
Posted By: jdfristromAnd Detect Evil is great if you just want to play a game where you kill monsters with no moral ambiguity. They needed killin'. (They nerfed this in Pathfinder, interestingly - you can't detect alignment on low level characters. It's like, "Look, how cute, he's trying to be evil, but he's such a n00b it doesn't show up on my evil detector." - we actually had some good moral-arguing-banter-roleplay in my last Pathfinder campaign.)
\nI really want to hear more about this.

I've played a lot of D&D, and I can't remember a single instance, ever, when alignments enhanced our gaming. The only times they ever even made sense was when they helped us reimagine characters (e.g. "What does it mean that your character is Neutral Evil?" "Well, what I really mean is that he is selfish, and doesn't see the world in terms of black and white, but rather in shades of grey." "Oh, OK, cool."). So I suppose that merely posing the question ("Is your character good or evil? Lawful or Chaotic?") puts you in a position to consider your character in a way you might not normally ever ponder. (Who among us ever wonders, walking through life, "Am I good or evil? Is that grocery store attendant good or evil?" "Good" and "evil" are rarely seen as personal identities, but more often assigned as descriptions of acts and choices.)

For my groups, dealing with Evil alignments among the "heroes" was particularly problematic. What does it mean to be evil? If I am evil, do I think of myself that way? (If detection magic has ever been used on you, how do you feel about discovering you are "evil"? That must be bizarre.) How can I, as a player, portray my character being evil without just, say, engaging in random violence or spoiling the game for everyone (D&D was rarely a good game for PvP play, as it generally assumes a team of heroes working together)? And so on. Problematic.

So:

In this thread, please tell us about a time when D&D alignments or something very similar (you know what I mean) made for some Truly Great Gaming, or elevated your game to something better than it would have been in their absence. Surprising events, revelations, unusual scenarios?

How can alignments awesome-ify a game? (You can also share some stories of how alignments really ruined a game, but if you do it had better be really really entertaining.)

Instead, given that the system comes with things like detect alignment spells, understanding must start by answering the question "what is it that spells like detect alignment are detecting"?

    How you choose to answer this question for your game makes a fundamental, if subtle, difference in the whole game world.

    In one game I played in, the idea was that since detect alignment was usually a devine spell, the answer it gave was filtered through the world-view of the god that gave it. Thus, any in-game discussion didn't involve words like "lawful evil", but rather "how righteous you were in the eyes of Thor". Similarly, spells like protection from evil or dispel evil used the same filtering concept.

    In another game, alignment was viewed as an "intrinsic nature" that you were born with and trapped in, and the spells detected this; however, because spells like detect alignment exist, basically everyone knew of this "intrinsic nature trap" as a proven fact and a good deal of the background story of the game world involved various factions and forces seeking a way to break out of the trap. It led to invasions, wars, flimsy justification, and so on. This game, naturally, required a good deal of buy-in from the players in order to play within their trap.

    You could argue that the "start by deciding why detect alignment works" approach is more of a house rule sort of thing. Maybe.       \n I've had fun with alignment a couple times.

      Once, I played a cleric of Shiva. I was focused on the dual nature of Shiva - Creation/Destruction, Man/Woman, etc. My character was unable to get his head completely around that dual nature and basically ended up with a split personality. But it split along racial lines. So my character was Good toward humans, but Evil toward demihumans (he particularly hated halfers, like centaurs and satyrs and mer and such). So if you used a Detect Evil on him, your answer would depend on who you were - I used the alignment system as a mechanical reinforcement of my character's pathological racism. We had one person playing a half-elf in the party when I played. So as a player, I talked with her about it outside of game and got her buy in to be gunning for her character - I made sure she was well stocked with magical healing and such, so she wouldn't be relying on my character to serve his party role toward her during game. During the game, there was an encounter where we ended up needing to perform some sort of sacrifice in order to move on. I got the monster to eat the half-elf's finger as the sacrifice (she suffered a semi-permanent -1 to damage or something like that). That was a perfect win for me and my character - cause grief to the hated half-elf without being a total jerkface out of game and making the other players hate me. Good times. And I think having the good/evil duality be present in the game let to that interesting character concept, which I don't think I would have thought about otherwise. That being said, it was a rare instance of alignments actually being interesting. And I was customizing the general mechanic a bit to achieve it.

      More recently, I spent over a year playing a Lawful Evil character in a LARP campaign. In that world, there's no detection spells and you don't have to have an alignment or anything. But the campaign world grew out of an old tabletop D&D campaign, so the faction I made the character in was created as an example of a Lawful Evil concept. The faction was a knightly order known as the Shadowblades. And the cool thing about them is that they have a well justified semi-good-guy-ness to them - one of the primary goals of the order is to fight demons. However, they do so in a "by any means necessary" sort of way - but not quite "any means" because he held a few things to be much closer to inviolate than the general player base. I created the character on purpose to throw a little more PvP conflict into the game, but, again, without being a total jerkface. Part of the fun was in the fact that, as you guys have been describing, a lot of the players were playing "whatever we do is right cause we're the good guys" style. So my character would call them on their shenanigans. If they made a deal with an NPC and then broke it because they decided he was a bad guy so their word didn't matter, I'd call them on that. If they got annoyed with an NPC that they were trying to get information from and started torturing them, I'd call them on that. I didn't believe in torture - I took prisoners for very specific purposes. If I was going to offer a prisoner an opportunity to earn their freedom by giving up information, I would do just that - I'd let them know how things were and then ask questions - If I got satisfying answers, I let them go and if not, I'd kill them. If I wanted to take prisoners in order to do an army to army prisoner exchange, then I did that. But torture was unacceptable. But some things that were considered culturally unacceptable in the world, I did. Like using necromancy to raise the corpses of my enemies to fight other enemies - I had no problem doing that - in fact I was saving lives by doing so. I had tons of fun playing a character with a bizarre sense of morality who felt 100% justified about his actions. He even had some good-guy tendencies, but those were tempered with a bizarre twist. For example, he would throw himself into danger in a way that looked very much like a heroic good guy. But if you asked him why he was willing to put himself into such dangerous situations, he would explain that everyone else was obviously too incompetent to handle such a situation, so he had to do it himself. One of my goals with the character was to make people like him, but feel bad about themselves for liking him. And he accomplished that goal admirably.

      \n Sorry for indulging the sidebar, but it has made me think about how Alignment has made for awesome gaming: it's kind of flagging what the game isn't about.

      I've played plenty of games where 'Chaotic Good' was a lot like more explicit flagging, along the lines of Beliefs. Alignments are not meant to be challenged like beliefs, they're a way of communicating what the game is explicitly not about. The way that alignment has made my gaming more awesome is by removing a potential fruitful void to focus on what the game is about.

      Writing down Chaotic Good specifically says you don't want to worry about law, just kick some evil ass, and have the people praise you for it. Lawful Evil says you want a hierarchy of evil you can climb, inspiring more fear and hatred along the way. It's an explicit way of communicating interests and assumptions.

      It's actually a great, sneaky way of communicating what the game's about without creating a lot of background. I don't have to explain that my fighter stands up for the weak by freeing the slaves of the evil empire of Kara'urh, or that my barbarian fights for the Redclaw Clan to drive off the demons of the dark woods. I can just say "Neutral Good" or "Chaotic Good" and get on with it. In a game where one of the most reasonable opening scenes is your characters outside the entrance to an undergound treasure trove, that's a powerful shortcut.

      It's also an explicit fantasy tag, telling you this is a fantasy game.

      \n Where I've seen the benefit with alignment was the one time I decided to knock it out of the game (playing AD&D 2E), but one of the characters was a Paladin. The player, being familiar with AD&D assumed Lawful Good, and when the Drow who had ambushed them, and previously taken a High-elf Prisoner (but not killed, interestingly enough), surrendered, the player realised that he couldn't go through with killing.

      Ostensibly the benefit of alignment is that in a game where the primary mechanical means of interaction is combat, and for which the reward is XP, having a mechanic that imposes an XP penalty is a good way to encourage alternative courses of action. Now, you could probably get the exact same result with a well thought out social combat mechanic, or even the direction 2E was going with giving rewards for completion of quests, as well as acting in character, and de-emphasising combat rewards.

      It makes sense really, the game gets interesting as you acquire new powers, and you acquire new powers by leveling up, which comes by way of XP, which explicitly comes by way of killing. Obviously you'll be looking at killing things as a primary course of action, so you need something to encourage alternate courses of action. Alignment encourages non-combat actions by way of discouraging combat in certain situations. Where it falls apart though is leaving the option for non-Good alignments. Once you're neutral, killing becomes a pretty viable option, and people would go for chaotic neutral simply because they could act based on mechanical benefit and not have their alignment conflict. It essentially became the metagaming alignment, and unless you played someone insane, wouldn't really lead to any roleplaying.

      Probably much more interesting would be to have each character with a code of ethics, and Dragonlance 5th Age went this direction by having nature and demeanor (and mechanically encouraging "good" type natures and demeanors by having them on the low numbered cards. Having a list of things you must do or must not do provides some good roleplaying constraints, and doesn't lead to the kinds of (out of game) arguments alignment leads to. In game are certainly quite possible, and barring planescape it probably works out better. It's also what Palladium/RIFTS did, which definitely confirms that's how people around the time were using alignment.

      \n Wrapping back around to the original question of times alignment makes gaming better:

      Last night, Dungeon World. John's playing a Thief, and he's decided he's a goblin (using the Halfling move). As we introduced characters, he says he's Good.       So, having the idea of being Good or Evil be a concrete thing gave us a great seed for lots of great moments. As it turns out, the spell that shifted the thief's alignment had been planted by the bard, etc.

      Yes, we can puzzle over who thinks of themselves as evil and so on, but I tend to view alignment as part of a game as being a cue. When I see alignment, it says: evil is not subtle or banal in this game, it's full-on mustachio-twirling cackling Evil.
