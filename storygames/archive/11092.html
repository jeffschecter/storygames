\n Unlike the "top five unplayed" thread recently, the games for this thread are things that aren't sitting and waiting for an opportunity to play. They're too weird, too confusing, or just plain too bad for you to play, and yet you can't throw them out.

DC Heroes - I love the game's exponential scaling, but it's too baroque for me these days with the lookup table, the AV/EV/OV/RV, and so forth.
TORG - Again, kind of a baroque system. Also perhaps too hokey a setting for my current players, and possibly me too. Too intriguing to chuck.
Chill - Donated to me by a friend. Sorry, Q - I'll probably never play it.
Iron Heroes - I love all the tactical options. It's probably an awesome play. I'll never be able to find gamers who want to do "3rd edition D&D but more complex and less familiar to us," even if I run Dark Sun with it.
Core Command - I was basically just disappointed.

You?

    \nShatterzone -- I own this for nostalgia's sake. There are plenty of broken rules in here... frex, it's easier to hit people when they're farther away. Why? Because, ostensibly, they can't move out of your way as much. I'd argue, they don't have to, because if you move the tip of your gun by just a tenth of an inch, then the distance is going to amplify your mistake, making this an idiotic argument. But I like the cards (same as TORG, I believe) and some other stuff.

    Iron Heroes -- While I love the tactical options in theory, they're full of fail. By that, I mean the whiff factor is really high. My favorite classes get just a token here and there, then they have to use that token before their roll to try and have an added (mostly minimal) effect, and then the to-hit roll is likely to miss. Cool character concepts, horrible rules execution. Maybe mostly because of the D20 baggage.

    Robotech -- Also a nostalgic thing for me. I've got the whole series, all core books plus expansions. Love the mecha, hate the rules.

    KULT -- I used to run this for years. I still love the setting, but there's no good guidance on what the hell to do with it. Nor do the rules support it (martial arts masters in a Hellraiser setting? Wha?).

    HeroQuest -- A vastly generic rule system, but then there are a million listings for all kinds of different things? I'm just not feeling it. The system sure has its good parts, but there's just something lacking for me.

    \nArtesia: Adventures in the Known World - this is something I'd dearly love to play, but to do it properly would involve finding a group that is committed the minutia of the setting as well as willing to engage with a moderately crunchy rule set. I set the probability of that every happening as quite low.

    CthuluTech - marvelous central conceit, married to rules that are clunky and uninteresting and do little to advance that. I have a someday vision of using Reign to support large scale factional feuding and importing Unnamed Armies derived sanity mechanics from Nemesis. Perhaps something to return to when Reign Enchiridion is out, but lets face it, I don't have the kind of time to invest into such a wholesale rewrite.

    Swordbearer - obscure early fantasy RPG with some intriguing subsystems (the node based magic system, and the social class based equipment/encumbrance system) but with its fair share of clunk as well. I'd play it, but again, I'm unlikely to find a group willing to engage with a quirky, crufty rule system over time just for the sake of the answering the question "what would engaging with these rules be like?"

    Dragon Warriors - Britain's answer to Red Box D&D, and broadly similar (but with its own unique fillips). Recently revived as a line by Mongoose. Most distinctive in terms of tone, which is more authentically medieval/fairy tale than D&D's pulp picaresque. Likely to remain a source of inspiration, rather than actual play.

    Miles Christi - a fabulously evocative, out of print, French language RPG about the historical crusades, with a distinctly magical realist bent. I have the original books, which I cannot read, plus an unofficial translation prepared by a person with whom I have since lost contact.

    \n If I were to hack Everway, I'd give each player a hand of cards that they could play in order to "trump" the GM's narration and take narration rights for themselves. But if the GM chose instead to base the decision on karma, players could give up narration rights in order to gain a card. Because reading cards to determine the consequences of a conflict is fun. Trust me.

    So, for example, if the consequence card is "Law" to the action "I swing my sword," the implication is that the blow is true, unless the defender has been noted to be particularly well-armored. But of course, "I swing my sword" is task resolution, not conflict resolution, so no wonder the game falls flat.

    An alternate hack is to let each side in the conflict declare an intention and a related action and then draw a card for that intention/action, and use the juxtaposition of declared intentions and actions, simultaneously revealed cards, and overall context to determine the outcome of the conflict. GM: "They skulk at the edges of the wood, seeming to grow bolder as the sun gets lower in the sky," and then Player: "I charge at them headlong in order to frighten them away." The GM draws "Drowning in Armor" (Protective Measures Turn Dangerous) and the player draws "Law." This seems to me to mean that the hero's ploy scatters the skulkers as the shadowy woods conceals his position and makes his attacks more nerve-wracking.

    It takes practice, but it's fun.

    \nOwn And Probably Will Never Play
    Burning Empires - My crew digs sci fi... but not military sci fi. My crew digs Burning games... but not rigid scene structure/timing. My crew will not read that much, nor will pay that much to read that much. It is ill-suited to being a one-shot at a con (but I'd love to see an argument to the contrary!).

    Exalted 2E - Got the core rules and three or four supplements, new, at a MACE con auction for $30. Just not my style of game anymore, nor my crew's. Will probably sell on eBay when I retire.

    Dust Devils - Only purchased because of its reputation and threat that it would never be in print again (grr). Unless we drift, I doubt my crew would be into the default setting (Old West). But still worth the price, if only for a look at the guts of the system.

    Own And Will Probably Never Play Again
    AD&D - Unless we get an urge for some nostalgic hack and loot, these are merely for the collection and reminiscing. Just too many other games which can scratch this itch AND give a reach-around for good measure.

    Pendragon - I'd like to think not, but it might be a LONG time before I can muster someone to play in this setting again.

    Warhammer FRPG - Like AD&D, too many other good games scratch this itch. Also, the new game will probably supplant the old one, if anyone in my crew buys it--we're already Descent geeks; WRPG3E would push all kinds of buttons! (Yes, I know they are very little alike.)

    Game I WILL Play Someday, Even If I Have To Kidnap Someone To Do So
    Everway - Too interesting, too flavorful, too weird and influential NOT to be played some day. Probably while tripping.

    Sufficiently Advanced - Transhumanism FTW. Then again... I might live long enough to "LARP" this setting... or even live in it for real (minus time-traveling AI, which I think is kind of silly-awesome). No other game I've read is more like Iain Banks' Culture; and The Culture is hella-fucking-cool transhumanism RPG fodder, be it serious or rollicking. (I'll knife fight anyone who wants to use this as a springboard for their pet theories about transhumanist progress and, therefore, why Banks is a nit-wit.)

    Fiasco - I played "Remi's Sorta-Kinda-Close Rules Variant" at MACE; and I'm ONE scene into a month-long Play-by-Wave game. I will play this game... someday.

    \n Vampire / Werewolf / Mage / Changeling / Wraith
    These are the games the honed my GM skills, and lead me to want a more story based game. I still love much of the setting of these games, but to tell these stories I would use a game like Sorcerer.

    Kult / Chill / Witchcraft
    I got these to help me better understand running horror style games. Kult has a setting and world which I love, but I'd use another system to tell these stories as well.

    AD&D 1E & 2E / OD&D
    I never made the move to 3E or 4E, and now that I have discoverd Fudge and Fate never will

    Cyberpunk / Heaver Gear / Bubblegum Crisis / Action! / Fuzion
    This is still my favorite rule heavy system. I have nearly everything for Cyberpunk from the original box set to 2030 revision. It's the second RPG I ever played, and the first one I GMed. I currently want to run a power-armored anime game along the lines of NeonGeneses or BubbleGum Crisis, but I want a system with a better social and relationship mechanic, so I can bring out those aspects. I'm still looking for the system I will use.

    Dust Devil / The Pool / Troll Babe
    These games are too narrative for me. I found I did not like just handing over of narrative control to the winner or loser of a conflict, to give a monologue. But I did find that adding a fact type system worked well, so something like Houses of the Blooded is in the right balance for this style of games.

    Burning Wheel Revised
    It just a little heavy and the multiple conflicts system are a little convoluted. Now the I have Mouse Guard which I think nearly perfected the Burning wheel system, I see no reason to play the original.

    ZoZ / T&J / PDQ
    I found that Risus just played smother then PDQ. I have some interest in PDQ# I like the style stuff, but it's unlikely I will move away from Risus for our quick hit games.

    Some Games I hope to play soon
    DitV / IaWA
    I love both these games. Though my play group has been resisting playing either of these. I hope to find some people to play at least one of them over the holidays.

    StarBlazers
    Waiting for one of the current campaign to end before starting this.

But then I discovered Indie Games and yer basic Skills'n'Feats, Roll-Dice+mod vs Target Number tasky ruleset wouldn't cut it for this kind of thing. Plus it was horribly broken. Nowdays if I ever run a Middle Earth game I'll use Heroquest.
