\n I just gotta vent somewhere...

It seems that the one thing that bugs me more than anything else with Game Chef critiques (either of my own game or someone else's) is when someone pops up to say "this isn't really a roleplaying game!" Usually followed by their definition of what a roleplaying game is, should be, or needs to have.

It's not just that the objection itself is annoyingly presumptive -- it is, though! -- or that it's not helpful. (I mean, obviously whoever created the game, as part of Game Chef, thinks it's a game and wants it to be treated as a game.)

It's also that these types of definitional arguments, these fixed ideas about what makes "a roleplaying game" and what doesn't, really do prevent us from pushing the limits on roleplaying games. They drive us back into a box, in a game design event that, at its best, is about climbing out of that box. They set boundaries on what games can and can't be -- and pile doubtful skepticism on any games which leave the roleplaying game ghetto.

This is why I don't ever want to hear, in a critique, "is this really a roleplaying game?" Obviously the designer of the game thinks it is, and she wants you to engage it on that level. Your opinion doesn't matter more than hers. Put that particular question aside and never ask it.

It's the worst thing you can ask in a GC critique.

    \n I don't know where to draw the line with critiques. To critique is to judge. To cast judgments. Critiques are opinions, not laws. Kynn, my problem with you saying "But is it a role-playing game?" is presumptive is that ALL judgment is presumptive. All critique. Where does it stop?

    I, now (finally!) have gotten three critiques and, well....

    "Hi, I'm David, and my game is not a role playing game." ("Hi, David.")

    I intentionally wanted to place the players squarely, dead on the line between self and character, by expecting players to address real, personal issues, but through the fictional lens of a nebulous setting ("The Party Under The Bed") and a stylized character form (a plush toy--NOT a voodoo doll or self-portrait or photo). And my reviewers are all--aside from being terrified to actually play it to the hilt--failing to find the "role playing" in it... or the "game," for one reviewer. They see it as only playing oneself, with a mask on. I say the mask is a role; and even the self is one, most times--who among us REALLY, ALWAYS is true to their personality and needs, and NEVER plays a role to suit some fiction in "real life"?

    Anyway... I can use that feedback ONLY because the reviewers unpack the points they found contrary (which I'd call Zen, but that's another thread). One spoke of "light, nuthin-but-characters" play, and sure, I could expand some sections to cover that. One spoke of "starting small, with less intense issues," and sure, I can give advice about that in there, too--the art doesn't quite fit a Scar for "Lazy," but fuck it. I can DO that, ya see?

    But those reviewers who, for instance, chose to ignore or gloss-over my quoting of Meguey's "I will not abandon you is not no one gets hurt" article in full--including IWNAY and NGH abbreviations which, dig it, one reviewer though were game titles (?!?)--means they basically tossed the true core of the game out... and then, yeah, wow, there's not much reason to play, here, now, huh? Entirely fictional characters with minor, nuisance issues, playing with other players who promise never to hurt each other... fuck, it, man, go play D&D, I say. They want to strip away or ignore all three legs of the stool that is FMA, and then wonder at the useless board left behind ("it would make a good horror game"--seriously? With Plush dolls?!?). That's the yang of the yin of good definitional work (i.e. "I think an RPG is THIS")--you end up having to adjust a critique to accommodate what they have ignored or what they expect to work differently, while simultaneously avoiding the (understandable) tendency to make the very changes they suggest, which might only lead an "innovative" game back to the herd of "proper, complete" RPGs.

    Yeah, I'm trying to sort my thinking out before replying to my reviewers. At this point, I am largely inclined to ask direct questions--"Which part was under-written; where would you like to see more examples of play; in what sort of play would you feel safe to engage?"--and just use them as eyes on pages. I mean, seriously--if you wrote a game which had, in its third paragraph, a rule that is called "The Prime Directive"... and your reviewer begins with "I'm gonna skip over this stuff because I don't understand it"... what in heavens' name can I do with that? You've all ready Meguey's article--it's one page of superior conciseness in theory and example; it's perfect. I will do a VERY "soft edit" on it in the final pub--expand IWNAY and NHG, and include "play" after those terms, to be totally clear--but I don't know what to say to someone who can't understand that article and, thus, rather than work at it, skips it.

    "The Prime Directive"... ignored... because it's a new concept for you... and might take two readings of a single page of examples to figure out?

    What do ya do with THAT?

    \n As the guy who introduced the rule originally I think my explanation was something like:
    \n
    \n"We have to narrow down the contest in some way, so we don't get just any sort of entry. It's part of the limitations in general that make the contest work. Look at your work, and determine for yourself if it's a RPG. If it's not, don't submit it. If you're not sure, submit it, and perhaps you'll have expanded the field of RPGs."
    \n
    \nI don't want to search up my actual quote, but it's something like that.
    \n
    \nI did say that I might downgrade the score for an entry (this was back when I alone scored every entry), if I thought it wasn't substantively a RPG. But even if Andy's theoretical "Poo" had been entered, I would have simply rated it very low, not disqualified it. For me, at least, it wasn't an attempt at all to define RPGs or something, but instead to create yet another constraint for the contest. The basic one. It's a RPG. It has these ingredients. Etc.
    \n
    \nAnd, yeah, though somebody who won could crow about having won, my ratings were really just a method of feedback. In practice, since I'm one guy with my own odd tastes, the winners didn't tend to be the games that were published or successful in actual play. So obviously the "winners" are really not especially so, but instead are merely getting critique that says that the game looks cool.
    \n
    \nIt's more about challenging yourself inside of a set of constraints which tend to make people actually produce something. Which is useful in that some designs come out of it, but even more useful in that it's practice for people which hopefully translates into later, better designs.
    \n
    \nTo that extent, I laud the folks who make games that get the question "Is it a RPG" because I believe that all of them were trying to make good RPGs that simply are innovative in some ways. Yeah, there is a tendency for some of them to be "boardgamey" (what's sometimes called "parlor games" in some cases), but in making these, and checking out whether or not this is the case, we learn something about what we want from our designs. John now understands that he doesn't want a boardgame, but something that is somehow fundamentally to him a RPG. And he knows some places where that might fall away, if not supported.
    \n
    \nNote that I consider every failure in Game Chef to be as important as every success. I've always maintained that producing a game in such short order is bad for designs, other than it tends to make them happen. You would never do this if you wanted to make a good game. It's an exercize. If somebody does manage to come up with a design that they then further develop over time into something that equates to good actual play, great! But that's not why we have the contest. We have the contest to practice the skills of design, and perhaps to generate inspiration. But not to create good playable games. That's nigh impossible.
    \n
    \nAs such, any critique of the game is saying, "I know it's not a real game, but if it were, it would have these problems, so watch for that next time you actually take the time to do the design. Apologies if you would have done this had you had more time, but I cannot know that." In that context, it's just fine to say, "it's not a RPG" as long as you go the extra mile to explain what that means, and perhaps try to suggest some way to fix it.
    \n
    \nI don't understand at all how any of this is controversial.

    \nDavid, what do you do when people didn't understand what you thought you wrote clearly? Write it again.
    \n
    \nOK, if it's one person, and you think that they might not have put in a basic level of effort to learn the rules... then you can ignore that data point. As soon as it's more than one person, however, you have a phenomenon, and you need to address it.
    \n
    \nNo matter how clear you think the text is, that doesn't mean that it actually is clear to others. When this is the case, it's hard to see why they can't understand, and it's also hard for the person in question to see why they would be able to understand, and, thus explain the disconnect.
    \n
    \nThis is why editors exist. Because they sit in the middle between the author and the reader. They are neither so close to the writing that they fail to see the problems in communication, nor are they oblivious as to the intent of the text. So they can tell you how to re-write so that others can understand.
    \n
    \nAssume that your text communicates what you think it does at your own risk. If people didn't get Meg's lines... well frankly the first time I read her stuff on that subject I had to take some time to figure out what she was on about. In fact, if I had to recapitulate it right now, I think I could not. There's something about that idea that seems to be very intuitive to some, but for people like me, is very confusing.
