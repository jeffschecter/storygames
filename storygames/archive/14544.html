    \n In a comment over on Crapping on your Dream: Freelancing 101, Owen Stephens wrote:\n
    There are two or more totally different sets numbers about what is \u201cstarting\u201d full-time pay, and what is \u201cupper end\u201d full-time pay for a tabletop RPG writer \u2014 and neither is ever going to be very accurate. One is for freelancer/small company writers, and the other is for major company writers which at this point may be limited to Paizo and WotC).

    As a neophyte if you successfully manage full-time small company/freelance work, you\u2019re likely looking at the poverty level for a household of one \u2014 $12k give-or-take. It is very hard to say what the upper limit for that is, and there will be outliers. I will say for \u201can Owen Stephens\u201d you\u2019re looking at an average pretty close to $25k/year. That average is, however, wildly useless for planning purposes. People I know at my current rate (which hasn\u2019t gone up in the past few years), have made as little as $15k, and as much as $42k. And as the company I am lead developer and part owner for (Super Genius Games) continues to grow, and other freelance continues to shrink, I may find myself wildly outside what I consider average for people at my level.

    For a major company, you aren\u2019t likely to start for less than $27k, and I\u2019d consider $31k more typical. Getting to $50 over the course of a career is certainly possible. Exceeding $60k while remaining an rpg game designer is unlikely, though your career may well lead to different places, different games, or different jobs that do reach higher.

    All of this is the wildest of estimation, so take it all with two grains of salt and a suspicious look.
    \nThose numbers ring true to what other people have told me.

    \n My question these days is: why would you try to make a living in RPG design?

    There are people doing this for fun, in their free time, putting out content that's on par with anything that was made as part of a full time job. Lady Blackbird is better looking than many "full-time" products, and John more or less just made it as a lark. Apocalypse World and Fiasco are some of my favorite recent games, and both Vincent and Jason have other jobs that (I presume) put the food on the table. They don't seem to need more time to develop their games, or more funding for art or whatever.

    From what I can tell, once you're putting out a product that's your livelihood, you're less likely to take chances, more likely to push yourself, and more likely to burn out. None of those are absolutes, but they seem more common when what you're designing is your bread and butter.

    Which isn't to say you can't make money. I will happily pay John, Jason, and Vincent (and many others) a fair price for their products. Hell, I'll probably give them a little more because they're great people who make great stuff. I just don't see why anyone would try to turn a fun, (presumably) profitable side-venture into a full time job.

    \n*Skeptical face*. If something is your full time job you will get better at it, not worse. If you go from doing something as a hobby to spending all your time on it, you will get better at taking risks, because you will have a better understanding of what you are doing, what risks are fruitful, recognizing when to abandon a risky project, etc.; and you will be able to devote more time to taking risks (in absolute terms) even if more of your time (proportionately) goes towards 'non-risky' pursuits, because you will have more time in total to devote to the pursuit.

    Artists who only paint on the weekend do not generally take more risks than full-time artists who are devoted to a career in art, because part of any creative pursuit is the ability to take taking risks seriously.

    Posted By: lumpleyI can see $50k profit a year (to pull the number from Owen Stephen's passage) in lumpley games' future, sure, piece of cake.
    \nSage and other folks seem to be ignoring this post, but I think it's pretty important. What the threads originally linked above seem to be arguing is that you can't make a living doing it in certain specific ways that many people have tried before, which doesn't mean that it's impossible. It just means, if it's going to be possible for you, you might have to go about it in a very different way.

    Like, to riff off Vincent's case, you could release a game -- designed largely based on your own preferences, not the demands of the market -- every couple years, make each one really good, and gradually build up a back-catalog of games and a large audience ready to purchase your next one -- all while maintaining a full-time job. Basically, you follow the "indie game publishing model" and repeat this process until you're making enough every year to quit your day job or at least cut way back and work part-time.

    But that requires a much more long-term plan than most of the linked threads have in mind: a ten-year plan at least. And there's no guarantee that it's going to work, in the end, if you can't build up enough of a following. But, in the meantime, it's much more economically feasible and totally under your creative control.

    \n Here are three game designers. I'm not sure any of them exist, they're just examples.

    Game designer A follows her heart, makes the games she loves, works hard at it. The games she loves are also loved by the market. She enjoys her work, and makes money off it. She could do this forever.

    Game designer B follows her heart, makes the games she loves, works hard at it. The games she loves are ignored by the market. She enjoys her work, but makes nothing off it. She could do this forever, but must spend time & energy doing other things for money.

    Game designer C makes games aimed at the market, that aren't really games she likes herself, but she works hard at it. Her work feels like just another job after a while. She makes money off it, but won't go on forever.

    My point: Getting artistic fulfilment AND making money can only happen if you're hard-working AND the thing you love to make also happens to be what people want to buy.

    \n The long tail is where it's at. Selling a copy of your swank new game at GenCon is swell, sure, but may yield you $5 - $10 in profits after booth and travel expenses are paid for. Getting someone to play that game, love it, and return to purchase your other 5 games by mail-order or PDF sales will actually pay for a couple of days of rent. Until you have that back catalogue to rely on for real, sustainable income, don't quit your dayjob - and don't blow your savings on a hardcover, full-colour, 300-page, 1000-unit print run.

    The "supplement treadmill" is what kept the bigger game publishers afloat for as long as it did, and that only lasted for so long before new editions and limited bookshelf space caused people to slow down and be happy with what they had. Since Vincent is our prime example in this thread, I'd like to high-light what I believe makes him so successful, other than his excellent design, hard work, and subliminal promotional activities - Vincent actually has a "brand" of consistent design following the same core game theory and values, with each game exploring a slightly different or more evolved viewpoint or theme, and the games are narrow enough in scope that you can't, won't, just play one forever until you croak. If you fall in love with Apocalypse World, you're going to also want to play Dogs, and IAWA, and Poison'd. Similarly, if you fall in love with Burning Wheel you're going to want to at least try Burning Empires or Mouse Guard, and if you love Spirit of the Century you're going to want to be chomping at the bits to try Dresden Files.

    So, build a back catalogue, stay consistent with your values, explore new territory, and keep your games narrow in scope, until your income is stable enough that you can focus your efforts solely on game design. Make bigger and smaller games; promote yourself and your designs through free PDF games, mods or supplements to other people's games, and interface with (but do not be enslaved by) your audience. Don't try to create a magnus opus and expect to retire. Gamers today aren't hankering for a massive system-to-end-all-systems - or worse, setting-to-end-all-settings.

    On the 1000 fans topic, I think it's feasible, but nobody's really figured out the magic sauce that makes today's relatively liquid RPG gamer consistently loosen their purse strings in support of their hobby. I'll easily drop $30 for a WFRP3 supplement, but I know well-off gamers who won't pay $20 for a whole RPG - and even I will balk at ponying up $20 for a PDF. Fantasy Flight sells a bazillion board games at $80 a pop, but getting someone to pay $50+ for an RPG core book is painfully difficult. I'm guessing most groups spend a combined $20 - $40 just on snacks and drinks for their weekly sessions. My 14yo's Warhammer 40k army has cost him (well, me, in the end) over $200 so far, and he barely plays (I don't even want to think about his Magic cards). When someone figures out a new formula for getting gamers to put $20 / month into their hobby, this will become more feasible. Of course, RPGs being an intrinsically creative endeavour, the eternal question is how to deliver $20 of value to a player of your game per month when they are essentially generating 90% of the value themselves.

I've also caught myself enjoying countless hours of prep work for games as disparate as D&D and Sorcerer, even when the games themselves fell down in play. Lonely Fun is absolutely a selling point, even for games as social as RPGs.

Easy to pick up, play with different people - you can introduce the rules for games like A Penny for my Thoughts or Lacuna to a new player as you play and they'll pick it up right away, which means you can start (and finish - see #2) a game with any pool of players you happen to have available.

I'd rather pay $10 extra for a game that includes the cards, or $20 extra for a game that includes the board, than spend time printing and cutting and gluing (hear that, Montsegur 1244?!?) - especially if the acquired product looks more professional.     8. Playable out of the box - A Penny for my Thoughts allows for this, as does Zombie Cinema and the games you describe.     a) you want me to read this whole setting before I can run the game, which conflicts with 6 and 8 and only marginally benefits 1.
    b) you probably want my players to read parts of the setting too, which conflicts with 3 and 4.
    c) exploring a pre-defined setting again is not as much fun, which conflicts with 2.
    d) most of us enjoy setting-creation, and providing it as gospel conflicts with 1 and 5.

    All interesting points for any game developer who wants to sell their games - and who perhaps wants to build a catalogue of games which all provide customer value - to a reasonably-sized audience to consider.

      \n I can talk a bit about time investment. I don't carefully track my time - when called upon, I much prefer to pay myself by the word, like a writer - but I can make some good guesses.

      I've got a full time job, a family, I do most of the dinner cooking, I go to violin recitals, I like to have internet arguments, I play some games and watch my fair share of movies and TV. I can only spend a few hours a week designing and publishing games. In the final weeks' rush up to release, I might spend 10 hours a week on a game, but usually, nothing like that. 5 hours a week when I'm actively working on a serious project, maybe, and 1 or 2 most of the time.

      Dogs in the Vineyard took me at most 200 hours to create, top to bottom. At the moment I published it, it had paid me back $0 per hour, naturally. A year out, it had paid me back, oh, not super lots, maybe $15 per hour? It's existed for 6 years now, and it's paid me back something insane like $225 per hour. No lie!

      It's not like a job, for sure. You don't get paid while you're working, so if you've got bills, you might want to find something more timely. But also, you keep getting paid, year after year, for the work you did back in 2004. If I'd been able to pick up more work in 2004 instead, maybe I would have, but there's NO WAY it would have turned out as well. Those were the most lucrative 200 hours I've ever spent, ever, doing anything.

      The big problem I see, for me and the question of making a living, is that even if I were suddenly able to spend 40 hours a week creating games, could I? I can see doubling my production, I think, if I could spend 8 hours a week on games instead of 4. Just maybe - creating games is awfully hard. But ten-timesing it? No way on this beautiful green earth.
