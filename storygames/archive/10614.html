\n I love Call of Cthulhu, let's start there. Grew up with it, have fond memories, love what it does. And I just got to musing today about what other system you might play some Delta Green adventures in. I have one or two ideas but I'd like to hear yours. Might be a one- or two-shot or perhaps something longer. Whatever.

If you were to choose something other than CoC to run Delta Green, what would it be? And perhaps more importantly, why?

Even better: if you have run DG in something other than CoC, what was it and what was your experience?

EDIT: Curse me for starting a discussion that has been addressed in almost the same way in another thread. Well, if you have opinions to share anyway, let's have 'em. /shrug

    \n Oh, that's Paul Tevis there. Hi, Paul!

    Which reminds me: Paul wrote a game you might (seriously!) want to try. A Penny For My Thoughts is perfect for stories where, as a kind of fate play, the protagonists will lose their minds. It is amazing in every possible way, no matter what you do with it. No one in your group needs to have played before; the rules literally teach themselves while you play, so you can go from zero to system mastery in one scene. One person should have read the book and prepared the play materials. The included Lovecraftian "The Shadow Out of Memory" questionnaire and Facts & Reassurances document can be trivially hacked to support Delta Green play (add just a pinch of "The ??? Identity", also included). My group played in a generically modern setting, and it kicked ass.

    A one-point tweak to the F&R to make the characters federal agents involved in an illegal conspiracy would have made it DG. Going further, you could make it so the characters were all found together, and the treatment is coming from DGers rather than a hospital, maybe using alien tech... season to taste, and take advantage of your group's shared understanding of the DG-verse. And then, for questions, something like:

    2 pennies: How did you first come to be involved in Delta Green? (How did your first encounter with the Mythos change you?)
    3 pennies: How did you learn about the threat that cost you your memory? (Did you discover a way to deal with it?)
    4 pennies: What were the events that led to your losing your memory? (How did they affect the way you see yourself?)

    If you chose to have everyone found together, the second two questions should tie everyone's stories together. Each player builds on, or adds detail to, the previous players' answers. So the first question gives each player a backstory, possibly all to him/herself. The second question endows each player with a share in creating the common threat, and the third paces the climax, possibly by having players lose their memories one at a time, in sort of a "Red-Rimmed Eyes" fashion.
