\n There is a small hobby shop in Montreal that offer a "thursday RPG evening". A friend of mine have run one-shots of Mouse Guard and Burning Wheel there and the shop owner was super interested and ordered a few Burning Wheel books and is waiting for the MG box set.

I would be running Lady Blackbird, but I thought it could be nice to run a indy game that could also be ordered for the store.

Here are some info maybe to consider:
- I will play with strangers.
- We will play one session by two week.
- Some players will return regularly, some other not.
- It would be great to be able to include new players each session if needed.

    \n Fiasco is a great game to have on hand. Takes only 15 mins to explain and setup, 2-3 hrs tops to run, and supports 3, 4 or 5 person tables.

    Plus there are at least 20+ free playsets out there to use. For most tables I've played with, it really sells itself as the "Coen Brothers movie game". Which the store owner will like:)

    It's certainly not as complex as games like Burning Wheel or even Mouse Guard. But if they get a lot of new players and especially non-RPGers, it's very approachable.

    \n Episodic? Misspent Youth.
    For a store run game, you might want to do some world creation ahead of time, in case they aren't motivated to build it themselves.
    So long as you can do a full 'episode' each week, should be easy enough to add and drop people.

    And for pageantry and "hey, what are they doing over there" you can't beat a good game of Ganakagok. That can draw a crowd piece of cake if you know what you're doing. Still, maybe only once a month on that one. It has an end game you are working towards.

    Yes to Fiasco and Zombie Cinema. They sell easily and can demo quickly.

    Remember Tomorrow is excellent for adding and dropping characters, let alone players, each week. It could even enhance the game to add that complexity. Good suggestion.

    \n Second on Do, which envisions characters coming back until their pillgrimage ends, but is tolerant of shuffling precisely which pilgrims descend on any given world. Play sessions are quite short, so you can take a break and play a second Letter, or play another short game, in the same game night - a bonus in my book. And if you're flexible about how continuous continuity is, Happy Birthday, Robot! is an amazing game that gets people out of their shells and expressing their enthusiasm quickly.

    I haven't played Cthulhu Dark yet, but (1) a book (Stealing Cthulhu), and a damn good one too, is coming soon that includes it; (2) instant system mastery, thanks to Graham's elegant design; (3) everyone loves Cthulhu, right? Character continuity is possible but hardly required.

    Going back to your OP, Mouse Guard seems like a perfect match, but played as a loose campaign rather than a one-shot. New mission each session. If a player misses a Winter session, you can try to make that up if you think it would be fun, but otherwise, you can just be like "hey, it's Spring, and you've got a new mission, and Robert is back, and... do you want to do a quick Trait switch around your absence?" Again, cast continuity is fun but not required.
