    \n I've been thinking of this same thing, actually, and specifically in the context of D&D. I've come to conclude that dice notation is useful and should not be discarded trivially, especially if you find yourself writing "take highest", "take lowest" and other such things all the time. Most specifically, I've come to conclude that if you're comfortable with "2d6", then you should belong in a demography that will be able to handle a bit more esoteric specialty notation without your head exploding. I sympathize with those who think that simple is always good, but that's just not going to be the case with all audiences and all purposes of writing.

    Another thing I've decided is that "2d6" does not mean "roll two dice", it means "produce the sum of two rolls of d6". That is, the "d" notation entails summing up the dice. There are publications that do not think this way, but the overwhelming majority of "d" notated game texts assume that "2d6" means the sum of two dice, not a set of two separate numbers. This is a foundational observation in that if you disagree and think that "2d6" is simply implied by most contexts to mean a summed value, then you'd presumably want to use something akin to what Cam and others suggest above: "2d6" with some follow-up sign for what you're going to do after you've rolled those two dice. "2d6+" would be a hypercorrect example for "roll two dice and then sum the results together".

    Based on the above opinions my current thinking is that "pick highest" is simply a different, alternate function for processing dice instead of summing them. In an "universal" context of writing where you might be doing traditional notations like "2d4+3" alongside pool-based math it makes the most sense to treat the basic function form "XdY" as a dice pool description, where the "d" is the function that is applied to the X dice of type Y. Thus, replacing the "d" with some other symbol should logically give us a dice pool where we do something else than summing with the dice.

    (One might remark that the "XdY" notation is flawed in not being able to describe pools of multiple dice types; traditionally these are handled by using a separate sum operator, like "2d6+3d4", but that is not generizable to other functions aside from ordinary mathematical operations. If I had the need, I'd probably write a more complex pool as e.g. "d[6, 6, 4, 4, 4]" or "+[2d6, 3d4]" or something similar.)

    As for the symbols to use, what I'm currently using for "pick highest" and "pick lowest" are diacritical letters: "2\u1e0b6" (note the dot) means "roll two 6-sided dice, pick highest" and "2\u1e0d6+3" equivalently means "roll two, pick lowest and add three". For "pick the middle value" I might use e.g. "\u0111". The symbology is obviously arbitrary, as there is no established tradition. And I would certainly only use this sort of thing in a dense game text that needs that added expressive power. If you already know that you won't be needing "pick lowest", then there's little reason to worry about that when choosing your notation. Some game texts use "2d" for "roll 2d6 pick highest" simply because in that game you're always picking highest and only using six-sided dice, so a notation any longer would fall into hyper-correctness.

    For more complex cases, I would write "roll 2d6 twice and pick higher result" as "2\u1e0b(2d6)". Logical (as mathematical notation, that is) and easy to read. The different "d" symbols are admittedly potentially difficult to differentiate, so it might not be a bad idea to just drop the "d" glyph (it's just a simple abbreviation anyway) and use entirely different glyphs for the different functions you want to apply to dice. "2d6" could as well be "2p6" for "plus".

    If it feels like I'm being flippantly arbitrary here, it's probably because mathematical notations are entirely arbitrary. High-level math is routinely written with hieroglyphs invented more or less on the spot, and I think that dice notations need to be no different. Only the most ordinary mathematical operations in the most ordinary contexts have regular expressions, just as is the case with rpg dice notations. And just like math, if we're seeing increasing popularity for a new function like "pick highest", then perhaps it is time to start using some special notation for it, too. Presumably the best notation will take root and become common knowledge if we try things out without preconception. Could be that the best notation is "2d6 pick highest", of course, but we won't know without bold experimentation.

    \n I'm of a like mind to Eero. "Roll 3d6 take highest" is certainly clearer than some notation, but "Roll 3 6-sided dice and add them together" is also clearer than "3d6."

    The reason I'm interested in notation is that this is likely to come up in monster stat blocks, which I'd like to keep as compact as possible, but also noticeable. Think of a monster's damage entry saying "3d6 take highest," that feels bulky to me.

    I love the diacriticals, but they're not always easy to see. Probably not a problem with a well-designed font, but a slight weakness.

    The caret (^) is attractive for the prior art and the ease of use. I do find sticking it in the middle a little odd, putting it at the beginning or end allows extensibility (but adds confusion with the common keyboard notation for "to the xth power"). ^3d6 for take 1, 2^3d6 for two, etc.

    The arrows are nice and comprehensible but a little harder to type.

    Thinking along the lines of standard American keyboard keys that don't already have too much overloading in this context: `, #, |. None of those really suggest "highest" to me.

    Right now I'm leaning towards ^, but I'm still questing for a better answer.

    \n Capital "H" and "L" still don't stand out well; it must be something about acculturation and psychology of perception and so forth, but the "k" is much easier for me (as for others) to parse. If you needed to specify, I'd prefer that the "L" or other notation come at the end (and preferable in lower case):

    3d6k2 means roll three six-siders, keep two highest.

    4d6k(3) means roll four six-siders, keep the three lowest. (I like parathenses for the lowest and no parathenses for the highest; it's like accounting, when negative numbers are in parathenses b/c it is too easy to lose a negative sign in a column of numbers. Yay real world synergy!)

      \n A vertical line like a slash or pipe wouldn't be wide enough... Others:

      Not really good, either. I think the space is the clearest choice here. Maybe "2d6_k1" or "2d6.k1"? Both are pretty ugly, but they avoid the ambiguity of the space.

      Actually, the interpunct might be perfect punctuation for this:

      2d6\xb7k1

      You could even string more steps together with the interpunct if the system required it:

      3d6\xb7k2\xb7a1 (roll three, keep two, add one to something or other)

      Ultimately the right choice is probably dependent on the specific system and how it uses dice. The only reason we have an universal dice code at all is the overwhelming popularity of D&D.
