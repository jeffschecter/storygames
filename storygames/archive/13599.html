\n I'm tempted to say Apocalypse World or FreeMarket.

And I haven't played Blowback or Smallville enough to say if they're my best 2010 purchase. I have to go with...



The Armitage Files

This wasn't even on my radar till Steve Dempsey mentioned it at Gencon. I like and trust Steve and although I haven't played much Trail of Cthulhu my wife is a huge fan and I've been hearing from people who don't even like Trail that they love their scenarios. So although it's rare for me to buy a game product without reading a review first, I jumped in!

Why do I love it?

Most modules don't fit my style of GMing. I'm a very improvisational GM who likes to build scenarios around my players (rather than run railroady static scenarios where the players feel disconnected and "dropped in"). The Armitage Files works perfectly with how I run games.

First, the hook is killer...

The Armitage Files are ten documents written in the handwriting of Dr. Henry Armitage... except the Dr. claims he didn't write them! The documents appear one by one in mysterious fashion (through the coarse of your campaign) written in an increasingly desperate and deranged tone. Upon inspection, they seem to be from the future, describing events that haven't happened yet... leading to the encroaching apocalypse.

But how do you use it...

The documents themselves are provided as realistic looking handouts and plain text versions to be given to the players.

They're full of clues pointing to people, organisations, places, and arcane tomes that may have knowingly or unknowingly caused the apocalypse. The exact details of what happens is up to the GM. And the way the GM decides what to include in their scenarios is based on which clues the players are most interested in. In effect, the players' actions drive and help create the mystery.

The GM hands the documents (as they appear) to the players. The players decide which clues to investigate. The GM improvises a scenario around those clues. Every clue (person, group, place, or tome) comes with 3 descriptions. 1 innocent, 1 helpful, and 1 sinister. The GM decides which version is appropriate based on the player's actions. And the GM can even reuse clues as each description comes with alternate names. It's a toolkit for building dozens of linked adventures around the coming apocalypse. And when the players want more, you hand them a new document from the future.

The book also includes useful "how to improvise" advice for GMs, example scenarios, and essential document keys that index the clues in each document.

That said, it's not perfect...

The game references many NPCs not described in the book (although they are described in the main Trail of Cthulhu book and will be familiar to hardcore Lovecraft fans). It won't be a problem for most Trail of Cthulhu players but if you're running a game where your characters don't have connections to these NPCs, their importance in the emerging story may not have much emotional impact.

The plain text versions of the handouts have a few formatting irregularities. For example, in the handouts, there are places where text is crossed out, specifically so you can insert the names of your player characters. But in the plain text version, those blanks are not included or indicated. So you have text that should say, "and the killer is ___________ who is long gone." and instead it says "and the killer is who is long gone." With enough of these missing blanks, it's not too difficult to lose track or miss one.

And I wish the illustrated handouts were available as editable documents (the plain text versions are available). The book itself says you may have to rewrite some of the details in these handouts depending on what actions the players take... but then you can't really rewrite anything without making the handouts almost useless and begin relying solely on the plain text versions. Which is disappointing because the handouts add a lot to the experience. You could create editable versions with the included images in a free online app like Google Docs Presentations. The font wouldn't be as nice but you can layer images in front and in back of the text. That said, you could write on top of the existing handouts and explain the inconsistencies as caused by the players' actions changing the future.

Thankfully these potential negatives are a little annoying but few and minor compared to The Armitage File's positives.

I give The Armitage Files a big thumbs up!

What was your best RPG purchase of 2010?

Rock,

    \n S/lay w/Me.

    Every game is sword and sorcery, and every story has to have a Lover and a Monster. When I first heard that, I didn't know how it could have legs. Sounds boring and repetitive, right? It's not. There's a Monster in probably every RPG session we play in, but we stay interested. And the Lover just means the introduction of a romantic interest that you can ignore if you want, but it's there.

    I'm about eight sessions in, and I still get excited to play. I've guided a tribal, giant snake-worshipping warrior to unite the tribes and lead them to conquer the empire of the Iron Men, and Carly just captured a demon and learned the secrets of a city built along the edge of a hole leading to hell and is going to take her lover and companion, Anala (who was her guide in the Necropolis) to confront the sorcerer who was her master.

    The mechanics are simple, and loose enough so that you can do so many things with it, but make sure that your game is full of character growth and characters sympathetic and terrible (and sometimes both).

    It makes me want to buy Ron Edwards flowers.

    Honorable Mentions: Apocalypse World, Mars Colony

    \n I will say Ry's purchase of Fiasco (and my later purchase) was one of the best things to happen. I run a gaming club of about 400 members all clamouring to play more D&D. I kept running two one-shots a month of Fiasco and more and more people came out saying they wanted to try Indie gaming. All had a great time.
    \n
    \nIt was also a game I could turn to when I wasn't in a mental place to truly GM. I remember after G20 (here in Toronto), I came home after a sixteen hour stint in a detention centre, my partner with 24 hours, and my best friend with 26, and had a game in two days. I was shaken, terrified, starving, and more than a little traumatized. I still ran Fiasco and had a fantastic time because it wasn't something I needed to do work for, and it's, well, a great time.
    \n
    \nNow I run a monthly rpg session where four different games are being run--rarely is there D&D, in fact, most of the games being run are now Indie games, and well, the turn out is still awesome and usually has a waiting list.
    \n
    \nI also ran a lot of Penny For My Thoughts this year, so that's my runner up.
