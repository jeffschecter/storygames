\n This a quick "help me out" thread.

I'm going to be running a one-shot of Lady Blackbird for some strangers (five of them!) in a little while. I'm modifying quite a few things to make it into more of an explosive one-shot type of game (more built-in situation, more Keys that might set characters at odds with one another, stuff like that).

But one thing I keep wondering about:

What should I do about refreshment scenes? My feeling is that with a short time slot, five players who are new to everything in the game, and no "continuing" play, they might be either unnecessary or too vague (yet another rule to worry about "doing right", without a long period of play for us as a group to figure out what they're for, as I think would happen in a regular Lady Blackbird game).

My own thoughts so far:

1. Scrap them altogether. Just play out the session with the starting Pools. This might mean more people might take dice instead of XP from their Keys, which means less advancement, but that's not all that terrible. Since it's a one-shot, accumulating Conditions and reducing Pools as we play out might not be a bad thing.

2. Make slightly more specific guidelines for refreshment, and something that will still move play forward quickly in the context of a one-shot. Something like this:

"When you make yourself vulnerable to someone, by entrusting them with something you hold dear (a valuable secret, your life, your heart, your honour), refresh your Pool up to seven dice."

What do you think?

    \n Nope, never came up.

    In my experience running the game (which, of course, is limited), new players will throw a lot of dice in early conflicts. Then, once you grok how the mechanics work (which happens quickly, because the mechanics are elegant and simple), you realize that you just BLEW ALL YOUR DICE ON SOME BULLSHIT. So you start key-grubbing to get some more. Which makes the game more fun for everyone. Hurrah!

    I guess cautious player might end up maxing their pools, and thus not engaging with the reward cycle. But I think that becomes really obvious to the player in that situation. Like "Hey, you just saw how easy it is to get new dice, so do some crazy stuff and spend the ones you've got. Go!"

    Oh, also, having a large enough pile of dice so that every player can have their own pile in front of them is good, as we all probably know. If you can immediately SEE how everyone's pile o' dice is behaving, you'll understand the cycle better, I think.

    \n When I ran Bloody Forks of the Ohio (another of Jason's Blackbird hacks) as a one-shot, it seemed like everyone saved their dice for the big attack on Fort Duquesne at the end. So they were able to demolish any obstacles thrown at them at that point. No refreshment scenes came up, though none would have been needed.

    I also played the version with no pool at all, and that seemed to work just fine. For a one-shot, simplifying the system down makes it easier to manage everything. I'd go with scrapping the pool and refreshment both, if I were to run a Blackbird based game again.

    \n My latest thought:

    Forget refreshment scenes. Keys just generate Pool dice, as suggested above. Instead, a Key Buyoff does two things:

    * Refreshes your Pool (or maybe just adds five dice to it)
    * Gives you an advance (buy a new Key to replace it, or get a new Tag or a new Trait)

    Should work for a one-shot, I think. Then I don't have to explain refreshment OR experience points.
