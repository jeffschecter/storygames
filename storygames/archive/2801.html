\n I saw this and my jaw dropped:\n
Posted By: Alex
I'm just happy that the people who were supposed to be handling Star Wars realized that Star Wars is absolutelybeggingfor, like, the opposite of a "rules-heavy" hardcore min/max system.
\nWhen you take out the stuff from the Star Wars movies that sucks (that is, anything where there's any dialogue beyond one-liners like "I am your father" and "I love you"/"I know" and "I'd rather kiss a Wookiee" and "Fear leads to anger, anger leads to suffering...") it's all about the action... Jedi leaping from platform to platform hanging above an impossibly deep pit, starfighters flying at top speed impossibly close to the trench walls and trying to avoid getting blasted by the force-wielding Empire pilot behind him, a pilot and his Wookiee chasing a whole squad of stormtroopers down the hallway... All of that seems to scream for a system that focuses on action.

The exception, to me, is something like Knights of the Old Republic. There, I can see it... tough moral choices, actions and consequences, destiny, yadda yadda yadda... that works just fine with a more rules-light system like Dogs in the Vineyard, and in fact we played a few sessions, in my group, of Jedi Dogs and had a blast with it.

Am I totally off base here?

    \n I wrote a Star Wars RPG in 1985, before I'd heard of West End Games. It was very Sim. I devoured all the source material I could find, including a paperback "encyclopedia" for the Star Wars universe. Our games in that universe weren't that much about the Empire vs. the Rebellion but more like a bunch of paramilitary people trying to survive in that universe. Eventually, the Empire would piss off the PCs enough that they'd at least empathize with the Rebellion if not outright join. Basically, they were all Han Solo types looking out for themselves. They weren't always heroes but it was good fun.

    Later on, when we started playing the WEG Star Wars game, that trend stuck. The occasional Jedi character didn't really fit in with the ragtag mercenaries that just wanted to make a credit or two. I called it the "Fuck the Rebellion" campaign, not because they were fighting against the Rebels but because they just didn't seem to give a shit.

    \n I was looking over the preview material for Star Wars Saga Edition, and thought that a lot of things there (like skill condensation, and flat Trained/Untrained distinction, rather than skill points) were pointing things in the right direction for simpler, "jump into the action!" type play. That says, I'm largely ignorant of Star Wars D20, and what it may do wrong or right in the promotion of ACTION!

    I think (untested by me, I've never gotten to play the game) that one of the best elements in Star Wars D6 for bringing out the ACTION! would seem to be the Wild Die. For anyone who doesn't know, you would roll one different-colored die with all your rolls, and if it rolled a 6, it would "exolode," letting you roll again to boost your success, but if you rolled a 1, it was either: A) a catastrophic failure or fumble, or a complication. To me, the "Fumble" option is ass, I've never liked those "you toss your sword through the air like an idiot and impale your buddy" type results/interpretations. But the complication. . .man, I would totally play with that option. 'Cause the thing is, you still get to check the roll for success or failure, and in addition, succeed or fail, the GM adds in some mishap to furl the character into greater inconvenience or peril. Total Star Wars. "Hah, I told you I could fly us through the Asteroid field to escape!" "Yeah, but. . .the cave you've hidden in is the belly of a freakin' space worm!"

    Hmm, I wonder if it wouldn't be cool to use the 6 on the Wild Die for the same sort of thing--a plot twist that is helpful to the character. "Ooh, you took a nasty tumble on your Speeder Bike. . .but you met a helpful little Ewok pal!" Then again, while fortuitous turns of events do happen in the series, nobody ever mutters, "I have a good feeling about this."
