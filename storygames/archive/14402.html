    \nI chose "This game focuses on resolving conflicts with ease and style" because it sounds the best (which doesn't mean it is the best). "Ease" and "style" are attractive words that actually tell me something. I found the other options unclear. I may be interested in them but there isn't enough detail to get me excited. EDIT: vote changed to "This game will teach you how to GM or play better".

    I've run similar polls and I've found 2 things (actually dozens of things but 2 seem immediately applicable here): 1. how you say something is more important that what it is and 2. why someone chose something is more important than what they chose.

    That said, here is why I didn't choose the other options...

    This game contains novel mechanics
    First thought was... "cool", followed by, "I don't like novel for novel's sake", finished with, "this seems like a huge promise with a high chance of disappointment."

    This game will teach you how to GM or play better
    Better how? Throwing "GM" or "Player" made this seem less focused. Also, if someone is going to teach me, I need to know more about the teacher and their qualifications.

    This game puts pacing and focus in the hands of the players
    I almost chose this. I've just seen too many games promise this and not deliver. The how is everything here. Also, I'm not sure what "focus" means. Do players have narrative control? Do the players have a way of communicating to the GM what they want the game to be about in real time to make game prep easier? I'm not sure.

    This game propels the players towards dramatic action
    I feel like most games promise this implicitly or explicitly. Does this mean that the mechanics push the players forward somehow? What do we mean by dramatic action? Do players make decisions that are not based on tactics but purely on drama? I've seen games promise this and in reality all they mean is, "we have a combat system that lets you slow down the action and zoom into the dramatic blow by blow action"... like most traditional games.

    \n You shouldn't let whether or not you think the claims can be backed up or not influence what is most interesting to you. Unless of course, you are so skeptical of such a claim being backed up that the claim becomes uninteresting because you think it couldn't possibly be anything other than BS. If the claim gets you to investigate further, even if it is just so you can confirm to yourself that the person making the claim must be talking BS, then its done its job...

    the 'teaches you to be a better gm or player' is purposely provocative. If you think "oh yeah, we'll see about that you arrogant dick! and furiously look for evidence that this game has nothing to teach the likes of you, then great!

    I find the claim of novel mechanics dubious, at this point I've usually seen everything, and at most it's an interesting re-implementation of existing mechanics. I figure teaching how to GM or play better means imposing the designers ideas on me. The focus on resolving conflicts with ease and style to me reads as a fancy way of saying 'rules lite'. This game propels the players towards dramatic action sounds like a buzz phrase, I really wouldn't know what to expect.

    I chose This game puts pacing and focus in the hands of the players because it describes the game in a way which can be objectively verified. It's also something that can be done multiple ways, so I'd be curious how it's implemented.

    \n For me, it's kind of a tie between Novel Mechanics, Teach how to GM/Play Better, or Propelling Towards Dramatic Action.

    However, in the end I figure that these days I actually truly aim towards Novel Mechanics. Theoretically, I like all those three equally, but given all three I'd aim for the one with Something New.

    Finally, a lot of the games that taught me how to GM/Play better did so without actually thinking they were doing so. The two biggest GMing lessons I learned that improved my gaming in the last five years were:
    * Let the players play all NPCs, including and especially MAJOR NPCs. Other games have suggested the former, but only With Great Power suggested the latter (first).
    * The Mountain Witch Trick ("fishing"), from Mountain Witch.

    Both of these games didn't aim to Teach people to be Better GMs, they simply had "a rule that helps you play THIS game better." But taken outside of the game, it became a valuable tool applied to other games, and taught me to be a better GM.

    So I think the current tally is:

    [8]

    This game focuses on resolving conflicts with ease and style
    [2]

    This game puts pacing and focus in the hands of the players
    [2]

      The significant majority of rpg.netters want conflicts resolved with ease and style, which is equal last for story gamers.

      Curiously rpg.netters are into novel mechanics, but dont prioritize the idea of 'being taught how to gm or play better' Whilst story gamers are the reverse.

      I think this is interesting and you can draw your own conclusions, but the reason for doing it is basically marketing. In 'making it stick', which is a book recommended to me about getting your message across and having people remember it, you have to cut everything out of your message except for the one core thing. This is much harder than it sounds. Its like only keeping your favorite child. So if you are only going to have one core concept in your message, it better be the right one! And that depends on your audience.

      Obviously I will be going with some variation on 'resolving conflicts with ease and style'.

      \n Survey design seems really easy.

      It's very, very hard.

      Having the choices be mutually exclusive is generally a bad idea unless they either actually are or you've taken the steps to make the options exhaustive. Interesting doesn't usually mean anything beyond some level of emphasis, or sometimes literally as interested in. Tying the question to a specific and personal belief, experience, behavior or action generally yields better data when doing these of things. But with the questions you've posted you might have written it like this (five minute question design go!):

      Rate the following statements from one to ten, with one being negative and ten being positive. [You could use a Likert-type scale too.]

      This game contains no or few novel methods, mechanics or otherwise has little innovation in game design

      This game will advise you on new techniques and/or styles of play, as Game Master, Player or both.

      This game has an complex method of play or steep learning curve.

      This game gives the Players greater authority in play than what is common

      This game supports [or encourages] dramatic [or cinematic] play [or narratives]

      Have the respondents anonymously reply via a survey website, like SurveyGizmo or similar, and then post up the distributions and means.

      \n Not to mention, if this is a marketing exercise, you're only really comparing a fraction of the inputs -- because you're not incorporating how effectively and convincingly the message is conveyed. In fact you specifically asked people (jenskot, but others followed suit) to ignore that part and just assume perfect transparency/belief -- and even then you see lots of comments about 'what does that mean specifically' or 'sounds kind of pie in the sky to me'.

      I would guess that a really effectively conveyed message about how a game does any one of those 5 things would trump a generic, unconvincing message about any of the other 4. This is particularly true of vague concepts like 'makes you a better player' and 'creates dramatic situations' -- the 'winner' is going to be all in the delivery.

      In other words, I really wouldn't recommend narrowing your 'what is the one thing' too much at this point -- much better to see which of the things you can really get people excited about -- and really get you excited, which will be strongly related -- once you put in the effort to write them all up, etc.
