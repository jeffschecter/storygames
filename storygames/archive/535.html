    Funny enough, I think I generally know what Jack means without being filled in.

    "Play this game ... at the same time, maintain a consistent character of X, Y and Z description" ... that's two activities. Or, more accurately, it's one activity and one creative constraint.

    Creative constraints can inspire creativity, if you let them. If you say "Hrmmm ... I want this guy to weep like a baby now, but he's a hardened marine. That's gonna require real creativity! Oh, wait! I've got the perfect thing!" then you're totally welcome at my table. You've engaged your brain, and you're using your constraints to do better.

    If you say "Hrm... I want this guy to weep like a baby, but he's a hardened marine, so I guess I can't do that," then you're wimping out on me. You've given your brain one pass at the problem, and then you're using your creative constraints as an excuse not to try any hard than that.

    It is ironic that a constraint that can be (in the right hands) roleplaying's greatest strength is (in the wrong hands) also its greatest weakness.

    Tony, working with your example, I think you've hit on two different levels of role-playing. One is where you get completely into character, thinking of how you want your character to react to a situation, then figuring out how they would *actually* respond to a situation. The other is just playing the surface. For example:

    you want your hardened marine to cry like a baby, but he's a hardened marine, so what would he do instead? Punch the bulkhead of his ship, let out a grunt/yell of frustration, then keep going. (I'm assuming he's not crying out of fear, but for some other reason, like his best friend just died horribly and he found the body.)

    you want your hardened marine to cry like a baby, but he's a hardened marine, so he doesn't.

    I think this difference is part of what Jack is talking about. I don't know his experiences, but the former is a great deal more difficult to do as a player than the latter. It also changes the point of the game. If the goal of your game is to run around, killing monsters, solving puzzles, saving towns, then the deep emotional basis of your character detracts from the main purpose of the game and makes you lose focus of the ultimate goal. This is not true for all people, but for some. Deep characterisation also takes away from group play, as it focuses everything on only one character for as much time as it takes to get it sorted out, if it's role-played. It can also slow down game-play because you have to think about all these character flaws and histories before you can make a decision as to how your character would react. Take my example above, it takes a lot less time to think of the second than the first.

    All that being said, I'm a fan of deep characterisation and find the best games i've played are those where most or all players go at least to the level of my first example. Some games I've played go way way beyond that, and that's an extreme that I find off-putting simply because it's not all about that character.

    So while I support it, I can see why some people would not like it.

    --Nancy

    I think Tony is spot on. Here's why:

    When you get to a particular decision point about your character, you decide how they act. You make up something about the character in that very moment. The character has never been in this exact same situation before. The character could act in accordance with other decisions they've made before, or in a different way. Either way, however, you're making up something new about the character at that moment. It's either "He's also a hardened marine in this case" or "This is an exception."

    I could also say it as "I hereby decide that there is nothing in his background/personality that would make him cry" versus "I hereby decide that there is something in his background/personality that would make him cry." Both are authoritarial. Both mean the player makes a choice and determines something new about the character. Neither is more or less "playing the character" than the other.

    Framing the difference as playing the character versus changing the character to fit the player's interest is a false dichotomy that's plagued rpg discussions for a long time.
