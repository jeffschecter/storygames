    \nI keep a 30-second and a 90-second hourglass on hand when running D&D combat for this reason. The only reason to use something as big as a chess clock is if you are having exactly two parties being competitive about how little time they're using, which is a pretty unusual situation for RPGs. Plus, have you ever tried to track down a good source for chess clocks? They aren't cheap, and most game stores don't stock them. Plus, bulky to travel with. Still, hitting it to signify the end of your turn (at... something) would be good fun.

    \n A cheap alternative (with a slightly different effect) would be to use an hourglass timer. We used to use that when we played "Blood Bowl", GW's football game. It was a good game, but it could really drag sometimes, with people agonizing over every decision. In your turn, you'd turn it with your end up. Your opponent would flip it over to their end when their turn started. If you ever ran out of sand, your turn ended immediately. It was cool because you could really put the pressure on your opponent by having a series of really short turns, leaving them with very little time. It added a whole other tactical egde to the game, trying to decide if that extra block was worth the time it would give your opponent.
