\n Okay, so what's up with this?

Game Master (or DM/ST/SG/HG/whatever) has always been a social role to me: it's something that somebody does, usually because a game needs somebody doing that something. Who actually does it is totally up in the air.

I've encountered a lot of folks who treat Game Master as a social identity: it's something that somebody is, and the group recognizes that person according to that label, be it GM or DM or what-have you. Other members of the group will refer to them and say, "Jim's our DM."

Now, part of it can be the conflation between host/organizer and GM, sure, but it seems to go deeper than that.

Have you ever said, to yourself or to others, "I am a Game Master" rather than "I am the Game Master for this game?" Why?
Alternately, have you ever identified someone else in your group (and there's another assumption right there) as the group's GM? Why?
What goes into taking on GM as an identity, or assigning GM as an identity, rather than as a temporary role?

    \n There are lots of prestige and power issues with being a GM. Some of them make it positive to be a GM, others negative.

    For example,many "GMs" get a lot of self-value out of being able to run a good game. (It ties in with how a lot of folks get self-value from being able to design games. You are not a game designer, you are a guy that designs games, right?) Hell, for a lot of games running a good one isn't easy and being able to make it work evenly is something to be proud of. Get used to it, and the pride that comes with it, and its an easy slide to make in order to bring it to the level of "this is what I am, I am this skillful thing."

    Now, that's something, and the GM reinforcing that to keep their role is the obvious outcome. However, it goes deeper than that, as a lot of players reinforce the "you're the GM because you're so excellent at it!" thing because it means they don't have to do it, and get to keep the GM running games for them while they do nothing. They like being "the peons" because while it might suck sometimes, they also don't have to do the work and (if they have the right agendas) can still reap the rewards.

    So both sides have something to get out of it, and something to get trapped into by it.

    Bastard players. Sneaky monkeys, all of them. That's why I am a GM.

    P.S. I also sometimes say "I'm a GM." But when I say it I am saying "I am the guy that usually ends up GMing because everyone else is a big coward baby, oh, and I actually like GMing most of the time, so I don't really mind, because it helps me get the kind of fun I want and I'm good at leadership roles to help others get what they want too." But saying "Yea, I'm usually the GM" or even "Yea, I'm a GM" is easier than rolling all that out.

    \n Um... okay, I feel like I'm about to dive in head first against the current here.

    Being a gamemaster is a huge part of my identity. Not my role within gaming, but how I have constructed my understanding of myself. It says that I am a facilitator of creative energies, that I work hard to create synergy in the creative energies around me, that I have a vision that I want to bring to others (and bring hard), and that I am willing to push hard against my friends because I know antagonism is important to a good story.

    I am not saying these are tools that I employ while GMing. Yes they are, but... I am saying that these are things which GMing helps inform my identity on.

    I've recently considered exploring Vancouver's kink scene, and am a little hesitant... but at the same time I try to validate the curiousity with parts of my identity that already exist. One of which is "you do roleplaying, blah blah blah", but another of which is "you're a GM, so you're connected to antagonism and power dynamics within social situations, blah blah blah".

    I very much allow the fact that I am often a GM inform parts of who I am, and I let other parts of who I am inform how I GM.

    I think that's perfectly healthy, and I like to own the fact that I am (often) the GM. It's part of who I am.

    \nThe depiction of the GM role as a dead-end trap for the poor loser with organizational skills doesn't ring true for me at all. I've belonged to at least two dozen groups since college, playing everything from PTA to Hero. All the long-running groups rotated GMs like tires, and the role of the host and the GM was never conflated. The GM was simply the person who, by consensus, was the GM at the time. The host was whomever had the best rec room or the most accommodating family or who was agoraphobic (not kidding!) or whatever.

    I really had no idea that this was not the usual configuration. No wonder Shreyas is trying to start a revolution.\n
    What goes into taking on GM as an identity, or assigning GM as an identity, rather than as a temporary role?
    \nSudden realization! -- when I think about the one-off groups I've played in, the groups that came together to do a single limited-run game and then disbanded -- those games had more of a "Jeff's Deadlands" or "Matt's Hegemony" vibe to them, where the groups were very strongly identified with their gamemaster. But since those gamemasters were part of the larger cloud of gamers and were not exclusively gamemasters, this identity didn't persist. It was temporary.

    The longer-running, more formal groups, like UGX or SGA -- those had a recognizable group identity and weren't dominated by a single game master, so no one in those groups could reasonably claim the gamemaster mantle all for themselves.

    \n I am a GM, although Mrs. Ork still calls me "the DM" and did so during our 7 year Vampire the Masquerade campaign. Go fig.

    The role of GM is a big part of my identity. It is something I claim with pride. Since RPGs are my pacifier for theater (no time to attend all the rehearsals of plays as a grownup), running dramatic and entertaining games is an important outlet for my creativity. When I have a campaign die, I feel responsible. When the players don't have fun, I try to figure out how I fix it.

    In the past I've heard gamers state, "Everyone at my table GMs" and it makes complete sense to me. It instantly tells me that each member of the group has been "behind the screen"; they know the work involved, the multi-tasking and negotiation needed to make the game sing. It tells me that the table would more likely see cooperation and coordination, less turtling, less orphaned bada$$ PCs, more open-ended backgrounds, all that good stuff you crave from players. That doesn't make it true, but it paints a picture for me.
