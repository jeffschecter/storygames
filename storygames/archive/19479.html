\n Long time lurker, occasional poster, never designer here. I recently caught a dose of the fabled spare time I've heard so much about and I was challenged by my RPG playing group last we met to make and run a rules lite Story gamey approach to Pendragon by this Saturday. My initial brainstorming was done where all good ideas come from (the shower) and I decided to collect my thoughts into the 3 question (+1) structure that John Wick uses as well as a side list of stuff I like to see in games in general.

1. What is your game about?: The game is about the universal themes of Arthurian Literature, The Once and Future King in particular being my model work. The relationship of force & justice, the role of conflict in a society, and the esteem that comes with a title etc. The themes are to be treated in such away that while they are perfect for knights of Dark Ages England they also fit with paladins of medieval fantasy or White Hat Hackers of the cyberpunk near future. Balance of fraternity, duty, and your own moral compass becomes the defining conflict but at the same time including high adventure moments.

2. How Does your game do that?: By focusing on the reasons why we do things and what we do over how or if we can do them. The mechanics are entirely theoretical at this point but the focus will be on emotion desire, personal morals, and the pressure of the group more so than whither or not your sword can crack the armor or line of code crack the system. You don't pierce the armor because you were strong, you did it out of Loyalty to the king that gave the order. You didn't hack the system because you were smart enough, but more because of your Love for the person the Corporate bastards took from you.

3. What behaviors does your game reward?: Compromise among the group as well as sticking to your guns and assuring you avoid compromising your code of ethics even in the face of a situation that paints it in a negative light. The latter is still up for debate in my mind as the archetypical Chaotic Good character still has a moral code it is just not as written while genocidal crusaders would stick to one without question to negative results.

4. Why is that fun? Being on adventures that raise moral questions and are about people more than about their equipment has been the main reason I've enjoyed the story games movement. I want the same epic feel we get from games like Pendragon but without all the mechanical fuss bogging down the parts that made the game shine, I also want it to be transferable to other settings of similar style but with different trappings.

Stuff I'd like but do not need.:
1. Preferably GMless as I like games that require the entire group to forge a story without prep as they always come out as a surprise to everyone.
2. Stats reflective of personality traits, vices, and virtues with skill and gear being modifiers.
3. A system of lineage similar to Pendragon that contains narrative weight.
4.A "knights of the round table" motif consistent throughout whatever comes out of this just for rule of cool.

My only question to you is how would you do this? 0_0 As one who has never designed a game before I feel I've dove a bit to deep for a first outing as you could tell by my initial sketch being a little unfocused on what I really want out of it. I'd like to hear from some people on the subject and if it works out maybe I can make something tangible out of this mess.....even if it is just Pendragon "Pretentious Rules-lite Edition" (now with hipster mechanics!)

    \n Writing a specific Fiasco playset comes to mind, but it seems like you're much more interested in having the personal struggle of your own character mechanized more. Smallville, despite its flaws, majorly focuses on the interpersonal aspect, but requires a GM and is probably crunchier than you want. So there are some clear guide posts you can use instead of just feeling lost.

    When you feel like you're all over the place, refer back to the clearest core as opposed to any peripheral ideas. So that means figuring out the absolute most essential spirit. I think you've articulated your thought process and desires well, so crystallizing something even more basic shouldn't be hard. Yours might differ, but I'll take a swing: a team struggling to do what is Capital-R Right in a world of forces that don't play by any rules.
    Then you mechanize. First, the team. My thoughts go to incentivizing players to assert their wills in the face of dissension within the team. So it's important for the team members to have differing visions. How these become mechanical is important. There could be a relationship mapping exercise that requires players to create positive bonds and troublesome tensions before the game even starts. Or you could make these things happen in play by having a scene begin with one player making a statement and requiring some or all of the other players to find a reason why they disagree.

    Personally, I find the idea of discovering disagreement in play to be pretty compelling, because I've played countless games with predetermined relationships. They work, but tend to encourage players to default to what has been written instead of feeling encouraged to explore new territory. So, for me, all of the other parts of my version of your idea would be built around that in-character discovery of disagreement.
    There are many ways to go from there, but just off the top of my head: hands of cards are a natural mechanic for a group of maneuvering equals. Even though everyone is working together, hands you cannot share can create a feeling of secrecy and opposition that promotes conflict.
    I have hacked Fate Core to be GM-less, in part by making decks of Aspects that are drawn to seed scenes and conflicts. It could be interesting to have players write a small stack of cards representing their characters' fears. And as play proceeds, those cards cycle into play, representing external forces that might subtly or drastically change the argument at hand. And the way that those fears are dealt with by the group might significantly impact the characters to which they are linked.

    This is already sounding cool to me, but it might not be to your taste, so I'll stop there unless you want to toss this back and forth some more.

    I was thinking about Lineage and how to incorporate it easily and usefully. So, character creation. Let's say characters have traits. Let's also, for now, assume three family trees. Each tree has a colour (red, blue, green - think Heraldry). Each branch of the tree reflects a Trait as well as off-spring.     Blue family may start with Wealth. Green may have Political Power. Just notions off the top of my head. We'll have to determine the Traits (again Pendragon) and how many but it's a quick,easy way to determine a Knight's Traits through Lineage. Keep certain Traits out of each Family colour to make unique characters just by following the branches down.

    \n Probably my favorite thing about playing a *World game with Bonds, such as Dungeon World, is filling those Bonds in during play. You could make a list of interesting Bonds and instead of putting them in playbooks, write them on index cards and deal them to players or pass around a stack and let players take ones they want one-at-a-time draft-style. A big part of the game, then, is getting rewards for initiating and resolving those bonds.

    The rewards could be generic, like points/chips that give you power/flexibility, or highly specific to each bond, as written on its card. And if you can manage to tie that into a secondary engine, such as linking bonds and fears or points and responsibility of some kind, you're well over halfway there.

    I have the beginnings of an idea for using Order/Peers/People/Self as categories, each one with an effectiveness Trait and a Motive that's both a source of strength and a concern. Higher Trait values would be good for short-term success in actions, while higher Motive values would be good for long-term success in recovery (from injury, fatigue, etc.) and advancement. GMs would create events based around the Motives, and any character who forsakes one of their concerns will see its rating decrease. One way to cause value conflicts, then, would be to pair the low Traits with the high Motives and the high Traits with the low Motives -- the stuff you value most is the stuff you're worst at, at least initially. So, do you take the better odds of less reward, or pursue long odds for that which matters most? There could be some rules about when the GM can target two Motives simultaneously, forcing a literal choice between them, or that could be an element of scenario design.

    \n I don't want to flood the thread so I'll link some documents tomorrow. This may not be what you're after but there may be some ideas in here to use. It certainly fits some of the criteria you initially posted about, especially portability to other genres and malleability of rules. Anyway, here's a little taste of a Setting for PNP.

    Plug N Play: Pen and Dragon

    We each play a Young Knight in the Age of Chivalry just after Arthur is made King.

    What's It All About?
    Knights strive to bring Glory to their name and their lineage (both once and future - Ha! See what I did there?).
    No Knight wishes to be remembered for their misdeeds. Therefore the accomplishment of great deeds by way of Virtue is the path to Glory. The ultimate test of Knight-hood will be the Grail Quest where only a Knight of complete Virtue will succeed. This is some years off, however and there is plenty of time to adventure and falter and redeem yourself. Even Lancelot gave in to Lust, and Wrath was ever his weakness...

    Cynric Names: Aeron, Aeddon, Blaen, Bradwen, etc.
    Saxon Names: Aelfric, Bassa, Cerdic, etc
    Malorian Names: Alein, Bandelaine, Berel, Briant, etc

    Role: Your Role is defined by your lineage. Your family (past and present) encompasses titles, patents and Heraldry. It is your first identity before making a name for yourself.
    For game purposes, lineage determines what Traits you may start with and one Trait that is lacking. This missing Trait assumes that the Vice is common, the Virtue is lacking. The descendents of Oswulf, for example, lack Humility; therefor Pride is common amongst them.

    Lineages:

    Aeron of Cymro........Colour: Red.......Heraldry:Cambenet
    Proud and boastful warriors. Less courteous than some, but loyal and brave to a fault.

    Oswulf the Saxon......Colour: Green ...Heraldry: Pallets
    Land rich but gold poor, the Oswulf line has many holdings and kin is never far away if you need aid or shelter.

    Pedivere du Mont......Colour: Gold......Heraldry: A Fess
    With many ties to the Court, this line is well known for its wealth, power and political intrigues.

    Traits: All knightly Traits are summed up by the Seven Virtues and corresponding Seven Vices.

    Chastity / Lust
    Temperance/ Gluttony
    Charity / Greed
    Diligence / Sloth
    Patience / Wrath
    Kindness / Envy
    Humility / Pride

    The goal of a Knight should be to eventually gain all Seven Virtues. Young knights begin with only
    four. As noted, each line has one Virtue missing by default and thus can never start with it. The six others are available to choose from.

    Optional Bond Rule: Your word is your Bond. Your Bond is represented by a token. You may ask another Actor to Support you in a Dilemma. If they accept, they take your Bond and hold on to it. They can there-after compel you to Support them in any Dilemma they face (whether they are the Lead or just Supporting) by returning your Bond to you. You can't refuse. There may be some fictional reason that your character can't be Compelled to a scene (too far away, for example) but if any way can be reasonably imagined that your character could make it, then that is what happens.

    \n@CynicalSleuth
    I started writing PnP's basic rules last Thursday but got distracted. Your post got me thinking about it again because it is easily multi-genre. So I started on Pen and Dragon. The original Settings I had in mind were Dungeon Crawl, Drawing Room Mystery and Survival Horror (especially Zombie Apocalypse). I don't want to tout PnP here but I think it hits on many of the things you are going for and I wanted to say why so that you can consider them in your own design.

    First off, I have the Pendragon box-set beside me as I type this. Wonderful source book, lousy game. It is Arthurian Legend on top of a variation of Chaosium's BRP. Arthurian Legend isn't about how strong you are, or how fast, or how many hit points you have. It's about larger than life characters with recognizable flaws. It is the Greek legends of Gods brought down to human scale. It is, to my mind, best summed up by Arthur, in his Wrath, striking Lancelot with Excalibur. The Irresistible Force striking the Immovable Object. Arthur "breaks that which cannot be broken" striking down the paragon knight, who suffers a "wound that would not heal". The infinities cancel out. Try porting that to a game of numbers.

    It's a game of myth where how you do things is secondary to why. So, in PnP, "how" is the narration; anything that fits is good to go. Why is based on dice rolls, available Descriptors, the viability of lending Support, who holds your Bond, what is there to gain and what is there to lose; and, most importantly, what would your character do.
    Consider the Support mechanism. What if the Dilemma roll is 10, or 11 or even 12! Who will willingly join you in this scene? They can help a little but they have as much to lose as you do. Where is their courage and loyalty now? Some knights, I bet, will say: "No, Sir Loin is answering a 'call of nature' against yonder tree when that happens." They aren't always brave, and right and true, That's kinda the point.
    Consider the Optional Bond Rule. I hold up my token and say to you: "This is my Bond. It is my Oath, my Word. I give it to you, in promise of future aid, if you help me this day." Is it you, the player, saying this or your character? It is, in fact, both. Same words, same effect in game and in fiction. Powerful stuff. Find a way to include this in your game.

    I'd like to hear where your design thoughts are. Also, I request that you stop Quoting entire posts in your replies. It wreaks havoc on the mouse scroller : )
