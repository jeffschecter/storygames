    \n Hi! Thanks for asking. We've got a few AP accounts coming forth in the near future. I'll link to those here when they're ready.

    In short, though, Dark gets some of its tension from a combination of narrative presentation and limited-information systems, plus an element of systemic reinforcement. Getting caught is a big deal because combat is pretty dangerous and Dark characters are pretty squishy. Without a save system, there's often a matter of pride, too, that comes from the fact that elegance and suavity are prized above carnage. Screw up and you've got to live with it.

    Failure isn't quite as common in Dark as it is in some games, but it happens and it's costly. Because players select the cards to play, rather than succumbing to the luck of the dice, the choice in the moment captures some of the agony of the character-creation process, too. Whereas I can safely max out my Stealth skill in other games and then blame the dice when I fail, Dark emphasizes the player choices during play and gives most everything an in-game (i.e. fictional, narrative) context, so parsing what and how things happen is a little more streamlined.

    Players can guess at the difficulty of actions, but there are certain parts of the environment that are unknowable to the players at first \u2014 What will that guard do if she sees me? What's behind this door? \u2014\xa0and that generates tension, too.

    Because some of it is also a feature of adventure and level design, I'll be offering up adventures to teach some of what I've learned from running this game and to give GMs lots of concrete stuff to use or reuse.

    I'll be explaining more of this in updates to the Kickstarter campaign, too.
