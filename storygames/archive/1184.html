    I have to weigh in with Annie and Jason on this one. Having a stronger female presence at GenCon would be awesome, but I think putting female game designers and players on a pedestal makes them something extra special and rare instead of something normal. One of the most powerful tools in feminist activism, in my opinion, is inviting a bunch of cool women to be a part of a traditionally male activity (like roleplaying or GenCon) and then not making a big deal about them being women. Because why the hell shouldn't they be a part of it? This is what I did in finding people from underrepresented groups to work on Push (in regards to women, people of color, and folks from abroad) and it enables you to seduce people into giving up their prejudices, because you aren't necessarily in their faces with all this progressive politiking (which turns many people off and prevents them from listening to your message). You're just presenting stuff that rocks, which happens to be by a different set of voices than they normally get to hear from.

    So I'd suggest something more along the lines of organizing events that are about subjects other than "women in gaming" and having women be in charge of running them or be major participants in the seminars or whatever. Because otherwise it sounds like women are only able to speak about being women, while men can speak about all sorts of different things. Sure, they can bring up stuff about being a woman in a largely male hobby, but I think it'd be cool to give women a forum where they can speak authoritatively on roleplaying in general. Just my two cents.

    I think that there are good reasons for the ambivalency in this thread.

    On the one hand, we all want more women in gaming. On the other hand, nobody wants to see it accomplished in a way that heightens the perception of there being a problem playing in mixed gender groups.

    This reminds me of Curves (http://www.curvesinformation.com/), the "women-only" gym. As I understand it, there are two reasons for the existence of Curves. First, that women have unique fitness needs, and so having a place that specializes in them is a good idea. But there's a second reason, which is that many women don't like to be oogled in gyms, which is a common phenomenon. I think that the fact that it's done in part defensively is sad.

    That is, if the only reason that the space exists is to make women more comfortable, then the problem is that we're not making them more comfortable in the regular games we play. In this case, then Jason would be right, we'd be solving the problem incorrectly. I'm not going to make a claim one way or the other about the games we run at GenCon. Instead I'll ask the female gamers here - are they comfortable to play in? Yeah, go ahead and comment on the difference between games run by the Forge crowd and those run by the general public if there's a difference.

    Because if, in fact, Meg and others like her are comfortable playing with the men, then her support of special female games has to be based on something else. You and I, Jason, may not be able to determine what that is, so it might make it look like the subtextual reason for the existence of Curves. But if your wife, Meg, and other women see some value in it that's not simply male avoidance, then it's got to be a good thing.

    Alex's point is good as a general thing, but it's got his own biases within it. That is, I'd hate to see the women do women-only gaming, too, but that's because I like playing with women (heck, I go out of my way to get female players). I'd think that most women would want to play with men, too, but if they didn't for some reason, well, that's their own damn right to play with whomever they wish (I tend to reserve the right for men to play intentionally witn men-only, too, as long as it's a well-examined idea, and not simply hidden misogyny). I don't think you can say objectively that mixed gender is better than not, I think it's just the most common preference.

    Given that it seems to be the most common preference, even for women, I find it odd that women would want single-gender games. But, again, I'm not going to even ask why, but just say that as long as it's not hiding from the men, that we should put it together. Nothing about it would force players like Lisa to participate anyhow.

    I have to say that I'm skeptical that it would have a high impact - a seminar or two and a few sessions of play is a drop in the bucket at GenCon, as is just about any other event that a single group can run. If you really wanted high impact, you'd want to get WotC aboard with this at the very least. If not figure out some way to create a network event that was bought into by a lot of companies.

    On the other hand, every little bit counts. If even one more female gamer attends than would have, it's a step in the right direction, IMO.

    Meg, others, I'd be interested to hear what you would say in such a seminar. Could you give a bullet point overview of what such a seminar might contain? That might shed some light.

    I think that the "Gamer Widow" tracks exist for women who are, quite simply, not in the Gamer tribe. That is, I think that they don't feel excluded because they're women, they feel excluded because they don't know who Cthulhu is. So when somebody shoves a plushy Cthulhu under their noses, they just don't see what a wonderful thing that is. It's just the world's ugliest plushy to them. They can't recite Monty Python. They don't even know what "Dee-twenty" is. So that track exists so that they can socialize with normies, or just to have something to do while their gamer husbands mix with the tribe. Doesn't really matter what it is that they do. I understand at one Origins version, they tautght knitting, and it was a big success.

    Now, I think that most such folks might be a bit more comfortable at Forge events, because we tend to push the "gamer vibe" a little less (I've been known not to show up on Game-dar, when shaven). But I'm not sure it's quite enough to get them out of the protective creche of their track, and out into the general gamer population. We could certainly try, however.
