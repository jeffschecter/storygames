    \n I don't think Dread would work with anything other than Horror. The whole point is that eventually the tower falls over and somebody dies. That's pretty dark. But if you change it to be "eventually the tower falls over and... something kinda bad happens" then frankly you might as well just be rolling dice because you've sucked most of the tension out of it.

    I too generally don't pursue immersion -- I generally roll my eyes a little bit, quietly, every time someone says something along the lines of "I'm only interested in playing games that let me immerse over the course of a 200 session campaign!" I also have a hard time recommending games as "immersion friendly" because I find the logic of what disrupts a given person's immersion to generally be very personal and unlikely to match the logic of what disrupts a different person's immersion.

    For example, which of these things do you find immersion breaking:
    Having to make up some details of a thing your character knows about
    Having to make up some details of a thing in the world that your character doesn't know about
    Having to keep in mind character "personality traits" ala Burning Wheel's Beliefs
    Having to reward other players for good roleplaying by passing them a token
    Having to reward other players for playing their "personality traits" by passing them a token
    Having to roll dice for social interactions
    Having to frame a scene
    Having to switch to control of another character in a scene in which your "main" character does not appear
    Paying a "Fate point" to add a "detail" to a scene
    Accepting a "Fate point" for a "compel" that requires your character to act in a certain way
    Spending any kind of "out of game" currency for an ingame effect


    I also have a hard time reconciling my personal definition of "story games" with a request for immersive play, because in general, to me, "story games" imply that the players occasionally step into Author Stance and create something, or...well... tell some story. So games like Apocalypse World to be skirt the very very edge of "story game" and are mostly really just traditional RPGs with some different design choices. I get annoyed everytime someone calls Fate a "story game"

    I hope this doesn't sound too much like I am bashing immersion, and it's DEFINITELY not my intent to bash "traditional" RPGs - most of my gaming and many of my favorite games are fairly traditional. It's just that pretty much every single one of them falls prey to SOME of the "potentially immersion breaking" points, above.

    I guess I could recommend The One Ring, but that's....really not a storygame by any definition I can think of.

    \n Oh good; I was worried that was going to come across as too much of a "Get yer damn immersion off my lawn!" post. I too very much enjoy both types of games, but I feel like I must not be immersing the same way people who are really All About The Immersion(tm) are, and I honestly have trouble understanding how they can reach that level of immersion while sitting around a table rolling dice - which is pretty much all it takes to remind ME that I am a person sitting around a table rolling dice.

    All that nonsense about my personal hangups out of the way, I would recommend:

    Blades in the Dark - this game has some bits where I'm sure NO ONE immerses, because you are definitely making decisions for a game perspective, but I think it definitely has potential for immersion in the parts where you really ARE playing your character, down in the gloom of Duskwall. This is probably no more "storygame" than your Dungeon World sort of stuff, but it's really GOOD. And it supports potentially very long campaigns.

    Polaris (The Ben Lehman one, not the translated from the French Sci fi one) is, on the other hand, very MUCH a storygame. But the way the roles are divided up allows for the "Heart" player for each character to do some pretty serious immersing while the other three players sortof co-GM for them. The Heart does frame scenes though. Also, while this one supports longer term play in the sense that it's probably going to take you several sessions to get through it, it's not really suitable for "campaign" play in my opinion. Though you might be able to make that happen, it would involve transitioning through different characters.

    Masks - I haven't played this yet, but it's the first superhero RPG to interest me, basically, ever. It's all about being a teenaged superhero with some sort of issues. The game is less about what your powers are and more about how you VIEW yourself. Very interesting design, as suitable for long term play as any PbtA game.

    World of Dew - I kinda un-recommended this on in the PvP thread, but even though I felt it was heavier than it needed to be, I think it has the potential to create a very interesting game. And it all but demands long term play to engage with the character advancement/city development aspects of the game. It's ALMOST like Fate with a bunch of other stuff added on.

    Burning Wheel or Mouse Guard - I lump these together because they're very similar systems. They're not super storygamey, but the focus on the characters and their Beliefs is what drives play, and they support long term character development and play very nicely.

    Also, see the above recommendation for The One Ring, which basically does Tolkien amazingly.

    \n I think it's basically impossible to get people on the same page regarding what "immersion" means. Fortunately, it's still possible to have some kind of conversations about it by focusing on your subjective experience. I might mean something totally different from you when I saw "immersion", but it's still meaningful for you to say that *this experience* feels immersive, but *that one* does not - to you.

    If I were to speculate a little, I have my own mental model of immersion. I think that immersion as a state of willing suspension of disbelief. When you read that fantasy novel as a young child, and it seemed completely real to you, you made the choice to create it that way in your mind - to believe the words on the page and imbue them with the power of your imagination.

    I believe that immersion for most people requires two things: familiarity and trust.

    First, you have to find the process of play familiar enough that it can fade to the back of your mind. This could include the setting for some people ("I feel immersed when I'm playing in my friend's basement, but not at the Cafe."), noise level, presentation, the game system being used, and so forth.

    It's not about complexity; some people have very "immersive" games in really rules-heavy systems once they are familiar with them. D&D has a lot of counter-immersive elements (e.g. only being able to use an ability once per day, hit points, classes, etc.), but people who are used to the game and have played for years can internalize all those things and find D&D highly immersive (while a game with an unfamiliar mechanic, like Keys or Plot Points or Dice Pools or some other thing will totally throw them).

    You see this in other media, too. People who have spent their whole lives watching movies and getting engrossed in them find it hard to fully relate to the characters in a film which is shot in 3-D, digitally, or in some unfamiliar style of storytelling. The novelty is distracting.

    The trust issue is similar: for you to enter a state of willing suspension of disbelief, you have to trust that the process will deliver. You must, similarly, trust the people you are playing with, and their ability to engage with you. When that is in place, you can let down your guard - so to speak - and allow your mind to pretend that what you are experiencing is real.

    So, trust is that other factor. Do you trust the people involved? Do you trust the process? Do you trust the flow of play to create something you find engaging? Do you trust the mechanics to deliver consistently? Then you can be ready to engage.

    Sometimes you can jump in head-first, and simply *choose* to place your trust in something unfamiliar. Generally, though, that's difficult for people.

    For instance, a trusted and beloved GM could run some weird game with mechanics I don't like, and I can get over it and dig deep into the game. On the other hand, playing a favourite game with all the design features I like might now work for me if I don't know what the people I'm playing with are doing with it, or which way they're going to turn.

    All this makes it hard to answer a general query like "which story games help with immersion?" Everyone will have different needs, depending on who they're playing with and what they are familiar with.

    What assumptions do you, in particular, bring to this particular table? We all have a bunch of these (and they could be different, even changing day-to-day!).

    I find a lot of "story gamey" mechanics, in particular, conducive to my immersion, even when, in theory, they force me to make decisions outside of my character's head - because, much like the D&D veteran can rationalize away using his abilities 1/day or refusing to pick up a sword to defend himself because he's a Wizard, I can reinterpret them effortlessly as choices my character is making. I find negotiating unclear distribution of narrative authority (e.g. Fiasco) very difficult for immersion, on the other hand.

    I accept that these are personal quirks of mine, and may change over time, as well as depending on who I'm playing with.

    \n I'm DMing a 5e campaign and using a few story game tricks I learned from here and other games. Namely, I always ask players for input from their character's point of view, either mountain-witch way or brainstorming stuff. We brainstormed the setting using some specific questions and I asked questions for each player to respond as a representative of their race or class.

    Then there's all the info they pour down on their character sheets: every high or low stat, their choice of weapons and defenses, equipment, etc. There's a lot of story there if you try to read it and imagine where does everything comes from. As a DM, I didn't even had to ask the players for this, I just took note of all I can to use it as flags.

    Next there's the actual character background they have come up with. Flags are more obvious here.

    After taking note of all this, I started tracing the campaign idea connecting the flags and adding whatever felt logic or cool to have. It's only here that I bring my own tastes as a GM into the equation.

    And later in-game, we started using taverns as quest generators. I just asked the players what rumours did they heard over the conversations of the customers and gave them a couple of random words if they got stuck, so they could spin out their own quest ideas. It's fine if they want specific things too, like looking for magic weapons, you just turn that into a quest and you're set.

    Next thing I'd like to try is to prompt players to connect some dots in character now that several things have happened. Like, why there were sharks on a lake and a bunch of insane elfs on the island of that lake? Where do the groups of low level demons come from and why were they trying to open a portal near this lake? I'd like to have them make a few statements and maybe roll INT to come up with a way to find proof of those statements, maybe just get their ideas to happen (cause often they come up with better, more interesting evil ideas than I do)

    Actually, we all have probably done this last thing more than once; hear the players jokes or worst fears after we have framed some strange detail on a scene and found their idea so awesome we just have to make it happen. Most of the time they will notice you just changed plans right there and sarcastically thank the player who gave the idea, but that is okey, it has never stopped the game nor ruined the fun when it happened. You can even bullshit your way out of it putting a poker face and asking the player if she's been reading your notes while you were away. It will probably still not convince anyone you were stealing ideas from the players anyway, but nobody said you couldn't do it.

    So, my point is that you can actually take any trad RPG you feel comfortable with and use a couple of tricks like these. It won't turn it into a story game, but it will allow heavy use of player input without breaking your suspension of disbelief too much nor too often.
