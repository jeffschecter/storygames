Leveling should ONLY make you better at combat.

\n
\n
\n
\n\nValyrianSteelKatana \n\n\n
\n
\n\nJune 2017 \n in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n Forgive the clickbait title. More accurately: "There are some definite benefits to leveling only impacting your character's combat prowess."

The D&D paradigm is one equally frustrating and liberating. In every edition, characters are almost exclusively defined by their combat prowess. Hit dice, THAC0/attack bonus, saving throws, weapon and armor restrictions. Swathes of material was written and directed at the combat minigame: magical weapons and armor, spells, wondrous items, etc. Modern D&D desperately seeks to break free from the wargame roots, the system is hamstrung by its emphasis on combat rules. The designers want D&D to be a high fantasy storytelling engine, but character builds have a stranglehold over gameplay.

In "modern" editions of D&D--and I use "modern" liberally--is the introduction of the skill system. Non-weapon proficiencies were changed from a side a l\xe1 carte to main course in 3.0, and they're gradually taking up more room on the plate: skill tricks, skill challenges, background skills, tool proficiencies. This torment offers not a moment of respite!

Most of all, it seems patently silly to me. Why does getting better at killing monsters--because modern D&D is about setpiece fights--make a character better at public speaking? There are certainly justifications one can offer, but let us not dwell on those. Instead, I wish to discuss the merits of leveling up (lots of a priori assumptions here) as a method of increasing combat abilities only.

Traditional methods of leveling make a character more resilient in combat, mitigating risk when engaging physically. A level one character simply cannot fight a balor. He will be vaporized. By that same token, in a level-dependent skill system, a level one character cannot barter, negotiate, or persuade a balor with any real level of success. Taken to its extremes, a level one character must be incompetent in anything involving the task resolution system: crafting items, socializing, investigating, etc., so that the skills have "room to grow."

This likewise means that the people who are best at their craft are likewise only high-level adventurers, which is made all the more ridiculous. The local sage who possess a vast knowledge of the planar cosmology? The blacksmith whose weapons are renowned for their beauty? The manipulative vizier who whispers lies in the king's ear? All of them have 100 hit points and can kick your ass until you grind through the requisite number of quests to reach a high enough level to stand against them. (More assumptions! Bear with me.)

This is great if you're looking for the standard D&D high fantasy romp, but it strikes me as overwhelmingly limited for characterization.

Instead, allowing characters to segregate fighting prowess from virtually every other aspect of characterization offers an opportunity to player characters who have an established history and background - and moreover, breaks free from the need to "level up" to have a character who can contribute to the game.

    \nD&D 3 (and its descendants) is actually surprisingly good, when it comes to this.

    Your typical blacksmith has a +5 bonus on their skill checks. +4 from skill ranks, +1 from ability score. If they are not talented, then only +4, since they only get +0 from ability modifier. Crafting a high-quality item is DC 15, so they can do that routinely (by taking 10). Crafting a masterwork item is DC 20 and requires several checks, so they might succeed, but it is not likely.

    Your excellent first level smith has +4 from skill ranks, +4 from ability score and +3 from skill focus feat, for a total of +11. They can routinely create masterwork items.

    (If they are a dwarf, they get an additional +2, and masterwork tools give an additional +2, for a total of +15. They always succeed at creating high-quality items, even under pressure where they can't take ten.)

    The bonuses are similar for other skills, except humans may get +2 due to another feat and non-humans may get +1 or -1 depending on their ability score modifiers.

    For Pathfinder, add +1 to +4 to the highest numbers you can reach; the characters are slightly more competent there.


    One has to make a number of assumptions, often violated by written adventures, for the world to remain sensible. First, pretty much everyone is first level. Second, even the most competent real life people can typically be modelled by characters of less than fifth level. My interpretation is that at around fifth level the characters become pulp heroes and at some very high level, maybe 10 or 15, they become demigods from Greek mythology. Heracles et al. Highly optimized characters reach the thresholds some levels earlier.

    Sure, there is some non-combat improvement for everyone. If you've gained four levels, you've presumably used your skills under adverse circumstances quite frequently.

    Meanwhile, a beginner is quite competitive with someone further along in a straight opposed roll. Let's say the beginner has +3 to their stat and +2 for proficiency, and the 10th-level character has +5 to their stat (which is assuming it's their core stat\u2014not necessarily a given!) and +4 for proficiency. So there's only a 20% point difference between them--not trivial, but if the 1st level person can contrive to gain Advantage (or impose Disadvantage), the odds are essentially even.

    \n Now let's consider a few other examples, all in 5E.

    1st level: skill untrained, and uses an off-stat (with a bonus of +0 or +1)
    10th level: ditto

    \n It's straightforward to make D&D do this thing, or not do this thing. Whether one should is an intensely interesting philosophical question for the design of a given D&D variant, I think. There's good reasons to go either way.

    I personally feel that 4th edition D&D as a miniatures chess game should have a strict build barrier between a character's combat build and its non-combat features. The way the game's written, it's sort of half-way there, and taking it all the way wouldn't be that hard - I'm definitely doing that if and when I do something with 4th edition.

    On the other hand, the concept of "combat" is not nearly as well-defined or important to some other varieties of D&D, so the matter's not so clear-cut there. The sort of old-school-with-lotsa-rules D&D I've been playing a lot wants to be agnostic about this matter, for example: the system does not know what is a "combat skill" and what is something else, it just lets the characters develop all sorts of amazing capabilities. Character level in this kind of D&D doesn't represent their combat provess, but rather their "dramatic exceptionality": a 0-level character gets no leeway from the merciless cosmos while the more levels you have, the more stuff you can get away with. It depends on your character class what it is precisely that you're getting away with, but it's always going to be something romantic, adventurous, valiant, because that's what leveling measures. Probably not basket-weaving, except as some sort of magical specialty of an artificer wizard of some sort.

    I personally feel that the class/level paradigm is so very overpowering as a conceptual focus that if you have it in the game, it needs to be front and center. And this means that if class/level is simply about combat (as in 4th ed), then the game needs to be predominantly about combat. If the game is about a wider set of concerns, then those concerns are what you want the classes and levels to address. A mis-match in this matter results in an ugly and mis-focused game, overall.

    The way I cut this particular knot in my homebrew is with a robust skill system detached from the class/level paradigm, and the presumption that characters do not improve in various exotic skills simply by leveling, but that appropriate classes enable characters to specialize in skills if they want to. In practice this means that the local non-leveled blacksmith will be ridiculously better at it than a 5th level adventurer, unless the adventurer is also a blacksmith, in which case the odds are that he'll have more special crunch for blacksmithing than the civilian smith does, on top of a reasonable skill level. I think that this is a fine way to go in a traditional adventure D&D - player characters should be able to shine as master smiths if that's what the players are into, but that should not happen by accident.

    Anybody wanting to go that way in their treatments, I suggest doing something like this:
    * Define a skill check that utilizes Ability scores and whatever sort of skill bonuses you prefer, but don't make the skill bonuses scale with level - so no skill points you get by leveling or anything like that.
    * Whatever the skill factor in your skill checks is, make it something that is determined organically on the basis of the game fiction: to be trained in skill X, get trained in skill X. Let players choose what skills their characters have on basis of their background - class does not come into it. Let characters learn new skills during downtime.
    * Make sure that your game doesn't get "unbalanced" by any particular skills that characters might have. This is easy as long as you don't insist skills to be the be-all, end-all of character building. You want to achieve a place where it's cool for your character to have some skill, but not so cool that players will be big babies about playing characters who don't have those skills.
    * For extra flair, give character classes some cool ways to be mythic about specific skills. As a traditional example, let the Thief add their level (or whatever the appropriate scaling bonus is for you) to their skill checks when doing thief-stuff; they start out almost as mediocre as a non-Thief trained in those skills, but at higher levels they'll have an insurmountable, mythic advantage.

    The specific way I accomplish these bullet points myself is by making skill checks on the formula [Ability score]*[Skill %]+d20 vs DC - in other words, a "fully trained" character uses their full Ability score in skill checks, while less skilled characters have a bonus ranging from zero to their full Ability score, depending on their skill level. Getting and raising skills is relatively casual; because skill "mastery" as a concept is cleanly capped by the character's Ability score, there's limited room for min-maxing on skills.

    A mathematically more conventional approach would be to e.g. say that characters get an Ability modifier between -3 and +3 from their Ability to appropriate skill checks, and on top of that they can get a skill proficiency modifier of +4 if they're journeyman level, or +6 if they're master level in that skill. Then you just add and roll [Ability bonus]+[skill bonus]+d20 vs. DC (or roll under [Ability bonus]+[skill bonus]+10 if that's more your style), no different from how 3rd ed. or such do it. The only important difference is that the skills are not connected to the class/level paradigm by default; it's all about natural inclinations (Ability scores) and actual background-based skill proficiency. Also, there's a distinct cap to how high skills can go ("master" is as high as you go without extraordinary means), rather than it being an arbitrary numbers-game based on ever-inflating character levels.

    shimrod said:

    Why do you want the 1st-level PC to be able to compete evenly with a 10th-level PC in a craftsmanship contest, or a court of law? But not at combat or magic? What about an archery contest, a non-combat activity that has a lot of overlap and would typically use a lot of the combat system?

    \nTwo reasons:

    1. A consistent world in which the rules as the laws of nature for the D&D universe. This is personal preference.

    2. Player empowerment. In a game in which death is a single saving throw, critical hit, or unlucky surprise round away, combat is not the first resort for PCs. Or NPCs, for that matter. PCs should thus be capable of affecting the game without resorting to combat.\n

    What does level mean then? As a default, I take it to be a measure of overall awesomeness. If you want a normal guy to be able to compete with an utterly awesome guy, it seems to me you're saying the other guy really isn't that awesome, and shouldn't be 10th level.
    \nA "level" is a unit that roughly measures a character's ability to endure hardship. A higher level increases his capacity for punishment in the form of increased hit points and better saving throw values. Both are measures of "protagonist power," in the form of resistance to death. Those who accumulate a high level are more likely to survive danger, allowing them to be bolder in their deeds.

    What @Eero_Tuovinen has suggested is essentially what I'm considering doing once I drag my players away from 5e and that blasted, awful, terrible no-good very bad skill system ingrained into it. (Not the exact percentage based system, mind you, as that would cause grave consternation among the mathematically-challenged.) My ideal form of roleplaying is the players engaging with the fictional universe and building from there. PCs should desire to learn and grow in ways that are not defined by leveling up, and the system should support that.
