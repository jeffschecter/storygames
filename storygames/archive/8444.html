    Posted By: AndyCan you tell us again how it went down? You mentioned some "manga-art-crafting software". What was it, and how much work did you put into the cards above that? Spill it!
    I'm using Manga Studio EX, which is a fairly straightforward drawing program with one particular quirk that I find wonderful: It rejects antialiasing in its entirety, instead working down to every individual pixel at 1200DPI (at least that's the resolution I'm using). So any greys you see? Those are dithered splashes of individual black and white pixels, not a true grey at all.

    This has a few benefits: First, it can make really, really sharp ink lines. Like, cut your finger sharp. Second, because it doesn't have any half-way-black pixels, its "select a swath of a given color" tool is mathematically perfect, removing any haloing effect (which really comes in handy in coloring). Third, because transforming black-and-white images (even really big ones) is less computationally expensive than transforming more complicated images, it gives you the ability to rotate your virtual "page" any which way, to draw in the way most convenient for you.

    The fourth thing that I really like (though it's not a black-and-white thing, and I do not know why Illustrator doesn't include it) is the ability to lay down a multi-point vector curve as your own custom french curve, then lock the cursor to it as you move over it. For folks with a tablet that reads pressure, this lets you vary the weight on an ink-line without having to worry about the curvature. Since I use the "undo" function, like, a LOT, this is a must-have for me.

    I lay out poses and roughs in broad pencil strokes, then refine, then ink. Under the inks, I then lay swatches of color (you can assign a given black-and-white layer a color, so that it's, like, blue-and-white instead), then make some colored pencil layers and rough in shadow, deep shadow and highlights on each base color. Then I go back over those with inks in the same color. On the most recent picture, I actually inked the exterior of each shadow in a slightly more saturated shadow-color, then filled the interior in a slightly less saturated one, so there's a more distinct edge to some of the shadows. It only takes up, like 1/300th of an inch on the printed card, but it's visible and I like it, so what the hey.

    Then export to PSD, into Adobe Illustrator to overlay all of the "card" stuff around the borders, and done.

    I'm turning out one of these a day, pretty consistently. I try to push the pace a little, but what I don't want is to put a lot of effort into something that (because of hasty judgment in the rough) I just end up not liking the look of at all ... that drains me of energy for the next piece, and slows down my pace more than I originally gained.

    Re: The tears: I've been wondering whether to remove those (they're just an extra layer) and I think you and (more viscerally) Andy have convinced me to do so. Sick, Andy, just sick.

    Re: The backgrounds: That's an aliasing effect of scaling down in Illustrator ... they're much softer in print (sixty-per-inch tone, if I recall correctly). I actually had to pull the fourth card into photoshop, because Illustrator's half-assed image processing butchered all of the tones.

    \n I really need to buckle down and get a marketing page for this up on my site, but in the meantime: Misery Bubblegum is an RPG of sweet, sad, heart-warming high-school drama. It's fast, running reliably in 60-90 minutes for a full start-to-finish session including character creation. It's portable, built as a deck of cards which lets you slip everything you'll ever need to run the game (except friends!) in your pants pocket. It's the perfect complement to any unplanned game night, giving you a bite-sized RPG that fits in the loose time-slots (before the chinese food arrives, after a long session, etc.) that you'd otherwise fill (for lack of an RPG alternative) with a game of Ticket to Ride.
