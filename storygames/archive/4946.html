\n I've gotten burned recenly on a couple of gaming products. These products did not meet my standards or their own promotional materials on a variety of fronts. I hate shelling out my limited resources for what is frankly crap. Yeah, I'm out triple-digit dollars on products I will never use, Cest la vie. What I would like to discuss is the minimum standards that a game should meet before it is released.

What are your minimum expectations when you purchace a gaming product? Also, what is the absolute minimum that should be done before releasing a game as "done?"

I expect any gaming product I pay for to facilitate the play experience they have advertised, even if that advertisement is simply the book's cover.

I expect any gaming product I pay for to have been Beta (outside) playtested, and that those playtests occurred prior to the finalization of the text.

Of dice, I expect that they will be at least nominally readable, and properly constructed. Yeah, I got burned on dice, too.

    \n I expect the rules to work. I've bought big-company RPG supplements that needed errata to make character creation possible. Everything that needs to be in the game, should be in the game.

    Ditto on beta-testing; it's great if you've had awesome tests with the game author playing and explaining everything. What about a group that has never played the game before, with no external help?

    Proper spelling and grammar. I realize it doesn't break a game, but I include it in "minimum expectations" because if you haven't proofread the whole thing, cover to cover, it's not ready to go to print.

    In a "slightly more than minimum" sense, I'd rather get a book with no art than a book with bad art.

    Hm.

    \n
      \n
    • Completeness. I expect that this product is complete and self-contained. I don't need any other product to make it work.
      • \n
    • Cleanness. Every part of the product is geared to making it work for play. There aren't fatty useless things like fiction, setting that isn't intimately tied up in interesting situations, etc.
      • \n
    • Tightness. There isn't too much product. I want what I buy to be a teaching text, not a reference manual; that means that there's a maximum level of complexity. I don't want my text cluttered with mindless extravagances like indices and tables of contents; if the piece is written well, I won't need these.
      • \n
    • Freshness. I won't buy something if it's too similar to something I already own. I want my play experiences to keep changing.
      • \n
    • Craftsmanship. Spelling, grammar, layout, illustration, writing. These all have to meet a minimum technical standard.
      • \n
    • Artistry. Spelling, grammar, layout, illustration, writing. These all have to display a strength of artistic vision.
      • \n
    • Design. The rules have to work and they have to be good rules. No THAC0 for me thank you.

    \n Well, the number one thing is that the game must be playable as written.

    I've run into this issue with a few small-press games -- the rules are written with the assumption that every consumer knows what is going on inside of the author's mind and, as a result, the game is either very confusing or completely unplayable as written. Often, games that suffer from this also suffer from a total (or near total) lack of examples.

    If the game is advertised as providing a different play result than other games, it had better deliver.

    I've run into several games that are heavy on gimmicky resolution mechanics (often ones that revolve around replacing dice with cards or some other form of randomizer) but otherwise provide nothing that dozens of existing generic game systems (such as GURPS or RISUS) can't also provide. These games are ultimately a waste of my time.

    If you're charging me money for a game, make certain that it has been thoroughly tested.

    I loved how a certain game got released as a commercial product first and then playtested after the fact (It is now available as a free download and has been tagged with the "beta" label. I paid $8.50 for the same incomplete game). On the up side, I only paid $8.50 for it. I could have been paying the $20 asking price for a recently released fantasy game that was played by only the designer and three other people in the six month run-up to publication. Jesus.
