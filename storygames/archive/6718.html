\n So my job leaves my brain pretty unoccupied. For some reason today I was thinking about the potential possibilities of using Go as either an RPG task/conflict resolution system or to create structure for a story game.
Here's my weird idea so far:
I'm thinking something like Sons of Liberty, but much slower, and with only 2 people.
Just like playing cards of particular suits in Sons of Liberty are meant to cue descriptions of certain kinds of actions, I'm thinking playing stones in different areas of the Go board would cue descriptions of various types of action. If stones are captured from the play, some kind of aggressive action would be narrated, possibly filled in by the person who lost the stones. The more lost, the greater the set-back in the narration. Similarly, some placement is clearly aggressive or defensive, and that would guide the narration as well.
And that's pretty much all I've got so far.

Clearly this isn't really a new game so much as it's Go with some story tacked on. Or some freeform roleplay given structure by the flow of a game of Go. I don't know how much sense this really makes or if it would be interesting to expand on... but it's been running through my head this morning, so I thought I'd throw it out here for public consumption and open myself to the commentary or ridicule of the masses.

    \n I fiddled with a chess-based oracular rpg system in 2005 for theoretical purposes, so I have some experience with this. The basic lesson is that you won't actually get an inspiring rpg out of it without having some way for the fiction to influence the boardgame underneath - my game was full of ways for the board to influence the fiction but nothing in the other direction, which basicly meant that it was just a storytelling oracle which the players considered heavy and tiring to use, as they needed to both play chess and tell a story, which was often rather disjointed itself. Because I wasn't interested in actually breaking Chess to make the game fun (my theoretical goal was to learn what happens when you don't have the fiction influence the mechanics except through player choice in tactics), I abandoned the project at that point.

    \n I beleive there's some tradition in Asia of stories that revolve around a game of Go, where a long game of Go has some relationship with the events in the lives of the character. "The Master of Go" (which I have read) is an example. For a more modern take, you could use the anime Hikaru no Go as an example.

    So you have rounds of storytelling punctuated by playing Go. What happens in the Go phase effects the resources you have to tell the story. Let's say a group of stones on the board that are under attack manage to live. Then we switch to our story game. The fact that your group lived affects the story that both you and I now tell.

    Maybe you could build the story around elements relevant to the Go game. One example that springs to mind is the way that in Go you have to give something to get something. You can't win everywhere, so you choose where you want to win territory and where you're willing to lose territory.

    That's pretty vague, but maybe it'll spark someone else to come up wtih a concrete mechanic.

    \nWell I wouldn't suggest codifying it in rules. Whether or not something is an "attack" is determined by the attacker, isn't it? If I do something to try to hurt you, then it's an attack. It may not actually succeed in hurting you (in which case, it's a poor, failed attack) but it's still an attack. Similarly, if I do something to try to protect myself, it's a defense, even if it doesn't actually help save me.
    There's no reason to have a giant list of moves to look up to see whether it should be narrated as an attack or defense or something else. Let the intentions of the players determine it.

    \n One of the problems I've personally found in ideas like this (I've experimented with these kinds of things for a while, and have been meaning to write a thread about it) is the fact that you have to constantly be handling the board. All the time. It must not leave your awareness.

    So in games like, I dunno, let's say The Shadow of Yesterday or SOTC or In a Wicked Age. You have your character sheet. Thing is, it's not all-pervasive. You can decide to put it down, not look at it for a while, and simply roleplay. You're not absolutely tied to the sheet or rolling the dice. You can leave them behind for a while, and then come back to them when you want to.

    With games that have their central mechanic be based on an entire game of something else (I've tried with Mahjongg and Shogi), you can not step away from the board to roleplay for a while, and return. The gains, losses, or effects that the board has is so tied to the narrative that you simply can't leave it.

    It's fun for an experiement, but over time I found it in practice to be really tiring; not being able to leave the board/game when I want to, when a scene is flowing awesomely with simply roleplaying: We have to break what we're doing and return to the board to have it tell us what happens next, rather than simply roleplaying it out.

    -Andy

    Thoughts on the idea of "breaking the game" with changes to the rules:
    If you're really serious about wanting to play Go, you should just be playing Go. As soon as you decide you want to play The Go RPG you've decided you're interested in something beyond or aside from the standard game of Go. So I don't see it being a huge issue to add rules modifications.
    The simplest ones would be something akin to, again Sons of Liberty. Each character could have a couple special strengths, things that when narrated into the story would allow him or her to do something different on the board. Perhaps play two stones at once. Perhaps move a stone that's already on the board, or recover stones that have already been captured (not to place them on the board, probably, just take back the points).
    And if both players know that one is a better Go player than the other, these abilities could be used instead of, or along with, standard Go handicapping. The weaker player could be given more "special abilities" to even things, possibly.
