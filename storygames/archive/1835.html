    1. Sometimes there's just social contract stuff in the way. Sometimes it feels weird to articulate your intentions toward another person in advance. Some people may not like it, actually.

    2. Sometimes you can't, or really shouldn't, because you don't quite know where things are going in-game. Then IGMYA becomes vague, or weak. "I'm gonna make you awesome by ... supporting whatever it is you do!"

    Hang on, hang on.

    I'm Gonna Make You Awesome is a very specific exercise, designed to get players to focus on what they can do to support other players. It does two things: it gets players into the mindset of supporting each other; and it opens up a discussion of what players want from each other.

    Now, sure, you can use the mechanics to let players reward each other. But that's a rather different thing from the original exercise. I'm Gonna Make You Awesome isn't about designing games: it's much more of a social contract thing.

    We're in danger of widening this discussion to anything that can be done to "Make players awesome", whatever that means. That's, surely, an impossibly broad discussion.

    Graham

  • Larry
    November 2006
    If I can go by negative example, I think the bad gamer habit that IGMYA specifically works to kill off is fomented by a particularly nasty strain of "My Guy"-ism. You have a certain school of game texts, GURPS 3e for example, which specifically encourages players not to communicate with or make helpful suggestions to one another unless they could realistically do it "in character." The logical extension of this behavior is the sort of turtling up where the only encouraged attempts at contributing to the SIS are through the lens of what "my guy" can realistically do. Cue player apathy.

    So I suppose IGMYA as applied in game texts involves explicitly soliciting players to throw out positive suggestions and feedback to one another, kibbitz about what's the coolest action to take before it actually hits the SIS, to build off of contributions instead of blocking, and generally practice basic, healthy social communication. I see most Forge-spawned indie titles explicitly or implicitly endorsing these things, at least not actively working against IGMYA.

    But I think IGMYA goes beyond those things too. The actual stating that maximum exaltation of the creative esteem of others is the agreed-upon social goal seems pretty key.

    I think your protest, Tim, is ironic. You say, "It can't be done mechanically - see here in TMW how I did it mechanically?"

    Yes it's an ethic, and one that you can instil through exercises. But the question at the beginning of the thread included "ways to make this easier?" I think that mechanics can be put in place that facilitate this.

    And, actually, I think that the ones cited are the most rudimentary ones. Fan Mail is dead simple (and that has it's own advantages), it gives you a currency that moves the game with which to reward folks. And even this little thing does help from what I've seen in play. Especially with players who may not know the ethic. That is, you can get the sort of play you're looking for, perhaps without the exercise.

    But Fan Mail and such, again, are rudimentary. As very simple ideas, they don't go very deeep into promoting this sort of thing, IMO. Chris, above, is starting to delve into something deeper. But I have an example from an actual game that works pretty well. From the game Unsung, Kirt Dankmeyer has "Gifts." Basically this mechanic allows other players to create adversity for other player characters, and get direct awards for it, if the player for whom the adversity is created accepts the suggestion.

    "Gift! Your character finds out that the villain is his father!"
    "Uh, wow. Didn't see that coming. Cool, I accept, here's your point."

    And I think that even that is only sorta rudimentary, too. That is, these mechanics all go straight for the clitoris. "Do good, get points." I think that they could be incorporated more intrinsically with other parts of the game in more complex ways that could serve to really propel players to constantly be "awesomeizing" just by playing the rest of the game. That is, not some mechanic tacked on, but something more integral to the game.

    Actually Trust mechanics are pretty close already, too. :-)

    Mike

    P.S. Oh, BTW, I don't like the exercise as presented, because it smacks to me of "playing before you play." That is, if you say, "I'm going to make your character awesome by having the villain be his father" that really saps the drama out of the presentation. It should happen in play, and spontaneously. Mechanics can facilitate and incentivize.

    It also helps to focus on a particular way that you can make other players awesome.

    Like, other players are awesome when they step up to morally invest in a conflict, and win it as a result. So Capes gives players tokens that (for various reasons) always get spent in the moments that they're morally investing. Those tokens become rewards for the adversaries that forced them to spend them, and thus are rewards for making the player awesome in that particular way.

    Likewise, players are awesome when they are confronted with the choice between being trustworthy or taking advantage of trust. In Mountain Witch, when I trust someone, I am making them awesome, and I reap the rewards every time they use the trust tokens I gave them to help my samurai up the mountain.

    Specific means of making other players awesome ... specific techniques to reward it.

    Joshua BishopRoby has a good mechanic in Full Light, Full Steam: the "scrip". Characters in FLFS each have "thematic batteries" (= flags), and if you reference another player's character's thematic battery in your narration you pass the other player a scrip (a piece of paper) and they sign it. You get "spoils" (= XP) for this - and in no other way. You cannot advance your character without hitting other people's flags.

    Big thanks to Thor for having started this thread and getting me thinking about awesome.
    I'll have a new thread later on with a more in depth thing like this for my own game.

    This idea is mostly a Burning Empires/Wheel hack. It seems that it needs a few thing to really make it go but its not a bad start I think.
    Each character/player has Beliefs/Flags. Beliefs tell everyone about the characters. Flags tell everyone what the player wants. (1)
    Artha can be spent to give you a die bonuses (More dice, easier/better successes.).
    Artha is awarded (By the GM) for hitting on your beliefs or on another player's flags/beliefs. Everyone nominates and votes on artha handouts at the end of the session. (2)
    Artha can be donated no strings attached or for a specific action. (3)
    When artha is spent on another player for a specific skill roll you test for advancement as well. (4)

    Now this is how I see these making things more awesome.
    (1) Gets what people think is awesome out in the open.
    (2) The GM gives rewards as for things as things happen to put more currency into the system and encourage players to do interesting things. The players voting on artha handouts at the end get people to talk about and highlight what they liked.
    (3) The players can give rewards to the other players to encourage each other.
    (4) Encourages the use of Artha on the other guy..

    Since Artha is in limited quantities and different types not everyone can make you awesome at once or all the time.
