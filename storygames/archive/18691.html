\n Can you think of any RPGs/story games with explicit rules or guidelines for handling in-character dialog?
If so, which, and how?
And finally, if any such thing does exist, do you find it adds much to the game?

To be clear, I know that many games implicitly encourage speaking in a certain way, often by including a sample back-and-forth between GM and players, or by presenting short stories or vignettes in the game universe. I'm wondering, though, about how feasible it would be (or how useful it has already been for others) to present actual rules and guidelines for "proper" in-game dialog for a given game.

Just for example: Let's say you want to set a game based in the world of Cormac McCarthy's The Road. If you've ever read that book, you know that it has a very distinctive style of dialog; no quotation marks, brief sentences, very little in the way of following or preceding dialog with things like "he said." Would you gain much by ruling that players should not make any explicit distinction between speaking in-character and out-of-character to try to preserve that tone? Or, alternatively, say you're doing a game in an over-the-top noir setting \u2013 can you imagine players being rewarded for speaking tersely in their dialog, but offering lengthy and detailed voice-overs to fill in all the gaps?

I suppose I'm thinking about this because there's a huge range in my own gaming groups in terms of how comfortable players are with the "improvisational acting" part of roleplaying and story gaming. I'm wondering if clearer guidelines, or even reward mechanisms, might in some cases help struggling players in "creating" dialog that fits appropriately in the setting, or even remove the responsibility to speak in-character at all (in a genre where dialog tends to be sparse anyway). In other words, I don't mean to suggest this as a way of clamping down on creative players who really get into their roles, but as a way to help out players who are more comfortable with character optimization than narration, but still want to participate in actual storytelling, you know?

    My Daughter, the Queen of France explicitly limits the use of dialogue in a variety of ways, which was in part intended to support players in building up their "improvisational acting" chops. For example, the first time you play a scene it is composed entirely of dialogue, without any description of character action. Character emotion is also explicitly forbidden. The goal was to imitate a certain sort of 'cold reading' of Shakespeare (or plays in general) that is most common in high school English classes, but it also means that the players generally have several chances to consider a scene, character motivation, etc. before they need to worry about conveying emotion through dialogue.

    I think the most common ways that games encourage/support specific modes of dialogue is through modelling/example, usually in the game text and also sometimes the mechanics. Polaris would be probably the best example of this; not only does it have an extremely consistent, borderline-heavyhanded tone that is conveyed through the fictional excerpts (which you are explicitly instructed to read aloud, etc.) in the book, but more importantly the ritual phrases themselves place focus on dialogue and word-choice and support players in their attempts to render a specific sort of verbal style. I think the almost over-the-top flowery style of parts of the book are also important: it encourages players to go all-out in a way that a more reserved or subtle style would not.

    Apocalypse World also falls in this category; though it lacks the same tonal support in terms of ritual phrases, the extensive use of lists and the extremely evocative prose (in move names, etc.) helps model and encourage a certain type of in-game dialogue.

    --

    Noir is a good test-case, I think, but I haven't played a lot of noir-inspired games. There was a game at Gamestorm this year (whose name I am blanking on, sorry Morgan!) where one of the players plays the Detective's hat, and the hat only ever speaks in voice-over. In addition, many of the mechanics of the game limit both which characters can speak at what time, and what sort of things the characters/players can say. So while the game does not support specific styles of dialogue directly, it does place direct focus on dialogue by subjecting it to such rigorous mechanics. When there are rules that stop you from speaking, or force you to say specific sorts of things at specific times, that naturally creates a focus on the dialogue that is produced. I think that this approach, in combination with players who are familiar with the genre/style in question, is extremely fruitful. It's much harder when there isn't a genre touchstone to draw on, of course, but it sounds like that might be outside of the scope anyways.

    \n For me, games that come to mind with actual rules for in-character dialogue:

    (1) Land of Og, by Aldo Ghiozzi. This is a comedic game of cartoon cavemen, where one the character traits is the list of words you can say - like "you", "me", "fire", "bang", and "go".

    (2) Puppetland, by John Tynes - One of the rules of this is that everything said at the table is in-character. (You can speak out of character by getting up out of your seat.) In keeping with puppet show tradition, the usual way to narrate is by having your character speak out what they are doing, like "I hit you with my knobby club, you nasty alligator. Take that!" This doesn't strongly affect dialogue per se, but it affects in-character speech a lot.

    (3) A Thousand and One Nights, by Meguey Baker - The stories told between the courtier characters are in some ways narrated action, but they are also in-character dialogue. Some groups just focus on the stories told, but in my favorite games, the stories really are dialogue and interaction between the characters.

    \nA technique I always go out of my way to stress to new players -- like far, far beyond any other single thing that comes to mind -- is the ability to switch between third- and first- person dialogue. It is so easy as a player to get hung up on saying exactly the wittiest or most cutting or most flirty/sexy thing, or to struggle to live up to the manner-of-speech you imagine for your character. Reminding players that they can just narrate their dialogue is a huge deal, and the ability to freely mix the two modes is IMO ideal, since it allows you to convey the character you want while still inserting bits and pieces of actual first-person dialogue when it happens to come easily to mind.

    I'd be interested in hearing about any games (besides Puppetland, already mentioned) that make explicit use of the first/third person division, or otherwise train players in their use. Delve's pacing mechanic comes to mind, though it's a bit more indirect.

    I think there's a bunch of different ways you could employ mechanics around IC speech:

    * Control which words you pick. You can limit someone to talk only in-character, only in 1st person, only in monosyllabic words, and so on.
    * Control how many words you say. You can limit how much people can talk before they have to stop.
    * Control when people can talk. You can require people to wait for certain things to happen before they can talk.
    * Control tone. You can require people to whisper or shout, to speak in a certain accent, to say everything sarcastically...
    * Trigger effects on certain words. Like a drinking game, you can make something happen when people say certain words. "Oh, you said FIGHT so the dragon gets a +1 to hit you."

    I'm sure you all have more cool ideas!

    \nLegend of the Five Rings has explicit rules for etiquette, sincerity and various other sorts of conversation. For example, it is customary to turn down a gift twice so that the giver may show sincerity by offering it a third time. To not do this leads an automatic failure on any etiquette roll. Similarly, the restriction on transactions to gift giving and not bartering really sets the mood of the game, and is represented by an honor mechanic.

    An example of an implicit rule is how the warrior monks of the Togashi family must speak in riddles. This is not covered by rules, so there is no way to enforce it.

    Certain people don't have to follow any rules when they speak. That's because they are tainted (by the Shadowlands, or demons, or a variety of other things). This links the ability to speak in controlled ways (represented by the skills) to a pure soul, but speaking like someone who is pure does not guarantee purity. It's just one clue.

    Call of Cthulhu could really benefit from such a link between politeness and sanity. They were connected in the fiction, but not in the game.

    \n I've had a lot of fun in various games playing creatures with alien frames of reference. I remember a Shadowrun game where we contacted the spirit of an elevator, who could only speak in terms of up/down and go/stop. Air elementals in fantasy homebrews could only speak in terms of wind, moisture, temperature and pressure. I think this is a combination of two premises:
    1) you can only say what your character could say, based on their knowledge and perspective
    2) their knowledge and perspective is really friggin' weird

    I've also played in noir games where every player was encouraged to talk like an archetypal noir character (so, a lot of Bogart), action parodies where a high stat means you must talk like Vin Diesel, supervillain games that give you points for talking like a classic Marvel villain, etc.

    In Burning Wheel Duel of Wits, you might script a conversational move like "Obfuscate" because it's mechanically advantageous. When it comes time to roleplay out your plan, you then have to speak (or otherwise narrate, but usually in-character) in a way that serves to obfuscate, even if that isn't what comes naturally for you when you normally speak in-character.

    Some horror games advice the GM to use slow, quiet speech to put players on edge, and then loud, fast speech to startle them when introducing danger. (This isn't actually about in-character speech at all, just a dialogue technique, but this thread seems to be including those. If you want more, TomasHVM has a ton of good ones!)

    I was originally thinking of this in terms of, "How might I make this easier on players who have a tough time speaking in character?" I suspect that several of the games that bother to explicitly address dialogue, however, would actually provide an added challenge.
    \nAbsolutely. When you described how your group would find it challenging to have a scene entirely in dialogue form (as I described happening in MDtQoF), my reaction was 'well exactly.' It's meant to be difficult. (Both the whole game is meant to be difficult, but also that specific restriction is meant to be difficult within the context of the game's dynamic.)

    But also, you mostly get better at things by doing them. If your group compulsively avoids first- or even third-person dialogue, then they're unlikely to ever feel more comfortable speaking in-character, because they will never have the opportunity to practice. A game can support practice in a few ways, but one of those is just 'not giving you an option not to do it' (I mean obviously within the context of the game, rather than the larger social contract.) AW helps people become better GMs by refusing to let them off the hook for principled actions, for example; it says 'this is how you play, these are rules' and so encourages MCs to be more self-conscious and directed about how they run the game. This is true even though most of the things the rules tell you to do are things you probably already want to do, or are already trying to do.

    Of course games can also help by providing guidelines on how to speak in-character, and also encourage practice by providing lots of opportunities to do so. Ideally those opportunities are somehow set up in the players' favour, so that the very act of speaking in-character is likely to be dramatically effective even if their dialogue choices are just ok. Genres that call for dramatic monologues of various sorts can be good at this, even though they also put a lot of pressure on the player. Games that restrict speech in challenging/entertaining ways are also good at this, because 'speak only in emotional monotone' or 'use only this limited caveman vocabulary' are just kind of funny ways of talking, all on their own.

    \n French game Prosopop\xe9e by Fr\xe9d\xe9ric Sintes has three rules controlling all talking. They're there much to control what you say and how you say it when narrating, but since the rules apply to dialog as well, it influences how people talk in character.

    The first such rule has no in-character consequences. It states that everything that has been narrated is true, so you cannot contradict another player. Of course, characters can contradict each other, so this rule doesn't affect character dialog.

    The second one says that you cannot interrupt another player. You have to wait for them to finish speaking. This also influences dialog in that people can get away with longer, more ornate speech. They won't be interrupted in the middle of making a grand statement. It also contributes to the "epic fairy tale" style of the game.

    The third rule is that there are no names in the world (called, appropriately, Le monde sans nom, "The world without a name"), and no names can be created. This means that instead of calling someone by name, you say something like "my golden-haired friend" or "the city across the mountains where the rivers smell of flowers", "the king with a heart of stone" or somesuch. You describe things, both in narration and in dialog. This is pure awesome and genius.

    How might I make this easier on players who have a tough time speaking in character?
    Depends on why they have a tough time with it.
    - For "I have no idea what to say" (drawing complete blanks), specific mechanics like "well, I'm using the Obfuscate move," can be a helpful prompt: "so I guess I'll say something misleading or distracting".
    - For "I know what I want to achieve, but I'm nervous about speaking now", rules about turn-taking and silence and attention can do wonders. Or, the opposite: guidelines about not taking the game too seriously.
    - For "I am a terrible actor", you can try (a) playing yourself in the game, so no need to act, or (b) embodying the character aka method acting.

    Is this kind of stuff useful? Is there any particular hurdle you'd like to overcome?

    \nWell, I've been trying to speak of my own group's issues mostly abstractly because I'm also just interested in finding out how games have approached this kind of thing before. If I had to get more specific, though, I'd have to admit that it's more than one issue, and more than one kind of player. Everything noted in your examples above has come up at one point or another. I've already had someone come to me about spotlight management issues (so I'm more careful now when GMing to check in with quieter players more often). I've also played with people who seem to have a tough time acting, but then when they try to make things easier by typecasting themselves, other players give them crap for playing the same cliched characters over and over.

    I think part of the issue at our table shared even by different players, though, is that when one or two people set them tone for "this is how dialogue goes," then others feel like they need to follow suit (and that can sometimes leave you feeling like, "Man, that's a tough act to follow"). In other words, in the absence of a rule dictating how (or whether) you handle dialogue, the less confident players will look to the more confident players to demonstrate how it's done. When I put it that way, though, it occurs to me that my own group's issue may be better resolved through an open discussion of techniques than an actual rule governing dialogue.

    * * *

    All of that said, there is another side to this issue that I was thinking of when I started this thread: Not just how to help players having trouble, but whether it's a problem when I don't perceive in-character dialogue as actually contributing to the story we're telling.

    For example: I was playing in a fantasy game with some friends the other night, and we needed a certain item from a PC's brother (who was an NPC). The player goes and strikes up a conversation in-character. They chat for a while about things that we as players already all know, so there's not a lot to actually reveal here, but it goes on for kind of an uncomfortably long time, with three of us just sitting around while this guy shoots the shit with the GM. Finally, the player looks at the rest of us, like, "Okay ... so ... what next?" and somebody reminds him, "You still have to ask him for the thing we need."

    So, we all have a good laugh, but I'm left wondering: Could we, should we have run that differently? It certainly would've been more economical to simply say, out of character, "I go to my brother, exchange some pleasantries, and ask him to part with this item." But would have have been better? I mean, just as a fight scene can be boring if you don't give any more description than, "I hit him," a conversation scene might be boring if there's no actual dialogue spoken. But then again, there's the question of whether all conversations need to be scenes of their own \u2013 can't some of the basic details that just need to be established to move the story forward simply happen "off-camera"?

    Perhaps that's an issue of story pacing and scene focus more so than dialogue, but it's an issue (at least for my group) that seems uniquely related to the way a game implicitly handles conversation. If you expect that all conversation should be handled through in-game dialogue, then you're going to have a lot of pointless conversations. If you decide that not all conversation should be handled through in-game dialogue, then how do you \u2013\xa0as a GM, as a player, or as a game designer actually adjudicating this \u2013\xa0decide what makes the cut, and what doesn't?

    If you decide that not all conversation should be handled through in-game dialogue, then how do you \u2013\xa0as a GM, as a player, or as a game designer actually adjudicating this \u2013\xa0decide what makes the cut, and what doesn't?
    \nI think the basic idea here is that not everything should appear "on-screen". It's not so much about whether things are handled through in-character dialogue; it's more about "what do we care enough to spend time playing through, and what do we prefer to skip over?"

    There's no magic answer to this question, since each game and each group will find a different balance. In practice, I find one useful technique is to think about this before entering any scene: "Do I have something important to do here? Is what we're about to play through something which needs to be appear in the story?" If you do not, ask the other players if they do. "Hey, do you want to actually play this through? Because all I need to do is grab this item from my brother: I don't have anything particularly interesting to do or say here." If they also have nothing to say, then skip that scene altogether, or summarize it quickly if necessary.

    A good story needs an editor: don't forget that when you're playing, and edit ruthlessly as you go along. Not all scenes make it into the final cut of a movie. Ask each other, as a group, at each step of the way, "Why do we care about this particular moment?"

    Maybe it's because there's an important challenge, or a question to be answered. Maybe you're both really enjoying just playing out the dialogue - that's a worthy answer too. Maybe it's a pivotal moment where a character's decision is really important. There are many possible answers to that question. But if you don't have one at all... press the "fast forward" button. Your games will be better for it.
