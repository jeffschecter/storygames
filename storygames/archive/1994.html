I promised in another SG thread that I'd post some "Buzz uses indie thinking with HERO AP. Apologies in advance for the length of this. Vanilla is limiting the length of comments, so I'm breaking this up over the next few posts.

This past Friday I ran the first session of a short Star HERO campaign for my regular HERO group. We play biweekly and usually do supers, though we do have a Fantasy HERO campaign that we rotate in occasionally. I've been with this group for almost four years now.

I rarely GM HERO, and this was the first time I'd ever run a Heroic game (as opposed to Superheroic). I tried to leverage some of my nascent Indie/Forge-fu. The group has a lot of "bad" habits, IMO, and I wanted to use some new techniques and see how they reacted. I know that Mr. Holmes says "you can't sneak up on mode," but I wanted to try getting away from our usual "Push players through the plot and fudge while doing so." I'm not sure I actually accomplished this. Overall, I wasn't too happy with the results, though my players claim they had fun.

So, the players...

* Steck: Steck was playing our starship captain, Amelia Bjorn. She's got a DNPC parrot, Marvin, who has been with the ship for decades.
* Bart: Steck's husband, playing Dr. Emily Summerland, the ship's medic. A malpractice suit has blemished her career.
* Mike: Playing disgraced ex-marine colonel Eas E. Slutt (yeah, that's his name, and he took it as a Disad), the ship's weapon expert. He hopes to one day clear his name.
* Kathy: Mike's wife, playing a fex (feline race), is the ship's cook. She's also a talented hacker and budding information-broker.
* Jason: Playing the ship's engineer, is a "space dwarf" (basically), and owns a mining/cargo mecha (Jason was insistent that he get to have a mech in this campaign). Jason is actually an operative for his planet's government, who are currently involved in a cold war with an opposing planetary faction.

The setting is Hero Games' Terran Empire, filtered through Traveller. I slimmed down the races a bit and added Niven's Kzin and the Fraal from Alternity's Star*Drive setting. The PCs are the crew of the IMS Beggar's Banquet, a rickety old free trader.

In prepping the session, I tried to make use of Chris Chinn's advice on Flag Framing and the Conflict Web. So, rather than cerate some sort of overall plot, I made up the following:

* A relationship map of the PCs and NPCs
* A list of all their Disadvantages
* A disadvantage map for the PCs.

The last was kind of an experiment. The color of the Disads indicates their point value, from little (green) to lots (red). Their position indicates (sorta) how much they relate to each other, in terms of similarity or opposition. I was pretty much flying by the seat of my pants here to see if this would be an at all useful tool. As you can tell, I was using HERO's Disads as my character flags. Whether this was a good idea or not, I dunno. They seemed the most obvious aspect of the system to use.

The basic situation is...

Steck's mom had been a sometime smuggler for many decades. Recently, she'd finally been caught and was under "house arrest," i.e., unable to leave her current planet of residence. However, she had recently outfitted the Banquet with a near-state-of-the-art displacer drive, and had recruited Steck to assemble a new crew. Before she could make clear to Steck what exactly is going on, she died under mysterious circumstances. So, Steck inherited the ship.

I then wanted to start off with everyone as part of the crew and get going on the main "bang," but the players seemed interested in playing out how they joined the crew. So, I offered the idea of rp'ing their job interviews with Steck. These scenes were mostly:

Steck: "What do you have to offer as a member of my crew?"
Player: "I'm good at x, y, and z."

Kathy also did make a point of doing some background investigation into Steck's mom before her interview. She found out that Mom had been seen meeting with a hzeel (think Ferengi) and his kzin bodyguard.

    And that's where we stopped. I'd say we ran from about 8pm to 11:30pm.

    So, why was I so disappointed?

    First off, I had thought that I was setting up a situation, but presenting it felt more like a plot. I wasn't fudging (many) rolls, but I felt like I was fudging details and events (such as the 'bot fiasco). I didn't want to just pull them from A to B to C, but that's what I felt like I ended up doing. I partly think this was just poor timing of all the bits I had wanted to use, partly fear of improvising.

    Second, the players and I seemed to be working at cross-purposes. I wanted to "spill my guts" and give them all the basic info about the situation so we could get to the point where they took that info and had to make some decisions. They seemed to want to go through the whole process of figuring out the hook. "What can I find out about the mother?" "I want to scan the hold and see if I can find anything." I honestly started to feel bad that I was just giving it all to them; it's like they wanted to work for it.

    Conversely, sometimes I would lay what I thought was some big event on them (e.g., "The police want to leave an armed 'bot on your ship for the duration"), and they'd respond with, "Okay. I head back to my quarters and make my bed." There were dead spots when I figured they'd want to take action, but would just kind of sit there waiting for me to move on.

    Third, we hardly rolled any dice. Granted, this is usually how our games go, but I didn't want this game to be like that. It just seems pointless to me to play such a crunchy system like HERO and then ignore most of it. Of course, the fact that I managed to almost completely blank on all the rules didn't really help this situation. On top of this, I forgot to use my GM screen, and my copious notes and NPC sheets worked against me, in that it took me forever to find what I needed at any given time.

    Lastly, just the fact that they looked as listless as I often feel during our games. I know I shouldn't have been expecting all-night gamergasms from them, but I really didn't want to see the usual tired faces and flipping through rulebooks. Of course, I was probably suffering from high expectations, like I was going to "show them how's it's really done" or something. I dunno.

    So... I'm hoping to rectify this in time for the next session (many weeks away in our rotation). I'm cutting myself some slack given how infrequently I GM, much less GM a homebrew scenario. Still, I want figure out how to pull this off more successfully.

    Interesting. I'm a fairly experienced Hero GM, and I've got some notes. Incidentally, just last week I ran a space opera HERO System pickup game. It was an original series Star Trek scenario that I had prepared for convention games. I posted about the run on my LiveJournal as "Ethical Dilemmas in Star Trek".

    As for your run... In my opinion, you have too many plots and especially too many scenes for only one characters. It seems to me that you introduced a lot of elements, and tried to drop things (like the telepath and the police robot) because you didn't know where they would go. This made for a bunch of half-hearted dropping of stuff, rather than strong follow-through.

    You also are trying to put some culpability on the players for not wanting to get the information too easily, but I am skeptical of this. It would have been pretty trivial for you to simply have a message from Amelia's mom to her explaining what she was doing and what she wanted her daughter to do. However, you kept everything a secret until the last minute -- not even knowing who they were meeting before they came on the comm screen, etc. If you want the players making important decisions, they need to know what's going on. At the least, they should know more than any of the individual NPCs.

    Especially, I think having Major Barth come in with authoritative demands and bring in a telepath was over the top in overpowering the PCs -- especially with them having no way to resist. (Really, that one seems sort of obvious to me. It's not a bang as far as I can tell.)

    The one-on-one scenes are also problematic, I think. The HERO System doesn't have a way for non-involved players to affect the scene, so it favors having a more central situations which the PCs interact over. You want to look for ways to enhance the PCs ties and conflicts with each other. The disad map has some nice ones: the clashes over religion and violence, notably.

    Lastly, if you're going to use the Hero System, then your combats should be fun and involving. Never throw in a fight just to have a fight -- your fights should generally be setpieces. A minor fight in the Hero System is a real waste. Minimize your fight-possible NPCs, but make sure that the ones which might fight will be interesting opponents.

    As for how to proceed from here. First of all, the players need an info dump, stat. I'd start with an emergency action scene as they face off and the pirates give them a strict countdown. The obvious way out is for them to use the weapon on the pirates. I'd go do that -- if they have a superweapon, it's a seed for a lot of future possibilities. Once the emergency is past one way or another, then you need to spill the beans on the background here -- the likely source is from interrogating Zee'Gansh and searching his stuff. If you don't have a good explanation to spill to them, then you need to come up with one. You might want to announce a retcon or two if you have some trouble.

    Mark,

    First, toldyaso. :-P

    OK, that's not too helpful. John and Larry are giving good advice above. But if what Larry is saying is true, and the players either are unaware that they're allowed to do things out of the scene, and play with author stance (not Pawn Stance, Larry, that's about having the character at player whim act sans respect for plausibility), and all of this stuff, you're going to have to show them that you want it.

    Show. Don't tell. You can talk till you're blue in the face, and it isn't going to matter. You have to demonstrate in actual play.

    Here are some of the best "clubs" you can use for being blunt about this sort of play:

    - Use third person narration instead of second person. This seems minor, but you'll find it's both hard to break out of the old habit and that people will definitely notice a difference. "You see a light ahead, what do you do?" becomes, "Your character sees a light ahead, what's he doing?" This tends to catapult players into thinking of their character as their character, and not as though they and their character are one and the same (so they don't have the "I can't do that because my character can't do that logic" going on in their head).
    - Ask metagame questions and foster people talking about things when they're not in the scene. "Let's stop this scene for a second. What do you all think of what's going on?"
    - Continue with your out of character player knowledge barrage. But step it up. Don't just reveal things about the player's secrets, reveal them about your plot (Larry or somebody hints at this above). That's the problem, they're still seeing you holding on to secrets, but they're not supposed to. For instance, "OK, we're going to have your character roll to see if he can determine that those are Kzinti ships, OK? Oh, failed? Well that's too bad, isn't it?"
    - Ask a player if he'd rather just succeed at a die roll. "Is this dramatic to roll for the docking, or should we just say he does it and moves on?" For really high impact, do this with a combat. This way you can avoid the sorts of combats that John says aren't any fun to play out. Let the player decide. Or, heck, let the whole group vote. "Do you all want to see him beat up on this robot by the numbers or can we just narrate our way through it?"
    - Ask players to frame scenes. Don't say, "What do you do next?" That won't cut it. Ask instead, "What sort of scene would you like to have next? What characters are in it?" If you really want to press them add, "What conflict do you want to resolve or set up with the scene?"
    - Get really radical and ask Player A to frame for Player B. "What sort of scene should we have for his character?"
    - Allow them to create the plot. "You know, the telepath would normally tell his boss, of course, what he's learned. But I think it would be more fun if there's some reason for him to hide that information. What interesting reason can we come up with for why he'd do that?
    - Announce that you're changing your plans to fit the player's idea. "Hmm, I was going to have some murder mystery on Arcturus Station, but given that you've decided to head to Mora, I'm thinking now that we'll slant things into some political action. What do you think?"

    Note that I think that people get the idea that I use this stuff all the time in my games. I don't. Occassionally I'll pull something like this out. But mostly I only use it for demonstrative purposes. Yeah, I'm a real pedantic bastard about it. Like:

    Player: "Oh, the robot is a plant? Imagine that? I go make my bed."
    Mike: "Huh, I thought that would make for some interesting decisions for your character, but apparently not? What should I be hitting you with then? What would you like to see?"

    Or even:

    Player: "Huh, the bot's a plant..."
    Mike: "Yep. How do you feel about that?"
    Player: "Uh, well, my guy goes back to his cabin..."
    Mike (interrupting): "No, I didn't ask what your character feels like, I asked how you feel about it."

    You're a player in the game, too. If you want the other players to play in this particular style, you have to show them by the example of your play. Bangs alone won't do it.

    That said, your bang-fu is weak! That's OK, most everybody's is, even mine. The key is to keep on working at it. Keep in mind the two principles of a bang:
    1. Unavoidable - even "walking away" is somehow dramatic.
    2. Open-ended - not only don't you know what's going to happen, but you feel that the player will have to think about it.

    Once you've done this for a bit, you'll start to pick up on what sort of stuff interests the players, and the bangs will get better. As you say, the characters aren't communicating to you through either complete characters, or through using metagame methods. So it's no surprise you don't know where to hit them.


    Oh, and here's a mechanical note. Give the players 1 of your special reward points each time they play to one of their disads...whether to emphasize it, or to resist it! Like the guy with the code vs. killing. "The alien scum writhes before you, do you kill him or not? If you walk away and leave it to the other characters, no reward. But if you either stop them from killing it, or kill it yourself, you get a reward point!" Make him roll to kill the enemy if you want to keep with the HS rules. Even the fact that the player is rolling is interesting.

    Put it this way, in how many games have you ever seen anyone roll against their psych lims to overcome them? The rule is there to inform the player that they're generally not supposed to do so. So most HS play involves little rolling (this is a sim thing, if they are rolling, then they're probably gamists). Change that attitude, and reward players for character dynamism. This way each time the disad comes up, it's a decision point, not a "duh, he does what the psych lim says" moment.

    Mike

    Mike: Whoops! I actually meant "actor stance" there, which is what Mark's group (especially Bart, as noted above) seem to assume is the "one true way" to play RPGs. Not at all unusual, when the texts themselves sermonize about it.

    Anyway, the general problem I'm seeing here (incoherency) is an very common one. For some reason, it's not always obvious to us longtime gamers what's going on here unless you've really assimilated all the Forge stuff about Social Contract as the outer and all-encompassing layer of the game. Let me try to explain my take on it, as I currently understand it.

    Basically, a lot of gaming groups will change rulesets and genres and what not from time to time, and think of this as playing different games. But... they're still carrying forward all of original unspoken assumptions about the group's social contract, often from way back when. So, to a very real degree, they're still pretty much playing the same game they learned to play in the very beginning. Doesn't matter if you're calling it "D&D" or "Champions," "sci-fi" or "horror," "rules-heavy" or "freeform." They're playing the game called "role-playing games," as though that's some monolithic, universally understood meaning, but where certain nebulously identified things that featured in previous games must arbitrarily be in place. It's a nasty circular definition.

    My thought is that the only particularly good way to break out of this trend, short of some sort of unimaginably ideal pow-wow where you communicate everything you've learned about gaming, is to present players with a game that will in no way be mistaken as "the same old game." Games like My Life With Master or even Donjon, where the mechanics and narration structure are so radically different in an up-front and visible way, it just clearly communicates "we are playing a totally different sort of game, and that's okay and it can still be fun." Something where if they're not genuinely willing to try something new (despite claims to the contrary) they'll stamp their feet and bail out, and nobody's under any illusions.

    I wish I knew of reliable ways to successful transform a clunky, traditional-style game into dazzlingly coherent, fresh gameplay. Mike's suggestions above are as excellent as you'll find to pursuing that end. But I don't have a lot of confidence in the "patch-kit" approach.

    OK, here's my two cents.

    1) The Xenovores should expect to go through with the deal. I'm assuming here that the PCs are going to reject giving the superweapon to the Xenovores -- so there's going to be conflict anyway. This way the PCs are the ones who get to make the choice and screw over the opposition. Let's say the Xenovores have made deals with the hzeel before and want to keep him as a useful contact. However, to keep the pressue, though, the Xenovores can be very curt and say something like "Deliver the goods in five minutes or we will destroy you."

    2) Assume that there are way too many of the Xenovores, and that the PCs are probably going to have to use the superweapon on them. In a fight, the Xenovores will try to disable the ship (but not destroy it for fear of losing the weapon), and then attack in waves. Having a few waves of increasing toughness gives you a chance to twiddle their numbers and/or stats if you're having trouble with the combat rules.

    3) You need to work out a backstory that makes sense, and dump it to the players after the fight is over. The best way for a dump is probably for the hzeel to spill the beans relatively easily (or have documents which can be found).     buzz, your description of "everyone waits an hour while I play through other people's scenes, plays their own scene for ten minutes, and then there's a fight" maps almost perfectly to one of my most successful (and most hair-pulling) games that I've ever run, my Adventure! Gotham City game. I post about it a lot because both its successes and its failures drive me absolutely bugnuts and I have a hard time grasping either one.

    What was driving this structural choice in my game was not any dedication to any kind of narrative principle or indie hippie hoohaw, it was my dedication to the genre, where the heroes are broody loners with serious emotional problems and private lives that are not only kept highly private but which are deeply troublesome to them. I got some great characters designed to this standard, but it meant that outside of fighting crime together (although of course in Gotham City games there's no shortage of crime to fight) they didn't hardly do anything together.

    I asked the group what they thought about this and they confirmed what I guessed from looking around the room during the game: they were largely bored during other people's individual scenes. The weird part is that they were so highly engaged in their individual scenes that there was insane mutiny threatened if I pulled the plug or even severely altered the project. They wanted their 15 minutes. They wanted it bad. The source material and my interpretation of it spoke so strongly to them that they were willing to sit there and read or chat about this and that, or play X-Box, or whatever they did when they were bored just so they could get to it.

    The very simple method I've used to avoid this kind of thing in future campaign designs is JUST DON'T DO IT. Make games where 'team' scenes are the norm and not the exception. Minimize 'I go off and do this' scenes, use scene-level resolutions, summarize conversations, etc.

    That to me is the best way to handle structural changes if they like the basic way the game is played but are sort of twiddling their thumbs waiting to actually play it.
