\n We're planning a Call of Cthulhu "tournament" scenario at this year's HammerCon (November 6th, Hamilton, Ontario, Canada). The convention is a community organized and volunteer-based event, with a focus on celebrating play rather than trade, but despite us being a grassroots effort with meager corporate backing we always aim for professional production quality in what we do.

This year, we're looking to run a classic CoC tournament for 3 or 4 concurrent tables of 4-5 players each (plus Keepers), who will compete with each other in areas of problem solving / investigation and entertaining roleplaying / character acting to roughly equal degrees.

For this purpose, we require an original scenario based around traditional CoC investigation activities, written for a convention format (roughly 3-4 hours in length) with highly entertaining and interesting characters. The writer we select to write this scenario for us will get writer's credit as well as their selection of ~ $100 MSRP of new Chaosium products from our convention library.

Our default preference is as classic as possible: Chaosium's Call of Cthulhu 6th Edition, 1920s. We also want something that anyone can participate in regardless of their prior experience with the Mythos, so the "newbie" experience is important. We'd need your synopsis by August 15th at the latest, from which we'll pick our writer and notify by September 1st. Your final manuscript would be due on October 10th. We'd like to make the scenario available under a Creative Commons International Share-Alike Attribution license.

If you're interested in writing for us, please reply to this post and/or email events@hammercon.ca for more details!

If you're interested in attending, please visit http://www.hammercon.ca for more details, newsletter and signup info. You can also join us on Facebook and Twitter.

Looking forward to hearing from you!

    \n Graham: Great! Just hang tight and I'll send you further guidelines (example scoring sheet, synopsis template etc) in a few days.

    Jason: Competition occurs between groups, not between individuals within groups. In the end, there will be a single group who wins, based on a scoring sheet. Points on the scoring sheet will be split equally between problem solving and story progression (did the group find a clue leading them towards the stone circle, did the group understand the significance of the three-headed worm statue) and entertaining role-play (did the group play their respective roles well, did in-character banter stay clear of anachronisms). Problem solving points will be awarded largely based on player ingenuity, i.e. failing at skill checks should not be the main cause the group loses points. These will be awarded by each individual Keeper and the parameters are kept secret from the players. Role-play points are awarded by a judge who will be circulating between groups during play, with the Keepers as advisors. Each group's performance in each category will be rated on a scale, with the highest scoring receiving max points in that category, the second receiving max - 1 points, and so on. A sample scoring sheet will be provided, but the final scoring sheet will without a doubt be heavily based on the scenario particulars, so yes: shaping the competitive aspect is also part of the design.

    \n Hi Colin,

    We'd be open to entertaining your synopsis for a modern day scenario. As long as the rationale for setting it in modern-day is strong, that'd make up for the fact that it's not exactly in line with our preferences. Our goal is to ensure a classic CoC experience that long-term CoC aficionados will enjoy and which would accurately represent and "sell" the game to newcomers.

    If you'd like me to put you on my mailing list (I should be able to crank out the example scoring sheet and synopsis template today) please shoot me a quick email at events@hammercon.ca.

    Thanks for your interest!
