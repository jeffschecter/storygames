So I was chatting with Wicked Dead game design diva Annie Rush this morning:

12:08 PM AR: It's a good thing to have friends who are willing to whack us melancholy introspectives with the wake-up hammers when need be.
me: indeed.
12:09 PM that's what friends are for.
12:11 PM AR: "trite" sayings became "trite" through overuse. but they were overused because they are very often true.
12:16 PM me: i like "that's what friends are for" because it can apply to anything, including, say, killing hordes of zombies with a weedwhacker.
or, in this case, hitting you with a metaphorical hammer.
12:17 PM AR: *doodles comic of that*
12:18 PM me: there should be a game about relationships in which the characters engage in metaphorical combat with each other.
like, when you break up with someone, you stab them in the heart with a knife, metaphorically.
12:20 PM AR: what kind of game?
12:22 PM me: i was thinking roleplaying.
12:23 PM most roleplayers pay the most attention to the combat system anyway. so if it was completely metaphorical, you might actually be able to do something interesting with combat. instead of the normal yawn
AR: now there's a thought that's gonna fester.
12:24 PM And when you see someone walking around in-game with an arsenal of weapons strapped to them, it means something totally different.
12:25 PM me: indeed. it means you probably don't want to be their friend.
but everybody has some weapons; sometimes they're carefully concealed though.
AR: "Walk softly and carry a big stick"
me: and you could actually carry around your emotional baggage and stuff.

12:27 PM AR: Yeah. and if you have an "experience" down and don't learn from it, it hurts your character
12:29 PM me: i've been wanting to write a game called "the wars of the roses" for a while. this could be it.

    Two spin-off thoughts.

    (1) The artist James C. Christensen often depicts characters as physically caring the weight of whats of their mind (see the "Responsible Woman", "Responsible Man"). In fact, an entire segment of his art is dedicated to physical/visible manifestations of intangible properties. Neat Stuff, IMO.

    (2) For a couple of months now, I've had a "little idea" about a game where in one trades "Fire" or "Passion" for "Peace" or "Success". Inspired somewhat by the personal interactions in the show Smallville, "The Secret Life of Walter Mitty" and real life. The basic idea is that a character has an internal metaphysical fire that drives them toward a goal. However, along the way to that goal they must live life and encounter conflicts as a result. "Fire" points can be spent to achieve small goals, but are obviously depleted along the way. For example, a character who is passionate about becoming a world-famous artist might trade some of their passion to 'get a real job' in order to eat. Do that often enough, and the process of living replaces the passion....

    Hrm... writing this up feels a little dark. But then some people like that.

    A great movie for symbolic combat is Jet Li's Tai Chi Master. The really cool thing is that the fighting styles express the dramatic thrust of the movie. The flowing, balanced moves of Tai Chi are contrasted with the harsh moves of the other styles -- which matches the growth in his life from the monastery to living with a family.

    I've tried this in some of my games. So, for example, the second-to-last episode of Season Two of our Buffy campaign was called The Glass Ceiling. There, the characters entered into a pyramid in faerie and fought with a host of the Sluagh.

    Here, the evil faeries were symbolic of the glorification of conflict. The PCs charged in to rescue their friend Laney who had been kidnapped. However, the sluagh didn't care in the slightest about Laney. They purely wanted to fight. So a battle raged below, while in an office in the top of the pyramid, the mastermind of the plot Samuel Devins walked about watched while eating popcorn. I feel like this one worked really well. The Sluagh were amusing in a violent way, but they were really nasty and were causing real wounds to all the PCs. The interesting thing was in how the different PCs reacted to this.
