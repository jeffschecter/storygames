\n

I'm really irritated about this whole ashcan fad, in no small part because it stains the initial releases of games that are complete, but also because it shames and irks me that my colleagues are ripping people off. There is absolutely zero excuse for charging money for unripe fruits, guys. It's unforgiveable.

\n

Half of these games are well-nigh unplayable, and sure we know you're excited, and sometimes you get overexcited and do some insane arts-and-crafts project which involves blinging out your ashcan to some ridiculous degree. This, too, is unacceptable. Putting lipstick on a pig.

\n

Go back to the roots of ashcans and stop robbing friends and strangers. If your game isn't done, then channel your enthusiasm into making it done, rather than making it pretty. The fewer resources you put into fancy dresses for your broken text, the less justified you'll feel taking money away from people for it, which is all right and proper.

    \n What about the following arguements made in support of charging for Ashcans?
    \n
    \n1) The game needs outside help to reach completion. Good dedicated help is hard to find and casual half assed help is often counter productive. Selling as a Ashcan is both a way to attract the attention of those who are interested in providing help while filtering out those who are only casually interested.
    \n
    \n2) You aren't paying money for an unfinished design, you're paying money to get a back stage pass, a behind the scenes look, a membership into the design club. (I'll note that various Fan Clubs have been charging money for these sorts of things for decades, so its hardly a new / unexpected / or unwanted concept)
    \n
    \n3) No one is being ripped off if they're clearly told what it is they're buying. If the purchasers consider that they got a good value for their money why should anyone suggest otherwise.
    \n
    \n
    \nLet's leave aside for the moment the question of whether the model actually works. We can come back to that later, but I'm not convinced its been around long enough or used effectively enough times to really gather statistically relevant success data.
    \nAnd let's leave aside for the moment the commitment the designer should have to the project subsequent to the sale -- especially if it was billed on the membership angle.
    \nAnd let's ignore for the time being those designers (if any) who've intentionally sold broken games they've no desire to work on further just to cash in on some money and cred.
    \n
    \nI'm interested first in philosophical thoughts on the above ideas (and any others that have been put forth as reasons to Ashcan) especially as they relate to this notion of theft and deminishing the release of games.
    \n
    \nI've done it twice now (the first time was long before the term Ashcan even got Coined) and have had mixxed results, so I'd like to hear more about this philosophical angle of it just being Wrong.

    \n People publish half-finished games anyway. Or games that have critical rules missing so they are impossible to run based on the book. And these aren't just games you haven't heard of. Shock: 1.0 was pretty notorious in this regard, as I recall.

    The ashcan thing was originally started as a way to try to fix this problem, by allowing folks to do a limited release of games that they thought were pretty much done, to make sure that they weren't missing anything. I'm not sure what bad experiences people have had with ashcans that make them have this attitude. Personally, the ones I've seen -- Matt Snyder's 44, Paul Czege's Acts of Evil, Rob Bohl's Misspent Youth, Jeff HImmelman's Kingdom of Nothing -- have all been great and totally worth whatever I paid for them (I don't even remember what I paid for them; some of them may have been free).

    Honestly, it seems like Shreyas is mostly pissed off because people assume his games are ashcans, not finished products, and then don't buy them for that reason. Nathan has said as much about Annalise. Honestly, that doesn't seem like a problem wish ashcans so much as an issue between indie designers and their target audience, who don't seem confident about buying ashcans and are wary of buying (or even playing) anything that might not be "finished." (whatever that means).

    My personal feelings are that initial playtesting generally should happen at home. That by the time a game is put out there for other folks to play it, it should run pretty smoothly. I'm not sure what the big deal is about "finished" stuff. When I play Danger Patrol, for example, it doesn't feel like playtesting, even if I can give John some thoughts afterwards that might influence the direction of the game. I just play the game and reflect on it afterwards. And John's working on another version of the rules right now. And I'll play those when they come out too. No big deal.

    I don't charge for pre-release copies of Geiger Counter, but that's my choice as a designer. And, lord knows, I'm not going to try to tell other designers what they should and shouldn't do when it comes to printing and selling their games. That's what I think is really bugging me about the anti-ashcan attitude. If the ashcan thing ultimately doesn't pan out, folks will discover that on their own. Heck, they may have already discovered that. I haven't heard of any notable ashcan releases in a while. Are folks kicking a publication strategy that's already changing or only being used for very specific purposes now?

    \n There's two sides to it, I think. On the positive side, ashcans let you label your unfinished stuff as unfinished. You can get your game out there without claiming it's finished.

    On the negative side, ashcans give a way of publishing unfinished stuff - with all the financial and social benefits publishing brings, like being part of a GenCon stall - rather than doing the work and fixing the mistakes.

    Even if you like the ashcan process, you've got to acknowledge the negative side, because that's how you make the process work better. In my opinion.

    \n I charged for Reality Cops at Gencon. I was back-and-forth on the whole issue right up until Thursday morning.

    I charged for a few reasons:
    -I spent money to print them.
    -I spent money to be at Gencon, in a booth, selling stuff.
    -I spent time putting this edition out that I would not have spent if it was free.
    -Ralph's 1 and 3 from up there. Hopefully not his #2.

    But my two big reasons for charging the paltry $5?
    One, because nobody - NOBODY - gives a shit about free stuff from Gencon. If you give out a hundred free copies, 50 will go in the trash before they leave Indianapolis, another 20 will go in the trash when they get home. Of the 30 that are left, 25 will sit on the shelf until the heat death of the universe. The last 5 will get looked at, maybe, in the next year. Someone will consider playing one of those 5.

    Two, my time and yours is worth more than your five bucks. I could achieve the same basic effect that Ralph is talking about with his #1 by, instead of charging a little bit of cash, I spent the whole weekend stumping and shilling my free game. But then I've burnt through my weekend, and I've wasted the time of a whole ton of people who aren't interested even a little bit, and caught a few people who seem genuinely interested enough to take it home and read it, and maybe play it. Having the book at a booth in the dealers hall, for five bucks means neither I nor the people who aren't interested have wasted any time or energy trying to connect, and the people who are genuinely interested have taken it home, and because they've spent money on it, are unlikely to throw it out, and more likely to read it. I think the jury is still out on whether they are more likely to play it.

    \n I brought my game to the Ashcan Front in 2008. I didn't really understand what an ashcan was; I saw an opportunity to get on the floor at Gen Con without breaking the bank. I couldn't go myself, so I asked a dealer friend to show it for me.

    In exchange, he got to set the price and keep whatever he sold them for. To me, the hardcopies were a way to get the game into the hands of serious gamers. I'm interested in selling PDFs. My understanding was that by charging for it, people would become invested in it and give me feedback on it.

    But I never intended to put out an unfinished product. I was blinded by the 'success' of signing with a PDF distributor and thought that the game was ready to go. I now see that it was not. If I had known that, I would have given them away to anyone who would sit down for a game and given me the feedback on the spot. That's what I tried to do this year at Gen Con. It didn't work out, but I don't blame the model, it was the amazing time I had playing other games.

    This year, I made a new version and I intend to revise it again as soon as I get some substantial feedback. I hired a writer for additional setting and I have a layout person lined up. For the people who bought the game online, they will be notified each time the PDF changes. I only plan to change it once more, but if not, they aren't out any more money. This may be closer to a 'virtual ashcan' model, but it isn't what I intended.

    But I am seeing the other side of the covenant break down. Not one person who bought or traded for the game has told me what they think about it, what is wrong with it, what might make it better. It's not just me; Julia Ellingboe said on a podcast that no one gives her feedback on Tales of the Fisherman's Wife. I gave my opinion on other ashcans, in public or in private as requested.

    I would rather that it be thought of as a bad game than be thought of as an ashcan. I don't have an answer for the conundrum of getting feedback before releasing product. But it was always my intention to deliver a good game, ready for a fun evening.

    \n To me, ashcans are about investment.

    From the consumer perspective, if you're willing to invest money in a game, I believe you're more likely to play it. I've seen evidence of this.

    From the designer perspective, if you put the effort in to creating a physical object and then going through the hassle of putting it out there for sale and supporting it, you're telling your audience that you are serious.

    I love people who give away free games. I also fear for those who make good free games that they fade into the background noise of people who do stuff that the consider to be worthwhile. Someone who is willing to invest time, money, and effort into a thing and back it with her name is someone that you can be marginally more sure is actually trying to put out a complete game.

    So why not just make a game? Because you want to make sure the "final game" you sell is of high quality.

    Why not just wait until it's of high quality? You can never be sure it's of high quality, in my opinion, until you've had a variety of people trying it. I played Misspent Youth dozens of times before putting it out as an ashcan. I decided to publish it as one because (someone asked for evidence) I had given the document to lots of people and gotten one play report, ever.

    I think ashcans are easy to discount if you happen to already have a name or are heavily plugged in. But putting something out there that says "I'm serious about this," and asking a nuisance price ($10 is a big lunch), you get what you need. I did, and it worked for me. (Also, it made me take my own work on the game much more seriously.)

    As an aside, the nasty tone that pervades this discussion, from start to finish, is really annoying. I wish people wouldn't try to make swinging-dick points over this issue. I don't really see what people have to be upset about. If you're not part of the designer-to-purchaser exchange, I don't see where you have any right to have a problem with ashcans. It's none of your business, as far as I can tell.

    \n Is it possible that the problems with Ashcans, whatever they may be, ultimately stem from miscommunication between the publisher and the customer? Here's my theory: When the Ashcan Front debuted, it was during a time when everyone just *had* to have the latest and greatest games. In the frenzy, ashcan copies moved, but customers realized only afterward that they had paid for games that weren't 100% done. That left a bad taste in the customer's mouth. Designers didn't get feedback. Hence, the current backlash.

    What problems do exist now are ones of misconception as to what an Ashcan actually is and is not. Customers are afraid to buy anything that looks like an ashcan. Some designers think they're a waste of time and/or confusing customers.

    Moving forward, what should be clear with an ashcan is that the designer is asking the customer for feedback on a game that is "fun," but perhaps not 100% delivering on his design goals. The customer should know what he is buying upfront. The designer should have a certain expectation of feedback. If that commitment on both ends isn't there, then don't publish or buy an ashcan. If all of the above happens, then I don't see a problem -- just another avenue of publication for those who are interested.

      Posted By: Ben LehmanThe only important thing to talk about is the results.
      \nResults, from a designer's standpoint:

      I sold something like 40 copies of the Acts of Evil ashcan at Gen Con in 2007. And a couple of the purchasers played it and gave me feedback, and some other folks who just read it also gave me feedback. And some of them were people I wouldn't have otherwise interacted with if they hadn't discovered the ashcan.

      That's exactly what I wanted to get from doing it: playtesting, feedback, and insights from the game's eventual target audience.

      Acts of Evil is a pretty ambitious design. Having the production of the ashcan as a goal really helped me get the evolving text into a form that others could playtest. But if I could have wrangled the text into shape without the focusing effect of planning toward the ashcan, probably I could have achieved a similar feedback and playtesting result to what came from ashcan purchasers by circulating the text within the design community. Though if I'd done it this way, I don't think I'd have found contacts and insights from the game's eventual target audience.

      Danielle sold between sixteen and nineteen copies of the Kagematsu ashcan at Gen Con last year. Despite that it was an eminently more accessible and playable and fun game as an ashcan than my Acts of Evil ashcan, I don't think she received any playtesting or feedback from folks who bought it.

      I'm not sure what the take-away is from that. It's either that you need to sell more copies to get the playtesting, feedback, and insights from the game's eventual target audience, or it's that unless you have earlier games with fans that you're unlikely to get playtesting and feedback, even if someone pays money for your ashcan.

      Still, I think both of us had a uniquely valuable result from doing the ashcans that I didn't initially understand: we had created an audience of folks who were invested in the games, who could react to our questions with an informed perspective if we initiated discussions.

      So, yes, there are other ways, but you can provoke playtesting and feedback by doing an ashcan.

      And I don't know of a better way to find contacts and insights from your game's eventual target audience, or to create an invested audience who can react to your questions from an informed perspective, if that's what you need.

      \nI've been thinking the same thing. Not so much the actual word "beta", but the concept of beta testing software is reasonably well-known and, importantly to this particular discussion, provides the end user with a reasonable expectation of what he or she is getting.
      \n
      \nNow, I've never produced an ashcan, but I've run a number of beta tests in my life. It's not exactly what you expect, so maybe some guidelines from beta testing might be of some use to those who actually do make ashcans. (If not, well, electrons are cheap).
      \n
      \nFirst, what does "beta" even mean? There are many definitions, but two are fairly common. The "alhpa" stage of software is when all the features are written. This is "functionally complete". It may not have seen much testing, but every base you wanted to cover is covered. At this point, testing is still mostly internal. Some companies release alpha software, but not many. When all the serious problems you know of are handled, you release "beta". The expectation is that you may still need to change stuff, but the final result will at least recognizable what is in the beta.
      \n
      \nIn order of importance, the rules of thumb for beta testing are:
      \n
      \nOne: Find Problems This sounds obvious, but is easy to forget in the middle of it all. Everything you do has to be filtered through the lens of "is this helping me discover flaws in my product". This is much harder than it sounds.
      \n
      \nTwo: Triage Vet every problem you hear about, with the realization that many of of them are not worth solving. The quickest way to kill a beta is to treat everything as important. Many things will not be. Some things will be, but fixing them will do so much collateral damage that you are better off not bothering (for this version, at least). It's rare, but you may also run into a deal-breaker issue: a design flaw so jarring that you need to head back to the drawing board. (This is rare, but every time I've seen it happen was because of misunderstanding what the user's needs were.)
      \n
      \nThree: Keep Costs Down Don't print documentation if electronic will do. Don't burn CDs if downloading will do. Don't pay bribes unless you have to. Don't advertise unless you have no other choice. Don't spend time with people who aren't helping you if you could be spending it with people who are.
      \n
      \nFour: Control the Product The type of control here refers more to control of the product's destiny than its content; that is, managing the "intellectual property rights" and market share. How this is done will be radically different depending on the product and circumstances. For some products, this means super-secret betas with NDAs signed by everyone. For others it means massive public betas for maximum exposure. If there is an art to beta testing, figuring out the right mechanism for control of your product is it.
      \n
      \nFive: Finish The beta is useless if it never ends. A common mistake is to set a "finished condition" based on the problems found, such as "when all issues of Medium severity or higher are fixed". Done this way, either the beta will never end, or it will end only because issues are managed artificially to meet the condition by a certain time. Generally, a timed deadline works, and everything else doesn't. The goal isn't a perfect product; that doesn't exist. Instead the goal is to make the the product the best that you can make it in the time allotted. If you aren't proud of the result, no one else will be either; have the decency to kill it and move on to the next thing.
      \n
      \nOf all of these, most of the techniques used in the software industry would work for RPGs. The big exception is #4, as the mechanisms for controlling software different than those controlling RPGs. (You can, for example, make software expire, a trick not really tolerated in the PDF world.)
      \n
      \nIn any case, the point here is that when customers sign on to a beta (whether voluntarily or paying for the "privilege"), there is a clear indication of the quality they can expect and what they are in for. This thread suggests that (right or wrong) that same expectation isn't universally held regarding ashcans.

      \n On definitions:
      1908 - http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ashcan_School
      1939(?) - http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ashcan_copy
      2006(?) - http://www.ashcanfront.net/2008faq.html

      So:
      If you are aiming for a gritty, American realistic presentation ("punk zine"), you are making an Ashcan School RPG.
      If you are merely holding a concept or title for prior art or copyright purposes (see my own Ice Soccer), then you are producing an Ashcan Copy of your RPG.
      If you are presenting raw rules, with a lot of direct communication to the player/reader, soliciting particular feedback or suggesting play variations to try to fine-tune a 90% complete game (see my own Chicken Run) then you are delivering an Ashcan RPG.

      Cost is orthogonal--for example, TriStat was given to me at Gen Con years ago, just to solicit play and feedback (they weren't sure, I suppose, if the BESM system could drift?); conversely, some folks can't afford the costs or want buy-in or whatever. None of the above definitions reference cost, do they?

      Quality is orthogonal--1909, quality was in "the eye of the beholder" like any art, but it was presumably a primary goal of its creators; 1939, quality was meaningless, they were putting out a few pages to stake out turf; 2007, quality was antithetical, the aesthetic was supposed to be bare bones, just the RAW, with no production value (probably the better to control costs and very clearly convey that, no, this ain't a completely polished, top-quality mainstream game, kid).

      Subjectivism in language is the death of communication. There's reasons we invent "dictionaries" and "encyclopedias."

      All that said... if you're making something that doesn't fit Ashcan School, Ashcan Copy, or Ashcan, then don't use the word. At all. Borrow a term from computer science, like System Test Document (30%ish), Alpha Test Document (50%ish), Beta Test Rules (75%ish) or Gold Master Candidate (99%ish). And then, of course, DEFINE that right on the cover and back cover, maybe even with a watermark on each page (watermarks scream "not done yet").

      Hope this helps the conversation... if there's any of one left.

      For many products, ashcan or not, the answer is no, and that's a bad deal.
      For many products, ashcan or not, the answer is yes, and that's a good deal.

      This is the only method of thinking about RPG purchases that makes any sense, given the social realities of gaming.

      Playtesting, whether paid or not, is a different beast entirely. This is just about the purchase of a product.
