\n A comment made in another thread prompted me to start this one.

How have folks dealt with games that address RL issues, in terms either of design or of attempting to attract players for existing games or playtesters for ones in progress?

Here\u2019s my first comment, taken from the other thread:\n
This is a tricky one. A couple of Where The Heart Is playtesters commented that it's difficult to get people to play games verging on real life as they roleplay in order to escape from RL. My current solution for the game in general is to produce playsets set in a future far enough away that it doesn't seem like real life, and ones that draw on a specific setting, forex Downtown Abbey (masters and servants) or war settings (officers and men) etc. In terms of the standard game I encourage players to pick characters as unlike themselves as possible.
\nAnd here\u2019s a reply, again taken from the other thread:\n
I think it might be an acquired taste? Many players might not be used to rpgs dealing with real-life issues, and prefer escapist fun when they game. Which is totally cool. I mean, I can totally relate to that. I\u2019ve played a bunch of these Nordic style larps and freeforms dealing with heavy subjects, but sometimes they\u2019re just not my cup of tea and I\u2019d rather just play a silly soap opera game or Shadowrun or something. I like the variation. So it can be a hard sell, even to me who has both experience with such games and am currently designing in this tradition, right? You can, as you say, deal with contemporary RL issues through the lens of sci-fi or history or fantasy. You can also attack it head on. I mean, movies, books and theatre have all these different genres and approaches. Some are fantastical; some are more down to earth. Why shouldn\u2019t the same variation be possible with rpgs?

I agree that role playing games are a good, interesting and valid approach to exploring lots of issues and subjects. And one which might give different perspectives and experiences than, say, literature, movies and plays.

    \n My experience is that some people are innately attracted to this sort of roleplay. Some people secretly want to try it but are nervous (so you have to nurture them a bit more) and some people like their narrative distance from real life, and there's pretty much nothing you can do to get them into a style of game that doesn't suit their desires.

    One question, I think, is what do you mean by "games that address RL issues"? Do you mean some of the more naturalistic Nordic freeform games, which often take place in the mundane world and deal with real-people drama? or do you mean a Cthulhu Dark scenario aimed at addressing the ravages of colonialism in India? Because I think that the angles are different depending on the end goal.

    I've had some success getting people into Nordic freeform here on the east coast, so I can speak to that angle. One of the nice things about these semi-live games is that they are pretty accessible because they don't involve a lot of rules (lowers the barrier to entry for many people), and for me at least, they lessen the idea that I need to play a character, which lowers the social awkwardness for me. They also don't require much in the way of costuming, set, or prep.

    My tack on introducing freeform to folks is:

    a) explaining the style of game and how it differs from other roleplaying games people may have played.
    b) explaining why someone might want to play this style of game. (self-discovery, to feel strong emotions, etc etc)
    c) taking no for an answer. You want to encourage people to try new stuff, but if people aren't up for these sorts of games, they shouldn't be playing them.
    d) create a supportive atmosphere at the game session and afterward. (Read: always have a debrief)_
    e) choose games good for people new to the play style.

    These sorts of games have slowly found a following on my local convention scene. First time around, I put four games on the schedule and I think three of them ran, with a few people who showed up more than once. Second convention, five games, and four of them ran, including some repeaters from the first convention...over time you build up a base of people interested in this sort of gaming. This has translated over into similar games not run at conventions, like Mad About the Boy, or more recently, Limbo.

    Does this help at all? I guess what I'm saying is, "start doing it, and eventually people will come." It might take a moment, but these gamers are out there. I'd say there's a whole subset of gamers who grew up on pure adventure stuff but got hooked on the emotion who are ripe for the crossover. But I think there's also a whole set of non-gamers who are or might be interested in this sort of thing. In my experience, it has taken a while to find a crew of folks interested in this sort of thing, but they're out there, just waiting for you to bring the RL drama!

    \n I think it helps to find ways to generalize your experience. Probably very few people have dealt with being a half-Filipino kid growing up at a preppy school in Hawaii. But lots of people have faced some sort of pressure to assimilate with a superior societal force, so that's what Dog Eat Dog is about. If you make the issue larger, people can relate it more easily to the problems in their own life. At the same time, by making it a little more general, you have less of those immediate collisions you're talking about where your game is about a specific life issue somebody is currently dealing with.

    The other thing that helps is letting people provide their own distancing. Colonialism is a very stressful topic for many people, so they sometimes want a little space. Dog Eat Dog allows you to define your own statements about the setting. I've seen a lot of Dog Eat Dog games with more or less silly or fantastic setting elements, because then it's a little less threatening. But even if you have jetpacks or super powers, the core mechanics still work to provide pressure, so you still get the experience the game is meant to create.

    \n Just want to offer a different perspective on the idea that games about sensitive subjects are more uncomfortable to roleplay. In my experience, games that concern real life dilemmas are much easier and more comfortable to roleplay, because I don't have to worry about what life is like on mars and therefore I have an easier time improving about them. The absence of setting means that as a player I can focus on the core emotions of the story without distracting action or adventure. It reduces the awkwardness of playing a game and increases the fluidity of roleplay. And from the conversations I've had with others, I know I'm not alone on that front.

    I also want to make the point that it's possible to access the universal through the specific -- this is what most journalistic ledes are based on. I can't make you care about abstract issues of race, but if I introduce you to a half-Filipino kid growing up at a preppy school in Hawaii, then I can make you care about him because he's human, just like you are, and humans everywhere have stuff in common. I think this can cross over to gaming too, that it's possible to flip the paradigm @pigeon suggested above in some games. What if I don't make the character of this kid too specific, but give you a sense of some of the dynamics? Then you have to fill in the gaps, the specific stuff that makes this kid unique, with stuff from your own life, and you'll build a fish-out-of-water narrative that fills in and is resonant with your own personal engagement with the pressure to assimilate. It's just a different way of getting at the same core experience.

    \nThanks! The playtests so far have all generated interesting stories, and all sorts of high-emotion issues have come out. What's good about it I think is the fact that, apart from character gen, where players build the gameworld, set the problems the family has to deal with (in the form of themes, goals and character objectives etc.) and create the PCs as a group, the game could literally go anywhere. However, unless you\u2019re happy with bleed (and, correct me if I\u2019m wrong, but I get the impression that Nordic freeform games positively encourage it) there is obviously a risk that issues arise which might prove uncomfortable for one or more of the players for the reasons alluded to above. The question then is, how to deal with bleed, if/when it occurs?

    There are three methods that I\u2019m currently aware of: 1) having an L&V discussion pre-game- generally accepted in the UK nowadays as being more harmful than beneficial, because players are being asked to be upfront and open about the topics that make them uncomfortable; 2) the X/O card- useful because with it players can not only opt to have less of a particular topic (X) but also more of it (O), and 3) the palette in Microscope. In a game I facilitated at UK Games Expo, in which we mapped out and roleplayed the rise and fall of the British Solar Empire from the late 1890s to the 1970s, we had MUST HAVE integration and mixing of Human and non-Human races, and MUSTN\u2019T HAVE deliberate genocide. Finally, 4) allowing any player to call a halt to the game if they feel uncomfortable, without having to say why or about what. Interestingly, during a discussion on this topic after the Microscope game one of the players suggested simply scheduling lots of breaks as a way of defusing possible tension. Simple but effective!

    \n Part of it, as others have said, is make it clear what the game is about. Whether it's escapist fantasy, high level treatment of an issue or very focussed experiences on personal level of something players generally want to know thetype of game they are getting into.

    An important issue, in termsof design, is the rationale. Why is there some reason to deal with this topic in the next few hours? If you are looking at personal issues (some mentioend above were racism, drug abuse, job loss) in real life years will go by with all that happens is that the problems simmer away. Why is something interesting from a play experience point of view going to happen in the next few hours? Unless you, as designer, are look for a "slice-of-life" experience you need to think about what the motivation is for the players to activel explore these issues. they might just do it, as they are players, but without an in-game motivation the overall effect can be artificial.

    I'm not saying anything too deep here, clarity and motivation are very basic building blocks, but they are worth bearing in mind.
