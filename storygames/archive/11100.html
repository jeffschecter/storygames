\n Anyone ever heard of a game trying to articulate the subject matter of films like Frankenstein, The Addams Family, Pan's Labyrinth, etc? I'm thinking they all tie together because there are, sort of, two "factions" - monsters and humans - and rather than just straight-up conflict or violence, they fight AND dispute things and collaborate and stuff, basically wherein humans have rather complicated interactions with monsters or monstrous people.

    \n There's a few different approaches here.

    In My Life with Master, you play minions working for a dark master, but trying to establish loving connections from the people they prey on, in order to overcome the cycle and kill their master. It doesn't mirror Pan's Labrynth really, but definitely is THE Frankenstein game.

    In Heart of Ashes, a game I'm developing, you play children who've stumbled into a dark fairytale kingdom on the brink of civil war - humans and monsters and things which are both are drawing factions and preparing for a dangerous war. You are children who must solve the problems of adults - adults who would rather use your powers than heed your advice.

    In Don't Rest Your Head, you stumble into an anachronistic and nightmarish world constructed mostly out of inconsistencies and the strange. People and monsters co-exist, but there are layers and layers of pervasive fear, weirdness and mystery to wade through.

    \nHeart of Ashes sounds pretty sweet, Joe! I've considered playing MLWM, but never really got around to it.

    I'll put some of my thoughts together and maybe post them here, and see how things compare to stuff that's out there already, so I can get a better sense of what I want to do with this. In my tentative, bare-bones design idea, Pitchforks and Torches, you play either a villager or monster seeking to get the most out of your relationship with the monsters of the wild or the people of the village, respectively. You earn their trust, learn more about them, and maybe find a way to make a lasting peace between your species. Alternately, things could take a bad turn and you could decide to call the Mob down on the beasts, or sic your Company of Fiends on the blundering mortals. Doing so is a bold, relationship-ending move that severs any chance of peace between you.
    Thass what I have so far - it looks a little flat at the moment, but maybe it'll end up being relatively simple; I dunno.
