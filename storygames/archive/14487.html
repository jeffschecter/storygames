\n I may use some jargon, I will probably not use it in any kind of strict definitional way. Try not to fret about that too much. You can whisper to me about it if it really irks you.

The reward cycle: your character does stuff and you get some reward which more often than not improves the chance of the character doing that thing again in the future (XP, levels etc in many games), allows you to do other exciting things you couldn't otherwise do (e.g. FATE points), buy off penalties (e.g. Burning Wheel FPs, Warhammer), or chuck more resources at something (FATE, BW etc).

The idea is that by rewarding the player for what their character does you increase engagement in the game. People like rewards and will keep pressing that button to get more of them.

So here's the beef. I'm bored with rewards. I don't play the game to press the button and I strive for engagement without these particular paybacks. I think in our last game of BW, through following my beliefs and instincts, I amassed half the deck of Fate Points but had nothing much on which to spend them. I would have quite happily done these things without the bennie. The belief and instincts are lovely rules setting out freeform constraints on your characters actions making them much more believeable and real in the process.

So my reward is the direct engagement in the game not the secondary effect.

However when you play these games, some players engage with the reward system to a much greater degree and the focus of their game becomes gaming the system rather than engagement in the whatever is happening in the SIS or between the players (don't you love it when you invite another player to really mess with your character and they don't hold back?). To a certain extent, the game turns into Monopoly with added fiction rather than Storytelling under contraints.

How would you balance character improvement and other rewards against avoiding the reward treadmill?

    \n [As usual with forge stuff, the following is purely my understanding, interpretation and conjecture.]

    There was this discussion on the Forge a short while back (I can find the link if you want) about reward cycles and what I got away from it was that reward mechanics =/= reward cycle. Stuff like "get XP for this" or "get a bennie when your characacter does X" are reward mechanics, but they're not necessarily the same or even part of the "reward cycle".

    As with a lot of Forge jargon, I think "reward cycle" is an unfortunate name for a concept. It refers to "reward" in a very meta, big-picture sort of way, in the sense of "what's rewarding about this?". So when you say "playing the game is reward enough", yeah, that's kinda what this is about (at least the way I understand it).

    Here's the thing: when we play, something has to change - either something on my character sheet, or the character or the situation, or the world. In chess, some figures need to get off the board and someone has to win eventually. Imagine chess if you weren't allowed to take other people's figures...you'd just move them arund and nothing would happen - boring! So: change, change is why play itself is rewarding. The largest, longest circle of change that a roleplaying game contains, that's the reward cycle as I understand it.

    So in BW, getting Artha for following your beliefs is a reward mechanic, but the reward cycle is when you erase an old, completed Belief and write a new one. That's satisfying, as in, a circle has been completed, a wheel has turned, a cycle has passed, something was accomplished (at the longest time scale). They are related but not equal. Does that make sense to you?

    [edit: Also yeah, you can't make people have fun doing something simply by offering rewards. You can bribe them, sure, but giving XP for something won't make people automatically enjoy it.]

    \n Yeah, reward cycles have nothing in particular to do with character improvement - that's just a misconstruement caused by the usual misunderstanding of creative agenda; if you don't know where to look in the first place, then you're just trying to pin the label "reward cycle" on whatever happens to be at hand, which most easily are the mechanics of the game.

    Reward cycle is, however, a very simple thing to understand: you have some cause for being there to play the game, and the reward cycle is the process that gives you whatever it is. Some games give it in quick, small bursts, other games give it as a large climax, some fail to provide it at all (which is a problem). The "it" is creative agenda. Character improvement is only "it" if you're playing a long-term D&D campaign, basically - not a surprise that this is the platonic ideal to such a degree that many roleplayers will play like this even when the game they're playing is neither D&D nor an indefinitely long campaign. It's an inability to shift gears.

    As for the actual question, this has been addressed a lot in game design; people have been conflating character improvement with the purpose of play so hard ever since early D&D that it's an entirely real design issue that whatever game you make, it will get played with mixed groups where half of the people are there to play this game while the others are seeing whether the game might provide them with a new flavour of character-buildup exercise, and to hell with what the game text says. To some degree you can't really help this situation with system design, as people will only do things they want to do: many games have been made that don't play the character improvement dreadmill, but if the people you play with are only interested in that one particular kind of game, then those are not relevant to your problem, and you're stuck playing games that do that one thing, and maybe lie about doing something else on the side (trad design has a lot of this, Whitewolf style: make claims about being about something else while providing the treadmill to keep the actual rpg gamer demographic happy).

    If I absolutely had to play with CA-blind gamers who are stuck on character development, I would probably play their game and make the best of it. You can't redirect people with clever rules tricks, they need to want it to get something different. If the players are theoretically willing to try something else, then playing Polaris or Shadow of Yesterday might be worth a try: both of those games completely deconstruct the character development treadmill to such a degree that you can still hear the gnashing teeth of gamers who can't accept that at the end of either game your character is going to be removed from the game exactly because you were so good with building up that experience load. I've seen this experience miss the point completely on some more blinkered players, but with others it helps them broaden their horizons, so maybe, maybe.

    \nI'll address this because it's directly relevant to the thread's topic. Namely, I think that game mechanics are consistently misinterpreted due to the historical bias discussed above: anything that looks even vaguely like D&D will get interpreted as D&D at all levels, all the time. To get any sort of sense of the matter one simply has to distinguish entirely between character and player, and between D&D and the general idea of rewarding roleplaying games.

    Talking of Keys in The Shadow of Yesterday, they are not a reward mechanism in any sense except perhaps that players will often instinctually approach collecting xp as a reward because that's how they're wired from prior experience. The actual purpose for having Keys and experience points in the game is that they're an elaborate flagging system (for the player himself, other players and the Story Guide) and a gauge for dramatic pacing, respectively. I do not deny that many people will instinctively find collecting XP fun, but that's an incidental effect of prior experience, one that is perhaps utilized by the design in introducing the game (TSoY is often cited as a "lure" you can use to introduce dramatic narrativism to traditional gamers), but not in any way central to actually enjoying it long-term: insofar as TSoY as a system is concerned, you collect and spend experience points according to how fast you want to pace the game, and according to which facets of the fiction you want to highlight. Gaining xp poses no challenge whatsoever in this game, and rarely will a player who fully grogs the system be particularly excited about scoring yet another Advance; he's interested in why that Advance was gained and what it was spent on, but merely having more of them means extremely little to him. It is not uncommon for an experienced player to actually gain experience points slower as he realizes that the true benefit of claiming the points is in highlighting the events of the game rather than in getting as many as you can; activating your Keys lightly will simply mean that the act loses its significance.

    (Were the above not true I would personally have no means of distinguishing TSoY from D&D aside from incidental matters of mechanical aesthetics. There would be no moral difference between TSoY and a game like Vampire, say, with its lofty dramatic claims.)

    If I had to name a "reward mechanic" (itself as suspect concept when understood as a mechanic that rewards the player; presumably all mechanics do, for why have them otherwise) as such in TSoY, I would probably point at the Transcendence mechanic as a pretty easily isolated, one-time spotlight reward. It's mechanical in that the game flat out goes into a Transcendence when you arrive at a given game-state, and it's rewarding because it enforces a definite end to the individual character's story while allowing the player the authority to resolve the currently remaining issues in a manner that relaxes all dramatic tension at once. Aside from that most reward cycles of the game involve complex interactions of the Pool mechanics, crunch and judicious use of scene framing.

    \n A few thoughts.

    Human psychology likes "bennies" (rewards of some sort even if it's really completely abstract) - also being attracted to it (like the TV in pub as noted above).

Systems what give "non-permanent" currency (fate points etc.) are easier to avoid designing to not break than those that give "accruing adjustments" (character advancement).

    Posted By: Marshall BurnsI've been thinking about rewards in OD&D lately. You get gold, which gets you XP, which increases your level, which gets you access to some stuff that, for the longest time, made me go, "Huh? What's this junk about building and maintaining castles and shit? I thought this was about dungeons."

    Gradually I realized that the castles and retainers and everything were a scoring mechanism. You gain this stuff by playing well, and it's evidence of how well you played. Furthermore, it'scustomizable-- the real reward isn't getting castle stuff, but gettingoptions to build your castle, your way. You get to make a mark on the gameworld in your own image (and apparently segue into large-scale minis battles, but I'm not sure how often that actually happened). And you decide your goals on your own and struggle with the game to make them happen, which is always cool.

    Then I realized that AD&D3 was the same thing. Except, instead of options for a castle or whatever, you get more options to design your character.
    \nIt is kind of important to remember that in a lot of ways, OD&D was a bit of a culmination of trends and experiments in the miniatures wargaming hobby that pre-dated D&D. In this it parallels the way Vampire The Masquerade was a product of converging trends in the RPG hobby through the 1980s, post D&D as Fad Success at the turn of the 1970s/early 1980s.

    Marshall, in my experience, those big minis battles probably did not end up happening very often, at least not for folks who came into the hobby as kids during the Fad Success period (what Dave "grubman" Bezio named the Boxed Set Generation. People like me, for example). I strongly suspect those rules and that particular play arc was designed for the slightly earlier gamer generation that bridged the older minis gamer generation of the 1960s and the initial RPG as Type of Minis Wargame Generation of the initial D&D release period.

    In other words, the crossover generation that included the designers of D&D and early RPGs, who were also the recipients of the theorizing/philosophizing/experimenting of the 1960s Minis Gamer Generation and vanguard thinkers who were going even farther with some of the more niche concepts proposed by those Old Guard Minis Guys.

    I actually had a lengthier post written on the history of all that stuff. I'll just sum it up. Those kinds of improvement mechanics, first for armies and kingdoms and later for individual heroes, made sense in that early context (and still do in games played in the present day with similar intents).

    The current day context of most RPGs and the goals in playing them, however, are vastly different. I doubt, for eample, that those older generations of grognards would have ever conceptualized open-ended powering up as in D&D as it developed over time. Or even open-ended, continual play for that matter, since in the old gamer context, campaigns were meant to end at some point. If they didn't, how could you tell who'd won???

    Getting back to the original topic, however, I'd hazard that all rewards and reward cycles are essentially about creating incentives to continue to meet and play.

    At the core of that, the reward is Social Recognition. The other stuff, the bennies and whatever else are attempts to create some kind of marker for that social recognition that also encourages return play.

    If I'm on the right track with that bit, even a little, it might be worth starting from that perspective and naming off stuff that provides social recognition, without being goofy or embarassing for the participants.

    \n Reward mechanics are a topic I'm covering now in an online course on gamification. An interesting side I'm learning about is Self-Determination Theory as given particularly by Ed Deci and Richard Ryan.

    They find broad support for the idea that reward mechanics can sometimes de-motivate behaviors that are intrinsically rewarding. For example, in an experiment with kids drawing - many children would draw for fun given crayons or markers and paper. However, experiments found that after a period of being given rewards for drawing (even as simple as verbal praise), kids would draw less when those rewards were stopped. The course I'm taking recommended two books on the subject: Daniel H. Pink, "Drive - The Surprising Truth About What Motivates Us" and Scott Rigby and Richard Ryan, "Glued to Games".

    According to my class (I haven't read the books yet), reward mechanics can be less demotivating if they are intangible, unexpected, and/or come across as data on the performance rather than reward for the performance. I'm still pondering if this has implications for RPG design.

      \n Generally speaking, no.

      So, a bunch of anecdotal reasons as to why:

      They're often confusing, demotivating, and increase handling time. They break immersion. They sometimes cause jealousy between players. They're sometimes a pain-in-the-ass to manage as a GM.       I like having resources to spend, mechanical options to peruse, and constraints built into the fiction.

      I have not yet encountered a reward system that I liked. I think Dogs comes close.

      \n Mouse Guard has neat Reward Cycles!

      Some people complain that Beliefs & Goals are a way to force or reward players to play their characters a very specific way.

      They're not.

      You're rewarded when you play your Beliefs & Goals AND when you play against them. And you can change them! So you decide who you are and how you want to play.

      You\u2019re not rewarded for how your character behaves. You\u2019re rewarded for how you... the player... communicates. It\u2019s a feedback system.

      The game's resolution system is rigged against you. It's very difficult to succeed without consequences by yourself. You need help (reinforcing this is a co-op team based game first, not a solo exercise) and often you need the benefits you get from the game's Reward system.

      So if you are failing a lot, it's often a clear sign something is wrong.

      If at the end of the Player's Turn, you don't receive any rewards for your Beliefs & Goals (and other considerations) something is wrong.

      The Reward System makes these issues transparent and encourages communication between players.

      Players can then change their Beliefs & Goals or the GM can work around the existing player priorities. Either way, there are clear indicators to set and measure expectations. And the resolution system makes this very non-optional.

      \n I don't think of "reward cycle" as meaning "a cycle in which I get a reward at the end." I think of it as meaning, "a cycle of play that is rewarding." Follow that?

      I mean, if your play is clearly rewarding, then there was probably some kind of reward cycle there. You did something and interacted with the system somehow and as a result your play was clearly rewarding, generally in the form of feedback from /the other players/, not from the mechanics.       \n Funny thing, that about rewards, and how people in general, in my experience, tend to think that rewards are just about improving the character. In the French game Psychodrame (that uses an improved Dogs in the Vineyard mechanism), improving the characters is actually a drawback. Just think of that for a moment. It's a drawback.

      ...

      Now, think about rewards. There was one experiment (of many in this certain area of how rewards affect people) with children where they were supposed to draw paintings. They said to one group that they were given a reward in form of a poster if they finished a painting, but didn't tell the second group anything. What happened? The group that didn't get a reward did more drawings. (I'm telling from my mind, so bare with me if it's not 100 % accurate.)

      So what does this example show when it comes to attract players into acting in a certain pattern with XP? It takes the fun of it. They do it for the reward rather than wanting to do it.

      There are several kind of rewards. Mechanical ones, like XP and gear, are just one of them.

      In-game rewards, like getting improved status, building relations or getting information by actually doing something. I don't mean improving your status trait or roll against Library Use to get information. No, I mean that the GM is telling the players through the rewards that they are on the right track. "Cool, you went to the library. Here are the information you got". "It was nice of you to buy rounds on the tavern. It gives you some new friends for the evening and you talk about the adventures that you've been in". It's actually quiet like mechanical rewards, but more bound to what actually happens in the world.

      Appreciation rewards are really easy to implement and I'm surprised that it's not a general rule in all roleplaying games. It's were you acknowledge something the other players done. It can be as simple as saying "Cool, you ..." or "It was nice of you ...". When I took a course to be a soccer trainer for children, the teacher told the class that he had a goal to give every player in the team a compliment every session. That's a guideline that I tend to follow as a game master as well, and after a while, you do it without thinking of it. You laugh with the players, you acknowledge them by words or the best thing of all rewards: you make sure that whatever they made up, what ever action they did, it matters. If they invent a cousin on the spot, use that cousin. If they hatch a scheme, let them succeed, but with a possible twist. Then the game becomes fun in itself.
