    \n In Star Wars the world of the protagonist starts small and understandable. So much so, in fact, that he is bored with it and wants out of the farm. Not an unfamiliar situation to us living in a safe and regulated world. Then, in the manner of fantasy literature, the protagonist gets a revelation: the old guy he's known all his life is a wizard, and he's a wizard's son himself. And now he's going on an adventure to save a princess.

    This sort of revelation about the nature of the world is very central to classical high fantasy. Fantasy literature in general tends to make heavy use of an unknown setting that is put together piece by piece, challenging the assumptions of the audience. Star Wars does this repeatedly, starting with the jedi revelation, the war in the heavens, the total war as illustrated by the Death Star, the nature and position of the rebel alliance... the whole movie from start to finish is a journey of revelation about the nature of the world. The movie shows us a specific fantasy world with its own internal logic, and I think that's primarily what I like about it. It's not like laser swords are inherently so godly that putting them into any movie makes it cool; the laser swords in Star Wars are cool because they're a weapon of a more civilized era, and rather out of place in a setting where everybody is brandishing laser guns. So they're a symbol of this powerful old order of paladins, which is what makes them cool.

    I constantly return to the new movies as a comparison simply because they illustrate well how the same superficial symbols put together in a different way make for a movie that is not cool at all. Take the mysterious, fresh fantasy world sketched with minimal strokes out of the picture and you get a pretty routine action movie. It's sort of like the difference between The Lord of the Rings and the MERP sourcebooks: the former provide a journey of discovery, the latter break down the subject matter in clinical terms. Which is more interesting?

    \n I also want to throw in "Great villains," at least as far as the honest-to-god Empire goes: right out of the box, Darth Vader shows up looking ten feet tall and mean as hell, choking guys to death. Grand Moff Tarkin blows up an unarmed planet full of peaceful citizens just because he's annoyed at having to ask the same question twice. The officers have these awesome charcoal grey uniforms, their soldiers are faceless multitudes in shiny white armor, their ships look threatening and precise (compared to the sloppy ragtags of the Rebellion). You love to hate these guys.

    And, as much as stormtrooper marksmanship is derided, the Empire doesn't feel like a bunch of punks waiting to get slapped down. They're menacing. You believe that if the Rebel Alliance was any less noble, determined, and plucky, the entire galaxy would be crushed. When the Empire shows up, they earn every ominous note of the Imperial March.


    The prequels didn't do such a good job with this, perhaps intentionally. You could argue that the droid army wasn't meant to be a serious threat, so it's okay that they're...well, unimpressive and goofy (hey, at least it takes two wizard-ninjas with laser swords to kill a hundred of them, unlike the poor Stormtroopers who got annihilated by two dozen teddy bears). You could argue that the need for secrecy is why Darth Maul didn't do anything but follow around the good guys for a while before having his big swordfight (and losing). You could definitely argue that the Fetts were never anything more than nobodies in cool costumes.

    But the overall Empire as a villain, that's just unassailable. Great bad guys make for great heroics.

    The single biggest thing for me is the implied setting elements. We just don't have time to go into all of the questions. How does a lightsaber work? Who are Boba Fett's people? How far is it from Hoth to Bespin? What's the governmental structure of Alderaan? Why does Darth Vader have a robot body? What's Lobot's deal and why does he look like Phil Collins?

    \n

    What matters is that it looks like it has a reason. We explore the things in the movies that Lucas wanted to explore. Leia never misses a shot (OK, once, but she rectifies the issue immediately). Luke grows up. Han recognizes that he's a good person and that there's a reason to not be cynical. The weakest can help and strength doesn't make you brave. Even in a galaxy saturated by soulless technologies, everything \u2014 you, the rock, the ship \u2014 all are tied together in the fabric of the universe. Judge me by my size will you?

    \n

    Like, we know that the Millennium Falcon used to be Lando's ship, and that he lost it "fair and square" to Han. It might be nice to know how that happened, but other stuff is going on. Are there other Sarlaccs and Rancors and Asteroid Worms? It doesn't matter! The universe is full of monsters, one for every kind of situation.

    \n

    In this vastness, people (human or otherwise) can do good or evil, be in harmony with the Universe or cause pain, fight for what is right or fight because it feels good to have people be smaller than you.

    \n

    Also, I really like the message that, if you're going to fight, it makes you stronger to see your opponent's face, to be able to empathize with them, and to make sure the fight is between the two of you. Lightsaber battles are over something. Something big.

    \n

    Clumsy, random blasters make a mess and frankly don't work that great. Even on the vast scale of the Death Star, it's just vastly clumsy and random. A lightsaber is infinitely powerful in a very small radius.

    \n

    Warren, you'll note that Ben Burtt did sound design for all three movies, and John Williams did all the music. On the other hand, Ralph McQuarrie designed IV-VI and Doug Chiang did I-III. Doug Chiang is a pretty cool designer. I've got a non-Star Wars book of his that I'm pretty fond of. But Ralph McQuarrie's design is fucking flawless. Mr. Chiang was put in an impossible position, I feel. You'll also note the differences in writers and directors. Some people know how to make better art.

    Posted By: Joshua A.C. NewmanThe single biggest thing for me is the implied setting elements. We just don't havetimeto go into all of the questions.
    \nThis touches on the magic of the original trilogy for me. The movies are full of references to things that sound extraordinarily cool, but which are never explained: The Clone Wars; the Kessel Run; The Jedi Knights. By giving things cool names and then leaving all the details to the imagination, the films got an iron grip on my psyche as a kid. I spent so much time speculating and creating my own stories about that world that Star Wars remained central to my 'fantasy life' for years after the films had ended.

    This was something I really thought Marvel's Star Wars comics got right. While they did a lot of extrapolating about what we knew from the films, they never tried to define the 'ancient history' that had been alluded to in the movies. Rather than trivializing those mysteries, the comics just made them more alluring. (Or at least they did when I was 9. Not too sure how they hold up today.)
