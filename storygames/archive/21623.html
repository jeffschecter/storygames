\n I\u2019m thinking about using locations for your skills or traits rather than the bog-standard names.

So rather than using: Survival, Thievery, Melee, Ranged, Barter, Diplomacy, Sense Danger, Hide, Notice, and so on\u2014I go with locations, such as:

Hex \u2014 covers overland, wilderness skills
Square \u2014 dungeoneering skills
Tavern \u2014 drinking, social skills
Inn \u2014 hospitality, dealing with townsfolk
Bazaar (shop, market)\u2014 haggling, procurement and so on.
Brothel \u2014 carousing, hedonism
Library \u2014 research
Castle \u2014 chivalry and courtly matters
Armory \u2014 combat
Apothecary \u2014 alchemy and herblore
Stable \u2014 riding
Sanitarium \u2014 health, first aid, medicine
Tower \u2014 magic
Abbey (church, temple) \u2014 divine miracles, turning undead
Prison (jail, hideout) \u2014 criminal activities
Theatre \u2014 performance, musical
Workshop \u2014 craft, repair

You could narrow the focus down on certain skills. For instance:

Hex [Bog] [Mountain] [Forest] [Cove]

You\u2019d get a bonus or roll advantage if you\u2019re in this location.

Armory [Sword] [Bow] [Axe]

This gives you favored weapon and proficiency

Tower [Magic-Missile] [Sleep] [Fireball]

Your spells.

A fighter character might look like:

Stefan the Strong

Hex
Square
Tavern
Armory [Long Sword]
Castle
Stable

Anyways, something just a bit different.

    shimrod said:

    It feels off that Hex and Square switch from the in-game terminology of the rest to an out-of-game terminology. Is there any reason why they're not called Wilderness and Dungeon?

    \nYes, I agree\u2014but Hex and Square really equate well to mapping.

    A thought was to have Hex (wilderness) , Square (dungeon), Circle (town) as the three primary attributes.

    A poster on rpgnet mentioned: One is that you genuinely build it into a theme; not just that people are good in environments they are familiar with, but that those places have important ideas that leave marks upon those people and are strongly represented in the symbology of the culture in which the game takes place. The other is a sort of Lasers and Feelings thing... you've got Wild and Civilised, and those two eat each other.

    For the former, lets say that marking your character with environments, and that has some value beyond just "good when I'm here". If your character has high Desert, then yes, they're good in the sprawling sands, but it also gifts them Clear of Eye and Thought. The Village means you understand peasants and agriculture, but also that They Know Everyone. Swamp dwellers have probably wrestled their share of gators, but just like the waters of their homeland, they Get In Everywhere.

    So I guess... think about these environments as if they were a coat of arms, and think about what that would be said to represent. Then work from there for character, not just proficiency.

    In some ways, it\u2019s similar to John Harper\u2019s Wildlings.
