The curse of system development

gryphons_quill
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Play Advice
Hi all,

I'm new to the site. I had contributed to and play-tested for other systems in the past. I had several ideas for games and began to work on them. So, over the past several years I've been developing a multi-universal points-allocation RPG as well as a few other projects on the back burner. The main project has had some interesting hang-ups in the play-testing department. Being multi-universal I have had to test the system in different genre. But, playtesters are reluctant at best to try out a "homebrew" system. By the way, may I say I hate that term: "homebrew". I have had groups of playtesters vanish because of a new MMORPG that spread like a virus. An emotional entanglement that undermined another group. I've went to local cons to run one-shot adventures and had no one sign up to play. But the last group I will detail, really left me wondering about the nature of playtesting.

The final playtest group was involved in a super-powered campaign set in my system. I had managed to get five players together for the playtest, most of them with decades of experience. One player, Player A, had a character that acted violently beyond all reason. So much so that in-character official censure came down on his character. Player B wanted to set up a secret organization of "heroes" by recruiting villains from jail. He directed his efforts to establish an alter-ego with a front man, and when all his plans were coming to fruition, he accused me of forcing his hand and making him set up the organization. Player C dropped because when he deduced what a clue left by a villain meant, Player B said, "that's what I have dice for". Player D dropped shortly there after because role-playing was becoming impossible in the group. Player E tried to stick it out and compromise with Player A and Player B. Player A and Player B decided the game system was broken. I asked for feedback as to what the issues were so I could fix them.

Amazingly, there were no issues with the system, but rather with my GMing-style. They didn't like having consequences to character actions. Player D actually agreed to participate in a group discussion online, though Player C wanted nothing to do with the others at that point. Player D agreed that the system wasn't the issue, and said that the only fault I had was not screening my playtesters thouroughly. Players A and B continued on and each and every "system" issue revolved around PC interactions (with both NPCs and PCs), storyline plots, and consequences for overt actions. Player E kept quite as she didn't want to alienate any of her friends, but confidentially gave feedback that the system was "okay". Playtesters seem to fall over themselves to test established systems. Is it the lure of swag? Is it the feeling of legitimacy? Is it a bid at a "high paying job" with Palladium? I've tried having other GMs run scenarios in the system, and they can never get enough players to commit to the test. I have seen the topic about online playtesting discussed here, but that seems to have died out over a month ago.

In these MMORPG days, can system mechanics be tested in a game setting with actual role-play or should I just run gladiatorial exchanges to test viability of the system? If actual RPG is an option, what should I do to encourage playtesters to give valid system feedback and keep that seperate from group dynamics in and out of character? I am particpating in a few gamer meet-ups in the area to try and find new play-testers. So any advice at this point would be greatly appreciated.

Thanks.

    You cannot properly finish writing your game without playtesting first. You cannot properly playtest a game -- or just plain play -- without a having socially functional gaming group first.

    As Andrew said, you need "good gaming buddies you can rely on" to do the initial playtesting, so that when they tell you to your face, "uh, this sucks," you know they mean the game, not you as a human being, and that they will explain the specific details of what doesn't work so you can fix them.

    It sounds like you're having some struggles getting any kind of group together. "An emotional entanglement [had] undermined another group," you said -- you don't have to answer, but I presume that's shorthand for the standard "somebody is/was/wants to be dating the GM, and the resulting jealousy/break-up/infatuation, instead of being openly and honestly discussed among adults, was denied in public but constantly channelled into in-character bickering/fighting/favoritism." That's a classic sign of a group of people that should NOT be gaming with each other (or sleeping with each other until they grow up a little bit, for that matter). So does the array of strongly held, incompatible preferences among your playtest group: In Ron Edwards terminology, that group moved beyond "Creative Agenda incoherence" to "social disfunction."

    Have you read the "Fun Now Manifesto":

    1. Not everyone likes the same thing
    2. Play with people you like
    3. Play with rules you like
    4. Everyone is a player
    5. Talking is good
    6. Trust, not fear or power
    7. It's a game, not a marriage
    8. Fun stuff at least every 10 minutes
    9. Fix problems, don't endure them

    See http://bankuei.blogspot.com/2006/02/fun-now-manifesto.html for links to much more.

    Posted By: JDCorleyI agree that you don't need your game to be playtested by people you like. Ideally it should be playtested by total strangers who have no contact with you other than through the text that you send them to playtest....
    Yes, absolutely, this kind of "blind" playtest is ideal.

    BUT:

    The issue isn't "playtest with people that personally like the designer." It's "playtest with people that like EACH OTHER." If a group can't get along when they're playing their favorite game, they're not going to give you a useful playtest of your game.

    You'll get something like, "Well, we all made each other miserable, the way we normally do, plus we didn't understand all of the rules, so your game sucks." It's as useful as sending deaf people a demo tape of your band's new album: "Well, when we turned up the volume all the way like we normally do, we could feel a pleasant thumping through the floor -- is that what you wrote your song to do?"

In the magic system, spells can have benefits beyond buying a supernatural power directly, but they are not inherently cheaper, for game balance purposes.

    I don't think players should disrupt the game for rules arguments, but knowing that they will is one of those opportunities masquerading as problems. Burning Wheel handles it with Duel of Wits, Agon handles it with Orate, and I know of a D20 campaign that has instituted a "screw you, I'm awesome" house rule for when a PC attempts something heroic but is shot down by a rules technicality: you may spend one "screw you, I'm awesome" token per game session to take your action anyway. If you add a rule that gives that kind of power to players, you take the same power away from rules lawyers, and that is a very good thing.

    Similarly, players will sometimes think they have to hide information from a GM. There are numerous ways to try to combat this in the rules - With Great Power recommends an actual, physical "thought balloon" to hold above your head and voice your concerns - but I think the best approach is the one in Dogs in the Vineyard, which boils down to "set the example by sharing information with the players that the characters might not have." Treat your players a little bit like co-directors, and they will tend to step up to the responsibility for the story.

    The one thing I'm still grappling with is not being able to find gamers. I almost just want to say that gaming is like, say, modern dance - it's simply become too esoteric a thing to find lots of appreciators outside of major metropolitan areas. But really, I think that we can, and must, make lots of new RPG participants, but we may have to change our games - or at least answer some hard questions - to draw them in, and we may have to accept that they will not necessarily call themselves "gamers" when we're done.
