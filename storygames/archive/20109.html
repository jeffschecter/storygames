What are the components of social combat?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nLuzelli \n\n\n
\n

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n When putting together combat mechanics, or just in general mechanics, I try to isolate the various components of tasks and then look for ways to mechanize them (assign to a RNG outcome, or a core stat, etc). For example, some common and key components of combat are: line of sight, cover, movement, positioning, accuracy and precision. From what I've seen, games tend to pick and choose out of lists like this, deciding how detailed an experience they want to give ("Are we going full simulationist, more gamey, or straight narrative?"). Now, I find it very easy to break down combat actions into their subsequent components. I can even break down other kinds of physical activity with relative ease. Maybe its cause of years of video gaming or maybe its cause these are primarily physical/kinetic tasks so it's easier to break them down into essential pieces. However, I have a much tougher time deconstructing social interactions.

Are there any similar common and key components to Social combat? What quantifiable components of social combat can a designer mess with?

In an attempt to help identify some of the parameters of social combat, I've made a small list of what I consider key aspects to social interaction. This list is nowhere near complete, and it's full of ideas and terms that I've defined a certain way (sometimes not the way that most people would use the term).\n
\n
Sympathy/Empathy with the Target
\n
How well do you know the person OR how well can you read the person. If you are experienced at noticing the minutia in body language and speech patterns, it is easier to understand the mental/emotional state of the subject you are engaged with.
\n
Ability to express oneself
\n
The very basic ability to communicate your thoughts and project your intentions to a target. The more "expressive" you can be, the more succinctly you can communicate an idea or intention.
\n
Colloquial Etiquette/Expression
\n
Expressing yourself in a manner that is expected by and consistent with your environment. Knowing the appropriate behavior for your environment. The term colloquial refers to the social environment you find yourself in. If your environment is the streets, its knowing the lexicon to say that you have a key of fishscales ready to go and knowing not to mention any names on the phone. Likewise, if you're environment is an upper class social club, this is your understanding of how to make a witty comment that is just sharp enough to barb your opponent without being too overbearing.
\n
Ability to shield your own tells
\n
The conscious or unconscious ability to guard yourself from being read too closely. Or, preventing others from seeing the intention behind your words/behavior too clearly. Essentially, presenting what you want to present while hiding what you want to hide.
\n
\nMy intention was to create a lexicon for myself that I can build off and say, "I'll target this aspect of social combat and work out ways to mechanize it." What other aspects of social interaction am I missing? What aspects listed deserve their own section or should not be on that list?

    \n Hmmm thinking about mechanization, I'd separate the social interaction in

    Emotion: The higher (or more intense), the more difficult to hide. It can reveal the true intentions of a person and/or make her prone to mistakes or rushing to conclussions.
    Empathy: The ability to perceive other people's emotions and reflect it to gain their trust
    Pure emotion can be used instead of empathy if you're being completely honest about your intentions. I'm taking empathy here as a conscious effort to control the emotional output. Also, an actor could use a "performance" or "acting" skill instead of this, if it's higher (that would mean he has an actual method for acting)
    Reason/Logic: the ability to find, relate and arrange one or several convincing arguments, either if they are true or not, or if the character actually believes them himself. It can also be used to see through other people's bullshit or even to invent excuses to yourself for anything. A failure in that would lead to rising your Emotion (Guilt) ranking, for example. Actually, this same stat could be used as a stress threshold: if any emotion goes higher than the Reason of the character, he stops thinking and starts acting.

    As stated above, other learned skills could be used instead of empathy and reason/logic, if they are higher (more trained)

    Dunno if anyone can see through my descrption, but I've thought this design in terms of game possibilities. I'd recommend that way of thiking for any design you want to try. Check every variable and think "What kind of game situations may arise if this value is high or low?" "What other variables are similar and can be bring together into a single variable with a wider descriptor?" "Which ones generate enough interesting complexity to be separated into new ones?" In the case above, it perhaps a bit minimalistic but I think it can produce several different interesting results.

    \n The main problem is that modeling trust, rapport, friendship and love IS a sociopathic behavior. It's logical to go sociopathic on the system once it's established that way, because players are confronted to a game about resource management, where trust, rapport, love and friendship have become resources that can be controlled by whoever exhibits a predetermined behavior. It would be best if no dice rolling was involved on roleplaying characters, but there will always be GMs and players with too poor social skills that would prefer to roll the dice to avoid uncomfortable talking and acting. And we can't forget about truly uncomfortable scenes that are best avoided or resumed into a dice roll. Talking about never-ending cycles...

    Thinking about that and other things, let me re-state a mechanic:

    Whenever a social interaction scene implicates an exchange (of resources, services, status, information, conditions, demands, etc) that can be solved inmediatly or that they would like to see develop because it could add an interesting twist to the story, players should use the situation to define the Emotion variable for each character. Let's take a d20 system as an example, scale it up later to the system of your choice.

    Emotion should start at 5 (1/4 of the highest dice value of the system) and get modified according to the situation (+1d6 if the argument has been going on for a while without solution, if there has been previous hostilities, etc). We will use INT score as Reason/logic threshold. Once emotion goes higher that that score, the emotion it's evident for other people, though you may want to apply WIS modifier of the observers if it's negative: for those people, detecting emotions on others would be difficult anyway.

    If Emotion hasn't gone beyond the INT score, to find out what that emotion is, players make Empathy rolls against reason logic rolls of the oponent. These rolls could use any skill or stat the palyer wants. Sense motive, insight, perception, spot, wisdom, investigation, you name it.

    To either control and/or hide the own emotions as well as for interacting with others you can use any skill for the Reason/Logic roll: Diplomacy, intimidate, persuasion, leadership, concentration, Intelligence, etiquette, seduction, etc. This roll will either hide emotions, or make them go up or down. The actual score and the emotion won't be know by anyone but the GM (if it's an NPC) and the player (if it's his character, until it goes beyond the logic/reason threshold. Then the emotion and it's intensity is clear for everyone as the character loses it's temper.

    You can try to influentiate another character's emotion even if you don't know what it is. It's like the real world: you never know what someone is thinking until they state it clearly, go mad or break up in tears. Perhaps you will never know, even when you tried so hard to make them react.

    \n Luzelli, I think your initial 4 categories and some of the subsequent suggestions cover varying levels of specificity. "Hiding your tells" could be a subset of "expressing yourself", for example. Or not, if you have a narrower meaning in mind for the latter. Anyway, without worrying about which of these items belong inside which other items, here's my list of things you might want to do in a social interaction:
    - create an impression of value -- why they want what you're offering
    - create an impression of danger -- why they fear what you're threatening
    - establish credibility -- prove that whatever you're tempting them with is real
    - establish trust -- make them believe you'll follow through on your end
    - fix prices -- establish how much each side's part is worth to the other side
    - convincingly relay true info
    - convincingly relay false info
    - provide evidence
    - create urgency -- get them to commit before gathering other info
    - control the focus of the conversation -- keep it about what you want it to be about
    - distract
    - obfuscate
    - dismiss
    - make them look good for whoever else is there, if they do what you want
    - make them look good for whoever else is there, period
    - make them look bad for whoever else is there, if they don't do what you want
    - make them look bad for whoever else is there, period
    - diffuse a grudge or animosity
    - calm them so they deal with you rationally
    - shock or alarm them so they deal with you confusedly
    - fluster them so they do something that's bad for them
    - get them to like you -- bring a positive disposition into future dealings
    - get them to value your favor -- act for, not against, your interests in the future
    - set it in stone -- make it harder to back out of an arrangement
    - put it on the books -- create policy that governs others
    - notice when they lie
    - learn their values
    - learn their goals and priorities
    - learn their secrets -- what don't they want generally known?
    - establish confidentiality -- make them believe you'll safeguard their secrets
    - act covertly -- avoid giving away their secrets
    - manipulation -- get them to behave as you desire, for whatever purpose
    - evoke in them a certain emotion
    - mesmerize them
    - get them aroused
    - get them to fall in love
    - get them to discard protection and leverage -- e.g. in a room alone
    - get them to like your side -- party, faction, nation, whoever you represent
    - get them to hate your enemies/competitors
    - create the desired image of yourself -- e.g. you may want to seem incompetent
    - learn all they know
    - crush their spirit
    - coerce
    - bribe
    - drug
    - alter your appearance
    - control your body language
    - control your facial expressions
    - control your voice -- pitch, timber, accent, cadence, diction, everything
    - impersonate -- locale, role, rank, specific person

    For my inchoate game Blackmailed Kingdoms, I want to be able to model pretty complex negotiations, where, for example, a player character who's a rich ambassador of a powerful nation can trade a promise of eventual aid to a local militia leader for immediate help in a fight, but only with the right amount of cash for weapons as a sweetener, plus the right amount of public deference to boost the leader's standing with his own people. \n

    \n Most social interaction is about establishing or maintaining relationships - the best place I've scene this appear is in Chuubo's handing of pastoral XP actions. This happens all the time, you don't need to mechanize achieving it, so much as highlight it. The other half of this is the judgement side of "what relationship is this" - and that is a very open area. Years ago I designed an entire game built on structure of convincing other players that your character had become friends, enemies, or lovers with another specific character.

    Another big chunk of social interaction involves communicating information. We have enough trouble with this during RPG play we tend to downplay this in the fiction. Certainly there are some alternatives to asking if anyone in the party speaks Gnomish.

    Some social interaction involves inflicting emotional harm - I think of this as emotional combat. This is very common in drama. It represents when people are fighting, struggling for dominance, generating emotional blackmail, falling in or struggling to escape destructive or abusive relationships. I happen to like framing this in D&D combat style, with emotional armor and hit points and various nasty maneuvers. After all, emotional violence is still violence.

    Even less social interaction involves influencing or changing beliefs and behaviors. In reality these often involve leveraging relationships or harm to encourage or discourage changes in the other person. This can also involve using the tools of confidence, cognitive tricks to exploit biases, and even sometimes the tools of rationality. Lies are harder because confidence and consistency may be more difficult for the persuader, but just because something is true or you believe it to be true, doesn't make the process of persuasion much easier. Beliefs versus behaviors is an important distinction: one interrogator could be trying to get a suspect to believe they should tell the truth, while a different interrogator could be trying to get a suspect to slip up and mention something they would habitually keep secret.

    There is also the halo of non-interactive social elements: dressing to impress, cold reading, hiding tells - most of which involve either the complexities of culture or perception. There are also group dynamics, which largely build on maintaining relationships and communicating information, but also involve the way groups evolve (and occasionally develop) social tools, shared experiences, unspoken assumptions, and implicit hierarchies. If you extrapolate that even further you enter the realm of politics and culture, as well as social formalisms like laws.

    - Mendel

    \n Well certainly it depends on scope and style. For Potemkin's fast-paced OSR experience a simple zero-sum game is probably totally sufficient. For a more detailed "simulation" experience a more complex mechanic is required, and this makes the differences between zero-sum games and multivariant spaces painfully evident - which is why we've all seen NPC dialog-response mechanics that seemed woefully... um... mechanical. We end up ditching them and improvising.

    One answer might be to borrow narrativist "oracle" techniques (which are similar in many ways to surrealist literary techniques) and mount them on top of such a mechanic. Written flexibly and evocatively, they might cover the joints in the mechanic with narrative prompts which spur the interpretive powers of the person narrating.

    I would love to think that one could achieve a "one size fits all situations" model by keeping the actionable terms vague but suggestive. Maybe it's possible. But my vocabulary is pretty large and I haven't hit on it yet, despite lots of trying.

    In DayTrippers I included a mechanic I call a "Complex Dialog Action" which simulates any kind of extended verbal interaction in which multiple stakes are at risk (arguing a legal case, for instance). The variables used for both parties' rolls depend on the characters themselves; could be a skill, could be their innate CHARM stat. For each stake, rolls are modified and compared, and the result will usually be a bipartite "YES AND", "NO BUT", etc. Then somebody gets a -1 on their next stake roll. This seemed sufficient to me.

    But in playtesting I found this to be a place people sometimes get stuck. Interpreting the "BUTs" and "ANDs" of an imagined conversation gives people pause in a way that interpreting combat mishaps doesn't. Perhaps it's because we haven't HEARD the actual WORDS that apparently caused the secondary result, and it's not something you can run back and picture in your mind like a combat scene in this here room on the map with these dimensions and these other contents... so it's hard to think of what the secondary result might reasonably be. And that's why the GM Guide (wip) will break out Complex Dialog into a separate table of its own, adding short lists of suggestive phrases to help contextualize the results. It's less elegant, but hopefully more useful.

    \n When I think of social "conflicts" in games, it's usually one of these situations:

    1. A PC is making some kind of deal with an NPC. This is a negotiation of costs vs. benefits for both sides, usually. For example, "Hey, we want into the castle. Let us in and we'll give you some money and promise not to tell your boss that you were asleep on your watch."

    This doesn't have to be sociopathic. Both sides can work to find the best deal for everyone and not try to screw the other guy over.


    2. A PC is tricking or fooling an NPC. This is usually a one-sided thing, but it can look like a negotiation (but the NPC who thinks he gains from the trade doesn't actually get that thing). For example, "I'm a noble. Let me into the castle, and you'll have my favor."

    This is fairly sociopathic, in the sense I've been using the word. The PC is using the NPC, and might even bring the NPC to harm, probably without much remorse.


    3. A PC is trying to sway an NPC to prevent a violent conflict. Here, the NPC holds most of the cards and it's about the PC showing her true colors so the NPC has a change of heart. For example, "Whoa whoa, slow down there, pointy-spear. I'm not here to hurt anyone. I just wanted to sneak into the king's ball so that I could get a message to the princess. I swear I'm not up to no good--well, not the kind of 'no good' you think. Once my message is delivered, I'm out of there. I swear by the god Brom."

    This doesn't have to be sociopathic, either, if the PC has truly good motives. This is about clearing up misunderstandings, de-escalating conflict, and other good things. If the PC has terrible motives and is lying about being a good dude, then it's just -pathic con artistry.

      \n One thing that comes to mind is that there exist certain models and conceptual classifications of how people engage in conflicts (kind of like the Myers-Briggs stuff, but for people's tactics in social conflicts rather than basic "personality").

      I've read a few of these and taken tests (which tell you what "kind" you are). They are obviously not objectively and quantitatively verifiable, but may be worth investigating for stuff to represent in your game.

      For example, one model separated "conflict styles" into four quadrants. One included people who prioritize maintaining a positive relationship with their opponent over winning the actual argument. While that sounds like a losing strategy, it may not necessarily be (particularly if it allows them to get more of what they want in the long-term, appeal to sympathy, or use other similar tactics). For instance, you might say, "Hey, why are we arguing? This is all out of hand. Why don't we cool down for a minute and go over here, I'll buy you a drink?" And perhaps later you'll magically find that your opponent is no longer as hard-headed as they were at the beginning, despite nothing about your actual disagreement having changed.

      Another consisted of people who approach conflict very directly and head-on: strong emotional tactics, bullying, very direct stuff. A third had to do with people who prefer to appeal to reason and persuasion, and remain relatively distant emotionally. There were also categories for indirect approaches (these have to do with appeals to tribal identity, winning the sympathy of the other party by telling stories demonstrating shared values or background, analogy/metaphor, and so on - politician stuff definitely fits in here, I'd say).

      There was also a category of people who would dynamically switch strategies whenever they saw one wasn't working. They weren't tied to any particular approach, only winning. A bit scary, but also very effective!

      Some interesting takeaways were that if you didn't try to mirror your opponent's strategy/approach to at least some extent, you were likely to run into trouble. For instance, in a culture where direct confrontation is the way people settle disputes, someone who attempts to placate their opposition and deal with the problem indirectly, instead of facing it head on, might anger their "opponent", who feels that he or she doesn't respect them enough to meet them halfway.

      \n Actually I do not consider ethical persuasion at all "sociopathic"...why should logic/reason automatically win out over values? Why privilege the mind or even the heart over the soul?

      Anyway, here's how I do it:

      First, you give up your weaknesses before you talk about your strengths. Only someone's ally would do that!

      Second, acknowledge points of agreement by saying WHY we are in agreement on them. It is because we value the same things.

      Third, openly make clear promises and explain why you're doing them and why you'll follow through no matter what the other party/parties decide. (Then do - you're able to do this because you're identifying areas where you're in agreement.)

      Fourth, acknowledge and preserve the strength in the other side's position and explain why it is worthy. Or, if you want to propose a different route, do so in a way that preserves both the strength and the value the strength is defending. ("Rather than have a trial, what about if you made a statement to the court; this would mean the court could hear what you're feeling about the situation and draw on your experience in making future decisions." is something I do a lot in my current (extremely invasive and personal) job.)

      Cultural aspects definitely play a role, but sometimes it can be helpful to be the guy who will break the rules. "Finally someone who will give it to me straight, even if he is a vulgar jerk", or "Of course this guy will go the extra mile for me - he clearly doesn't have any boundaries at all!"
