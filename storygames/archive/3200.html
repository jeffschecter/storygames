    \n I'll start (and will edit this post later when I think of more).

    I would totally love to see a rules-light game based on a quasi-historical/mythical, or "strongly inspired by historical" (see Conspiracy of Shadows) Ancient India. I have no idea what the game would be like, what it would be about, or what sorts of rules it would have (Neel Krishnamurthy gave a cool lecture on Mythic Indian Bow Weapons, so probably awesome bow combat stunts), but I'd love to see one nonetheless.

    I want a game about dimension-hunting savage jungle-dwellers, who set wrongs right.

    I want a game like the Guild Wars expansion (forgot the name, only have seen the box), which is basically "Play awesome minorities/black people", without necessarily taking it back to Historical African Roleplaying (which is fun, too- I played a Nyambe mini-campaign). I'm thinking maybe an intergalactic anime-themed far future African or South American Space Empire, that plays off of the coolness and trappings of cultures, 'done right'. Or a far future society where a civilization based in Old Mali rises from the ashes and takes over the galaxy.

    Elements:
    I'd love to see a new take on martial arts/kung fu. Anything. I'm looking to see how new minds creatively tackle cinematic kung-fu fighting.

    More relationship mechanics for love between characters in an otherwise standard game (ie: A rules subsystem that you could apply to D&D, Savage Worlds or TSOY).

    More ways for GM and players to reward each other: We've seen fan mail and gift dice. I'm looking for even more ideas (I'm thinking adopting AP from Beast Hunters might be a key to a new form of ruley-based audience feedback).

    -Andy

    \n Hmm, so I'm gonna restrict this to games that I actually don't plan to write myself.

    1) A game about everyday life, but better. Like, say, Gilmore Girls.
    2) More games about specialized professions, like Jason Morningstar's hospital game.
    3) A game in which you play a real, sentient non-human species, like dolphins or gorillas.
    4) Games that don't try to recreate pre-existing literary or cinematic genres, that aim to create new genres that are tailor made for roleplaying games, based on what RPGs can do better than other mediums.

    EDIT: To add, 5) Games in which you play normal people from another culture or time period, without, like, being ninjas or princelings or supernatural creatures or other lasersharked things.

      \n

      2. \n

    3. more games that share core mechanics with other games and add special mechanics for the unique aspects of the setting, characters and situations that game was specifically designed for.
      4. \n
    4. cultural or historical "look and feel" games, designed to evoke a setting rather than describe it exactly.

    \n I'd like an apocalyptic game. Not a post-apocalyptic game, where civilization has fallen and there's either Gamma World's mutant badgers or Paranoia's Alpha Complex or Twilight 2000's slog across an irradiated Europe. I want something now, where you're having a nice cup of tea and civilization starts going downhill. It's less Jeremiah and more Jericho. Something like what I think GURPS Y2K is.

    The important thing about the game is it's primarily about social conflict, not about physical conflict. If it's a zombie game, the zombies are in the background -- the real meat of the story is the relationships between the survivors and the mechanics of the game reinforce this. (Which is why All Flesh Must Be Eaten isn't my perfect candidate to run a Zombie Apocalypse game.)

    \n Elfworld, or some game that addresses the idea of how to play an evil mastermind. Give me power, I'll deal with the consequence (or not!)
    \n

    \n
    \nI'd like a game of fantasy science, pulp-science taken all the way to transhuman potential, with every player bending the laws of physics as people know to do...almost anything.
    \n
    \nAlso, a weird Phillip K. Dick conspiracy game, where conspiracies are active entities themselves, attempting to take over the world. PCs are forced to make a choice: dissolve into their conspiracies, where they are active important players in shaping the direction of the conspiracy, or they can turn their back on it, and kill the conspiracy before it becomes real.
