    Ok, what he says is true in general, but is also a failure of metaphor. Tools have a quality - how well they're made - but they also have a type - what they're designed to do. He is pretty clearly confusing the two.

    To illustrate this divide, by his logic, a Master Craftsman should be able to drive a nail with a screwdriver better than I could with a hammer. It's not the tool, after all.

    Now, the problem here is that he has very clearly reached _some_ level of understanding. To hit upon the idea that All tools are just tools and the final effort is an expression of the wielder is one of those things that hits like a flash of insight when it comes to you and, like most layered thinking, seems like the truth that other people just don't understand for a while.

    I wouldn't sweat his zealotry, or even his implicit understanding that he is, of course, one of the virtuosos. He's taken a step, and he's looking around at the thousands of crappy tools around him, and can't tell the difference between the ones that are just there and the ones that will really help him, and that discovery can't be forced, but in time, he may see that the tools are the craftsman partner, not his bitch.

    Or maybe he won't. Wouldn't be the first time.

    -Rob D.

    Nah. You should cockpunch him.

    He has a point in that almost all systems like D&D's Challenge Rating system are going to have some flaws and places where it breaks down to a certain extent. This is especially true in a game that has a complex combat resolution system like D&D. There are just too many variables to keep up with to get the CR perfect for all possible encounters.

    However, he's also an idiot if he thinks a tool is useless just because it has limitations. It's there to help as a guideline and a "true master" would find a way to use the system to help himself. The judge of the tool's usefulness isn't whether or not it will make a newbie GM perform to the level of a GM with vast experience. It's whether it can make the game prep easier and more efficient for both the newbie GM and the GM with vast experience. As a GM with quite a bit of D&D experience, the CR system and those like it help. They don't replace personal judgement calls but they sure do aid them.

    One of these days, I will be a True Master and able to do the Crane Kick at the table. Nothing will stop me.

    Like he says, mechanics are tools, or set of tools combined into a package called A Game. Some tools make some jobs easier, more reliable, more predictable or something else. Some tools look like a hammer, but then you find out that they're made out of rubber. There are plenty of good tools and bad tools, though there aren't tools for every possible job. At least, not yet.

    Tools are also set procedures that work in certain ways. Humans are not; they are flexible and adaptable, in both good and bad sense of the words.

    So, yes - human actions produce a more nuanced performance and wider results, both worse and better than tools. There are no True Masters; there probably are committed craftsmen with limited range of specialties. If want new furniture, tools are like Ikea - you can buy a full package or mix and match for pre-made items, which are delivered to you complete with instructions on how to set things up, and necessary equipment. The human equivlaent, on the other hand, goes to buy wood and takes them to his craft shop, where he meticuously crafts a chair or something. It takes longer and it's harder, but the result could or could not be exactly what he wants, depending on his craftmanship. Or he could just go, get a log, and call it a chair.

    And so on. If it's a System Doesen't Matter -thing, then it's that only in scope of actual mechanics.
