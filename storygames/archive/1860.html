Useful Inclusions in a Gaming Text

MikeRM
November 2006 edited November 2006 in Play Advice
I was reading the "How shall we criticize?" thread and thought, well, maybe one place to start would be: What's missing from the text? What's not there that we expect to be there or that usefully could be there?

And to turn that round and make it positive, what useful things have we seen in existing game texts that could be profitably included in future ones?

Some initial thoughts, top of head:

* Sample adventure
* Sample characters (in the form of filled-in character sheets)
* Random generation tables
* GM advice
* Player advice
* Social contract advice
* Narration advice
* Bibliography
* "If you like this game, you may like..."
* Situation generators like Dogs town generation or FLFS Situation Engineering

Obviously, not everything belongs in every text. But what can be useful?

    Very serious.

    edit: That is to say, it's useful to me to pay meticulous attention to any and all aspects of the physical situation of the game. If I were running, say, a Neverwhere game, I would find a sort of threadbare black t-shirt to wear, and so forth. I like to prepare character sheets beforhand - this is a great way to get your craft on. A bowl of musty old potpourri for Wuthering Heights, sweet grape juice and flaky pastries for Agon...

    Some of this stuff goes in way under the level of conscious observation unless you look for it, but it all makes a difference. Little sensual touches are great ways to subtly move people onto the same wavelength.

  • Levi Kornelsen

    I'd like the game to discuss how....

    Someone pitches the game to the group, hoping to convince them to play.

    Someone teaches the background in the game, trying to get everyone "on the same page".

    Someone creates a character.

    Someone plays a character, speaking about or for that character.

    Someone plays a character, speaking AS that character.

    Someone creates or modifies a setting or situation.

    Someone describes a scene.

    Someone describes actions, hears responses, and describes more actions.

    Someone hears actions, describes situational responses, listens again.

    Someone uses a rule or group of rules to resolve a conflict.

    Someone spurs on or stalls the action of the game, deliberately or by mistake.

    Someone closes out a session.

    Someone opens a session.

    Someone hosts the game.

    Somone brings the snacks.

    And more.
