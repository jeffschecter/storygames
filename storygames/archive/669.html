    The way I see it:

    Pushing is taking an action that you expect will engage other players by inducing them to react, because you're making inaction have in-game consequences that you believe they will not like.

    Pulling is taking an action that you expect will engage other players by inducing them to act, because you're making inaction boring compared to moving in and picking up the plot threads you're offering.

    Sometimes people will react to something that you intended as a push, as if it were a pull, and vice versa. Sometimes people will ignore a push or a pull. Neither of these conditions matter to whether it's a push or pull or something else, because they happen AFTER the push or pull is made.

    A push or a pull is a play made with the expectation that it will engage the interest of one or more of the other participants. It's the play, PLUS the expectation. Is the expectation a 'feeling'? Maybe. It can be instinctive or reasoned, well-considered or impulsive, and may not even be entirely conscious, but it has to be there.

    This means that you can't ALWAYS tell whether a particular bit of play was a push or a pull without talking to the people involved, but sometimes it's obvious.

    I didn't participate in that other thread, but I read through it and think somewhere along the line the definitions of Push/Pull became a lot more muddy, mostly because of a heavy reliance on a "how you feel" component, which results in fuzzy definitions. I have a deliberately more focussed definition that is "what you do" only, which may make it more appealing to you but might turn off a number of the other people discussing Push/Pull.

    I think discussions of exact ontext and the intentions or unspoken desires of the players involved is a red herring, even if this may actually occur in play. I see Push and Pull as actual techniques of roleplaying identified by analogy to "soft" and "hard" martial art techniques. Push is the karate of roleplaying, Pull is the aikido.

    The two techniques are about how you, the player, get another player to act in some way. How the other player reacts is unimportant; you are only concerned with what the player reacts to. They are ways of focusing play on specific topics. Push focuses play on a topic by confrontation; Pull focuses by offering a tempting hook.

    Examples from a game of Burning Wheel in the Agone setting I played at Dundracon. The supposed focus of play is a nobleman's annual census and inventory of his territory. One player has a satyr character that is descrbed as sex-obsessed. During play, he repeatedly uses magic to seduce the female characters, grabs them and attempts to physically carry them off, and so on. That's Push: he's focusing play on the satyr and his sex-obsession by confronting the other players with actions they can't ignore. The GM at one point attacks the party with spiders; that's another Push. When we selected characters, I chose one nobleman who was described as the Duke's somewhat frivolous cousin; the GM made a slight suggestion to the Duke's player that he he keeps his cousin close to the court because he has no way of knowing if the cousin is just self-absorbed or is plotting against him. That's Pull, since there was no requirement for either of us to act on that suggestion, even though it was an open suggestiona and not subtle. I opted to take that suggestion and twist it a little: the cousin, who was ten years older than the Duke, swore an oath to the Duke's father to protect the Duke, but his own dislike of the young Duke's personality and of getting personally involved in anything makes him choose to carry out his oath as behind-the-scenes manipulation and stealthy behavior. So, I deliberately act many times in an ambiguous manner, staying behind in camp at first as a scouting expedition is sent out, then suddenly following when the Duke decides to join the scouting, and so on. That's Pull; I'm not directly confronting anyone, but I am sublty suggesting interpretations of my character's behavior that could provoke action.

    To take your Major Victory example: since it's a demo, you are trying to provoke any kind of engagement with the system at all, especially with Major Victory. Your opening statement is just a characterization of Major Victory inviting the other player to form an opinion and act on it, but isn't a direct confrontation. That's Pull, even if it seems a little pushy (Pull/Push is probably more a matter of degree, anyways, rather than a binary toggle.) Your second statememt is an actual confrontational action, attempting to humiliate the other character: that's Push.

    Make sense?
