TonyLB
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Story Games

Okay guys ... everything I learned about decentralized, enthusiasm-based, empowered organizing came from the Go Play thread. So I blame you for sticking this idea in my head, and now I insist that you folks help me work out how to present it.

Over on the Forge they're having a thread about how to improve the booth. People are very eager to find one or two leaders (characterized by their good ideas) and to organize behind those leaders. People want to be followers. They're dripping with enthusiasm to be followers.

I'm just basically not cool with that, and I'm doing a crappy job of explaining why. It's not that it wouldn't work: I'm sure it would work quite nicely. It's that I think we could do so much more if everyone were creating their own cool ideas and executing them.

Picking the best idea and lining up behind it ... that makes sense in a world where it takes the concerted, full-time effort of many people in order to bring any idea to fruition. But the Forge generally (making games all on our own) and Go-Play more recently (unconnected efforts that all came to a wonderful head) show that we don't live in that world. We live in a world where we are each empowered to create tools for our community, and just to hand them out.

The old "get behind the leader" model is outdated. But it's also seductive, because you get to be lazy and call it virtue. You're not being a slacker, you're being a team player. You're showing servant-leadership, and proactive synergy.

Blood-pressure ... rising. Help me Story Games! Help me figure out how to explain this issue (and to think about it) in a way that helps me to inspire people with how cool they can be, without harping on how cool they're not being at the moment.

    Posted By: Gaerik

    You essentially want people to participate in a large brainstorming activity where everyone brings their ideas to the table. They even go start acting on these ideas.

    No, actually. That sounds like a really cool idea, but it's not what I'm aiming for.

    I'm aiming, in fact, to systematically cut out the brainstorming-before-acting phase of things. I'm saying let's not make everything a group project. Let's make everything an individual project, with an awareness that it needs hooks for other people to add to it, if they want to. Again ... think Go-Play.

    Let's take a look at how we publish books, for inspiration. We start small ... within the resources we, personally, have on hand. We don't hold off on publishing a book until we have enough outside investment to publish 5000 copies. We scrape some money together and print 100 ... or we Lulu and print on demand. In any event, during the time when we would have been trying to organize other people to help, we are instead selling games. If nobody ever buys ... no biggie. The project was worth the effort, and you can walk away with a useful experience. If other people dump their resources in, they (a) get something concrete and immediate and (b) contribute to growing the size of the project (more print runs, etc).

    I'd love to see people following the same process as regards our personal/volunteer projects. I'll take GoD as an example, and talk about how it could have been done differently. Not better, just differently.

    Suppose Kat said "Okay, I'm going to provide some people with Games On Demand. I'll do it for four hours on friday, and four on saturday. If anyone else wants to use the same moniker and run tables, that's cool with me. Here's the schedule of what's running, so we don't end up with five people in one slot and none in another. If we get full coverage, great. If not, that's cool too."

    That's another way to organize ... one which doesn't require any help or public brainstorming or organizing before you take the first steps. It just requires a willingness to do purely what you can do, and to feel that if that's all that happens, that's enough.

    There are important constraints on what you can do within that context. You obviously cannot undertake a project whose first useful step requires more resources (in terms of time, energy, money, whatever) than you personally can contribute. But there is an absolutely huge amount that you can do, and there is really no conceivable downside. It's a bootstrapping operation. The worst that happens is you get to do a fun, personal project.

    You can't build a cathedral on your own, but you can worship God on your own, and invite anyone who wants to join you. If enough people do then the cathedral gets built.

    Just as a side note... I think your statement "Think up something cool on my own OR help someone else do something cool." You can do both. is true. However, "Help someone else do something cool AND do my own cool thing." is not always a possibility. Again... I could have decided to do my own cool thing at GenCon but I would not have had the time or energy to do the GoD tables AND something else that required similar commitment.

    Sure. You can't do more than you can do on your own resources, and if those resources are already bespoke then you can't spend them twice. But that just means you take on projects that don't require those resources. I mean, seriously, how much time-at-Gencon did anyone put in to creating Go-Play stickers? Maybe a few minutes distributing them.

    The big projects that require assistance from everyone are not the only projects that can make a difference. I'm a big fan of the projects that start with one person, an idea, and a willingness to fail.

    August 2006
    Great thread, and it reflects exactly the frustrations I'm having in another community I'm part of, where ideas never seem to come to anything because people lack time, energy and organization. My approach has gradually evolved to:

    * If I have an idea that I can do on my own with nobody's support, and that I want to do because I want to do it - I do it. If anyone later picks it up, uses it, joins in, builds on it - that's jam. I make sure I leave hooks for people to do these things (like Tony's description of how Kat could have run GoD).

    * If I see someone else doing something that I think I can engage with or build on, and they're offering that opportunity, I do that too.

    * If I have an idea that requires other people to join in in order to make it fly - I toss it out into the wild and see if it thrives. If so, cool. If not, well, I have three or four ideas a week which would take a month each to implement. Plenty more where those came from.

    * If I have a project that I'm working on, and other people's input would really improve it, I ask the people I'm connected with for their comments. They don't have to "work on the project" with me, they just have to look at what I've done and bounce suggestions. This improves my ideas notably. This is my answer to Nancy's question: I say, "Hey, I thought of this, I'm planning to do it, what's your reaction?" Nobody feels like responding will commit them to anything, but they get a degree of involvement and ownership. This is basically what the Go Play thread did, too.
