What is this?
A setting and rules companion to Lady Blackbird.

Who is this for?
Anyone who enjoys Lady Blackbird or other Tales from the Wild Blue Yonder.
Anyone interested in fantasy steampunk adventure role-play.

Why do you use this?
Extending or augmenting the adventures of Lady Blackbird and associates.
Having other exciting adventures in the Blue.
Writing new chapters of Tales from the Wild Blue Yonder.

Where and when do you use this?
At the table, when you play.

How do you use this?
Find bits of setting detail to add into your game.
As a GM: find ideas for obstacles, questions, and NPCs.
As a player: find ideas for your character, places to go, and things to do.
Ignore what you don't like and---probably---what contradicts what you do like.


Although I initially planned to do more with this (specifically more People & Places and Questions), it's come time for me to release this into the wild.


Some notes for those interested:
1) All work was done using free (as in freedom) software including: LaTeX, the GIMP, FontForge, OpenOffice then LibreOffice, Inkscape, and some *NIX programs like sed, cut, and paste. I may have used Acrobat Reader to look up some font names. This wasn't so much for any deep philosophical reasoning, but it didn't cost me any money.

2) When people tell you that LaTeX is bad for layout they are not lying. I used it because I could already do some stuff with it, but ended up learning a lot more (I am now the master of boxes!). While I think the end result looks pretty good, some of it took a lot of effort, and the later pages leave something to be desired regarding text wrapping at the bottom of the pages.

3) I tried to match the style of the original Lady Blackbird as much as possible. I ripped the border out of the PDF using Inkscape (and modified it for full page use), used the same header font, using FontForge to extract the plus sign looking glyph from the PDF. I used the same header typeface, but didn't have (or could find for free) the original body typeface. Instead I used Gentium for reasons that escape me at the reason (perhaps it was getting some love in the type thread on story-games). Because Gentium doesn't have everything, I also used Gentium Basic for bold, and a smaller size Gentium Book Basic for small caps, which I think actually looks pretty good. I didn't spend too much time worrying about matching all the point sizes with the original.

4) The traits, tags, keys, and secrets are a pretty exhaustive combination from all the credited games. For the most part, I tried to remove variants that were just renamed versions from the original Lady Blackbird, possibly incorporate new tags. Stuff that was added once I started this process (due to new hacks being released, or me finding them) may not follow this as much. I also trimmed down tag lists that were too long. I've also added some.

5) Dealing with Creative Commons licenses is interesting. A quirk I ran into is that BY-SA cannot be combined with BY-NC-SA, which kept me from using some elements I wanted to "steal". That, some BY-SA stuff I though I would use but didn't in the end, and some BY-NC-ND stuff are why the thing is licensed as a collection.

6) My proficiency at photo editing with the GIMP increased significantly during the project, especially as I graduated to the better methods of cutting an element out of a photo. However, I did not go back and do the earlier ones better. This probably shows.

    We're excited to give these rules a go, especially the character creation. However in the "additional and optional rules," section, it doesn't really say which rules are additional and which are optional.
    \nThe additional and optional rules are purposefully not separated. I don't want any arguing, or anybody thinking that you have to use certain ones. Note there are little checkboxes next to each rule. If you're using it, put a check in it. If not, don't. Some of them are more advice than rules, and you can pretty much take or leave anything.

    The only real exception is the alternate dice rolling method, which is why it's after the art.\n
    Also, I was confused by this line: "To take Master Sorcerer or any blooded trait you must have a relevant Secret, otherwise you\u2019re relegated to magic that only uses one spell tag at a time."

    Whispblood and Petty Magic are in the clear, because they come under that "one spell tag at a time" rule. Warpblood doesn't have a trait.

    But, how do you get Dreamblood, Dustblood, Flameblood, Ghostblood, Stoneblood, or Voidblood. What is a "Relevant Secret?" The only one I saw was "Secret of Stormblood." How do you get the rest?
    \nMy point was to extrapolate somewhat from the characters in Chapter I. Lady Blackbird has a Secret related to her spellcasting (which doesn't actually do anything, while Kale doesn't and is limited to one spell tag at a time.

    For conciseness, I don't list a bunch of different versions of the same Secret. So modify The Secret of Stormblood to whatever name you want that matches the type of *blood that you have. So you may have The Secret of Stoneblood. Something like The Secret of Warpblood may or may not qualify, depending on what you and your group think about it. My inclination is either no, or it allows multiple spell tags on the weaker bloods, but not the purer ones. So it may work for Wispblood and Dustblood, but not Stormblood and Stoneblood.
