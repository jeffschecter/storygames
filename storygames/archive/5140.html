\n Hi all,
I\u2019m a long-time loiterer and very infrequent poster here who finally managed to meet up with some of the UK contingent at the recent Dragonmeet convention as well as actually getting the chance to play with some of them at GrahamW\u2019s post-con game night.

I was fortunate enough to be involved in my first game of Polaris, which incidentally was my first stab at playing in a GM-less, narrative based, story-now game. My obvious fumbling was kindly overlooked by my co-players and I came away with a real desire to learn more about the why and how of the session.

While I have GM\u2019d sessions of Burning Wheel and Inspectres, I certainly don\u2019t feel that I was able to really hit the \u2018story-now\u2019 nail on the head, and to actually play outside the story-before box was enlightening and I feel will provide a valuable lesson for the next time my BW group comes together.

This is where this rambling monologue comes in. My current (non-BW) group, for whom I have run a 4-session BW, Inspectres and Dead of Night games \u2013 to mixed results \u2013 firmly refuse to move away from their story-before D20 comfort zones. Their response to such games as Cold City, TSOY or Conspiracy of Shadows is that they \u201care far too complicated\u201d and that they aren\u2019t D20.

While I really enjoy the company of the chaps I game with, I am becoming increasingly frustrated with the endless dungeon crawls or the, effectively, non-interactive sessions I am participating in. However, if I can\u2019t convince the gang to play some of the new and very exciting games out their, then part of me is starting to think that if I can\u2019t beat them I should join them\u2026

So, is it possible to do story-now with D20? Can such a system which rewards purely combat based on task resolution do story-now, or am I stuck in an endless cycle of story-before dungeoneering? I have tried searching the Story-games forum for such topics, but can only find discussions related to general story-gaming which doesn't seem to quite scratch the itch.

John

    \n It looks like you've discovered that you like a certain kind of game and you're dissatisfied with your current game. Your friends are happy with it. Given all that, it basically follows that you're the odd man out. Don't try to force them into something they don't want.

    That said, I have found that D&D 3e is best left as a Gamist game. Sure, there are things you can bolt on to allow some Narrativist techniques, but they mostly ignore the fact that the big reward cycle (play, gain XP, level up) is fundamentally Gamist. You can Address Premise or get your Story Now only so long with those rules before your players are leveling up and begin itching to try out their new feats and abilities on some monster ass. Then you're back to Gamist play. That's the beauty and power of the 3E rules. Don't fight it; embrace it. Or try another game, if you can talk your friends into it.

    \n If we're talking about the Big Model's definition of Story Now (i.e. Narrativism) then all that's required is that over-time the ultimate point of play is addressing a Premise (i.e. coming to a thematic resolution of an recognizably emotionally charged situation). So yes, absolutely you can do Story Now with d20, even unaltered.

    I once ran a d20 one-shot Star Wars game that had this setup. The scenario took place between Episode 3 and 4. Two surviving Jedi, an old republic diplomat, and a couple of droids are sent out to a planet to rescue another surviving Jedi.

    Key Components of the Scenario.

    1) The Empire has beat them there and has setup blockades and scout teams but are holding back for the arrival of Lord Vader.
    2) On the planet is a matriarchal tribe of force users who oppress their men because they think they are strong in the dark side.
    3) The surviving Jedi in question has taken advantage of this gender rift to start a "liberation" movement among the men. In doing so he has set himself up as a Heart of Darkness style leader over these people. He believes he is training the new Jedi Order.
    4) The matriarchs are more or less right in that the men of their tribe do channel the dark side easier than the light side. The has tempted the surviving Jedi to take advantage of this fact to empower his army. The Jedi is very close to going over to the dark side himself.
    5) The Jedi knows the Empire has found him and he relishes testing his new army against Lord Vader.

    Do you see the issues here? What do the players do about the Jedi? Save him? Call him a lost cause? Side with him? What if anything do the players do about the gender issues in the now divided tribe? What impact does the Imperial presence have on the situation?

    As long as you simply play the whole thing straight and honestly d20 will do just fine.

    \n I've been adding story game elements to my D&D 3.5 game for some time now, with some good results.

    Whenever we're not on the battlemap, I try to keep things moving narratively. Each PC has a story-arc that I've worked out with the player. I've let my players pick "aspects" for their characters (like in Spirit of the Century). The aspects have no game mechanics, but they're a good short description of the various plots revolving around the characters. Before each session, I ask the players which aspect they'd like to emphasize in the next game.

    So, the game is very narrativist off the battlemat, and very d20 when a fight breaks out. Since D20 doesn't pay much attention to things that happen out of combat, I find that it works pretty well.

    \n I haven't really posted about it before, except for the game's journal, but I'll try to give some helpful hints.

    First off, I find it's usually good to start a session with a fight. It gets everyone focused and let's them do all the "fighting stuff" right off the bat. You have to find the right balance of fighting and story for your group. It takes some work.

    Second, realize that not every player is going to want to do heavy-duty roleplay. If they don't want to do it, don't force it on them. Just work extra hard on giving them moments to shine in combat.

    Third, and this is something I picked up from listening to podcasts about improvisation and gaming, say YES to the players. If they want to do something, let them do it, or at least find a good compromise.

    Here's an example. I'm currently running a game set in Monte Cook's Ptolus setting. One of my players said, "I want to play an evil Orc Duskblade." (Almost every group has a player who likes to play the odd characters) Now, Duskblade is a class that combine spell-casting and fighting, and it was supposedly developed by the elves (ancient enemies of the orcs). I said YES, and we worked out a story where a Elven Duskblade Master made a foolish bet that left him training an orc in the arts of the Duskblade. One of the other PCs was a fellow student. There are story hooks right there.

    Later, the same player said, "I want to multiclass with the Crusader (a class from Tome of Battle, a very experimental supplement.)" I said, Sure thing. And even later "I want to take this Jade Phoenix Mage prestige class from the ToB." I looked at the JPM. Its basic schtick is that there are 13 JPM in the world who keep reincarnating to prevent some great evil from breaking free of its prison. The player and I talk about this, discussing options like the JPMs are all elves, usually, and the PC was reincarnated as an orc this time (and the other JPMs might want to fix the "mistake.") Or the PC was a traitorous JPM that the others hunt down and kill every lifetime and was reincarnated this time. Eventually, we changed the JPMs into an evil organization, dedicated to REVIVING an ancient evil.

    OK, so, the player wants into an evil prestige class. As part of the roleplay, I have an older JPM show up, and offer to "awaken your ancient memories." The PC agrees. The JPM tells him that, as part of his initiation, he must kill the NPC girlfriend of another PC. The player agrees to this as well. We do it all, right there in front of the others, who are watching in amazement as the PC lures the girlfriend into an alley with an illusion and carves her heart out.

    Now, you might think that this would cause problems with the group, but because we did it all above the board, with the consent of all the players, it's really added something to the game. Every now and then, the players talk about how cool it's going to be when their players learn of what the one player did. And that player is just as happy as can be that he got to do what he wanted. He says it's the best game he's ever been in (and he's notoriously hard to please.)

    So, the corollary to saying YES is to make sure that everyone agrees to an action before it goes forward. Talk it out. Another part of saying YES in D&D for me is to poll my players about what they'd like to do in the next session, with the agreement that they'll do that and not change their mind at the last second. It lets me still plan out things in advance, while letting the players still have the freedom to choose what they want to do. Cuts down on railroading and wasted planning time.

    Let's see, what else...? I've stolen several ideas from indie RPGs. We have "fan mail" (taken from Prime Time Adventures) where the players can give each other poker chips whenever they do something "cool." The chips can later be used to get an extra 1d6 to add to a d20 roll. I find this acts as a positive feedback loop, with the players constantly trying to find "cool" things to do to earn chips from the other players.

    I also took the idea of "aspects" from Spirit of the Century. In my game, they are a list of small phrases that describe the various "sub-plots" that surround a character. For instance, the evil PC I described up above has the following aspects:

    Phoenix Mage
    The Black Knight (Arena fighting title)
    Enemy: Snew
    Chosen One of Ochremeshk
    "There's no problem 1,000 orcs can't solve!"
    Murderer of Yori
    In Debt to the Balacazar Crime Family

    So, for each session, he can pick one aspect that I will do my best to work into the story of that session.

    Well, that's about all I can think of right now... hope this helps a little.

    \n I think there are two issues to contend with...

    1. It's one thing to go on some forums and claim "your not having fun." That's dumb. It's another thing entirely when you know what the people close to you will most enjoy but they are being too afraid or too stubborn to recognize it. Thus if you can stretch that trust a little bit and get them to try a little newness (not the whole bag) then they may find that they are actually having more fun at the table. It's not too much of a stretch to guess that if your unhappy gaming with the folks you play with regularly, chances are they are reaching a similar level of disatisfaction but are assuming it's other factors as the problem, not the game.

    2. My changes were to make D&D a better game, not to story-ify it. Let me explain. My changes are to have things moving continuously and allow the group to build the adventures as we go. Why is that "better?" Collaborative Adventure Building: It ensures that the players are doing what they want, the responsibility is evenly distributed and everyone gets to enjoy "reveals." Steady pacing: Instead of the GM directing most of the action the group all participates evenly and thus has more to do during more of the time. Lets say a scene takes 5 minutes to resolve. In most games that's 3 minutes of one person telling the group what's going on, a minte of the players looking at one another and maybe a minute of one or two people taking the reins. In a scene based, evenly distributed authorship everyone gets more regular game time and the space between points they play is far shorter. Sure, I did a few things that I think are going to add more story-punch to the narrative, but I designed my changes to allow the players to better game the system and have more input on all three levels (rules, story and development). Most folks would agree (As Adam mentioned) that D&D is a gamist playground but I even think it falls short in this respect due to the above failings.
