I grant you The Dark Tower is a big universe, which can make suggesting a system quite hard. So let me see if I can isolate the things I'm trying to emulate (not necessarily all at the same time):
- "The man in black fled across the desert, and the gunslinger followed." Yup.
- The party full of strangers that has to cooperate to survive... whether they like it or not.
- The Fall of Gilead
- The Battle of Lud
- Pretty much all of Wolves of the Calla

Honestly... Burning Wheel with guns would probably get me most of the way there, but I've had many tell me they don't recommend porting the Burning Sands mechanics into standard Burning Wheel and I can sort of see why (they're very finicky.)

So. Open up those firehoses you call brains and let me have it.

    \n Well, Sorcerer is heavily about the individual, unique snowflake player characters and their personal mythologies. You can intentionally sharpen the focus if you work on it, but the default outcome of the extensive character/scenario creation process is usually a bunch of player characters who sort of each have their own little mythology going on. That is, the character creation develops not only a character, but also his own little slice of setting. Each is an exceptional individual with a possibly unique history (especially if you're doing pulp fantasy along the lines of Sorcerer & Sword), and each has an unique demon (read: force outside the setting cosmology) in an intimate relationship with them to begin with.

    As Sorcerer is so much about relationships with inhuman, mythic forces, it likes a setting that harmonizes with the insider/outsider distinction in some interesting way. I think that the Dark Tower has plenty of this sort of action going in it - major characters are often unique and have relationships to extraordinary forces, like that gunslinger who's the last heir of an order of his ilk. Major plot points revolve around encountering ambiguously human creatures like that train thing in what, book three. (Been a while since I've read these, must have been in the '90s. Should probably reread and check out the last volumes that have come out since then, now that I think of it.)

    Oh, also: Dark Tower is generally speaking about individual characters with powerful passions, which goes well with Sorcerer as well. By this I mean that the protagonists are strongly self-directed individuals who feel the weight of fate upon them. Sorcerer is like that as well, it's not a game about GM-delivered mission of the week.

    All that being said, I should note that Sorcerer is not good for emulating any specific material exactly. So doing Dark Tower with it wouldn't be so much about modeling characters from the book directly, but rather about developing your own story and extending the mythology, inventing new stuff. I think that this fits the spirit of the source material well, what with all the multiversal metatextual wackiness King gets into as the series develops. Perhaps you'd use individual elements from the books, like Randall Flag (that was the villain?) and so on.

    Also, eff paying for Solar System, it's free as beer. Just add personal insight.

    \n Actually, before digesting the above, I should explain the key bit that's obvious if you've read Sorcerer but doesn't make any sense if you've just heard about it:

    Sorcerer characters are "sorcerers" and they summon and control "demons". However, what a "demon" is depends on your setting, the rules just treat that term as a literary structural device. It's all good so long as the "demon" is inhuman, from outside the human frame of reference (whatever that means in the setting) and maybe has an inscrutable agenda of its own. A sorcerer, then, is an exceptional individual who deals with forces like these.

    Dark Tower doesn't maybe involve literal demons or devils very often, but it's constantly breaking the cosmological constraints in exactly this manner. A gunman in a world without gunpowder, drawing on the dead precepts of his ancient order? This order that lives only in his memories is clearly his demon. That sort of thinking. I'd go into details, but I find that I've just about regurgitated everything I remember about the novels already

    \n Fate could be good. When people ask about using it for this or that media property (which happens surprisingly often when I go on the road with my retail hobby), I usually ask them whether they're interested in depicting or challenging the property in question.

    Fate and the afore-mentioned Solar System are very similar in their mechanical ideas and the overall structure of the game, but the purpose of it, and thus some key systems, differ between the two pretty fundamentally. SS is more along the same lines as Burning Wheel in that it's about challenging and breaking and rediscovering the player characters through dramatic adversity. Fate in the games that I've seen is wielded less like a hammer and more like a video projector: this is the character you created, now breathe life into him and depict him for the group's enjoyment. Essentially, the difference is that in Fate you start the game with the character you want, and then derive enjoyment from depicting him, and the game for the most part works with you to ensure that your character gets to be cool in the way you envision. In Solar System and Burning Wheel and such you start with a kernel that only becomes the true character through the process of discovery in play, and there's no guarantee that it's going to go the way you envisioned in the beginning.

    All of the above in my opinion, of course - Fate's been used a lot in different popular games, so there's a lot of different approach floating around.
