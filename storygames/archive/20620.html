    \n It is true that constantly correcting other people's terminology is not the most productive way to spend anybody's time.

    On the other hand, a clarity of communication is a nice thing to have as well.

    When it comes to these specific words (there are many other terminological debates one could have, too), the debate is probably fueled by the in-group vs. out-group usage differences; as both groups are essentially stable socially (even if for different reasons) and usage is uniform within them, neither really makes any inroads in convincing the other to change the way they use words.

    To be specific, here's what I'm seeing regarding "indie":

    In-Group: The small, internally well-connected clique of indie gaming activists, many of whom started e.g. a decade ago, consistently use the term "indie" in the Forgite way, the way Ron originally defined it when he started promoting it. These people often have some history of publishing themselves, and often have convictions about the artist-publisher relationship; therefore "indie" not being some sort of a marketing label for drama games matters to them, and using the term their way is effortless and useful. The other usage is exclusionary and entirely unacceptable when you're trying to e.g. organize a joint convention appearance with other indie designers, as you usually do not want to start excluding people because their games don't use conflict resolution systems, or whatever.

    Out-Group: People who have experienced the indie phenomenon from the "outside" started early on to use the term to basically indicate "the kind of new style experimental dramatic games they make at the Forge". After the diaspora new emphasises have developed with e.g. the Nordic freeform influences and such, resulting in "indie" basically meaning "a roleplaying game different from the usual assumptions". There are many more of the people who use the term this way than there are members of the in-group, and newcomers naturally join this group first as they adopt language; some may move on to adopt the in-group terminology for whatever reason later on, but a constant presence of people using the term this way is practically guaranteed.

    Other words: Both the in-group and the out-group use "traditional" in roughly the same way. "Narrativist" and "story game" are examples of words that have also had currency at different points to gesture towards the out-group meaning of indie as "this new experimental way, different from trad". However, it seems that "indie" is more popular, probably because it suits better as an umbrella term for a cultural trend, not having any analytical content for nit-picking to latch on. I've tried to suggest "progressive" as an alternative, as that word's not in use elsewhere, and it's also traditional in other fields, but of course that sort of well-meaning terminological invention never gets anywhere.

    Jason: Jason Corley there represents a special fringe position in this terminology, so it's probably a good idea to note it separately: as far as I know (not trying to put words into his mouth), Jason agrees with the afore-defined in-group on "indie" (that is, it just means that the game is creator-owned), but disagrees with both the in- and out-groups on "traditional", claiming that such a phenomenon does not exist historically, and should therefore not be discussed. It's an entirely honorable position in terms of cultural studies, but as Jason pushes it so eagerly, he probably causes as much debate over this terminological issue here at SG as the other two camps do.

    There probably isn't any sensible way to eliminate either usage of "indie" to a point where people would feel unjustified in continuing with it; as things stand, both ways of using the term are apparently considered entirely legitimate by the people who use the word that way. Certainly both usages are needed: we need a way to say "this game is creator-owned", and we need a way to say "this game takes radical freedoms from the way rpgs are traditionally designed".

    It seems to me that unless you're specifically teaching people, it is useless to make this a sticking-point, it's just derailing discussion; just use some other words if you're worried about being understood, or explain yourself each time. I suppose that the ideal situation would be one where people had a rich vocabulary of words to indicate what they mean, and I generally like to be precise myself, but apparently the scene in general is happy enough with the way things are. I don't even want to seriously suggest introducing new words, as that'd also mean new ways to misuse them

    \n Oh, I guess I'll also give the way I use these words myself:

    As far as indie goes, I'm happy with the Forgite definition, focusing strictly on practical publishing arrangements of the product. I've never used the word to describe content, except maybe in a second-hand way, by e.g. remarking upon how much more common it is for an indie game to be experimental than it is for a corporate game.

    OK, so that wasn't a surprise - same definition as Potemkin there. For traditional, however, I differ clearly, because what he's defining is simply corporate ownership, which while interesting in itself, is not what I usually mean when I say "trad". Rather, for me the concept of "traditional rpg design" is an important tool in examining the cultural history of roleplaying, and consequently the term finds much usage in that context: a trad game is one that partakes in the game design and production culture of the short-lived 1980s rpg industry. Many, many games were published through-out the decade, and the culture continued to limp along until the late '90s, forming the basis of what we think of as the normal baseline of rpg product today. As a cultural study term I find "trad" extremely valuable, as it is a concise shorthand to indicate a game that shares values, design ideas and cultural context with this historical rpg design "school".

    As I mentioned shortly above, the counter-parts that I use for these words are corporate (or "mainstream") for games that aren't indie, and progressive (or "experimental") for games that aren't traditional.

    As one might imagine, I don't feel much need to debate these definitions. Just thought it interesting to note that while "indie" is the word people mostly seem to disagree on, we can certainly manage to differ on "trad" as well.

    \n The meaning of language can depend on context.

    A game can be "indie" for some purposes and not for others.

    Take for instance the term "civil law" which has at least five distinct meanings:-

    1) Roman Law
    2) Roman Law and legal systems descended from Roman Law (including the law applied in the Court of Chivalry)
    3) private law
    4) private law plus public law with the exception of criminal law
    5) (in continental Europe) private law excluding (or principally excluding) commercial law

    I think it might also mean "law that isn't canon law".

    Likewise, "common law" has at least five different meanings:-

    1) case-law descended wholly from the English legal system, excluding equitable jurisprudence, the case-law of the Court of Chivalry and interpretations of statute
    2) case-law descended wholly from the English legal system, including equitable jurisprudence but excluding interpretations of statute and (probably, I guess) the case-law of the Court of Chivalry
    3) (I'm hazy on the exact limits of this definition) case-law in a legal system descended wholly or partly from the English system (thus I expect including Scots common law which may be of Roman origin), excluding interpretations of statute
    4) areas of legal practice within private law with certain exceptions, specifically excluding commercial law, family law and chancery
    5) (in common parlance, used loosely) "customary" or "fictional" or "treated as if", as in the phrase "common law husband" (which may have no legal status at all in any given jurisdiction)

    And yet, lawyers can still use the phrases "civil law" and "common law" in discussion with each other and everyone knows what they mean.

    So, chill!

    To me "trad" means "in the traditional style" which boils down to:- most players not having shared narrative authority; the GM can bend the rules and can determine how time flows and populate the fictional setting freely. This is the traditional style of almost all RPGs prior to the Forge (but not strictly all).

    So the essence of the traditional style is that it is "GM-controlled" or "GM-supervised" even if at the same time the players might, in substance, be leading the development of the plot (as they do, in most games of the sort I prefer).

    A simple note.

    Some of you have been on, or read The Forge.

    That's great. This forum isn't that one.

    Similarly, a lot of work on The Forge was about creating terms and taxonomies for analyzing roleplaying games.

    That's great. This forum doesn't assume you know any of them.

    It doesn't matter whether people commonly use a term, or whether you've been using it for years; we're all still grappling with how to talk about these games, and often times, the ideas new people have can be even more interesting than the ones that people who have been steeped in a discourse for a decade.

    When reaching for terms, ask yourself:
    *Am I making this as accessible as possible?
    *Am I willing to make space for different definitions?
    *Am I willing to explain these terms to people who ask?
    *Are my terms making the conversation easier or harder for new people to follow?\ufeff

    So you should aim to explain yourself, link to explanations, and deal with confusion over terms in the nicest way possible.

    I started roleplaying seriously about two and a half years ago. People join the hobby everyday, and those are the people we want to embrace.

    So be happy to share old and new ideas with each other.

    \nMost would call it traditional - firmly at the core of the concept, actually. As we've seen, there are different definitions.

    If you want a term that distinguishes between mid-school D&D and other trad games of the period (a distinction that I rarely find important in comparison to e.g. the differences between early and later D&D), what might be a common phrasing there? People used to talk about "dramatic" games at some point, and about "roll playing vs. role playing", and such. The distinction between D&D and new wave games was important in e.g. the '80s scene. Whitewolf famously called their games "storytelling games" (or rather, Storyteller system, but anyway) specifically to distinguish from D&D and other perceived low-brow games.

    Interestingly we don't really have a high-profile word for being traditional yet not D&D, despite this being an important and distinction for many gamers. The closest that people around here come is "not D&D" or "normal roleplaying games" - D&D is often not counted as "normal" here in Finland due to how different it is from the realism-immersion through-line of the later tradition. More normal than Forgite games, yes, but less normal than say Call of Cthulhu.
