\n The scion/exalted battle wheel I have noticed is one of those things that people seem to love, or hate.
\n
\nFor those who have never used it the battle wheel is essentially a wheel of numbers players roll and depending on their result are placed on the wheel. The person furthest back acts then ticks themselves further on the wheel depending on the action taken. If they move past some one that person then become the active player and they continue ticking forward as they take actions.
\n
\nI like it personally but I am kind of interested in every ones thoughts here? The gripe my group has is that it overly complicates things have you found this when using it?

    \n The timer for the Neuland game is the same idea. It works pretty slick in that game. There's a timer marker on the track. Each time a player takes their turn their actions move them forward on the track. After their turn, the timer marker is advanced. The first player it comes to (i.e. farthest back on the track) then takes their turn, and so on.

    \n I'm not familiar with this in RPGs, but it's used in the co-op board game "Red November" and works really well there. It lets all the players perform 'simultaneous' actions that take varying amounts of time. (Which plays into the way it represents difficulty of tasks: basically, you say how long you're taking to put out a fire or unblock a hatch, and the longer you take the better your odds of success ... but the closer the timer gets to the end of the game.)

    \n We set a mark on the wheel to indicate a turn...once the marlers has progressed around to that point again...then a turn can be considered to have passed.

    Conversely...if someone sets off an effect at a hypothetical "point 4" on the wheel, and the effect is meant to last a turn, then the effect concludes once the cycles has moved around past "point 4" again.

    A turn would then be defined within the context of the game we're applying the wheel to (RIFTS = 15 seconds, etc...).

    \n I like the battle wheel for the extra dimension (or just finer resolution, however you see it) of time-as-resource. But I've had players hate it because it seems like another character will get two turns before they get their next turn.

    My one complaint is that it does take a little more brain-space than simply taking turns around the table. In a game like Exalted, brain-space is at a premium.

    Adam, default time per tick is squishy, so yeah, not concrete. But it's only that way for cinematic reasons. Unless you're in Shaping Combat, you could just rule that it always takes default time per tick. Maybe a 10-section battle wheel might make it easier to keep track of total time elapsed (at least, easier than multiplying by 7).

    \n Oh, sure, you can say that each pass around the wheel is a certain number of seconds; we probably even did. But nobody ever bothers to keep track of how many times we went around the wheel, leastways not in my group.

    I guess it wasn't hugely necessary, since at the end of an action scene, we never said "Gee, I wonder how many seconds that was?"

    If we were having a battle scene and a bomb was ticking away in the background, ready to detonate the Hoover Dam, then when the fight finished, we always had exactly 0:15 left on the clock. I just know some people are pickier about that sort of thing.
