Over the last week or so I have been noodleing around with a design for a new game. Just today I decided to can the idea. \xa0I think that, that decision deserves some examining.\xa0

The idea itself isn't really important to this discussion but what work I had done on it is. What I had at the time of the project's demise was; a rather long note on my iPhone, two pages of short hand rules, and three sheets of graphs, heiroglyphs and numbers which where beguining to hurt my brain. All together this represented maybe three hours of real thought and effort and about an hour total (over the course of a few days) of just throwing ideas into my phone.

When I knew that the project needed serious evaluation was when I got to page three of charting out rule interactions. \xa0I looked at where I was and then looked ahead. In my estimation I had far more to do than I had already done just to bring the design to a point where it could turn from doodles and charts into a working prototype. I also guessed that when I was done the prototype would be horribly broken (I know I forgot to carry the one somewhere I just didn't know where). Looking at the work nessessary to bring a broken prototype to reality along with concidering the likelyhood that I would ever make the changes nessessary to make the design work (not likely among my other projects) I decided to can the project.\xa0

If I had looked ahead and seen a shorter work cycle, a quicker path from prototype to finished design, or a higher probability that the project would keep my interest I would likely have kept working. Canning a project before prototype stage is something new to me and I was suprised by how good it felt. Before, abandoning a design I had put so much work into (getting it to prototype by hook or by crook) hurt a little. I always felt that canning a project that far along was a betrail of all the work I had put in. This time, however, I felt reassured that the design's time was up and felt good that I had saved myself the time and effort I might have wasted.\xa0

Do I still feel like there may be useful nuggets hidden within that design? \xa0Sure I do, thats why the two page rules sketch went back into my folder of horrible ideas. The rest of the work, the actuary chart from hell and the gibbering notes on a madman's phone, got tossed as I felt they held nothing of value to future projects.\xa0

Learning to let go is valuable but I fear it may be dangerous as well. I do not regret scrapping this game at this point but I need to add that into my conciderations in the future. \xa0Will I regret letting this go? \xa0I hope I can contenue makeing wise decisions about my designs on into future projects.\xa0

    \n I made a New Year's resolution this year to only work on new projects, letting go all the old stuff that I've been hammering away at (like in Metallica's classic video for "The Unforgiven"), and only dredging up older stuff if I was willing to completely rethink it and rebuild it anew.

    Another thing I've added to that is: only work on games that I'm excited about playing. If I can't bring myself to want to play the game, clearly I'm not in the right mood to work on it and it can sit in the corner gathering dust until it captures my attention and imagination again.

    \n There was another thing I was talking about with some Seattle folks recently, which is somewhat related.

    Sometimes you begin projects in the wrong order. Like, you start working on game A before game B, but -- really -- you need to finish B before you can finish A, because there are a few problems you need to solve and B is a better place to solve them than A.

    A good example is how working on Super Suit is solving many of the problems that I had with both Geiger Counter and An Ill-Fated Descent. But I'm already too invested in Geiger Counter to be willing to be experimental and gut that project entirely before rewriting it, plus the people who already liked Geiger Counter would freak out if I released versions that got substantially worse before they got better. Consequently, I need to work on my problems with Geiger Counter in another game -- a game that I have much less history and investment in -- and then come back later.

    John Harper said that he was feeling similarly about Danger Patrol and -- while those are both unfinished games that have seen a lot of play in their alpha and beta drafts -- I think that can hold true for other games as well. Forking certain concepts off into a "side game" is not really about managing audience expectations so much as it is clearing away everything else and giving yourself space and permission to really go to work on a design problem, digging into the guts and trying a few new ideas without any expectations.

    \n My ex-wife had the potential to be a good artist. However, her technique consisted of starting out by drawing the eyes.. and then the reast of the picture.. however it filled out the page. So, she would end up with fantastic eyes and the rest of it would be really cruddy.

    I recently discovered that I would tend to do something similar. I would have an idea for an interesting mechanic, and then try to grow a game around it. That didn't usually work. I've dumped dozens of newborn systems because of that habit.

    \n If you look at designs that you steal from for other designs as living things then, no, I really never give up on a game. I don't see my designs that way however. I have always kobbled together my designs from broken pieces of other designs (mine and others), so I don't see thos scavenged bits as "part" of an older design. Viewing the borrowed bits in their old context tends to leave me with dangling pieces of design where I forgot to cut a cord. For me new design requires bleaching the old idea of all it's color before I work with it.

    \n I have lots of little ideas. I tend to play with them for a while, then abandon them.

    I'd also like to be like the cool kids around these parts; I'd loe to self publish a game and sell, like, at least a copy or two. Really, mostly to massage my own ego.

    I haven't done that (yet). Poor Ego!

    What I did do, a while back, was take a couple of those little ideas, throw stuff together using them and run a couple of entirely one-shot gaming sessions.

    The thing about these sessions was...they were both a playtest/test of concept and the entirety of The Game.

    I never went on to publish, but I did learn some things ( good and bad) about my Game. And it was awfully satisfying to take the thing from concept to presentation and play.

    I think that this approach might actually be a really good one for designers with the kernel of a really nifty idea. I suspect that I'm not alone in that, when I have a good kernel of idea, I actually kinda have a one-off scenario vaguely in mind that my golden kernel would apply really well to.

    I tend to bog down badly when I over-expand on the concept, or I strive for perfection, or get all ahead of myself and think about being one of the cool kids who self-publishes.

    Anyway, that's the basics of my advice: Try taking that kernel and just writing one cool scenario based around it, with the idea of being really specific and actually running the thing just once.

    If you do, you've succeeded in taking the thing from concept through play, which tends to be a whole lot farther along than a lot of great ideas get.

    ( I'd also point out that this is the origin of really early D&D and its immediate predecessors)

The solution was right in front of me all this time, but I refused to take it because I wanted the game to fit the mold I made for it. When I let go and changed it dramatically, the game was done in a few days.

    So now I don't abandon designs, I just charge blindly forward.
