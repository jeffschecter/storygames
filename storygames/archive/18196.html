\n Hello, I'm Johnstone Metzger.

I have a new book out: Adventures on Dungeon Planet.



You may know me from my previous work, which includes: The Metamorphica, Heralds of Hell, World of Algol, Sexy Deadly, the tables on page 14 of Dark Heart of the Dreamer, and a few other things, like my semi-regular gaming group Red Box Vancouver. But nevermind that stuff now.

Adventures on Dungeon Planet is a science fantasy supplement for the award-winning role-playing game Dungeon World. It has all sorts of cool stuff in it:
* Four new character classes: the Earthling, the Engine of Destruction, the Mutant, and the Technician.
* Three new PC races: aliens, androids, and white apes.
* Four new compendium classes: the Alien, the Scientist, the Sniper, and the Visitor.
* Futuristic gadgets, special equipment, and robots.
* New rules for spaceships.
* New dangers and two example fronts that use them.
* Procedures for creating alien planets and cultures.
* More than 30 new science fantasy monsters.

It is also full of really old pulp science fiction art from the early part of the 20th century!
And a few pieces from the Prismatic Art Collection.

You can: buy the pdf by itself for $8 from Lulu.

Or: buy the softcover and the pdf together for $20 from Lulu.
[Once you buy the print version, you will receive a link to a free pdf version.]

I was going to include the character sheets along with the pdf of the rulebook, but Lulu makes combining print and pdf too damn complicated already, so I guess will just have to give the sheets away for free. Enjoy!

If you have any questions, please ask.

If you have any problems purchasing from Lulu, whisper me here and I will do what I can to fix the problem.

    \n I've read through it now, and I'm impressed. In fact I was chortling out loud at several points while reading (the description of the Multitool is hilarious \u2014 one of the few places the Hitchhiker's Guide influence shows through.)

    When I first heard about this project I was unsure about layering atop Dungeon World instead of making it a standalone AW hack. But it really seems to work out, since having a base of magic and fantasy go go along with the pulp sci-fi really helps nail the planetary-romance genre.

    Retooling the core classes into sci-fi equivalents was a brilliant idea, especially the Cleric-into-Technician: the existing spell memorization rules turn into a solid mechanic for the ability to whip up various flavors of weird science devices on brief notice, without giving the character too much power. I think I've rarely been so eager to start playing a particular character type, as while reading through this section.

    The layout is well done too, with a great selection of old pulp artwork along with simple but clean typography. I can't wait to have the physical book in my hands!

    My group has been playing stock DW for the past few sessions. We just cleaned out a dungeon, but I think our storyline is still going; I'm going to push for playing Dungeon Planet next. We're all Lovecraft fans too, so I could sweeten the deal by promising to include some Elder Things...

    \n I also feel compelled to list some more classic reference works for inspiration \u2014

    "Flash Gordon" by Alex Raymond \u2014 The original comic strip is much better than any of the movie adaptations, with really beautiful artwork, and the plotline is pure Planetary Romance.
    "The Dying Earth" by Jack Vance \u2014 super-influential tales of a far future where technology and magic are almost completely indistinguishable.
    "People Of The Talisman" and all the rest of the Eric John Smith stories by Leigh Brackett \u2014 Hardboiled adventure on Mars and elsewhere, by one of the groundbreaking female SF writers (who co-wrote the screenplay for The Empire Strikes Back decades later.)
    "The Dark World" by Henry Kuttner \u2014 Very much fantasy on the surface, but set in a future world with technological underpinnings explaining much of the magical stuff.
    "Before The Golden Age" \u2014 Not sure if this is still in print; it's an anthology (edited by Asimov, I think?) of 1920s pulp sci-fi stories from before people started figuring out how to write "good" SF.
    The "Tom Swift" novels by "Victor Appleton" and "Victor Appleton, Jr." \u2014 Wow, these are terrible literature (the product of the same sweatshops that gave us Nancy Drew and the Hardy Boys), but full of thrilling super-science. For some reason these were my gateway drug into SF, when I discovered them in 4th grade.
    "The Witches Of Karres" by James Schmitz \u2014 Unjustly-obscure classic of humorous SF. Set in a galactic empire but has plenty of weird magic and psionics.

    \n Sure!

    Planet Algol is one planet, with a specific setting, developed by Blair Fitzpatrick. So it has specific races and cities and dungeons and stuff in it, and anything he makes up for it conforms to a specific aesthetic style. World of Algol is just me hacking AW so I can run games in his setting. You can make up the setting yourself, of course, but Planet Algol comes with one (or I should say it is one).

    Adventures on Dungeon Planet is all science fantasy. It's John Carter, Buck Rogers, EC comics, H.P. Lovecraft, Jack Vance, the Strugatsky brothers, it's all sci-fi from the last half of the 19th century and the first half of the 20th century and whatever else you want to throw in there, but there's no specific setting as a default. You still have to make that up just like you do in Dungeon World. There's no yellow Gresh men and no Agog city.

      \n I usually run either of the two fronts in the book.

      If I run Robot Invasion, I start off with the PCs investigating a crashed robot spaceship on an asteroid. I use one of the 2010 One Page Dungeon Contest entries, Stuart Robertson's Dungeon From a Distant Star, for the ship. It is available for free on Drivethrurpg, here, or you can find it amongst the winners on this page. After a little bit of exploration, I do a flashback to the Humanspace capital, with the princess giving them a mission. I go for a James-Bond-in-space kind of vibe and I improvise what is in the actual rooms of the spaceship a lot. Robots attack them, there is a comms link to the Iron Warlord so the PCs can have scenes with her, there is evidence of treachery, etc. Then the enemy ships show up and there is a spaceship battle, the PCs get back to Earth or wherever the capital of the empire is and that's usually the end of the adventure.

      If I run World Painted Black, the PCs start on a remote research lab with two NPC scientists. There's an animal migration and scorpion-pigs attack the station. One or two break in and there's a fight. They also discover a signal running between the black wave and a spaceship in orbit. They make the spaceship crash, and you can use Dungeon From a Distant Star here, too. Or just have them get to the alien wizard real quick. One time he was a little grey man who used a dimensional portal to escape, so the PCs followed him and wound up on Barsoom, where they captured him but crashed the ship and ended the game on foot. Another time I used the carnivorous tree and just made it intelligent. It was super-brutal because the players for that game were my old-school D&D group and they felt pretty good with all those HP, like level 3 dudes, and then I wiped out like 80% of one guy's HP in a single hit!

      So, those have been pretty good starting situations for me (I should have put them in the book!). And once I start, I just pick stuff out of the grim portents and suggested scenes that I think would be interesting to have happen. And always, always, always, print out half a dozen of your favourite one-page dungeons as a back-up.

      \n You should totally run DnPl at GPNW. If you want to see me run it first, we can probably work that out, too.

      For a space battle, the short answer is yep, you got it. Long answer is long:

      First, you need to know the objective. So like, the PCs are in a scout ship and the robot ships are in pursuit and they want to get back to Capitolopolis. Cool. Then I draw a map of space that has their objective on it, plus all the obstacles in the way between it and their position, and the position of the enemy ships. Make sure there are multiple ways to get there, but each with its own hazards: this is space, after all, and it is big.

      What kind of stuff is there in space? Let your imagination run wild. Planets, suns, electrified nebulas, ion beam arrays lancing through the void, asteroid belts, neon cloud storms, gravity wells, black holes, wormholes, giant space worms, zenon nets, plasma gardens, continuity disturbances, warp clusters, etc etc. Give each of them some kind of environmental effect or hazard or whatever -- make each one unique and not just like normal space. Some are easy: you go into the asteroid belt real fast you better defy danger with DEX or you're gonna crash. You fly through a charged particle net, you take damage.

      Then let the players decide what they want to do. Use the tags as cues: if one ship is faster than another, it can outrun it -- provided nothing happens to it. If you're being chased by a fast ship, go to the asteroid belt, that negates the fast tag (but not the agile tag). If your ship has more HP, try escaping through the corrosive oort swamp. Just make sure you take the tags into account before a roll, but don't let them act as mechanical modifiers. Use only the PCs stats for the rolls, and use the tags to determine if they can actually roll or not. Like, your ship is slow, their ship is fast? You don't get to roll to outrun them, sorry. Find another way!

      Also, the real genius of ships with similar HP and damage ratings to monsters is that PCs can defend their ship, and take the damage themselves instead. I didn't actually plan for this myself I only discovered it in playtesting, when I said the ship's engines were overheating due to the strain on the shields and the Mutant (who has a high CON) wanted to use defend. So he burned his hands getting the engine back in order and the shields managed to keep out the enemy lasers. It makes a big difference when the PCs are outclassed, outgunned, and outnumbered and actually I think it elevates it from a wargame/boardgame type thing to the heroic adventure that DW is supposed to be.

      \n I mostly just wing it, I haven't written up any full-blown custom move for space obstacles (though that's a good idea). I have a vague idea of what they do when I draw them, and then what the PCs do and how the fiction shakes out determines what we focus in on.
