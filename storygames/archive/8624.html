Houses of the Blooded: Bloody good fun

\n
\n
\n
\n\nGraham \n\n\n
\n
\n\nFebruary 2009 \n edited February 2009 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n My first impressions of Houses of the Blooded weren't good. It looked like a "Generic Indie" system, something I'd seen many times before: descriptors, additional narration, relationships. The style of writing, too, put me off.

However, I'm gradually admitting that, every time I play Houses of the Blooded, I have a really fucking good time. Moreover, I have a really fucking good time within the genre that the game aims for: bloody, romantic court politics.

I first played at Dragonmeet. The GM was some American guy in a hat and waistcoat, seemed really proud of the game, like he'd written it or something. Despite being a short session, it was intriguingly good: it took court politics and made it fun. At the time, I rather churlishly attributed this to the group rather than the game itself.

Then, last week, I played at Conception. This game was absolutely superb: really bloody court politics, lots of romance. I kept noticing little things the game did: for example, the mechanical bonus you get for a romance is one of the largest in the game. It constantly pushes you towards a particular style of play.

So I'm now fascinated by Houses of the Blooded. I think I've seriously underrated it. I realise, by the way, that this entire post reads as a back-handed compliment, but it's a back-handed compliment well intended: I'm now excited by Houses of the Blooded and wanting to play again.

Graham

    \n I do enjoy the game too but the book and system have some serious flaws.

    For the system flaws I generally agree with Judd but want to generalize it some more: There are a lot of subsystems, and not all of them seem to work that well. The Manuevers and Duel system is supposed to bring out the dramatich back and forth of feint and riposte in a duel, but I find that it does the opposite. It obstructs the narrative with too much rules. The same is to be said for the Blessings which are this crunchy "KWEL POWERZ" thing that feels out of place in the otherwise (exclusing the sub-systems) rather slim system. Of course a system for Romance on the other hand is an excellent incentive for players to get involved with that kind of play.

    And as far as the book is concerned: It's a horrible mess. I've run half a dozen sessions by now and I'm still having a lot of trouble finding a rule when I need it. It's following no clearly discernible structure and it's a mess of fluff ("the ven are real!" - which kinda rubs me the wrong way) and crunch.

    Regardless, it's a good game and I did have some fun with it but I'm not sure if the crunch brings out the setting feeling all that well. Too many subsystems seems to obstruct it.

    \n Just to confirm a few things.

    Roll a d6 each Year. If you roll a 6, roll again and add the result.
    Maximum three Stories (sessions) per Season. Four Seasons per Year.
    A character moves from Spring to Summer after a total of 60 Age points is reached. With an average roll of 4 (that re-roll 6's pushes it up), that means you hit 60 in about 60 sessions. That's a little more than a real year of play.

    I designed Houses to be a game you play in the long term. That is, a year of real time is a Season in a character's life. I know that's a bit long for indie standards, but that's the way it is.

    If you want time to go faster... honestly, nothing's stopping you from speeding it up. Play one Season per session if you want. Or even one Year per session. Nobody's stopping you.
