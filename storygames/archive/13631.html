    \n I would try and keep it simple and flexible. Keeping the "innocence" of the old-school, with the ability to create some in-depth characters without a whole lot of work. There would still need to be a great deal of crunch, mostly in providing a wide variety of spells for the magic-users, equipment, and magical items. I'd want each "class" to have a host of abilities the player could potentially choose from, that would make each character somewhat unique, but keeping them largely rules-lite and balanced, rather than everything being defined around combat. I'd want to avoid the feel that you had to "optimize" your characters for butt-kicking, and instead could focus on making the character something interesting and imaginative.

    ...and TONS of paper minis. Preferably the standee type. And I mean TONS.

    \n
    \n Major strokes, in no particular order:
    * Classless, point-buy build system, with ability ladders/trees (e.g., must buy Martial Skill before Sword Specialty)
    * Reduction in routine handling elements (e.g., rolling to hit also figures damage--see Spread in Passages)
    * Improved skill challenge system (see Dual of Wits in BW, abductive investigation, and DitV conflict resolution)
    * Introduction of Keys/Beliefs/Flags aspect to XP system (see Artha Cycle in BW)
    * One must use a skill to improve it (see original CoC or BW skill advancement)
    * More emphasis on the plausibility of monsters (e.g., origins, ecological impact, social impact)
    * More advice or examples of the variety of "wildernesses," "caverns," and "dungeons" that one can explore (more adventure design advice, in general)
    * More attention to logistics of exploration (e.g., encumbrance, orienteering, navigation, equipment, food, and water)

    Basically, I think it could have more verisimilitude; and I would strive to make the "fiddly bits" for complex planning fun to actually engage while reducing the "fiddly bits" for simple tasks like causing blunt trauma with a bludgeon.

    \n Hey, I know the answer to this one! My (and Tony and Adam's) version of D&D is Dungeon World. Really, when I run 4E these days I find myself wishing I was playing the same game using the DW rules. I really feel like DW is what I'd do with D&D. To whit:\n
      \n
    • Move the resolution granularity up from single attacks to broader actions (moves).
      • \n
    • Give the GM a strong framework to work in beyond "it's a series of rooms."
      • \n
    • Allow more improvisation, especially in fights. I should be able to make a fight on the fly, without hours of prep.
      • \n
    • Keep the fun of levelling up and picking new cool stuff. Keep the 4E ideal of every level giving a choice.
      • \n
    • Make the fiction matter more, make fictional action a part of mechanical action.
      • \n
    \nAlso make it as open and collaborative as possible. Maybe because game design isn't a job for me, I don't mind if other people can create content for my game.

    Keep the ability to add new content (splat-book style stuff). Being able to write new classes, items, monsters, and GM moves is a great way to keep that same ongoing discovery feel that the constant stream of D&D books produces.

    \n This is an interesting question!

    I have a few different takes:

    The game I'd like to play:

    I'm designing this already! It's called Dungeonfuckers. It's Red Box D&D as if by David Cronenberg.

    The game I think would sell:

    Tough call. I think nostalgia and "the D&D way" are important to the game's success. To that end, I think something kind of baroque and arcane would be popular. No core system, strong class differentiation, unique rules for unusual situations, and strong support for the group to construct their own system in play would be key. You'd also need to keep the iconic six stats, most of the spells, and the d20 attack roll, I think, for it to "feel" like D&D. Dungeon World achieves a lot of those things.

    The game as I think it should be:

    I think the strength of D&D is in problem-solving. Obviously, people find different things fun about the game, and you can't please everybody, but I think that the problem solving aspect is what D&D does better than many other games. It's fun to learn dungeoneering skills as a player (checking for traps, memorising the abilities of monsters, approaching situations cautiously, bringing the right equipment, finding novel solutions), and then use those skills in a way that other people can appreciate. The difficulty is making a game that supports this without relying on the GM to adjudicate the thing unsupported. I think the game would become a set of guidelines and tools for the GM to use to resolve complex situations.

    \n I don't think you can recapture the past of D&D, any more than you can capture the real fun of tag or hide-and-seek that you had when a child. The rules were incomplete and because of that we had to make our own up. We filled in so many blanks it become Our Game. It was only when every rule was cross-referenced and footnoted that D&D lost most of what made it fun. It's not just that the game has changed, but we have as well. So, I am not sure you could recapture the innocence of the game we used to play.

    That being said, were I given D&D as a brand to rerelease, I would redo the game as a sort of advanced Mad-Libs with a few rules to fill in blanks. There would be a few examples, but mostly you'd have to come up with your own. As you grow in level, there are more and more blanks until you are completely telling the story yourselves. You would have a shared story and have come up with most of it without being told what to roll, what powers you can use, etc. Pretty lame, I admit, but to appeal to more people (and kids, I think this is key for D&D) you have to drop the idea that more skills, more weapons, more dice rolls, more rules make the game better.

    \n I think this thread is a testimony to the fact that there is no "one true D&D". Say D&D to ten different people and you'll get ten different games.

    Me, I would take the LBB/B/X books and put them in a proton collider together with the Essentials line. Forget everything that happened in between, go back to barebones D&D, the sheer fundamentals of what makes it what it is, then update it with a more rationalized framework and upgrade the basic ideas with some sleeker resolution mechanics and then put some Moves/Powers sugar on top.

    So a really lightweight, small tight ruleset for characters that leaves a lot to build upon. Then add subsystems/modules for three kinds of play: dungeoncrawling, sandbox hexcrawling, urban. Each subsystem would have a few rules and mechanics to support it but would mostly consist of frameworks and tools for the DM. It would be fairly easy to transition between them. I would fit all that into one book, a couple of hundred pages at the very most.

    Then I would add a big monster book to that, and an "Advanced" book that would expand on the original game in different ways, mostly with worthwhile bits of crunch. And then a spinoff game that would be more of a mini tactics/boardgame thing and would use the same monster book, but with some different assumptions/extra rules.

    Basically kinda what sage, John and Johnstone are saying, but with my own spin. On one hand a "basic" game that's more like oD&D + Apocalypse World with a dash of Essentials, on the other hand a minis spinoff that's more Essentials + Chainmail + Wrath of Ashardalon/Castle Ravenloft. Then a Danger Patrol + Gamma World product that's related, but not really D&D. It would be more card based.

    All three games would be obviously related and have some overlap between them, enough that you could even, say, use a few cards from GW in D&D or whatever, but they would be separate products (pure RP, minis/board, cards).

    That said, I am in fact writing my D&D heartbreaker. Everyone and their mothers is these days.

    \n I think I'd try to get back to the basics, and by that I mean of course "the way my group played the Red Box back in the days". PCs are adventurers who seek fame and fortune by getting rid of monsters plaguing the land.
    \n
    \nBackground-wise, I wouldn't detail too much, only provides informations that ties the setting with the themes and rules and let each table add their own fluff as they go along. I'd take a hint from Earthdawn : the land was once ruled by this great civilization of magic, science and wonder but it's long gone and all that's left are ruins filled with ancient artefacts and monsters. It's a good excuse for roaming the land, getting into dungeons and smashing orcs heads.
    \n
    \nRule-Wise, I'd keep the fighting mechanics at the core, but I'd streamline the hell out of what it has become. A fight with four PCs should last 15-20 min, tops. Another thing I'd keep are the Levels. The way you gain them would be streamlined too, but the goal of the game is to hoard gold and experience. I'd of course keep the Classes, maybe limit them to the Big Four with multiclassing for those who want something fancy (Cleric + Fighter = Paladin, for instance).
    \n
    \nAlso, when you gain Levels, you gain Power, both magic, fighting and temporal. I really liked that you were supposed, back in the days, to get a shitload of followers. I'd device a simple system that would feel like you have 50-something underlings at level 12, something that would both feel like you're in charge without the hassle of really managing each one of them as it was done.
    \n
    \nSo, it's a matter of scale. As the game progress, the basics stay the same but the scope widens. You're always, at every level, exploring ruins, killing things and taking their stuff but at level 1, you're a band of rag-tag mercenaries lost in a corridor who honk on that ork's head with a big metal stick. At level 12, you besiege the whole ruin and fight an army of orcs with your own followers. At level 1 you gain a magic sword. At level 12 you gain a magitek ballista. At level 1 you steal a gem. At level 12 you steal a city.
    \n
    \nI'd keep the thing uber-simple compared to modern incarnations of D&D.; Something clear-cut, more complex than, say 3:16 but less than Reign. I'd keep the funky dice because it feels D&D.; I'd try and make a simple way to prepare the game, up to the point of including rotating GM rules that shift the control form scene to scene.
    \n
    \nPresentation-Wise, I'd take a hint from the JRPG scene and include a big-ass Replay somewhere. Maybe I'd make a Replay comics instead, with each couple of pages explaining a topic of the game, which I'd put at the end of the corresponding chapter. And all that would fit in two or three booklets, with a combined page count of 90-120.
    \n
    \nMan, just talking about it makes me want to fire up my word processor...

    \n Dungeon World looks wonderful, I'd start with that.
