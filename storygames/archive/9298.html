    \n Run the game yourself. If you can't run it face-to-face, run it online. If you aren't running your game and talking about it, it's hard to get other people to run it.

    Talk about your game. Every time you post about your game, link to the rules and give a one- or two-line summary of what it's about. Learn to pitch your game.

    Talk about your game. Yes, it's important, so I said it twice. I don't mean injecting your game into every thread possible. I mean, find stuff in your game that you really want to talk about -- problems you have, actual play that was amazingly cool, and so on -- and share with others. We want to see that stuff. Make your posts intriguing. You are selling and entertaining at the same time.

    \n My success with playtesting has gone like this:

    1. Playtest a bunch with people I know.

    2. Playtest my game at local/regional cons like JiffyCon.

    3. Post about my game on my blog a lot and maybe some folks pick it up and try it out.

    4. Link on my game's website to any AP post or thread that gets started about my game. Track these through internet tools as well, in case I miss some.

    5. Encourage folks to run my games at region meetups, not just big cons. The Seattle/GPNW crew has been amazingly supportive of my stuff, even though none of it has been formally published yet.

    6. Give away free cheap photocopies of my game at GenCon. Not sure if this resulted in any AP, but maybe. It made sure people knew my game existed and was being beta tested.

    7. Make sure the electronic and print versions of my playtest document A) looked really nice (DO NOT underestimate this, see Lady Blackbird) and were easy to use. Sure, you could save layout until the last step before publishing, but why not do it earlier if it helps your game get more attention / be easier to run. I did layout for Brennan's playtest draft of How We Came To Live Here. I'd be glad to do layout for other playtest docs too, as long as they've been playtested locally first.

    8. Make sure the people who've been playtesting your game know when a new draft of the rules is out (beta, gamma, final, etc.)

    9. Most important of all, BE PATIENT. Geiger Counter has been in development since... let me check... April 2007. During that time it's had several early unreleased drafts, two playtest drafts (alpha, beta) with gamma on its way, and 11 independent playtests with the first ones happening in... let me check again... April 2008, a full year after I started working on the game! That's a whole year of investment before any outside validation from people who aren't people I've played with first-hand.

    10. Also, wading through outside playtesting can be tough, since you weren't there and have to get good at asking questions of playtesters that they can answer easily and give you useful information. If you just throw a string of questions at them, they may not respond at all or may give you short responses that aren't helpful. Just because outside playtesting is necessary, that doesn't make it easy or the end of the road as far as making sure your game works. If other people are playing your game, great. Does that mean it's ready for print? Your game is printed whenever you decide, in whatever form its in, so only you can make that choice.
