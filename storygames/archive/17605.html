    \n A Rashomon style game where you decide up front what happened because that's not the important stuff, and the rest of the game is interpreting and re-interpreting what happened through the perceptions and emotional landscapes of the protagonists. You could get even further away from the movie, which still pays a lot of attention to what happened, and not even let the players add new events (or key events) during the game, and instead they have to negotiate their experiences of those events. And probably sharing those experiences with each other changes them? Something like that.

    \n
    \n * what does it mean?
    * how do we feel about it?
    * does it change anything?
    * does anybody remember it 50 years from now?
    * how does it affect those who experienced it?
    * what is it like (look like, sound like, taste like, feel like)?
    * what soundtrack plays while it is happening?
    * what is happening in other parts of the world at the same time?
    * will it ever happen again?
    * what would it be like if it was an 8-bit video game instead?
    * how much does it suck?
    * yeah, but what happens now?
    * what do cats / dolphins / gorillas think about it?
    * is it better or worse than these other things?
    * is it more [insert your favorite adjective] than these other things?
    * who cares?
    * what does it look like from this other angle?
    * yeah, but what about penguins?

    \n Ars Armandi is a interesting piece of game design that not about deciding fictional outcomes, but to simulate a something you can't act out.

    I also like nice little designs like:

    If you ask a character a emotional question once, they may give whatever answer they feel like. but if you ask them twice you will have the real answer. For example

    "Are you angry me?"
    "I'm fine."
    "But are you angry at me?"
    "Yes. I'm angry at you. I hate you right now and I hate myself. We should not have killed the prisoners."

    \n A Grail Epoch gives each PC an inevitable fate, which we all know must happen to them in the end. There's no getting around that or avoiding it or rolling to see if it happens. But the game is about how your characters move toward that fate, even if they are fighting against it. (Durance's oaths are actually pretty similar, though not quite as binding.)

    Lots of nonlinear storytelling options in that sort of thing. I just started watching a show where every episode starts with the protagonist on trial for a long list of crimes. Then the show illustrates, episode by episode, his committing those crimes and the motion toward his inevitable trial.

    \n How to write a story. (Could come in handy when playing by forum.)

    How to get a flow in a collaborate game.

    How to create houserules, new cards and such.

    How to frame scenes.

    How to talk to affect other people.

    Understanding in how an economical principle works.

    Understanding about the human being through designed social experiments. ("Why would anyone choose to burn instead of converting into another religion?")

    Understanding about the human being through introducing the setting while playing.

    How to create active listening (so even if you're not in the scene, you still want to listen).

    et c.

    \n You can design rules for world, premise, setting and situation building. Even if some of these are provided by the designer, you can design rules for how to fill out the details, how to make it ours.

    You can design rules for what narrative control each player has. Can I say that it's raining? Can I say there's a knife on the table? Can I say what your character is feeling? Can I say what your character is feeling if we have sex? Who gets to set the next scene? Can I declare what happened 10 years ago if I sacrifice something now? Can dip into resolution, but I think it can be used for much more. More about creating in an orderly way than resolving.

    Then there's that fancy new blackbox concept, where playing is done on a stage and props like music, stage light, and masks are used to direct mood, which layer of fiction you're playing in, what character you currently are and more. I've yet to play any of it, unfortunately.

    I feel there should be more to this, but I'm coming up short for now. It needn't necessarily be a terribly esoteric topic.

    \n In this old Forge thread, I had a fun list of "other stuff" the players could possibly roll for in a railroad game where the GM determines things like the success and failure of actions. Here it is:

    1) Coloring action attempts and outcomes. The GM determines what happens, but the mechanics determine what it looks like.

    2) Resolving fictional positioning (possibly including effectiveness/resource), which feeds into subsequent situation. The GM says you win the fight, but the dice say you lost your sword, your eye, and the deed to your land. Now the next scenes aren't going to be about sniping and hacking and bossing around; they might be about going back for another deed and squabbling with nobles while your enemies gather strength, or about learning how to wrestle.

    3) Adding context. Adding flashbacks, cutaways, or other fictional content that adds context to the characters' actions and the GM's outcome.

    4) Manipulating the medium. Are the facts of the fiction described as if in a movie, or as if read from an ancient scroll? Do we speak loudly or quietly, all at once or in turns? Do we invoke key phrases or other rituals?

    5) Developing character. Beyond what the actions and cutaways show us, is there anything else the audience ought to see or know now about your guy?

    6) Resolving relationships between participants and fictional elements. Who controls which character, etc. (Heck, this could even include the recruiting of a 2nd GM, or a conspirator who knows a certain portion of the Story Before! Or a player getting to introduce a subplot! Or the GM upgrading NPCs to player-character status in the eyes of the mechanics!)

    7) Refining the possibility space for "what could happen" going forward, potentially including changes to the system for producing "what does happen". This could create new modes of play, or simply alternate between pre-designed ones.
