\n We're going to start a Sword and Sorcery Solar System game tomorrow where the characters are traveling to a distant land in order to escape the Post Apocalyptic Hell of their current home (Cormac McCarthy's "The Road" meets Robert E. Howard). Most of the characters have keys related to their quest (reaching the fabled West) or their party. I've not run many games where the group was traveling a great distance for the majority of the campaign and am struggling with techniques to prep.

An R-Map is basically worthless for such a scenario where the group will not stay put unless the relationships are also traveling. The R-Map and development of antagonism through complicated and de-evolving relationships is my sort of shtick and now I have nothing to lean on. Obviously I can just hit them with new situations and bangs which attempt to break up the party and tear apart the mission but I'm looking for perhaps some more sophisticated techniques or advice. Examples would be great too. Thanks.

    \n Build a challenge map. (mind you I am typing by the seat of my pants here)
    \n
    \nIt is kinda like an r-map but instead of just people (though there should be recurring people, and they kan be used as challenges) there are other antromorphic elements (the rust-storms, ruins of old days, the hordes that plague the lands). Tie these all to the the big hairy goal (the west) and then link them with one of the following links:
    \nAn obstacle that must be overcome
    \nHelp that can be won, but there is a problem to be overcome
    \nHelp that is freely given but at a price
    \nthen tie them to each other, or write up other elements. Find three links that fit the kind of narrative you are trying to build (in r-map it is blood, sex and money, here it could be food/water, magic, apocalypse).
    \nFor your own sake tie a few standard assosiated obstacles to the elements as resources the element can "use" to further its agenda against the BHG.
    \nThen play these things like you would play an npc in an r-map.
    \nTie them up to the playercharacters' keys and hit them for what it is worth.
    \n
    \nby the way, this is just an idea, so it needs some more thinking.

    \n Okay, so a while ago, I ran an Unknown Armies game that was an odyssey. The characters started in Philadelphia and were headed cross-country to Seattle. In their possession, an infant child with unexplained power. In pursuit...well, everyone. At least, so it seemed to the two PCs, neither of whom were initiates in the Occult Underground. But there were people in Seattle who would be able to keep the child safe.

    Each adventure then ended up having something of a pattern:

    1) Recap what had happened last session.

    2) Play out the characters in the car/van/whatever vehicle they had currently liberated. This was essentially a period of reflection on what had come before, but in-character. This is where we'd reveal the changes that the characters had undergone because of the last session.

    3) Thing intervenes. And...go! Usually, "go" meant "survive what's coming at you while protecting the infant", though sometimes it meant "figure out how to feed yourselves and buy diapers for the child with $20. Oh yeah, plus buy gas to keep moving". Given that the child was essentially a beacon of magical power, this was easy to explain and embrace.

    4) Lather, rinse, repeat.

    Of course, the greatest moment of the campaign was when the PCs decided to abandon the quest to protect the child....

    The campaign was technically episodic with recurring villains. However, the *real* story of the game was about the evolving relationships between the PCs and their ongoing acts of sacrifice to protect this child.

    Hopefully this helps.

    Seth Ben-Ezra
    Great Wolf

    \n Can you have people they've left behind? Memories that haunt them? Secrets that will have to come out? A lot of people along the way will want things from them, hindering their progress - find out what will make them have to make hard choices or sacrifices. Or disagree with one another, whether about methods or principles. And maybe the West doesn't want folks from Mutationville stinking up the place, and catches wind of their efforts, and acts to counter. But then they're divided about these heroes or whatever. Human relationships are all around on the road and what you walk away from doesn't always leave you alone. Where you're going may not be all it's cracked up to be. Progress may rely on getting involved in other people's problems. Sorry, not chock full of actual examples.

    \n Travel can be a metaphor for self-transformation, each challenge reflecting character development, a lesson or realization that must be learned or accepted. For this approach to succeed, characters must begin somewhere. Let me present the approach I took in a homebrew game called Bandele (Born Away From Home).

    What a character finds nostalgic about the place from which he or she came is their "root."

    What drove a character to leave his or her home is called "wind."

    What hope the character hopes to find through travel is his or her "wings."

    While all of these things are set at character creation, they were all malleable during play. They did all have mechanical definitions and effects. The struggle to change them was also handled mechanically, too. The precise system for doing so would not likely fit your game, but I found it was important that they did have mechanics, because then challenges could be set up against them.

    \n A few random challenge ideas:
    - getting low on resources with no civilization in sight (play out the survival, don't just allow a roll)
    - a town mistrusting of strangers
    - border guards demanding "authorization to travel" papers, and the demands of whatever patrons might aid in obtaining those papers
    - hard bargains with travelling merchants
    - bandits (who should always offer as part of their initial threat to leave the characters with enough to get to the next town, and who love to steal money, but not as much in the way of bulky goods)
    - odd items used as currency on the road (tea bricks were common in China) making currency exchanges difficult
    - offers to work as smugglers
    - wolves or other beastly dangers
    - trade opportunities (some of which may turn out to be pitfalls, as well as taxation problems and guilds that try to enforce monopolies)
    - territorial spirits and gods (especially if your setting is multi-pantheonistic)
    - a washed-out bridge (do you look for another way or try to fix this path?)

    I really suggest seeking out and watching the television series Wolf and Spice.

    \n I really like Kaare's idea, which I basically read as 'make a relationship map that is not constrained to people' -- kind of like a one-step-abstracted relationship map, where the thing on the map could manifest as a person in any given encounter, but could also manifest as an event or as a thematic challenge. Or where it could manifest as another person who has no actual relationship to the first other than that they both are manifestations of the larger concept.

    The key to making it a relationship map is to a) map out interdependancy between those one-step-abstracted concepts, and b) have them react organically, in the same way a single person might react, and spin those reactions into all later manifestations.

    So you know, ultra-simple example, a love triangle relationship between Wind, Starvation, and Children. Both Starvation and the Wind are competing for the love of Children, who begins the game equally enamored of them both. Also, Wind is Starvation's father, and so feels responsible for Starvation, which complicates the relationship.

    So Wind could manifest as a dust storm, or maybe someone yearning for travel or distant news; it could seek the love of Children by killing them, or by making them into messengers, or by telling them secrets or leading them to secret places. Starvation could manifest straightforwardly or in more complex political situations involving the distribution of food; the ultimate consummation with Children would presumably involve children starving to death, but it could also involve children gaining power by starving others, some kind of spiritual emaciation, etc. Children are the easiest to manifest -- any child that appears in the story is a manifestation of Children that resonates back to the R-map.

    Anyways, the point being that if the PCs encounter a starving town, where the adults are at death's door because they give what little food they have to their children -- you can see how solutions or reactions to this problem could resonate back and affect the overall relationship.

    So let's say the next encounter is all about a wandering child who has visions in the wilderness -- if the PC's reaction to the first encounter involved a child starving to death, then perhaps this wandering child is now starving, and no longer having visions. But if the PC's reaction to the first encounter was to save the children but not the adults, the wandering child is healthy and friendly.

    --

    Anyways it is a clumsy example, particularly because the PCs are not central to my relationship map -- but if you imagine that one PC is a child, and another is a farmer or the priest of a fertility god, you can see how it starts to look more and more like your usual R-map.

    More concretely, you can make an r-map for the game, you just need to talk in terms of larger entities.

    \n

    In other words, you can make a map. But not just a geographical map. You create settlements and peoples and define the relationships between them; the folks in Red Ridge want salted fish from Johannaborg, but the Johannaborgers don't just have fish to sell because they're in tax disputes with the king in Ingridsmeade, but if you want to get some food on the black market...

    \n

    Roads make links between the nodes on your map here, except where particular people live directly next to one another, so you're effectively having the characters traverse the map of the relationships and conflicts that happen around them.

    \n

    You can probably even consider a 'journey' from one node to another to be a basic unit of time, and say like, "So if you kill the king in Ingridsmeade, it takes two journeys for the news to spread and become well-known in each adjacent town, and so on outward, so if you move fast you can escape your reputation of being a cadre of regicides, but don't meander or dawdle!"
