If you're playing a game where the main question is "How does your character change today, what do they become?" then the adversity that the GM provides should (I think!) be about trying to make them become something other.

Having done some playtests, I will say that it is hard to get that across if you don't have an in-character agent who is deliberately pursuing that goal. In the same way that DitV is easier when you ask "What does this character want the Dogs to do or decide?" I suspect that Misery Bubblegum will be easier when I ask "What does this character want protagonist X to become?"

But I have only a fuzzy, unexamined sense of what motivates person A to want to make person B into something other than what they were this morning. I can give specific instances (you are vying with Jesse for a limited resource (say, the love of Eddie) and that motivates you to want her to be a bad person), but I don't have any words for the overall pattern. And, until I have those words, it's going to be very hard for me to code that pattern into an NPC generator that lets the GM make NPCs that will, by their nature, provide the right adversity for the protagonists.

So ... why do people do this? Why does a girlfriend want to "discover" that her boyfriend is secretly a romantic (i.e. change who he explicitly is by either bringing forward hidden traits or inventing the traits wholesale)? Why does a bitter man want to make naive and happy people bitter like him? Why does a wise soul want to help others find redemption?

    You want a word that speaks to why character X wants character Y to change? I think that's going to have to be somewhat of a synthetic term, since it's so specific here.

    The simplest would be: Change Motive

    But that could apply to oneself. How about : Alteration Impetus

    I don't think that there's one general urge to try to change other people. In fact, given that it's really hard to actually change another person, I think that people generally don't try. Hardly ever. What's happening in these examples, in fact, aren't really being thought of by the changer as changes. That is, the changer doesn't really care if you become X, they really only want whatever their motive is. To that extent, it's fine with them if you simply temporarily engage in the behavior they need to you engage in. Which may constitute a change in a moral sense to some extent, but isn't what people think of as a change on a day to day basis.

    If I make an exception to my moral code, and steal a small item, sure, on one level I've changed. I've become a person who has now made this one exception. But often exceptions prove the rule. If I then feel guilty for my infraction, have I really changed? That is, as a predictor of future action, am I now more or less prone to the behavior in question? That can't be answered merely by looking at the act in question, but if you changed internally in response.

    "Oh, that was fun stealing - I think I'll drop that rule against it, and steal all the time now!"
    "Oh, that sucked, stealing - I vow never to do it again."

    A person who tries to get you to steal isn't trying to get either of these particular outcomes. They just want you to steal. So their motive isn't to change you, it's to do X. Which happens, in this case, to be a catalyst for change, sure. But you shouldn't probably call it a Change Motive. Instead you could call it something like Change Catalyst Motive.

    Of course there are exceptions. My wife harps on me doing the dishes in more timely fashion in an ever ongoing campaign to change me into somebody who does the dishes in a more timely fashion without having to be bugged into doing them. So actual "Change Motives" do exist. I just think that they're pretty rare, and that mostly what a person wants is just to have clean dishes. My wife would be satisfied if I increased my income and got a maid to do the dishes (if such a thing exists).

    So I think that the "adversity" that creates can create these changes is simply serendipitous. That in most cases, there's really no direct motive to change a person. Even if it's validation, again, all the person wants is to be able to look at the other person and say, "See, you stole once, I'm not bad for stealing." They don't need the other person to become a thief for validation (though it might be extremely validating for that to happen). In fact a person might be horrified to learn in validating their own rules that they've changed a person into something unintentionally. "Oh, I just wanted you to steal once! I never thought you'd turn into a full-time theif!"

    Being as the motives may have more to do with eliciting a short-term behavior, and not be intended to produce a permanent change (though they might, in fact, do that even on a small scale), I think you don't have a single nameable type of motive. As such, you're going to have to make up a name.

    Mike

    Permit me to change the viewpoint and spout a little psychobabble in the process, but only because I think it would be insightful.

    I remember reading that Eric Berne (the father of Transactional Analysis and script theory) believed that people are looking for other people to become actors in their life scripts. In particular, he thought that children grow up believing they should recreate their parent's relationships, as they understand them, casting themselves in the role of the parent they feel most similar to, and seeking out partners who resemble either the other parent, or the partners their role model sought out. Children of alcoholic parents seek out alcoholics or become alcoholics, switching roles if necessary. Children of abusive parents become abusive parents, or seek a mate who will become abusive. It's not necessarily masochism; sometimes it's just seeking out the familiar, other times it's a misguided attempt to prove the original relationship was fixable.

    Now, ditch the psychobabble and generalize it: when two people form a relationship, they think of it in terms of a relationship they remember seeing before. It could be their parents' relationship, or their mother's relationship with a sibling, or even one of their own relationships, or an idealized relationship from a book or movie. So, Teacher X sees Student Y, who reminds her of herself as a young child; Teacher X recasts herself as her own mother and tries to "fix" the old relationship by becoming a better version of her mother, either by taking her mother's goals as her own and succeeding where she failed, or by being the mother she wished her own mother had been. Either way, the teacher has a script written out for Student Y that Student Y might not want to play a part in; maybe she doesn't want to be Teacher-Y-as-a-little-girl.

    Student Y may also meet Boy Z, who's a little older than her. Boy Z reminds Student Y of her older brother, whom she was fiercely competitive with, and so begins to relive that relationship, trying to beat her "older brother". But maybe Boy Z looks at Student Y and thinks about a romance in some movie, and tries to recast the relationship as that script. At best, the two are going to interpret each other's actions as fulfilling the script they're following; at worst, neither one will live up to the other's expectations.

    What you might be able to distill out of this is a way to frame an NPC's desires for what they want a PC to become in terms of who the NPC becomes when the PC reminds the NPC of someone else.

    Well, I'm all for the original decision emerging from player wants, but I am not so thrilled with the idea of continually rewriting the characters during play to more closely fit those wants. I'm not sure whether that's what you were implying. I tried it that way, and I think that coding it into the NPC's personality makes it much easier on the GM. Yes, it means that the NPC cannot be suddenly rewritten ... but if you do this right in the first place, why would you need to rewrite them during the course of a one hour session?

    The benefit (and the one that shows so well with its Town Creation system) is that once you have all of the behavior of the system you want in place coded into the characters you're ready to play then you can just play. Playing the characters with integrity will always be the same thing as driving that system in the ways you built it to be driven. So you've got easy access to the problem from two angles.

    If I just say "I want Edward to loosen up and be less of a stick in the mud" then I have to figure out at every turn "Why would Shary want to do something to make Edward less of a stick in the mud?" Until I figure that out, I can't just do the first thing that comes to mind that Shary "would do," because it might run directly counter to my goals.

    By contrast, if I say "Shary finds Edward's stuck up attitude amusing, and wants to show that deep down he can be a party animal like everyone else," then I don't have to think as hard. Edward comes in saying "Tut, tut ... such brash behavior, Ms. Ventura," and Shary staggers over, flops a limp arm around his shoulders, loosens his tie and presses a beer on him. "Oh, Eddie," she says, "Yer funny. Have a drink! Lighten up!" That's the first thing I come up with that she would do, and I no longer have to think about any of the rest of it, because everything she could possibly do (without violating her integrity as a character) is going to fit with my goals.

    Follow-up: I used this to prep for a game last night, and I like the results.

    Cassandra (the PC) is a moody, emotionally crippled young girl (yes, those who know my friends know that I was playing with Sydney). She had, in a previous game, done all sorts of terrible things to Jessica, a sweet young kid who just wanted to be friends. So this time Cassandra wanted to somehow make it up to Jessica ... WITH CUNNING PLANS! She would escape the school and go to the mall, there to find The Perfect Gift which would be so awesome that, once presented to Jessica, it would force her against her will to sincerely forgive Cassandra.

    I made up a new NPC, Lucrezia, who was a goth-rebel-angst-monkey. I decided that she wanted "Lucrezia and Cassandra" to be "Two misfits who are superior to the stupid mundanes." From that point on, things got very easy. Just playing Lucrezia as written was constantly jamming Cassandra up against the questions she needed to be asking herself. Like, Lucrezia helped her break out of school (setting most of it on -fire- in the process), and that was cool. Cassandra liked that. Cassandra liked the teamwork, and having somebody who liked her for her strangeness. The role that Lucrezia wanted her to play out was attractive.

    But then, when Cassandra wanted to go to the mall to try to find The Perfect Apology Gift for Jessica, Lucrezia was very down on the idea. "Look, this Jessica girl, she's ... NORMAL. You don't want to hang out with her. You're better than that. Besides, you'll never make her happy. She won't understand you." It was lovely and horrific, because both Sydney and Cassandra had to concede the truth of every single statement, but still ... Cassandra didn't want to be that person. She wanted to change into a person who would no longer easily fit into Lucrezia's image of the two, and so they were just naturally tugging back and forth every time they talked.

    I think that I got that conflict by luck and/or intuition. I could just as easily have made NPCs who wanted her to fill roles all of which she could easily reconcile, and then the magic wouldn't have occurred. So there's still some stuff missing here, but I think it's on the right track.
