As of yet I have never played a game of 4th ed and I think I want to get a bi-weekly game going with my friends. The problem I have is the play space we will be using at a friends house while nice and comfy there is no room for mini's.     \n Positioning is half the game in 4th edition. I suggest trying to make a map of some sort work. Do you have a lack of space because the table is small or because everybody sits in different parts of the room and there is no common table at all? If the former, consider drawing the map on paper and just marking positions with the pen, drafting over things and recopying as necessary. It'll be messy, but you can throw out the paper as it becomes unintelligible, and you don't need to be as exact as a battlemap set-up would be: just mark down approximate positions, draw arrows to indicate movement and so on. You might even consider using pins instead of figures to save space; get some paper with very small squares and use that with different colored pin-heads; a typical battle should fit easily in space smaller than a character sheet. You could even pass the situation around for players to look at if you pin the map on a suitably pincushiony writing board.

    If you don't have a table at all, then do the pin-table thing I suggested there and have the map make the rounds, or get a portable video projector and project the entire battle set-up on a wall/ceiling/floor. The DM (or a dedicated cartographer) can keep the map up to date easily enough on a laptop, while the players call out their actions to him.

    \n The graph paper on cardboard with pins is a good idea.

    Another, tech heavy, option might be to use Photoshop or Excel or some other program and display it on a monitor or TV screen that everyone can see. You can change the Excel spreadsheet to be square grids, and either cut and paste images or just write in names and make it text based (you could also add in conditions or HP tracking this way). In Photoshop, you just have one layer with the grid, and then use the other layers for various pieces. Both of these let you pre-make maps to cut down time, without limit, although making your own during the game is an option, seems like it could be quite slow if you are not comfortable with the program in question.

    \n You should be able to find presentation-sized pads of graph paper with 1" squares on them at any office supply superstore. You can lay them up on an easel and do an overhead view of the battle by setting it up in a corner of the room. Put a fidgety player ib charge of moving everyone's markers. Pins or those small post-it tags would work fine, as would buying a few sheets of cuttable magnetic backing from a craft store for your chits/markers/tokens and then tacking or clipping the grid to a metal surface for display. Snag a few cool images from ddi or whatever and you're good to go.

    Abstract distance really do not work with the system as written. Movement of single squares is disproportionately important to the tactical play.

    -Jim C.

    \n When it comes to the necessity of the map in 4th edition, a lot depends on the overall attitude the group has towards the rules set. If the players engage primarily with the rules as a tool of overcoming challenges, then fudging the map just took away 80% of the meaning in their play. On the other hand, if the players primarily concern themselves with fictional strategy and tactics, then I could see DM-imposed fudging, just like Dev describes. Then again, playing 4th edition with a fiction first attitude is pretty much missing the point of the game, it seems to me.

    An easel is a nice idea, although it'd need to be large enough for the players to see it comfortably at whatever distance they'd sit. A video projector (they're getting to be pretty cheap nowadays, you know, and they're useful for this sort of gaming) has the advantage of copious graphics that help distinguish things from each other on the map.

    \n Another easel-ish option: get a dry-erase whiteboard, paint grid lines on it (or draw them with a permanent marker). You can even find magnetic dry-erase boards, which let you use little magnets for characters and slide them around easily.

    I do agree that from what I've seen of 4e, a lot of the fun of several classes and many, many powers is heavily dependent on position. You can half-ass it and make positioning more abstract, but the result will be to make those classes and powers less appealing (and probably less useful). This may not be a deadly flaw for some groups, but it's something to consider before doing it. You'll end up having to rebalance some things on the fly, for one thing.

    \n Somewhere or other someone suggested dividing battlefields into abstract areas (so the evil god's shrine might have Entrance Hall, Main Chamber, Altar Steps, Altar, etc.). Some things like basic movement, area effects, and flanking could translate over pretty easily, but I think it'd take some pretty extensive hacking to make it really work solidly.

    For a while in my group we were just setting up a card table with the DM and map and such in the middle of the room. For the role-playing oriented parts it didn't really matter that most of the group is scattered around the room, and during battles we'd head over to the table or tell the DM or closer players what we want to do. It's not the best arrangement, and I'm glad my friend finally got the leaves out of storage to make his dining room table big enough for everyone to sit around.

    Hi Quinn! I'm more interested now that I hear it was actually played/playtested, honestly. How did the players receive it?

    If I'm intimidated by Fluid 4e, it's because there's a new parallel set of mechanics - move save / move advantage - to be rolled for and tracks, and that I'd have to teach to players. Moving becomes something uncertain (like combat) requiring rolling and mechanical handling, rather than a predictable kind of basis on which to make your less-predictable attack. On the other hand, the collapsing of movement-squares into bands of movement seems simple to me. I think I'm comparing the system you propose to my idealized sensed of how it work if players described what they wanted to accomplish vs the DM's reckoning, and those precise squares of movement are just part of the players' justifications (that they could move 30' without trouble, or that they could push a foe 10' on a hit).
