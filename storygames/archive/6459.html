    \n Heya Ajax!

    The [1] [2] thing is just an unofficial-yet-soon-to-be-official rule for the "Stuff To Watch" monthly threads. I realized it was in the March and April threads (it was just "developed" recently), but not in the April thread, so I added it to Judd's first post here:

    http://www.story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=6411

    Here's the rule:

    "Feel free to respond here in-thread to another link posted.
    - If you're looking to start a conversation, perhaps take your comment and create a new thread with it in the Stuff to Watch category.
    - Otherwise, to prevent too many comments in the thread, number your response (first response: [1]; second response [2]; third response [3]). If you see that you're the second person to post, "Wow, that looks cool!" to a post here, then put a "[2]" at the top of your post. Three posts ([3]) is the limit: The fourth person to post should just take their comment and create a new thread, to carry the discussion over there further."

    So what is the "rule" for?

    Basically, the Stuff To Watch is a great series of threads: Every month, as little news articles, or clips, or data bits, or one-line promos happen, they're a way to collect those things in sort of a "feed-like" bloggy manner. It's stuff that we want to share with other gamers here, but it's probably too small of news to discuss it in its own special thread.

    However, some folks noted that some of these "stuff to watch" elements would spawn discussions: Some folks just want to peek in and see the Latest Hot Small Thing, and instead would have to wade through 15-20 posts of conversation that weaves in and out around those posts.

    So this method (ONLY for the Stuff to Watch threads, it is not needed for any other kind of thread) means that you can still comment on those Stuff To Watch posts... to a limited degree. If a post deserves a "wow!" or "Hey, that also reminds me of X" or whatever, then cool, you can drop that info. Just put a [1] in your reply, or [2] if you're the second responder, etc. [3] is the "soft limit", at that point we've found that maybe the topic is interesting enough to people to warrant its own thread. So the fourth person who wants to reply should go ahead and create a new thread on that topic, add their comments, and carry the discussion further.

    News on the upcoming 4th Edition D&D was like that: News came in bits and bites, so people would post in the Stuff to Watch thread that month, but the news elements were interesting enough that dozens of people had something to weigh in on. That's what [1], [2], [3], ("OK, time to take it to its own thread") is all about.

    \n Ryan hit on it in brief. Personally, I am always mixed on anonymity. On other boards I see that the people who snap the loudest are the ones who treat others not as people but as little digital characters. Also, no matter how hard BUTTMASTER666 wants to be taken seriously, it's a little hard to.

    Now, there is ABSOLUTELY NOTHING REQUIRING you to ditch anonymity. If you want to remain anonymous and post, that's totally 100% fine, and you're not breaking any mores. Some folks are just absolutely uncomfortable with talking to people who don't use their real name, but that's not your problem, it's theirs. So if someone says, "Hey, uh.. BUTTMASTER666? What's your first name?" you don't have to respond or anything.

    Now, your account profile can be seen by other members of the forum. I have at least one friend here who goes by a pseudonym handle, and even his "First and Last name" in his user profile (that you would see if you mouse over his name) is the same as his pseudonym. Or, the above BUTTMASTER666 could use MASTER and 666 as first and last names, respectively (BUTT would be title, perhaps).

    If you want to reply to Valamir aka Ralph Mazza, it's totally fine to reply to him as Valamir. Or Ralph. Folks who have known him online for years know that Valamir is Ralph. And people who haven't met him? Feel free to use whichever you wish. Lukzu? Luke or Lukezu is totally fine. If someone wants other people to call them something, they'll let them know.

    So again, there's no protocol here about how to call people, not even in a "Well, it's a hidden but known rule that you should call people by their X names" or anything of the sort. And I agree, sometimes it's difficult to follow: I don't know everyone here myself, so sometimes I have to mouse-over to see which person was "Jason" that the latest poster was referring to. But in the end, it allows people to keep themselves anonymous if they want, to be open if they want, or to do the "I have a handle, but my name is known as well" thing if they want to, too. The best of all worlds, and only minor confusion.

    \n In my case, I don't use my handle because of the anonymity. I don't care much about that really. It is more of a practical habit. I started using it when I first joined a forum about 6 years ago. And everyone I met got to know me by that name. So I got used to it. Since then, I have always used it in any forum I join. I have no problem with people addressing me by name (even while sometimes it does feel a little odd to me).

    About "formal" and "informal" talking, it is a little more complicated in Spanish. You see, we have two different "voices" for casual and formal talk. (Something similar happens in Japanese.) The thing is that before people used the formal talk for every person they met, and reserved the casual talk for close friends and family, when of a similar age (while talking to parents, uncles and the like, the formal talk was also the norm).

    As time passed, people started reserving the formal talk for working or formal environments, and when talking to elders. It was a common practice to speak in the formal voice until the person granted you permission to use the casual one ("puedes hablarme de tu").

    Nowadays, more and more people use the casual talk (even in working environments). Many people argue that the formal talk make them feel old. So as long as you are talking to "young" people (50 years old or younger), most people use casual.

    Anyway... You might refer to me as Dami\xe1n, Mr. Holmes. That is ok with me. I would feel weird if anyone called me Mr. Fraustro (that is my dad!). Besides, my last name is really weird, and most people mispronounce it. And I'd rather have them telling me Dami\xe1n than misspelling my last name.

    \n When I first started communicating online, via a non-internet chat program in college, I started with a handle. I freaked out when someone guessed who I was.

The chat was something you logged into directly, not run from your account, but your chat ID (which could be separate from your handle, in fact, you could choose a different handle each time) had to be tied to a computer account. Then they added a feature where a specific text file in your computer account could be displayed from the chat program. People put all sorts of things there, but a bunch of people put basically a resume, with their real name. Then a group of people started hosting an invitation only channel and invited people who had their real names in their text file. Then someone was making a big pot of pea soup, and invited people in the channel to dinner. The power of online social networking sparking face to face social networking was so awesome that I lost all fear of having an open identity.
