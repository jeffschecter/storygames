    \n Here's the Primetime Adventures hack I wrote several years back:\n
    Here's how you play Scott Pilgrim using Matt Wilson's Primetime Adventures.

    Volumes

    Pick a number of Volumes you're going to play out. The real Scott Pilgrim has 7 [six, it turns out], but that may be too many, if you want something focused, or two few, if you'd rather do something as involved as Ranma 1/2.

    Characters

    Your series will be centered around a main protagonist who we'll call "Scott," even though they can be called anything you want in your game. All the other PCs must be connected to Scott in some way, but it doesn't have to be directly. If one player is Scott's gay brother Jimmy, an art school student, another player can play Jimmy's middle school boyfriend/rival, Howard, a record store clerk. If it's not obvious already, all the characters should be hipsters or other jaded cool-losers between 15-30.

    Character names are always ridiculous. I'm not sure how to describe the right kind of ridiculous. Remember this is a hipster power fantasy and I think you'll do fine. A banal or classic-but-unusual first name followed by something that's not really a last name is a good call. If they sound a bit like bad James Bond characters, you know you've nailed it. Edward Gunslinger. Ginger Soir\xe9e. Rick Adroit. Meredith Flagship.

    The Premise

    The premise of your game should fit the following format:

    [PROTAGONIST] must [DRAMATIC VERB] [A PC]'s [NUMBER OF VOLUMES] [MALEVOLENT GENRE FICTION COMICS TRAIT] [BANAL AUTOBIOGRAPHICAL INTROSPECTIVE SAD HIPSTER COMICS STAPLE]


    For example, in the real Scott Pilgrim series, the premise is:

    Scott must defeat Ramona's seven evil ex-boyfriends.

    But in your series, Alicia Excelsior, the premise might be:

    Alicia must exorcise Robert's 18 demonic step-fathers.


    Scott

    Scott is always the Spotlight Character of any particular Volume, but he does not have a potency of 3 like normal. Instead, Scott as 0 potency and is effective in conflicts thanks solely to Fanmail, either Fanmail he earns or Fanmail that is spent on his behalf.

    That's It!

    Play and be merry! The rest of PTA should work pretty well just as it is.

    If we go by the comics, rather than the movie (which I'll do, because the comic was awesome), there's actually a considerable amount of, if not wuxia, then at least weirdness, going on all the time in Scott's world. For example, Wallace (Scott's cool gay roommate) has a psychic boyfriend who shows up briefly in volume 5. Michael Comeau (the guy who tells Scott Ramona's name) gets a spooky talking skull ring from the future.

    So I'm thinking maybe there's weird stuff going on all the time, but possibly it doesn't kick into high gear until some emotional drama happens.

    I thought about 4E myself, but it would require So Much Reskinning! Or perhaps not. Just as the game world accepts, without question, that there is the intrusion of video-game/dramatic logic into mundane relationships, with various implied metaphors; in that case, the structures of tabletop fantasy RPGs (with some video game infusion) can be the structure used for the interpersonal metaphors. ("Wow, Jane's new boyfriend is Half Warlock?! That's... that makes sense, actually.")

    So Scott Pilgrim 4E: frame RP scenes (a la JWalt's PTA mod) + skill challenges where creative/ironic use of your D&D powers give you bonuses, and basically freely use whatever mechanics you can to comment on the events that happen. (I will totally make you take Falling damage when you get kicked out of the band.) Create some "banter" powers or maybe some custom Themes a la Dark Sun to add some interpersonal drama to a combat, and limit it to one or two battlemap encounters per session; these should have multiple axes of fictional stakes / conditions that matter / difficult conditions. (Alternately: you can only use a power once you've used it in a RP/noncombat situation and tied it to some personality traits.)

    Also, I had a blog post about Scott Pilgrim and D&D, or basically how abrupt changes of context within a game/narrative isn't necessarily a problem.

    Now I really am obsessing, but that's what I do. So, here's a proposal for a Scott Pilgrim/Danger Patrol hack.
    Styles will be based on typical young adult archetypes: slackers, gamers, preppies, etc.
    The Roles and the abilities they represent will totally be based on actual video games (names subject to change with a good suggestion, since my video game knowledge is limited nowadays):
    Crazy Taxi (replacing Flyboy) for vehicular prowess and mechanical talent.
    Guitar Hero (replacing Professor) for general coolness and musical performance.
    Dungeon Master (replacing Explorer) for giving directions, as well as finding trouble and strange things.
    Ninja Gaiden (replacing Agent) for sneaking, dirty tricks, and stabbing people in the kidney.
    Smash Bros. (replacing Warrior) for beating things up with fists, hadokens, and assorted objects.
    Team Fortress (replacing Commando) for group tactics and shooting things (likely with NERF guns...)
    To Heart (replacing Detective) for expertise in relationships, gossip, and group dynamics.
    Tony Hawk (replacing Daredevil) for physical stunts, skating, and other sport-like activities.

    I'm also thinking that the Style would provide only stuff, and the Role would provide one specific power. Then characters would then get to draw a card with other Stuff (3 or 4 other items, sayings, motivations...), and two cards with other Power. Or perhaps add a third descriptive category, such as Personality or Hooks, that will determine additional stuff and powers, just to add flavor?

    \n Ok, y'all are gonna have to bear with me. I just saw the movie over last weekend at a friend's house, played a couple stages of the video game, and then rented the movie and watched it three more times. I can't stop thinking about this movie, and I want to talk about it to other people so much that it's starting to get on their nerves. I went online and ordered the whole damn series of books.

    So, naturally, I'ma have to weigh in on this now.

    First thing:\n
    The Premise

    The premise of your game should fit the following format:


    [PROTAGONIST] must [DRAMATIC VERB] [A PC]'s [NUMBER OF VOLUMES] [MALEVOLENT GENRE FICTION COMICS TRAIT] [BANAL AUTOBIOGRAPHICAL INTROSPECTIVE SAD HIPSTER COMICS STAPLE]



    For example, in the real Scott Pilgrim series, the premise is:


    Scott must defeat Ramona's seven evil ex-boyfriends.


    But in your series, Alicia Excelsior, the premise might be:


    Alicia must exorcise Robert's 18 demonic step-fathers.
    \nNo offense meant, Jonathan, but as awesome and funny as that is, it's not quite right.

    Here's the thing. Scott Pilgrim is fantasy fiction. As we all know (or should know by now, as I've said it dozens of times already and so has Paul Czege), fantasy is born of the friction between intense, often irrational desire and the TOTAL, UTTER DISAPPOINTMENT that is the real world.

    I can tell you from experience that dating someone and dealing with the baggage they've accumulated from past relationships feels a helluva lot like having to battle their seven evil exes. And it's hard, dammit. I would so love if it could be handled as video game bossfights. 'Cause I'm GOOD at video games! (BAM, fantasy.)

    You see where I'm going here? That madlib is cool but it isn't where you need to start. You have to take a real-world, emotionally-complex problem that people (especially late-teens-to-20-somethings) have to tanglewith, then transform that into a video game-esque goal, with stages and bosses.

    Second thing:
    Scott Pilgrim's story isn't battling the seven evil exes. That's not the big-C Conflict in his dramatic arc. His conflict is Scott vs. Negascott. His story is the process of changing himself from a cad (an awkward, nerdy cad, but a cad nonetheless) into someone who is capable of emotional sincerity, love, and self-respect. The whole League of Evil Exes is just a trial that gives him the opportunity to improve in the ways he needs to. Until he develops those qualities, he REALLY IS just another evil ex waiting to happen.

    Take note that the villains are all COMPLETELY self-satisfied, with no desire or motivation to improve or otherwise change in any fashion. They got powerful and decided they didn't need to level up anymore; they can stay as they are and just take what they want. It is the heroes' capacity and desire to self-improve that enables them to defeat the villains. That goes for all the heroes, even if the movie doesn't have time to play their arcs out. Keep in mind that Ramona moved to Toronto to separate herself from her past so she could begin to correct her character flaws. Also note that Matthew Patel hasn't progressed since 7th grade -- and he is therefore the least of the villains.

    Also take note that defeating enemies doesn't level Scott up. That only happens when improves himself as a person.

    So a Scott Pilgrim (-esque) RPG needs to be about that. That's also why it needs to be late-teens-to-20-somethings, when people are obtaining their first independence and agency, and must shape themselves into the people they need to be or else wind up either a loser or evil (and people turn evil because they want to be effective). So the game needs to provide A.) a conflict that requires the protagonist(s) to build character or else turn evil or into a loser, B.) a system by which that change can be enacted, and C.) a trial (or set thereof) that provides an opportunity to resolve A via B, derived via the thing I said in the First Thing. The question is whether they will use that opportunity to change themselves for the better (and will it be enough?), or whether they'll give up/fail, and why.

    Finally:
    You guys, man. I know that coming up with stat schema is fun all by itself, but you're very unlikely to come up with something useful if you do that first. You oughtn't start designing system before you've got a handle on character, setting, color, and MOST IMPORTANTLY situation -- which, in the case of story games, means big-C Conflict. Once that's in place, then it's time to start looking at system. Even if you're just hacking, you don't start even considering which games you might hack until you get to that point.

    Given how blown away I am by Scott Pilgrim, and how deeply and directly it speaks to me personally, and the fact that I already went to the trouble to write this much already, I'm gonna take my own crack at it. I've got some ideas, but I'ma develop them a bit before posting about them. If you know me, you know I'm not one for hacking -- nothing ever quite fits enough for me (I'm going through this thread like, "PTA? Close but nah. Bliss Stage? Cool but not it at all" and so on) -- but rather for ground-up design. But expect some out-loud thinking, or even a set of scratch rules, for my Scott Pilgrim-esque RPG, coming to a Praxis near you.
