\n There's two problems, for me, with being honest about games:

1. I often know the person who wrote it, at least vaguely, so I don't want to offend them
2. If I don't like the game, I haven't played it, so it's unfair to pass comments

So here's a half-arsed solution. I'm going to retitle some games, then talk about them, and see if it's any use.

Hermione

This is the game that persuaded me to be very careful before spending money on indie games. It's not a well-known game, but it's one you can buy at IPR. I think it was at the Forge booth last year.

I first played Hermione last year. The game went OK: not superb, but OK. Later, at a convention, I felt the urge to buy something and, partly out of curiosity, bought Hermione.

It's a glossy, beautiful book. And there's a good game somewhere in there.

Problem is, it's barely more than a conflict resolution mechanic. It turns out all the setting and character stuff that got me enthused was added in by the person who ran the game. The game itself is an interesting project that needs a year or two's playtesting: at the moment, it's half-baked.

The writing is lazy. Really, the author should have known better: it's like a blog post.

Overall, it's not worth the cover price: bad writing; an average game that needed work before publishing.

Father Time

This one's a pretty well-known game, one I've seen mentioned a lot. It's not one of the big games that everyone plays, but it's a respected Forge game.

I've heard the game's great. Problem is, I can't read the text. It's so densely written I can hardly get a sense of the rules. It's...just...painful to read.

Sometimes, I've thought of running this. But then I try to read the text and...it's too much effort. Can't do it. Play something else instead.

Pursued by a bear

So here's an odd one. This game's great. It looks great. It plays well. I've played it a couple of times. You've seen this game mentioned quite a bit on forums.

The tragedy of this game is that, with a little more work, it could have been genius. As it is, it's a good, playable game, but it feels like it's been released a year too early. Some rules don't quite fit. Some things need tweaking.

This game could have been another My Life With Master or Dogs In The Vineyard. Indeed, it's halfway there: a good game, not quite together, released too early.

The Oracle at Delphos

Oh, man, this game is shit. Total, total shit. I cannot believe anyone charges money for this. But they do. It's on sale in my local games store.

So the setting's great. I love the setting.

But the author's taken a generic indie-ish resolution system, shoved it in, and hopes it works. The rules have nothing specifically to do with the central idea. It's all just traits and skills and shit.

And there's odd little rules floating about that just don't make sense.

Also, the layout is shit: courtesy of Microsoft Word. And the writing is total shit: puerile adolescent blog-post writing. It looks like the guy's written it once, then never looked at it again.

See...perhaps I'm being unfair, because I haven't played the game. But I don't want to. I can tell this game's shit. There's just nothing interesting about it. And the writing is so shit that I can't begin to read it.

___

...all right, and that's it. Obviously, that's an unrepresentative sample of what I've bought: for each four like those above, there's another four games I really like.

It's less use, of course, than naming names. But I hope it's vaguely interesting: at least you get a feeling of the sorts of reaction I have to various games I've bought.

Graham

    \n I think "half-arsed" was accurate. Graham, while I respect the emotions that gave impetus to this experiment, I think its safe to say that it is a failed experiment. Witholding names to protect everyone's feelings leads to reviews that aren't useful and aren't really interesting. It helps me not at all to vaguely know that there is a game out there in indieland that you bought and didn't like.

    I only see two ways you can viablly review games. You can give only positive reviews, which means reviewing games you have played and liked, games you have read and liked but not played, or the parts you like in games which also have negative features. Second, you can give positive and negative feedback, doing your best to be both frank and sensitive to the feelings of the designers. The only other way to do it (I guess there's a third way) is to just speak in the abstract, be vague about "the types of games you like" or "games that have scene framing" and not name actual games at all.

    While I love the first type of review, I think there is a lot of that out there in podcast land. I would appreciate hearing more about things that didn't work or weren't quite baked. Of course, I'm not saying just "let her rip!" and trash games. But, I thoughtful discussion, one which points out the positive stuff as well and stuff that other people might like even if you don't, would be well received.

    This community (and other connected ones) are built largely around the concept of support and good will. The downside of this is that speaking negatively about a published game (especially one published by someone we all know) is still pretty taboo, even with the New Honesty. And we all know that word of mouth will make or break a game.

    I think that if you really have a problem with a game (to the point where you feel like you need to warn people against buying it) then you shouldn't feel too bad about publicly saying so. If you are worried about hurting someones feeling, just send them a private email and share your thoughts.

    \n You can be critical without being mean or rude, but yes being critical may cause hurt feelings.

    When I first started posting at StoryGames with any frequency Andy appeared to tell me to name names and get everything out in the open. I did that even though I was strongly disinclined to do so, and I've continued to do so. If you value quality in small press games you need to do this, otherwise the happy group promotion of all things indie and story games attitude around here will continue to encourage folk to produce games that just aren't as good as they could be.

    I think your concerns revolve around rejection by your friends and the community and a concern that criticizing a game you haven't played is unfair. On the first account, I can't think of anything the community needs more than polite criticism. It may be that an author decides they no longer care for you due to that criticism, but all social groups come with the risk of dislike on the part of one of the members. Ultimately that's their problem for being unable to deal with criticism of their work from friends and fans, not yours for doing the best job you can.       \n Interesting thread. I design and think a lot about games but haven't published anything yet.

      I come down 100% on the side of honesty, but that can mean a lot of different things. There's honesty within a community of designers and players with constructive intent and consequence. There's also just people stating preferences. There's a lot of honest commentary that isn't particularly useful, whether damning or praising. I really like and play less than half the games I've bought. But I can find effusive gushing on rpgnet or other places about just about any game I can name. Buyer beware and all that.

      I'm reminded of a half dozen threads here that try to discuss the roles of different fora. Whether between Story Games and the Forge, or other. Honesty is meaningless apart from context. No one wants to hurt anyone's feelings. Most designers want useful criticism which they can apply to hone their craft. And different people have different thresholds for critique. If a designer claims to value it, give it to them with both guns. Doing it publicly or whether Story Games is the province for it are separate issues.

      I recognized Father Time immediately. I have a pretty good guess about Hermione. The other two could be any number of games, fortunately and unfortunately.

      Here's the thing about honest and thorough criticism (the only kind I find of much use): I can find good things to say about just about any game, and there are things I would really like to change about my absolute favorites. Scope, context, and intent I think are the concern of those who seem to be in favor of something less than complete and public "honesty." Well, that and sales, apparently. Too much criticism is one-sided and more about advocacy.

      While I agree generally with Graham's comments about Universalis, I'm really glad it was written and glad I bought it. It's not an easy read, but it's influenced my play and design tremendously. Having bought most of what IPR has to offer, I can see its influence on a dozen other games, all to the better. Honesty about its perceived flaws in some contexts is not helpful, either to players and designers that might benefit from it. Honesty directly to it's author is possibly helpful, depending on how well it's provided. And then there's just being a bastard. (not saying at all that's Graham's purpose)

      Honesty should be well-rounded. I think Contenders needs a lot of work. I said so on my blog. Its author responded to my criticism and similar criticisms of others, has provided some nice hacks, and I look forward to a second edition. I also indicated truthfully that it helped provide one of the best times I've had role-playing in the last couple years.

      I tend to try to err on the positive side of criticism on the internet whether I know the designer or not. One is handicapped by the medium with regard for expressing intent or in permitting immediate emotive feedback.

      Rambling now. Bottom line: analyze motives for criticism. If you have regard for others' feelings, ask your target whether they'd like public or private criticism. Either kind can potentially be valuable.
