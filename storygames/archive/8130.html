    \n I posted over in another thread that I think its a matter of consumers being spoilt for choice and choosing what they think are higher quality products, on the assumption that non-free products had higher production costs (true for print items at least), and that is some sort of assurance of better quality.

    i.e. if someone is going to stump up the money to print and retail their game, and the front to charge money for it, then the chances of it being playtested, functional, and complete, as opposed to badly designed and throwaway, are raised.

But I do believe that in a saturated market (and we are saturated, there are more games than anyone could read or play) people grasp for anything that helps them choose what to spend their time on.

    \n My data is based on my own play patterns, and those of Finnish hobbyists. In Finland American indie games are usually only noted in media and by retailers if they're for sale. This is because retailers (me) won't import and represent free games at conventions, and because free games have not been productized - packaged, branded, quality-controlled and clearly published as an event that is news-worthy. Thus free games are only ever even mentioned in discussion when one such happens to hit the exact bullseye of relevance for some particular conversation.

    If you want your free game to get over this hump, consider putting out a print version that you sell at-cost to retailers, who can then get a modest profit for selling your game to others. Also make a point of putting your game up on a nice website, making it nice-looking and finishing it in a clear manner that signals a completed product instead of an on-going and unfinished project. And think up some marketing material and some ritual of commitment for people who want to get the game to entice curiousity. All of these will help your game compete with for-pay products.

    Personally I think that there is no reason for a "free game" (as in, a game that does not produce profit for the publisher) to not gain a similar opportunity on the market that a for-pay game gets. It's just that usually a person publishing a free game is really saying that he's not interested in pushing his game to the degree that the other guy is - he's already giving up on venues of sales and marketing strategies the other guy takes for granted, such as retailers, webstores, press releases, guarantees of quality and all sorts of other stuff, and he's not really taking up any new methods the other guy isn't also using for his own project. Of course your game is going to get played less if your marketing plan is to not market at all; and that is surprisingly common when "for free" is code for "can't stand the heat" or "no faith in my project" or "couldn't care less".

    \n Dungeon Squad and the Red Box Hack are definitely great examples of free games that have found their market, even though RBH, like Geiger, isn't totally finished. It's also interesting, I think, that many folks who try out things like DS, RBH, and Geiger are working on their own "alt dungeon" or "alt horror" game, and are picking up these free games to see what other folks are doing, whether to steal ideas or just see how other folks handle the same issues. So, in that regard, I think the free games that have seen some extended play definitely feed into this consume-but-also-create loop. Ben Robbins, for example, keeps generating cool alternate rules for Geiger Counter and those guys Jason mentioned are doing their own crazy stuff with Dungeon Squad. That's the sweet sweetness right there, for sure.

How many folks who buy Weapons of the Gods or Mist-Robed Gate also have their own martial arts system on the backburner? I'd say at least 75% of them. Free games that can tap into that kind of interest definitely have legs.

    \n Riffing off Ralph, it seems to me that the Handicap Principle is heavily in play here: getting into a game is always initiated without proof of quality, so it's up to the publisher to try to prove the quality in whatever way he can. One of those ways is to commit to actions that would be simply insane if he didn't genuinely believe in his product, such as producing an expensive print run. The message is then further compounded by showing signs of experience and competence which make it even more unlikely that this guy is just stupid and mistaken in his belief that his game is good enough to warrant a commitment of funds.

    Perhaps a free game should be made into a stream video or some other sort of low-barrier thing that doesn't require the commitment of time and interest that the written form requires. By lowering the barriers of entry you make the initial signs less important for choosing to commit to looking at the product. I for one know that often enough I don't look at a free game simply because I can't be bothered to find the download link on the webpage, or wait for the free file host to divulge the file, or whatever other barriers the publisher has provided - and even if I get the file, I still need to read it, which is not at all entertaining if the game is not good.

    \nI have no concrete data. But for many, perception is reality.
    \n
    \nThese aren't my opinions, but rather what I've collected from the 150+ gamer community in NY and NJ that I am closely connected to. Free games are played less because:
    \n
    \n- no print version
    \n- no representation in game stores
    \n- no reviews / actual play
    \n- no fiction to read
    \n- no art
    \n- no marketing / awareness / advertising / publicity / buzz
    \n- no representation at conventions
    \n- no quality control
    \n- no character sheets
    \n- no community
    \n- no playtesting
    \n- no blind playtesting with a wide assortment of people with different backgrounds or different size groups
    \n- too many free games, no clear way to filter the good from bad
    \n- no consistent product support
    \n- no barrier to entry as a filter
    \n- no outside editing
    \n- previous bad experience with free games or even free software (it's interesting how the 2 are lumped together with some people)
    \n- incomplete products or products that are constantly changing
    \n- no offline representation
    \n- "even I could do that, if I want a free game I will just make one"
    \n- time is money, how much time will I need to spend figuring out how this works?
    \n- could be gone tomorrow
    \n- no useable layout or graphic design
    \n- secret agenda: if the game is good, why not charge money? how else are they making their money? will it cost money later?
    \n- only 1 person involved, no checks or balances
    \n- without the pressure to sell, designers design for themselves, not their audience
    \n- many people acquire games to collect them, without a physical reminder or pressure of investment, they are often forgotten
    \n- many people acquire games just for few rule snippets to use in their other games, without a physical reminder or pressure of investment, they are often forgotten
    \n- potential usage of stolen software
    \n- a large percentage of the audience is composed of designers or future designers with a fear that giving too much away for free kills their dream of doing this for money one day (even though the likelihood that they will make money themselves may be non-existent)
    \n- anti-capitalist: if it has value, we should pay for it
    \n- class warfare: if a game does equal the production values of a commercial release, where is the publisher getting their money from? are they paying their artists? are they rich and taking away opportunities from the middle class?
    \n
    \nInterestingly, many consider free to not be limited to products that we pay for with money. If we pay for it via exposure to advertising, it isn't free. Additionally, people I've spoken to have less problems with free games that are open source with some clear and easy way to contribute than they do with free games that are NOT open source.

Have you played them? If you have the time, interest, and access to players, why not?

    \n That's what I was getting at, Moreno and Dave - it's not that free games are inherently unable to sell, it's just that being free correlates with factors such as inability to market your game, which then leads to less play in a roundabout way. If you think about it, a person who is strongly committed to having his game played and willing to use many means to make that happen is rather unlikely to be acting this way while simultaneously being willing to do it without monetary compensation and the prestige value of being good enough to ask for money. Thus, in the field of all published games, there is a discernible bias for worthwhile games to be for-pay. This bias might not initially even be very large, but it is big enough to be discerned by the playing public, and consequently magnified as it becomes received wisdom that for-pay games are better. This established knowledge then further strengthens the original bias of good games likely being for-pay, as the serious designers opt to associate with the positive image instead of the negative one and thus choose to ask money for their games. The end-result of the process is what we have: there's nothing inherent in making a free game that would force the game to be worse, but there are enough reasons for a strong bias of expectation to exist in general. Thus each free game has this additional hurdle of believability to get over before the prospective audience gives it a chance.

    This is not unlike making a game without any art, to pick an example. That doesn't make a game any worse objectively speaking by itself, but it's another signal that is associated with a non-serious slapdash job (or perhaps amateurish and low-budget, like happened with Fastlane), and will therefore act as a damper to your marketing signal. Any and all production choices can be analyzed in this regard: how will your target audience's positive and negative expectations line up with the product itself? Many of those expectations have nothing to do with your own product, but they're right often enough to be preserved as part of the overall zeitgeist.

    To continue with the example, Jonathan and others interested in changing the image of free games might think in terms of cutting losses and dividing the field by forming some sort of alternate concept for why a game might be free. Take Spione, which has no art: the reason for this not being any sort of problem for the game is that it situates itself as a non-gamebook to begin with, and an essay collection about Berlin and the cold war is not exactly something you'd expect to be amply illustrated with chicks in bikini or other atmospheric illustration. So a trait that would have been negative in a different cultural context becomes a matter of course for a different sort of product. What would be the context in which being free would not only be value-neutral, but also expected?

    Posted By: Eero TuovinenWhat would be the context in which being free would not only be value-neutral, but also expected?
    \nOr, in other words, why else would one make his game free if not because he doesn't have the knowhow and confidence to make a product for sale?

    One form of free games that might have potential in combating preconceptions are the open source projects that try to emulate the free software phenomenon. The reason for why freeness works in this context is that it's elevated into a matter of ideology: nobody would think that your game is below-average or unfinished because it's free when the freeness of the game is very much a central rallying cry of the endeavour. Unfortunately failed projects of this sort have done a lot to poison this particular well: at least my first reaction to a "free and open game design community with the purpose of creating the super rpg to rule all rpgs", after having witnessed this sort of thing several times, is to assume that the guy running the project is a professional in the software field and is just messing with rpgs on a hobby level, and doesn't really have any sort of expertise in this different field. The results have always dried up and been mediocre due to design by committee and no real goalposts, just like happens with many software projects that try to work in this manner, too.

    Another potentially fruitful context, as Jonathan quite clearly elucidates, is living practice. I'm doing this right now here in Sonkaj\xe4rvi, as I'm running a fantasy rpg of my own creation for some teenagers, of which one has already asked me to teach him how to run the game, which only exists as some random blog notes and in my head. I did not create the game to publish it but to play, but nobody who's encountered it can really doubt the quality of the game in the way an Internet-encountered free game gets doubted. However, cultural evolution is against this sort of thing: my game would have to be fucking amazing, the best thing since Dragonlance, for it to spread to wider audiences in any real manner by merely playing it and waiting for others to pick it up in a lineage of transmit. Still, larps work like this, having individual techniques spread by people visiting in each other's games: nobody judges a larp based on whether its text is for-pay; we can't really say yet if this is a transitory phase, though, as there's not yet any real scene for larp texts to begin with. Most larpers write some notes, but these are only rarely intended for replay by others. Even actual play reports are usually private.

    Yet another out-there idea to combat the preconception would be to set yourself up as a GM for hire. That takes the sort of balls that pretty much strike the typical preconceptions out of the water. Just present your web page not as "this is a game I made with my friends" but as "I'm a professional GM for hire, and this is the game I'm going to run, because it's just that good". Even if this might situate your commercial product to fail (being that nobody's pulled off commercial GMing yet), it well might provide a fruitful context for your game - people are going to take it seriously if it's the chosen system of a professional GM. You could do this stratagem with other sorts of product (heck, sell special dice only used by your game) and you wouldn't actually ever need to sell very well for the game itself to gain the prestigious aura of commercial endeavour while still staying free.

    I'm sure others have other ideas for how to recontextualize the game in this manner. Almost anything is better than "I have this game here for free, and I don't want to say it, but it's pretty clear that if it were any good I'd have already produced it for commerce". Even exclaiming how you're not interested in money comes off as a lame excuse in this context, as that disinterest is so easily interpreted as a lack of passion and ambition, a lack of commitment to providing the best product you can. You need something more solid than empty words to make another person believe you when you decide to go free and want to convince them that it's not because you lack confidence and ambition.

    However, combating the preconceptions is still just one half of the fight, as you still have to do the soul-seeking and planning related to your factual marketing efforts. As I said up above, the original and primary reason for free games being less known than commercial games is that the publisher of the free game made his game free to avoid the commitment to marketing a commercial game requires. This is a set-up for obscurity, so even if you recontextualize your game to get rid of the impressions this obscure niche has gathered over the years, you'll still need to figure out separately what sort of marketing you want to make to reach that audience you want. Not getting relegated to the same box with all the other free games in the Internet of course helps, but that alone won't make the game popular.

      \n Ok, so the whole "known quantity for validity" thing...

      Premise: If you're an unknown, you're screwed for trying to do something unless you've _very_ lucky.

      As I see it, if you're going to try and get an rpg out there, you're basically fucked unless you're already established or manage to get some other "established" person to essentially vouch for you by saying how good the game is.

      That's kind of what reviewers are: they're not just telling you what the game is about, in some ways they're going to be a known quantity that the reader can use to help them decide if they want to get a game or not.

      Now, some folks claim to never bother paying attention to reviews and all that static. Fine. I'd be _very_ surprised if they don't already have _some_ sort of vouchsafe system in place in lieu of relying on a reviewer. Unless they've just got enough disposable income and free time to be able to pick up games without consideration and read them. Who know about actual play.

      Anyway...

      Reviewers are the most obvious vouchsafe system. I know I personally tend to decide on a reviewer's "credibility" based in part on how they've reviewed games (or whatever) that I do and do not like. This gives me a feel for where they're coming from and how much I can really rely on them. There's of course a lot of other stuff that goes into it, but I want to keep things simple.

      Another issue is the name of the creator or writer. Of course, if you're brand new you don't have a name. Too bad. For those that are already established, you've already got at least a potential pool of people. Some might dislike you, but chances are you've at least got some fans. This one is pretty obvious too, just like reviewers.

      After that is the general "buzz". This is the hardest thing. It relies on people within the online community already having built up a reputation, and that their reputation is enough that their word about how good (or bad) a game is will be enough to vouchsafe quality.

      Around some place like rpg.net, the "obvious" initial thing is post-count. Like it or not, people with low post counts tend to be disregarded on message boards. High post count people are clearly more "hardcore" because they've posted so many bloody messages, so clearly they should know "quality" when they see it.

      Of course, this is a tricky thing. If the person with a decent post count tends to only pop up in some sort of theory thread or a Palladium bashing one, then they're probably going to have less credibility than someone that's across a spectrum of threads. To a certain (and limited) extent, there's a built-in limiter for the amount of influence any one person is going to have. They've got to be fairly active and across a wide range of things, in order to really make it to a certain level of trust.

      Then there's the already established designers that are (or are not) playing the game.

      Let's take around here for example.

      If someone hears about...oh, I don't know... 3:16. They poke around a bit to see if they can dig up some more info.

      Hmmm. Looks like there was a free version done earlier. Oh great. I can get it for free or I can buy the 2nd edition of it. Whatever. Of course the pay version is going to talk about how improved it is, it's not like it's going to say "I'm ripping you off and this is just a shitty rehash of the 1st version but costing you money". Poke around some more... oh, hey look. They're talking about it over on Story Games. Maybe it isn't just another rpg.net darling/flavor of the week.

      Say... those are all guys that are making and selling games. I like some of those games, and I've heard really good things about those others. They really seem to like it...

      Of course, when it comes to something like an established name talking about how good some unknown game is, if the author of the game isn't recognized from previous work, it means that the established person is basically staking at least a portion of their reputation for "quality". If the established person doesn't have that many games available, or it's been a long time since they put anything out, then they don't have that much of a reputation to be "risking" in endorsement.

      Because of course, what we're basically talking about is product endorsement.

      It's either happening by a reviewer, other consumers, or some person of "note" (fame). Now, the "fame" or "person of note" aspect is pretty flexible/fluid as I've already said. It could be an established game designer, it could be someone that is calm and reasonable and that other game designers seem to respect, or whatever.

      So, this is _the_ place for something like a free game to be able to succeed. Or at least be more likely to succeed. It's "indy" or "small press" or whatever label you want to stick on it. People here are beyond the crass commercialism that infects places like rpg.net, and they're also not locked into some single game like you'd expect over at ENWorld.

      Oh yeah... where people are talking about the game is going to wind up being a bit of a vouchsafe check as well. I might check out ENWorld to see if anyone around there knows of Houses of the Blooded, because at least it'll let me know what the "average" gamer is likely going to think about it. RPG.net is going to let me know what a rather different sort of gamer thinks about it, and of course Story Games is appealing to yet another type.

      What's the whole point?

      It's a complicated mess for somebody to navigate this whole thing.

      If someone is going to try going for the "guerrilla marketing" or "viral marketing" approach to getting their game out there and into people's hands, it's possible for it to succeed but they're going to have to work at it.

      Before, it was easier in some ways. Publish it in a fan magazine, or make copies of it and pass it out at conventions. Because things in general were so... limited... in terms of the amount of things available, the ease with which they can be acquired, the expectation of production value, and so forth, doing this kind of thing in the past was a different sort of animal than now.

      If I were going to try and do this, how would I do it?

      I guess my approach would be to first make a game that looked like it cost money and that I expected people to pay for it. Meaning I'd see if I could work out some sort of a deal to get art that actually matched my game premise, and wasn't just a black and white line drawing from clipart.com. Nice cover, some pretty pictures inside, and something that doesn't look like it's from the 80's, unless I'm making some sort of a game explicitly evoking the 80's in the first place.

      I'd also do my darnedest to see if I could get someone to do at least _something_ of an editing job on the text. I've got no idea what sort of a "writer" I am in comparison to other folks, but I know I'd like to have some sort of "professional" go over whatever it is I've got to say and turn it into a more cohesive text.

      Layout... *sigh* By this point I've already spent a bunch of money out of pocket. I'd probably go ahead and take a look at how expensive it'd be to have someone do a nice layout job. I'm guessing it's going to be more than I've got to spend at this point, which means I'll have to try and do it myself. I personally happen to have access to InDesign and have even used it a bit for work purposes, so I'd try and do this myself. But if I had the money? I'd consider trying to tap someone to do this for me as well.

      I'd also try and use people from the "community" for all of this. Meaning, folks I could find here or maybe at rpg.net. People around here have already had to go through the "growing pains" and might either be able to steer me in the proper direction, or could use a few extra bucks.

      Then I'd try and set up some sort of page for buying it. Lulu seems like the way to go, but maybe I'd try some other place that wouldn't be too difficult (IPR?). I'd price the game low enough so that it's easy for people to get as an "impulse" buy, but not so low that it looks like I'm saying "this is a piece of crap you shouldn't pay too much for". I'd probably try and make it so that it seemed like it'd be worth paying at least $5 for the game.

      After that... I'd try and get it into as many hands as I could. See if I could get people around here talking about it, or rpg.net or _something_. That usual round of promotions that people seem to struggle with.

      I'd also try and figure out some way that it could be passed along to the pirates and torrent sites. Maybe include some sort of "disclaimer" message or something as part of the front page or intro or something. A request that if you illegally downloaded this game and enjoyed it, please buy it. Support the creator so that you can continue to enjoy these sorts of things.

      I'd also try and make sure I could run some sessions online for the game. Something along the lines of, "I know that this game is available for free from torrent sites. I'm not going to say anything about that. No questions asked, I'm going to run a game online at [blah blah blah]. If you've bought the game and want to see how it plays and don't have a group available, come on by and I thank you for buying it. If you haven't bought the game, come on by anyway. Maybe you'll enjoy yourself enough that you'll pay for it afterwards."

      That sort of thing.

      Would it work? I don't know right now. I don't _think_ there's anyone out there "deliberately sabotaging" themselves. It's kind of an involved bit of misdirection: make a free game, but put the work into it like it's a "for pay" game, just to get your game out there and being played. Of course, it's not going to really work if you make it obvious that you're doing that in the first place.
