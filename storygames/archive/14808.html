    \n "That," says Dr. Smickley, "is a Foon."

    We take the egghead's word for it, since, like a Martian spaceship, no one has ever seen a Foon1. It has the usual five legs of a Martian alpha predator, each terminating in a wicked basket of lobster-like-claws. It smells like gunpowder and scorched plastic.

    "It is a badge of office for a Martian Senator," Smickley says, sotto voce, "to have a domesticated Foon as a pet." No human has ever seen the floor of the Martian Senate.

    "Es ist zum Lachen!" Heidi cries, rolling up her sleeves. You know Heidi; she's going to wrestle that thing into submission.

    How do you stop Heidi from charging into certain death?

    1 The Moeller expedition almost certainly saw a Foon but did not live to share their experiences.

    \n There was a reason the canal was soft, warm, and organic - it led to the nest, the birthing place, the heart of the Martian city. Row upon row of enormous fat, blind queens secreted their young, five to a glistening sac.

    Hal, master of the obvious, said, "This is not the shuttle bay." And then the martian larvae were upon them. Punching, kicking, clawing, and running, they managed to make it back the way they came - the larvae were ferocious, sharp of tooth, but slow.

    But when they were back at the Senate floor, the Doctor said, "Um - Heidi? Are you all right?"

    Heidi was clutching her arm - it was bleeding fluently. Her eyes had already started to take on a glassy sheen. Martian toxins were always fatal. And without Heidi, no way off this rock.

    How do they cheat death?

    \n "Nun, leider bin ich tot" Heidi said matter-of-factly, surveying the damage to her arm. Hal Jones, normally a cool customer, started crying. Heidi dropped down heavily.

    "How much time does she have, Dr. Smeckley?" you asked, but Smeckley had vanished. "Smeckley?"

    Your pilot and navigator shared a tender, heartbreaking moment, and you gave them some privacy. You found Smeckley ripping a gigantic blue crystal from an ornamental pedestal. "We're not here to loot," you said, thinking the damn egghead is acting pretty cavalier about Heidi's impending death.

    "No," Smeckley replied, "but we do have a mission to finish, and for that we need Heidi!" He delicately held the magnificent crystal as he hustled back to the dying pilot. "She's doomed, of course. Her organs have already failed; only love sustains her. This is a consciousness accumulator," he said, "it contains the ... essence, I suppose ... of countless dead Martian Senators. And, if my calculations are correct, soon one lovely Teutonic pilot will be joining them."

    "That's crazy!" Hal shouted, but Heidi understood. It only took a moment.

    You left her lifeless body behind. Strange lights whirled within the consciousness accumulator, somehow guiding us away form the horrors beneath the city and toward the shuttle bay. We entered the vast hangar at a run, ready for anything, even eager to settle a few scores with these Martian barbarians. But none of us was prepared for what awaited beyond the threshold. "I must be mad," Dr. Smeckley stammered before falling to his knees in awe.

    What do they find in the hangar bay?
