\n I'm in the process of making a magazine - well, me and a dozen other people, obviously. And we're probably going to need some good tools for the job.

Something that keeps track of different versions of documents; helps the work flow so we can send stuff along to editors, proofreaders, designers, printeres; that helps us plan the content of each issue...

Any ideas? Any experiences?

    \n Well, I've never edited anything more than an annual literature magazine (3 years in college) and the staff was usually myself with one other person (just me in one case) not counting contributors, so I don't have much helpful experience on coordinating work flow, but here's what I can offer (if it's already answered or taken care of, my apologies!)

    Adobe Pagemaker (or InDesign, these days) is the layout program de rigeur. One nice feature is that it can export a .pdf version of the document that is easy to send to people for proofread and such like. I don't know if it will keep track of different versions (it wouldn't surprise me, but I'm not familiar with that feature). Another benefit is that it allows you to link to image files rather than imbedding them, so that you can keep super high-res print quality images as separate files without your layout file becoming gargantuan. Since it is pretty much the industry standard (to my understanding) pretty much everybody can accept and use the file formats.

    I hope that's helpful, and I hope somebody with more experience hops in!

    \n For document management, we've been using Dropbox lately and it has been really useful. I also collaborate using Google Docs all the time, and for distributed proofing and processing raw text it is pretty convenient. Finding version control that meets my needs has proven elusive, because my needs include "stupid, lazy people can use it effectively".

    \n You need management tools as well as IT.

    You need someone to be in charge, or at least a decision making mechanism which doesn't alienate. And you need to decide what that is before you need someone to make decisions. Also, everyone needs to know what their responsibilities are and not to step on the toes of others. Advice and interest is fine as is consultation but not sticking your oar in. You also need to not be jealous of your own work and to allow for the comments of others.

    \n I'd look hard at Joomla (Content Management System, including multiple levels of control, e.g. Author, Editor, Publisher) and at any plug-ins that let you export a full Joomla site (or a month of content) to PDF or some other editable format (for typesetting and then PDFing). That way, you'd get both an online and print publication simultaneously.

    Offline tools: all I know about is Documentum (tied into FrameMaker, PageMaker, or InDesign)... but that's several thousand dollars to setup. Per contributor.
