    Mark, did you come up with this "alternative development" idea on your own? Awesome parallel thinking! It's something that Rich Forest and I discussed in January 2005 in Hong Kong. There have already been two alternative roleplaying games written with this basic concept idea in mind.

    Shreyas wrote Mridangam as if roleplaying developed from classical Indian dance. We've revised it and that version will be published in the first issue of my roleplaying journal.

    And then I wrote Waiting for the Queen/Tea at Midnight (PDF) as if computer-based "get lamp" text games came first and roleplaying came second. It's going in the journal too.

    I wrote part of an game as if roleplaying developed from Chinese opera, but it's not really playable yet.

    Personally, I think this is a great tool for developing alternative style of roleplaying. If I'd remembered earlier, I would have suggested it to Andy as a Game Chef theme. Game Chef: Alternate Roleplaying History!

    What if the primary split had been from a different sort of game, not wargames but boardgames as a whole? Like this:

    Source: Boardgames (Clue, Monopoly)

    Twist: Players just want a little more, some human context to hang the dice rolling and card swapping on.

    What's different? There is no default setting--the diversity of boardgames filters down into a diversity of rpg's. Combat is not the default problem for early rpg's--social conflict (Clue) and economic conflict (Monopoly) enter the fray. The popularity of boardgames feeds into a broader base of roleplayers. A normal gaming session is shorter and more self-contained, meant to be played through over the course of a single evening.

    More rpg's aimed at a young audience (Candyland, the rpg) and with a stronger 'moral lesson' component (Chutes and Ladders where children can discover how it feels to help each other (up ladders), to be mean to each other (pushed down chutes), where the goal ends up being "remember how it felt to be pushed down the chute? Wouldn't you rather be helped up a ladder?").

    With the kiddie audience, a small but vibrant community of gamers curious about diceless play in a more robust sense, where color, sound, and objects become potential randomizers.
