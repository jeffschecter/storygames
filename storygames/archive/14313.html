\n Over in Jason's thread, Michael said:\n
Posted By: Michael PfaffPosted By: sageIt could even be arrived at in the moment - Steve misses a roll, so Jason thinks "oh yeah, there's probably some sewer goblin traps around, maybe that can be my move"
\nThis is sort of interesting.

I'd always assumed that traps, monsters, hazards, etc. had been a part of prep - something established that you're drawing on when a missed roll happens, but not something you make up on the fly.

Thief: "I want to look for traps." *rolling trap expert* "Crap! Miss!"
GM: *consulting his prep, there's a trap... mmmm announce future bad stuff* "As you move into the chamber to get a better look, you feel the ground shift beneath your feet, and a quick clicking sound start to emanate from the walls! What do you do?"

But, you're saying it's ok for the GM to plant traps that weren't in the prep on a missed roll.

Thief: "I want to look for traps." *rolling trap expert* "Crap! Miss!"
GM: *consulting his prep, there's no trap... mmmm separate them* "As you move into the chamber, the floor opens up and you momentarily become weightless before your stomach lurches up into your throat. You hit the chute hard as you fall and slide, slide, slide! It happens so fast there's no time to react."

Hmmm. Not sure how I feel about that. There's a part of me that wants to say, "if it's not in the prep I shouldn't add stuff, but use stuff already there for badness".

Taking this to a new thread, so Jason's awesome can keep going.

Your prep establishes things, yes. However, when its time to make a move, you have to make a move, and that move always represents something in the fiction, which can establish new facts (so long as they don't contradict old ones).

That isn't to say it's entirely separate from your fronts. In my example, I figure the sewers or the goblins are probably already a front, so of course they have traps. This isn't all that different from them failing a roll and you thinking "oh, there's probably a sewer monster about, how about if it finds them?"

That isn't to say don't prep! Prep, in the form of fronts, is key. But you can also improvise. Your prep is your fallback, not something you're bound to. Until the players know about it, it's still up for grabs.

    Just to clarify: I'm not saying the idea of improvisation is odd to me. Jason's example of switching which person was going to be there with the leech and horseflies makes sense to me. There was an event happening in the dungeon, he just switched up the minor details to make it more compelling.

    Maybe it's just my old habits. But, prepping wandering monsters for missed rolls just seems to make more "sense" to me, than what we could term "wandering traps" I guess.

    So, during my prep I might make a list of monsters that inhabit an area... "Oh, there's some trolls that live in this area and some goblins who fear the trolls who live down here..." And, when a PC misses a roll, I can look to that prep and be like, "Well, maybe this goblin squad went too far over here and that's the random encounter..." or whatever.

    Traps feel stationary to me. They are in specific places for specific purposes. But, I suppose you could be like, "These are the sort of traps in this dungeon (goblin traps, pits, spear gauntlets, etc...), and a missed roll means there might be one of these things lurking."

    ---

    I guess another part of me that harkens back to the "old school" mentality is the existence of this environment that's been constructed and we're here to overcome the environment as it stood and not as it's popping into existence because of snowballing of moves.

    I'm not saying that approach is wrong! So, please don't misinterpret me. I can see how random traps popping into existence is totally exhilarating and makes the game more intense. And, maybe I should try it out and see how it plays out.

    \n My prep is: "The cave goblins set crude traps to catch the unwary."

    My move is: "You see a desiccated body here, half-buried under stones that seem to have fallen from a plastered-over hole in the ceiling." (announce future badness)

    My hard move is: "You lunge at the goblin, and it seems to suddenly vanish in the gloom. But then you're falling and crashing against stone in the dark and you realize the creature must have lured you forward into a hidden pit."

    So, the traps are prep. But they're not like, "pit trap at location 3C" prep. If I'm feeling clever, I might make a custom move, "When you run afoul of a goblin trap," or something.

    I think it's really important to always look at the fiction (as if you're standing there, looking around) to determine the content of your moves as GM. You don't have to make up a pit trap when a PC misses a roll. What you do, instead, is situate the PCs in a dangerous situation (as informed by your prep), and paint the picture as evocatively as you can. Then, when it's time for you to make a hard move, it's easy: you just follow through on the dangers as established. Some of those dangers might not be fully known to the PCs yet, but they're still there, in your fictional world, and you (the GM) can see them clearly when you look around. Nothing has to be improvised right then, after the dice are rolled.

    \n Michael, remember that moves don't just come into play on failures. Like John's non-hard move above, you can make that any time everyone looks to you to find out what happens. Since the trap's pretty crude, that's not much of a threat once announced, but consider: "You see a desiccated body here, stiff, with it's hands frozen clawing at it's throat." Sounds like a gas trap to me...

    Not searching for traps is a golden opportunity, yeah. But you could also hit them with something else. Not searching for traps doesn't mean there are traps, it means you have some freedom to make traps, as appropriate, carry weight. Instead of John's non-hard example, you hit them with John's hard example.

    John's reminding us of the principle "begin and end with the fiction." A trap is never just a move, it's a fictional thing.

    As far as prep and locations, John's prep ("The cave goblins set crude traps to catch the unwary.") is what I would expect before my example ("oh yeah, there's probably some sewer goblin traps around, maybe that can be my move"). I'm certainly not suggesting that traps get placed whenever you feel like making them up. But if the players are in a trap-infested area, they haven't checked for traps, and it's your time to make a move... I'd think about using a trap.

I'd be careful to stay flexible around them, since you don't know when you'll be making moves, but you can totally mark your map "ARROW TRAP HERE." Are you staying standing to take out the orc, or dropping to the floor to avoid the arrows?"

    \n Yeah, Michael, I dig your prep style. That's great stuff. I sometimes go into that much detail, too. My cave goblins line was shorthand for one small part, not meant to be the entirety of my prep. (Although, to be honest, I've run games with less)

    You can definitely do it by placing things ahead of time (even drawing out a specific dungeon with secret doors, traps, monsters and everything) Totally viable for DW. When I say that you should "look around at the fiction" to see what your move should be, you might be 1) looking around in your imagination of the fictional scene, 2) looking at the map of the fictional space, 3) looking at your specific prep about exactly what's there in that room, or 4) any combo of the above.

    Regardless, it's all fiction first. That's where the old-skool "figure out the challenge" stuff comes in. There's no way in DW to "roll to beat the challenge" or anything like that. When confronted with weird and dangerous stuff, the players have to get in there and examine the fiction, and then say what the characters actually do. Then, the moves are triggered, or not, completely in the context of the precise fictional details. In that way, DW is a lot like classic OSR-style play (what Eero calls challenge-based play -- though there are some differences).
