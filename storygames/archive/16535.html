\n Hi all!

A friend of mine has requested that I run a victorian "action-adventure" game for him tomorrow. He suggested the "League of Extraordinary Gentleman" movie as a genre guide, but I'm still somewhat in the twist here. I proposed that we play something loosely based on Archipelago, to give him maximum authorial capabilities, since he knows what he's looking for.

There are going to be four of us. I have three main questions:

1) how the hell do you play "Victorian action-adventure?"

2) general tips on how to make Archipelago sing for a bunch of first-timers would be much appreciated.

3) I'm building a deck of cards to draw for inspiration - I'm thinking they will have a character and a theme on them. Suggestions of characters and theme would be great. So far I have:

Characters:
The Orphan
The Miser
The Prostitute
The Gentleman
The Beggar
The Rake
The Lunatic
The Lady
The Detective

Themes:
The cardinal sins and the catholic virtues (so that's 14: Lust, Gluttony, Greed, Sloth, Wrath, Envy, Pride, Prudence, Justice, Temperance, Courage, Faith, Hope, and Love)

In addition, general kibbitzing and comments from the peanut gallery are welcome. Although... I guess that makes it not kibbitzing?

    \n I think the big trickery with Victorian adventure, moreso than with adventures of other sorts, is nailing down the grittiness vs romanticism level.

    It was a great time for people who were white, male, and had money. For everyone else it varied from bearable to dreadful.

    But if you want to ring on full Castle Falkenstein rainbows and lollipops, that's a fine way to go with it too.

    Classic sources for inspiration include Dracula, Frankenstein, Jeckyll & Hyde, 20K Leagues, the Time Machine, Sherlock Holmes, etc.

    Catholic? Catholic! God damn you Sir, and your infernal popistry. There is only one sin and that is to have your base desires paraded in public. What matter if a man dress as a wombat and have nonagenarians chase him round his club with feather dusters, as long as it doesn't get into the press, damn scoundrels. Again, I say God damn you Sir.

    Rakes? What have gardening tools or, worse, Georgian archetypes got to do with Victoriana? Read a bloody book man! You might learn something! Try some Allan Quatermain, Varney the Vampire, Sexton Blake or Baroness Orczy. Rakes! What next? Trowels, trugs and hoes?

    Victorian action adventure is easy. Victoria is a goddess and all the bloody foreigners want to get their filthy (Yes, FILTHY!) hands, nay hooves, on her glorious person. So some evil foreigner is up to no good, occasionally in league with some dirty scoundrel of an Englishman who bears a grudge for not being filthy rich, or Irish or something. He dastardly machinations must be undone by the flower of English manhood. Occasionally gels get involved too, and that's a much stickier wicket.

    \n You guys are great.

    We played in "Prague" - which is to say, we polled each other about our knowledge of Prague, collectively established that we knew nothing about it, and promptly used it as a bucket for all of our pseudo-slavic stereotypes. It was fantastic!

    I played a british soldier-of-fortune, on the search for the bones of a saint. The friend who requested the game played a purveyor of curiosities who moonlighted as an opium dealer. Our other two friends played a union gang boss and a rum smuggler.

    I got sold out by my crew, the purveyor of curiosities got turned into a monstrous beast by a cabal of occultists, the rum smuggler burned down "Prague" and the gang boss agreed to start a proto-Socialist experiment on the grounds of his factory.

    It was awesome. Thank you for all of your inspiration!
