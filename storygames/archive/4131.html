\n Hi, there was another thread where we were touching upon reincorporation as a story technique. Reincorporation is when you take an element that was brought in to the story early, and change it's meaning by revealing it to be connected to a later, often climactic, development. It's different than foreshadowing, because the connotation of reincorporation is that when you introduce the thing, it has no connections, but when a later plot point is revealed, it gets assigned.

For instance, in some Scooby Doo a rpg with shared narrative control, one player might introduce an old caretaker early on in the game, and, at the end, another player might narrate that they unmask the ghost to reveal... that old caretaker who we met in the first session!

I wanted to get into it a little bit because it will illustrate a fundemental difference between a couple of different playstyles. You'll have to bear with me because you guys will probably have vocabulary that I don't know for a lot of the stuff that I'm talking about.

In games where everyone understands the past, present, and future of the game world is always in flux (such as improv games), reincorporation seems like it would always be awesome.

But, in some cases, reincorporation could be taken as a betrayal of trust.

In some styles (generally simulationist/illusionist, but I'm leery of using those terms), players expect certain aspects of the game world, like past events, to be set in stone. In these games, players are often trying to solve a mystery or find a quest item. So, if a player creates a game element in the process of the adventure and then the GM incorporates it into the past events of the plot, it's innapropriate.

Example- as an aside in a Shadowrun adventure where the heroes are looking for a serial killer, a player says "I go buy a red leather jacket". Later, the DM reveals that the killer they are looking for "always wears a red leather jacket", a fact which gets the player mistaken for the killer. It's a cool plot element, but the player gets mad. His choices are supposed to be protected from involvement in unconnected elements of the game world.

In these games, the GM's keys may react to the players only through direct consequences of the PC's actions. Huge, far-reaching developments, inspired from direct consequences, are great. But even trivial connections inspired from unconnected actions feel like a gyp.

If the DM omnisiently changes the unnconnected world around them to react to their actions (and especially if he changes the past), he's cheating. It's the kind of a thing where the players might not know he's cheating, but could easily guess it if he uses something as overt as the leather jacket example. This can be done intentionally, as part of the "illusion" of illusionist games. But, in some circles, there's a morality issue here. Reincorporation can be a cheat, unless it can be introduced as a product of direct, consequential repercussions.

    \n I personally don't care if history is fluid except for the actions we've already taken. For the most part, I'll never know whether or not the history we're learning is what the GM had in mind all along or not. But that sort of power also comes with the responsibility to (you guessed it) not be a dick. Bear in mind that your dramatic complications may be viewed as dickery.

    To use the red jacket example, switching it over so that the killer always wears a red jacket is fine. If the player wants to play out a scene where he gets mistaken as the killer he'll have that option. Played right he may even be able to learn some of the stuff that the police never tell the newspapers, and come away with some extra info for his troubles. But to simply say "and oh yeah, the killer always wears red, so you get arrested" is probably an abuse of power.

    Also be wary of coincidences. For example, if the killer really does always wear a red jacket, and has since you thought up the adventure six weeks ago, you might even want to consider changing that tidbit when the PC buys a red jacket, if your group's dynamic is such that it would look like you're screwing the guy over because of a choice he meant as flavor text for his PC's appearance.

    Posted By: RemiAm I misreading? Is there a place for new information to be introduced that can put characters in a difficult situation that won't be 'cheating'? Isn't a sense of interconnectivity important to a simulation?
    \nYes, a sense of interconnectivity is very important, but it is best created by utilizing existing facts. Again, this pertains to games where the players assume certain facts do exist, even though they have not yet been revealed. Regarding the librarian- if it was pre-set that she was the head vampire, when the player eventually finds out, they can say "that explains why she would never go on dates during the day!", and it would be a real revelation to the player, rather than a manufactured one. If she was adapted to be a vampire after introduction because it seemed like it would be a cool connection, the player can think "oh yeah that's clever", but they could never think "holy shit I can't believe I missed that!"

    The cheating idea, (and this is what this thread is examining) is when the DM takes player choices, and uses them to change elements that he had ostensibly pre-prepared. For instance, say the characters have spent days following clues to find the evil vampire hideout and failing, so they go to McDonalds out of boredom, and the DM says "Surprise,this Mcdonalds happens to be the vampire hideout!". It's a break in the trust that the players have about elements of the world that were supposed to be set in stone. It wouldn't be a problem in improv (where no one is expected to be hiding pre-existing elements of the gameworld), but is a breach of trust in, for instance, a simulationist game.\n
    Posted By: Remi I don't understand how the game works if all of the elements have to be at the table at the front of the game, and anything applied beyond that is seen as 'cheating'.
    \nNobody expects that every minute detail of the game world exists at the outset of the game. However, the players expect certain main plot elements to exist and be somewhat fleshed out before they start the game, and feel cheated if those expectations are not met. One example that has been used to describe this kind of irritant- when the players look at a map of the country, decide to go to a random city, and find that any city they choose turns out to contain "The fortress of the witch king". It can be a beautiful thing to use your player's choices to create new elements of the game world, but it can also be a betrayal that makes the players feel as if nothing they do really matters, because the world will just change to adapt to it.
