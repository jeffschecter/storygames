    \n Step 1 in my successful forays has been to find out/ contemplate what genres might get the group excited.
    Step 2 is often (for me) to then make a rule set that does what I want to do.

    BUT!

    Other people are certainly making very fine things that can be used in that step 2. Check out Lady Black Bird, for example. Is anyone in your group a Firefly fan? Or into steampunk aesthetic? Hit them with that picture of the spacecraft, and then hand out character sheets, each of which has the entire (brief!) rules set printed on the bottom half of the character sheet.

    But if they don't buy into the coolness of the genre/color/setting, it's going to be an uphill fight. So make sure you pick that part wisely.

    What genre/aesthetic/setting do you think your group would go for?

    \n Well, the successful introductions I've been doing through the last five years have used these games, primarily:\n
      \n
    • The Mountain Witch is easy to learn and generally attracts both adults and teenagers - those who aren't captured by anime stylings will go for the Kurosawa-style serious depiction.
      • \n
    • Old-school, simplified-rules D&D is surprisingly good as an introductory game. My experience with teenagers is that hitpoints is a tad too much, but class/level and ability scores are just right. Most importantly, the "GM is the window to the world" thing and clearly set fictional challenges and goals works to keep the game going.
      • \n
    • Zombie Cinema is my own newbie-portal design which relies on being quick to teach and quick to play, and boardgame-based insofar as the components and their use is concerned. This has been generally successful with teenage boys and girls - all but the geekiest have little problem in thinking up lurid zombie stories.
      • \n
    \nOthers I've used this decade include Primetime Adventures, Dust Devils, My Life with Master... they mostly reflect my own preferences in games, really; if you know your game well and know how it should be played, you can then explain that to others in an engaging manner as well.

    \n So, I'm going to do a mild topic drift here from which RPGs to pitch to how to pitch the idea to noobs.

    I've done this for a fair number of groups. Many have a strong console-gaming background. Many are rather geeky but not gamers. Some are just plain "normals."

    The pitch that seems to do the best for me is something like:

    "We're going to make our own story here, like a move or TV show. You will each have an important, central character in the story that you are responsible for. I am going to be a sort of referee, keep things moving, and play background characters and stuff. Sometimes, we'll roll some dice to see what happens when you try to do something. Not always, but sometimes. We'll get some randomness that way, to inspire our imaginations. Also, sometimes that might make you fail at what you were trying to do, but that's OK. Sometimes a story is a lot more interesting when we fail. OK? Ready?" And then concrete, step-by-step stuff for a little while so they get the hang.

    \n Excuse me, but I'm a fanboy. There, I said it upfront.

    The Mountain Witch.

    I sat there at the table, having played games for years, and was blown away by what happened. And when I've run the game, even for new players, they come away excited.

    It's so lite the rules can and should be taught as they happen. It preserves a highly competitive state much like a board game. The Fates demand and encourage personal investment in the character, as well as offer group authorship of the setting and narrative.

    Newbie mistakes to avoid: Don't try to encourage characters to get along. Players should get along, in setting up their characters to "not" get along. Always encourage and congratulate even the smallest betrayal. Characters will be "forced" to cooperate "despite" their mistrust and Fate related issues, hence the game.

    The game is still tasky. "I want to kill the archer on the tower wall." gets broken down to "I want to cross the bridge; I want to scale the wall; I want to kill the archer." Three actions, three rounds, three counter-stakes. Abilities (like "Can shoot fire from his fist") are the only things that can change that.

    Newbie advice that makes it flow way better: Always, when the opportunity presents itself, do something oddly specific. Always. It gives all the other players something to build on.

    For example:
    GM "Night begins to fall as you wind your way out of the forest and back to the path. The snow is thick on the ground, but not so thick that you cannot see a small shrine where incense burns over an offering bowl."

    Green "Hoichi kneels and offers a few coins, uttering a phrase in Cantonese."
    White "Izu places an orange flower against the shrine and says nothing."
    Blue "Hoichi, Izu, quite fooling around. We have much to do."

    Because later, when it's time to reveal Fates, it might be that orange flower that seals the deal.

    Blue "It was an orange flower left outside my master's bedroom the night he was murdered. It was identical to the one you left at the shrine. It was you, Izu, who betrayed me."
    Or perhaps
    Green "All of my dealings with the Witch have been in Cantonese. All along I have warned him of your coming. I have brought you all here as my part of the bargain. Now see what he offered me in return."

    All that aside, the discussion linked above by Jason has a ton of good games and advice. Other than The Mountain Witch, I've personally had good experiences showing new people Kagematsu, The Shadow of Yesterday (if you avoid Bringing Down the Pain for at least three or four sessions), Dogs in the Vineyard, and Ganakagok.

    \n For me, the qualities of a good starter game for non-gamers are:

    1. the premise and setting are accessible to people outside fandom
    2. the game lets you start doing fun things immediately
    3. the rules are as simple as a game like Monopoly -- probably much simpler, since non-gamers will be worried about the role-playing stuff
    4. the rules don't require lots of weird dice
    5. the game is finished in a couple hours

    I suspect few or none of the games listed above meet all of my criteria. Zombie Cinema definitely scores here, but you need friends who think zombies are cool. That rules out my wife and my parents. I haven't played Lady Blackbird, but from what I've read, it scores here, though I suspect it can't be played in two hours it's fandom-y (your friends have to like the Firefly series and such... which rules out my parents, but not my wife). Can you finish a game of It's Complicated in two hours? If so, it's a winner.

    D&D fails on many levels. Fantasy is weird. Rules are fiddly and complicated. Takes too long to play. Chargen isn't immediately fun. Lots of weird dice.
    D6 Star Wars, too.

    I think a lot of these games are simple as far as RPGs go, but if you're successful introducing your non-gamer friends to them, then they'll be gamers when you're done. That is, they're transition games; they're not role-playing games for non-gamers per se. I think transition games have a high risk of failure with non-gamers.

    No one has mentioned Fiasco yet, right? Fiasco is an amazing game for non-gamers. It meets all of my criteria.

    \n David,

    I think the problems you attribute to non-gamers are unlikely to come up in Fiasco. To me, those problems seem like a gamer's reaction to a ruleset with fewer points of contact (i.e., simple rules). Non-gamers aren't going to be flummoxed by freeform story-telling, because they already know how to tell stories. Non-gamers aren't going to have the expectation that they have "my guy" or that their guy should "win." That is, non-gamers don't have all the damned baggage that gamers have.

    When I ran Fiasco recently, one of the players had almost no tabletop role-playing experience at all. Now, she did have a lot of MUSH role-playing experience, so I'm not sure I'd call her a non-gamer. Still the world of tabletop RPGs was totally alien to her and she approached Fiasco with a lot of trepidation -- and did just fine and had a lot of fun.

    I really want to see if I can get my parents to play Fiasco with me some time.
