\n EDIT Jan2014: new year and new progress. Updates to come.

Hey everyone, thanks for taking a look at my thread!

I've recently been working on a *World hack with which to play an Evangelion game, focusing mostly on representation of canonical 'magic' effects of the AT Field, the berserk state, and extremely dangerous combat. Tying it all together into a cohesive whole has been the primary labor of this work, mostly because there are so many separate events and effects that I feel should have ripples throughout play.

At this point the things I've drawn and altered from other *World games are:
- combat and damage mechanics from The Regiment
- stats and moves from Apocalypse World and Dungeon World, and fan-made playbooks for them
- equipment and customization from Adeptus Evangelion
- Strings from Monsterhearts

Something that is a big step away from *World games is a sort of 'soft-playbook' idea; not only do you have background effects, but you also have your career moves, Evangelion equipment and Unit Type effects, and weapon or equipment use. Whether or not this idea will blow up in my face, I'm not sure yet! Play-testing begins next week, so we'll see. Some of the playbooks are still in the idea stage at this point, too, but there are certainly enough to play with the basic mechanics.

Oh, and I cut out individual XP - you advance after every sortie versus the enemy. Because Evangelion is quite thematically arranged around each Angel (18 in the original series), I thought it appropriate to have Advancement based around those milestones.

At this point I would really like it if more experienced *World players and hackers could take a look over what I have so far. Is it too much? Too little? Too many or too few subsystems?

Thank you for your attention!

    \n It's hard to codify certain plot elements of Evangelion that are implicit to the viewer, such as the mysteries and body-horror, but also keep the players from turning into the Scooby Gang at the first sign of trouble.

    Instead of showing plot horrors, I've tried to convey the incredible stresses of Evangelion combat, and the constant risk of failure therein. One way I'm trying to do that is with the Stress-Rejection and Strain system; when a character goes stress critical (see: The Regiment) they may trigger a rejection by one (or more) of their peers, who take on relationship strain. Too much strain, and you lash out at the person on the other end of the strained bond.

    To keep people from coming to blows though, it's not restricted to insults. Anything sufficiently upsetting to the target character will suffice, such as The Silent Treatment\u2122 or saying "I love you" just as you take a killing blow in their place.

    As for NPC's and organization, I'll leave that entirely in the GM's hands with the Front/Danger systems of AW and DW.

    The slice-of-life of Evangelion is the building of tension. Nothing is happening, and the reason it isn't boring is because you know that something will happen, and it will be a battle against an Angel, and the characters might die. It's not that the slice-of-life stuff is intrinsically interesting - it is only interesting as a dichotomy of emotional tension against the combat, and would be nothing without it. That is why I have put the majority of the rules' weight onto combat, rather than kids being unable to communicate with one another. Because really, almost nothing happens unless there's an Eva involved.

    To elaborate on your idea,
    "...some kind of stress counter that effects you. Gives certain moves, bonuses and negatives..... Would fit in greatly and a unique feature"
    here, the reason I'm not granting bonuses and moves for Strain is that it would incentivize specific actions in relationships, rather than free play. The function of Strain at this point is to force change, not to enforce specific behaviors. Too much Strain, and you do something based on the relationship you already have, not a specific thing based on your chosen moves or background.

    The effects of Strain build and grow worse over time, as the more Strain you hold and are forced to release, the more you hurt (purposefully or inadvertently) the people around you, which means your Strain-release actions the next time will be worse, because your relationship is worse. That is combined with the Hedgehog's Dilemma, in which the only way to release Strain is to roll a reversed-value Nerve move. Because Nerve is the 'defensive' stat for combat and social conflict, you are hard-pressed socially with Strain if you are cool and aloof, or more endangered in combat if you are gentle and open-hearted and have better relationships.

    \n@horn_head_o - I looked at some math in the past couple of hours and the result-spread remains the same as long as there is a math of
    "N or N- is fail, N+1,2,3 is pass-with-cost, and N+4 and up is pass."
    So feasibly, I could have stat spreads of 9, 10, 11, 12 and 13, for -1, 0, +1, +2, and +3, respectively. The 2d6 base just provides the result curve across the bottom. In this example, all move result values would be increased by 10.

    Changing the dice' size-value, such as d8's or d12's, would increase the result curve spread, offering more roll-result granularity. I could have instead of 3 degrees of result (fail, hit-with-cost, hit) maybe 4 or 5, with hit-with-cost in the mathematical middle-ground. It would also bias the rolls more heavily towards the middle result, in this case "hit-with-cost". This would be good if I wanted more things with more effects and more sub-rules and resultant cause-effect chains, but that's not really the sort of game I want this to be. The more quickly it can play, while still modeling canonical Evangelion cause-effect events, the better.

    Physical size difference is a good idea - especially with the damage dice. Rolling up to 8 or 9 big d6's across a table would be pretty fun, and a good comparative scale against miniaturized pilot dice.
