\n I have only played three sessions of FreeMarket (one one-shot, one two-shot), but I have some thoughts on it that Daniel H. encouraged me to share here. I'll start with the one-shot from Nerd by Nerd East.

I made a character who was a creepy historian / fortune-teller. His main abilities centered on identifying "what's cool" and finding ways to claim "...so what we're doing is cool too, dammit!" However, my vision was to do this in some sort of majestic "word of fate" context. The other players had different ideas when creating their characters, and when we went to tie them all together in a MRCZ (aka company), there was no way for me to push for something grandiose. The vibe was silly, so we went with "Teeth is the new black."

In a longer campaign, I probably would have eventually found a way to work in my preferred aesthetic for the character, and might have enjoyed the challenge of forcing it to fit... but for a one-shot, I found this less than ideal. If I was running this, I'd brainstorm the MRCZ (at least a teeny bit) before char-gen.

As for the mechanics, the card-drawing and turn-spending is its own fun game. The cards are pretty, the strategy is simple but usually not boringly obvious. The handling time is way too long for me. The fiction moves along in tiny increments amidst several minutes of staring at cards and character sheets.

The mechanical decisions lead, and then it's up to the player to shoehorn in a fictional description to account for the decision. (This reminded me of my Burning Empires game from last summer: in 8 sessions of play in a 5 player game, we had two players who were great at this, one player who started out bad but gradually improved, and two who started out bad and never improved.) "I activate my geneline!" "Which part?" "Uh... I guess 'witty'." "And how is wit going to help you convince the pubic to adore your ugly tooth fairy?" "Uh..." (Some floundering and failed attempts occur before we find something good enough to say "done" and move on.)

The high handling time produces a lot of watching for players whose characters aren't involved. And, strategically, it's often wise to not be involved. This bugs me.

A lot of this is simply my personal taste -- when I get psyched about the fiction, I prefer to stay inside it. And FreeMarket definitely gets me psyched about the fiction. "Let's all team up and attempt cool projects together!" is one of my favorite things to do in roleplaying. The all-needs-met space station and vie-for-social-status culture is a fantastic setting to do that in. Here's what happened in this game:

We started with a slogan of "teeth is the new black" (mishearing when one player said "tea is the new black"). We printed a tooth fairy to be our marketing tool, but we didn't have any printing skills, so it came out hideous. Still, we decided to run with its "train wreck, can't look away" appeal. We produced a video with the fairy, lit it and marionetted it to make it appealing, loaded in a lot of tooth fairy historical info to prove an enduring fad, and even got across "pliers and bags of teeth" as a new fashion trend.

We then started getting requests, but we had no pliers and bags to fill them. So we did a deal with a guy who could provide these. In return, he wanted our guy Kimball to lose to Joe at Snozzball. Kimball refused, but was willing to make the target (Joe's daughter) THINK Joe had beaten him by implanting her with a false memory. This worked, and our contact brought all our items to Pliers Promotion Night at the Snozzball arena. While we led a "teeth!" chant in the crowd, Kimball proceeded to knock out a bunch of Joe's teeth, which our tooth fairy abomination collected. The end.

The upshot of all these projects is a change in Flow. I immediately latched onto the Flow concept as a sort of "coolness metric". To me, that's fun, and fits in with all the stuff I like best about the game. However, you only get Flow for certain game-defined activities and mechanics-uses, but not necessarily for accomplishing something in the fiction that seems popularity-raising. "We started and supported a big fad, but because of our expenditures in the process, our Flow went down. Huh?"

I mentioned this to Daniel, and he reminded me that Flow is also a measure of how many resources the station aggregate is willing to allocate to your projects. Once he said it, I remembered it. But it was easy for me to forget, because I didn't care about that part of the game. Getting popular is fun! Getting approval from the aggregate so you can advance a MRCZ Tier and move your operation somewhere comfy is (at least relatively speaking) boring. We don't spend any play time on how uncomfortable the characters are in their storage unit, so I'm not motivated to improve that.

I guess I might be excited to have more station resources so I could do more ambitious projects, but I'm not clear on any cap on ambition in the first place, and my understanding of Flow as a "rating" inclines me to forget that it's also a resource you can spend. Wait, am I using up the limited materials the station grants me? I thought I was just putting my reputation on the line in a venture that could make me cooler or less cool!

I have no doubt that a long chat with Luke or Jared could make all of this make sense to me. But without that, the fuzziness becomes distracting when relating the mechanics to the fiction. The mechanics are telling me that I have a Margin of Victory that I can spend on either customizing my project outcome or on Efficiency, and that Efficiency is the one that immediately gets me a Flow "rebate" (which may exceed the Flow I spent/staked in the first place) -- and I'm wondering what the fuck just happened in the fiction as a result of printing a tooth fairy.

    \n If you're finding that the challenges are resulting in tedium with not enough fiction, your superuser should probably back off and let you guys automatically win some challenges until something big comes along so you can focus on the roleplaying and all that. I've gotten the impression you want to be doing a handful of conflicts a session and that's it.     I don't know why you say it's not wise for a group to be involved in a project. If a group of you are involved then you can actually get extra Flow back from the challenge if you succeed, unlike when you are doing a challenge alone. When a group invests flow in a project, individuals get back a percentage of the total Flow invested so having everyone throw in on a project is a way to rake in some major Flow. If four of us throw in 10 Flow apiece, if we succeed with 75% efficiency I get back 30 Flow even though I only threw in 10. It is totally wise for a group to be involved and the system rewards it. If the group is sitting out on challenges and watching, they are wasting an opportunity.

    I guess I'd need an example of where you had a hard time relating mechanics to fiction, but I think maybe you are worrying a little too much about directly mapping one onto the other. Like, every mathematical function applied to Flow doesn't need to be narrated.

    \n
    \n Flow loss is never random for users. If you don't want to lose flow, don't engage in challenges. The superuser can never force a user to lose flow without a reason. There is no "wandering monster table" where owlbears show up and eat your flow. Even frownies are delivered because of some action on your part that elicited a response. And if this happens, it's part of the story to find out a) why you were frownied and b) if you can amends by the end of the session.

    Flow in FreeMarket, the space station, allows the user to live his dreams, and not just live. Flow in FreeMarket, the game, allows you to tell a story and not just take part in one.

    Also: Aggie doesn't care about anything. It can't get pissed off. It can monitor users' biometrics, energy expenditure and time spent on task and then take and give back flow accordingly. But it doesn't have feelings, it just has a program.

    Also: how the hell do I turn 3 Wool into 1 Brick?
