    \n In Misspent Youth, there are a couple of things that do this, I think. At the start of each session, each YO player asks the YO player to his left a question about the friendship between their characters. Each YO also comes up with an Authority Figure for the session. During play, all players take turns setting some scene framing details. One of these details is which of the AFs or friendship questions will be present/featured in the scene. You're not necessarily using your own stuff, and The Authority, who also gives these details for at least one scene, is obviously not using her own stuff.

    Also, at the the start of the series, you give The Authority certain powers/abilities (Systems of Control), and there's a mechanical advantage to reincorporating and attacking these things in a session (in an attempt to subvert them to your use).

    \n Thanks, that's a lot wider sampling of stuff than I expected! I initially had a hard time thinking of examples on my own. I'm very interested in the ways that mechanics can create situation (as opposed, specifically, to where mechanics mandate the players creating situation). Most or many games seem to have a mix of both. Doing it one way as opposed to the other seems to affect players very differently. I'm trying to understand how this is so. Maybe some people can make suggestions.

    Is a situation that was created mechanically different to players than one that was made up by them or by someone else? When, as I designer, do you use one or the other?

    A few things I can think of (both pro and con):

    Players are less inclined to think its "unfair" if an unusually difficult situation is generated by mechanics
    Mechanical generation can spark creativity by creating juxtapositions that challenge the imagination, or that suggest a connection without defining it
    When mechanical generation uses specific elements to generate situation (like random tables or oracles), it provides a way to insert desired elements into the situation without any player having to know them beforehand
    Mechanical situations aren't going to be specifically tailored to the players or their characters

    Posted By: tony dowlerJosh, I'd love to hear how Agora does that. I've got a playtest copy, and I'm reading it now. I'd love to hear about how it works in play.
    \nHeya, Tony.

    The big thing for me about mechanical generation of situation is that roleplaying already does this by its nature \u2014 when you roleplay, you are constantly generating new situation out of the old situation. When we talk about situation generation, we're usually talking about creating the starting situation, but we also tend to ignore the fact that we already have a ton of tools that do this.

    Every game of Agora begins with the same starting situation, or rather, the same seed of starting situation: There was a War, now you're refugees landing on Agora. That's all you need to start when you have really strong and driven characters. The characters that you create will skew that situation seed in a number of different ways: are you coming down to the planet guns blazing, are you scrounging for food, are you building your city on a hill as soon as you touch ground? Whatever you do there sets up the situation for the next round of play, and what you do there sets up the next round, and so on and so on.

    The game is designed to record what happens in a meaningful way (and by meaningful, I don't mean 'thematic cock-punching moment,' I mean procedurally significant enough that it affects later play): the simplest example is the obstacle library, which stores the sources of adversity you encounter so they can be used again later. A much more complicated example is the ideogogue himself, because every time you play, you put your ideogogue, his resources, and his ideals at risk \u2014 and that gets recorded on your character sheet and gets carried forward into later play. You can lose your lieutenant to enemy fire, you can tame velociraptors and use them as cavalry, you can come to the conclusion that the law should only protect your people and not anybody else, and lots, lots more. What happens in round 4 determines what resources and motivations you have in round 6, and in round 20.

    Another piece of Agora's situation is that the main characters, the ideogogues, are driven people with pretty big plans (even if it's just 'survive' because that's 'survive on Agora in the wake of galactic armageddon'), and incidents center on those plans. Players identify opportunities and declare goals, and it is the struggle over those goals that gives play direction. An incident might not be about building a dam and hydroelectric plant, but if the incident start there, ensuing action will be situated there, and the under-construction power station will give context to whatever actually happens, even if it's a torrid love scene. Pair that with the procedural reincorporation I've already talked about, and you get a game that takes player input and then folds/spindles/mutilates it into something organic and constantly developing but always based on player input.

    So that's a very brief, highly arcane, and only marginally specific answer to your question. Does any of it make sense? I'm happy to unpack further.

    \u2014 Josh
