\n In the thread "Criticizing Shock: ..." downtime was mentioned, thus:
Posted By: SunaBoardgames ... There has always been a very strict criticism in system flow and functionality, and a downtime is seen as an unforgivable design mistake. You never see boardgamers disagree on that.
I took up the point, thus: Interesting observation! I've often wondered at how weak the discourse on this point has been relating to role-playing games. By comparing it to boardgames, I believe you have shed some light on that. Thanks!

And "kobutsu" answered me thus:
Posted By: kobutsuInteresting. I suspect that downtime isessentialto TRPGs. They seem to carry a larger cognitive load than many other forms of game\u2014and certainly a larger load than conversation. (I say this based on my observation that the pauses between turns-at-speech in TRPGs are much longer and more frequent than in normal conversation. I have yet to do a systematic study of this, but I hope to!) So downtime gives you a bit of time to re-collect your thoughts, and plan where to go next. I think a trick to good design and good play is to have exactly enough at the right times.
I believe it is good for the interaction to reduce the amount of downtime as much as possible. There are other elements to consider, of course, but I do not see downtime as anything we should strive to have in a game.

I do not believe that the "larger cognitive load" is something that demands a lot of delays in interaction. We are quite capable of handling the cognitive load, if the game is designed to make it easy for us. So we should strive to make easy-flowing games.

    \n I don't think the problem is downtime in general so much as downtime that marginalizes one of more players.
    \n
    \nThere are a lot of ways games can do this. In a mechanically cumbersome game, this may be the moment when the one player who has been able to memorize all the rules and mathematical minutia uses this knowledge to gain an advantage in play that makes the other players feel like their characters are superfluous. In a game with a strong GM role, this is when play devolves into, "Shut up and let me tell you a story." In a narrative game with clearly distinguished roles, this is when one role dictates that its player spends large portions of the game observing quietly from the sidelines.
    \n
    \nThese are extreme examples, but I think they might help to illustrate what we're really talking about when we say "downtime." It's not just ceasing to be the center of attention for a scene or two, it's actively being denied any hope of playing an important role in a series of scenes or a full session because of a failure of game design and group dynamic.
    \n
    \nOr, at least, that's how I've always interpreted it. I don't think there is something fundamentally wrong with having moments in a game when some of the players take a more passive or supportive role, but no player (or players) should be railroaded into passivity or supporting status because the game design arbitrarily benefits one player or role.

    \n We are really talking different kinds of "downtime" here. Generally we are talking of being more or less "out of the fiction". Here's a tentative list:

    1 - Audience time = you are not participating in the current scene, but stay with the game
    2 - Mech-time = the time we take fiddling with mechanics
    3 - House time = you go to the kitchen, the loo, the balcony to take a sig, etc.
    4 - Social time = the time we spend talking of other things, greeting each other, bantering, etc.

    Pause and flow = natural shiftings in the fiction, and our engagement in it. Not really "downtime", but different grades of interaction and engagement.

    Posted By: TomasHVM
    Downtime = the time we take fiddling with mechanics
    \nIMO nothing good will come of taking the time spent on the rules mechanics and calling it 'downtime'.

    At least to my understanding as a trad-player 'downtime' means, 'the off camera time between adventures, when my PC raises a family, advances skills and gets new equipment'.

    As a boardgamer I mean that 'downtime' is the time when other players are doing their turns and there is NOTHING for me to do but to wait for my next turn.

    I know that it is Suna's terminology, but it is bad, for the reasons stated above. As his definition has nothing to do with the previous ones.

    I propose that we call the "I need to stop and do [too much, unnecessary, for this game] math" 'bad mechanic stop', that is when play stops (in the active player's turn) to make a mechanical resolution of such complexity that it should have been weeded out in play testing, according to the player.

    The previous definitions of downtime were objective, there simply was nothing to do, except for to reorganizing your resource tokens, stacking dice or perhaps pondering your next move.

    But a bad mechanic stop is highly subjective to the player, since it's happening on the active player's own turn, there is clearly something to do (and hence not downtime in the traditional sense), but the player finds it tedious and game breaking.

    I have played games where I have experienced such bad mechanic stops, especially during combat. But where other players have found the same situations to be the heart of the game. They could still experience mechanic stops, i.e. how story focused play stops in order to go into rules focused play, but since they didn't think that the stops were out of proportion, or rewarding enough the stops where not bad mechanic stops.

    \n Here is the clean beef from a board game designer

    Downtime is not a problem, not controlling the flow of downtime IS A BIG PROBLEM!

    Unless a game has mechanics that engage multiple players simultaneously (the programing phase in robo rally is a good example of this) then the play time of any particular game will be divided between the players.

    The trick to keeping player's engaged is to have the shortest possible amount of downtime between turns. Games like ticket to ride accomplish this by simply offering players the choice between a few options each turn and letting them pick only one. in other words if ticket to ride had been designed in the nineties each turn would have looked like this.

    build phase
    draw train card phase
    draw ticket card phase

    And it would take forever for the game to get to your turn again.

    shorter turns are a very popular trend in board and miniatures gaming and i think RPGS would highly benefit from this practice.

    Posted By: TylerTshorter turns are a very popular trend in board and miniatures gaming and i think RPGS would highly benefit from this practice.
    Ja, and some games do it quite well. How well depends on how fast the game is in going trough a scene, and framing the next.\n
    Posted By: GB Steveit's not always easy to determine the extent to which someone is participating in the game
    We don't have to determine that; the player will do it himself. If a player is left outside a long scene, there's a chance his interest in the game will dwindle. If this happens regularly he may come to think of the game as boring, and drop out.

    Most designers want to avoid that.

    What we, as designers, need to do, is to look for clues to how players experience downtime in our game. Testing our game should leave us with comments on this, and maybe some proposals on how to fix anything that the players perceive as wrong. And we may take a critical look on how we organize our game, and look for better solutions than the ones already there. "Better solutions" may be faster framing and scenes, shifting player-focus, better fiction-framing, etc.

    The point is to be aware of downtime as a sometimes necessary, and potentially dangerous, phenomenon. A wise designer will minimize negative effects of it, and make use of it in intelligent ways.

    Posted By: TylerTi have seen a few turn structures work really well in narrative based games.
    I've created an up-tempo play-mode for the new edition of my classical fantasy role-playing game Fabula, to be used in situations with lots of people (market, party, battle, etc.).

    This play-mode is strictly turn-based (each player will be focus for a scene, or gets to frame a scene, each turn). The turns are up-tempo, often with scenes that are dealt with in seconds (in battle the scenes are little more than initial situation, one reaction, and a final point, before racing on ...). The point is to run through lots of scenes, to simulate the chaos of human interaction in situations with a lot happening at the same time. Most scenes are dealt with outside the resolution system (although the res-system in Fabula is fast).

    The audience time is so short no one have any reason to leave the table.
    - And this is important: all players present makes all players part-take in the full effect of the play-mode; the whole group experience the chaotic nature of the situation.
    - Equally important: when we go around the table again and again, the scenes start to feed into each other, and make the social weave even more interesting. It's like the town/celebration/tournament becoming something of its own, through the eyes of all our characters. Even though the mode allows for a lot of characters going their own ways, the players still create a shared story out of it.

    I find this play-mode to be easy to do, effective, popular amongst the players, and great fun for the GM.

    \n Actual play, up-tempo mode, barn-party:

    Player A (Pront): me and Gonder sits behind the barn, drinking.
    Player B (Gonder): Jihaaa! Cheers! How about Mulia, Pront; she's nice!
    A: Cheers! No, she's too prickly!
    B: Prickly!?
    A: Ja, she said no to that man from Kos, you know!
    B: Asked her to dance, did he?
    A: Ja, and she just refused!
    B: But you do like her, eh?

    GM: Cut! Next scene!

    Player C (Mulia): me and Gonder are dancing.
    Player B (Gonder): Nice of you to ask me, Mulia!
    C: Dance! Don't talk!
    B: Sorry! (is doing his best to dance well)
    GM: Roll for dancing! Both of you!
    (B rolls good, C rolls abysmal)
    GM: Continue, with rolls woven in!
    C: Sorry!
    B: No problem! Got solid toes! Humph!
    C: Sorry again!
    B: No problem! Share a pint of beer?
    C: Oh yes, thanks! (goes off the floor)

    GM: Cut! Next scene!

    Player D (Roona): me and Pront standing by the door.
    You like her?
    Player A (Pront): Well ... she is good looking, but ...
    But what?
    A: She's not very friendly.
    Looks friendly enough with Gonder, over there.
    A: What? Where?

    GM: Cut! Next scene!

    Player B (Gonder): me and Pront out back.
    B: I only bought her a beer to get her off the floor! She dance like a mad bull!
    Player A (Pront): Oh, ok!
    B: (indicates the scene is over with his hands. Players may cut their own scenes)

    GM: Good! The next scene is mine!

    GM: Pront, Gonder, Mulia and Roona is out by the fire. Your father is there too, Mulia.
    GM (Mulias Pa): How dare you say no to a nice man, Mulia!
    Player C (Mulia): But I did dance with Gonder, Pa!
    Player B (Gonder): Yes, she asked me to dance, sir.
    GM (Pa): Oh, and that's ok, is it; a woman asking a man to dance! (he spits in the fire) I'm talking about you being a proper woman! You just don't say no to someone asking politely!
    Player D (Roona): (rolls her eyes)
    C: (notice Roona, but keeps her face serious) Sorry, Pa!
    GM (Pa): Good! (suddenly smiles) Now, Gonder, you seems like a nice man. Who's your father?
    ...

    - and so on.
    ___________________________

    The scenes don't tie into each other thematically like this all the time. Mostly the focus will shift from one thing to another, and a third, especially in the early stages of the play-mode. Keeping several balls in the air is easy, and fun, and one of the reasons I invented this particular play-mode.

    The key to it is the tempo; as a GM you must be ready to cut a scene, and start the next, with no words to "fill the gaps", just: "Cut! Next scene!" (Cut & Go). It has modifications for different situations, but essentially it works the same for all kinds of situations with lots of people interacting.

    \n I believe we need to talk of different kinds of "downtime" in relation to role-playing games, so here's my "Map of Downtimes" again:

    1 - Audience time= you are not participating in the current scene, but stay with the game
    2 - Mech-time= the time we take making the mechanics influence the game
    3 - House time= you go to the kitchen, the loo, the balcony to take a sig, etc.
    4 - Social time= the time we spend talking of other things, greeting each other, bantering, etc.

    Pause and flow= natural shiftings in the fiction, and our engagement in it. Not really "downtime", but different grades of interaction and engagement.

    Posted By: Paul T.Do you use any such aids in this style of gameplay? Physical cues, gestures, etc?
    I tend to use my hands when cutting the scenes, but otherwise: no.

    Often I set a kind of "fiction frame". It is simple:
    - Now we play the barn-dance. Each scene must be in or around the barn, during the dance.
    - Now we play the city. Keep your scenes in the city.
    - When battle ensues, we go into battle-mode. The pace is high, so be ready to act! And be ready to frame your own scenes when I give you the right to do that. Here we go ...

    And then we play out a string of tiny scenes, more or less as in the example given above. Sometimes the scenes is set by the GM, and sometimes the GM and some of the players, but mostly the scenes is set by all, in fixed or fleeting turn.

    The point is to keep your GMing simple and effective. You set the pace by cutting the scenes sharply, to ensure a rapid succession of them. You may use words and hand-gestures to cut, and are ready to jump over players not ready to set a scene (and tell them to be ready for the next scene). The GM is free to set scenes whenever she wants to. And then she has her own turn too, which is usually a scene with all characters present (depending on the nature of the group and place). The ordinary GM-scene signals that one whole turn of scenes has been done, and that a new one begins (with any extra premise the GM want to include). You may go for any number of scenes in this play-mode, until the setting element has been properly explored.

    To contrast the rapid succession of scenes in this mode, it is effective to make a "reflective scene" afterwards (or in-between), in which the pace is totally different; slow and laid-back, to facilitate a reflective mood amongst the players (and characters). The moment you start working with contrasting tempo as a GM, you may sense some greater rhythm to your game-play, and start acting like a conductor of an orchestra. As a conductor your first mission is to serve up different moods, by shifts in tempo, for the players to interpret through their characters.

    This is, of course, a bit outside the theme of the thread. But it is fascinating stuff.
