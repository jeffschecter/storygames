    So if your intention is to go through the Knutepunkt/Solmukohta books, you're discussing the Nordic larp scene/tradition, mostly. Not jeepform as such.

    \n Vow of Chastity / Dogma 99


    This is sort of a strange artifact, like a recipe for poaching dodo eggs. It's an instrument for solving a problem I've never encountered. Still, it's interesting enough on its own merits.

    The title is intriguing. Vows of chastity are unusual, as far as vows go, insofar as they are vows to *not* do something. The rules laid out here follow that format -- they are all promises to abstain from certain actions.

    The design goals of this document are unusual as well. Its purpose is to lead its signatories towards designing LARPs that are *different* from what has gone before. Not "better", in any sense of the word, except insofar as being different may be inherently better.

    As such, we end up with a list of presumably the most common features of LARPs extant at the time it was authored, with the constant admonition to avoid that common thing.

    Lists of this sort tend to cry out to have their various line items treated individually, so I suppose I can address it at that level.

    Item 1 is sort of mysterious. It "forbids all action in the written character descriptions past." What exactly remains allowable, if anything, for character backstory isn't very clear to me.

    Item 2 is perhaps more straightforward. No main plot. Alright. That would seem to imply that non-main plots are just fine. But some other parts imply that those are no good either. It seems like the easiest way to comply with these directives is to charge ahead with no backstory, no plot. I guess that would be different.

    Item 3 is most useful, I think, to the extent that it illuminates the former one. It implies that "no main plot" must mean something other than "no purely supporting characters", as that gets its own point right here.

    Item 4 isn't so much a design consideration as it is a directive regarding execution. It tends to reinforce the first two items -- with no backstories and no plot, there is very little to keep secret even accidentally.

    Item 5 is remarkably hardcore even by the strict standards of this list so far. No organizer influence allowed. Not that they have much left to do by this point anyway.

    Item 6 is mysterious in the usage of its word "superficial." I'm not sure if that's a quirk of translation or what's going on. In any case, its warning that "it is for many LARPers difficult to imagine a combat-free LARP" is perhaps my clearest indication that this document comes to me from distant shores, a long long time ago.

    Item 7 is very clearly drawn directly from the "if it's popular you can't do it" mission statement. It's sort of strange that other inspirations -- movies, books, plays, etc -- remain entirely acceptable.

    Item 8 seems to want to relate to the superficial prohibition, but perhaps it casts a wider net. It pairs well with Item 9. Together they make it very difficult, if not impossible, to distance the game appreciably from the local reality in which the players happen to live.

    Item 10 makes a liar out of me -- it is not a vow to *not* do something, per se. Nor is it really related to game design nor game execution. This rule seems to go nowhere and do nothing, so I'm not inclined to spend a lot of time with it.


    Taken as a whole, we find ourselves promising to not do a lot of things. Things that, presumably, everyone else is doing. It's an unflinchingly-reactionary approach to design. It doesn't promise the results will be good. Only that they'll be different.

    \n I have played in one Dogma 99 game. It was an updated version of the Last Supper, held in Oslo, 2009. We had brief info on the characters and an elaborate dinner, served to us on a boat in the harbor.

    All the game material that we used in play was generated in two rounds of sharing that we did about our characters. 1st round: we told what was on our character sheets. 2nd round: we re-capped to bring in people who came in late, this time we riffed off of discussion we'd had about what the characters would be like in today's world (John the Apostle was a blogger, the fishers were heads of big canning businesses, etc.).

    It was a great experience in player empowerment, but also I felt like we were exposed and left out there. It is a matter of trust of the players, and also trust in innate creativity of players. I like building in room for this kind of creative coordination to happen.

    Looking at the Vow of Chastity, what I see most is the things it was reacting against. Really, it was a great step out of old habits.

    Posted By: Jason Morningstar
    This stuff all sounds like suck to me:
    "Hallmarks of conventional LARP are: an emphasis on game mechanics, a high level of secrecy, structures that automatically discern between \xbbimportant\xab and \xbbunimportant\xab characters, and a dependence on gamesmaster control and intervention for the LARP to work."
    \nKeep in mind that it's a description written by a reactionary person who doesn't like the current crop of games and is therefore emphasizing weaknesses that may not be consistently problematic. Sure, the worst possible "other game" would have all of those elements to the nth degree. But a better one might make use of the fruitful possibilities that game mechanics, secrecy, unimportant characters, or gamemaster intervention might contain.

    That being said, having lots of all of those things at the same time sounds like a recipe for badness.

    However, jeepform, for example, makes fruitful use of the possibilities of GM intervention, providing techniques such as bird-in-ear to allow the GM to nudge the game toward interesting directions.

    East coast "horde larps" make fruitful use of the possibilities of unimportant characters. And System Danmarc specifically explored class differences as part of the point of play.

    I play regularly in a scifi game called Starship Valkyrie that makes fruitful use of game mechanics - the dramatic tension in the game is driven through interaction with the system mechanics.

    And the occasional secret (with multitudes of ways to ensure that the secret is revealed during play) can be lots of fun.

    Very good games can be made without entirely abstaining from those things. But I agree with the notion that they should not be used reflexively as an assumption of "that's how it has been therefore that's how it must be". And I agree that they can definitely be problematic areas when mishandled.

    I also agree that this article should be looked at as an historical artifact. It's one of the big pieces that lead to the definition of the Nordic art larp as a "thing", separate from the typical escapist boffer fantasy larp (which are, still, to the best of my knowledge, the typical games over there, as well, though they have differences from their American counterparts).

    The manifesto documents serve a function extremely similar to the early Forge writings and GNS definitions, which served primarily to define Narrativism as a "thing", and to rally people around to explore more about what that thing could be. As such, the first parts of the defining process consist of drawing differentiations between the different styles. After that, more subtle terms and definitions could emerge.

    \n The Manifesto of the Turku School and Larper's Vow of Chastity

    Having seen a manifesto for the game writers, we now get one for the game players. And it lives up to its billing as a manifesto.

    It's pretty much all hardcore immersionism all the time. Never break character. That doesn't seem like an especially radical approach these days, which perhaps speaks to its impact.

    Much like the tabletop theory side of things, there's an acknowledgement of how efficiently the gamists can come to dominate play. So this sort of hardline approach has a certain sensibility to it, if you can see past all their One True Wayism.

    At the same time, I can't shake the feeling that this is a document written by game authors for game players, rather than an authentic grassroots movement by players themselves. That isn't necessarily problematic, but I sorta feel like the state of the art has moved on.

    There's a certain refreshing purity that comes from this vow of chastity. In a lot of ways, it asks the player to do less. As a baseline, a new player could do a lot worse than to start here.

    Although it has a very personal, even intimate, tone, I find this a very social document. It really points out to me that I have a great deal of impact on my fellow players, with all the implications of social duty that carries. It's the first time I've personally seen this notion of subjugating my own desires to the greater demands of the game, and it's intriguing. While Dogma 99 beats the drum that every character is equally important, here I get the sense that every character is equally unimportant. Two sides of the same coin, but the emphasis changes the message for me.

    It's as pure a Purist-for-System expression as I've ever seen, which is a little surprising for me to realize, although it seems obvious in retrospect. This Vow of Chastity isn't just about System, it *is* System. I'm not sure if it's a particularly effective System, but at the very least it is articulated with great clarity.

    The old saying in public speaking is that if you don't know what you're talking about, at least say it loudly. I appreciate the similar sentiment here.

    \n The Turku Manifesto impressed me far less than Dogma 99. First of all, the bombastic tone, I don't know whether the writer thought that by copying from the Communist Manifesto he was implying a tone of irony or exaggeration for comic effect, but anyone who seriously writes:

    "Turkuists openly admit that their goals can only be achieved by taking down by force the current system of roleplaying."

    is not someone who I want to be in the sandpit with.

    This article reads like its trying to be about what larp _can_ be, however the result is a dictatorial instruction as to what larp _should_ be and what larp should be is what _this writer_ thinks it should be, with everything else dismissed as inferior.

    To be honest, I'm not sure how seriously I should be taking this entire thing.

    As I read it, fundamentally, what this writer thinks larp should be is a tool for education rather than enjoyment (or "pointless entertainment" to quote Turku). That simulationist larps can be "used as a tool for experimenting with different social models" and be considered a "(method of) science" and that immersion/el\xe4ytyjist can "give great, subjective insight into difficult topics".

    (Mentioning el\xe4ytyjist, is this truly a word that cannot be properly translated - and hence why it is the only untranslated word in the entire article? If not, its repeated use comes across as quite pretentious.)

    There is a particular mention of the larpwright/GM's 'message'. The problem with this is that, while you can control what the GM says, you can't control the ultimate message that the participants take away from it. Suppose you are writing a larp and you want to deliver the message 'War is hell'. While you can set up the situation so that the characters have a miserable time of it, an immersed player can end up having a very different experience, revelling in the carnage he creates or the liberty he's afforded.

    To be honest, I don't see the great benefit in drawing a distinction between the simulationist and immersive approach. You cannot have simulation without immersion. You cannot have "social philosophy and behavioral psychology put into practice" if the players are not working to immerse themselves into their characters.

    The the majority of the vows themselves are slightly different derivations of the basic thrust to encourage 'method acting' (or, more precisely, what people mistakenly think is method acting) in larping.

    I agee that immersion has value, but it should be a tool in the larping toolkit rather than the be-all and end-all of the pursuit.

    I personally am very glad that - whatever achievements made by the Turku manifesto alluded to in the introduction - that there are groups, like Jeep, that explicitly subordinate immersion to other goals and have done quite well in the process.

    \nThe Meilahti Model

    For a change, we get a descriptive, rather than prescriptive, model.

    Its insistence on the presence of a gamemaster is a little quaint now, but it seems plausible enough that the only successful game instances they observed included that role. And they do eventually address the notion that the duties of gamemaster can be spread across space and time.

    The idea of the gamemaster as the keeper of diegetic truth is sort of interesting; lumpley explores the issue further in http://www.lumpley.com/comment.php?entry=460 which I recommend to everyone's consideration. I would say it remains an area of controversy.

    Also interesting is that they see the fundamental problem with computer-as-gamemaster as being merely a technical limitation. I certainly find it an inviting avenue of exploration.

    This essay has a simplicity to it which can be a bit deceptive. As a structural framework it really only gives us a handful of blocks:

    players
    gamemasters
    roles
    interactions
    diegetic frames

    Yet there continues to this day very important work based very closely on this foundation. As an example, the idea that "RPGs exist so people can say interesting things to each other" is really just saying "Of those blocks, 'interactions' is the most important one." Meanwhile the hardcore simulationist might be tempted to say that the purity of the diegetic frame is actually the important thing. Gamists are about the players, immersionists about the roles. There's still a lot in here.

    It's also intriguing in what it leaves out; notably, System. It implies here and there that System happens, somehow, yet it's easy to walk away from this with the impression that perhaps System Doesn't Matter -- or, at least, doesn't matter as much as you might think. I'm not sure if that's intentional on their part or not.

    I suspect I'll find myself coming back to this model on occasion as we progress along through the book club.

    \nInteraction: The Key Element of Larp

    I'm not sure I have much to say about this one. It's yet another attempt to tackle that whole "so what the heck is larp anyway" question, which apparently was quite the burning question back in 2003.

    His answer sort of boils down to "it's the interaction, duh" which seems necessary but perhaps not sufficient. He doesn't really address at all the cases where there's lots of interaction but it's still not a larp. Which is fine, but he's playing a bit on the fast-and-loose side of things.

    This may seem to stand in contrast to the Turku thing we just saw, which as we recall suggests that eight guys all sitting alone in their own closets for an hour is perfectly-acceptable as larp. However, here we have an exceptionally-wide version of "interaction" -- the interaction between the roles and the world and the surroundings are explicitly included. So the mere absence of role-to-role interaction is not actually a problem.

    It is useful to get into this idea of the distinction between the character as its written in the ideal and the role as it is implemented during play. The difference between Sean Connery's James Bond and Roger Moore's, as an example. The terminology isn't terribly strong but it'll do.

    I think the author paints himself into a bit of a corner when he suggests, theoretically, all non-diegetic objects can be excluded from a larp. The problem is our participants, who are explicitly identified as non-diegetic. A larp without participants is like a forest without trees.

    As obvious as it seems, there's still no shortage of games of various sorts which just don't have enough interaction. So maybe it is worth saying again after all.

    As an example, I suspect some people's complaints about their own games of PrimeTime Adventure boil down to "lots of participant interaction, not enough role interaction." It might be a useful way to look at the issue.
