\n I've been lingering on this question for the past few weeks and have trotted it out at two dinner parties without much success.

I'm curious if in short games (single session / one shot) if you can successfully build character development AND build story?

Mind you my actual play experience is exceptionally limited but i would characterize something like the Roach/Roanoke as a story building games and Grey Ranks / (pos. My Life with Master and Annalise) and numerous Jeep games as character building. I'd be happy to be shown where i'm wrong if these are oversimplifications.

Presently, I'm stuck on a short form game that i'd like to incorporate both and haven't been able to overcome that obstacle.

thanks in advance for your thoughts, Eric.

    \n I think that the problem, at the start, is dissociating "story" from "character development". Yes, there are stories in books, comics and movie where there is no character development. And you know... they usually suck. They are the movie good enough (sometimes) to pass two hours and then you forget you saw them after five minutes. I don't know why in rpg country there is often a concept of "story" so limited to "the worst kind of story". Yes, I know that the first "stories" in rpgs were of that kind ("kill monsters and take their stuff") but there is a pervasive idea around that "these are the only kind of stories that you can do in rpg" that is, simply, bollocks.

    In GOOD stories, you always have character development. Even in a short story.

    You use as an example My Life With Master. Well, even if played for a one-shot (not the best way to play it), HOW can you play a game of My Life with master without (1) a complete story with drama, betrayals, horrors revealed and a big fight at the end, and (2) characters that develop and change during the game, finding love and in that love the strength to defy the master? The game is build in a way that you really simply can't avoid doing both during play...

    So, I think there is some disconnection in the meaning of the word used: what is that you see missing in your one-shot of My Life With Master?

    \n I would say that it's pretty difficult to do all of:

    a) develop a story
    b) develop all of the characters
    c) with a large number of characters
    d) over a short space of time

    You could easily do it with just one protagonist, say, or perhaps two or three. For something like that, DitV would probably work well. But the avergae RPG has at least three protagonists (PCs) and probably more, and then we start to run into problems where there's just not enough time to flesh out all of these characters in an eveneing AND have a developed story to boot. Something has to be simplified.

    \n The lack of tools is also an issue. Movies, books, comics, etc, use a bunch of tools that gamers either don't have access to or don't how to use, like internal monologues, a soundtrack (appropriate music during a certain scene can tell us a ton about a character without interrupting dialogue, etc), first-person description ("I shook at the thought of meeting John again, here in this store, where the floor was the colour of my vomit after that night we'd shared...") which brings the scenes to life and develops character at the same time, and so on. In a film, while someone is talking or something is happening, the facial expressions of other characters, camera angles, etc, can simultaneously deliver a lot of emotionally relevant content. In an RPG, most of the time those reactions would be described or acted out separately, taking more time.

    A short story that's only a few pages long can deliver a lot of info about one character if every word of those few pages is written with that goal in mind. Roleplayers don't always get the focus right away or know how to deliver that information.

    Perhaps most importantly, RPGs typically don't have an editor, who can remove all the stuff that doesn't develop character.

    ...I'm not saying this is impossible; quite the contrary. But it's worth pointing out some of the challenges faced.

    I'd love to hear about how some people might have developed such tools at their tables, in their games. Can we discuss that here, or does it need a new thread?

    \n ok, with regards to the "story is the stuff that happens" lobbyists, i think you all have good points. the idea that setting a stage and letting the improv sally forth does create a sort of fiction. however, i'm not sure that this is a responsible answer. it certainly is a default answer for that style of play. i'm ALL for char development. Bring it. But stumbling about in a loose mechanic isn't the same as generating story. and from an audience pov, i'm much more interested in where the characters plug into the story with their idiosyncrasies than i am in enduring a tour of histrionics.

    i can't pretend to be a master author here, but i get different experiences from types of play that set up character driven vs. story driven. and as ptevis indicates above, the likely outcome is that unless you're whipping the character the rest is derided as narration. At present i see people placing arguments for either style of play but not for both. That praxis seems to be the sweet spot to me. I'm frustrated that the recent crop of games is so decisive and not as muddled as i am.
