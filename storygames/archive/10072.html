    \n Oh good lord. I don't know how much of it I might still have (especially as my wife has a month and a half with all of our stuff before I can get back and prevent her from throwing away my "junk") but I do know I made a lot of it. I couldn't afford RPGs as a pre-teen, and I didn't think my mom would approve anyhow, so I made several of my own. My most ambitious was this thing I called "Dragon's Legend". In the kingdom of Ar, all of the cities had 'ar' worked into their name somehow.. Thefar was the city of thieves, naturally. This was pure, unadulterated heartbreaker schlock, as it came after my exposure to D&D; Basic (red box?). I had some doozies. Elves and dwarves were *races*, not classes! Ninja was a class, too. I had a spell that would summon 1d4 imps, which in my minds eye looked exactly like the ones from Final Fantasy I. One of them stuck around and became a PC, too. Eventually, you could work your way up to becoming a Dragon Rider!
    \n
    \nMan. I used to sit in the bathroom of the trailer I lived in with my parents and grandparents (the bathroom was the only relatively private place in such a crowded mobile home) and play Dragon's Legend, by myself, for hours on end. I once did 'the grind' for gold so I could buy enough wish rings to bring one character's stats all up to 18. I basically never played the game with anyone else, though I tried a few times.
    \n
    \n...that reads as much more disturbing than I remember it being.

    \n I remember playing one of my games (the one that gradually, slowly mutated into the Rustbelt, but really bears no apparent relation to it now) with my brother. His big greenie and my gaunt (read "his orc and my dark elf" -- and there were no hitpoints, there was "damage threshold" instead, "hit save" instead of armor class, and similar refusals to use any words that anyone else had used, ever) were always mixing it up. For no reason except that I was playing one, and my brother was playing the other. Pawn stance pwns!

    Somewhere, there's character sheets. Somewhere, somewhere, there's the combat record* of a fight in which Kragor (the orc) was driving a bus, and Dirc (the dark elf) was driving a truck and wearing a crash helmet. They collided head on, which was my plan, and Dirc dove into the bus (both windshields had been destroyed in a previous altercation) -- see what the crash helmet was for? -- shaved off part of Kragor's face with a shotgun blast, and then rolled to the back of the bus, where he released an angry, caged, giant red ant, so that it would go after Kragor. Kragor rolled a grenade to the back of the bus.

    And then, AND THEN, Kragor crashed the bus into the water tower. Everybody drowned for, like, a block.

    *This consists of a hand-drawn map with final positions evident and many poorly erased ones, a status sheet in which you can see damage (I liked tracking injuries in detail), and another sheet, the function of which I only vaguely remember, but it worked out timing and initiative based on a stat and what your actions were. I was all excited, "It's a simulation of real time! So realistic!" and stuff. (Oddly enough, I find today's The Rustbelt to be more realistic, and also far simpler)

    I'm gonna have to look for this stuff.

    \n Let's see, early influences...

    I first played Dungeons and Dragons as a 1st grader in an afterschool program. No physical evidence remained.

    In 5th grade, I taught myself the rules and started a AD&D club. I ran a homemade 'Mount Dread" campaign, following the hack and slash adventures of Harly's Mercenary Team. I bet that I could find a character sheet or two from those days.

    When I was 15, I discovered Brennan Taylor's now defunct online interactive history, "The Known World", which he was using to discover the history of his Legend of Yore world. I think that yahoo group is still up somewhere.

    When I was 17, I started designing "Deity" a sandbox civ game. I designed five versions of Deity over as many years, the final of which is sitting on my shelf, and though none of them satisfied me as games, that process taught me more about the art than anything else.

    \n I have my series of AD&D; modules that I made.

    \n
    \nOn the front of the cover I'd draw a cool cover pic and put the title up in stencil. I'd even number them and put a "company" logo on them. Each module featured a unique monster I created.
    \n
    \nLet's see, I had "Creatures of the Sand Pits" which included these lion headed devilish things that lived at the bottom of big pits in the desert and used telepathy to control humanoid armies.
