\n Okay, so over in the "What Currency are you Paid in?" thread, I asked Eero a question about paid AP, and he whispered me back an interesting and insightful answer, and suggested we start up a new thread about it. So below is the quote, and I'll follow up with my response.\n
Posted By: Eero TuovinenPosted By: jprussellPosted By: Eero TuovinenI'd have offered 20 \u20ac gift tokens in exchange for AP a long time ago if I didn't consider paid AP a form of Internet community spamming.
\nHmmm, a) is this different from offering a full/partial refund to folks who buy your game when they post AP, and b) if that is different, would you still consider it spamming/shilling/et cetera? What about if the reward is not straight up pecuniary, but rather added game-related goodness (you get the exclusive AP writers' supplement!)


This is really a separate topic - you should start a new thread if you'd like to talk this over in length. I'll answer this shortly from my viewpoint, feel free to quote me if you want to start a new thread with a bang

I don't really see a difference between forms of payment here; they all basically lower the threshold of posting artificially, causing communication that is privately motivated - not that private motivation is bad by definition (just look at playtest posting at the Forge), but when that private motivation is one without sympathy from the community all you're doing as advertiser is exploiting a medium not intended for what you're doing. I'm not even touching the issue of how this influences the contents or interpretation of the paid AP report; it is sufficient to know that this is material that would not have been written out of the motivations usually assumed for AP, such as excitement over the game or whatnot. Not that different from paid reviewing to my mind, really. (Do note that mine is apparently a minority viewpoint - most games media assumes that reviews should be paid for in free product if nothing else.)

Indies need all the good angles they can get, and being proactive about encouraging our primary form of publicity, word of mouth, is an attractive proposition. What the eager beawer might not realize is that the reason for why actual play reports are effective is because they are genuine communications pre-filtered by the notability threshold of the AP author; in other words, only the noteworthy sessions get AP reports, which is a part of why the reader might bother to read the report in the first place. Advertisement business has ever since its invention worked by flogging the public communications landscape, stealing attention and trust from legitimate forms of communication; the great men of advertising are often the ones who figured out how to make the jump to new medias or new modes of communication that bypass the cynicism and personal filters of the audience. From this historical perspective it's obvious that actual play reports are an attractive, lucrative advertising media simply because anybody spending their advertising money on AP will be exploiting public benefits developed in a non-commercialized medium of communication: even as it decreases public trust in the AP report institution, your paid actual play report will gain more attention, trust and goodwill from the audience than a normal paid advertisement would merit. Of course a wide-spread practice of paying for AP would soon weaken the benefits of the practice, just like all forms of advertisement have reduced in value as they've become established and the audience has learned to disregard them.

    \n First off, I'm personally not anywhere near the phase of pushing hard to promote a game, so this is mostly abstract to me at this point. So anybody with real experience promoting games, please take my comments with a grain of salt.

    Now, I'm of mixed feelings here. On the one hand, I think that the idea that advertising and otherwise commodotizing existing forms of communication cheapens them is rather insightful and almost definitely true. That being said, I'm also a rabid American capitalist pig dog, who's all about the free market (I've got a shiny new boxed game to prove it) and incentivizing behavior we want to see and apparently adding "izing" to the end of nouns to make buzz words (if it was good enough for Plato, it's good enough for me).

    More seriously, I feel like there has to be some way to provide an incentive to do AP reports that does not in and of itself encrappen AP reporting in general. Certainly, genuine AP reports are the product of an enthusiastic roleplayer with a great experience. I definitely don't want to mess with that. On the other hand, it's the product of an enthusiastic roleplayer with a great experience who happens to be familiar with the concept of AP reports and regularly posts in fora online. I think this might be the thing to focus on, here (or a thing, at least). See, until last year when I started checking out places like SG and the Forge and what not, I didn't know that AP was "a thing". I had read battle reports for miniature wargames, I had related stories to friends about cool events in roleplaying games, but I had never thought of going online and sharing my awesome experience with this awesome game until I found out 'oh hey, that's a part of the gamer culture online'.

    So, my thinking is, how do you get the people who are excited about your game to be excited in public? And is there a way to do that that doesn't generate false enthusiasm or shilling or whatevs? Putting a little thing in your game that says "please go talk about your play experience at X website if you had a good time!" is something I've seen done, but seems like fairly weak sauce. It seems to me that if you have a Venn diagram where one circle is "people excited about a game" and the other circle is "people motivated to talk about games on the internet (or otherwise in public)" that there could be a way to push the first circle to overlap the second, rather than bringing the second circle over to the first one with promises of shiny gamer loot.

    Unfortunately, I have no idea *how* to do that, and so I ask the floor for comments. Is paid AP a bad thing? Are there alternatives? What are some other/better ways to get your game's name out there?

    Posted By: jprussellMore seriously, I feel like there has to be some way to provide an incentive to do AP reports that does not in and of itself encrappen AP reporting in general.
    \nI agree with you in this. However, I'm thinking that we're looking at the incentive from the wrong direction to begin with if we think that it's the publisher who needs to incentivize AP reporting. That way lies a conflict of interests, for the value gains for the publisher and the audience are different. What I mean is simple if you stop to consider it: the publisher wants AP reports for the (ideally positive) publicity, while the audience wants AP reports for their honesty, insight, excitement and other qualities of which some (but not all) are at conflict with the publisher's wishes. I personally think that the most important quality of AP is the harsh threshold that personal excitement needs to cross before anybody cares enough to write one; this is a much more effective means of content-selection than many we see in this age of overflowing information - a game will only get AP attention if it produces experiences people want to write about. The publisher's benefit, however, is the opposite: he wants to flood the medium with AP about his own game no matter the veracity of the reporting. This difference in goals is fundamental, the best you can do is to dress it up in nice clothes in all the various ways people use in justifying advertising.

    How do we incentivize AP without getting that conflict of interest? Seems to me that the right way is for those who benefit of true AP reports to do the incentivizing. Because the benefit we're interested in here is audience benefit, it needs to be the audience who incentivizes, not the publisher. This can be done in many ways, but one of the oldest in the book is to set up a concerned specialist who calls for and edits submissions into pleasing material for the audience; a magazine or website, in other words. This age-old structure allows you to pay the AP writer without compromising the neutrality of the AP phenomenon; you can even have the publishers of games subsidize the medium if they find the AP content valuable without compromising the integrity of the process, thanks to the firewalls afforded by the editorial process and the fact that any moneys granted to the magazine are not earmarked, but rather distributed according to the editorial policy.

    Of course I'm not seriously suggesting a magazine here, I'm just illustrating the flaw in thinking that game publishers should be the ones incentivizing AP writing. If you truly think that there is too little AP but don't want to your subsidized AP to be branded as commercial advertising, the simplest solution to that is to not mix commercial interests with it. Promise to pay $10 for every AP for the next week no matter the game (add editorial requirements to taste) and nobody will mind; to the contrary, you'll be hailed as a valuable culture activist and patron of the art.

    Even more seriously, you know what I think is the single greatest incentive for AP reporting? Historically the only reason AP even is a "thing" is the forum culture of the Forge, which made AP a central substance of theory and design discussion at a time when the whole idea of talking about your play was just something you occasionally and randomly did with no shared culture or purpose to it at all. Since then AP reporting has ebbed and vaned with the forum cultures of the various forums. Thus: the single best incentive for AP reporting is probably a forum culture that encourages and facilitates AP reporting of high quality. The distaste many of us have for paid AP is part of this, note: we uphold standards by being distasteful or disinterested in low-quality AP.

    \n I do totally say to people that they should buy my game if they want to review it. Maybe it's a dick move. This is an important point to me because I am seriously tempted every time somebody asks for a review copy. It's such an obvious and widely accepted thing to do, and I don't even usually have anything against the people doing the asking, so it's not easy for me to brush off the offers without apologizing for my unreasonable attitude. We used to go to ridiculous contortions with the Finnish roleplaying magazine when they wanted review copies of our stuff - ended up trading product for product or doing personal gifts or whatnot to get over the fact that the mag had a principle of not reviewing anything they paid for and us having a principle of not giving out review copies.

    I guess that if there were more serious journalism in the field then I'd be more comfortable with free review copies. I don't really worry about the objectiveness of mainstream art critique (for this reason at least), as I can trust that the critic is so clearly established as a professional that some comp copies are a minor matter of course for him - he gets them all the time, he gets more of them than he ever ends up reviewing, he has no reason whatsoever to let it affect him. This is not the case with roleplaying; people review as a hobby, and they certainly have much more reason to be influenced by gifts.

    It's a grey area for me, and if it was really serious I'd probably have to rethink my attitude and wrestle more with it. Luckily I never need to worry about this for anybody else than myself, and so far I've had no trouble refusing the review offers, as the very worst I'm causing is some additional obscurity for my games and some annoyment for others with my holier-than-thou attitude.

    \n I may be drifting the thread a bit here. If so, let me know and I'll find my own thread. But I've been thinking about this thing for a while now and it's very much related.

    Imagine, if you will, a collection of games from a variety of designers. These games are free, but not freely available. They may be playtests or ashcans or full-fledge games or even games that are otherwise available for a price, but free in this collection. This collection is The Ziggurat.

    In order to gain access to these games one would have to join The Ziggurat. Once you do, you're allowed to take any three games. Pick and play with them at your leisure. But three is all you get.

    If you want more, write an AP about one of them and post it somewhere. Tell The Ziggurat what you've done and The Ziggurat will let you grab two more games.

    And the circle of life continues. Each time you're hungry for more games all you have to do is write up an AP for one of the games you haven't written an AP for yet and poof! Two more games.

    There it is in a nutshell. A way to incentivize AP. A way to make free games more attractive because you have to work for them. A way to put your playtest documents in front of players looking to give feedback. And there's more fun things to be done from there. The Ziggurat could have a tier structure. Tier Zero games are available to anyone who joins. Tier One games can only be downloaded after your first AP, Tier Two after your second, and so forth. Exciting, big name games that you'd normally have to pay for could be waiting for you at the next tier. Who knows?

    And if you had a large enough body of contributors, both from the design end and from the playing end, you'd produce a collection of AP that could be used for all sorts of fun things like annual awards for players and GMs.
