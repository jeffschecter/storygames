    \n I'm not convinced there's an awful lot of value to be gained by these sorts of examinations. Many of my meetings at work are less than perfect due to the behaviour of the participants - needing to comment without having anything of substance to add, refusal to take responsibility for decision making, aimless digressions from the purpose of the meeting, and so on, and very few of them would fit the standard classification of geek. 'How over why' is pretty common in my experience, as is favouring complexity over simple descriptions - especially in conversations relating to business jargon.

    There's an ideal standard, most of the time we fall short, but I'm not convinced this is to do with being geeks as much as it is just a common feature of any human interactions. I'm all for navel-gazing, but the geeks vs, normals distinction seems a bit tired.

    Posted By: AndySo, it's an interesting article. It has some behaviors I'd never see at a table, and some behaviors that I do see at the table but never put my finger on until now. Also, it lacks a few, like "Monty Python quotes" and "15-year-old boy humor".

    Here they are, for people who think they know what the article is about without reading it. Seriously, check it out. But in short:

    Struggling with turn-taking

    Obsessing over correctness and completeness

    Preferring exact numerical responses

    Using technical terms without checking for understanding

    Focusing on the how rather than the what or the why

    Favoring complexity and detail over simplicity in descriptions

    Rapidly enumerating long lists of items

    Showing a lack of interest in outward appearances

    Evangelizing their favorite technologies
    \nTo my mind, some of these seem to apply to particular brands of geeks rather than geeks in general. For example, using technical terms is universal among geeks - but the types of technical terms vary widely. Theater geeks will talk about particular plays or stage direction terms - fashion geeks will drop the names of brands or designers as if they expect you to know them, etc. Similarly, obsessiveness is basically definitional to geeks, and geeks will always favor detail within their domain of geekery. Music geeks will want to know which symphony you went to hear and who was conducting, etc.

    On the other hand, numerical responses and technology evangelizing are typical of technical geeks (duh). Other geeks evangelize other things, like geeky sports fans who evangelize for their favorite team. Lack of interest in outside appearances is usual in a number of geek branches that aren't appearance related.

    Geeks generally tend to get along well with other geeks of overlapping interests, but can seem off-putting or cliquish to those that don't share their obsessions. In games, I find that sometimes one needs to mediate between those with different social standards. So, for example, sometimes there is a problem in a geeky crowd with a non-geeky player who comes to the game and just wants to hang out and chat about non-game-related things, whereas others want to focus on the game. No one is inherently right or wrong here, but there are different priorities. I don't like to go into detail with meta-discussion that can go around in circles - I think it's better to find a quick compromise a the time, and later talk things out a little more.

    \n It's not a geek or not geek thing. The behaviors he's describing are not binary. They're on a scale, and there are some people who desperately need a nudge in the "normal" direction. I am a pretty geeky guy and I am sure I demonstrate some of these behaviors, but listening to some of my friends tell stories while constantly interrupting one another to make sure details are included or are accurate that do not matter to me one bit as the listener drives me insane. I have two friends who are brothers and who watch movies together regularly. Getting them to give me a movie summary in, say, two minutes is impossible because there is this absurd back-and-forth that occurs when one misses or is incorrect on the most minute of facts regarding the movie. There are geeks all along the spectrum, and I am sure that if anyone who is offended by the article went to a gaming convention, chances are you would bump into one or two people who you wish would read and reflect on this article. I got cornered by a guy at a convention once who wanted to go on, at length, about all his Vampire character concepts including a Brujah with a five in all of his physical attributes and a Malkavian who worshipped duct tape.

    I have tried over the years to scale back some of my behavior that other people find off-putting. It's not selling out on my identity or acquiescing to the unreasonable demands of "normal people," it's wanting to make other people feel comfortable and at ease in their interactions with me. It's wanting to make sure that I don't inflict the behaviors I find socially frustrating on others. It's wanting to be self-aware and making sure I am doing things in the way that I want to do them. It's wanting to cultivate a kind of social generosity. Oh and it's also about wanting to meet tons of babes.

    I am not Anna, but I can shine a light on this, through a slightly different lens.

    I can speak French. Vraiment, je peux! But when I try to speak French "casually" or "naturally," I am all-but-tongue-tied. Why? Because my natural vocabulary--the one with which I think (and, no, I don't think in French, yet)--is larger than my French vocabulary. By probably an order of magnitude (I can't say "order of magnitude" in French). The result? I don't speak French to French people. I know some, even. But having to constantly self-edit, just so I can find a French word I know, is remarkably, REMARKABLY tiring and tedious. So much so that I don't even try.

    Much like I almost never try to constrain my diction in a "mixed" crowd. I say what I mean, with the best, most-nuanced words at my command. And when folks don't understand a word, 9 times out of 10 they don't ask what it means (I am always impressed when someone does) but rather rely on context, word parts, and guesswork to "keep up with me." So... how do I know that I'm "speaking over someone's head," barring obvious visual cues like eyes glassing over or dropping the subject?

    (This all sets aside the very common situation in which another person is not listen to you but listening for key words on which to hang their own statements. Dueling Monologues pretending to be conversation.
    It also sets aside the obvious question of "Why should I bother to dumb down my conversation, when it's likely I won't want further conversation with that person at that lower level?" Or "why should I not be myself, to accommodate someone else, in a purely social situation?")

It's not about dumbing things down (far from it, I love weird and obscure words too much to not use them when I can), it's about getting into the proper groove with someone else and finding a natural conversational style in common, so that at the end of the dialogue we're both pleased by how it went.

      In fact, it's so much fun that my most common linguistic sin is doing it inappropriately -- I've been dinged by more than a few professors and supervisors for being too colloquial and casual when giving "serious" presentations, because even when I know I should be adhering strictly to the best vocabulary available, I keep searching for the best vocabulary for different audiences (even audiences who are not actually there). Sometimes it comes across as me having an irrepressible urge to drag down high-minded proper discourse with slang, profanity, and wordplay, and gives the impression that I am not serious about what I am saying.

      But then, I think that's fun, too. It doesn't work out so well on the internet, though.
