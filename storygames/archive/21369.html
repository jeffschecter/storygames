\n This game and its supporters seem to make a big deal of it solving the problem of "planning" a mission to get straight to the fun action part.
Where "planning" seems to be this long winded, tedious, problematic and in the end utterly useless activity that can bog down an otherwise fun and enjoyable game session.

I don't really get it.
I don't recognise "planning" as a problem, and actually enjoy it a lot myself, and in the last 10 years of coherent roleplaying I've never experienced it as a problem... or even as something remotely resembling this horrible thing that sucks time and fun away from the game session.

So maybe there is a communication hiccup?
What BitD calls "planning" is not the same thing I do and enjoy and call "planning"?
Am I not getting something?

I've been watching the series of youtube APs set up by John Harper himself... and at least in the 1st episode they DO what I would call "planning".
And it is brief and tight and useful and the game doesn't seem to do anything about it... one way or the other.
What I see is the GM presenting a problem, the Players asking themselves "what should we do about it?", and then one Player starting the conversation with an idea.
At least in their first mission, Adam offers an idea and everyone likes it and they roll with it and that's that... 7 minutes of "planning" from start to end.
Plus 1 more minute when the actual mission starts and they have to pick a mission template (or whatever one of the 5 standard plans is called).
Again one Player comes up with an actual plan, and everyone likes it, and they roll with it... it all takes 1 minute, literally, and in this case they make the fictional plan fit one of the available templates, and then keep describing how they enact it... which kicks off the active part of the mission.

Which is pretty much what happened to me and my group (I'm a Player, not the GM) now that we are trying out the game.
We were presented with a bunch of opportunities, and we discussed which one to take.
This is planning to me: what do we do? We do this thing here because of those reasons there!

Then when time came to pick a plan template it felt awkward to just pick one at random, so we too came up with an actual plan.
This was a discussion that took maybe 2 minutes, again with one person throwing an idea on the table that everyone likes, and then mapping it to one of the available templates.

Specifically.
We need to "kidnap" a ghost being held in the basement of a house near the docks and being guarded by some powerful gang people.
We decide to get into the target basement digging a hole in the wall of an adjacent basement. Someone adds that it would be good to get in this other basement from the wall facing one of the many city canals.
And we all agree and roll with it.

So... what IS in practical terms this dreaded "planning" that the game helps avoid?
Can anyone offer specific actual play examples?

Or is "bad planning" only that thing when Players disagree on something and start endlessly bickering over it?
Because that's what I call plain and simple "Player disagreement" and BitD doesn't seem (as far as I understand it) to handle it at all.
So, what gives?

    \n Let me talk about my experience with bad planning.

    I once played in a Shadowrun game at a convention. There were 6-7 players of all different types. Pre-generated characters. I took the "Face" type.

    Our mission was some kind of heist but we had reason to be paranoid about our patron. I was to meet with the patron at a coffee shop to get some information about the job, if I remember correctly.

    But first we had to PLAN.

    The group all kicked into gear, doing what each one did best. There was recon, drone surveillance, hacking, infiltration, and so on. First we came up with a course of action -- that took 15-20 minutes.

    Then each player took 10-15 minutes to play out their part, with exposition, dice rolls, and narration of results. Failures produced new avenues to try. Successes sometimes offered new things to worry about, new obstacles for the plan. More things for preparation.

    After about an hour of this, we were SET UP. It was my turn to go in and negotiate a deal, gather what information I could, figure out what was Really Going On. I was going to go in alone, but our bodyguard thug type insisted on accompanying me for backup. Fine.

    We go in, my character sits down with the contact. The bodyguard goes Leroy Jenkins on the situation (I think he was reacting to a heavily armed dude there) and violence breaks out. All planning was now invalid and I never got say more than "hello" in character.

    Now everyone else went back into planning mode. What's the NEW PLAN?

    The hotel fire alarm went off and we all had to go outside for five minutes. I used that opportunity to gather my stuff and leave and just not come back. I had not had any fun in about 90 minutes.

    ----------

    Blades in the Dark makes most of that detailed planning activity unnecessary. If you're going to do a heist, come up with a general plan (5-10 minutes of discussion) and get into the thick of it. You can skip all the SETUP activities, though, because the resolution system lets you take the least interesting steps and turn them into Assist actions after the GM tosses an obstacle in your way. Take a Stress, explain how you're assisting, give the other player an extra die. Granted, only one player can Assist any given roll, but it works out nicely in play.

    I've only played Blades once (this past weekend), but it obviated all the issues I've had with traditional heist games.

    \n I\u2019m the GM in hasimir\u2019s (the OP) game, and I think some of his worry is based on me rushing things since we\u2019re only running a couple session demo, and me being a nervous first time Blades GM.

    The basic Blades idea as I see it is \u201cdon\u2019t (over) plan for what might happen, plan for what is actually happening\u201d, and any explanation of that must include the idea of flashbacks. The players can at any time say \u201cI planned for that!\u201d, play a short flashback showing how they prepared something in advance for what\u2019s going on right now, and have a useful thing in place when they snap back to \u201cnow\u201d.

    Of course players should establish some sort of plan, at least enough to decide on a basic approach (the plan templates), but not engage in what Adam_Dray describes. And of course, if your group genuinely enjoys long, drawn out planning, trying to account for every eventuality, you can do that too. But considering the basic \u201ccut to the chase\u201d idea of Blades, you probably don\u2019t need that system for your game.

    \n I don't think Adam's example is really an example of what Blades is looking to avoid, except for the part where the party spend a bunch of thinking up (and then implementing, which I guess can be "action" if you want) stuff that turned out to have no impact on anything.

    But I am amazed that people here have never sat down with a group who are "willing" to spend almost an entire session trying to figure out what they THINK will be the least dangerous approach to a problem, only for them to discover that a fundamental assumption was wrong or botch the very first die roll and have literally four hours of discussion become irrelevant. Because this has totally happened to me. I put "willing" in quotes above, because it's not really something the players WANT to do, but they feel like they need to find the best/safest way to do the thing. This is the problem Blades is trying to address, and, in my experience, does. The group needs a basic plan - "We'll go in through the skylight, take out the guards, grab the loot, and get the hell out of there." but is discouraged from "Well, what if Drayven the Fist is guarding the place? We need a way to take him out." and then spending two hours of play time coming up with an elaborate method to neutralize a character who turns out not even to be there at all.

    The objective of the design is to avoid planning for stuff that isn't relevant. Because you can flashback and deal with it once you know it is relevant.

    So no, Hasimir, it's not some secret code word. It's "planning" - as in "trying to come up with answers to all the contingencies" and if you've never had a group that's done it, it's not because you have a different definition or that it's some "pet peeve" of John Harper's.

    When that happens, it can be exhausting after a while. Plus, I noticed these things happening:

    - after a while, you're not sure how much you're in-character anymore. Perhaps, a player who's character was not supposed to be physically there chimed in, and I had to remark, as the GM: "Wait a moment! Where were you having this conversation, again, and when?" Not as a way to shut down the intervening player, you know (they should be excused, as it's boring as hell to watch people making plans and not even being able to chime in!) - but because I, the GM, actually got confused!

    - after a while more, confusion starts arising between what we've said and was actually a done, accomplished fact and what we'd said as a possible course of action, still subject to confirmation or revision. Like, someone mentioned buying rope, and someone else is now assuming they have rope, while I as the GM thought they were merely inquiring about the price of rope but this conversation was supposedly taking place in the dead of night when markets are closed, so of course one could go for rope, but tomorrow morning, on the way to Aktebeth's mansion... "Oh, and have we all agreed we're going to murder the wizard, then?" "What, wait, I thought I'd said no, I'm not gonna murder anybody at all!"

    That's the Shared Imagined Space breaking down, at the fundamental level of not being quite fully and clearly shared anymore. Like, not a big, dramatic breakdown - more of a dull, unexciting erosion, but it's entropy nonetheless!

    \n Here's my take:

    Planning in RPGs is subject to all the pitfalls of real-life decision-making. Most games make your decisions simpler in some way. When it comes to planning, RPGs don't (not unless they're very rules-first, fiction-second).

    So, if you have a table full of players who agonize over decisions and make themselves miserable trying to make sure they get everything just right, then that'll be your planning experience. Or if you have a table full of players who avoid tricky decisions and just do something else until fate intervenes, then that will be your planning experience. This means that mileage varies immensely. A table full of inspired and efficient decision-makers will have a great time planning.

    So, if your table has some hitches in its decision-making, what to do? Well, some RPGs have said, "Don't plan. Retcon anything you need." I'm thinking of the Cortex-based Leverage game, but I know I've seen several others. I think this is the right approach for a story-making game, because watching characters plan isn't usually that dramatic or interesting, and when it is, it's just as good or better as a flashback.

    For a challenge game, though, "Retcon whatever you need," is terrible. It takes the single most important type of player decision the game could potentially feature and just removes it. I'm not saying that every challenge game results in players stealing victory from the jaws of defeat via clever planning. But the potential is there. I've done it. A lot. And it feels awesome.

    I think Blades attempts an interesting middle ground. If you're not gonna play through every planning decision, at least settle on an approach well enough to pick a Template, and then allow that Template to factor into what happens. Or, if you do want to play through every planning decision (I know that's not by the book, but stuff happens), the set of Templates provides useful inspiration and guidance!

    Perhaps even more interesting is The Sprawl, where there's a little resource management around what you've done as prep before the caper itself. It can't be an infinite amount of just anything, so ration accordingly! (Disclaimer: for all I know, Leverage is like this too; I haven't played it.)

    I've done some tinkering of my own with strategic planning mechanics, both a risk-reward deal for prep (the more ambitious the thing you wish to have done, the more risk that your attempt to achieve it had fallout which will now catch up to you), and a decision-making guide for inefficient planners. I consider this problem -- optimizing planning in RPGs -- as yet unsolved, but ultimately solvable (probably!).

    \n I usually hate planning in RPGs for several reasons.

    1) It's often about controlling other players. Instead of the shy or the individualist player making his decision on the fly, he's pressured by the controlling players to proceed by the plan. The ideas that make it to the plan are the ones that the controlling players approve. Any power relationships in the group tend to be emphasized in the planning phase. What is being proposed is not nearly as important as who's saying it.

    2) It's about making "smart" decisions. Emotional decisions are framed as irrational decisions, even if they make total sense in the game's supposed genre. Everyone starts to play their smartest self, to look as smart as possible to the other players. Playing an emotional character just makes you an outsider and a troublemaker.

    3) It's intensely speculative. The whole point of planning is to prepare for everything, and imagining a parallel, "smart" world becomes almost like a contest.

    I end up feeling like a free jazz player who's forced to play a swing tune composed by control freaks, or becoming one myself.
