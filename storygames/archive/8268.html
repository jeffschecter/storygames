    \n It really depends on the mechanics and the players. If the mechanics are particularly flavorless or the GM just fiats most stuff or the players are prone to "roleplaying" a certain way despite what the rules reward or penalize then the effect is likely very slight. If the mechanics are geared strongly toward a certain playstyle (intentionally or not), or the players are the sort to seek every available bonus or reward the rules provide, then the game will likely play much differently. That said, "much differently" is still pretty mild. As a rule, the game will probably just "feel" different. Only at the extreme will players become frustrated and look for a different set of rules.

    My group found, for example, that GURPS didn't handle "D&D-style" fantasy very well (for us) by the rules. Even the "fantasy adventure mode" rules tweaks didn't quite fix the issue. This is because GURPS' default "realism" seemed to (for us) discourage heroic activity, as things were too "gritty" and heroism often wasn't worth the risk (for us).

    \n Mechanics certainly affect the way I play my characters. For one thing, mechanics dictate the sorts of characters you can make. If my group is playing, say, second edition AD&D with just the core rules, then I can't effectively create a character who's an artificer who creates mechanical creatures and devices and uses them to do things. I could try to use that as "color" for one of the existing classes, but the mechanics don't really support the concept, so I'd be kludging it at best. In first edition AD&D, I can't make a thief who specializes in a few of the thieving skills -- the mechanics simply don't allow it. In later editions, I can.

    For another, mechanics determine the effectiveness of your character. If I play a thief in first edition AD&D, I have to resign myself to the fact that, except for Climb Walls, none of my thieving skills are going to be reliable, until I get to very high levels. In GURPS, on the other hand, I can make a starting thief who's very good at what he does.

    On the flip side of things, GURPS' combat system is oriented toward more gritty, realistic combat than xD&D's. If I want to make an epic warrior who routinely takes on dozens of foes and wins, xD&D supports me in that more than GURPS does.

    Going further, there are major differences in style between types of games. A big part of point-based games, like GURPS and, even more strongly, the Hero System, is paying for your equipment. In xD&D, magic items are a big part of character power... but you get them essentially by GM fiat. The difference in how much magic GMs give out is a large part of the difference between a "gritty" D&D game and a "Monty Haul" one. In Hero, however, players are supposed to buy magic items with points -- which gives the players much more control over what magic items they have, but also means that the GM isn't supposed to simply take those magic items away -- and if they do, the players get those points back to spend on something else (unless they've taken particular disadvantages, such as "independent", on those points).

    System also dictates more basic things. What races are available to select from. What roles are available to those races -- I can be a dwarf wizard in GURPS, but not in AD&D1 or 2. Random-roll attributes can make a huge difference, if you're lucky or unlucky on your rolls. To take it to the extreme, being lucky enough to roll an 18 while making a fighter in first or second edition AD&D both gives you access to percentile strength -- and the huge bonuses it can give -- and gives you bonus experience points for having high strength, compounding your advantage over "normal" fighters over time. On the flip side of things, having an 18 Intelligence as a wizard in first or second edition AD&D means very little until you get to high levels -- especially since many groups ignored the rules for learning spells (which in itself is another example of how system makes a difference in play!). You'd be better off putting that 18 into Dexterity (giving you a +4 to AC, a big bonus for wizards who can't wear armor) or Constitution (roughly doubling your hit points).

    Which brings me to another example of how system matters in play -- early xD&D's quirky multiclassing and dual-classing rules. In GURPS or Hero, I can easily create a character who is mostly a warrior, but knows a little magic. Early versions of xD&D make that very difficult. If I'm demi-human, I can multiclass as a fighter/mage, but there's no option to do anything but a 50/50 split of my XP, so I'm going to wind up about 50/50 fighter/mage. If I'm human, I can dual-class, but that both requires very high ability scores (at least a 17 in the prime req for the class I want to switch to), and has some funky requirements. Further, I can't *start* as a fighter who knows a little about magic.

    So, yes... system affects play in a lot of ways, I'd say.

    \n Mechanics in traditional games tend to be highly optional. Depending on the group any given mechanics will be used or ignored, modified or preserved. In a real way, the group comes to discover a game among themselves as they play it. From this perspective it seems like the mechanics which aren't used have no effect on play. But this is not quite accurate.

    Mechanics define the game in a conceptual way. This means that players will make a conscious choice to engage or disengage with those mechanics, but they rarely make their choices irrespective of those mechanics. Thus the social structuring of play becomes a series of binary choices, about when to incorporate the mechanics and when to expel them. This really narrows the space of play, because before considering the mechanics the group could have done any number of things, but now all their decisions are made in the context of the mechanics.

    Specific options tend to polarize our thinking to accepting, declining, or perhaps permuting that option. Truly different choices which might have occurred to us before hand are now nearly unthinkable. This is even more the case when variant mechanics are used, they become conceptually an exhaustive set.

    All of this exerts a tendency for everything explicit in the rules, like stats defining a character, to be either explicitly relevant or explicitly irrelevant. The rules become a language for what happens in play, they determine what is easy to say, and what is hard.

    - Mendel S.

    \n I think the most important impact of the system and the mechanics is how they shape the role of the participants in play.

    The GM and players will approach a game with certain default assumptions about how it's supposed to work and what they're supposed to "do". If the mechanics of the ruleset all apply to other stuff, then they won't change gameplay in any way except cosmetically. However, if and when the mechanics DO intersect with those assumed procedures, they most definitely will change gameplay.

    For instance, consider a game group whose heroes fight and overcome monsters, and never really have a serious chance of dying. They could pick up GURPS, make characters that are significantly more powerful than the opposition (either by making the heroes stronger or making the opposition weaker) and go for it, with the GM fudging any die rolls that might kill the heroes. Their games wouldn't be all that different. They might feel it's "harder" or less natural to get that preferred gameplay in that system, but it won't stop them, per se.

    However, if they pick up a game that doesn't allow anyone to fudge results, or a game that contains mechanics which do not allow player characters to be heroes (say, some kind of morale system), or (heaven forbid) a game that doesn't have a GM-figure, their playstyle will HAVE to change. Because the "unwritten" parts of their group's System will clash with the mechanics provided by the game.

    I think that a lot of groups in that situation just modify the mechanics until their preferred playstyle becomes possible again, often because they perceive them as broken. After all, they can't play the way they know they're "supposed to" play, so there must be something wrong with the rules, right?

    Realize, the conceptual limits caused by mechanics are neutral. They can be beneficial or detrimental, depending on the group and situation. I often find understanding this useful because it can help me avoid accidentally taking play somewhere that will require a reference frame shift on the part of the other players (because I share a different set of conceptual limits than the other players). It also helps me avoid feeling off when players break my limits. Intentionally expanding the conceptual scope of play is sometimes fine, but it pays to be careful.

    Ultimately, of course it is very difficult to realize what it is you are not thinking. I find this idea more useful in game design. Effects like this influence play behavior as much or more than hard rules. It's part of the whole process of induction - how the game text becomes the game as it's played. Something I strongly feel is the next frontier of RPG design and theory.

    \n Dave,

    Following on the coat tails of others, I would say that mechanics defiantly affect play, specifically as they are tied to the rewards system.

    Agon - Your characters cannot replenish unless they rest, to rest they must play games and complete. You must be willing to compete with your fellow players to play this game.
    Burning Wheel - By pursuing your character's goals you gain Artha. By taking dangerous risks your characters advances. You don't have to do either but if either is absent the game will be very boring for the players ("nothing is happening") and very frustrating for the GM ("Why aren't my players perusing their beliefs and taking risks?")
    D&D - You are rewarded for killing things, and to a lesser extent overcoming skill challenges. Further, 90% of what is on the character sheet revolves around killing things. If you don't kill things you will not reasonably progress.

    In each of these games there is a strong carrot and stick response caused by the mechanical reward system. Sure, in any of those games you're character might feel a great sense of victory killing the man that killed his father, but of them only Burning Wheel will give him a reward specifically for achieving that goal. In D&D he might get experience points, but he'd get the same experience points if he killed some monster of equal level (4th) or CR (3.x).

    So, yes, my experience is that mechanics strongly affect play styles because players want rewards for play. If they play a game that doesn't reward them for the type of actions their characters take, it quickly becomes frustrating for the players.

    Now... if you include Shadows of Yesterday in the mix, that's a whole other story.

      \n See, I don't think the character exists separately from the rules. It's some kind of mind-body dualistic argument that just doesn't wash with me. The character is an artifact of the game.

      There might be, in your head, this Platonic version of your character, and you express that character through the lens of an RPG. If that's the case, then it's impossible to perfectly play the character in any system at all. But let's say that System XYZ just happens to be perfect for playing Bob the Barbarian or whatever. System ABC won't be. Maybe System ABC is fucking Bunnies and Burrows, or Maid, or Paranoia, and it's not gonna come close to letting you represent your Ideal Bob the Barbarian.

      Why not? Well, first, because RPGs have Character rules. You have to express Bob as a character with those rules, which might not address some vital aspect ("There's no Strength attribute of any kind?!") or which may require you to address some aspect that is totally irrelevant to your character ideal ("What do you mean, choose a sexual fetish?!").

      Second, the game's Setting may not fit into your vision for the character. Bob the Barbarian probably has no place in a Ghostbusters game.

      Third, the game's Situation may not be appropriate for the character. Where does Bob fit into, "You're a crime detective, searching for clues to stop a serial killer before he kills again"?

      Fourth, the game's Color may not permit your character. "What do you mean, there are no barbarians in this game? It's a fantasy game, so there must be barbarians. No?"

      Last, the game's System may get in the way of playing your character. Other people above talk about this kind of stuff, but it may ultimately be less important than the other four things I mention. Maybe stakes-setting and director stance fuck up your ability to get into Bob's head and immerse properly. Maybe you want to start out Bob as a low-level barbarian and see him climb to power over time, and a game without heroic advancement rules won't do that.

      And the topic is, how much do the mechanics (not necessarily the "system" as that is too broad for this topic) affect play?

      \nMost people are answering the "how different is your actual play, and the stories that are told by it" part. And that's huge. Character types (like classes or other assumptions) change things. What kind of GM prep can be done and needs to be done changes what kind of play you will get. Character creation will change things--people will make different characters in different systems. For instance, I won't make the same character in random chargen D&D and point-buy D&D unless I just happen to roll exactly the same stats, will I? The assumed power levels, risk levels, and all that other stuff will change things. The implied tone of the game books and the presentation of the game will change things.

      As for character actions, here are some simple ones:

      If I am playing a magician, how much time will I spend studying my spellbooks, if any?

      In D&D, it'll be up to 15 minutes per spell level, or whatever it is, depending on how much fighting has been going on.
      In GURPS, it might be "only rarely, when I need to research new spells".
      In some other game it might be, "no, magicians don't know how to read".

      If I am playing a swordsman, how often will I try to parry an opponent's attack in combat instead of attacking myself?

      In D&D, never. Parrying is not an option. You always attack; parrying is kinda maybe assumed to happen all the time anyway.
      In Rapiers Combat (a made-up game), I will Parry about 60% of the time, because it lets me accumulate an advantage over my opponent.
      In System d65, I will parry each time I roll a 6 in combat, because the book says "When you roll a 6, you have successfully parried your opponent's attack. You may proceed to roll your attack now."
      In yet another game, I will never parry. I'd love to, because it suits my character, but the rules are set up so that parrying almost always means you get your arm cut off, and that DOESN'T suit my character.

      etc.

      The stuff that won't vary very much ("will I rescue the little boy?") likely is the same from game to game precisely because it isn't covered by mechanics. In other words, those choices are exactly the same, governed the same way by the mechanics, in those different games. But the stuff that IS governed by mechanics... is probably going to be pretty different!

      \n Mechanics set expectations in games I have played. For example: how effective a character with a .44 or an assault rifle will be is often determined by the rules. How safe jumping from a four-story building is (in Hero we had characters jump off of high buildings to escape certain-death monsters--not so in GURPS--and a lot of debate in DitV as to how it'd work).

      Even if--especially if--a rule is not invoked it can effect play. Even in the case where house-rules are used (by surprise--something I do not advocate) over printed rules, the presence of the rules can effect expectations (and that includes the complex case where the player knows the printed rule is either considered weak or otherwise suspects it may do something counter to the flow of the game and may or may not take action based on it being used).

      In a Hero espionage game where players were from competing agencies and were forbidden to use "deadly force" against each other, one guy (KGB) shot an American agent with a low-caliber handgun since the 1d6 RKA damage couldn't kill them. I, as the GM, disallowed that (I allowed the action but told them the agency would interpret it as deadly force--the player, a pretty rules-laywerish guy--was okay with that interpretation and didn't pull the trigger. In GURPS that probably wouldn't have been an issue.)

      As Adam has astutely noted: char-gen effects the game from the starting point on and, notably, IME changing rules during char-gen is different than during play since the "surprise" element is often taken out of the equation (yes, you can be surprised--but more likely there will be discussion of the changes before any impact to the fiction happens).

      Finally, there are subtle, experiential elements to mechanics that can impact play in a variety of ways. Many people can better calculate odds in a percentile system. Some folks find "exploding dice" add to the excitement (others hate it). People have mathematically irrational beliefs about roll-over-vs-roll under (as well as legitimate preferences). Some people find systemic "break points" (where one set of numbers is treated differently than others) bizarrely rules-breaking. And so on (playing with playing cards has a different tactile feel than dice).

      How this impacts play is probably very hard to measure--but I submit that it does (VtM's use of fictional characters to delineate different levels of skill, for example, really impacted the feel of the otherwise to-me-boring number-of-dots system. D6 Star Wars' use of dice for stats made me feel like my character's abilities were less reliable even though there was no mathematical basis for that.

      I was going to say that rules were optional in indie games too--that's from posts, reading them, and experience--I'm glad someone else said it here first.
