    \n If you don't want to potentially have to roll lots of d6 before you get a 1-4 result:

    1st die:
    1-4 produces the result
    5 roll a 2nd die, 1-3 ->1, 4-6 -> 2
    6 roll a 2nd die, 1-3 ->3, 4-6 -> 4

    \n Another thing you could to (to guarantee that you reroll no more than once) is to say:
    \n
    \n1 = 1
    \n2 = 2
    \n3 = 3
    \n4 = 4
    \n5 = odd result, roll d6: 1-3=1, 4-6=3
    \n6 = even result, roll d6: 1-3=2, 4-6=4
    \n
    \nDistribution would be the same as a d4.
    \n
    \nMuch more obscurely, you could roll 2d6, with the following results (of the 36 possible combinations, the following distribution assigns exactly 9 to each result from 1 to 4):
    \n
    \n2=1 (comes up 1 way)
    \n3=1 (comes up 2 ways)
    \n4=1 (comes up 3 ways)
    \n5=2 (comes up 4 ways)
    \n6=2 (comes up 5 ways)
    \n7=3 (comes up 6 ways)
    \n8=4 (comes up 5 ways)
    \n9=4 (comes up 4 ways)
    \n10=3 (comes up 3 ways)
    \n11=1 (comes up 2 ways)
    \n12=1 (comes up 1 ways)
    \n
    \nLooks strange, but gives the same distribution as a d4.
