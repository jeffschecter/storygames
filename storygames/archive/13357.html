    \n I call 'em Muffin Games ( in opposition to most other games, which are more like Cake Mix Games or Cake Recipe Book Games).

    With a muffin, you get a a smallish bit of goodness straight off, and then it's basically done.

    With a cake mix, you put in a bit more work and haveta bring a couple of ingredients yerself.

    With a recipe book, it tells you how to get some sweet goodness, probably in a whole lot of flexible ways and in variety, but ya gotta put in a bunch more work and ingredients.

    Me, I want more muffins. There are plenty of cake mixes and recipe books already.

    \n The word "pocket" is sometimes used as a modifier to suggest a smaller or more compact version of something else, e.g. a "pocket carrier" refers to a small aircraft carrier. The conceit is that it's small enough to fit in one's pocket. By that reasoning, something like LB is a "pocket RPG." I'd call things like TMW and MLWM one-shot RPGs or limited-run games, though, in contrast to campaign-oriented RPGs.

    \n I'm on the playset (or similar) bandwagon. Module implies that it's part of a larger whole and needs to be plugged into that bigger thing in order to be workable. Oracle Game has the problem of the name not being all that descriptive of what it does, plus every time someone writes oracle game I think about a game that can tell the future. Scenario Game has the same issue as module, since scenario has a connotation of being designed for something else - con scenarios, one shot scenarios, etc...

    Playset is pretty descriptive of what it is and what it does, as would something like Seed Game or Capsule Game. Something that gives a sense of being everything you need to start play - a scenario plus a system. Maybe Quickstart Game.

    For example, I can see a game of Nordic culture easily played with the AW rules, agenda, and principles as written. It's just an interesting set of rules that makes things fun to play out.

    In short: I vote playset! I am very drunk. The end!
