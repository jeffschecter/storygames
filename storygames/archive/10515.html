\n Just some thoughts, all IMO:
\n
\nLandscape is the way to go. I don't know what percentage of PDFs are read/played off of the computer as opposed to being printed out, but I'd say it's a lot, at least half if not more. Screens are longer than they are wide, so PDFs should go with the flow!
\n
\nAnd in that vein, two-column formats in a portrait layout? Dammit, nothing more annoying than the scroll down SCROLL BACK UP and then scroll back down. Grrrrr.
\n
\nMargins are awesome. Page-count is pretty much "free" when you're digitally publishing, right? If I do print out your PDF, I feel much more prone to marking it up, highlighting stuff all over the place, writing notes, etc. simply because it's a print-out, not just a book. So the more room you can give me, the better! It's also good design - white space centers your attention and prevents eye-dragging.
\n
\nIf your design program supports linkable tables of contents (and embedded bookmarking) than I, for one, will love you forever and ever if you make good use of these. Search is nice and all but being able to click chapter headings and just go to them is awesome.
\n
\nThoughts about how you like your PDFs? Share 'em! This is hopefully meant to be constructive criticism, not just a complaint thread.

    \n I take PDF design pretty seriously, since a lot of my play experience has been online, a lot of the people who play my game aren't in the US and don't want to pay shipping, and I never would have gotten the playtesting I needed for It's Complicated done without playing online. So when it came to designing the PDF for It's Complicated, I wanted to make something which was first and foremost usable on a screen.

    The dimensions of the book are really small and originally a one-column layout anyway, so on my screen, a single page fits nicely. If it were bigger, I probably would have redone the layout completely. I'm leaning towards a landscape layout for my next game too, and a part of that is because it'll make the PDF nice without having to lay it out a second time. With the PDF, I also made the table of contents clickable, as well as all of the ads in the back of the book. There were also a couple internet addresses listed in the text of the book, which are also clickable. I also added sticky notes with conversational asides about things that have come up in the course of sharing the game with people (not errata or anything, just "This is how I do it" advice), as well as highlighting stuff.

    This is a screenshot of what it looks like:



    There was a thread a year or more ago where the general consensus was "I'd pay more for a PDF that really took advantage of what PDFs can do." My experience lead me to believe it wasn't just talk, people really put their money where their mouths were. I really hope other designers catch on; I know it'd increase the amount of PDFs I'd buy.

    \n There's a sweet spot, though: although many people buy PDFs to read on their computers, just as many buy their PDFs to print out. Making landscape PDFs (or availing yourself too grandly of the "free pagecount") may make it difficult for people to use your PDF in the form factor they want (sticking it in a three-ring binder that they keep all of their PDFs in, for example, or just using way too much paper for them to be comfortable with). And, due to what Andy said above, it's just too much work to make a landscape PDF and then a standard 8.5x11" PDF, and have both of them look good.

    So really, you can basically do what people do now-- simply offer the same PDF your book prints from-- or say "screw it!" and optimize for either print or screen, and apologize to people who wanted the PDF optimized for the other.

    \n I buy PDFs to save on the (often insane) international shipping costs, then print them out. You won't easily see me bringing a PC to the gaming table.

    For PDFs which are portrait A4/letter, exact replicas of their print book counterpart, I like to print them half-sized (I'm pretty good at reading small print): most laser printers can do that automatically. I then bind the book myself, obtaining something which is functionally identical to the print edition except it takes half the space, used up half the paper, and has a waaay more rugged, "indier" look.

    Landscape PDFs usually screw me up: I have to print those full-size, so I end wasting twice the paper. I also don't have as many options to bind them - either pay for those sucky plastic-thingy bindings they do in copy centers, or settle for a do-it-myself one which is nowhere as sturdy or nice looking than what I can do on the smaller books. A recipe for suck.

    Want to know the way to go to make everybody happy? (well, to make me and possibly the OP happy: that's two of us already)
    Single column text on portrait-oriented, small size pages.
    This way, when printing, two pages fit on each side of an A4/letter sheet. When reading on a large screen, you set the window width to whatever is most confortable and just scroll down (on a wide screen, you can easily view two documents side by side, for easy cross-referencing in crunchy games). On the small screen of a netbook or portable reader, you run the app fullscreen and there you are. A win-win-win scenario.

    Edit: of course, Elizabeth did it right on It's Complicated. As do Vincent Baker (except for Mechaton, damn it!), Jason Morningstar (on Grey Ranks, given the sidebars are really sidebars in that text) and a lot of other people...

    \n There's another thing to take into account as well; the rise of e-books. Though I expect that we're in the minority, some people use electronic readers like the iLiad or the Kindle to view PDF files on the move. For these devices, a portrait layout is much better. With landscape you'll have to scroll around the screen to view it all, and the screen update speed is slow on e-ink readers. The alternative is to turn the book through 90 degrees, but this makes the controls more awkward to use and what's more I don't think all e-ink readers have the functionality to turn a PDF through 90 degrees. Mine will, so this factor doesn't trouble me too much, but I would much rather see a smaller size of page and adjust my computer PDF viewer to display two of them at a time, which solves both the scrolling problem and the 'fold in half' problem nicely.

    On the other hand, I do like the landscape format in print products. Makes it easier to flip through the pages, especially in the smaller-than-average size books we usually see in the Indie genre.

    So yeah, I think my perfect PDF would be A5 size (or half-letter if you prefer) and just adjust the reader to accommodate.

    Re: Near. I have one book that uses part-landscape and part-portrait layout, and all it really does is make it a pain to refer to. In PDF it's not so bad, but in print I don't like it much at all.

    Re: Folding - Graham, wouldn't it also be nice to have a 'printer booklet format' with the pages arranged in such an order that you can print the lot out and staple them together into a handy booklet? At least for the smaller ones, wouldn't want to try it with a 100-page document.

    \n Brothers and sisters, you are gnashing your teeth in the wilderness because you have not heard the Good News of XML.

    You do not need to decide what is the best single format for your entire audience; XML will let you pop out as many different formats as you like.

    If you put your manuscript into XML, you can import it into InDesign (or whatever layout you use), auto-flow it across as many pages as you like, and then map paragraph and character styles onto the tags. This takes ten minutes (if that), and you can do it across landscape pages and then portrait pages and then teeny-tiny iPhone-sized pages and whatever else you need.

    This is the process I use for Rooksbridge, and it allows me to release the digital files in three variations at the cost of maybe a half-hour for each set.

    You can also translate XML into HTML if you want a web-accessible version, or into epubb if you want an e-reader version. The key is using XML as the source file for everything else.

    XML will lift the burdens from your backs and usher you into the promised land of endless publishing possibilities, if only you take its hand...
