    OK, let's keep in mind that as a member of the HQ demo team, and a dedicated "story-game" sort of guy that my selection should be no surprise. But I'll try to be specific.

    RQ is the old chaosium house rules, essentially. What was also used for Call of Cthulhu, and published generically as Basic Role-Playing (BRP). Being invented in '77, I think, it shows its roots. Quite innovative for the time, it still has fun stuff like hit points and armor for individual limbs, and works off of skills instead of levels, etc. Creating a character in RQ means selecting from a universal list of skills, with the help of setting material that indicates what's appropriate for X sort of character.

    By the way, you may also be refering to the new RQ from Green Ronin. That's a d20 version. I'm assuming you're familiar with D&D? It's very much just D&D in Glorantha.


    Hero Quest, on the other hand, is what Greg Stafford has said is an evolution: from the best emulation of Glorantha with a D&Dish sort of system, to a system that supports Glorantha directly as he envisions it. Characterization is about selecting keywords that define the character in large chunks, and then tailoring the character with additional abilities. These abilities can be anything from "Intimidating Beard" (to use a favorite) to "Run Up Cliff Spell" to "Loves Brunhilda" to "Spear of Darkfar." Anything you can imagine.

    This makes it very easy to tailor your character. Chargen is simple if you want to just choose from already written up keywords, or you can decide to make your own keywords and get into a whole lot of detail on what it means to you for a character to be from Boldhome vs. from Whitehall. The usual keywords are Homeland (including Religion), Occupation, and Magic. What's fascinating is how two characters each with the same keywords can be drastically different from each other. To say nothing of characters with different keywords. So the keywords are just aids in character creation, not straightjackets.

    There are actually three methods for building a character. One is to write a 100 word narrative about the character, from which you can pick out abilities. Another is simply to list them. And yet another is to make up your character in play "As You Go."


    More importantly, the system tends to bring out the character you create in play. A fight can be as much about who your character is protecting as it is about how well he uses a sword. A character's Grim Determination can make all the difference in many contests. People may well learn to fear your character and his Intimidating Beard. Your character's dedication to his deity may be what makes the difference in a long-distance run.

    You tend to learn more about the character with each contest.

    If you want to get a sense of what HQ is like, check out our wiki site here: http://random.average-bear.com/Heroquest/HomePage

    Lots of characters there to check out. If you want to see it in action, check into the Indie Netgaming IRC chat room where we play a lot: #indierpgs on linuxguy.magicstar.net

    Come on over and lurk, and we can show you what it's about.

    Mike

    It's a rules-light foundation that has many elaborations, mostly in the area of magic. You can learn to PLAY very quickly but GMing magic takes some study.

    If you don't use the setting, but you want to use the magic rules, you've got a bit of work ahead of you making up all the details that you need to make the magic rules work. Or you can just make them up as you go along, that works too... but requires a better understanding of how the system works.

    the GM workload is very traditional.

    Prep time depends on how much improvising you want to do at the table. The basic system is light enough that it's not hard to improvise.

    The supplements are not vital, and they're very tied to Glorantha.

    At it's core, the game is still pretty old-school. Don't expect to be blown out of the water. What it's very good at is making ANY kind of conflict dice-able, and all using the same rules. This game is good for people who understand that losing can be as fun as winning, and for GM's who are willing to let the players win or lose in big ways.

    [Now I've crossed with Fred - note that he's played in my HQ game, I've played in one run by him, Shreyas and I both play in another run by Thomas Robertson...]

    To answer your specific questions:
    What is the relative weight of the system?
    Any weight of the system is tied up in the setting information. So nothing onerous. Very light in action. Most contests are resolved by comparing two d20 rolls against targets, and "bumping" with mastery or "Hero Points" (which also drive character development).
    I'm not particularly tied to the Glorantha setting, though it is the kind of setting that gets my particular geek on. How integral to the rules is the setting?
    The magic systems are sorta integral to the setting, though I'd challenge even that. Put another way, I play HQ in Shadow World (an old ICE setting for Rolemaster), and it works great. I haven't seen a fantasy setting yet for which it would not excel.

    (OH, yeah, and I'm involved in writing the "generic" version of the game right now called "Quest Worlds" so there's a little more disclosure for you.)
    What is the GM workload like? What is prep time and requirements?
    Well the text presents as somewhat traditional here. But what everyone agrees on is that you don't have to do any pre-statting of stuff. So prep is just what you need to come up with ideas for people, places, events, etc. Again my personal experience is that I can play with no prep at all most nights.

    In fact, I run a version called "Other World" where we start without a world at all, much less characters.
    Both games feature a veritable dumpload of suppliments. How vital are these to the game? Am I going to be on the hook for all twenty/thirty/hundred and twelve books to fully appreciate the game?
    I don't see either game as requiring the supplements much. They're basically all adventure material or supplemental setting info for Glorantha.
    What, exactly, makes the game amazing?
    Magic feels magical. And players play their characters heroically.
    Are there great tools for, as an example, situation generation?
    No situation generation per se, but there are "relationships" and "community" rules from which situation tends to spring by itself. It's like a situation generator in play without the need for prep. Or, at least that's how it works for me.
    What behaviors does the game reward and how?
    The reward system is actually pretty light, but the contest system rewards creatively interpreting your character in play. Finding new facets about the character. Exploring who they are, and what they'll do when things they value come into play.

    All linked to the setting that you play in, Glorantha or not. If you want to bring a setting to life, HQ is your game.

    Mike
