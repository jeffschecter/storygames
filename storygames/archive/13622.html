    \n I imagine a central entry point with a welcome table covered with books and someone standing nearby (maybe a whiteboard too, to post upcoming games). People walk up, find out what games are forming or mention what they want to play, and get channeled to one of the smaller (large conference room) spaces that has a few tables setup. Big spaces have more noise, and more problems with blocking walkways and such. Each smaller room has 2-3 tables in it for play.

    GPNW had a setup like this (the entryway was a little different) and it rocked. Each room could handle a couple of groups, and it's a lot easier to negotiate sound levels when it's only one other group to contend with.

    Coincidentally, I think the conference rooms along the 2nd floor of Embassy Suites (the ones the look down on the area we play in after hours) fit this pretty exactly.

    \n My thoughts (cross-posted with Sage, who is wise):

    The one-big-room approach is good for the spectacle of seeing what everyone is playing, but it can get pretty loud. I'm not sure if Gen Con has any sort of alternative to that model, but if so I think it would be good. More importantly, having something closer to the exhibitor hall would be great, as that's the center of activities during the day. Failing that, something close to the Embassy Suites / Food court part of downtown. People shouldn't have to work to find us.

    Having signage and maybe even sign-up sheets would be great. For signage I'm imagining a banner we can hang that says "Indie Games on Demand" or something obvious, perhaps with some additional information for the folks unfamiliar with the event. A good sign will cost money, however.

    The sign-up sheets would just be for passing messages and coordinating games- It's 11:30, the middle of a session and everybody is in a game. Somebody writes "I will run Dread at 2pm- 4 players, sign up here!" and then goes to lunch.

    Having the table full of ready games is good, but it's even better if there are folks who know those games and can run them. Last year we had a lot of stuff on the table that nobody was prepared for. Publishers- if you're releasing your game at Gen Con and want it to get play at GOD, you need to find some eager ninjas to run your game and get some advance copies in their hands.

    Having volunteers to meet and greet is pretty important, though it is a thankless job. If we could get enough people to do it, we could spread it out more into smaller shifts. Maybe a whole team of people for each shift would work- they could sit and play something casual together, taking turns keeping on eye on the door and talking to interested folks.

    The best way to get Gen Con to give us better space and more amenities is to show them we can bring the fun for a lot of folks. That means tickets getting purchased and collected at GOD games. Maybe the best way for us to do that is to buy the tickets ourselves and then re-sell them to folks at the tables. Maybe we pull IPR or groups of publishers together as sponsors and buy tickets, making the games free to play. Alternately, we could go through traditional Gen Con paths and simply sponsor the event outright for a few thousand dollars, in the same way that the crafts room, Cardhalla and other activities are handled.

    In any case, we should also talk it up and let folks know its a great place to find out about new games and have a blast.

    (As a side note, I say all of this as a publisher and an advocate of small press games like ours. I want more people, new to story games, to find Games on Demand. That may not be what everybody wants for GOD, and that's cool. Just putting my biases on the table.)

    \nMENU

    Ask the publishers/authors of the games to give you a one page pdf that advertises the essence of the game. (Or cobble one together if we have to, it's months away.)
    Assemble one copy of each into a few binders.

    Prospective players can flip through and pick what they want to see. Or can be guided through it by the guy/girl/hostess/maitre d at the front table.

    GAME PACKS

    I love that idea. Keep everything you need to run the game in manilla folders, so a GM just has to grab a pack and a table. Maybe even a bag for the dice, or just a shopping list so you can grab them from a larger stash.

    GAME SPACE

    I was in the Omni two years ago, second floor on the right. The room had really high ceilings, and as far as conventions go, it was one of the quieter rooms I have gamed in. Backgrund noise levels are worth mentioning, but I think anyone trying a game out at a convention knows the hazards. The idea here is to give people a taste, right? Hopefully they will be excited enough to take these games home and play them in a more controlled environment.

    VOLUNTEER

    If I'm still in the country, I'll be there with bells on. I said I would do this all day, and I mean it. I might, however, be overseas if I land this job prospect. Unless I can host games over a skype connection, I would have to decline.

    I will know in about a month.

    THANKLESS JOB

    It's hard being the meet and greet guy, but it's necessary. That initial enthusiasm, and that first responder to interest and questions is the best way to convince the casual interest to sit down and commit. If it seems thankless, I will personally thank anyone who takes a shift. It really does help. Maybe this is where we should focus some attention? This isn't really a job where we can incentivise someone into this, but maybe something else?

    CHEAP TRICKS

    It's worth mentioning that over the weekend I used a parking garage and I got change in dollar coins. EVERYONE I have talked to all week has asked me where I got them. Perhaps if we made change with those, it would increase traffic? I only say this because seriously, it got people talking. I have every confidence that we can show people the awesome that is the games, but anything to get the conversation started is worth it.

    A few years ago there were maps/flyers at some of the booths? Buttons with the green "play" button on them? Anything else? Do we have any marketing majors?

    \n Hey guys! Just to break the silence, we've been in contact with both Peter Adkison and Derek Guder about making Games On Demand a formal part of Gencon, including space inside the convention center itself. Now, in order to do that, we do have to monetize the operation- renting a room of our preferred size for the show ourselves for it to be free to play would be in the neighborhood of $1800-2400, which doesn't seem likely to be raisable. So, we're looking at the interest in having $2 ticket costs per game slot. Further, Gencon itself would be much more comfortable with some level of defined slots of games in advance- I think we can probably finess this with "one of this set of games will be played, based on player demand, at times X, Y and Z," but that's still being discussed. So let's open the question to the floor- are you interested in this, what do you want GoD to work like, and are these sorts of compromises worth the convenience, con support and access to great swaths of new potential players? Or would you prefer to keep this an informal, unsupported event off-site? dw

    \n I've got some doubts about sponsored tables, which I struggle to articulate, but here's a try. I'm afraid this will be rather rambling.

    The first doubt is about being tethered to a table. Last year, I would happily have run Trail demos at Games on Demand. The problem was: Games on Demand was much, much too noisy. There's no way I could have run a decent Cthulhu game there. So, I'd like the freedom to gather people together and go wherever we want, without the pressure to run it at a specific table.

    The second doubt is about the pressure to keep a game running. If Company X pays for a table, then there's a pressure to keep that game running with Company X games. That eats into some of the freedom of Games on Demand. Rather than standing around saying "What would we like to play?", there'll be a GM rostered on to Company X's Table, and a pressure to get players on to that table.

    That'll be fine with, say, Dresden Files. There'll be a neverending stream of people who want to run and play that. But with smaller games, I'm not sure.

    A third doubt: let's say Company X pays for a table. What happens when it's not in use? Does it stand empty: "You can't use that, it's the Company X table"? Or do you use it? If so, what happens if Company X suddenly wants to run a demo there: "Move along, guys, this is the Company X table"?

    None of this is insoluble, but it needs thinking about.

    \n I was imagining something a little less concrete with sponsoring, Graham.
