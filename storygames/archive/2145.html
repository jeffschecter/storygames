Need a term: Buffers to conflict immediacy

TonyLB
January 2007 edited January 2007 in Story Games
Okay, so imagine you have the basic conflict "Darth Vader is out to get you." We trace that through the Empire Strikes Back.

At the beginning, Darth Vader is out to get Han and Leia. But, y'know, he's out to get everybody. And he's doing things through the intermediary of storm troopers. And those troopers have sent out a scout droid. Hundreds of scout droids, actually, to many different planets. Vader is far away, shielded by many layers of intermediaries, and kept from immediate action against Han and Leia specifically by distance, ignorance and lack of focus.

By the middle of the movie, all of those layers have been stripped away. The scout droid leaves the picture, and the storm troopers make their appearance directly. Vader is chasing the Milennium Falcon specifically. Eventually, the conflict boils down to "Here's Vader, right fuckin' here in the dining room! And he's looking for you, just you, you alone! He's RIGHT THERE!"

So I'm thinking that explicitly wrapping a conflict in these ... layers? intermediaries? ... whatever, and then removing them piece by piece could be a great way to control pacing while still giving people a clear idea at all times of how a conflict should next appear in the scene.

I've got a big problem though: What the hell do you CALL those things? The layers, the intermediaries, the whatevers? What's the right word?

    Eric: Uh ... not exactly. Okay, example time:
    John is playing Victor, a young man who wants to admit his love to Gina, the fetching bookworm who works in the library. So the central conflict is "Make a connection with Gina." Then the other players start piling on Complications. First there is "Gina's reticence," and then there is "Victor's popularity," and on top of it all there is "Victor's friends."

    So the first time there is an instance of this conflict, it is mediated by all three of those factors. Say, for instance, that Victor convinces one of his friends, who knows Gina, to invite her to one of their beach parties. This works out ... poorly. Victor manages to be ham-handed enough that he grievously offends Gina, and she lashes out at him verbally, smacking him down pretty savagely as a worthless pretty-boy with no ambition beyond getting drunk. But! On the positive side, Gina's player, in addition to winning the conflict, gets the rights to remove or change a complication, and decides to take "Gina's reticence" out of action entirely.

    So, the next time there is an instance of this conflict, it is mediated only by Victor's popularity and his friends. Gina's reticence is no longer either a driving force or a barrier. Victor tries to show that there's more to him than just popularity, courting the scorn of the school by publicly apologizing to Gina. But he gets hammered again, this time by the GM, who arranges events such that it looks like Victor is only talking to Gina in order to convince her to help him steal answers for next weeks big exam. The GM chortles and changes "Victor's popularity" to "Grievous misunderstanding." Oh, evil GM.

    Victor has to clear his name. He goes after yet another conflict in this sequence. This one he's not even present for (as he need not be, given that he has his friends as intermediaries). Through one of his friends, he manages to get convincing information to Gina to show that their misunderstanding was just that. He eliminates "Grievous misunderstanding," and the make a connection of sorts (though not the deeper, more dramatically resolving connection that will occur if he can remove the last barrier and talk to her alone, without his friends in tow), bringing Gina into his social circle.

    Unfortunately, at this point John as a player is running out of resources. He can't push matters any further, and can't get that last conflict that he needs. So Victor and Gina have a connection of sorts, but they're only friends in a group of friends, and (for now) that's where things have to stay.
    So, yeah ... the complications are targets that people are aiming at, but they're also the structure of the conflict as it is played out in each iteration.

    There's whole other sections of how I intend to use the system, but I'll give a rough sketch of what I intend in Misery Bubblegum specifically: You will have opportunities to give dice of "your" color to other people, by having your character think or talk about theirs (whatever the relationship). Those dice will help the person you gave them to in order to win conflicts, but they (and your own dice) will help you, always you, in order to modify the circumstances.

    So how often you win will depend on how you play your own character plus how other people talk about you. How often you modify the circumstances will depend on how you play your own character plus how you talk about other people. Playing really well is good for both, but there are limits to how far that can take you. Being intertangled in other people's lives and being part of a network of opinion also helps you, but only part of that is under your control, and if it's lopsided (i.e. people think more about you than you think about them or you think more about others than they think about you) then you're going to get an imbalance where suffering and dramatic progress go hand in hand.

    Which is ... already way more about the Misery Bubblegum system than I intended to dump into this thread. If people want to hear more, I do have some outstanding design questions (particularly about whether to do zero-sum game or cooperation-friendly on some of these rolls) that I could decant off into another thread.

    EDIT: And, so far, there are no rules for adding complicationss. Because play proceeds over a limited time-frame with limiting resources, there's no need to punish people by adding complications. Not removing them is punishment enough. In a race against the clock, being forced to stand still for a while is as bad as going the wrong way.

      John: It's my front-runner right now, because it conveys something of the twin issues of "What structures the conflict" and "Things you get past." My main problem is that it also has connotations for hundreds of other things. It's certainly a fine label, in the same way that (for instance) "Action" is a fine label for the succession of turns in a competitive Librarian game ... but people who read the label and thought "Wow, it'll be packed with ACTION!" would be misconstruing the game because of the word, and I'm worried that "complications" may do the same thing.
