\n Why are you not playing it? I don't know. Maybe because you're not that cool. But I am! Friday night we had a group of three that sat down to play Black Seven for the first time.

The purpose of the game is to simulate stealth action technothrillers like Splinter Cell or Metal Gear Solid or even Thief: The Dark Project. Characters are black-clad super-agents with silenced pistols who say cool things like "tango down" and "control, whats the sitrep" and "going dark".

The system is very simple - roll 2d6 and add an appropriate action number to generate a total. 11 or above, you succeed, 10 or lower and you fail. You tend to succeed around 40-60 percent of the time. This is not the interesting part of the system.

The interesting part of the system is this:

The game takes place largely in Facilities - enemy areas where there is a Threat Level ranging from 3-5 (Threat Levels 1-2 are in public areas, see below.) At Threat Level 3, the guards are on patrol, the facility is operating normally, they don't know anything's wrong.

As people fail rolls (and the strict turn order and 40-60 percent success rate guarantees eventually someone will fail), they take on traits of Exposed or Noticed or (worst case) both. Exposed characters are not under cover or in a hiding place. Noticed characters have been identified by sentries. You can be Exposed without being Noticed (breaking from one hiding place to get up behind a guard to knock them out) or Noticed without being Exposed (you made a noise and the guards know where you are even if they can't directly see you). The game gives various ways for you to gain or lose Exposed or Noticed status traits.

The Noticed status trait controls where and how the Threat Level develops. If they know there's an intruder, and not manipulated into thinking it's a false alarm, the Threat Level goes to 5 and the sentries open fire - the more that are still standing, the more dangerous the situation is.

This means the real decision in the game is a push-your-luck mechanic. There are a lot of "side objectives" - going after them means more rolls, which means more chances to fail, be Noticed, and push up the Threat Level to the ranks where the sentries are hosing the place down with machine guns and yelling "the red spy is in the base" (or whatever). But if you don't go after enough of them, then you don't have good intel on the next facility and have to go in underequipped.

If an agent is taken out in a facility other than the last one, then rescuing them from the next facility (and getting their equipment back) is an additional objective. If they're taken out in the last facility, they get a bullet in the head or shipped off to Siberia or whatever.

There are also optional rules for psychic powers and cybernetic equipment, which are basically souped-up equipment that can be used more often and in more flexible ways.

All in all, given that it's only $3, you really should check it out.

There's also an Assassin's Creed-a-like add on called Animus, which I haven't explored as thoroughly.

    \n Joe McGuffin ran us through a Black Seven adventure last week--with cheat sheets as promised! It was very action movie oriented, with a futuristic touch--Bond plus Terminator, perhaps. Our characters were definitely agent types. The facilities we faced were a nightclub, an auto impound lot, and a scientific research/production facility (there was one more final facility that we did not get to). That was a nice mix, which demonstrated that there is room in the game for subterfuge and for situations where firepower could be counterproductive. The 'Notice' mechanic helps temper the characters' actions. Sure, we could shoot the civilian lot manager, but not without repercussions for the rest of the mission. Joe commented that the movie Raiders of the Lost Ark could be played with this ruleset--Raiders is all about infiltrating facilities and getting 'something' out--the medallion, the Ark, Marion, etc. I agree with him, and I'm excited to try variants of the game in other settings.
    One aspect of our session I thought interesting was that we originally tried to make a real 'Mission: Impossible' (the original TV show) type scheme. The goal would be to make the targets deliver the item we wanted to us, rather than having to go get it. The skill sets and conflict resolution didn't seem to support a plan of that kind, although we didn't push it.
    All in all, great fun!

    I absolutely adore the Exposed/Noticed/Alert Level 5 mechanic. Surely there are ways to incorporate this into other games' boring stealth systems.

    Normally stealth sucks in a game because if you succeed nothing happens and if you fail, blah, why even have stealth when you could just come in guns blazing and get to the same place? This lets you "fix" blown stealth situations, lets you "blow it a little" and recover, and rewards you for taking out people stealthily even if you blow it later.

    \n When you infiltrate a place by stealth, roll+cool. On a 10+, choose 1; on a 7\u20139, choose 2:
    - your infilitration is detected and the place is on high alert;
    - you expose yourself to danger, either from physical defenses or guards or both;
    - you don't get where you want to go;
    - you leave traces of your presence.
    On a miss, the GM will choose 3 or all.

    If you're detected and exposed, then guards attack you. If you're detected but not exposed, they don't know where you are nor what threat you pose, but they're actively looking for you so it's only a matter of time. If you're exposed but undetected, then you take a difficult route in and put yourself in danger from falling or traps or whatever physical defenses the place has. If you don't get where you want to go, then maybe you don't get in, or maybe you do get in but end up somewhere you didn't expect or had wanted to avoid. And if you leave traces of your presence then maybe they know how you got in and where you went, or maybe they just know someone got in or maybe they know it was you.

    Basically, no matter what, this move is going to snowball. What did you expect: you're going where the bad people don't want you to go, it's your own damn fault.
