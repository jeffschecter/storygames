What were the best mechanics for magic you've seen/used in play?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nWarriorMonk \n\n\n
\n
\n\nFebruary 2013 \n edited February 2013 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n I'm looking for mechanics that turn magic into something interesting to roleplay and/or allow a lot of flexibility for creative use.

For example, one of the reasons I really enjoyed Anima was for the Summoning. Your character needed to spend mana and 4 different skills for it: Summoning, Controlling, binding and Banishing. Fumble summoning and the creature summons you to it's realm. Fumble controlling and the creature gets a random reaction, etc. On the mod we played -since the corebook didn't came with a long enough monster list- we houseruled that any summoner could call any creature desired but the GM gave it generic stats according to the level of the summoned creature, which also raised the difficulty of the rolls to summon, control, bind and banish the creature.

This made summoning amazingly flexible. Summoners could psy-wrestle for the control of the creature, distract each other, banish each other's summon, call a powerful creature and run away instead of trying to control it, call forth a lot of low level creatures instead of a big one, you name it.

Another nice idea that a friend of mine gave me was to make illusory magic dependant on an artistic skill, like carving a wood figurine or making a paint of whatever image you wanted to create. The better the quality of the piece, the more real it seems.

So what's the best magic mechanics you've seen in action?

    ---

    On another note. I couldn't find a good flexible magic system, so I made one of my own. You choose a theme and can do anything with it. You got three ranks - damage, manipulate and create - and four different kinds of durations and areas. By increasing the rank (damage -> manipulate -> create), duration and area, the chance of the magic tearing on the mage will increase. If it tears to much, the magic skill is increased. When magic is approaching a certain value, the mage becomes more and more affected by the theme. For example, an illusionist starts to live in a world of illusions without realizing it.

    It became the magic system in my first published game.

    \n\nChris_Goodwin \n\n\n
    \n
    \n\nMarch 2013 \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n From Bonewits I got a good handle on what exactly, for instance, the principles of sympathy and contagion meant. I would take the game system I'm using, usually HERO, and say to myself, what does sympathy mean in terms of the mechanics I've got to work with in the system? What does contagion mean?

    I've also done a bit with using them for improvisational style magic. One that my group came up with with was very much like Rickard's, where you'd choose one or more "alliances" (nouns), which were pretty freeform, and back them up with "commands" (verbs) which were specified pairs of opposites, such as Bind/Loose, Create/Destroy, and so forth. You'd have skills in each of these, and quantify the effect you wanted based on things like volume, mass, range, damage, etc. This would get you a mana cost, then you'd have to start building up mana. A flub at the wrong time could cause damage or other unpredictable effects as you "dropped" the spell mid-working. You could add required "methodologies" which would reduce the cost of an alliance, which were basically limiting factors like "must use gestures with all spells in the alliance of fire". If you had an item which was sufficiently "close" to the target (via sympathy, contagion, etc.) you could treat the target as if it were within touch range.
