\n Hi friends,

Final Edition is in preorder! Order during July for 20% off the retail cover price. All the details and Paypal buy buttons are at this preorder page.

This thread is for news and updates about the Final Edition of Annalise, which is coming along nicely. It will be available in print at Gen Con Indy, and in PDF shortly before that. Annalise is a game about making Vampire stories in the gothic horror genre. It's GMless/GMful, no-prep and suitable for medium-term (i'd say 3-5 session) games, though one-shots are facilitated by the free Guided Play Scenarios.

This is culmination of Annalise as a publishing experiment and as a rules text. There have been five different Editions of the game (counting the Italian translation), and within those editions have been a variety of consumer choices in terms of format, cover, and so on. The Final Edition is, thankfully, very simple: a book and matching PDF, available separately or together.

Annalise as a game has consistently provided me with satisfaction and happiness! There is only one major rules change between the previous editions and this Final Edition, and it is contained in this 1-page PDF document: Annalise Errata PDF

For more information about the game, and to keep abreast of developments for the Final Edition, findannalise.com is the place to look. I just recreated the website, so actually if you run into any broken links or have any feedback on the user experience, I would really appreciate you letting me know.

Thanks!

    \n Paul,

    Good questions, thanks!

    The game is organized in 4 phases: Discovering Characters (establishing your characters and the setting/situation), Laying the Foundations (rising action and character development), The Confrontation (confronting the Vampire), and The Aftermath (falling action, denouement). I'd say that an "average" game of Annalise breaks down like this: 1 session for Discovering Characters, 1-3 for Laying the Foundations, 1-2 for The Confrontation, 0-1 for the Aftermath (which can be as little as one round of scenes at the end of the Confrontation, really).

    What the Guided Play Scenarios do is provide you with four characters that have already been Discovered, embedded in a setting and facing some kind of situation or pressures right off the bat. With all of those things already established, and assuming some existing rules knowledge on the part of the facilitator, the game starts right in the middle of Laying the Foundations. Especially in a convention setting, players generally are on board with driving games hard and getting to a resolution, so getting to the Confrontation in a couple of hours is totally possible. When I've run them, it's about half-an-hour of rules and getting up to speed, 2+ hours of Foundations, 1 hour of Confrontation, quick Aftermath. If playing at home, I wouldn't be surprised if a game stretched into two sessions even with a Guided Play Scenario.

    To answer your questions:\n
    Posted By: Paul T.Nathan,
    How much or how little of the experience of the "full" game is lost?
    \nYou lose the "blank-page" part of making characters and setting from nothing, and slowly molding the story towards a point where it gains it's own momentum. This process creates a highly localized experience for each group, but definitely takes time.\n
    What mechanical differences (if any) exist?
    \nNone, except that the characters in the Scenario start out with numbers that reflect that they've been engaged in the story, so the numbers are lower than a normal starting character (and they have resources already built up, where a starting character just has raw potential).\n
    What makes the basic game unfulfilling or incomplete in a one-session setting, and how does a Guided Scenario change that?
    \nI don't think it's impossible to run the basic game in one session (I've done that too), but it takes a lot of specific game skills that I don't assume everyone has (I've only done it with a group of good friends that were all total story-game-heads). It does feel rushed to me, though. The Scenario's replace time investment with this pre-generated content.\n
    Is there less (fictional) input from the players in a Guided Scenario, so a lot of fictional details are "preset"?
    \nOnce play starts, the players have the exact same fictional input and abilities to shape the game, but yes, the whole initial phase of the game is basically preset.

    Goodie? Anything else you're curious about?
