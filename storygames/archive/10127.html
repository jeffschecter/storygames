\n That's basically what I did with my girlfriend. She didn't really know what it was about, so the hardest part was explaining it to her, and it was probably complicated by my knowing so many systems that I could never give a clear answer, so we ended up sliding through suggesting a couple variations of D&D, HackMaster and then A Dirty World. She liked that idea the best. She then talked to one of her friends about it, who seemed interested. Said friend's boyfriend heard the conversation on the other end and perked up at the word "game". So asking 1 person to play has got me a group of 3 new people to play with.

Not surprisingly, that's how I started. One of my brother's friends introduced us to gaming in a "Hey, here's a cool game, let's play it" way when we were kids, and in a school with a student base of ~26, I had a solid group of 3 people playing, who I also got just by asking. 10% of the school playing D&D regularly, with it occasionally swelling to 30% I think is pretty good.

Anybody else have similarly easy experiences that fly in the face of the somewhat popular notion (which I've held, strangely enough, despite the aforementioned experiences with school) that something drastic needs to be done to make the hobby appealing to new people?

    I had an interesting conversation with a friend last night about this scenario gone bad. She was willing to try an RPG as were a few other new folks (all educated professionals in their late 20s early 30s, I think). The guy running the game was very enthusiastic and excited. But he insisted on emphasizing elaborate preparations and backstories so that everyone got bored and frustrated before they could even start playing. They apparently never rolled a die before everyone gave up.

    \n

    I think when non-RPGers decide to give a game a chance, they expect to sit down, spend about 10 minutes learning some rules, then get down to playing out a scenario, rolling dice, killing orcs, or otherwise doing something that feels more like "playing a game" and less like "doing homework."

    \n Robin, that was an answer to how D&D doesn't do that already.

    "That" being "Make the hobby appealing to new people."
    The hobby is not appealing in and of itself. If DnD truly did that, then in every big town a couple dozen people would pick up the books, each on their own with no knowledge of the others, and then they'd converge and play.

    What happens is that D&D is appealing enough to attract a single player, or a couple, and then they draw their friends to play with them. Their friends don't play with them because the "RPG Hobby" is appealing enough to them, they don't really care about it. They are playing because their friends are playing, and they want to engage in an activity with their friends. Then the RPG aspect may appeal to them and they'll keep at it even separate from their friends, but it's not the same as RPGs themselves appealing to them.

    It's the non-unique aspect of "Social activity with people I like" that they find appealing, with the side bonus of "My friend's excited about it, he's my friend, so let's see what he likes, and help him have fun."
