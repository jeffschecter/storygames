Danius writes:

After thirty pages or so Flaubert sets out to describe F\xe9licit\xe9\u2019s bedroom. He does it with his usual clarity and precision. Time slows down, a description flows out. The text shapes itself into a sober catalog as if an estate inventory. The room has a closet, a chest of drawers, a mirror, an unsprung bed, a small table. The essential furniture, not more. On the wall there\u2019s icon of Mary, rosaries and a coconut stoop for holy water. The top of the chest holds a shell-decorated box, a pairof boots, a balloon, notebooks and paper flowers. The table has a water jug, two combs \u2014 and a blue soap.

Flaubert only mentions it once, that soap, and never brings it up again. It\u2019s not used. It\u2019s no Chekhov\u2019s Gun \u2014 Chekhov, Flaubert\u2019s contemporary, said that if an author describes a room and says that there is a gun on the wall, they also need to fire it. Or the gun shouldn\u2019t be there. Everything needs a purpose, everything needs to be incorporated. But the soap, well, Flaubert never fires it. It\u2019s just there.

And she goes on for the rest of the book how awesome this soap is, this cube de savon bleu dans une assiette \xe9br\xe9ch\xe9e.

This morning a player in my Tomb of Annihilation game reached third level and decided to pick Arcane Archer for his human PC. I was like that\u2019s awesome. How come your PC learned this elven art? Sounds like there\u2019d be a great story there. And he said an elf had taught him and I rolled up a rando NPC, an elven boy who the PC had met Neverwinter Forest. I rolled randomly that the elven boy was still alive and that the PC was in good standing with him. And I said to write up a bond, duty, responsibility or debt to him.

And then I thought\u2026 hmm how am I going to use this? How am I going to reincorporate this piece of platform building when the PCs are on another continent right now? And he isn\u2019t death curse afflicted because I rolled that he was healthy. Huh\u2026

and then I thought oh I better dig up some aethestic theory of visibility here. Is there some sort of philosophy where adding detail in order to provide background, character, a sense of place? And I thought of Danius\u2019 book. (McCloud has similar thoughts in chapter four of Making Comics)

And I was thinking and writing for hours in order to justify not having to reincorporate this PC into the current goings-on of the PC.\u2665

Aesthetic philosophy > work

And then I remembered Clint\u2019s advice: \u201cuse that energy not to fight on the internet, but to do cool stuff for games\u201d and I guess I flubbed that T_T

because the chance of all of our work in defining this NPC actually mattering in the game? like maybe five percent.

but but but it\u2019s a sense of history for the PC!

I actually do like that. He is using this elf\u2019s teachings to infuse his arrows with magic

    \n It seems to me the thing you did that make things a little different than expected was to allow retroactive XP : the PC knew Elven Archery all along since Neverwinter Forest. Building on this reason, you built memories, not facts for the present or the future. Maybe there should be a different interpretation of the table for memories, then. Like what is a debt to the memory of a person ? Maybe before training, the PC salutes an item representing the elven master, maybe when the moon is full, he leaves a fruit and a candle in a makeshift altar. What would matter to me is that those little rituals ensure that naming the elven master was not just handwaving the cause. Like : my PC family died and the mentor with them, how unfortunate this character has no strings whatsoever. Then, if the bountyhunter finds the altar near the campfire, you've got your narrative payoff.

    The cause could be named just for the sake of realism, you've totally got the right to dream. This realist technique already has a name : https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effect_of_reality . I have found that I prefer the Effect of World. That's when when the greatest number of facts are incidentally correlated. Like in the bounty hunter example, there's no relation between bounty hunter and elven boy, which makes narrative causality look "non intentional", and yet leaves room for meaning. It is like destiny with sleight of hand. I wish I could formulate this better. Anyway, that's much more "Story" in the end.

    \n There's also the thing to be noted with Chekov's gun as generalized writing advice which is that Chekov wrotes plays and short stories.
    And in a play, if you're going to draw emphasis to a prop, it needs to be important, because with the distance between parts of the audience and the actors in playhouses of any size, you have to do a lot to draw emphasis to a prop, often specific lighting cues and stuff, to make sure that everything in the theatre can see it. And Chekov's gun has a lot to do with that context.
    And with Chekov also working in short stories, there's the fact that a short story doesn't have nearly the amount of space that the average campaign has, so short stories need to be really careful about what they do and don't detail, because you've maybe got 50 pages to work with, but are more often working with 10-30 pages. Whereas like, a ttrpg campaign is more similar in length to a series of long novels, or a television series, where you have much much more space to work with.
    And when you get into long-form stuff, you don't have to be nearly as careful with the conservation of detail, because you have as much narrative space as you need.

    \n The NPC Arcane Archery teacher, even if he won't appear in the foreseeable future of the campaign, is useful and valuable in at least three ways:
    - as a good excuse to have Arcane Archery, which is going to be actually featured in the game
    - as a detail about the PC: he/she is the sort of person who makes friends with elf boys in Neverwinter Forest
    - as a "gratuitous" world-building exercise which validates, consolidates and develops the game setting by setting a precedent (e.g. in this campaign elf boys are likely to friendly to strangers and passable Arcane Archery experts)

    I wouldn't feel pushed to feature him more prominently just because he's been introduced. If players expect this kind of peripheral NPC to be important just because he's been mentioned in excessive detail, they need to be reeducated with a barrage of irrelevant loose ends, partial knowledge and mysteries until they start applying common sense and treat the campaign like an open world rather than a closed world (in the formal logic sense).

    Regarding Chekhov's gun vs. Flaubert's soap bar, it's a divergence in writing style that is relevant for fiction authors (artificial simplicity and elegance, or dirty and confusing realism?) and fiction readers (is this apparently gratuitous detail important, but only at a later point in the story, or already more meaningful than it appears to be at a first impression?).

    In a roleplaying game both alternatives are wrong and irrelevant: players decide what's useful or important on their own, and improvising retroactively is fine.

    \n While I'm sorry to read the "disagreements" happening here (as an outside of sorts, it actually looks like you're all in agreement here!), this is a really fascinating topic and lots of interesting things are being discussed, which I love.

    There's another element here, though:

    Sometimes reincorporation or artistic coherence doesn't have to be literal. Maybe sometimes the gun gets fired in the third act... and maybe, other times, the gun is a symbol of the character's fears, hanging over his head. In a third case, it's just visually reminiscent of the prow of the ship on which he eventually dies.

    The soap might not get used, in other words, but perhaps there is a long-running sense of symbolism around cleanliness and dirt in the story, and the soap ties into that.

    Frank Herbert created subtle effects just by using the colour yellow selectively in his descriptions, for instance. Lots of different ways to do this!

    For reincorporating something like the Elven mentor, you can go similarly metaphorical or indirect - as simple as another important character also having a mentor who is far away, so they can compare their relationships to one another. Or the little carved wooden figurine has nothing to do with where they are, but, look! Here's a culture of forest dwellers who all love carving wooden figurines. There's no literal link or reincorporation here, but a bit of resonance which may affect the players or the audience.

    Fun to think about, in any case.
