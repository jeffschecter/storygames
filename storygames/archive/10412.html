I'm still reading through, but this game seems to scratch a whole lot of my itches. (Some I didn't even notice until it started scratching.) Still, I have a couple of questions:

2. If I order the cards now (Sadly, my FLGS doesn't stock them, just the book.) it will probably take a while 'til I get them. Is there away to get/buy/beg a pdf of them so I could print/craft my own set until the real one crosses the atlantic?

    \nFor con play, there are some things I simplify, particularly with respect to the metaplot rules. Once around the table and then Morning (end-game) is one, which I streamline as a process by ignoring the rules for "bonus dice" from Sun and Stars mana and not bothering to roll for the GM. Another thing I did recently to make the game go faster was let players use Medicine cards to add Gifts in the middle of a turn rather than at the end; that turned out quite well.

    Oh! One thing that's useful in a meta-gamey kind of way, I think, is for the first player turn to move pretty quickly to resolution. That is, after you figure out what the situation is, get the player's reaction and move right into the consequences rather than drawing out the build-up to the crux of the turn. Most of the role-playing in the game happens as players try to invoke their gifts to shift dice, and it's good for players to see that quickly in the first turn. By the same token, there's a temptation for players to say something like, "I use my..." to shift dice; encourage them to tell you what they do and then figure out how that works in their Gifts. Conversely, some players will want to describe what they do and not connect it to their Gifts. Be charitable with that: give them their "presence on the scene" shift and possibly their identity, if it's relevant. But absolutely encourage them to continue to roleplay their reactions, regardless of whether or not they get shifts out of it, because it helps develop the fiction.\n
    4. (With gifts of higher level or a Situation card with a high root value, for example.) Is that correct?
    \nNo. You can invoke as many Gifts as you like, so long as you can justify their invocation narratively. The GM will judge, and must be firm with himself (or herself) too. But you only get two opportunities at most to do so.\n

    Posted By: GrahamWe had a few issues when we played, but only minor ones: for example, there was the HeroQuest problem of narrating in every single trait to get more dice.
    \nOne game-mechanical brake on that is the "Law of Narrative Variety," in which the GM is allowed to say, "Narrate it a different way," if the player goes to the same fictional well too often. Matthjis has described something similar in one of his designs; I forget which. It's not a complete fix, but it ameliorates some of that sort of thing.\n
    Also, the GM didn't seem to stand a chance of winning, partly because players could narrate every single trait. But those weren't big issues.
    \nI'm of your opinion, but my friend Andrew Morris is convinced that it works the other way around: players have to fight like hell just to keep from losing everything. I think it depends on the initial distribution of Good and Bad Medicine as well as the degree of player cooperation and coordination.\n

    \nI can get three to five players through character generation in an about an hour (maybe an hour and a quarter); I'd assume that a novice group could do it in maybe an hour and a half to two hours. After that, if you get through the first turn in half an hour, then you can maybe get 20 minutes for each turn after that. At Gencon, I ran three players through a complete in game in just a hair over two hours.     (Also, the "Order of Reaction" part was ... confusing. I think I got it, but, well...)
    \nIf it's too confusing as written, do it this way: either the GM or the active player will react first, depending on who lost intermediate narration. Then play goes around the table and the person who won intermediate narration gets to react last. This mostly preserves the intent of the original rule, and is easier to remember. The way the rule is written is "fairer" in the sense that the active player should respond first to whatever the GM does (whether that's narration or reaction), but sometimes it's just too hard to remember to do it that way in the heat of play.
