\n Talk of "Losing a Turn" and Mouse Guard's GM and Player's turns has me inspired of sorts. Also I have a free day today.

It seems like the biggest and maybe easiest bit of board game technology that has really hasn't been incorporated into most rpgs that I read is taking turns. No, I'm not being silly. When who can do what seems important to define, and yet so many rpg texts are so murky when it comes to this. I get that it's not important to some GM's, but it is to me. Guidelines are nice, but rules are better.

I have a friend for whom Apocalypse World falls apart because there wasn't enough structure for who gets to do what when. I've felt that way many times as a player with lots of different games. There's a battle inside of me where being able to talk over others to get more spotlight, do more moves, etc goes against being a dick. My head is full of trying to read the social cues at the table. And that is crowding out what's going on in the story, mechanics, and even fun ways to play or think as my character. This leads to me getting either stressed out, frustrated, or bored.

I also have a friend who hates being confined to the Mouse Guard Turn structure. He wants to just be able to do stuff and play his character with complete freedom.

Yes, these two games are not for these two people, but is there a way to reconcile or use "turns" to make rpgs better?

I'm not looking for anything specific. If you've got a thought, share it. Go nuts.

    \n Have you seen Hot Guys Making Out, Jesse?

    It has a straight-up turn structure. On your turn, you play a card from your hand, which corresponds to the type of action you take in the game, which you then describe (a sentence or two). Then it's the player to the left's turn. That's pretty much the whole game. It works very well, in that it's easy to start playing, and as a player you feel very supported by the structure, and it's broken down into small turns so that it's not intimidating. I say one small thing, and then it's the next player's turn. It will come back around to me soon, because turns are short. Since they're short, and we say small things, it is easy to take what other people have said and build on it.

    \n Turns are important, we can't all go at the same time. I'm not a fan if games ignore it entirely.

    I like the ORE system where your die roll determines turn order. It really only holds for conflicts, and then combat is usually where it only matters. Though there are rules for determines the order of who gets to say what they are doing, that's more typical (and could be ignored).

    I really like Fiasco's turns. They're aren't turns about when you get to go, but turns about when you get to have major control over a scene. Once the scene is established, then people in the scene talk, act, and converse like people do and there's no need to have a formalized turn order. I really like this method.

    The Nobliis (3rd) method is interesting and works well in practice. It's basically just, whoever wants to go can go. If that is more than one person, find out if their actions need to have a specific order. If not, then it doesn't matter which goes first. If it does, then work out which one makes sense to go first. This could be based on the context of the action (e.g. throwing a car is faster than throwing the Moon) or player agreement, or failing that, the power of the miracle (e.g. throwing the moon is more powerful so goes first). It's kind of like the new Marvel RPG's system, but instead of the player deciding who goes next, the GM and the players do.

    Formalized turn orders that involve lengthy and complex turn actions can go hang, though. It just sucks to have the game focus on a single player for several minutes as they engage and wrestle with the mechanics to accomplish a discrete action. I understand why many traditional players brag when they make it through game sessions without touching the dice.

    \nIn an early version of my game Final Hour of a Storied Age I had an auction mechanic to see who would take various roles in the scene, but I abandoned it because it wasn't working in practice. People hated being forced to bid (I needed to make sure at least someone would go next), bidding "low" and accidentally winning when everyone else bid lower, and trying hard to win but getting edged out by somebody else. I initially thought it would be a good way to make sure people were invested in the scenes, but the way I implemented it seemed to frequently result in negative feelings. I switched to a dice-based system that seemed to work a lot better, at least for my game.\n

    \nI'm not sure if it would qualify as going beyond "merely spotlight-moving", but in my game I have a dice mechanic that determines which character/player gets spotlight in a scene and also which player is responsible for providing the adversity. The idea is that different players can provide a different "tone" for the scene -- the inspiration was thinking of The Lord of the Rings as Sauron's player providing the adversity against Frodo when the Black Riders were chasing him, but Gandalf's player providing the adversity when they were dealing with Tom Bombadil's strangeness. I think it works pretty well, but a lot of people seem to be uncomfortable with the idea, since they're more familiar with either round-robin or a human using judgment and social cues to move the spotlight around.

    Posted By: Dave LWhen I first started playing RPGs with Moldvay Basic, the turn structure really helped us understand how to play the game. (We ranged in age from 8-11.) We played it like a boardgame with the board to be discovered.
    \nThis is essentially my theory about turns. The more structure you can steal from board/card/party games, the more comfortable people who aren't already immersed in RPG culture will feel, and the easier your game will be to play the way you want it played. Your job as a game designer is to examine the negotiated structure that makes your game work with your friends and formalize that so that other people don't break it with their wildly different negotiated structure, and turns are a great tool to do that. (And as Jogesh mentioned, everybody can't talk at once, so your game already has a turn structure, even if you haven't written it down.)

    I like to use turns for scene framing -- it does a nice job, in my experience, of breaking up the spotlight. Most people have a couple of good scene ideas, but if one person sets every scene, they tend to get less interesting bit by bit -- so divide that labor up and use everybody's best idea. I like the Apocalypse World turn setup okay too, but it puts a ton of responsibility on the MC to distribute turns interestingly, and players can chafe at the bit.

    \n Most people already use a very complex and intuitive system of turn-taking in games, which we call "conversation".

    It's a tricky system, and we spend years learning it, but it's very useful. It means that most of us are very good at naturally pausing to let the person with the most immediate need to talk go first, we are good at picking up subtle cues about whose turn is next, and we can adapt to people butting in, or hanging back.

    Clever designs leverage this existing system rather than bolting an extra turn-taking mechanic on to of it, if at all possible.

    \n After thinking about it, turns in a rpg don't usually equate turns in a board game or sport. The action in a board game or sport is something physical done with the materials of the game. The players are talking to each other during these actions and on the periphery, but it's not usually considered to be part of the game mechanics.

    RPGs are way interesting because when players are talking to each other, this IS considered to be part of the game mechanics or the "action". The only time that it seems like it's not is when the players are talking about the game, it's rules, or something not related to the game.

    So spotlight issues come up when one player talks more than others or one player doesn't get to talk when they want to, etc.

    Off of the top of my head, the only rpg that I can think of that limits talking, or in this case writing, is Do: Pilgrims of the Flying Temple. (I'd argue that Happy Birthday Robot is not a rpg, but that's not really important.)

    What else does this? I'm thinking maybe Microscope?

    \n -Long turns may favor players acting individually (and the same happens when there are too many players OR player's attention span is short OR short term memory is weak among the players)
    -Short turns may favor players supporting each other or at least being more aware of each other's input
    -"Everything happens simultaneosly" may trigger a discussion over creative agenda, which resolves defining turns. If players agree on the creative agenda at some point or they don't interrupt each other's agenda, then simultaneous resolution effectively happens.
    -Each player has a different level of spotlight need. Each player has a different rythm for adding their input to the fiction. Whatever the reason, players that talk more and players that talk less will need a way to play together. If giving them both exactly the same treatment/screen time doesn't work, then something else has to be arranged. Sometimes it can be solved out of the game by groups with certain degree of social skills. However I prefer games that offer advice about solving this in-game, or better yet, offer a mechanic or procedure ingrained with the game mechanics to solve this in an interesting way.

    Curiously enough, I'd bet most of us have played in games where one player had a short turn, another took a long one, and a discussion was cut by the GM raining orcs over the party, all happening simultaneously.
