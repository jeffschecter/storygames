\n Hi there,

I fell in love with this game long ago and as I actually have sessions playing it, I am quite confident that this game likes me back. I am talking about a game which is not yet out in the open in its full glory but I feel we can have a really good grasp of its basics by looking at The City of Fire and Coin--which is a wonderful play&learn-by-read adventure--and Epidiah Ravachol's posts on the publisher's (basically his) site.
All that said, I feel a burning desire to understand these very basics more fully. The more direct one is about failing. On some specific conditions, failing (doubles, being stymied) seems a bit out of place for me. These occassions are:
- when your rogue's Trick is being knowledgeable about something (like Manyara in the demo adventure), so you show it by forcing a Discovery Phase and that you are asked, handed the dice
- when in a Rogue Phase, no matter if it's for a Trick or not
These two are situations when I understand the rules as you really want to show some strength of your character and I don't see how can you do that with a failure (double, stymy). So what is your opinion on this?

A completely unrelated thing with this game I find hard is the incorporation of prep. Now, I guess this is much harder than the previous one as there are much less rules and hints already publicated regarding this side of the game. The game gives the players so big freedom that it's an incentive in itself to build the world, the story from the ground up. How can you prep and incentivize the others using your prep as a base for their play?

Thank you in advance for your honest advice and thoughts!

Peter

    \n I am delighted to hear that the relationship seems mutual!

    So I might have some answers for you, but I have to warn you, the first may sound a bit like bullshit. It's not! But it may sound like it.

    I try to avoid using the term "failure" in the text, specifically when referring to when you roll a tie, because I want to avoid the traditional paradigm that accompanies it. Just like a traditional failure, if you roll a tie in Sw/oM your rogue is prevented from achieving their goal. But none of the other baggage comes along with it. It's not necessarily the rogue's fault or due to a lack of competence. It doesn't mean the rogue is forever prevented from achieving their goal. It does not have to be a "whiff."

    Instead, think of it as an opportunity to invest glory in your opposition, like I talk about here. You are not thwarted because you're not of a high enough level to deal with the situation. You're thwarted because no one is of a high enough level to deal with the situation. This is not a moment of failure, this is a moment of fantastic difficulty or hardship.

    To use Manyara's trick as an example, this thing you've encountered can be so weird, so unusual, so unique in the world, that even Manyara has never heard of its like. (This is one of the reasons why half the ties in the game lead to a Mystery.)

    Okay, so that's the change the paradigm solution. That's all well and good, but sometimes you need a more practical solution. You may find that in the Heroic Feat rules on page 21 of "The City of Fire & Coin." These let the Rogue Players ignore a roll they don't like, and ties certainly qualify.

    As for incorporating prep, here's a simple way to write an adventure for Swords Without Master, and one that anyone, not just the Overplayer, can do. (In fact, it's actually one of the Tricks available to Rogue Players.) Take your three motif cards and pre-fill out the last element on each card. So it will look a bit like this:

    Motif #1
    * __________________
    * __________________
    * An icy cavern that echoes in the voice of a child.

    Motif #2
    * __________________
    * __________________
    * The slaying of a 1,000 warriors in a single night.

    Motif #3
    * __________________
    * __________________
    * Astride leather-winged mounts.

    When it comes time to play, toss them out on the table. You play normally, but before you can move on from one motif to the next, you must have the pre-filled out element arise in play. Once it does arise, treat is as normal. You can even set up your echoes by doing things like describing "bat-winged silhouettes against the moons" while you're still on the first or second motif card.

    There are other ways to incorporate prep, but the key to them all is to prep only part of each idea. Draw a map, but don't fill it in (For Love of Dungeons is perfect for this.) And always be open with it. Put the entire map right out in front of everyone from the beginning. Let the other players write and draw on it. That sort of thing.

    Hopefully that's a little more helpful. I mean, it's not as helpful as publishing the damn game already, but I hope it's at least a little better than scrawling all this information out longhand in a notebook and then setting that notebook on fire before anyone else had a chance to read it.

    P.S. The miscellany in the back of the first issue of Worlds Without Master was designed, in part, to help you randomly generate those pre-filled out motif cards.

    \n@czipeter With a Moral in a Discovery phase you get to re-contextualize things, perhaps your own character, perhaps the world, to your regret. Your character may realize that the mark on the temple door is the same as a tattoo on their arm that appeared one night. Or, classically, learning something may then make them the target of some person/god/monster/lover's ire. Remembering something may suddenly make the chase you've been running--for example, away from a pirate crew--suddenly make sense: that woman you stole your sword from is the captain of the ship.

    Hope those examples help!
