\n I've been running a C&C game for a couple of months now. Last session, two of the players bowed out. Primarily, personal issues came up which prevented them from continuing with the game. But one of them also told me that they were vagualy dissatisfied with the direction of play, that they felt there hadn't been enough focus on the realtionship between their characters. These two players' characters were indeed linked (intimately so, they only bothered to tell me recently), so I can see the point, however a comment was made which set off warning bells.
\n
\nTo paraphrase, one of these two players said that they spent a lot of time between games talking to each other in character.
\n
\nI have ambivelent feelings about this. On one hand its neat that the players were so into their characters that they couldn't wait to play them once a week. But as I see it, during these isolated interactions they were developing ties and expectations that the rest of the group wasn't aware of and thus couldn't participate in or react to. So when these two characters showed up for sessions, they were essentially finding themselves more and more at odds with the rest of the groups expectations.
\n
\nIn the future, I'm tempted to speak out against this sort of thing, to decalre that, "if it doesn't happen in the session, it doesn't count," but that seems both draconic and futile. How can I expect to tell people when the are allowed to imagine their characters? Still, I think its ultimately bad for the group. Solutions?

    \n Bluebooking. Or just a report of what they do.

    See the problem isn't so much that it happened (though in this case I think it does point to some other possible social issues at the table, especially considering how things ended), but that it happened with no reporting back. If they'd told you and other players the gist of what happened, you could have dealt with that. Its the lack of communication that is killer.

    So have them write it down, or summarize it. At the very least make sure that everyone lets everyone know that it is happening and what they would like out of the game from it. Else you get into the magical land of "read my mind!"

    \n What Brand said.

    Back in the day, when I spent all my time MUSHing instead of playing WoW, the vast majority of roleplay on those games were away from other players and away from the STs. This wasn't a problem, however, because it was sort of a foregone conclusion that such was happening, and there was a lively community based around posting logs of what had happened in those semiprivate scenes. The better STs would also ask players to send them short synopses and progress reports on what had happened that week, just to keep them informed. Which is all to say: there's absolutely nothing dysfunctional about this sort of play; the greater play contract just needs to take it into account -- which means you need a mode of communication and an understanding of the scope of out-of-session roleplaying.

    \n E.,

    I had something kinda like that happen in a D&D game, though it wasn't as bad.

    Here was the crucial difference: it was in the form of written fiction rather than in-character conversation, which the players took to sharing through LiveJournal. So, I think the biggest thing was that nothing was a secret from other players (well, as long as you felt like reading the things). Also, since they were individual fiction rather than conversations, they started off rather introspective; then the players slowly got more comfortable using other characters in their little stories.

    The main problem I noticed was that, well, not everyone was on the same page. The two more skillful writers did a really good job riffing off each other with subtle and unobtrusive scenes (a conversation here, a memory there, that kind of thing). Two other players posted a thing or two but generally didn't care for the fiction that much; I got the sense that they were fairly invested in the world but didn't like to be in author stance that much. And the fifth player had a tendency to write scenes that were quite a bit more active and showy, which is what caused some problems. Mostly it was just one thing... there was a point where he wrote up something that made one of the others immediately respond with "No. This is a bad idea. This ruins the thing we all wanted to do in play next week." That certainly made me subconsciously frustrated as GM, because it felt like an attack on the integrity of the shared fiction. He wrote some good stuff, but you could tell that he was reaching for something the system didn't let him do (well, it did let him do it, just clunkily: he had a fairly high-level wizard, so he ended up having his character do nonsensical things that caused character drama to happen, particularly trying to use teleportation to randomly drive scenes around). His fiction did the most to hook in the other characters, but that's why it always seemed forced when he did. Overall, the sense I got was that it wasn't working because there was more of an investment in just plain "story" rather than "our story" on his part. Unsurprisingly, after we finished, that player went and GMed his own game.

    I think what kept it together is that, throughout the whole thing, there was a sense that anything folks made up out of game was 90% true rather than 100%: the players encouraged each other to go "Hmm, I'd imagine it happening like this..." or "FYI, the evil knight's name was ______," which really helped ease off the pressure since these didn't feel like unilateral contributions to the shared imagined content. The fact that the first fiction was a joke piece helped that along, I think.

    So, hmm, the blog-stories were a mixed blessing. Definitely mildly dysfunctional, but not as bad as the alternative, which, as I was suffering pretty bad GM burnout at the time, would've been a really lackluster game. It was a bit frustrating as the self- (and system-) appointed guardian of the shared imagined content to see people distort it or to see more happen out of play than in play, but, when it worked, it worked. By the end of the campaign, the people who had really gotten into it had basically figured out how to do functional story-driven freeform through just instinct and trial-and-error, which was pretty cool. And that was more or less what got me looking at Narrativist games, so I'm grateful for that.

    -- Alex

    \n Cautionary note: This is a potential area where differential levels of player commitment can really wreak havoc. If the content of out-of-session play has meaningful feedback into the fiction that can affect other players, the players who are inclined to do out-of-session stuff will, over time, dominate contributions to the fiction. Other players may feel that they have to do the same just to keep up. In my experience, this is a pretty thorny issue of social contract.

    \n Mark,

    What you say is true. It's even more true in more decentered and larger-sphere games (MUSHes, LARPs, etc). I've seen LARPs go right to hell because some players play outside game and do so in ways that directly effect the politics in the game. (Such as the Harpy and the Prince spending hours chatting ICly outside of game with the Primogen.) MUSHes where some folks have heavy control over the direction of play for political or social reasons also get into trouble here.

    I wonder if this would be equally difficult to deal with in GMless games. Like, a Polaris game where one Heart and his New Moon are constantly flirting IC away from the table could change the game in ways that won't be visible to the other players, but which could become things players expect to be important in play. I mean, a lot of the stuff I said about proper communication applies here as well, but the truth is that works best in a game in which there is a central GM who keeps track of things and acts (at the least) as a central hub for information. In a decentralized information network, individual agents having access to privileged information can be ruinous.

    Maybe wikis and the like can take up some of the burden, as a central source to replace the GM, but even then you have the problem of information rather than emotional content being stored.

    I think that one's part of a family of problems. You can see similar problems in-session sometime.

    And I wouldn't be surprised if sometimes -- very rarely but sometimes -- out-of-play contributions could actually balance out an in-play weakness (e.g. for someone who has trouble making stuff up in real time), too. Overall, I think of this kind of thing more as a playstyle patch than a problem in an of itself: people going somewhere else to contribute to the shared imagined fiction means that they feel unable or unwilling to do it in play. A symptom, if you will.

    \nI think that a good game setup needs to be able to deal with people with varying degrees of commitment. Especially, if you're going to have multi-session play, then you need a way of dealing with absences. I think it is common that if someone misses a session, say, then they have more trouble being relevant in the sessions where they do show up. However, I think that's a deterrent to the less hard-core players, which keeps them away rather than drawing them in.

    If you have a game where someone can shine without having put in a lot of time, then you can not only satisfy them -- but you can have people who do extra role-play, like the extra out-of-game scenes and so forth. The out-of-game stuff can enhance the game rather than causing problems for it.

    To put it a little more concretely -- Say you've got two players who have a lot of enthusiasm and play scenes outside of the game; and two players who only get involved during the session. The latter two feel a little shut out by the extra material of the first two, because the former have deeper relationships, more processed personalities, or whatever. My preferred solution would be to introduce stuff to get the latter two more involved, rather than trying to shut down the former two in an effort to level the playing field.
    For example, as GM, I have often taken the least-connected character (i.e. the PC with the fewest hooks, enemies, or whatever) and made them central to the plotline. So, for example, in Season Two of our Buffy game -- we set up that Roberta was not only made Guardian of an interdimensional nexus, but also the Big Bad was an ex-boyfriend of hers from college.

    \n I think there's a tension here: If you don't make the out-of-session RP feed back meaningfully into the game then it's an inside joke, in the same way it would be if people were constantly exchanging one-liners from obscure movies that only the two of them had ever seen ... except with the added problem that the one-liners (for them) have inevitable context, and are considered part of the game.
    \n
    \nIf you do make the out-of-session RP feed back meaningfully into the game then you have to think carefully about what sort of authorial power that gives the players, and how that fits with the rest of the game. Hence the concern about people dominating the game ... and the even more clear hypothetical of people in Polaris essentially using out-of-session play to play scenes without adversity in a way that the rest of the system would never allow.
    \n
    \nNow one could theoretically design a system which is built for distributed play of that sort, so that people playing in out-of-session play and feeding that back into the in-session play would still be taking part in a system that balances people's contributions and power. But you're not going to get that simply by letting people RP without rules of any sort, then accepting the results of that RP as a done deal.
    \n
    \nIn LARP, for instance, the opportunity cost of associating with people during an event is that (a) you're seen associating with them and (b) you spend time that you could be spending with other people. Out-of-session IC play bypasses both of those, and should justly be seen as unbalancing. To balance that you might (a) force people to post the fact of their association to a central message board and (b) give them a limited number of hours that they could do such things and have them "count." That would be a step toward balancing creative input by extending the same give-and-take from the in-session play to the out-of-session play.
    \n
    \nI don't really know what the opportunity cost of the two of them chatting at the table and ignoring everyone else (during a session) would be in your game. Any thoughts? Is it the kind of thing that could be extended into their out-of-session play?

    \n Yes, what Tony said about authorial power.

    One problem with between-session scenes is that there's no GM. So how far can the characters go? If they decide they're talking in a restaurant, fine. If they decide they go and rob banks, that's difficult: firstly, because they're doing things that other characters, not present, might want a hand in. Secondly, because there's no resistance: robbing banks should really come with a possibility of getting caught.

    I hate between-session scenes. I hate them because they play using a different system. In a game session, if two players wanted to rob banks, there'd be an hour of play to determine how it went. Between-sessions, it's dependent on player agreement.

    So you've got two systems of play but it's all meant to be affecting the same Shared Imagined Space. But, of course, it affects it in a different way, because it's a different system. And, in a between-session scene, it's no longer a Shared Imagined Space, or, at least, not shared by the whole group. It's a mini-SIS that has to be reincorporated into the group SIS at the start of the next game session. To give it its technical term, it's a fucking nightmare.

    Personally (and moving into a rant), I'd ban between-session scenes. If you want to play in between sessions, go find a different game to play. It's supremely arrogant for two players to turn up and say "Hey, this happened between our characters since last week", because they've got no right to decide that on behalf of the group. The group has every right to say "No, that didn't happen", and they should.

    Graham

    \n So, when I started in my current Exalted game, and two of the players were like, "Hey, let's mini," I was like, "what is this mini?" And when they started to PLAY -- WITHOUT A GM -- well, you can imagine my shock.

    Now I do it all the time, because frankly, it's awesome. There are hundreds of potential scenes that are important, exciting or revelatory, but that don't involve all the players, and that would eat up way too much session time, to the degree that we would spend our games doing nothing but having individual scenes with one another. Moreover, the vast majority of these mini-scenes don't require the GM -- we're not trying to accomplish anything, we're just indulging in character drama.

    I respectfully submit that many of the problems described in this thread are actually issues of social contract. Sure, minis suck if the entire game is designed to give every player narrative power in every scene. Yeah, obviously if people are using minis to circumvent the system in order to accomplish difficult tasks, then they're being abused. But, uh, just like all the examples of line-crossing or assumption breaking play on this and other boards, it has nothing to do with what they're doing and everything to do with what relationships they're screwing up in doing it. Don't blame the tool for the acts of the craftsman.

    I do agree, though, that Brand is right, and that a clear record of what exactly happened is, maybe not absolutely necessary, but extremely valuable and play-improving. This is another great thing about IRC play. In games I run, I'd encourage the players to have mini-scenes, both with me and with each other, as long as they're clearly documented, so people know what's going on. It's good for the game, because I don't want to run "Bob and Carol awkwardly dance around the subject of sex" for an hour and a half while Ted and Alice are trapped under the Jade Mountain and can't participate, but I also don't want to deprive the players of the chance to have the scenes they want to have. I, uh, probably wouldn't play with people who disrespected social contract in the ways described above, though.

    \n I'm pigeon's GM in the game he mentions, and I wanted to note that, in my experience, when used well (in a group with good trust and a reasonable amount of flexibility) the miniscenes can actually make managing spotlight time easier. Because our group does miniscenes regularly (both with and without myself involved), every player has a fairly strong ability to throttle their own amount of spotlight time. If someone wants to see an aspect of their character get more focus, they arrange the scenes necessary for it to come out, regardless of whether it can easily involve the other players.
    \n
    \nThe added benefit is that this makes it easier for me to focus regular weekly sessions narrowly on individual characters; my players have a lot of room to explore their goals outside the regular session, so they're less likely to feel put out if events conspire to focus heavily on one particular person in a given week.
    \n
    \nThe passive audience provided by playing on IRC and the posterity provided by saving the logs are vital for this to work the way it does for us; it makes it easy for everyone to cross-reference the developments that happen in minis and make it clear that they're part of the "real game."

    \n John, you've got a point.

    I'm thinking back to being a Storyteller for a Vampire LARP. Our game was part of an organisation, One World By Night, that linked with other Vampire LARPs by email lists. Players would play scenes on that email list and on IM.

    It led to weird situations. You could become powerful in the game by being powerful on the email list: for example, player would turn up with 30 or 40 Status points, loaned to them on the email list, for use in our game.

    But what bothered me most was the out-of-session RPing. Players would say "Oh, we played this scene where we robbed banks" or "I flew to Washington DC". Now, if they'd done that in the game itself, there'd have been risk associated with it: I'd have run a scene with them. But because it was in a private scene, they've added something to the game world without risk associated that it could go wrong.

    It also led to a situation where you couldn't keep up with the LARP without keeping up with the email. Newcomers to the game were at a disadvantage until they'd found the email game.

    So that's what's led to the dislike of out-of-game roleplay.

    \n Sometimes in our games the GM will go off and deal with one or two PCs in a scene that the rest of us aren't involved in, and instead of twiddling our thumbs or talking about what we watched on TV or whatever, we'll chat in-character and make up our own little scenes. Sometimes we're even actively encouraged to do so: the GM will say something like "This would be a great time for your characters to fill each other in on what you've found out," or more commonly, "This is going to take a while...go ahead and amuse yourselves." And I guess I just don't see how that's so different from a couple of players doing basically the same thing via e-mail or on a different night than the game.

    Mind you, there are certain lines that don't get crossed. For example, if a conversation is naturally going to involve another PC and that player's not available to participate, the conversation gets postponed. If the conversation turns towards the PCs actively doing something that will change the course of the game, the GM is cc:ed so that we can play it out in person (or so the GM can say "sure, cool, you do it," if it's no biggie). If the conversation has an interesting result (two PCs fall in love, make a pact, etc.), everyone gets a summary of the conversation and what the players think was decided by it. Common sense stuff, really.

I don't think it always worked, but I think that's what turned it from potential trainwreck into mostly-functional technique.

    \n Coordination and cooperation among the group, and everyone being okay with this, really is a key issue.

    For an AP example, Mo was in an Exalted game that lasted for something over 4 years. It was a huge game and went through a period in which everyone in the group went from being single to being married and having children, and there were lots of other life changes through the course of the game. Partly because of that it was pretty common that folks would sometimes have to miss the regularly scheduled game -- especially in summers when everyone was traveling.

    However, everyone else still wanted to play, but due to the nature of the game's narrative simply playing the present game without them wasn't really doable. So the GM came up with the idea of doing flash-back First Age games when the whole group wasn't present. Those who did show up would get to rock out with MEGA WIDE SCREEN FIRST AGE BADNESS and maybe learn a little about who their character was a lifetime ago.

    At first there were no problems with this, and people more and less enjoyed the first age games. Over time some folks came to like them less, as their focus was on the current age and their "real" character. And after that some resentment issues cropped up because the First Age games would occasionally contain secrets about the First Age and players did get XP for taking part in the game. Over the course of years and years this meant that the players who never missed game had significantly more XP and a fistful of secret knowledge that those who missed game more regularly didn't. Those who didn't miss game felt some justification in this -- as they made it to game -- and those who did miss game still felt that it was getting out of hand. In the end the issue got resolved by stopping the First Age games but letting the players keep the secrets and extra XP they'd earned.

    The thing about it is that is a situation in which the play is still at the table, with the GM, with the majority (if narrowly) at the table and with everyone having had an equal opportunity (in the abstract, at least) to participate. It wasn't things people were doing outside the game, away from the table, or without the direct and explicit knowledge of everyone else in the group.

    OTOH, I've seen games where there were floating GMs played in an ad-hoc manner where the world could radically change between one session you were in and the next, because three of the eight total pool of players had blown up New York while you were in China. People in that game expected that, however, and would roll with it. Over the course of two years that game never had a problem with folks feeling out of the loop of things that happened while they weren't at the table, and I think the reason is that game had a social contract specifically stating that it was going to happen. Everyone went in with their eyes open.

    \n We had only the canon rule, "If the GM didn't acknowledge, it didn't happen." I keep that one in my own games; it's a little more than, "If the GM didn't see it," because sometimes I can be talking to someone, and didn't catch all of what happened with someone else.

    This does not limit the play significantly. Oh sure, every time the GM left the room in our group we'd suggest we just found "bags and bags of treasure" and had "solved the current problem," but that was silly and we relented the point. There were other outlets; "Dark Side Diaries" which was, "If my character was truly twisted, (s)he would have done this...", and other alternate-perspective journals sent to the group in-between sessions, backstory developments that brought PCs closer together, sent to GM to be accepted as canon, and of course, other various creative outlets. (Mix CDs with character-oriented songs, filks of the adventures, artist renditions of brochures of places visited, etc.)

    Just because the game was only 4-5 hours once a week didn't mean you had to stop investing in the universe except inside that time frame.

    Now, do I feel weird and voyeuristic when I read the scenes done without my input in my world? No, I feel exuberant, especially the more the players see the same stuff I do...but it took me a while to let go the reins. [shrug]
