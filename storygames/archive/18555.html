\n Spurred by another thread...let's talk about workshopping.

Many (but not all!) Nordic larps use workshops before a game. Sometimes, they take as long as the games themselves -- several hours or more -- but in Nordica, many players view them as an integral part of the game experience, and one that vastly improves the quality of play. Generally, workshops consist of a series of exercises that fulfill one or more of the following functions:

- Ice breakers, getting people to laugh with each other, and developing out-of-game relationships
- teaching the techniques of the game. This can include stuff like "cut and brake," physical mechanics, such as boffer fighting, etc.
- communicating the vision for the game and the style of play (is this a comedy or tragedy? a competition or playing to lose? should players avoid leaving this room because it will break the game?)
- developing characters either partially (in the case of pre-written characters)
- helping players embody characters through acting exercises
- developing relationships between characters so that people have stuff to play on.
- auxiliary exercises like guided meditations or conversations to develop the themes of the game.

Read more here on the Nordic Larp Wiki -- http://nordiclarp.org/wiki/Pre-larp_Workshop


Workshopping seems slow to catch on here in the states. I'm wondering why. Also:

What's important to include? How do people approach running them before larps? Has anyone used them before tabletop games? What are the advantages/disadvantages?

    \n This is such an interesting topic. It's clear that workshops are often afforded value approaching that of the experience of play itself. We (tabletop nerds) prioritize play so intensely that this is hard to process.

    Sharp and arbitrary time limits are definitely a factor. Available space and the physical environment are a factor. Meeting at a convention, playing with acquaintances or strangers can be surprisingly impersonal for such an intimate activity.

    I think a lot of what happens in a workshop is, in tabletop play, telegraphed (and merely sketched) by a facilitator who models expectations - for example, taking the first scene in a Fiasco game and establishing tone and pacing.

    Lots to learn from workshop culture!

    \n In the American LARPs I've experienced first hand, the format mirrors that of D&D. Play is expected to follow established mechanisms and span multiple events. Your first time you are a new player and there is orientation, after that you are considered to "know how to play" and "don't need further teaching." Players are usually a mix of new, intermediate, and veteran players. Play is also selfishly focused on the player-character relationship which is your sacred thing rather than character-character relationships, which would need to be established and practiced. From this prospective, beginning an instance of play with a workshop for everyone may seem unnecessary and may contribute to their not taking off among "traditional" American LARPers. [I'm all for workshops personally]

    \n Live combat games in the US have featured "fighter practices" and "system playtests" for like 25+ years. They don't call them workshops, and they don't force every single player to participate in them, but they have been using the concept of having a get-together to prepare for the game for a long long time. In that respect, it's not slow to catch on at all - I'd say more new live combat games use pre-event-events than don't, as far as I've seen in my local scene and the impression I get of how things go in other scenes.

    It is true, though, that one-shot larps in the US usually don't have workshop-like pre-events. But, really, one-shot larps in the US have rarely been much of a thing outside a small sprinkling of things here and there and 2 consistent groups (more than 5 years of more than 3-4 games per year) that I know of - the Intercon group on the east coast and the UCLA Enigma group on the west coast. And most of the one-shots played have tended toward the whimsical rather than the serious, so the extra time commitment of workshops and formal post-game discussions were seen as an unnecessary barrier to entry.

    It's really hard to get new people into the hobby in the US (though the stigma is fading as more positive media representations appear). So asking people to commit to extra time to do pre-game work before they've had a chance to experience the fun parts of larping is generally seen as something too many new folks won't be willing to risk up front. (Which is ironic, because doing good workshopping greatly reduces the risk of having a negative experience during the game. But it is what it is.)

    \n I have come to believe that one of the keys to succesful tabletop workshopping is to start producing juicy, game-relevant fiction as early on as possible. When I make workshops of some length for a tabletop or chamber larp, I split into a warm-up phase and a prologue phase, with as much prologue as is practical, where players will be either in character or describing and telling game-relevant and game-true stuff, leaving as little time "wasted" as possible.

    Pros and cons? Con, there's the issue of time consumption and time constraints, and lengthy non-fiction-producing workshops can get when-do-we-get-to-the-game boring. With a little care to keep workshops fiction-relevant and not boring, I have trouble seeing other cons than time constraints. Pros: Too many to list. I particularly like evidence based casting, if I have to name a favourite.

    \n But here's the thing: workshopping is also part of the "good part" of the game. It builds anticipation, settles anxiety, introduces you to people, and settles unrest.

    I've found that it is good for luring first-time larpers in. You can say stuff like, "I know you're nervous about playing a character, but don't worry about it -- we'll teach you how to do it in the workshop." Or, "come try the workshop, and if you decide it's not your thing, you can leave before the game starts, without feeling awkward about it."

    I think that stuff like character creation in indie games can have some stuff that's similar to workshopping. Things like fighter practice have some similarities too, though I think that the purpose of workshopping is defeated when not everyone participates.

    I'm a big fan of Troels' evidence-based casting thing too, where you basically peg people as introverts or extroverts and assign roles accordingly. Lots of interesting theory in there, I think.

    \n Obstacles I've seen to pregame workshopping:

    New game:
    We're worried about running out of time to actually play. Introducing the concept, learning the rules, filling out sheets, etc. takes long enough. In a game with no rules or sheets, this would be different. I'm sure that if I could get a freeform tabletop game together, I could spend the set-up time on exercises instead. But freeform doesn't appear to be popular here, as far as I can tell.

    Game we've played before:
    I think doing a few warm-up exercises is great for transitioning from "we're just hanging out" to "game time". But I've had trouble getting players to buy in to working at something they already know how to do. "This requires skills that could stand to be practiced and improved" is an unwelcome message to folks who just want to play and have fun. The board game Cranium requires a facility with words and letters, but no one would want to do spelling exercises before hand. That would emphasize performance and increase pressure. "Describe what your characters look like at the beginning of every session," was about all I could get my group to really invest in. Anything more involved was grudging and awkward.

    \n Jason -- my take on workshopping for tabletop would be that its best use is probably to help people get into character a bit and build fiction together. I don't know all that many exercises, but here are a few I'm thinking of:

    So like, doing the hot-seat exercise, where one person is "on" and the others zap them with questions ranging from "what is your favorite color?" to "what is your biggest regret?" with the understanding that these answers don't need to be set in stone.

    And then a little talking about relationships, how people view the group, and so on. This is stuff that naturally happens already during many character-creation phases. But giving it a little time and having the GM prompt with some questions (or maybe playing out a scene where people first met?) could add extra depth, I'd imagine. I often have a hard time a) remembering people's character names, b) remembering my relationships with them well enough to play on that during a game. A few reinforcing exercises helps me a lot. It'd also be possible to use that time talking about the themes we're about to play on and for the GM and players to get a sense of how the group wants to play the game (for comedy, for tragedy, close to home, etc.).

    I'd imagine that with four players, you could probably do the two above things in about half an hour. When you add more players, it seems like the required time increases, in part because there are more potential relationships to explore. So with big larps I've also seen stuff like status lines and so on. I really like exercises like that because it divorces the status of my character from me and in doing so, makes it easier to play on.

    \n Here are two relationship-building exercises.

    1. Take a ball of yarn. Throw the ball to someone and hold on to one end. Say something to that person in a form of a statement. Don't hint. Say it out loud what your intention is. Examples: a) "You left me for dead" b) "It was you and me that summer." c) "I was the one who helped you during your divorce."

    The person who catches the ball then answers back by twisting what have already been said. Say the first thing that comes into mind. Examples: a) "It was either you or the town. I needed that bag!" b) "But you were never my friend." c) "It was just sex! Get over it!"

    After a while, there will be a web of relations that hopefully entangles the group.

    2. The GM gives out a theme for the group - a high school gang, dragon slayers, or breakers. The GM then asks questions about status. "Who is the leader?" - "Who is the real leader?" - "Who is the soldier?" - "Who is the caring one?" and so on. Continue to build relations by putting each character in a scene with another. Give out where it is and what has happened. "The soldier and the leader: you're at the scrap yard when a new gang arrives." - "The real leader and the caring one: you're at a playground and drinking while planning what to do that evening."

    Related: a thread of ice-breakers exercises
