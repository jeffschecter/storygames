\n Gamers in all places along the continuum from grognard to space hippie often get accused of being frustrated authors.

Are you a writer? Are you frustrated? What do you write - poetry, nonfiction, fiction, drama, other? Have you been published? Do you want to be? How does gaming factor into all that?

For my part I've written all kinds of stuff, from awful lit-mag poetry to ad copy to one-act plays about Nazis, but gaming (and design, and publishing) scratches all my literary aspiration itches. Zero frustration. That said, I'm embarking on a new project that will involve tons of writing, and it feels great! Irony. How about you?

    \n I've written two manuscripts. The second one, whose word count was somewhere in the 80,000s, went through a year and four drafts worth of revision. I'm taking a break from it right now because I've realized that I need to rewrite the whole damn thing from a different POV, as the protagonist turned out to be someone else than I had planned. I just can't find the time to commit to it. Lisa has written five full manuscripts, one measuring in at over 250k words, and she's still working on cleaning those up.

    I did NaNo a couple of times, successfully each time. I went to the Surrey International Writer's Conference and had chats with Terry Brooks, Teresa Nielsen Hayden, and some other writers, editors, and agents. Turns out they're real people just like everyone else Including bad moods, insecurities, and other issues. That was a good experience, because it made the whole field less intimidating.

    There is some relationship to roleplaying, sure. When Lisa and I play via chat interfaces, it's more like a collaborative writing effort rather than storytelling, which is why we often play that way even when we're in the same room together.

    But there are also traps in there, when people think that playing somehow directly translates to writing. The pacing, manner of description, points of view, focus on endless character interactions instead of concise plots, no clear endings, and so on, are vastly different in many ways. (Unless you write like Stephen King.)

    \n In highschool and college I did some poetry, some of it apparently pretty good. I've barely tried my hand at poetry since then, but the one thing I did do this year (for a theatre class of all things) everyone said was great. It seems I have natural talent, but no training. I also dabbled in fiction, something that's also kind of faded away, but recently has become rekindled (there's even a story in my head trying to get out that's inspired by the tumor thing). Yes, I am frustrated, but only with myself for not getting off my butt and actually committing to writing.

    EDITED TO ADD: The fiction in question is mostly short, though sometimes long (or at least aspires to be long) and generally would probably be considered "speculative fiction." I live enough in the real world to care about writing about it - new worlds are more interesting to me.

    Sometimes I think my desires to write come from frustrated gaming desires - it seems the more I play story-games, the less I actually sit down to try writing (though then again, the more I watch TV, or read books, the less I want to write). But of course that doesn't explain the poetry. Sometimes I think it's just atmosphere - in college the first time around I was taking fiction writing classes, and surrounded by fellow artsy-writing types, and now I'm not so much. Or it might just be age, I've wound down since I was 22 (and most of my poetry stopped before 20, so "adolescence" can explain that). Mostly I think it's laziness and procrastination taken to ridiculous extremes.

    I am not published (well, my name is on one story on an erotic story website, so I'm technically published in the Forge sense, but it's really someone else's work and I really only edited and helped rewrite some paragraphs, so I don't think of it as "my work" even though maybe half the sentences are at least partly "mine"), but everything I've finished (mostly stuff for classes) has been lauded. Most of my unfinished stuff got good reviews from the few people I let read them, but I'd quickly hit, like 40-50 pages, get tired of the story, and trash it. This is the other half of frustration - the problem with being an author who doesn't enjoy and generally refuses to outline. It winds up like those endless campaigns, winding without a clear destination in sight...

    I consider setting-design (which I used to do a lot of, now not so much but dabble on occasion) and character-making (pre-sheet) to be vaguely similar to writing since both writing and game-prep can take advantage of it, but it's a separate creative animal thaneither one. But otherwise I see these activities as very different. Gaming is one way to make a collaborative story in almost theatrical style, certainly more-or-less orally (maybe a little less so in pbem/pbp games), but with lots of people working with you at all times, in real time. Writing is a way to make a story more-or-less alone, or at least one-at-a-time in the case of most collaborative pieces (which is why pbem/pbp is more writing-like).

    That said, I think lessons from trying one can be translated to the other, somehow. I think my new experiences with story-games since the Forge will help me write better stories, because I have new ways of thinking about narratives. The actions aren't all that related apart from the edge case of pbem/pbp (in that way I agree with Ben) but I think since both are narratives, thinking about the theory of one can help the theory of the other (in a venn diagram sort of way).

    \n I write some fiction. Magnus Jakobsson and I have written a novel for kids/young teens which will be published this fall. No RPG connection at all... except that the book was actually first envisioned (and partly written) as an RPG for kids, and mutated into straight fiction at the request of our editor And we're both game designers.

    For our next book, which will probably be a follow-up, we're going to play PTA for a session or two and use that as an inspiration. It'll be an interesting experiment.

    I prefer playing games to writing, I think, as a process. Less hard work, more instant fiction. OTOH, you know, having a book out there... you can polish and polish and make it just right.

    A strange thing about writing, though: The text mutates slowly, and it's been very interesting to me to see how you can change certain aspects while keeping others like they were. At one point, we changed the gender of one of the two protagonists. It works great now. At another, we made them darker, more troubled. Weird and wonderful.

    \n I'm a dilettante. I can write (well, from some reports), but typically don't because it's generally more work than I want to do. Writing something that I'd actually be proud of would require a lot of time and effort, and very little fun...so I just don't. It's not like there aren't dozens of other things that I can do with my time that have significantly lower entry costs and far greater rewards.

    Gaming fits into that niche: I tend to use it as a place to put ideas that I could have used in some other creative work. Where a real honest-to-god author might sit around thinking up plots and characters and dialogue for use in a novel, I'll do the same thing for a character I'm playing in our weekly game. It's better for me because the deadline is much more relaxed, and because spooling out a dozen half-formed ideas is easier to do than developing one fully-formed, "publication-quality" idea would be. (Not to mention being more useful in a game where my ideas have to shift and interact with contributions made by other people.)

    In other words, gaming gives me a creative outlet which enables me to be lazier and less stressed-out. Without it, I would absolutely be a frustrated writer, in the sense that I'd most likely have to write something, and would then feel compelled to do it properly, and would inevitably end up bitter and pissed off all the time.
