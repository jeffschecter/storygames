Generally the biggest sticking point I have with many DMs and GMs is that they talk so much. They overdescribe and I lose attention. In my own game I want us at the table to get into dialogue rather than monologue.

\u201cThere\u2019s a quux here, what do you do?\u201d
\u201cWe don\u2019t know what a quux is, what does it look like?\u201d
\u201cIt\u2019s the size of four red grapes in a ponnuki shape, what do you do?\u201d
\u201cIs it moving?\u201d
\u201cIt\u2019s seems to have a pulse, but it\u2019s dududum dududum, a bit of an unusual heartbeat\u201d

\n

Whereas so many DMs would be like \u201cIn the land of far away, you find yourself in the company of a rare quux, size of four red grapes in a ponnuki shape, beating to the triplicate pulse of dududum, dududum. The thought of it weighs heavy on your mind. You can see\u2026\u201d and I don\u2019t know what comes next b/c I fell asleep since it was story time.

\n

Minutes go by before there\u2019s a \u201cwhat do you do\u201d. I can use (CW: violent horror) this as an example because this guy doesn\u2019t usually GM this way when there\u2019s actually players present. In the example 18 minutes go by without a \u201cWhat do you do?\u201d. DMs take pride in being great storytellers and yarn-spinners and describers and poets. But, I want to hear what the players say and do.

\n

I\u2019m always trying to be brief and to be about what exits are here, what is here, what do they do? I can\u2019t always live up to that but that\u2019s the goal.

    \n As a GM, I tend to be very self-aware about this (I'm also self-aware about dominating conversations outside of RPGs - what kind of personalities the "GM" role tends to attract is perhaps a factor here).

    A realistic metric is, in most games with a "GM" role, the GM talks about as much as all the other players combined - that's to be expected by design, I think.

    When I realize I'm talking less than that metric, I tend to think the game is doing well! If I notice I have to talk more than that, or the table will fall silent, I take it as a strong hint of shy or disengaged players and start phrasing a larger percentage of my input as direct questions. When I catch myself talking more despite engaged, active players, I know I'm being a bad GM.

    \n I'm an odd duck, perhaps, on this. My preferred games tend to be all about player empowerment, but (assuming the GM is good), I really *like* long stretches of the GM talking. I love to hear the GM tell us, in detail, about this or that. I would find this frustrating:\n

    \u201cThere\u2019s a quux here, what do you do?\u201d
    \u201cWe don\u2019t know what a quux is, what does it look like?\u201d
    \nI'd prefer an excellent and interesting description, no matter how long it is, and I love finding out what a quux is, what it looks like, and any other relevant details. I really don't mind the GM spewing forth on various and sundry subjects and painting a picture of what we're playing or doing. If they're boring, for me, it's not because of the ratio of talking; it's about the content.

    What matters to me, rather, is not the ratio of who talks (although that can also get important, obviously, in extreme cases), but the ratio of who's making meaningful input and making important decisions or contributions.

    These aren't always the same.

    For instance, let's say a player's role is to choose between two possible criminals, at a critical point in the plot, and to send one of them to the executioner. In that scene/moment, I'd totally expect most of the 'talking' to be the GM telling the player stuff about the criminals. The player needs information to make her decision, after all. However, the meaningful choice, here, is in the player's hands. (Let's assume that's true, for this particular example, anyway; I can imagine versions where it's not, but that's not what I'm talking about.)

    When I run games, I'm always keeping an eye on this, and I find it totally fascinating. As a GM, I want to produce enough engaging content and detail to keep myself (and the players) entertained, but, ideally, the players have most of the meaningful input. I enjoy sessions where it felt like I was playing, rather than steering the game at every step of the way (which is more like work). One useful metric is, "How much of what happened could I have predicted before the session started/before a particular scene started?"

    At the end of a session, it's usually really clear, in retrospect, and I like to think about that as a gauge for what to do in the next session.

    \n I use longer GM narration as a sort of ritual space.

    If I go into my story voice, it's for one of two reasons:

    1. I am setting the mood.
    2. I am setting the time and place.

    For example, whenever a group goes into the Druid's Grove and then into the dungeon, they have to unlock a wrought iron gate (or climb it) and they have to push a circular plate (Imperial Key) into an impression on another gate inside a cave. This has become Standard Operation Procedure (SOP) by this point. However, I will always say something like:

    "You carefully make your way through the northern part of the city to the Druid's Grove near sundown, when the druids leave the park and lock the gate. Once they're clear, you sneak up to the eight-foot-tall wrought iron fence with barbs on top, and Omid effortlessly picks the lock with her lock tools. The gate swings open and you make your way into the woods to the left of the path, staying out of the clear areas.

    "A few hundred feet inside the park, off the trail, is a ridge, and a stone stair leading up to a cave in the side of the hill. It's blocked by a gate of brass bars with a rectangular brass plate in the middle. Omid pulls out her Imperial Disc and presses it into a circular impression in the plate and there's a metallic clanking in the stone around you and a sort of magical thrumming vibration. A puff of wet, earthy air escapes from the cave, obviously sealed off magically from the outside until now, and the gate swings open with a squeak."

    This is my reminder to the players that they are leaving the city and entering a magical space, both character and player. It's a ritual that I'll bet my regular players could repeat, if I asked them to do so. It quiets the (virtual) table and gets them to focus on the game. It draws them into my world.

    The first time they encounter a new, weird location, I will likely narrate what they see. I don't prepare text for this in advance. I loathe boxed text most of the time. I generate it from my notes, off the cuff, suited to the group and what they will see first.

    "You've never been this far into the caves. Moving past where you were attacked by the piercers, off to the right of that area, the narrow caves open into a 50-foot-wide cave as deep as you can see. The ceilings are quite high here, barely within your torchlight. It's a sort of mushroom forest, with mushrooms growing as tall as 15 or 20 feet. But even the ground is covered with tiny mushrooms that crush under foot. The dirt is soft and squishy, moist but not wet. You can smell it too: the rich earth, the loamy mushrooms, mold and spores and mildew, and the faint whiff of death."

    That's what a character will notice in 5-10 seconds of being in a new location. After that, I stop with story voice. Now it's exploration time. Characters interact with the environment. I check passive Perception, if necessary. I'm also using a feature-rich map in Roll20, so the players can move to things on the map and ask about them. They can SEE the monsters, if they move to the right spot (giant mushrooms literally block monsters in Roll20, after all).

    So my goal here is to give them a flash impression of a place to make it memorable. It's also ritualistic in the sense that it reminds them that they're in a weird, new place. This is an adventure.

    I love ritual aspects of GMing. It's the one bit of "storytelling" I get to do. The story is that they have come to this place and will encounter whatever is here, and that even that act is one of bravery, outside the realm of what normal people consider doing.

    \n Part of the issue is how you and your group think of the game. Is it another media or a conversation? On a conversation we're used to hear someone for a while and then give our feedback with a rythm outlined by our own emotions, interests and speed of thought and people we're talking with.

    On a media we have expectations for some particular entertainment; we want to experience emotions that could be related to a mood established by the GM or the interaction between our avatars and the fictional world. But if we're thinking of the game as a media we will probably have a bit more of tolerance, especially if our tastes are related with an slower speed of information consumption. Like, if I loved Tolkien I would probably be okay with long descriptions and even feel cheated if the GM didn't use those.

    So, part of the issue is about tastes: some people love the long poetic descriptions, some will hate them and nobody wins when the argument is about which one is better. But there's another viewpoint about the issue that goes into efficiency, where we can say that mood can be projected with economy of words as long as you get to the point. There I agree, you don't need long GM monologues when you know how to instill the right mood into the scene or even better, follow the player's mood and fit your description there.

    You can even play the questions game as part of the exploration: the idea is that the GM describes what the characters see, but doesn't say anything about additional information and lets them ask questions so they figure out little by little what's going on here. It could look like:\n

    \u201cThere\u2019s a small thing here, the size and shape of four red grapes in diamond shape, it pulsates. What do you do?\u201d
    \u201cWhat the heck is that?\u201d
    \u201cAnybody has some Knowledge (nature)?"
    \u201cI do"
    "Then you know the thing is a Quux, a very rare creature, normally harmless"
    "Is it moving? Did you say it was pulsating?\u201d
    \u201cIt\u2019s seems to have a pulse, but it\u2019s dududum dududum, a bit of an unusual heartbeat\u201d
    \nTL:DR: it depends on both your group's tastes and on how you all think of the game.

    \n Having done both the long-winded description thing and the all player action all the time thing, my takeaways are:

    The more I talk, the more vivid and detailed the fiction is, and the more grounded the action.

    The less I talk, the more the players work into a proactive, fast and energetic pace.

    So most of the time I try to mix both.

      \n For Adam and Sandra:

      I did, indeed, play in Adam's game online, and the pace/tempo was very similar to face-to-face play.

      I really liked the "ritual story voice narration" which kicked things off, because after that it was all play all the time. A really nice way to transition from "social talk" to "play", and Adam handles it really well.

      However, I would say that, in general, online play tends more towards people talking for longer periods of time, because interrupting each other is much more difficult and much more annoying than face-to-face (you can't as easily - or at all, without video - judge body language to figure out when to step in). Some games (especially with lots of tactile mechanics) run noticeably slower.

      As an except to that rule, we've been playing Sorcerer, and we've discovered (somewhat by accident) that using a dice roller which automatically sorts the results means that rolling dice and resolving actions is actually much faster than at a real table! That's a nice surprise.

      \n I do all my running and playing in PBP and it's surprisingly similar to Adam's description back on Page One; usually introducing a new scene or setting with a paragraph of description or so and then a short prompt. I think the balance is a little different in that format because it's quicker to read than talk and because the players can always scroll back up to read what was said previously.

      I tend to think of this as being like the cutscene in a video game: there's a nice high-resolution video of the scene that doesn't take too long, then the video stops and you get to do things again. If the cutscene is too long, not interesting, or very different than the interactive part of the game, the players will be unhappy.

      I also try not to describe the player characters doing things, but I like to point things out to specific characters based on their characteristics: "Bob notices that the man's suit is hand-tailored and very expensive" even when they're part of the general description. Players tend to like it when you call out their background and expertise.

      \nYes, and those are all fine and I count them as valid ways of saying "What do you do?" in another way.

      What's important is the act of leaving the word in their hands.

      I personally love the ritual phrase because having a consistent prompt is good UX. It helps me remember to give them the word and it helps them understand that it's up to them what to do.

      But as long as you're letting the players choose and express what their characters do, the actual words aren't the point. If you have a certain way of looking at them, a certain way of raising or lowering your tone, a certain pause between your words, a mix of various phrases to use etc etc, you're golden. If in doubt, use "What do you do?".

      \n Taking something from delivering lectures and seminars here, there are times and places for both styles.

      The lecture style, where the GM presents information, is best for delivering information on which the players can base their next moves. It is no good asking 'what do you do' if the players don't understand the situation in which they find themselves. However, just like a good lecture presentation, the information should be delivered as concisely as possible. There is room for flavour description but not if it turns into a 30 minute monologue.

      The seminar style, which is more of a Q&A, works off the back of the initial lecture. The players now have the information and it is up them what they do with it. This is where the 'what do you do' becomes essential.

      The initial monologue is the opening credits to a film or the essential backstory ('long ago, in a galaxy far far away'). It might be a description of the marketplace or the cavern. Importantly, it should include all the information that the players need to make their decisions. Missing out little details such as the patrol of armed guards or the fact that the marketplace appears to be deserted is inexcusable.
