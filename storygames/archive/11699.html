\n I want to think up fun things you can actually do, that represent other things. Performative, using player skill, hopefully evocative of the thing they simulate. Like solving a ring puzzle to pick a lock, or puzzling out a crossword to decode a military cipher. I know Graham had some system of bolts, washers and nuts in his Meat Loaf biker gang LARP.

So ... a variety of paper and pencil puzzles and cryptograms, right? I want to go beyond that. Some random examples:

How do I diagnose and fix an airplane engine?

How do I navigate through dangerous shoals?

How do I synthesize a chemical in a lab?

How do I decipher an ancient tablet without using a tired substitution cipher?

Get the idea? Go!

    \n Activities of this sort, called "mechanics," are quite popular amongst the MIT larp community. They range greatly in terms of complexity. Two clever ones I've heard of:

    Repairing a spaceship. There was a lego model representing the completed engine. To complete the goal, the players had to create a copy of the model, using legos they collected or purchased or traded for throughout the game.

    Defusing a bomb. This consisted of a simple electronic device (I've seen them used for other purposes - maybe you can buy these as toys?). You pass a loop of wire along the length of a much longer wire which twists and turns; if the two wires touch, a circuit is completed and the device beeps - BOOM!

    Other stuff is simple - some kind of solitaire card game to represent hacking, for instance.

    If this is the sort of stuff you're looking for, I might be able to put you in touch with a friend of mine who's active in the MIT larps (these examples come from his anecdotes).

    \n For deciphering an ancient tablet, use Mad Libs. The players and GM provide words to fill in the blanks, either by the GM asking or using some other sort of random generation, like drawing word tiles out of a bag. Then, the player assembles a meaning out of the finished text. While this won't lead to the players coming to a certain "correct" conclusion, it allows for a cool amount of narrative randomness and player input into the narrative.

    \n "How do I decipher an ancient tablet without using a tired substitution cipher?"

    I like this problem - I'd start by assuming that translation is *not* like decryption, except in special cases.

    In philosophy, we worry a lot about "radical translation," hypothetical situations in which you're trying to understand language with only behavioral cues to go on (and, implicitly, enough similiarity to recognize some part of behavior as language - I think Wittgenstein famously said that if lions had language we wouldn't be able to recognize it as such). Not sure how to make that into a minigame, but one of the upshots is that it foregrounds language as a communication tool, rather than a code (it punctures the "language of thought" hypothesis).

    But a lot of interesting deciphering problems seem to be ones of interpretation rather than substitution. I own a French-English dictionary, and so could brute force my way through, say, _No Exit_. But I'm unlikely to really understand it. If you want an interesting puzzle that's not a code of some sort, maybe give the players something like a (partial?) very literal translation - then they have to use their own ingenuity and exploration of the ancient tablet's history and context to make *sense* of it.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarHow do I hunt food to feed the village?
    \nWhen the hunters leave the village on a hunt, they will find in the wilderness different prey animals to hunt, which have specific difficulties in how tough they are to kill (and vary in how common they are, as the GMs seed them differently in the wilderness). When a hunter kills a beast (Using darts? Bocce?), they get a number of tokens, depending on the animal. So bears might be tough to kill, but give a lot of food tokens. Rabbits might be tough to catch but give very little. Dodo birds are easy to kill but give very little.

    When the hunters return to the village, the tokens are cooked in a pot and thereby transformed into action tokens (perhaps they also need tokens from the gatherers in the village?). Action tokens are the basic currency of the game for other tasks, so everyone wants them. But there's only so much food to go around, so everyone has to hash out how to subdivide the action tokens when they get them.

    \n Oh, hey, geocaching. Seeking pirate treasure? Hunting an ancient tomb? Do you have one dude with different magical senses than everyone else who can douse for water in the desert? Have the GM go around the LARP area ahead of time with a GPS device, hiding small packets of stuff around. Then during the game, the player gets the GPS unit and can acquire directions to the hidden thingies. Perhaps other players have the directions, but no GPS unit. Perhaps the player gets the instructions via completing other tasks or minigames. Perhaps he starts with a list of ten locations, but only one or two have what he's looking for. Perhaps the GPS unit has a limited number of uses or battery life.


    This page describes how to build a set of Infrared goggles for under $10. I've never tried it, but if it could be made to work, you could set up a system of panels that are opaque to visible light but transparent to IR light. Then the GM could hide information behind the panels for the Geordi LaForge type character. Or for whoever int he party has the Helmet of Detect Magic or whatever.

    \nHow do I diagnose and fix an airplane engine? I keep thinking of stuff that's too complicated for this one. What if you just get (or fabricate similar) a set of Frigits and make them get marbles from one point to another on a magnetic surface using too few elements to make it trivial? (Oh, and also, these are just fun to have around -- we bought ours 12-13 years ago before they went to plastic and the kids (of all ages) love them.)

    How do I navigate through dangerous shoals? Model underwater hazards with objects fixed to the bottom of a shoe-box. Obscure the interior with some blue fabric stretched across the top with a grid of squares marked out. The navigator has a thin metal rod that can be poked through the cloth to feel the depth of the bottom at a given grid coordinate. There should be some in-game pressure to go fast so that the player(s) must decide how many soundings they get before being forced to the next row. Their soundings will inform their steering decisions.

    How do I synthesize a chemical in a lab? Provide the player(s) with a few test tubes, a normal set of food colors, some water and a paint chip (from the paint section of the hardware store -- you can have several and choose one at random each time this challenge is invoked). The players must closely match the color of the paint chip given the resources provided. This is something that most anyone will succeed at, so they need to be racing the clock.

    How do I decipher an ancient tablet without using a tired substitution cipher? Maybe you could do something with syntactic/semantic ambiguity. Perhaps provide a few sentences that can be interpreted with multiple meanings and have the player interpret them. See examples here and here and the analysis of "time flies like an arrow" here.

    How do I hunt food to feed the village? For hunting, put silhouettes of birds on the wall and have the player shoot some limited number of spit balls at them with a straw or pen-case. For gathering, play a concentration/memory card or number game.

    How do I compose a symphony? Provide the player with twelve small noise-making items (bells, electronics, finger cymbals, taught strings, whatever) that the player must place on a 3x4 grid such that every adjacent pair shares some attribute that the player lists. You award a point for each pairing that you buy, an extra point or two for anything the coolness of which surprises you and maybe bonus points for speed.

    How do I bug the embassy? Give a map of the office suite to be bugged to the players. Then provide them with some plastic templates of where the bugs are good for listening made to scale with the map. There could be directional and non-directional. Larger bugs might provide clearer tranmissions. Each piece of hardware they have available has a risk and quality as well as a coverage template. Something like Quality * Coverage / Risk is their score? Or maybe have them dress in utility coveralls and crawl silently through ductwork.

    How do I encrypt the secret message? Provide a one-time pad and a strict time limit. Count down while they work to keep them sweating. Take it away as they're still writing when the time is up. Make them obliterate the pad when done. Have another player decrypt it with their copy of the one-time pad.

    How do I bug the embassy?
    \nIn a LARP? Two words: baby monitor.

    The spy team tries to hide the mic/transmitter part of the monitor in the room well enough that it won't be found. But they need to place it centrally and openly enough that it can pick up sound and transmit without interference. If the other team discovers that the room is bugged and/or finds the monitor, the bug is destroyed.



    Actually, a baby monitor surreptitiously hidden in your gaming room could work for tabletop purposes, too. When Group of PCs A is spying on Group B, the GM sends Group A out of the room "so that they don't know what Group B is doing". But Group A goes in the room with the other half of the baby monitor, and can hear some (but maybe not all) of what happens to Group B.

    \n Alrighty, then:
    * Invisible Maze - String monofilament fishing line to form a maze in trees. Attach bells to the line. Send in the PCs--if a bell rings, Bad Things.
    * Agility Check - A balance beam about 6" off the ground (or just a 4x4 on the ground). Cross it without falling off (while being barraged by Evil Magicks\xae). Fall off, Bad Things.
    * Wooden Keys - A simple piece of wood, with a unique shape cut out of it to make a "key." Then, several similar (but non-fitting) keys are also made. Find and fit the right key under time pressures, or Bad Things. (This is how any sort of lock and key is made in the local LARPs that I've attended.)
    * Hacking A System - Pick your favorite number puzzle (e.g. Sodoku) and require it to be completed (accurately) to bypass it and open... whatever: a computer file, a safe, a magically-locked chest. You can even include "skill levle" (i.e. soft skills) by allowing a PC with a high "hacking" or "cryptology" to do an easier puzzle (attach a Hard, Medium, and Easy one as assign skill ranges to attempt the easier ones).
    * Minigames - I've seen pics of a French Star Wars LARP actually using X-Wing v Tie Fighter to determine how a ship-to-ship battle went--the pilot PC was the only one would could sit down to play the game (and they did some cool shit with the set hiding the computer running the game). Other games use minis rules to resolve large set battles (i.e. army v army, not just a large field battle for present players) that crop up over the course of the live game, sometimes even during the game's uptime. Generalizing this method is an exercise for the reader.
    * Blindfolds - All sorts of things can be simulated by just blindfolding someone and giving them a simple manual task, like stacking blocks or sorting coins into piles by denomination.
    * Encryption - There's whole sites devoted to this, but a simple way to encrypt in-game messages is to use a common book, and let the receiver know what page you're using to encode. Then, find the word you want on the page, and use its number (i.e. the count of the word on the page). That's basically substitution, but it involves a 'real' method and the PCs could have a field day deciding which book(s) they will use in-game to encode (e.g. the monastic order might use a specific King James Bible; the thieves guild might use a specific edition of The Anarchist Cookbook, etc).

    I'll dig a bit at RPG.net--there's an old thread which is basically asking the same thing that you are: how can hard skills be employed in a game for things other than boffer/Airsoft combat?
