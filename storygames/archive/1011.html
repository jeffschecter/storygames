Thanks in advance, everyone.

    Posted By: MikeRMIt is satire also, on how women have to "become men" to succeed in a patriarchal society.
    I've tested something on lots of women I've played with, since one wise one informed me of a general problem in RPGs. I ask, "Do you like playing out the struggle of women to unleash themselves from a patriarchical society?" And the answer, almost universally, is, "Not in every game I play."

    Basically, it would seem that the "enlightened" of us males have taken to keeping the patriarchical societies, making the only interesting premise for a female character inside of the overall premise exactly what you quote above. While not specifically sexist itself, would you like it if your only choice was to either play a character opposite of your sex, or always have to play the same premise game after game after game?

    And here's where it is sexist...there's no particular reason for the setting to be that way. Oh, sure, we're all inspired by Tolkien, and thus the societies all end up being victorian (really, not medieval, but victorian visions of the medieval world), in which the only interesting female character is? Eowyn. And her issue is? Overcoming a patriarchical blah, blah, blah...

    It's fantasy, it shouldn't be difficult to posit egalitarian societies. So that women and other minorities playing can experience something other than this one omnipresent premise. Note that this happens with other issues as well - I've actually asked if we could skip playing out the whole cliche "Half-elf who isn't accepted by either society." It's just old. Move on to something new, and let the player decide if they want to rehash these old issues. Give them something more interesting to do.

    Posted By: MegueyConsider what would be an appropriate premise for a *person* in your game or story.
    This is mostly what I'm getting at. Outside of raging against the patriarchal machine, which is a premise based on the societal role of the woman in the given setting, I don't see any premises that aren't applicable to any character. By creating the setting with that role for women, you're creating that premise for women characters. There is the pale-to-invisible parallel premise of the man dealing with his role in a patriarchal society -- accepting his inequal rights, rejecting them, trying to balance them -- and this also arises from the societal role that women have in the setting. It's also worthwhile to point out that you can invert this, creating a matriarchy in your setting, and get the exact opposite set of premises -- men who rage against their buttonholed positions in a matriarchy, and women who deal with the place that their society puts them. Or you can apply this outside of gender, to any setting where one race is set above another (actual example: sidhe in Changeling). By creating the setting, you are constraining (or at least guiding) the potential premises for play. It's not something that you can escape.

    You could create an egalitarian setting where there is no distinction between men and women, sure. Or you could make some fantastic setting where there is both a patriarchy and a matriarchy available for character backgrounds, in some attempt to preserve choice. But I think the idea of making this sort of gender-neutral setting has some pretty flawed assumptions behind it. It's tossing the baby out with the bathwater, and it's ditching the possibility of playing that rage-against-patriarchy premise -- both sides of it and all the myriad ways that players can approach it. It's also, to get a little abstract about things, kind of intellectually dishonest to pretend that gender issues don't touch every single aspect of life, or that there always is a nice, sensible, gender-friendly alternative to any given problem. I'm pretty profoundly disinterested in playing an ungendered character, and erasing gender roles from a setting or making them so faint that they don't impact play seems to be an attempt to do just that.

    Well, since it's clear the 'no man post here' request is entirely null and void at this point, I'll add my two cents. I'll never forget something a friend of mine said really bugged her about a certain kind of guy (not all) who played female characters: "they *act* like women"--i.e. they have this idea that women are so radically different than men that they have to act in some artificial pattern to 'be' feminine. It was as if their thought pattern went something like "oh, well I'm playing a woman, and we all know women have a soft spot for kids (or what have you), so..." or "oh, I'm a woman in this game, therefore I should be offended that this character doesn't think of me as a fighter..."

    To expand this out a little--what is particularly sexist in game design is to establish certain roles (with privileges or not) that simply repeat gendered stereotypes. While it is true that there has been a long-standing, historically real set of obstacles facing women, a lot of more recent feminist scholarship has gone into showing how overstated this case has been. That in many 'patriarchal dark ages' you find women (individually and in groups) working as agents of change in their society--and not necessarily for strictly 'feminist' (i.e. women gaining political power) agendas.

    Which gets us back to the patriarchy question in a game--patriarchy has almost never been as powerful as certain scholars (patriarchal and otherwise) would like to believe. Games that 'get real' by going all patriarchal are often *more metaphorical* and *more symbolic* than games that take less polar routes. To make a game 'realistic' does not require there be some terrible oppression, nor does it require that only 'traditional' roles be allowed to women. Even women who have embraced traditional female values have found themselves in positions of great influence throughout history.

    In my fantasy setting, there are various race/class/cultural groups you can choose as a background, and some are more egalitarian than others. I'm also spending time to try and make the female members of the fantasy races more interesting and (hopefully) more appealing for women gamers.

    I'm really happy with the way the Dwarven culture is developing. Most fantasy literature simply makes female dwarves (if they're even mentioned) a duplicate of the men, often going as far as making them stout and bearded as well! They don't seem to be a very women friendly character choice.

    In my fantasy setting, the dwarves are the most "traditional" in their assigned gender roles. Dwarven men are stereotypically masculine traits: Bushy Beards, Miners, Blacksmiths, Stone-workers, Warriors. So the Dwarven women should also be stereotypical / traditional -- but still interesting and powerful. Dwarven women have long, braided hair to match the men's beards. Rather than being stout, they're slender and elf-like. Just like the men, they are the best at the crafts they undertake, and so it's not Elven cloaks and boots, but Dwarven cloaks and boots that are magical. Even their cooking is of magical quality (perhaps explaining the stoutness of the men). This suggests they would make the best alchemists / potion makers. The dwarven men are the warriors, while the women are the spell casters.

    Very traditional gender roles, but in a fantasy context the women don't seem less powerful than the men. Or at least that's the idea. ;-)

    The Sylvan and Goblin races are also developing quite nicely, with very different gender roles / balances. I'm hoping that women would actually want to play the females of the various fantasy races -- and not just the elves.

    If my "Shadow of Yesterday" game has a premise I can put my finger on, at this point, it's something along the lines of, "Can we ever build a society that allows us to treat each individual (including ourselves), not as a role, but as a complete human being?"
    And this would make a good statement of the premise of City of Masks as well.

    The underlying theme of City of Masks (the novel as well as the storygame) could be said to be "role vs person" - in fact, there are two factions, the Characterists and the Personalists, who externalize and represent this conflict. Because gender is an important part of how we define ourselves, inevitably that is one of the questions which come up (not the only one or indeed necessarily the main one).

    Since you initially reviewed it, Meguey, I've added in explicit mechanics to create direct conflict between the mask and the "Hidden Face", the underlying personality of the avatar (player character). This is partly because the avatars are young people just establishing their identities, and I think we all remember as young people trying on masks that various authority figures pushed at us, and discovering who we were by the ways in which the masks didn't fit.

    Becoming our true selves - as in the Zen challenge "Show me the face you had before you were born" - is a bit of a theme of mine, and that's really what City of Masks is all about. In the novel, people who've done remarkable things (which mark them out as someone worthy of being added to the list of Characters that other people can portray) get to wear a mask of their own face, signalling that they have portrayed themselves. There's a recent book called something like "The Woman Who Pretended To Be Who She Was" which is going along the same lines.

    Of course, masking, acting, playing storygames and indeed reading fiction and identifying with the characters can be important ways of finding out who you are in order that you may portray yourself.
