\n Hi!
\n I was trying to come up with a way to make a Supers game without the laundry list of powers. I think I have it, but I need to know if its broken:
\nCharacters are made of Traits, Traits are divided into 7 categories:
\n1) Superpowers (This is a Trait with the Power Tag and Super Tag and at least one other Arena Tag - i.e., Physical, Mental or Social).
\n2) Powers (This is a Trait with the Power Tag and at least one Arena Tag) - Powers differ from Super Powers in that they cannot always be brought into play in a conflict in its Arena (sometimes you are not strong enough to overcome that physical challenge). While Super Powers can always be used to overcome challenges in their Arena.
\n3) Abilities (Abilities only have the Ability Tag). Abilities fall into the realm of human potential. They impact challenges, but not on the same scale as Powers or Super Powers.
\n4) Standard Traits (These have no Tags). Standard Traits have no mechanical weight and are simply used to define your character in terms other than kewl powerz.
\n5) Marks (A Mark only has the Mark Tag). A Mark is a weakness that can be applied to any arena of conflict. This represents an aspect of human weakness that can be overcome with effort (Such as Mark of Greed or Mark of Witlessness).
\n6) Weaknesses (This will have the Weakness Tag and at least one Arena Tag) a Weakness will impair your progress in its Arena most of the time. These are extraordinary weaknesses that require special care to overcome, like Batman's reliance on a Utility Belt.
\n7) Super Weakness (This is a Trait with the Weakness Tag, the Super Tag and at least one Arena Tag). This is a catastrophically bad weakness. It will almost always come into play in conflicts in this arena and will be nearly impossible to overcome. Think of Samuel L Jackson's character in Unbreakable when you want to imagine a Super Weakness with a Physical Arena Tag.
\n
\nRemember, there is not list of Traits, just come up with a Trait and assign it Tags. For instance Deceptive could be a Super Weakness, Weakness, Mark, Standard Trait, Ability, Power or Super Power. Its up to you.
\n
\nSo, based on that introduction, Chargen is as follows:
\nHave a complete character design in mind, then do the following:
\nSelect 3 Arena Tags for Powers and/or Super Powers. In order to get a Super Power you must spend at least 2 of these Tags (one for Super and one for each Arena)
\nSelect 3 Abilities
\nSelect 3 Standard Traits
\nSelect 1 Mark
\nSelect one Arena Tag for Weaknesses. In order to get a Super Weakness you must spend at least 2 of these Tags (one for Super and one for each Arena)
\nYou can get extra Abilities by selecting extra Marks (on a one-for-one basis)
\nYou can get extra Tags for Powers by selecting extra Tags for Weaknesses (on a one-for-one basis)
\n
\nEpiphany: Describe the moment when your character decided to use their powers to make the world a better place.
\n
\nNemesis: Using the same chargen system as above, design a super villain for the GM to play. You can give him more power tagds without giving him more weaknesses.
\n
\nGrit: Players start with 3, this number is modified by the following:
\nEvery Power with a Super Tag: -1
\nEvery Weakness Tag: +1
\nEvery Power with a Super Tag that the Nemesis has: +1
\nEvery Weakness Tag: -1
\n
\nYou are done!
\n And all of this (except for Standard Traits) feeds directly into the Resolution mechanic.
\n
\nExample:
\nSteve Forester
\nIce powers (Super, Power, Physical)
\nTotal recall (Power, Mental)
\nCharming (Ability)
\nQuick (Ability)
\nThorough (Ability)
\nLikes people
\nDoesn't like attention
\nImpulsive
\nMark of the Soft touch (Mark)
\nCan never trust again (Weakness, Social)
\nGrit: 3 (3-1+1+1-1)
\n
\nInferno
\nHeat ray (Super,Power, Physical)
\nHot body (Power, Social)
\nInstinct (Ability)
\nDaring (Ability)
\nMethodical (Ability)
\nManic
\nNarcissist
\nDriven
\nMark of Greed (Mark)
\nSuper predictable (Weakness, Mental)
\n
\n So, what I need from you is broken characters. What that means is, they have to fit two criteria:
\n1) You would want to play this character (so yes, you could make a character with the power to make saran wrap opaque, but would you want to play that character?)
\n2) You would not want to GM in a game that featured that player character.
\n What have you got?

    \n Brian,
    \n Great question, Grit is a CCG-like mechanic that lets you "break the rules." some examples:
    \nSpend a Grit Token to perform one of the following:
    \nEnd a Challenge - No more Traits may be narrated into the Challenge
    \nEscalate - Increase the size of the Scope Die one level (e.g., 1d10, becomes 1d12)
    \nDe-escalate - Decrease the size of the Scope Die one level (e.g., 1d10 becomes 1d8)
    \nAnticipate - Cancel the use of one Trait (yours or theirs) for this Challenge
    \nInnovate - Use a Trait that does not have the appropriate Arena Tag for this Challenge
    \nOverdrive - Double one Trait for this Challenge
    \n
    \n I am thinking of adding more, but you get the idea.
    \n Grit is a fixed economy, you don't give the Grit token back to the bank, you give it to the player on the other side of the Conflict. So, your win, is a boon for them as well.
    \n Does that make sense, does it answer your question well enough?

    \n Well, in my most recent effort, I overcame them by scrapping them, which isn't too useful to you.
    \n
    \nAlot of it is game feel and flow and making sure player expectations match the flow you need to get this to work. For instance you say "one side picks the Arena, the other side picks the Scope" that's super easy to write it that way, but much harder to get people to play it that way.
    \n
    \nWhat usually (IME) winds up happening is people do the usual roleplaying talky talk, and eventually someone realizes they should be rolling dice for something and then everybody scratches their head trying to figure out how to shoehorn 5 minutes of talky talk into a formal conflict arena, especially when the whole gamut of DitV escalations happened in that 5 minutes (i.e. well when we started we were guests at the governors party trying to make the other guy look bad, and then we started exchanging words, trying to get the villain to give up his evil ways, and that's when she slapped me, so now I'm throwing down with my powerz...what arena is that?...) usually this winds up with a very uncomfortable degree of ret conning and rewinding.
    \n
    \nThe alternative is to make the game force you to not do the talky talk thing and always to act within the framework of the current conflict with very hard line distinctions made between conflict scenes and non conflict scenes and easy to remember triggers for when one becomes the other. This IME steers the game away from that "just playing my character" thing that is the default mode for many players. So you'll want to pay very particular attention as to whether the feel of play when enforcing hard coded conflict arenas is the feel you're looking for.
    \n
    \nIf you go with this, my advice is to go with absolutely clear procedures. Not definitions. Definitions are helpful but they won't make it happen. Default play procedures (i.e. "I'm just playing my character") won't get you there, no matter how clear your definitions are. You'll need very clear and concise triggers for unmistakably identifying when a conflict starts and a simple and direct sequence of "and then you do X" type of rules to make sure all the toggles are properly set.
    \n
    \nAs an alternative:
    \nIf the primary purpose of Conflict Arena are to determine when powers (etc.) apply, then you probably don't want to go full on hard coded to get there as doing so has alot of other ramifications that really aren't necessary to deal with. Instead, let players buy the arena they want.
    \n
    \nA brainstormed example that might or might not fit with your current rules:
    \n
    \n1) define a conflict in very simple terms such as "anything you are trying to do that impacts anything written on another characters character sheet (including preventing such from being used)" is a "conflict"
    \n2) Whenever a player narrates doing such a thing to another character a conflict begins and they select the arena both characters will be operating under (ideally to their advantage).
    \n3) the other player can spend a benie to use an ability normally reserved for a different arena (or to change the arena entirely for both parties, or whatever seems to work).
    \n
    \nThat way you avoid some of the "rubberbanding" of Hero Quest and Dogs Traits by making players pay for their trait stretching, but you don't need to shoehorn everything into a box.

    \n Ralph,
    \n OK, the mechanics work like this (I'll just do a brief overview):
    \n - One player picks the Arena (physical, mental, social)
    \n - One player picks Scope (this determines which die both players roll. For instance, if one person is at risk, you each roll a d4, if a nation is at risk you roll a d20)
    \n - Players take turns narrating bringing various Traits into play (powers, weaknesses, etc) or use Grit to mod existing Traits
    \n - When this is done, both players roll their Scope die and add their traits to that roll
    \n - The Total is called Effect Points, the player with the highest total is the Winner.
    \n - The Winner can Spend Effect Points to lower the Loser's total.
    \n - If the Loser has any EPs left, they can spend them to lower the Winner's total
    \n - The Winner can buy whatever game world effects they want (this basically means buying Traits for characters in the setting) including Traits, Tags, number of characters affected and duration.To clarify, it is theoretically possible for the players to Permanently give everyone in the world a Super Power (or Super weakness for that matter). It would just take a large coordinated effort that massively overcame the opposition.
    \n - The Loser can buy Traits that do not contravene the Trais the Winner bought if they have any EPs left
    \n - The GM narrates all of this happening
    \n
    \n So, what I mean is, the characters literally can't change the world without using this system (There isn't even an XP system). And that someone HAS to be opposing them in order for them to effect that change.
    \n The point of this mechanic is twofold: One, I want the players to actually be able to change the world in a way they find interesting. Two, I want the characters to be public figures, not skulking in the shadows.
    \nDoes that make sense? Does it sound fun or interesting?

    \n Hey Dave, is that Conflict system inspired by Universalis at all? cuz its really close to the way conflicts work in Uni. If so, super cool. If not, you might find a few helpful tidbits in the Uni rules.
    \n
    \nThree things make the Uni rules work, which may or may not currently exist in your game.
    \n
    \n1) The trigger for a conflict is clear and unequivacable. Its this "whenever a character I control trys to do something to a character you control, its a conflict (i.e. Complication in Uni jargon)".
    \n
    \n2) the rules for all the stuff that isn't a Complication is equally clear and unequivacable. Its this "I can say and do anything I want with any of the things under my authority (subject to being challenged) by paying the appropriate Game Resources to make it happen".
    \n
    \n3) The results are completely Fortune in the Middle, which means first we narrate all the ways and reasons to have Traits in the complicationk, then we determine who wins, then we figure out the whys and the wherefors. FitM works best when the only constraints on the whys and the wherefors are the traits (and such) that went into the conflict to begin with. Having a hard coded Arena puts an arbitrary limit on the output that I'm not sure adds any benefit.
    \n
    \nSo what the game does is clearly delineate my sandbox from your sandbox and let me do most anything I want in my sandbox, but then we roll dice when we want to play in the other guys sandbox. In Uni the sandboxes are also completely malleable which probably isn't pertinent to your game.
    \n
    \n
    \nSo the telling question for me is this:
    \nyou say\n
    \n
    One player picks the Arena (physical, mental, social)
    \n
    \nbut what I want to know is...why? what tells a player "its now time to pick an Arena, conflict has begun".
    \n
    \nIs the entire game played 100% of the time in Arena mode. As soon as this is resolved we frame up another conflict in a different Arena?
    \n
    \nIf not then you'll have a bunch of time wheres people are saying stuff that sounds like it should change the world that you aren't using this system for. If I say "Hard Rock is pissed, he smashes his way into the news room demanding that the paper print a retraction of their recent smear piece" do we have to have a conflict where we go through that proceedure, just to let my Thing hack bust up the joint? If not then here's a time where the world is being changed outside of the mechanic. Do we then have a conflict about whether the newspeople agree to the retraction? If so is it social (because I'm trying to change their minds) or Physical (because I'm smashing the joint up)? If its social...then you have a situation where I'll never be able to intimidate anyone into doing anything unless I specifically define an "intimidate" power. If its physical...then you have a situation where I can win any social conflict just by smashing stuff up...as an example of how those seemingly simple arenas get blurry right away.
    \n
    \nSo I guess the next obvious question is: what are you trying to accomplish with the Arenas. They seem more of an obstacle than a boon, but I might be missing the purpose.
    \n
    \n
    \nSay Yes or Roll the Dice can work, but I'm not sure it will for this application. It works effectively in Dogs because Dogs is a fairly loose conflict system. You can rubberband the Traits, you can escalate, you can do all kinds of things. You'd be hard put to find a conflict in which you COULDN'T eventually justify every die on your sheet (save relationships, which someone oddly are nailed down pretty tightly). But that works well for Dogs...because the point is exactly to tempt you into using all those dice...its less "do you when" and more "what did you have to do to win" (which btw, isn't a bad theme for a supers game either).
    \n
    \nI'm not sure whether SYoRtD will work for you because there are tactical elements leading up to a Conflict (which you indicate are desireable) that may or may not play nice with it. Without having played...it occurs to me that the GM would have pretty tremendous power to help or hose you based on when they decide to say yes and when they decide to roll. Saying yes to things that otherwise would be difficult and going to dice for things that are a characters strengths will produce a vastly different feel than going to dice over things the character isn't good at and saying yes to the things they could actually roll well in.
    \n
    \nAlot of that is just musings, more along the lines of "something to watch out for in your playtests" then "something that you need to fix ASAP"
