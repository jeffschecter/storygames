\n This comes up every now and then. To me, it's a bit like saying "everyone can build a house" or "everyone can write a novel" or "everyone can cross the Atlantic on a sailing ship" - well, yeah, and no.

People have different skills and personal abilities. It is quite possible that I can make better games than person X, no matter how hard they try. And that person Y can make better games than me, even if I do my very very best.

[EDIT] Retracting the following statement on second thought, leaving it here only so people can understand the thread:

And, indeed, it's possible that person Z just... shouldn't really try to design a game, because it would be crap.

[/EDIT]

Or that, even if they tried, they actually wouldn't be able to finish it.

Does this mean I think people should not try to make games? No, it doesn't. That would just be stupid. Of course anyone who wants to design a game should go ahead! And, to be honest, we won't know if we're any good until we've tried it out for a pretty long time.

But we're not all going to be great at it. And some of us are going to suck really bad. Game design does take specific skills and talents.

Main point: It's cool to be a good designer. You're allowed to be proud of it. You're allowed to have fans who think you're cool. Not everyone can do what you do.

    \n You know, there was a time (and I think if anyone wanted to define "trad" they might want to look real close right here) when play was, to greater or lesser extent for every player, both manipulation of the rules and modification of the rules. We didn't think too hard about it -- it's what we were always doing. It was part of why we were there. Every game was an invitation to fix, expand, extend, revise, and reskin. Often it was an inspiration to build something new from scratch (because a lot of early games sucked in a lot of awesome ways).

    We played boardgames and wondered if we could use them as a basis for role-playing games. We found a cool set of miniatures and tried to make an existing game bend to incorporate them (goddam, that guy has armour and a light saber wtf? I want to play HIM). We built thin frameworks that let us make huge strategic campaigns out of wargames, stringing together many battles with a continuous political theme.

    Every person at every table at every session had something to say about game design. It was the secret part of why we were all there. We had great times.

    From about 1975 to 1995 every single person I encountered who was into role-playing games was doing this. I'm not saying everyone can or wants to, but this was the demographic that was playing everywhere I looked.

    So I'll flip the OP around a little: if you're designing a game that doesn't invite this, what the fuck?

    \n So, with Game Chef coming up again, I have to say that I firmly believe that:

    1. Everyone can design a game. It's like how everyone can draw a picture of a flower. You just do it, period. And there it is: a game.

    2. Whether a game is "good" or not is completely subjective, depending on what you want from it. Maybe it isn't particularly successful as a game, but tells you a great deal about the author (insight) or, 20 years down the road, becomes a record of what they were thinking about at the time (nostalgia). Everything is potentially valuable and useful and "good" to someone. And the rest doesn't matter. Why would anyone want to judge all games by the same set of criteria? Why should every game aspire to be D&D or Dogs in the Vineyard? That's bullshit. You have to know your (subjective) criteria before you decide how to judge a game.

    3. Everyone can learn to make games that work better as games (i.e. creating a consistently enjoyable experience for their players, based on whatever subjective criteria you have for play), given practice and a desire to learn from others. Actually, you can learn to make games that are better at whatever subjective criteria you have, if it's selling more copies, or causing more controversy, or making you more famous amongst your peers or whatever. People are good at learning to do things. All it takes is time and dedication. Of course, people have different capacities for getting better and different learning speeds, especially as we get older, but I believe that everyone can make incremental progress if they put in the time and energy. That's one of the simple joys and rewards of being alive.

    4. Does that mean that everyone can make a game that will be hugely successful at their own subjective criteria? No. You can definitely get closer to your criteria or more successful, but nothing guarantees that you will be successful at anything you want to do. The challenge and uncertainty is also part of life simple pleasures and vexing frustrations.

    5. Sometimes this means, in order to be hugely successful by your own (subjective) standards, you have to change the criteria by which you measure yourself and your games. Maybe you just want to write the best 2-player game about zombies ever written. That's probably possible. Will it sell a billion copies and make you world famous? Probably not. Who cares though? You did it. You met your criteria. Maybe you want to hack an existing game and run a really memorable campaign that your home playgroup will never forget. Badass. Do that.

    A lot of this comes out of my own frustrations and personal journey over the past 10 years or so, coming to terms with my own design and publishing goals and ability to execute on them (at least at this stage in my life). So, changing your personal criteria for success is something near and dear to my heart. I do it all the time and feel like it's probably critical for human beings to stay sane and satisfied.

    \n Physiology exists. Take language as an example. After the age of about 12 you have almost 0% chance to learn a new language with the fluency of a native speaker. I imagine that similar benchmarks and early development strongly shape peoples ability to do anything: design games, play music, draw pictures. If I have to spend the next decade to make a game I will be satisfied with, is that worth it? What about 20, what about 50. How many other things will I have to not do in order to make my game? As far as excellence by some sort of societal or cultural standard, well, that functions within the same boundaries: is it worth it. Given an infinite span of time I imagine anyone could be a Jackson Pollack, but we don't have infinity, we have mortality, so, people just have to choose what to do with it and they should (if they want to be effective in their life times) be realistic about it.

    \n So, here's how I see it.


    There are a lot of good things that have happened over the last 10 years. As an ever-changing group of people, we've changed a lot about how people perceive games and game design. This has been a positive thing on the whole, and we're all very pleased with having done it. Yay us.

    For example, we "democratized" design, which is to say we made design something that any schmo could do, and that also chipped away at the notion that companies and their employees should be giving us fun designs. And, that we should also elevate the status of those people for having done so.

    However, there is ALWAYS a tradeoff.

    Other things happened in doing so, too.

    For one, we created an environment that cherished particular values, and either criticized or felt threated by others. I believe this results in a blinding effect, and probably also an environment where challenging certain things is a no-no.

    For example, we cherish innovation. We also cherish the craftsmanship in individuals creating interesting looking publications.

    We do not cherish, say, design that does not innovate in the interest of appealing to a wider audience of gamers ("trad" gamers, or whatever you wish to call it -- you get the point). We don't do this, despite the probability that doing so would mean individual publishers would have more sales.


    When Keith says democratization is nonsense, I understand what he means. I don't think Jonathan has the same perspective. It's no crime; they're just coming at it from different angles.

    I share Keith's perspective on a bunch of these topics above. It's something he and I share and talk about -- things like how limited in business scope we approach things in this community. We haven't talked about it with Jonathan ever, as far as I can recall.

    I'm pretty sure I also understand what Jonathan means, and why it's good, not nonsense, that we've democratized design.

    But, I think we have a situation here in which the community I have been a part of for 10 years is at a point where it's quite happy with the viability, direction, and commercial success of its endeavors. And, I'm wildly disatisfied with that same situation.

    So, I read "nonsense" from that perspective. Democratization has been a fantastic thing. It's achieved good.

    But, when that good is sort of abstractly compared to the good other things have done in gaming, the scope is painfully small.

    This is not to say we accomplished zero. It is to say that our reach and resources have limited things far more than I ever would have guessed back in, say, 2006.

    One can pretty easily argue that, say, Organized Play, did good for the hobby in terms of commercial viability, at least. In comparison to the indie scene, that amount of good is "bigger." So are several other things, games, companies, people, etc. And, thus, I wish we could have done more.

    I'm beginning to believe, based on my experiences and observations, that our community is apathetic to this in general. That its values and mine diverged about 1-2(?) years ago. I'd love to find out I'm wrong about that, and our values and priorities overlap far more than I suspect currently.

    This isn't a "fight" -- hippie games and I simply go in parallel directions or something. That's ok.

    And, so, I think these kinds of things are hard to talk about without a pissing match because of the difference in those values and priorities. I'm VERY interested in talking about these priorities and how they differ from what I see in this community (i.e. hippe indie games, not just SG!). Another thread, I think.

    \n There is a book that gave me a lot to think about recently by psychologist Carol Dweck, called "Mindset."

    It talks about two mindsets which it calls the "Fixed" and the "Growth" mindset, and suggests that the fixed mindset, while it provides some short-term ego protection and validation, is in the long term crippling, and the growth mindset is on the whole healthier and more useful.

    The two mindsets go like this (this is off the top of my head, my own interpretation; it may be laid out definitively on the web somewhere; if you can find that, take that instead of my summary) --

    ====

    FIXED:

    People have different amounts of inherent ability to do different things, different amounts of inherent intelligence and talent. How well you do something shows how much inherent ability you have. Things that come easy to you define your talents; if you stick with those things, you'll do well. Things that don't come easy to you show your inherent flaws and weaknesses. You can't do anything well if you don't have a natural talent for it. Failing is scary because it can prove that the talents you thought you had aren't real after all.

    GROWTH:

    People's inherent abilities are only starting points; you can't know from your starting point how far you can possibly go. A given person may or may not be able to reach a given level of ability, but there is no way of knowing that until you try -- and in fact, there's no way of knowing even if you do try and fail, whether you could get there by trying more. There is always the possibility of success with greater effort -- the only question is whether you want to expend that effort in the hope of success or put your efforts elsewhere. There is always room to improve, in one direction or another -- there isn't "competent" and "incompetent" but many levels of relative competence. Most people who perform with excellence once performed badly, but kept working at it. Saying "so-and-so isn't good at X" doesn't say anything one way or the other about whether they will ever be good at it -- and saying "so-and-so is good at X" doesn't imply that they were always good at it.

    =====


    It sounds like some parts of this thread are a clash between the fixed and growth mindsets.

    Other parts are about a lot of other things.

    But coming at the issue of "can everyone design games" from the fixed mindset, the answer is -- of course not, only the talented ones can. And from the growth mindset, the answer is -- probably yes, eventually, if they are willing to work at it and learn; there's no way of knowing you *can* except by trying and succeeding; there's no way of knowing that you can't, definitively; you can only know "I couldn't do so successfully given the amount of effort I have put forth so far, and I've invested as much effort as I'm willing to do on this."

    I find Dweck's work convincing, though I think there are other things to consider along with her two mindsets.
