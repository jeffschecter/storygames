\n I'm creating a game where the PCs are powerplayers in Hollywood. The rules are coming along nicely, but I want to know:
What kind of stories do you want to tell about Hollywood?

Right now, the levels of notoriety are "Starting out/a little bit of fame/precarious big time/dramatic comeback" and the classes are divided by what they do instead of job. (There are department managers/crew heads, managers, actors, up-their-own-ass artistes, gossip mongers, studio functionaries, in-the-mix producers and duplicitious self-servers.)

The reason I ask is because so little of the source material is ensemble. Most movies about the biz either:
-Focus on one or two people
and/or
-Have a murder mystery

neither of which work great for campaign play.

So what stories do you want to tell about life in the entertainment business?

    \nHubristic tragedies where charismatic but morally bankrupt stars get what's coming to them. (Justice and schadenfreude.)

    Struggles for identity, as young stars lose their sense of self and connection while immersing in roles, networking, socializing with other hollywood types, dating and marrying and divorcing other hollywood types, and otherwise gradually forgetting how to be normal human beings. (Mature character study.)

    Struggles to make great art. Who has vision, and something meaningful to say, and what obstacles will they encounter to realizing their cinematic masterpiece, and can they overcome those? As a subset of this: with many worthy visions in play, can they cohere into a meaningful whole? Or, a slightly different subset: can everyone else contribute to the auteur's vision without clashing or becoming passive? (Story and project.)

    Struggles to make great art-generating machines. Connecting the dots from money to vision to talent to get a studio up and making good movie after good movie without anyone getting screwed over. Forming relationships, optimizing processes, impressing investors, finding synergies. Maybe assembling something like a Scorcese-Schrader-De Niro unit too. Learning to work together in a way that produces great films. (Story and project.)

    Reinforcing or resisting government action or cultural trends. Promoting or vilifying war, lionizing or casting suspicion on the feds, etc. The pressures involved -- the rewards of cooperation, and the risks of resistance. The splitting and in-fighting, loyalty and betrayal. Trumbo and Good Night and Good Luck and The Insider, but about movies. (Thematic premise-addressing.)

    Corruption, and some sort of fight against it. The dark underbelly of drugs, booze, sex for promise of favors, callous narcissism and opportunism, back-stabbing, nepotism, shallowness, appearance-obsession, bribery, exploitative pornography, blackballing, grudges, politics, and general bad behavior. It'd be nice to see that crap under siege, either by virtue or just a different sort of vice. A few friends with principles, a new guild or union, the cops, the feds, the mafia, etc. (Thematic premise-addressing.)

    I could also see some black comedy, satirical, Cards Against Humanity / Very Bad Things type thing where everyone is just awful, and they all prosper by it, and we play to see who prospers the most and whose awfulness makes us gasp or giggle the most.

    \n The most likely kinds of "ensemble" are small; typically two people only.
    Brothers who, unusually, work together and trust each other.
    The boss and his woman, imposed as an actress.
    Variant: a director and his muse, with the latter in a dominating role.
    Old friends, often dating back from the start of their career or even earlier.
    Affine spirits who like each other so much in the professional sphere that they become friends and work together.
    A happy couple, consisting of basically good people, until something happens.
    A happy couple... of complementary psychopaths.
    An unstable or troubled couple where either or both parties suffer from some combination of propensity to be unfaithful, cruel insensitiveness, naive obliviousness to the other's defects and psychotic optimism, addiction problems, etc.. Common subtypes include a man of power, a whorish trophy wife and an impending divorce; two addicts racing towards premature death; attempts to hide one's homosexuality with a fake marriage (historically); a playboy and his latest prey.

    The most promising extensions of this sort of relationships to more PCs are: a network of lust and adultery, linking multiple couples together; making characters parent and child to ensure that they have some combination of strong mutual feelings, shared history and leverage on each other; placing characters in the same project, either randomly or getting the job as the culmination of campaigns of begging, threatening and sex.

    \n In my experience, players:

    -Enjoy old and new romance, especially love triangles;
    -Want to play important, assholish people, getting what they want;
    -Desire to learn about the actual process of filmmaking;
    -Do drugs, especially if celebrities are around.

    They also enjoy actual celebrities being in the game, as well as fictionalized versions thereof, which makes NPC creation a lot easier. The current movie, Spore, ended up starring Nic Cage and Ellen Page and was written by the latter.

    Oddly, it's multi-faceted relationships and very little sex-to-get-what-you-want. I don't even have a sex move mechanic (and the players haven't gone that direction.)

    CURRENTLY there's a lot of focus on great art vs commerce vs fame, with one class being powered by winning arguments about their vision. Consequently, they have to be arbitrary-- they don't score points if everyone starts out agreeing with their vision.

    \n I'll just drop in the two themes that first come to mind when this milieu is suggested:
    * The prostitution of the casting couch, and the in-built misogyny and travesty of art. (Yes, this is no different from traditional theater. Still interesting in all sorts of ways ranging from the vicarious to the horrifying.)
    * Engaging fully in the creative promise of Hollywood, and having it actually pay off beautifully in artistic and financial terms. Somehow even all the managers and accountants are honest, and everybody leaves a project happy and fulfilled. Hollywood works for once just as it is supposed to, all the quirky institutions showing their good sides as the historical reasons for their existence all make an appearance, justifying the wisdom of it all.

    I wouldn't particularly look for both of those at once, obviously.

    \n That makes me imagine a game where you play the One Good Production, or the One Good Film Studio (or some other such) in Hollywood. Every character is a beautiful artist who has some incredible bad blood with another studio/company/director - they were taken advantage of, sold out, blackmailed, ripped off, or some other such thing.

    You play trying to make your artistic dream work, with a team of great people, while the other productions/studios try to keep you down, using every underhanded means at their disposal. Sometimes this means burying you in bad publicity; sometimes it means hiring a bunch of arsonists to set fire to your studio set in the middle of the night. Will you stick to your morals and keep your work clean, or will you stoop to the same kind of illegal behaviour your competitors are engaging in?
