    \n This is one of those questions where the monolithic traditional model of the D&D-like adventure game is the only reason to even ask. That is to say: of course you can make games without character advancement, and of course it's going to be a good or a bad idea solely on the basis of what the game is trying to do, and how character advancement plays into that. You won't be the only person ever to have created a game that is not a modestly hacked D&D clone.

    There is an audience segment that will reject your game if it does not have character advancement. However, that same segment - "traditionalists", or however one might term it - will also reject it if it doesn't have foot-loose adventurer characters, party-based gameplay, long campaigns and a strong GM. If your game is otherwise a traditional adventure game, then it may make commercial sense to have advancement for non-artistic reasons. A different kind of game will not gain on the market merely by including this feature, because other audience segments will not care.

    Personally, my only opinion on this matter is that you as the designer should have passionate opinions about what it is that people do when they're playing your game. It is conceivable that character advancement falls into a blind spot in this regard, but it is much more likely that it's something on which it is well possible to form an opinion. Do that, and craft the game accordingly. I won't be turned off by having character advancement, or not having it, but I will be bored by a game that does not take a stand.

    \n Leave it out to make the statement that players should not strive to keep using characters, but be aware that some tables will do so anyway and probably hack in an advancement system.

    Add an optional rule to have some control over what the hack is, but also encourage players to keep using characters.

    My take: add a first-class bomb rule that lets you "advance" your character after ~a handful of sessions for a great short term effect and long term loss of effectiveness so players can have their characters "go out with a bang".

    \n Character advancement is included in many RPGs because of the wargame roots of the hobby. It is very certainly not required for good RPG design, however. Many of the most successful and well-regarded games coming out in the last decade or so don't feature any character advancement at all.

    Instead of advancement, think of change.

    How does the game change from the first chapter/scene to the last? What has transformed, what has been magnified, what is different?

    This doesn't have to be advancement, and it doesn't need to be mechanical in nature.

    \n Eero and Paul pretty much summed my take on this issue ... the requirement for an advancement depends on the fact whether you wish to represent characters that grow in proficiency during the arc of their adventure.

    I have to add the fact that a designer might have an emotional relationship to the advancement system. I know since I had one for a long time. In every game I made (most of them scrappy little heartbreakers but anyway) I obsessively added an advancement system. And not just any system, but something that tried to mimic the feel of the system that was in Runequest: I always tried to measure the in-game actions of the characters and tried to tie the advancement into that.

    I think it is better to judge seriously whether the game really is better with the advancement system and base the decision on that.

    \nIt depends how loosely you want to define "reward". For instance, in Follow, you, a group of like-minded individuals, move towards a distant goal.

    The "advancement" that happens is that a) you move closer to your eventual goal, scene by scene (although this isn't in question), and b) certain characters get killed off (or lost) along the way (which is something you play to find out).

    That's certainly fun, but isn't, strictly speaking, any kind of "player reward" nor "character advancement.

    That's why I prefer to think of it as a change in the state of the game - how does it transform over time?

    Witnessing transformation over time allows us to have a sense of progress and achievement (which then becomes the reward).

    \n Paul,

    "reward" is indeed supposed to be loose in the sense I meant it in my comment. That's because all that "reward" means is that the game needs to have elements to keep player interest. It doesn't need to be strictly mechanic, but there has to be something in the game's design that manifests itself through gameplay and generates interest for the players to keep them playing. It could certainly be simply a social phenomenon with no built-in mechanical rewarding system. An example: in Primetime Adventures, just letting players set scenes, choose characters for scenes, etc is already a reward in the sense I mean it. That empowerment of the players allows them to add a lot of creative input and that is extremely satisfying for them and keeps them coming back for more. You mention "a change in the state of the game", and that's fine as a reward too, as long as in practice whatever form that takes manages to sustain player interest due to the way the game is played. But changes in the state of the game by itself, or a common goal for the players, is not a universal solution for every game. Some games will need more than that to sustain interest, especially if long term play is intended.

    Not sure if I was clear. Anyway, I don't think we are actually in disagreement.
