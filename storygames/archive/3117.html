    \n Yes. Art and design are... well, the first reason I generally pick up a game. Absolutely. I also use a lot of art at the table. When I was 15 I started photocopying all the art from the Vampire the Masquerade books so I could always (always) say, "You see this guy." I did the same thing with Street Fighter ("you are here," throw down picture of Fei Long stage). And I do the same thing with D&D. Except now, the internet has mostly replaced the photocopier.

    Also, since we're on the topic of Pathfinder -- I also love the goblins. Paizo has been really good about making art available to supplement their publications. The Dungeon online supplements provide free, high resolution images and maps that you can re-purpose for all sorts of things. (Get 'em while the getting is good.)

    Btw, Tim, I think you may be confused about the relative popularity of D&D, Exalted, and CoC ;-)

    \n I've said this elsewhere, but, while art might catch my eye or something, I buy games based solely on the quality of the rules. If the book has no art in it at all, that's fine with me. I'm actually pretty fond of the Traveller "Little Black Book" presentation.
    \n
    \nThat doesn't mean I wont' buy a game with art in it. I don't mind art, for the most part. One exception: if the art depicts something that can't happen in play of the game, I consider that a "visual typo." Same thing as flava text that would never occur in play.

    Posted By: Mike HolmesI've said this elsewhere, but, while art might catch my eye or something, I buy games based solely on the quality of the rules. If the book has no art in it at all, that's fine with me.
    \nThis is me in a nutshell. Fastlane's layout includes no art on purpose (except the cover, which I kind of feel bad for compromising on), and Snowball only has art because that was a requisite for getting into the No Press Anthology. That's probably something that will continue through my designs. Art is an optional extra (and much more useful for games with a defined setting than ones without). Honestly, when I see a designer has spent what appears to be too much time or page-count on art (or even on page layout) that sets off warning bells in my head.

    I am not against art in games, mostly I don't even mind it as long as it seems to serve a purpose, or doesn't get in the way.

    \n I hate art in gaming books and consider it on par with fiction in game books and being poked in the eye with a sharp stick.

    Of course, I am a mean old man. These days the kids are so very virtuous and so very, very clean. They love their art, and their glossy pages, and 40 pages of blabbing about the designer's favorite NPCs. They laugh as they pass me, money in their hands, running as hard as they can to throw it into the purifying flames. "If you were a real gamer," they say, "you'd love that picture on page 93!"

    "Fuck that." I say. "All my books on how to write stories and my actual books which contain stories don't have any art in them at all. "

    But it is too late, they have already passed. They were wearing those earbud headphones anyway and couldn't possibly have heard me. When they get back, I'll be banned.
