\n Hello! First post. I made this thread out of curiosity, since I have no plans to buy an RPG soon.

So. I'm playing Pathfinder (6 players + GM). And while I do find some things interesting... I find combat kinda boring. Yes, there's variety and it's not monotone, and I would have loved it 10 years ago, but now... it just doesn't have that spark for me. However, that's what the group is playing so that will be my RPG for the near future. (I don't hate it. I'm just not in love with it.)

I sometimes think about the concept of NVRPGs (NonViolent RPGs), but I'm not too knowledgeable regarding the RPG scene. From lurking I can recognize some RPGs -- Apocalypse World, Fiasco, etc. But my experience has been limited to Pathfinder. So if I try to come up with what I think is an NVRPG I draw a blank.

First question-- Can you list RPGs that are fun and have no violence? And I mean ZERO violence. Games with a "little violence" don't count. But, games where violence is entirely optional so that it can be fully sidestepped do count. BTW, horror genre is out no matter what.

Second question-- Have you (members of this forum) done some research towards NVRPGs or the like? I think it would be nice to set up a contest, maybe like that Chef contest, but geared towards development of NVRPGs or systems. What do you think of it?

Third question-- I've been lurking for a long time, and I remember at some point someone said that they had spent years having fun without combat. (Those weren't the exact words, just similar.) If you have experience with NVRPGs (lots or little), can you please describe your experiences?

A final note-- I don't post often but I'll be lurking. If I don't post it doesn't mean I've left, just that I'm not compelled to post yet. However, I will post if someone asks me to.

Thank you.

    \n To answer your question in short: yes.

    Clover is about a five-year old girl and her friends.
    GxB is about high school dating.
    Breaking the Ice is about adults dating.
    Mars Colony is about political crises on Mars.

    I'm leaving off games where you can avoid violence with effort (Like Dogs in the Vineyard or Drifter's Escape), and trying to concentrate on games where violence is just not really the point at all. The reason I'm doing this is the "can be avoided with effort" games are way too many to list.

    I'd love to see a contest. More games like this are better.

    My experiences are... pretty much like any other role-playing game? I guess I don't see this as a significant category divide, much in the way I don't divide "novels with violence" from "novels without violence."

    \nThe Quiet Year has complete potential to be non-violent. There's struggle, but if none of the players actively try to put a violent scenario into effect, it doesn't have to be that way (the struggle can be against stuff like scarcity and the forces of nature bearing down on the community). I haven't played a game yet where we haven't had violence of some kind, but from the few times I've facilitated it, I see no reason that violence would be necessary, and it's not really a huge theme of the game.

    \nGolden Sky Stories is a Japanese RPG, self-described as "heartwarming roleplaying", about animals that can take human form and help people out. (The English translation isn't finished yet, but there are some previews you can download.)

    Do: Pilgrims Of The Flying Temple is a "slapstick fantasy storytelling game about helping people and getting into trouble." The game is freeform enough that while you could narrate violent events (probably in a silly cartoonish way, given the game's theme) it's definitely not necessary, and a group could easily agree ahead of time to skip violence.

    In fact that applies to a lot of story-games \u2014 while there are generally rules for handling inter-player conflicts, they aren't specific enough to require that those conflicts be violent, so they could just as easily be applied to arguments or persuasion or chases or whatever. It just depends on how you set up the scenario and how the GM chooses to set up the game and the group chooses to play it.

    \n Thinking about this further \u2014 violence is a form of physical conflict. As in other media, it can be played as light or dark. Emotional or social conflict is treated as a form of combat in some games (e.g. Burning Wheel) and can also be made light or dark. Imagine an RPG based on "Who's Afraid Of Virginia Woolf".

    What makes these get dark, it seems, is a recognition that conflicts can be pushed beyond limits and that people can be severely hurt. So, what if you had a game where the mechanics enforced that conflicts always have limits and that they always have to end in some kind of consensus. The obstacle becomes not dealing damage to an enemy until it succumbs, but finding a way to resolve the situation so everyone's satisfied. (Which could easily devolve into stalemate, but maybe the rules could have some way to avoid that?)

    This sounds like it could become the ultimate clich\xe9 hippie game ("OK, so we're sitting in a circle with the goblins and sharing our feelings about how the speciesist culture we live in forces us into cycles of oppression\u2026") but if it avoided that it could be pretty cool. Is there any precedent for this?

    \nPrimetime Adventures (a modern classic) is about making an ideal TV series. Thus, it only involves violence if the players want a violent series \u2014 romance, comedy, etc. are all viable options.

    A game of Microscope where you put "violent conflict" in the "NO" column of the Palette would be an interesting one indeed.

    What about a relationship-game such as Doubt? Or It's Complicated?

    Personally, I'm being sort of hazy having to list "non violent" RPGs not because there aren't (there are many!) but, rather, because I stopped thinking in terms of violent vs. non-violent years ago, as soon as the staple of my role-playing wasn't violence-fetish games anymore. I recognize the fetish for violence is still prevalent in commercial RPGs (cue the juvenile male audience they're mostly marketed to, or the older-but-nostalgic geek audience)\u2026 But, for a number of years, I've been playing mostly historical, science-fiction or relationship-focused games, where violence does happen sometimes but is not a primary focus of play. For some historical games, violence might shape a huge part of their background (I'm thinking i.e. Grey Ranks or Montsegur 1244), but murder still doesn't need happen on-screen frequently, or at all.

    \n I've designed a number of nonviolent games: Ribbon Drive, Keep It Sunny, and some might argue The Quiet Year.

    Ben Lehman has written a number of nonviolent games: Clover, Hot Guys Making Out, and On the Ecology of the Mud Dragon.

    Emily Care has written a number of nonviolent games: Breaking the Ice, Shooting the Moon, Under My Skin, and Sign in Stranger.

    Jackson Tegu has written a number of nonviolent games: Silver & White, Anna the Miller's Daughter, and Slammin'. He's also written a game about processing your real-world anger, and that game is called Temper.

    Filamena Young wrote Flatpack: Fix the Future.
    Paul Czege and Michele Gelli created Bacchanalia.
    Tim Koppang wrote Mars Colony.
    Ben Robbins wrote Microscope.
    Daniel Wood wrote My Daughter, the Queen of France.

    Most of the games I play these days are not about combat*, and the violence involved is of a deeply personal or a deeply institutional nature. There's lots of options out there!

    (*with the exception of Apocalypse World. Yo, I stole a jeep from some thugs this evening!)

    \n It think its important to breakdown the difference between violence, and combat.

    Physical aggression could be part of a tense emotional exchange:

    Your character is addicted to the mystical drug quicksilver, and my character is just screaming at you to get your shit together, and physically grabs you by the front of your blouse to pull you in face to face.

    As apposed to:

    As the goblin marauders approach, we raise our shields, and lower our spears, ready to receive their charge!

    A lot of games have whole systems for dealing with a particular paradigm of interpersonal conflict, where characters set-to in tactical battles. There are some games that don't have combat systems, but will still have conflict, and that conflict may or may not be violent. I always think that RPG style combat is its own thing and not really approaching violence thematically, does that make sense?

    My Question to the OP is are you looking for a game without combat, or without physical violence?

    But an example of an action game without direct violence by the player characters:

    The Cubicle 7 game: Doctor who: adventures in time and space has a "conflict" system but it deals mainly with the characters doing clever things or running away! It's exciting and action packed but is built on the concept that violence is always the worst strategy, and being quick and clever is much more important, (Quick Doctor! We need to reverse the polarity!)
