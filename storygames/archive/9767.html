    \n So, you're playing an Anchor, and there's really two stances you can have as a player- you want the Anchor and the pilot to work out, fall in love and be happy, or you don't want the Anchor and the pilot to work out, fall in love and be happy.

    If it's the former, you're job is now to be the bearer of bad news and talk them through the shithole that is their job. Heartbreaking.

    If it's the latter, you have all the tools to engineer that little tragedy. Horrible.

    Either way, fun.

    It's interesting to watch how players use their Anchors as play continues, especially if their position changes on whether they want that relationship to go or fall. Especially if there's petty jealousy dramas going on, which, is almost inevitable because you've got a bunch of youth under immense stress living in a near Lord of the Flies situation. Heroism, then, is overcoming the worst, and petty within humanity, and still finding a way to get the job done.

In Bliss Stage, you're meant to: It's directed enough so that you don't end up crying in a corner, but it still delivers a gut punch.     \n Ok, Anchors.

    I assume your "profound and awful way" is that they are in control of narration during a mission scene. If that's not it, please clarify the profound awfulness.

    So, in running a mission, there's only so many dice to distribute. Losing control of the dream is a nasty, nasty thing. So is failing a mission goal, or damaging a relationship.

    The authority the anchor has is to make things seem like they're going awesome, except that we don't really know what's actually going on until the dice hit the table, and we see what's safe and what's risked. So it's a little bit of a shell game. The anchor is guiding, to the best they know how, the pilot towards the mission goals while keeping them sane and stable. Except that they might be wrong. In fact, they probably are wrong, because you can't get everything right.

    So in essence, the anchor is trying to lie so much and so well that everyone, including the anchor, falls for it. I think realizing this is the key to good anchor play. When you get characters in the fiction also realizing this, then you've really got gold.

    The names thing is there to help make the game immediate and visceral and creepy. It's actually creepy in two ways. One is if you pick the name of a crush, it's creepy as intended. If you wimp out and lie, then you spend the whole game with that lie, just like an anchor.

    I'm pretty sure the incest rule is there because in playtest, people kept asking, and Ben kept having to say "No, you can't get more intimate than sex. Even if it's your sister." Creepy, but for a kinda different reason...

    James

    \n I should add a technical point: The "no bonus for incest" rule is mechanical, not fictional. The real rule is actually "no intimacy 6 relationships." Intimacy 6 relationships are more powerful than I wanted in the game. You can have all the incest you want but you don't get more powerful than 5 intimacy.

    Why it's there: Incest comes up a lot in Bliss Stage games (not necessarily "actually" happening in the fiction, but people talk about it, think about it, and sometimes plan about it.) And the natural interpretation (6 intimacy) is non-fun. So there has to be a rule.

    As for anchor names, if your entire group is really squeamish*, and your name is Shreyas, use the default names but change them to a the gender of someone you had a crush on in high school. Change like: Nathan to Nathalia, Jenny to James, etc. Consider that errata.

    The actual role of anchors in the game? Other people explain better than I do.

    yrs--
    --Ben

    * It occurs to me that if you're really sqeamish about personal revelation, you're better off playing a game which is not Bliss Stage. But maybe you're squeamish about personal revelation except via the mask of fiction. So okay.
