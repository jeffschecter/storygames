\n The thoughts in my brain didn't stop swirling last night, and I started to think of an old released game of mine called Matin\xe9. The game is said to be swashbuckling, and it's a game that made the players describe their actions, mostly by combining skills with each other. Someone could combine Close Combat with Acrobatics, but had to describe the action to include both skills. Anything went, Knowledge: taverns could be combined with anything, as long as the players described the actions. It was a "roll first, describe later" kind of game.

I never had professions or classes in that game. Instead, I tried to make the players think in specific patterns by providing a life path so they could think up a profession for their characters. It went well when it was fantasy but when I did conversions to other types of games, like one with an apocalyptic setting, people had a harder time to understand what they could play.

So my first draft yesterday to change the skills into professions.

    \n The setting takes place in a huge fantasy city, where the buildings are so tall the lower regions are set in dusk, sometimes darkness. People walk around on bridges between the towers in the sky, messengers fly reptiles, zeppelins are pumping out white clouds from the gas they are spending, while on the surface - in the darkness - lurks streetmages, dubious bands, city whisperers, and common folks.

    PHYSIQUE (brawling, athletics, etc.)
    - Archer
    - Coachman (can drive/ride anything really)
    - Hiresword

    DEXTERITY (balance, swiftness, sneaking, etc.)
    - Burglar
    - Jester
    - Thief (alt. pickpocket)

    SAGACITY (general knowledge about the city)
    - Constructor (Leonardo da Vinci / McGyver)
    - Healer
    - Scholar

    BLISS (anything based on luck. Also includes first appearance.)
    - Alchemist
    - Believer
    - Mage

    VIGILANCE (using senses, reading situations, etc.)
    - Charlatan
    - Diplomat
    - Streetfinder

    Five attributes where each one got three professions. The attributes are broad and should cover what adventurers should be able to do or know. I google translated them so the words can be a little bit off.

    The professions are meant to be broad as well, where the player can use any profession for any roll, as long as they can describe it. They include theoretic knowledge, practical use, and opens up for knowing people in the city that can relate to that profession.

    \n The trouble I'm having is that, in the old version, I had skills that were broad but could be narrowed by combining them with other skills. You could fight with all kinds of weapons, but if you combined it with Animal Wrangling, it made you into an individual. That's how I made the players describe their actions, and every roll they did told something about who they are.

    I like the basic idea of having skills be professions which helps the players to understand what they can do in the city. There are more professions than what the players can choose from, but these are relevant to the scenarios they are about to play. But can I use the same thinking of combining skills (now professions)? Should I add something else to the mix, like character traits, so the players need to take those into account when rolling?

    Perhaps I should just shut up, not worry, and playtest instead? But when I look at these professions, I cannot see any good combinations. I want the players to be creative in their descriptions, and it's possible that these design ideas are bad for what I want to accomplish. (I should perhaps not base each profession on an attribute, but leave the players to do the combination themselves.)

    \n I can see lots of fruitful combinations, but perhaps you are right that it isn't as easy to do as with "skills".

    However, this is a presentation/thematic issue, not a design issue. Repackage the descriptions and get the effect you want.

    For instance, try describing the professions as backgrounds ("Raised by wolves"), or the skills as verbs ("Fooling the unwary"). That kind of phrasing might lead to better descriptions or more versatility.
