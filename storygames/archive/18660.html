\n I've been tossing some ideas around in my head for a spy game for a couple of months. Bond-flavour, that is; globe-trotting, explosions, nice cars and nicer women, that sort of stuff.

Before I commit to developing it into a full game, I'd like to have a look at what already exists in that space. If there's something that already scratches a lot of the same itches, I'd rather know about it now - even if I decide that it's different enough to what I'm thinking about, maybe there's some insights in there that might be helpful.

So. Spy games. What's out there? What do you guys recommend? What's good-but-dated? What's still a good time?

(And, bonus question: is there anything you're looking for in a spy game that's never quite come together?)

    \n I don't know too much about it, but I played Spione (by Ron Edwards) at Go Play NW this year. It's a game about spies in Cold War Berlin (with the exact decade being randomly generated). It's a GMless game that is categorically NOT about globe-trotting, explosions, nice cars and nicer women, instead being about the very cold harsh realities of being a spy during the Cold War while also trying to maintain relationships as a human being. It was intense and fun, and its got a playing card based mechanic for conflicts and stuff. One mechanic in particular keeps it quite grounded and that was pretty serious and interesting. If you care I'll explain it, but it does the opposite of what you're talking about so it probably doesn't matter.
    So, uh, not at all the sort of spy game you're talking about, but still a member of the spy game genre and a cool thing.

    What I'm curious about from your game is how you'd handle the crazy gadgetry. That's where my interests tend to lie in the over-the-top spy genre.

    \n Well, Spycraft D20, right? It covers a pretty wide range of approaches to spy stories, but definitely favors the action/explosion material.

    Going back to the days of yore, there's James Bond 007, from the tail-end of role-playing's first major innovative phase. On the one hand you have some pretty complicated tables and multiplication and stuff to resolve events, but on the other, you have points players can spend to add significantly to the fictional situation above and beyond the character.

    I guess my main recommendations would be GURPS: Espionage and GURPS: Covert Ops. They're both available very reasonably priced at Steve Jackson Games in PDF.

    (thanks for the Spione plug! you're right, not in the zone of the request, but it was nice)

    \n Agents of S.W.I.N.G. is a FATE interpretation that is pretty close to a Bond-meets-Austin-Powers flavour. If your group is all on the same page with the setting and flavour, it'd be really straightforward to use Fate Core or FAE to do Bond-style games.

    Agents of Oblivion is an espionagey setting for Savage Worlds - the Savage Worlds rules fit really well for high-action spy capers, where the PCs are powerful but still vulnerable to, eg, being shot at.

    I hear lots of good things about Night's Black Agents - it's espionage vs. Vampires, but I've seen AP reports of people playing it as a straight thriller.

    \n In my library there are two primary references for doing the classic spy set up:

    Blowback \u2013 inspired by one of the greater offbeat entries in the genre, it focuses on the side effects of your actions on those who support you (i.e. your family, your friends, your business partners, what have you). It uses A sort of GM-driven Mission structure to help guide what's going on, but the real meat of the system is in the way that it lets the players interact through the medium of conflicts between characters. The emotional state of the agents in question is extremely important.

    Killshot\u2013 something like Blowback above, but with agents who are on the inside still supported by the agency such as it is.. If you're interested in the nitty-gritty of going about planning and then executing an operation with defined goals and all the ways that such things can go wrong along the way, this game is for you. Planning is rewarded, and well it is. Plots can sometimes end up seeming a little labyrinth-like but reflect the interests of the players very nicely.

    Covenant \u2013 the dark horse runner in this particular pack, it's been on my shelves for quite a while as one of the front runners in the new wave of indie design RPGs. Early on in setting up, the players collectively create the agency for which they work and what their positions therein are. A relationship map gets built as part of this process, and through filling that out and studying it between moments of excitement, tons of inspiration falls out. This is the game you want to run if the characters may not necessarily be street agents but could have interactions both within and outside the agency. (If you pressed me, I might say that it's the best of the lot; the mechanics and simple die pool which drives the engine between players is quite good indeed.)

    Getting your hands on any of these things should be pretty easy; one of the great advantages of living on the Internet is that the smart producers never go out of print. If you can only try one of these, make it Covenant. You won't be sorry.

    \n I dont know whats out there, but I do know what wanted to see out there:

    Probe Team Compromised

    A spy-thriller rpg based on Sid Meier Alpha Centauri' PC game universe "Probe Teams" (for those who dont know, they are the "spy unit" of the game - Alpha Centauri is a Civilzation sucessor - that use spy-psych-meme-bio-hacking techniques for infiltrating and sabotaging the different political factions). The PCs would work together for their mother Faction trying to sabotage the others, and each faction would confer wildly different assets, resources and psychological profiles for the team, that could reflect the factions in-game atributes (Gaians = hippie psychics mind-worm lovers).

    Give it a cool thematic-narrative system in the veins of Mountain Witch, Cold City and Lady Blackbird, where the players work together but also against each other for accomplishing their own secret agendas, and trust and betrayal come to the fore. Bonus if they have secret agenda cards in the vein of Mountain Witch Fates and one of those is "deserting with a faction SECRET PROJECT", ala Datajack "Cinder" Rose in Alien Crossfire. Also give it a stylish minimalist presentation ala Lady Blackbird or Apocalypse World and I would toss lots of money in it.

    Or do it in the Vampire splatbooks fashion..


    1. Core book describing the setting and all factions, with a built-in Probe Team "inter-faction" campaign.

    2. 7 "Splat books", each describing a faction individually, with campaigns thematicaly related to it (Believer Inquisitors, Gaian Eco-terrorists, Hive Thought-Police, Spartan Commandos, etc). And maybe even describing the Alien Crossfire sub-factions (eg: Morganite Sourcebook with stuff on the Data Angels; Gaians Sourcebook with stuff on the Prophet; Academician Sourcebook would contain stuff on the Cybernetic Consciousness; etc).

    3. release the "Aliens book", describing the Progenitors and its sub-factions Caretakers and Usurpers. And a campaign for pitting all other factions against the aliens (so one could have a crackshot team of an Spartan Commando, a Believer Inquisitor, a Gaian Eco-terrorist and a Data Angel Hacker side by side doing black ops against the aliens).

    Oh man, I would kill for such a game.

    \n Mercenaries, Spies & Private Eyes (MSPE) is based on a modified version of the Tunnels & Trolls system--known for its fast and deadly firearm combat.

    I started working on a spy game called Cold Friends. It never really got very far, it was aimed at recreating something like The Spy Who Came In From The World rather than say a James Bond film.

    In COLD FRIENDS the system exists not simply to describe an individual's traits, but rather to highlight those motives and methods which relate to interactions between people. Any such system has to make generalizations, as well as trying to find a balance between accurate portrayals and a usable number of personalities. The resolutions here focuses instead more on the \u2018why and how\u2019 in which actions are performed, rather than simply the core attributes that allow you to accomplish them.

    About half of the variations in human behavior are defined by genetic factors. However, the personality itself is built up of two completely different traits; type and temperament. Recruitment for a field operative into and their ultimate stayability while in the spy trade must be sanctioned with these factors in mind. There are four monikers used to determine the motives of all operatives in COLD FRIENDS, these are: [DESIRE] [FEAR] [GRIEF] [RAGE].

    Your character will begin play with one point for each MOTIVE.

    Whenever you are confronted with a problem and the outcome is not automatically successful, you'll need to roll the dice. You'll solve each problem first by choosing a WHY motive. Your character will use a particular motive based on their strongest behavioral and/or emotional state. For example, leading an assault on an enemy safe-house could be done for a number of reasons: a fellow operative was just assassinated, you could use GRIEF; they are torturing a friend, go with RAGE; first time in the field, maybe it's FEAR; or perhaps it's for selfish reasons and you pick DESIRE.

    Now here is where it gets real interesting. To have a better chance at success you'll want to bump up a motive, but to do so you'll have to BURN a point from one of your STAY scores. These stay scores all start at four and are: [COMPOSURE] [HOPE] [LOYALTY] [TRUST]. If any of these drop to zero, then you CRASH. You don't want to crash.

    Each SUCCESS ROLL [SR] is made up of two parts, [CLEARANCE] and [NEED-TO-KNOW]. Roll 4D4 and first compare the numbers to your MOTIVE. The higher your motive score, the easier it is to get 'clear'. Add up the clearances you get and if it meets or beats the first SR number, then you been cleared. Then you can move on to need-to-know.

    First SR Number

    1 Clearance = Restricted [least difficult]
    2 Clearances = Confidential
    3 Clearances = Secret
    4 Clearances = Top Secret [most difficult]

    NEED-TO-KNOW is the second part of the [SR] and this encompassed by the HOW question. Total your CLEARANCE scores and add it to your METHOD used. These are: [LIE] [CHEAT] [STEAL] [HARM] [SEDUCE] [COERCE] [DEFRAUD] [SABOTAGE] and will each have a range of 1 to 8. If you equal or exceed the second SR number then you've succeeded.

    You can enhance a METHOD with an ENDORSEMENT. These are special qualities with a narrow focus and allow you to increase the THRESHOLD of your success.
