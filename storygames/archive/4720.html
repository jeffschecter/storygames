So, this is a thread to talk about how judging will work for The Elaborate Ploy design contest. I wanted to let those who will be creating entries have a say in how their work is evaluated and shared.

First of all, as the contest organizer I will say:
a.) I am 100% fine with doing all the judging myself
b.) I am 100% fine with putting the judging to a small judging panel, which may or may not include me
c.) I am 100% fine with doing peer-based judging

That said, here's what I hold sacred for this contest:
a.) There will be a judging process, and winners declared. I realize some people think that the competition element isn't important, but I think that it is, for this.
b.) I want to award those creating sound, thoughtful designs. There's innovative, cutting-edge, alternative ideas... and then there's ridiculous pipe dreams. I want the contest to reward the former but not the latter.
c.) I would like feedback to be given not just as a general review, and not just as a score. I want it to critically engage weak and strong points in the entry. I don't think this needs to be extensive, but I'd like it to exist. That way, when someone gets their reviews back, they know what their judges thought was unrealistic, overdone, powerful, innovative, etc, etc.


So, that all said... Who has any ideas about how judging should work?

    Posted By: Graham WAny score will be subjective, anyway, so why pretend at objectivity? (With respect to Ryan and Jared, of course).
    \nWhen I'm doing these kinds of things, I find having criteria to use helps keep me honest.

    I love giant robots - if you write a marketing strategy for a game about giant robots I'm inclined to like it (or conversely, judge it harshly against our rich robot culture). But if I have some previously established criteria, I find it's easier to put myself in the "disinterested third person" mindset that, while still subjective, focuses my attention at the stuff that I should be judging. That also helps make my comments on the way out more valuable to the person being evaluated.
