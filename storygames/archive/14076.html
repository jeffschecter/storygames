\n I finally had enough material together and enough time to create this:

Second Edition Quickstart
Conflict Cards (ideally print two sets per player)

Setting material has been shrunk somewhat to give the new rules ideas enough space to shine. If you want to flip back to the 1st edition, it's on 1km1kt. If you've played the 1st edition already, the second edition expands the effectiveness of Core Values, rewinds the default start point for the game, widens the expected PC base beyond the Patent Office (not sure how I feel about that yet), and we're trying a new diceless system. Many other minor alterations.

I would love to get comments, both from folks who've played before and folks who haven't.

edit: changed the conflict cards so they use less ink.

    \n Yes, eventually. My current job is a six-day-a-week, 10-12-hour-a-day job on average, so it may be a while (depending on how similar next year's job is).

    The second edition arose from some things (Neuroform, Wonder, Terror) that are evolutions of the game from the wiki site, some things (the diceless system) that have been design goals all along, and some things (the "existentialism" page) that were me pondering on the nature of reality. Right now it's definitely still in the early stages, and needs some playtesting, but there was enough structure for it all to hang together. Let me know your impressions if you get a chance!

    \n Wilmer: The 2nd edition is much more permissive in terms of the types of character you can play without nerfing yourself (e.g. Replicants). It's also much more tolerant of the "I kill myself so I can be revived later" strategy. Body-switching stuff (like "sleeves" in Eclipse Phase) will come in during the next build, but it isn't there right now. The game's primary balance of in-game power vs. metagame power is still there. If you play someone who's enhanced in multiple ways and has thousands of years of expertise, you'll pay for it in terms of your ability to influence the plot in out-of-game ways. Of course, you're also so capable that you won't need to most of the time, while the Old-Worlder with no enhancements and ordinary training will need to use that plot power a lot. Are there any other ways that you're thinking of?

    \n I'll see if I can explain.

    You line up your Brute Force / String card with his Avoid/Meta card. (Imagine that you're across the table and each of you can read your own card. Arrows point to dots on your opponent's card.)

    Your score is 4 (String) + 1 (Brute Force works well with your own Stringtech) = 5.
    His score is 2 (Meta) + 2 (His Avoid beats your BF) = 4.
    You win this bout.

    When you're done with these cards, each of you shifts them to your own left, flips over the face-down cards, and puts a new set face-down.

    Next bout:
    Your score is 4 (String) + 1 (for following BF with Avoid) = 5.
    Your opponent gets 4 (Nano) + 1 (for following Avoid with Examine) + 1 (for pairing Nano with Examine) 2 (for Examine working well against your Stringtech) = 8
    He wins this bout.
