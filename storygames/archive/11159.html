\n I was looking over at the thread "Show me the game you want" and was thinking about the games people want. Some of the images reminded me of games I wanted to play IN, but I decided would never happen. I think they're games that would require the GM to be devilishly clever. There were two pictures in particular that made me think of this.
1) The picture of 24 (tv show)
2) The picture of the House/Gordan Freeman hybrid (one is from a tv show and one is from a video game)

To me, these games would require complex problem solving with intense, intricate plots.

Does anyone else feel like their ideal game requires a devilishly clever GM? Provided you don't have a devishly clever GM handy (perhaps in your bag of holding), do you think there's any way to get these games off the ground?

    \n The game I want more than anything is a sort of "Werewolf" variation in which players are con artists trying to scam either other players or NPCs. I actually started to think about possible rules for this kind of game, till I realized that it'd require a GM who could either play such NPCs in a way that would make them neither too easy, nor too difficult to deceive, or else who could task PCs with playing these sorts of stooges in a fun and effective manner. A total genius, in other words.

    \n If what interests you is the "Problem Solving" aspect then ignore what I'm about to say. If what interests you is the idea of a layered and intricate plot then I suggest looking at both Spione and Dirty Secrets.

    Spione is GMless and constructs a Cold War-era John LeCarre style spy drama in Berlin. There is no prep. The whole thing happens in play.

    Dirty Secrets is a GM-inverse setup where one player is a noir-style Investigator looking into crimes. Again, no prep, the solution is generated through play.

    These games don't need a devilishly clever GM because they harness the imaginative power of the group as whole. Neither of these would give you 24 out of the box but the ideas in them might be adaptable into something you could use.

    But again if *actually* "solving problems" is part of the appeal these games won't give you that.

    \nI don't know if my contribution(s) to the "show me" thread represent my ideal game, but whatever the case they wouldn't require a devilishly clever GM so much as they would need one steeped in a particular kind of fantasy and able to create a feedback loop with his/her players powerful enough to reach the requisite level of Awesome Cool.

    Just take a look at my most recent addition to the thread and a Manowar album cover I posted to another thread to see what I'm talking about. Heroic fantasy but rendered in splashy colors with mighty-thewed warriors, nubile women and hordes of inferior bad guys to slay. The GM for this kind of thing has to be willing to just let things happen as they should and not muck it up with commentary. Like I said, feed creative energy into the loop and let it grow stronger and stronger until the tone is just right.

    \n It seems to me that you would find more fertile ground for a very intricate, detailed plot with a GM-less game. A GM has the limitation of however clever a single person can get, whereas a GM-less game can leverage the cleverness of the whole group, with the added impact of how things that seem obvious to one person might seem like a genius twist to everyone else.

    Posted By: Accounting for TasteI often wonder how you guys manage to keep your GM-less plots from being intricate, detailed, nearly-incoherent messes
    We've been adapting the Jeep technique "fate play": early in the game, once we've established a little color, we flash-forward and do a scene set "in the future", which establishes a story state that we know must happen. Then, we do scenes leading up to that, and since we can see the goalposts, it's easy to avoid getting lost, even as we pile on intrigues and intricacies. Also, obviously, what Jason said.
