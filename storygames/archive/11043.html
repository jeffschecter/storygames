    \n Personally I like Burning Wheel for that stuff. I find the Duel of Wits to be an interesting and exciting way to resolve important debates and arguements, while simple social skill tests take care of the rest.

    Also, Belief driven characters help out too.

    In fact, one of the demo scenarios on the BW website is based around an Elven diplomatic envoy arriving to negotiate with a newly crowned Dwarven king. Of course, it often ends in violence, but the social intrigue often comes pretty heavy too.

    That said, there's LOTS of other systems out there that could do about the same thing. I can second what Wilhelm said about Housed of the Blooded.

    Edit: Oh yeah, BW stories are heavily GM guided, but they are based on the characters perusing their Beliefs in play. Players can also use "wise" skills like "court gossip-wise" to narrate facts about the setting and situation. For example, a successful court gossip-wise test could establish that "Duke Rolf has a secret lover amongst his kitchen staff," or something similar.

    Finally, since you want players to have greater influence on NPCs I should mention that Burning Wheel has a mechanic called "Circles" which represents a characters social connections and can be used to locate NPCs of the players choosing. This includes inventing new NPCs. Last BW session I played I had a character roll circles to find "an Elven spy who knows if the Sultan is rearming the north eastern mountain fortresses." He failed the roll, which means that the Elven spy (who did not previously exist) now knows the PC is looking for him and is suspicious of it. Basically, (at the GM's discression) the test can indicate how helpful the NPC is and not if he exists or not.

    Does that sound something like what you are looking for?

    Posted By: AndyCan someone describe (or point a link to a detailed description) how Houses of the Blooded's social stuff works?
    Social stuff is really a risk like any other risk. So, if its about persuading you we're talking a Beauty risk.
    \n
      \n
    • We build our pools, invoking and tagging aspects to get more dice, spending style points, etc.
      • \n
    • We each decide how many dice to hold back to make declarations later.
      • \n
    • We roll and whoever rolls highest has Priviledge. They get to decide if they succeed or fail and what that looks like. They also decide who spends the first wager.
      • \n
    • Anyone who rolls under 10 does loses their wagers.
      • \n
    • Anyone who rolls over 10 but isn't the highest keeps half their wagers.
      • \n
    • The highest keeps all wagers if he rolled over 10
      • \n
    • Then we take turns making declarations to add to narration, essentially with "And..." statements or "But..." statements.
      • \n
    \nI think I have those mechanics right, but its been a while.

    Burning Wheel and HotB are both solid for this kind of gaming, IMHO.

    Posted By: David COne of my players is always trying to make characters wrapped in intrigue and social skills. The problem is, I've never played a game that handles that well. I'm also hoping to find a game that puts more control of the story and npcs into the player's hands. Does anybody have a good suggestion?
    \n
    ANY game can do this... you just need to allow and encourage it as GM, then use the rule system for any randomizing needs. Then be actively interested in doing the same with NPCs and following up with NPC reactions and "world" consequences for the choices both the PC and NPCs make to influence.
    It's called role-playing.

    To help understand HOW to Game Master for these sorts of things you could reference any of the dozens of motivational type books for business.

    My primary suggestion though is to build a world (and NPCs) that don't "actively" plot in terms of your story need... Build NPCs and world events that set the PCs (the "heroes" of the story) into conflict but not "plots". Give the world events and NPC actions/reactions the type of choices that are natural to THEIR personality. I try to build NPC personalities in-that I never "need" to make a plot. I simply need to interpret their choice and role-play it. Same with the world events. Make the choice on the direction the story will take - then use the NPCs and world environment to naturally express those events through their established personalities or style.
    In other words - build your world so that it runs the intrigue and social events FOR you. Let the NPCs run themselves. Once you have established the style of personality, you can just sit back and react to the PCs choices naturally. For having the NPCs (or world) make active choices (for what PCs will learn that's happening) listen to what the PLAYERS are asking or discussing regarding the situation or NPC, then "react" actively....
    Example: if my players are talking casually about an NPC and they question "Why is he doing that, do you think he will...?" THEN you make the choice based on the NPC's established personality.

    In a strange way...
    To get great intrigue and political situations I build the NPC and let THEM run me.


    -kev-

    \n Honestly, I would go with In A Wicked Age.

    See, social intrigue and manipulation involves threats that, usually, don't come to pass, right? You don't want to outright kill or ruin someone (most of the time), you want them to do something specific, to help with your ambitions. You can either do this with incentives, or you can threaten to do something to them, even if the threat is completely veiled.

    IAWA does this wonderfully with threats. You have a conflict, someone wins, and they say: Do x for me, or I'll damage your dice, which makes it less likely you'll get what you want next time. That way you can constantly manipulate people to get the fictional outcome you want, without ever actually getting your hands dirty. (I once made a quick one-sheet system system based on IAWA (and Sorcerer and others) that you can check out here.) I'd allow "holding" the threat, though, until the negotiated deed is done. Because in intrigue, unlike in sword and sorcery, you don't do everything on the spot, and broken promises have consequences.

    There's also this game that I'm developing, but it's not quite ready for testing yet. Still, that'll feature intrigue as a vital part.

    \n My husband ran Reign for quite some time, and while it is designed so that the Players can all be the decision makers in some kind of organization representing a country, it doesn't necessarily mean the plots will lead you to political intrigue. It will mean that it'll be like running a country, at least, that's how we played it. The social rules weren't really anything related to interpersonal intrigue, but rather the rolls your company will make toward the bigger outcome. Companies (countries, organizations) become NPCs, so each Company has to be detailed in stats by you, the GM. That, my husband found, was pretty time consuming. But, it is awesome to play a game on that scale.

    If you're looking for more interpersonal drama and politicking, I would go for Legend of the Five Rings. Japanese cultural etiquette galore, as far as the setting and genre is concerned. I hear that other BW game does it well too, falling blossoms whatnot, and burning sands. Also, Spirit of the Century, as noted above, has a social system that works exactly like a combat system, you have a certain amount of hit points allocated to social stats that you can take before you lose the argument. You can use aspects and effectively "stunt" your argument in really fun ways.
