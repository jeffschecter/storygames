So in the car on the way to the airport last Friday, I was telling Meredith about how Galactic allows for the death of primary characters, and she wasn't too keen on it, and I've been thinking about it on and off for the last week. I got only the briefest opportunity to talk to Vincent about it on Sunday, but it wasn't really much of a discussion, what with there being FIRE to distract us, fire with its flame and smoke and stuff. And also a looming thunderstorm.

So, I remember once someone saying there's fates worse than death for characters, and I'm wondering what happens to play when you allow for it to happen vs. when you say it's not possible.

I'm not expecting there to be a specific list, but the possibilities are really holding my attention right now.

I'll give you an example: In Galactic, if the captain dies, the player picks a supporting character to sorta 'pick up the banner' and become the new captain. So if I'm playing Iona and Iona dies, I pick maybe Paco, who was previously played by John, and John creates a new crewmember to replace Paco.

And that's pretty cool, I think.

But the game would be totally different if the captain can't die. That option makes me think, kinda, of the Big Red One, where they keep on going and all their squadmates keep getting killed. You'd make it to the endgame and if you won, you'd have to look back on a complete epic's worth of hard decisions and loss and stuff. Ironically, it's darker than allowing for main character death.

And that's pretty cool as well.

So players and designers: what do you like or dislike about character death (or just removal from the game)? When designing a game, what makes you decide on death vs. not death? When in play have you had character death that was just kind of dull and unsatisfying? When was it awesome?

    Character Death is part of the reason I'm going a little old school for my next game, and actually using the Shadowrun Rules* - I think it's a powerful and useful thing so long as everyone knows it's on the table.

    I got a lot of love of death back in my Rolemaster days. Realistically, we didn't actually end up dying any more than we did in, say, D&D, but there was always more of a sense of death being on the line. The constant awareness that a critical could go just-so, and we could end up with our teeth driven into our brain. Since we were definately all about strong character identification, it completely jacked up the tension, which in turn, jacked up the fun for us.

    I sort of moved past that, and on into games where death is somethign that just doesn't happen. "Fates worth than death" certainly keep things interesting, especially because they keep the GM challenged, but the depend on a certain amount of infrastructure. If you have players who are willing to invest their characters in something, then you still get tasty pain, but that really ends up undercutting certain sorts of grim and gritty play.

    So, as I go back to cyberpunk, which I want to be grim and gritty, I'm going crunchier, and putting death on the table, in hopes of recapturing some of that tension.

    So, having framed all that, I would throw onto the list:

    Why Allow Death?
    Pro
    * Risk forces characters to consider wider array of options to solve problems.
    * Without Death, players can turtle, or otherwise keep at arms length form enagement.
    * Risk of change (from character loss) keeps everyone flexible and on their toes.
    * Surviving can be a valid victory

    Con
    * Risk paralyzes players, or causes the planning to action ratio to get completely skewed.
    * Death is Sad, and paperwork can be hard.
    * Can totally derail future plans
    * Requires extra layer of rules padding to keep death from being _too_ common.
    * Most GM-abusable thing evar.


    There's more, but I figured it worth starting somewhere.

    -Rob D.

    I think a protagonist dying is an action like any other: it's a statement a player can make.

    In some cases (like in Dogs), you're saying "My dude is willing to risk his life on this."

    In some cases (like in Shock:), you're saying "My Protag dying is a way to make a final point."

If you get a spectacularly unlucky series of rolls and die from a fistfight in Dogs. I love seeing my dude in a pool of his own blood for reasons that are good or bad, but never meaningless.     So players and designers: what do you like or dislike about character death (or just removal from the game)? When designing a game, what makes you decide on death vs. not death?

    I usually don't design for death. In FLFS, there is literally no way for any character to die, even if you want to, because there's always the possibility of somebody coming back in a later session. This parallels lots of period literature, where ostensible deaths turn out to be misunderstandings or fakes, and the supposedly-dead folks turn up as plot twists. Players can certainly abandon characters, even after they ostensibly die (blown out into the vaccum of spaaaace!), but there's nothing stopping them from picking the character back up later, or the GM from bringing said character back as an NPC (I woke up to the sound of a mad scientist gleefully clapping his hands, and found myself in a hollowed-out asteroid...).

    There are death rules in Agora, but I very well may take them out since they're tacked on and don't do anything really interesting. The only glimmer of possibility is promoting one of your lieutenants to a more leadery position, which I'm thinking as I write this that that ought to be the entire mechanic right there.

    One thing that I think is important to point out is that games often bind player authority tools (ie, stats and resources) to the character, and the only way to affect the fiction is through the character. Further, a lot of games mark the progress of the game on the character through XP, and you can look at the entire game as the process of building up and investing in the character. In games like these, death is really tricky to incorporate without making it gruelingly punishing. Even Dogs which lets you keep all your dice, just reassign them to new stats, is taking away the character that you invested your interest in over howevermany sessions you've played. On the other hand, games like PTA or Capes where player authority tools are almost completely divorced from the character can feature death with almost gleeful abandon; killing of a character loses the player nothing, since the focus is less on characters and more on other things (the series for PTA, the players for Capes).

    When in play have you had character death that was just kind of dull and unsatisfying? When was it awesome?

    When I was 14, my brother and I were playing Teenaged Mutant Ninja Turtles, specifically Mutants Down Under. We were flying zeppelins, and for some reason we decided to pit our characters against each other. I fired a missile at his zeppelin, scored a critical, and hit the crew compartment. Forever after, I kept reminding him that I had killed his character with a missile to the face. Which I actually felt pretty bad about.

    I don't think I've ever had an awesome character death (which may color the views I expressed above), but from a narrative framework, the noble death has always seemed like punking out to me, especially the revolutionaries who die for their cause of totally turning the world on its ear. Sure, they'll die to screw everything up, but what they won't do is live through the havoc that they've wreaked.

    Related to Rob is saying about old-school games, and nostalgia...

    One interesting thing I have found in modern setting games with high likelihood of random lethality like Shadowrun (I gm'ed that for years) is that the players will often plan far, far more than they do in other games. That is, if I say, as the GM through Mr. Johnson, "Your job is to steal the plans for the new McGuffin from Big Nippon Corp HQ", the players will spend a lot of time planning out the job, in order to minimize, as much as possible, the random elements (like that Machine Gun emplacement you didn't notice, or the razored mercs they brought in at the last 2nd) that are liable to get your character killed. The goal of the players is always to be the ambushers, not the ambushees; to be the people perpetrating massive violence, and not on the receiving end.

    This is quite different from both D&D style fantasy games (where the players rarely have the ability to aquire the information in order to plan, and where the characters (at least at higher levels) are almost immune to death simply through bad luck) and narrativist games (where character death is almost always the result of choice). In games like these, regardless of how long you have been playing standing in the wrong place when the grenade goes off will still kill your character, period. (I think their are similarities between this and the Riddle of Steel, but that is another thread).

    Now, for some, this is boring as all get out. Spending two hours of game time in surveillance, talking over methods of entry, who will go first and second, how much ammo to bring, etc. is just about as dead dull as you can get for some people. This is because it is incredibly non-narrative. You don't see Frank Sinatra and Peter Lawford slaving over plans of the Los Vegas casinos figuring out the plan to steal all that money in "Ocean's 11", you just see them present it, complete, and then execute it. It is also not character simulationism either; in my experience, almost all of this planning takes place "out of charater" on a player to player level. Very little, if any, "role-playing" occurs during this planning. Its enough to make some players stand up, slam their Coke cans down on the table, and yell "Enough talking, lets get to the action!"

    However, there is an enjoyment to this kind of play for many (myself included), and its totally gamist, not narrative. The enjoyment is in anticipating the problems, working through the possibilities, and coming up with a good way to do the job. As Col. Smith used to say; "I love it when a plan comes together!"

    An advantage to this kind of play with regards to character death is that, while it can happen, it almost always happens near the end of the session, when the plan is being executed, bullets are flying, etc., so that an individual player is usually not left characterless for that long. Also, at least in the games I ran of Shadowrun, the players had a lot of control over the level of risk their characters were going to face, through the planning process.

    Its something you have to be in the right mind set for to enjoy, no doubt about it. Its not "compelling, character-driven story" but if you are in the mood, it can be a regular riot.

I've sacrificed my life to save my buddies, I've shot myself in the head, I've had nano-bots eat my insides and my torso explode (The gore effects for that were cool!)

    I've died loads in that system, and some of the deaths have been some of my great roleplaying moments. There's nothing like charging a horde of Zombies single handedly, screaming to your mates "RUN! RUN FOR YOUR LIVES!!!" and then being ripped to pieces and eaten. (I was terrified, I really was. The ref was standing by with a bucket of blood and gore effects which, as the Zombies ate me, were splattered all over me, giving the horrible impression that my guts really were being ripped out. It ruled. It was the closest I'll get to actually being in Dawn of the Dead.)

    The reason is that 1) the player can control how it happens, which means you only die if as the player you feel it works for you. and 2) you immediately make a new character and just slot straight back into the action (or play an NPC for a while if you feel like it) So you don't miss out by dying.

    The game is pure pure Sim. No theme is really possible, nor are there any real gamist challenge aspects to it. (hell, there's nothing you can lose unless you want to) The fun is simply in the interactive enactment of a movie genre coupled with immersive character roleplay.       Edit: I think the key here is separating player from character resources. In Dogs, you get resources for dying, for instance. In Prime Time Adventures, everyone's character has the same number of resources and the players get XP. Imagine the D&D collective play I described above where you got the XP that each character would get, but you distribute it to the characters as the team sees fit. You can save up XP to buy new comrades at the Tavern at the level you can afford. The XP doesn't go away, even under TPK; the players have it. I realize this is a big hack and the XP might not come out right. It would take some playtesting: When a character dies, do you get back their XP or not? Maybe the XP bank loses the equivalent of one level of the dead character's? Maybe XP, once invested can't be regained? I dunno. But the point is to give the players input into the game with the mechanics in such a way that all rewards are to the players, and detriments are in Gamist terms: "We failed this mission, so we didn't get the 20000 XP we needed to get the Wizard we needed, so we need to do something else. Let's recruit an assassin gnome this time." The GM looks at the party as it exists, makes a legal adventure, and they're off again, with the PCs matching abilities to the GM.
