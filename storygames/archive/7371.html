    \n@Thomas: assuming you don't imply simply the absence of numeric values or randomizers...

    Well, in the late nineties the "new, mature and advanced way" of playing was ignoring the system and "just roleplay". Thus, what is called sometimes "ruleless", "systemless" or "freeform" play.

    If you boil it down enough, it simply is "ask the GM", or as others have put it "mother, may I?" if there actually IS a GM. Otherwise I think it's called "conch shell" or "pass the stick" narration.

    In the first case players tell the gm what they'd like their characters to do, and the GM decides (by whatever kind of judgement). In the second, people take turns telling a story, passing the "talking stick" round the table.

    Neither of the approaches is particularly attractive for my sensibilies, and I personally find both light years away from the kind of play and game I enjoy.

    According to that thread, Braunstein, apparently, so perhaps it goes back a bit further than the 90s.

    I'm also not entirely clear what you're asking, but Murder Mystery Partygames don't have a whole lot in the way of mechanical/numerical representations of the characters. There are fairly distinct procedures to follow, however, which I suppose is a mechanic of a different sort.

    Matrix Games primarily use verbal description, although some variants especially the wargame ones) will at least address some sort of quantified quality as appropriate ( for example, noting range of vision or travel times), although in a very brief way. It does however have a referee who regularly calls for engagement with a die-roll resolution system of a verfy minimalist sort.

    I understand that Prof Barker, the Tekumel guy, simply uses description with an occasional die throw where high=Good, Low=Bad and context determines how bad, how good, and what, interpreted by the GM. So, basically, the sort of thing Renatoram hates. Rumor has it he's been doing this for quite a long time now, certainly before the 90s, perhaps back to the 70s.

    Then of course there's the old kriegspiel and frei kriegspiel, ancestors to modern wargames, that were used for training and were all about "Mother may I?" as a mechanic. I believe Elliot Wilen has tracked down similar books by American military trainers as well.

    The upside, frankly, is ease of play, especially with new people who aren't gamers. It's also less to hide behind in terms of authority. The GM ( or another player) makes the call they think is right based on what they've understood or imagined, and that tends to get accepted or rejected straight out.

    A downside, and one not much mentioned, is that these don't leave much written record. I mean, after a brief description of how it works, the rules boil down to : Say what you want to do, have the GM make a judgement call, use dice or other randomizer as you feel appropriate.

    Posted By: ptevis
    To clarify: InA Penny For My Thoughts, any time the Traveler wants to describe what she did, she asks the Guides for two alternatives and chooses one. Is this a mechanic?
    Paul: I would call this conflict resolution or an interaction driver (or something). As it is resolved with no mechanical means, it would not be mechanics, no.

    Here is my take on how a classical roleplaying game may be broken down:
    - setting elements: society, politics, NPCs, locations, monsters, cosmology
    - plot elements: situations, conflicts, hooks, motivations
    - character elements: names, titles, tools, trades, manners, family & friends
    - leadership: invitation, trust, authority, group dynamics, atmosphere, starting and steering the discourse
    - material elements: character sheets, maps, dice and/or cards, table, room, costumes, food & drink
    - system elements: conflict mechanics (the use of dice and/or cards), interaction drivers, simulation tools, consequence meters

    The totality of this is what I call a game method. As you see the "mechanics" are but a minor part of it. Hope this clarifies.

    Mind you: in my view designers of classical roleplaying games have focused too much of their energies (and book space) on the system elements (an old complaint, yes), so if you are to heed this list of mine; please do so by discussing the other points in the list and how those elements may be used to make games without mechanics.

    Sidenote: I'm designing games with mechanics myself, so I have no quarrel with such design. Still: this thread is about unearthing another takes on game design. Levi got my intentions very well.

    \n Warning: half those games are experiments that I wrote for various contests. I'd stick to playing the finished ones and just glance over the others for ideas.

    Yeah, I haven't quite figured out the best way to categorize things in this design area yet. There's heavily-GM-centric stuff like Amber and the non-dice-centric parts of Engle Matrix Games. There's resource-distribution based games like Nobilis and Marvel Universe. There's formalized negotiation systems like Polaris, Mridangam, and Mist-Robed Gate. There's the lead-with-the-narrative stuff that Vincent's doing. There are games that generate content and results through visual, board-game elements, like Avatar, Zombie Cinema, It's Complicated, Transantiago, and Mwaantaangaand (and Grey Ranks, kinda, though it uses fortune as well). I might count Bacchanal in the latter group too, because it's glass of dice serves a similar function. There's a group that's still somewhat vague (at least to me) that does thing more like Jeep, setting up a variety of situations and social structures and then cutting the players lose to resolve them. And then there's a bunch of games that are currently still fairly unique and don't have clearly associated games with which to form a category.

    All in all though, I'm really excited. I really love this style of design and it's great to see so many folks fiddling with it.

    \n I read Dulse by Jason Morningstar now, and that is really a very nice game of the type I'm talking about. It's all about character, relationships, and principles.

    I see that he could make it simpler still, and that he by simplifying it would make it a better game (supposedly). I reckon that if he had played the wonderfully elegant "Until we sink ...", by Magnus Jakobson, he would have taken the use of written scene cards to heart, and used a similar technique to strenghten the gameplay of Dulse (again: supposedly).

    However: I really liked reading this game, as it connects with the stuff I'm doing myself in my modern games: focusing on human interrelations. It's all in the word: interrelation is too close to interaction to be ignored as THE best focal-point for roleplaying games. And if you do make interrelations your focal-point, then you need only set the direction for the players, giving them a framework for the the gameplay, and great drama will grow by itself.

    You really don't need mechanics at all to do that (you may use mechanics, but it is important to know that you may do without).

    Let grown ups play, but give them grown up toys to play with, so they may play their souls up on the table!

    \n I got a group together to experiment with a play-by-IRC game with only two rules:
    \n
    \n1. The group will make decisions concerning bylaws and game rules by parliamentary procedure.
    \n2. Any player may contribute or modify story elements at any time. The story comprises all elements in play.
    \n
    \nI had several rule-mod proposals lined up for us to vote on (including one that detailed what "parliamentary procedure" meant, and one that defined a "canon" of story elements that would trump non-canon elements), but they turned out largely to be unnecessary. I expected (and kind of hoped) that we would generate a "system" as we needed it, but it turned out we largely didn't.
    \n
    \nWe only played for three sessions. I got us on track telling the story of a dwarf kingdom (I got the notion from playing too much Dwarf Fortress). The sessions felt like collaborative DMing: no one was yet willing to take on the roles of characters by the end of the third session. I expect that would have changed eventually, or it might have been different if we played more often (sessions were three weeks apart). We gave it up for scheduling reasons. I consider the experiment a surprising success -- "surprising" insofar as I thought we would have to change those two rules above pretty much as soon as we began play, but in fact we didn't have to change them at all, and immediately focused on the story pretty much exclusively.

      Posted By: gmdiceI feel some rules have to be in place just like the laws of nature to help make the game realistic.
      Ah, realism! I believe that will be a road all by itself, discussing realism in roleplaying games. We'll have to do that in another thread, sometime ...

      I prefer to use the term "method" in these discussions, as I consider the term "system" to be misguiding. It has normative force, and is at the same time descriptive: pertaining to classical roleplaying games as we know them.

      System points to the concrete and systematic rules in the tradition of roleplaying games.

      Method points to the way we play, opening up for tools pertaining to group dynamics, psychology and verbal manipulation.

      Norwegian game designers has blossomed partly due to us discussing RPGs with other terms from the 90ties and onward, opening up for original takes on how to make game methods. We have ended up making games based on different principles than the traditional one, while at the same time beginning to understand more of the classical RPG too.

      The same is true for the Danish scenario-design. Some of the Danish designers have left traditional system-thinking and gone for a totally new approach. They call it "free form", but in fact these de-facto roleplaying games are as methodical as any other game. The stringent method found in some of the Danish "scenarios" makes them full and beautiful roleplaying games in their own right.

      Sorry for casting a Nordic light over this discussion. It may be that my ramblings are hard to understand. Hope some of it makes sense to you.
