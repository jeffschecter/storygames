\n Hi,

I'm gonna first write that I'm indeed serious, and what I'm about to write is in no way tongue-in-cheek.

So, have any of you played a game in which you played to find out the roles in a story or even like the typical roles assigned in an rpg?

What I mean by that is, have you played a game in which a role wasn't assigned, but instead the idea of story role was explored, like a dungeon?

Could you imagine playing to find your niche in a story. Not knowing whether you are the protagonist or the antagonist. Or maybe you're just a supporting character, or comedic foil?

Do you think such a thing would interest you?

Maybe the more you played, the more likely you would succeed at fulfilling the story's need because you would've figured out your role in the story? That would hint at some sort of success/failure paradigm. Failure does happen in story, but it's usually something the protagonist does. Supporting characters rarely fail, that is until the protagonist doesn't require their support.

It's wrong-headed, but so are rpgs trying to be stories.

Have other games done this?

    \n I've seen a bunch of this in game drafts and contest games. Two examples:

    Roll for screen presence.

    Alexander Cherry ( @Lxndr I think) came up with a very interesting mechanic for our contest game Out There (a sort of "make an X-Files episode" game). In each scene, you rolled for which detectives appeared, but also for which NPCs did.

    I never discussed his intent with Alexander, but one of the results was that "whose story is this?" wound up being emergent. It wouldn't take much tweaking to extrapolate "oh, weird, so I guess this character is in this scene" into "...so I guess that implies a role other than what we thought!" I could absolutely see myself making an investigator character and then finding out partway through play that they're the killer the party is hunting. I can also see starting out as GM and finding one of my NPCs turning into a star protagonist (as long as traditional GM duties aren't fixed in the hands of one person; easy enough to let that fall out from scene roles too, e.g. "if you have no character in this scene, GM it").

    Compete to be GM.

    I have a draft of a game where one player starts as the protagonist and everyone else competes to be GM by offering the character various opportunities. Whoever the player picks most wins, and gets to be GM. The losing GMs could take on other roles depending on how they lost or what happened in the meantime.

    \nDayTrippers isn't quite as tabula rasa as Roll For Shoes, but I was definitely influenced by the concept, which led me to build what I call "Progressive Character Generation" into the DT rules. Aside from the whole "jump right in" thing, the other thing I like about this approach is that it allows psychological projection and the flow of associated ideas to drive the character creation experience, even as the story emerges, which makes it a good technique for both evoking Bleed-In and guaranteeing Plot Relevance.

    \n This is how Ribbon Drive works. At the end of the game, various choices the characters have made throughout the game decide whether or not the story had a single protagonist (and who it was) or if it was an ensemble piece. This is a remarkably sound way to do this sort of thing, since stories in roleplaying are generally best constructed retroactively.

    This is not, however, what you play Ribbon Drive to find out; it is not a goal towards which most players are working, and only someone who has played the game a few times is likely to really be aware of this rule enough to take it into account in their play. Rather, the rule is a post-hoc narrative aid; a suggestion of a specific perspective from which to look back at the game that has already happened.

    \n I think "the party" is the protagonist in truly old-school play. Especially if you do funnels, start at level 0, blank slate character descriptions, etc. Yeah, you have a character class but you don't even remember your dude's name.

    But/Then, individual characters and narrative roles like Pro- or Antagonist, Comic Foil, Supporting Character, etc, ... begin to emerge from whatever happens. As they progress (if they progress) they begin to stand out, to take on the illusion of unique characters with individual traits and foibles. This is mostly a matter of randomness and projection, of course, but hey, what else are we talkin' about?

    \n There are some murder mystery games, like A Taste For Murder, where you play not knowing who the murderer is - that's definitely "playing without knowing your role", I'd think.

    Similarly, I've written a game where characters pursue powerful desires, and, at the end, we find out whether they succeed or fail. One of the things that's always surprised me in the game is how immediately and completely the players tend to assign roles to the characters at the end of the story. For instance, if someone has a certain goal, and fails, they often immediately are seen as the "villain" of the story (even if they were a likeable character up until then).

    \n I remember seeing a game played in the Inglorious Bastards movie. Can't remember the name of the game and haven't playet it myself. Players write down the name of a character (either fictional or real) on a card in secret, another player puts that card on his forehead in a way that everyone can see except him and then they start making one question about themselves to the rest of the players, in turns. The game ends whenever one player guesses correctly what character he is.

    Now, think of a D&D like one-shot where all players have a card on their forehead telling their race and class, but no character sheet. They will all have to play their way out of a 3 room dungeon and can try one "skill check", use a class ability or racial feature. The rest of the players just say if they fail or not based on the info they can se on the card , but not why.

    Heh, ok, it would certainly need tons of adjustment, many race/class variables and players with an encyclopedic knowledge of those to be playable, but I think if could be cool.

    Obviously, this is not a generic mechanic. However, having characters with unknown backgrounds or amnesia can give them a chance to change or redefine their loyalties.

We barely get out to be rescued by U.S. Marines and taken to a carrier. We're still unsure if that's good or bad for us, as we still hadn't much clues about who we were supposed to be.

    Then, in an awesome plot twist, each of our characters turns out to be the leaders of secret underworld and powerful international factions. Like, a nuclear submarine surfaces right next to the carrier to pick up my character, who turns out to be the next fuhrer; my pal awakes in a cuban medical facility where he discovers he's the leader of a Templar order eight levels above Vatican authority. Then the last one awakes in an isolated tent on the desert and a friggin giantic helicarrier gets down to pick him and return him his command as the leader of a Shield-like organization. It was epically evil, we thought all the time we were civilians, while we had gathered there as evil plot masters to decide the fate of the world.

    \n Here's a move I'm working on for a fantasy game with a start-out-blank ethos:

    Search your soul
    When you have a realization about yourself or the world, answer a question below or cross out a previous answer. The first two times you do this during a session, [some tangible reward.] Share your answer with the table.
    \u2022 What do you believe about yourself or the world?
    \u2022 What do you want?
    \u2022 Who do you love or admire?
    \u2022 What regret or memory haunts you?
    \u2022 Who do you fear or hate?
    \u2022 Who do you owe?
