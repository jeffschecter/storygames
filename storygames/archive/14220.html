\n Hey JD:

Why do you hate game designers?

Actually, you don't really seem to hate them, you just seem to think that whatever their intentions are and whatever they wrote in their game doesn't really matter so much when you get people together to play a game. And I'm honestly interested in talking about why that is.

Is it:\n
  • because you're sick of games not really delivering on what they promise, so you've decided to take things into your own hands, so to speak, and take a more direct hand in shaping how the game is experienced at the table?
    • \n
  • because you have a strong "the author is dead" feeling, in the sense of modern literary theory, like game designers have been put on a pedestal and people spend way to much time trying to decipher what they really mean, when it's way more important to look at what actually happens at the table?
    • \n
  • because you feel like you know everything worth knowing about roleplaying (or, at least, roleplaying the way you want to play), so that you can't really learn anything by adopting new or different techniques or approaches?
    • \n
  • because you've played too many games where people don't actually tell you how to play, so you're like "Forget this! I'll figure it out myself!" and tend to skip to the parts of a game text that seem important to you?
    • \n
  • because some game designer was too cool to sign your copy of Vampire 1st Edition as a kid?
\nI assume it's probably something more interesting than all of those, actually, but just wanted to throw some possibilities out there. I mean, you seem like a smart dude who's thought a bunch about games, so it can't just be for some superficial or dumb reason.

    \n I think it was because the first time any prolonged communication took place between gamers and game designers - on Usenet in the early 90s - it became amazingly clear amazingly fast that the designers had no real idea how the games were actually being played, and were really quite horrified at what they found. At one point I was in the killfile of every White Wolf developer and freelancer that had ever done any work for their game, just because I talked a lot about their games and had lots of brilliant, brilliant I say ideas for them. It didn't bother me, I took a gleeful sort of pride in it, and kept giving them money for really good stuff.

    This led me to think about the difference between what I did as a GM and as a player and what the game designers did. Once I worked that out, it became overwhelmingly clear that the designer's contribution was, whether significant or insignificant, static, whereas the key element of RPG play (as compared to board or card games) is that it's dynamic. (In this respect the "author is dead" option is closest.) I don't exactly know what the borders of RPG play are, nobody really does because "game" is inherently a fuzzy word applied to all sorts of very different things, but one characteristic I've been able to discern is that at any given moment, the choices of the players are virtually infinite. The power of dialogue alone is enough to scuttle any designer's intended experience, make the most serious dramas into hilarious comedies or wild-eyed surrealiana, and it's pretty rare that RPGs don't give you even more power than that, they let you make characters, which is a sure sign that they've given up, or more generously, they recognize the limits of their reach. I mean, characters! Isn't that a dead giveaway?

    Smallville: I like how every time you roll anything in this game you are telling the table WHY you are doing it. It's like the speech bubble from With Great Power given new life as a die-rolling system. This is a game about expressing what your character thinks and feels at every moment of their soap operatic laser beam shooting experience, soooo good.\n

It's why I put the local GM Conference together, because, selfishly, I wanted to learn from other people's approaches and ideas. Michael Jordan played a lot of basketball, Bobby Fischer played a lot of chess, Muhammad Ali got punched by a shitload of people.

    I usually end up agreeing with you on most things. But here's a thing:

    I'm as down with "death of the author" as anybody, and I think it has pretty big implications for rpg play. I think, as a whole, we're probably too hung up on "author's intent", to the detriment of experimental play.

    \n Also, cool. A couple other questions, right now, following from some of the discussion earlier. Is it all really ONE toolbox? I mean, don't some of those techniques and procedures and bits of styles and stuff just flat-out not go together?

    \nWell, sure, but the same's true for all toolboxes, ya know. Flathead and phillips head and all that metaphorical shit. I change up what I pull out of the toolbox for every game that I'm in, and the moment that I'm in, depending on my goals and the goals of the group at that exact time.\n

    \nBy talking about it? It ain't rocket science, we say, "did that work" or "what did you like about that" or "were you into that or not" and so on. I try to make it really clear up front what we're doing.

    I do this in con games too. Here's what I said at the beginning of my Call of Cthulhu game last time:

    "Hey, I'm Jason and this is my Call of Cthulhu game. Call of Chtulhu is a really fun game in which you are playing characters like those that might appear in the horror stories of the groundbreaking 1920s pulp author H.P. Lovecraft. I'm going to be the GM. My job is to portray the other characters and give you creepy situations to dig around in, and dangers. Your characters all have reasons to dig around in creepy situations and get into trouble, so play those up! You also all know each other and have opinions about each other, those are on the back of your sheet. I like when people talk to each other in character, so please don't feel silly about giving it a try. If you don't like it, or aren't sure what to say, that's all right too, just speak up when the time comes. This is a four hour block - I try to take a 5-10 minute break every hour, and I try to end before the block is over. If you don't understand something or are confused, let me know. The second page of your packet is a simple cheat sheet about the system, which I'll go over now, but I'm happy to explain more at any time."

    Like, you get really specific about what you want, what they are going to get, where they should look to know things, and when you want it.\n

    \n I know a lot of people who've given up on the author's intent. Quotes (paraphrased)\u2026

    "Originally I wanted to play by the rules, but with core books spanning 300+ pages and supplement after supplement updating, revising, re-explaining, adding errata, contradicting and expanding the rules\u2026 it became nearly impossible. It was more work than my job."

    "The rules seem endless and poorly written with terrible indexes and are difficult to reference! I can't keep this all straight. I once wrote a house rule that later the designer told us was actually a rule in the game but we just couldn't find it in their ocean of rules."

    "It's important to me to get the game right! Which means I have to re-read the book at least 2 to 3 times before I feel comfortable even thinking about playing our first game. And I still get it wrong! But honestly, when we finally do get it right we realize many of these rules are terrible! What do you mean after I roll a natural 20, I need to confirm my critical? What do you mean I have to re-roll my Spot skill every 5 feet?"

    "I played with the designers at a convention and even they don't play by their own rules! And hell, the house rules we came up with ended up being in the second edition. The fact that games even have new editions means they can be changed and still work fine."

    "The rules seemed wrong to me but we kept playing them thinking we were missing something. Then we found out how little playtesting they did and we feel like suckers. Never again. If the rules don't seem right, we will make them right."

    "This game is 95% perfect for our group except this one part. It's less work to change this game than search for and possibly never find the perfect game. And the perfect game doesn't exist. No game was made specifically for us. So we need to be the ones to make games fit us."

    "The game designer doesn't know my group. We're friends first, players second. We get together to hang out. Most of us like different things in games but we make it work because being friends is more important. So no game is going to work for us. It can't. We all want different things."

    Some of these problems have to do with inaccessible or hard to use rules.

    BUT Prime Time Adventures is only 48 pages. And I've played PTA with a lot of you. I don't think I've ever personally played with anyone (beyond Thor and Luke) on Story Game that ran PTA rules as written. I'm not saying that is a bad thing. I've had incredible PTA games with many of you. More often than not, the rules changes are on purpose (especially because PTA isn't meant to be played as a one shot and we often play it at conventions). But I've also met people (most not on Story Games) that were convinced they were playing PTA, rules as written, and they weren't. And it's only a 48 pages!

    I know I've gamed with many designers who have worked on D&D, Pathfinder, Shadowrun and many other games. And even they didn't play rules as written!

    Full disclosure: I've run Dogs over a 100 times and when I run it now I make changes. I didn't for the first 20 games and most of the changes I do make are incorporating rules from Afraid, Vincent's horror game using the Dogs ruleset or making it easier to run at conventions. I believe it is important to know how a game works before you tamper with it (although sometimes tampering is a great way to understand how a game works).

    But there are also a lot of bad house rules out there. I've been in many D&D games where the DM's house rules completely invalidated many of our characters because they didn't understand how the game worked. But there are also a lot of bad games, bad designers, bad players, and bad GMs out there as well. The bad isn't an indicator of what the good can do. And people who do know what they are doing don't always get it right. And sometimes the people who have no idea get lucky! It's hard to make absolute statements.

    \n I think what Jason's saying, or, fuck it, actually this is what I'm saying:

    People have a dialectical relationship to the text in play. The text influences how people play, but also people actively interpret the text - their understanding of it is shaped by their preconceptions, what they want out of play, what they're expecting to see in there. For that part, people are even more adrift, their decision-space influenced by a much wider range of factors. I drew a diagram about this once.

    Posted By: Simon CThere are virtually infinite things a personcouldsay when they're playing a game, but they don't. I'm not in the habit (usually) of uttering random exhortations at the table. What I say is shaped by the expectations of play. The text, the fact that we've all sat down at the table motivated by this text (to a greater or lesser extent), our (more or less) shared understanding of play, creates a powerful expectation between us. Now, that expectation encompasses a pretty broad range, but it's wrong to say it "overwhelms" the text on the page. It's powerfully shaped by the text on the page.
    \nIt can be, sure. But it isn't necessarily. Groups choose how much their actions are shaped by what's on the page at any given time. Sometimes they choose to really adhere as closely as they can to what's on the page. That's what Jesse was talking about in the earlier thread. Their goal is: "We're going to learn to play this the way Mark Rein-bjork-Hagen would have wanted us to! Leave no comma unturned in our search for the true meaning of this text! Both players are pursuing their goals, the text is only a tool that both of them use in their own way.

    \n Muddled up and changing all the time: Yes.

    I think that before and during play, players come to a shared understanding about what the game is. A lot of the time, that's influenced strongly by a game text. Someone says "Let's play Dogs", and sure, that means a different thing to each person, but it's close enough that functional play is possible.

    Someone says "Let's play Apocalypse World" and everyone's idea of what that game will be is influenced by that game text, but also by a bunch of other post-apocalyptic media. But then AW does this neat thing where character creation often guides people towards a more cohesive creative vision.

    Someone says "Let's play Vampire!" and they're probably gonna have to do a bunch more talking before functional play is possible, simply because the space of possible games within Vampire is so huge.

    But then there's this other thing: Someone says "Let's play Star Wars, using Dogs." Now we're in a wholly more complex space. What does Star Wars mean to people? What does Dogs mean in this context? I suggest a lot of talking, or a strong directorial voice is required in order to make that game work.

    Would I be right, Jason, in thinking that your normal approach is closer to the latter? You have a concept of the game that you want to play, and while a game text is one of the tools you're using to get to that, the idea of the game itself is not neccessarily directly inspired by a single text?

    Also, of course, our idea of "what the game is" is constantly changing, altered by the events of the game, and other things.
