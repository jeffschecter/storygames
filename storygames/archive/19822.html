    \n In my experience, a maze-like D&D dungeon gets me drawing maps, a setting book like Chicago By Night provides a fleshed-out environment, many GMs use the Wilderness Survival skill for orienteering checks, and any GM who gets inspired by a fictional world will introduce cool new places.

    I like the same stuff you do, but I view it as essentially a genre -- fantasy/sci-fi/supernatural foundation + setting book + GM who cares.

    You asked about rules, though, and I can't think of a published game that makes all that cool stuff happen mechanically. The best example I can think of is probably my friend's Pathfinder hack Goblin Uprising. The rules I like are almost all GM-facing, and are intended to quickly populate any new area entered by the player characters, while tracking relevant goings-on in places they've already visited.

    The key player-facing aspect is that a given 2-mile hex can either be Pacified or not. "Pacified" means the characters have overcome the area's obstacles and uncovered its resources. They may now pass through the area at double movement speed en route to other places, avoid the Wandering Monster roll, and harvest resources at appropriate intervals.

    So, players start with a map of a few hexes defining the coast of the continent they've landed on at the beginning of the game. Cursory exploration adds hexes, and thorough exploration (plus fighting etc.) brings more of these hexes into a "safe zone", beyond whose borders more exploration lies.

    Could you summarize those mechanics?
    \nHi \u2013 the game is in the process of being designed by John Keyworth, who designed a game called \u2018Intrepid\u2019.

    Basically you start with a hand-drawn map based on a historical (or fictional) journey & then line-trace the journey with scenes (plot-points) & vignettes dotted along the way.

    The version I played in was based on Napoleon\u2019s march on Moscow; hence, the siege of Moscow had three scenes based there, while other points on the journey had just one or two.

    History (or fiction) is just the launch-point. You might start, say, with two historical figures as characters \u2013 in our case, Napoleon & the Tzar \u2013 and then add one invented character per player. Anyone can play any character. The players use tokens to keep their contributions proportionate.

    It struck me as easily-adaptable to any map-based narrative. I think John is still working on the gameplay.

    \n I had a concept for an RPG inspired by the Myst series I was calling The Sojourn. The idea was something of a "linear sandbox", which I guess is structurally like a dungeon, but the game took place on open worlds in outdoors environments.

    The game was meant to be paced using puzzles. Example: you arrive on an island and discover sites containing remains from a previous island community that has seemingly abandoned the place. You can explore these areas and mess with the remains, but you can't really "progress" until you solve a puzzle allowing you to construct a raft to sail to the only other nearby island, using their strange tools.

    I think it the right hands maybe it could work, but I pretty much gave up because of the challenge of designing puzzles that were both interesting and solvable, but not too obvious or obscure. Because not being able to solve them would have stopped play dead in its tracks, as in Myst.

    \n Hey, here's something I'm working on to attempt to address this issue of frustration. It's the Stargate: Universe-inspired Spelljammer 5E campaign that I'm planning on co-GMing with my partner.

    Each session, each PC can choose to either go on an "away" mission or attempt to unlock more of the Destiny's functionality. In other words, the PCs' *conveyance* is the puzzle-rich environment, whereas the worlds they stop at will mostly contain more normal D&D adventure stuff. So the players can choose whether they want "puzzle" or "adventure" each session. (And, even if you're on board the ship, you can choose to spend more time roleplaying with NPC crewmembers instead of working on the puzzles.) Not that there won't be the occasional puzzle-like thing on an island, of course...

    ETA: With both me and N GM-ing, we're hoping to get 6-8 people to come to each session, so we can run simultaneous away missions and shipboard investigation.

    \n Thanks! To respond in more detail...

    Basically, yes to your second question. Nat and I intend to do a lot of work to make the Parseley-style puzzle stuff quite challenging. And if the PCs tried to simply poke at the puzzles all session long all the time, they'd starve to death or be eaten by the NPC crew members very, very quickly. I have a whole clever system worked out for resource management, and that can only be managed by going on away missions.

    However... without giving too much away, I can tell you that unlocking certain of the puzzle elements on the ship will make some (but not all) of the resource problems more manageable if not eliminate them entirely. So as the game progresses, the PCs may naturally be more incentivized towards working on the ship.

    And when all the puzzles are unlocked, the campaign will enter end-game, which the players will be aware of, so they can basically self-pace the ending.

    \n I think the obvious tough part is designing the right kind of puzzles.

    But honestly, my experience with that says that How You Tackle Them is more important than the puzzles themselves*. As long as the players can quickly draw the line between "this is fun even if we're not sure we're getting anywhere; let's keep going" and "this is not fun but I am now obsessed with solving this", then you've got some leeway. I'm not always good at spotting this and yelling at the players, "move on and come back to this later!" but I don't think Matt will have that problem.

    Plus, the resource system and the not starving thing will prevent some dragging probing. It depends a little on how time is tracked, though. If the players can pause in-game time to bust out graph paper or whatnot and do real-world puzzling, the "don't starve" pressure gets harder to apply.

    So the less obvious tough part is managing the players' interaction with the puzzles. Some see that as simply "good GMing" while others view it as a nearly impossible goal that needs as much formal support as possible. In Matt's game, I imagine a few simple principles will go a long way. "Okay, I have to stop you guys now" is much more palatable when you know you can come back to the puzzle for free later.

    * Not that awesome puzzles aren't a great bonus! And simply making an RPG puzzle that doesn't suck is no mean feat.
