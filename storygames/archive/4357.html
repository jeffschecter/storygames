\n So I'm listening to the most recent episode of The Durham 3 about how the size of your group affects the play you get out of it and it helped me crystallize why I like usually like GMs in roleplaying games.

I tend to prefer games where the protagonists do not represent the main source of resistance to one another. I also prefer games where the default tends toward scenes involving most or all of the protagonists at the same time. Finally, I don't like people sitting around, bored. If most or all of the protagonists are together, that tends to serve the last point of my trinity there. I also don't like to break the Czege principle, which states that it's not fun for you to provide your own adversity.

So, I like games where protagonists provide secondary or less opposition to one another, where they are often together in scenes, where people aren't ignored, and where the Czege principle is not violated. To accommodate those preferences, a GM is usually required: a player of non-protagonist characters who can provide adversity to assembled protagonists.

    \n Glad you got something out of it, Rob!

    Like you I really like it when all the players are engaged, regardless of what their characters are doing. That can be accomplished on various levels, but I definitely prefer games that support it systemically and people who support it by being aware and enthusiastic.

    Unlike you, my personal taste runs toward conflict between players and their characters. I don't think you're violating the Czege Principle when you say "I really want my ex-wife to be in the next scene giving me hell, guys." At what point you feel like you are providing your own adversity certainly varies from person to person.

    I don't think any of this is influenced by how you divide up authority, though. I can just as easily play an NPC and kick your guy in the nuts as required regardless of my role, fellow player or GM. The only thing you might lose in a theoretical completely equal distribution of authority is full-bore protagonism from every player all at the exact same time. This doesn't mean that investing a lot of that power in a GM is a bad idea, though, if that's what works.

    \n A NOTE: This is going to be long-winded, so I'll summarize at the bottom for those who don't feel like reading through the whole thing. I'll even put it in bold.

    Jason,

    You picked up something that was niggling in my head as I wrote this. I failed to talk about it when I posted because it wasn't very clear in my brain. So thanks for once again helping me crystallize my thoughts. To whit:

    I agree with you that you can certainly achieve full player participation without having the all or most of the players' protagonists in every or almost every scene. I can think of two ways to do this. The first I'll call The system made me do it and the second, I'll be that guy.

    The system made me do it: The system can encourage player attention that is separated from the pretend actions that that player takes with a character in the scene. An easy example is Primetime Adventures's fan mail: You need to watch what's happening in a scene to know who to give fan mail to. Another is Ben Lehman's Adventures in the Land of 1,000 Kings, where your character knows what's going on with the other protagonists because your character is you and knows what you know.

    I'll be that guy: This can be an at-the-table convention or part of the rules, where players may be encouraged to take the role of non-protagonist characters present in a scene that their protagonist is not in.

    That said, I usually prefer protagonists in most scenes together most of the time.

    \n On my livejournal, a man named John Burdick has been asking me some very interesting questions. One of them was, what does a GM not have to do? I found the reply might be worthwhile here, so:\n
    Me on my livejournal edited a little bitThe truth is I'm pretty flexible [on what a GM should not have to be responsible for]. I can have fun with games in which there is no GM, for example. I just tend to prefer one where there is a GM. Right now my gaming is 1) a bi-weekly Shadowrun game which is fairly traditional in terms of GM role but there's a little wiggle room, 2) a weekly Burning Wheel game run pretty close to the book which means it's a strong GM for the indie game scene but not for the traditional one, and 3) conventions, where I prefer to run Primetime Adventures and my game, Misspent Youth, both of which like to have GM as plate-spinner, scene cutter, sort of an intermediary through which a lot of the rest of the story flows.

    Now that that incredibly long-winded disclaimer is out of the way (to summarize: I like lots of things but I do have preferences), I'll talk about what I prefer for the GM not to have to do.

    First, motivation. I think motivation should come from the players. The GM should be very cognizant of what the other players want, either by reading it in the flags on their character sheets or by asking them player-to-player about it. She should use these techniques to figure out what the players want from the game, combine that with what she wants, and then try to give it to them and solicit it from them.

    Second, mechanical rewards (xp, hero points, fan mail, etc.). I prefer game systems where players claim their own reward based on clear rules and/or the assent of the table.

    Third, protagonist improvement and change. I prefer games where protagonist improvement or change--and the resources that allow for such--are player or table generated and players are responsible for keeping track of their own advancement/change or for making a case for it to the table.

    There are very likely to be many others but these leap to mind fairly easily.
