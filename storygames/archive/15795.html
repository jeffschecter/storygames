\n Okay, I know, it's been talked to death. I played DW tonight (new rules), and getting XP was hard. I was a LVL 1 Evil Wizard (mark XP when you bind or control something using magic), and it was too tricky. It was a puzzle: "How do I bind/control something with Identify, Magic Missile, or Invisibility?" I felt like I had to maneuver the situation towards getting my XP, as opposed to acting a certain way or approaching a situation in a certain way. I wanted XP to be a carrot to act a certain way in a situation, or attack the problem in a certain way, and it felt like I had to be in the right situation with the right tools to gain my XP, which felt like too much.

I dunno, maybe Judd's idea of Dungeon XP is a good way to go? I feel like there needs to be a middle ground between immediate action-based XP (I gain it when I roll STR) and situational maneuvering. I've read all the XP hacks/ideas out there (I think), and I'm not sure anything quite does it for me, though Ryan's comes closest. Maybe there just needs to be more, like the Evil Wizard has three ways to gain XP instead of one (mark XP when you use magic for your own gain, mark XP when you display how magic has corrupted you, etc.).

I guess I'm not looking for new XP ideas or rehashing the old ones, but more: does XP in (new) Dungeon World feel this way to you? Like a situation-maneuvering puzzle? Maybe it was just a weird one-off experience.

(as a note, to those who might ask: I didn't mark XP at all during the session, even w/ the end-of-session moves. Only one person did--the neutral thief--so it may have been the adventure--Spanterhook--I dunno).

    \n I for one would REALLY like to see some Judd-style XP triggers built into the bonds, because "resolving" a bond feels pretty weak and muddy as a rule to me. Giving an explicit trigger for which you gain XP (which was done with Alignment but not with Bonds, for some reason) just feels more like an actual rule to me.

    For my buck, the "perfect" XP system in DW would be Alignments as they are, Bonds as Judd envisions them, End of Session questions as written, and BBC XP (with easy-to-remember icons for each type of Move, like crossed swords for Attack, a shield for Defend, a speech bubble for Converse, a magnifying glass for Discover, two right hands doing the Beast Hunters salute for Aid, and...something for stunt. I dunno, the jackass symbol of a helmet with crossed crutches comes to mind, even though it's wildliy out of place). The Dungeon XP really sounds like an alternate rule to me, and falls flat as a standard for gaining XP.

And I'd keep the level +7 for the level-up amount, because x10 was always going to be ludicrous, given that you don't gain more XP as the game progresses like you do in D&D.

    \n Personally, I'm leaning more and more towards mission-based or Burning Wheel-style end of session XP. There's something to be said for Key-style XP, where the XP system helps remind you of what you character is about and how they should approach problems, but honestly -- if you're leading with the fiction all the time -- that should be less of a problem, I think, and more of a distraction, since you have to do a lot of fictional maneuvering to hit your XP conditions, as Hans points out.

    However, if you get XP at the end of the session/mission, after you've actually accomplished something, that fits in well with the natural "spent loot" / "buy new gear" / "rest" part of the cycle of play that already exists in dungeon-y games. Sure, you can't level up just in time to beat the crap out of the demon lord, but that's always felt a bit artificial anyway (and leveling up in the middle of a fight can take time, if it's more than just "extra HP + pick a new move").

    Consequently, I want to try out lowering XP requirements (like level + 2) and only giving out XP at the end of sessions: for completing missions and "having played your alignment" during the session (that's also, per the new rules, a great chance to change your alignment, whether you got XP for it or not).

    \n Some thoughts on XP:

    We're fine tuning all the starting Alignments (and the new ones you can switch to) to make sure they're possible and not disruptive.

    Judd's XP conditions from Bonds are pretty close to resolving them, which we are adding examples of. "________ has magic in them that they have not yet tapped into; I will show it to them" is resolved when they learn to tap into that magic, when you give up in disgust of their ignorance, when they take you as their master to continue trying to unlock the power, anything that means the Bond has been dealt with. There's now a cycle to bonds where there's a reason to pursue them, deal with them, and form new ones.

    I'm not a huge fan of BBC XP myself. It's an improvement on stat highlighting, but I think we're beyond the highlighting idiom.

    Our feedback so far has been that the end of session questions are getting hit by most groups, with two being hit in most sessions. I'd be interested in hearing about the sessions where that isn't happening.

    \n All three of those examples are from Alignments, though they do overlap with end of session, yeah. You still get XP for doing stuff, adventury stuff. Some of them aren't as good for that, but we'll be getting to those and revising them. Your alignment should be something that you do a few times per session and they should be about clear present action.

    To give an example of what I'm talking about, you might get this as starting Wizard alignments:

    Good
    When you use magic to save someone else from danger, mark XP.

    Neutral
    When you discover a magical secret, mark XP.

    Evil
    When you use magic to inflict cruelty, mark XP.


    Or for the Cleric:

    Good
    When you enter danger to heal someone, mark XP.

    Evil
    When you kill or maim in your deity's service, mark XP.


    I feel like these do get that hindbrain reward cycle going. You get into situations where you can immediately do these things. Bond XP is still 1/session, so the only 1-infinity XP option is your alignment, which is an action-oriented NOW thing. I like that.

    I say that the current system degrades gracefully because you can forget about alignments entirely, just throw them out, and the game still works. Levelling will be slower, around half the speed, but you don't stop advancing.

    \n Ok, so two sessions with the Beta 1.1 RAW down and I'm already hacking (again). I stuck with the XP system as given though, even if I did re-incorporate the Loot Move and our own 'circles' variant move.
    \nBut...... The one thing I did dredge up from the old Apoc D&D days (such an old skool MC man) was my hack to reward player motivation and roleplay with bennies instead of xp. I stole the idea of 'awesome dice' from Old Skool Hack and combined it with the BBC keys. So when one of the players did something they had nominated (or had been nominated by the GM) as their key and would normally have marked xp, they now grabbed an awesome d6. They could then use that die to add to any roll thereafter (or after the roll) and choose which ever two results they wanted for the move.
    \n
    \nThis had some remarkably interesting effects on play. The 'lizard brain' wanted bennies. The awesome dice became little jewels of want want want for the players. It sorta helped that they were Q-workshop translucent elvish, dwarvish, runic, draconic and tribal d6s! I specifically choose keys (in my DM allocation) that were not part of the player's normal 'style'; Sketchy the thief was marked with 'stunt' and 'converse', Arwen with 'attack' and 'discover'. I had to be very present in the conversation, reminding the players to do it, do it. They really wanted to acquire and spend awesome dice, and it would have been all too easy to have become way more D&D tactical and mechanical about it, ignoring the fiction.
    \n
    \nBut that's not why we play Dungeon World! I stuck true to my principles and insisted that the moves stem from the narration. It lead to a very action packed and move-snowbally sort of game, where I hardly ever had to make a move to advance the fiction from the 'blank-look' syndrome. My moves always seemed to be in response to player moves or failed rolls, it was rather un-relenting! We had the interesting experience whereby a player indulged in some fictional 'key' behaviour to achieve an awesome die and then used that awesome die ensuring a 10+ for an aid/interfere move, resulting in the resolution of a Bond, thus gifting her with an xp.
    \n
    \nAll in all it was a successful iteration of an odd 'add-on' that I just can't seem to let go of. I Love giving players in game rewards that are far more than just a +1 forward. Something tied to what they are doing and where they want to go. It wasn't perfect but it worked just fine.
