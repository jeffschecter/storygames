RELATEDNESS OF WORDS (see Figure 1)
Relatedness of the 10 most frequent words in the collection

Figure 1

This figure was created using a clustering algorithm in R. The algorithm looks at frequency and concurrence of words. It then calculates a distance from each word to each other word. Words that occur close together in the text are represented as being close (by length of the branches) to each other on the diagram.

There are three broad groups:
A store, play, roleplay
B creat, role, world, rule
C player, charact, game

Groups A & B are more closely related to each other then group C. In group B creat and role have short branch lengths reflecting a close concurrence.

    \n@DWeird good questions!

    I am not using the full text of any rpg book just the section that says What is a RPG (or something like that). So length is not an issue. All of the texts so far have really short sections on What is a RPG. By converting to words to frequency of occurrence instead of just raw counts I can remove most of the bias between texts. I use another method where I chunk that data (break up the text into small pieces) that greatly reduces the variability within and between texts.

    I am still working reducing bias from sampling issues. Mostly making sure we have enough texts represented.

    The implications of data so far point to trad and story games don't talk about what rpgs are differently.

    If you want to moderately push this data (like JD said in another thread) the trad/story thing is just false dichotomy used for social currency.

    There are other things we can query like how much of a text is player facing vs gm facing. I suspect we would see something there but not necessarily trad and story.

    \n What is role-play? Glad you asked. If you are new to the concept of a role-play game, let me try to explain. Role-play is the ability to put yourself into uncommon situations and try to play (act) out your resolution of problems. When I say act, I do not mean brandish sword and take the stage. Rather, role-play is imagination under a set of guidelines. It is a structured form of daydreaming.

    Some groups of role-players use elaborate maps, finely-painted miniatures, and a plethora of visual and mechanical aids; mostly these are used to represent spatial relationships. These may be used, but role-play is the interaction of real people in surreal conditions. Intrigued or confused into continuing? Then, I will try to explain the attitude you should take to play Crimson Cutlass.

    Referee will develop situations, missions, and encounters for the player to conquer or be vanquished, to profit or be swindled, and to command or be controlled. The player should remain loyal to his King and proud of his homeland. He should set priorities: gold, adventure, personal victory and power. He should always be plotting and investing, at times risking all on ventures with little chance of success, but with payback that justifies the danger.

    When you role-play, it is easy to be yourself and this is adequate, but strive to diversify play by adopting extremes in personality: heroic or ruthless, eccentric or demanding, and frivolous or meticulous. At times become unpredictable, brutal, even bloodthirsty, but always with a motive and never out of randomness. Be patient and knowledgeable, yet aloof to the mundane realities and responsibilities of the "paper-world". You must be equally as charitable, boisterous and gallant.

    You should never prey on those unrelated to your mission; however, if anyone is involved, even slightly, he is both suspect and victim to the bulk of your actions. Try to respect and marvel the workings of the State, the intricacies of trade, and the delicacies of war on both your homeland and allies. But also try to disrupt enemy nations, especially when instructed by Church or King. You are a piece in the grand political game, but usually more than a pawn. Always remember when wronged; you should be vengeful and duty-bound, but as each time your "paper-self" dies - start anew - play from the new person's point of view.

    If your "paper-self" should falter or veer in loyalty, either seek to atone or complete your switch to a new homeland and alliances. Be a pious player with religious zeal; conquest for your beliefs as much as your greed. The "paper-self", you play, should always pay debts and return services loyally. The "paper-self" will never refuse free food or drink, unless something is foul. Please never be trite, but above all enjoy. If you're not having fun, tell someone; everyone is important. State your views openly and keep from becoming a servitor - be a character in the plots of Crimson Cutlass.

    - "Crimson Cutlass", George Rahm, 1979 (quote from 1989 ed.)

    adventure character world
    For adventure facts, experience, and ability occur most often.
    For character player, game, and action occur most often.
    For world it is fact, experience, and ability

    Figure 3
    player gamemaster game
    Character, game, and create have the highest occurrence with player
    For gamemaster it is dungeon, challenging, and ability
    For game it is play, character, and player or play, player character, create

    Figure 4
    story drama roleplay
    For story it is understand, make, and long (I laughed when I saw that one)
    Scene, support, and play occur with drama

    \n It's my favorite of all time. I worked with George for a while at a film sound FX company. That cat was deep in his world. At the same time he was writing that, I was writing "CyberSpace" for Iron Crown, for which the "What Is" section is quoted below...\n
    A role playing game needs \u201crules\u201d (or guidelines) to define and control the physical realities of the world in which adventures may take place. Ideally, such rules are able to handle most of the common situations that might arise without detracting from the flavor and detail of the plot or setting of the game. This specific set of rules handles the physical realities of people, places and things in the dark future world of Cyberspace, where the ultrapowerful MegaCorps have all but enslaved mankind and doomed life on planet Earth to a dismal shadowy existance; where human beings have mastered the incredible interface of mind and machine; where fully one-third of the general population possesses cybernetic implants or enhancements; and tens of thousands have escaped to the orbital and lunar space colonies. In setting the tone and technological mood of the game, this work has drawn heavily from the writings of such dark-future masters as William Gibson, Bruce Sterling, Philip K. Dick , Walter J. Williams, and John Brunner, as well as many others whose visions are not quite so grim but nonetheless insightful. This text contains all the prospective Gamemaster (\u201cGM\u201d) needs to know about setting up a Cyberspace campaign.

    The GM should keep in mind that these \u201crules\u201d are really only guidelines to aid in the creation and running of a dark future role playing campaign. The GM should feel free to modify them to fit any specific view or style of role playing, and the Players should realize that the GM is the final authority when it comes to rules interpretations or changes. On the other hand, these rules and the GM\u2019s decisions concerning them are the only guide that the Players have to their characters\u2019 capabilities and the reality of the Cyberspace world. The GM must be consistent and fair when making decisions or the Players will lose the trust and confidence necessary for a truly enjoyable and satisfying role playing game.
