    Well, if you accept the premise in this here book, (which I do), then the answer is that TV is finally catching up with the potentiality of roleplaying games.

    In a nutshell, the author says that if you look at a show like Dallas from the 1970s, which was very complex for its day, and compare it to even what we would consider 'low-brow' entertainment, like Friends or Buffy, and you find that there's a whole lot more information that the viewer retains and manipulates in order to properly understand the show's contents.

    His thesis, then, is that popular entertainment is actually a lot more complex than we give it credit for, and its getting more complex all the time.

    So what that says to me is that pop culture is catching up with the complexity inherent in roleplaying games. Even a game like PTA is quite complex (and it's a very simple game in relative RPG terms) - meaning that there's a coming convergence of the complexity of the overall culture and of the structure and form of roleplaying games. RPGs were (and are) still ahead of the curve, but noticing more convergence between the two is natural.

    Ok... this is how I remember it:

    It's GM-less, with roles rotating each scene.

    Each scene is framed by someone playing their character. You either set it up to work towards your goal this episode or to help out someone else.

    The person on their left has to keep things moving, which they do by asking questions like "what happened next?" "why did you do that?" and so on.

    The person on their right creates adversity. They do this by knocking on the table at the moment they choose for greatest tension and humour. Then they describe the worst possible conslusion to the scene and the main player describes the best possible conclusion. The main player then makes a roll to see which option happens.

    The other players all take the roles of their main character (if in the scene) or extras, as required.

    The use of the player-generating-adversity (Steve had names for each role, but I can't remember them) to stop the scene at any moment with a worst-case result made for great play. And it generated bizzare sitcom plots almost effortlessly.

    I'll point Steve at this to see if he has more to add. I'm sure I have missed some stuff that could well be of interest.

    Josh,

    That reminds me, the other day Mo and I were fiddling around with ideas from her theatre days about things we could add to RPGs -- masks, method acting, dramaturges as underwriters, and so on.

    One of the things Mo suggested was making a mechanic where you outline or breif-play out a scene multiple different ways and then use resolution to decide which of the possibilities actually enters play. Much like the way some stage plays will run the same scene different times durring practice to see which works, and then put the final in for the play the audience sees. Depending on how much you'd played out before resoultion you could then just note which became reality, or replay it, or play out the thing you'd only thumbnailed before.

    After all, if actors get dress rehersals, why shouldn't we?

    (Of course, we were also talking about this in the context of a game where the characters are competing insane voices, so the surreality of the same scene three different ways has a particular level of grounding there it might not in other games.)
