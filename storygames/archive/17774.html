    \n BAH! I typed up an enormous post about TF2 strategy, and it's TOO LONG.

    Here's part 1:

    So, JD and I talked a bit about what the best maps to run tomorrow would be. This got me thinking about a topic that I love, TF2 STRATEGY! So I'm going to talk about that a bit here, as well as why I picked the maps that I did.

    This might get a bit serious, and may even sound not fun. If you're interested, read this post and learn. If you're not interested say "Shut up and quit over thinking this crap, you big dope!" and then go back to having fun and shooting things.

    Mostly I'm talking about this because I think it's fun, and because sometimes new players are scared to play because they "will suck" or because "they won't be very good." TF2 is an awesome shooter for beginners because the game is fun and the community is relatively laid back and silly, but also because it rewards teamwork and strategy as much as it does quick reflexes and precise aim. If you learn a few things, you won't suck, even if you've never played before!

    Rule #1 of being a good TF2 player is this:

    KNOW YOUR OBJECTIVE!

    TF2 is not a game about shooting people! It might SEEM like a game about shooting people, but it's actually a game about CONTROL OVER SPACE AND TIME! Shooting people is just your primary method for controlling space and time.

    So, know what game mode you're playing and what you're trying to do. Lots of maps will start with a helpful video explaining the game mode and the objective, but in case it doesn't play, or you're too excited when the time comes and you skip it, I'll talk a bit about the game modes I like. (There are a few others, but I don't like them, and they don't fit my theories, so I'm not going to talk about them. Take THAT Capture the Flag!)

    My favorite game mode is called "5 Capture Point" or "5cp". These maps tend to work better with larger teams and more experienced players, so we might not play any, but they're a good illustration, and they're my favorite, so I'll talk about them first.

    The name comes from the fact that there are five capture points, usually arranged linearly, and to win you have to capture all five. You capture a point by standing on it for a set period of time without any enemies standing on it. Some points take more time than others, and more people capturing at once capture faster.

    You have to capture the points in order so, the overall strategy plays like a tug of war. You start with everyone rushing to the middle, and whoever manages to take the middle point (often just called "mid") gets the momentum to keep pushing.

    There are TWO major game states that your team can be in: Pushing and Holding.

    Pushing is when you are trying to move into territory held by the other team.

    Holding is when you are trying to stop the enemy team from pushing into your territory.

    Once you "cap mid" your team is probably Pushing, and the other team should be scrambling to hold "second".

    This dynamic of push and hold is the key to TF2 strategy, and I think it's expressed most dynamically in 5cp. However, that can be a bit much for new players, and it's pretty easy for one team to win the mid fight and then just COMPLETELY STEAMROLL an inexperienced team for an easy win. That's not fun.

    However, there are a bunch of game modes that are SUPER FUN and express that same concept in simpler ways.

    For example, there's "Attack/Defend". I got started playing Attack/Defend, so I'll probably try to sneak a map or two in there.

    Attack/Defend is sort of like 5cp, only there is no back and forth. There's usually only two control points, and RED team starts with BOTH. BLUE wins if they can capture both points, and RED wins if BLUE hasn't captured the last point before the timer runs out.

    This is simpler because you don't have to worry about figuring out when it's a good time to push and when it's a good time to hold. If you're RED, you're holding. If you're BLUE, you're pushing. (Usually... Sometimes BLUE wants to hold for a bit to have time to heal, gather troops in one place, and build a good push. Sometimes RED wants to push BLUE back a bit so they can defend a chokepoint that's further from the objective. But GENERALLY speaking, BLUE pushes, and RED holds.)

    Then, there's PAYLOAD. Payload is actually one of the most fun game types in TF2.

    In Payload, BLUE is pushing and RED is holding, just like Attack/Defend. BUT in this mode BLUE is LITERALLY pushing, because their job is to push a cart with a GIANT BOMB in it all the way to the RED base! (Pushing the cart works like capturing a point. If a BLUE player stands near it, it moves forward, unless a RED player is nearby to stop it. More BLUE players push faster.)

    It's sort of like Attack/Defend, but pushes can be incremental, not all or nothing. There are checkpoints that you want to get the cart too along the way, but they're not dramatically important. They usually just move both teams spawns further down the tracks.

    Payload also gives a slight boost to the BLUE team, since the cart supplies nearby allies with health and ammo. Pushing is usually harder than holding, so having a mobile base a nice perk.

    Finally, there's King of the Hill.

    King of the Hill has that same back and forth push/hold/counterpush thing that 5cp has, but there's ONLY ONE POINT. You have to hold the point for a total of three minutes to win, which means that you don't LOSE as soon as the other team gets the point. This prevents those sudden and easy win/loss situations from 5cp and makes it easier to figure out WHEN to push and when to hold while still making you do BOTH.

    So, as you may have figured out, winning isn't about killing people. It's about being in the right place, or keeping the enemy from being in the right place. Killing people just magically teleports them back to their base, which means they're not in the right place anymore.

    This means you can be CREDIT TO TEAM without having to shoot a single enemy. You just need to know where to stand, and you can figure out where to stand by knowing your objective.

    \n And here's part 2:


    Rule #2 of TF2 is this:

    ALWAYS WORK AS A TEAM

    I just talked about knowing where to stand, and there's one factor to that beyond where the objective is, and that's where the rest of the team is. You don't want to be pushing or holding on your own.

    As a general rule, there are two players on your team who will dictate the location of your push and hold.

    When you're pushing, look for your Medic. The medic is the king of the push, even thought the medic can't really push themselves. This is because the medic can heal you while sticking close to the front lines (no one wants to push while they're hurt) and because the medic has the pusher's SECRET WEAPON, the Ubercharge.

    With a charged medigun, the medic can give their heal target NINE SECONDS of TOTAL INVULNERABILITY. That may seem short, but it's just enough to rush past a chokepoint and take out the key to the enemy's defense.

    If you see a few guys hanging around a chokepoint with a medic, they're getting ready to push. Wait nearby and go in when they do.

    When you are holding, find your Engineer. The Engineer is the king of the hold. They've got a dispenser that will heal you (not quite as well as a medic) AND give you ammo, and they've got a sentry gun that's super tough and deals a LOT of damage. If they've got both of those buildings set up, you're holding THERE even if there's no one else around. You might as well try to make it a good hold and join in.

    Engineers will sometimes try to set up a forward base to build a push from (they've also got a teleporter that can be really useful for this), like a Medic, and a Medic might try to lead a hold like an Engineer, but the Engineer is best when dug into a secure area and the Medic is best on the frontline, so Medics tend to push while Engineers hold. Still, those guys both tend to shape their teams position on the battlefield.

    Clever players will have also noted that the ENEMY team's location must also be an important consideration. They are RIGHT. If you see an enemy Engineer setting up a sentry, let your team know. If you see an enemy Medic hiding in a chokepoint building Ubercharge, let your team know. Good communication wins games!

    Now, the second part of working as a team is knowing the strengths and weaknesses of the classes and picking a class that will help your team win the objective. (It's all coming together.)

    Say you're defending on a Payload map. There should probably be an Engineer on your team. Is there? If not, you should play Engineer! If there is, can you play something that supports an Engineer's weaknesses? There are two major threats to an Engineer's sentry: Spies and Ubercharges. Pyros are helpful against BOTH, so if you play a Pyro and defend the sentry gun, your Engineer will love you. (Can you tell that I play Engineer?) Does your team have an Engineer AND a Pyro? Try a Sniper or a Demoman. Both of those guys are great for rounding out a defense.

    Maybe you're on the attacking team. Do you have a Medic? If not, play a Medic! Your whole team will love you! If you do, they're going to want to be healing someone, so which classes are best to heal? Heavy Weapons Guy is the "classic" Medic buddy. He's got the highest health, so he benefits most from a Medic's overheal (Did I mention that the Medic can heal you to %150 of your original health? Those guys ROCK.) and are most likely to survive being shot in order to get healed. They also can get the totally awesome Sandvich, which can heal them. Try not to do that though. You can ALSO right click to give the Sandvich to someone else. If you use your Sandvich to keep your Medic alive, you will be INVINCIBLE (note: not actually invincible) and also CREDIT TO TEAM (note: actually a credit to the team). Is there already a Heavy around? Demomen and Soldiers also play well with Medics, and are useful guys to have around. Play one of them! Scouts also capture points and push carts twice as fast as anyone else, so they're good to have around. They can also be useful for running after the snipers who are bound to be trying to shoot the heads of your Heavy and Medic! Feeling DEVIOUS and BOLD? Play a Spy! Spies are great at compromising enemy defenses, but can't do much on their own. They're awesome at creating an opening right before a push though. If you've got a solid offense running into a solid defense (especially one backed by a good Engineer) a good Spy can make all the difference.

    Anyway, I'm going to take a break for now. The important thing to take away from this is that there are THREE reasons that TF2 is great:
    1. It's a fast paced and light hearted game with a fun atmosphere!
    2. It's a deep game that rewards strategy and teamwork!
    3. Hats! HATS! HAAAAATS! Seriously though, you get to wear silly hats. If you think wearing silly hats is a bad thing, maybe you shouldn't be playing TF2...

      Heck, I'll just make the post.

      HOW TO PLAY PYRO
      Step 1: Walk around burning things. If you can do this, you can play pyro to at least basic ability. You can get along just doing this, and actually doing pretty good from time to time.

      Step 2: Learn your drawbacks, and what fights to not pick. You've got two big problems: survivability and range.
      Survivability means you have not that much health. Big hits can put you down quite quickly. The big things to look for are Soldiers and Demomen, and keep a weather eye out for Snipers. The first two have explosive weapons, which deal damage quickly. Pyros can't burn you, and Heavies can be strafed if you can get in close, so it's mostly those guys that make problems.
      Range means you don't have any long-range weaponry. In fact, your secondary weapon is generally situational/backup, and your melee weapon can get kinda useless if you still have fuel. The threats are, again, Soldiers and Snipers. Well, plus one more...
      The Sentry Gun.
      The Sentry Guns set up by Engineers do horrible things Pyros. They can get you at long range, with near-perfect accuracy, and can use explosive rockets at Level 3. Engineers hate everything you do since you're great at busting up support structures like Dispensers, but Sentry Guns tear you up.

      So what fights do you pick or not pick? Large groups, if caught unawares, are good picking. But avoid long corridors of any kind - the long-range classes can blow you away from the ends, and if it's a blind corner you could be walking into a Sentry Gun. Also beware picking fights with other Pyros - they don't get lit on fire, which makes them harder to kill. Hunt Snipers because they have a hard time once you're up next to them, hunt Medics because the enemy needs them, hunt Heavies because the big slow brutes can be strafed around while you deal good damage, and hunt Spies for a reason you'll see below.

      Step 3: Learn to play as a team. This class operates okay even if you pretend you're all alone against the other team, but it's always better to be a team player. As a Pyro, you have two best friends: Medic and Spy.
      Medics can't Overheal you very much since that's percentage-based, but as a Pyro you're one of the classes that can do the most damage in the shortest time, making you great at Ubercharge. Plus, Ubercharge is one of the best ways to smash irritating Sentry Guns.
      The Spy, on the other hand, can get rid of everything that plagues you - they can get up close without being noticed to remove long-range threats, and can sap Sentry Guns to let you get right in there.
      But getting help is only half the battle! How do you help your team? A couple ways. The most obvious is that you burn everything in everyone's path. Less-obvious are your defensive roles.
      As a Pyro, you excel at Point Coverage. At least in Attack/Defend, many Capture Points have nice out-of-sight spots to hide in. Stick by a point, and when enemies arrive you can pop out, immediately in your optimal range. Lone Scouts running out ahead are especially vulnerable. Additionally, the second point also helps with coverage:
      Spy-Checking is the Pyro's secret skill. See, when you swing your flames over a spy, they light on fire. It doesn't matter if they're disguised or not, they light up. TF2 doesn't have friendly fire (haha) though, so if you light up someone "on your team" you found an enemy spy! Those spies who are running around invisible? They still burn, and the fire's visible. It's harder to catch them, but it becomes a helpful part of Point Coverage. When you're on the point, at relatively frequent rates do a basic little sweep around with your flamethrower, see if you catch anything. Additionally, whenever you pass a friend (and personally I do it almost EVERY time) give them a little puff of your flames to make sure they don't start burnin'. This is what makes you invaluable to Engineers - a well-placed Sentry Gun can take care of everything it sees, and a Pyro to check everything that comes near keeps the Spies away.
      Note to all new players, not just Pyros: As far as I can tell, disguised spies can't swap weapons. Shuffling your weapons around at random while running around shows you're not a spy. It can be helpful to people who're concerned about spies - like me!

      Step 4: Your Compression Blast. With your flamethrower, press Mouse 2 to let loose a blast of air. This is the most versatile thing a Pyro has, and probably the most underutilized. Personally, I'm not very good with it, but that's also because my weapon of choice gives me only 4 uses rather than 10 from a full fuel tank.
      What does it do? The easy examples: Use it to blow out allies on fire and use it to push back enemies who get a little too close.
      The more advanced examples: Reflection, clearing, and breaking Ubercharges. The airblast bounces away rockets coming at you, allowing you to turn a Soldier or Level 3 Sentry Gun on itself. This is the best counter you have against these threats. Clearing is a technique against the Demoman's Sticky Bombs - if there's sticking bombs on a surface keeping you out, a compression blast knocks them away. Breaking Ubercharges is the craziest of these skills - so you have an enemy medic ubercharging one of their guys. If you compression blast the medic away, once his beam is too far from his ally the charge turns off but the timer keeps counting, stalling their precious time.
      You can even juggle enemies in the air with successive compression blasts.

      Step 5: Your secondary gun. Largely unimportant, this is used as your best impression of a longer-ranged attack. The shotgun is okay, but I like the Flare Gun. Personally, I now use its cousin, the Detonator. If things are far away and you've nothing better to be doing, lob shots. Maybe you'll get lucky and light their Sniper on fire or something. As for your melee weapons, just try and not pick something that gives an active bonus even when you're holding the flamethrower. My personal favorite is the Powerjack.

      Step 5: Walk around burning things. Seriously, this is what you're good at. Everything else is gravy. You're a murder machine, just get up and kill them all.
      M1+W indeed.

Keep your Medic alive so they can build Uber!) so they're usually not worried about gradual damage, and the really dangerous stuff (crit rockets, sticky traps, ambush heavies, snipers, spies...) were going to kill you in one hit anyway.

      Medic loadouts are actually very preference based. The stock Medigun is probably the "best", and the stock Bonesaw is the worst, but there's a lot of choice available depending on your style.
