\n When I play games, I'm usually driving at the Revolutionist creative agenda, or "Call to Action" play.

I hear a couple different definitions for "Call to Action" being bandied about, here, so I want to Clarify what I mean. I mean playing games for the express intent of raising class consciousness and arming players with the tools of the forthcoming revolution.

Obviously, most games fail to support the Revolutionist CA out of the box. Dungeons & Dragons has some good meat to it, what with privileged tool-owners conducting genocide against the independent peoples, but it fails to address this content in a Call to Action way - instead prefering to focus on Gamist and Collectionist tenets.

Shock: Social Science Fiction (ShoSoc SciFi) is, of course, a veritable treatise on Call to Action play. Everything from the grid to praxis to the gender-deconstructivist language used throughout the text... my guess is that Joshua wrote Shock: as a proof of concept, that Call to Action could be fun and engaging play.

What is your creative agenda?

    \n My agenda is solidly Eternalist, and I'll stick by that regardless of the crap* that's been said about it recently.

    I'm frequently angered by the anti-Eternalist sentiment present in D&D4e, what with its "Endgame" mechanics, though they sometimes go by other names.

    For those new to Story Games, Eternalist play puts a priority on ensuring that the same setting is used in all future gaming sessions, creating a continuous - if not necessarily coherent - thread connecting all play experiences. This continuous thread is meant to survive even the death of the members of the group, who pass the torch on to younger players. This is related to Expansionist play with subtle differences, notably the lack of need to recruit every possible player.

    * Specifically by Ron Edwards

    \n It's funny you should mention Collectionist, Joe! I played in a group for years whose Agenda could only be identified as such.

    Now, when I posted in forums, people tried to pigeonhole us into Gamist play, and indeed more than one player would probably describe themselves as a "step on up" player. But really, when it comes down to it, proving our Mettle through various challenges was just a thin veneer for the DM to parade before us his sweet catalog of terrifying monsters and wondrous beings. The proof of this is that if a creature was peaceful, we never got to fight it! parading its peace-iness was enough to satisfy the GM's itch.

    And of course it was also our excuse to amass an amazing collection ourselves of magic items, armor tailor-made to our unique concepts, and an enchanted weapon for every occasion. Fighting stuff kinda-sorta Affirmed how cool these items were, but really just having them, on display in an imagined shrine in our heads, was the point.

    D&D 3.5 facilitated Collectionism very Coherently and Robustly. Not only did we have a great catalog of items to draw on, but Feats and Class Abilities themselves--Prestige Classes especially--made a wonderful addition to our collection of pretty shiny aspects to acquire and never use. We did have to tweak with Challenge Rating, Treasure by level and recommended party size, though--if the challenge was too fair and balanced, we might not fell the enemies in 1-2 rounds, tops--and then would be in danger of actually using a large number of our powers and items, diluting their purity. We also tried to port our Collectionist bent over to other systems, but it always fell flat--a system like Champions or Shadow of Yesterday where you can just make up any old item or power, then "reskin" it with whatever game mechanics you want, defeats the purpose of collecting it at all. If we could just make up a collection, we wouldn't need to play! it's a disgusting methodology that far too many games have adopted since the Champions opened that can of worms.

    Oh, and let me just say BEFORE ANYONE BRINGS IT UP, that "Collectionist" is NOT the same as the Collectivist Agenda. That kind of willful misunderstanding of what Collectionism is all about has polluted far too many potentially useful discussions, so just don't even start, OK? Someone else will have to elaborate on the point and methodology of Collectivism; I have no interest in it. That's what's meant by "dirty hippie gaming," I believe?

    Oh, and Collectionism is nothing to do with Preservationism, though that's an understandable mistake.

    \n (Oh, and from Yee's taxonomy.)

    The Griefist Manifesto: Right To Sabotage Others Fun

    Not limited to MMORPG's, Griefism takes on its ultimate form when done in the intimate setting of the tabletop. It takes raw courage to ruin other people's fun when you're not anonymous and they're sitting next to you at the table! While some may mistake killing another's PC or not sharing loot as part of the Griefist agenda, that is merely agendaless Griefing, and not Griefism. True Griefism mandates the detailed study of a game text in the search for ways to make it less fun for everyone else. For example, when playing free-form, a True Griefist would never pass up the chance to sabotage the fiction, and when playing Archipelago, a True Griefist would always use <<Try it another way>> until the other players simply give up trying to contribute to the story from exhaustion, and then say, "See? I told you it was a bad game," and go on to enjoy their smug superiority. But that's like shooting ducks in a barrel to the Griefist; games that really support the Griefist agenda, ironically, are the ones that try the hardest to prevent it. For example, Universalis with its democratic limits on narrative control and 3rd edition with its excruciating detail on how any fictional act is modeled in the rules. These texts give the True Griefist the opportunity to really shine...and if the True Griefist fails to break the game he can always drag it down to a crawl by harping on minutia.

    (Actually, I don't really know anybody like this. One or two of my friends might fall into this mode of play on occasion - the unfortunate byproduct of putting on their game-designer hat while playing a new game - or from their desire to Win - but never really with the intent to sabotage...)

    \n Transactionist, or Contend Now: we aim to move the focus of the gaming and of the techniques, lifting it off the various level of the ephemera and the system, and pointing it to the Social Contract above. Transcending the actual game, the atmosphere and the rules themselves, we aim at putting pure social capital at stake, bargaining and contending every opinion, exchanging vantage points, either for the pure joy of it, or to assess and establish power positions in the player group. It's even funnier if the player group is composed of friends, but one must not be soft just because of friendships.

    \n My creative agenda, Monologism, is of course one of the most seldom understood. The goal is not to be the only one talking - never let common English meanings of words be your guide in understanding RPG jargon, after all - but to simply direct your play, and other people's to the degree possible, to enable the best lines of dialog, the occasional stirring speech, and frequent, awesome, context-dependent jokes. (This is not about going off into arias of pop-culture quotation, a sadly common form of dysfunctional Monologism.)
