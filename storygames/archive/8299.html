\n There's this thing that's popular in Japan called the replay - basically a transcript of a play session. You can use it as a learning tool, an extended example, or they might be entertaining in their own right.

I'd like to hear from our Japanese-culture-savvy contingent to get a better feel for the purpose replays serve, and I also want to talk in general about the value of the extended example.

I'm thinking, for my current project, of including a replay, with links in the text to points where the current topic is illustrated. So you'd get a very clean presentation of procedural information, with notes saying "to see how this shakes out at the table, go to page 7 of the replay where Joel is tempting Jeff into narrative failure when he mechanically wants success."

As a way to organize information, does this seem useful or appealing? Does a substantial and complete example of play seem like something you'd value, particularly beyond an initial reading? Good examples in RPGs in English? Bad ones?

Replays! Go!

    \n Well, I skip the examples in rpgs consistently. So consistently in fact, that I don't even realize that they're there anymore. I only ever make an exception when the main text is so incomplete that it simply can't be parsed. In a Wicked Age, for instance.

    That being said, I sometimes enjoy the play fiction on my second read-through of some games. Can't remember any names right now, but I seem to remember that some game I read recently had some mildly amusing verbal stuff... ah, it was Heroquest! Heroquest has pretty interesting examples because they're funny, opinionated and form a greater whole that'd be called a replay if it were all collected together instead of being dispersed in the book. I just took my copy of Heroquest home after it spent a couple of years in Helsinki, and while I wasn't interested in reading the rules, I read through all the examples on my way home. That was some wacky shit, the designer seemed to think that the game'd be played by old-school adventure roleplayers.

    So my call would be that I'd do a replay only for artistic purposes, not to teach rules or procedures. Doing the latter through a replay is like teaching sciences in school by showing Procidis animations - perhaps entertaining, but ultimately futile and inefficient. Say what you want to say in a direct and structured manner, if you're going to say it at all. Save fiction for leisure.

    \n I there's a case to be made for text examples as an adjunct to procedural instruction, Eero. People absorb information in different ways, and it's often easier to demonstrate certain concepts than it is to explain them. It would suit me, as a reader, to have the crunchy bits all up front, and a colorful long example in the back that was both informative and fun to read, but I want to talk about it and weigh the possibilities.

    \n I wouldn't be able to follow most indie rpg texts without procedural examples. I need to switch between rules text and examples to figure out exactly what the other is talking about.

    I listened to the full transcript of the D&D 4e Penny Arcade. It had anticipation and amusement going for it back when it first aired. However, I still couldn't pay close attention much of the time. The most useful moments were when the GM interrupted the "normal" session to give rhyme and reason for the system, its mechanics, his techniques, etc. Or rather, those were the moments when the transcript as a whole seemed most useful. The moments themselves wouldn't have been as ding! outside the broader context.

    So I think you could have a rules text interrupted by relevant examples, or a transcript interrupted by explanations, the optimum being somewhere between. I'd stick closer to the former. With a transcript, a discussion of what's possible may be railroaded. If you say one thing is possible, but all the rest of your transcript is dependent on what actually happened in that session, then what's actually transcribed may be what your game seems to be really for.

    Ah, but then, maybe that's just lack of imagination on my part. A little experimentation probably wouldn't hurt, after all.

    \n From my observations, the replays in Japanese RPGs tend to be biased towards either introducing beginners to the game or serving as entertainment. The former is a standard thing in many J-RPG rulebooks, while the latter is published in bunko (Japanese paperback) format. I haven't yet bothered with the latter yet, but I'd imagine they'd be long enough to cover a campaign or a decent chunk thereof. (Magazines like Role&Roll also publish and serialize replays).

    The introductory replay in Maid RPG is good for showcasing the random-event-driven style of play, but I'm not sure it's all that great for much else. The introductory replay in Yuuyake Koyake (that other game by the same designer) has little asides that explain the rules, and is aimed at giving the GM/narrator a more complete understanding of how to go about things, both in terms of the GM's job and the game's unusual style. (This being a non-violent game about helping people and making friends, in which a session normally involves one to three NPCs).

    I am genuinely curious (and maybe Andy could shed some light on this?) how effective introductory replays really are. Not all J-RPGs have them (F.E.A.R. doesn't bother with them in any of their rulebooks, for example), and I wonder how much of their inclusion is just because of what's become convention.

    \n I think there are some distinctions to be made hereabouts. We've immediately fallen into talking about replays as being the same as examples. By my perception they aren't the same thing at all. An example is a made up transcript designed to explicate or clarify some procedure of play. I see replays potentially adding at least three additional elements:

    1) They're supposed to be entertaining in their own right
    2) They show how to play the game better, i.e. focus on technique rather than procedure
    3) They provide a snapshot of the culture* of the game

    I see all these things in the Maid replays, for example, and I don't see them happening much in rules examples.

    * By culture I guess I mean something like social contract + humor / inside jokes / tribal geekiness

    \n Interestingly, the second edition of Nobilis contains a long-form campaign excerpt that Rebecca Borgstrom (the author) noted, on one occasion that I can remember, gives a somewhat distorted picture of what she expects Nobilis play to look like. Specifically, it starts with a big combat example, because players of the first edition were always asking how to do big battles, but she didn't expect that would really be a major part of most games. In that specific case, then, using the example to demonstrate how the rules worked in certain situations (battles) actually ended up working against the goal of showing players how play was normally expected to go. That illustrates, I think, how complicated it can be to come up with examples that really do what you want.

    \n In the past, I've reformatted PbFs into transcripts. It occurs to me that they are probably easier to deal with than transcribing audio of actual play. I'm guessing that some appropriate art and formatting would make replays pretty pleasurable reading. If I were going to include one in a game I'd include it as an appendix and, in the PDF, hyperlink the crap out of it as Jason suggests (rule/technique to example and vice versa). In a book, I think I'd want the names of the players to be changed so that I wasn't distracted by knowing of them. As a separate thing (e.g. a kickstarter bonus, a collection, a chapbook) the replay would gain value if I recognized one or more of the players (e.g. the designer, a podcast crew, other notable personages). For session games with shared elements, I'd love to see a collection of replays bound together. For example, I'd totally read a replay collection for Lady Blackbird, Kagematsu, Mountain Witch, or Ghost/Echo to see how other people worked with the same material.
