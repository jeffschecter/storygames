\n
\n Okay, so here's my premise: There are people out there (NPCs) who end up with a tightly closed world wrapped around them ... the world has certain self-reinforcing points of view, and creates a pattern that stifles change.
\n
\nThis isn't some sort of metaphysical mumbo-jumbo ... this is just life. A humble young musician gets a big break, and gets surrounded by an accretion of agents, fans, groupies and sycophants. The more that happens, the harder it becomes for him to be anything but an arrogant, entitled celebrity: the expectations and actions of everyone around him reinforces that pattern. He gets trapped in the world of being a celebrity.
\n
\nAnd yet, there are people out there (PCs) whose strength is that they don't work in patterns. Rather, they make direct human connections. Those human connections are what a person locked in this sort of pattern needs, in order to change themselves and break out of their own pattern. Those human connections are made when someone makes themselves vulnerable to another person. They are pattern-breakers. Again, this isn't metaphysical, it's just my observation of life.
\n
\nSo PCs in this game are folks who get shafted by the patterns of another character's screwed-up world, and then act to change it ... often (if not exclusively) by revealing some human connection and vulnerability.
\n
\nSo, to create an NPC for a session, one obviously needs the world-view they're trapped in. I can model that. But the other thing that (I think) one needs is a sense of their potential interactions with the PC. How are they going to react to vulnerability and human contact? With longing? With fear? With disdain? All great options, and all ones that make for very different NPCs. A Lonely Celebrity is different from a Fearful Celebrity is different from a Disdainful Celebrity ... and they're going to create different landscapes in which for the players to tell their stories.
\n
\nI just don't know how to break this thing down properly into formula. What is it, in understandable terms, that distinguishes these NPCs? How can I describe it in terms that will inspire, rather than confound? And what's the right break-down?

    \n I think this is the next-door-neighbor of an idea that I got from the recent (locked, flame-y) relationship map battle on rpg.net.

    One aspect of Sorcerer r-maps that is not necessarily true of r-maps in general is that the relationships listed there are permanent - now, the text that was quoted in the thread emphasized family relationships but I can also see worth in recording relationships that are about events that happened in the past. ("Cheated out of $50 grand", "fired after she resisted sexual harassment", and so on.) These are immutable - or, maybe more accurately, all change that occurs along that relationship "line" is additive. You might make it up to the guy you cheated out of $50 grand by paying him back and setting him up with a hot girlfriend but you can never un-cheat him. It's not just family that we can't change - we can't change our past either.

    Then (by contrast) there are relationships that can change, in other words, the stuff on the line is not connected to immutable defining events.

    Now, the difference between the two sorts of relationships is itself different for different kinds of stories. Here I am thinking of a Vampire r-map from the early nineties in which two powerful vampires are described as enemies. The line there isn't "you fucked me over, I fucked you over, that can't be changed", because that's inherent in the relationships of powerful vampires, due to the setting. They can have fucked each other over 100 years ago and still be as good a friends as it's possible for vampires to be. The immutable part of the relationship is "we are both powerful vampires in a world where powerful vampires' relationships are founded on distrust and paranoia".

    So if you want a game where it's easy for relationships to change, you give a big amount of latitude to the mutable bits of the R-map and not so much to the immutable bits. If you want a game where it's not-so-easy, you give a lot of latitude to the immutable bits and not much to the mutable bits. (This is how classic Western tragedy does things.)

    Anyway, that's the half-baked idea your comment reminded me of. Interesting stuff.

    \n It's the cousin to something I'm working on as well - "burnups". A burnup is something your character values, a secret they hold, an important relationship, something that defines them very centrally - and when they put it at risk that releases considerable power.

    My implementation is that you roll the dice representing the thing; for purposes of the conflict, all of them succeed, but any that would, under the usual rules, fail are lost and you can't use them again, plus your character gets more serious consequences from those failures than would otherwise be the case.

    You're focussing on the NPCs, though, Tony; I think they need some kind of "handle", a vulnerability that, when pushed against, evokes - not change, presumably, given your premise, but perhaps a desire that things were different or a resistance to things being different.

    \n What if we incorporated a mechanic that was invented for revealing secrets in a game:

    Write on the front of an index card a change in a relationship/personality/status.
    Write on the back of the index card what has to be done to trigger that change.

    Put the index cards out face down where all the players can see them:

    So they see on the back: "Uncle Steve loses all his money". If that happens, you turn it over and see: "Uncle Steve is happier with his new Simple Life."

    \n Hey Tony,

    I have to say, the title "Misery Bubblegum" just doesn't have any resonance for me. But when you describe the game like this, I'm hooked. It's a game about interacting with gods.

    It seems to me that for the celebrity, their reaction is a question of identity. How invested are they in who they've become. The celebrity identity gives them power in their interactions. And it protects them. The consequence of responding to the humanity (i.e. the expression of vulnerability) of the NPC is that the celebrity identity will be altered, and the alteration will have consequences.

    If you have a GM running the celebrities, what you need is to give some structure to the celebrity identity, how the celebrity is empowered and protected, so the GM can simply decide on the reaction of the NPC, and everyone's on the same page about what that means for the identity of the NPC relative to his life and his relationship with his identity reinforcing peeps.

    \n "Pick one from A and one from B" is always more fun than "Pick one from A," especially (indeed, primarily) because you can build in paradox -- internal tension -- and that generates story. And "Perfectionist Idol" etc. sound very good.

    Question: Do NPCs need a whole different set of As and Bs to combine than PCs? I suspect they do, because fundamentally they're foils, waiting to react to the player-characters, who are the real protagonists. This requires figuring out clearly how the two sets of As and Bs need to be different.

    Posted By: misubaMaybe I'm dumb, but I'm not quite seeing how vulnerability in particular, as opposed to any other way of breaking through an NPC's protective shell of people and context, is the engine making this stuff go. Can you say a little more about that connection, Tony?
    Absolutely.

    I think that, in the fiction of this genre (shoujo manga, mostly) the dysfunctional worlds that these NPCs inhabit are held together by people's drive not to be hurt and humiliated. These worlds are, themselves, a way that people avoid being vulnerable. The Idol might not be happy, he might not be satisfied, he might in fact be actively miserable ... but he's not forced to expose himself. And, since he hasn't exposed his feelings in so long, his fear of doing so is compounded by massive ignorance. He fears more than just the consequences of being vulnerable ... he fears the unknown.

    The core story of the genre (as best I can dig it out this morning) is "PCs encounter such a person. PCs want to break through that shell (for various reasons ... love, friendship, hatred, compassion, rivalry, principles, practicality, etc., etc.) By caring about breaking through that shell, the PCs make themselves emotionally vulnerable. The NPC sees a person who, unlike every person inside his structured world, makes themselves vulnerable ... and he sees that being vulnerable, and even being hurt, is not so bad. After seeing that, he cannot view the prison he's hiding inside in the same way."

    It's sort of like GMing for a turtling player: You can't, actually, break through their shell. They have to want to come out ... and they want to come out when their connections to what is happening outside exceed their fear.

    That's my fuzzy description of why I think vulnerability is the tool that makes a difference.
