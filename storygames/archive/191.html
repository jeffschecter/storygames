    I've always been a fan of Grand Unified Mechanics that handle all sorts of situations in the same way (which is basic Forgethink, of course). Then I encountered "Dogs in the Vineyard"* and discovered the fundamental coolness of having rules to generate situation, and of having conflict change your character.

    So in "apocalypse girl" I've gone one further: Conflict resolution is character generation is situation generation -- it's all one mechanic. Unresolved conflicts, on the table, are symbolized by back-and-forth links that look like a relationship map (because, y'know, what is a dramatically interesting relationship except an unresolved conflict?). The only possible stakes of a conflict are whether you change a character (or aspect of a character), and how. The only way to create and modify a character is through resolving conflicts.

    So I feel very Holy Grail-y about this, but I suspect no one else cares. And it may be, in fact, tightly integrated for fun play.

    I'm still totally grooving on my notion for Misery Bubblegum that the GM-role, the whole shebang, rotates from one player to the next, and that what you do when someone else is a GM gives you the resources you need to pursue your own goals when you're the GM.

    Rather than making a new and unintuitive structure that people need to learn from scratch, I'm just taking the idea "The GM runs things, but the other players get input and can screw up the GMs plans" and adding one teeny-tiny tweak ... and suddenly you've got a much more open system. I'm not throwing away RPG tradition, I'm subverting it.

    Plus, I get to have a game that has both a Storyteller and a GM, at the same time (and often at odds). I crack myself up!

    My project of the week is Waiting for the Queen/Tea At Midnight, my third two-player chat-based game about China, and my personal contribution to Push vol 1.

    It's a game that's all about creating a single scene (a first meeting) between two fixed characters (Gai Zheng & Ruhi Nankachema) and it's back-constructed from early text-RPGs of the "get lamp" variety. Play consists of choosing one of a limited number of fixed statements and attaching it to your character's name with an adverb. For example: Chema + reluctantly + picks up a bucket.
