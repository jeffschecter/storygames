\n Last weekend I ran a game of Dogs in the Vineyard at the local con, which far exceeded my expectations. None of the players had any experience with the game and I knew only one of the players from a game last year. I started off with a short introduction into the setting, highlighting the aspects of the Faith which would come into play in the town. One thing that seems to be inherent to Dogs is the tendency of new players to make snap judgements with their characters, trying to nail NPCs on some slip of the tongue and generally build their characters up to be McCarthyist witch hunters. One player was especially prone to this which before long led to a lot of infighting among the Dogs. (He later said that years of playing Vampire had hardened him to character violence and injustice, which I think might have been one of the reasons for this). In fact, all of the conflicts we played were mostly about arguments about how to proceed. Only the very first conflict included an NPC opposing them in some way. Still the way they played out was riveting and exciting and I realized that there was a simple reason for this.

Narration is slave to the character and slave to the world.

Whenever somebody wanted to roll in a new trait, Raise or See their narration had to fit the character and make sense as part of the game world. I tried to made sure that everybody kept an eye on that. Something that we handled a little more loosely in my regular group (with both Dogs and Galactic), which weakened the overall experience. This is probably obvious to all of you, but it helped me to realize that being strict about these things - even though they occasionally lead to no-win situations for some players - strengthens the overall game and makes it far more involving. Once you accept that your character is simply screwed sometimes (regardless of whether you can come up with a bit of narration to help him out), the story gains much more weight and the situations become more intense.

It's a bit of a contradiction of course. Both character and world are after all just the sum of more or less freely chosen narration, but holding integrity of character and world sacrosanct is essential for the game to work. Evolving one or the other is ok, radically changing them to have your character come out on top or to steer the story in another direction is not.

Any other "things you mustn't forget when playing"-advice?

    \nTo Give is better than to Take (more Fallout that is)

    You see, this "no-win" situation players find themselves in are more often than not an intrinsic part of pacing. Take for instance, the DitV NPCs - You roll them up, and assign traits on the spot. Why is that so? IMHO, it's a pacing mechanism: In the first conflict with an NPC, the GM has good chances to win, because he can easily find aspects the Dog(s) currently can't hold up against. BUT as he is revealing them during the conflict, the Dogs are at an advantage in following conflicts - You can send a Dog that handles the NPC's traits better, or wait for/create a situation where the aspects are disadvantageous to the NPC. It all leads to a common form of story - getting to know your opposition while losing, but winning in the end.

    \nSometimes, to take is better than to Give

    I had a recent game in which one Dog was trying to convince his cousin's wife to take her back after she had strayed from the faith. He made an insulting remark and the Dog escalated to violence and punched him. I looked at my dice and I had nothing, everyone started saying "I think you better Give." But I was thinking "But wouldn't it be cool if in the process of trying to convince him to take his wife back, the Dog accidently killed his cousin's husband." I took the fallout and narrated how the punch connected knocking the man off the porch backwards, down the stairs. He was grievous injured and we had an immediate healing conflict to save the man's life. In the end, everyone lived happily ever after.

    \nWhen you Take the Blow, the Raise happens

    This is something we didn't handle well in our first few Dogs games. It has a huge impact on how conflicts play out, and keeps them from being a simple game of dice and numbers. Here's the simple example: You raise with "I gouge your eye out." I look down at my dice, and I decide if I take the blow here, I can use my big dice to beat you. So I Take the Blow. So long, eye! I just got my eye gouged out. If I want to keep my eye, I need to come up with a Block/Dodge or Reversal.

    This gets to be really important when you have a raise like, "I shoot the orphan right in front of you." Are you really going to just "play the dice game" and take the blow there?

    \n I think Eric (accidentally?) hit on a very important point about DitV.

    There has to be more to your game than dice and numbers.

    If you disengage from the 'story' and don't care about what happens to your character or anybody else's the game dies. It falls apart and becomes just a highly modified version of Yahtzee. That's why the reflection phase after the town is important. It's there that you talk about what you thought about the characters you met and/or played. It's where everybody at the table gets to hear other people's take on this story they just finished. Although paying close attention never to criticise or judge the player for how I perceived his character and talking about how I felt about the NPCs I've played.

    I don't believe in making players care about NPCs or their lot. It runs too close to emotional manipulation for me. But I do want to make it easy for the players to care, if they choose to do so. I don't want to put stumbling blocks in the way of sympathy. That's why I try to avoid black hat villains in my games. They're just people. Everyone of them. Even the sorcerers are just trying to do the right thing. They are (or at least used to be) good people who made mistakes and wrong choices for which others had to pay the price. Fire & brimstone justice is easy, when you have some murdering, raping sociopath terrorising a village. It gets messy and challenging when you actually have to deal with human beings, who are as frail, insecure and confused as you and me.

    Admittedly, I can understand why one might not find this appropriate fun for a roleplaying game. I do, though.
