Okay, so in my Heroquest games there are these people who have surrendered their identities to "The Mystery". They're kind of like Men in Black; they're anonymous, and magically forgettable. Anyone who interacts with them has trouble remembering the interaction, except for others of their kind.

Originally, I had thought of a mechanic for handling the case of a Mysterious NPC meeting a non-Mysterious PC. At the BEGINNING of the encounter, we'd roll the contest to see how much the PC remembered, and play out the scene at the level of detail that the result implied. For example, in a minor defeat:

"He asked some questions about people you knew. You don't remember exactly who, though. Did you give him a hard time?"
"No, I was too shaken. I told him everything." (This may be the result of another contest)
"Okay. After that, he gave you some instructions, but you don't remember those, either. Then he left."

After having worked this out, at this point it strikes me that this is a very actor-stance way of going about the interaction. It puts the player firmly into the point of view of the character directly after the encounter, wondering what happened and trying to fit the pieces together. An author-stance way of going about it would let the player in on the whole conversation, and then roll for the memory magic at the end of the conversation, and let the player play his character's ignorance at a remove.

So I think for this kind of situation I'm going to give the player a choice of how to proceed, depending on whether he wants to know what his character missed, or not.
