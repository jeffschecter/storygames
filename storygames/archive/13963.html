\n Every so often, when someone starts a project - a game, a forum, a magazine, a competition - someone will reply with this question, in some form.

It always annoys me a little. "Why?" Well, because they want to, of course. "It doesn't seem fun to me!" Okay? But so what?

That's all I wanted to say, really. Not very constructive, I guess.

(Of course, now you can reply: "So you're annoyed. So what?", which is perfectly legitimate.)

    \n I like the formation "how is this fun?" Perhaps "What's fun about this?" is even better. There's an element of skepticism, sure, but also of open-mindedness, like, "I'm taking your word for it that it IS fun, but I'd like you to explain it for me so I can understand the fun better!"

    This is how a lot of constructive critique and feedback works. When someone asks a challenging question, they're not saying "I don't believe you, prove it to me!" they're saying "I believe you, and maybe even already agree and understand, but if you articulate it it'll be helpful to both of us."



    [Forgecontroversy]
    This is also the purpose of the question "Can you describe an actual play situation that demonstrates what you're talking about?"

    \n To what Ross said. I agree 100%

    If the fruit of the game is fun, but the peeling of the fruit is catharsis or intellectual debate or problem solving or community building or even inner reflection... what is wrong with that? The THEORY OF FUN speaks to the idea that FUN is an inherent tool of growing up. All cultures engage in fun, and without knowing it, learn about the world around them. A child would never want to realize that sharing his hot wheels is teaching something about the universe around him, but from a "book-learner" point of view, that's exactly what play and fun teach.

    However, the older we get, the more SKIN we need on that fun because a video game about hot wheels wouldn't entertain the way killing zombies would. Or would it?

    I have been working closely with a small university on helping them build a game design program at their school. And one of the headaches I continue to encounter in dealing with Admin, Profs, and Students is the difference between games and entertainment. You'd be surprised how entrenched people get in their opinions without knowing a thing about how the gaming industries operate.

    "I don't like art. But I know it when I see it."
