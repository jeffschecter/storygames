19. Roach Alley

Why is it a damn cool place to fight?

Garbage is piled high against a heavy wooden box; sure-footed combatants can scale it to access second-floor windows.
AND
If knocked into the refuse, combatants will find themselves immersed in roaches. The hungry vermin swarm just beneath the surface of the trash.

    \n 30. Gasford County Park

    At one time a beautifully groomed park with stately ancient trees, three ponds and a bandshell, Gasford Park fell into a bureaucratic black hole when an attempt to transfer it from county control to city control failed. With no money allocated for its upkeep, the park is now an overgrown jungle, visited only by the homeless, the criminal and the desperate. However, if you look carefully, you can find narrow, twisty paths through the overgrowth -- perfect for ambushes.

    \n 17. The Capitol Building

    This is one of the few buildings that survived the Capitol Riots, which shook the city and the state to its very core.

    The mayor of the time made the choice not to restore the damage done to the building's exterior, in recognition of the acknowledgments that must be made to the discontented. If fighting were ever to break out here again, it would re-ignite a legacy of violent change and momentous civil action. These white marble steps and gothic buttresses hold an air of distinguish, but also of foreboding.

    Between two rundown tenement buildings is a long, narrow blind alley of misaligned ledges and skewed clotheslines from which petrified sheets dangle like leeches. Black sludge seeps through rotten basement windows.

    Fight amongst jump mines, electric fences, auto turrets, gas emitters, flame throwers, and automated saw blade tanks, and battle it out on the shoulders of the genetically mutated Cyber-Behemoths that shake the ground with their car-sized feet as they roam the border with just enough intelligence to recognize and annihilate any and all living creatures.

    \n 41. Industrial area docks

    This place is filled with cranes to swing in or use to grab your opponent, crates to throw at people, or throw people at, and trucks to run people over with. It's always bustling with activity, even at night, as the wheels of commerce never stop turning. Huge ships are always docked outside, and the cargo often presents opportunities for the creative fighter. An unloading of gallons of fuel can create massive explosions, and it's not uncommon to find shady weapons smugglers unloading heavy firepower disguised in easily breakable crates (but guarded by heavily armed thugs). Also, some of the cranes use powerful electromagnets. And cargo is often piled high, so that a forceful enough push (say, from a weight swinging in a crane) can topple it unto an unsuspecting opponent.

    \n 29. Senator Pallmire's Garden

    Yvil Pallmire made his fortune at YvGen the world's foremost genetics research lab. The only thing he was more passionate about than cold hard cash were his horticultural experiments. Here, in his garden, surrounded by four meters of stone wall and pointy wrought iron lie a symmetric pattern of gravel paths among the specimen plants, gazebos and glass-houses. Rumor has it that some of the plants move. And fast! The homeless dregs from Gasford Park rarely climb the wall into the garden and when they do, they rarely climb back out. For some reason, every morning -- even after a midnight brawl of epic destruction, everything is pristine.

    \n 39. Bogg Hill

    Bogg Hill is an old, or even ancient neighborhood of three and four story tall brick apartment buildings (often with stores or taverns in their ground floors or basements), narrow, sharply-sloped cobblestone streets and haphazardly strung electrical wires. Bogg Hill (so named as it was once a hilly island rising from the Great Gasford Swamp) is connected to the rest of the city by only by the narrow, treacherous Bogg Street that passes between the wilds of Gasford County Park and forbidding walls of Senator Pallmire's Garden. Few are willing to make that trip at night. In fact, it is generally easier to reach Bogg Hill from outside the city than within.

    The folk of Bogg hill are very insular, and families that moved there three generations ago are still considered "outsiders". If in a fight, a local is hurt or property is damaged, the folk of Bogg Hill, armed with kitchen knives, stout oak sticks and hastily pried-up cobblestones, will boil out like bees from a disturbed hive to defend their neighborhood, attacking all sides regardless of who caused the harm.

    \n 25. Maroni's Italian Cuisine, a big cafeteria-like restaurant, a workhorse for feeding the blue collar masses. Has a tired, run-down appearance with blinking, half-working neon lights. Beneath the layers of cooked garlic and tomato, it always smells of dirty dish water. While the tables and chairs provide ample fodder for shoving and bashing, the large, steamy kitchen's where the real action goes down.

    As the combatants desperately try to keep each other from reaching the chopping knives near the vegetable station, one grabs a fistful of dried spaghetti and strikes the other across her cheek. Recoiling from the sting, the other combatant's eyes lock onto an opened spice pot of ground cayenne pepper - whoosh! into her opponent's face. Then she reaches for the meat cleaver, lying next to the chuck roast, strings of fat stuck to it. Her opponent, half-blind, panics and runs, opening drawers and doors behind him - anything that will slow her pace. In desperation, he knocks over an industrial jar of olive oil - crash! all over the floor. She cannot react quickly enough and slips, falling hard by the stove. He stands over her, eyes running like faucets. "It's over, you bitch," and pulls down the vat of boiling water on top of her, feeling nothing as she screams.

    \n 32: Sunside Valley

    A year ago, Sunside Valley was called Valley Creek, and it was the worst slum area in the city. The crime rate was off the scales, and even the police hesitated to patrol the area. The current mayor built his election victory on promising to "rid the city of that cesspool Valley Creek". Apart from the merely cosmetic renaming of the area to Sunside Valley, he is evacuating the entire area. Anyone who can produce documents proving they own property in the area can sell it for good money (well, more than it's worth, anyway). All population is to be moved to other places, and the entire district will be demolished to make room for a luxury community. As a result, a third of the area is a construction site of luxury apartments, a third is a demolition zone, full of huge machines capable of wreaking havoc on the old buildings of Valley Creek, and the final third is still battered old run-down houses, about to fall apart and inhabited by squatters and criminals. At night, and sometimes during the day, the demolition machines are attacked with explosives, firearms, baseball bats and all sorts of creative, home-made weapons that can sometimes be found lying around even during the daytime.

    \n 22. The Steamworks

    At the turn of the century, this huge industrial complex was the bleeding edge of technology and modernity. Now it is half abandoned, and the remainder is only in use due to the availability of dirt cheap labor from the neighboring slums, the city's disinterest in enforcing any form of labor protection or job safety laws, and because it was built to last.

    At the center is a huge, central steam plant. It is hot as the hinges of hell, and surrounded by mountains of loose coal. The air around the plant is full of smoke and sparks, and visibility is very limited. The workers, wearing kerchiefs across their mouths and stripped to waist, wield black iron shovels that make excellent melee weapons in a pinch.

    Steam pipes, large enough for a man to crawl through, go everywhere, both under and over the ground. Some are always full of superheated steam, some are never and used, and some are used only some of the time.

    Surrounding the steam plant are various factories full of huge gears and steam-driven machinery. Some are long abandoned, full of rats and broken glass and rust, some have been carefully boarded up for future use, and some are still in use. The latter are the most dangerous, as steam and unprotected moving parts are everywhere. The roofs are mostly wrought iron frameworks holding glass panels to let in the light, but many are panels missing or replaced by plywood.

    \n 8) Bruno's Ski World

    On the very edge of the city -- where the first dump was located 150 years ago stands a testament to market-driven land-use reform. In only twenty years, "Uncle" Bruno Vergerhofenskiwrldn transformed the rat- and chemical-infested mountains of concrete chunks, smoldering mattresses and ancient bullet-pocked refrigerators into the city's greatest entertainment asset.

    From 10 AM to 1 AM every day of the year, people can be found skiing, snow-boarding and tubing down the multitude of slopes available at BSW. A common fun-for-the-whole-family scenario includes medicated three-year olds tubing in classes on the bunny slope while their mothers watch hunky olympic ski-jumpers practice and their fathers drink at the Barcade(tm) and drool over busty teen-age attendants.

    At 1 AM the slopes close, the lights go out, the network of three dozen ski-lift-lines slide to a halt and the snow machines kick on. One million cubic feet of snow are generated every night for Bruno's operation -- much of it shooting through the air for hundreds of feet for precise placement on the slopes.

    Fighting at BSW during the day has an incredible array of moving obstacles, both on the ground and through the air, but worse -- Bruno has been known to get testy when the fighting disrupts his smoothly oiled revenue machine. At night, unpredictable and massive jets of ice crystals can make the fight tricky, but come morning, all signs of your activity will be hidden by a pristine coat of snow.

    \n 16 - The Mausoleum of Darker Arts
    \n
    \nOnce a great burial place for the great families who founded the city, it nows sits deep below the subway lines above it, gathering energies from the riders above. Decades ago, a cult found this place once more and uses it to sanctify ancient rites to dark Gods. You got here just in time to stop the latest ritual!
    \n
    \nWhy this place is cool for a fight - Lots of old, soggy, moldy rugs and tapestries to pull down on people or set fire to. The pace is off center after the Great Quake and water has risen up to cover some of the lower levels and part of the floor here in the Rotunda.
    \n
    \nWhen the subway trains pass over, all sorts of noise vibrations shake the place, and a sickly green smoke falls into the center of the pentacle in the room (is this the soul-essence of the riders?)
    \n
    \nThe Cults use a variety of occult paraphernalia, including a huge altar, a golden pentacle, and deep red and black robes. Candles burn in ancient fireboxes from long ago. Shadows dance here. All of these can give the right person an edge in a fight.
    \n
    \nLest we not forget, the honored dead of this city's founding surround us. What if they rose up to join either side (to aid the necromancers who abuse their former lives or the heroes who come to set things right at last)?
