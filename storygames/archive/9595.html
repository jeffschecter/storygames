\n I've played a handful of 4th edition D&D (literally, 5 or less sessions), and all the fun I had was within combat*.

I've also played a bit of Warmachine, and had a great time.

So: I'm thinking that I may really love 4e as a pure wargame. Make up a PC class, have her + a bunch of MM monsters be my army, then go at it against someone else's army. This sounds like a lot of fun. The rules are clearly very good for this sort of play, and I like the idea that my characters can die and then come back next game. This would seem to make pull-no-punches combat front and center. I don't like (in the context of 4e) having to clear out a bunch of mooks that I know I can kill with ease. I want a challenge, and the 4e combat system seems to provide this for me. Also, skills can play into this: one could list all the skills and how they would interact with certain terrain or combat situations to grant bonuses in combat.

In short: cut out the roleplay shit and use 4e as a minis/counters/scribbles-on-graph-paper wargaming system. I know there's already a D&D minis game, but I don't care 'bout that, and I'm not sure the status of that right now, anyway.

Anyone tried this? Does this sound like fun to anyone?




*I haven't had a chance to play the skill challenge system, and while I acknowledge it as very cool, I don't really care about it for my purposes here.

    \n D&D monsters aren't really built for this purpose, though. They're built to fight PCs. If you have monsters fight other monsters, they don't really function as designed. Some will be wildly more useful, some will be wildly less useful.

    Two examples of fights where monsters used inappropriately didn't work right:

    A) In our 4e Spelljammer game, we acquired three Umber Hulks as crew members on our ship. Umber Hulks are elite monsters two levels or so above the actual PCs. We then had a big githyanki space pirate fight*, which was supposed to be balanced XP wise with the Umber Hulks as allies.

    It was possibly the worst 4e combat experience I've had. Not bad for the PCs at all, since we never were in noticeable danger. But three umber hulks pumping out area of effect daze and push as a minor action meant the githyanki basically never could accomplish anything. Meanwhile, the githyanki's defenses were a little bit too difficult for the PCs to hit, so we couldn't do much damage to them. so the fight dragged on and on.

    Now, the Umber Hulk's gaze ability works fine as a monster ability on an elite versus PCs: area of effect Daze reduces the PC pool of action, meaning the Umber Hulk isn't at a disadvantage for the economy of actions. But Umber Hulk as allies to the PCs create unfun fights.


    The Orcus versus Demogorgon fight which they even mention is not how the rules are intended to work. Orcus wins the fight with a single lucky attack. Now, fighting against a group of epic level PCs (Orcus's intended opponents), the instant death attack is fine: they probably have a half dozen ways of rising from the dead before the end of the encounter, and at worst have to wait out the fight until the party Cleric can do the Raise Dead ritual. And even if the PC stays dead the whole fight, there are still three to five other PCs on the battlefield fighting Orcus, so the PC side isn't out as much. But against another Solo monster, Orcus's instant death attack is much stronger, and way swingier in how it affects the battle.



    Bottom line: the designers and developers made these guys to fight Player Characters, put up a good fight, but ultimately lose. And they do this job pretty well. But the monsters weren't designed with fighting other monsters in mind, so they'll be a lot more haphazard in terms of effectiveness and fun factor.



    *I was very pleased to see how the Githyanki racial encounter power makes them perfect space pirates. But that's a different thread.

    \n plus in the older version you had three levels of monsters and characters - can't remember the term, but it was Yellow (weakest), Blue (medium) and Red (strongest). Usually there was some element of the mini painted with the corresponding color, but otherwise was the same sculpt/mini, so you potentially could replace a piece as it wins at scenarios with the next strongest piece. The pieces go up in stats and point values depending on the color, and some rare (unique) pieces you could actually remove and insert a new dial depending on how strong you wanted them to be.


    In later editions of Mage Knight 2.0 they added spell books (basically cards you could play for in-game effects that added to the point total of your army build) and magic items (same as spells) you could "slot" into a figure's base.

    \n Just as a reaction to all the people insisting that playing D&D4e as a fairly pure wargame can't be fun.

    At last year's Dragonflight (Seattle area con) there was dealer that was running a "D&D skirmish" event, which basically consisted of grouping people together, helping them make characters, teaching them the solo-dungeon stuff from the DMG, and setting them loose. The players also ran the monsters they encountered according to the tactics described for that monster in the MM. A generous supply of minis and dungeon tiles was made available.

    My group played for four hours, and I had a *great* time. We (barely) killed a young White Dragon. I walked away with a 2nd level Dwarven Warlock in my character folder who I'd love to play some more.

    So yeah, it works
