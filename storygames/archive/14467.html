\n Yesterday we played our second session of Technoir. I was the GM. Andy, Lisa, Mark, and Matt played PCs.

Ostensibly we were playing by the newest beta edition. However, the copy Andy printed out was difficult to read, so we ended up using my 11.05.16 edition as reference a majority of the time.

Our total play session was about 4 hours long. We spent about two hours making characters and discussing the system before we got into our first scene. There was only one Contention which went uncompleted. I'll come back to that Contention in detail in a bit.

Character Generation
The only quibble I have with making characters is the section on assigning relationship adjectives. While I enjoy the creative constraint it provides, I have to say that it felt like an overly long and wasted step. We fleshed out relationships to NPCs before we even met them. Before we even knew with certainty that we would meet them.

Otherwise, I enjoy character creation quite a bit.

Objects
It's a minor complaint, but I feel that some of the object tags are really quite weak. I like it when tags either add something to the narration or are limited in scope. "Short Range" limits me and adjusts my narration. "Blunt" doesn't do anything for me.

A katana has "deadly"? Really? What does that add to the contention? Does one really need to be inspired to kill with a katana? I'd go with "stylish", myself. Everyone knows you can kill with a sword. Tell us something else about it.

Transmissions
I love them. I eat them up. Without any preparation at all, without having to take time out from helping players make their characters, I was able to create a plot seed. That plot seed inspired multiple potential starting scenes. The moment a player started interrogating a contact, I rolled for where it might lead. Bam! Plot was added that I never would have considered. New story emerged.

I never once hesitated to come up with an awesome scene, even though I really had no idea where the plot was taking us. And yet, I still had the sensation of a fully formed noir plot revolving around us. It's brilliant.

Contentions
I do not like them. The word "broken" got tossed around our table once or twice. I don't think contentions are broken. I just don't like them. I like the idea that PCs can get hurt in such a way as to force them into more cyber, but I think I can honestly say that this is the only aspect I do like. The rest turns me off.

To be more specific without digging too deeply at the game, I don't like the way any of the three options for removing a character from the contention work. That is, I enjoy the idea that characters can give, get killed, or fall out from excessive damage, I just don't like the mechanisms that Technoir uses to support those three options.

While all the other parts of Technoir I don't like can be house-ruled, contentions are a deal breaker for me. If I am going to play this game again, it will be with the contention system full stripped out and replaced with something else. A system still gritty and dangerous, but altered to provide the moments of decision and narration that I enjoy.

    \n It's not that it can't be hacked. It cannot be hacked to my satisfaction. There is simply too much that would have to change. One may as well re-write the whole thing.

    Andy has asked me to go into some details that we discussed at the table the other day. I'm going to try to do that, but I want to point out that the specific issues I'm about to outline are not the sole issues I have with the system. They are just the issues that are most important to me. Please bear in mind, when I say "issue", what I really mean is "a thing that was not fun for me".

    Also, I hesitate to write this up here because I'm not particularly interested in discussing my dislikes in public. I've expressed my opinion to my friends and I have received their feedback. I am posting my opinions here this evening for no other reason than feedback. And I'm not sure this feedback will have any value to the game's designer, as it is primarily composed of taste and opinion.

    The very top issue I have is with the giving in mechanisms. Specifically, what happens when the GM uses three push dice to kill the PC.
    *If the player of the PC does not have any push dice, the character dies and is removed from the conflict. Therefore, once you give up your last push die, you have opened your character to a sudden death.
    *If the player chooses to avoid death via a push die, the GM keeps the three dice they offered up to kill the PC and adds the one push die the PC player used to avoid death. Now the GM has at least four push dice. All other PCs in the conflict are now in greater danger of dying or being ousted from the conflict.
    *If the player does have a push die left to avoid the death of their PC, the choice they have to make is unsatisfying. Either the PC can be ousted from the conflict or they can be dead... and ousted from the conflict. The only benefit gained from accepting death is to remove the GM's push dice from play, so that the other PCs have a greater chance of surviving and succeeding in this conflict.
    *I love give in mechanisms. But I only love them when the situation escalation foreshadows future harm. I don't want to give in because my character lost his nose. I want to give in because my character is afraid to lose his nose. And, if I'm going to do that, I'd better save my points.
    *All these things add up to "pulling punches". I can't play to the hilt as a PC or as the GM without stepping into mechanisms that I don't enjoy.

    Please understand that my distaste for the give in mechanism is not due to any of those bullet points individually. It's the sum of the total that puts a bad taste in my mouth.

    I don't like the knock-out method of ending a conflict because I cannot depend on it. It's much too random. Like you said, Ralph, one hurt die could oust a character and a dozen might never get in the way. This rule can't do anything for me but disappoint.

    The idea that the GM should have his NPCs give in when the players have hit them with the an adjective that he thinks would make the NPC give up, or when the narration reaches its natural conclusion... I cannot properly express how much I don't like that. At least, not without unintentionally talking trash. Let me leave my opinion of that option at: No thank you. That's not for me.

    \n * Eric's comments re pulling punches are kinda huge. So, basically, we had a contention between my Vietnamese yakuza hacker ("Ngin3ar") and basically Angela Bassett-as-Strange-Days-street samurai ("Door") and two hitmen of sorts, "Nevada and Alaska". They were getting away, I hacked their car while Door punched them in their face-holes. This was the second contention, so Eric (GM) had two Push dice from before, and we each lay one Stickied adjective on them, which meant Eric had, in his hands, 4 Push dice.

    This is when he looked down at his four dice, and the contention kind of ground to a halt because we started walking through what must logically come next, and since Antagonists succeed quite a bit in their areas of expertise, these were logical assumptions based on their success:

    -=Eric can have the NPCs run away with the damage they have now. Weakish.

    -=Eric pulls his punch and narrates in a Permanent 2-Push-dice injury (and that's what he ended up doing: Blast to the face, Door loses her eyes, will need cyber-eyes). But he's really kinda pulling his punches, because...

    -=...it's been about 15 minutes into the game, and the GM has the capacity to kill off a PC. 3 of those 4 dice narrated in; if successful, "You're dead, start over". Which does sorta fit the pulp nature of the game, but 15 minutes into the game? Hmmm.

    -= Or, and this is the other gotcha: Eric pushes the 3 dice to Lisa: "Door dies". Lisa has one Push Die left. She says, "No, I'm still alive" and to do that has to give Eric her Push die. That was Too Easy. Now Eric has *5* Push dice, and can basically turn to the next character who enters a Contention: "3 push dice, you die. Oh you don't? Then give me one of your dice. And now I have 6. 7. 8" and so on. And then we're still left with the conclusion of "needing to pull your punches with the system else PC death is pretty quick and fast as soon as they start tagging NPCs with Sticky/Permanent adjectives.

    To summarize: There's this cool gamey aspect of Technoir that is basically a blow-by-blow of narration and trading dice. But to basically keep the game fun, the GM and Players have to pull their punches, they can't simply go "all in". And here I don't mean "You have to restrain yourself from being a dick" or anything like that. But the GM has to aim to Sticky and Permanent on adjectives when they Could kill a PC, otherwise the game will get its first PC kill pretty much right after she gets 3 or 4 Push dice. And she could (without realizing the system implications) accidentally bully all of the players out of their dice by playing hard in the rules, "Your character dies. No? Then give me a Push Die. Now I have More."

    Now, after we realized this, we thought of all the emergent behavior of the players not pushing all their Push dice, basically trying to keep one behind in case they need it to resist death, and we indeed found that there is in fact this majestic, complex, and very interesting depth to the whole Use or Non-Use of Push Dice for the players... but it seems to be really undermined by the fact that when the GM says "Your character dies", if you don't want that to happen (and this condition can be met VERY early in the game, I'm not talking the end of a 4-hour game where that sort of thing seems likely and favorable to the story) you have to give away your only resource to stop it to happen again next time.

    How to fix it? Dunno. We thought of a few things, but all fell apart upon further thinking it through.
    I might posit that you may need another resource entirely to ward off death. Something like: All players get one "life stone". If the GM says "you die", then someone can toss in their own life stone to cancel that from happening: If the player themselves, then they are saved from their own skill and luck. If another player, then that character somehow saved the character-in-danger. And the 3 Push dice go away from the GM back to the Player whose character was in danger. You only get one ever (or one per session). Which means that the GM has some weight to play harder/really use the system to either whittle the players down through sticky/permanent adjectives, or aim for the kill to lower the party's Life Stones, which also means that death can happen if the GM plays hard, it just happens later in the game and not in the first 20 minutes. Something like that.

    * Ralph/Eric: The Wound Die system doesn't seem bad as-is. I actually like it a lot. When rolling a Verb, you have a 33% chance of rolling a 5 or 6 which is almost always a success (or a 16.5% chance of rolling a 6, which IS always a success). When you get wounded, you better use the Story Elements that help you heal (Escorts + Treat == This Game Wins, both Eric and I agree) or you are really risking the 33% chance that the Hurt die will come up 5 or 6, and take out all those 5s or 6es. That risk seems fine IMO. Most contentions we played out didn't go to more than 3 Hurt dice before concluding (in a Give In, see below). I don't see a problem there...

    * ...but I think we uncovered the problem with the math in the "Way that Contentions End without Giving In: Having your Hurt Dice cancel out all your Action/Push dice", aka "Force-Outs". By the math alone, I don't think Force-Outs will ever happen. That is to say, "unless you are rolling only 1-2 Action/Push dice and have 4+ hurt dice, someone will give in due to... well, basically boredom... before a Force-Out would ever happen." In many conflicts we are rolling 3 Action dice (to do things our character is good at). Think of the odds of rolling three dice (usually getting 2-3 separate numbers) and then rolling 2, 3, 4... even 5 or 6 dice and have them come up the exact same as those numbers you threw. In practice, we never saw it happen or get anywhere close to a Force Out.

    -= Which is kinda important, because many of the conflicts we were in, the players were very invested in success (and thus not conceding by Giving In), and the antagonists (as portrayed by the GM) were very interested in not failing. For example, in the street fight I indicate above, we weren't just out to shoot a couple of thieves: These two just killed a witness (and "nice girl") who was our main lead in a job that was going to pay us enough money to stay off the streets. Likewise, the Hit Squad wanted to not get caught to protect their employer and their boss. This was honestly a conflict where neither side was really going to Give In until the rules made us, and we were fine with that: But the rules never made us, because no one ever got close to having their Action/Push dice cancelled by their Hurt dice on either side of the contention.

    -= Which makes the game basically one of "Enter into contentions until one side or the other Gives In and accepts the consequences. And that's really the only way to play." Which is fine if explained such up-front, but that Force-Out option actually seems viable until the dice hit the table and you see the results.

    -= Of course, after several rounds of conflict where neither side is going to back down, things start to get boring, so one of the players, out of being nice (or wanting to move things along) will Give In, but not for any story reason; just to keep the game moving.

    -= And as Ralph noted, the consequence of Giving In is basically, "Yep I accept whatever you were going to do to me", which is brutal for the Giver. Frex, in the streetfight above, the "bad guys running away" turned into a "gunfight in the middle of an intersection". The initial goal of the bad guys was to get away, but now they're in the middle of a gunfight shooting at the PCs. At that point the goal isn't "I want to get away", but "I want to take these guys down (kill them) so I can get away." Maybe the solution would have been for Eric as GM to say, "OK guys, if you want to Give In, they won't kill you but rather Get Away, and tag you with the Adjective "frustrated" ." ...but I guess at that point the fight was so furious that it was hard to consider that. I dunno.

    Conclusion: Forced Out doesn't seem to work by the numbers. Without a viable option to semi-quickly (read "not take forever") Force Out, then every conflict simply plays out until one side Gives In. If both sides are really invested in the result, then that Giving In is problematic.

    \n * Finally, in the interest of disclosure, there was one part I realize now that conceptually we messed up: Contentions don't *really* go "back and forth". A combat scene is not really a combat scene: It is simply You declaring your attack on me, after which we roll and resolve. Then it's done, adjectives are assigned. Again, done. Then, if it is something that we would call a combat scene, it begins totally anew with the "defender" for the last contention being the attacker for this new contention: Verbs assigned, Push dice assigned, dice rolled, adjectives assigned, then done, Done, DONE.

    Our conceptual mess-up was basically thinking that a Contention was a true back-and-forth, and that at the end of step 8 it was NATURALLY the defender's turn to attack (like there's a hidden Step 9, which says "Go back to Step 1, now the defender is the attacker"). If we take the Contention is a true one-way isolated attack action with no reciprocation... maybe that's the conceptual balance we needed to make things work out.

    In the street fight above, we literally did do the "OK, they attacked, you lost your eyes. Now it's your turn, what are you doing back at them?" When in truth it should have been "OK, they attacked, you lost your eyes. That contention is Complete, done. Do you want to do anything back to them? It looks like they're just trying to get away more than kill you."

    If we were conceptually in that mindset, I think we might have not "Given In", but we might have simply re-assessed the situation more carefully post-Contention, deciding that it was best not to get into a deeper fight and simply let them go. Likewise, for the antagonists around round two, instead of attacking back at our attacks, they might have simply accepted the stickied/permanent adjectives and said "I split".

    Not sure if removing yourself from the scene is an option, though. If we Gave In or didn't fight back, they probably would have just left us and ran away. If they gave in or didn't fight back, that likely would have meant "we caught them, they can't escape. Interrogation time."

    So bits of this and that aside, I'm starting to think now that some of the issues we had with Giving In might be conceptually still stuck in "rounds of combat/conflict" without stopping clearly between each and re-assessing the situation, maybe.

    \n Andy,

    I think you have the right of it. In our post play discussion on Saturday I kept using the phrase, "respect the adjective." That is, after each time an adjective is inflicted on you, have to STOP and seriously consider the implications. They can not be considered fleeting moments of color because they are the only mechanism by which narrative change is going to happen. In other words, the adjective "Bruised" is not about how Indiana Jones looks kind of beat all to hell by the end of the movie but is otherwise operating in top form, instead it's "Jesus, I've just been beaten to hell by a baseball bat. Am I still going?"

    I kind of caught on to this early on. There was point where some police officials hacked my car to forcibly pull me over. One of the other PCs wanted to jump up and start trying to counter act the hacking. But I IMMEDIATELY saw the potential of getting in a very UNFUN loop of escalating adjectives. The car is "Hacked." But now they're "Delayed" Oh, but now the car is "Uncontrolled". Oh but now they're "Lost" and so on. So I immediately stopped the other player and just said, "No, the car's hacked, it's pulling over to stop, okay now we're going to do...." and moved on from there.

    Have you ever seen any of those stories Ron tells about people pointing to the descriptors in a Sorcerer game to justify their actions. They say things like, "Well, I'm a Trained Solider so I do X." as if they had to no choice. To them because they are a Trained Solider they are compelled to act in a certain way. That's EXACTLY what this game is.

    And yes, that means you really can't get passionate about things. You have to stop and re-evaluate after each adjective and RESPECT them. This guy is clearly trying to get your character in bed. He's sweet talked you. You're now "smitten". You have stop and re-evaluate. You have to think, "Huh, I'm smitten. Is that enough? Would I go to bed with a guy after being smitten? No, no, I don't think so." Then a moment later after buying lots of drinks he hits you "inebriated." You again, have to stop and consider. "Huh. I'm smitten AND inebriated.... is that enough? Yeah, probably at this point she goes and sleeps with the guy."

    So, no, you can't get all passionate about how come hell or high water there is NO WAY she's sleeping with this creep. You have to look at it with that kind of weird fatalistic eye that leads to strange phrases like, "Well, because he's a Trained Soldier he has to do X". She's smitten and inebriated, OF COURSE, she goes home with him. There's no other choice.

    Edited Note: I would go so far as to say that if you have to take things to a mechanical force-out that's a pretty good sign that someone at the table got passionate and stopped actively listening to and respecting the actual adjectives being created.

    \n Sorry it's taken me a while to respond to this thread. I read it yesterday, but didn't have a chance to write a reply because I was being hauled off to my cousin's wedding.

    Anyway, that's probably a good thing. I took a lot of time mulling this over yesterday and last night.

    First off, thank you Eric for your thoughtful feedback. It's very appreciated that you point out the things you do like along with the things you don't and that you state that some of it is opinion and taste. I couldn't ask for a better rejection of my game.

    Thanks Ralph, Andy, and Jesse for weighing in. Lots of good stuff for me to consider.

    So, obviously there's a big element to contention that I'm not articulating very well. I think I've given you a lot of pieces to work with, but I've made no clear indication how they're supposed to fit together. Though it sounds like, from Andy and Jesse's comments, that you're starting to get at it anyway. That's cool, but obviously I need to find a way to explain this up front rather than hoping people with reverse engineer the solution after a couple sessions of play.

    So, ignoring issues with Push dice economy and death for the moment, I hope you'll let me work some of this out here in order to figure out how I need to fix my text. Or, if it comes down to it, figure out if I need to kill a darling.

    One of my design goals within the contention system is to de-couple the achievement of narrative success from the defeating of your enemies. Lots of conflict systems are based on the idea of: 1) set stakes, 2) defeat your enemy, 3) get what you want. Chronica works that way. I have no problem with games working that way. But...

    I want a system where you can a) defeat your enemy but still not get what you want and b) get what you want without necessarily defeating your enemy to get it.

    So this is where vectors and the narrative truth of adjectives come into play. They are tools to achieve that design. But looking through that chapter, I don't really ever show how it all works together. My running example does a pretty good job of totally avoiding any illustration of how that's supposed to work. Instead, my example focuses on all the backup plans for when the primary system doesn't work. Giving-in, forcing-out, and death are all just safety nets. And I think I forgot to mention that there's a high-wire (or at least I never shine the spotlight on it).

    Every time you inflict an adjective, you're moving the narrative one step closer to your goal at that moment. Jesse's phrase of "respecting" the adjective is a great one. If you don't mind, Jesse, I might just steal that from you. Besides whatever mechanical weight might be assigned to an adjective in terms of Hurt dice, the story has to proceed as if that adjective is real. Therefor, you can get what you want through the narrative steps of inflicting adjective without ever having to worry if your enemy gives-in, is forced-out, or killed.

    Example: I'm a big thug. There's this guy walking down the street who I want to kidnap. Here are the actions I'm going to take:

    \u2022 Slam my hands down on his shoulders to make him grabbed.
    \u2022 Throw him over my shoulder, making him carried and lug him over to my hauler truck.
    \u2022 Toss him into the cage in the back of my hauler, making him detained.

    If those three actions are successful, it doesn't matter if the guy gives or if at some point those Hurt dice he takes force him out, because through the narrative effect of my adjectives I got him off the ground and into my truck. And that was my goal\u2014job done. Because the guy is in a cage and no-longer has any good vectors for attacking, we can drop out of contention mode and see where the story goes from there.

    Vectors come into play by limiting what each action can do. Could I have made him detained with one roll? Well, only if he was standing in front of the hauler doors to begin with. Each time you try to achieve something narratively with an adjective, you have to ask yourselves if you can do that with a single action without the target having a chance to struggle back. If not, you just zoom in on a smaller step towards that goal.

    So through that process, the guy has a chance to fight back after each of my actions. He could have struggled free of my grabbed. He could have pulled a gun and shot me. So, like Andy and Jesse are getting at, after every roll you have to look at the adjectives on the table and reconsider your goals. If I got shot, maybe I'm less worried about carrying out the kidnapping and instead am more worried about getting away with my life.

    Because goals can change mid-stream like that, I've avoided any straight mechanical metric for success. I don't want to say it takes two adjectives to do this and four adjectives to do that. Because then we would just be playing the game. I want the narrative state to be the metric for success.

    Another example: I'm a hardboiled grifter and I want to meet with an elusive crime boss. It's not easy to get a meeting, so I go up to one of his known goons who is running around town. Right now, the goon's goal is just to run his errand. He doesn't know me from Adam and has no agenda towards me what so ever.

    \u2022 I say something mean about the goon's mother, making him insulted.
    \u2022 Now that he's been insulted, he has an agenda towards me. He punches me in the gut making me doubled over.

    Now this guy's goal is changing. He has to figure out what to do with me. So he decides to take me to his boss and let his boss figure it out. Suddenly his goal is the same is mine. We don't have to play out any more contention, we just advance the narrative to where he throws me in his trunk and brings me to the boss's hideout. New scene.

    And what's cool is I made that insulted adjective sticky. So sometime later when the goon and I get into it again, that's still going to be in play.

    So one of the reasons that forcing-out statistically takes a long time to ever happen, is just to make sure that there's plenty of time for these narrative stories to play out without forcing-out coming in to complicate matters. And that's part of the reason that giving-in is so arbitrary as well (though I think there's something I can do to fix that process up a bit).

    Is that making anything more clear? Are these better examples?

      \nAll fictional consequences aren't encapsulated in negative adjectives. The fiction has its own weight, its own repercussions on play. Negative adjectives are discrete bits of fiction given additional weight by the mechanics. In your footrace example, even a basic success where you spend no Push dice will allow you to give your opponent a fleeting adjective like outpaced or surpassed. That would necessitate your opponent using his action to do something that would neutralize the negative fleeting adjective. Depending on the length of the race and if your opponent recovers that one fleeting adjective might be enough to win. Maybe you could spend two Push dice to give him that sticky pwned adjective and win the race flat out. Maybe you and your group decide that pwned is a fleeting adjective because the loss won't cause your opponent any serious ego bruising. There is no hard rule or formula that can account for every situation or perspective. You and your group need to evaluate the fiction and handle the assignation of adjectives accordingly.

      There's a spectrum of results:

      ~action is ineffective on a failed roll (the action was achieved narratively but had no noticeable effect)

      ~action has temporary effect on a successful roll (fleeting adjective)

      ~action has lingering effect on a successful roll and spending one Push die (sticky adjective)

      ~action has permanent effect on successful roll and spending two Push dice (locked adjective)

      The weight of the change on the fiction is directly proportional to the weight of the mechanics (roll result + Push dice spent) applied to the fiction. The adjectives being assigned not only need to fit within their vector of application but also need to fit within their place on that spectrum of effect, fictionally AND mechanically. This is why making an adjective like grabbed sticky isn't going to sit quite right most of the time. We recognize being grabbed as a condition that is prone to rapid change.

      If you use the mechanics to force an effect on the fiction you're going to be in for a struggle. The fiction has to be created and then the mechanics applied to fit. Before you even pick up the the dice you describe the action you're taking, and you probably have an idea of the immediate result you want to achieve with that action. But you have to be flexible when it comes to describing the results of your roll and how you use and spend Push dice. You may intend to spend 2 Push dice but if you lose one or both of them due to Hurt dice results then you're going to have to alter the narrative result of your action to fit what you're now capable of inflicting mechanically. In this way you move, action by action, towards some ultimate result.

      The description of your action, your opponent's defense description, any adjective inflicted, any Push dice spent, and any description of the result of your action should all be in harmony with each other. One should flow naturally from the next. It is up to you and your group to determine where edge cases fall. Much of the time a fleeting adjective will give you the result you want. Other times it may take a sticky adjective. It depends on your conception of the fiction and needs to be taken into account when you narrate or describe actions.

      When it comes to showing your character's capability with negative adjectives applied to your opponent Technoir is solidly in the category of "results show your capability, verb ratings and positive adjectives show your potential." You use that potential by giving your opponents negative adjectives which in turn shows your degree of capability.

      That's my take, hope it helps.

      \n When we played again earlier this evening there were particular instructions in the contention process that really stood out as being very important for smooth play. Things that should maybe have their importance reinforced in the text. I've bolded the relevant bits I'm talking about.
      \n
      Page 70 Technoir Beta
      2. ASSEMBLE THE DICE
      \u25b6\u25b6 As the attacker describes the action her character is performing, she
      selects the verb that best governs that action. She might even use the
      verb itself in the description, putting emphasis on the word to indicate
      the one she\u2019s using to the rest of the table. Then she picks up a number
      of Action dice equal to her character\u2019s rating in that verb.

      \u25b6\u25b6 She may add a charged Push dice for each positive adjective or object
      tag she works into the description that aids her in the action.       She can
      add all of her Push dice this way, but she may want to save some of
      them for defensive purposes if someone attacks her between now and
      her next attack.

      \u25b6\u25b6 For each negative adjective on her protagonist sheet, she adds a Hurt
      die. She must ensure her action is something reasonable given her neg-
      ative adjectives.
      \nRather than working positive adjectives into the action description there was a tendency to want to describe first, decide to use Push dice, then go back and justify adjectives and therefore Push dice base upon the narration. It can work this way but it goes much more smoothly when keeping your adjectives in mind during your initial action description. We got it sorted out quickly but it wouldn't hurt to emphasize the order here.
      \n
      Page 72 Technoir Beta
      4. DETERMINE DEFENSE
      \u25b6\u25b6 The person playing the target character describes what he is doing in
      defense and selects the verb that governs that action.       The verb\u2019s rating
      is his base defensive rating.

      \u25b6\u25b6 For each positive adjective or object tag that helps his defense, he can
      discharge a charged Push die to increase his defensive rating by 1. He
      can\u2019t use a tag that is trumped by a tag the attacker used (see page XX).
      \nNarrating what you're doing in defense after your attacker has already rolled felt strange at first. One reason is that you know after the attacker's roll whether you even have a chance of successfully defending or not. I got used to it, which is good, because the inclusion of the defensive action narration shapes and gives context to the attacker's following narration and any adjective inflicted upon you. This could really use some instruction, similar to that for describing the outcome of the attacking action in step 5, to give the player some idea of the bounds within which to frame the defense action narration. We hit it mostly right, but some more guidance would have helped I felt.
      \n
      Page 72 Technoir Beta
      5. RESOLVE THE ROLL
      \u25b6\u25b6 If the attacker\u2019s result (including a \u2018.1\u2019) is higher than the target\u2019s defen-
      sive rating, the attacker can inflict a fleeting adjective of her choosing
      on the target. She describes the outcome of the attack only so far as to
      warrant that adjective, nothing more.

      \u25b6\u25b6 If the attacker\u2019s result was equal to or less than the target\u2019s defensive
      rating, the attack happened as described but was not effective enough
      to inflict a new adjective. In this case, if the attacker is a player, she
      should mark the circle next to the verb she used in the attack. This verb
      is now primed.
      \nThis turned out to be a key instruction. Particularly for IRC play, but I suspect for play in general. Violating this rule can result in skipping past the very situation that you're trying to resolve and moving past important opportunity points for subsequent attackers. It also tends to negate space for the GM to add in important details and color.

      If anybody else has some experience or thoughts about these aspects they'd like to share I would love to hear them.

It means the attacker has to be really explicit with her action if she wants to create particular consequences. Just saying "I shoot him" isn't good enough. She has to create enough narration to limit what adjectives the defender can choose. I love mechanics that naturally encourage more narration like that. It also represents some sort of inherent agreement between the attacker and defender over what happened in the fiction.

      In practice, it didn't work. Well, it didn't work for us. It might be just because we're used to the other way now. But I think there's a particular strength in having the attacker narrating the effect of their actions and naming that adjective. It allows for more confident talk at the table.

      Mind you, either way, we still do a lot of group brainstorming as to what the adjective should be. We shout out different synonyms and metaphors until we find one that entertains us the most. So it really shouldn't be that different that the defender has final authority rather than the attacker. But it just seemed like it required this extra step of negotiation we hadn't needed before and it slowed down play. Again, maybe it's because we're not used to it.

      But what I learned is we're really enjoying the game we've been playing. There isn't a need to change it much. It's fun for us. It allows us to tell these amazing stories. I'm assuming it will be fun for others if I can figure out how to best communicate it and get them playing the same game we are. I'll be able to do that better with my next draft of the text. Better, but never perfect. And I realize I can't make everyone happy.

      That doesn't mean you shouldn't drift and hack the hell out of it if you want to.

      As to Ralph's suggestion about adjectives and getting rid of the positive and negative indicators: the design went down that road at one point and it didn't work out well. What it comes down to is that the three positive adjectives and the plethora of tags you have are usually plenty. The limiting factor is the Push dice. I've thought about treating negative adjectives as positive, tagging other character's adjectives and tags, having positive adjectives attached to locations and events, creating additional adjectives on the scene and all sorts of things to give you more stuff to work with. To paraphrase Ralph, all that sounds really awesome just typing it out. But in play, that just turns out that's a lot of book keeping. It's enough to look through your three positive adjectives and your list of tags to find stuff to spend Push on.

      As to negative adjectives, the reason they're clearly delineated as negative is to make sure there's not confusion over whether you roll Hurt dice for them or not. I don't want to slow down play with having to decide which adjectives are hindering your actions and which ones aren't. You roll Hurt dice for each negative adjective you have, period. Roll dice and move the story forward. I realize there's sometimes going to be a disconnect ("Why does my hacked submachine gun hinder me from coaxing this guy?") but there's going to be problems with any abstractions. There's a couple of moves like recover and ditch that give you a way to deal with that.

      (Plus, the way the dice mechanics work, the Hurt die from your hacked gun only has a chance of penalizing your highest result from your roll\u2014it's not like it's a definite penalty.)

      Changing the terms from sticky and locked to damaging and permanent doesn't work. That's also something I tried. Damaging doesn't apply well to positive adjectives (I'd rather not have to use separate severity terms for positive and negative) and permanent isn't actually permanent. As much as I would like more evocative terms, those are the ones that have proved the best over time.

      \n When GMing another session today we changed up the way we handle the fiction of hacking. What we were doing previously was making it more in line with modern real world hacking. A very hand-wavy layman's version of hacking anyway, with a dash of THE FUTURE. It worked well enough but felt like we weren't taking advantage of one of Technoir's strong points. Which is doing cool action-y conflicts between people. So in the interest of trying to capture the cool action-y-ness, the fiction of hacking became more virtual reality. Sort of Otherland meets Shadowrun.

      Jack "One-Eye" Denton is sitting in a diner while trying to hack into the system for the dive motel, Electric Palms, next door. He locates the node address and enters, finding himself in a tropical forest of luminescent trees. Through gaps in the tree cover he can see a golden ziggurat. Searching around for any security measures, he spies a white tiger with flaming green eyes stalking him. He runs through the forest, toward the ziggurat, while keeping an eye on the tiger. The entrance to the ziggurat is blocked by a golden door and there is an interface panel on the wall next to it. The tiger lunges at Jack from behind. As tiger claws swipe at Jack's head he spins around to defend himself. Claws rake across the encryption of his Specks, sending sparks flying. While the tiger paces back and forth looking for an opening, Jack moves in a blur of speed, busting the golden door into pixelated oblivion. He enters the ziggurat and finds himself in a darkened room, standing at a control terminal that gives him system-wide access. After finding the surveillance footage he needs Jack disconnects, pays for his coffee, and moves to the next stage of his plan.

      Of course there were rolls and adjectives and all that good stuff in there. This worked a lot better for us and was certainly more engaging than how we were handling it. I'm sure other people have been handling it this way all along, and I'm not sure why I didn't think to do it sooner. So for anybody that hasn't gone a more virtual reality route with hacking, and isn't satisfied with how you're doing it now, you might want to give it a try.
