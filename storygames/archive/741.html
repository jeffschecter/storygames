Pulling the GM

TonyLB
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Actual Play

Every Amber game I've ever played (and a very, very wide number of other systems):

"C'mon! I know he's got a one-rank edge in Warfare, but I've totally got the advantage of numbers and a well trained army, plus I'm trying to shift the battle to Psyche! I'm totally sure that should work, c'mon, c'mon!"

I've mostly seen Pull discussed from the point of view of empowered (often equal) participants in the game, so that a person's choice to view the actions of another player as important is voluntary.

It strikes me that it is very different in tenor when the target of the pull is in a privileged position relative to the ... well, "supplicant" starts to sound like a good word. The GM in many traditional games ends up in this position a fair amount of the time, so I expect that quite a few of us have a good amount of experience on both sides of the GM-screen with this phenomenon.

How do people go about achieving a functional social dynamic with that imbalance of power, and all that it implies for the modes in which they will be communicating?

What sort of responses are available to the (classic of "Amber") situation where two players are both beseeching the GM to grant them victory over the other? Does Push/Pull give us any insight here?

It's a game about dysfunctional families that spend most of their time fighting with each other, and the way the game is set up very often makes you replicate exactly that, with the PC group spending most of their time screwing each other rather than dealing with the issues in the larger world that they're supposed to care about.

    But really, now that you say it, it makes me realize in the old days (3 years ago, so long and ancient ago) when I was an illusionist GM, my players would often spend vast amounts of time arguing and bickering with each other IC and (sometimes) OOC. They knew, I think, that they couldn't push me, and they wanted something they could have authority over, and so they pushed on each other and resorted to my-guyisms to get their ability to feel in control.

    OTOH, the more participationist games I played often avoided this. Everyone there knew the way to power, by pleasing me. And I, in turn, knew the way to keep that power was to please them. I think the difference in the power dynamic was that in this type of game I had to worry about keeping the players engaged, and so had to give them what they wanted in order to lead them on. Its the difference between forcing them down the road and bribing them down it, and the result was, odd as it may sound, a lot less dysfunction. (Though a lot of freakishly hard work for me as GM, because this was before I even had systems with proper flags and crap like that.)

    Really, I think there is a real way in which simply being able to see and openly acknowledge the power issues in a game opens the door to more functional play.

    My Amber used such a different resolution system from what the two of you are describing I don't even know if it is worth discussing in the same thread.

    But, for what it's worth: We took the attributes very seriously, and the question of overcoming an attribute imbalance was a matter of how much you were willing to escalate in terms of damage to the other and damage to yourself (so, for instance, the guy with slightly lower warfare can win if he wants to KILL while the other guy is just fooling around. "But I was clever" was regarded as generally a pretty paltry defense (that's my recollection -- I was GM most of the time.)

    Player competition was also not terribly the focus of the game, though. A lot of the game was simply a question of fantasy fulfillment, socialization (large mixed-gender high school group) and pure joy of exploration. People really liked going to new shadows, making up shadows, and finding cool things in shadow to show their siblings. I still have 50 pages of notes in my closet about the ecology of Gregor's Shadow Jungle.

    May 2006
    It's hard to step away from Amber in this, if only because most of the personal examples I can think of come from it. The degree of GM Fiat makes, as Tony has noted, a fascinating social game with the limiter being how well you can pitch your idea as cool to the GM (or how well you can figure out what the GM thinks is cool, and go towards it).

    However, this is actually soemthign we've run into specifically and concretely in Fate. Here's the situation: aspects that cause problems for a player grant them fate points, but grant them the option to buy off the trouble. As such, ideally, if I have the aspect "alchoholic" and the GM puts a drink in front of me, he does so while putting down a fate point. If I take the drink (whihc presumably causes soem other problem), I get the point, but if I decide that I don't want to go down that path, I can pay one of my own fate points, and pass up on the drink.

    This is a pretty simple exchange when everyone is aware what aspects are ont he table and in play, but in reality, the GM is not always on top of all his player's aspects, and situations frequently arise where a player feels that this would be an excellent time for their aspect to come up but the GM hasn't made the connection. The question that comes up is "how can the player express that to the GM?"

    Now, no answer to that is going to be completely satisfying because, in the end, the GM can say no, so there's no way to _make_ this happen. Still, the GM is hopefully more than happy to bring aspect-flavored pain to the characters, so assumign the GM is not purely obstructionist, the player has a few options.

    1) Sulking - This is.....problematic

    2) Explicitly stating their need - This works just fine, but it's awkward. If it's during a pause between scenes or the like, then it can be ideal, but in the midst of a scene, it can throw off pacing. it also puts the GM on the spot, which can be good or bad depending on the dynamic.

    3) Ritual/structured language - The model that has evolved in play has been most successful for us has revolved around the player addressing the GM, holding up a fate point (the poker chip, bead or whatever) and making a statement about their character with an emphasis on the specific wording of the aspect. Flipping a token in hand, looking at the GM and stating "This should sounds like _Risky Business_"
    tends to do the job.

    Now, while #3 probably works best for the specific needs of Fate, the real key has been making sure it's clear to the players that this is something they _can_ do. Once that lesson is in place, it changes the relationship with the GM for players coming from a less open environment, and is all in all, a change for the better.

    Anyway, dnot really an answer, but it is at least reasonably specific. :)

    -Rob D.
