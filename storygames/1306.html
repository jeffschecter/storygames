Game fiction

Judson_Lester
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Story Games
A frequent theme in recent discussions about RPGs-as-business has come back to the idea that there's a significant fraction of gamers who buy books just for the joy of reading them. And there's the push toward the idea of story-games with this rulebooks and boards and pawns.

Now, I'll admit, for a long time I was a member of the buy-to-read crowd. And I wasn't really being honest with myself; I was in fact only getting a reader's enjoyment out of a lot of the stuff I bought, even though I told myself I might use it...someday, maybe. Now, trying to read old RPG books is like chewing tinfoil. I want either a good story, or to be able to run wild in the setting, without this weird outside narrative force.

Game fiction serves purposes though. It gives us a canon to riff off of, and inspiration for our own plots and characters. It can serve as the seed of player fiction, and a channel to keep a game going in one direction.

What if a game were presented as a very spare rulebook, that discussed the setting material as lightly as possible, along with really solid game fiction to present the author's view of the world. I'm mostly shaky on how this doesn't turn into Dragonlance novels.

Well, I'm back to my opium.

    That's something like my plan with Full Light, Full Steam. The book will be about half "game fiction" which outlines the setting through a variety of different perspectives, all of them in-world artifacts: journals, reports, advertisements, newspaper articles, et cetera.

    All of that material was written with a very specific intent to convey the mood and feel of the setting, drop very few specific details, and leave vast tracts of the setting undefined so that the players can make up whatever they like. There are no dates, for instance. Only three specific asteroids are detailed in the asteroid belt section, and the rest is left open. The technology is described but not proscribed, so engineer characters can just make shit up as appropriate.

    Playtesting looks like it informs play without overburdening it. We'll see how well it's received when it comes out in November.

    So that I'm clearer, I'm realizing that by saying "game fiction" I implied I meant the chunks of prose that get incorporated one way or another into various games to give an idea about the setting. It's my opinion that they have the extra baggage of having to serve two masters simultaneously, and ultimately function very poorly as either fiction or setting as a result.

    What I'm suggesting, concretely, is something like:

    A terse premise and spare setting statement up front. Rules in a flowing but clear prose, with setting references as constrained as possible. Then, either the remainder of the text, or a separate companion volume, a setting description, in engaging prose, with the intention that it be read as simple prose.

    As a complete counter example, the most useful setting fiction I've ever encountered was in The Shadow of Yesterday. Whenever I need to make a character, or even a whole situation, I give it a read through until something jumps out at me. Doesn't hurt that as a result the rules are fresh with me.

    Amusingly, I posted about Writing to Serve Five Masters a long while ago. Funny we should use the same terms.

    I actually disagree with you, though. I think fiction and bursts of fiction are the best possible way to convey multiple things at once. Because, as Andy points out, reading a timeline of fictional events? Only marginally more interesting than schoolwork. But embedding events into pieces of fiction that also feature sympathetic characters, interesting bits of setting, action that hopefully inspires play, and letting that grand gestalt give players the context that a dry timeline might have given them -- that's far more interesting and engaging, to my eye.

    Additionally, there's the lovely lovely possibility of ambiguity. All of my fiction pieces are either first-person (journals, letters, etc) or clearly biased expository writing (propoganda, sales pitches, academic bluster). This allows me to have, say, some jingoistic patriot declare that "Britain is the best hope for continuance of proper civilisation" and the reader will understand that (a) this guy believes this, (b) this opinion is relatively common in the world, (c) there are fictional "facts" which support his opinion -- Britain does do some good -- but (d) as the narrators are all unreliable to some degree, some part of his bluster is totally wrong, and Britain is also doing damage and eroding away at "proper civilisation." Alternately, I can include a journal entry where a group of sailors explore the abandoned mining tunnels under Victoria Station, go way deeper than they thought the tunnels went, find strange-looking technology, and get scared away by approaching noises. Something is down there, but as a designer I don't say what it is and allow the players to make it whatever they like, according to their aesthetics.

    The thing of it is, almost all writing everywhere serves more than one master at a time; it's the nature of discourse. That some products haven't done fiction + setting well may reflect more on the quality of writing than the magnitude of the task.

  • Brand_Robins
    September 2006 edited September 2006
    Judd,

    My fault for being oblique. The ways that Tribe 8 failed are something I've talked about so much that I tend to short hand out of context.

    Tribe 8 did three things, all together, that ended up knee-capping large parts of what the game could have been.

    1) It had a setting that was presented in a carnivalesque "many non-authoritative voices" manner that gave huge amounts of tone and color, but less in terms of factual information.

    2) It then ran on a game engine and assumed playstyle that was pretty relentlessly Sim.

    3) It had an ongoing metaplot that was published faster (about twice as fast) as any of the players of the game were actually able to move through the metaplot.

    So what you got was a game about color, nebulous choices, and distrust of authority or any one perspective run as a very Sim and details heavy game using a trad style system born (partly) from minatures combat, being run along a company controled conceptual railroad.

    Yea.

    Whereas if you'd hooked Tribe 8 to something like HeroQuest, made it about communities in conflict, and used the fictional content as a way to spur players into making their own views, visions, and conflicts with and about the world... it coud have rocked. Or, if you'd given it a heavy dose of factuality, added some micro-fiction for color, and run it as a Sim game it could have worked. But the combination of tone, spirit, and ambiguity with the need for hard facts and the ongoing railroad whose turns and twists even the GM can't see coming....

    Led to problems.

    September 2006
    "And all this hints and mystery about what happened."

    Yeah, this is precisely why, "Let me tell you about my character..." is so offputting. It's not because of the nebishness that it implies. Oh, that can be a factor, too. But even if you're the coolest person on earth, and you tell somebody about your character, what you're saying to them is, "My group and I are good at playing; we had a great time; you aren't near as cool, because you weren't there."

    Says that even if you don't mean to say it. And who wants to hear that?

    Oh, BTW, the prophecy of Joshua is great stuff! How's that not setting info, and not fiction? How's that any different than if I said, "There's this book in the game called the Necronomicon, and it tends to cause people who read it to go mad?" It's just a fact of the setting, and a good one.

    What sucks is finding out that the metaplot tells us what the outcome of the prophecy is. I used to think that the problem had to do with the fact that I couldn't use the supplement information if I wanted to play it out myself. But then everyone cries the same thing - quite true - "Then just don't use the supplement!"

    But here's the real problem, and it's the same as the problem with the fiction: when I read the supplement, I realize that it's already been played out. Or at least feels that way. How can I get an original take on it, when my play of it will neccessarily be biased by having read the metaplot version?

    This is precisely the same problem that Ron cites with Over the Edge. The book even says that the funnest part of OTE was creating the stuff as you go along. And then the book is nothing but a sourcebook listing all of the stuff that got created from playing the game.

    It's akin to the "Fruitful Void" concept, when looking at these concepts in general. Quite simply, if it was fun to create in play in playtest, or in developing the game, then don't include it in the book. It seems like the right thing to do...this worked the first time, so we'll include it for all players to encounter. But it misses that part of why it worked the first time wasn't so much the nature of the material, but the fact that it was created in response to the play happening.

    Don't fill these voids that are the real fun behind these games. That doesn't mean you have to leave the setting entirely undetailed - maybe the game isn't about coming up with cool setting elements as in OTE. In fact, maybe a certain game will be fine with a metaplot, as long as the play is largely about creating some other element than the plot (see the "Underbelly" concept). The point is to identify all of the areas that are fun to create in play and enable all of them, not to finish them off before play begins.

    That goes for "metaplot" problems, "playing before you play," "game fiction" etc, etc, etc.

    Mike

      MtFierce, all books are dialogues.

      One of the best things that anybody can learn to do in their writing is to open up spaces for readers to interpret, question, and answer. In fiction, the pace and flow of the story should leave places where the reader can go, "I wonder what will happen now?" or "I wonder what that means?" Later, there's a reader payoff when they were either right or you surprise them with something they didn't see coming.

      In technical writing, especially with something like game procedures, the reader should be given time and space to put the system together in their head. It's sort of the equivalent of letting them go, "I wonder if those successes carry over to victory points?" or "Why would I want to accumulate Debt tokens?" And then, again, there's a payoff when they find out that they were right or when you surprise them with a clever twist. If you go look through Dogs again, you'll see Vincent doing this over and over and over.

      Meera, that's a writing-for-your-audience question, and in the end, it all comes down to you, who you decide your audience is, and how you think they'd like to be addressed.

      I'd caution, however, against simply equating dialogue between friends and written communication between strangers. They are very different animals, follow different rules, utilize different tools, and have different constraints to them. There's a big difference between transcribing how you'd talk with your friends and writing in a friendly fashion.
