Building tension with a card-based mechanic

Caesar_X
February 2007 edited February 2007 in Game Design Help
I've been searching for a way to build tension with the card-based mechanic I'm using for 'The Naked City: a game of Film Noir'. Let me explain the mechanic and how I am trying to build tension so I can get some feedback from you all.

Each player gets a hand of playing cards to use during conflicts. There are various ways to get more cards when needed or to exchange unwanted cards for new ones.

The cards range in value from 2-Ace, and the different suits mean different things for narration purposes and for upping the stakes.
-Diamonds (conversation) leads to Clubs (material) leads to Hearts (emotional) leads to Spades (power/domination)

When a conflict begins the "protagonist" in the scene lays down a card from their hand. The "antagonist" can either:
1. Cede the conflict to the other player.
2. Play a higher card of the same suit (Raising).
3. Play a higher card of a higher suit (Trumping).

Play continues back and forth until one player cedes the conflict either because they don't have the cards to continue or because the "price" is too high to continue.

The winner of the conflict gets to narrate an outcome, which assumes a certain level of concession to the loser depending on how far the winning card was from the card played before it.

Example:
Darla Burgess (played by Chris), a snide lounge singer who has confronted her ex-lover Martin Aimes (played by Tony), a corrupt bookmaker who in his office at the time. Darla's intent is to get him to admit that he is sleeping with one of Darla's friends named Amanda. Martin is trying to keep the relationship a secret for other reasons.

Chris wants to start things off simple and leads with a 6 of diamonds (talking). The suit defines the types of narration and the card value defines how far up the temperature scale things are.
"Martin, why did you leave the club with her last night? There were plenty of other guys who could have given her a ride."
Tony wants to keep Darla talking and responds with a raise to 8 of diamonds.
His body language is still a bit defensive as he lights a cigarette. "Listen, she is having a really tough time with her father in the hospital and needed someone to talk to."
Chris doesn't have a higher diamond card to respond with, doesn't want to cede and conflict and chooses not to spend currency to get another card. He has Darla trump and raise the stakes with a 9 of clubs (material).
"I saw you flashing the cash around her last night. Trying to play the "big man", huh? She won't tell anyone where she got that rock on her finger from. Just admit what's been going on!" She is starting to seethe at this point.
Tony needs to hide the relationship and he doesn't have any club cards, so he takes a chance and trumps with a Jack of hearts (emotional).
Martin walks over to Darla and cradles her face in his hand. "Darla, you still mean a lot to me. You know I wouldn't hurt you like that."
Chris has already used two cards in this conflict and doesn't want it to spill over at this point. So he decides to cede the conflict knowing that the difference between his last 9 of clubs and Tony's last Jack of hearts means that Jack will need to concede quite a bit during his narration and that Martin can't just run rampant over Darla.

So that is the simple version of the conflict mechanic minus using traits, trust and the Past/Present timing of the scene.

And I've been trying to figure out how to build in a mechanic for the increasing guilt and desperation of Noir characters. Characters in the game accumulate Guilt Points and when they reach seven points, the character has their Downfall, which could be going insane and being committed or driving through the police barracades into certain death. The narration would get more desperate of course. But I wanted there to be a real effect on gameplay as these Guilt Points were accumulated. So last night I came up with a first pass of ideas for how a player would be constrained during conflicts based on how many Guilt Points they have.

Guilt Level: Effects (cumulative):
0: No effect
1: Cannot lead a conflict with a 2-4
2: Must raise at least by 3 points or else trump
3: Cannot lead with a diamond
4: Must raise at least by 5 points or else trump
5: Cannot lead with a diamond
6: Must always trump is possible
7: DOWNFALL

A bit extreme, but that's my first pass.

I'm looking for feedback on the mechanic and tension-building with trumps and increase in Guilt Points.

Any thoughts?
Chris

    It's hard to bluff if you know what is on the table. When the first player plays his card, things are uncertain, but after that each player is reacting to a known thing (the last card his opponent played). What if you set things up so that both players put down cards at the same time?

    Here a 30-second idea. Make it so that you need a certain number of victories or a given card total to win a conflict. Each time both players play cards. High suit wins, followed by high rank. However, if you win by suit instead of by rank, you pay a penalty (e.g. gain Guilt). Also, once a given suit is played, you can no longer play cards from a lower suit.

    This gives you an insentive to play good cards of a low suit early, since you won't be able to play them later. But you may also want to escalate to a higher suit, depending on you cards. If you escalate too soon, you may be left with not enough cards of the higher suit, but if you escalate early you can squeeze out some victories with low cards, but at a cost.

    It's not really a full mechanic, but something based on simultaneous card play adds a lot of uncertainty and an element of bluffing into the game.

    Also, I like Graham's suggestion that guilt makes you play low cards. It makes guilt a real penalty and fits the situation: you are guilty about past mistakes and holding back.
