\n In the $1000 thread, Ralph wrote this:\n
Posted By: ValamirThis is something I've actually been thinking about off and on for awhile.

Folks who frequent here have heard my rants about game competitions and how I think their focus on the fun early brainstorming part of design has created a culture of really bad habits among designers over the years. I don't want to revist those rants here, but one of the reasonable questions that arose from such is "what could you do to encourage things differently?" (*)

One idea I've been contemplating is more of an incubator approach. Where a design that was past the "hey this is fun" stage and needed the extra effort, and potentially guidance and motivation to get to the "really truly fully baked and done" stage (as opposed to the "not really fully baked but I'm tired of working on it done stage").

The potential of a more patron / grant model to encourage...dare I say...healthier...design habits vs. the game competition model is intriguing to me.

(*) which I why I'm very interested in seeing how JW's revamped Game Chef does or doesn't encourage different design habits than previous incarnations.
\nI happen to be a "design what you want, how you want" kind of a guy. Lots of people probably don't want to be encouraged to finish their games. This isn't about them. I'd like to talk about ways to encourage excellence and closure in game design by rewarding it with money. Ralph mentioned the patron / grant model. Microfinancing or investment capital might also be viable models.

Microfinancing is where you elicit small donations into a project. I don't know a ton about it, but what I do know intrigues me. It's essentially eliciting a bunch of investments of a small amount, say $10, to raise a significant amount of money, say $1000 or even $10,000.

    \n Jason, yeah...once you get out of the airy musings around "how about something like this" you'd need to put some specific practical functionality to it like that.
    \n
    \nloan vs. grant vs. investment are all possible funding methods each of which have their own pros and cons.
    \n
    \nA loan for instance has an advantage over a grant in that paying it back is a built in incentive to "do it right" and go out and promote it and so forth. But in the end...really? Because ultimately if the choice comes down to paying back the guy who loaned you money for your RPG vs. paying the rent and utility bills...we know how that's going to turn out...and people for whom paying regular bills is "no big concern" probably don't need a load for a print run anyway. So treating it like a loan sounds good in theory but seems more likely to turn ugly.
    \n
    \nAn investment (i.e. taking a claim on profits rather than a fixed payment) carries with it all the baggage of control issues that are just messy.
    \n
    \nSo a grant approach, perhaps one with a strong social pressure to "pay it foward" seemed the least likely to result in ugliness to me. I'd kind of love to see some sort of incubator / group mentorship model...kind of like the Forge was back in the day but a bit more focused on the financial side of publication rather than the design theory and basic into to publishing side. Of course, all of that falls into the category "Sounds good Ralph, go do it" which isn't really feasible for me, at least at this point, which is why I haven't really been talking it up.

    Posted By: Ben LehmanIf someone were to pay me $30,000 a year, that'd be enough. I would write at least one game a year and I would dedicate it to the glory of their name.
    \nOn a model like that, one game a year isn't nearly enough either. To make the kind of returns necessary for such a project to be successful you'd have to look at turning out a constant stream of material, including core books and supplements. To put that model into perspective, if we assume an approximate wordcount per book of 200,000, you'd be getting paid 15 cents a word. That's good for almost any form of writing work, but for RPG writing it's phenomenal!

    Of course that's ignoring other budgetary concerns, I guess you'll be paying some of that out to artists and stuff, but even if you spend half on outsourcing, printing and advertising you're still getting a tidy sum by this industry's standards.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarWhat about a model where the cost of a print run is loaned/donated/whatever upon completion of a game?
    \nDoesn't this already exist, in as far as Daniel's Do and Evil Hat are concerned? As far as I know, Fred's got a deal with Daniel to co-publish Do once it's finished and, while I don't know what that means about paying for the first print run, it might include something like that. This is how some music labels work, yeah? You find an up-and-coming band who's doing music you like and you give them some money to go into a studio and record something that you license the rights to for however long.

    For me, finishing a game is not at all about the money, but the time away from work and the energy and that's not something you can really bestow on someone else. I'm not going to quit school or my job to write games, so it's something I eck away at on the side or don't do at all. I still haven't found a set of incentives that work for me, aside from possibly scheduling playtests which force me to get material together to facilitate play for people. In that vein, I'd actually suggest a playtesting agreement or something where folks agree to constantly bug the designer about the game until they give them something to play and keep bugging them until it's done. Recently, Troy Holiday has been asking me questions about Geiger Counter and that's the biggest push I've had in a while, since moving out here and prepping for play with Harper, Robbins, and Ping.

    In general, nothing gets a game finished faster than playing it a lot.
