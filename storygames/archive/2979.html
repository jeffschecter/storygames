\n So, I started three threads on three sites with much the same query, essentially what games people bought and which of them they played.

Note, this is not about people not playing games, it's about whether they're playing the games they buy. Most people who responded were actively gaming, they just often weren't actively playing much of the stuff they spent money on.

Links are as follows:

http://www.story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=2912&page=1

http://forum.rpg.net/showthread.php?p=7236241#post7236241

http://www.therpgsite.com/forums/showthread.php?p=99050#post99050

The rpg.net thread got a disappointing response, but I know that site well and already have a pretty good idea how few games people there are purchasing are actually seeing actual play.

One interesting point was that lot's of folk were playing a wide range of games, it wasn't that unusual to find a White Wolf game sitting cheek by jowl with a storygame or PTA with D&D. I find that rather heartening personally. As I both hoped and expected, there are no camps in roleplaying, just a spectrum of tastes.

A lot of folk, maybe most, were buying more games than they actually played. Indeed, it was fairly common across all sites for there to be people who had already bought more than they could credibly play over the next few years but who were showing no signs of slowing their purchases.

Time was a common factor, unsurprisingly, people not having the time to buy all the games they bought. That does raise the question of why people buy them though, which may be worth following up on.

Lack of confidence came up occasionally with indie/storygames, a perception that they couldn't be sold to the group or that the person wouldn't do them right, that sounds worth addressing to me but I'm not a designer so I leave that to others. I do think though that potential actual play is being lost by people feeling for whatever reason that they're not up to the task.

A lot of people were buying games for inspiration, or to pick out a couple of ideas. That's interesting, albeit expensive. I think that's often a way of justifying purchases that the person knows are basically useless, which is probably an incredibly arrogant thing for me to think but there you go.

In terms of conclusions, my conclusions are these:

Many gamers buy far more games than they can or ever will actually play.

They do so from a mix of hope that they will play a game, that hope winning over experience, from a desire to pick up some inspiration and ideas or simply because they enjoy collecting.

The result is that many published rpgs, and to be clear I'm not talking especially about storygames here though I think it's now starting to hit this sector too, see little actual play compared to how much they are actually purchased.

I think the outcome of that is that playability is not a major factor in game purchases, because most purchases see little to no play. Instead idea stealability and the shiny factor tend to dominate. I think that results potentially in weaker rpgs generally, but now I'm leaving the horrifically biased and useless data behind and going off on an extrapolation of my own.

And so I think we have a gaming industry which is supported by sales of games that by and large are seeing little actual play, and that is not because the people buying them are not actual players, it's because they're actually playing a tiny subset of the stuff they buy.

Of course, these purchasers are a small subset of the hobby, but I suspect they are a large element of the part of the hobby that buys games.

I'll give more thought and say if anything else strikes me.

    \n Max, I can think of another reason for the phenomenon - I've no real idea how widespread it is, but one reason I end up with a fair number of unplayed games on my shelf is that I want an "arsenal" of games to pitch. In the times I've had a regular group that rotated shorter-run games, we've often chosen games to play by asking the prospective GM(s) to pitch multiple different games and doing a ranked preference vote to choose what gets played. In order for that to work, it helps to have a brace of different games in your library that you can pull out. Inevitably, some of them are not going to work out for some combination of reasons involving personnel, time, genre, mechanics....

    I suspect that some degree of your perceptions are the result of your preferences: If you like a technically-minimal "fade away" approach to mechanics, and play many different settings/genres with an eye toward prioritizing a personally-customized spin on those settings/genres... you only really need a few games. Find one or two systems that work for you mechanically, and then all else is reference works.

    If you play a lot of short-run games, have diverse appetites in terms of genre/setting, AND like mechanics-forward designs, a big arsenal of games (and probably a fair number of "read this to steal things" games) is a worthwhile thing to have.

    \n You misunderstand. Short-run players are just as likely to have the problem, maybe more so. Every time we change games, there's between 1 and (say) 8 games getting pitched. Only one of them is going to get played this time. There are 7 "bought, but not played" games sitting on shelves. In the X months before the next pitch round, other cool new games will intrigue someone enough that they'll want to buy, read, and decide whether to pitch. Some of those purchases will ultimately not get pitched for one reason or another after reading/discussion. So that's some additional number of "bought, not played" games.

    The low-volatility people potentially have the same problem, but there are two mitigating factors: first, they're more likely to pitch new variations on setting/situation/color/character within an existing system (so they don't buy a new game - at most, a supplement or two); second, they pitch less often, so there's less hope that any "cool new thing" is going to see play in a reasonable time window (thus depressing the urge to buy and read said thing in time for the next pitch round).

    \n As I said in my original reply to your inquiry, I have bought a very large number of RPG and RPG products with full knowledge that there was exactly zero chance of me ever running or playing them.

    I have a large collection of comics that I've never used in an RPG session. An equally large collection of videogames and DVDs that I've never used in an RPG session. Does that make them a waste of money? Of course not, that's absurd. If I buy an RPG product with the intention of running it, there's a high chance it'll never get played for simple logistical reasons. That's a straight numbers game:

    I am old, so are my mates.

    Therefore I have F free time, where F is quite small.

    They have similarly small values of F.

    The overlap between my free time and theirs is even smaller. Well call that f.

    f is very small indeed. In this case, f is about equal to one game session per week.

    Because I am old, I have plenty of money. Once I've paid my mortgage, pension, gas/electric bills and whatever, there's still quite a bit left over. So I can pretty much buy whatever I want when it comes to RPG books and other gaming stuff. I can afford to buy way more RPGs than I do. I can afford to buy way more than I have time to read. Have time to read way more than I have time to run.

    But, in order to make sure that f is used as effectively as possible, I spend some of my other free time (F-f) in various other RPG related pursuits. One of these is online discourse. Another is reading games so that I can choose to run only the best ones when I get my one weekly session.

    And, y'know, I like and enjoy reading decent RPG books. I recently read a whole bunch of the Torg product line with no intention of ever running it. I consider those purchases worthwhile because, to be honest, they actually represent a pretty good entertainment/cost ratio.
