    \n Some thoughts:

    Troupe-style Play: There are online character generators that let you create up to 16 Classic Traveller characters in one go. Crank out a ship's crew or mercenary team and play up the interactions with them as well as sending them on "missions".

    Narrow the Focus: There is an old box set for Traveller called "Tarsus: World Beyond the Frontier" which details a single world on the frontiers of the Empire. Apart from the railroady scenario that's included, it's a pretty good box. By confining all the action to a single planet you preclude the need for a spaceship and give yourself some creative constraint.

    Add Theme: We played a Firefly-themed GURPS Traveller game previously, complete with a Companion and a gun-nut. In the future I plan on running a First Blood/Billy Jack type game about war vets returning to their rural homeworld (using the Tarsus box). This is similar to the creative constraints above.

    Tweak the setting: Make the Imperium weaker or more corrupt? Give the local nobles more power? Setting things on the lawless frontier tends to help too.

    ...oh, and run it using Fate 3.0 ^-^

    \n When I first saw John Huston's The Man Who Would be King, I thought, "That's it! That's a Traveller game!"
    \n
    \nIt stars Sean Connery and Michael Caine as two British soldiers drummed out of the British Empire's Army while serving in India.
    \n
    \nThey scam some money, buy a ton of guns, and head into the mountains of Afghanistan to set themselves up as warlords. They've been serving everyone else all their lives, but now they're going to have people serve them!
    \n
    \nThey find this peaceful nation buried far out of the way and take it over. But then one of them is seen as a King by the natives, jealousy starts to creep in... it's great.
    \n
    \nNo, it's not about space. But it is about guys who have been part of an Imperial military who have to figure out what to do. It's set at the fringes of the empire -- and then beyond -- which is an easy set up for a Traveller game. All this is portable to a space setting -- and I think Marc Miller and others had this sort of set up in mind when coming up with the game.
    \n
    \nAnd it has strong issues for the characters, and goals.
    \n
    \nWhile the Firefly model is a good one for Traveller, I was always intrigued with spinning SF into situations like Captain Sir Richard Burton tracking down the source of the Nile and so on.... Men with ambition, like the characters in The Man Who Would be King who had failed to make impression in society that they wanted, and were driven to extreme measures to make that impression. (Which, also, is part of the Traveller set-up if you look at the Social Rank, the social climbing through the services, and so on...)
    \n
    \nAmbition -- social ambition specifically, with the money and assets only there to serve that, strike me as an excellent way to go (among many) to Awesomize Black Box Traveller.

    \n Well, I know what I like about Judd's idea... and this is one of the things I liked about the rules in the LBBs:
    \n
    \nAlthough later materials built out this huge simmy back story, the fact that the rules suggested the GM randomly generate worlds meant there was no big "clockwork setting"t that the GM had control of and would keep going no matter what the players did.
    \n
    \nLet's say the PC are in a ship, they're cheating their fuel costs, and they have a misjump. (Remember that blast from the past, kids?)
    \n
    \nSo, they travel parsecs off course and end up in a new star system. The GM tosses some dice to find out where the heck they are. He figures out what the main planet is like, tech-level and more. No color yet, just a string of digits that make you go... "Hmmmmm.... I wonder what's going on here."
    \n
    \nNow, there's suddenly two ways of moving forward on this:
    \n
    \nOne is to stop play for a week as the GM comes back with 40 pages of notes, colored maps diving the planet into little areas of interest and so on... (And then freaking out if the players say, "Nah..." and start making their way back to civilization without so much a backward glance at the world the GM just created.) This is sort of the "GM must be master of the of Sim world" way of handling it.
    \n
    \nThe other way is for everyone to look at the numbers and go, "Okay, now what?" This is more the Narrativism-Primtime Adventures series creation-Sorcerer & Sword build the world as you go approach, where the GM isn't keeping everything secret, but everyone has input on the details so everyone is vested in what's going on. Sure the GM will be making up MORE stuff... But using the randomly generated world details as a group exercise in creative Narrative Juice just seems healthy. (Frankly, I think taking Ron's comments from Sorcery & Sword's world building section and mixing them with the Traveller LBBs is vital to the Traveller Awesomizing Process.)
    \n
    \nFor me the random world generation process acts as a sort of humbling stopgap for the GM -- "Dude, you're not going to be able to map 11,000 worlds -- and there's no point!"
    \n
    \nIt gets everyone closer on the shared distribution of Narrative authority. It means we're all kind of in this together as we explore this strange thing called space.
    \n
    \n(Caveat: again, I'm not saying the GM is always walking around blind. I'm saying, every world becomes a chance to slow things down and to pre-play work that is always helplful to Nar games.)

    \nSomething I was playing around with a couple of months ago (really, this very thing!), was adding on some sort of Riddle of Steel/Spiritual Attributes Dice modifier... The idea is you keep the rules pretty much intact, switching the values out to dice pools, so you have a die for every point instead of Dice Modifiers.
    \n
    \n(This way Character creation and stuff stays the same.)
    \n
    \nSo...
    \n
    \n
    \n*You start with 2D6 for all rolls.
    \n
    \n*Instead of a DM +1 for a skill, you get +1D
    \n
    \n*SA's get added into the dice pool, but you reduce the number of lowest dice equal to the SAs added in, so the total number of dice for a roll stays the same, but you increase your odds for better totals with the SAs.

    \n*You' would need to alter the target number from 8+ to something that someone smarter than i could come up with.

    \n I loved the LBBs but never actually played a game with them: It was all about rolling up characters and, especially, worlds by myself in my isolated early adolescent days. So take my comments with appropriate salty granularity.

    What I loved about Traveller was the world generation system. What drove me crazy about Traveller was the world generation system. Specifically, you get a bunch of neat and semi-relevant facts about each particular planet, but no guidance on how to connect different worlds to one another or on how to fill in the details of a single world: All the rules-structure is on one level with nothing above or below.

    So I ended up inventing my own rules for those. For example, if you roll a "Balkanized" world, i.e. one without a unified government, you roll a random number of contending governments assumed to be all of the same size/power, and if one of them gets a "Balkanized" result, you roll subgovernments for that representing even smaller countries, etc. potentially ad infinitum. Or, in the biggest thing I ever did, I rolled up six subsectors for which the backstory was three waves of settlement, starting with indigenous species building pocket empires, moving to a Vargr invasion, and finally human colonists; so I rolled two additional stats for each world indicating human:Vargr population mix and presence of indigenous races -- and of course each subsector had modifiers reflecting which population tended to be locally dominant. That gave me a huge amount of interstellar and individual-planet detail to work with.

    So what I'd really like to see for Traveller is a set of standard cultural types (say, Vilani, Solomani, Sylean, etc.) with some real guidance on how each one tends to act -- not just "Vilani are traditional and by the book blah blah blah" -- and rules on how to roll up the relative influence of each culture on a specific world depending on its astropolitical and historical context. For example, instead of rolling every world individually, you could start with the first world colonized n the system, and then roll for the worlds it established colonies on, and then for the worlds they colonized in turn, getting deviations from the original "seed" culture at each step, and potential for cross-fertilization or conflict with other cultures that had been there before or which were expanding into the same area of space along a different route.

    In the process I'd want to throw out the "Imperium" altogether, honestly. The LBB's intro text promised an 18th-century setting of swashbuckling adventure and intrigue, so I'd want to have multiple empires (with distinct cultures) competing to colonize new worlds and conquer long-settled ones in their own distinctive ways. I'd like to be able to make distinctions like those that occurred in history, e.g. the French having a fairly light presence on the ground in North America with traders and fur trappers, but generally good relations with the Indians, while the English sent larger numbers of settlers who stuck to themselves and tended to piss off the Indians in the process, while the Spanish tended to have gotten to the richest, most sophisticated American civilizations first, which they tended to plunder, enslave, and intermarry with.

    \nThis has been a pernicious problem with every science fictiony thing my group has attempted. We've all got different influences and mental media references - everybody else's stuff always feels slightly off and it never works out very well.

    I believe this is one of the great unsolved design challenges: how do you teach genre through play?

    And isn't it strange how we're always blindsided when it manifests as an issue in play? It was the most significant issue in my 2004 playtest of The World, the Flesh, and the Devil, and I hadn't anticipated it at all. And it provoked full scale social meltdown in an Earthdawn game our group attempted back in the early 90s.

    Historically, games either try to teach genre through a) colorful fictiony stuff and illustrations in the rulebooks, and maybe ancillary novels, which requires the players reading the same source material as the GM, or the GM digesting and subsetting it for them somehow, or the GM riding herd on genre during play, or b) they allow genre to emerge in play via some combination of reward mechanics and player preferences.

    Primetime Adventures and My Life with Master both use solution-b. Primetime Adventures skews more toward creating genre from player preferences. My Life with Master skews toward genre from reward mechanics. From the perspective of genre, the Intimacy, Desperation, and Sincerity dice in My Life with Master are functionally equivalent to experience point awards for killing folks and taking their stuff in AD&D.

    Clearly solution-a isn't working for Traveller. And the "player preferences" half of solution-b doesn't seem to be what folks are looking for. So, is there some option other than targeted reward mechanics? Because yes, Trust and Circles mechanics are fun, but I'm not seeing how they're particularly tuned to the Traveller genre. The design question isn't, "What reward structure might be interesting?" It's "What reward structure would deliver the Traveller genre?"

    Paul

    \n You folks are obviously using terms like "theme" in a formal way that I'm not party to, which is cool but places me at something of a disadvantage.     \n
    \nHere's what really drives a Traveller game -- the essence of the genre -- and Firefly fans will be familiar: the only way to be free is to have your own ship. You can't afford a great ship. The ship you can afford you owe money on for 20 more years and it's falling apart. Traveller is about constantly making ends meet and doing whatever you have to to keep your head above water. Payment's due and there's nothing but high volume, low-return cargo here except for that one shady Vargr who's willing to pay you triple a high passage ticket price to make two jumps with a stop in between. You KNOW something's wrong but you need the money to stay free. So you take a chance and the dilemmas begin to multiply.
    \n
    \nThere's a reason why the most awesome "adventure" book in Traveller is a list of more than 70 people in three lines each who all want something done and will pay you for it. It's because you need the money and you need it bad.
    \n
    \nI don't know if that's what you mean by "theme" but it is what the game is about, and the reward mechanics are built into it not through metagame bennies but through in game needs being satisfied.
