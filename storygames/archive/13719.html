\n My Wednesday group is without a game... and tomorrow is Wednesday!

Help!

Background:

I play 1-4 times a week (probably too much) but at present I rarely have a group that meets weekly. Usually I email random people from my 100+ person gaming email list... who wants to game? And then we go! And it's usually more specific... who wants to play... Polaris... bam! So people who have no interest in Polaris don't play. And those who do... light a candle!

But you said you have a Wednesday group?

Yes!

My friend just had a kid and Wednesday free away from family. She told me she was sad she wasn't gaming so BAM... Wednesday group was born to run a short campaign. We played 10 sessions of Mouse Guard and it was ridiculously epic. You can read about it here.

So what's the problem?

The group was composed of good friends who live in the neighborhood or relatively close by. So instead of saying... Polaris... and Polaris lovers came over... it's more find the right game to fit the interests of 5 different people (which given my usual lack of a weekly gaming group, I'm not used to it). I've found Mouse Guard is a super safe and convenient game for groups of different interests. But now what?

What different interests?

Lots!

But a game that has the following works...

- fast paced
- you don't spend 30 minutes talking and never roll anything
- but the game isn't combat, combat, combat
- character focused / character driven
- characters grow / change
- no emo (I'm so sad, I will now cry or become a furry)
- no player vs player
- high drama / heroics
- the game isn't "whiffy" (I failed every roll for the last 3 hours and nothing moved forward)
- it's not a 1 shot, we can play 3-6+ sessions
- strong GM role, players do not have GM powers
- realism and historical accuracy doesn't matter
- less prep for the GM the better

I actually like a lot of the above, for example I enjoy PvP, but this is the middle ground.

What should we play tomorrow?

    \nLady Blackbird. I ran it for my regular group last night for the first time and it was practically effortless. Moreover, it encompassed pretty much every point on your list:

    - fast paced (Check.)
    - you don't spend 30 minutes talking and never roll anything (You roll all the time, but it's not involved or laborious.)
    - but the game isn't combat, combat, combat (Nope, the characters are constantly rolling trying to do clever things.)
    - character focused / character driven (Yep. There's 5 pre-gen characters who all have personal allegiances and goals. What's more, you refresh your character's die pool by having heart-to-heart conversations that flesh out the story.)
    - characters grow / change (Each character has goals (or Keys, as the case may be) and works toward fulfilling them.)
    - no emo (I'm so sad, I will now cry or become a furry) (It's a pretty funny, swashbuckling game.)
    - no player vs player (PvP in the context of Luke bickering with Han, but not in terms of out-and-out combat.)
    - high drama / heroics (Lemme just say this again; 'swashbuckling'.)
    - the game isn't "whiffy" (I failed every roll for the last 3 hours and nothing moved forward) (Oh things move forward even when you screw up... just maybe not in the direction the players want.)
    - it's not a 1 shot, we can play 3-6+ sessions (I'd say 3 would probably be likely, but it can last as long as the players keep the story rolling.)
    - strong GM role, players do not have GM powers (It's not a GM-less system. The GM is definitely the orchestrator, but I think there's more latitude to allow the characters the freedom to try crazy stuff that just might work.)
    - realism and historical accuracy doesn't matter (It's a magical steampunk spaceship story with aerial combat and SKY SQUIDS! 'Nuff said.)
    - less prep for the GM the better (You could probably run the game from scratch with 15 minutes notice.)

    So, yeah. That would be my vote.

    \n Oh, heres my pitch - the intro from the text. It seems to tick your boxes.
    \n
    \nINTRO:
    \nThis is a low-prep, rules-lite RPG system where the players participate in the story invention, and also strive to achieve character goals using their character\u2019s abilities. General play alternates between a STORY phase and a CHALLENGE phase. For reduced setup time, it is recommended (as a starting point at least) to take the setting from a fictional source that all the players are familiar with.
    \n
    \nThe game concentrates on:
    \n\u2022\tDetailed characterization
    \n\u2022\tCollaborative narration during Story phase
    \n\u2022\tCharacter goal setting deriving from characterization and situation.
    \n\u2022\t100% Character-POV play during Challenge phase
    \n\u2022\tDramatic/cinematic resolution style during Challenge phase
    \n
    \nAs such it would be best suited to action-oriented games with a dramatic/cinematic resolution style, and best suit players who like to contribute to the narration (either a little or lot) and like their characterization to play a meaningful part in the story.

    \n Now that you bring it up, there is a bit of a grey area there. I really haven't decided how story phase should happen with respect to : what point of view do players have when in story mode? I mean, optionally contributing suggestions about what happens is one thing -- "wouldnt it be cool if..." is just chipping in with an idea. As you say, describing actions of NPCs is another thing -- thats narrative authority -- players saying 'this actualyl does happen'. I havent described any advice or structure for that because I dont know much about it in practice.

    I would like this game to be on the immersive end of the scale rather than story boarding exercise end, so I am leaning towards players being able to play their characters during story phase, but give them a little more freedom to assert situations and interactions that involve their character. Obviously they can suggest things that might occur with other PCs, but not assert. but when it involves their own character, I would like the player to be able to assert stuff in story phase like "An old girlfriend rings up and seems really upset, and demands that I meet her straight away but she wont tell me why." I can see how that would be outside of some groups comfort zones, even as it is optional. Of course a certain group could play without that kind of story phase contribution, and just stick 100% to 'this is what my character is doing now.. what happens?' and 'these are my characters goals for the next challenge phase'. I would be interested to see how a playtest in that comes out in that mode of play...

    Anyway , enough hijacking your thread. good luck!
