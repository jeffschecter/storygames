\n Hey, so in my Vampire 1ed thread, some people talked about their different experiences with Call of Cthulhu.

My experience with Call of Cthulhu was much more module-based than with many other games. So I knew there were some that were railroady, some that were chokepointy, and some that were neither. I thought, wouldn't it be interesting to go back through and try to figure out how many modules contained chokepoints or railroads and how many didn't?

But when I started to go through and analyze, I was a bit stymied, so I'm coming to the thread a bit puzzled where to go next. Maybe the people who were discussing it want to discuss it again and we can settle on some kind of methodology for looking at this.

* Call of Cthulhu gives clear instructions on making investigators and people who want to investigate and dig into things. I think we have to exclude situations where the characters would just Refuse The Call, so to speak. It's not likely that someone sitting down to play a CoC module who followed the instructions on making characters would do that.

* Interestingly, Call of Cthulhu modules almost universally give clear "failure states" - you don't figure out the mystery, okay, that's fine, you see in the newspaper where a bunch more people get killed and you never get to the bottom of it, but you SOMEHOW KNOW aaaaaagh my SANITY. And that's a pretty Lovecraftian ending, so I'm cool with that. But at the same time I don't really think this excuses a chokepoint in a module. Just because the game doesn't break down if you "lose" doesn't mean that it's a good idea to have a singular "make or break" roll in the middle of the game (especially a flat percentile roll without much in the way of situational modifiers supported in the system). (There are a couple of modules where there are failure states that involve the destruction of humanity - these are clearly aberrations, uh, so to speak.)

So here is my first glance at the scenarios in the 5e rulebook (1992):

The Edge of Darkness: An aged dude asks the investigators to get rid of a monster in an old house. Can't find any railroading or chokepoints. It's essentially a location adventure. Everything you need is in the location and no rolls are necessary to find any of the necessary clues, you get them going into the right place.

The Haunting (this is what used to be called The Haunted House in earlier editions): A landlord asks the characters to look into an old house where a creepy old wizard died. It has an interesting suggestion: if the characters don't do research, and decide to just rush straight to the house, the Keeper should "caution" them, but let them do it. I like that there's a "shady grifter investigators" option: If they just dig around a little, then go to the landlord and say everything's cool, the guy is killed when spending a night in the house to make sure, and he gets murdered, and one or more of the player characters may be suspected. There's a lot of "if the players look here, they get X" (where X is sometimes "nothing"), but no clear path through the scenario.

The Madman: A crazy old hermit has taken up with aliens and a newspaperman has disappeared with weird lights in the sky. There's a near-chokepoint/magician's force. If the characters make a Spot Hidden roll in the newspaperman's lodgings, they find a scratchpad with the name of the crazy old hermit. "If the investigator's miss this clue, have someone else in town mention that Oldman McHermit lives closer to the fires than anyone else; maybe he knows something." I have a hard time toting up if this should count or not, because it's not really a chokepoint or a railroad, once the characters are pushing towards the town, the hermit is the pretty obvious direction (there are no red herrings in this adventure, in other words).

Anyway, because I was puzzled on how to "score" this analysis, I decided I would take a break. I have a metric buttload of Call of Cthulhu modules on my shelf, and I'm interested in working out traits they might have, but I'm not sure how to proceed. HEeeeeelp!

But, the choke point I cringe at comes about not because of railroading but because -- atypically for this campaign -- in one place, there is only one way to do a very important thing, on the Find the Plot / Core Clue level, and it requires reading the scenario author's mind -- or the Keeper to go, "That sucks" and add a few more ways to find the information. You need to know that to find a lead, you have to look for people in the appropriate museum departments, which is totally non-intuitive, although the Investigators may be used to considering museums on their "where to do research list" by then, make your way to the right person, and figure out the right approach to use, all with absolutely no help from the author. Otherwise, well, I guess you don't find a huge chunk of plot and spend at least one full session, if not more, with no idea of what to do. (I really must listen to podcasts of groups going through this to see how it's handled.)

    2. Someone once complained about a scenario where, having found about three separate types of journals, the Investigators have to figure out that they still need to look for a Dream Journal -- all from the same guy. If they don't figure it out, things come to a crashing halt. I'm guessing this refers to Spawn of Azathoth. Now, while I discovered that this is actually realistic, all these different types of journals -- I did a quick inventory of all the different types of journals I kept, how widely they were scattered in the apartment, and so on -- having a scenario come to a crashing halt sucks. I think what one does here is assume a full search will turn up all the goodies, because this is the beginning of the campaign, and they Investigators need their leads. I did the Spot Hidden rolls as "Who got the best one? Okay, you're the one who finds the clue."

    3. Tatters of the King. I love, love, love this campaign. I ran it twice. I'm kind of itching to run it again. It has kickass set pieces. The author totally gets what I love about the Hastur mythos. The author totally gets how human beings who are cultists act. The author explains battle tactics of spellcasters so that I know what I'm doing. The author calibrates so that, while it is possible to kill off one or all of the Investigators, the odds are really good that a party will be able to see the campaign through to its bitter, bitter end.

    BUT. Okay, first of all, ignore the stuff near the end about running certain things until the players are bored and frustrated, unless you are very, very certain your group likes that sort of thing. And, the stitching between those wonderful set pieces leave much to be desired. At one point, there's nothing the Investigators can do until an NPC they've never heard of writes them a letter from out of the blue telling them to do something -- and they have no reason to trust this person. There's a place they're supposed to be totally unable to break into until the plot says, "Okay, time to break into this place." There's a yearlong gap during which time the Investigators aren't supposed to be able to do anything until they receive another letter. My theory about this gap is that it was added to give Investigators a chance to recover from potential battle wounds, both mental and physical. I did a lot of prep when I ran this one to make sure that the Investigators didn't have to sit and twiddle their thumbs until the clue dispenser coughed up the next thing. (I wrote about running that on Yog-sothoth.net.)

    I'm sure I could find more, given time to dedicate to this, and I'm sure I could talk about scenarios that are really good. There are also scenarios that read like recipes for more advanced chefs than I. Fr'ex, I've played in "Funkuthlhu", and I've read it. It worked just fine (presuming you want to play a Call of Cthulhu scenario as if it were a blaxploitation film), but I am not sure I could make it work. I see the scenario. I know everything's there. But I don't see how to make the cake.

    I can think of very few bad CoC games I've played at conventions. Oh, there are some, and sometimes, railroading is an element of that. Chokepoints generally aren't. In two cases, I was less than thrilled by the way the scenario was run. In one of those cases, there was a little too much of the GM breaking into out of character humor and a little too much of his pulling his hair to try to figure out how to get the group together when, really, all he has to do is say, "Okay, you're all together here when the scenario starts." Dude, I'm not gonna argue about that. You don't have to ask me where my character is and rack your brains trying to figure out how to get her from the library to the quad. The other was a scenario that was partly for laughs and partly serious, and I don't think the author got the blend right.

    \n Well for an investigation genre you sometimes need a certain clue to get to the players for the whole scenario to work. In that case I would not even be mad if they got it one way or another and more annoyed if it could only be had with a certain roll and if that fails the scenario does. Ideally it would work without any certain clue and even the worst investigators could come to some kind of ending.
    Other people approaching lovecraftian or at least Call of Cthulhuian Systems seem to agree. Cthulhu Dark and Trail of Cthulhu both tell you to always give out the needed clue for the story to progress and maybe update it by rolls. Which makes sense for certain investigative adventures. So if a CoC module gives the clue to the players one way or another, that does not seem to different or bad, it just avoids the chockepoint and lets them continue. Using a more traditional game approach (magician's choice).
    I think some of the railroading in written CoC grows from the importance of pacing in horror and the author trying to transfer the pacing how he or she imagines it at the table to paper. Some scenarios really lose lots of appeal if the pacing changes in a major way. But writing a roleplaying module that makes the GM understand the needed pace and gives the characters all the freedom can be rather hard. Many CoC scenarios just give the outlines and let the GM and players handle that issue at the table. Which can ask to much of some groups.

    \n Jason, I happen to have just been reading Delta Green. I don't know if this stuff counts as quintessentially Cthulhu to you, but to me it's exactly what I've come to expect of the game: absolutely amazing flavour and literary values, coupled with essentially static and linear adventure design. For a gamer like me who prizes interaction above pretty much everything else this stuff is painful to read.

    For example, check this out, I'll list a couple of most annoying details in the first sample adventure, "Puppet Shows and Shadow Plays". The adventure is in no way exceptional in the CoC culture of module writing, I think, and it is featured as a suitable adventure scenario to start your campaign with. This is how the module author Adam Scott Glancy railroads us:
    • The scenario involves a car buried in the Arizona desert. The investigators might or might not choose to do a helicopter survey (and Spot Hidden rolls) to find the partially exposed car. If they don't, or miss it, then NPCs will find the car automatically the next day. To me this indicates that the writer is not willing or able to play dynamically on even on such minor issues as conjunctive clues that by themselves are secondary to the plot; he has to go through the motions of sort of playing the game, but really he's just running a cutscene the players will get no matter what they do.
    • The first combat encounter with the Traveler (the monster of the week) is described like this: "The Keeper should take this opportunity to demonstrate how deadly the combination of the Traveler and Emanuel Santana is, but you shouldn't wipe the investigators out." The text goes on to describe in detail how the tactical situation shold play out, ending with the escape of the Traveler. This reads to me, again, as a blatant abrigation of any responsibilities one might think that a GM has towards actually running an interactive game where events unfurl dynamically.
    • The Traveler's spaceship is taken by the local police department no matter what the investigators do. Fair enough, but again we learn immediately that this has a plot-related reason instead of just being how the setting works: the people with the ship all die horribly the next night, so apparently the writer doesn't want the investigators to be there. Again the writer is unwilling to let the bones fall as they may, as he wishes to postpone the final confrontation between the investigators and the threat.
    • The centerpiece of the adventure is when the investigators organize a full-blown manhunt for the Traveler and its mortal shell. "Encourage the players to draw up their plans." and so on; little may the players suspect that apparently nothing they do at this juncture will affect the success of the manhunt. It's all the same stuff we get all the time in CoC, a ritual of immersion into what it's like to be a FBI agent. The Traveler will apparently be found and the next scene will commence regardless of player performance here.
    • The description of the final showdown begins: "The Keeper should craft the firefight with Santana to meet the abilities of the investigators." I read this to mean that again, the writer has no intention to actually play ball with the players; he's there to provide an exciting story, so let's pretend for a bit that what you say on your side of the table actually matters - it doesn't, but you get to roll the dice and speak now and then. As an example of the advice here, the writer authors the GM to use any available NPCs present as "bullet-stoppers" to demonstrate how lethal the enemy is without actually killing a player character. This is not how the CoC combat rules tell me they work.
    • Interestingly the one place in the adventure where it would be totally cool to say that you've got a cutscene doesn't get one: after everything's happened the investigators are approached by a member of Delta Green about the investigators joining the eponymous conspiracy. The writer spends several paragraphs figuring out what to do if the players say no. He even says that it's pretty much the premise of the Delta Green campaign that PCs will join Delta Green, but he can't for some reason just say that this is what's going to happen. It's probably some hangup about how it's mandatory to let players make their characters do whatever the player wants, but at the same time the GM gets to completely determine what consequences (if any) those actions might have.
    \n(We could quibble about what "railroading" means, I imagine, but I don't quite feel like it now. If you think that I have such a wide definition of railroading that my examples don't count, then fair enough. For my own purposes it suffices that these types of techniques, whatever name we might use, exemplify a static attitude towards roleplaying, with no room for genuine interaction where the players as well as the GM determine the course of play.)

    To be entirely fair to the adventure, I should note that after the final showdown the writer does allow actual play to determine the ultimate fate of the Traveler: it might escape to continue its murder spree in a different part of the country, or the investigators might succeed in killing and stopping it. I'm not entirely sure why this part is not scripted, but I suspect that the reason is simply that this work, as so many others, is written as a railroad not out of malice but rather due to a failure of technique. The scenario-writer has exactly one place in the adventure where giving a genuine choice with actual consequences does not force him to split his linear narrative, and that's at the end of the piece: whatever happens then is no longer his problem, he doesn't need to write a detailed description of it. So I suspect that the author railroads out of habit and because he doesn't know how to write an adventure without making it a prose narrative; if he could, he probably would allow other situations aside from the very last one to play out freely, too. Of course truly free play would require reworking parts of the adventure, dependent on consequential events as it is.

    Again, I don't think that the adventure I cite here is particularly exceptional in this regard, this sort of stuff has been all over the CoC stuff I read. To me it reads basically as an instituted method of adventure-writing. It's just the only way these guys know how to write an adventure, so this is what they do. I don't even believe that they run these games as linearly as they present them on paper. It's just that their cultural context prevents them from phrasing the venues of freedom adequately while simultaneously actually communicating how their adventure runs.

    It is also important to note that not all CoC adventures are railroads. "Haunted House", which Jason mentions, is not like that. These non-linear investigatory adventures make more sense to me as a gamer, but they have their own issues that have been widely discussed. For example, when I ran the Haunted House for some gamers two years back we had to quit out of frustration without ever getting anywhere, all simply because the players spent their time investigating the wrong things (the crazy former resident who knew nothing) and never happened to go down into the basement. I'm sure that these are solvable issues, either by rules or different GMing technique.

    \n Here are the questions I'd ask (as a player) of a module, and their related potential failure points:

    1) Am I going to be applying my wits to solve a problem? Does cleverness matter? Or is cleverness irrelevant, and solving is entirely up to some combo of the GM, the dice, and whether I happen to look in the right place?

    FAIL: If the module does not support clever problem-solving, but I don't know that, and I spend hours trying to fit puzzle pieces which are not actually puzzle pieces but mere color... That SUCKS.

    1a) If there's no solving, does the module include lots of cool atmosphere and supernatural shit to drive our characters toward insanity?

    FAIL: If there's no solving and no CoC madness fun, this is a shitty module. No further analysis required.

    2) If the module is a test of wits, is it a good test?

    2a) Is the relevance of my choices clear? Do I know which choices to just pick quickly so we can move on, and which choices to apply my full cleverness to because one option is way better than the other?

    FAIL: If I don't know which choices are important, I may spend too much play time on ones that aren't. One player may want to go faster, another player may want to go slower, and argument may ensue. Whatever effort we spend on the choice may subsequently be revealed as wasted, putting a big damper on our enthusiasm for the next choice.

    2b) Are my choices reasonably informed, with at least some sense of risk/reward?

    FAIL: If I'm choosing at random, that's not actually a cleverness test. Which is fine for some choices, but not for the keys to solving the module. If the key to the module is "you see two doors; left or right?" then that can effectively ruin the premise that I am solving this module (unless I just succeeded at some solving task for which the known reward was "50% chance of victory" and the known failure condition was something less than that).

    3) Do I know how I'm doing? How have my choices impacted where I'm at now? Did I lose, and we can skip to the epilogue now? Did killing the monster count as total victory, and we can skip to the epilogue now?

    FAIL: If I don't get the key puzzle piece, and now there's no reason to expect I ever will, does the GM notice and end it, or do we just keep playing pointlessly? (You'd think every module would tell the GM when this is, but no such luck.)

    Jason, I agree with you that "the investigators didn't get it done; you see in the paper that more people died; oh, the horror!" is fine. But virtually all of the chokepoint/roadblock misery I've seen could have been avoided by invoking that outcome when it was truly decided, e.g. 10 minutes into the game. If the secret to beating the monster is in the diary in the toybox in the cellar down the stairs under the ancient rug, and the players fail to deduce that the rug is important (whether that's because they weren't clever enough or because the module failed 2a and they ignored it), then the problem-solving endeavor is already over.

    To sum up, my approach would be to read through a module, imagining playing through it, and whenever one of the above FAIL conditions comes up, I'd deem it a bad module. If there's enough other cool stuff in it, then I'd see if I can fix the FAIL myself; if so, then I guess I'd run it, and maybe call it "a good module if you do x."

    \n Well, in the case of Haunted House my problems are strictly methodological: in hindsight there are obviously ways to deal with these things, they're just ways that are never described in the rules, and thus ways that I've never learned in my occasional CoC-play. The next time I run the adventure I'm totally going to explain in advance that you the players are responsible for efficient use of our time, and if at the end of the night you're stuck and not getting anywhere, we'll just be saying that your investigators forfeit their supposed reward for the case and go home.     As for the railroad-type scenarios, I personally think that individual scenes and how they're presented are a bit of a red herring in comparison to the general attitude of the game. The question that matters to me is whether a game and an adventure provide me with dynamic gameplay where players interact with each other and the GM, influencing where the game goes. If they don't, that's what I call "railroading". In this way all the verbiage in "Puppet Shows and Shadowplays" that is wasted on static scenery is totally indicative of railroading. The car hidden in the desert, for instance, is presented as a supposedly interactive scene where players get to roll dice about stuff. The GM knows, though, that in reality that dice roll doesn't matter at all. It's not interactive and it's not dynamic, the writer already determined this content at the drawing table. How much of your playtime content is passive regurgitation of precreated material? That's my measure of railroading.\n

    \nThis is a very good question, and one that's under active investigation by I imagine the vast majority of tabletop rpg designers everywhere.

    My first suggestion is absolute honesty and principle in writing about methodology. I'm sure that this comes as no surprise. Following this ideal we may come to conclude that it is not possible at this time to write adventure modules for this specific game under consideration, simply because the type of play the game endorses is not possible to prepare meaningfully in advance with the tools we currently have in our possession.

    The above is not as fanatical as it sounds, there are many games for which people simply don't create adventures in advance because doing so would defeat the point. Polaris, for example.

    I find this notion a very serious charge against all that traditional rpg design stands for: could it actually be the case that by encouraging a post-dungeon adventure module culture publishers have pushed for substandard, dishonest and dysfunctional game-writing? This conclusion would be inescapable if we concluded both that CoC is intended to be played in a specific way, and that way is impossible to transcribe on paper in the form of anything but linear prose that is inadequate in capturing how the game is played.

    However, such absolutism aside (I don't agree with it myself, anyway), do we actually have some concrete means to write adventures for a game like CoC without resorting to trickery? That is, should I view CoC adventures as simply inadequate in design, or is it actually impossible to solve the railroading conundrum to my standards? Have CoC writers been victims of insufficient rpg theory that we now possess to do better, or are just as stranded as the people writing these exercises in track-laying?

    My personal belief is that it is possible to prep and run Call of Cthulhu without reducing the game to the type of static experience the Delta Green rulebook paints in its adventures. I haven't done the practical fieldwork myself yet, so I don't presume to go into the details at this point. A lot obviously depends on whether you're looking to challenge the group of investigators, or whether it's merely important to maximize the drama and horror. Either way, interactivity and real stakes should be possible.

    It is interesting and indicative, though, that the great majority of every single Delta Green book is dedicated to extremely open-ended writing that I would characterize as adventure scenarios in any other game.     \n Okay, if you are like me, you bought Call of Cthulhu 4th edition when you were 12 and learned how to run it from the ready-to-play scenarios. So I'll go a little farther back than Jason and start there.

    1. The Haunted House (aka The Haunting in 5th ed):
    Landlord says "my house is haunted," the investigators check it out. Players have two options here, the first being search the house. Finding both the monster and papers that contain a partial explanation require no rolls. There is a roll required to find an item. Solving the mystery by charging into the house is dangerous and may kill some characters, but is completely do-able. The scenario gives the monster's motivations and some behaviours that are based on PC actions. The PCs are then left to their own devices.

    The second option is, of course research. Why would the players think to do research? Because of what it says on page 88, in the "How to Play the Game" section:
    "The first step in a Call of Cthulhu scenario is to determine what you are up against. This may involve going to the library, speaking with local residents, trying to obtain journals and diaries of those involved, and comparing reports of the problem with the character's arcane books."
    If the PCs do this, no roll is required to find out exactly where and who the monster is. Some rolls are required to obtain additional clues, many of which seem to act as plot hooks for continuing the adventure.

    If the players do not investigate, then they do not know how to play the game. If they had followed the instructions in the first column of the first page of the "How to Play the Game" section, they would have known where the monster was, without having to roll.

    2.The Madman (also in 5e):
    Some hick has gone insane, the investigators check it out. How there could be a choke point in this scenario is beyond me, it is way too short. Players must roll to get info from anybody except the hick himself who will just tell them he is consorting with aliens. The aliens come at night and either the investigators stop them or they destroy the whole valley, the end.

    The 5e version adds the missing nespaperman, more clues, and some motivations for investigators to be investigating.

    3. The Brockford House
    Dude says his house is fucked up, the investigators check it out. Rolls are required to find clues unless the players dig in the one obvious place, in which case they find the secret stuff automatically. Finding the secret stuff turns this adventure into a dungeon crawl, essentially, only with no treasure, just traps and monsters.

    Sadly, and unlike "The Haunted House," there is no research section to this adventure, which makes it rather sub-par comparatively.

    A note follows these three scenarios:
    "The following adventures are by various authors, and are less to be regarded as models than to be simply enjoyed."
    Which suggests these three are models for properly playing Call of Cthulhu. Summarizing them in brief:
    1. Somebody asks you to investigate.
    2. You find some clues, maybe, and probably somebody tells you where the monster is. Or it's just obvious.
    3. You find the monster.
    4a. You go insane.
    4b. You are killed.
    4c. You go home and the monster kills people.
    4d. You defeat the monster and go home.

    Interestingly, all four of the next adventures are actually better than "The Brockford House," in my opinion.

    4. Paper Chase
    Dude had some books stolen, wants them back, the investigators check it out. There are exactly 6 clues, each of which requires a roll, each of which should reasonably point out the monster's location, all on its own. The PCs must then interact with the monster. In order to reach a choke point in this adventure, the players must fail all their roll, not just one. This is a perfect example of how to design a mystery by creating a secret thing and scattering clues as to its location all over the place, so that if the players obtain even a third of them, they should have a pretty solid solution.

    5.The Underground Menace
    Unlike other 4e scenarios, this one gives several options for how PCs get involved (this innovation was also added to 5e scenarios). There are earthquakes in Michigan, the investigators check it out. Most of the investigation here pertains to the bad guy, and the clue (just one) that lead the investigators to him is a rather poor hook. It requires a roll, but this roll can be made over and over again until it succeeds. Once the bad guy is identified, however, the investigation leads pretty clearly to a monster. This scenario gives clear instructions on a variety of success and failure results, and combinations.

    6. The Rescue
    Dude dies in the woods, the investigators check it out. This adventure has a lot of NPCs and colour details to it. There are only two groups of people in this adventure causing trouble: the bad guys and the guys who will tell the investigators about the bad guys, so it is quite easy to identify the bad guys. Once they start investigating the bad guys, a fight is almost inevitable, and that's the end of the adventure.

    7. Mystery of Loch Feinn
    Dude is murdered in Scotland, the investigators check it out. A successful Spot Hidden roll will get the investigators all the inside dope. If they fail that roll, they can just ask any local who the bad guys are, without needing to roll. Once they know who the bad guys are, a few rolls for research uncover some more clues. Or they could just go to the bad guys' house and either fight them or secretly watch them summon monsters.

    So there you go. That's seven typical Call of Cthulhu scenarios, and specifically the seven that would have taught you how to run the game and write your own adventures if you started between 1989 and 1992. If this rulebook turned you into a railroading Keeper or left you frustrated with choke points, I don't know what to tell you, other than you fucked up. None of these adventures, except for "The Brockford House," try to hide the monster or the cultists, and honstly some of these would make great towns for Dogs in the Vineyard.

    \n I've seen two module-writing "systems" (for a lack of a better word) that consistently result in interactive and dynamic modules where the contribution of the players is clear and appreciated. One is the old school D&D location-based commando operation, also known as the dungeon: a D&D dungeon will lay out the land, set up treasure and monsters, and then leave the players to determine their own approach. Because the players control pacing, approach vector and their own advance knowledge of what is ahead, they have a massive amount of say on what actually happens in play, all despite also having a very detailed way to prepare a game, so much so that the D&D GM can actually be a "referee" much of the time, just adjudicating interaction instead of inventing stuff.

    The other system is the Forge-originating relationship map model that was established in Sorcerer's Soul and utilized in games like Trollbabe, Dogs in the Vineyard and many others. Heroquest is an interesting game in that several adventure modules have been written for it under this paradigm, but it has also gotten a big share of traditional linear prose adventures. The relationship adventure, as most of us know, looks a bit like a traditional adventure in that it introduces NPCs and perhaps provides a ready-made starting position for the player characters. The difference is in the fact that the relationship map adventure doesn't provide a string of scenes, it just communicates the likely motivations of the NPCs in clear and easily applicable ways. In play the GM then provides content by looking at this orchestra of NPCs and associated locations, and allows the actions of the characters (PC and NPC alike) to drive play. Typically a list of Bangs is involved: the scenario-author provides suggestions like hey, maybe this character I told you about could break into the PC's house sometime, assuming the PC catches their eye.

    Those two and the traditional linear prose adventure are basically the three types of module structures that I know about, I can't offhand name a fourth one. This is a strange situation, as one would assume that such a central type of rpg text would have more technical variety to it. It goes without saying that this is a very fruitful field for further rpg development - figuring out how to deliver premade content in new and exciting ways that leave different space for the players to fill at the table.

    (The CoC investigation adventure, while often not written in a strikingly different manner, is still a somewhat different beast compared to the linear adventure. It's sort of a halfway house between the D&D location-based challenge and the linear adventure in that the amount of clues and the responsiveness of the antagonist NPCs seems to ultimately determine whether play becomes a string of static scenes or a dynamic battlefield like in D&D. Even the most dynamic adventures in CoC are still written with verbiage that smacks of linear thinking: "after the investigators do this" and so on, things that are never said in interaction-aware game texts.)

    What is notable about the very structure of the linear adventure is that everything that myself and some others find annoying and dull about them directly derives from how these adventures are written. Once you have this image of what an adventure text is like, and that image involves a long string of scenes that build upon each other, what other choice do you have except to end up with a text that presumes outcomes? It's an integral outcome of how these texts are written, and the author's propensity towards railroading is to a degree secondary.

    If one wanted to write CoC adventures in a less sequential and linear manner, it'd require reimagining the structure of the entire adventure text. For example, "Puppet Shows and Shadow Plays" is an adventure that basically pits the investigators against a single alien being that crashlands in the USA and then proceeds to take control of people (it lives within their torso) for the purposes of serial killing. As the adventure begins the alien is in Arizona indian lands. As far as I can see, all the kewl parts of this adventure could have just as easily been delivered in a non-sequential manner if the author had the balls to let go of his setpiece details. The adventure is full of these small iconic scenes the author has preimagined for the GM, but setting them up requires a massively heavy hand: the alien has to kill at specific times in specific places, it has to escape to kill again, or all these lovely preworked setpiece encounters won't get to happen. The basic idea of the adventure has nothing in it that requires a linear treatment, it could just as easily be a detailed map of a 200 mile stretch of Arizona and a few instructions and ideas of what kinds of events might happen depending on what players do; the only thing this would require would be letting go of the idea that the module author is writing a horror story, and embracing the idea that the GM can do the little narrative detail in play.

    \nNaw. It puts player agency where it belongs. The question isn't "do you find the clue?" but "what do you do with it?"

    If you want player input on how they get the clue that they are going to get, then make it transparent. Either just ask them a leading question about how they get there (as some of the more contemporary games encourage a GM to do), or tell them up front that their roll is going to be about whether their character gets to be awesome or gets shown up by the locals.

    If you present the roll as "make this roll or you won't get to see the clue" and then give them the clue a different way when they fail, you are being dishonest as a GM about what the roll means. And the text is telling you to be dishonest in that way. Which is bad. So it needs fixed.

    Another option is to play up the dysfunctional interrelationships between the PCs as an important element of play. Once you do that, you can make the roll be about who finds the clue and whether or not they share that information fully with the rest of the players. There's some room for interesting play there, which is usually not explored in CoC games, and which the system doesn't seem to explicitly support. I think it would potentially play to the "the other humans are the real monsters" aspect that I love about a good zombie movie.       What's the Magician's Choice again?
      \nI think it's similar to the old magic trick principle called The Force. Ever learn that trick as a kid where you write down a playing card, seal up the note in an envelope, then layout all of the cards in some fashion and tell your pal to pick certain rows or columns?Then you remove some. It goes a couple of times usually, but inevitably, the final card left is exactly the card you wrote down!

      How does it work? Well, if you've got three rows of cards, and you ask them to pick one, what you do next depends on whther they picked a row with your chosen card in it. Anyway, it ends up with cards removed at each step, always inevitably leaving the card you wanted them to choose ("predicted") at the end of the trick.

      In gaming, it's a bit like moving around clues or otherwise limiting the flow of play, based on what the players do. It's moving the goblin ambush to the left fork in the forest road, when the players don't go down the right fork.

      \n My first experiences with CoC play was what I'd call "dungeon style" and involved the sort of location-centric sorts of adventures like the Haunted house that JD cites (I believe that was the first CoC adventure I ever played). These were never interesting to me, because what they involve is player participation to solve the mystery. Meaning you can fail to solve the mystery. I'm just not interested in that sort of challenge, and it didn't strike me playing CoC that this is what it should be about. Note that my play of CoC lead to me reading HPL, not the other way around. So I can't claim that I had some expectation of it being like the literature. It's just that I expected horror, and never got anything like that.

      I don't want such a game to be at all about player challenge, personally, because that puts me in an adversarial mindset where it's impossible to feel any sense of horror. Just like when I go to a horror movie, and challenge myself not to be scared. If I do that, I won't be scared. And then what's the point? When I play a horror game, I want to "play appropriately stupid," as I've quoted before. Which actually means that I want to play my character as an interestingly fallible being who makes the sort of mistakes that a real human would if they were to encounter such horrors. I just can't do that if I'm trying to "win" the scenario.

      So usually I didn't win the scenario, meaning that we usually didn't even discover the horrific bits. Oh, actually I would end up pushing the limits of my own belief on what my character might do in order to get more info, just so that we might encounter the horrific stuff. But even then, we often "lost."

      This is also the problem with any scenario that expects a lot of fighting to occur. Nothing in CoC games was less horrific than lots of die rolling to see how many hit points of damage I was doing. Dull. I'd rather play D&D if the game is going to be that way... and that's a big condemnation coming from me. It's not that I find combat a problem with the genre or something. It's that the old-school system makes it feel gamey again. Get the shotgun, it does more damage. Ack.

      Then there were scenarios that have the "Force" used in them. These come in two varieties, generally. In the case of finding the car, the issue is that it's not a magician's force, it's an obvious force. Oh, you failed your roll? Well you succeed anyway. Why did we bother rolling then? Sincerely, if you're intent on getting a player the info, just give them the info. This is completely non-controversial. All GM's do it with most info all the time. Take the Haunted House scenario, for instance. It begins with somebody coming to get the PCs to help. This could be written such that there's a story in the paper, and the GM could require the players to roll to notice the story, with the attendant chance that the scenario never happens. But why would you do that?

      Each roll should have a purpose, an interesting branching that occurs if it is failed. As Jonathan says, something consequential. Else don't bother to roll. As described, the roll to find the car could be framed as an attempt to look better than the local cops or something, and that's fine. It's just that as the scenario posits it, it's not interesting.

      The other sort of force is the sort that's done surreptitiously. The problem with this is that the players know. At least I always did, and all of my players always did when I was doing it. They even commented on it in ways that I knew meant that they weren't comfortable with it. They knew I was railroading behind the scenes, and it bothered them. All illusionism does is to make players uncertain of how much agency they have, which is a far cry from giving them actual agency. My point was that all of the play that I actually encountered was dysfunctional because of the culture of play that emerged at the time. And that has probably as much to do with trying to convert from playing D&D as it did with anything else, as well as a number of other factors.

      \n I'll give an example of how one can improve on the old CoC module writing methods. The first is that there's a tendency to write them as though they are something like a storyboard. That is, they tend to use language like "Then this happens." This suggests that either you are going to force the players to do X, or that there's a timeline for NPCs (like was mentioned above) that means that there's some real universe going on behind the scenes that make X necessarily happen at this time. Now, when reading these adventures, I mentally edit these texts to say what I think the writers really mean. Which is something like, "At this time it would probably be interesting for X to happen, but as always you have to take into account what has gone before, and what the current situation is like, and determine if in fact maybe something else might be more interesting or appropriate at this juncture."

      Now, all of that would take a lot to write added on to every sentence, and the notion is that the GM is supposed to be smart enough to edit this in. But this doesn't make for an efficient presentation. And the solution is simply to present the information to the GM as ideas for them to use. Events should be presented in the format where at the top it says, "When appropriate here are several horrific events to throw at the investigators as responses to their actions." And then simply list the events. Sure, put them in an interesting chronological order that might work, but have the understanding that there is no "reality" behind the scenario that the GM has to follow, and that the GM is following the player lead, and allowing things to develop naturally.

      Yeah, this can be R-map and Bangs. Or it can be less about theme if that's your thing, and the events can be meant to provoke player challenge, or simply horror etc. Make your scenarios do what you want. But ALL of the information in a scenario should be presented in such a way as to facilitate the GM being able to provide players with a chance to interact in some meaningful way. Anything that's in a scenario that's there to just get characters from point A to B should NOT be presented as an option, but should use overt GM authority. Such as you use when you present the scenario to them as a "this is what we're doing tonight."

      Terminology aside, call it force or authority or whatever, if the GM is using the powers that the game gives them to move the situation to a place where the players are enabled to interact in interesting ways, it's a good thing. If the GM is using their powers to make the interesting decisions themselves, or generally to make it so that the players aren't feeling that they are actually participating, it's a bad thing (barring a theoretical sort of player who likes this sort of treatment).

      Just make sure that the scenario does whatever it can to inform the GM of how to use the information therein to accomplish this goal.

      2. Look to the Future

      Temple of the Moon
      Somebody sends an artifact to the investigators for safe keeping, but gets attacked before he can collect it. Then he spills the beans about an archaeological dig and the scenario assumes that the investigators will have to convince him to go with them, instead of vice versa.

      Once the investigators get to the dig site, the adventure becomes a dungeoncrawl. On the way there, however, they will be followed and harassed by several different groups of NPCs. They all have motivations and tactics listed, and what they will attempt to do is described at each point, so the Keeper doesn't just have to make all this stuff up. How the adventure plays out depends partly on how the investigators interact with them, and how well their exploration of the once-secret places goes.

      Dark Carnival
      This scenario features a carnival with a dark secret. It's mostly a collection of NPCs in a single location that the investigators can explore, but the real secret is the secret lair. There are nine different ways to find the secret lair. Most of the paperwork research is provided up-front to the investigators, leaving only the questioning of people and the searching of their properties.

      The Secret of Castronegro
      A friend from Location 1 goes missing. The investigators get a handout that mentions three disappearances in Location 2 and mentions Location 3. In Location 1 there are two clues leading to Location 2, one needs a roll the other doesn't. A third clue leads to Location 3 but "should not be easy," requiring rolls and bribes. There are also two clues that mention the bad guy, which require rolls, one of which can also be found in Location 2. In Location 2 there are three clues that lead to Location 3. They require rolls and one requires communicating with people on the other side of the country. Once in Location 3, the investigators have no obstructions to finding out the secret by wandering around save for the bad guy who tries to kill them, which pretty much prevents them from failing to complete the investigation unless they pack it in and just leave.

      So my tally for this book is:
      Shadows of Yog-Sothoth: Some magician's choices to overcome chokepoints.
      The Warren: Some railroading.
      The Pits of Bendal-Dolum: Railroading by bad guy.
      Temple of the Moon: Nothing.
      Dark Carnival: Nothing.
      The Secret of Castronegro: Nothing.

Each investigator who does not notice the glasses (unwarned by other investigators) has a 50% of knocking over a glass on the way out and a 25% chance of knocking over two glasses. For each glass knocked over, the investigator may roll a luck roll and avoid damage on a roll of 01. Each glass knocked over by an investigator causes 1d6 damage that turn, and 1d3 damage the next, and creates an eerie glow on the investigator and the entryway.

The Eater, knowing this is going on, will be sure to feign horror and draw attention to the sounds, in such a way as to try to get the investigators to rush out and see what is going on.       \n I've tried to answer the question in the post above three times now, and I'm afraid there's a good chance that I'm just not understanding it, JD. But I'll try to just answer just the last sentence.

      There is a power disparity between players and GMs in a traditional game setup (which is intentional, and often a very beneficial thing). If a player attempts to find something, and misses it, it's because they either didn't meet a player challenge to look in the right place, or they simply missed a roll. If the NPCs find a thing, did the GM have them roll? Likely not, this decision will be made by fiat (if they do roll, then we have a potential bottleneck again). Why make this decision? Was it because the police "would" look there, and it would be unrealistic for them not to find the thing? Well the PCs failed, so apparently it wasn't a sure thing to find it.

      It's apparent that the GM is making this decision to move the plot onwards. Which means that the plot would have moved onward no matter what the PC did. For players like me, this feels like we are not participating in a meaningful way.

      Note that as I've already said, I'm really not all that concerned with rolls, since the player doesn't really contribute to the success or failure of the roll in most cases (the GM can assume that the player's comments about how they're looking or where may warrant a bonus perhaps, but mostly it's just a die roll). And whether or not my character finds something is not the sort of thing I'm particularly worried about contributing to in play (the question of whether we win). As I've said, I would prefer in this case just to find the vehicle, and have no chance of failure (ala Gumshoe). Simply having the roll is where the problem starts. What's the purpose of failing? If there's no interesting output from failure, if the end result will be the same in either case, then why have the roll at all?

      (BTW, I've always detested the advancement rules for CoC, as it leads to folks asking to attempt rolls even when there's no tension regarding the task at hand, simply to get a checkmark. So that's not helping in any way for me, personally. I suppose it's fine for those folks looking for a "tactical" CoC game, though.)

      But this finding the car example is just one example of the sorts of things that happen, others of which DO affect the sorts of decisions that I want to make. Again, I've played with GMs who, having read these texts as being scripts that were to be followed, have railroaded us right to the end of the plot, using illusion methods that were plainly visible to everyone playing. Or more "honest" GMs who, when we miss a roll at a bottleneck (or maybe miss many rolls), simply have the adventure fail to reach any interesting conclusion. Intelligent, well-meaning GMs.

I recall one example in a convention game where I was playing a plucky newspaper guy... sorta the everyman hero of the story.       \n Mike & JD, here's my two cents:

      I think CoC has been played as two basic games:

      Call of Cthulhu: the Reacting
      As a player, success and failure are effectively out of my hands, but I get lots of cool fodder for roleplaying the confrontation with cosmic horror and the descent into madness. (Or cool 1920s stuff that I dig; whatever it is that I like about Lovecraft fiction.)

      Call of Cthulhu: the Solving
      As a player, I put myself in the investigator's shoes and use my wits to unravel the mystery before it's too late.

      The post-game summary of the fiction might look the same, but what I do to contribute as a player is quite different. It's the opportunity for those contributions that will make or break the game for me.

      So, Mike's playing the Reacting. GM fiat and dice luck are fine for some things, but not if they take away his opportunity to encounter and react to cool Lovecraftiana.

      JD's points, regarding player agency to choose a direction, are potentially quite germane to the Solving, but are probably irrelevant to the Reacting.

      This is my biggest gripe with CoC: it often fails to get the group (or even a given player!) on the same page about what mode they're playing in. There's an abundance of content that seems to beg for clever solution, yet the game isn't really a solvable puzzle, and GMs and players are often split between trying to make it a solvable puzzle versus just digging into the atmosphere and freaky happenings like good Lovecraft protagonists.

      \n JD, I never said that the GM can't advance the story. In fact that's their job IMO, to provide information that moves the story forward. What I'm saying is that it's bad when they advance the story in such a way as to take away player agency, instead of creating more.

      Note that players can make the same mistake, too. They can, for example, refuse to engage with the adventure (or go off in a completely unwarranted direction, or any number of possible bad ideas). I agree wholeheartedly that agreeing to play a CoC adventure as a player means that you should adopt certain tropes and act in a certain way that's going to allow the GM to do their job as well. If you say that the investigators just stay home, you're sabotaging the adventure.

      You're setting up a false dichotomy. That either the GM gets to advance the plot, or they don't. The GM gets to advance the plot; they need to. But they need to do it in such a way as to give the players as much opportunity to feel like they're making meaningful contributions to the action as possible. And the players have the responsibility to take the GM's information and run with it and ensure that the GM has an easy time with moving on to the next event.

      It's a collaborative thing, or is best when it is, IMO. The problem is that too many GMs feel that players can't be trusted to advance the plot "properly" and so take it upon themselves to do it themselves, limiting player agency to ensure that this happens. The fact that the adventure plots are often written like this (as a series of events that happen in a specified order) has informed many a GM that this is the right way to proceed.

      (Note, Johnstone, that I don't say "all" GMs behave this way. It's quite possible that I've just had atrocious luck with my GMs or something not having figured out a good way to run these scenarios. As I've said, I myself personally can mentally re-write these adventures to run them in a way that I hope is entertaining. But then I've thought a lot about this stuff, and there was a time I would have made all of the errors that I am speaking about.)

      This is what I always tell GMs: good GMing is using your authority as GM to affect the world, NPCs, events, etc, in such a way as to create opportunities for players to collaboratively move the story forward. No differently than in more tactical RPGs where the GM's job is to set up tactical challenges for the players to have their characters face.

      \n Interesting thread. In the years I spent running Pagan Publishing, eliminating railroading was one of our major goals. We were seeing a lot of adventures published by Chaosium that felt like someone's screenplay with a die-roll chokepoint between each scene. So we very consciously set out to design adventures that were more like narrative landscapes where players had a lot of freedom. We didn't always succeed, but John Crowe's books such as Walker in the Wastes and Realm of Shadows were among our better examples.

      That said, when I took a really big swing at a non-railroading adventure ("Dream Factory" in Mortal Coils) it took almost 30,000 words to cover all the likely avenues of investigation that players might take. And that's just a single adventure, not a campaign. It has a simple formula: a cast of NPCs who know various things, locations to explore, a timeline of what happens if nothing changes, and a couple of set pieces that can be triggered at a variety of points by player actions. But the result was so long and detailed that I doubt many groups have ever run it.

      On the smaller scale, my short adventure "In Media Res" (from Unspeakable Oath 10, reprinted in From the Vault) is a very open scenario but it's not very investigatory. It's really just an improv piece where the players bounce off each other and occasionally find a new clue to have arguments about. It works great but it's a radical departure from typical CoC material. When I first ran it, one PC killed another in the first twenty seconds. I think that was a record.

      I definitely saw railroading as the enemy. Our internal playtests tended to be extremely detailed investigation simulations where we might spend forty-five minutes just dissecting clues and strategizing our next steps. They were awesome intellectual challenges but not what every gaming group wants to do.

      Tales of the Miskatonic Valley (1991)

      Freak Show:
      The investigators discover a baby Deep One at a carnval. The carnies want to make money, the Deep Ones want their spawn back.       Regiment of Dread:
      An old man is haunted by his experiences in the Civil War. This one also has a timeline of set supernatural events, including a climax which the PCs are forced to witness/participate in if they stick around past a certain point. The adventure says it is "essential" that they do so, and frankly it's kind of boring of they miss it?       A Painted Smile:
      An old lady gets a hate-on for the investigators and supernaturally harrasses them. This one is a series of events that happen to the investigators while they dig up clues. It kind a looks like if the PCs don't solve the mystery, it's possible for the Keeper to run out of material? Nothing.

      The Watcher in the Valley:
      The investigators go check out an archaeological dig and bad stuff happens. This one has a bunch of NPCs and a loose timeline of events. Basically the evil stuff has a goal and a set of tactics and the Keeper uses these to create scenes where horror happens. This one is clean. Nothing.

      Fade to Gray:
      The investigators go looking for some missing teenagers and find a supernatural villain. This is the only one in the book that I never ran, but I wish I had because it is my favourite here. There is a bunch of NPCs, some investigating to get information, and then a series of events as the villain tries to attack them supernaturally (spoilers: they are sort-of set-pieces, but they are dreams sent by villain as attacks). These come with instructions but are left to the Keeper's discretion, so this one is also clean. Nothing.

      Trail of Yig:
      The investigators attend a seance and shit goes wrong, finally leading them to a horror hole in the middle of nowhere. This one looks pretty easy for inexperienced players to screw up because there are only a few clues that lead to the end and they are easy to miss if you aren't used to reading things you're normally not supposed to. Also there is only one way to get the clue that totally tharts the evil. Verdict is: not enough clues, but not a railroad, because if you get the clues it's possible to skip right to the end. Chokepoint.

      One thing about these is that they almost all use timelines, either strict ones with set dates that events happen on, or flexible timelines of NPC behaviours, to dictate pace and flow, but only in one case are the PCs forced to abide by it if they have other plans. Presentation-wise, this structure could definitely benefit from some of the more recent developments in organization, like what is seen in Dungeon World, but content-wise I remember this method being pretty solid.

      Also, a couple of these have no Cthulhu Mythos stuff in them.

      \n Sure...module play has always had a significant amount of railroading in it. As noted in this thread and others, it's not universal, but it's consistent over games and over time. As noted in the Vampire 1ed thread, it even persisted in modules written for games whose primary textual instruction to its audience was "don't railroad!"

      And to a degree, it's not even "story" that creates the railroad. With the CoC modules we've analyzed, it's really just pacing, as others have noted really cogently in the thread. And if you think about what stories are, it makes perfect sense that railroading would not really be connected to them, because stories are, primarily, character decisions and consequences.

      Modules tend to railroad to get the players to as much content as possible. Video games do this too (although after Spec Ops: The Line, I expect many, many game companies to re-evaluate this, at least in games with a narrative.) You go to this dungeon because that's what's prepared. You seek out the magical dingus because that's where the adventure is. It's not really a "story" railroad in the sense that you make key decisions for the players. All the key decisions in your typical Dragonlance railroad were in the hands of the players - spells and attacks and movement and exploration and so on - but the players must be delivered to those decisions for them to make them.

      There are some exceptions. The really early Champions module I have is definitely railroading to preserve comic book reality, for example. But overall I think that modules-that-railroad are definitely a thing, and not even a thing that closely related to story. I think it's just related to the concept of "you bought this, you're gonna want to use as much of it as possible."

      Anyway, that's just my theory.

      \n I'm currently reading Black Bag Jobs for the Laundry RPG, which is close enough to Call of Cthulhu -- BRPS with a few tweaks, Lovecraftian horrors dealt with by modern government bureaucracy.

      The thing I notice about the first scenario is that the players have the freedom to lose big, as in Britain could become uninhabitable. I don't know that this in itself guarantees a scenario is not a railroad, but it helps. Anyway, the PCs are told, "Okay, find out what's gone wrong on an oil platform, deal with it, and keep the Deep Ones from getting pissed and destroying us all." I am taking it as read that if the players aren't buying in enough to go to the oil platform, then there's a bigger problem than some railroading going on.

      Once at the platform, the PCs have a number of trained NPCs willing to take instructions. Lots of freedom for the players. Communications with superiors are cut off, at least for a time, but... if this is railroading, it's a kind I can live with. At this stage, one could have a TPK. One could theoretically set off events that would lead to the Deep Ones destroying Britain. Either of these means the group probably doesn't get to the rest of the scenario, so there's freedom to screw things up so badly that the scenario ends early.

      The next part -- well, if "Okay, you now know the rest of the problem is over here, and that's where you have to go now" is a railroad, then there's railroading. I don't know if you consider this railroading, linear plotting, or just part of the scenario. The new location is likely to lead back to the old, short of a TPK, which is unlikely, but possible. At the old, the PCs may know enough to salvage the situation, may have been tricked into making things worse, or may have chosen to throw their lot in with the villain, which will make things worse. There are a couple of variations on these basic options, but that's what it boils down to.

      The second scenario is the one I'm finishing now. It has a double set up -- that it, it opens with the PCs being told, "Your job is to do X" where X is a false beginning, a comic scene, leading to the real mission -- although the false beginning does have a couple of things that are probably clues-in-retrospect.

      The PCs' actual job is to find a laptop than an NPC left behind in a cab. Again, I am assuming that refusing to do that is a sign of deeper problems than railroading. There are a lot of options detailed here, enough that I'm comfortable that the author figures that if the PCs come up with something unexpected, the Keeper should be able to roll with it.

      That's as far as I've gotten. I'm not yet sure whether tracking down the laptop is linear or railroaded, but so far, I am pleased with the scenario. I do note that certain minor bits of railroading-for-comedy are acceptable to me in the Laundry scenarios if this is done for the purpose of recreating a bit of the tone of the books.

      \n Sadly, the rest of the second scenario -- Okay, I might not use the term "railroading", but only because I'd be too busy screaming and pulling my hair in frustration.

      Now, to be fair, I can easily run the scenario and just take out what I consider The Stupid.

      So, the PCs have, one way or another, found a location they need to visit, and it has something they were looking for. Up to here, I'm good. There's an amusing complication tossed in, and I'm grinning at that. At the appropriate time, NPC X shows up.

      Now, NPC X and NPC Y are both villains. X is betraying Y, and decides to use the PCs. This means that X will make absolutely sure that the PCs put together the clues to learn of Y's villainy -- still fine. In fact, glorious -- there is an actual Reason for the NPC to walk the PCs through the clues.

      But, at this point, the author makes some odd assumptions:

      1. The PCs will believe or at least act like they believe X completely. Sure, Keepers are told to play X as always a step ahead of the PCs. You tell me if you feel that you're always a step ahead of your players and their PCs. I don't.

      2. They will go to where Y is without attempting to deal with X, and X will follow them, and they will have No Chance of spotting X. Okay, X is invisibly following them, but at this point, the PCs may well know enough to know that's a factor, and, last I checked, "invisible" does not mean "inaudible".

      3. Okay, they are now at the building where Y is, and there's a comedic bit I love where a zombie can't make a phone call until someone takes off the "Do Not Use" post it from the phone. Or, you know, makes the call herself. But, why is the author assuming that the PCs are going to want to call Y from the reception desk of his building? I give it at least even odds that the PCs will walk past the zombie and head straight for Y's office. I see nothing to indicate that this won't work.

      4. The PCs will then have the conversation with Y -- on the phone -- in such a way that Y will know that the gig is up. Y will then push the button to lock himself in his office and have security procedures target the PCs. And, again, we are presuming that the PCs aren't in the office to begin with.

      5. The PCs can't be in the office so that Y can try a different plan, as Y is actually smart enough to figure that the anti-intruder measures will merely slow the PCs down. So, there MUST BE this scene of Y using this different plan. Er, why?

      6. Once the PCs have dealt with Y, assuming Y is still alive, X will kill Y, No Matter What. Granted, X is invisible, and if the PCs have actually followed the script the author expects, they're not likely expecting invisible assassins -- but they might not be following that script. Also, I'd expect some consideration of a couple of basic tactics one could use against invisible enemies. It's lovely to know there's a make-the-invisible-person-visible device nearby, if the PCs recognize it for what it is, but I'm thinking about stuff like tossing buckets of water or rugs over invisible people.

      7. Oh, and what's at stake here is the job of the PCs' boss. What if the PCs decide that they're okay with the boss going down and back either X or Y? Heck, I'd expect the PCs to at least pretend to do so when they're dealing with Y, either on the phone or in Y's office.

      8. And, even if the PCs screw up, their boss will only temporarily lose some pull. In other words, the PCs do not truly have the ability to lose.

      There's a side issue as well. I sometimes wonder how carefully the author has calibrated opposition. It seems to me that there's a reasonable chance that one or more or maybe even all of the PCs will wind up dead in the scenario as she is write. Is this the author's intent?

      This is far from the worse scenario I've hit. It just happened to be right to hand. I can easily fix it -- but I don't consider that an excuse.

      \n Author weighing in for clarification.
      1 is a fair comment. Having the villain of the piece interact directly with the PCs is always risky.
      2... well, there isn't much "dealing with X to do." At this point in the scenario, there isn't that much evidence pointing to X as the villain - she just looks like a minor flunky who points the way to the next scene. As for following the characters, she can do that at a distance.
      Any prewritten investigative scenario is going to have sections where the writer assumes the most likely course of events happens (in this case, that the PCs follow X's suggestion to go after her boss). Otherwise, you end up with either absurd levels of if-thens...
      3. They can't phone Y directly from their cellphones (see page 35), but the zombie's desk phone is a direct line to Y's desk phone (and they don't have this number). They can just grab the phone themselves. (The zombie won't let them walk past without permission from Y.)
      If they do head to the office without talking to Y, then he can spot them on the security cameras and activate the defences then.
      4 & 5 both assume that Y doesn't activate his security measures, which he does. (Or, at least, is extremely likely to do so in most reasonable playthroughs. I didn't account for the possibility that the PCs might, say, set the building on fire or something like that.)
      6. I suppose I could have said "X TRIES to kill Y", or "if present, X tries to kill Y" instead of X kills Y. And yeah, advice on fighting invisible foes would have made a nice sidebar (although how many offices have buckets of water or rugs handy?)
      7. I made the assumption that the PCs are at least nominally loyal to their boss in general. In this specific case, the boss is explicitly stated to be James Angleton, who's a major character in the Laundry novels. Screwing over Angleton is like a Rebellion character in a Star Wars game handing Obi-Wan over to the dark side. Not unthinkable, but extremely unlikely. If they try bluffing Y - well, if they try bluffing him from the zombie's phone, he's got little reason to believe them. If they try it later on when he thinks he's got the upper hand, then it might work - and then X can shoot him.
      8. Well, the PCs might end up dead, which would count as a loss.

      PC death is certainly a possibility. It didn't happen in playtest, and I think a total party kill is extremely unlikely unless the GM is being vindictive. Other than her invisibility, Renne isn't a staggeringly tough foe.

      More generally, the Laundry Files rpg assumes that the characters are career officers in the civil service. It's their job to go on missions for their superiors, and they're obliged to carry out those missions with a modicum of concern for the law, secrecy and sanity, while also keeping unnecessary expenses and collateral damage to a minimum. I've tried to avoid any situations where the players are forced along an unlikely or undesirable course of action for no good reason, and to provide multiple options where possible.

      I also wonder if there's a tension between the quasi-narrative style of writing used for the adventures in Black Bag Jobs, and the perception of railroading. Let's take point 6.\n
      There's a gunshot, and Y' head jerks back. He collapses to the floor, blood gushing from the neat hole that just got punched in his forehead.

      'God, that was satisfying' says a disembodied voice. 'I was just going to frame him with that missing CD, but shooting him in the face...I think I need a cigarette.'

      It's X, or at least X's voice. You can't see her.
      \nI could equally have written that as:\n

      At a suitable juncture, X shoots Y at point-blank range, killing him. Her original intent was to frame Y using the CD from the laptop, but now she can kill him and blame it on the PCs
      \nor go even more abstract, say by listing the NPCs, their goals, and possible ways for them to accomplish these goals. Those approaches might seem less like railroading, but would be (to my mind) less fun to read, and less useful to the assumed target audience (a busy GM who wants the scenario to do as much work as possible for them.)

      I hope I'm not coming across as defensive here - it's certainly not my intent. I really appreciate this level of scrutiny of my adventures!

      \n I think that's the only adventure in Black Bag Jobs where I started pulling my hair. I mean, we've got, let's see...

      1. Lambent Witch. My only complaint is that the map could have been clearer, especially when I tried to follow it with the text about what was going on that the PCs would have liked to know about. But, other than that -- have some troops! Have some equipment! The fate of Britain is in your hands, really, no fooling! What's not to love? (Also, given the contemporary setting, it lacks the usual weasel classic CoC scenarios have about "if they fail, that's why the world is the way it is, and if they succeed, well, the world would've been so much worse if they'd failed".)

      2. Lost and Found. As written, No Matter What, Angelton's job really isn't at stake. It says that at worst, he'll take some temporary fallout. Right there, that's a big problem -- it means the PCs can't really fail. Contrast this to Secret Agendas, which does it right. And remember, the first part is set up such that the PCs, if they are on the ball, will know, before they find the cab, they're dealing with someone who can be invisible. If they're amazingly on the ball, they'll have an idea of whom. This is good -- and the follow up is "Well, but, it's not like they're going to be suspicious of the NPC, and anyway, just fiat her actions." Yuck, seriously yuck. And I don't care how you write fiat killing an NPC. It's still fiat killing, nope, nothing the PCs could do, Despite All Your Precautions. Yawn. Why am I playing again? To witness the brilliant plot? Please, just write the story if you've already decided what Has To Happen. As for the loyalty issue, despite appearances, loyalty to the Laundry is not synonymous with Loyalty to Angleton or to whomever is in the Angelton role. Players often find the NPCs you're sure are wonderful to be really annoying, and vice versa. I could see a group deciding, "Well, if he dropped the ball on this, you know, he is losing it." (I am assuming the PCs don't start by magically knowing everything Bob in the books knows.) As for the security measures, it reads like the guy doesn't lock down his office until the PCs call him. I am thinking of an awful lot of players who wouldn't bother calling ahead.

      3. Shadow Over Kafiristan. This could totally be CoC or Trail of Cthulhu. The point isn't to solve the mystery, but to decide what to do with one's knowledge. I'm good with this. It means that it's railroaded in so far as the PCs will get all the clues, but this just doesn't bother me. It does bother me that Keepers are told that the PCs must come away feeling soiled, not heroic. Sure, I love the Laundry take on the news -- always assuming the PCs do make it out alive, which isn't guaranteed -- but if the PCs can somehow find a way to carry the day, ethics-wise, or even to stick to their guns in their report, even knowing that the Laundry may still disregard their advice, I'm okay with players and PCs feeling like they pulled as much of a win as they could.

      4. The Wild Hunt. PCs sent to a place to check it out. Okay, that's the set up, not railroading. Problems ensue. Well, yes, otherwise we wouldn't have an adventure. One thing I think was overlooked: The PCs' cellphones are likely to be Laundry issue, correct? If so, they have a good case for saying they can't hand them over. Mind, this isn't a huge issue, as the scenario doesn't assume they will hand them over. I also would like some idea of what would happen if the PCs, oh, decided to return to London, or to visit Paris or New York City. I don't know how likely this is -- it's a possible course of action, but not so obvious I'd expect my home group to go for it.

      5. Secret Agendas. I love this one to pieces. It's everything Lost and Found isn't. The PCs have to figure out which of three people is to blame for an information leak. Set up, hence not railroad. Things get complicated. Also, the three suspects can't lie to the PCs, which is something I really like. The situation is complicated enough without having to worry about blowing the "fail to realize NPC is lying" roll. The stakes are high. The one thing I think could be improved is that the PCs could be made more aware of what's at stake in terms of the future direction of the Laundry, and just what they can do about it. I think as written, it's a bit too subtle. Secret Agendas is a little like Shadow Over Kafiristan in that it's "what do you do with what you learned?", but very different in feel. And it pulls out all the stops.

      6. The Signal. This is almost two scenarios in one, except that the first is the foreshadowing of the second. That's fine -- it plays like a good Laundry scenario. PCs are told "There's a problem here" and need to fix it. There's a few ways they can find out what's going on, and a couple of ways they can drop the ball. Hm, I note the concept of "railroading" gets interesting when the premise is "you're a group of people who follow orders". The second part is more complicated. It's kind of a combination of Lambent Witch and Kafiristan, in that there are a lot of resources available, what's going on will almost certainly be revealed, and the question becomes, "Okay, what do you do about it?"

      So, five scenarios I consider basically solid, and one that makes me scream. It's not a bad record, actually. And Lost and Found might well work out as envisioned most of the time -- it's just that it wouldn't take much to make it better written for the rest of the time, when a player says, "Wait a minute..." (I had more of a problem with bits of the Zalozhniy Quartet, but that's another story.)

      \n I'm pondering a few convention games I've played in. It's hard to be sure whether there's a railroad sometimes, especially if the Keeper is good. Ditto for Magician's Choice.

      Two weaker-but-still-acceptable games I've been in had set ups that could have been better, but once one got past that, the rest of the adventure was decent.

      One, which I played in years ago, had an obvious "You have to leave the NPC all alone in his hotel room so he can be murdered" bit. This was annoying because the PCs and this NPC were a group of investigators who were aware of the Mythos and knew that they had enemies. Did it destroy the rest of the adventure? No. But, it did mean I cared that much less. If you've got to do this, start with the PCs discovering their friend's body, okay?

      Another involved checking out a mine. The big question: Could it open the next day, Monday? If not... a lot of people, including the PCs, would lose their jobs. And none of the PCs knew from Mythos. So far, so cool.

      And the checking out the mine part was fine. Well, okay, it was an utter disaster, death and destruction and explosions, and the survivors carefully agreeing on the story they'd tell -- in other words, good Cthulhu fun. It was a bit in the middle that I had a problem with, and it was a bit that didn't need to be a problem.

      So, we all knew that there might be a killer loose in the area. Okay, not a problem. We're just going down into a mine. And we all went down, and went to the room with all the equipment, including the explosives.

      Or not. The explosives were gone. All of them. And there had been a lot of explosives.

      Now, at this point, I and one other player thought that the sensible thing to do was to go back to the surface, make one little cell phone call to the police to say, "Okay, we're checking out the mine, but just so you know, someone has stolen a fuckload of explosives." I mean, Missing Explosives + Possible Killer = Alert the Authorities -- and then, go right back into the adventure. You understand, we weren't trying to stop the adventure.

      But, we were informed that this wasn't possible. The other guy and I exchanged a look, told the Keeper that, for the record, this hurt our suspension of disbelief, and then went back to the game, as we didn't want to break the game.

      Again, in both cases, the railroad bit was absolutely disconnected from what made the scenario tick.

      I remember the names of some of the better convention runs I played in.

      I played "Lockdown" at Origins a few years back. The PCs are guards and inmates of a prison, along with an interview crew and some administrative staff. Things go south. For us, they went very, very south. At the end of the game, one player said, "So, was there any way we could have won?" The Keeper told us how we could have not wound up south of Antarctica, and there was a palpable sense of "Okay, that's all right, then." No, we didn't do any of those things. I'm not sure it would have occurred to us to do them. But, it might have, and it was fair.

      This GenCon, I played in "Hysteria". "Hysteria" is set in the late 19th century. All of the PCs are women who were institutionalized, which, right there, is plenty horrific. There's an odd tension in this one, because the more you play your characters as writ, rather than as Investigators, the less likely you are to figure out what's going on and deal correctly with it. But, again, there were several things we could have done, or to quote the Keeper, "There were actually a lot of people you might have talked to if you hadn't decided to attack them instead."

      "Hysteria" has a bit of railroading in that the PCs are patients at a mental institution, and there's a build up of the daily routine. But, even there, there's a lot of acting out the PCs can do, if they chose -- I had a skill called "Instigate", and I had fun using that -- and, when it came to plot relevant stuff, it seemed like we had a fair amount of freedom. I can't tell if that was an illusion, and there was a twist that might or might not be considered railroading, depending on your definitions. I consider it part of the setup and part clue.

      "The Stars Are Fallen", also a game I played in this GenCon, felt like it gave us pretty near total freedom. The Keeper had used The Apocalypse Machine to create a post-apocalyptic setting, so there were serious constraints when it came to food and water, and weather was an important factor, but I cannot think of any point in the session where it felt like the Keeper was herding us, rather than answering our questions and letting us decide what to do. I think we all expected a TPK, but managed to pull off everyone surviving and accomplishing the goals we set ourselves. Of course, we knew, although the PCs did not, how much trouble they'd gotten themselves into by doing what they did.

      Then, there are a couple of odd ones, ones I consider good, and which are intrinsically railroading.

      Oscar Rios's "Setting Sun, Rising Tide" is about Japanese soldiers in World War II. They're on an island that's about to get overrun by the Americans when they're offered a way out. At this point, just about anyone who signs up for the game has a pretty good idea, out of character, what's going on. But, in character? At this point, there's an intense group discussion about what to do. What is the honorable thing to do? Is that what your character would do? What happens as a result of that choice?

      I'd say this one works because all the railroading I think I'm seeing is aimed at the climax, which involves the PCs choosing what is important to them, knowing only what they know, not what we know. Mind, Oscar himself has said, "I write railroads. Okay, sometimes I write a sandbox and put it on a railroad, but really, I write railroads."

      Misty Fourtner's "A Night to Remember" is set on the Titanic. Every single PC was a passenger on that ship. We all knew where the adventure was going. I would not have thought I'd be all that interested in sitting and listening to a description of the Titanic sinking -- and yet, for the last five minutes or so of the game, we all sat in rapt attentiveness as Misty read that description. Heck, part of the draw of the game was finding out why the Titanic sank in the game reality.

      But the PCs had agency. The ship was doomed -- had been from the beginning of the adventure. Anything you don't want the PCs messing with should happen offstage before the adventure begins. So, the rest of the diabolical plan could be disrupted, and was -- the PCs were on the ball and worked together well, and we had one brainstorm that saved our bacon. Well, our spiritual bacon, at least. Physically.... all of the women PCs got off the sinking ship and into a lifeboat. All of the men stayed behind and drowned.

      \n Cthulhu Britannica (2009)

      Cthulhu Britannica has some scenarios I consider odd and challenging to run, probably because they don't fit the stereotype of a CoC adventure. All of these come with pregenerated PCs, should one wish to use them.

      Bad Company is my favorite. It's set in the Gaslight period. The Investigators are hired to find a baronet's younger son, a man seen in company of a disreputable woman. She's basically a Dorian Gray type sorceress, currently holding the man prisoner. But there's no straight trail of A then B then C then D then Climax. All of the key locations, NPCs, and clues are described, and the PCs can pretty much go where they want and do what they want. There are a couple of extra layers here, a minor villain in over his head, and a group hunting the major villain which might be allies or additional complications for the PCs. I don't see railroading or a chokepoint.

      Darkness Descending is set in the 1930s and inspired by Eddy C. Bertin's story, "Darkness, My Name Is". I've not read the story as yet. This is one of several CoC scenarios set at an archaeological dig. After all, archaeologists are always digging up things that would be better left hidden, and, in this scenario, they have already dug up two of five statues warding against an Outer God. If the PCs do not figure out what is going on and what to do about it quickly enough, Bad Things will happen. This is probably the most traditional of the scenarios in the book, and it's probably no coincidence that it's set in the 1930s.

      Wrong Turn has a near future setting, though I think it would work just as well in the present day. It is a complete railroad, as it's basically a horror movie. One by one, the PCs vanish, to be replaced with simulacra, until only one is left. And then? Well, that's up to the Keeper. It's interesting because it's not the usual CoC scenario, but it's also frustrating to me because of the combination of railroading and "Well, you make up the rest, dude." I'd probably do better as a player in this one than a Keeper. In some ways, it reminds me of "In Medias Res".

      King is set in the present day, though that could be adjusted. The PCs wake up in a hospital to discover that they've become experimental subjects of a mad doctor. The PCs must try to escape for anything to happen, and the author acknowledges that they may not succeed in freeing themselves from their restraints the first time, although the scenario comes with pregenerated characters, most of whom have a decent chance. So, there's a chokepoint here. The PCs might decide not to try to escape, in which case, I think the best thing to do is to say outright that there's no adventure if they don't. They might all fail to escape on the first attempt, but they can keep trying until they succeed. Since this is the case, you might want to just let them escape on the first try, saying, "It takes a while, but eventually, you wriggle free of your restraints." This also helps circumvent a situation where some PCs escape and decide not to free the others. The sequence isn't what I consider ideal, but okay, let's say that the scenario doesn't properly begin until the PCs are at large. The goal is to find out what was done to them and to escape. And, if the PCs decide not to escape once they know more, that's acceptable as well. So, heavy railroading / chokepoint at the beginning, then nothing, I'd say.

      My Little Sister Wants You to Suffer! is set in the future. The PCs have been given false memories and are actually in a sadistic reality show. The author describes the scenario up front as "linear", but assures readers that this is justified because there is an outside force in the scenario controlling things, not just the Keeper doing it. I think that's a bit disingenuous, as the Keeper is the one choosing and running the scenario. This would be tricky for me to run because it is simultaneouly sandboxy and railroady in ways that do not play to my strengths. As the PCs are herded through various hoops, the Keeper is given several options about what to throw at them, both physically and psychologically. Approach this one with care -- you want to be very sure your group is up for this kind of sadism. I'm not sure I would be, if I were kept in ignorance of the premise. If I knew going in about the mindfuck, I could probably have a lot of fun.

      So, out of five scenarios: Nothing, Nothing, Horror Movie Railroad, Chokepoint / Railroady set up with nothing once the plot starts, and Weird Railroad. There's a lot of Matter of Taste here, obviously, and full credit to the authors for stretching and experimenting.

      \n I just finished reading Bumps in the Night. We have:

      1. The Westerfield Incident. Sandbox, not railroad. The PCs can be investigators. Or local vigilantes. Or local law enforcement. Or parapsychologists. There's discussion about how the different groups will be perceived and what resources they have. They're there to find out what's killing people and then to deal with it. No chokepoints I could spot.

      2. The Vengeful Dead. Full Disclosure: I played in this one at GenCon in 2011, although we never actually got to the, um, scenario part. (Adam Scott Glancy created PCs that were just so delightfully bent on their own agendas that much havoc ensued.) So, I was surprised to learn that the Keeper decides who's actually the villain. In playtests, the author selected one NPC, and I think stuck to that one consistently.

      Premise: The PCs are at a resort in a rather secluded area of the country. Then, Stuff happens. This is really good for PCs that are not Investigators, but are normal folks. A couple of things annoyed me as railroading, although these are not only minor, but also not essential. First, there are about three NPCs who might feature in the next scenario in the book, so Keepers are told that if they intend to run that scenario, these NPCs must not be the villain and must be kept alive. Hmph. My take on that is that you can always invent new NPCs. (More on that when I discuss the next scenario.)

      The other issue is more interesting to me. There are certain courses of action that the PCs should not be taking, as these are actions taken by characters who know they are in a horror movie / a Call of Cthulhu game, and the PCs should not know that. The author says that the Keeper can flat out veto these things, and I'm not entirely comfortable with that. I mean, if a player says, "My PC breaks into the office and roots through the filing cabinet", it's one thing to hit the PC with consequences and quite another thing to say, "No, I'm vetoing that because I have decided your PC has no reason to do that." I could and would say, "Why? Remember, your character doesn't have reason to think anything is wrong."

      And, I'm fortunate enough to play with people who try their best not to metagame in this way. Heck, our problem is usually that we bend over so far backwards not to use Out of Character information that we're not always sure at what point the PC really should figure something out. But that said, I do feel for the author. What does one do when one has a group of players who insist on their PCs doing things that the PCs almost certainly would not do? Is it all right to railroad / use chokepoints to enforce good roleplaying?

      3. The Bitter Venom of the Gods. Okay, this starts with an NPC woman who has broken up with her fiance. There is absolutely no reason she has to be the same NPC as in the previous scenario. There are two other NPCs from that scenario who have skills that might be very useful to the PCs, but there's no reason there can't be other such NPCs around.

      That's a fairly minor fix. And "NPC X asks for your help" is premise. But...

      So, there's a woman who says, "Hi, I broke up with my fiance, and I don't want to give details. I want you to escort me to his family home so I can pick up my belongings, see him one last time, and tie up loose ends. Only, I don't want you actually coming in with me when his family decides to let only me in." And No Matter What, the PCs can't get in with her and can't dissuade her from her course of action.

      Forget Call of Cthulhu metagaming here. You're telling me you're enough afraid to want an escort, and I'm supposed to let you walk in alone. I have a problem with this. I have a problem with "And the PCs will have to do the stupid thing so that there is plot." However, this problem is mitigated (but NOT quashed! We are Not Amused.) because of the rest of the scenario design.

      So, your NPC friend isn't coming out. A servant says (paraphrasing for the gist), "Oh, she decided to stay, but thanks you for your concern. Go away now." What do you do about it?

      There are a number of options from the cowardly (walk away, in which case, the plot can still find you) to the stupidly brave (break in to rescue her) to the tricky-but-doable (get sufficient outside attention focused on the situation quickly enough), and it's entirely possible to get her out alive quickly. This does not break the scenario in the least.

      Whatever the NPC's fate, it is the beginning, not the end of the story. The fiance's family is trying to protect its secrets, and the PCs were with their friend, and so they have become targets. It is Totally Fine to rescue the woman quickly. I really like this.

      So, there is railroading. It is annoying, and I do not like it. But, it's not as dire as it might be, and the heart of the scenario is more open. Hm, I think the obvious patch I'd use is to let the PCs come in with the NPC if they are sufficiently forceful. It'll just convince the family even more that the PCs have to go. OTOH, that brings up the problem of showing the PCs a bit much of the house, although I'd try to curtail that. Then again, that might just mean the family tries to deal with the PCs on the spot, much as if they'd broken in to rescue the woman in the first place.

      4. Curse of the Screaming Skull. Sandbox, not railroad. The PCs are hired to find out what's going on with a supposedly haunted house. Here, as elsewhere, the author says (paraphrasing) "Don't make the players roll for clues they obviously need to solve the mystery if they're looking in the right places / for the right things." The mystery is tangled enough without that. I don't think there are chokepoints here.

      5. An Unsettled Mind. The PCs are law enforcement or coroners' assistants looking into a bizarre accident. No railroading, no chokepoint as far as finding out what's going on. The clues are laid out well, the neighborhood is described, and the PCs have a lot of resources. This adventure is intended for new players, and the Keeper is told to explain / remind them of ways to investigate and things to look for. Dealing with the situation is the tricky part, and the author has only two solutions in mind, both somewhat bleak. Now, I'm good with bleak in Call of Cthulhu, but I would want to be open to the players finding a third alternative if they tried.

      So, mostly sandboxes, some constraints in some of the adventures in the type of PCs involved, some railroading that I find annoying, but which one can work around. These are definitely in the "read through more than once and do your prep work" category, as the flow depends on the Keeper being familiar with all of the information and clues. All of these fit the Call of Cthulhu investigation / horror genre, but, as the back cover notes, they're original enough that reading the CoC source material won't give the players any kind of edge. This also means that I can use them for non-CoC games that can take a dollop of mystery horror.
