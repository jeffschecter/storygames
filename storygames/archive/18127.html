\n This has probably been done already, and if so, feel free to ignore. But I remember the war stories of the old "2d6 vs. 4d10" debates on the old Forge about dice and fortune mechanics completely missing the point of game design. In the spirit of completely missing the point:

I'm going to detail a resolution mechanic. It's blackjack. What can we make with it?

Style 1:
The GM selects a target number, from 17 to 21, and announces it. Player gets two cards, and plays the hand based on the usual rules of blackjack. Split, double down, etc. are all allowed. Feel free to set house rules as desired (five card charlie? Surrender allowed? Blackjack pays 2:1? Double down on any two cards? Split aces only get one card each?). Based on the total result of the hands, something happens to the character in question. Oh, and if you bust, you get something very bad.

+2 or more: awesome
+1.5 (blackjack): great
+1: good
+0.5: slightly good
+0: neutral
-0.5 (surrender, if allowed): slightly bad
-1: bad
-2 (or bust): very bad

Idea being that you can stop at any time and accept a failure (or surrender on your first two cards for a slightly bad outcome), or press on for something important, taking the risk of a disaster in order to get something important.

Style 2:
Same thing, only the GM plays the role of the dealer instead of setting a target number; one card up, one down, hits on soft 17, offer insurance/even money on a dealer ace upcard, same as regular blackjack.

    \n Something to keep in mind is that 21 is not an arbitrary number in blackjack; it's based on the distribution of cards in the deck. You are actually more likely to "bust" with lower target numbers because the most common card value in the game is 10 (because there are four 10s and four of every face card).

    If you really want to do blackjack with moving target numbers, a more forgiving system might be one where 21 is still the best result you can get, over 21 is bust, but anything between your target number and 21 is a success. The degree of success is based on how close to 21 you get. So, for a very easy challenge, you might have a TN of 15 (as it's pretty hard to bust trying to get to that, but still possible), where getting a 15 means you just barely succeeded, getting a 20 means you succeeded spectacularly, getting a 24 means you fail pretty badly, and folding at 14 means you just barely missed.
