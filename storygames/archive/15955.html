\n It wouldn't surprise me if someone has suggested this already, but what if there was an explicit initiative in Dungeon World, start at the player to the left of the GM and work clockwise or else roll +Dex and go high-to-low. Add two moves, something like this but without such terrible wording:
\n
\nDELAY: When you don't have anything you want to do, drop out of the initiative order. You can jump back into the initiative order at any point, immediately after the person whose turn it is when you announce your return.
\n
\nREADY: When you don't have anything you want to do just yet, drop out of the initiative order and name a condition. You can jump back into the initiative order whenever that condition occurs, immediately before the condition.
\n
\n"I ready an action to shoot whoever comes through the door" thus goes off as someone is coming through the door. If you delay, then when someone comes through the door you want to shoot them, they get to come all the way in and do whatever they're doing before you get to shoot them.

    \n There are lots of fantasy adventure games that model combat by having the GM describe the room and where things are and then use mechanics to determine who gets permission to act. Dungeon World combat is a dynamic conversation, where the GM decides what moves he or she is making organically, based on the Principles and the Agenda, and the resulting fiction translates into moves that generate more fiction on the fly. So, The GM can make a hard move "take Damage" or a soft move "reveal an unwelcome truth" and direct them to players based on what they think fits the fictional material best. I find that freeing and cool and I like the way this conversational approach shapes combats into a great story.

    I want the GM to create fiction and direct moves at the characters. I don't want the GM shackled to following a DEX order or play always moving to the left. If the GM wants an Orc to charge the Paladin, I want it to happen without regard for whether he is faster than the thief. I'm a big fan of the way DW avoids having the GM just provide a description with mechanics determine order of events.

    \n As far as the practical side of who goes first, we're adding some text to the game related to this excellent thread. Basically, when two players want to do something at the same time ask questions and refine the situation to the point where you know which would happen first, but first check if it actually matters who goes first or next.

    We did at one point have a general initiative move, which was a mistake. At most you need a "who draws first" move, for when "a split second makes all the difference" or something like that (thanks to Marshall for that wording).

    Also note that DW does have turns, as it says in the text, but they're turns like a conversation, not turns like Monopoly.

    As for why game-like turns are poisonous: because the flow needs to respond to actions, not turns. The GM's job is to present a fantastic world, when we start making rules about who does what when we're taking away the GM's tools to do that. If we set what a turn is to any fictional (unit of time) or game (number of moves made, or something like that) measure then the GM can't portray the world so much as they just follow who's next and how much time they have.
