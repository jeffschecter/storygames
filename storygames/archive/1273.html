Do Story-Games Tell Stories?

James_Nostack
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Story Games
In another thread, I proposed that the final outcome of a story-game is a story, and that the story--i.e., whatever happened in the shared imaginary space--that emerges is, y'know, a story. And that this story isn't much different than the story found in a movie, or a novel, or two people talking on the street about what happened to them. This apparently is a controversial opinion, so I'm giving it its own thread. (But I can't stay to argue it very much, because I have a ton of non-gaming things to do today.)

Thesis One: if you gave someone a "blind taste test" between the imaginary events from a jammin' game session, and a snippet from a published novel or movie, the taste-tester wouldn't be able to say, "Gee, the first one is from a game session."

Thesis Two: from the perspective of "creative output," playing a story-games differ from novel writing, film-making, and other creative endeavor mainly in terms of the procedures involved. While it's true that the players control and savor the story they create, and (hopefully) very much enjoy the process itself, it's not much different in that regard from acting, writing, whatever.

    Thesis One: if you gave someone a "blind taste test" between theimaginaryevents from a jammin' game session, and a snippet from a published novel or movie, the taste-tester wouldn't be able to say, "Gee, the first one is from a game session."
    I think they would be able to tell, because the structural requirements of the different forms impose different constraints on the narrative. A movie basically can't have both well-realized characters and a large cast, because it's just too short. A novel or rpg can.

    Games also tend to have much more complex narrative structures than novels do, because you have many more protagonists and many more lines of conflict -- what would be confusing in a novel can work in a game because each player is holding up a different set of strands.

    Games can also work with much flatter and less-well-realized characters than novels can, because the character identification arising from playing a character can carry you "over the hump". Movies do this even better, because cool visuals can work even better at this.

    Posted By: Matt_SnyderAny RPG you can play can produce a story, perhaps even agoodstory to those that hear the "transcript" afterward.
    This all goes back to the narratology v. ludology debate that continues to roil in video-game studies.

    For what it's worth, I land somewhere in the middle - that games are capable of producing a coherent narrative - but only if the narrative artifact produced by the game is extensively edited. At which point, you have to ask yourself whether you're dealing with a genuine product of the game, or a by-product of the game which is really a product of the editorial process.

    A D&D session 'transcript', or any other transcript of a game session, even one utterly devoid of chatter, is full of missteps on the part of the players, setbacks, cul-de-sacs and other narrative run-arounds that may make for a coherent narrative (meaning that a random reader can follow the action), but that the narrative is so unlike anything produced by 'traditional' media that it won't pass a sniff test. There's just too much chance and too much freedom of action involved in game-machine for it to produce something like what we understand to be a 'story'.

    That said, I think that the narratives that are provided by games are a variant form of narrative-as-we-know-it, and one that (given the increasing amount of computing power available) will begin to emerge as a new form of storytelling across media.

    But the 'story' from a 'story game' isn't a 'story' as in 'bedtime story'. It's just not.
