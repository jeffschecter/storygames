Thought on Stakes Setting

ndp
February 2006 edited February 2006 in Story Games
I think a common way to talk about setting stakes is talking about having stakes that are interesting either way. That is, whether the character in questions succeeds or fails, something interesting happens.

A moment from my playtest of Carry last night kind of realigned my thinking on the matter, and now I'm thinking that thats more an effect of good stakes-setting. A character was holding his gun to the head of a Vietnamese child, trying to bluff the papa-san into telling him the truth about NVA in the area. Conflict! He said "if I win, the guy spills and I don't have to go farther." I said "and if you lose, you do have to go farther before he spills." He pulls this face and says "I don't want that." I say "well, I want that. Let's roll 'em." And, what-da-ya-know, he did all he could to keep from losing that conflict, engaging fully with the conflict resolution mechanics in the game.

So. This clarifies for me that good stakes setting aren't just whats interesting, but that they have to be things that people want. Sometimes its the same person that wants both options. Sometimes different people want different options. Sometimes you have to fight to get what you want. But as long as sometime is attached to each option, thats what makes the stakes interesting.

I'm sure this is very much a "duh" moment, but I'm glad that my play clarified it for me.

    Yup. I agree. I think this is part of what Matt Snyder, John Kim, and I were trying to talk about in another thread, but weren't quite connecting on because of some semantic issues.

    "Engaging fully" are the key words, to me. Words like "agree" and "want" kind of muddy things, I think. The important thing is, the players involved are fully engaged. What makes a player engage fully? There's no single answer. Sometimes it's a kind of authorial curiosity -- "Oooh, this could go several different ways! Neat". Sometimes it's desperate character-identification -- "Oh no! My guy definitely doesn't want that. C'mon, let's win this one!" And there are several other ways, and most players switch between them all.

    Then again, some of these ways probably rub some people the wrong way, and hurt engagement.

    Maybe it's worth it to be very explicit about the different methods of engagement, and which ones you want and don't want. I suspect that there's some overlap here with "No one gets hurt" and "I will not abandon you."

    This is actually something that I was talking about with two different sets of people, recently. I'm finding that it's important to recognize that "Conflict Resolution" and specifically what constitutes the stakes in a conflict resolution system are specific to individual games.

    Case in point: Dogs works best without counterstakes. In Dogs, stakes are defined as what the Dog wants and if the Dog wins, he gets them, and if he loses, he doesn't. The NPC on the other side does not get stakes of his own because, in the scheme of how the game works, the NPC is not on an equal narrative footing as the PC, and what the NPC "wants" is more or less irrelevant outside of how the NPC's desires reveal the situation. In Dogs, the rules are all about putting the players in a position where they must take a moral stance on a complicated situation.

    However, FLFS has very explicit counterstakes. Any check is all about "if win, you narrate X happens, if lose I narrate Y happens." Because in this game, the die checks are all about moving the story forward and hot-potatoing the narration rights.

    Both are "Conflict Resolution" but they are not the same Conflict Resolution. So the important question to ask is "what does Carry need its Resolution to forward?"

    Actually, I find counterstakes to be poison to a Dogs game. Killing the kid is what fallout is there for. The kid will die if you decide to push so hard that it becomes dangerous.

    The thing of it is, killing the kid needs to be a decision made by the Dogs rather than a failure of the Dogs. Framing it as a decision gets into good, juicy Dogs play -- "how important is it that your flavor of justice is done here? Enough to kill a kid?" Framing it as a failure kills a lot of what drives Dogs -- "you tried hard, but the kid died. Them's the breaks." Both approaches have a unique flavor.

    Which is mostly what I'm getting at -- specific means of implementing conflict resolution have specific consequences to the experience of play.

    {Tangent: The captives getting mangled is either setting the stakes right the first time and/or a followup conflict against demonic influence. Actually, I'd argue that the status of the captives is pretty much a non-issue in a Dogs games. The point is what you do with the kid, not how safe the victims are. Dogs are judges, not heroes.)

    All these old threads I'm resurrecting, I feel like a cleric.

    Dogs gets so many mentions here that I went and read about it - Wikipedia, on its site, and the various reviews linked from there. My "duh moment", which I'd now like to check out with you, went something like this.

    What people seem to like about Dogs are two things. One, that it presents a setting - or rather, a setup - in which moral choices matter, not only subjectively but to everyone and the universe at large. And two (which is the bit that's relevant to this thread): that when a player commits to a course of action, the question that's asked is not "do you succeed?" but "what's it worth to you?"

    This same idea seems to come up in other games written by people who post here, like Capes or the Roach (it's pretty clear in the Roach's kicker*, and I misquote: Would you swallow a giant telepathic cockroach that wants to take over the world, if it would get you tenure?)

    "What's it worth to you?" seems to come in two flavours: "What are you willing to risk?" (the Gambler's Question) and "What are you willing to pay?" (the Trader's Question). And each of them has two forms: the non-escalating and the escalating. The escalating form of the trader's question is an auction; the escalating form of the gambler's question is as per Dogs. And the key to escalation is "OK, you're willing to risk/pay X. What if someone else values getting their outcome at X+1? Still interested? Can you better that offer?"

    And all that makes you think about value and cost and reward, and it's engaging, much more so than bashing monsters and taking their stuff.

    So, is this a worthwhile/accurate insight? And if so, how might we use it in our games?

    *Hopefully I am using the terminology correctly, I'm new to this.
