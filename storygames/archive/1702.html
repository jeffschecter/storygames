Help me see the flaws in my scenario prep!

DevP
November 2006 edited November 2006 in Story Games

When I'm trying to prepare a situation for the players (be it a series of dungeon traps, a bunch of ready Bangs, some plot hooks, or whatever), I seem to have a problem of looking beyond my own ideas at times. Once I have some barebones ideas out there, I feel like I'm set - try as I might, I can't see ahead of time what other player needs may arise. I suspectin these cases that I'm jumping to conclusions about the flow of the story.

I'd really appreciate advice or brainstorming exercises about taking a given scenario and flexing it out, finding alternate paths for it to take, adding more options - in other words, shaking myself out of what I'm already seeing in my head. Currently, I'm prepping a Pool-based scenario about vigilante journalism; the Pool, unlike DitV and some other games, doesn't seem to cover a lot of ground in terms of scenario preparation.

    I get a lot of mileage out of writing player flags on 3x5 cards. In prep, I can shuffle them around to see new combinations. In play, I can keep them out in front of me so I don't stray off topic.

    Plan what you'd do if you were given those Bangs. Then work out what kind of scenes you'd frame based on what you'd do. Carry on playing both roles: work out how you'd react as a player and then how you'd react as a GM. Do that until you've essentially mapped a whole game for yourself.

    Then do the same exercise again, making different player choices.

    In doing that, you're flexing your player-reaction muscles, and you're also storing up a library of cool stuff you can throw at your players later. One of your players will say "I'll do X" and you'll think "Oh, in that case, I could use that dead body scene".

    Graham
