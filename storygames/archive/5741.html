\n
\n First of all, sorry Clinton for eating your game's brain.

Anyway, we're playing a zombie survival horror game using The Shadow of Yesterday rules. Last night was our first session and it's going to be a lot of fun. We talked over where to set the game, and my friend Robo nailed it - "Let's set it on a cruise ship", he said.

In terms of social commentary, the cruise ship picks up where Dawn of the Dead's mall leaves off. It's pure gold, with a platinum topping of race and class issues. Rich Americans, rich Dutch officers, and a mob of Indonesian and Filipino service staff. We all gravitated toward crew. We've got two Filipino domestic staffers, a National Park Service naturalist/interpreter (we're cruising the Inside passage in Alaska), and a sorority sister. In addition, we sketched out a dozen random NPCs.

So the first session began with us holed up in a chain locker on "deck zero" as the madness raged all around us. We discovered how each of the survivors got there and first encountered the zombie menace in disconnected flashback scenes. In these scenes, everybody but the focus character played a random NPC. NPCs who survived ended up in the chain locker with us, usually with a Key that tied them to a PC or the group. We had a map of the ship, and Steve had recently been on board and really brought it to life for us in all its pornographic splendor. There was a lot of "the frat party is in the Breezeway lounge on the Lido deck!" going on. The initial body count was fairly low, but it looks like it is going to pick up. At the end of the session, our characters could hear the lifeboats dropping from their davits as the ship's mains went off-line, leaving everything bathed in shadowy red emergency lighting. There was a lot of screaming from outside the chain locker door. The naturalist sort of summed up our shitty situation with a pithy comment: "Who's driving the boat?"

    \n Hey Nate,

    I'm experimenting a bit with the zombies, but yeah, basically they're beasts because I'm not giving them Keys (or personalities). I'm using normal Pools and Abilities for them, however, and I cooked up a handful of "walking dead" Secrets as well. Partly this is because I wanted to diversify the kind of threat the zombies represent. I created one "creature" that is an individual, classic zombie. Then I set up another that is a "threat" zombie (your modern day fast zombie). Finally, I also created stats for a "pack of zombies", where the mass is represented by a higher Vigor pool. I'm hoping to interchange these with other types of conflicts (panic, dangerous survivors, environmental hazards, trust issues, heartbreak, etc.) to help us create a really fun game.

    \n This game is getting fun. Last night we played the *other* set of survivors (our player attendance has been irregular) - we had a 19-year-old sorority girl and the Filipino housekeeper who loved her from afar, the clueless events coordinator, and Kitkat Tampipi - also a housekeeper but rapidly evolving into a ruthless ninja with Key of the Exterminator. There was a great Bringing Down the Pain moment where Kitkat faced off with the leader of these yeggs and cowed him with her empty pistol.

    TSOY is really supporting the genre - zombies are essentially an environmental menace, occasionally stepping up as full-fledged physical opponents, but what's really driving the game our the character's Keys. Next week we've agreed that once we find out who makes it aboard the motor launch (which isn't at Boat Station 11), we'll switch GMs as the survivors come ashore somewhere somewhere on Kupreanof Island in southeast Alaska.

    \n Last night's game was interesting - a lot has transpired; I'm GMing now and the PCs have escaped the doomed MV Amsterdam (sunk by F-15s!) and made their way - hunted by biosuit wearing government helicopter goons - to a secluded cannery on Baranof island along the Chatham strait. I hadn't had a chance to do any real prep, so I employed the Mountain Witch Trick over and over again:

    "OK, you need an Instinct refresh? Flashback, you and your wife are hiking on Mt. Edgecumbe outside Sitka, it's six years ago. You have something important to tell her. What is it?"

    And I did some heavy-handed character-building stuff:

    "What's your daughter's name, and how old is she?" (Player didn't know his guy had a daughter)

    I also off-loaded quite a bit of narrative authority:

    "That zombie five year old is going to return at the worst possible moment - you guys tell me when that is."

    It was a good session - we finally got some classic "barricaded cabin in the woods" action going, and since all the zombies were neighbors and friends of one of the PCs, that added a frisson of calamity to the whole thing. One difficulty for me as GM is that one guy (thanks, Isbo) is now a Grand Master at zombie slaying, which makes providing challenges pretty difficult - he slices through my zombie mopes like butter. I keep waiting for him to transcend!

    \n I thought the use of flashbacks and the "Mountain Witch trick" for Refesh scenes worked out well, and it got me thinking about Key Scenes again. I've never figured out a really good way to use those as a GM- it always seemed a bit forced- "Hey, if you play this next scene I'll give you experience points!". However, using flashbacks (or "meanwhile, on the other side of town..." scenes) seems like a smoother way to integrate them.

    The story-prompting would help in Key Scenes because it could provide some structure to the experience point reward. a GM could give PCs xps based on the degree to which they accept new plot twists and complications from the scene. I guess it's a little like Fate points in that way- "Okay, so you guys are racing through the jungle to the airstrip. Flashback Scene to yesterday- you had an argument with Miguel, the charter pilot, about money." GM holds out some experience point tokens. "So how did that go? Do you think you convinced him to still be waiting for you?"

    \n Best use of the Secret of Contacts ever, from last night's game:

    ME: Wow, it's a terrible sight to behold. The National Guard UH-60's are just pummeling the tiny fishing village of Angoon with 2.75 inch rockets, flattening it. You know they saw your fishing boat and they'll be coming for you next.

    SPIGOT: I know the guy in charge.

    ME: You what now?

    SPIGOT: (burns three Vigor) I know the guy running this show. What's his name?

    ME: Uh, Lt. Col. David Osborn.

    SPIGOT: How do I know him?

    ME: Vigor, huh? You guided for him.

    SPIGOT: I'm getting on the radio. I want to talk to him.

    ME: OK. (through crackling radio static) Who the hell is this?

    SPIGOT: This is Joe Turner, Colonel.

    ME: Joe who?

    SPIGOT: Joe Turner, out of Carbon Lake. I guided for you last summer. We caught some trout up on Baranof.

    ME: Get off this channel.

    SPIGOT: Now listen, Colonel...

    (cue an extended Sway contest that results in the lethal helos standing down and leaving them alone.)

We explain to his mom what's going to happen in the next 30 minutes, but she's not hearing it. She manages to scatter her kids in the inky bowels of the lightless Costco warehouse, and we have to go hunt them down before the infected one turns and bites his three siblings (and maybe us). We know that if my guy finds the infected kid, he'll truss him up and wait until he turns and give his family a chance to say goodbye. We know that if another guy finds him, he'll get beaten to death on the spot. The other guy (Bongbong, one of the original Filipino cruise ship survivors) finds the now-zombie child and destroys him. He shows up with the other three struggling kids - and a human bite on his thigh. "It wasn't him, it was one of these," Bongbong says, and we so don't believe him. Awful!

    \n A little of both, I reckon. If you have bonus dice left over, that could mean that you have too few engaging moments, or disengaged players. So if, as GM, you angle to provide those opportunities and keep them going, a dwindling pool of bonus dice is a good measure of success and you can structure conflict frequency and intensity accordingly. I've never seen a session where everybody ran out of dice, but that seems like a worthwhile goal to me. Much of this is group style, I'm sure.

    \n We're probably three or four sessions from the conclusion of this game now. There are still a few original characters (two, actually, one an NPC), but most of the raggedy-ass crew has been picked up along the way. My current favorite is Walter, a twelve-year-old orphan (he didn't start out that way) with a prosthetic limb (it didn't start out that way). Naturally, he's got Secret of the Signature Weapon for his claw hand. Also, he's now undead and probably out to get us.

    We've been getting better and better at crafting effective and juicy intentions and setting appropriate stakes. One way you can tell this is happening is the distribution of gift dice - if the GM is getting as many as the player, thems some good stakes.

    Last night's session was a rescue mission to the University of Utah Hospital, where some of the last survivors in SLC (and my guy's grandmother) were being overrun. It was a classic horror moment - isolated, split up, in the dark, in a hospital, zombies thick as flies. There was a scary fight in an MRI room and a lot of cool action. Half the group was barricaded in a medical records office with a nurse who had been bitten. Some of the characters are hardened lunatics who would have killed her on the spot, but they were all elsewhere, so the nurse was safe. Of course she turned at the worst possible moment, and there was a terrible, blood-slippery fight in the record stacks, during which two characters got bitten, including little Walter. About this time the hard cases returned. Jody, who has had a thousand-yard stare since he put down his entire undead family in Alaska, is the hardest of hard cases. He heard the fighting, went in alone, and shot the zombie nurse. He looked over Walter and Erin, saw the bite marks, bit his lip and (with the hearty approval of the players in question) gunned them down. We all agreed that Walter, already infected, was sure to return.

    A big part of the fun was carefully crafting intentions, and giving those to the GM to set stakes. We all offered up our best ideas when they occurred but left it ultimately in the GM's hands. We also explicitly agreed that the death flag was flying, and nobody's character was off limits.

      \n Oh man, we had a great session last night. The big take-away for me is that if the conflict matters enough, it's extremely satisfying to be a spectator in Solar System rules. Zombie stories are really human stories, and we've played ours long enough to build up a good head of human interest steam.

      We're in a compound run by a fundamentalist Mormon cult. The leader of the cult, Elder Tobias, is a former government scientist who worked with our own PC government scientist on the Black Swan project, and he's discovered a way to pacify individual zombies. Unfortunately, he's also a complete lunatic who ascribes his miraculous ability to his new-found faith. We are desperate to get out of this situation.

      One PC, the useless drug-runner and con man Jack Casey, finally connects with the fellow survivor, an Indonesian prep cook named Artie, and although the Key of Unrequited Love is not bought off, they hook up. Jack's a happy guy. Not for long.

      Things get crazy and we end up getting summoned by Elder Tobias, where we learn his identity and that he is a Master of Sway. All this leads up to a fantastic 4-way conflict. We're given permission to leave, but Jack's eight-year-old daughter, Julie, wants to stay. The cultists are nice. It feels safe in the compound. She's sick of the bus and the zombies.

      Jack obviously loves his daughter.
      Jody, another survivor, has Key of the Guardian for her. He's pretty insane and lost his own little girl, about the same age.
      Julie, an NPC, wants to stay.
      Tobias, also an NPC and a powerful one, won't let her go.

      So it's a huge conflict, Jody and Julie have a side conflict to assign penalty dice to the larger conflict, Pain is Brought Down, and Jack loses. He hangs his head and abandons his daughter. At least he's got his new hottie, right? It was so, so good. XP was flying. Helping dice were flying on both sides.

      So the rest of us are standing there, between Elder Tobias and his six armed guards and two zombie brides*, and we're all "hell no". Each of us is at the breaking point. My guy is on the cusp of losing his faith. Jody is destroyed by guilt over what he did last session. And Chico isn't going to let a little girl fall into these people's zombie-lovin' hands. We all know that we're going to die, and we're all good with that. It's going to be a hell of a throw-down.

      One interesting thing is that Ian, one of my friends, basically had no character in the session. His main dude died last week and he played a string of NPCs and might have had more fun than any of us.

      \n We wrapped this up last night, and we knew it was the last session, so we played hard. I kept thinking "we should play this hard all the time". Each of us had a big issue to resolve, and we were all burning through Keys, harvesting XP like idiots and spending to to raise skills, buy up pools, and generally go nuts.       My guy, the evangelical Craig, had a fantastic end! There was this family - Hipster Dad, Hipster Mom, and little Walter - and they had their own tragic arc. Dad got bit and slowly turned, but Craig brought him to Jesus before he zombified. Then he converted Hipster Mom. Then, on the long cross-country slog, Hipster Mom was eaten by zombies and Walter lost his arm. Then Walter got fitted for a metal-hook prosthesis and got infected himself - we left him behind in a panic. So naturally he returned as a zombie to menace us! 13-year-old amputee zombie. We're in the dark, all hell is breaking loose, and we hear the scraping of metal. Craig's like "come out where we can see you, we know you're alive because zombies don't carry metal things that scrape on the wall..." His best friend (mutual Key of Fraternity) buys off his Key and abandons Craig. And Walter surprises him, and we fight, and Pain is Brought Down. Walter is winning bad - at one point, I've got three penalty dice, and the writing is on the wall. It was awesome.

      The game was just stuffed with memorable moments and wonderfully-drawn characters. The strong relationships our guys had with one another, and the other NPC survivors, coupled with a lethal and focused world we could all imagine pretty easily, just drove the game to a satisfying conclusion.
