Hey gang,
I've been into the Forge's whole trip for a few years now, and I've kept busy lately by a) looking for design theories other than the Big Model and b) close-reading critiques of the Big Model. As a result of these two goals, I've been thinking "What distinguishes the Forge, exactly?"
Thus, I've come to think that player agency is the big deal, the worth-writing-home-about idea that the Forge and others have given us (can't speak to others' experience, but I know that protagonization is just the bee's knees to most long-time Forgies). Take Creative Agenda, Conflict Resolution, various techniques and ideas, even the Big Model itself, and put them all next to each other in a row, and a concerted effort towards player agency leaps out at me.
It's been argued that Robin Laws' player-types are a proto-version of the Creative Agenda; I disagree. Seems to me that, without very firmly insisting on player agency and protagonization, the CAs really are just more slots to fit into; this isn't a bad thing, but we've already had a looong time to reap the benefits of that angle on things. I think Creative Agenda *points at* the importance of player agency; *that* is what takes games to another level.

Of course, playing in a new way will create new problems and opportunities. Once you make room for some agency, the CAs make more sense - it's about the kind of game we're all agreeing to play together, and many of us favor some kinds of games over others. Not in an "I'm a Gamist!" sort of way; that's much too narrow (for many of us) to map what's really going on at the table. But it's still a useful distinction to make about games: if you sit down for a gripping, nuanced tale, and use Donjon to run it, then the rules probably won't cover relationships and emotional connections in any meaningful way. If anything, they discourage that sort of thing simply because they are a total non-priority in the design.

Arguably, because Donjon is a Forge game, from the early years of the Big Model, it's been designed in a way that can appear "narrow" to mainstream gamers - it's Gamism through and through, so Clinton R. didn't try to put any kind of "here's how to have a big, moving story" advice (I'm looking at you, White Wolf). You see that kind of catchall approach in mainstream games - the bit about how to win at stuff (not endgame mechanics; just straight-up Challenge rules, rather), the bit about how to tell a good story (usually GM-only notes; with little player agency available, the players are not expected to even try to do this directly), and the dozens or scores of pages written to accurately portray the setting (alternately, a sprinkling of extra rules-details throughout guarantees the "crunch" to which we're accustomed).

I guess at this point I should make the standard "If you have fun, then that's just fine" point - I really mean it. And, given my next bit, I feel the need to just point that out.
Anyway - once we have agency and we know why we're at the gaming table, we might realize that we don't want the same things. Again, this isn't about the players as individuals; it's about the group, in that moment and with that exact game, deciding (aloud or otherwise) this is what we're on about, this is why we're here. Given that we've all been thoroughly exposed to the "hybrid" model, taking a stab at something more focused could be the only way to start really enjoying RPGs.
I'm not too adept at "reading" the meta-process going on in a game, while the game's in progress, so it's hard to say if I've ever observed a "functional" hybrid (i.e. one that doesn't trip all over itself trying to do too much). I know that most mainstream RPGs have rules for stuff I really don't care about (the minutiae of combat, especially gear; not to mention minutiae in general), but that comes down to more of a crunch-tolerance than anything, I suppose. It could well be that the biggest problem is not the Agenda of the game/group at all, but rather deprotagonization. Dysfunctional hybrids are full of flotsam, "ballast" rules that are there to make the game meet the standard thickness and weight that the average gamer (including me) instinctually perceives to be a "proper" or "real" game.
To continue the nautical analogy, let's point the rudder in the right direction, towards the Agency Isles, and only *then* can we start getting up a good head of speed by tossing out the excess weight.
So. Big point: if we're happy and we know it, ignore Zac *clap clap* but if you're not, chances are good that a great first step would be to play yourself some games with high player agency. Most games put almost all their credibility in the dice (or cards, or whatever; randomness itself is the source of authority) or the GM (in the form of GM fiat and "GM has the last say"), which means the players are there to ... what, exactly? If they enjoy this role, great! If not (like me; I hated being a "neutered" player), then they need to see what else is out there.
Once that happens, then we can go back to the occasionally-silly arguments about defining Creative Agenda and so on.

    \n@Devp: By "player agency" I mean "the players are the people who push the game in new directions, by the decisions their characters make. There is no 'GM story' to tell, so that is no longer a way of getting 'sidetracked'." For that matter, if we have player agency in our game, the GM is there to run with material the players provide him, and no longer has to just hope they like what sort of scenarios he devises. It's a play style that spreads the creative heavy lifting to the rest of the group, rather than just on the GM or the die results. Alternately, if players feel like their decisions don't matter, then players don't have agency, not in a meaningful way. [I actually amended the original version of this paragraph to include the fact that the players must feel deprived for there to be any actual problem or dysfunction going on. Otherwise, yeah, I'd just be dictating how people should play. That first bit is my personal definition; the second bit is a concession to fairness and diversity of play groups "out there"]

    Like Joshua said, sometimes people feel like they're the GM's audience, and sometimes they don't like it. I don't like that feeling at all, from a player or GM perspective. The rest of my definition falls under "what Joshua just said". ^_^

    @Destriarch: Yeah, I need to apologize for being overly broad, or coming across that way. I actually meant "Forge Theory" when I said "indie" in the title of this thread, and that's unfair and excluding to everybody who's not into that kind of thing. My bad. Logos7 was spot on about that.

    @Joshua: I would disagree about D&D and agency, at least in the attached gaming culture, the tone of the rules, and a lot of the text. For one thing, most people learn to do exactly what Robin Laws thinks/thought constitutes a "good GM" by reading the Dungeon Master's Guide. Again, sure, if folks are happy with what they've got, great! I realize that some of us Forgies, including me, have at times been just as exclusionary as the most partisan mainstream gamers about this issue. That being said, I think a way to frame my thinking here is "I want games that GMs would want to play". Basically, something that doesn't necessarily chop down the creative power you have, but pushes it into the Protagonist mold instead of keeping it in the Fifth Business mode (an opera term I heard from the Forge. Think a hands-off, supportive GM, and you've got it ^_^)

    Komradebob, as far as fiction-level and player-level stuff is concerned, that's a pretty popular line of thinking about game design these days, yeah. In a nutshell, without a lot of illustrations, it goes like this: to avoid moments where the game seems to sputter out, the following needs to happen (using D&D as an example) -->
    - the dice results need to affect both mechanical stuff (hit points, etc.) and the fiction (the orcs see their leader die, and decide to retreat). This already happens, generally.
    - the fiction needs to affect the dice results (your wife is being carried off by the wererats! You get +2 to hit!). This is an official D&D3.0 "GM fiat" bonus/penalty that can be added to any roll. I believe it to be underused.
    - there needs to be some way for players to create "effective" fiction ("Hey, GM, this guy has every reason to trust me. Can I get a +2 bonus on this check?" "Sure!"). Players need to have input on the use of the +2/-2 rule if they are going to be something other than a GM-audience.

    This doesn't *have* to mean explicitly using rules that say "let the group decide" instead of "the gm has the final say". All it means is that we should make the most of our dice-rolling, and draw inspiration for things to happen in response to dice results; secondly, we should make the most of our fiction, and infer bonuses and penalties to rolls and such if the fiction is changing circumstances. Opening up the table for suggestions (or assertions, if you want more power-sharing with the players) will start creating a situation where the dice and the narrative "talk to" each other, and we can get a lot more mileage out of what we're doing at the table. This isn't anything that Vincent Baker isn't saying, but it's my super-brief, two-paragraph version of it, sans diagrams.

    Joshua, or anyone else familiar with this theory-concept, am I on point with this description? Please correct me or add things on as you see fit.

    Hey, Matt, good call!
    I absolutely agree with you. If you're referring to the examples of shoving player agency into D&D3.0 (which is what I was using in my example), I suppose what I advocate is adding on social-context rules about Kickers and whatnot, rather than modifying anything in the game text.
    'course, a lot of mainstream game texts explicitly tell you to have a house-rule-heavy attitude about playing (WoD's "Golden Rule" for instance), and to me, that's both a historical tradition and an admission that the game has some rules that the designers don't think are very fun; whether they think this consciously or not is a separate question. Or, well, it could just be an admission that the degree of simulation a group aims for is variable; I hope more games take a page from Burning Wheel and explicitly make it a rule that much of the rules is, technically, optional.
    Rather than have lots of rules in the sort of scattergun approach to rule design (which is heavy on faithfulness to the simulation at hand), I definitely prefer something that's shorter and more to the point about how play should look. And yes, there can absolutely be lighter, more nimble games out there that hold the simulation as the highest priority; to my mind, they're the sonnets to standard games' epic poems (more economy of language, essentially). It seems like, beyond a certain level of handling time and intricacy of mechanics, the Golden Rule (or something like it) crops up as an unconscious safety valve against the sheer stream of rules and information.

    A really purpose-driven game design, also, for that matter, can do things like establish very clear play-roles (this is what players do; this is what GMs do; etc.) so as to achieve a more precise vision of play. I'm trying to remember the last time I read a mainstream game's mechanics with the same level of care and thoroughness as, say, Polaris or Sorcerer; knowing that the mainstream designer probably figures "you 'know' how to play".

    \n Good call, Graham

    I had a session last night for this game I'm play testing called The Hellenes - a game of Greek heroics and tragedy. I've been trying to get my players to grok conflict resolution, and oddly enough, they didn't really notice that I was employing it for the first few months.
    In any case, a Greek heroine, Apollonia, was checking out a Persian warship that had been anchored and then abandoned off the coast of Sparta. She's looking for the women the Persians took captive when they raided Helos, where the heroine had been stationed with the Spartan army. Anyway - I describe the cargo hold, and the crew quarters nearby, and my player, Katie, asks me, "Can I roll something to see if I find anyone?"
    I reply, "Sure. Do you want to find anyone?"
    She looks at me quizzically. "Yes?"
    "Okay, roll [something or other]." She does so, and succeeds. "I guess you found somebody."
    "Okay, cool."
    [I describe a harried, half-starved woman who has to be coaxed like a frightened animal from behind the bed.]
    Her character asks the woman her name. I sit there and think for a moment, stumped. Katie blurts out, "Larissa? That's the only Greek woman's name I can think of."
    "Did you say that in character?" I ask.
    "Oh, sure. I just the word 'Larissa', though."
    "Okay. The woman nods at you, and says that you can call her that. Clearly it's not her real name."

    You can see that I used dice rolls and table-talk to invest the player with authority, at least for selected moments. I see this as more "genuine" play - rather than me deciding what's going on secretly, in my head, and acting like it's objectively true, I put it to the table: what does everyone think should happen? Alternately, if I have a clear idea in my head, I might ask, "Here's my idea. Is everyone cool with that?"
    I don't constantly ask for permission in this way. But the general style of our OOC conversations during play is one of building consensus and sharing narrative responsibilities.

    Nathan, I'd say that your players are definitely invested with some kind of authority - it sounds like the group talked pretty openly about the social contract for the campaign, and decided together that the GM would be the custodian of the Dream; someone who makes sure the Dream keeps happening in a particular way. Granted, everybody has their own internal vision of the SIS, but if you have one person in charge of it, and that's what you're going for, that sounds like overall agency, if not moment-to-moment. I say that because their input was requested and gotten in that very major way.
