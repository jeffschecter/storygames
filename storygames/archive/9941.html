    \n I was thinking it might be helpful to describe the game to make sure the cover works with the content:
    \n
    \nRadiance is a narrative game with a focus on primordial storytelling. You and your friends will create vibrant exciting tales where the activities within the story push the narrative forward. You'll take a primitive stance on creating stories to immerse yourself in ways that are both natural and original. And while you're listening to your friends' narration, you'll be able to introduce disturbances which can change the plot of the story in fun and unexpected ways. Easy to learn rules and no character creation means you'll be ready to play in a few minutes
    \n
    \nThat was my blurb by the way.
    \n
    \nMy goals for Radiance was to create a game where the world takes precedence over any characters. To move away from Cartesian dualism (mind/body, subject/object, self/world) and create something more Heideggarian (characters are in the world and the world is primary, personhood is revealed through ongoing activity rather than choice being a consequence of character) I was influenced by childhood tales such as the Billy Goats Gruff, ancient stories, and some hunches I have about oral cultures and storytelling. Other influences come from animism (anything can be a character as long as it is involved in an activity: fight with a sword that doesn't like you and you may find yourself in trouble, climb a mountain that demands a sacrifice before it will allow you to reach the top and it may cast you down, release a balloon on a playful breeze and that balloon may guide you somewhere magical, etc.)
    \n
    \nRadiance has no setting other than what players give it. The closest game that I can compare it with is Universalis.

    Posted By: MatthijsFrom this blurb, I have no idea what to expect. What's "primordial storytelling"? How does the game help me to "create vibrant exciting tales"? Don't all games work so that "activities in the story push the narrative forward"? What's a "primitive stance"? Etc.

    It's your game, and your blurb. As a reader, I would want to know more concrete stuff - especially what this game does that's different from other games out there. Two or three hooks that make me go "hmmm, how does that work?" or "OMFG the designer must be insane".

    \nPrimordial storytelling means original elemental storytelling. It's what you get when you remove hundreds of years of bullshit theory and analysis out of story and return to the roots of storytelling. And it creates vibrant exciting stories because it works the way that we normally tell stories to ourselves and one another.

    Activities don't push the story forward in most games. Characters make decisions and these move the story forward. You start with characters thinking about what they should do and then you get activity. Which is the reverse of how things should be. In Radiance you start with activities and these activities reveal the characters present within them.

    So how about this for the blurb...

    Nearly everything you've been taught about creating stories is a lie. Maybe you think the characters you create matter, that their motives and beliefs makes a difference. Maybe you think that stories are about people making choices, developing from those choices, and resolving some internal issue. Or maybe you think that the stories are about people facing challenges and overcoming obstacle. If that's what you think, you've arrived too late, everyone's gone home, party's over. Radiance is a game which takes you to the primitive roots of storytelling. You'll tell stories where the world you create and the activities you narrate within that world are primary and elemental. Stories where anything can become a character. And while you're listening to your friends' narration, you'll be able to introduce disturbances which can change the plot of the story in fun and unexpected ways.

    \n I think this might be a good comparison:-


    What might happen in a normal roleplaying game:

    Player One: There's a guard standing before you, the network of scar tissue on his arms and face tell you that he has more than a passing familiarity with violence. What does you character want to do?

    Player Two: I think my character will try to bribe him. What do I need to roll?




    Here's a similar moment as it might happen in Radiance:

    Player One: A menacing looking guard stands before a small hunched figure. Something passes between them, glinting in the afternoon sunlight. A coin. The guard closes his fingers around it, mumbling to the smaller man and pointing towards the door.

    Player Two: I want to disturb the activity with another guard witnesses the bribe taking place. Do you want to accept it or should we let the dice decide?

    \nThe idea of an internal experience about an external world is a relatively recent concept. Go back far enough or look at how other cultures perceive things and there is no internal/external dichotomy; your thoughts, feelings, beliefs aren't private experiences inside of you, they happen in the world. In many RPGs the dichotomy between internal/external, subject/object, character/world is present in the way that characters are created separately from the world rather than with the world. Radiance is primitive and elemental because it goes back to earlier ways of thinking about how we relate to our world.

    You're right about being confrontational and I've also enjoyed the same things as you. I think I may tone down the confrontational beginning.

    Don't break up the word. Don't use two sans-serif typefaces together. Don't use a font because you think it looks cool, but rather because it enhances the flavor of the text.
