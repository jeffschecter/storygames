Here's the Idea:

Let's post games (with links where possible) that either exemplify a principle of good game design, show a spark of brilliance (even if flawed), or teach a vital skill. A quick run-down of what the game has to offer, or what pitfalls to watch out for, would be welcome as well.

I'm mostly interested in primary texts (i.e., games), but if anyone feels an overwhelming urge to share more other sources of useful knowledge to a game designer (layout, graphic design, statistics and probability, etc), those would be neat to hear about as well.

Important Note: I'm thinking here of a syllabus for the practice of game design, not so much the history of game design. With that in mind, landmark texts needn't be introduced if their contributions are better illustrated in later games.

With all of that in mind, I'll start us off with an old game, but one whose unique style I've yet to see replicated:

Puppetland Puppetland RPG
Puppetland has perhaps the most succinct and explicit expression of "what it's like when we play the game" I've ever read - the Three Rules. It relies heavily on cultural referents in the form of fairy-tales and children's books to convey the tone and themes of play, but the Three Rules capture the concept of what we do as players more eloquently than I've seen anywhere else.

    \n Hey, you said opinionated, so that means I get a say.

    My favorite thing I learned was to favour emergent complexity. i.e. you can design a complex game by having a multitude of rules, or a few simple rules that combine to create complexity. Like DNA. Or a better example is chess - you could write the entire ruleset on the back of a matchbox, but fill libraries with how to play it.

    The nutshell is implicit complexity > explicit complexity.
