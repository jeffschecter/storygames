\n So, I'm currently working on a game called Dostoevskyan Murder Ballad.

System-wise, it steals elements from PDQ, TSOY, Shock:, Victoriana 2nd Ed, and sorta Burning Wheel's Duel of Wits.
Setting-wise, it steals elements from Dostoevsky (fancy that), Russian lit, and my assumptions about what Russian lit is probably about.

I've played the game twice, and it totally (totally!) kicks ass. We've had bizarre love triangles (multiple), midnight poetry duels, incompetent zookeeps who's animals get out and eat people (multiple), inns burned down in fits of violent rage (multiple), chess games with the competitors' wives as ante. There's a lot of cheap Russian accents, howling laughter, and brutal pathological fuel.

But there's one thing I added to the system between the first and second game that made it really fly. And that's the new shared NPC creation system. Characters are defined by three major components: Qualities (ranging from +2 to +6, just like in PDQ), Keys (stolen directly from TSOY), and Ghosts (which are past traumas that threaten to swallow your dice during a conflict). When I need to engineer a new NPC for use in a conflict, I create a single +4 Quality and pick a single Key. Then I pass that NPC to the next person, and it goes all the way around the table. Everyone adds a single +2 Quality or a Ghost. They can pass if they want to (only one person passed once all game).

So, in a four player game, when that NPC gets back to you, it could have an additional +6 in Qualities or it could have 3 Ghosts - or something in between. And it's fun to see what others have added, how they've twisted the character, and how powerful the character will actually be.

It's also awesome because it challenges players to step outside of the "my guy" mindset in a very low buy-in, easy, fun, and low-pressure way. That, combined with how rotating GMing works*, gives people the comfort, safety and transitional support to try something new. I had a few people say, "I'd never, NEVER GM a game... but Dostoevskyan Murder Ballad made it really easy for me to slip into those roles, and it prompted me to try something new." Rock.


*If I introduce the game, I GM a scene for every other player, going around the circle. When it gets to me, someone else volunteers to GM my scene. It goes free-form, scene by scene, from there on. This gives everyone a solid foundation, kicks things off, and then gives them an easy "in" to try it themselves.

    \n I have tried to institute a similar system for PC creation in almost any game where I can fit it in. Typically, it goes like this: Each player makes their character concept, then suggests an additional detail for every other PC. The player controlling that PC can accept each suggestion, and receive a small mechanical bonus, or refuse the suggestion and retain their ideological purity. It helps players with writer's block, allows for creating more group cohesion ("My guy is your PC's brother/cousin/boss/ex-lover/whatever" is a good suggestion) and can make people play character they wouldn't have thought of on their own.
