\nRiffing off the RPG-definition thread here, Luke talks about ethics being hard-coded into a game, but asks for examples of games that don't follow this trait.

What I'm asking for here, rather than clouding Luke's thread, is examples of how games hard-code ethics into the gameplay and mechanics.

For example, i don't believe that a game which has alignments is the same as hard-coded ethics, unless there is an in-game effect for following or breaking those alignments. The paladin might be an exception for some version's of D&D, because if a paladin "breaks" alignment, s/he loses in-game abilities.

In Vampire, you have Humanity. Lose too much and your character becomes a monster, effectively an NPC, so there is a mechanical reason to try to keep or regain Humanity.

I also would argue The Shadow of Yesterday has ethics hard-coded into gameplay through Secrets and Keys, because these reward certain kinds of gameplay while having benefits and penalties to buying off those traits.

What other games do these sorts of things?

    \n Two cases:

    In Palladium games, you have an alignment, one of nine-or-so lists of stuff you do and do not do. IIRC, when you consistently break your alignment, the GM is directed to give you less roleplaying XP. The converse, more XP when you play to your alignment, is sort of implied but not really stated. There are also no explicit rules for changing alignment, but it's Palladium \u2014 there are no explicit rules for a lot of stuff, and players are sort of expected to make up procedures of their own. So in rough form, alignment is really just a set of guidelines for how your dude can pick up some roleplaying XP (amusingly similar to TSOY).

    In GURPS, there are a number of disadvantages you can take, such as Code of Honor or Vow. Taking these disadvantages gives you more character points to play with but constrain your choices in play. Importantly, if you have, say, "Honest," you are not allowed to lie. Disadvantages are iron-clad in this respect \u2014 instead of opening up moral and ethical choices for play, disadvantages actually remove choice from the picture. Now, I suspect actual play sees this rule ignored often, but as far as rules-as-written goes, "ethical" disadvantages function to remove, not define, choice.

    \n Michael, that's exactly the thing. In the game world there are temptations, that you don't have to listen to stifling leaders, that your Sith teacher will help you, etc.

    The thing is, many of these things we can recognize as horrible. But the carrots are also in-game. The ethical choice, if it to be in-game, should also have its carrots in game. If you go to the dark side for an out-of-game bennies, then it's not really an ethical choice the character made, at best, you create an ethical excuse in game to excuse the player-level bennies.

    It creates an ethical choice in the fiction, when you construct the story later. But the source of the ethical choice, the question on whether to make it or not, are not really inside the game.

    Some people say it's ok. In another way. When we speak of, "Think what would be interesting to the story, then do that." That's another way of saying we decide what happens inside the game based on out of game decisions, and that's ok.
    The difference is that when you care about the story, it's ok to make story-based decisions. But if you want ethical questions inside the story, then the question for doing or not doing something should be, "Does it make for an interesting ethical question, and the addressing of such?" and not "Hm, it gives me mechanical powers, so let's do it! Oh, and it also gives the appearance of an ethical 'choice' by the character."

    \n Here's the thing.

    So long as it remains a real choice, it's ok. Like an incentive that keeps things equal. When it becomes an overwhelming bonus, for one-time, it can still remain a choice. When it's always better to do something, then it's not really a choice. Because the mechanics make the choice for you.     I don't know what Cold City is like, so I'll address The Mountain Witch. The Mountain Witch uses the Trust mechanic to create a specific narrative. It helps that during the mountain-climb you know there will be other, worse conflicts above, and when you get to the Wtich, you know that you don't have what to save for. As such, and seeing how it's built, The Mountain Witch is a bona fide Story Game. It uses the mechanics to help goad the players into creating a specific sort of story, with a specific progression and pacing.

    The characters' ethical choices are often not real choices, but the narrative is that of ethical choices made by the characters. And as a narrative, it can be plenty powerful, and it helps that the players know going in what kind of story they're telling.

    \n This subject really hits a certain geek-spot for me, so I must resist posting at length or I'll never get my work done (which is a moral choice.)

    Arguing only from my opinion, the hard-coding of a moral code within a game serves the function of trying to make moral choices have meaning. Making "good" (morally commendable) choices frequently means making sacrifice, and players do not often experience what that sacrifice entails; when a PC goes without lunch, the player isn't hungry. Consider the number of D&D characters that have slept on the side of the road without even a bedroll because there was no penalty for it. Sleeping at an inn would cost them a silver, after all, and a tent could cost as much as half a gold. The player doesn't experience the cold, hard ground, so there is literally no consequence other than saving a trifling amount of cash.

    Adding mechanics gives a concrete reason to the player for the choice. I will not make any comment as to the quality of any particular system. As Ralph commented, there can easily be narrative reasons for a moral decision. Designers, being what they are, will naturally be inclined to provide tools including tools for making moral choices mechanically meaningful.

    I have back-burnered my Existentialist game for now, but I would be interested in games that have actual hard-coded moral theories in them. "What if this were the way the world worked?" in the same fashion. Most games, at least those that mechanize moral choices, seem to be coming from an Objectivist/Rational Egoism approach. TSOY's keys could be hacked into a Relativist (of a variety of flavors) framework without too much challenge. What would Emotivist or Intuitionist moral mechanics look like?

    \n Michael there are good ways to have mechanics that highlight certain types of choices. Typically when I've seen these executed well there is a certain double edge to them.
    \n
    \nIn MW when you trust somebody that person is able to help you. But they're also able to harm you. So the mechanic isn't really incentivizing a particular decision so much as highlighting an available decision. Its not the sort of mechanic that would be good for all games, but it is effective for focusing your attention in MW on the issue of trust and betrayal...or more broadly honor, which is the specific purpose of the Witch.
    \n
    \nIn Vincent's Apocalypse World there is a similar mechanic around History (Hx) which is specifically not how much you trust someone but how well you know them. Being an open extroverted person typically means you know other people well and they know you. Being a more solitarty person can make it harder for others to screw you but it also makes it harder for you to help them. Being a Brainer (a psychic) makes it easier for you to know others and harder for them to know you. Which reduces (initially) your exposure to being screwed, but increases (initially) your ability to screw others.
    \n
    \nThis itself is not mechanizing ethics but it does lead to interesting ethical questions. For example. Given that we are exposed to a greater potential of screwage from a Brainer, does that justify taking a discriminatory stance against them. In our game for instance, the group's Brainer is banned from the upper levels of our settlement (where the "important" people live) because she's dangerous (and crazy). Fair? not Fair? There's no mechanical benefit directly to this ban, but it is an interesting ethical question that falls out of the history mechanic.

    I'm doing something similar to encoding character ethic into the mechanics with my current line of projects.

    Rajah Spiny Rat is where I really started putting together the cogs that form the mechanism, but it's gradually being refined through a series of playtests.

    The idea actually goes back to a notion I had about Tarot Cards.

    Characters at their essence are a block of stats, but if characters use only their stats they are at a certain disadvantage in the system. Character are capable of following paths that are allow them to manipulate certain game mechanisms to their advantage, but every path also imposes some kind of restriction on the character.

    The more strongly they adhere to their path, the more moral; dilemmas they will face, but the more powerful they will eventually become.

    Do I adhere to my path now for the chance at some extra strength later on?

    Do I forsake my path for a bit of instant gain?

    Players deliberately choose a range of paths to reflect the values of the characters and the aspects the characters most strongly identify with.

    To make this more meaningful, players often invoke one another's moral dilemmas. They activate their own strengths, but they activate the weaknesses of others.

    It's still a work in progress, but it's been interesting to see where different groups have taken the idea.

    \n Sweet Jesu, Ralph is like the Living Embodiment of RIGHT in this thread. I can't say anything better than he has.

    Two bits of (hopeful) clarity I'd like to add: "hard coded" is a red herring. Ralph's pointed this out, but hard coded does not equal system. The game's setting, premise or even optional rules text can present the characters in an position to make ethical choices. Those are all very valid elements to a roleplaying game.

    And, as Ralph acutely pointed out, system can provide opportunities for ethical decisions in many ways. Sure, you can have "STATE ETHICS/MAKE ETHICAL DECISION NOW" mechanics like my retarded games or you can provide a player with the tools to commit violence, rape, harassment, theft in the game and leave it up to the player's own (ethical) choice as to if and how he'll engage with those rules. On one hand, if the game doesn't enforce an ethical position, players can easily devolve their characters into a band of sociopaths. On the other hand, allowing the player the space to make a real meaningful decision to take his finger off the (proverbial) trigger can be very powerful. Both are ethical decisions, I think, but both have different impact on play.

    \n If you are trying to create ethics mechanics, the easiest way would be to not reward unethical behavior, but then the choices must be defined by the creator, or gm. Unfortunately, you run the risk of the choice ceasing to be a choice. If I played a game where my character lost some kind of resource for killing npcs, I would stop so that I don't run out of resources. After a while, I wouldn't even consider that action. My choice is limited, but my character's behavior is curbed.
    \n
    \nDogs wants the idea of right or wrong to be considered, but it doesn't specifically reward, or punish either. You can take fallout doing something as simple as a can shooting contest depending on the dice (as the rules are written).
    \n
    \nI'm unsure of any games with specific morality mechanics, and I would shy away from those because I play to escape certain realities; moral choices being one of them. I have plenty of time in real life to consider morality, especially since in real life morality matters where in imaginationland, it doesn't.
