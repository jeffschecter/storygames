\n I've made a story game called Judgement. It's my first one and it's not playtested yet, but I hope to do that for the first time at London Indiemeet this weekend.

Here's the pitch:

An accused soul stands in judgement after death, the good and evil within its life in perfect balance. A celestial court gathers to investigate whether ambiguous, pivotal moments \u2013 the cruxes \u2013 during the accused\u2019s life were more good or evil and so tip its fate to Heaven or to Hell.

And here's the link. Take a look if you're interested and let me know what you think.

    The Inquisitor is a self-repairing simulant who survived until the end of time, and, coming to the conclusion that there is no God and no afterlife, decided that the only point of life was to "seize the gift of life". He is on a journey through time, seeking out those he judges worthless and erasing them from existence, allowing a different person to exist in their place\u2014one of the many that could have been conceived had they not been.

    Each crew member comes up for judgment, with the Inquisitor morphing into each crewmember during their respective trial, meaning that they are actually being judged by themselves (which the Inquisitor claims is "the only fair way" in spite of the metaphysical implications).

    \n From the point of view of design, one interesting thing about that episode is that each crewmember must, in the end, pass judgement by their own self: someone who knows all their secrets, thoughts, and desires.

    The ones who "pass" the test, then, are the ones who set the lowest standards for their own behaviour, while those with high aspirations and ideals fail dramatically...

I agree the method for doing it sounded clunky, but I quite liked the idea that you couldn't plan because you didn't know which role you would take next. While this would probably mean someone never tried one of the roles, I'm not sure I'd see that as a failing, more a hook to want to play the game again...     \n They don't have to be in order, but the default assumption is that they are.

    The random allocation was to prevent the meta gaming that using cards could have created. The original point was to ensure you had a good hand of cards to ensure a good outcome should you be the accused in the final judgement scene. With that gone there was no reason to keep the randomness. It was a balance thing: better to have simplicity and give everyone a chance to play every character versus mechanics for mechanics' sake.

    Also, in the playtest, Fabio drew being the judge three times in a row!
