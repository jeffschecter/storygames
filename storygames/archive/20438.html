    \n This is definitely an issue for gamers everywhere. Here's an example of a mini-essay recommending one particular approach:

    Slanting Character Actions

    The specifics depend heavily on the game, as Vincent and others say. For instance, many games have no interest in a conscious "story" at all, while still creating narrative. An old-school game of D&D is a good example: you're here to get into the dungeon and escape with treasure, not to create story. And yet at some level there is a tradeoff between more fun/exciting character actions (in this case, climbing into the dungeon and braving terrible dangers) and less drama-producing actions (let's convince the local Duke to build a new fort near the dungeon site; within a decade or two someone will have blocked off or cleared out the dungeon).

    \nSince I wrote Terror Network, I just wanted to clarify a couple of things regarding the use of the word story and storyline in the rulebook. When we wrote that, we were not all aware of the way the term "Story" had evolved in online discussions about RPGs. We were using those labels very casually to mean anything from scenario, in game events, plot elements the GM throws in to make the game more interesting and just the connective thread running threw the campaign. The Storylines mentioned in the operations chapter are more about introducing complications that make the character's lives away from the counter terrorism arena intrude. Fundamentally the game became more about Investigations and Missions in actual play (which is why the modules all have a kind of sandbox structure to them).

    This was our first venture into publishing on our own, and there was a bit of a gap in my opinion between the promise of the text and what we were actually doing at the table with the game and the mechanics (partly because we were just aping the rulebooks we'd been reading our whole lives without giving it a lot of reflection). Really the modules and the agency book are the best guides for what the game is meant to do (more so than the rulebook which, judging by the emails I got, confused a number of people who didn't seem to know what a Terror Network campaign was supposed to look like---and this is something we tried to address in the agency book).

    If people want to use Terror Network for creating stories that is cool. But I think it would be misleading for people to buy it thinking the mechanics are designed to help them tell a story or that that is the central focus of the game (most of the mechanics in Terror Network are focused on managing a counter terrorism investigation). Telling a story can certainly be the focus of play if that is what people want, it can also be part of a much bigger pie. But I wouldn't call it the central activity of the game. Given how loaded the term is in these conversations, if I were to revise Terror Network, the language would be more carefully crafted in that respect. I wouldn't completely avoid the word, but I'd be more careful about how it was addressed.

    \n Most traditional (caveat: Western, modern) stories are told from only a couple points-of-view, maybe just one. If more than one, POV tends to switch per-chapter. Concentrating on one POV at a time lets themes develop. The author controls pacing carefully to maximize emotional impact for the reader. A good story has something of a mystery or question to it, to avoid predictability so the reader doesn't get bored. What will the character do? How will this story turn out? A lot of stories interweave themes and foreshadowing and other devices.

    Most RPGs don't really facilitate that. You have 3-5 characters all vying for screen time, without really sharing spotlight. Pace is haphazard, especially in traditional games, unless there's a certain amount of railroading happening. Predictability is often a problem, except for the "will they succeed?" question, but even that gets forced sometimes. Themes and foreshadowing are usually accidental.

    Some notable exceptions: Primetime Adventures, Annalise, My Life with Master, and Fiasco have strong POV rules. These help with pacing, too. Misspent Youth has excellent pacing / story-arc rules. Annalise's "claims" rule is basically a foreshadowing device for modern horror. Themes crop up in design, especially certain modern "Narrativist" designs, and they seep into play.

    \n Three thoughts:

    1) In real time, being the audience of an RPG session is never going to be like viewing a movie or reading a novel. Those two get planned and rehearsed and edited and refined before the audience sees them; an RPG session does not. Watching a story-strong RPG session is like spending 30% of your time reading a novel and 70% of your time watching the novelist brainstorm or goof off, something that I doubt any novel-reader would do.

    2) In retrospect, summarized after the fact, some RPG sessions and campaigns are every bit as satisfying to remember and think on and share as good movies or novels. I've played games of Fiasco that I remember as fondly as Burn After Reading. I've played long homebrew campaigns that I recall more favorably than the Chronicles of Amber (and I loved Amber).

    3) The design spec for "Story Now" RPGs seems to me to be simply about tweaking that 70%-30% ratio. Whereas a dungeon crawl with no interest in exploring any sort of compelling human crisis might produce satisfying narrative developments at only the slowest and most erratic pace (99%-1%), a focused game of Sorcerer or Primetime Adventures might reliably exceed that 70%-30% mark .

    I'm sure this isn't a new formulation, but this seemed like a good spot for it.

    \n By principle, playing an RPG is not like making a story; the human brain makes the difference blurry by mixing how we remember/understand the whole deal with the actual reality. It's not like, but it can be compared to -trying to make a story with several handicaps:

    -Several people intervene, so as much as you would like, you don't have full agency or even agency enough to focus on important parts of the story, like the theme, character development, coherence, etc.
    -Nobody (most of the times not even the GM) has full access to all the information available all the time. Players can keep plans and thoughts secret, there's randomization of conflict resolution which in turn may affect narrative rhythm.
    -(on most games) you can't freely go back time, replay scenes nor rebuilt them in any way to enforce some of the aspects mentioned.
    -Everyone is expecting entertainment and even have different expectations about it. Also, they have agency enough to try and push their expectations into the game. Like the guy who starts a fight when everything was set up for a tense diplomatic scene.

    So, no wonder good, deep interesting RPG game stories are rare. It's still possible for those to happen because there are groups that click, with the disposition to mix their individual talents into memorable moments. Like the GM who adds that incredible twist in the right time, the player who takes that as a cue to roleplay her character so amazingly everyone forgets for a moment it's a fictional character or the quiet guy who suddenly came up with the idea that saved everyone's neck without pulling up a fight.

    Also, some games add procedures with better chances to help groups collaborate into making more deep material, or plainly call for players more prone/used to collaborate into a good impro.

    \n So I really think we can get pretty close to good storytelling in RPG:s. Stories don't all need to follow the Hollywood 101 structure. They can look like all kinds of things. When I'm playing a game ...
    * with a social contract explicitly stating that we're telling a story,
    * which has rules forbidding any speech which is not part of the narrative during scenes
    * with tools that support developing themes, story arcs and pacing
    * with players experienced in playing such games and equipped with all those implicit techniques for making it work
    ... I really feel that we can get some really good stories going. We'll never show a gun in act one and not fire it in act three. We'll never have pointless digressions that don't tie into the theme. We'll make some good stories that we enjoy, because we're the ones making them, and the structure might not be clean and polished all the time, but the same is true of some of the best movies and books I know. Stories aren't just this rigid thing. They can have loads of different structures.

    Often the point of the game is not telling a good story, but sometimes it totally is.

    Since I wrote Terror Network, I just wanted to clarify a couple of things regarding the use of the word story and storyline in the rulebook. When we wrote that, we were not all aware of the way the term "Story" had evolved in online discussions about RPGs. We were using those labels very casually to mean anything from scenario, in game events, plot elements the GM throws in to make the game more interesting and just the connective thread running threw the campaign.
    \nThat is, overwhelmingly, what story means in the context of RPGs, and it's perfectly fine because that's how story's used (with a few additional bits here and there which most RPGs, including Terror Network, also have: characters, conflict, etc.) so you didn't use it wrong and you shouldn't change it just because RPG people go berserk and start vomiting blood as soon as they hear the word "story" instead of just thinking about it the way the rest of the world does.
    \nI would never tell anyone else what words to use or not. If you find story gets your meaning across to your audience, then go for it. And if you find Terror Network helps with play you'd use the word story to describe, go for it. This isn't about vomiting blood or going berserk, it is about expectations. For me it is a matter of clarity and the word 'story' created some issues there. While I agree for most gamers who are not as into the online discussions it is still used fairly loosely to mean a number of different things, a lot has happened in the hobby and there are communities who use it in a particular way. I carried that banner for a while of trying to reclaim the term online to its more general use, but it really seems to mean something rather specific to a number of different groups. It can be a confusing word for that reason. Also because it is a sort of malleable word for many gamers, it does lack precision, so even there I've learned to explain what I mean when I use it. If I drop the word story into my GM advice section a number of people will read it to mean railroad, others will read it to mean narrative mechanics, while others will read it to mean putting story as the first or only consideration when ruling on outcomes. None of these match well how I actually play, or what I imagine my games doing for people at the table. That doesn't mean conflict, drama and characters are not a part of my game, but it does mean I give very little consideration to things like pacing (a particular pet peeve of mine as a player and GM), making things feel like an actual story, etc. I would describe my style as a combination of character-driven-vaguely sandbox and situational GMing (as described by Clash Bowley), where the situations flow naturally from the NPCs and groups in play. If I could use the word 'story' to convey that to people, I would. I just don't find it works well for a large enough segment of the people who read our books to do so. So when I am making games, that is typically the kind of play things are designed around (obviously people can play the games however they want, but just in terms of the environment the games emerged from, it could be misleading for me to emphasize story. Terror Network was play tested around sessions that looked a lot like the module the Patriot Incident. Given that there is a whole segment of RPGS called story games, it can definitely help to at least be careful when using the term if you are writing an RPG book (I am not interested in policing language, so if people want to use that word, they should feel free, I've just found throwing it out there with little clarification can create confusion among my readers).

    I just wanted to clarify Terror Network as I don't want people buying it thinking they are getting a system designed for something it really isn't (and I've received emails from people confused about a number of things). When I read Terror Network now, while it is a game I am very proud of in a lot of ways, there are things about it that I would like to change (almost none of it is mechanics though). The GM advice and talk about story, to me reads more like how things were written in the 90s (which is what I cut my teeth on so it makes sense). I would have liked to have put more thought into that stuff, because even as I was writing it, I had long moved away from a lot of that kind of thinking (my GMing style changed pretty radically around 2006 as a result of frustration with railroading and adventure path style play; as well as play designed around encounters and challenge ratings that was really common in 3E). But I hadn't really figured out how to talk about that in a way that reflected my style and with Terror Network I was writing the way I thought you just had to write stuff. Like I said, it was our first effort. I learned a lot about what we were communicating to people following its release (because we were largely just writing the way we talked in our own game group).

    Again, if the word works for you, I am not telling you that you should use another term. I'm not out to eradicate the word story. I've simply found it is a word that mislead some people if you are not careful. If I were just talking with my gaming friends, I wouldn't think much about its use. When you write a rulebook, the readers just have the text, there is no follow up question for clarifying what you mean.

      \nI think it is a big combination of things unique to the hobby itself. It isn't good or bad, it just the way things developed. I think it is my responsibility to figure out how to communicate with my readers. It doesn't do much good for me to blame people if they are confused by the words I select. Unfortunately story has become a legitimately confusing term to a lot of folks if it isn't clarified. And this is something that has just become clear to me with each book we release. It is also bound up in a number of arguments, getting used as a football over things like play style and the best way to design a game (and a lot of that turns on the various meanings the word affords).

      Even if it wasn't a hot button word that set off debates or whatever, there clearly is a style of play and type of design that is focused on story (which is why you have forums like Story-Games.com. I was entirely unaware of this fact when I made Terror Network. I had gotten whiffs of it by poking around online and occasionally seeing stuff like the forge, but it never really absorbed that there was this subgenere of the hobby. So it is understandable people might get confused if you say something like the focus of play in Terror Network is story. Some might see that and just think it is story in the way it was used in the 90s (which really just means roleplaying is important or the GM is kind of going to railroad a plot) or they could take it to mean its a story RPG. I simply meant it in the sense that fun stuff happens and drama naturally flows from the characters doing things and the GM throwing things at them.

      I've enjoyed this discussion though because it has forced me to take a second look at some things. Thanks to this conversation I just re-read Terror Network actually. So that has lead to some good insights for me. What we really should have stated in the rulebook was that the focus was investigations (because that is actually an assumption running through the book now that I read it again). It is built around that. That doesn't exclude other things from arising or other play styles being used. But the hardest thing to deviate from with the game, in my view, is the assumed investigative structure of the adventures. The other issue in the game, that someone looking for story might object to, is the intentional lethality of the system. We cover this quite a bit and basically players are, in a sense, deprotagonized by the mechanics and the GM advice (GMs are instructed to let players characters die and all NPCs and PCs die just as easy). What we do say is don't let a good death go to waste. So there is nothing wrong with squeezing some drama or plot developments out of a death (i.e. Special Agent Diego was was shot and killed but he got a glimpse of the suspect's face and is able to share that before he dies).

      These days, I use the word "drama" more to describe that aspect of play in my games where they occur. It feels less tethered to things like assumptions about plot structure or potentially connected to a particular style of play.

      So, since you seem to have some well-formed thoughts on the subject, I want to get your take -- when you feel something impinging on your enjoyment of play, where does that typically come from?

      This may seem like a tangent in the immediate sense, but I think it might be highly relevant to the thread title.
      \nI should clarify, mainly my complaint in the above post was about discussions regarding RPGs and RPG design, not so much about actual play (these days I try not to overthink things at the table and I wouldn't ever rule something out simply because it might impinge on immersion in principle, rather I take everything on a case by case basis----if I don't like it, I avoid it,but I at least see how it plays at the table first).

      For me the biggest annoyance is being told by someone "your doing it wrong" or "this will do it better" even after I've explained my preferences. When it comes to design discussions, I will occasional find the same thing either in discussions of my own approaches to design or in talking about other games (i.e. this game isn't good because it doesn't do X). Don't get me wrong, I like a good debate and to hear conflicting views. I just don't like it when a particular word gets used to equivocate and confuse a discussion (intentionally or unintentionally----this happens with other terms too).

      That said, I am probably someone you could label an immersionist. I am also a mod at Therpgsite and a poster there (which is pretty much an immersions forum). As immersionists go, I may not be the best person to ask that question because there are people with much stronger views on the subject than myself but I will do the best I can to answer for me and try to give answers for immersionists in general (just a sidetone, I think few people I'm thinking about here would actively label themselves immersionists).

      I think techniques, approaches to GMing and mechanics can all potentially disrupt immersion. But you will find the line varies a lot from person to person, and often the explanations are made after the fact (something bothers someone, they think about it, and decide it was because it disrupts immersion---but not everyone will have that experience with a given technique or mechanic). The big ones people mention are anything that forces you to make a decision out of character. My old business partner used to hate Bennies for this reason (despite liking Savage Worlds a lot). He hated them because A) He felt they took him out of his character's head space and He felt they were this extra thing that wasn't connected to what was going on in the setting (he also didn't like the way they were handed out). However, he was perfectly happy to play 4E and I found that to be a game that seriously disrupted my immersion in a way no other version of D&D had before. I don't want to rehash old arguments but just in my experience stuff like martial powers that were not at will, really mucked with me.

      Another thing you'll hear from folks is stuff around GMIng (explanations for reaching certain decisions for example). Instances of this would be "say yes" or "fail forward" (these can also be built into the rules themselves). People who are into immersion generally want to feel like they are there in a world that is separate from there character. If the GM creates things in response to the players asking if something is there, for some folks that disrupts the realness. Fail Forward confclits with some peoples' sense of immersion because it places story as the measure of where things should go rather than basing it on what should happen logically as an outcome of failure.

      Opinions do vary here though. There is also a split between people who basically want a setting and game that feels more real world. Not realistic and simulations necessarily, but a more grounded world like our own (even if the mechanics used to achieve it are abstract and simple). For that crowd too much genre physics will be disruptive.

      However some folks, and I include myself here, like genre physics and genre emulation when that is what the game calls for (not all games call for it, so in some I'd find it odd). Here I am okay with building physics into the game that emulate the way things work in genre (so having rules for 1 hit guards, having heroes who grow in health and are hard to kill the more they advance, etc). I want the world to feel real around my character, but I don't mind if that world abides by the physics of an action movie or Doctor Who.

      So in forums or tables where immersion is a big deal you might see a lot of debates over games like Numenera (which some immersions like but others have issues with). The same with games like Doctor Who. A game like Gumshoe can often be an issue for people as well (I've played it and think it is well designed/well written but there are things with the system that really muck with my GMing style----and a couple of my players had similar issues from their side of the table....I ran it as-written so I could experience it the way it was intended to be played). Interesting and cool game, but just not for me. And some games will be total non-starters for people (4E often is, anything with heavy narrative mechanics will be as well).

      These days, I try to be pretty easy going on this stuff. Rather than walk around with an idea that certain types of mechanics or approaches will disrupt my immersion, I only worry about it if I notice in play. I figure some games might have things that in theory would be an issue, but they are not present in enough volume to attract my attention. Sort of how like when people were talking about 4E and dissociated mechanics, there were arguments pointing out that dissociated mechanics had long existed in the game. This lead me to conclude it was largely about volume and how easy it was to ignore.

      But to answer your question, the things that impinge on my play are anything that reminds me the world isn't real and that I am playing a game. I've told this story a lot but the first time I ever played an RPG was I believe a version of Mech Warrior that the GM had tweaked to his taste. The biggest thing that struck me once I started playing was I felt transported completely to another place. It was way more powerful than a movie or book and really had an impact on me. So I think when I play, this is the experience I am chasing. I've realized though not everyone is chasing that. And when you have someone else in control of the game who is chasing something else, that can affect my enjoyment.

      To be clear here, I am not a jerk about it. If one of my players is really excited about a game that is built more around making decisions out of character and telling a story or something, I am not going to crap on him and tell him I hate that stuff. I'll try it and see what it is about it he likes so much. I'll probably have fun. I just won't be getting the experience I'm typically there for. This is more about knowing what clicks for me and what kinds of games I want to make. It isn't about saying this game or that game shouldn't exist (or that this style of play is inferior).

      Also it is worth pointing out, few groups are made of a homogenous crowd. My current group has a range of players who come to the table for different reasons and with different preferences. Since I am the GM the sessions always reflect my desire for immersion to an extent, but I am not inflexible and I will adjust to suit the players I have at my table (because an immersion heavy game that doesn't worry at all about story would bore some players to tears...if those folks are present and it isn't disruptive to the rest of the group, I'll include things I know they'll like). Obviously there are cases where someone is just in a group that doesn't fit them though.

      I hope that answers your question. If not I am happy to clarify.

      I had a chat on RPGnet recently about whether there's a real tension between "literature or narrative" story-play and "the sense of being somewhere else where anything can happen" immersive play. A lot of the replies centered on techniques. Basically, it wasn't so much the aims of story-prioritizing players that gave immersion-seekers trouble, it was the tools of the games they preferred.
      \nI had an experience with this a while back which, I think, matches your point of view.

      I'd designed a game you know quite well ("Land of Nodd"), and got together with some new gamers to play it. We also played a session of "Polaris".

      There is fairly little question in my mind that we were very much on the same page as far as the creative goals of the games were concerned: we were trying to create exciting and dramatic narratives in a GMless context, and to find out where the games would take us. In terms of creative goals, I'd say we were perfectly aligned.

      However, at the table we had some issues. Their approach to this kind of narration was far more bare-bones than mine, getting "to the punch" very quickly and with very little build-up. I prefer a slower pace, more vivid fictional detail, and their narration style, heavy-hitting and dramatic but very low in detail, was something I had trouble connecting to.

      The outcome was a lot of fun in some ways, but it was a constant struggle for me to adjust to their pace, and I was constantly wishing the game would just slow down a little so I could enjoy the events which were taking place a little more.

      It seems pretty clear to me that, while our creative goals were aligned (almost perfectly, as far as I can see), the *style* of gameplay and our expectations clashed a fair bit. It would never have caused interpersonal friction or major disagreements - the gaming was interesting and memorable - but it did limit my enjoyment of the games significantly.

      Having read all your examples of things that mess with your immersive fun, I think they all fall into one category: transparent contrivance*. That moment where you can see that something that just happened in the fiction happened for some reason other than fictional causality.

      Examples of such techniques include:
      - the player or GM has their PC or NPC do something that that character wouldn't do
      - violations of established physics
      - rejecting dice outcomes despite a prior agreement that dice mechanics model and represent fictional causality
      - the undo: "no, wait, let's have that not happen"
      - the railroad -- "all your other options suddenly disappear; please go this way"

      Examples of reasons for employing these techniques include:
      - GM or player wants things to go a certain way because it'd be cool (as per their aesthetics)
      - GM or player doesn't want play to go in the direction hitherto indicated (e.g. character death)
      - GM or player is trying to do "what would make a good story"
      - GM wants things to go a certain way because it's their plan
      - GM feels it's their job to exert authorial vision over the proceedings

      Does that sound about right?

      If so, then I would agree that a GM's or group's focus on "making a good story" over and above immersion would indeed make such breaches more likely.

      It still strikes me as very possible to pursue good stories without violating internal causality, but the "pursue story" part needs to be done more in the set-up phase and in between play, as opposed to during play.       One type of game/group/gamer wants their roleplaying to produce amazing stories, and fully embraces the nature of the game as a story-producing process, including lots of discussion about what would be "coolest" and a great willingness to transform the story as it goes along. (For instance, such a group might say, "Hey, the Q
      ueen's wedding didn't quite go as well as it could have. How about we play that part again, and make it better?"       They see themselves as authors of a story and want to develop as creatively as possible, and all try to challenge themselves and each other constantly to improve the quality of the resulting fiction and how "cool" or clever or memorable it is.

      (There is an analogue here with fiction writers who follow models of storytelling, including ideas like the need to "introduce a twist in Act 3", and plan out their stories in advance.)

      Another type, however, aims at a similar result - hard-hitting, memorable stories - but sees the process as one which prioritizes emotional engagement. The desired process is to immerse in the emotions of the characters and the gut-wrenching moments and dilemmas created as the story goes along. They throw difficult situations at passionate characters and want to be surprised by what happens.       \n Interesting discussion. I'm working my way through the thread, only on page 1/4 so far, so forgive me if this is backtracking.

      As others have said, I think you can look back after the conclusion of a gaming session and find (if you look hard enough) what amounts to some semblance of a "story," but I don't think creating coherent stories is an inherent part of gaming. At least, not most gaming.

      Sure, some games are designed to create stories through play, quite a few have been mentioned, such as PTA and Fiasco. But those lack something fundamental to satisfying stories: unity of purpose.

      Even games designed around telling stories hit dead ends and narrative cul-de-sacs that most "real" stories simply wouldn't have. They'd be rewritten or edited out. Up to a point, things like meta-currency can help with that problem. By retconning a scene or changing a poorly timed bad roll that would wreck a story.

      And that speaks to something of the problem with trying to make games that directly create stories. I'll get back to that, but first there's a game that hasn't been mentioned that really should...

      Look at one example that surprisingly hasn't been mentioned yet: Trail of Cthulhu. It was specifically designed around a fairly common problem with its parent game: the missing of clues essential to the continuation of the story because of bad rolls. So ToC fixed that by simply removing the randomness of whether a clue would be found, and made the game work by more closely mirroring the fiction it was striving to emulate... the characters in detective fiction don't fail to find clues, nor do they fail to use their lab equipment, rather it's how those clues are interpreted by the characters that drives the story.

      It's an interesting example because it did the one thing a game probably shouldn't do... it removed the game element (i.e. the randomness) from the game so that it could more closely capture the fiction it was trying to emulate.

      To me, that sums up the problem of trying to make games that directly create stories. The more inherently "story-like" it becomes, the less "game-like" it will have to be. Everything that a game is, randomness, chance, player and/or character choices mattering, etc are all basically diametrically opposed to satisfying stories.

      Stories work in a very particular way that's basically not gameable, at least not in a way that would be satisfying to most gamers. Everything about most satisfying stories (TV, film, comics, etc) is strictly regimented and samey. Acts, act breaks, character arcs, B stories that are thematically linked, plot twists at just the right times, minor success being the ball in a tennis match (one side wins a little then the other side wins a little... over and over and over), lasting success only at the right time, etc.

      That kind of sameness is enjoyable when it's done by extremely talented TV and film writers, performed by actors who know their roles inside and out, and are wrapped up in 22-minutes to 2 hours, tops. But that same things as a sprawling beer and pretzels gaming session that lasts anywhere from 4-7 hours or more?

      Fiasco pulls this off (sort of) by only having two acts and it being about the utter chaos of a certain genre of films. PTA pulls this off by being about any TV show, but it's more like a collaborative writing system than a game in many ways. Not knocking either, they're great fun, but nearly everything about "game" seems to be largely going in the opposite direction from "story".

      If you wanted to make this exercise slightly more usable, I would involve character goals.

      i.e. How granular do you make these player intentions? At one end is "I hit him with my sword" and at the other is "I kill him". Another way of saying that is how closely are we looking at this current situation. Obviously if the situation is not very important, you can stand back and the PCs can make "larger" intentions such as "I kill him". If the situation is quite important then we can examine it closely - the PCs can make more granular intentions such as "I strike him". The GM tailors the scope of the consequences to the scope of the action for the most part.

      The measure of importance can simply be, how close is the current situation to immediately resolving any of the PCs goals? IF not so close, stand back, if very close examine closely.

      One last thing - capability - The players must make plausible intentions for their characters capability. That doesnt mean the GM is a fascist about it though - only veto absolutely obviously implausible intentions. However, as a rule of thumb, if the character is kind of incompetent in skills required to execute an intention, the degree of the complication can be proportionately worse.

      i.e. if, in a situation of not very much importance to a character goal. a skilled warrior character says "I kill the thug", then bammo it just happens without too much ado. However if a librarian says "I kill the thug" then (a) the player needs to go into some detail about how that is even plausible (perhaps a distraction etc...?) and (b) things could get complicated in a bad way - OK, you distract the thug and smash him over the head with the lamp, but he doesnt go down, there is a struggle in which you take several bad hits before you manage to beat him to death with repeated blows with the lamp - you have a badly broken nose, missing a tooth and are covered in blood.

      I would not add any more complexity to this exercise other than that though, or the purpose of the exercise starts to get obscured beneath cruft.

      \n There are many other uses of dice (randomizers), as well. One I'm particularly fond of is how players assess risk. You may be willing to agree to a heavy consequence, penalty, or repercussion, but will refuse more dangerous/severe consequences, because of their weight.

      However, when the brain is considering a gamble, decisions are made differently. First of all, this can help reach agreement: agreeing on a *probability* is almost always less socially-fraught than agreeing on an outcome. Second, people are more willing to accept extreme outcomes if there's a chance of avoiding them, so (given the right design) adding a random can increase the overall stakes your players are willing to play for.
