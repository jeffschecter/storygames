\n For those interested, you can download an updated version of Design Patterns of Successful Role-Playing Games from http://legendaryquest.com . It is located at the bottom of the Downloads page.

If you haven't heard of it before, the book contains common design patterns gleaned from a study of many traditional and indie role-playing games.

The new revision differs from the previously released draft in organization and it has a much improved diagramming technique. Further, the book was edited by Mike Cantrell, who did a great job in helping me improve the text and remove as much of my personal bias from the original work as possible.

I welcome any questions and/or comments you might have on it.

    \n There was some discussion a while back about creating an online wiki for RPG Design Patterns. I'm in favor of the idea, but I currently have way too many projects in the works to devote to moderating the thing and I fear any such site would degenerate into a turf war without strong guidance. I've learned that many people take a harsh view of criticisms concerning design techniques used by their favored games, and can be quite vocal in their defense. However, I would welcome anyone wanting to set one up who is willing to devote the necessary time to it. As you point out, the contents of the book can be used liberally as long as the requirements of the CC license are followed.

    I think anti-patterns are a good idea too, although I personally find design patterns more useful in practice. In the original draft, I was actually intending to make Alignment and Anonymous Rules anti-patterns, but didn't want to unilaterally do so without community discussion on the topic. That's probably a good thing, too, since I was convinced that they shouldn't actually be anti-patterns. In fact, I completely misinterpreted the design goal of Alignment in the original draft, and had to alter it completely in this release. And, while I believe Anonymous Rules should be used with care, there are times when they are appropriate. Strict avoidance of the pattern can tend to make rule descriptions read too much like technical manuals.

    \n The only specific anti-pattern I can think of are rules that attempt to enforce behavior by penalizing non-conformity, rather than rewarding conformity. These almost never work correctly and irritate players. A classic example is the xp penalty in d20 for multi-classing, a rule so bad that nearly no one uses it. Compare with Pathfinder's notion of giving a bonus every time you level in a "favored class". Both seek to prevent multi-classing from getting out of control, but the former is never used, while the latter isn't a horrible way to do it. Exalted does similar things with its virtue system, which also mostly fails.
