\n There's been a fair amount of discussion lately about how prone collaborative games are to going off the rails into silly gonzo territory. Do: Pilgrims of the Flying Temple is a collaborative game, so I need some way for players to be able to discuss these issues without spending a bunch of time on the eye-glazing RPG theory stuff. Just telling players "Agree on a 1-10 rating for your gonzoness" wouldn't work.

So I'm actually creating a formalized Gonzometer for Do. Actually, it just packages up some old ideas in the game and gives them a catchy name. It'll be a tool for helping players decide how big or small they want the story's scope to be and how heavy or light they want the mood to be.

You can see a rough introduction to the concept on the live draft here and its actual implementation here. By using a common vocabulary for scope and mood, players can at least the beginning of creating a mutual agreement about how gonzo the story can be. \n

    \n For me, gonzo isn't pejorative, it's what Terry Gilliam does or Hunter S Thompson did. It's about going native and taking things to their fullest extreme.

    It tends to happen in games with fewer constraints on creativity, especially those in which narrative control is distributed. It can happen in Mortal Coil or Cold City, although it tends not too (unless Joshua is playing), it happens more often in octaNe or inSpectres because that's the kind of thing Jared encourages in those games. For some reason, it didn't happen in Lacuna, presumably because the tone is very strong and guides creativity more than in those other games.

    It doesn't happen in Dying Earth because the language of the game serves as a filter to constrain creativity to certain kinds of thing.

    It pretty much happened in a game of Misery Bubblegum that we played in a pub, but it was the last scene and was brought on by a player who hadn't been there for much of the game and by the fact that I hadn't handled the set-up properly. And it really didn't feel right. MB has strong creative constraints set up at the start. I pretty much think of MB as Melo.

    IMO, obviously.

    \n Oh, good idea! I love these sorts of x/y plots.

    I see the point people are making, though, about things that are big-and-light but not gonzo. The distinction might be between "light" as a mood vs. a play style \u2014 to me, the problematic "gonzo" or "silly" breakdown of collaborative storytelling comes when people stop caring about the dramatic structure of the story and the consistency of its world, in favor of easy lolz.

    So for example, the Dying Earth stories, or even "Time Bandits", are funny but still have a dramatic structure and sufficient internal consistency to keep the narrative intact. A typical episode of "Monty Python's Flying Circus" didn't have that, and often actively attacked those concepts; but Monty Python only worked because the writing and acting were so good. In the hands of everyday gamers improvising without a net, that lack of structure or consistency fall flat.

    I hate to introduce a third axis, but maybe this is one \u2014\xa0dramatic/anti-dramatic or consistent/inconsistent or something like that:

    Light + Big + Dramatic = Time Bandits, The Dying Earth, Discworld
    Light + Big + Anti-dramatic = Hitchhiker's Guide To The Galaxy, Catch-22, Gravity's Rainbow
    Heavy + Big + Dramatic = Opera
    Heavy + Big + Anti-Dramatic = 20th-century existential/surrealist/postmodern fiction (Kafka, Camus, later Delany)
    Light + Small + Dramatic = Sitcom
    Light + Small + Anti-Dramatic = Monty Python's Flying Circus
    Heavy + Small + Dramatic = Melodrama
    Heavy + Small + Anti-Dramatic = Waiting For Godot

    \nWhat does gonzo mean to you? I consider it to be a semi-negative version of "over the top", with more of a nod towards humor
    What is good about gonzo? I love games that get crazy, since they're usually pretty awesome, and it's a kind of story that other mediums besides RPGs tend to shy away from.

    When is gonzo appropriate and fun? I think it's appropriate when agreed upon ahead of time, and when it's either the focus of things, or restricted to a very limited time (in media, it's often regulated to the comic relief character). Still, done right I think it can be amazing!

    \n Daniel, you're doing something I tried with GLASS--in particular, something akin to "tone" of play--and maybe you'll come to the realization I had: tone is tricky to enforce if it's merely something established by a term (any term, never mind complex ones like "gonzo"). What I ended up doing was "baking" tone into other aspects of the game--Toggles one switches on which, in turn, prevent or allow certain abilities.

    The point being that "mechanically induced" tone comes easier, I find, than mere consensus. Maybe an example:
    * I had a "Dark" Tone as a consensus option (or, more likely, Organizer dictate). It was intended to allow for horror or "dark' genres (e.g. dark fantasy).
    * The more I looked at what MAKES those genres dark, the more I realized it had a lot to do with (a) constrained efficacy of PCs or (b) the verisimilitude of threats to life and limb.
    * So I finally stripped Tone as a concept in the game, instead making Power Level a form of tone control (i.e. efficacy, limitations on resources) and created World priority (i.e. you can die from anything that kills you when bound or unconscious) as opposed to Player Priority (i.e. you can only die if you CHOOSE to take that risk, for a temporary buff).
    :: If one wants a "dark" game, one dials down Power Level and makes it World Priority. For even darker, toggle to No Resurrect.

    And the best take-away was that I had FAR more axes for tuning general levity (usually a factor of no fear as a result of extreme confidence, at least in LARPs), "heroicness" or desperation, reward levels and types (and, thus, different levers of motivation), and other minor elements (mainly to deal with power-level disparity). Perhaps such thinking would work for you, if you can find mechanical "controls" in Do's system?

    \nStealing a boat: all too common, and not inherently 'gonzo' in your sense. Gonzo journalism could be employed in a story about this.

    Stealing a boat via airlift: reasonable for small boats, improbable but possible for large ones. Not inherently 'gonzo' in your sense. Gonzo journalism could be employed in a story about this.

    Stealing a boat via airlift using animals: impractical and implausible. Not inherently 'gonzo' in your sense, provided justification that is reasonable in-fiction, but certainly a bold and attention-grabbing stroke that must be supported. Might simply be a non sequitur.

    Stealing a boat via airlift using extinct animals: ridiculous, at best a non sequitur. This becomes properly 'gonzo' in your sense.

    Stealing a boat via airlift using extinct animals ridden by trained animals: more ridiculous. I want to leave the game.

    Stealing a boat via airlift using extinct animals ridden by trained animals with harpoon guns: the harpoon guns seem reasonable compared to the rest (you have to grab in to the boat somehow), indicating that the nonsense level has reached saturation. Additional details will not alter my perception of the scene in either direction; the Rubicon has already been crossed.

    \n Oooh! Perhaps, perhaps. Precedents, baselines, and internal consistency. This is juicy stuff I haven't touched on in the current draft yet, but, man, I really ought to.

    There is one way that players can establish precedents in the game right now. See, the setting is presented in several letters written by people asking the Pilgrims for help. Each letter comes from a different world, which have very wide variance in their respective gonzocity. Some have robots, some have ghosts, some feature mundane problems, some present epic world-spanning catastophes. That's partly because I didn't impose many constraints when I asked people for submissions, but I've got no regrets about that.

    The trick is that players choose the letters they want to answer at the beginning of the whole pilgrimage. That's a point where players can make sure the letters (and worlds) are internally consistent with each other. They can make sure they follow a theme throughout, like only choosing letters that have robots and avoiding any mysticism. (Or vice versa.)

    To answer your question specifically, Ralph, while I am reluctant to add yet another layer of setting-consensus-discussion, "Are you seeing what I'm seeing?" is a pretty damned elegant way of quickly establishing precedents up front. I could see this being a step in the beginning of each session, where the pilgrims are flying over the world and making observations about what they see before diving down into the meat of the story. Perhaps that should be its own thread.

    \n Why is gonzo undesirable in many cases?

    First, it takes us a step away from being genuine. That means it makes it harder to play with sincerity, intimately, in dangerous territory, close to home and/or realistically.

    It perverts reality. It also perverts genre*. This means that if you're playing hard-boiled noir cop drama... and you have a dude named The Mutt who has a trained army of attack dogs that he sends out as assassins to track down his rivals and kill them... not only does it distance us from reality (less genuine, less sincere, harder to play close to home), it also distances us from the essence of hard-boiled noir drama (damaging our tropes, damaging tone and coherency, making for inconsistent expectations, damaging trust).

    It's possible to use absurd/over-the-top/curveball imagery and situations and events to genuinely address themes/characters/situations/whatever. It's not gonzo to have pterodactyls in New York, necessarily. When you fail to be genuine to the fiction (and the SIS), then it hedges towards gonzo. In other words, when you prioritize the moment over the long run.



    *Ignore, for now, absurdist situations arising that concur with genre expectations and tone expectations. If people sit down to play a game with a zany dial set at 11, and they sit down to have light-hearted (possibly insincere) plotting... power to them. I am not talking about them.
