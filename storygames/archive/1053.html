However, my uncooperative subconscious appears to disagree.

Genre: Courtly romance (think Marie de France, Gawain and the Green Knight, Nicolette and Aucassin, Malory, and William Morris's The Well at the World's End - 19th-century, but to my mind completely authentic to the genre). This is medieval fantasy as the medievals knew it, without all the modern accretions.

Setting: The kind of half-imaginary Europe and Near East that figures in the above. Castles, cathedrals, forests, magical pools, knights, damsels, hermits, and so forth. You can have monsters made out of parts of other animals with no particular regard for biology (like, say, owlbears), plus heraldic beasts like wyverns, basilisks and unicorns. Dragons, too, naturally.

Situation: Episodic, like the examples mentioned. The large-scale movement is usually a quest (for the Grail, to rescue someone, to find the Well at the World's End, to show one's knightly worth when doubt has been cast thereon, etc.). The small-scale movement (scene by scene) is usually the overcoming of an obstacle, such as a monster, a knight, or a perilous castle inhabited by - well, monsters or knights, usually, come to think of it. If you're familiar with the Odyssey you'll know what I'm talking about, it seems to have been the pattern text. However, you are likely to emerge from each encounter with something which will help you in your quest.

System: I'm seriously considering Clinton Nixon's Solar System (as in The Shadow of Yesterday). I think secrets and keys would fit this extremely well.

    It looks nice so far. I, for one, would love to see some good storyguide suggestions, keys, and secrets that are wired to courtly setting. I would love to see a game that really emphasized the 'court' part of 'courtly romance' so that the quests really flowed from it, were defined by the issues of the court. That seems to be the part of medieval romance most overlooked by modern (esp. rpg) portrayals.

    I find the piety as the source of spirit and knowledge a little jarring. I don't think that is a particularly 'medieval' fusion--by the middle ages you are well into a strong distinction between faith and knowledge.

    That being said, it doesn't seem like knowledge of creation is particularly important to a medieval romance. In fact, I feel like having anything like a 'knowledge' skill would be antithetical to the atmosphere--you don't know the why and wherefore of the strange stuff that happens, it just happens. I would hate to see a player trying an 'identify hippogrif' with a roll, for example. You don't know about things, you experience them.

    Not really my intention, Isbo. In fact, forget the phrase "courtly romance" altogether, folks, this is a game of quests. Without a GM/storyguide. The "courtly" part I'm keeping for my City of Masks game, which is all about court intrigue.

    The reason I said "piety" rather than "faith" was partly because I wanted to include a little knowledge (or rather, understanding) of the universe - it had to go somewhere. You're right, the knowledge skill (Reason, in Clinton's original system) doesn't fit the setting well, but there needs to be somewhere to put the lore that hermits have (I plan on having lots of hermits) and the learning that, say, a friar would have, and Piety seemed to me to fit. It's a catchall for anything connected to how the universe functions (since, in medieval terms, theology is the "Queen of Sciences" - just as, today, science is the queen of theologies). That the functioning of the universe is mysterious is very much part of the point, though.
