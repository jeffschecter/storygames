\nLeading a Horse to Water
\n
\nMark Rosewater is the head designer of Magic the Gathering. Smart guy. And is very open about his design process. You can read more of his posts here.
\n
\nIn the above post, he discusses learning from his mistakes in designing Odyssey, one of the poorest received Magic sets in his career (from 2001). But where he grew the most as a designer. Essentially he says his biggest mistakes were:
\n
\nMistake #1 \u2013 I Designed the Set to Please Myself Rather Than the Audience
\nMistake #2 \u2013 I Tried to Force the Audience to Have Fun My Way
\nMistake #3 \u2013 I Made the Players Have to Care about Something They Didn't Want to Care About
\n
\nDesigning as a full time job for a huge success like Magic with a massively pre-established audience is different than working on self published games on your spare time (where you are your own audience in some ways) but there is probably info here that is still useful.
\n

    \n Good points. It's important to note that Mark Rosewater openly states that his priority is to make as much money as possible. His articles are written from that perspective. With that in mind:
    \n
    \n- I agree that shaping users behavior is the core of game design.
    \n
    \n- But shaping a users behavior does not have to be the same as going against human nature or making the audience care about something they don't want to care about. Although it can be.
    \n
    \n- Alexandre rightfully points out that Mark Rosewater didn't say going against human nature is wrong, just that it is harder.
    \n
    \n- There are games that probably do successfully go against human nature. How well do these games sell compared to games that don't? How many of them outsell comparable games that don't go against human nature? Is it that majority? I suspect they don't sell nearly as well. But I could be wrong. I personally prefer games that go against or challenge human nature. But I don't know if I'm in the majority.
    \n
    \n- To argue against myself, it's very possible that with a tighter design, Mark Rosewater could have made his design goals with Odyssey successful. It's possible that Odyssey was an example of simultaneously poor design and difficult goals. And that the two were not as connected as he believes. I don't know. But it's worth noting.

    \n True, I just meant that it's more of a warning that you maybe should be more careful when introducing totally left-field ideas into an existing property than if you were introducing those ideas in a new product.

    Case in point: Nintendo initially introduced the DS as a "third platform," separate from the Nintendo consoles or the Gameboy handhelds. Kind of an obvious marketing ploy, but the idea is sound. If the DS failed, then it would just join the Virtual Boy in the graveyard of out-there game platform concepts. If it succeeded, then they could advance the DS research and development, thus making it the de facto successor to the Gameboy even if that wasn't the initial intent.

    \n Mark Rosewater's article is about what he learned as a designer in light of a poorly-received set. It's about recognizing and learning from one's mistakes. Odyssey had a lot of things going against it, not just complex interactions in Limited play. It followed what was at the time, I believe, the most popular Magic set ever. It was picking up with new characters and a new setting upon the conclusion of a five-year story arc spanning the previous half-decade of releases. There weren\u2019t any elves. Odyssey had a tough row to hoe, although he notes that there a sub-set of competitive players who really enjoyed it.

    Mark goals are different from a lot of what is important to people designing games in this community. Nonetheless, there's still great advice in his articles. He's head designer of an incredibly popular game that tries to be all things to a number of different player populations. This has been a huge part of Magic's success, the fact this it appeals to people playing on Tour for tens of thousands of dollars and those playing at a kitchen table; it appeals to multiplayer aficionados and those who turn up their noses at anything other than a one-on-one duel. Understanding these different groups is something he recognizes well and talks about at length in his columns.
