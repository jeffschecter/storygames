\n I have undertaken a project to write a "subjective history of ideas" of RPGs. I would like to sketch out the great landmarks, the aesthetic trends, the developments, the big "aha!"-moments that have shaped the hobby during the course of its life, since its birth back in -74.

I'm looking for the big BANGS! as well as the subtle, gradual changes in attitude. The sudden challenging of a dogma, as well as the processes that brought that dogma into existence in the first place.

Now, a lot has happened the last 7-8 years or so, but that is just a fourth of the entire history of the hobby, and I would really like this chronological proportion to be reflected by the textual proportions in my text.

So, from your, purely subjective, point of view (remember that it's supposed to be a subjective history of ideas): what would you consider to be the landmarks of RPG history? And as previously stated, I refer to the aesthetic or ideological history, rather than, for instance, the economical \u2014 so "WotC's buying of D&D" does not qualify.

Grateful for every answer.

- Karl

    \n Hey Karl,

    For me, Dragonlance was a shot heard round the world. It changed the way gamers understood the nature of game scenarios. I'm unaware of any metaplot-driven AD&D scenario published prior to Dragonlance. Prior to Dragonlance, the characters were basically the center of the universe. And for all practical purposes, their actions were what the world reacted to. But Dragonlance was the opposite of that. It was a plot that dragged the players along for the ride.

    It was a massive publishing initiative that had the effect of unconsciously educating gamemasters that this is how a campaign is supposed to be done. Dragonlance became the way we did things. Even when we weren't playing Dragonlance, we emulated the structure. We created plots and pushed the players from event to event. Dragonlance was the template from which future campaigns were made. And it was the template from which future companies were made. I can't say I've ever played a scenario of Vampire or Werewolf that that hasn't featured significant events that were entirely pre-plotted by the GM or the published materials he was using. And what was a series of modules to TSR became a series of core books to White Wolf.

    I'd be interested in some coverage of the earlier GMing shift from open to closed worlds if you can find a good source. John Kim maybe? Or someone with a comprehensive archive of Alarums & Excursions?

    Paul

    \n Big aesthetic changes:

    White Wolf coming in with Vampire and causing a whole different bunch of games to be played. This coincided with other dark 90s trends in media like in comics.

    AD&D's "cover up the naughty bits" 2nd edition - that had an impact on what was produced and what got played especially by those who (like me) began with that edition.
