    Best parts for me:

    1. Information hiding, in big ways. Not seeing your character sheet. (The advancement list is nice too.) Being able to fake your real strength. Never knowing whether you have an alliance unless you've paid points for it.

    2. Four impossible things before breakfast. Need an army? Go get one. They think you're a god, by the way. Need an entire alternate reality? Single character point. Shadow-dweller pissing you off? Killed, turned into an ally, or wiped from existence at your whim.

    3. The only thing that can challenge you is your relatives. You can wander off into shadow for a thousand years, studying from the greatest swordfighters in a hundred shadows... and it could all fall apart the second you face someone real.

    4. To my knowledge, the first game that officially rewarded player contributions. (Note: I typically house-ruled it to five points up front, one point per game that you continue your contribution.)

    5. The ability to play on the expectations of those who have played or read Amber before. Is Bleys going to be a good guy this time, or is he going to shank me over the pattern again?

    I ran seven interconnected campaigns over the course of four years. I was a big fan.

    Well, for Amber, the part of me that wants the web to be a little more complex says that an NPC really is what they want and what they have (and perhaps what they _appear_ to want and have), and where those things intersect with players makes for rich, delicious play.

    Amber is a bit weird in that sense, and varies from a lot of indie games in that it has strongly proactive NPCs, which is a bad thing in the forgotten realms, but for Amber, where players are _inside_ the circle rather than outside looking in, it's an essential part of flavor.     PS - And to answer Joshua, CRITICAL. In the absence of the NPCs, Amber isn't much of a setting at all, literally. There are only a handful of things in the books of any import that are not people, and most of those are tied to somebody. A lot of the reason it's still around is on the strength of it's characters and the fact the one of Zelazny's greatest strengths as an author is expressing a very full character in a very short time.

    Now, you will absolutely find that some folks disagree, and many Amber games have successfully disposed of the canon's NPCs, but you'll also find that most such games have done so in such a way to take advantage of the resonance chamber their absence creates.

    It's also worth noting, the core book provides three different version of each named NPC, and almost every game I've ever played has customized them further. Zelazny created some wonderfully _essential_ character, to such an extent that drastic changes in power, motives and interests can be made without rendering the character unrecognizable. The upshot of this (combined with a mechanical system almost entirely lacking in dependancies) is that Amber is probably the most customized-yet-still-recognizable game in existence. This fact has a lot to do with why some awesome games (like Matt Snyder's 9 Worlds) which seem almost perfect for this (enthusiastic, passionate) demographic have not moved th eneedle at all.

    In short: Amber is very, very, very weird.

    Though to bring it all back to mechanical strength, this points to something that the Amber crowd views as a strength, but the Indie crowd may not.

    One character in the series, Benedict, is a badass. He's thousands of years old, all spent as a soldier, general and duellist, honing his trade. He's older than any of his brothers by millenia, and trained them all in the sword. His other borthers think he's scary enough that he could probably have unseated their father if he wanted to. He is the elder sibling who will always be better than you, and mechanically speaking, he represents the apex of Warfare, the stat which covers most sorts of conflict.

    Given that, bear in mind that the first time we see him in the books, he's lost his hand because he'd hesitated to strike down the woman he was fighting because they'd previously been intimate. A little bit later, we see Corwin (the protagonist) defeat him by taking advantage of his rage and tricking him into bad terrain. So in the context of his invincibility, we definately see some flex.

    Now, within the guidlines in the core Amber DRPG, Benedict is described as being able to do some, well, stupid things. Parry invisible attacker. Guess the range of the firearm you have concealed under your coat. Kill you with a toothpick. Throw a knife into heaven. In short, he's a mostly invincible ninja killing machine. The problem here is less in the mechanical description of what Benedict's warfare means (mechanically, it means "He's in UR Base. He's Killing UR Mans") than in the implict GM advice that goes with it: "Nothing your characters ever ever ever do can beat him, or any other elder."

    A lot of games turn down that particular dial. Benedict's history is still all the same, but he doesn't dodge bullets, kill with a stare or run arround parrying unseen assassins. He's badass, sure, probably Amber's greatest general and swordsman, but he doesn't maintain that distinction casually. A PC could challenge or possibly maybe even surpass him given time and a compelling enough story. Maybe not. Here's a bit of heresy that comes out of Amber play: You always get to be awesome, you don't always get what you want.

    Anyway, here's the fantastic thing. Drop a player from either of those two games in the other one, and Benedict is still recognizable.

    I cannot overstate how huge that is, and it's a big reason why Amber has conventions far in excess of its size as a game.

    So yeah, there's a lot of crap in it, but there's magic to it too.

    Doyce, CHECK. BIG CHECK.

    One of the first elements of character generation is to pick a face card from his deck, and hold it face down on the table. In order, around the table, the players flip their cards.

    If it is a king, the player says, "I am character name, and I will rule Amber."

    If it is a queen, the player says, "I am character name, and I will defend Amber and her rightful ruler."

    If it is a jack, the player says, "I am character name, and Amber will be destroyed."

    How many players take each option determines what form the game takes.

    For example, if there's only one king... then that person is King of Amber.

    If there are none... then the king is an NPC, and the PC's are his children.

    If there is more than one, then the king is dead or dying, and the question of inheritance is an open one.

    There is no option for the PC to have no relationship to Amber at all.

If, in fact, the setting is all about these characters, then this could be a huge problem. If, on the other hand, you can set up conflict through the Elders, or other NPCs, then it's not such a big problem. But it couldn't hurt to try to slant things so that the players are in conflict.

    I think this would both be in the spirit of Amber, and effective. No, that's not the same thing as saying that everyone is always out to screw you. In fact, what I'd advocate is the idea way up above where every relationship is it's own character sheet. No, this doesn't mean that the relationships have to be static. In fact, the way that I envision it, play would be, in fact, solely about how those relationships are changing mechanically in play.

    Consider this like it would be in Hero Quest for a moment (sorry for using my favorite example). You have all sort of relationships like "Corwin once nearly killed me" and "Corwin and I have been allies in the past" and "Corwin and I once were in love with X." Like in HQ, these abilities do not proscribe the player in playing the character. They're just there to offer incentives to play to them. But what's fun is going against these types when it's appropriate. And then mechanically altering the relationships to represent the new situation as it emerges.

    The "fruitful void" in this case is not "what is the relationship?" but "how will the relationship change, or not change, over time?"

    Now you're talking. In a world where characters have as vast powers as the Amberites do, play can't be about who is better than who at what. Benedict is defeated solely by a relationship in losing his hand in the example given above. In another case due to taunting he's bested because he can be lead to ground he's no good fighting on. In fact, the entirity of the drama is based on who is willing to do what to whom and why. Not if they can.

    Mike

    There's a concept here that has been left out. You only have a piece of the mechanic here, so of course you have concerns.

    There's also going to be a role that has a different declaration. One or more players can say, "I am the Serpent, and I am Chaos." This is the player whose job it is to create disposable NPC's, organizations, situations, etc. to oppose the PC's. He also mediates trump calls, keeps track of which NPC's know which secrets and who they've told them to, that sort of thing. He doesn't have the kind of conflict-resolution power that an ADRP GM has, but he does have much of the situation creation power.

    It's his job to push the PC's into conflict, if not with each other then with his NPC's.
