\n I had a bad experience the other day and now I\u2019ve been caught in this weird loop of shellshock. It is like going back 10 years in the way I play. All of that gamer-baggage came crashing back down as the kryptonite tumbled out of the bags. I just sat there not even knowing how to participate in the game, and what finally came out was fairly passive-aggressive, as I shut down and refused to roll dice.

What I\u2019m wondering is if there is a \u201cright\u201d way of bringing up the issues. So I turn to you random anonymous internet people. I know one can\u2019t force a particular play-style on a group/person (Mike Holmes even said so).

A little bit of background: the group in question is one of two (and a half) groups I\u2019m currently playing in, and this particular group is fairly \u201cmainstream\u201d in its play-style: the GM makes an adventure/plot and the players are expected to participate in a manner conducive to that pre-planned plot. Fair enough! I know what they want, but then I hit a wall.

I was told to roll dice again and again for the same thing. I was told to roll dice to drive down an empty road, to descend and climb stairs, to make a perception check for something that wasn\u2019t there, et cetera.

There was no \u201cSay Yes or Roll the Dice\u201d, no \u201cLet it Ride\u201d, not even any consequence for rolling bad. At best if somebody failed a roll their character looked silly (usually we were told to just rolled again though). Bwah?!

All of this on top of the obvious rail-road to get us \u201cback to the plot\u201d. Which by itself I could handle! But combined with the dice rolling weirdness my brain completely shut down. (It even affected my ability to game with the indie group later that week and they commented on it).

I thought I had left this type of dysfunctional roleplaying, that caused me such baggage, behind a decade ago.

The thing is: I like this group. The people are all fun and interesting individuals. Plus they are a heck of a lot more consistent and dependable than the indie group! But dang. It was bad. Maybe it was a fluke? Maybe the Storyteller was just having a bad day, we all do. I even got the feeling that the Storyteller knew something was wrong subconsciously, but didn\u2019t know how to correct it -- yet I also got the feeling that if I would\u2019ve brought it up I would have been smacked down, or worse, it would have been taken as an insult!

And for complete transparency: I ran a Burning Wheel game for them recently -- before \u201cthe incident\u201d as it happens (because the ST was gone, but the group still wanted to game) so I offered.

So, yeah, is there a good way to bring this up without seeming all hoity-toity story gamey?

Or should I get over it and just game with a cool bunch of people that I enjoy hanging out with once a week?

    \n Irminsul, I recognise both the situation and the feeling. I went through the same a year ago. After playing hippie games for a few years I returned to the trad-oriented group that I once had played with. They had not changed, at all.

    I was very frustrated at first. I thought the game sucked and I didn't have fun.

    I found two things that turned the tide and allowed me to have fun again.

    1. Establish stakes before rolling the dice. Before I rolled any dice I said, 'If I succeed at the roll I want this to happen...' and then negotiated with the GM for the penalty for failure, basically accepting whatever he wanted.

    2. Fast forward, if a situation bogged down I'd just ask 'Is it ok if we fast forward the story until we are through the forest, I am more excited about the stuff that awaits us.' Usually everyone was fine with that, even the GM. Trad gamers get bogged down a lot, and usually don't have the tools to get free by themselves, a gentle nudge will help.

    So I sort of imposed my playstyle on the group, but I did it while affirming the game that they already played, so no sour faces.

I can see why someone expecting more from the "Storyteller System" would inflate the number of dice rolls, waiting for that botch or critical success that might be sufficient to convince the table that something ought to happen here.

    \nRobin Laws (I believe it was him anyway), had some great advice for this. He uses a computer program to pre-roll a bunch of random rolls, and he just marks them off one by one as needed. It's an unbiased system, and any time the GM needs a roll that's disguised he doesn't need to ask at all, or roll behind the screen, he can just cross the next result off. I think it's a reasonable solution to suggest in this situation anyway as it actually enhances not knowing when something is actually happening and in general speeds things up. It might be presentable as saying "This would be more imersive for me if I don't actually have to roll dice at all and don't even know they're being rolled."     \n One could also use the die checks to see how long something took, whether other factors came into play or not. Even with a linear pass/fail system like the d20 one or GURPS checks you can have 3 regions, below a threshold the task takes longer, the product isn't as good, maybe the guards show up while you're searching the mansion, and you have to adapt. In the middle region, it takes average time or nothing significant happens, maybe it takes a little longer depending on the result. And above a given threshold you finish it faster, the product is better etc. This meshes with the GUMSHOE idea that if you're looking for the secret compartment, you find the secret compartment. Instead of the other variant which is roll a perception check to see the plot-crucial journal on the desk. If you don't then it derails the adventure and you're sitting there rolling over and over to get an action to work to move the plot along. At least the newest edition of D&D made it that it was the journal who had to beat your perception score on a check to go unnoticed otherwise you see it. The oWoD Storyteller system allows for this, instead of botches and the 1s subtract from the successes and lead to failure rule, 1s can still subtract from successes. If you have no successes and at least a 1, the action can still succeed but something happens to complicate your life, if you just have a simple failure the action succeeds but it takes longer. Normal success the action succeeds and is average result. Critical successes take far less time than base, or are of superlative quality. People can roll dice, the whiffs are less likely to be immersion breakingly illogical (you failed to see the Necronomicon under the table) and more about how long does it take? How well does it work? What other complications are there?
    \n
    \nUsing an oWoD ST roll and TNG example: the crew of the enterprise modifies the deflector dish in Best of Both Worlds to take the Borg down. The deflector fires as it was intended to but does no damage. This could be represented by the roll being what oWoD calls a botch. The characters did it right, the modifications were successful but the complication that came because of the botch was that the Borg had already adapted to it. This is one way that fail and critical misses can be interpreted without altering the mechanics of a game. 4E has this to a degree with Powers where a miss on the check still has an effect (half-damage on an attack, for example)
