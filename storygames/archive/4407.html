    \n I didn't want to rain on that other thread but... while the urge to play in established universes is strong, and we think that it would be a blast... it's just usually not good.
    \n
    \nI could go on about why I feel this way, and I have elsewhere. But the core of the problem is that you can't go back home again. By reading the book (or seeing Star Wars), you've already had that setting "play out" for you. You can't do better than the experience in the other media, in part becuase the world created for that other media was not generated for you to play characters within it that are central to the story. That's already been done by other characters.
    \n
    \nDune is classic this way. When do you play? The interesting scenario that the setting sets up has already been played out.
    \n
    \nWhat you need for good play is, essentially, a blank slate in terms of story. Imagine if you had a universe like that of Dune, and it was set up with you playing Paul and buddies... but Dune had never been written? Well that would be a great situation to play through. But, it having already been written, what's left to play?
    \n
    \nI say all of this having played a ton of Rolemaster in Middle Earth. It's not just that the system was terrible for the game (it is, but that's not the only problem). It's that I always felt like the story-goodness had already been used up by Tolkien. I was an interloper in his realm, at best living below the level of his stories, and at worst, sullying them with our additions.
    \n
    \nNot everyone will feel like I do. But I, too, went in thinking that it would be a great idea to play in the settings that I most loved to read or see in movies. And every time the same thing happened. That sense of potential did not pan out.
    \n
    \nWheras if I play in a setting designed for an RPG, almost all such designers do not make the mistake of sucking up the story. The world is presented as being on the cusp of something happening, but we don't know what. And it's up to you and the players to figure out that story.
    \n
    \nSo... just consider for a moment that this urge to play in established settings may not be a well-considered one, in all cases. Especially in a setting where there are world-changing events caused by the characters in the fiction, you may well find, as I have, that play is flat and lifeless.
    \n
    \nThis is why Jihad works, in my opinion. It's NOT Dune. It sets up situations and elements that are "Dunesque" perhaps, and that's all good. But it doesn't try to actually reside in Dune. Just as Burning Wheel doesn't actually try to reside in Middle Earth, despite it being a complete homage to that realm.
    \n
    \nJust sayin'

    \nOh dear... Before this gets out of hand I'm going to quote from the Glossary over at The Forge:

    Narrativism (Narrativist play)
    One of the three currently-recognized Creative Agendas. See Story Now.

    Story Now
    Commitment to Addressing (producing, heightening, and resolving) Premise through play itself. The epiphenomenal outcome for the Transcript from such play is almost always a story. One of the three currently-recognized Creative Agendas. As a top priority of role-playing, the defining feature of Narrativist play.

    Premise (adapted from Egri)
    A generalizable, problematic aspect of human interactions. Early in the process of creating or experiencing a story, a Premise is best understood as a proposition or perhaps an ideological challenge to the world represented by the protagonist's passions. Later in the process, resolving the conflicts of the story transforms Premise into a theme - a judgmental statement about how to act, behave, or believe. In role-playing, "protagonist" typically indicates a character mainly controlled by one person. A defining feature of Story Now.

    Hope this helps.

    \n I don't agree. It's perfectly possible under the PTA rules with no changing to only address the characters' Issues in a simulative way - in other words, "how would a TV show operating under these constraints and with this situation presented have this character address this issue in this way" or "how would this character resolve this situation and how can I describe it so it sounds badass and would make a great TV show". The latter in fact is more supported, by the fan mail mechanic. Narrativism is entirely and only about moral judgments and statments made by the players. There is no requirement in PTA for a player to make any such judgment or statement. You can play it in the same way you play GURPS or D&D and I suspect many or most do, and I have observed it many times.

    In other words, and to get this back on course a little, simulating a dramatic piece of literature in which a moral dilemma happens to appear is not narrativism unless the players make and express a moral judgment. Not the characters, the designer, the game, the genre, the format or the situation. Because a lot of genres require some degree of moral dilemmas (sometimes very strictly confined sorts, sometimes with very strictly defined resolutions), this can get foggy unless you keep very careful track of whether the player is making a moral judgment and statement, or just resolving a situation in the fiction.

    In a Gulliver's Travels-esque game, for example, there would be many ways to react as players to the allegories for the absurdities of political and social life:

    1 - The player could just have the character react as that character would react in those situations. Not narrativism: no moral statement.

    2 - The player could have the character "play along" with the allegory, feeding into the absurdity but also incorporating their character into it. Not narrativism: the character and even the narration may be making a moral statement, but it's just at the level of the genre, "in the game world", so to speak.

    3 - The player could have the character "play along" with the allegory but make it clear from their narration and description that the character is not fully aware of the allegory and in fact makes dreadful mistakes or apologizes for brilliant maneuvers, thinking they're mistakes. Narrativism: the player has made a moral statement about the allegory and the underlying reality through play of the character.

    This is why I don't think narrativist games are necessary for "playing" most of great literature. (This is also why I don't think GNS is worth much.)

    \n I'd take the time to disagree with you, JD, except that it's really off topic for this thread. But I will agree with you that narrativism isn't required for "playing" literature, as you put it. Nobody ever said it was. It's not "story" for a reason (we all shot Ron down when he tried to claim it was), it's "Story Now."
    \n
    \nI think that Pendragon doesn't "do" the Arthur thing. It specifically gives you something else to do... be a more lowly knight in the setting working his way up. Even then I'm not convinced that it works well. Playing the "little people" as proposed above is what Ron has called "Underbelly Play." And while it's viable, I think, I don't see why there's any compelling reason to use the particular backdrop if you're going to do underbelly play... what do you gain over using any other setting? My point being that these settings exist and were created to facilitate the stories of the characters created.
    \n
    \nThe fact of the nature of the existence of Middle Earth as a linguistics excercise notwithstanding. Yes, Tolkien invented the elvish first, and then created a world to put it in. But he did so because he believed that a realistic language cannot exist without a mythic underpinning which explains the evolution of the language (and we'd be fools to defy the professor). As such, Middle Earth exists only insamuch as it supports the myths that create the language, and a lot of it is left undefined. In other words, if you're not playing those myths out, the set up isn't really all that useful for you.
    \n
    \nBut all that said, I agree with the more general point that some books are more suitable than others. I think to the extent that a world is used for episodic stuff, as opposed to epic, the more likely it is to be useful. For instance I think that Hyperborea, as presented to use as somewhat of a travellogue in the Conan books makes a relatively fine place to play. Which is because many of Conan's stories are about simply visiting places, not altering them. Don't get me wrong, playing King of Aquilonia is all used up. But there are many places that are just places where you could have play occur without worrying about having to trample over the Conan themes (or simply repeat them, which is even less interesting).
    \n
    \nBut in this case, there's actually so little material involved that it's pretty much as easy to make up your own setting.
    \n
    \nHere's the key: the settings that are created in books and media are done so to support the author's needs in terms of creating situations for the characters. Which he then proceeds to use up. The Star Wars universe is so obviously this way: Lucas creates whatever he needs the moment he needs it. The universe exists solely to enable the telling of the Skywalker Saga. It sets up a situation of a burgeoning palace revolution and subsequent rebellion. This situation then gets "played out" in the movies.
    \n
    \nThe only thing "left over" from this process are the creative trappings of the setting... what the aliens look like. But... what does Greedo's home culture look like? Are there other Hutts? Or is Jabba just some sort of mutant? Is Hutt a race? Or a position? OK, I think that one of the movies made after Jedi does actually have more of Jabba's species, which I think we're to assume are also called Hutts? But that's odd. Why is Han not, then, Han the Human? Are they rare?
    \n
    \nOh, now somebody will come on and assure me that there's secondary literature in which all of this is explained well, and in which we find out that Chewbacca is female, and what her home culture is like. Know what? I've never read that. And, as such, it's not even slightly compelling to me.
    \n
    \nMoreover, there's no situation in Star Wars that is presented that needs playing out. Sure, I as GM can make one up. But making up the trappings is pretty fun... and learning about them. After all we didn't all go to Star Wars knowing what he aliens would look like. Why should we want to play in a world just because we already know what the aliens will look like? Why not be surprised instead?
    \n
    \nWhy not create those trappings the same way that Lucas does (I find it to be a fun act)? And if you have to come up with a situation yourself for the players to deal with, then what are you getting from the setting in the book?
    \n
    \nBaggage. That's all you get, from what I've seen of such play. The negatives, to me, seem to either balance out, or even outweigh the small positives. The perception that it's going to be very fun to play in these settings is proved wrong after the first couple of sessions. "Look, there's one of those Greedo-guys! Cool! Let's go shoot him like Han did!"
    \n
    \nI'd really rather just play in a setting that starts with the notion of existing primarily to support the action that's going on around the characters.
    \n
    \nLet's put it this way: my actual play in settings from books has blown chunks. Can somebody give me an example of play from a setting from a book that was good... because the setting was from the book? As opposed to being good because the GM or system came up with a way to work around the fact that the setting had been all used up?
    \n
    \nFor example, in Amber... how do you deal with the existence of the canon Amberites? I know there are actually several solutions to this. But all of them seem to come down to not being able to either actually use the setting as it was written, or having to play "underbelly" which means not getting the support of the setting to play the sorts of themes for which the setting was created.
    \n
    \nWhen I hear reports of people having fun in these settings, they always smack of them having fun *despite* the setting, not because of it. What's fun about Amber is not the details of the setting, I'd argue, but the basic premise of playing godlike beings. Nobilis, anyone? Why play in the Amber universe, when you can play in the virgin territory of the Nobilis universe? Does "the Pattern" make playing in Amber somehow compelling?
    \n
    \nWell, obviously not for me.

    \n It really is a catch-22, and to some extent, this is the advantage of working with something other than the masters. What is a question is how well it captures what other people want out of Tolkien, and how well it conveys that image to the players? Obviously, it will succeed to some extent, but those are non-trivial hurdles. That creates a pretty simple opportunity/cost question. Is the cognitive dissonance of using the setting-as-written more or less disruptive than capturing and expressing the spirit of that setting in some other way?

Elves, hobbits, rangers and wizards? We're ready to rock!)

      \n
      \nSo, as far as gaming in the realms of our favourite novel or film, is concerned...
      \n
      \nFirst of all, I agree with Mike Holmes on most points.
      \n
      \nThe setup in most stories exists to tell THAT particular story. Characters like Darth Vader and Luke were created from the get-go to tell the whole "fall and redemption" thing. Once the situation is resolved, well, it's done. If you want to tell that story again, you need someone other than Darth and Luke, and you're going to have to pretty much create all that from scratch.
      \n
      \nAs for retelling or participating in those narratives, part of what makes great stories truly mythical and/or memorable is that we get the feeling that _things could not have gone any other way_.
      \n
      \nThese factors put together mean that most existing fiction is not good fodder for roleplaying.
      \n
      \nFurthermore, there are problems you need to deal with that don't come up in "blank slate" roleplaying or story creation. When you're dealing with material everyone is emotionally attached to, there's a lot more room for error, for anger, and for disappointment. In a "blank slate" game, you can say, "Hey, Bob, wouldn't the Red Sorcerers be a better foil for your character if they were from Egypt instead of France?" And thereby you can solve a major problem, whether in the fiction, or between the players.
      \n
      \nWith a "canon setting", on the other hand, this is usually impossible. In addition, there is the dangerous illusion created by choosing such a setting: the players feel that have all agreed on something by claiming that setting, whereas in reality their conceptions of that setting may vary and clash wildly. If this leads people to handle the material less carefully or less thoughtfully than they normally would ("Would bringing in the Ring help Jane
      \naddress her Issue?"), you have another potential disaster.
      \n
      \nMaybe I saw the issue in Lord of the Rings as a loyalty thing, whereas you saw it as a World War II allegory, and Bob thought it was a Christian allegory.
      \n
      \nOverall, I think working from a "canon setting" is more dangerous or misleading than it is useful--i.e. it will generally take MORE WORK rather than less to pull it off successfully.
      \n
      \nThat said, there are a couple of interesting ways your favorite setting can enhance your game, and I think it might be interesting to look at them. (Many have been mentioned already.) Here's my contribution:
      \n
      \n--Using a familiar setting can serve as a shortcut to fleshing out a situation or setting. For defining setting: if everyone knows what kind of food will be served in a particular culture, there's no need to describe it. Likewise for situation--if there is a Harkonnen and an Atreides in your relationship map, you don't need to dig deep to find a reason for them to dislike each other. It's sort of a shortcut to setting up and justifying a dynamic situation.
      \n
      \n--You can take advantage of players' emotional connections to the material to heighten the drama of the story. Telling an Elf character that the Orc he just killed has features that look uncannily like the portrait of his grandfather has instant emotional meaning and impact. There's no need to establish background facts to make that sort of statement possible. If the Elf decides to "go into the West", everyone can draw on some of the melancholy feelings they experienced while reading LoTR. If Galadriel sacrifices Lothlorien to an Orcish army in orderto stop some greater evil, everyone at the table knows how big a deal that is. You don't need to build up to it as much.
      \n
      \nOf course, everyone takes different things from their fiction. As an Actual Play example, I have a group of friends who have been playing out a long, long Star Wars campaign. They might get angry if they heard me say this, but I don't think their game has _anything whatsoever_ to do with Star Wars, not in terms of themes, or myths, or archetypes...
      \n
      \nI mean, there's not a single Jedi in the game. But they have a great time, dealing with crime, politics, economics, and plain old adventure, all while staying away from the Star Wars story itself. However, I do not think it is a "waste" for them to be using the Star Wars setting in that way. Why not? It's a pure celebration of colour. Their characters may not be archetypal heroes dealing with themes of good, evil, and redemption. They may be more of a film noit detective/hardened mercenary/traveling circus freak. But when they shoot each other they do it with _blasters_, and afterwards they blast off into _hyperspace_. The players even put on the Star Wars soundtrack while they're playing.
      \n
      \nObviously, this works for them. I could get into it as well (and have), but only if I seriously lowered my expectations first. I have to remember I won't see any evil Emperors or attempts to corrupt the good and powerful or save the universe. Then I can play with them.
      \n
      \nFinally, I want to suggest an alternative, or twist, on the "underbelly" model. More precisely, how can one play an "underbelly"-type situation while still making the story about the characters, and possibly about the major themes of that story.
      \n
      \nThe twist is that you set up the situation in the "underbelly" style, with minor characters unmentioned in the canon, but you set up the situation so that those characters are in fact revealed to be responsible for the major event of the book or film.
      \n
      \nFor instance, consider a story that takes place on the Titanic... and reveals that the main characters, unknown to history, were actually responsible for its demise. Or perhaps in Star Wars: A New Hope, there was a plan by Darth Vader to somehow protect the Death Star's "weak spot", anticipating the rebels' tactics. But a group of unsung heroes,
      \nperhaps prisoners or sympathizers aboard the Death Star are working to make sure that countermeasure does not get installed in time for the rebel attack...
      \n
      \nThere is also a thematic equivalent to this--perhaps the outcome is not so much at stake, but the story can be retold to highlight a different theme, or to reveal a different answer. There are few examples of this, but "Ender's Shadow" by Orson Scott Card is a very interesting attempt. (Any of have any thoughts on what makes that book work... or fail?)
      \n
      \nNow, my question: how can one use this approach without running into railroad/classic "Impossible Thing" territory? Are there systems that can pull thisoff? (I use the word "system" loosely here--either a published set of rules, or
      \nperhaps just a different approach you can take with your group.)
      \n

I can deal with a character that's very similar to another, just with the serial numbers filed off, in a different setting. In fact, I think that's what the vast majority of new characters are, whether in novels, movies, or in RPGs. And that's what I'm advocating as the "safe" thing to do... play something else, neccessarily employing the tropes we're all familiar with.

      \nI'm not saying we can't steal. In fact, I do little else. Consider that my long running HQ game, both in FTF and IRC incarnations, is run using ICE's old Shadow World setting. This setting was developed from leftover setting information that the ICE crew had from developing the Middle Earth setting. To be very precise, I believe that the islands from the Iron Wind module are the same ones that are in the ICE extended map of Middle Earth in the bay in the north central region. They lost the license, but had this information copyrighted. So they just decided to plunk it down on another map.
      \n
      \nFrom that, the Shadow World setting emerges. It's neccessarily derivative of Tolkien, what with it's three types of elves that match Tolkien's three families, and it's orc cognates (lugroki use precisely the same stats as orcs). It also emulates D&D, perhaps unsurprisingly.
      \n
      \nDerivative though it may be, it's far enough removed from Middle Earth that I have no qualms whatsoever about playing in it. In fact, being removed from Middle Earth, it, of course, has it's own quirks. Like a history of high technology, and more technology than one ever sees in Middle Earth (it's interesting about how much is implied with Saruman, of course, he being allegorical for modernization and pollution, etc). And instead of one dark lord, there is a whole ring of them!
      \n
      \nYeah it's somewhat lasersharked, but that's another issue.
      \n
      \nAnd, again as I've said above, I'm not even so much against playing the game as a distinct "what if?" "What if Boromir gets the ring?" Fine, let's do that as an experiment and see where it leads. Using FASERIP Marvel, I used to do "What if the Thing fought Spiderman?" experiments all the time.
      \n
      \nWhat bugs me is the generalized notion that such games seem to tend to begin with a notion of "Let's just make up characters, and put them in Middle Earth, and I'm sure we'll get Tolkien-esque adventure." Because that's the promise that I've never seen delivered upon. What's interesting is that I'm sure I could get it if I played in the Midnight setting (using a system like HQ). I just can't get Tolkien-esque in Middle Earth, ironically enough.
      \n
      \nPaul makes some excellent additional points. I also feel like it's more work than it's worth. If I'm playing in Middle Earth, it's to do an homage, and to honor the canon, and I'm going to be feeling like I have to stick to that canon - else why use such a setting, again? If I have to stick to the canon, I have to look things up, or have it all memorized...
      \n
      \nSure, you don't have to do this, but then I'm not attracted to using Middle Earth. And the slight feeling I get that I'm messing up a masterpiece sneaks in and makes it not at all worthwhile.
      \n
      \nNo disrespect to Terry Amthor (designer of Shadow World), but his setting isn't a literary masterpiece. He shouldn't be insulted, however, he didn't make his world to be thematically complete, it's a compliment to him that the world seems attractive to play in, demanding that the players thematically complete it. And that's despite his best attempts to ruin in by the inclusion of a rather detailed metaplot (including, I should add, fiction that he writes about the setting; I try not to read it, and I wish he'd stop writing it).
      \n
      \nThink of it this way... let's say you came to a game, and you were handed a character, and then told that the character had been played for five years in that game, and that he had this glorious history that had ended with him becoming king. Looking at the sheet, it screams "And he lived happily ever after." Would you want to play that character, or make up a new one, one of your own with thematic potential that you created him with, his story untold? Well, I, for one, would prefer a new character to play, please.
      \n
      \nSettings are just very complex characters, from my POV. Their story either stands ready to be played out, or it already has been. I'd prefer the fresh setting, the one that I haven't seen the end of.
      \n
      \nNote that this fits well into my theory of why it's so dread dull to hear about another player's PC. "And then we went and slew Loki, because he was threatening to overrun the world with evil." I understand why people want to relate these events... to them they are of great mythic significance. But it's the relation of the events in the crucible of play that makes them significant. If I was not there for that creation, the events mean nothing to me.
      \n
      \nTabula Rasa. I want a clean slate. This is why I've developed the pure "other world" method of play, where the setting is created during play, and only during play. I'm not against having references, but I find that we all already do have references that we can rely upon. We have Middle Earth in common, so when I say, "A hulking Troll walks into the room" I really don't have to describe it much.
      \n
      \nGreg Stafford has developed a saying, "Your Glorantha Will Vary" or YGWV. It was, "Your Glorantha May Vary" YGMV, but he found that wasn't potent enough. The notion he's trying to get across is that in playing in Glorantha (be it with HQ or RQ or whatever system), you neccessarily alter it from his vision of Glorantha. He's saying that, not only is it permissible, but, in fact, it's unavoidable.
      \n
      \nThe problem with the phrase is that it's largely used with the subtext, "I can't modify Glorantha to fit your vision, so any answer I give to your question pertains only to Glorantha as I see it, and you'll have to modify the answer to fit any alterations that you've made to Glorantha in play." At a certain point it becomes more burdensome to pay any attention to what Glorantha is like, than it is to just make up your own answer every time.
      \n
      \nIn which case, why bother Greg for an answer? That is, this is the point at which I decide that I'm not going to use Glorantha, because I can avoid the temptation to ask Greg for a solution by having a setting in which only I know any of the answers.
      \n
      \nYeah, people think I'm crazy for this, too.
      \n
      \nLet's put this perspective on it... RPGs are a creative activity. Is it so crazy to want to make up my own worlds and play in them? Instead of playing in worn out ones? When we all agree that the creative act of making up the world is, itself, fun? I'm not saying that playing in Middle Earth isn't creative, there's still the question of the particular story created. I'm just saying that I'd prefer not to be constrained by the urge to conform to a pre-existing setting. And to the extent that I do not cherish a setting, it's easier to resist that urge.
