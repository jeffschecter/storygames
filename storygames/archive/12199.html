    \n Neat little mini-game. Two questions:

    1) If you gang up and your total equals the other die, do you then still have to do a roll-off? Or does the ganging-up player have the advantage?
    2) You're only allowed to re-roll if you don't have a single die that can take out any of your opponent's dice. Can you still do a re-roll even if a ganging up attack is possible?

    And one comment:
    When you have a tie, you need to bring in two external dice to do the roll-off. That's a little sloppy. Could you just use the combatting dice themselves for the roll-off, so there's a chance of the victor's die ending up more powerful than when it started? It adds a little bonus for the increased risk of initiating a non-sure-thing combat. (A bit like a tie in the card game War.)

    \n So, I played it about 10 times with my brother.


    There was an emergent strategy.

    Gang up with your lowest dice that aren't ones (because ones disappear), and take out their highest numbers first.
    Don't attack with your big cannons until its a game winning move to do so.

    So, if you have 6, 4, 4, 3, 2, 1...
    The best strategy is to Gang Up with your 4's and your 3 and your 2 (using your smallest non-1s to take out their biggest dice), each turn.
    Ideally ending up with a 6 and a couple of 1s (which you then re-roll).

    The Gang Up With Your Lowest Dice That Won't Vanish After Attack strategy leaves your opponent with few options (spend the rest of his dice taking down your 6, re-roll, or take out a tiny die each turn).

    I'm not explaining well. I suck at probability stuff. But it became clear to both of us quickly that this was the best thing to do in a given situation.

    \n So, after reading the suggestions and trying them out in play here is the revised version. [Download PDF]

    I made some significant changes, but over-all I made it more simple and (I think) more fun.

    Here are some of the changes:

    - Changed Fair Fight to balthazar's suggested method (thanks for that).
    Fair Fights are now a combination of a Re-roll and a regular Attack, with the risk of losing a die and giving your opponent a free Re-roll.

    - Eliminated Ganging Up (which brought with it a lot of complexity)
    It was a tough choice but it focuses the game on one-on-one fights and forces more Fair Fights. It produced some really exciting game endings once I implemented the rule.

    - Reworded a few things to improve clarity

    I'm thinking about trying out some optional rules that I can include in a later version.

    \n Hey, call me Dave. I keep meaning to change that officially.

    I like the changes, although I think that taking out the Ganging Up does eliminate a whole bunch of strategy. I'd have to play the game a bit to be sure.

    Reading over the rules this time, I noticed something that might have been present before. By the letter of the rules, you're allowed to launch a losing attack, and I think it might sometimes be to your benefit.

    For example, suppose that I have a 5 and a 1, and you have a 6. It's my turn. I can attack your 6 with my 1. The 1 is removed, but your 6 gets reduced to a 5, since it's the winning die. This gives me a 50% chance of winning the game, whereas if I chose to reroll the 1 instead the best I could do is get a 6, which still doesn't guarantee me a win. It might not come up that often, but it's valid.

    In order to do this, though, you have to read the second paragraph of your "re-roll" section as advice, rather than a rule. I'm not sure if you want to make that explicit or not.

    By the way, if you've ever played "Button Men" from Cheapass Games, it resembles this a lot. But Button Men is more complex - you use different polyhedral dice, there are special attacks, and there's definitely a ganging-up mechanism, though I don't remember the specifics.

    \n I just "solo playtested" the version I suggested above, and it seems like fun.

    My variation is to add a simple "map": one "home zone" for each army/team, and two "no man's lands" in the middle, so you can pass through either one in order to get to the opponent's home zone--like a 2 x 2 square, with no diagonal movement. All your dice start in your home zone. You lose the game if your home zone is occupied by enemy dice (and none of your own) for two turns in a row.

    On your turn, you can:

    * Reroll a die
    * Attack an enemy's die (via Attack, Fair Fight, or Ganging Up). You may use dice already in the enemy's zone, or move the attacking dice into an adjacent zone as part of the Attack.
    * Move any number of dice from one zone to an adjacent zone, but only if there are no enemy dice there.

    My version's not all that playtested just yet! Just brainstorming. I think there might be an advantage (like a free reroll) to retreating into your home zone.

    \n Just for illustration:

    You have a 5, a 4, and a 1 remaining.

    I have a single 6.

    It's your move.

    Normally, all you could do would be to reroll, while my 6 eats up your 5, and then the following turn eats up your 4.

    With my version of the Ganging Up, you have two other options:

    1. You can Gang Up on my 6 with your 5 and your 1. You don't risk your remaining four this way, but your odds of winning are 50/50, and you're risking two dice.
    2. OR you can Gang Up on my 6 with your 5 and your 4. Now you're risking pretty much your whole army, but your odds of winning are much higher (because of that 4).

    I find that adds an interesting dimension: you can gamble or you can go for the "safe" move.

    Posted By: ZartesPersonally, I'm seeing it as finally being a way to make Risus combat tactical *grin*
    \nNice idea!

    Here's how I would do it: put some of the decisions in the defender's hands. So, using the second version of the rules:

    On your turn, you can Feint/Step Back or Attack.

    * If you Feint or Step Back, say how and reroll one die of your choice.
    * If you Attack, push forward one die. Any die that is smaller than all your opponent's dice cannot be used to attack.

    The defender then must decide to Take the Blow, Block, or Challenge.

    * To Block, the defender discards one die that is one pip smaller than the attacking die. The attacking die loses one pip.
    * To Take the Blow, the defender discards one die that is smaller than the attacking die by two or more. The difference between the two dice becomes damage to the defender; the attacking die loses one pip.
    * To Challenge, the defender pushes forward one die of equal size. Reroll both dice; the smaller of the two is discarded.

    At the end, the winner's remaining dice are also damage to the loser, if desired.

    That's Risus combat for you, Dogs-style. I think it could be fun.
