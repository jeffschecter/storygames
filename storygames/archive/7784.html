\n So we all know it's important to know your players and know what gets them going, right?

Let's say I'm gaming with a bunch of new people. I've never met them before in my life. Let's say I'm GMing them at a con. I've got four hours to give them an unforgettable gaming experience. Maybe they're new to gaming, maybe they're new to story games. They're not familiar with theory and such stuff, and not used to communicating their preferences. Let's say we're playing a game that can do many different things, like PTA or maybe IaWA.

What do I do? What info about these people do I need to know to give them something they'll enjoy? And how do I get that info in the first half hour of play? I'm looking not so much for general advice (though if you have some, shoot), but more for specific things I can throw at them. "Do X. Does anyone of them react by doing Y? In that case, that player is into Z, and you should make sure to do, uh, Q, during the session."

Of course, it'll be generalizations, but there's got to be some good play advice to squeeze out of this subject.

    \n Simon, when I play Lacuna with strangers, I generally give them a photo of the guy they need to hunt ("Here's your target"), start them off in a hotel, then say "What do you do?".

    Some players will hack into computers; some will interview people; some will requisition weapons. I get a good idea, from that, of what sort of game they want. Then I give it to them.

    \n Warm ups appropriate to the event:

    * If it is a crazy zombie-game, then get people to run around and be zombiefied
    * If it is a Shakespearean game, then set scenes where people can act out classical scenes
    * If it is a story telling game, then do association on the theme or have everybody share stories about e.g. how they got to the convention
    * If it is a horror game, don't socialise, exploit the uneasiness of people not knowing each other
    * If there is rich setting material, ask people to contribute facts
    * If content or form can break personal limits, give them an explicit rule to say no/stop - and then fire up the engines

    \n Hi Simon,

    For me, I do two things.

    First, as mentioned above: I start off by greeting each player with a handshake, exchanging names, getting everyone to introduce themselves. It seems like a simple thing, but I think it sets an important socializing tone for the next few hours of play. And it's amazing to me how many people show up at a convention table and simple start with character sheets. Hello! We're going to be interacting with each other for hours! Even the guy who tests you at the DMV introduces himself!

    Second, I use games where what the player is interested in will end up on the character sheet. Primetime Adventures, HeroQuest, Pendragon, Sorcerer or Sorcerer & Sword... I do character creation at the table for this very reason (no pre-gens), walking the Players through the opportunity to create character with issues or abilities or traits or Kickers that interest them. When you write, "....and not used to communicating their preferences" all I can think is, "Who cares about that? Let them just write it down!"

    Now, sometimes people spit out cliches or whatnot... and I'll call them on it. I'll say out loud, "No. What interests you?" I pay attention to when people are really getting engaged. It's not that hard to tell when someone is thinking through ideas that engage them. They tend to perk up!

    Listen. You're not going to get to know anybody quickly. You can't. But you can -- depending what game you're using -- get data on what they'd like to spend the next few hours focusing on as everyone creates a tale. And then, when they write those things down, use them!

    You won't hit every cylinder of the person's Fun Engine. But got knows you'll be hitting a few. It's a convention game. You'll be doing better than average on delivering the person fun.

    Don't worry about tossing out pop quizzes is my view. Just let them tell you want they want to focus on for a few hours. And then do it.
