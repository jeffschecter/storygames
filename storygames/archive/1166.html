Game as Ritual

MikeRM
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Story Games
This is a child of (at least) three threads, which lead to its three questions.

1. An old thread about alternative possible origins of RPGs - what if RPGs had arisen, not from miniatures wargaming, but from therapeutic roleplaying, or improv theatre, or... So this is question 1: What might RPGs look like if they had arisen from religious ritual?

(After all, there is a lot of roleplaying in religious ritual - priests roleplaying the gods, and so forth. In fact, this is one of the reasons the Catholic powers-that-be give for not allowing women priests: The priest represents Christ, who is male. The College of Cardinals obviously need to play more RPGs.)

2. Luke's "System as Trance" thread. I can't find it in the thread, but somehow connected with that thread in my mind is a suggestion from someone of using an RPG as a coming-of-age ritual. That would so rock. In fact, my ignorance of the details of the two games, and therefore the reasons that it isn't possible, leads me to propose: The Prince's Kingdom played in a different way, where when a prince reaches age 13 or whatever it is he/she doesn't retire from the game but transitions into using full Dogs in the Vineyard rules.

So, question 2: How could we use the tools of RPGs, and RPGs themselves, to act as effective rituals - by which I mean experiences which enable the participants' sense of selfhood to shift, or be reinforced, in a significant manner?

3. The Opening/Closing Ritual Roundup thread, which gives me question 3: Can anyone report instances where this has already happened?

Feel free to answer any or all of the questions in your posts.

    This particular iteration of the games-from-alternate-sources kind of makes my skin crawl. If you were to create such a game "for kicks" and all, you'd need to construct or adopt an entire dogmatic background from which to build the ritual/game, and build the ritual/game to reinforce that dogma. This sounds disturbingly similar to the disturbing GMs who believe they're "teaching" their players things through the game. Roleplaying is an incredible and powerful pedagogical tool. This is straying very close to dangerous territory all in the name of idle speculation.

    I can see two ways to sidestep the spookiness, here: (a) adopt a totally alien dogma from which to work -- say, Zoroastrianism -- in order to experiment and see what you come up with; the alien nature of the dogma would be part of the exploratory nature of the game. Alternately, you could (b) frame the dogmatic ritual as something that other people do and the players happen upon it -- I can imagine a very viable frame of Victorian-era explorers "experimenting" with the traditions of some far-flung tribe they encounter in an expedition. This set up allows the players some distance from the ritual-game-experience, and an easy "escape hatch" out if necessary.

    In one last fit of clarification, this is exactly what Dogs in the Vineyard looks like but isn't. When you first pitch Dogs to people, they look at you funny because they don't understand why you'd want to be a paladin representative of a misogynistic, oppressive society. The thing is, in Actual Play, it's more about issues and questions that are important to you the player. In this ritual-game thing we're talking about here, though, there is no such caveat -- play of such a ritual-game would reinforce the dogma. And that's spooky.

    Q. 1.
    First off, I don't have a problem with religious roleplaying--I rarely find it oppressive and controlling. It is, however, designed to work for people who have deep affective connections to the symbolic network deployed in the ritual. If you don't have those connections, well, the ritual tends to fall flat. Or, as I recently experienced, the ritual only has flashes of meaning for you.

    That being said, I think there is some whackiness in talk about roleplaying *games* emerging from those traditions. For the very reason that those involved in a religious ritual will have deep affective commitments, the notion of a game is antithetical to it. At least, game as we use it in a colloquial, let's have some fun sense. And a roleplaying ritual that falls far enough away from the core to become a roleplaying game, well, is actually counter-religious, undermining the uniqueness of the religious setting.

    It is, if you will, a counter-movement to organized religious structures. I sort of imagine this *what if* scenario with scary questions of heresy, schism, and so on. Sort of a hardcore protestantism (in the classic, you don't need clergy to tell you what God wants--you can figure that out yourself). Of course, no dice, no regalia--all about shared guided visualization, perhaps with a touch of the 'Midrash' style mode of interpretation (what happened between these two stories? Let's meditate upon it, brother--you tell me what you think he did next...). In turn, with a still more radical branch pushing toward a right-proper enlightenment game, where the ritual devolves into theater...oh, wait, this actually sounds a little familiar. Nietzsche's Birth of Tragedy, anyone?

    Dude, Snakes and Ladders. Fun (for kids), but highly moral and pedagogical.

    But I find Isbo's other point to be intriguing, and I'll spin it out further to where I'm standing. It goes like this: religion is a system by which common answers are distributed amongst a diverse population. Everybody wants to know why bad things happen in the world (the Problem of Evil), and so religion gives its believers an answer -- in fact, as far as the religion is concerned, it gives its believers the answer. Roleplaying, by contrast, is exploratory and innovative. It does not give players an answer, it challenges players to create an answer. As such, it seems to me that the foundation of roleplaying is categorically at odds with the goals of religion.

    So while I think game-as-ritual is a very intriguing thing to explore (and I think we already do it more than we think, and are deeper in it than we think), overlaying religious overtones to it or postulating religious pedigree turns the whole thing inside-out in ways that destroy the roleplaying in the name of reinventing it.
