    \n I've hosted a murder or two over the years (and the police hasn't caught me yet). It's fairly easy, really, even with non-roleplayers. Simply don't tell them it's actually roleplaying, just a party-game, and they tend to lose their preconceptions about orcs, elves and badly smelling teens.
    \n
    \nIf I were to give you some advice, it would be to give everyone their roles before the event so they're in character as soon as they step in the door, and also to cook a nicelooking, tasty and thematically suitable meal. This also means cooking something that'll fit with the costumes, such as avoiding food to be eaten with your hands if the ladies wear long gloves as part of the theme and such.
    \n
    \nAlso, a spot of wine helps loosen tension and inhibitions the beginning and makes for a more immersive game. And no, don't get everyone roaring drunk, that's not what I mean. Just a glass or two.

    \n I'll concur with those on the "blah" side--most of the commercial LARPs like that are "find someone whse name is on my card; read my card to that someone as they read their card to me." Meh.

    Mike Young has a number of single-session LARPs that are actually grabby and not railroads:
    http://www.interactivitiesink.com/larps/index.html

    They might take a TAD more prep and some care and feeding of your players to get them up to speed on mechanics. But at least they are "real LARPs" and not walk-throughs.

    If you want something less fantastic for your non-gamers, I'd seriously consider a Jeepform game:
    http://jeepen.org/games/

    VERY rules light (I like the term "rules contemptuous") and touching on serious, non-fantastical situations. Read carefully, though--some are a TAD too heavy or steamy for a casual game.

    \n I think the biggest obstacle that you're likely to run into with your gamer friends is that there's really no way around the hand-holding that these sets enforce. Typically one of the players ends up being the murderer, but they're not informed of that fact until the last round of play, when everyone opens their last envelope. So there's no planning ahead, or scheming or anything of that sort - you just follow along and are pleasantly surprised at the end to find out that you did it. This can be a little off-putting to gamers, in my experience.

    That doesn't mean they can't be fun, though. It's sort of midway between playing a roleplaying game and getting people together to read the script of a radio play.

    I'll also second the recommendation for the Interactivities Ink LARPs that David mentioned, if you're looking for something different.

    \n Give them full disclosure before they even arrive. Give them each pieces of the puzzle and roles to play. Do a one-on-one with each player where they can ask about their character. See to it that the characters have interpersonal relationships, and a reason to meet. Give them a damn good reason to keep secrets from each other.
    \n
    \nThose envelopes simply mean that people are basically playing a jigsaw puzzle. Extremely boring.
    \n
    \nAlso, who says there even needs to be a murder? It could be "Who really stole the money", or "Who had an affair", who's the embezzler in the family firm and so on.
    \n
    \nJust give them a character to play and all the knowledge that character possesses, same as you would in an intrigue scenario, because, let's face it, that's all it is. You can be the moderator and play at the same time, just make the roles easy to distinguish, such as wearing a hat when you're the gm.
    \nIt's easy, there's plenty of scenarios online to use, and people won't even know they're really LARP'ing because there's not a rubber sword in sight.

    \n I have played a couple (with regular gamers and non gamers), and I had a blast. People really got into their roles, and cared about achieving their objectives.

    My best experience was when a player gutted information out of me simply by using his flair and some efficient psychological manipulation. As I was the murderer, it was in a tough spot, but in the end I managed to save the situation by turning the rest of the audience against him so that he could not use his information. Excellent duels of wits and roleplay, totally unencumbered by rules and logistics, and this years before the development of "indie" games.

    I also had some of my best roleplay moments in these kind of evenings, which is surprising considering how little preparation they required compared to regular tabletop games (although with some indie games, the situation has improved in terms if of reduced workload, not in roleplay).
