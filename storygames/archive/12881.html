\n This is a hypothesis that came to me while I was thinking about how I strip down systems when playing with new players. What defines a gamer (to me) is the willingness to spend time to get to the fun, be it 3 hour character creation, 2 weeks of studying the rule book to kind of understand it, and regular interuptions of play while the rules are consulted when something is unclear. Eventually we might not put up with that, but certainly to start with, when the available systems were AD&D, Rifts and GURPS, it's something we did, even if it wasn't happily, 'cause if we didn't, we wouldn't be gamers. Things like schedules and times throw a wrench into that, pushing people who were normally willing to spend the time towards games that only require the time investment of playing.

Now non-gamers won't do that. If character creation takes long and gets tedious, they'll just quit saying it's not fun. If they have to read chapters of rules (or even pages), they'll get fed up with it. At that barrier to entry, they'll just give up. They're perfectly happy to play if they don't have to worry about the complicated stuff, but they won't put the time investment in.

Not terribly useful hypothesis by itself, but I've lately been running games for people who mostly just play computer games, and computer games let you start playing right away, and learn as you go (as the computer knows the rules), so I've been taking a system I really like (ORE), and stripping it down to the point that there's really no sense in using ORE. Also, the aspect that seems perfectly natural to me (sacrifice a die to make an extra attack) isn't intuitive at all to them. It seems like the finely crafted system of ORE just goes to waste.

So, do you agree with this? That what sets a gamer apart is the willingness to slog through the unfun to get to the fun?

    \n Strong disagree here: if something is actively Not Fun, I don't do it, because there are other ways of getting to things that are just as much fun.     \n For many people who play such games, fiddly character creation is a fun subsystem in its own right. There are definitely people who will try building different Burning Wheel or Champions or D&D3.5 or D&D4 characters for the fun of it, rather than for a specific upcoming game. In much the same way, deck-building for collectible card games like Magic: The Gathering is often considered a fun activity in itself, rather than something that has to be gotten through in order to get to the fun of play.

    While there are often templates or pregens offered for quick start, I think viewing fiddly character creation as "un-fun" grunt work is seriously missing the point. While there are some players who enjoy, say, using only premade M:tG decks - I suspect that most enjoy putting together decks and consider that strategy step as part of the fun of the game. I think the same goes for Champions players and many others. These games are designed for and appreciated by those who enjoy fiddling with system stuff.

    Computer games are quite different from tabletop RPGs, because you can play at any time. Tabletop RPGs and collectible card games are often considered part of the "hobby games" market - because they have a component that you can do while not actively playing. (I suspect that for some games, online discussion fills this aspect of the game.)

    \nYou're making an assumption here that there's pretty much only one type of gamer. In fact, some gamers love things like character creation and optimization, while others loathe it and only play games that allow them to get into the action immediately. The key is knowing what you like, and what the people around you like, such that you can find a way to engage everybody.

    The D&D 4th Edition Dungeon Masters Guide has a whole section near the beginning that details a variety of different types of gamers: what they like, how to give them what the need, and how to avoid certain problems that might crop up around them. I'd recommend giving it a read-through.

    \n I guess this means that despite 20 years of playing and running RPGs, I would never have qualified as a gamer. Interesting.

    I think it's difficult to simply slap a label on folks. Those that self-identify as "gamers" do so because they feel that they share values with others that identify as "gamers". While there's wriggle room, on the whole the group helps to define what the term represents. It may or may not adopt some/all of the definitions that might be applied to the group by those that are outside it. Much of the definition tends to be implicit (approved behaviours, popular topics/opinions, etc) rather than explicit (if you don't D&D youre not a real gamer).

    I for example don't self-identify as a gamer. Primarily because I feel I'm out of step with the majority of "gamers" and their values. I don't self-identify as some sort of "story-gamer" either. Again, I don't feel I'm especially in line with what appears to be group values.

    This doesn't mean that someone won't slap a label on me. Folks are always labelling others. Just that I don't voluntarily label myself in that fashion.

    Also, a while back on rpg.net there was a post about "15 minutes of fun in 4 hours", I think specifically in relation to D&D. Most of the people that hang at rpg.net consider themselves gamers, but there was an awful lot of angst and whatnot about that whole topic.

    So I'm going to disagree and say that while you might use that particular yardstick for yourself and how you evaluate others, not many others will agree with that measurement.
