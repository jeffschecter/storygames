    \n (x-posted with Andy)

    In response to Shreyas' question about "What can people do to improve this?"...


    One thing that I noticed was working was when people made "I statements" about their own stance. For example, "I don't feel like a racist when..." came up several times.

    And one response to those "I statements" that I saw working really well was "Well, have you considered....?" and "Try thinking about the situation in terms of...", etc. Some people have a really good habit of engaging a viewpoint, instead of shooting it down.

    Other people tended to say "No, actually..." and that's where shit stopped moving forward.

    So, some things that can work well:

    1. If you're on an opinion thread, or a thread about a sensitive topic, make your statements about you. Use "I statements". Sometimes this means radically recasting your argument/contribution, whereas sometimes it simply means prefacing what you say with "In my experience..."

    2. Instead of discrediting someone's viewpoint, engage it. Seek to show people an alternate viewpoint as a first priority, as opposed to shutting their viewpoint down as a first priority.

    \n One thing I realized here in a Whisper to someone (talking more about the philosophy/sociology degree undergrad experience). A lame parable, maybe, but it may help shed light on what I'm seeing:

    -----
    In my circles (we were the nietzsche - wittgenstein (later) - rorty - reading pragmatists) there were folks in the philosophy department (like the "Christian Morales Uber Alles neo-platanist" guy, or the "I LOVE BUSINESS AND CAPITALISM! CAPITALISM IS NEVER WRONG!" philosophy/business major enthusiast dude, or the "I don't think much about anything, but will destroy any argument you make for the fun of it" chick) that I was totally at odds with. Like, "holy shit I can't believe I'm not wringing this guy's neck", whether we were talking about gender issues, business ethics, religion, society issues, or hippie-postmodern sociological garbage. It was literally amazing that there weren't fistfights. But I think part of it was that we forced ourselves to at least get to know the other people a little more.

    Basically, somewhere along the line (I dunno how it happened. By accident, probably) we forced ourselves to have relationships with the other people in our classes, including (especially) the ones that we 'hated'. After a while, we noticed some good and bad things:

    The good: you realize you respect your 'enemy' more than you thought, and they respect you. Your ideas rub off on each other, and sometimes you see things in a new light. The more closed-off you are the longer it takes, but it eventually happens.

    The bad: it's harder to hate them and cast their ideas aside as rubbish, if you know them by first name and have gone out for drinks with them. Plus, if you wanted your pristine ideas to stay untainted by self-doubt, starting a relationship (ie "aquaintence") with that person is the wrong way to do it. Ostracism is easier than Empathy.

    It seems that for a lot of ugly interactions here (or on TEH INTERNETS), most people involved just want to do the latter (the things associated with "the bad": Keep their ideas, not start a conversation, not defend their ideas, stay untouched by self-doubt in their righteousness, and WANT to have a reason to hate someone). They don't spend the time investing in a relationship to get the good benefits.

    Hey, it's The Internet, so there's no reason to start a relationship with every person you disagree with, or stay in a community surrounded by people who you can't stand (or vice versa); you can always bounce to another peer community where everyone is on the same page. But if you find a community that you want to interact with, and think that there's enough of a positive quotient that you could get some benefits over time, investing that time into relationships (mostly: Just by communication, dropping posts; but specifically by Being Nice, Enhancing Empathy, Not dismissing another viewpoint, etc) pays off for all parties, according to my little lame parable's "the good" list.

    Again, just a parable, not a Way of Living or anything.

    -Andy

    \n Please note that the following is only how I deal with those rather heated and sensitive threads. It's not meant to be an authoritative method.

    Most times I simply keep my mouth shut and don't bother to post. That's not my first reaction. My first reaction is to sit down and type out my argument and viewpoint, burying everyone under my vast intellect and superior reasoning. Then I do what Alexandr suggested. I stop and step back. Once I've done this I generally come to the following conclusions:

    1.) The heated discussion is happening due to a clash of core value systems.
    2.) No one is likely to change my core values on this discussion forum.
    3.) I'm not likely to change anyone else's core values on this discussion forum.

    So, most of the time I just decide that it isn't worth stirring the pot any more than it is already stirred. I'll just get upset and irritated. I'll make other people upset and irritated. Most of the time for no good reason. Why go there?

    Every once in a while I feel like there is something I can add to that kind of discussion that isn't going to stir things up too much and might contribute more than just bad feelings. In that case, I post.

    \n On the racism thread, because it seems to be the hot button of the moment:

    Sure, it sucked. But its a fucking thread about racism. On the internet. Its going to suck.

    There was some dumb shit pulled. Once again, thread about racism, internet.

    However, in general, I found that thread to be less offensive, stupid, and rhetorically dodgy than a lot of similar threads, including ones on this very board. Yes things got hot, but people also did manage to have some actually communication going on. I was actually enjoying the back and forth with like Tad and Jasper and some others, because there are a lot of subtle points around the core issue that are well worth talking about.

    The only unfortunate thing that happened, in context of my POV given thread about racism|internet, is that we kind of got to a point where sides were drawn up. Which is unfortunate, because I'm willing to bet that no one on any given "side" fully agrees in every detail with every member of their "side." I know that Jonathan and I have different opinions about several issues of racism, but we never got a chance to really talk about them because it did start to feel like any crack in the damn would let loose a flood. I'm sure others felt the same -- if I admit that Chris may have a point on 'this', then Brand is going to smack me about 'that', and while 'this' may be true I still don't think 'that' is.

    Honestly, there isn't a whole lot to be done about this without changing a bunch of deeper order communications issues in the way we all talk to each other. Polite words and I statements can help, but they won't change the fundamental paradigmatic disconnects and they often can become shields for dodgy rhetoric themselves. But, in the spirit of looking like I'm trying to help at least, let me suggest the following:

    1. Always respond to a specific person and what they actually said. Quote when needed. There is a real problem in theory threads (be it game theory or social theory) with people responding to what "they said" or even "what I thought they said" rather than to what a single person actually said. As soon as we start responding to "them" rather than "that jerk Brand" we have drawn up sides. Once we have sides, we have problems.

    2. Always respond to the points people are making. If you are not sure what points they are making, ask for clarification rather than assuming. It is far too easy on the intertron to assume that people are saying things just like the demons in your head, the jerks at the FLGS, the twits at your highschool, or whatever would say. Sometimes its even true. More often its false. Quoting, as mentioned above, can help with this. Asking questions rather than making rejecting responses can also help.

    3. Accept the fact that people are going to say stupid shit. Know that just because one person says it doesn't mean that the conversation is over, either with them or more importantly with the other people in the thread. No amount of I statements or careful phrasing are going to stop us from pissing each other off sometimes. When pissed you can strike back, flame out, and effectively end the communication, or you can just accept that such things happen but are not the end of the world, and try again later.

    4. Spin off. When you aren't getting the answers you want in a thread, or when you're starting to run hard down a tangent, spin off a new thread. Seriously, taking a conversation to a new place where there isn't already a weight of 150 posts behind it full of unexamined assumptions, people who've only read the first 25 posts and are now responding to something that has already been covered 15 times, and a lot of invested emotion, can really help to get things focused on what you need them focused on.

    5. Bitch, but bitch quietly. Andy's gonna hate my ass for this, but the truth is that venting is a normal part of human interactions. When you're starting to get ready to head-butt someone in the face, stop posting, find someone on IRC or IM and have a little bitch fest. Or make Andy's day and whisper the rage. Don't do this to work yourself up into a higher state of righteous indignation though. Use it as a chance for catharsis. Clear the system, then come back when you feel you can be rational.

    6. Take it slower. This one is my personal issue, and judging by how fast hot button threads grow around here I'm betting I'm not the only one. A thread hitting 150 posts in a day is really not a good thing for communications, nor for the community being able to actually use and absorb the material there in. Someone logged on this morning and saw the racism thread and was like "the fuck? how did that get so big so fast? No way I'm going in there, there's no point -- its either all hate or I'm so far behind that I'll never catch up." There is a good chance a lot of people said that, and that a lot of those people would have had good things to contribute. But we all had to have our say as fast as possible, we had to respond now, and now, and NOW, and so the thread got long fast with everyone reacting in the moment rather than building a conversation.

    7. Listen to what people tell you about your communication style. If someone you are arguing with tells you that you are being an ass, you may be being an ass. If someone you like who is on "your side" tells you that you are being an ass, you are probably being an ass. When someone from outside the conversation whispers you to tell you that you are being an ass, you are being an ass.

    8. Do not talk about Storygames.

    9. Do Not Talk About Storygames.

    10. DO NOT TALK ABOUT STORYGAMES.

    There. All of those suggestions are based in communications theory at one level or another. They may help some of us. They may help all of us. Most likely they won't help at all, because things don't change until enough of the community actually wants them to change rather than just wanting others to change.
