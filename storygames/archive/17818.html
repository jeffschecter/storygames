\n I find that different games make me act very differently as a GM - and some games I just can't seem to run without really wanting to turn the screws tighter on the characters ALL the time.

Sorcerer is probably the best example. It's as if all the tools I'm given just make it so easy for me to make things worse and harder for the characters. Sometimes it seems every single choice they make will be between two evils. And the ideas I come up with on the fly tend to make their lives more difficult, always.

How about you? Do you have a sadist in you? How does that work out during play?

    \n My sadistic side, if you may say so, is based on two questions:
    • How will they fail?
      • \n
    • How can I threaten it?
    \nThe two questions can be summarized into "How can I fuck it up [for the others]?", but I feel that the two questions gives better guidelines. It's strange, but the more invincible the characters are, the more sadistic I can be. In This Is Pulp, they can't be harmed in any way which mean I can put them in any kind of situation. Tied to a driving car? Sure. While falling into a volcano? Why not?

    And that's one of the beauties of playing gods, vampires and anything else that's hard to kill.

    \n I've got the opposite feeling - for me it's easier to go sadistic knowing that PCs can be killed easily, perhaps because usually the game rules include a way to bring them back into play inmediatly either with their old characters or with fresh ones. If they can't be killed easily then the players keep feeling invincible, kicking doors and doing stupid things just because they believe they will get away with it. For me, going sadistic is actually manage to scare them a bit in the fiction with the help of some good inmersion.

    I recall one trick I used to make them have second thoughts about kicking the ass of an NPC who was actually going to help them (yes, my group is that kind of group) The party was invited to the palace, in the presence of the empress, when the mentioned NPC was announced. As they began making plans to kick his ass just for the fun of it I explained that they were in a room with a really tall ceiling (say, five meters maybe a bit less) ... and that the new arrived had to bow to get in. That worked. We had an interesting scene with lots of social conflict.

    \n I find that I'm generally more like a movie director who's a big fan of everyone he's working with; we go in to do a scene, and most of my brain is figuring out how to position the camera to catch their very best side, and giving notes to the NPCs about how they can act/react so as to draw out the best performance from the "stars," and I'm desperately, desperately conscious of how much screen time everyone is getting.

    Setting up problems and conflicts and raising the stakes are just part of giving them the most impressive and satisfying situation to succeed in, because man, I want them to succeed at whatever it is they want to do. If they don't succeed, I'm already scheming about things I can put into the next scene that will let them shine all the brighter.

    So, y'know, just as pathological and weird as being a sadistic GM, only in the opposite direction.
