    \n The term hero has lots of meanings. Stefan hits one of the important ones, a protagonist who decides to do the right thing when pressures could cause him not to do so. Frodo is a hero this way because he decides to take the ring to Mordor when nobody expects him to, and he could much more easily go home with a purple heart for action at Weathertop.
    \n
    \nI think you see a lot of this in RPG play. I do, at least.
    \n
    \nIn Hero Quest, the term "hero" is synonomous with Player Character. What's interesting is that you as often get anti-heroes or villains as heroes in my experience playing that game. There's another meaning that's explicit in that game, however, which is "very powerful individual." I suppose it makes sense to take the question of superheroes to another thread, but it's been endemic in RPGs to have hero mean a powerful character ever since D&D; had levels named hero and superhero. Basically since levels allowed a character to perform "heroic deeds" because they have lots of hit points.
    \n
    \nThis is heroism ala Beowulf. And I think that there might be a tendency to see it as infantile power fantasies, or powergaming, or some sort of escapism. But I think it's a valid form of expression to have mythic stories that heighten the sense of importance of moral questions by having the world hanging on a thread depending on the character's actions and fate.
    \n
    \n
    \nWhat's fascinating is how most fantasy games assume that we'll want to take the "Hero's Journey" (to use a Campbellism), starting from the character being a modest farmer or something, to being the warrior who saves the world. That's actually understandable. The idea that we'd want that development to take years of actual play, however, is odd. Trying to force us to have to experience every step of the training process in the character's development, instead of just skipping young Luke Skywalker's Padwan training for the most part, and just getting to the last episode where he's a Jedi (a function of gamism, actually).

    \n I associate being a hero with sacrifice. A hero, as opposed to an adventurer or whatever other title you care to use, evokes images in my mind of someone giving of themselves, even their own life, in order to help or save something else. It doesn't even have to be a noble idea, a simple 1st level Fighter in classic D&D dying to protect his village is just fine. He gave the ultimate sacrifice, his own life, in order to protect those he cared about.

    This is why I think With Great Power... is such an incredible game, since all the mechanics revolve around sacrifice. It really plays up the "you are a hero" aspect of the game.
