\n I'm interested in your favorite mechanics that help players get into the mindset of eras long past!

Some examples of mindset differences I'm talking about:

- Almost nobody is trustworthy, and there are no society-wide trust rating systems (e.g. criminal records, credit scores). Accordingly, making new contacts is best done through acquaintances who are willing to introduce you (or them) and vouch for you.

- Society is deeply hierarchical, to the extent that being 'masterless' is an insult - you have no proper place! Your success in social relations is going to be dependent on your master's reputation and relative power.

    \n I'm interested in your favorite mechanics that help players get into the mindset of eras long past!

    I like lifepaths. When you are making your character, you are not only saying, my character is "A" but my character is not B, C or D.

    I like Keys in Solar System.Shadow of Yesterday/Lady Blackbird. Sure, characters pick their motivations, but the Keys keep them playing those motivations until they really don;t want to anymore.

    I like Refresh Scenes in Solar System.Shadow of Yesterday, it turns the passive act of character recovery to the active act of interacting with other characters.

    I like the laughter rules in Toon, it helps you play a better comedy game.

    I like the cyberware rules from CP2020, They remind the players that their characters can be more powerful if they don't care what other characters think about them.

    I like Compels, but I learned to like them more once we started using self-compels. It really brings the characters and their portrayal front and center.

    The Sin Progression in DitV puts the players in the right mindset as well.

    The Narrative Truth in wushu rules allow players to actually affect the characters, setting and story they are playing in.

    Fanmail keeps the players focused on entertaining the audience (other players).

    The tilt and aftermath tables in Fiasco keeps players moving towards a Fiasco

    The Stress Rules in InSpectres help keep the players realizing that they have a job to do.

    Thiws is a cool thread, thanks for starting it.

    \n Dude - Perfect Unrevised.

    The game is in an Orwellian Pseudo-British dystopia. So you get more "Freedoms" the higher up in society you are. But Freedoms are at best a two edged sword and at worst doublespeak: each Freedom let's you ignore a standard rule (thereby establishing the standard rules of society) while also coming with a built in restriction (which is often bigger than the Freedom it grants).

    Then you also each are attached to Guilds, which tells us about how society works without pages of detail. But more importantly, each Guild suggests one or two crimes or conspiracies the Guild is suspected of... thereby suggesting to the players crimes that their criminal may decide to commit.

    \n Dog Eat Dog gets you into the mindset of living in an occupied country. If you conform to the Occupation's rules, you get a token that helps you do things. But when you take a token, you're taking some of the Occupation's culture, and if you've got too many tokens at the endgame, you're assimilated.

    (This is certainly something that happened in the past. I hesitate to say it belongs entirely to "eras long past".)
