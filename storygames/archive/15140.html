    \n So, here's an example of an online one in Japanese:

    http://zerotoki.web.fc2.com/tennra01.html

    You can see after the bolded stat blocks and the adventure begins, that it's basically the character name (instead of the player name) and the text they say or do, in or out of character.

    They're super popular in Japan. Fans make them and sell them. Publishers make them and apparently most RPG publishers make more of their money from replay sales than core book sales.

    Like, how in the 90s the Thing was to release a core book and a supplement almost at the same time to "start the line off", in Japan a publisher will sell a game and have a replay ready either at the time of sale or within a week or three after. In fact, here's the first official Ryuutama replay, which was released almost at the same time as the core book:



    In short, a complete account of how a game is supposed to be run (complete with side chatter, laughter at side-jokes, etc), including usually being run by the designer of the game themselves, totally prevents the thing we see in the west of "Well, I've GOT the game, but I really don't know how I'm supposed to run it, based on the rules? It makes no sense!" Even if the core book is hard to grasp, reading a real life account of a game group having fun with it, using the rules etc, it helps clarify everything.

    I'm torn between wanting to start a replay culture in the US, and the amount of time/editing the transcriptions cost...

    \n@Andy - if you organize a game in a very structured way and you play it online the effort of editing and transcription would plummet.

    It is worth thinking about how you would design an online game around the concept of it being replayed.

    You have to put enough structure that either generates interesting content for an audience, or you it makes easy to just cut it.
    You could have a metagame currency that rewards players that stick to a specific format that you want to use.

    I actually worked on this very concept this summer, and I will do it again during winter holidays.

    Having a subscription-based computer mediated format to do storygames and a constant stream of potential upsells could reinject some cash into our poor hobby and allow more people to do it fulltime.
