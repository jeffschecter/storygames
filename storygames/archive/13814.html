\n I got a "flow"-instructor, which I train with once each week (The "flow" here is a mix of yoga, pilates and thai-chi). She is short in her instructions, but clear. So she communicate very efficiently what we are to do, and leave us to follow her example. I find her short instructions, and silent example, to leave a space for me to go into, searching for the elusive flow in the balance and movements of the training.

I have another instructor, in yoga, and he is trying to aim for the same state of "meditation in motion". But he has the small flaw of talking too much. When he tells us to empty our minds, and "be only here and now", he can't stop talking. So he fills our minds, again and again, undermining the objective he has stated.

I've worked as a professional GM for 16 years, and have developed a lot in that time. I'm still not flawless in my leadership, but I'm a bit simpler. I tend to prefer a very short framing, when entering a new situation, "cutting to the chase" so to say, of the character-action.

And i believe this to be very effective, when done right, for the same reason that my flow-instructor makes it easier for me to get into flow in her training-sessions. Her leadership is simple and to the point, and leaves me with the necessary space to reach for my inner tranquility.

What a short and to-the-point framing do, in role-playing games, is to leave the players with a dramatic space, in which they reach for their inner understanding of the characters dilemma ...

- maybe.

    \n So; we keep the framing short and efficient, and then we go into interaction. If details needs to be added, we do so with speed, and go on with the interaction. We can go on doing this again and again, feeding the game-play with tidbits of details at great speed, until the scene ends. The same is true for narrating consequences; we do it with few words, to keep the game rolling and to leave the players with a larger imaginary space to fill with emotions and inspirations.

    \n No, mostly I do not have the time to ponder what to say. So I try to follow my intuition.

    And experience has thought me that a little goes a long way, in how you frame a scene. I've been a professional storyteller too, and have come to realize that the way we "narrate" in role-playing games is much closer to conversation than any storytelling. The games we play got dramatic dialogue at their core, and this dialogue is better off with the GM reining her authority tightly.

    An example:
    - Your ex-girlfriend walks into you in an alley: Hey! Are you home? (look at the player with big eyes)

    Another example:
    - Something is attacking you! What do you do? (GM raise a threatening fist)

    A third example:
    - The market is a chaos of people, stalls and traders cries! A big man bumps into you ... (give the shoulder of the player a quick push)

    \n I think I should add that I'm talking about the default mode of framing here.

    My default mode of framing is short and effective, and then I vary it with some slower, more detailed, scene-framing.

    I believe the most common default mode of framing is a slow, detailed one, with little or no variation.

    I believe the speed of it is essential, as it jumps right into interaction, with as little delay as possible. And then you feed the scene throughout, with tidbits of details, if there's need for them. It makes for a more flexible way of doing scenes.

    Posted By: GrahamI think ritual is important, too.
    So do I. The whole damn thing is a ritual. And by that ritual we make magic; a magic that mesmerize ourselves.\n
    Posted By: David BergMinimal framing + no instruction on fleshing out + no natural player urge to flesh out = non-agreed-upon fiction = chaos.
    Exactly what are you describing here? Anything you have experienced?

    The players ability to "flesh out" is inherent in his natural urge to act; when the player make his character act, it will have consequences, and the scene will blossom; there will be flesh, and blood, and gore a'plenty! And then; when you tie scene to scene to scene to scene, in a stream of hard knuckled drama, you will see scenes feeding each other, strengthening and deepening the plot, and the game will come alive like a roaring monster in your midst!

    It sounds crazy, I know, but I believe most of us have witnessed the birth of that "monster" in the midst of a player circle. I certainly have, and more so since I began heeding the pace and rhythm of play, keeping my love for details away from my gaming. The devil is NOT in the details! It's in the dialogue!
