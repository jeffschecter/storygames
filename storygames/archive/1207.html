In other discussions that I've participated in here and in person, and listened to elsewhere, I've become aware of a general class of issue that I'm finding it difficult to completely identify.

In broad strokes, I've noticed that certain games have a tendency to encourage play that is not a little wacky. The result of which tends to be that neither characters nor shared world, or story are well explored or well served. I tend to think of this as the "Oldest Game" problem, from the scene early in the run of The Sandman, but the same situation exists in the Wizard's Duel in The Sword in the Stone (certainly the Disney version, I don't recall the book well enough to say for certain whether it's there or not.) In essence, both games are challenges of narrative ability; trying to defeat your opponent by storytelling.

Before I go off pissing on everything, let me bring up an counter-example or two. Games that tend to keep things grounded, where characters and world and story do tend to work together. My first example: Cyberpunk. For all it's warts, dear old CP kept things well grounded. Mostly by keeping narrative agency in the hands of the GM, and as an example it stands for pretty much all games of that kind. As another example though, Dogs in the Vineyard does very well. In Dogs, narrative power lives in everyone's hands. Certainly the GM sets up the town, and that gives the story direction, but for the most part the game is run by the players after that. Prime Time Adventures keeps well on track, as well.

Several story telling games that I've played had a decided tendency towards wackiness, regardless of the intended tone, and developed characters, PC or NPC, only shallowly, if at all. Although it's not billed at all as an RPG, Once Upon A Time exhibits this behavior. My experience with Capes and Polaris demonstrated this issue. My intuition is that Donjon would as well, although I think it was written with wacky improv and disregard for character at heart.

I don't think I'm alone in this observation, and I don't think that any of the above are bad games, or broken, or anything. But I do play role-playing games to enjoy them, and one of the things I like to get is an involvement in the story of the game. And a lot of the time, I feel like there's a colossal amount of discipline required to keep these games on track.

The trouble with the Wizard's Duel/Oldest Game is that it doesn't work as an actual game. You just can't narrate an opponent into a corner. The greenest of narrators can skirt the line between getting what they want (a win, some dice, the girl) and popcorn. But on the other hand, there is a lot of game to be had in narrative play, because even if there's no question about if another player can take your narration and get what they want out of it, there can be a lot of interest in how they get there.

One of my open questions is whether the issue is a matter of there being a GM. Because there's a big difference between another player resisting your narration, and the Impartial Arbitrator saying "Come on guys." I don't think that's necessarily so, especially given how little say the GM in Dogs and the Producer in PTA have. I also hear that Full Light, Full Steam plays remarkably well GMless. So while I think that having a GM helps guard against the problem, I don't think that a GM is the only solution, or that designing a game without a GM will necessarily lead to freewheeling, silly improv.

The other thought I have on the matter is that it's a question of comparing apples and oranges. That narrative statements lead to mechanical benefits. In Dogs that's not really so. It comes down to an elegant dice game. I compare my dice to yours, but I have to justify my dice with narration.

So maybe I'm just coming at the Lumpley Principle another way. That Once Upon a Time allows concepts into the SIS only by consensus, and a lot of the time, that results in wacky play.

All of this, I think, sounds very musing, and that's because it is. I haven't been able to hammer down in my own thoughts a decided statement to make here. So I guess that's what I'm asking: can I get you guys to help me beat this idea into shape?

In Cyberpunk, it's all the chrome and gritty skills you put on your character sheet; in Dogs, it's the talking->physical->fighting->gunplay conflict resolution; in PTA, it's character issues and the pervasive influence of the "scenes should be about the plot or an issue" rule. Once Upon a Time is even weaker, with incredibly generic cards the only thing that keeps you in a vaguely fantasy-ish vein. Capes has exactly zero flagging outside of player interactions; as Tony portrays it, it's all about reading your fellow players to see what they're interested in and going there to reap the rewards in tokens. So that's the "centering" mechanic, but it's something that requires a little experience before you see it clearly.

    I've alluded to this in another topic, but Game Design differs from Government of Application.

    I've been working on a hypothesis around this general realm for the past few days.

    Designers have control over Mechanical Aspects and Seed Aspects of games, but do not control the Participant Aspects of the games.
    Mechanical Aspects are purely system design and I've broken them up for my purposes. Seed Aspects are setting/story elements that are intended for use by the game's designer(s), but have no mechanical mandate and thus may be ignored by the participants at a game level. Particpant Aspects are variables that the players bring to the table that will result in differing results depending on the grouping of participants.

    I've born witness to TSR's ancient Top Secret spy game being used for super heroic characters over two decades ago. Clearly not the intent of the designers, but the participants used the mechanism to create something beyond the Seed Aspect of the game. The Wizard Duel/Oldest Game aspects that appear in any number of games, could be centered around Particpant Priority Mechanics of the game. Where does the lead lie with particpants? Does game X or game Y have some sort of heirarchy built in among participants? A game like Panty Explosion has no GM per se, but has a Superintendent that has clear priority among the participants. Could integration of clear Participant Priority Mechanics add moderation to a degree of the wacky game syndrome? It is human nature to test boundaries to see how far something can go. My opinion, ultimately in forms of imaginative play this tends toward the silly in early stages of testing the limits.

    Once I've finished writing my ideas around the hypothesis I've mentioned, I'll post it to the forums for feedback.

    August 2006 edited August 2006
    Hands down yes, Jonathan. One of the biggest and least-recognized differences between "indie" and "mainstream" is where they put their directions. Mainstream games have loads of crypto-directions embedded in their setting chapters and flavor text, which work rather well for a lot of people. On the indie side of the spectrum, this sort of stuff is often explicitly outlined in the rules and procedures, in part because there's a lot less subtlety in indie games (or if you prefer, it's far more straightforward) but also because, since the rules are often looser in terms of who gets to contribute to the story, the guidelines need to be more obvious and rigid. When the GM-god is telling "his story" and the players are "playing along" they don't need the sort of robust directions provided by strong rules procedures.

    Addendum: it's the difference between making your game inspirational versus procedural. Not that these are mutually exclusive, mind: just two paths up the mountain.

Will all this work? We'll see in playtest.

    I'd like to think that a GMless game need not suffer from this problem any more than a game with a GM, but you're right that they do at this point. While I see many GMless games spending time discussing how the group needs to get on the same page before play begins, in practice I've seen many groups just dive right in without this discussion and end up with wacky play because of it. So maybe these games need to have that GM authority to guide (censor?) inappropriate content during play distributed among the players in some fashion. Even so, though, in many groups the existing social dynamics might prevent such distributed authority from actually being used.

    Judson, the text socializes players, hands down. When the players read the text, at least. But consider all the Exalted players out there -- their understanding of what is and is not acceptable or appropriate for an Exalted game is, I'd bet money on, far more influenced by the text than anything else. Even when one player hasn't read the book, the rest of the group probably has, and the non-reader takes his cues from the readers.

    This doesn't mean that System Doesn't Matter; it means that the text is an element of their common and assumed system. Since system is just the procedures by which the group dispenses credibility (lumpley principle), a group referencing the text explicitly or implicitly makes the text part of that system. In such a system, credibility is often withheld because there is no textual support for a proposed action or fictional element. No space aliens in Exalted because it's not in the book.

    One important point, though: withholding credibility is one way to go about this, sure, but it's sort of the brute-force way of doing things. There's a step before group consensus uses system to apply credibility, and that's the individual player deciding what he's going to suggest. If there are mechanics and procedures which either constrain or influence that decision, there's less need for the system to come crashing down on players' heads when they make an unacceptable suggestion. So if the field is wide open, a player may make any suggestion and be subjected to the tender mercies of his fellow players; however, if the player's options are constrained (all scenes must deal with the character's issue) or influenced (I can charge my thematic battery if I do something about my character being a Rake), the suggestion that they make for approval of the rest of the group is more likely to already be in line with what is acceptable and less likely to require the rest of the group to withhold credibility and say no.

    I think the difference to me comes from the source of authority on those edits. Basically, I see two kinds:
    Table Consensus
    What the players at the table think is cool. I discard ideas without contributing them because I don't think they're worthwhile, or my group overrides my contribution because it isn't cool. I think there are a lot of valid variations to this, including limits of squick, or aesthetic preferences. But one way or another this is a value judgment that takes place at the table. If we're playing together, consensus based edits are inherently between you and I.
    Systemic Authority
    Upon consideration of the rules, and our play contract, the contribution is invalid. There's a value judgment again, but there isn't a direct judgment about me or my creative efforts, only about how well they fit the criteria of the system. I discard suggestions unvoiced because I don't think they fit within the bounds of the rules, or would be too much of a stretch, or definitely over a social contract line. It's so easy to discard ideas that are outside a "strong" system I don't feel it, if that makes sense, and there's much more at stake if I inject an iffy idea with "weak" systemic tools available.
