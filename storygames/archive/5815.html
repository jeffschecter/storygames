    \n So for our french-impaired friends ^o^ some more info that were given to us by the thread starter Pollux (kudos to him !) :

    Beside your character class, you have 4 roles in each party :

    -the leader (whose word is final if players can't agree on what they'll do)
    -cartographer : draws maps for the party
    -steward : in charge of the equipment, buys and sells stuff for the whole party
    -the "scribe" : may change at each session, keeps a log of the PCs adventures, written from the PC perspective, not the player's...

    A character is defined by 4 attributes :

    Brawn
    Agility
    Intellect
    Will

    Each attribute is assigned a die type \xe0 la Savage Worlds.

    Task resolution
    There are no skills in Ryuu Tama. So you choose two attributes related to the task at hand and throw the corresponding dice then compare the result a target number set between 2 and 20.

    If both dice come as "1"s it's a critical failure, but the whole party gains 1Xp (the group learns from his failures)

    If both dice show their maximum result or two "6"s it's a critical success (which means D4s and D6s have only one chance to score a critical hit, while other dice have two chances).

    Pollux has given us many more infos, I'll hope he'll drop by here !

    \n So, first of all, the cover :



    The character classes :


    Minstrels are travelers (bonus to Travel related tests), good at obtaining information and entertainers (giving bonus to allies)


    Merchants are Eloquent (bonus to related tests), know how to handle animals (can take care of 3 animals instead of one for other classes) and of course can buy and sell equipmement at much better prices.


    Hunters are good at tracking (this helps finding a monster and fight him), using the monster as source of material (food, clothing, "treasure"...) and finding food in the wild


    Healers, well, heal, treat adverse status effect (poisoned, sick...) and can find medicinal herbs in the wild.


    Farmers are tough, know how to handle animals (like the merchant) and are versatile (can have one ability coming from another class)


    Artisans can "loot" animals (as the hunter), repair broken objects and create a specific type of objects.


    Nobles are educated ( helps with knowledge related tests), can specialize in one type of weapon and adapt well to any social circle.

    (All the info here is a translation/adaptation of Pollux original thread an he's the one who found the images)

    \nToo many. Seriously. :-)

    Here's the other issue: Good Japanese translators make on average 20 cents per word for a professional publication (you don't want to know how much I get for translating technical documents). The average RPG writer makes 2-4 cents per word, IIRC.

    I have to exploit my local friendly J-Translators to get translation done (far less than 20 cents/word). Which IMO sucks. But when I compared what I was charging to what is industry standard for manga, anime and the like I didn't feel so bad. Still, most of the effort for this thing comes from:

    RPG players
    Who are also new game enthusiasts (rare)
    Who also are fluent in reading Japanese (double rare)
    And who aren't already tied up completely on translation work or day jobs (triple rare)

    Still, let's see what happens after Tenra Bansho ero. If it works out (my plan is "break even", but we'll see), we might see another game in the future... 5 years in the future, but still. At this point, right now, though, I think TBZ will be it for me: I'd rather encourage other Established game companies to pick up the rights to those games, translate and release them themselves. That would be hot, and free me up to Play the games rather than Translate them all. :-)

    \n Hmmm. I'm more partial to "underground". No complications: That is, translate as much text as possible, and don't consider the "ransom" as a real ransom, because unlike the Ransom Model the translator does not have the rights to distribute the product.

    The money is nothing more than an upfront tip jar to the person putting the effort into doing the translation. It gives no obligation to distributing or freely releasing the translation. It's simply a pat on the back to the translator for doing a good job. How the translator distributes their own personal-use translation is entirely up to them.

    I would be against publicly providing the text in full with like copies of the art from the book and all. That's ethically bogus, in my book.

    I'd be up if the translator(s) kept their notes private somewhere. Like, you'd have to email the translator to get the notes. Or subscribe to a private mailing list/Google Group. This isn't to keep it "hidden from the cops", this is to basically keep the project an extension of Private Use (that is, the translator translates the game for his own private gaming use... Oh, and here's a copy or two for "friends" to peruse... but not releasing it on a website to everyone with a browser and a mouse). I'm actually currently working on such a repository (the aforementioned http://www.j-rpg.com/talk ) but haven't had to clearly sit down and crank out the rules yet.

    So keep it all private use, where people have to email or contact someone for the translation. That ensures that you create a community of interest, of people who could probably be encouraged to go to Amazon.co.jp to buy a copy of the book for themselves, to use in tandem with the translation.

    And then, ONLY THEN, when it is completely done... Then heck, why not have the translator contact the company to see if they'd be interested in an Official English (French) Release? Since you have the work already done, it won't be too hard to go the extra step, I think. This is actually what Ewen is doing now with an even MORE secret project than the Maid RPG (and what I am starting to possibly do with another FEAR game):
    \u3068\u308a\u3042\u3048\u305a\u7ffb\u8a33\u3092\u3057\u3066\u3001\u3057\u3066\u3001\u3057\u307e\u304f\u308b\u306e\u3060\uff01\u3084\u3063\u3068\u7d42\u308f\u3063\u305f\u3089\u3001\u305d\u306e\u3068\u304d\u3053\u305d\u30e1\u30fc\u30ab\u30fc\u3068\u9023\u7d61\u3092\u53d6\u308a\u3001\u897f\u6d0b\u306e\u30ea\u30ea\u30fc\u30b9\u306b\u3064\u3044\u3066\u76f8\u8ac7\u3092\u2026

    But in the mean time, it's a Private Project. By a Private Person. Who will distribute it Privately. And us? We're just stuffing a tip jar in advance.
    Don't go public, don't release "to the world" in the Stolze Ransom Model with a media you don't own the rights to. That would create... complications. Legal AND Ethical.
    The Private Project model fits, too, the model of Guerrilla J-Translators at the Comiket who translate all the latest WW core books and release them as pamphlets at cost at conventions, highly recommending folks jot over to Shinjuku's Yellow Sub to buy the English core book for themselves (in fact, I think they set it up deliberately so that you actually need the core book to understand and play their translation), but basically keep it a private affair, not posting their translations on the internet, and of course dissolving the project as soon as Suzaku or whomever picks up the rights to an Official Translation. I give that model a thumbs up.

      Posted By: zircherThe 'Oregon Trail' element is an interesting detail/teaser.
      \nI wanted to be as clear as possible in the description of the game of what it Is and Isn't, in case people were on the edge. If you go in looking to use Every Rule at All Times (combat, condition, travel, rations, camping etc) then the game becomes "Ryuutama: The game of buying and managing travel equipment/gear, and dying likely because of bad weather and topological conditions."

      I ran it twice thus far, one group we carefully planned and purchased the equipment we were going to use for the 3-day journey from A to B (weapons, tents, sundries, etc). The second group I just said "You have basic starting equipment and your favored weapon, plus a tent and two "dots"/days of food and water, LESSGO! The first one was fun because (to quote the players) it felt like those initial purchase choices matter, and there's finally a reason to be concerned about/use pack animals. BUT, it did take a while, about 30 mins. Not that there are tons of charts, in fact all together all the equipment was (just confirmed) far less than what you find in Pathfinder or other games. But if you're "ready to go adventure" from the get-go, starting the game with 10-30 minutes of consulting some sheets to see how much food you can buy for the trail (and which animals to pack it on) might be tedious in comparison to "we'll worry about that later, you have the basics, but you're one day short of food (which gives the Hunter a chance to find food for the group), GO!" which might work better for one-shots.

      Another interesting aspect: Each character at the table is assigned a "Role" each session: The Leader, the Mapper, the Quartermaster and the Scribe. Within each of these roles lies the opportunity for some "group lonely fun": The Leader can tell people what to do. The Mapper player can quietly draw the map of their travel. The Scribe player can "blue-book" their experiences through the eyes of their character. The Quartermaster can take the group money and be the one responsible for purchasing/tracking equipment for everyone. So in truth, if you play the roles strongly, then that 10-30 mins above can be shuffled to one player to manage over the adventure rather than everyone at the beginning.

      I'm thinking it won't work well for solo play. Much of the majesty of the game is exploring new places, talking in character to strangers and PCs alike. Having the GM describe fantastical new places and reacting to that. It'd be reaaaaally hard as anything other than a meditation. However, the roles above? Totally solo-playable. The Quartermaster, Mapper or Scribe can do their thing in between sessions by themselves. But this assumes that you have a session with real players to go back to.

      There should be enough interest in Skype, maybe drum up some in a new thread!

I'm not a big fan of that stuff as a player (I can't even finish making a Shadowrun character any more), but they seemed to dig it, the Merchant grumbling all the while about needing to keep enough funds on hand to make a profit on the trip.

      After that, they set off to Ifa. Notable incidents on the road included the Hunter giving some thought to mugging Joshua the weather wizard if his cloak turned out to be an expensive raincoat (as Joshua scanned the skies, totally oblivious to the appraisal of his wardrobe), the Minstrel's Spring magic creating a leaf large enough to shield everyone from the next day's downpour, and the group considering filling poor Kotaro the Nekogoblin with arrows while his back was turned (but once he introduced himself and they saw how cute and guileless he was they were happy to help search for his lost earring). Traveling the grasslands was an easy way to get used to the rhythm of the road, making skill checks, and using class abilities and Concentrating on rolls; no one Fumbled, and I think they only failed one traveling roll, so they were only half a day off-schedule. Then they reached Ifa and everyone reached second level, including the dragon.

      We had a couple more hours to fill, so everyone bought Cute goods (the scenario says Ifa is having a sale on them but gives no particular details, so I just ruled they were half off-- a good profit for the Merchant to resell them, especially after using his class skill to haggle) and restocked on rations. The Minstrel's player had to leave, but the game at the other table broke up and a couple of those players rolled up an Attack-Minstrel and a Technical-Healer, dripping in Cute equipment.

      The Green Dragon swapped out the Encyclopedia (no effect) for the Torch (double XP) and equipped The Tale of the Traveler's Journal (in-game gold for the first two players to write up a session log) and Guidance of the Dragon (turn a failed Direction Check into a success) in its new Blessing and Awakening slots.

      I had a second scenario ready to go; I liked the idea of the characters visiting a town cursed into an eternal winter, so I jotted that down on the town creation sheet and passed it around the table for everyone else to fill in, and we ended up with the town of Chillblaine, where the few remaining villagers hide in underground dwellings, both from the cold and a nearby tribe of maddened dwarves. Our tiny merchant company decided to lighten the Chillblainers' spirits with some Cute goods, and off we went.

      I went by the book's guidelines for difficulties and enemies for a second-level party, but I apparently highballed it a bit. The first day of the trip was through the woods (diff 8), which the Minstrel failed and lost half her HP. On the second day, they traveled through a snowy wasteland where the permafrost had killed all the plants (diff 9), and they failed their Direction check so they had to spend another day trekking through it. At that point they were in the final region, the mountain pass leading to Chillblaine (diff 10), and that started to prove a bit much for them; the failed Direction check put them behind schedule so their food stores were low, and the Hunter was having a hard time foraging for more food (I wanted to see how this panned out, so I didn't use the dragon's Awakening, though I would have if they actually started starving). The Minstrel was consistently down on HP from the harsh travels, and the Healer had to leave partway through, so there wasn't any medical care available beyond a night's rest.

      It wasn't all miserable slogging though the cold, though; in the mountains, they found a cave entrance with thin veins of Symphonic Crystal, suggesting a deeper seam within. After some deliberation, they lit a lantern and went in, finding mysterious glyphs carved into the walls-- they couldn't read it, but the Minstrel could tell it was dragonscript-- the story of some other band of travelers, chronicled by a dragon in ages gone by. Further in, they found the remains of that Dragon's study, filled with statues carved from ice (the likenesses of those travelers it followed? Who can say?) and the Crystal itself, purring a song that invigorated the party. The Hunter, having tracked the Crystal veins here, was tempted to pry it from the wall, even if that meant killing it, but the Minstrel talked him down and the Merchant suggested they might find a higher profit selling the cave's location to sightseers.

      After that, they bedded down for the night (the Hunter foraging for food, which I ruled were edible cave mushrooms that would inflict a High 6 status ailment), and the next day is when the trouble really started. The poor put-upon Minstrel botched her Condition roll, and we decided she'd used a particularly soft-looking mushroom for a pillow, so she was High. Then the group traveled farther into the cave network and emerged from the mountains, near their destination... but nearby, something like a human body was lying half-covered in snow. Moving closer to investigate, they found an unlucky half-dead traveler in the thorny clutches of a vampiric Tyrant Rose, which attacked!

      This proved to be a trickier fight than I'd planned, perhaps because we only had three PCs, and also perhaps one of them was a level lower, but the Tyrant Rose's point of damage resistance, plus its ability to heal 3 HP on a successful attack proved to be a serious problem. The Minstrel quickly realized that her botched Condition roll meant she'd die if she went to -2 HP, and she only had half HP to begin with thanks to yet another failed Movement check. She and the Merchant quickly retreated to the back row and peppered it with bows while the Hunter tanked the Rose in the front row. The fight dragged on just a little longer than I'd have liked (though the Merchant Feinted to reduce the Rose's initiative near the end, which was a big help), but we finished without anyone dying, the victim was saved, and the Hunter managed to salvage some Beautiful Perfume from the monster plant's corpse.

      Everyone had a pretty good time; the mellow feel did manage to come through, and I really like how hit points represent fatigue maybe even moreso than pure health, and how the classes and types make for easy hooks at character creation without really restricting your character's growth as you level up. Initiative doubling as your defense value is also pretty interesting, especially since you can spend your action to reroll it, or feint to reduce someone else's. Ryuutama almost feels like a mildly story-gamed version of D&D; the basic mechanics and play structure are reasonably trad, but the Ryuujin stuff is a pretty interesting set of training wheels for GMs to actively play with different themes. I'd definitely try this again, but I'm not sure how long I could run it as a completely picaresque hexcrawl. Next time I'll have to try a scenario that doesn't involve long travel.
