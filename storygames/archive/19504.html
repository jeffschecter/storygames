    \n It *is* crazy. I really dig the setting and, while dark, it's um... flamboyant. But tons of ideas in there that get me motivated to run this. I will point out that the first supplement -- which has the terrible label of an "advanced" rulebook. Regardless, there's lots of nifty setting details and character options; don't let the advanced bit warn you off. I don't have Public Enemy, the 2nd supplement, just yet.

    The rules are fun and do a good job with combining story-game mechanics with a bit (and just a bit) of crunchier system. There are folks that say this is where Exalted 3rd edition should be -- simplification plus story-friendly system levers and options.

    \n I have so much to say about this game, but I have no clue where to start?
    It's as if Burning Wheel made love to a horny teenager in the 1990's who was really into Vampire the Masquerade and Akira.
    You have ready your fists.
    There's a million kooky things going on here.
    I do think Loises are a very cool idea. I'd maybe have the player to your left play your Lois, as long as they're of the relationship kind. There are things called Trait Loises, which aren't tied to another character. I'd stick with one Lois per player character.
    So many god-damn crazy things with this game.
    Character creation is crazy-involved. Charts! I swear half the book is charts or text broken up into little boxes.
    The powers would be so better suited to cards. I have no clue if this is a thing in Japan, but I wouldn't be surprised if they already existed, just not in English.

    I'm more then 200 pages in, and I have yet to wrap my head around designing a story for this.
    \nDX is a little counter-intuitive for me, too. In the general J-RPG thread, I shared a little AP from one of the two times I've run it, and yeah, I did not even try and bring in any of the non-Scenario Loises unless one of the players initiated contact, that's just way too many NPCs to juggle, especially in a one-shot game.

    I also am pretty leery of trying to stat up my own NPCs, because the book doesn't really give any kind of baseline for stats or advice on builds, and the ones in the scenarios are all over the place (like, the climax of Tomorrow's Children I think it was, where you need to shoot down the missiles would have easily been a total fail if one PC didn't have an attack that flat-out ignored armor, and even as is he needed to burn some Tituses). Forunately this game does have a fair number of scenarios, and I'm very interested in the Scenario System that apparently randomizes a lot of details to make them replayable, but I haven't picked up the False Hearts book it first shows up in.

    \n I'm still reading it too, along with anything I've found out there about it. So far it looks like something that needs to be railroaded but only on the side of the pseudo sci-fy events, facts and how the exposition is made (in small parts, but simple enough so players will get the whole picture on three scenes at best).

    The rest of it though, needs to be completely played unsafe: the way the relationships between characters, loises, tituses and fronts develops will definitely be handled by players interaction and perhaps some random rolls on the encounter table and the positive/negative emotion chart. In general, it looks like you would fare better by using as much material from the book as you can, specially all the provided NPCs to keep things going.

    I'm thinking of picking a couple syndromes and powers at random and see what they do. Even if that doesn't make a functional character that's a good story by itself: It's not like all overeds get to choose what they turn into. The rest comes from thinking: what kind of issues would a person like that bring into the world? How could people react to him/her? How would that shape his/her future actions? So now you have a story that begins with a rumor about an unusual event that the UGN thinks may be related to an Overed. The false Hearts may be involved too, so now it's a race to find the person and the intentions behind.

    Use the same trick (pick a syndrome or two and another couple powers with those and see what they do) and place it with an Overed who is already part of one of the groups. Roll a few times on the tables and see what comes up and what kind of chanbes that character could bring into the organization, and how good or bad that goes into the organization's agenda. Now you've got the story of a brilliant newcomer that turns the whole organization upside down either for good or bad. Figure out how the main players (NPCs) would react to this and go from there.

    Looks like I could go on for hours, this book simply fills my mind with ideas, it's awesome ^_^
