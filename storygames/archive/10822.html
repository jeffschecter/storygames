    \n [3 Social Success]
    \n
    \nI'm very impressed by Zynga.
    \n
    \nThey are a huge well oiled machine. Check out their job lists. That's a lot of specialized people working together! Edit: And apparently they may be making close to $100 million in revenue this year.
    \n
    \nThe interview is with their VP of business development which is probably why the focus is more on business.
    \n
    \nI know a lot of people who play Farmville. And they love it. They mention how people's children are managing their parent's farms. It's definitely true. My friends 5 year old excels at it. It scratches the same itch that Sims provided for many people. In fact most of my friends who play are the same ones who used to play Sims and stopped gaming since (outside of cell phone games).
    \n
    \nI find these quotes interesting:
    \n
    \n"I think the days of the indie developer are beyond us."
    \n
    \n"We've actually made investments into some innovative games that were incredibly hardcore. If you look at Guild of Heroes, for example, we did roll that out. It was a version of Diablo built in Flash, and it wasn't successful, and we didn't support it any longer."
    \n
    \n"The truth of the matter is that we kind of did, and were mindful of it, and we tried to build specifically for that casual, social platform."
    \n
    \nThat said, indie developer can definitely break ground in these spaces just enough to show the potential of an idea without having to go all the way through production, marketing, sales, maintenance, and customer support. Looking at their job descriptions for game designers, they ask for people who have experience testing ideas in Excel first before going into full development. That's incredibly smart.

    \nPlaystation Sued For Discrimination Against the Blind
    \n
    \n
    Visually impaired gamer Alexander Stern believes that Sony is violating the Americans with Disabilities Act by discriminating against the blind, and has filed a lawsuit to get the company to comply with his demands.
    \n
    \n
    \n
    Stern claims that he has been sending both emails and snail mail to Sony for a while, requesting simple ways that Sony can make its games easier to play, though these requests have been ignored. Other games, such as World of Warcraft, already allow for third-party modifications that have been aiding visually impaired gamers. Two examples include an audio compass and a text-to-voice inventory system; these are what Stern hopes to get Sony to implement, or to at least allow for the possibility.
    \n
    \n
    \n
    Stern also wants Sony to make its online auction system, through which players auction off in-game items for real money, to be accessible to the visually impaired. Because Stern can't access this feature, Sony is costing Stern money, says the suit.

    \n EA:
    \nsafety vs. quality?
    \nquality = layoffs?
    \n
    \nParaphrasing a snippet from a recent Penny Arcade webcomic:
    \nWhen EA was pumping out shit, they were making money. When they started making quality games, everyone loved them, and 600 people lost their jobs.
    \n
    \nNow its 3,200.
    \n
    \nhttp://www.escapistmagazine.com/forums/read/7.154804\n
    \n
    For years, EA was seen as the industry's "Big Bad", a soulless corporation that existed only to churn out sports sequels, to acquire young, thriving studios and put them to work on crappy movie tie-ins, and to cut corners and rush development, all for the sake of a few extra bucks.
    \n
    \nEA might have been the gaming Antichrist, but in the past two or so years, the publisher made a concerted effort to turn its act around. Riccitiello stated that he didn't see any inherent conflict between creativity and profitability, went on public record as being against DRM, and stated that he viewed the pirated Sims 3 as a demo of sorts to entice people to buy the full game. It was almost a complete turnaround in public perception for the once-reviled giant (it didn't hurt that Activision seemed more than willing to step up into its place as the industry's own "Devil at the Crossroads"), and while certainly not perfect, the New EA was a move in the right direction.
    \n
    \nUnfortunately, moving in the right direction apparently means that the company has axed 3200 jobs since last October, and if Riccitiello's comments here are any indication, we may see the New EA turning back into the Old EA - a company that emphasizes profitability as the bottom line, with a focus on safe titles that the higher-ups know will sell.

    \n
    \n
    \n [EA 1]

    I was a game designer at EA, and was caught in the layoffs back in January. I don't think EA is going to go back to its old system.

    1) EA's change in direction largely coincides with a change of CEOs from Larry Probst to John Riccitiello. If EA's going to change direction, John will step down. If you don't see that happen, assume it's steady as she goes. In any case, EA's a big ship, and it takes a long time for it to change direction.
    2) Movie IP licenses are not giving the same return on investment that they did in the late 90's. This is mostly because studios are charging more, not because fewer people are buying.
    3) Successful company-owned IPs can turn into valuable franchises that can be mined for long-term revenue. Why pay a movie studio to make a game about their movie when they'll pay you to make a movie about your game?

      \nEA Seen Needing More Than Cost Cuts
      \nElectronic Arts Inc. (ERTS) is moving to boost its performance by aggressively cutting costs. But shares of the pioneering videogame maker likely won't jump until the company is finally able to do more of what its competitors are doing: selling more blockbuster titles.
      \n
      \nEarlier this month, Redwood City, Calif.-based Electronic Arts said it would cut 1,500 jobs and has already closed at least one facility. The move follows an earlier 6% reduction of its workforce announced in October 2008.
      \n
      \nWhile no one doubts that EA's efforts to streamline its operations are smart, few analysts or investors said cuts alone will put the company on track for rapid growth. For that to happen, they say, EA needs in part to publish more games with better graphics, game play and characters, which is something it hasn't done consistently for at least a year. While EA's games have been winning higher critical ratings over the last two years, the relatively slow results hasn't pleased some on Wall Street.
      \n
      \n"The quality of the title line-up remains a major concern for EA," Deutsche Bank analyst Jeetil Patel said in a research note. Patel, who has a sell rating on EA, added that the company's competitors also appear to be taking market share.
      \n
      \nOne recent example of this competitive pressure is Activision Blizzard Inc.'s (ATVI) "Call of Duty: Modern Warfare 2," which smashed records when it was released two weeks ago and will likely notch $1 billion in sales.
      \n
      \nJeff Brown, an EA spokesman, said any criticism of the company's game line-up is short-sighted if it doesn't take into account EA's recent investments in digital and mobile games--resulting in EA rising to No. 1 in worldwide mobile revenue, and leading others in revenue from Apple Inc. (AAPL) iPhone downloads.
      \n
      \n"If someone said that Avatar is going to be the movie of 2009, and that all others will fail by comparison--you'd probably think that was pretty stupid," Brown said. "Whether or not EA has the single top seller of the year, we are number one in global market share and have more top selling hits than any other game company."
      \n
      \nEA was once the world's premier game maker and remains among the industry's elite. Its "Madden NFL" and "Sims" franchises set standards for technology, sales and game play.
      \n
      \nBut now EA has stalled, say critics, while its competitors get better at making games. In October, the company's "NBA Live 10"--the latest in a basketball series--sold just 141,000 copies, compared with the 575,000 copies of "NBA 2K10" that competitor Take-Two Interactive Software Inc. (TTWO) sold, according to Patel.
      \n
      \n
