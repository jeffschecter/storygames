\n Back in the day we created ashcans, crude versons of games to drum up funds to get a "real" version of the game out there.

A very different concept was the living rulebook; as far as I'm aware, this has probably seen the most success with Games Workshop's "Necromunda", "Bloodbowl" and "Mordheim". The concept is a set of rules that constantly sees updating, available to the public (or signed up members of a forum). These rules gradually evolve and improve based on feedback from those who are a part of a community of regular users, the rules never get "formalised" in a static printed version, they remain electronic, dynamic and fluid.

I'm toying with an idea that fuses these two concepts. A living ashcan.

Participants buy into the project, and if they buy in early it doesn't have much cost. Once they have bought into the project they continue to get updates for the life of the game (such updates might be weekly, monthly, quarterly)...as the game gets more refined, it costs a little more for new participants to access the rulebook, but they are buying into a more refined product (so there is less risk for them, and they can see more of what they're buying into).

The living rulebook will keep evolving and advancing until specific points in the project's timeline or funding. Once these points are reached, a specific hardcopy is released and everyone who is a part of the project gets subsidies for their dedication and input.

I've started this concept with a game called Voidstone Chronicles...it went live on DrivethruRPG/RPGNow yesterday. If you're interested, go over and take a look. If you've got any quyestions about the idea, feel free to ask.

    \n This is basically the application of a software industry development & sales model to RPGs. Which makes a lot of sense; game rules can be seen as "code" that incrementally develops. What remains to be seen is whether that model can successfully be applied to an industry for which the market already had another model.

    Another radical idea that began with software developers is the "opensource" model, which began with things like webservers and content management systems but since has been applied to everything from industrial machine plans to technical engineering solutions. An opensource project is like a big public "library" that not only allows people to check things out, but also put things in. A typical opensource project has many contributors but is owned by no one, it develops emergently based on community feedback and is in a constant state of flux, it is comprised of hundreds or thousands of small functional contributions or edits made by different people who will probably never meet each other, and may possess an infinite number of divergent "branches" or specific applications which assemble the available parts in different ways. Unlike most other innovations from the software industry, one thing opensource projects are not designed to do is generate profit for the people who create them. Opensource projects are free in both senses of the word. They may be seen as the application of socialist or anarchist philosophy to problems of technological innovation and knowledge distribution.

    I'm currently involved in (what may be the first?) opensource RPG design project, right here on this forum [DayTrippers]. I hope to spread more awareness not only of this project, but of the opensource development model in general, to RPG designers. Opensource is The Future(TM).

    \n@AsIf, aren't you conflating "open source" with "crowdsourced"? I mean, the idea of releasing RPG texts as free cultural works is pretty widespread by now: just check out the FOSsil Bank. My own works are available as such (only one "finished" English language game for now, I'm sorry), and indeed one of the many advantages is being able to keep refining and perfecting both design and text over a long time without ever feeling "at a fault" toward end-users who might have given me money at a given moment (possibly receiving a copy of the "most current state" of the text as a token of gratitude).
    But\u2026 the idea of licensing off game texts in independently published and creator-owned RPGs is at least as old as Clinton R. Nixon (one of the founding fathers of the Forge and, to my knowledge, a free software user/advocate) releasing The Shadow of Yesterday under permissive terms (might be full-on Public Domain, I would need to check) \u2014 which did in fact spark a significant "new edition" in the form of @Eero_Tuovinen's Solar System (which I think is licensed CC BY-SA or something close) and The World of Near.
    All of the above would qualify as "open source" projects in software code terms, even if the number of people involved in creating each is small (often as small as a single author-designer). Another significant implementation of the open source concept in role-playing games, explicitly taken from software coding, was with the d20 System: whatever one's overall opinion of it, one could conceivably think of the whole of D&D3 + compatible third party books as a single huge crowd-sourced effort. It also spawned new ideas or significantly affected more efforts, such as introducing the concept of a System Reference Document (SRD, now employed by such game engines as FATE or Gumshoe to initiate crowd-sourced projects affiliated with those brands) or producing the D&D retro-clones which, arguably, became a foundation for the OSR movement (though, ironically, you wouldn't actually need any license at all from the copyright holders of D&D texts to lawfully clone the game mechanics of D&D to any extent).

    Gosh, this became a long rambling post\u2026

    I was just trying to say: DayTrippers might well be the first purposeful crowd-sourced RPG design effort, for all I know, but I don't believe it accurate to call it the first "open source" one. And by saying this I don't mean to diminish the value of your work, not at all: rather, I think you should rejoice in the knowledge that you're not alone in your endeavors, at all, but indeed in quite the merry company.

    \n This is my last tangential post in Vulpinoid's thread. Just want to make clear one very important difference:
    In the word "crowdsourced", the morpheme "source" refers to the origin of the contributions.
    In the word "open source", the morpheme "source" refers to the source code.
    This is a big semantic difference. On reflection, that should be enough to clarify.
    PM me if you would like to discuss this further.
