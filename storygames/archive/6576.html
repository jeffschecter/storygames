\nGAMESMASHER: No Holds Barred Reviews From The Bleeding Edge Of Honesty

In this weekly column, Gamesstabber, I will provide the indie gaming scene with what it has hencesoforth lacked: fearless, brutal criticism of everything.

For too long, now, the indie community has told itself how great everything is. Critical voices, like mine, have been silenced. It's time to speak up and speak out.

So, with Gamesquasher, I put this right. I criticize everything, brutally and without fear, without pandering to the so-called "icons" of the gaming community. This is honesty that reaches beyond new honesty into postmodern honesty.

In an effort to sound authoritative, I use over-formal language and talk about "the consumer". I also send my reviews to two friends, before publication, so I can say they have been "peer reviewed".

But, most of all, you know these reviews are authoritative, because they are brutal, fearless and honest.

Let's start right here, right now and right away by criticizing those much-loved, much-praised pillars of the indie gaming community, polyhedral dice.

______________________________________________

THE D4

For a long time, the indie gaming community has loved the d4. It has been used in much-praised indie games from My Life With Master to Dogs In The Vineyard.

It is clear, however, that the D4 has one fatal flaw. Unlike other dice, the D4 does not have an upward side when rolled. The solution of putting numbers around the bottom edge (much beloved by the indie gaming community) is nothing more than a hack.

Any mathematician could have told the designer of the D4 this. However, it seems the D4 was rushed into production without consulting with a competent mathematician.

Gamestabber rating: FAIL


THE D6

Although the indie gaming community has appropriated the D6, it has in fact been used even before indie games, in games such as Backgammon, Clue and Chess.

It is unclear, however, why the consumer should pay for a D6 when, for the same money, they could have a D12 (getting twice the number of sides for their money) or D20 (getting 3.33 times the number of sides for their money).

In short, the D6, although well-designed, does not represent good value for the critical consumer.

Gamestabber rating: FAIL


THE D8

The central problem with the D8 is that it looks like a diamond. This harks back to games such as D&D, which contained many gemstones.

It is clear that, in appropriating the D8 for its so-called games, the indie gaming community has not moved on from D&D. If the designers of the D8 had employed a qualified jeweller in their design process, as they should have done if they were doing it right, they would have known this.

As it is, the D8 is a poor excuse for a die, badly designed and badly executed.

Gamestabber rating: FAIL


THE D10

The fatal flaw with the D10 is that it is not based around a platonic solid. Instead, in their enthusiasm to create a ten-sided die, the designers have jammed together ten sides into a rough diamond shape.

As such, the D10 suffers from the same problems as the D8, above. It is unclear why the consumer, for the same money, should not simply buy a D20 and cut it in half.

In my copy of the D10, there is an unforgivable printing error, in which the 1 has been missed out of the number 10. Hence, this D10 appears to roll only numbers from 0 to 9. This will, of course, make my games unplayable. This mistake should have been spotted in the design process.

Gamestabber rating: EPIC FAIL


THE D12

The D12 has one obvious flaw: it contains both the numbers 6 and 9. Since 9 can be read as 6, if turned upside down, this is unnecessary duplication.

However, this is a single flaw in an otherwise well-executed die. It is easily fixed: on my copy of the D12, I have crossed out the number "9" and put "13" instead, thus increasing the range of my D12 at a stroke.

Gamestabber rating: PARTIAL SUCCESS


THE D20

It is unforgiveable that there are no other sizes of die between D12 and D20. Had no-one in the indie gaming community thought to design a D15 or D17? This, itself, is a point against the D20.

However, the major problem with the D20 is that it is too prone to rolling over. Once you have rolled a number, a gust of wind is sufficient to change the number. I have tested this thoroughly in my bedroom and it is definitely true.

This central flaw with the D20 is enough to undermine any so-called "benefit" it may have had.

Gamestabber rating: FAIL


Next week, on Gamestabber, I will be aiming my bile at tables. Tables, for so long beloved by the indie gaming community, have gone too long being uncriticized. Do they need four legs? Why are they always horizontal? It's time for a fearless, brutal, critical look at tables, all aimed at benefitting the consumer and improving your game.
