    \n I love that shock: social science fiction tracks to the setup of a scientific experiment. In an experiment, you have the independent variable(s) (the thing(s) that causes the change) and the dependent variable(s) (the thing(s) that are changed). This is closely mapped in shock:. The shock (the science fictiony thing that touches off the world) is the independent variable and the issues are the dependent variables. That really makes me smile.

    \n I love Rafael Chandler's RPG Dread: The First Book of Pandemoneum in an entirely healthy yet obsessive way. I have 3 copies of the corebook. Each copy has a different cover.

    I love that, because of the focus, the simple rules and the enigmatic and creepy spells, the player characters take on asskickers who can kick ass and take names right out of the gate, without levelling up or power-playing: The magic is in the fact that the characters are all pretty much the dredgelings of society: They work to protect humanity, but remain totally unchained and unburdened by the need to do what is lawful in order to do what is right. It is the ultimate "Fuck the System" game, and yet it keeps a core of humanity. "Fuck the system in order to help and save people better".

    That is a theme that appeals to me greatly.

    -Andy

    \n OK, I'll do some games that others have talked about before going on to new ones, but I just have to drop this:
    In a Wicked Age is a goddamned phenomenal RPG.

    So, 15 years ago ARIA: Canticle of the Monomyth came out. It featured "Multi generational", and you could create PCs, PC tribes, nations, and so on and play them through time. But, in reality, you couldn't. And the author never explained how to do any of this above stuff (written on the back of the book), avoided all con demos and promos, and the game died. But that multi-generational roleplaying idea was a gadfly that bit a lot of us: Multi-generational. Just like those ancient stories or epic novels we like. Less of a focus on one single character, and more of a focus on them with the world they inhabit.

    Well, for me anyway, that element is perfectly captured in In a Wicked Age. It's like how Sorcerer is all about disfunctional relationships, IAWA is all about the stories that can be told with a pool of characters, focusing on the characters and the relationships they have (the world around them). And it does multi-generational right out of the box, with no fiddling or house rules. It now carries the baton that ARIA was said to have carried (but really didn't). Well, at least on the multi-generational aspect, not on the "PC nations/etc" aspect.

    So I love it because it does what ARIA was supposed to do, and better.

    -Andy

    \nArtesia: Adventures in the Known World, because the arcana system while complex produces all these wonderful byzantine feedback loops into play. You really can't appreciate this game unless you play a long version of it.

    Weapons of the Gods, as the River mechanics is delightful, being able to store dice has this dual tactical and tactile element which makes this game stand out.       \n There are lots of games where I like almost every bit, so I won't talk about any of those. Instead, I'll talk about an otherwise unremarkable game:

      I don't love Weapons of the Gods. But I do love the way it deals with setting information. Characters "buy" chunks of setting knowledge with destiny points, and there are mechanical effects of doing so. This is a very clever solution to the problem that reading novel-length setting information is rarely fun, but that setting-heavy games have a lot of appeal anyway.
