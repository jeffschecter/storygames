\n So, Game Chef felt kind of racist this year.

Basically this all centres around the "Coyote" ingredient. A certain kind of... group thought phenomenon, I don't know what it is exactly, tends to settle around some Game Chef ingredients, where one particular interpretation of an ingredient really dominates and shows up in lots of the games that use it. This year, that was "Coyote" with the interpretation "Native American trickster god".

Some of this made me feel uncomfortable. I'll say up front that I myself know next to nothing about real Native American culture or mythology. I abandoned my own game based around the Coyote ingredient for just this reason; I felt like anything I did would just be a recapitulation of shopworn (and racist!) Native American themes drawn from cinema and other pop culture.

Of course, Native American cultural appropriation is all over the place, from dreamcatchers to the Washington Redskins, and a lot of it gets passed over or waved way, largely because of how successful a genocide was perpetrated against them, meaning that there are few left to complain about it. But cultural appropriations and aggressions of this kind exist at many levels of varying blatancy, and Game Chef is no exception.

I don't want to single out particular games; this is more of a collective impression viewed over the competition as a whole rather than being informed by a close reading of any games in particular.

If you're not yourself from a particular culture or ethnicity, or are not sufficiently immersed in it, it can be difficult both to a) utilise referents from that culture respectfully and appropriately and b) see why the inappropriate and disrespectful ways in which you might want to use them are disrespectful and inappropriate.

Game Chef, by its nature a seat-of-the-pants get-inspiration-then-write-it-fast week-long game jam deal, does not lend itself to thorough respectful research and sober reflection on inter-cultural ethics. So I'm hardly surprised that we ended up with this kind of result.

But I feel like, as a community, this is something worth highlighting and being aware of. The hobby as a whole has had a terrible history with regard to minority representation and cultural appropriation; but a lot of it is inadvertent, brought on by simple ignorance or lack of awareness. Consciousness needs to be raised.

    \n Native appropriation is the easiest racism to engage in as an American, because it's one you never have to think about. The Native Americans we picture in our head aren't the same people we pass on the street, because if they aren't wearing a headdress most people couldn't pick a Native American out of a lineup of other vaguely brown people. I grew up in Montana, near a Blackfoot Reservation, and this was still true. You only heard about Natives with feathers on their heads or a bottle in their hands. It was disgusting. Even the most open-minded, liberal, race-sensitive people I knew in Montana didn't think anything of appropriating their food and culture and stereotyping them as drunks and criminals.

    I heard from a friend that he mentioned to someone else how racially problematic GC was this year, and the response was basically "Well, we've already done everything we can to the Native Americans so what does it matter? We can't make it worse."

    And that's the big lie. That they don't exist any more. That the caricatures we think of when we think of Native Americans belong to us. That we, as non-natives, can write about them and remix their history and their culture because it's not like we're going to run into a Native who is going to be EXTREMELY UNCOMFORTABLE due to our behavior. We're okay. I mean, we have black friends and everything.

    Note the "we." I am not absolving myself of this, although I did not participate in GC this year (largely because of the ingredients). I agree with what Hans is saying, about there being no Native side vs. White side. But a big part of that is the fact that for so many, when we think Native American our first thought is not an actual person we have met before. And if you have that little interaction with a culture, you shouldn't take it as your own. Shit, I've been a part of a crazy Indian family for four years now and I wouldn't feel comfortable writing a game about Indian culture the way that these games are written about Natives.

    \n After trying, and failing, to start this post six different ways, here's what I got. I have this love/hate relationship with appropriation. A lot of appropriation is exuberant, if ignorant, appreciation of the subject matter. Being forgiving and a flawed human being myself, I understand how and why that happens. Being a sometimes artist, I value the aesthetic and power that some images, concepts, and symbols have even if they're not my own. To an extent, I also feel that we don't own the contents of our cultures. They are us, but they're also bigger than us.

    But it's also obvious that a lot of appropriation takes place without any internal checks or balances from those doing the appropriating. Exuberance isn't enough. Appreciation isn't enough. We also have to ask ourselves, "Does this in any way impinge upon the dignity of real, living human beings." If we don't care it's going to be obvious. Even if we do care, but fail to really examine and weigh our desires versus the emotional welfare of others, it's probably going to look like we don't care. We don't even have to necessarily get it all right, but we do have to try.

    The Native American aspects of some of the games have gotten a lot of attention. That's understandable considering the extent of, and still ongoing state of, their oppression. I'm curious how people feel about the other games that have elements from cultures foreign to the designers. Just that I know of, we have games with Persian, Japanese, and South American content. What makes the elements of some of these other games different than the Native American content? Is there no difference? I ask out of genuine curiosity. Appropriation is a sticky subject. It doesn't help that it's been going on since ancient times, as the neighbors of powerful civilizations absorbed cultural elements from them and conquerors absorbed the cultures of the conquered.

    \nSo, appropriation in general is an issue. Appropriating someone else's culture is sticky, and maybe OK and maybe not, depending on the context.

    The thing that happens with appropriation of Native American culture, though, that may or may not be happening with appropriation of the cultures you mention, is that the folks appropriating Native American culture (by and large) have directly benefited from the oppression of Natives. There is an unequal power relationship to this day. You can say, "that's not my fault, that's my ancestors or someone else's ancestors" or whatever. This isn't about laying blame. It's about being cognizant of reality and what that means for your responsibility.

    Speaking as myself, a white U.S. citizen, appropriating Native culture is a direct extension of that oppression, and that oppression is why it's easy to appropriate Native culture (as Elizabeth has said).

    If you want to get down to details, I'm Norwegian-American. Writing a game about the German-American experience, as me, while still problematic, is vastly different from writing about the Native American experience. You can see why, right?

    \n This issue was first really brought to my attention back in... 2007? The Art First Game Chef, whenever that was. One of the art sets was a bunch of tiki-headed dudes, often with sci-fi-like trappings, and I was totally going to write a game about that until people started mentioning that maybe reducing the symbols of another culture -- one I didn't know anything about -- to cartoons like that wasn't cool. I wasn't sure where I landed on that, but I did know that whatever I did wasn't going to make any sort of interesting statement about tiki or Polynesian cultures or anything, so I dropped it. (Then I wrote a game about cavemen and dinosaurs. Hubris!)

    And I'll admit, I purposely steered clear of the Coyote ingredient this time around partially because of that memory. I mean, I also didn't have any great ideas for Coyote, and it seemed like it'd be a popular ingredient because it's immediately evocative of something, but the danger of resurrecting that cultural-appropriation issue was a part of it, too.

    I certainly think the ingredient could be (and in some cases was) used tastefully and thoughtfully. If you're using it to evoke Native American cultures, though, you have to go into it knowing what kind of statement you're making about those cultures. (This is a reason I think Liam really nailed it with Big Chiefs, because he managed to flip that around and make something of a meta-statement about the whole idea of white people making a statement about Native Americans.)

    stefoid, the simple answer is: There are actually real, live people of color who are actively involved in playing and creating games. For instance, I am one of them.

    Whenever you create any kind of act that draws upon a real-world culture, no matter what culture it is, it is your own personal responsibility to educate yourself about that culture, and failing that, pay someone a fair wage so that you can use their knowledge, so that you can incorporate that culture into your work with respect and understanding.     \n "Coyote" is not a racist thing in and of itself, just as "ice" isn't racist in and of itself. There are countless ways "coyote" could be used in a game and not come off as racist. How about a game about the Roadrunner and Wile E. Coyote? How about use coyote in the context of a pack of coyotes? They sound like (mimic) laughing children. There's two elements right there. How about the notion that when coyotes come into contact with humans, bad things happen? Coyotes routinely hunt and kill cats and dogs in our area and they're one of the few animals you can legally use bait when hunting them. Is that racist? It sucks for coyotes, but it's not racist. Killing coyotes because they're considered nuisance animals when they "invade" your country homestead and kill your cat (who should be indoors anyway if you live in the woods) certainly reflects the arrogance of humanity, but not racism.

    Of course any of the above coyote elements can be modified in a hateful, racist way. Or not.

    I used coyote in my game. Originally one of the PCs was an actual coyote. Then he became a kitsune, because I set the game in a mythological Japanese setting, and because I know next to nothing about Native American mythology. On the other hand, I could write a thesis about Japanese folklore and its use in modern and historic works. The most I know about Old Man Coyote in Native American folklore is that like kitsune, he's considered a trickster archetype. I might have changed coyote the trickster into a Brer Rabbit-type character if I had changed the setting to an African American folkloric setting. What would have been ridiculous cultural appropriation is if I had taken Old Man Coyote and put him in my Japanese mythological setting without any context or valid explanation. That would have been a cop out, too. Roadrunner vs. Wile E. would have made a better use of the element. I personally have a stronger association with "coyote" as cat-killing, laughing children-mimicking, dog-like wild animals that I am psyched to see and hear on the rare occasions that I see and hear them. They're like foxes, too, right? And in folklore they're used as trickster archetypes like foxes, rabbits, spiders, and wolves. I don't know enough about Old Man Coyote and Native American folklore to use "coyote" in such a game where I have less than a week to write. Native American folklore is too broad a term to even touch in less than a week. Which Native American culture?

    "Trickster" isn't racist either. The trickster archetype is universal and has a variety of functions, depending on the culture it's in. So Brer Rabbit in African American folklore represents the slave. He always outwits Brer Fox, and in doing so attains something of value, like a good meal or some measure of rest and safety. In Japanese folklore, there are stories of kitsune who take the form of a beautiful woman, marries a human man, and they have a family. Someone betrays her (often unintentionally) and reveals her true nature. She leaves her human family behind, never to return.

    If you take some element of a culture about which you know little, and you use that element without proper context, you're engaging in cultural appropriation. It's irresponsible and lazy at the very least, and racist at its worst. Being ironic or doing this accidentally is still irresponsible, lazy, and possible racist. Ironic racism and accidental racism are still racism. So non-game related example: I have a friend who is a tattoo artist. He generally will not do kanji or Chinese characters in tattoos because he has no reliable source of translation, and he doesn't want to do a tattoo of the word "shit baby" when the client hands him a character to copy (that he copied from a website), and the client thinks it says "wise and clever." Perhaps the onus is really on the client, but my friend feels a great deal of responsibility as the artist and doesn't want to cause harm to his client.

    So those of us who decided to use coyote in our games really owed it to ourselves and our games and the community as a whole to look at other facets of "coyote," and avoid misuing it. I didn't see anywhere in the Game Chef rules that stated "coyote" had to carry Native American connotations with it. A coyote can just be a coyote in a game with no Native American themes and it can be a perfectly good game.

      \n I think there has been a lot of really interesting discussion here and was impressed at the thoughtfulness displayed, especially in the early posts.

      The idea that I haven't seen discussed is "why are we appropriating things from other cultures?" Thinking about that question leads to some insight into appropriateness and sensitivity.
      For me it falls into a couple of different categories. Sometimes it is a shorthand for a stereotyped idea. This is used positively and negatively. When we want a character to represent independence and toughness we make him a cowboy. When we want the expectation to be combative and drunk we make him Irish.

We want divinity to play an active role, but we don't want it to be too "real" so we grab for another culture's stories. The insensitivity comes from dismissing another's religion as, "just stories." I don't know if there are still practitioners of traditional religions in Scandinavia, but there are lots of Native Americans for whom those myths are active and vibrant part of cultural heritage.
      In my last grouping we learn a little bit about something and that sliver captures our imagination. Rather than learning what lies beyond the boundaries of that sliver we just magnify it until it fills the space we want filled. I think this is where the Chinese character tattoos fall. Someone gets entranced by the shape without bothering to understand the meaning. We associate people of a particular culture with toughness and throw their likeness on the side of a football helmet. The idea that divinity has a playful, mischievous side reflects something in our experience we want to understand better, so we take another culture's stories of a divine trickster and put it into a game pulling it from the cultural context.

To have people react badly to your actual game (not your postings about it), is a signal that you have failed, possibly by ignoring some of the challenges inherent in the chosen theme, or not handling them well.       \n Here's my conclusions for this thread, as I see it.

      I understand why "appropriation" is so central in your relationship to indian culture; it has been the victim of much of that kind, having been forced to see others "interpreting" their culture en'masse. In films for sure, and maybe in games too. So I do see how this is important for you, and why you react when something is claimed to be cultural appropriation.

      But ...

      APPROPRIATION
      The claims that certain games/text in the Game Chef competition is "appropriating" indian culture in a bad way, has not been convincingly put. It is not enough to cite a text, and say it is "appropriating". Self-evident discourse is not sufficient. I expect people to say more; how they appropriate, and what consequences said appropriation may have. None of the texts cited appropriate indian culture in any bad way. I will say that they use elements from indian culture in a creative and constructive way, to inspire good design. There is nothing bad in it.

      RACISM
      The claims that any of the Game Chef texts are "racist", is totally unfounded. I have seen no evidence to that effect, and have heard no arguments close to being convincing. None of the texts cited has any racism to them.

      THE PREMISE FOR ROLE-PLAYING GAMES
      As I see it, the term "appropriating" is troublesome. At least in the way it is used here. It is used in a way that is anathema to role-playing games.

      Role-playing games is all about "walking in the shoes of another being", and that is only possible if we accept the fact that some understanding is required to do this, and indeed; may be gleaned from doing it; that when you play another being you actually unlock a deeper understanding of how that being act, talk, think and feel. The whole idea of role-playing games hinges on this premise.

      Those of us a bit experienced in playing serious roles in rpgs, know this premise to be true; we do unlock a deeper understanding of other beings by playing them.

      REDUCING HUMANS TO BIGOTS
      The term "appropriating" has been used to hammer home that only indians can talk about indians here, or in game texts. All others doing that must be "bad" in some way, according to the ones propagating this view. This is a view of the world (and indians) that totally disregard the human capacity of understanding other humans, showing empathy and feeling sympathy. It disregard our capacity to understand how our actions may affect other human beings, and our capacity to predict and understand the actions of others.

      I do not believe in such a reductionist view of humans. We are far better. Most humans are not bigots, but socially intelligent beings with strong ethics to guide them. We are fallible, but also conscientious and forgiving, and able to create joyful interaction with our fellow beings. That is at the core of being human.
