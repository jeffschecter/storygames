\n I have loved random character creation rules since forever. I know a lot of people dislike rolling 3d6 down the row of your stats in D&D, but man I always thought it was great. I love seeing what a random system spits out and making sense of it.

So last night we make characters for Houses of the Blooded and I sit down with this vague character concept of a cross between Peter Steele and a sort of proto-Buddha - depressed, angsty, self-destructive, but also wise and compassionate and forsaking material possessions and whatnot. Then we randomly roll parents families and I have to interpret that. Father's a loremaster and trader in secrets, mother's a crazy warrior (and the more dominant one). I'm totally seeing parental expectations and disappointment. Randomly roll birth order. Firstborn? Oof. Okay, this is really making sense. I roll a younger brother - totally set up to be the ambitious antagonist and usurper of the firstborn, and might even have the mother's blessing to do so.

And the thing is anything I rolled could make sense. Sometimes it's not apparent at first but all it takes is some creative mental gymnastics and the results are usually cool. My character could just as easily been the youngest of six and he would be angsty because he was ignored and he would have forsaken material gain because nothing was expected of him.

Doing that, taking random input and spitting out fiction, is pretty much a distillation of why I love gaming so having that in chargen is just another part of play.

    \n I love random chargen, so long as it doesn't affect the character's overall effectiveness (whatever that may mean for the game in question). Backgrounds and story-level info is great in this sense.

    If it's something like D&D, though, then there had better be some reason why the character with really high stats can play alongside the guy with really wimpy stats, and they can both have fun.

    I really liked how Ryan's unnamed game had you roll random stats but then gave you a higher level or more abilities if you rolled poorly.

    Even better if the chargen process is a bit of a gamble. In my Fudgeified version of Red Box Hack, you have some risky choices to make in chargen ("Am I willing to risk a really low score in another ability to get an exceptionally high score in this ability NOW?").

    Those types of "checks and balances" make it more fun for me. For instance, imagine something like PTA, but with some kind of character effectiveness stats added in. In that game, maybe rolling higher stats would mean you have a lower Screen Presence...

    \n Two games of late with random chargen:

    MAID RPG: Yeah yeah, I don't mean to plug, but it's applicable. Technically "roll or choice", but if you go by your rolls, literally the only thing you decide about your character is her (his) name. Everything from abilities, quirks, speical abilities, weapons, traumas, affects, and the reasons for becoming a maid are all generated randomly.

    Normally, I hate this. For some reason, with Maid I love this. I think it's because you're not tied to this character for a year: You'll play 1-3 sessions with this one character and move on.

    HELLAS: You decide all the numeric stuff yourself: Stats, skills, and the story stuff like "Your Goal", "Your Personality/Relationship Drawbacks", etc. Thing is, you decide stuff like family relationships, events that happened in the job paths you've chosen, etc by random rolls.
    This... this was awesome. I had a vague concept of my character "A Nephelai (pale dude with wings) Ambassador-Type!", but nothing about his personality or history. Based on a few random rolls, I weaved this whole mini-backstory about losing a good friend in battle, and inheriting his blade. Then, as a senator, we found that he made enemies (which worked their way into both my character's goals as well as personal drawbacks, which I got to decide). Meanwhile, his "family" is looking for him for some reason, hunting him down for some reason that will become clear in play.

    Here's the character. The random roll stuff is the stuff at the bottom of the page, but since I did that first, that influenced a lot of the other things on the character sheet that I was able to decide for myself:

    Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles, Erick Wujcik
    RANDOM CHARACTER GENERATION: A Rationalization
    Some readers will be surprised to discover that there is a "point system" for creating the animal characters in this book. There is a good reason for it; we want to make sure that every player could create an animal character to suit themselves. Which, if you think about it, is really not all that different from the way things are handled in Heroes Unlimited. After all, Bionic and Robotic characters also build themselves. But this game, just like Heroes Unlimited (and most other Palladium games), is based on random character generation.
    There's a fair amount of controversy in the roleplaying game community about character generation. Chiefly, some players complain that it is not as much fun or as challenging to roll up characters off a table as it is to 'create' the characters.
    There is some validity to the argument. Regardless of our personal opinions on the subject you are free to do what you will with the game. If you are a game master, then simply let the players create their characters according to your own preferences. If you are a player, then lobby your game master for whatever changes you feel will make for the most playable game. However, here are a few good reasons to retain the random character generation system:
    1. Excellent players can role-play ANYTHING; granted, the play testers that I and the other folks of Palladium have at their disposal are superb. An excellent player can play any character, and sees a weak character as a challenge, not a liability.
    When running my own campaigns, I frequently deprive characters of all their various powers and possessions. It is when the character is in the direst of straits that fantastic role-playing comes out.
    Let's look at one example. Recently a pair of players in fantasy game were captured by enemies. They managed to escape, but they found themselves lost in a strange desert with nothing but a piece of wire. The roleplaying went on for several weeks, alternating between the lost survivors dodging armoured hunters and straining to get water, shelter and food in hostile land. It was an intensely interesting role-playing adventure.
    2. Another advantage of random character generation is in the convenience that it provides to the game master. Instead of laboriously 'constructing' every new villain and N.P.C., the game master can just 'roll-up the new encounters. This makes scenario design and quick response to player actions much easier.
    3. Finally, let's not forget that random rolls reflect real life. Even in the 'perfect' lives of fantasy characters there is no control over their origins. Super characters are usually created by accident or by forces over which the character has little or no control.

    \n The Over the Edge Player's Guide provided a sort of random character generation system called CUTUP, where you rolled on a table and got key words to associate meaningfully with your character somehow. I always loved this, because it was both random and yet still required you to choose how you were going to interpret the data.

    So as an example, you could get the words "doubt", "heart", and "training". (This is, in fact, the example from the book.) Right off the top of my head, that suggests to me a person who was initiated into a secret cult at a young age, sent to Al Amarja to commit ritual assassinations for the cult that require him to consume the hearts of his enemies. However, some event that occurred in his past has left him unsure of the rightness of the cult's cause, and he isn't sure if he should complete the killings or try to escape the cult's influence.

    It also suggests to me an ex-champion martial artist who fights underground after being falsely accused of doping up in the ring. He wants to prove his worthiness as a fighter to scores of doubters, and he's heard that the people who set him up regularly do business deals on Al Amarja, and that the fighting circuit there is particularly profitable and brutal... all the better to test his skills and perhaps attain revenge...

    Three words, scores of possible characters. Probably one of my favorite chargen devices ever.
