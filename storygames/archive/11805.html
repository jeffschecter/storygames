\n Many may know that Paul and I attended STAPLE! Austin at the beginning of the month. STAPLE! started out as a comic show and while the organizer is serious about expanding the scope to other indie media, its still 90 to 95% comics. A day or two before the show, I thought I should have some kind of signage to go along with Kagematsu, something that says just what this weird looking book is about. As Paul and I sat in the hotel\u2019s business center the night before the show brainstorming what to say, I must have been channeling the undergrad classes in museum studies about statics for interpretative text:
\xb7\t8th grade reading level
\xb7\tshould take no longer than 20-30 seconds
\xb7\tfont changes, paragraph breaks etc for emphasis and to draw the eye

So what I needed to do was have my sign make a person: A- read it; B- pick up the book; and C- be compelled to buy it. So here\u2019s what I came up with.

\tsignStaple Sign

Did it work?
Paul and I had the same regular guy off the aisle customer sales (he had some retail sales which I am excluding). We were at the end of a row and people DID stop to read it. Without being exact, more people stopped to read the sign than pick up Paul\u2019s game and read the back of it.

What went wrong?
\xb7\tI didn\u2019t give much thought into the text or format, it could be refined more. Some image or symbols added?
\xb7\tThe hotel printer was ass and had little toner so it was not very dark and it really lost some dynamic quality.
\xb7\tThis was still a comic show. People wanted to relate it somehow with what I had and asked if I had done the illustrations.

We are going to a local creativity arts show in June and I intend to improve my signage for the show and see again how it works in practice. So, here is the discussion:
\xb7\tIs the existing signage compelling as a narrative? Draws the eye?
\xb7\tWhat do you think of the use of museum interpretative theory representing games to a non gaming crowd?

    \n I've done tables and booths and I've attended lots of conventions and trade shows, and so before this I had pretty much believed I'd seen the general range of effective presentation of games. What's the likelihood of coming up with something different that's more effective than what folks who've been at this for years are doing?

    So I was pretty surprised at how strong this little slip of signage was. We had a eight foot table, a very nice black fabric tablecloth, and just a few products laid out (Kagematsu, Bitch, My Life with Master, Bacchanal, and my Well of Else cards), so the whole thing was already a bit like a curated museum display. But the little slip of signage was really grabby. People don't know what to do with an object like My Life with Master or Kagematsu. (The saddle-stitched form factor and page count suggests they might be comic books. But then they're full of text. And the prices are too high for comic books. And they say they're games, but what kind of game is all text and no game pieces?) But everyone knows what to do with museum style interpretive signage. You read it. And it tells you how to appreciate what you're looking at.

    Yes, this time it didn't translate to sales. But the number of people who read the little slip of signage that didn't pick up My Life with Master really has my head turning on the potential of it. Absolutely My Life with Master and Bacchanal will have such signage, done on nice cardstock, when we're at SMACC in July.

    Paul

    \n I may be part of the ADHD/MTV generation, but I think a marketing sign could be a little more succinct. You are vying for the attention of showfloor-stunned passer-bys, after all.

    A marketing pitch has about 10-20 seconds to tell someone:

    1. What is this thing?
    2. What's in it for me?
    3. Where can I get this? (URL?)

    Additional details and color of the game can on the table. People lured from sign to table are probably "good leads" for sales.

    I would also format the text to use both larger and smaller fonts to create visual interest and lead the eye from most-to-least important text.

    \n Some critique:

    1. If you've never played an RPG before, the text is difficult to understand. Hell, its hard for me to understand. I would simplify. For example:
    A samurai speaks to a woman of a small village. "No," the samurai says, "I have not seen the fireflies in the meadow at dusk. Perhaps I could escort you this evening?" But the woman's spoiled younger sister, passing by with but a coy glance behind a fan, steals the samurai's attention, and his heart." (or something) I think this is a more clearly framed situation. Describing the actual narrative of the game. Then when you say: "In a single evening 3-5 of your friends can play such and such characters", you can get into which gender can play what and who can take on what roles (ie men playing women and women playing men).

    2. Illustrate this situation in general! Have a samurai and a girl on the poster! Its not just about katanas, its about love, right! Illustrate that idea somehow. Even the poster says "Its about the collaborative definition of love." Show that.

    3. I love the idea of RPGs being curated. Describing them in such a manner takes it up a notch in the seriousness that they're approached with, as well as their value as an art form. Awesome.

    \n My instinct was similar to anasigirl's. Aggressively red-line that isn't directly supporting your main "sales thrust." Currently, your sign is trying to yell "gender-bending indie RPG!" which is arguably hard to approach and/or unintelligible to the target audience. Now, this is basically the pitch that got me to look at the game, and then buy it after a demo at last GenCon, but I am likely not much like the audience in question. So, working from anasigirl's text:

    KAGEMATSU

    The samurai turns his attention from the small village's meager defenses to glance at the confident young woman. "No," the samurai says, "I have not seen the fireflies in the meadow at dusk. Perhaps I could escort you this evening?" But the woman's spoiled younger sister, with but a coy glance from behind her fan, steals the samurai's attention, and then his heart.

    In a single evening, 3-5 of your friends will play out the fate of a threatened Japanese village, and the fates of the samurai and village women who woo him.


    Quickly jab out with color, brief description, and an easy-to-understand description of "what is this?" I was quickly trying to evoke Seven Samurai to work on my side. I'm soft-selling gender issues, RPG, and indie for now.

    And then someone either starts asking/investigating or not.
