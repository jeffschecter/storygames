\n Hey, this is just a thought, and one that might already be being implemented, but I thought I'd throw it out there.

So you buy an ashcan game -- simple layout, a little art, saddle-stitched, and so on -- for X dollars. One of the last pages is a coupon with a registration code and a URL. You go to the site, fill out a middlingly-specific form asking you about your AP and addressing specific playtesting questions, and you get Y dollars off of the finished product game when it comes out.

To my mind, this has the following advantages:
a) You have a much greater chance of getting useful, actionable, specific feedback.
b) You increase your sales of the finished product.
c) You can track (via registration code) how many ashcans produced feedback.
d) You collate the feedback you do get into one place.
e) You can easily produce a list of playtesters for the credits page.
f) You can easily turn this into a registrations-at-website drive and get participants in forums and the like.

Questions:
What should $X and $Y be, and what relationship should they have? $10 cost, $5 coupon seems "about right" to me, but what are the parameters?
How much server-side tech would this really require? At the low-tech end, could this be "start a forum thread and answer these three questions"?
Would you do this? Would you feel a part of the process if the ashcan was "interactive" like this? If so, what part tickles your fancy? If not, why not?

    \n Hey, Paul.

    Personally, I'm a bit of an odd duck because I literally playtest stuff every week. That said, and with the base assumption that the game in all three situations in interesting and grabby, I think I'd be most likely to provide feedback, and provide elaborate feedback, under option (a).

    To me, the explicit understanding that buying the ashcan is buying into a larger process, that I will get a discount off the finished product, and that I will have the finished product in my hands at some point, really drives it home for me. The coupon makes me a stakeholder in the finished game, whereas simply buying the ashcan -- no matter what the cost is -- means I bought the opportunity to participate in the process. Buying the ashcan under (b) and (c) can be a fire-and-forget sort of deal, where I will not feel like I lost anything if I go home and don't actually playtest; buying the ashcan with the coupon, on the other hand, would make me feel like I'm part of the process, on the team, and I have some assured interaction with the creator/publisher in the future.

    As to the second question, (a) increases the chances that at the end of the process I'll have a finished product in my hand, whereas (b) and (c) have far less encouragement to do so. It's a lot easier feeling satisfied with my participation in a creative effort when I have what came out of it in my hand. Which is sort of a dodgy answer, but the bulk of any satisfaction would be the quality and reception of the game when it was published -- which of these three options the ashcan worked under would be, I think, rather minor comparatively.

    Now I turn the tables! What about you, Paul?
