Judson_Lester

This is going to be the most inarticulate junk ever, and I'm mostly looking to be disabused of this notion, but here it is:

It seems to me that one of the basic principles of role-playing theory is that the more concretely we understand what we're doing, the better our play will be. Ron and Josh's thread over at the Forge has led to the coinage "murk" (or possibly only brought it up) to refer to play that isn't conscience of technique. Now, while I think that theory can directly improve our design, I'm realizing that I'm beginning to believe that rigid taxonomy is injurious to actual at the table play.

I'm not saying that no one interested in role-playing should ever examine theory. But I think theory needs to be internalized before it's useful. Like a bicycle. You can build a bike, and ride it, with no idea how it works. But it takes a lot of study to understand how it works and improve it. But you're not going to learn to ride a bike (or get to be an Olympic cyclist) by reading a book. But someone needs to be reading the book to help you get to the Olympics, you know?

Now that I've qualified that into trivia, does anyone have any input?

    For some reason people increasinly miss where the arrow is pointed in Ron's discussions. The disconnect always seems to be here:

    Player A does X and Player B does Y in response. (Where "does" can be almost anything. Announcing an in character action, describing a bit of scenery, ending a scene, starting a scene, speaking dialogue, whatever....)

    The fundamental mistake people make when discussing Forge theory is to attribute an idea to either A, X, B or Y individually. Most (not all) of the ideas in theory have to do with the space inbetween. The social interaction, the communication, the cues, the tells, the unspoken language, whatever the hell you want to call it.

    Murk in that discussion is refering to Player A doing X and (given the game and social context) what is the range of EXPECTED acceptable RESPONSES Player B can give. I've played in games with all kinds of murk. I've played games where I've done X fully expected any reponse at all and instead getting a huffy player because clearly I *want* them do some specific Y in response and they don't think is cool. I've played in games where I've done X fully expecting a very specific Y in response and found myself getting huffy because the Y I get is competely out of scope as far as I'm concerned.

    Illuminating what purpose action X serves and what are appropriate Y responses can only help make a game more clear in my opinion. And that can be done without one lick of theory or jargon.

    Ok, I've read over Ron's post again - and here's something I take away from it: You only need to worry about murkiness if you're completely intent on a driving narrative.

    Here's the part of Ron's post that tipped me off:
    Ron, on the Forge:Historically, role-playing is practically defined by a bunch of techniques hidden or at least isolated within a bunch of unconstructed dialogue. When I say "unconstructed," I don't merely mean "not rolling dice," and not even "open dialogue." I mean: primal chaos that cannot itself produce a solution (in the technical sense of that term). No one knows what this interaction is supposed to be like. No one knows how it's supposed to get anywhere or accomplish anything. Gamer culture avoids the question as if it's a deadly disease; "game-mastering advice" in a hundred texts dances around it and focuses instead on mood and superficial style, with few exceptions. Existing solutions are guesses, tacit interjections, tacit impositions, and often desperation measures.
    Emphasis mine.

    Look at the language: 'how it's supposed to get anywhere or accomplish anything'. This implies that if a scene is being played out, ipso facto that scene must be 'going somewhere' and 'accomplishing something'. This is at the heart of narrative RPG play - not all RPG play. In RPG play closer in style to, say, GTA III - what I'd call environmental play - the players are making decisions within an environment loaded with play potential. In that mode of play, Ron's idea of 'murk' is pointless, since the purpose any given scene is existential - the scene is its own means and end.

    Through what Ron's writing, it's clear that he has no use for something like 'traditional' dungeon delving - with the players moving their characters through an environment. Or rather, not a mode of play where the in-between bits of dungeon delving are played out. In the murkless dungeon, it's: ENTRANCE, MONSTER, MONSTER, TRAP, ANGSTY DIALOGUE, TRAP, DIALOGUE, [...], FINAL BOSS; with every scene being played being a significant, driving event.

    Again, a perfectly valid mode of play - but what needs to be recognized here is that Ron's talking about a very specific mode of narrative play, and not a general end-state that all play should eventually reach. In other words - there are modes of play that are intentionally murky.

    And to address PTA: That's another game that is very fiercely narrative. Every character a scene, every scene a purpose. This is because it's emulating another narrative mode, the television show. And so it fits within the murkless ideal.

    [To elaborate on Chris]
    Getting rid of the murk in a dungeon-crawl without it turning to chess:
    Beginning your first delve. The characters are going to need to need a rationale to work together. These are three tried and tested options:

    1a. Each player should write down either a relationship between their PC and another, or a desire that only another PC can help them achieve. Once character generation is complete, this is subject to review by the GM.
    1b. The first part of the adventure will then be 'group formation'. This is a scene or a series of scenes where the indidivuals are brought together by special circumstances and the ties that connect them (think of the Magnificent 7 or Ocean's 11 for this).
    2. The characters are part of a unit from the beginning - an elite black ops team, a sect of believers, or a revolutionary organisation. You could employ a short version of step 1b or simply cut to a meeting of the group.
    3. The characters meet through happenstance, but in a way that forces them to recognise the merits of acting as a team. They could all turn up to loot the dungeon simultaneously, and find strength in numbers; a character might be battling out of the dungeon after an aborted raid, and the other recognises that their first hand experience will be vital.

    We strongly recommend you choose one of these options or have a clear and articulated idea of an alternative. For an experience of real and immersive play (the play that Angels of the Earth hopes to give you) it's vital that where the PCs are and who they are with makes sense.
    This should not be 'expert GM' advice picked up by trawling the web or Dragon magazine. This is fundamental information for the fun play of the game. Sure, you can come up with it from the top of your head, just as you could come up with the movement rules to Cluedo if they are omitted. Why should you? It also has nothing to do with supporting a narrative-only style of play [edit: what I meant was thematic play; I'm not sure what narrative-only is.].

    I think the OP is nonsense, to be blunt.

    Can better understanding lead some people to better gaming? Undoubtedly.

    Does it follow that better understanding necessarily leads to better gaming, of course not.

    Firstly, there is an assumption that the players are interested in a theme. In most groups I doubt that, the players are likely to be there for a variety of reasons, only some of which may be caught by the concept of theme, and such themes as interest them may not be common to each other or even consistent moment to moment.

    Creating interesting challenges and creating an interesting world are things that are routine to decent gaming, most groups that are at all stable and having fun manage this, only some give any thought at all to theory. Logically, it must follow that theory is not necessary to fun.

    Can theory help fun? For some people sure, particularly if you are not having fun and are having trouble working out why. Is theory necessary to fun? No, if it were our hobby would not exist. People play for fun, a very small number are even aware of the theories out there, if theory were needed for fun the people without knowledge of theory would not be having fun and so would not play.

    Would everyone have more fun with more theory? I sincerely doubt that, people are already having plenty of fun. Some might have more with theory, but as I think Joshua posted in another thread recently people have different learning models and only some benefit from preverbalising activities in this way.

    This murkiness is a chimera, created in the main by people who were not having fun and found theory helpful in finding out why and then make a false generalisation from their unfun experiences to other people's fun experiences. It's an error of extrapolation, a dead end. I'm glad those guys found a way to fun, that makes this a useful endeavour for those people and others in their situation, but it is an error to extrapolate that to the experiences of people who never faced that particular problem.

    On the First Point: With Ron-Murk, "Clear Procedures" are not the same as "we have a rule or mechanic for everything that happens in play." Just whatever procedures there are, they should be clear. I submit, Max, that your stated preference is NOT Ron-murk, but rather a particular aligning of procedural preferences for your desired playstyle. Murk is not "we have rules for A and B, and for C we just decide informally as it comes up." Murk is Person 1 going in thinking there are rules for C, and wondering with shock and confusion why Person 2 just skipped right past any rules-procedure and decided C informally.

    Regarding Judson-murk--whether to invoke/consciously apply theory at the table--I think there's a misunderstanding of theory (theory theory, nut just RPG theory) going on here. A lot of folks are talking as if people who "just play,"without thinking through their processes or techniques (and have fun) are not "using" theory. But theory, inasmuch as it is a description of how something works, is in operation regardless of the knowledge of the practitioner. A self-taught guitarist may play a chord progression, having NO idea what the explanation in Music Theory is for that collection of sounds and why it produces a desired result. He just knows it sounds sweet, and sounds sweeter than it would if he played some different progression. That guitarist is applying theory. Even folks like me who have studied music theory, when we sing or play we aren't by and large thinking, "OK, now the piece is concluding, so I'm singing the root of a V to I chord, creating a Perfect Cadence." We just do it, and maybe afterward think, "Man, that Perfect Cadence is sweet!" And in fact, that's how Music Theory developed, from people looking at different collections of sounds and noting which ones were most pleasing and satisfying to the ear.

    Same with RPG theory. If you hear Ron talk about his old Champions play (the videos Settembrini has up at the Forge are fascinating!), he describes striving toward Narrativism and utilizing techniques to that end, long before he knew what "narrativism" or the Big Model or anything else was. Formulating the theory helped him identify what that "itch" was he was trying to scratch, and develop techniques to get it more satisfyingly and consistently. So I think the dichotomy being erected of "apply theory at the table" and "just play and have fun" is a false one. By all means, just play and have fun--and if you're having trouble with some specific part of the fun, there may be a technique (Forge or otherwise) to help you. Implementing it may seem awkward the first few times, but with practice it can become as fluid and internalized as your previous palette of techniques.

    Second, on the "us vs. them" thing: this is a total red herring, and one that's taking up much of the debate. This isn't a case of "our awesome mirkless indie games" vs. "Your stupid mirkful trad games." At least that's not how I read Ron's comments. Yes, he says that a lot of older games are more mirky, but as someone said, that's just to be expected in the case of a developing artform. Yes, many indie games are crystal-clear about play procedure. We can endlessly debate the reasons for that, but it's not a technique only suited to narr-facilitating (or "story creation", or whatever) games. As has been pointed out, D&D 3E apparently has clearly defined procedural options for framing the "adventuring party" to the dungeon. I was looking at the new Marvel RPG in a store yesterday and it seems to have excellent procedural support, including three play styles, "Power and Responsibility" (Theme-based), "Clobberin' Time" (fun, bitt-kickin' stories), and "Brawlin'" (story-free, one on one battle). This is something that any type of game with any kind of goals can benefit from having--particularly so that it may communicate those goals, instead of leaving it to individual groups to be hopefully on the same page.

    Also, murk isn't necessarily a matter of game design. For instance, in a group of D&D players, maybe one of them (me, for instance) hasn't read the DMG, and is unaware of the text on forming adventuring parties, so when the DM and other players use a technique outlined in there (like, I dunno, assuming the PCs are already a group, or having them meet in a bar and expect them to team up), then my expectations, such as "forming a party artificially is so trite and cliche; characters should move and interact freely and naturally without the expectation of forming a team" is going to clash with the group. If I was told what to expect beforehand, I could decide whether I want to play, and if so align myself with the group mode.

    The point from a game design perspective is that if I outline procedure clearly in my text, at least I can say I've done my part. There could still be murk within a play group based on differing assumptions, and various distributions of textual knowledge (i.e. "only the GM reads the GM section," or my situation in my childhood Marvel Super-Heroes group, where only one player had read the book because he owned it).

    I'm going to try to drawn an analogy. I'm hoping it's not a straw man, and I'm hoping it turns out to be useful. We'll see.

    The vast majority of people don't have a solid grasp of social power dynamics. They don't understand or think about how we assume roles (teacher, student, parent, child, friend, mentor, whatever), and how the socialized dynamics in those assumed roles impact interaction with other people. But they assume the roles anyway, and they derive the advantages of power dynamics (which doesn't mean that they have power, sometimes it's an advantage to assume a lower-power position).

    I take Ron to be saying the same thing: most people don't understand or think about what's happening when they roleplay. But they do it anyway, and they derive the fun that comes from doing that sort of thing. The problem that arises is when people fail to assume complimentary positions. Think about the chaos that happens when two people try to take the position of 'teacher' in reference to the other in reference to the same subject. You get social tension and chaos and not much gets done. I take it that the same thing can happen in roleplaying (it's happened to me at least): you can assume non-complimentary positions and thus have your fun mangled.

    Of course a lot of people (most?) don't have any problems with assuming complimentary roles in social power dynamics, and I assume that the same is true of roleplaying. So my read on any statement about confusion or non-understanding is that it's no big deal. Most people don't need to understand something to do it. I can play a game of pick-up basketball and have a lot of fun doing it, but I know pretty much nothing about strategy in basketball. Would I have more fun playing if I knew some strategy? Maybe. Knowing me, probably. But I don't want to spend the time required to learn it, I've got other stuff to do (like play basketball).

    Thomas

      Posted By: JDCorleyYep, Tony got it. I knew he would. The part of the quote I was looking at was right before the bolded section: "Historically, role-playing is practically defined by a bunch of techniques hidden or at least isolated within a bunch of unconstructed dialogue. When I say "unconstructed," I don't merely mean "not rolling dice," and not even "open dialogue." I mean: primal chaos that cannot itself produce a solution (in the technical sense of that term)."

      Primal chaos with no solutions? That's my Vampire game? Fucking A! I thought it was a great game! Oh well, I guess all that fun was just my imagination. After all, the "unconstructed" things I was doing cannot itself produce a solution. Only the "constructed" theory could have saved me.
      Except you've taken what he said out of context and drawn a conclusion from it that doesn't make sense in context.

      I think the logical conclusion to draw from it is this one: if your game text doesn't explain the elements and procedures of play, and your game is successful and fun, then THE GROUP must have established the elements and procedures of play.

      So Vampire doesn't explain all those procedures. So what? Burning Wheel doesn't either. There are LOADS of things that are not explained in Burning Wheel, like how to set a scene, what a scene is, when a scene begins, when a scene ends, who gets to narrate a success, who gets to narrate failure, how to add complications as a result of failure, and even how to build a campaign! That doesn't mean it's a bad game or that no one who plays it has fun. In fact, Ron himself quite likes it, even if it is "murk-ful"

      The fact remains, when we sit down to play Burning Wheel, there are procedures and techniques that we use that are not in the text. If we successfully play a game, we have dealt with that issue and gotten rid of Ron's murk. For ourselves. And that's just fine.

      But aware play, in which we watch and analyze what we do and why, and what effects we cause, can allow us to design games that make those procedures and techniques part of our designs. And if done correctly, the players of those games won't need to know anything about theory because it will already be embedded in the game.

      So that's the big, evil point that Ron and others have about theory: Looking at your play that is fun and trying to understand the principles behind what made it fun, and looking at your play that is not fun and trying to understand the principles behind what made it not fun, can help you design better games if you apply those principles.

      And since we will never get to the point where all games are totally clear about how to play, if a group is not having fun, if communication is breaking down or someone is not getting everything they want out of play, those people have the option to use aware, analyzed play to figure out why, and maybe make it better. Or, with just as much validity, they could stop engaging in a hobby they don't enjoy.
