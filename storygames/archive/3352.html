    \n My tip is: don't compare the game to other games, especially not to "traditional" games. Just be warm and welcoming, don't talk about "indie-ness", and move on to a clear, brief set up for "what we're going to do next." Guide with a firm hand and expect everyone to play the game you're there to play.

    Just like you would when introducing someone to Scrabble or Blue Moon City or whatever.

    \n What John said. I think saying it's perfect for people who play X is a little creepy. X is perfect for people who play X. In terms of easing discomfort, you'd be way better off making sure everybody knew everybody else as a person.

    As far as running TSoY or whatever for people who have never played an RPG, that's cake - I promise. People really used to other systems will need a lot more help, but just being clear about procedures and (importantly) explaining why they are cool is all you need. They are there to play, they will work with you.

    \n Introduce yourself and let everyone else make eye contact, shake hands, say their names, make some small talk, etc. This seems obvious, but it can get lost in the shuffle of excitement if you're not careful. This bit of non-gamey human contact pays huge dividends.

    \n Ack! I sure hope every game will be newbie friendly. If a game is not newbie-friendly, in my opinion it's not friendly at all.

    That said, system-wise I think Questers of the Middle Realms is very newbie-friendly because it gives a little bit of structure and a little bit of known territory. I believe my husband (a.k.a. Shosuro Kando) will be running his T\xc3\xa9kumel game using the QotMR system.

    Other very newcomer-friendly story games that come to mind include Cat, The Zorcerer of Zo, Faery's Tale, Everway, Prince Valiant, etc. Their premises are easily grasped, the mechanics are simple, and they have enough handrails not to leave the neophytes completely lost.
