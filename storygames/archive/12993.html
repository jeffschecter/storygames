\n So PAX Prime 2010 was a pretty big hit. I've started thinking about PAX (Prime and East) 2011. What can we do to make those events even better?

Make it official - get support from the PAX staff.
Get on the PAX 10
Set times for games (but no scheduling)
Get a mention in the event program
Have more people available to run games (maybe by offering badges if you run X games)
Do an indie games panel

(Based on my blog post here)

Anyone else have suggestions? What would you like to see at PAX 2011?

    \n Oh, the PAX 10 thing is a great idea!

    As far as scheduling goes, I would have loved to see a vague schedule and a list of people looking for games so that I could sign up and have somebody text me when a group was forming (or something like that).     Some other ideas based on things I saw around me:
    * An experience point card (or similar) where you get points for playing different story-games and can win some swag if you play enough
    * A bigger selection of games and convention scenarios so that people can play with little or no supervision
    * Some information in swag form that helps match gamers up to games they haven't played before

    I am so down with helping with this in any way I can!

    \n My biggest concerns:

    1.) No signage in the indie location.
    2.) I felt like I was fighting for space.

    My biggest appreciations:

    1.) Sage, by the door, with a spread of indie games, and a friendly smile for strangers.
    2.) Good energy.

    And so building on those things...

    Have a welcoming area, with a big poster that explains what we're doing, little fliers explaining "story games are X. We're doing Y. Approach us and ask about Z, and we can play a game." Don't do this to replace the smiling Sage, but to augment and to lighten the load. Have others step up and be the friendly smiling Sage from time to time, because that man worked his ass off.

    Strengthen partnership with The Dreaming, who're keen to celebrate and support indie RPGs. They've said that they would love to sponsor an indie RPGs room: an RPGs/etc booth run by The Dreaming, a bunch of tables, us, and our games. The Dreaming was awesome enthusiastic about indie RPGs and partnering with us this year. They took a 20% cut off the cover price of any books they sold through their booth, which is a pretty damn reasonable cut IMO.

    Have a clear set of expectations for indie games room posse/volunteers/crew/team. Which means: organize. Have people step into leadership roles. Delegate responsibilities. Expect commitment. Sage did a fucking awesome job of organizing and representing and facilitating this year. I want infrastructure and friends behind him so that he doesn't burn himself out and crawl away into a corner. Which means: have people step up and say "Sage, do you need a person from 12-6 on Saturday?" And Sage can say, "Yeah, PERSON A is in charge of scheduling manpower, talk to them. We definitely need people who're willing to just play house for an hour and greet strangers, if you're down for that."

    Dedicated space. We need to secure it. Through The Dreaming, through PAX organizers, or through armed militant action. We need a space that we don't need to fight for.

    \n I'm with Adam on this one. I think maybe a good way to start the indie conversation is to say "Hey, that style of game you already like? We have a different way of doing that, and we think it's pretty cool, want to try it?"

    I say that DW's only real similarity to D&D is the trappings of, ya know, dungeons and dragons and spells and such. The fiction genre. And people are already pretty aware you can go outside of that genre in RPGs: Wizard's is doing Post-apocalypse, Wheaton blogs about Fiasco and Penny, etc.

    Mechanically, DW is a whole heck of a lot more like AW. Which is something I like the idea of people learning more about.

    So, my pitch for why DW would be a good PAX 10 game to show off indie RPGs is pretty much the same reason Joe doesn't like it: it's familiar fictional setting with indie mechanics that (will someday) rock. I Fiasco to death, but I think DW might have a better chance of working for a traditional gamer group just trying something indie for the first time without someone knowledgable at the table.

    Not to say something a little further out there couldn't be a hit, but I don't think DW is a bad first step. I'm not looking for the SG stamp of approval, I'm just pondering throwing my product into the running.

    \n Seminars bringing attention to indie games is mainly why they are popular at PAX.
    \n
    \nMy seminar suggestion:
    \n
    \nHow to prototype your video game ideas quickly and cheaply using tabletop games.
    \n
    \nMobile games are exploding and console game sales are declining. Console developers are looking to cut costs and drastically reduce development time. The need is there for large companies. And for individuals who have ideas that are bigger than their budgets or skill sets, a tabletop option is a great first step. Linking indie games to video games not only is relevant to PAX's audience but has the bonus of showcasing existing indie games as sources for new ideas.
    \n
    \nBeyond seminars, another way to make PAX (or really any con) more awesome is bring versions of your games that people can play together while waiting on line. This would also be great for Comic Con.
