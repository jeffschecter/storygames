\n I posted this on rpg.net as well. i know there isn't huge amounts of love for nWoD over here, but well, I like you guys better. so i thought i'd see if anyone had any ideas for me...
---
I just started up an nWoD game last saturday. As Hunter and Changeling are my two favorites of the new line, I decided to combine them a bit. The players (there are only two) will start out as human hunters, and they will find themselves primarily going up against fae-type "monsters." Eventually they will attract the attention of one or two of the Gentry and be hauled off to Arcadia, returning as changelings, things they had learned to hate. What happens then I have less of a vision for (it'll be a ways down the road anyway) though I want the game to end with them rising to Wyrd 10 and becoming Others themselves, thus once again becoming the monsters they hated.

None of this is going to be a surprise to the players. I've already told them all of this, and they're on board. I have a few problems. I don't intend to run this as a game with a strong pre-planned plotline. I plan to sketch out some NPCs and relationships and come up with a few "weird things" the PCs might hear about at the beginning to start investigating. Beyond that (and coming up with more "monsters" from time to time as they deal with others) I plan to play it by ear, letting things be mostly player/PC driven.

My problem is that, for all I love Changeling, I'm not entirely sure how to do changelings as scary antagonists to hunters. The Gentry, and hobs, and other fae-things can be all kinds of monstrous, and changelings can certainly be as awful and evil as any person can. But they also seem to generally be more subtle. They're not generally drinking blood or tearing people limb from limb and the Mask generally keeps them looking human.

So does anyone have any good suggestions for things that changelings could do that would seem weird and paranormal if rumors were heard about them?

And as a side question: I'm pretty sure my players probably have the assumption that changelings won't be particularly terrible to face in combat. So I'd like to disabuse them of this notion by having them run into a nasty one fairly early. Anyone got any advice for making a particularly combat-nasty changeling?

    \n My first suggestion is to not let the Hunters easily know who is a Changeling and who is a Hob or a Fae. The supernatural is difficult to understand, after all, so the Hunters may not easily be able to tell one from the other.

    Once you've done that, the Hunters may never know what they're facing in combat -- it could be a Changeling, or a Fae, or something else.

    The other thing about it is that it may allow you to relax the "Changelings as the Heroes" motif in your own mind. Once you look at them from the outside, Changelings can be pretty fucking scary, and as Clarity declines they're not all that subtle at all. Beasts and Ogres who lose their Clarity are like to eat people. And as people don't like to be eaten, there is likely going to be a struggle involved -- blood, tearing, screaming. Or a clever Ogre may well keep folks chained up in a pantry for when it wants to eat, so you've got all sorts of nastiness to work with human food, chains, pots, and big cold rooms to keep the meat fresh. Fairest and Wizened make good "inside killers" -- black widows who kill of husband after husband, or butlers who really did do it... over and over again. Serial killers are always fun, and magical serial killers are even more fun. Darklings make for good kidnappers, and Mirrorskins are always fun for a "kill and replace" caper.

    It'd also be an interesting thing to have the players, once they're Changelings themselves, review their old cases -- now that they're the monsters they once hated, they may realize that in their past lives they weren't the saints they thought they were.

    \n A couple of things:

    First, the challenge with any White Wolf game (or any detailed game in general) is not finding a new thing to incorporate, it's mercilessly cutting things out. You are never going to get across all of ANY White Wolf game, let alone any TWO White Wolf games. PS if you did it would be terrible.

    So once you have a basic setup, and it definitely sounds like you do, you should make a little circle on the center of a piece of paper marked "THE PCs", draw arrows pointing towards that circle and each arrow is a method of applying pressure to them. That can be a Changeling that needs their help as privateers rend reality to re-capture them. That can be a fae spirit or hob of some kind that's trying to get set up in the real world and need to bump off the PCs to do it. Whatever it happens to be, that pressure is what makes the actual scenarios have dramatic tension.

    When you read through a Changeling book now that you have a campaign idea in mind, you shouldn't be thinking "oh that's cool, I can put that in", not anymore, you've done the brainstorming, now you are an editor. Now you should be thinking, "Does that push me in the direction I want the game to go or in another direction, or in no direction?" If the answer is "wrong way" or "doesn't matter", do not, do not use or introduce it and discourage discussion of it at the table.

    I used a Changeling-related bad guy in my current game of mortal immigrant smugglers/organized criminals. (Naturally Changelings have to get across a border into a land that doesn't want them, they need new drivers licenses and birth certificates and they're being chased by grim uncaring authorities far more powerful than them, and their sole reason for doing it is because they crave in their heart of hearts a better life and know that this is where they belong. Goddamn Changelings coming into this country and stealing our jobs!) I introduced some Changelings and they were initially sympathetic, indeed, some low-ranking ones were outright friendly to the criminals that gave them their start, but I made it crystal fucking clear that when it eventually comes down to you or them, they never, ever ever pick you. It is always them. That let me have enough tension that the characters still regarded them as creepy and stayed away from them, but it permitted the kind of information sharing that was necessary to banish the Gentry back to his prison.

    \n Well, sure, to some degree you want a large variety of cool things, though I would hold back on most of them until they are torn from their lives and plunged into the new and horrific world they only got to scratch the surface of before. Once you're down the rabbit hole you should have a long list of crazy shit to throw at them, from books and just inspired by the books. The important thing is to be thinking about not "what does WW think would be in the game world at this point or that point" but "what do I want to introduce, explore or conclude in my game at various points".

    One niggly balance issue that occurs to me - though perhaps it's not really an issue - is that a lot of the Hunter teamwork-style techniques might be overwhelmingly effective when performed by a supernatural creature.
