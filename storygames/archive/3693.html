\n I posted earlier today... a theory I had about what it is that I think gamers want. I'm not very good at guessing, but I took a stab at it. I posted in a snarky tone that was mistaken for hostility. And instead of the discussion I was expecting, I found myself defending my point.

My fault.

I have endeavored to make a smarter, more concise post this time.

It is my understanding that fans of anything, want simple, evocative marketing text and/or visuals to engage them in whatever their pleasure zones are.

Am I correct in this assumption?

If so, no matter how complex a novel might be, someone has to parse down the information to smaller consumable bits. This seems like a relatively simple concept, but one I had moved on from a long time ago. It took Josh's post to remind me of that very simple fact...

"touch my pleasure zone, dummy!"

Moving on. I have two questions.

How then, can someone present something with an air of mystery (players are already dead, but don't know it ala Sixth Sense), while still getting them to show up for a game?

Two. Just because DOGS (or something like that) is alternative, doesn't mean it's "complex." I'm not being snarky. What is the threshold for acceptability on how far you can take DOGS from the vineyard and still make it fun?

And I'm not talking about DOGS in revolutionary times or DOGS as dolphins.

What happens if the plot of a DOGS game is about SIX DOGS dying in the desert with only on canteen amongst them?

What if the plot is to expand the influence of the dogs into towns that don't WANT the dogs? And the DOGS themselves have varying degrees of belief in this idea?

Are these SORTS of IDEAS sluggish? Interesting? Different for different sake? How far can you move from the THREE ACT structure before you lose your audience? Any audience.

    Sure, but you can ask. (And, be warned, doing so sometimes doesn't work well.)

    I put forth that the reason you can't do this at a con is that you and your pals quite rightly share a system -- an unspoken means to know what they can and can't do, know how they go about doing it, and can't wait to come back for more precisely because the other people at the table "get this."

    Sounds like you have such a system. It's more robust than you might think. It never need be written down, and still it works quite well most of the time. Good! Good for you and the group! I'm saying that sincerely.

    Whereas, people at a con have very little notion at all, certainly no unspoken one, how to go about all this fun you're having at home. They have no trust. They have no prior experience. They have no idea that Bob over there really like to push Charlie's buttons via his character in play AND that Charlie actually enjoys this! And so on. So, they have to feel around, cautiously, which means that play is tepid a boring, frequently.

    But, if you jump into, say, a three-act structure, well then! Now they have some common language. They ALREADY KNOW that Mr. Johnson is going to betray them after the run, or that So-and-so is really a necromancer who will capture the girl and we'll have to save her in the last act.

    The three-act thing is a common denominator. It lets people who embrace that thing (and to be clear, it's 99% of time NOT my thing) just get over the non-surprise about Mr. Johnson and have fun anyway.

    Do these seem plausible? I am not certain about them, but my experience is that these kinds of patterns repeat frequently.

    \n Jim, I think you're getting hung up by trying to write the pitch that will appeal to some vaguely defined "other people." Why not write us up a few pitches that would appeal hugely to you? Like, the ones that would have you bouncing on the balls of your feet, ready to sign up.
    \n
    \nOn some technical points: You write these blurbs a little bit like you're paid by the word. That's fine for a start, but then you need to ruthlessly edit with a heavy red pen. Rip out every single word that you can. Work it like you're an editor who gets paid by the word removed.
    \n
    \nTo start with, you can remove anything the audience knows you need to tell them. If you're pitching an RPG you can remove any text like "This text, right here, is a pitch for an RPG." They pick that up from context. And If you're offering them a game system they've never heard of before you can remove things like "new and unique." I mean, really ... they weren't assuming that you wanted them to think it's old and derivative. They know you're trying to pitch it as new, and so they suspend judgment on whether it's true until they see evidence. All of that is verbiage that's prime for being excised.
    \n
    \nAlso, you can cross off the second instance of anything you've already told them. If you tell them that they can play commissioners, bookers and wrestlers you don't then have to tell them that they can play every aspect of wrestling (though I note that you don't describe rules for playing the scantily clad groupie-girls who hand folding chairs into the ring). If you tell them that it's two-fisted you probably don't need to tell them that it's over-the-top. Again, more words you can get rewarded for removing. You get the idea.
    \n
    \nOnce you've ripped out all that stuff, what's left will be only the stuff that hits right below the solar plexus. It will be the raw, gut-level appeal of your game. Don't tell them it's cool ... show them it's cool. Use only the words that show them how cool it is. Less is totally more in this case.
    \n
