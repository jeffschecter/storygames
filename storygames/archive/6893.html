    \n How on earth did I miss this thread? It must have fallen off the front page while I was in Kyrgyzstan (again). [Oh yes, that's what Ole Peder was saying].

    Many years ago (fifteen years?) I designed an Earthsea RPG. My design principle back then was to introduce only those rules that were needed during play. The problem was that I didn't have the toolbox I have now, and "rules" meant only "rolling dice for task resolution, combat and magic". The campaign was fun and poetic at best, but marred by some serious problems: Players didn't have much input on each others' stories if their characters weren't in the same place - and when we experimented with letting players run NPCs on the fly, there were some ugly scenes where one players's character was hosed for no good reason.

    I've picked up the idea now and again, but never done much with it - I didn't know how. For some reason (don't remember why) the idea came back, and in a few minutes of inspiration I knew what tools I wanted to use. They were:

    - Domains of control. Having a shared background where every player has control of a specific field, such as "culture", "geography" or "magic" would allow players to co-create without stepping on each others' toes.
    - Player-owned stories. Each player's story is theirs, and they decide what input other players are allowed to have.
    - Destiny points. Each story would move toward certain fixed points, selected/created by the player, but how those points were reached would be up to the player.

    (And, of course, I tend to fall back on thoughts from the Hippie Method Manifesto, although not intentionally or consciously - the manifesto isn't prescriptive, it's descriptive).

    The game in play requires certain improvisational and storytelling skills, but even more a certain attitude of respect for the other players' stories. It's designed to help create subtle and poetic stories - it's a spider web of thin threads, not a steel framework.

    I've only played it once. However, I may use it for a campaign I'm planning, called "The Fog Campaign"; it looks like the system will match the group (old friends who have the time to let a story develop) and the setting (pseudo-Japanese poetic action) perfectly.

    There are no AP reports that I know of.
