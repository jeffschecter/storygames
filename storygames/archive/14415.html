    \n Todd, you are evoking some author with your posts here, but I can't place who it is.

    A couple of cons ago I played a pirate game in which someone introduced a character who was an evil voodoo priestess. The game was hewing pretty closely to pulp pirate tropes, so a non-serious voodoo priestess character was not especially unexpected, but I was still a little worried because unflattering portrayal of a marginalized religion (voodoo), even in a game context, made me a little uncomfortable. I realized I didn't want to shut someone's idea down directly ("that character can't be in the game"), so instead I took a more active approach: I rolled a voodoo priest character, and played him very close to a realistic portrayal of a Haitian houngan. By acting through him, I was able to act out a positive view of voodoo practitioners, introduce elements of the story which fit accurately with voodoo theology, and oppose the wicked voodoo priestess as a cultural appropriator and co-opter of righteous religion. The characters actually had a really evocative and engaging rivalry, and I was ultimately happy with the story in terms of how we portrayed voodoo.

    \n I don't think there is a definitive way to avoid triggers. For 2 reasons...

    1. It's often unclear what will be a trigger. First, there are many triggers for many negative experiences that we may not even be aware of. Second, something doesn't have to be explicit or obvious to be a trigger. I have many friends, male and female, that have been raped. For a few of them, explicit rape in game isn't their only trigger. For one, any kidnapping triggered rape since they were raped after they were kidnapped. For another, religion was a trigger since they were raped by a priest. For another, something as unobvious as physical contact one one's shoulder's was a trigger. You don't know what will be a trigger and often it's too complex to discuss in a lines and veil conversation.

    2. Even sensitive, educated, multicultural people use stereotypes. I have friends who are feminist minorities and spent many years working with anti-racist activist groups that unintentionally roleplayed poor stereotypes in games. I have one friend who helped teach seminars in how to avoid problematic stereotypes in writing... and used them without thinking when we roleplayed.

    Then what do you do?

    1. I suggest giving people the option to censor content in game, for any reason, no questions asked. If something makes you uncomfortable, says so, it's gone. It helps to make it clear this is an option by codifying it. I've played with the idea of using an index card that says "censor" on it.

    2. Instead of asking, "what don't we want in our game", it might be more effective to start with a list of common sensitive subjects and ask, "which of these are we ok with in our game?" You can have a list that includes: violence, violence against children, violence against animals, sex, rape, cursing, sexism, racism, PC death, and so on. Additionally, someone can say, "I'm ok with X but only if Y." For example, "I'm ok with sex as long as it is off screen or I'm ok with cursing as long as you don't say fagot."
