\n I am excited for Marvel, I don't know too much about it's specifics but I really like everything i'm hearing. I love the event structure and I'm looking forward to running the game when it's released. It feels like it may address some of my favorite design goals. Anyway i'm happy to see something interesting being done with a high profile license. I'm really hoping it will reach marvel fans.

Everything I hear about DnD 5 kind of upsets me in an odd way. In such a small industry i'm kind of tired of seeing resources thrown in the wrong direction. At first I thought the designers did not understand the what the actual problems were. but then I read this kind of stuff in jenscot's "What can D&D 5E mean for Indie Games?" discussion

"And yet another different D&D freelancer friend said:

"This is why D&D 4E is called an indie game. It tells you how to play. Many of us know that the rules are better than they have ever been design wise but that's not the point. Look at Iron Heroes designed by Mike Mearls. The same people who loved that game, a game which was similar to 4E in many ways, hated 4E. Why? They actually like the rules in many cases. But they don't want to be told this is the way to play. These games are about people. They are about friends. And you don't know my friends better than I do.""

So I'm starting to see how the problems of DnD are a little intractable with a vocal portion of the audience. I think pathfinder's success clearly shows that a portion of the purchasing audience agrees.

But I sort of think they still don't understand the hart of what is wrong. I have this feeling that DnD 4 failed both the old audience and failed to fully grasp a new audience. It was something that worked for such a tiny sliver of people because it had contradictory goals.

Trying to attract a wide audience does not quite mesh with pushing a hard core monthly content subscription that needs a printer (with ink) to play the game. Requiring the audience to source printing is the sort of low rent thing I would do. Same thing with character creation and session prep, really why do people think it's ok to require a computer for these tasks? They knew what their sales figures were compared to their online traffic.

Now they must go back to leaning on "great GM advice" and train an army of people the incredibly difficult task of game design instead of trying to sell them a finished product? What about that audience they wanted to reach with the new red box? Was that only worth one try? Was the same method that worked in the 80's really the best approach?

So let me go back to why i'm sort of excited for marvel. i'm hoping it will be a finished product. That I can get those events and play them without the prep, without the pain, of typical rpg. That maybe marvel will be what redbox was not. I don't know if that is the truth, if i'm hoping too much but that is my hope.

Honestly I don't know what to expect from DnD 5 and until I read it I should probably shut up and get a life.

also what is fantasy flight going to do with star wars!?

    \nYou assume my preference is to not use an online character creator. I am that buyer and facilitator, i am the nerd who prints out and constructs the paper craft terrain.

    For a poorly formatted and presented game a computer aid is totally preferable. I used Army Builder for 40k and loved it! it's so much easier to build a 40k list with army builder than with paper. But then I played a miniatures game with a rational and well formatted army construction method and learned that there are far better ways to solve format and communication problems than resorting to a computer program to get the task done.

    So when i say "why not try something new", i mean you can accomplish the same goals but with better methods.

    I get it, i'm a dreamer and you are a "reality" sort of guy. what are these better methods?

    check these out
    here is a character sheet
    Star wars saga edition sheet
    Here are some cards that create a matching set.
    star wars saga edition cards
    The cards plug into that character sheet and a GM encounter sheet, thus labeling all the information on the card. For character creation the card feeds information into the sheet.

    If I finished that set of cards and made all the character class cards, species cards, and talent tree cards. Players could build a saga edition character in under a minute. The bodyguard character card is an npc stat block and can be read using the GM encounter sheet. Overall this component based presentation entirely streamlines dealing with the rules in saga edition. It goes further than that. the product format draws every player at the table into buying cards for their character. plays well/sells well, a reasonable modern RPG format.

    Those cards are just a retrofit and just a tiny part of the whole solution so they look a little ugly.

    I have been saying that the book presentation does not work, books do not have enough bandwidth to make a complete role playing game possible.

    What we see in games like D20 is part of the puzzle, what we see in games like fiasco is another part of the same puzzle. The thing is a book does not have the bandwidth to do both, it barely has enough for a game like fiasco.

    When I say try something new I do not mean writing great gm advice. It does not mean capitulating to the malformed psychologies and social structures that develop around rpg groups. when i say it's not working it's because people stop playing for a reason dammit! Not everyone chooses to bend their entire life to fit these games.

    If you are a DnD player, it really sucks if you have 7 close friends. let's solve that.

    \n I didn't mean to pour cold water on someone's dream. I thought we were talking about 5e, an actual thing that will exist. Proceed with whatever you like. (Those Saga things are awesome.)

    Here is some food for thought on the role of DDI in the business. A subscriber count from the WotC forums shows Wizards got revenues at least $400,000 last month just from DDI. Now the subscriber count is not a perfect indicator - it shows people who have been subscribed forever, as well as people who are just "dipping in" this month, updating their offline character creator, etc. Let's say that $400,000 number is inflated by 1/3, really what they can expect is around $300,000 a month. Around $3.9 million a year. To put that in perspective, they would have to sell more than 100,000 more $34.95 books (or whatever) a year just to catch up to the revenues.
