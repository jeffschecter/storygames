\n I realised something today. My game The Outpost has reverse flags. And that's awesome.

"What's a 'reverse flag'?" you say. "Well," says I, "A reverse flag is something on your character sheet that gives you an indication of what another player wants to see from your character in the game." In my game, since characters are made communally and then chosen from, it means that the character's Focus was likely set down by another player. And when you're playing that character, the Focus acts as a flag for you to act upon, and others to act upon, too!

I could see this be even more straightforward, though. I make a character and then you get to put something on my character sheet that tells me what excites you about him/her. You go "Your character Zorro is so cool! I wanna see him charming a woman, so I'm gonna put down 'In love with Se\xf1orita Escalante' on your sheet." Then I have that on my sheet and I play on that, because that's what you want to see. I in turn get to put something on your character sheet that's a flag for you to play off of.

Are there any games out there that do this?

    \n I'd just consider them flags- flags indicate what the players want story-wise, regardless of whether it's aimed at the GM or each other.

    \n Perhaps "cross-character flags" or "cross flags" would be a better term. You could do this pretty easily in Spirit of the Century. When you get to the phases of character creation where you are guest starring in other people's novels, you write two aspects for the novel's character rather than your own.

    As for "what I'm not interested in"-flags, I think those are perhaps best termed as "anti-flags." I have those in my game, Chronica Feudalis, as "backgrounds."

    "Reverse flags" should be ways for the GM to indicate to the players what type of contents she would like to have in the game.

    \n In Chronica Feudalis, the Backgrounds act as anti flags. it's a bit paradoxical at first read : it's supposed to illustrate a well established trait about a protagonist while at the same time conveying "I don't want it to appear during game play. At the end of each session a player can transform a background into a regular aspect or vice versa. Jeremy Keller illustrated by an example in the context of his historical medieval game on the Rpg Haven.\n
    I should think it might be fun to play an Arab or African character from some Islamic province who is traveling through Europe and having adventures. The fact that he is Muslim might be an important detail that I want my fellow players to know about. It's part of the flavor of the character. But I don't want game sessions where at every turn, my character is confronted about his religion. Having a background says that I've dealt with that already and we can move on. Or that if it comes up, we'll assume my character will win that discussion and we'll fast-forward past it.
