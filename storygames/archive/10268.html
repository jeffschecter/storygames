\n I have a long-term plan to brand Do: Pilgrims of the Flying Temple and Happy Birthday, Robot! as part of a series that gradually introduce people (kids, usually) to story games. I'm looking at the Step Into Reading series of books by Random House for inspiration. According to their website, the series is designed to gradually get kids more confident in their reading ability. \n
Step into Reading program is a unique leveled reader series that offers books at five carefully developed skill levels. This program offers a wide variety of fiction and nonfiction books. We have chosen topics of interest and colorful illustrations to make learning to read fun and exciting. Each step provides new challenges for young readers to master without being too difficult. The program is designed to give every child a successful reading experience- the grade levels are only guidelines; children can progress through the steps at their own speed, developing confidence and pride in their reading.
\nThey split up books into five levels:\n
STEP 1: READY TO READ
Preschool\u2013Kindergarten

* Big type and easy words
* Rhyme and rhythm
* Picture clues

For children who know the alphabet and are eager to begin reading.


STEP 2: READING WITH HELP
Preschool\u2013Grade 1

* Basic vocabulary
* Short sentences
* Simple stories

For children who recognize familiar words and sound out new words with help.


STEP 3: READING ON YOUR OWN
Grades 1\u20133

* Longer sentences
* Engaging characters
* Easy-to-follow plots

For children who are ready to read on their own.


STEP 4: READING PARAGRAPHS
Grades 2\u20133

* More challenging vocabulary
* Short paragraphs
* Exciting stories

For newly independent readers who read simple sentences with confidence.


STEP 5: READY FOR CHAPTERS
Grades 2\u20134

* Chapters
* Long paragraphs
* Full color art

For children who want to take the plunge into chapter books, but still like colorful pictures.
\nHow would you re-define these levels in reference to story games? What are some foundational concepts that you think would need to be established in early levels before going on to later levels? Bonus points if you can think of five games and organize them as examples of each of the levels.

    \n 1. Short games that don't tax the attention span. Skills: Active listening and reincorporation, metacognition. Example: "My Uncle The Baron", minus the rule that you are eliminated if you can't make a play (as suggested in the text).

    2. Longer games, although still easy for short attention spans. Skills: Formal constraints, simple strategy, character versus player. Example: "Happy Birthday, Robot!", "Puppetland" (assuming an adult GM).

    3. Movie-length games. Skills: Pacing, plot construction, trust building, more complex strategy. Example: Fiasco, Munchausen proper, Penny, Showdown. This is a well-populated tier of story games.

    4. Miniseries games. Skills: Metaplot construction, deeper characterization, theme and premise, critique. Example: PTA, InSpectres.

    5. Hard. Skills: Tactics/strategy/operations, lots of other things. Example: Mouse Guard (seriously - think about it), D&D.

    \n Speaking as a parent:

    I don't think story-gaming or following a set of procedures in order to play make-believe in a more structured and interesting way is not something you have to teach kids to do, the same way that you have to teach a kid how to read. Kids start playing make-believe at very young ages, and not long after, start organically creating rules and adhering to them, etc.

    I think this is a cool idea, and I think it's a good idea to write games to play with your kids (Shreyas and Vincent are big on this, I know). But I don't think the "Step into gaming" series is super necessary. It's less like reading and more like music appreciation; for some kids, their first concert is the Doodlebops, and for some kids it's Elvis Costello-- it's all about what their parents introduce them to and allow their kids to interact with and appreciate.

    I know that personally, it's more likely that I'll help my kids play something I already love and feel is appropriate, even if I have to simplify it a bit first, and then once the girls show an interest and want games of their own, I'll go looking for kid-friendly games. But that's because I'm buying them and they'll come into any new kids' games with some amount of experience with the games I enjoy. I'd suspect that, since parents are the ones making the money decisions for kids, I'm not the only one.

    Of course, what do I know? There might be a ton of hardcore gamers out there who'd be more interested in introducing their kids to the hobby, as opposed to specific games they like to play themselves. In which case, good on you.

    \n Yeah, that helps! I think Colin had a good idea in breaking down some essential elements common to a broad spectrum of story games and then organizing them into some kind of hierarchy of prerequisite skills. Colin proposes:

    Active listening
    Reincorporation
    Metacognition
    Formal constraints
    Simple strategy
    Character versus player
    Pacing
    Plot construction
    Trust building
    More complex strategy
    Metaplot construction
    Deeper characterization
    Theme and premise
    Critique
    Tactics/strategy/operations

    Somewhere in that list, I would probably include an introduction to the whole concept of a GM, which is somewhat unique to RPGs (unless you count referees in sports or parents who get called in to settle arguments over Monopoly).
