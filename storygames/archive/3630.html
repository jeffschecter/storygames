\n So I'm working on this zombie RPG. Its most central feature is a mechanic for dealing with fear which - I hope - will not suck. This is my wishlist of things I want it to do:
\n
\n* Not remove control of the character from the player (i.e. no "you rolled a 5, so according to this table you soil yourself and pass out")
\n* Be interesting enough to the player that "rolling a fear check" doesn't become the dreaded mood-killer it tends to be in other games
\n* Have the potential of giving a character greater, rather than lesser, ability if frightened (panicked adrenaline bursts)
\n* Notwithstanding the above, not encourage the player to keep the character in a mentally unbalanced state
\n
\nMy idea of this so far:
\n
\n* Allow the system to affect dice mechanics only and allow players to interpret how their characters are actually affected in the fiction.
\n* Have a "track" ranging from -5 to +5, with balanced characters at +/-0. As characters do fear checks, they have the ability to either climb or drop on the track, thereby increasing or decreasing their abilities.
\n* Give a character a permanent instability (derangement) if they drop off the chart, either above or below.
\n
\nThere's some more details but that's the gist of it.
\n
\nWhat say you? Are there any glaring holes or inconsistencies? Am I naive in thinking this mechanic could work for the purposes I outlined? Is it possible to reinvent a fear mechanic in a way that does not suck?
\nIt seems a little mechanical and clumsy to me - I had hoped for something a bit more intuitive and inventive. If you have any suggestions, please throw them my way.
\n
\nThanks!!

    \n Hey, I really appreciate what you're trying to do here, in terms of making a fear mechanic that doesn't tell the players what to think, or how to play their characters. So many of these systems are very bad in this respect. For inspiration, perhaps you could look to how "trust" works in The Mountain Witch. Rather than being told how much you trust each other, and having to play accordingly, you play, and assign trust based on how your character is feeling.

    Translated to your game, perhaps a mechanic where people assign fear to their own characters, based on how they're feeling, and fear points can be used for mechanical advantage and disadvantage. Perhaps they're advantageous when dealing with zombies, but are a hindrance when trying to do anything else. So, for example, if you're terrified (you've given yourself lots of fear points) you get a big bonus to beating the zombies with your baseball bat, but you get a comensurate penalty when you're trying to convince people to follow your lead later.

    \n I think I nailed it! Let me know what you think. Points I've changed as a result of this conversation is marked with an asterisk.

    1. Characters have two balanced stats totaling 10 - Cool and Care. Cool represents how much stress they can take without blinking. Care represents how much emotional duress they can be suffer and still hold it together. No stat can be lower than 3 or higher than 7 to begin with.
    2. There is a "Fright level" that escalates during the course of a scenario. It normally spans between 1 and 10. Characters begin dealing with death around level 5.
    *3. In any skill test done in a stressful situation, if the Fright level exceeds your Cool, add one die to your pool for each level of difference.
    *4. Each extra die rolled has a chance of being converted into a Panic point, but also counts towards success. You are more likely to get Panic points if you fail at the roll than if you succeed.
    *5. When Panic exceeds your Care, you shut down - faint, go catatonic, suffer muscular blackout, whatever. You have to reduce your Panic (through various means) below Care to regain control.

    Panic points replaces Doom, no worries Ryan. Panic makes more sense, with the above modifications.

    Let me know if anything is whacked with this. It all makes sense to me but I have probably not considered every possibility available.

    \nVery valid question. I'll make sure that I work on the wording on this in the draft.

    Example #1: The Narc
    Joe is an undercover officer in LAPD's narc squad with considerable on-the-job experience. He's seen some ugly shit in the line of duty, and as a result his player decides to pick a Cool of 6, and thus a Care of 4. Character wise, the low Care rating means that Joe has sacrificed attachments to life that others take for granted - family, friends, faith, hope. Mechanically, this means that Joe will be able to operate normally in the face of sights that would make others cringe. However, once the Fright level escalates above levels that are considered routine for Joe, it won't take much to mess him up - essentially for his body to go "Screw this shit, it ain't worth it!"

    Example #2: The librarian
    Sheila is a librarian in any given library that does not reside on the Miskatonic University campus. Her player decides that she hasn't been subjected to a whole lot of the world's evils, having lived a fairly sheltered life in a pretty little suburban neighbourhood. She takes a Cool of 4 and gets a Care of 6, which to her character means that she is easily frightened, but she copes well - she's not likely to give up, she's got too much to live for and too many people that depend on her. The moment the Fright level escalates above 4, she will start to feel the effects of it, but until she has accumulated Panic points in excess of 6 she will only benefit from the additional stress - it makes her try harder and go faster.

    Note that the game doesn't support two types of characters - the zombie-killing Badass that doesn't stop firing until his arm gets bitten off (high Cool, high Care, played by Bruce Campbell) and the Victim that freezes up with an eardrum busting shriek the moment she lays eyes on a shambling corpse (low Cool, low Care, played by any big-breasted blonde). Other games would do these better - for example, All Flesh and Call of Cthulhu, respectively. This game is about characters that either conquers their fear and gets people out of trouble, or who succumbs to their fear and drags people down to hell with them. As such, it's important for these stats to be balanced, so that everyone in the game has a shot at both options above.

    \n I like Pulp-Fu's take on fear:
    \n
    \nEach character can choose their rank in Insanity. A low rank represents someone who is exceptionally well grounded and somewhat of a skeptic, while a character with a high rank in Insanity is loopy and believes in all sorts of occult things. The benefits of a low rank are that the character is less likely to loose control in a spooky situation (more likely to succeed a fear check), and is hardened against occult or psychic influences. The drawback of a low rank is that in spooky situations the character's chances of skill or combat success is restricted by the Insanity rank. The benefits of a high rank in Insanity is that even though you have a reduced chance of not flipping out, if you do make your fear check you are far more effective because your skills and actions are less restricted by your high Insanity rank. The consequences of failing a fear check for every character is that they have to choose between fight or flight - either they attack the horror directly or do everything they can to get away. Every time a character makes a fear check - win or lose - their Insanity rank increases.

    After having spent the better part of a decade running and playing games like Vampire, Kult, Call of Cthulhu, just about any horror game I could get my hands on, I have never been able to instill anything close to fear in my players. Tension and suspense, sure. Never fear.

    Kult, in particular, thrives on placing characters in danger and fucked up situations (think Hellraiser). With virgin players who know nothing of the setting, you can get a bit of a raise out of them when the Illusion shatters in the middle of your final exam, the invigilator starts bleeding profusely from underneath their fingernails, the blood seeping down the aisle towards your desk, where it turns into horrible clawed arms that reach up and starts tearing your paper apart, you black out, and the next thing you know you're in the Dean's Office trying to come up with some reasonable explanation why you stabbed pencil holes in your exam papers, shrieked and stormed out, in order not to get expelled.

    Maybe.

    After the fourth or fifth fucked up thing happens, you can no longer up the ante to continue to instill that type of response, and the players tend to just yawn and pick up the Fear dice - if they pass, they'll pick up their Kalashnikov and just "deal with the problem".

    Having not felt personal "fear" from watching a horror flick for the past umpteen years (though The Ring and The Curse came pretty close) I think this is a lost cause, at least for me. I'm perfectly satisfied with settling for "tension" on the player level, leaving "fear" on the character level.

    Posted By: noclueAnd reading the OP, I don't see anything in the wishlist about causing the emotion of fear. On the contrary, the fear mechanic is about "keeping things interesting" and "encouraging the players to keep the character in an unbalanced state."
    \nnoclue, you're absolutely right, I never aimed for causing players to be afraid by this mechanic, which is why it's not on the wishlist.
    However, to clarify, I do not want to encourage players to keep their characters in an unstable state, despite the bonuses they may receive for it. Since the character would prefer, when stressed, to reach a non-stressful situation, I wanted to ensure that the player's motivations would be similar enough to their character's that there wouldn't be a conflict between them.

    If I had cut out the Breakdown mechanic, for example, players would be encouraged to just go nuts and kill stuff rather than try to stay cool and survive. That's a totally different game.
