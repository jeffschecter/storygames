\n So, I've been enjoying the Compendium of Lost Words, and it's got me thinking about fun things to do with language.

It's become a bit of received wisdom that D&D renewed words like "orison" and "paladin" for their modern usage (and twisting some, like the latter, to the point where their original meanings are all-but-lost), and many trad games freature large glossaries of their in-game language.

So what remains to be mined? Well, how about borrowed words? English is full of words that are not, strictly-speaking, English words. I'm not talking about words that have faded into the background over time (like "faded" and "borrowed"), but more like obvious borrows like "psychiatrist" and "antibiotic" (a lot will be scientific or medical terms).

The play I am proposing is this: take a word that is clearly (by your definition of "clearly") borrowed into English, break it down into its component parts, and say something about how it might influence a setting or story.

I'll start:
Geographer
Literal translation: earth writer
The great monoliths of the People were more than just their places of power, the sites of their ceremonies. They were the People's very history, written upon the face of the Land by forces beyond the comprehension of most. Only a handful of each generation found the calling of the Earth Writer, and they could not explain how their abilities worked. It was just something they knew, they said, just something they could do, without thought.
