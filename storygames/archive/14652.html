\n For game night, I'd like to cook food from different parts of the world.

You know how game night food works, right? Something simple, which you can cook fairly quickly, then eat without interrupting your game too much. Maybe you stop the game to eat, but it's not too much trouble. And nothing too expensive.

In this thread, please ask about something you'd like to cook. Then someone will tell you how to cook it. We've got people from various parts of the world, so we can exchange recipes and cook some new things. Be aware, when you describe recipes, that ingredients aren't standard worldwide: thus, if you mention Turkish chilli peppers, they're not available everywhere. So describe anything odd and we'll find substitutes.

And you can totally ask about things like sausages and pork and...you know, it doesn't all have to be Szechuan cuisine. So, you can ask me how to cook sausages and mash and I'll tell you.

Oh, and you can volunteer recipes that are emblematic of your part of the world. So, I've no idea what a typical Australian dish is, but I'd like to hear it.

To start, could someone tell me how to cook bean chilli?

    \n My bean chili recipe is pretty simple.

    For two people:
    1 onion, chopped finely
    1 tin of tomatoes (I use Sainsbury's Chair de Tomate)
    1 lot of black beans (I use Sainsbury's organic black beans)
    1 pepper, chopped to a similar size to the beans
    1 clump of fresh coriander (some people don't like this so you could skip it), chopped
    Mole

    Mole is the sauce which consists of whatever you have to hand. In my case this usually means:
    Some chilis (I've got some Hatch chipotle chilis but if you can't get these, and it's not easy outside New Mexico, then your favourite chilis, sometimes I use Cholula chili sauce, from Asda or Waitrose). I remove the seeds and soak them in a little hot water. Then I use scissor to chop them more finely.
    Dried coriander 1/2 tsp
    Dried cumin 1/4 tsp
    Cocoa powder 1/2 tsp
    Some garlic
    Quite a lot of black pepper
    Cinnamon powder, ginger (root preferably) if you like

    You can get the mole together first, including that hot water, to make a paste in the frying pan but I usually do the onions first, browning them slowly, then chuck in everything else except the fresh coriander. I then cook for a bit, 10-15 minutes, take off the heat, add the chopped coriander and then serve with rice or tortillas, grated cheese, sour cream. The dish is even better if you cook the chili the day before and just leave to sit. Reheat and add the coriander just before serving.

    I'd like to cook the best cheesecake in the world. A cooked cheesecake, preferably with a pastry bottom.

    \n Here's Grit tofu, a staple at The Grit restaurant in Athens, Georgia, easy and delicious:

    You need one pound of firm tofu, soy sauce, oil, and nutritional yeast. Cut tofu into d6 sized cubes.

    Lightly oil a non-stick or cast iron skillet and place over medium high heat. Allow oil to heat slightly and add tofu and a dash of soy suace. Toss until evenly and lightly golden born. Remove from skillet and drain. Add a little oil back in and turn the heat way up, then throw the tofu back in. Saute until it gets nice and brown, adding a liberal jot of soy sauce and then a bunch of nutritional yeast to coat the tofu. Toss it hard to get the nutritional yeast on everything, then serve it hot. You can just eat it plain, and you might, but it's great protein with anything. Vegetables, peanut sauce or sriracha. At The Grit they serve it over rice with melted butter, which is crazy good.

    \n One thing we do during summer in Columbus Ohio is whip out the grill and make beer brats (bratwurst!):

    Fill some kind of grill proof pan or tinfoil container with beer (1 can or bottle) some onions, and a little water (1/2 a cup). Place brats inside.     \n New Mexican Salsa:

    8 oz can of tomatoes (preferably fire-roasted)
    2-5 Jalapeno peppers (or serranos, for a bit more heat)
    half a red onion (other onions will do in a pinch, but nothing too sweet)
    2-4 cloves of garlic
    salt to taste

    Optional:
    Cilantro, about .25 to .5 cup
    Juice of 1 lime
    New Mexico red chile powder to taste
    Anything else you can think of

    Peel the garlic. Remove the skin from the onion and roughly chop. Remove the stem from the chiles and roughly chop.

    In a blender or food processor, combine all ingredients and blend until chunky but dip-like. Season with salt as needed, but remember your chips may be salty too. Add chile powder for heat if needed, but beware that the salsa may get hotter after sitting for a while.

    Eat with tortilla chips, preferably salty fatty ones.

    Store in a sealed containter in the fridge for up to two weeks.

    \n A taste of Northern California: roasted kale chips.

    Get some kale, the crinklier and younger the better. You'll want at least two bunches, because kale is not the most substantial of foods. Wash it thoroughly and dry it even more thoroughly. YOUR LIFE DEPENDS ON IT. Do not suffer the kale to remain wet. Cut it into inch-wide strips and toss it in a big ol' bowl with just enough heat tolerant oil (I like grapeseed, but canola is good here) to coat the leaves, which is less than you think. I like to do all this before gamers appear.

    Once the fellowship is gathered, preheat your oven to 400F. Toss in hella salt. Spread the kale out in a single layer on a baking sheet or four - you'll probably wind up working in batches, unless you have a lot of oven space. Put the kale into the oven for about five minutes, then check for crispness, checking every 2 minutes after that. You're looking for the kale to be kind of like a fresh potato chip,but if it starts to burn, stop cooking it, obviously. Once it's out of the oven, immediately top with black pepper and, if you like, any other spices that appeal to you (I like smoked paprika). Then eat them while they're warm. This is way better than the usual chips.

    I like to serve this with thin slices of hard savory cheese, like Dry Jack or Parmesan, and a light-bodied white wine.

    \n Rob's Southwest Chili (aka Fajita Chili)

    As noted in the last chili recipe, chili is generally best when made a day ahead of time and allowed to sit under low heat for many hours. Like, you can put it in a crock pot on low all day.

    This recipe evolved after I learned how to make fajitas. Sometimes I call it fajita chili.

    Ingredients:
    1/2 onion (Red or sweet yellow is best. White are too harsh.)
    1 bell pepper (I prefer red, yellow, or orange ones. But you can use green if you like the stronger flavor.)
    1 pound chicken or cheap steak (a double batch with both is also good)
    1 can black beans
    1 can kidney beans
    1 small can corn
    1 can stewed or diced tomatoes, flavored (I often end up using "Italian Style")

    Preparing the meat
    Cumin is important, it's the most significan "mexican food" flavoring
    Chili Powder
    McCormick's Montreal Steak or Montreal Chicken, accordingly
    Lemon juice for chicken or red wine vinegar for steak

    Cube the meat. Marinade it with the "Preparing the meat" items while you chop the veggies.

    Chop the onion and pepper to pieces the size of kidney beans.

    In a large frying pan, heat some olive oil. Brown the meat lightly. Then add the peppers and onions. You are basically making fajitas in the frying pan, except that everything is cut into small pieces instead of long strips. Use pretty high heat, but not insanely high if you have a hardcore stove.

    In a large pot (or a crock pot, if you're going to let is sit for hours), heat the kidney beans, black beans, corn, and tomatoes. Drain the beans about halfway before adding them and drain the corn completely. Cook at about 3/4 heat (or start the crock pot at high).

    Once the onions have cooked clear and the peppers have started to blacken a hair, dump the fajitas into the beans. The beans should just be bubbling pretty good about this time.

    Turn to medium-low heat and let it cook for as long as you have time for. It needs at least 1/2 hour for the flavors to mix together. Taste it and add more spices if you like, but don't rush the spices - they can sneak up on you, especially because you spiced the meat and that flavor won't hit hard until you're a few bites in.

    Serve with tortilla chips. Shredded cheese sprinkled on top is good. A dollop of sour cream is also good. A sprinkle of chopped onions would be good too.

    \nRight on. Here are two dishes that my area is known for.

    So there's jota, a farmers' soup. It's the best the next day (leave it in the fridge overnight) and especially if you've been working outdoors, or hiking in the mountains or something. There are various recipes, but here's one I use. You're going to need:
    -half a kilo of sour (fermented) turnip mustard (if you can't get this, fermented cabbage or sauerkraut should do)
    -1 can of brown or white beans
    -several bay laurel leaves
    -a few cloves of garlic
    -a pair of sausages (I don't know how our sausages relate to British ones, but you can use pig shank instead)
    -salt & pepper
    -a spoon of butter

    Peel and mince the garlic. Melt the butter in a pot at low temperature, and add the garlic. Wash and peel the potatoes, then dice them. Put them in with the garlic. Cut the sausages into thick slices and put them in, too. Add water to cover everything and cook on low for as long as it takes to make the potatoes tender.
    Wash the shredded turnip and cook it in a different pot for about 15 minutes, drain the water then add the turnip to the first pot, along with the beans. Add the laurel leaves (depends on taste, I use around 5), and ground pepper. Keep on cooking slowly but take it off the fire before the potatoes start falling apart. It should look like this. Serve with bread.

    The second one is frtalja and it's much simpler. You're going to need:
    -one or two eggs per person
    -0,25l of milk per person
    -flour
    -salt
    -fresh fennel leaves
    -diced prosciutto or pancetta
    -parmigiano cheese and rucola optional

    Stir the eggs with milk and salt then add just enough flour that it gets a bit thicker (something like pancakes). Let it rest for a bit, chop the fennel and stir it into the batter. Heat up some olive oil in a pan and pour in the batter in very thick (depending on how much mixture you've got you might need to do this several times). Sprinkle the still liquid mixture with prosciutto and flip after it has been baked at the bottom. After it's finished pop it on a plate and cover with cheese and rucola. Cut in.
    It should look like this.

    \n My friends and I rarely prepare game night food. Usually, we're grabbing some fast food on the way to whomever's house. Occassionally, though, we will make some snacks.

    My particular favorite is mochi crunch popcorn:
    Take one part US buttery popcorn, one part Japanese chili bits crackers or arare, several dashes of Nori Komi Furikake, and a lot of Hawaiian ingenuity, and toss together while the popcorn is hot.

    ...for those who don't know what arare is (aside from Arare-chan a la Dr. Slump), they are small Japanese rice crackers (sometimes referred to as mochi crunch in Hawaii, though this tends to be a broad generalization), typically flavored with soy sauce, much like tinier cousins of the senbei cracker. Chili bits tend to be the most popular variety, which have a bit of spice to them (sometimes in varying degrees of pain)

    ...and as for Nori Komi Furikake, its a mixture of seaweed, sesame seeds, bonito flakes, and a little bit of salt and sugar. Typically served on top of rice, but friggin delicious on popcorn. There are actually many types of furikake, and maybe you'd like to experiment. I've had limited success with other varieties, like the curry flake furikake. Best to avoid the kim chee furikake, though.

    As for the Hawaiian bit? The idea I believe originated with the Hawaiian brand Typhoon Popcorn. But they tend to be expensive, so I get the ingredients individually instead (and I get to avoid their pouch of nasty butter-flavored oil).

    \n Ok, so here a few more. Non-local and semi-invented by me and my friends.

    Bachelors' salad
    -lots and lots of corn salad
    -a couple of big potatoes
    -mustard
    -balsamic vinegar
    -olive oil
    -mayo
    -bacon
    -a couple of eggs
    -smoked cheese
    -salt to taste
    -a huge bowl

    Wash, peel and dice then cook the potatoes, they shouldn't overcook. Let them cool, too. Heat up some olive oil in a pan and make crispy bacon. Let it cool.
    Wash the corn salad and put it in the huge bowl. In a cup mix a couple of spoonfuls of mustard, a spoonful of mayo and the balsamic vinegar and mix well till you get a runny dip. If it's too thick add more vinegar or a bit of olive oil. Dice the smoked cheese. Throw the cheese, potatoes and bacon in the salad and mix. Poach the eggs, throw them in, too (as soon as they cool a bit, but not too much as cold poached eggs aren't good). Pour the mustard-thingie dip all over it. Give everyone their own fork and fight for it.

    Devil Snacks
    -dried prunes, with pits removed
    -almonds, toasted
    -parmigiano
    -bacon

    Slice the parmigiano into tiny chunks. Take a prune and insert an almond and a bit of cheese where the pit used to be (if you can't find dried prunes without pits, make a cut, remove the pit and replace with almond & cheese). Wrap each individual prune in a slice of bacon and put on a tray covered with baking paper. Put try into a preheated oven (220\xb0C) and bake till the bacon gets crispy.

    (this is actually just a simple variation of the British Devils on Horseback snack as far as I'm aware)

    Strawberry Powerjuice
    -lots of strawberries
    -several bars of dark chocolate (85% pure or so)
    -a few fresh mint leaves

    Put everything in a container. Blend. Drink.

    \nThe Best Dip Ever
    From New Zealand

    This dip is kind of declasse, plus it uses some ingredients you might not find easily outside of New Zealand, but here it is like, the standard dip. So much so that many supermarkets stock the ingredients together, for your convenience.

    1 Packet "Maggi" brand onion soup powder. Substitute at your own risk.
    250ml Reduced Cream. It's only called this in New Zealand and maybe Australia. Try "Extra Thick Cream" as a substitute*, or else sour cream if you must.
    1 tsp lemon juice.

    Combine all ingredients. Chill for 1 hour.

    * DON'T just use regular cream. That would be disgusting. Definitely don't use clotted cream either. Reduced cream is heat-treated, which thickens it, so it's very thick while still only being about 20% butter fat. Seriously, if in doubt, use sour cream. It's not bad.

    \nSwedish korvstroganoff (Sausage Stroganoff)

    If you have ever read the Wikipedia entry for Beef stroganoff you have no doubt seen the reference to a Swedish variant made with sausages in the last paragraph. Maybe you have even wondered how to cook it yourself ...

    This dish is very common/popular here, I had it maybe once a month all through my school years. And these days we make a point of eating it once every time we gather for a minicon.

    (My English vocabulary for kitchen related stuff is even more limited than my gaming dito, but I hope you'll understand anyway.)

    Korvstroganoff
    Ingredients for 4 adults\n
      \n
    • 1 * 800g Falukorv* sausage
      • \n
    • 2 * 500g cans of tinned crushed tomatoes
      • \n
    • 1dl pur\xe9e of tomato (or ketchup if you can't find it)
      • \n
    • 4 onions
      • \n
    • 2dl cream (or milk if you forgot to buy cream)
      • \n
    • Some butter for frying
      • \n
    • 1 tablespoon of sugar
      • \n
    • 2 tablespoons of paprika powder
      • \n
    • Some salt and pepper
      • \n
    \nDo the following:\n
      \n
    1. Cut the onions into slices and fry them with the sugar & butter in a pot.
      2. \n
    2. Cut the Falukorv into short rods, and dump them into the pot when the onions have grown soft (not brown)
      3. \n
    3. Add the paprika powder, the pur\xe9e of tomatoes and stir
      4. \n
    4. When the sausage rods look "done", add the crushed tomatoes and stir
      5. \n

      6. \n

      7. \n

    * Falukorv is a staple of the Swedish kitchen, it is cheap and can be cooked in a multitude of ways. It is a sausage made from pork and beef or veal, held together by potato starch flour. Usually it has a low content of meat, around 60%-70%, explaining the low price. It is not spicy, rather bland to be honest so it is popular with children too. If I could not find actual Falukorv I'd go looking for something cheap with the meat ground to a fine paste, no chunky sausages here.

    \nLayered Bean Dip

    This is a TexMex sort of dip that has the advantages that you can prepare a bunch ahead of time, then only heat when needed. Good for games, but slightly messy. You can also use quick ingredients (cans, packages, etc.) if you want. You need:

    One or more pizza pans (just flat-ish, circular, cookie sheets, basically)
    Refried beans (for some reason, a tall order in some parts of the world. I don't know why.)
    Taco seasoning (can buy as mix in some places; otherwise use cumin, chili powder, pepper, other southwest US spices)
    Salsa (any kind)
    Sour cream
    Cheese, thinly grated (some places sell packs of shredded "Mexican" cheese; otherwise, cheddar or similar will do)
    Green onions, diced (aka scallions, spring onions, salad onions, green shallots, onion sticks, or syboes)
    Lettuce, shredded
    Corn chips


    Mix the seasoning into the refried beans.
    Spread the beans onto the pizza pan in a flat layer. Thickness to taste. About the height of a USB plug is about right (under 5mm).
    Spread a thin layer of sour cream on top of the bean layer.
    Spread a layer of salsa over the sour cream.
    Scatter the cheese in a layer.
    Scatter the onions.

    Refrigerate assembled pizza pan(s) until ready to use.

    Heat pan at around 400\xba F until the cheese starts to melt. Remove from oven, scatter lettuce on (if desired) and serve.

    Eat by scooping with corn chips.

    \n Part 1.

    A New Mexico game night meal starts the day before. It's easy except for the mole

    The recipes are kinda vague since this is how they were passed down to me. All the measurements are done in hand or with whatever cup you have around. It would be easier to show you, so stop by sometime

    Beans
    ~1 cup dried beans into the slow cooker. Add about 3 inches of water over the top. Cook for most of the day. Can add a bay lead if you want. Or some onions, garlic, and cumin near the end of the cook time.

    Posole
    ~1 cup of dried posole (white or blue). Cover with about 3-4 inches of water. Cook until the posole blossom open. At this point you can add stuff in like sliced onions, garlic, ~ 1 cup broth. If you are a meat eater toss in your pork or mutton now. Cook over low heat for the rest of the day. At the end of the cooking day add 1/2 - 3/4 cup red sauce and salt to taste.

    Mole
    This is a complicated recipe. It will need it's own post. I'll see if I can get something typed up tonight,

    Red Sauce
    New Mexican red chilies (15-20) with removed stems. Put in a bowl and pour boiling water over the chilies. Cover and let sit until soft. Place soft chilies into a blender with some garlic cloves, lime juice, and salt. Add chili water as needed. Puree. In a saucepan make a golden rue (~3 Tbl butter and 4~Tbl flour). Add the ground chili mixture to the rue and cook down. Add chili water as needed to get the right consistency. Should cook slowly for a few hours.

    \n Part 2

    Day Of

    Reheat beans, posole, an red sauce.

    Tortillas - made by hand. You can use white or whole wheat flour. Basically you add some flour into a bowl. Add some fat (veggie shortening, lard, renderings, NOT BUTTER). Add some cold water. Mix well and form into a ball. Let rest for awhile. The break off pieces and roll out flat. You can do this by hand or use a rolling pin. Cook on a cast iron griddle unto done. You'll see little brown spots and smell cooked dough. These will be used to get at the beans, etc..

    Fry Bread, Dine - Flour, some baking powder, salt, fat (same as for tortillas). Do the exact same thing as the tortillas except \u2026. in a cast iron skillet pour 1/4 in oil (high temp). Heat up really hot and place a rolled out dough into the oil. It will puff up like crazy. Flip and brown on both sides. Make a lot. Serve hot with honey and/or adobo sauce. Works with whole wheat flour too.

    Salad - Take a large salad bowl. Add olive oil, basaltic vinegar, local dark honey, black pepper. Mix well and coat the bowl with the dressing. Add in your mixed green salad and toss to coat. Add coarse grain salt.

    Spinach Dish - I don't remember the Spanish name for this. In a pan add some butter and olive oil. Heat up. Add spinach leaves, red peppers flakes, and salt. Saute quickly. Add 1/2 water and cover. Simmer until done.

    Sometimes the Fry Bread - Dine is swapped out for Zuni bread which is a oven fired sourdough type of bread. All of these recipes vary (I have several of each) depending on who you talk to.

    Sometime the Spinach Dish is swapped out for native corn and native squash cooked in butter with onions, garlic, salt, pepper

    \n(True Mexican) Guacamole
    1. Mash 1 or 2 avocados. (You could blend, but a manual mashing gives you a better texture.)
    2. Add 1/4 onion and a couple of garlic cloves, finely minced.
    3. Mince and mix some fresh green chilis to taste. (Use canned jalape\xf1os if you must, but there's nothing like the juicy and extra-hot taste of some fresh chilis. Just beware the seeds.)
    (Some people add half a tomato, in very small cubes. This is completely optional, and personally I prefer mine un-tomatoed.)
    4. Add half a lemon. (Just make sure it's the sour variety.)
    5. Put some salt, black pepper and a splash of olive oil.
    6. Mix well and enjoy! (Try to consume it as soon as possible. The avocado will brown in time, which doesn't change the taste, but makes it look not so good. Sour lemon helps, but only for a while.)

    \nHuevos Rancheros (Rancher Eggs)
    1. In a pan, fry some corn tortillas (only for 30 to 45 seconds per side per tortilla in really hot oil, you want it soft, not crunchy). (2 per portion)
    2. In the same pan, make some sunny side up eggs. (1 per portion)
    3. Serve. 1 tortilla on bottom, egg in the middle, add liberal quantities of Salsa Roja, 1 tortilla on top. Eat while hot.


    For the Salsa Roja (Red Chili Sauce) you can use Ara's recipe (back at comment #32). Here's an alternative:

    Salsa Roja (Mexico City Style)
    - 1/4 minced onion
    - 1 clove of garlic
    - 1 tomato
    - 3 to 5 fresh chilis of your choice (more if small or tame ones, less if bigger or hotter ones)
    - Fresh parsley
    - Salt and pepper

    Saute the onion and garlic. Blend everything together (add 1/2 cup of water) and return to the same pot. Stir until it boils, then let it cool down. Taste and add salt if needed. (Beware, it might be hot.)

    Enfrijoladas
    1. Fry some corn tortillas (they must be soft, not crunchy). Put on a paper towel to eliminate excess fat.
    2. Blend some refried beans with some water (in a 4 to 1 proportion).
    3. Heat the watered down beans in a pot (no need to add more oil).
    4. Serve. Bend the tortillas in half and put some 3 or 4 in the same plate. Pour the beans on top and add some sour cream, minced fresh onion and cheese. (You might add any Chili Salsa you want.)

    (A true mexican would never use cheddar. Luckily you're most likely not Mexicans, so no problem. Best to use some Mexican Cheese if you can aford to. Best ones are Queso Fresco, Oaxaca or Manchego.)

    For a +protein variant, put some stewed chicken in the tortillas.

    \n New Mexican huevos rancheros follow just about the same recipe, but always use a green or red chile sauce on top, along with some cheese (cheddar, sorry).

    Green chile sauce varies from northern to southern NM but its pretty much the cornerstone of NM cooking. Northern NM is more of a thin soup, southern is more likely to be thick and creamy, similar to a cream of mushroom soup. Either way, you throw it on just about anything (even ice cream).

    To be truly New Mexican you have to be using Hatch green chile. Hatch chile is hotter than similar breeds like Anaheim and pretty much only grows in New Mexico. You always roast it first, ideally in an open fire basket, but in an oven set to broil if need be.
