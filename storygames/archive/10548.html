\n We're talking about Ideal games and my friend writes this stuff, and I'm like, there's got to be something to say that strikes a good balance for him, but I'm drawing a blank here.
I like......
-the less mechanical feel of IAWA when it comes to combat.
\xa0
-but at the same time d20 games tend to have a way of quantifying advantages/disadvantages more clearly (e.g. bringing a knife to a gun fight won't affect you in a wicked age).
\xa0
-the A-team "when a plan comes together" style of problem solving.\xa0 But this has to be in small doses because it can move from roleplaying to just planning.
\xa0
-cool scenes that you laugh or talk about afterwards
\xa0
-for themes and genres I'm pretty flexible I struggle to think of a RPG where the concept sounds like I wouldn't have fun..... well maybe that game the D&D characters were playing in a one panel comic in the original DMG about accountants.\xa0 But I won't lie.... I do like a good fight rather than a game of diplomacy.\xa0 I'd rather shoot the senator than debate him/her.
\xa0
-I like a faster flow of action so that each game session feels like a lot happened.... but not at the risk of glossing over a key point.\xa0
\xa0
-variety.....using a common setting but different systems has allowed a wide range of game styles.\xa0 Probably IAWA is the game that I could play the most of repeatedly.\xa0
\xa0
My dream game would be one where the control of the story is more equally shared by GM and PCs, the prep is light, with some action and problem solving.

    \n The way I see it, Ryan, many of those suggestions involve no problem-solving and fictional realism at all. That really was the part that got my attention, and the reason for why I suggested T&T. In general I'd throw out every title on the list that relies on purely mechanical adjudication of conflicts, as those simply won't provide a meaningful sense of accomplishment and realism. For instance, Agon - it's a game where everything that matters is carefully mechanically quantified, so nobody ever has to evaluate the fiction and solve problems. I don't know if your friend means this with his list, but that's what it sounds like to me, that he'd prefer it if the game focused on the fiction and allowed problems and solutions to come up from there instead of being all mechanized.
