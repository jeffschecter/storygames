\n So we had the thread on The Death of GM Craft and the rise of non-traditional game design and then Traditional RPGs GMing Best Practices.

I felt as an addition to the talk about traditional GMing advice, I feel like there should be some attempt to categorize types of traditional RPG play. The advice for a game of Ars Magica may be very different from the advice for Toon, say. For example, I gave one shot at defining categories of RPGs in
A History of Fashion in RPG Design. Even within a given game system, there can be very different types of play, though.

So, let me try a few on for size:

Dungeon and/or Mission based play have non-linear collections of challenges where a broad goal is set by the GM, and the players work together to tackle it. At each location, there are a number of choices of where the PCs may go. The players tend to control the pacing and scene breaks by choosing when they want to move ahead and where they go.

Action / Set-piece Scene based play jumps back and forth between (1) set-piece scenes that are partly open-ended such as combat or perhaps drama; and (2) strongly GM-controlled narration as the action moves from one scene to another. This is similar to the advice for games like Star Wars and Deadlands.

Sandbox includes a number of different styles of play based on having a region/scope of play not designed for a particular mission or challenge. The players can create goals within that scope, but are not assigned missions. The GM may vary in how actively they toss in complications for the PCs, but they do not supply goals or missions.

PC Rivalry is a style of play based around rivalry among PCs as a driver - such as a throne war or similar scenario in the Amber Diceless RPG. The GM may set the stage and throw in complications, but much of the action is driven by clashes between PCs and shifting alliances.
There is a lot of variation in these and possibly whole other categories, but I thought I'd start there and ask for comments.

    \n There's also Situational GMing that brings a play style that's similar to Sandbox without the map obsession. Clash discussed it here. I feel very close to this type of GMing.\n
    Sandbox GMs create everything upfront. That way, everywhere the players go or do, there is something in place. Every hex is stocked with an encounter or a roll on an encounter table or a place of some sort. This requires an immense amount of prep work.

    Situational GMing is more like improv within constraints - these constraints being the NPC or organization's Goals, Personality, and Resources. The goal of an NPC is not to oppose or support the PCs. Those are consequences of the NPC's goals and the PCs' actions. If the PCs go one way, their actions may align with the goals of the NPC, in which case there would be sympathy. If the the actions of the PCs go against the goals of the NPC, there will be opposition. If the actions of the PCs are orthogonal to the NPC's goalsthere will be neither sympathy nor opposition.

    What the NPC's actions are are also constrained by his Personality and his Resources. The actions of a character with a vengeful, embittered personality will be very different from those of a character with a laid back, wry personality. Resources limit the scale of the NPC's actions - the response of a wealthy NPC can be much greater than that of a poor one. The same goes for organizations.

    The name comes from how the GM works. The GM presents his players with a Situation, which the PCs must respond to. the response of the PCs establishes a vector, with both direction and energy, which is where the PCs want to take things. Depending on what this vector is, the GM responds appropriately from the NPCs, trying to achieve their goals. This goes on until an NPC achieves his goal, the NPCs' Resources are exhausted, or the PCs get what they want.

    Along the way, new Situations can be thrown at the PCs. This serves to recharge the group's energy. Situations are the Situational GM's main tool for controlling the pace of the game. These situations can be set up between sessions, or improvised on the spot. The Situational GM does very little prep work. With the three constraints for each major NPC, a simple quick reference of generic opponents, and the initial situation, the GM is good to go.

    \n I like this project, John. I'm now thinking back to earlier attempts to break up GNS-Simulationism into useful categories. I've begun thinking of these more in terms of the proper social attitude and what the GM's responsible for, and these are what jump to mind:

    Play my story - Discover the GM's metaplot bit by bit, adding reactions and color along the way and showing how the big picture affects your character moment to moment. Who knows, in the final scene you might even get some say in whether the badguys destroy the world or not!

    A sandbox of opportunities - Choose missions and projects from many options, hopefully embedded in an active world that glues them together.

    Storybox - A blend of the above. The GM has a plot, and the characters will interact with it, but they have a lot of freedom in how to engage with it and which parts to experience.

    Emulation - Pick a source fiction and copy the parts that are important to you as well as you can, with the whole group as the arbiter of what's important. (If the GM's the arbiter, then their attempt to produce a "correct" story arc will become my story or storybox.)

    Run a Simulation - Set up a fictional situation and a process for exploring it, and then go through that process without any bias or alterations, to find out what will result. Example: the high concept premise, like Nazi Supers or Cthulhu in Space.

    System-based challenge - Min-max and find clever rules exploits to win! GM creates a fair challenge and then tries to beat you with it.

    Fiction-based challenge - Take your knowledge of how the setting works (usually your knowledge of the real world, applied to a realistic setting) and use it to solve fictional problems.
