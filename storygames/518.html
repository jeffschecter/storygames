Turtle-Players

TonyLB
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Play Advice

Turtle players are those that, when presented with risk or the appearance of risk, retreat into an impregnable shell. They can drain a game of fun in about no time flat. If you haven't ever encountered one then I recommend several of the other lovely threads in the forum, because I'm not going to argue about whether or not they exist (though I'll certainly discuss their origin and nature if people have thoughts).

My question is the perennial question: How do you prevent such play from ruining your fun as a GM?

There seem (to me) to be several entirely different ways you could go about this:

  • Try to "reform" the turtle players behavior to make him more willing to take chances (a dicey proposition at best)
  • Find a way, as GM, to enjoy torturing the turtle player, often by beating on his shell with a sledge-hammer and watching him frantically patch it
  • Brick over the neck-hole of the shell, disempower the player as much as you need to, and then have fun with the other players until such time as the turtle player breaks out of the prison you've built around him (if he ever does)

Do other folks have other ways they've dealt with it?

Also,do you think that as GMs we have any moral obligation to try to either (a) change our players so that they are people who can have fun in our game or (b) change our game so that it is one that the people we have can have fun with? How far does that moral obligation extend, if it gets ... y'know ... really, really hard to do?

    People come to games with different expectations.

    Some people are there to take risks, accomplish great things, and be brash and loud and boisterous.

    Some people are there to watch other people take risks and be brash and loud.

    Your post, Tony, presupposes that there's something WRONG with sitting back, participating on a minimal level, and enjoying the show.

    You don't try to reform them.

    You don't try to break them down.

    You don't try to exclude them.

    What you do, is you realize that you don't need the validation of everyone at the table in order to have fun. Engage with the people who are engaging, give the people who are sitting back the opportunity to jump in if they like, and you don't hold it against them if they don't.

    Sure, there are different circumstances that will trigger turtling in "borderline cases", after all, there's never going to be a clear line between turtling and just being cautious, it's a broad spectrum.

    I want to throw in something that I've observed to attach onto the side of the motivation for turtling that's previously been identified. I fully agree that a lot of turtles get how they are because of bad experiences in the past, where they were trying to not be turtles and got savagely punished. But there's one word that you can add in that makes things even more turtly: deconstruction.

    You'll find that turtles are most often the ones not interested in celebrating source material as much as breaking it down and deconstructing it, pointing out the seams rather than breezing over them, and so on. After all, being cynical and breaking down what others are building is easier than contributing and it doesn't expose you. So you'll often find turtles sniping away at genre conventions, complaining with villains make dramatic escapes in stylized games, refusing to go on the good king's quest to slay the dragon, and so on.

    There's a lesser form of turtling that is Reluctant Hero Syndrome which turtles will often lay claim to when cornered. More on this later, perhaps.

This is his timid little signal to you that he's willing to make a suggestion in what the group does. My advice is to take that suggestion and go with it. You might have to make an effort to do so, like if he wants to order Indian food or rent Brain from the Planet Arous. But take his suggestion. This is your timid little signal back that his voice will be heard and it is indeed possible for his suggestions to be taken. This will make him feel better about himself and help him to open up more.

    Now, it may not always be possible to go with the suggestion, especially if you've got a whole room full of turtles, but try your best. They might slam the door, but in time it'll open again.

    it's all about making the guy feel comfortable with raising his concerns with you or the group and no one can make him do that. He has to do it himself. All you can do it be encouraging when he does try in a way that will make him likely try again. So, don't be dismissive, don't be condescending, don't crack jokes. You have to make like what he said matters and was taken seriously.

    That's a lot more useful that working out an elaborate taxonomy. I mean, how would that help?
