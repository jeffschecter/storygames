\n We've gotten into an interesting conversation on the IPR message group about elevator pitches, i.e., quick pitches to live people. It started with Fred calling for pitches to help sell games at conventions. Then, people started chiming in with pitches for their own games. So Malcolm drops pretty, shiny sell text on us and adds that someone else summed the game up as "motherf*&!?ing Nazi zombies!" My reaction was that pitch number two was much, much stronger. (I'm guilty of this with my own games, e.g., Passages.) I posited that maybe we're not the best ones to write our own pitches. I suspect it's just another stripe of our hesitancy to shoot our own sacred cows.

Thoughts?

Even better, do an elevator pitch for someone else's game.

    \nHmm, that's a great tagline and would probably be great on the cover. But my position is that the face-to-face pitch should be specific, concrete, and detailed--less polished, more frantic. So, turning the critical eye to myself:

    My "official" Passages pitch was: "Literary adventure in the Victorian era--take the helm of the Nautilus, cross swords with the Scarlet Pimpernel, match wits with the devious Professor Moriarty."

    But the best pitch I've had for it was: "The first adventure I ran for Passages was War of the Worlds--except the Martians are actually part of a fiendish plot by Dr. Fu Manchu to destroy England."

    Maybe the distinction is one of media, i.e., spoken word vs. written word.

    This reminds me of the for-YouTube (and similar services) commercial that I keep dreaming about directing for Dogs: two people in period clothing & Coats getting into an argument, going through the stages of escalation, and ending with one drawing down on the other, who looks back in fear. In between each escalation is a quick cut to a black screen with white lettering saying "Escalate", in ever-increasing font.     \n Stepping in as a lover of The Fast Pitch/Elevator Pitch, and a big seller dude at the Forge booth for a couple years (Ron gave me a gold star and everything!), I have to say that the perfect elevator pitch is more than one sentence. One sentence, and it's a tagline, or a sound byte, or a blurb for the bottom of a poster. A real elevator pitch makes an evocative statement or two, but it hangs together with a few tight sentences. And as Firefly states, it's also great when speaking live to recognize your audience.

    It's usually 2-3 sentences, and can be said in its entirety in like one to two breaths. Also, since it's a game, I think a better one will focus both a little on the game's coolness, and the coolness that you will get out of the game.

    Fred's GenCon one is awesome. I also think Eric's is really evocative, too.\n
    Posted By: Firefly Games
    'You're a tiny faery in a big, big world. It's a storytelling game of fairy-tale adventure for ages 6 & up, so it's also a great way to introduce people to gaming.'
    \nFirefly, I think the latter half is awesome and really directed to people: They will know right there whether it's going to be something they're interested in trying or not. The first part (You're a tiny faery in a big, big world) kinda sounds too much like a soundbyte, and doesn't really tell me much about the game. I'm thinking you might want to drop the Faery reference in there of course, and also say like 1-2 cool things about the setting or what the faeries do (or,"why they are tiny and the world is so big", something like that).

    BTW, total aside: Are you coming to GenCon? Will your super-expanded-deluxe-shouryuken edition of FT be there?

    EDIT: That said, it's easier to criticize than to provide. Here's an example, one that I might use for Tenra Bansho:

    "It's an anime game for 4 people that takes place on this futuristic-magic world, like feudal Japan but with high tech and magic. Ninjas, Sorcerers, mecha, dudes that control bugs that live in their bodies, android geisha, stuff like that. Sounds fucked up, right? Well, the real cool thing is that all of the rules make it play out like it was a theatrical play. You also get points for roleplaying well that you use to do badass anime-style things."
    Hmmm, I think I need to tighten that shit up into 3 sentences...

    -Andy

    \n Pitches.

    You need a two second pitch. This is a tagline, where I say "fairy-tale apocolypse" or "wall to wall decapitations and illicit sex" for Polaris.

    Fastlane's is "Fastlane is about living fast, dying young, and leaving a beautiful corpse."

    That hooks someone's interest. "Tell me more," they say, or they just give you this "tell me more" look.

    Then you give the longer pitch. For Polaris this is "you're living in the ruins of the greatest civilization that will ever be, sworn by the stars to save the last remnants of it from the demons that will eventually devour it. You're going to fail. So the question of the game isn't 'can you save the world,' but 'knowing that you will fail to save the world, what do you do?'"

    For Fastlane, this might be "In Fastlane, your entire life is a gamble. You might burn out fast, or you might hit it big and keep rolling, but walking away from the table is never an option. Oh, and it uses a *fucking roulette wheel*"
