\nHi,

\nRecently, I've been thinking about death spirals in a lot of RPGs I commonly play or have played in the past, in the context of some of the games that I am trying to design.
\n
\nI am wondering if there are games out there where, when your character is wounded/takes damage/etc., he gets mechanically more effective. I am struggling to think of real examples at the moment.
\n
\nDo you have any?
\n
\nIf so, what do you think the merits are of this sort of mechanic?
\n
\nWhat are the flaws?

    \n D&D 4E contains quite a few "blooded" effects; when below half health, your blooded effect kicks in.

    In Lacuna, your peak heart rate, wherein you add dice according to preference as opposed to stats, comes not too much before your maximum heart rate, wherein every roll risks possible injury or death.

    I've built a few mechanics into my games in a similar vein to these, as I believe they encourage characters to get out there, take a beating, and cause some real story to happen.

    \n This occurs in D&D 4th edition to some extent. There are some powers that are only effective when your character is at 'bloodied' status, which is when they reach half their hit points or lower. The merit of the mechanic is that it encourages characters to get into the fray and take some chances, which has a good chance of making fights more dramatic. Do you keep fighting on as bloodied to keep your bonuses from the related power, or do you squeal to your healer for help, thus negating the bonuses but putting you in a better position of safety? I can't think of an obvious flaw, though obviously the level of mechanical bonuses should be equal to the risk one takes by having them activate.

    \n Sirlin used the term perpetual comeback to refer to games where the closer you are to losing, the more resources you have to win with. He contrasts this against slippery slope games, where being closer to losing also robs you of resources.

    It's not focused on RPGs, but it might help give you more ideas.

    \n So, the first game where I saw this in all of its bloody glory was the one I'm translating, Tenra Bansho Zero. Basically, here's the character sheet (so you can see what I'm talking about):

    http://www.tenra-rpg.com/contest/sheets/TB0_characterSheet_01.jpg

    So, when you take damage ("10 points of damage"), you have this system:
    You have "Vitality" (I think it still says "Health" on the sheet), basically they're stun points, like HP Lite: When you run out of Vitality, your character passes out, and they're tapped out of the fight.
    You have "Wounds": Light, Heavy, Critical, and The Dead Box.
    When you take damage, you get to split the damage up however you want: 10 points can be All in Vit, All in Wounds, 3 in Vit and 7 in Wounds, 9 in Vit and 1 in Wounds, etc.
    Also, on top of that, you can pick exactly where to start to fill in wounds: So if you want, if you take 1 single point of damage from a punch or something, you can be all "OK, I take that as a Heavy Wound. I feel the punch crack a rib, and I double over in pain."
    When you check off the Dead Box, you can remove any amount of damage: Like if you take 5, 50, or 5000 damage (impossible, but still) you can either drop Vitality to 0 and say "I'm out of the fight", or you can stay in the fight by checking your "Dead Box", but this leaves you vulnerable to dying: If your Dead Box is checked, and you then lose all your Vitality, you die for reals (probably after saying a word or two after the battle ends).
    You can heal the dead box naturally, but magic (Buddhist Magic) doesn't work, since "you've already determined your fate to die."

    So the above is kinda how damage works, but here's more on the reverse death spiral:

    When you take a Heavy Wound, you get an additional +1 die to roll on any roll you make (in combat, out of combat, etc). If you have more than one Heavy Wound, the dice effect does not stack (you don't get "+6 dice" for having 6 Heavy Wounds).
    When you take a Critical Wound, you get +2 dice to roll. If you have a Heavy and a Critical wound, you only get the +2 dice for your highest ranking wound. If you have 3 critical wounds, you still only get the +2 dice (not +6). However, there's a risk: You lose 1 Vit point a round, "bleeding out".
    When you check the Dead Box, all other effects are cancelled (bonuses, bleeding out, etc) and you get +3 dice to all wounds until you heal yourself.

    Does that make sense?

    I like this system so much, that when we played our L5R first edition game (which has the most horrible death spiral of any game I know), I modified the wound track considerably: It basically went from 0,-1,-2,-3,-4,-5, Dead (etc) to 0,0,-1,-1,-2,-2,+3,+5,Dead.

    Merits: Dramatically simulates all movies, TV, anime and comics you've ever seen, ever.

    Drawbacks: If you're looking for Gritty Realism, keep on a'looking, because there's none here.

    -Andy

    \n (I believe you want the term "virtuous cycle" to contrast the "vicious cycle" of wounding -> less effectiveness.)

    It's not an RPG, but several HeroClix units become more powerful as they are hurt, before finally spiraling down to KO. The Hulk is the obvious example.

    Universalis (mentioned above) has two types of "damage": addition of a Fact stating damage to an entity, which adds to the entities total Facts; and Fact-elimination, which is the only way to actually remove an entity complete from the story. So, for instance, you can have the Fact "Dead" about, say, Obi Wan Kenobi... but he can still come back into the story in all sorts of ways (though someone in opposition to his return COULD use the "Dead" Fact against the player that is introducing the dead Jedi Master). Basically... Universalis doesn't do it the way the OP is thinking.

    Dread: TFBOP has a way to regain all of your lost Health and Fury... but it means you WILL die at the end of the scene/conflict, though with full Fury, you will probably take a couple of demons with you! For that matter, I have a similar effect in Player Priority rules for GLASS: if you Commit, you regain full Health and natural Armor, but if you go to 0 Health within 15-Psyche minutes of committing, you are permanently dead (on a related note, GLASS doesn't have a vicious spiral at all--like many games--although RPing injuries will probably earn extra Exp from a GM).

    \nNobilis and Greg Stolze's ...in Spaaace! have a bit of a reverse death spiral thing going on: You bid tokens from your pool, and the one who bid most wins the conflict. But that means that they have fewer for future conflicts, making them likely to lose later on.

    You could easily amp up the reverse death spiral system for those two games: When two characters are in a conflict, they bid how many tokens they're willing to spend. high bid wins, but then they trade the number of tokens that they had. So if you bid low, you gain tokens but lose the conflict. If you bid high, you win but wind up with fewer tokens. (I've been using a LARP system that does the same thing with playing cards. It works pretty well.)


    Dogs in the Vineyard is almost there, but not quite, as the system reward losing talking parts of conflicts, but the reward is for later conflicts, not this one.

    \n In a Wicked Age actually kinda does this, in a way. If you're beaten in the first round of a conflict, but not knocked entirely out of the conflict, you get your name on the Owe List.     \n I never twigged on that with EABA, although I only read Lite. That is a pretty cool idea, as it then goes to seriousness of damage, only being able to wound someone more with more serious damage. Not entirely realistic though, as in some situations you can ping someone to death, and it would also create a problem where someone with max wounds higher than the highest damage someone can cause is essentially invincible (unless there's something else mitigating in the system).

    Greg Stolze's A Dirty World has this to a degree, although it has both a normal death spiral and a reverse one. If you take small amounts of damage, your stats get shifted, so one goes down, the other goes up. The result is that while you get worse at one thing, you get better at another and can turn the tables by changing your tactics. If you take massive damage though (which is more rare), then it's a real death spiral. I liked having both.

    \n In 'My Life with Master', everytime a PC succeeds at an act of violence or villainy, their Self-Loathing increases... but Self-Loathing represents half the dice they roll for acts of violence and villainy! So the more bad deeds they perform, the better they get at carrying out bad deeds.

    \n
    \nOn the other hand, Weariness is the exact opposite; it is earned when minions fail at acts of violence and makes it harder to perform violence successfully, but in the Epilogue, a high Weariness score may lead to the minion simply running away... or being killed.
