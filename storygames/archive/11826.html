\n So, I wrote another Trail of Cthulhu scenario, which is released today. It's called The Watchers In The Sky.

It does some interesting things with the investigative structure. One thing that bothers me, about many Cthulhu scenarios, is that you eventually discover what the monsters are. Towards the end, the Keeper explains the monsters and their whole history. And it's always an anticlimax. It was more fun when they were mysterious.

In this scenario, that moment never comes. The monsters are never named (because, I think, naming monsters make people comfortable with them). And, although there's an investigative trail, many, many things are never explained.

There's a trick with reincorporation, too. Various things are reincorporated but never explained: for example, there's a spiralling star symbol that occurs many times, without explanation. It gives a weird, dreamlike effect.

One of my greatest pleasures, in writing these scenarios, is describing locations which Jerome then draws. So I write:

In the basement of the Temple Building, one of the more hideously Gothic of the University buildings, is the Dissection Theatre. It is pentagonal, five tiers of narrow wooden seating surrounding a central dissection table. When not packed with students, it is also a useful dissection room, if one does not mind the jars of pickled organs, animal, human and unidentifiable, that line the walls.

And Jerome draws this:



It's such a pleasure. Oh, and here's the cover.



There you go. I hope you enjoy it as much as I enjoyed writing it. Do tell me what you think.

    \n Thanks for the kind words. I really appreciate them.

    Jason, people seemed OK with the ending. (By contrast, the The Dying Of St Margaret's ending got me mentioned in an RPG.NET thread called Things so dumb in pre-made scenarios you should have beaten the author senseless. I am proud.)

    I stole a trick from Lovecraft, actually. There's a bit of pulpy action at the end, which disguises the fact that the mystery isn't really over. If you end a scenario with a fight/chase/ritual, it feels like the end, even though you didn't quite solve the mystery. And that hanging mystery leaves a really nice tension in the air.

    Alex, it's hardly changed since the first playtest. It's one of those things that just playtested very smoothly.

    Graham
