    \n For myself, I suffer from chronic depression, with episodes of major depression (I have excellent care, but right now am near the bottom).

    During these episodes, intense emotion can make me "crack", a feeling like I'm sobbing, the walls are closing in, and someone is sitting on my chest. This makes me want to avoid gaming, especially with people I don't know that well. I don't want to spoil anyone's game; people come to play, not to watch me implode.

    I'm very grateful that there are still some games where I'm still welcome. When I'm gaming, I can keep a lot of that stuff away, and the social contact definitely helps. But at times I worry that I'll become a drain on the table's resources.

    \n This is a great topic; thanks for raising it.

    I have ADHD \u2014 just the inattentive kind \u2014 although it wasn't diagnosed till a few years ago. (When I was a kid there was just "hyperactivity", and I wasn't hyperactive; so any problems I had were just absent-mindedness or procrastination or laziness...)

    I really enjoyed D&D in high school, but I tended to space out after a while during our all-day weekend game-athons, lose track of what was going on, and would often spend the later part of a session reading a book instead of contributing. I didn't game much after high school, except briefly in one campaign with some co-workers, where I again had trouble keeping up.

    I've been treating the disorder for a couple of years now, with dexedrine and counseling and some neurofeedback. That's been helpful in general, though not a silver bullet.

    I just recently got back into roleplaying, starting last fall, and it's going a lot better \u2014 I keep my concentration, stay involved, and I think I'm making a fair contribution. (It's not all due to the treatment, though \u2014 it helps that this is a smaller group (four players plus GM), more focused on roleplaying instead of interminable dungeon battles, and of course I'm older.)

    I'm happy about this, and even considering giving GM'ing a try once our current campaign is over. (I love the creative aspects of GM'ing, but I've never been confident of being able to keep track of all the information and make things up on the fly.)

    I know that ADHD is pretty common in geeks of all stripes. Asperger's is unfortunately common too (my son has a bit of it.) They seem to be intertwined with the more positive curiosity/creativity/deep-focus traits. I could see roleplaying being really helpful for people with autistic tendencies develop their social skills by working through imaginary scenarios; although it's likely to be frustrating sometimes for people playing with them.

    \n There's this story I'd like to tell, and I think this might be the right place for it.
    If not, apologies.

    I am a community support worker.
    That means I support adults with disabilities (developmental disabilities and physical disabilities, though many also deal with mental health issues).
    The way that I currently explain my job to friends: I get paid to hang out with people, support them in leading an active life, and not freak out about their shit.

    Currently, as part of my job, I'm expected to larp Star Trek.
    I support a young gentleman who is quite mobile but dealing with a lot of behavioral and mental illness challenges.
    His favourite pastime is to pretend that he's participating in an episode of Star Trek.
    He's never heard the word "larp" in his life, but that's exactly what he does... he pretends he's various members of the crew of Star Trek (The Next Generation), and introduces various conflicts (Borg hijinx are the most common, followed by Romulans accidentally firing on Federation ships)... which then need to be overcome by the two of us. He expects me to address him in character. His CA is pretty simulationist, and I am frequently corrected about how things are actually done in the Star Trek universe. Though, at the same time, there's cross-over with the Harry Potter universe, which is inexplicable save for the fact that he also likes Harry Potter, so it was a natural move to combine them.

    He has a much different social radar than I do, and thus feels no compunction to stop playing while in subways, on busy street corners, or passing by a school. It's been a big challenge for me... I realize that even though I talk and think constantly about roleplaying games, and have even spent time promoting public gaming events, I'm amazingly gun-shy compared to this guy. He has no problem exclaiming "Damn it, the Borg have triggered a dimensional portal into the mirror universe! Now we're going to have to fight our evil twins!" in public. And I feel like a dork because I'm too embarrassed to play along, because... there's people watching. So, it's interesting for me... someone with "normal social boundaries", to watch as someone without "normal social boundaries" just freely loves to do something that we both love to do*, while I shy away from it and balk.

    *I mean, roleplaying. I don't love to larp Star Trek, especially since it's a pretty Mary Sue game in which he is always Captain Jean-Luc Picard, and I am always Riker and/or Data. But still, that's time where I'm being paid, and I'd gladly be support cast in a sim-heavy Star Trek larp for money.

    So that's weird experience #1 - roleplaying with someone who has different social boundaries than I do, and feeling weird about letting down my social guard.

    The other weird part is that this guy has some pretty violent tendencies. And saying the wrong thing can escalate a situation pretty abruptly, possibly leading to assault. This isn't specific to the Star Trek game, but the dynamic is different. By default, I don't get a lot of character agency while we play Star Trek. And I get fed appropriate lines quite a lot. And so I have to push for the right to independently play my character, and introduce plot elements, and introduce solutions to the crises that arise. And sometimes that push/pull has led to me getting punched, or death threats, or the like. So this is the first time where I've roleplayed with someone and felt like playing my character wrong could result in physical violence. Which is a bit of a head trip. Which might lead one to think... maybe you should just repeat the lines he feeds you? Maybe. But after fifteen minutes of repeating scripted lines, you really want the autonomy to play your own character. And, as a story games person, I find it hard not to push "trad players" (lol) towards the whole shared story agency thing. And, actually, in all of my communication with this guy I'm not supposed to let him boss me around, but instead work towards cooperating and both being responsible for our own actions. So, in every way, advocating for my own rights in the story fits with how I'm "supposed" to interact with this man. Still, it's a strange spot to be in, where I'm risking aggressive backlash in order to advocate for my ability to choose for myself how Riker responds to the Borg invasion.

    So, that's an experience I've been having, roleplaying with someone who is dealing with some extensive mental health issues, as well as a developmental disability.

    \n BTW, this is indeed a tortuously difficult problem:
    Posted By: jackson teguWhat is mental health? It all seems so muddy to me.
    \nBecause
    1) Our hardwired assumptions about our own minds are demonstrably wrong.
    2) Our hardwired assumptions about other people's minds are demonstrably wrong.
    3) A lot of mental illness goes untreated.
    4) Mental illness crosses into lots of social contract territory, which is hard to talk about in the first place.
    5) Even among people who are trying to care, society has a lot of bullshit about mental illness.

    Example of 5: A lot of people will tell you that a depressed person needs to go out, have social contact, and have fun. But when they see a depressed person doing those things, laughing, feeling at ease, and having a good time, they think the person isn't -really- depressed, or that they're OK now. Depressed people can have fun, just like introverts can tell good jokes on stage.

    Posted By: RyExample of 5: A lot of people will tell you that a depressed person needs to go out, have social contact, and have fun.     \nI think that the reason for this is how mental illnesses are viewed in our society (or, rather, in my society. You can reflect on your own society and place in the world, and maybe it's different for your place).

    Mental illness is seen as a concrete and fixed thing with definite boundaries, much like a bicycle or a newspaper or a wart.
    And there's an assumption that mental illnesses can be yanked out of a person (like they were that bug-thing in Neo's belly in The Matrix), and that the person can be fixed.
    And that instead of being PERSON+illness, they'll just be PERSON. That you can remove something from someone and get a whole person at the end of the process.

    The same is true of how people view learning disabilities.
    I've heard a lot of people say "Oh, dyslexia? Yeah, I used to have that." Like it was a pog collection, or a sega genesis. A trifling possession from their youth.
    I worked for a year in a school for children with dyslexia, and it was absurd how many parents (all of whom had spent considerable time learning about dyslexia, in the process of trying to care for their children, and all of whom were paying $20,000 a year to send their children to this school) were operating on the assumption that I would fix their child, remove the dyslexia, and then they'd be normal.

    And so...
    when we see a depressed person laughing, we're like "Oh, the operation to remove the illness from PERSON+illness must be complete. Now this person is fixed."
    For various definitions of "we", all of which I want to exclude myself from.

    I don't know. That got pedantic and I didn't mean it to.
    But that's my experience, and having spent some time talking to well-meaning parents about the future of their "different" child's education, it's an experience that's been repeatedly confirmed.

    \n My wife is quite dangerously bi-polar insofar as her manic episodes are dysphoric, which is basically a high-energy depression: feeling like you are worse than worthless and yet empowered to do something about it. People in this condition generally have a very low survival rate -- where depression can be self-preserving (lacking the motivation even to kill oneself), the dysphoric manic is equipped to do whatever it takes.

    Anyway, there are three interesting states for my wife that bear on gaming.

    While depressed she is incapable of participating. She is interested and may even feel bad about not participating, but she simply cannot get past the effort necessary to engage with other people or even, sometimes, enter the room.

    While manic, she is a danger to the table. She's interested, sometimes very aggressively, but cannot be allowed to participate. Everything that happens is incredibly important to her the least issue with rules or inter-player conflict will result in an explosion, possibly including violence, and followed by a long and potentially lethal bout of vicious self-loathing. So when she is feeling anything like manic, she avoids people to save everyone the scene.

    While medicated she is uninterested in gaming. It's not that she's too whacked out on the drugs -- it's that she's as close to "healthy" as she ever gets and in this state she has no interest in gaming. She writes, paints, knits, or chats with us from the sidelines. She cooks, laughs, and plays with the pets. Zero interest in the gaming.

      Posted By: David BergI wonder if there's potential for the (relatively) safe space of RPG play to provide opportunities for positive practice or reinforcement in dealing with negative mental states.
      \nSomeone's already mentioned drama therapy, so I'll chime in by mentioning psychodrama. I find psychodrama and role-playing have a lot of overlap (there's role-taking, and the emerging narrative is a core element of both). I could totally see RPGs used as a therapeutic tool, but there are a few really, really important things that are missing - safety, a clear contract, and expertise.

      Things like confidentiality, the group's understanding that if William gets triggered (because this role-playing situation is very close to his real life, something you'd be aiming for at least tangentially if it's supposed to help William), the group's not going to abandon him (however subtly, such as by changing the subject) and the facilitator is going to have the practice to normalize what's happening (so it just doesn't become That Session We Don't Talk About), or help the group out if somebody's issue starts to manifest as conflict in the group itself.

      A clear contract is also key - providing someone with an environment where they can work through a deeply seated mental habit (e.g. giving up on themselves just as they're about to succeed) can't be done unless the group knows what it's aiming for, and this itself takes patience to uncover. Otherwise you get four people analyzing William with pop psychology and trying to 'fix' what they perceive the problem to be.

      There are other agendas at work in a role-playing game (e.g. making things overly dramatic, when often the opposite is helpful) that could be working at cross purposes. Someone could go home having had the opposite experience from the one that would have been most helpful to them.

      As an example, consider a role-playing game that evoked themes of of sexual abuse and its connection with suicidal behavior, being played by a group of people with histories of sexual abuse. Not to be undertaken lightly! (And yet, with the right supports in place, this is what psychodrama does and it's fucking magical.)

      \n Yeah - people don't react to suicide ideation well. They overreact or underreact (and run away). Most people just don't know how to respond and be present.

      After my Dad committed suicide (almost thirty years ago now) I remember people just not talking to me. I didn't want to talk so that worked out but had I wanted to there was no one there. I know that that experience was what lead me to become a psychotherapist.

      I think about suicidal thoughts now as an experience of pain. If my hand is in a fire, pain tells me to pull it out. It still hurts after I pull out but it is a healthy move. If I have a suicidal thought it is pain that says "Something isn't right in my life - Can I change something?" So if I respond to it I'm being healthy. Kind of the definition of not an illness.

      What I'm bugged by is how people overreact to cutting. They assume it is a suicidal move when it's usually just a way to distract one's self from another kind of pain. New therapists always seem to fall into that. That and avoiding or diving into crying.
