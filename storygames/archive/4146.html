\n The first Role-playing game I designed was Kitties and Catnip, in junior high school. You played cats. It was heavily influenced by Bunnies and Burrows and The Morrow Project, an unholy combination if ever there was one. I remember that your cat had blood points, and could easily bleed to death from an untended wound. Who tended the wounds? That I do not recall, but combat was stuffed with martial arts combo tables taken directly from B&B. You were not special cats and did not have thumbs. I don't remember what, if anything, you actually did.

You probably wrote your own RPG at some long-ago point - something excellent and goofy. Spill it! Andy Kitkowski, I'm looking at you. The rest of you will not escape my gaze.

    \n The first game that I ever wrote was called No Idea What We're Fighting For (NIWWFF, pronounced "nuh-wiff"), based on the arcade fighting games (Street Fighter II, Mortal Combat, etc.) that were all the rage when I was in middle school. It involved rolling 1d6 against various stats and was entirely one-on-one imaginary combat. I remember each character had a trademark "set" and you would choose which character's set you would fight in before dueling. Sets had various objects and scenery in them that you could use for bonuses, throwing people into pillars or setting the room on fire. Characters gained XP for winning fights, which was instantly convertible into new abilities. However, the rules involved enough randomness that your character could lose 3 straight and still triumph over someone who'd won 3 straight (and had the XP from doing so).

    We ran it at lunch on the bleachers in the gym (which was where we ate during the winter). I bet we played out at least a dozen or so fights before we got bored with it and started trading Magic cards (but never actually playing Magic) instead.

    \n I'm not sure how much of a RPG this is, but my friend and I used to play this game during our sleepovers in 6th/7th grade amongst the shag carpet and Wayne Gretzky posters.

    We made a matrix of different girls at school that we liked (and I few we didn't, for color or humor I suppose). Then we switched off rolling dice to see which girl on the matrix we were "going after". One roll meant you went out with her. A second roll meant you kissed her. And a third roll meant that you were "going out" and the other player couldn't roll her anymore. Kind of like closing off numbers in the Darts game "Cricket".

    In retrospect, this game tells me a lot of things:
    1. We were completely terrified of girls and had to distill all of our social anxiety down to a game mechanic we could understand (and win).
    2. We were careful not to step on the other person's "territory" by dating someone else's girl. This actually continued into our dating years as we never swapped girls back and forth.
    3. By eventually dividing up the entire girl population between the two of us, we certainly had illusions of romantic grandeur!

    But 25+ years later, I will give it a name..."Players"

    \n The very first one ever was, circa 1981, a space-themed D&D type game (as in, the only game I'd had any experience with was *D&D, so it was "D&D with the stats renamed"), using race names cribbed from a Dragon Magazine ad for Star Frontiers. I think it would have had psionics if I had understood the D&D psionics rules at the time.

    The second was a pirate themed game rather imaginatively entitled Pirates, circa 1983 or 1984. A friend and I were going to design this game, and I'd checked several books out of the public library to read about pirates. I kinda got bogged down in coming up with ship construction rules. I'd had experience with a few more games by this time, but it was still going to look like D&D (I think the ship design rules were going to be very much like Car Wars).

    The third was going to be somewhat based on the Laser Tag sets (the toy with this actual name) as a way of playing out Laser Tag-like duels ('85 or '86). You had several sets of paired stats -- in each pair there was an active stat and a resistant stat, for instance Shoot and Dodge, or Perceive and Hide. When attempting to do something to another character you used the one against the other, and there were also ways you could mix and match them (I think). It was either each side roll 1d6 and add the appropriate stat, or roll 1d6 + stat - opposing stat + some number, I can't recall. There were also rules for fumbles and "going hand-to-hand" (whereby you and your opponent run up to each other, shove your gun at the other's sensor, and pull the trigger as many times as you could until one of you died). By this time I'd played a lot of Champions and other Hero System games, and maybe GURPS.

    \n My first role-playing game design was a science-fiction RPG called Infinity. I was in junior high circa 1981. I wanted a game where I could design and play in settings like Dune. I wasn't happy with AD&D; or Traveller. I think I copied RuneQuest's percentile and skilled-based system. It was an ambitious game, trying to be something like a sci-fi GURPS. You could design space ships and there were nasty weapons like "retina eye destroyers." I still have the design docs somewhere.
    \n
    \nThe first game I ever designed wasn't an RPG. It was a Star Wars variation of Stratego with three different boards: Tatooine, space, and the Death Star. I was in 5th grade, Star Wars had just come out, and I wanted to design a game as soon as I saw the movie. I got up the gumption to submit my design to Milton Bradley. It was rejected, of course. I was disappointed, especially since the Parker Bros. game that came out was terrible.

    \n The first few that I did were the following:

    In sixth grade, I made an RPG when I was bored in math class. It was about Ninjas in a Ninja Pagoda Dungeon. You had like 2 stats. It was based 90% on the system behind the Lone Wolf book series. It was meant for one to four people, but you were playing against the "book".

    In seventh grade or eighth grade (I get them confused; neither was much fun) I made two RPGs, one incomplete, the other complete.

    Incomplete: I forget the name, but it was after I read the original Justifiers and was inspired by 80s era cartoons like Inhumanoids and the one with the Holographic Standards/Banners, and the box art to Gammarauders. It was a game where you played Dinosaurs who came to life again, but in the future. You fought bad dinosaurs for the sake of the human race. Of course, you did this via guns and melee weapons. I think that Mail Order Monsters provided some inspiration as well.

    Complete: a game called, no lie, PLASTIC STORAGE CONTAINERS. I was experimenting with comedy, including subtlety and irony, in those days and I think that's where I got the idea. As well as the "Storage Jars" skit from Monty Python (Begins at 3 minutes and 40 seconds) and the movie Aliens. I made the title as a joke to my best friend, then I very seriously and earnestly set out to write the rules, using Paranoia 2E as the basic framework.

    Basically, it is the Far Future. You are a soldier for this empire that has spawned across the galaxy, conquering just about everything. However, dangers still abound (namely in the form of ancient ruins with artifacts to steal and death traps to avoid). Your mission is to make use of your weapons, cyber gear, psionic powers, and other RIFTS-like abilities, and scout around the galaxy looking for artifacts of other civilizations... and then placing these priceless artifacts in, you guessed it, plastic storage containers.

    The rules for the game were almost all in my head, but I used really fine graph paper to make this HUGE elaborate character sheet. When you were a kid, did you ever draw elaborate fortresses on paper with your friends, and compare them to see which was the most secure? ("Nope, if you go there this laser turret zaps you! Nope, if you dig there, these worms come and eat you!") It was like the Character Sheet version of that. The only things I remember about it were:
    1) It took me a long time to write, and
    2) It was very complicated, featuring dozens if not hundreds of boxes, and
    3) There was a box in the middle of the sheet called "POG SPOT", where you recorded the current size of your Pog Spot, a type of birthmark that all citizens of the Empire had. It could grow or shrink in size. It had, literally, NO GAME EFFECT WHATSOEVER. I thought that this was very clever.

    I would actually pay $100 if I could find that character sheet again, seriously. That was one of the first things I ever really created, crafted from nothing (save Paranoia 2e).

    -Andy

    \n The first pen & paper RPG I can remember designing (and I know I have board game designs and computer games well older than this, since I didn't get exposed to RPGs until my senior year of high school), you were this totally badass immortal. I don't remember much of the fiddly bits now, but I recall you had an Immortal Rating, and essentially if you had a higher rating than someone else they were screwed.

    Magic and planeshifting was probably a big deal. I think that was in my "dude, planeshifting is hot" phase.

    I don't remember the title of this game, but here was a big element of the design: a tank was Immortal Rating 0. A hundred tanks: Immortal Rating 0. A dude with a sword & an Immortal Rating of 1 could totally kill a hundred tanks easy.

    Since I don't remember the title, I'll now name it: "My Sword-Dude Could Totally Kick A Tank Army's Ass, Oh, And There's Probably Planeshifting"

    Edit: Oh! I totally remember the name now! It was based on an outgrowth of a computer solo RPG I made before I got into pen & paper RPGs: Conjoure. (The original being a 8000+ line QuickBASIC game that I couldn't compile, thanks to QB only using conventional memory.) There wasn't any planeshifting in that game, but oh man, the sequel was going to be all about planeshifting.

    \n My first game was called Government Assassins for Hire. You were CIA hit men who were loaned out to shadowy masters who told you go somewhere and blow all sorts of stuff up. It was kind of D&Dish, but probably TMNT was a closer influence. Skills could change your stats and things like that.     \nMy first game was something I designed at school. As I remember it only had one mechanic, roll a d6, on a 1-3 something worked, otherwise it failed. Like, 1-3 lose a life, 4-6 survive. Eventually I think it grew a few more, but that was the main thing.
    \n
    \nIt started as a little map I drew on graph paper in maths class, which someone else explored around, collecting treasure and trying not to die in traps. Because it was so fatal I changed it so that the whole game was one big virtual reality system. The characters (if you can call them that, they still had no stats or backgrounds) were rich members of a future society who spent their whole life competing in the game trying to see who could gain the most treasure and loose the least lives, just like the players did.
    \n
    \nBy a process I can't fully remember the game expanded until the main map was about 7 or 8 feet across (made out of many many sheets of graph paper stuck together) with piles of extra dungeon levels and maybe 10-12 people were playing on a semi-regular basis. I would take the map and my folder of other maps into the school library at lunchtimes and lay it out on the main table and people would come and tell me what they were doing next, roll some dice, take turns and so on till lunch was over.
    \n
    \nNot quite an RPG, but a game certainly.

    \n BARREN:
    \nPost-apocalyptic science fiction on a desert world populated by composite-bow wielding, 2-headed alligator riding polar bear men who wore body armor and goggles.
    \n
    \nYES YOU SEE WHERE I AM GOING WITH THIS.
    \n
    \nAlso: cybernetic humans and dogs, and four-armed alien dudes. Used the FASERIP system, more or less. Mostly just the Universal Table and percentile dice. Derivative and bad.
    \n
    \nTHE SCOURGE:
    \nPost-apocalyptic horror sci-fi ala THE MIST by Stephen King. Zombies + giant animals in a decimated USA's wildnerness (cities were overrun with fucking monsters!). I used the Talislanta 2nd edition system and wrote up my own list of archetypes based on human occupations. I remember that the quotes for each archetype were pretty rad and my favorite characters were the priest, the ex-cop, the paramedic and the wandering musician. This was derivative but good!
    \n
    \nReal Death Roleplaying:
    \nA LARP. Using a version of the Storyteller System's character sheet (ie: bullets ranked 1-5 with attributes). You played yourself in the house the game took place in. Zombies attack! What do you do?! Everything was rated on a 1-5 scale of "could i do this?" and it was strict karma system. No die rolls. No skills either... you wrote down a skill and rating when you used it. The number of skills you wrote down in a single session = the number of experience you got to spend on what you wrote down. Cool and kinda novel...my step down the dark path of design.
