\n While reading through some wikipedia articles on one of my favorite cartoons, I was reminded of something that happened to me last year, and various ideas on that event started running through my mind. I'd like to bounce them around and get some input.

Sometime last year, I was approached on an internet forum by a gamer from my town who was looking to put together a Werewolf game. I've never had any feelings more favorable than "Agree to disagree" on the subject of World of Darkness games (a subject for another discussion), so I was pretty hesitant about this one. However, the fellow was pretty encouraging, and seemed open-minded enough with his concepts of the game, so I took the bite and went with it. I'm pretty down with free-form, player-driven games, so I was excited.

After the initial interface was when the breakdown happened. When trying to come up with a character idea, I was hitting some blanks. It'd been a while since I played in a tabletop WoD game, and even longer since that game was specifically one about werewolves. So I pressed the guy for some more input into the situation. Since I never like playing "lone wolf" characters, I asked for some guideline information on the other characters, how to relate to them, and maybe some basic information on some of the directions the GM wanted to go with. What was the planned focus? What story was being told here? Was there a main theme at hand? Was this going to be a global game, or exist mostly within the society and intrigues of the local community? What elements can you give me that would be a good idea to work into my concept and backstory?

Due to a problem with our communication (at least, I hope it was just that), the GM immediately startd accusing me of trying to meta-game, trying to "break his story" and such. He said that by asking for background information, I was trying to "win the game" without even playing it, and that if he were to tell me anything about the story, there would be no point in playing it, and it wouldn't be fair, and yadda yadda yadda. He said "just make a character" and actually refused to give any guidelines or input at all. Huh. Weird. I'm not used to 100% hands-off GMs, but still what the hey? A bit put off by his rigidity and his accusatory attitude, I shrugged it off and gave it a second go. As a career GM, I wanted to play again, for it had been so long!

So I figured I would just come up with some random concepts, throw them out as a test to see what the guy thought of them, and if rejected, hopefully I would get some criticism or direction on character ideas more suitable for the game. I mulled over some "just crate a character" ideas, and finally started banging one out. I had been watching a lot of "Avatar: The Last Airbender" at that time, and was very much in love with the character portrayal of Uncle Iroh. I took that premise and tooled it into a character that seemed would be fun to play. The idea was for an older man, maybe early 50s, who had been a powerful werewolf in the past, perhaps a notable figure in the community. However, due to some dark and tragic "unstated plot device event from the past", he would no longer have these powers, refuse to acknowledge his past, and refuse to live his role as a werewolf. He would be exiled from his old community for some reason or another, and play a similar role to that of Iroh, or perhaps the exiled firebender guy from another episode of that show: the grizzled old advisor, unwilling guide who must rediscover, redevelop, and accept the responsibilities of his powers, "The Beast" who must learn to love again, the heart of gold that must be warmed once more. A big homage to the "with great power comes great responsibility" concept, with a twist.

I got excited. The character sounded like a lot of fun! When I bounced this idea at the GM, however, instead of discussing it with me and working with the idea, or suggesting a way to tone it down, or even just offering advice on how to make a more fitting character, he threw insults at me and accused me of wanting to meta-game, power-game, and spoil his game.

End of transaction, I bailed. I just couldn't put up with it anymore. I felt like I was being accused of multiple offenses of which I was innocent, and my only crime was actually being interested in playing in a game run by a GM who refused to offer any help or insight. I should have stuck with my initial hesitancies and the whole negative interactions could have been avoided.

Perhaps there were communication issues. Perhaps there were personal issues. Perhaps we were incompatible. Perhaps he was trying to run one of those visionless, unfocused, ambiguous games with no story or direction that are expected to just go on and on but in reality tend to fail hard. I'm not sure. My question: is my analysis of the breakdown correct? Was I power-gaming by asking to play a "once powerful leader of men" type of character? Was I meta-gaming by asking the GM to tell me a bit more about the story he wanted to tell? Or was the GM overreacting?
