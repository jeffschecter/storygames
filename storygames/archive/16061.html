\n This is a thread for bibliography. Let's put together a really solid list of books about the experience of contemporary infantry warfare. Fiction and non-fiction are both fine.

Add a book if:

1. You've read it
2. It has something to say about war and the people who fight it
3. That insight is applicable to making the experience of playing The Regiment better

Bonus points if:

1. It is not written from an American point of view
2. Is not about World War Two or Vietnam
3. You can directly tie it to usefulness in play of The Regiment

    \nGeneral Die in Bed by Charles Yale Harrison. This is the Canadian version of the "we were betrayed by our officers in WWI" novel. So that's two bonus points so far... usefulness in play? If you want to portray a cynical, emotionally manipulative command structure, and/or you want some gruesome scenes of trench warfare, this is the book for you. Before the book's final battle, the soldiers are told that the Germans sunk a hospital ship and machinegunned those who tried to escape on lifeboats, so they are bloodthirsty and want revenge. After the battle, in which his last friend dies of blood loss as they huddle in a hole together, the protagonist finds out it was just a supply ship, and they were duped into hating the enemy so they wouldn't balk at making yet another suicidal advance.

    (Fun fact! In real life, the Germans actually did sink the hospital ship.)

    \n Hm.

    Going for bonus points:

    (Not about WW2, but can be directly tied to usefulness in play of an ostensibly WW2 game? Don't ask for much, do you?)

    The Bear Went Over The Mountain & The Other Side Of The Mountain, both by Lester Grau, et al. Afghanistan from Soviet and Muj sides, often about the same event. Rather dry (okay, really dry) but you get an interesting view into a barely competent command structure (Soviet) and an initially desperately undersupplied insurgency (Muj).

    Far less dry: Afghanistan: A Russian Soldier's Story, by Vladislav Tamarov. Grau's top-down style is given life by this book.

    (Then watch 9th Rota (9th Company) & The Beast.)

    More recent: One Soldier's War, by Arkady Babchenko. Very, very good. Babchenko fought in both Chechen wars, and the differences are interesting. The details of life in the Russian Army are more than a little harrowing at times. Probably one of the best-written (well...best-translated?) accounts of modern war from the Russian/Soviet point of view.

    Assault On Grozny Downtown, by Vyacheslav Mironov. Low-level view of part of a Chechen war. Not the best, but not a bad read, and free.

    Less bonus points:

    House to House, by David Bellavia. Primarily the Second Battle of Fallujah. Full of the unpleasant, ugly day-to-day details of life in an urban combat zone.

    Ivan's War, by Catherine Merridale. Good 'human interest' view of the Red Army of WW2.

    SLA Marshall's Men Against Fire is really good, even though his "research" (hint: He did no research) has been discredited. he still interviewed tons of dudes while they were actually fighting a war.

    \n The best command-level book on the early Chinese Civil War (the part before the Japanese invasion and WWII) is the autobiography of Zhang Guotao, one of the co-founders of the Chinese Communist Party and a Long March survivor. Zhang never really got along with Mao, and eventually defected to the Nationalists and moved to Hong Kong and then Canada. I say command-level, but the early Chinese Red Army was a tattered, messy affair and -- in multiple major battles -- barely had any organization above the brigade level. Zhang's account (originally called Wo de Huiyi, "My Memories") is epic and authoritative. A mostly complete English translation was published in 1971, under the title The Rise of the Chinese Communist Party: The Autobiography of Chang Kuo-t'ao. The second volume covers 1928-1938, which is the stuff most relevant for The Regiment.

    \nRed Cavalry by Isaac Babel is one of my favorite books of war stories of all time. Babel was a Russian Jew who served with the Cossacks in the Russian Civil War. The stories are tragic, comic, and gripping. The portraits of the Cossacks are unforgettable.

    Dispatches by Michael Herr qualifies for none of the bonus points, but it's still essential reading if you haven't read it yet. People have criticized it for inaccuracy; these people are missing the point. It served as a massive inspiration for Apocalypse Now. Gonzo poetic writing about Vietnam as war and as cultural phenomenon.

    Dog Soldiers by Robert Stone -- a novel also about Vietnam but which also takes in PTSD, drugs and drug dealing, communes and how war is a business. Read it and find out about the Great Elephant Zap and the Battle of Bob Hope.

    Bonus, for military science fans: Most military SF is utter shite. One book which isn't is Hammer's Slammers, by David Drake, probably because it's a science fiction version of Drake's experiences as a tanker in Vietnam and Laos. I think the Slammers would make great inspiration for a Regiment game.

    \n How about John Keegan's The Face of Battle? It talks about Agincourt, Waterloo, and the Somme from the operational level and then drills down to the experience of fighting as a common soldier in each of those battles. Really smart and compelling; the operational level stuff provides a context that goes a long way toward explaining the experience of each particular kind of battle (medieval, 19th century, and 20th century). Reading it--particularly the chapter on the Somme--should help a GM run The Regiment by providing an understanding of how the decisions of generals and their staffs affect the experience of the soldier in the trenches. Also see: Peter Weir's Gallipoli

    In terms of military sf, I always liked Joe Haldeman's Forever War, which seemed to me to capture pretty well the bureaucratic aspects of military life.

    \n I've neither played nor read The Regiment, so I lose those bonus points. Still...

    Anyidoho, Guns Over Kigali: an account of the Rwandan genocide from the Ghanaian Battalion commander (and 2iC for Dallaire). Ghana kept an unathorized number of peacekeepers in the field after the UN ordered a drawdown, and they protected a lot of people, often using more negotiation than force. You especially owe it to yourself to read this if your only knowledge of Rwanda comes from reading Dallaire's Shake Hands with the Devil (which might also fit this request, though it's as much a general memoir as anything else).

    Further on Rwanda, Hatzfeld, Machete Season: The Killers in Rwanda Speak. Drawn from interviews with the genocidaires, good for both thinking about their motivations and for vignettes from the day-to-day business of killing a lot of people.

    A bizarre and interesting read if you can find it is The Joshua Martin Blayhi Story, a piece of apologetics-cum-autobiography by the man formerly known as "General Butt-Naked." It focuses a lot on both his religious upbringing (he was Samuel Doe's right-hand black magician during the first Liberian war, then a leader in ULIMO-J after Prince Johnson, um, ate parts of Doe) and his eventual conversion to Christianity, which may not be of as much interest, but a wild read. Probably as good for a Sorcerer game! Not on the experience-of-war front, but parts of Blayhi's book will probably make more sense if you read Ellis' The Mask of Anarchy, esp. the chapters on Liberian religion and the war.

    I don't know how much The Regiment cares about civilians, but if you do, you might want to check out: Pouligny, Peace Operations Seen from Below, which is what it says on the tin (covers mostly civilian experience in Cambodia, Somalia, El Salvador, Haiti, IIRC). And Stearns, Dancing in the Glory of Monsters. Stearns' book is largely about the geopolitics of the war in DR Congo, but also draws on lots of anecdotes from militants, civilians, etc. caught up in things.

    \n I will happily forfeit the bonus points if my rec gets anyone to read the War Stories comics by Garth Ennis and a rotating cast of excellent mostly-British comics artists. The original 8 stories are in 2 trade paperbacks from DC/Vertigo and subsequent volumes have appeared via Dynamite under the title Garth Ennis' Battlefields.

    They're mostly WW2-related, but cover some wide-ranging interesting territory, including the Russian 'night witches', naval warfare in the North Atlantic, infantry in Italy and Africa, and the brutal fighting of the Spanish Civil War, with a strong emphasis on British forces. One story in particular, Dear Billy, about a nurse who survived torture by the Japanese, is gut-wrenching RPG material.

    They're pulpy and can be a little overly-macho at times, but in terms of role-playable moments and the sort of interpersonal complications that make AW games really sing, they're top-notch, and the art is uniformly great; it makes for good visual reference that can be cribbed for the table without looking like antique photojournalism.

    \n BREAKING THE THREAD RULES! If you want to know what the home front thinks of the exploits of your regiment, check out the super cool Google newspaper archive. They've got a ton of Canadian and American newspapers scanned and browsable and (somewhat) searchable, and it's all free.

    I've been reading the Montreal Gazette's coverage of the battle of Vimy Ridge lately. There's lots of fluffy language and selective viewpoint -- they speak of "drives" and "intense shellfire" and "sanguinary losses" and "dash" and don't ever get into any gruesome details. The 10 April 1917 issue, published the day after the battle, says "It was like the days at the beginning of the battle of the Somme again ... and may prove no less disastrous to the Germans." Which is funny because the first day of the battle of the Somme was the worst day in the history of the British army, with 58,000 young British men suffering pointless death or wounds.

    The most interesting thing I've found so far is in the 18 April 1917 issue. Officer casualty lists were compiled much more quickly than enlisted casualty lists. By 18 April, five days after the end of the main battle, the Canadian government had admitted that the Canadian Corps had suffered at least 400 officer casualties at Vimy Ridge but had released no other casualty information. Based on the 20:1 officer:enlisted casualty ratio of previous lists, the Gazette estimated at least 8,000 dead and wounded in the Vimy Ridge assault.

    That's life in the Regiment, I guess. They'll won't count your corpse until they're done counting all the officers' corpses.
