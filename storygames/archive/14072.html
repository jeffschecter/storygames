    \n Traditionally, RPGs have assumed that the GM and players have very different roles at the table. Most have grown accustomed to this format, where the GM provides the players with the "interpretation" of their die rolls, data searches, NPC interactions, sword strikes, and so on. Some new game share this authority with other players. I'm not arguing for OR against either format. I'm merely observing that this is how players have grown used to the paradigm of PC = protagonist, GM = interpreter. There are other roles at work in game play. But I'm not speaking to those. Just this phenomenon.

    I really think James' spoon analogy works pretty well, but doesn't speak DIRECTLY to the idea that it's the player's point of view that drives this paradigm and "spoon-feeding" can be taken as antagonistic.

    As an analogy, I put a quarter into a video game and I am expected to hit the START button. A video game may NOT do this and just go straight to game-play, but neither has a term. I put in a quarter, I expect the video game to challenge/entertain. I have expectations as a player. The first time I did this I didn't know what to expect. Now, after 20+ years of video games, I know what happens when I hit start. A video game that expected me to leave the house and ask my neighbor for directions to the pentagon would break the expectation-paradigm.

    An another analogy, I play in a WoD LARP twice a month. The storytellers have good stories, but extremely poor planning and administrative skills. They continue to invent a gaggle of different events each session that occur in the background of the environment, so that the players CANNOT interact with these elements of the story without them. If they ignore them and push forward with their agendas, the events become trivial. If the players investigate these events, they must wait in a queue for the GM to tell them how well they do at their interaction with these events. (I head up into the mountains to investigate, I meet the crazy one-eyed god in the parking lot, I fly my helicopter into city hall). Since there are 30 players and four storytellers, people are always waiting for a "turn."

    Intentional or not (and I presume not), this stops play, hinders fun, and generally takes authority out of people's hand who might otherwise be comfortable with said authority. [Recognizing of course, that if the GM puts something behind the rock he's the only one who knows what's behind it, but making me wait in a queue to find out how much blood I get from feeding is pedantic and just a continuation of this philosophy that the GM is "momma bird."]. If this was the first roleplaying game these 30 people had ever played, they would be "trained" to believe that this is the only method of roleplaying. ACTOR inspires ACTION. VOICE OF AUTHORITY interprets said ACTION. RINSE. REPEAT.

    To sum up, it's not a matter of "belief" in contrast to rules. It's a matter of "trained behavior" that the GM provides this function. But, again, it's similar to a baby-bird mentality to sit and wait for the GM to say, "good job jim. you found the sword. yeah!!!" I'm not judging it, I'm trying to come up with a clinical signifier to describe it, like the "hamster pellet feeder syndrome," but more scholastic. STIMULUS-RESPONSE-INTERACTIVITY-PARADIGM-THEORY is just dumb. Although, that does acronym into SPIRiT. [Ha. I just used acronym as a verb.]

    So. It's not really anything I'm trying to argue FOR or AGAINST. It is an observed phenomenon in our hobby that I've always tried to avoid, because I like games with shared authority. Crap. Maybe waiting to be spoon-fed really is the best term.

    \n Don't use the term black box. Especially if you want to relate the term to larp play (which your specific complaint was about). Blackbox larp is already a specific term in use. It is used by a large group of primarily Canadian players to describe a form they use that is similar to improvizational theatre. They purposely use blank sets, possibly with plain boxes when necessary for things like chairs and tables. They create a space and give it a time and place and create scenes in that space. People who are not in a space at the moment are out of play and can observe other scenes as an audience. It's a funky form of larp with a lot of improv theatre influence.

    I haven't heard anything big and new about it recently, but there are some neat threads on it at rpg.net in the larp forum. I think they're about 2, maybe 3 years old.

    Based on the actual rant post - #14, about the WoD LARP, I don't think you need a term regarding tabletop play. I think what you need is a term for trying to shoehorn tabletop-style play into larp.

    In tabletop play, you expect to have a lot of interaction with the GM. You expect them to come up with stuff for you to interact with it. You expect them to interpret for you how that interaction went.

    Larp design doesn't work well in that mode. Larp works best when the players are interacting primarily with each other and the GM doesn't need to be there to facilitate.

    I wrote more about it here: http://www.mortalisrpg.com/forum/index.php?action=downloads;sa=view;down=6
    The article is called LARP is Simultaneous Action. My premise is that the thing that larp does best, better than any other medium, is to give the players the sense that multiple significant things are going on at the same time. Better larp design generally takes strong advantage of this element of the medium. Your GMs aren't doing so.

    So I'll throw out a term for you. Shoehorning. Trying to run a larp like a tabletop. It is often the source of poor larp design and less fun had by the players.

    Primary indicators of shoehorning:
    The GMs say something is interesting. But you have to talk to one to find out more about it, rather than having a non-GM NPC with plenty of information and decision-making power available to talk to players about it.
    GMs who play major NPCs themselves while trying to do heavy system adjudication at the same time.
    GMs who structure information so that they have to trickle it out themselves during the event, rather than giving interesting bits to various players, along with giving them reasons for sharing those bits.

    A lot of that is restating the same things in different ways, but there are multiple, related parts to it.

      \n You know, I've been staying out of this conversation because it could get a little... inflammatory.

      But, that said, there is a phenomenon here that is at least of some interest. Here's how I've experienced it:

      There are a great variety of ways to play roleplaying games, different distributions of authority, different techniques, etc. But there has been, for a long time, a school of thought that was very dominant, and that describes a very specific kind of distribution of authority and style of play. Have you all never seen this? People who play as follows:

      * The GM provides all descriptions, players never do. Players have to ask, "What does it look like?", or even, "Is there a bottle on the shelf behind the bar?" They ask for permission before providing any description or doing anything other than stating in-character actions.

      * Similarly, players never introduce any game-world facts without asking the GM for permission to do so first.

      This is a certain play-style that, while functional and fun, is seen by many gamers as the Only Way to Play. Players new to RPGs, in particular, are often subjected to angry glares or worse if they tread on these "rules".

      Consider that, when the Forge was starting up, people laughed at the idea that a player could describe the way their attacks harmed an enemy, or made up any features of the setting: "Give any control to the players, and the game will be destroyed!" This was (and still is for many) a very common fear among RPG gamers.

      Playing in a group of people that glares at you anytime you tread on these boundaries creates a certain pattern of behaviour.

      I've seen people who have intense difficulties playing another type of game if this is the only kind of gaming they've been exposed to. In particular, I remember a game of In a Wicked Age... which would not go anywhere because every time a player narrated something into play (as you must in that game, when you make Challenges and Answers), they would trip over their own feet trying desperately NOT to narrate anything that would actually influence play in a meaningful way.

      In this style of play, players learn to add details to play, but only when those details do not impact anything meaningful (anything that could change the flow of the plot, for example).

      I've seen a number of game texts that include rules such as the following, to support this style of play:

      * A player may spend a Kool Point at any time to narrate some prop or detail into existence. However, any such player narration that might affect the outcome of the scene or derail the plot can and should be vetoed by the GM.

      Now, I'm not sure where to go with this, but to say it's not a thing at all... that doesn't jive with my experiences. Just wanted to add in my two cents here. I hope I'm on topic.

      Just for reference, here's how I see it:

      "Trad gaming" (let's make up a term, like Strong GM Authority, why not?) may or may not involve "spoon feeding" or whatever this is Jim is trying to describe. At least, I'm pretty sure I've seen "trad gaming" with very high "spoon feeding" as well as "trad gaming" with very little "spoon feeding".

      You can have passive players, active players, etc, in all different types. In particular, many OSR games rely very heavily on player authorship, just through different channels than "GMless story games".

      So those things are not necessarily linked: you can have very proactive players in an OSR game or passive boring players playing PTA. Sure, definitely.

      So,isthere a "syndrome" or a "problem"? Well, yes, sure. We can't conclude there isn't one just because all these different things work. What is it? Where is it?

      It's when someone WANTS TO play a game with high player authorship, and TRIES to do so. But ingrained habits keep them from enjoying themselves; they can't bring themselves to do it.

      That's a problem, then, at that point. How common is this? I don't know. But I've seen it at least a couple of times. It's not hard to create a pattern of behaviour, especially if there's a whole group of your friends (or strangers!) glaring at you when you "do it wrong". After that happens a few times, you'll be afraid to do it again, you'll probably even be afraid if someone else tries to do it, and do your best to stop them. ("No! Not that. You're doing it wrong!")

      So, you try to play a game that says, "Hey, something cool happens now. What is it?" ...and you can't, it's painful. Not that you do it and you find you don't like: rather that you WANT to do it, and you just can't override those negative experience.

      That seems like a good analogy for dependency/addiction, if you want to go that way: "addiction" is often defined as "when it begins to interfere with whatever else you're trying to do" (like, say, your work). Not that I recommend this kind of terminology, though: I probably wouldn't recommend using any more language that sounds this... dangerous.

      All I'm saying is, "This is when it might be useful to realize it happens so you can fix it in your group (if that's your desire, of course)."

      The solution, I think, is simply spreading the word, letting people know, everywhere, that, hey, there are lots of different ways to play, and why don't you try some different ones rather than assuming that the way you play is the only way?


      (Another time this sort of thing becomes a problem is when people new to roleplaying try to play a certain way, and are told by others that, no, you can't do that. You inventing stuff would ruin the game. And with the implication that this is true for all roleplaying, in any form.

      Basically any time people think it's the Only True Way is when it's a problem, in my eyes. Otherwise, hey, carry on: do whatever you find fun, right? I like both games with tons of player authorship and games with Strong GM Authority: they can both be a heck of a good time.)

      A new thought, which may not be relevant:

      The behavior of the players expecting the GM to interpret the results of any given resolution, right? So you've got description description description, fine, whatever, but there comes a moment where the description isn't set in stone yet. The moment gets fuzzy. There is uncertainty. I want something and you want something. We roll dice, say, and what the GM says is what clarifies that moment. Suddenly things pull into focus and we continue along with our descriptions and whatnot.

      I think there are two things going on here. This may be retreading Forge territory but let's just pretend it's not.

      First, someone has to decide that the moment has gotten fuzzy. Who is that? Is that the GM, always? Do players traditionally have the opportunity to identify those unresolved moments? IME at least, it's gone both ways. GMs identify unresolved moments I think mostly as a means of establishing pacing. An unresolved moment is a beat. It's tension awaiting release.

      There are, I think, some play groups that treat literally anything addressed by the rules as "unresolved moments": if you've got rules for digging a ditch, we don't really know if that ditch got dug 'til someone goes to the ditch digging rules and carries out the procedure.

      There are, I think, some play groups that are more discriminating in deciding what moments are best treated as unresolved. Someone feels that that tension is valuable or fun or whatever. That "someone" is typically the GM, right? But I'll bet individual players also advocate for that as well. Might come via social pressure ("come on, let's just roll it and see what happens") or smart tactical play ("my character has a lot of advantages in a social setting, so rather than just handwaving it I would really like to roll some dice here and see if I've got a shot") or, probably, some desire for genre/realism fidelity ("there's no script immunity for major characters in this sort of fiction we're playing toward, so we're going to let the dice fall where they may"). Whatever.

      I guess this is a long way toward my actual point here, in which it's a question of where authority lies.

      So when you're talking about "the dependency that prohibits participation because players expect the GM to interpret the results", I guess I'd suggest a reframe of this: some players prefer that the GM have final authority.

      I don't love that "authority" gets so quickly conflated with "power", or that either/both those words come so freighted. In a perfect world you could call this "dictator play" and not have advocates up in arms with the word "dictator," because I do think that's awfully darned accurate. A benevolent dictatorship in almost all cases to be sure, but it's got a lot of baggage.

      I've very lightly, weakly advocated a "lines of authority" theory of RPG play and design for a very long time now and I find it's a useful way to think about why some rules work well or poorly with some groups. I think this also gets to your OP, Jim, and I think I agree with it: Most traditional RPGs have, from the beginning, been designed around the implicit assumption that all authority rests with a dictator, and that players like it that way.

      I find that looking at things in terms of responsibility as well as, or instead of, authority can be enlightening.
