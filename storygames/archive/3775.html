    \n Pick the right people. Let them know it may not be fun.
    Know what you want and communicate that to them. Know in advance what you are going to do if something breaks horribly.
    Take notes. I use a copy of the manuscript to write on.
    Pay attention to everything, not just system. Note when they are engaged and when they are not.
    Don't expound on the why of things while you are playing.
    Be gracious and treat them like the rock stars they are for suffering your half-baked game.

    \n - hand each player a copy of the rules
    \n- have each player write notes / comments on their copy as they play
    \n- don't answer questions if they are explained by the rules, let the players figure it out and write notes about what is not clear
    \n- don't do a debriefing immediately after the game is over, eat snacks, hang out, play a quick card game, go for a walk
    \n- then have each player look through their notes and read them out loud with any additional thoughts
    \n- make sure every player has a chance to express their thoughts
    \n- listening to what the players are not saying is as important as what they are saying
    \n- listening is crucial, I will often record the feedback portion of the playtest so I am not distracted by taking my own notes
    \n- don't spend more than 5 minutes on any 1 issue, go around first and make sure everyone expressed all their thoughts and then go back to any comments that need further exploration
    \n- let the players know to email you if they think of something later that they forgot to say
    \n
    \nGood luck!

    \n Lots of good advice here. John/Jenskot's advice covers most of what I would say. I've got only a few additions:

    You should very carefully watch your players during the playtest (don't stare at them or anything, just watch them). What trips them up? What confuses them? What do they dislike? What makes them uncomfortable? You'll be able to read these things from their facial expressions. Make a note of it if necessary.

    Also! Do they use any little tricks to make the rules work/work better? Do you? Do they have a little pow wow about what they're going to do before they break into the "real" game? Are you drawing little maps of relationships? Etc. These are techniques they are developing in order to play your game. You should think about these techniques and how they work. Maybe they belong in the text of your game!

    I'm with Andy about not changing your rules midstream in a session, unless you've specifically gone into a session with the intention of using two different versions of a rule to see the difference they make. Otherwise, it's too easy to make a change while playing and then forget, never putting it into your text. It is very easy to think that you've changed a rule that way, but never get it into the finished text.

    Finally, if you are still developing the game while you are playtesting (which is fine for in-house playtesting, and not good once you've moved on to blind outside playtesting), go into each playtest session with a plan! Identify and flag problem areas in the rules, rules that you are uncertain about, and rules that you are uncertain are expressed clearly, and strive to bring them into play organically.

    \n I sometimes like a much more freeform playtest, where I just sort of wing it from my notes.     \n Wow, lots of really great advice here guys. Thanks so much! So here's an overview of what I've gleaned so far.\n
    Gratitude: Repay the favor the playtesters are granting. I'm gonna treat 'em to a fancy dinner, after which we'll debrief for a little bit.

    Margins: Leave some space in the manuscript for notes.

    Consistency: Use the same set of rules from beginning to end, for various reasons including giving the full system a run-through as-is to find any sift the wheat from the chafe.

    Awareness: Note everything that happens somehow, some way, as long as it's everything. I'll probably tape the session itself, taking notes of visual cues and player engagement. During the debriefing, I'll take more mechanical notes.

    Shut up: Let the players talk. If they have a question, refer to the manuscript.

    Set a Goal: Look out for specific problems when you're going in so you have a metric against which to measure whether the playtest was successful and productive.

    \n shreyas' format sounds great.

    Overall, I'm leery of putting questions in the text. It's likely to cause the playtesters to focus on that area more for good or ill; they won't take the same organic approach to that portion of the rules that they otherwise would have.

    You should invite them to write down their comments and make sure to pay attention to anything they write. However, I think you'll find that the most useful information will come from watching them and how they interact with the rules. If they do something interesting or puzzling though, make sure to ask them about it, what they were thinking or feeling, etc.

    Because it's a playtest, they will almost surely offer you rules suggestions and tweaks. Listen to what they have to say, but remember that nobody but you has a better idea of the big picture that you are trying to capture. Ideas are great, but keep some perspective. Even a really neat idea that seems killer at the time may not be suitable for your vision. What you're really looking for are areas where the mechanics are not achieving your vision. You can attack the problem later.

    Finally, make sure to warn them ahead of time that they're not going to be having regular RPG-fun. If they're gearheads, they may enjoy it immensely. But on the whole, playtesting, especially early playtesting, can be frustrating and even tedious.

    You'll probably want to stop at some points and talk about what's going wrong while it's fresh. Inevitably, someone is going to get annoyed with delays and try to push the game along. That's fine with a normal game, but doesn't work for a playtest. They're going to have to be patient with you and the game.

    \n The thing I don't see on your list is managing expectations - communicate what you want and what you expect (good and bad) to your testers. If they've been 'round the bend with you before, they have some idea, but it's still a good idea to let them know it may not be "fun" - it may, in fact, be the opposite, which is part of why you are buying them dinner.

    With experienced playtesters this is critical, because a "play it hard until it breaks!" tester in a "play it as written and work around the problems!" test is going to mess everything up. Just make sure everybody knows what the goal is, and how you think you'll get there.

Yes, that breaks the flow of the game. No, we're not here to have fun, we're hear to make the game better.

    \nThe first segment of the playtest report is posted here.\n
    This is the first in a series of blog posts reporting and noting the results of the first playtest. To kick things off, here's the playtest document for version 1.0 of the rules, warts and all.\n

    The Document

    \nDo: Pilgrims of the Flying Temple
    Playtest Document v 1.0

    The bulk of this material was written, much like this post, early in the morning before Shannon has woken up for our morning run. It was finally compiled into a semi-coherent form at the 11th hour on the day before playtest. The stream-of-consciousness writing combined with some last-minute changes to make the text actually logical produced some unfortunate stumbling blocks in play.

    Also note that's why the character sheet is such a slap-dash quickie job.

    Thankfully, all of this was clear enough to be playable, despite a few hang-ups.

    The Soundtrack
    I assembled this playlist from the soundtracks of the .hack// and Kingdom Hearts video game franchises. I found them to be evocative enough to set a mood but not so intrusive they are counter to any particular segment of the story. Video game music is designed to fit into an asynchronous storyline with an unpredictable length of time spent on any particular section of the narrative. Very fitting for a tabletop game, I thought. This is the first time I put the hypothesis to the test.

    Character Creation
    The following are transcribed comments and notes directly from the recorded character creation session.

    Here are scans of the completed character sheets.

    Charlie notes how the temple is really the only fixed point in the universe, both in terms of cosmology and in the fiction as well.

    Seating order: To Box's left is seated Shannon, to her left is seated Charlie, to his left is seated Daniel, to my left is seated Box.

    Step 1: Name
    Make space on the character sheet for the name.
    Charlie asks whether the name-list is exhaustive or more of a source of inspiration. Charlie chooses a name that was not noted in the list.
    Box is playing Flood Well
    Shannon is playing Cheerful Orchard
    Charlie is playing Dutiful Floor
    Daniel is playing Jaunty Lotus

    Step 2: Memories
    Make space on the character sheet for the anecdotes.
    This game works best for four players, not three.
    When you're writing the anecdote, should use the character's pilgrim name or some pre-pilgrimage surname that we should use.
    Writing the second and third anecdote is interesting because you don't want to contradict previous anecdotes, but it also gives you some more guidance in terms of the character's established personality.
    It's a fun role-playing challenge to play a character you did not make.
    Include a part where everyone reads aloud the anecdote they wrote about each other's characters, right after it was written. This gives an opportunity to correct any grammar issues in the anecdotes.

    Step 3: Details
    Make space on the character sheet for the Details.
    Charlie asks whether the sensory details are literal. "How does the character taste?"
    Drawing from the anecdotes for the details is tricky.
    In the service of getting something completely playable for the playtest, some parts are lifted wholesale from other games such as 1001 Nights for the Detail examples and inSpectres for the number of Details.
    Foreshadowing of further difficulties in Step 4

    Step 4: Traits
    Make space on the character sheet for the Traits.
    This whole section needs an overhaul. Not coincidentally, it's the one that was most rushed through in writing.
    Even if ESPECIALLY if your game has DIY traits, you need to include an exhaustive list of examples.
    The color wheel diagram got confused with the Way of Ways diagram on the character sheet, which persisted in further confusion in gameplay. However, this also brings up the question of the necessity for a split between Trait and Way since the game was still technically playable.
    Including a summary of the whole character creation process up front would be useful so that there isn't this confusion. Or, perhaps, character creation should be simplified. Perhaps a little of both.
    Make it clear that a trait is six words and there are six traits that you create, but traits can be a sentence or phrase. Shannon tried stringing together six adjectives, which was really hard. 6x6 adjectives. Ouch. She was afraid that she would need to break out the thesaurus.
    The lack of examples was a major mistake. One primarily due to time constraints, but nevertheless one that should have been avoided. All the previous steps were clearly and efficiently executed. All previous steps have at least some examples.
    Players didn't know whether the traits were just strings of six thematically related words, or phrases, or DBZ-style powers, or a pre-made list of more traditional skills.
    Are the traits necessary? (Foreshadowing in further mechanical notes.)
    The trait-simplification over time should be explained here, even though it's relevant to the endgame mechanics.
    Easier method of trait creation: Underline six-word sections of the anecdotes or, if too long or not long enough, underline a section and rephrase it so it's six words.
    Shannon notes that because character creation is such a group activity, there might be peer pressure to not stay on-world or return to the temple.
    The significance of primary vs. secondary colors is not clear.

    Step 5: The Way of Ways
    Explain the setting-significance of The Way of Ways.
    This is a welcome return to easy character creation process from steps 1 and 2.
    Revise the significance of white and black beads so they're not a good/bad dichotomy.
    Also note the endgame "victory" conditions at this point or earlier so that players keep this in mind as they distribute their tokens.

    Total character creation time for four players, including the problems in step 4: About an hour.

    Conclusions
    Consider removing step 3 and merging step 4 and 5. Remove Balance from the Way of Ways and let players choose which Way is assigned to a particular color. Players said the anecdotes were still useful as a role-play aid and to loosen up creatively, so keep that part. Later gameplay shows a lack of relevance for the split between Trait and Way, so when you create a Trait, go ahead and assign it to a color, too. When you do something, the relevant attribute is the Way you do it, but if you need further inspiration for describing your character's action, you can look at the associated trait as well.

    One of the things I've experienced with my group playing a few indie games: On-the-spot creativity is hard. Hard isn't necessarily un-fun, but some of us need a little more help.
