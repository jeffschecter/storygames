    \n David, the question is meaningless. It is in no way shape or form, even remotely a board game in any fashion at all...at all. The question is mind boggling in its irrelevance. I suspect the rumor of WHFRPG being a board game came from posts on the FFG forum where the idea of an RPG in a box which uses tokens to track conditions triggered "OMG its a board game!" response from 1st ed and 2nd ed grognards.
    \n
    \nHaving played it, and having read the rules, it is no more a board game than any RPG you could imagine. Its orders of magnitude less board gamey than D&D; 4E.
    \n
    \nIts an RPG with streamlined character generation rules inspired by the "choose from a menu of options" method common to CRPGs. It takes all of the "special powerz" that other games might call "feats" or the like and puts them on cards so you can always refer to the rules without flipping through a book. It lets you track hit point damage by acquiring wound cards face down and healing by discarding the cards so you don't have to write and erase bunches of little hashmarks. It lets you determine criticals and their effects by flipping a wound card face up so you get random criticals without needing to roll on a table. It uses cards to represent important pieces of geography or terrain and tokens to denote range so you can move figures around abstractly without needing a map.
    \n
    \nWhat elements of any of that suggests its a board game?
    \n
    \nAs for the original question...Comparing WHFRP to Descent is like comparing In a Wicked Age to Risk Godstorm...the only reasonable response is...wha?

    \n Buzz, that's pretty much rules as written. If I flipped through the book I could find the page reference where "Say Yes" is official WHFRPG3 GM instructions with Fortune dice being specifically mentioned as an easy way you can say yes to clever things players have done that would help them in the current situation and Misfortune dice being an easy way to say yes to crazy things players want to try that might have ramifications if they go wrong.
    \n
    \nI didn't even mention the interplay of Boons and Banes. Axe symbols are successes, 1 success suceeds, additional successes can buy extra oompf. Eagles are Boons and Skulls are Banes.
    \n
    \nThey combine thusly:
    \nSuccess with Boons is a "Yes, and in addition this other good thing also happens"
    \nSuccess with Banes is a "Yes, but you also suffered this other thing"
    \nFailure with Boons is a "No, but at least you got this other thing"
    \nFailure with Banes is a "No, and further more you're also screwed thusly"
    \n
    \nThe division in the game isn't really combat vs. non combat. Its more "Encounter Mode" vs. "Story Mode" where "Encounter Mode" is combat...plus any other kind of conflict you want to resolve in a fighty-esque way.
    \n
    \nIn Encounter Mode the good and bad things you can do with Boons and Banes are typically spelled out on the card. 1 Axe does damage, 3 Axes does extra damage, 2 Boons knocks your opponent down, 2 Banes means you're off balance and your opponent gets a Fortune die, that sort of thing.
    \n
    \nBut in "story mode" your GM can pretty much make up how to interpret the results. So like if you were trying to convince the king to give you a promotion he could say that 1 axe means the king doesn't refuse but doesn't make it a high priority. 2 axes means the king agrees to the promotion but there's not currently an open position to give you. 3 axes means the king makes a new position especially for you. 2 Boons means he also give you a medal, 2 Banes means the court's opinion of is you're grasping and reaching beyond your station...then you roll and the GM gives you a Misfortune die because you earlier embarrassed the duke so now he's lobbying against you with the king...but that die comes up blank so you know the king didn't pay any attention to him.
    \n
    \nOne of the basic cards all characters get in Encounter mode is "Stunt" which essentially means "do something not covered by your other cards and use story mode logic to figure out how it works". They made it a card specifically so players wouldn't think they were only restricted to their specified actions and would remember they could, in fact, try anything.

    \n Having only cracked it open and perused, I have no idea how it plays but yes shiny.

    My set (purchased in Toronto) came with an additional bag of replacement d10's saying the originals weren't up to manufacturing specs. I can see why they went with special dice, using regular dice and interpreting numbers on a table would have been painfully slow whereas, "woo hoo 3 axes, oh oh, 4 skulls" is right there staring at you. That you sometimes have choices in how to spend your successes etc. is also very nice - it gives a level of player choice/control sometimes missing in "fortune at the end"-prone RPGs..

    Overall I think the "choices in what the dice mean" and the variety of "yes and, yes but; no, although" results would make for more interesting play.

    I think the tone can be a "your table" thing. I don't see any powers, "You belch so powerfully the goblins faint." (maybe there's one in there, I was mostly reading rules not cards so far). There's still a "snotling" reference and sometimes the artwork is a "punk dwarf with a mohawk" that isn't my thing - but I've played games where the artwork wasn't my thing before.

    There's nods to "Say Yes", and some 'scene structure' concepts that are interesting. Players are to author "character hooks" for adventures not the GM.

    \n Yes, although with no immediate campaign prospects I didn't study them as in depth.
    \n
    \nInitiative is pretty interesting. Every player rolls initiative and the GM does for each GROUP of opponents. The number of successes determines the order that actions are performed in. But there's a twist. While each turn is marked with a token on the initiative track, the tokens are differentiated only by side (players vs. GM). Not by individual characters or groups. So just because your elvish rogue type routinely rolls the most initiative successes, that doesn't mean he's the one who always acts first...any player in the party can act on that initiative (with any disagreements/strategy discussions that aren't quickly resolved potentially earning an increase on the party tension meter. This not only allows for some tactical stringing together of combos (by allowing players to choose which order they will act in) but also means initiative flys...the next token on the track is either a player token or a GM token...go. None of the complicated 4E initiative tracking lists. Plus, any initiative system that allows players to "delay" or "hold" actions pretty much means that on any round after the first the players are selecting their own turn order anyway...so why go through the rigamorole of a bunch of extra steps.
    \n
    \nThe monster groups are either single tougher monsters (like encounter bosses) while lesser henchment are organized into groups sized equally to the number of party characters. The groups all act together on one initiative but only ONE of them rolls. The others merely provide a Fortune die to the roll for their assistance. Further, each group is a single hit point pool e.g. 3 beast men of 5 wounds each are a pool of 15 points and every 5 points of damage fells a beast man. So there is no need to track individual HP totals or have a lot of damage waste from over kill (10 points of damage in a single blow will kill 2 beastmen). Monsters have Aggression / Cunning / Expertise pools which indicated the number of fortune dice the GM can freely add to their rolls in Fights / Non Fights. The Expertise Pool means that monsters don't need to be given Skill Lists the way characters are, there is simply a pool of Expertise dice (what characters get for having higher level skills) that the GM can add to a pool for rolls that that monster would be "skilled" in (like tracking by scent for wolves or what not). When the pools are gone, they are gone. Also monsters get Action Cards like characters but there is only 1 copy of each Action for the entire monster side. Some actions can be used as often as needed, but many need a "cool down" period. So even if there are a bunch of monsters with the same ability, they can't all use it at the same time (though it recharges on each GM turn so it will recycle faster than a player action). Further the monsters are all laid out in a two page spread that incorporates background and tactical information as well as stat blocks for several versions of the monster. The game has a CRPG / MMORPG conceit (like 4E) in that there are several "levels" of each type of monster and rules for upgrading and down grading them. The monsters are ranked in power relative to each other rather than relative to some default party standard which I think practically speaking is a much easier way to assemble suitable challenges.
    \n
    \nThere is a whole Acts --> Episodes ---> Adventure ---> Campaign structure with mechanisms like "Rally Phases" (similar to 4E short rests) and "Interludes" (a cross between a 4E extended rest and Mouse Guard Player Turn). There are mechanisms for players systematically generating hooks the GM is expected to use (much like Mouse Guard "Goals") and the suggestion that the GM build various "Triggers" (i.e. if / then type branches) into the adventure. Adventures are presumed to be largely of a branching / linear nature with the GM adjusting the branches on the fly using standard illusionist techniques, although a more sophisticated application of the text would create a more CRPG "Quest Area" type of feel. You know how in CRPGS when you enter into a new area you can wander around freely but there are only limited interactions that mean anything (like dudes with exclamation points saying "come talk to me and accept my mission") and then fulfilling certain requirements opens up other areas and other interactions. That's the sort of adventure construction it appears to me that the WHFRP text would support best. There's even a little pictoral flow chart of a sample adventure that looks like it could have been taken from a CRPG graphical "Walk Through".
    \n
    \nThe experience system is kind of interesting in its presumptions. The text is adamantly against awarding players differently (no Mouse Guard style MVP / work horse type of awards). It very clearly considers such badwrongfun and recommends against it several times. Interestingly it then goes on to provide an alternate reward system for those player specific rewards you want to hand out. The core experience system is to hand out 1 Advance per session. Each Career (i.e. class) has a number of available Advances (some provide lots of social skills, others provide more opportunities to increase attributes, others to get more Action cards, etc.). Each type of improvement costs an Advance until you've purchased all of the available advances from a career you wish to, then you change careers and get a new batch of advance options. Careers don't have a hard progression tree, but instead have an open ended tree. Each career has a number of traits. Changing careers costs a fair number of advances reduced by the number of traits the new and old career have in common. So there is a progression tree if you stick to the cheapest transitions, but you have the option to save up enough Advances to jump anywhere. This system does envision long term play (their definition of a "mini campaign" is 10 adventures) so if you're planning for shorter campaigns you would need to accelerate this if you want to see the career tracks in action.
    \n
    \nThe only GM tool that's "missing" (and by that I mean, *I* would have liked to see it because it would have made my life easier) is a Mouse Guard style structured approach to adventure building. While there is a structure for organizing adventures its still fairly old school in the sense of the GM needing to come up with a plot and a story arc and a bunch of characters and locations and set piece "episodes" to run. And that's the kind of thing I don't have much time for these days. But that's not to say one couldn't hack together a MG-esque system for generating adventure quickly.

    \n Party Stress.
    \n
    \nEvery party has its own card sheet. There are a number of standard parties, don't know if they'll eventually release rules for inventing your own. They're the fantasy equivalent of a super hero team. The rules direct picking your party after everyone makes characters, but it strikes me that deciding what sort of party you want to be before making characters would be useful. I haven't studied them all but the two I remember were "Gang of Thugs" and "Defenders of Justice" (or something like that).
    \n
    \nEach party sheet has 1) a couple of talent slots so that party members with the right category of talent can plug their talent into the party theirby giving all party members access to it. 2) a special party power...any member of the Defender of Justice can voluntarily take a point of Fatigue and a point of Stress to increase the damage from their attack by 1 (after calculating damage). A useful power for putting the finishing move on someone. and 3) a Stress Track. The purpose of the Stress Track is to give the GM a mechanical way of saying "let's move it along and get back on track" or to reflect intercharacter rivalries. Some of the spaces have effects when they're reached and when the last space is reached a bigger effect happens and then the track resets. I haven't looked through to catalog all the different effect, but the ones I've seen seem to be of the "everyone takes a point of Fatigue / Stress / Wound" sort of thing, representing increasing tension in the party.
    \n
    \nI haven't seen it in action really to judge, but it didn't strike me as a wierd blunder from just reading it.
    \n
    \nBuzz, I don't know what you're considering fundamental to D&D.; Is a fairly standard fantasy setting...party of heroes, evil monsters, kill things and take their stuff kind of game. WH did the "points of light" style setting long before 4E picked it up. There's a greater emphasis on investigation and rooting out internal threats (like cults) or clearing out external threats (like bands of roving beastmen) and less emphasis on underground dungeons. Back when I played 1E we cleared a few ruins but I don't recall ever doing an actual "dungeon" adventure. Their's a much greater sense of the characters being an actual part of a functioning society (not to the level of Storming the Wizards tower, but far greater than the usual gang of wandering psychopaths in the typical D&D; game). Combat has always been a little less attrition and a little more...one bad move and I'm dead. Mechanically WHFRP3 bears 0 resemblance to any version of D&D...perhaps; most especially 4E.

      \n Well I think its clear that WHFRPG3 was hugely influenced by story game design. But that's a far cry from saying it IS a story game. Its built on a very traditional chassis, and the seperation of Story Mode from Encounter mode, while sophisticated for a game of its type, is still very much a traditional seperation. I suspect if you posed the issue to the designers you'd get a blank look and a reply of "of COURSE the story mode isn't as worked out as combat...there are only a limited number of things you can do in combat, but story mode can be ANYTHING...of course each game table needs to work it out for themselves...how else would it happen?" And I suspect, most RPers out there would agree.
