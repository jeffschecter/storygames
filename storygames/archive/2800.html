\n In writing books, you'll find a concept called the crucible. That's the setup of the situation that keeps the main characters bouncing off each other. It's the reason why people don't just walk away when things get rough, why they escalate their conflicts instead of just taking a few days off.

Many horror movies have a clear crucible: the group needs to stick together to survive, even as their personalities clash. (I much prefer movies where the threat is a crucible to one where it's the main attraction; hence my love for Pitch Black.)

I just read an AP report on the Forge about the typical group of PCs: they have no real reason to stick together, but the players "don't want to be dicks," so they make the characters stick together.

Now, when I talk about the crucible, I'm not necessarily talking about something that keeps them all on the same side. They could be on opposite sides, but have to deal with each other a lot of the time, their stories becoming unavoidably interwoven.

So my questions to all of you thus are: What are well-structured crucibles, either given by the game or made up by the players? Do you address this explicitly in your games? Have you developed guidelines for making this happen? Are there crucible-creating techniques we could apply to any old game?

    Maybe that's a little heavy-handed, but it's worked for me in the past.

    I am experimenting with crucibles.

    \n

    In one Snow From Korea, the characters are literally contained by a mansion; they cannot leave its walls. They are all loyal to one lord and they are all arrayed against a common enemy, and they are all empowered to hurt each other very severely. It works.

    \n

    HeroQuest has an interesting crucible-thing, the presence of a community that's given weight and attention by mechanical means, and the network of relationships that connect the characters. You can't move out of the community's eyes without provoking some response. Everything you do affects and repositions your relationships. It's an emotional crucible - the system makes you look at every action through a particular lens.

    \n

    In another thing I'm working on, there is a place, a garden, that draws all the characters back to it. Each character might have his own reasons and needs for this, but because the garden keeps all in its orbit, it defines the parameters of conflict pretty tightly. The characters might even try to disperse, but the garden inevitably brings them back.

    \n Speaking for my own designs, the Reserve Police Agency and the situation it creates is the key crucible in Cold City. The characters, while they may not trust each other, while they may have totally conflicting hidden agendas that bring them into conflict, are drawn together by having to do the dirty and unrecognised job of hunting down monsters, twisted technology and dealing with the consequences of this. The unity of this shared, unpleasant experience is what brings the party together. And then they fracture into pieces as hidden agendas come to the surface.

    There are no particular guidelines in the game for this: it's an accepted part of the fiction that the characters are all working for the RPA. In this instance, I'm not sure any further formalisation would be required.

    Good topic!

    Cheers
    Malcolm

    \n I tend to handle the crucible very differently in long-term and in short-term play.

    In short-term play, I want people to start out right in the thick of things. "Okay, why are you all refugees?" All of a sudden, from the moment they conceive their characters, the players are working from a premise which puts them a) together and b) under pressure. "So you're on a reality TV show where people want to shoot you in the head. Why did you sign up?" The crucible is inherent in the premise from which we are working, together, to make a game.

    For a long-term game, I often don't know what the first crucible is going to be until the players and I create characters together. I find out, by listening to them talk to me and to each other, what they think is important to their characters. Then I work to build a situation where walking away means losing out on what's important to them, for whatever reason. Once the PCs are through the first crucible or two, I find that the crucible just has to create pressure on the group, not both create pressure and create the group at the same time. Make sense?

    For example, in my Rome game - which just finished, and I am still kind of reeling - one PC had originally been a slave, and the concept of freedom was very important to both player and character. I knew this from day one, and so in creating that first story I put his freedom at risk if he did nothing or walked away. Guess what? The PC got deeply involved in the crucible - and with the other PCs. By the third or fourth story of the game, I could be sure that the character wouldn't walk away from the other PCs even if he was less directly under threat, because he owed them so much and vice versa. By the end of the (four-year) game, the PCs really didn't like each other much, but they always turned to each other first because hell, no one else knew them so well or had any idea what they'd all been through together. They became, by the end of the first year of the game, like a family. And family's a hell of a crucible, as I'm sure we all know .....

    --Jess

    \n Great answers so far And nothing wrong with talking about your own game.

    Jason, I think premise can be the thing that invests players in each other's characters and scenes, but it doesn't necessarily tie the characters together. You could have a setup where the characters never meet nor impact each other with their actions, but share the same premise, and everyone's still invested in all scenes.

    It seems to me that we have so far:
    - hubs (the Queen that everyone's loyal to)
    - closed locations (the mansion, the garden, the refugee camp, the TV show)
    - shared competitive goals (the professors in the Roach competing with each other)
    - shared common goals (refugees helping each other out, apocalypse survivors, humans among undead)
    - relationships among the PCs, strong enough that they can't bear to walk away

    Fred, when you say crucible-after, do you mean after play?

    Posted By: xenopulseFred, when you say crucible-after, do you mean after play?
    \nI mean after play begins.

    There's a style of play where everyone makes their own characters completely independently, and plays them separately for a while, basically in short 1-1 scenes with the GM, to develop the character some. Then, gradually or suddenly, the GM crafts a situation which draws them all together.

    You can start character creation with your crucible, informing the players that their PC's must relate to it.

    You can start PLAY with your crucible, crafting something that will draw them all together from the start.

    You can introduce the crucible after play begins.

    \n From my design wiki, ways of connecting the PCs:

    The following suggestions for how the characters meet owe some inspiration to David Morgan-Mar (http://www.dangermouse.net/gurps/reject/tavern.html). There are basically these variations:

    1. The characters already know each other and are an established, current group of some kind, such as a military, police or Special Forces unit, ship\xe2\u20ac\u2122s crew, group of freelance troubleshooters, group of friends who get together regularly, relatives, co-workers, or people who live in the same small location.
    2. The characters know each other from the past (perhaps having gone to school or been in the military together) but they have been out of touch for some time. They are reunited by an event such as a funeral, a planned reunion, attacks upon others of their number, their old commander calling for help against an enemy\xe2\u20ac\xa6
    3. The characters are strangers to one another, but are in similar or complementary lines of work or belong to the same organization. Someone assembles them as a team to perform a project or mission or to staff something like a ship, fort, house or enterprise, either by approaching them individually or by advertising.
    4. The characters are strangers to one another and meet by chance, while traveling on a ship/train/spaceship etc., in a trade caravan, in an accommodation place, at a pilgrimage site, at a conference or simply in the public square or street. A crisis (accidental or engineered) throws them together.
    5. The characters are strangers to one another but are all victims of the same person, who has shanghaied, captured or imprisoned them.
    6. A combination of two or more of the above (some characters join the group in one way, some in another).
