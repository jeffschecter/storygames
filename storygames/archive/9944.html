\n Tonight I asked my group if they wanted to also become a book club. Everyone is up for it. We're planning on reading books that are more or less in the genre of whatever game we're playing, which is currently a low-tech dark fantasy with sorcery, evil wizards, and no elves (using TSoY/Solar System). We move around a bit in terms of genre and system, so it might be gothic horror one week and time-traveling police drama the next.

I'm a fan of Tanith Lee, Jack Vance, Henry Miller, Flanner O'Connor, Robert Heinlein, China Mieville, Fritz Leiber, and have been reading Robert E. Howard and Edgar Rice Burroughs lately.

I'd like you guys/gals to recommend books that you thing a gaming group would like to read and possibly inform/inspire play. I don't care for light reading, but would love books that have lots of interesting ideas or are just plain well written. I'd like you to list books you think are important, and if possible categorized by genre and a brief description of why they are so good.

Thanks!

    \n Well, we're playing Godlike right now so some of the books that have been opened up in our group lately include Studs Terkel's "The Good War", Stephen Ambrose's "Band of Brothers". The tone and feel of our campaign owes a lot to the oral history of WW2 so these books are almost required reading.

    I read a lot of WW2 history in general - most recently a biography of Hirohito and "Ivan's War" which is about the Red Army.

    We have also passed around some old issues of DC's "The Haunted Tank". ;-)

We move around a bit in terms of genre and system, so it might be gothic horror one week and time-traveling police drama the next.

    I'm a fan of Tanith Lee, Jack Vance, Henry Miller, Flanner O'Connor, Robert Heinlein, China Mieville, Fritz Leiber, and have been reading Robert E. Howard and Edgar Rice Burroughs lately.
    \nWhen I think of dark fantasy, one of the authors I think of first is Glen Cook. In particular his Dread Empire series and his The Instrumentality of the Night series, and particularly his Black Company series.

    The Dread Empire Books feature politics, treachery, weird wizards, and a story line complex enough to turn your brain inside-out. The Instrumentality of the Night takes place in a pseudo-historical analog of Europe and the Middle East, but with supernatural elements that range from minor spirits up to gods (and even God), all of which are of a similar nature, and they can be manipulated and even killed. Black Company follows a mercenary company with a history in world of rat-bastard mages, ancient powers, double-and-triple crosses while being very gritty and human-level. Steven Erikson's Malazan Book of the Fallen series has heavily inspired by Cook's work.

    Another dark fantasy writer is Karl Edward Wagner's Kane series, which are Sword & Sorcery about a man cursed with immortality.

    \n Yay, it's time for Random Book Lists!

    The Iron Dragon's Daughter, Michael Swanwick, for endless crazy urban fairy/magic ideas.
    To The Lighthouse, Virginia Woolf, for narrative style & structure.
    A Canticle For Leibowitz, Walter M. Miller Jr, for post-apocalyptic religiosity.
    Krapp's Last Tape, Samuel Beckett, for better monologues & internal narrative.

    Posted By: Ice Cream EmperorKrapp's Last Tape, Samuel Beckett, for better monologues & internal narrative.
    QFT. How is that so many people have read worthless drivel like Romeo and Juliet, and so few have read Krapp's Last Tape (or anything by Beckett)?

    I've wanted to game in the world of The Cardinal's Hat ever since I read the first chapter. The author, Mary Hollingsworth, has an excellent eye for detail and a great ability to conjure up the rough and worldly realities of a Borgia prince's day-to-day life. Really, any of the historical eras in The Bad Popes would be a blast, but I especially love the idea of playing impious hierarchs during the period between the Reformation and the Counter-reformation.

    \n If you want something highbrow-ish (ie - books not relegated to the "Fantasy/Sci-Fi" ghetto of your local chain bookstore), I'll recommend:

    The Enchantress of Florence by Salman Rushdie
    Gentlemen of the Road by Michael Chabon

    Both of those books have plenty of material (characters, settings, set pieces, cool bits, fantastical elements, etc) that lend themselves well to gaming.

    I can definitely see how something like this could add awesomeness to a campaign game, in the sense of getting everyone on the same page in terms of tone and fiction and what-not. (Although I have never once enjoyed a book club).

    It was originally serialized in the NYT Sunday Magazine, so each chapter is kind of a mini-adventure, complete with cliffhangers.
