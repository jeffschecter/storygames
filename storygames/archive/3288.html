\n I need a new name for Mathematica, because that name doesn't say anything about what the game is.

Mathematica is an over-to-top alternate history steam punk renaisance game where Da Vinci builds giant spider war machines and Machiavelli commands a cabal of vampire spies across Europe, or something like that. It could also be three musketeers with lasers or Henry VIII and his seven undead wives. There's a lot of flexibility.

Mathematica is a TSOY variant where the characters' action are tied to a wider war of ideas. Can women decide their own fate? Does the pope speak with the voice of the divine? Is it lawful to worship death? Is torture permissable to fight evil? Are good and evil valid categories at all? These are the kinds of questions that characters can fight for or against and ultimately answer.

Currently the only suggestion I have on the table is "Gonzo Renaissance Culture War Theatre with a Foreword by Machiavelli". I think I'm too close to this to name it. Based on the capsule above, anyone got a suggestion?

    \n Incidentally, the other problem with "Mathematica", besides lacking an obvious connection to the setting, is that it's the name of a prominent software package. All the top Google results for "Mathematica" point to it, and although Tony should be safe from trademark problems as the products are so dissimilar, he'd still be better off avoiding it.

    I don't like "The Schisms of Yesterday" only because it's not a good idea to introduce confusion over "TSOY". Regardless of Clinton's position on reusing the material and the Creative Commons license (and I have no idea off the top of my head what version he's using or what its terms are), that's a good route to creating consumer confusion and lawsuits over trademark rights. (I'm not saying Clinton would have a problem with another game referred to as "TSOY" or that he'd start a lawsuit, I'm just saying, don't make the possibility of trouble you can easily avoid.)

    I do like Mike's suggestion a lot. "Principia" is better than "Philosophia," especially at suggesting it's not just metaphysical concepts on the table but concrete principles, and adding "Secret Wars of the Renaissance" is grabby and succinctly conveys the setting.

    (Edit to add standard Internet disclaimer, I Am Not A Lawyer.)
