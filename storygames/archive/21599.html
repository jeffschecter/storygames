    \n While actually playing, I exclusively use character names in that context, because outside of dialogue, I do third person narration primarily. Outside of dialouge, I'm very much in a narrator-type role for describing what my character does, so it's always either [character name] or [character's appropriate pronoun].

    For an example, here's a little mini-scene that was an early scene in our current campaign:

    Me: I want just this scene of Sadie at home. I'm going to be trying to hit some XP triggers on Shrine Duties and A New Hobby, maybe not doing any XP actions, but... Just... Sadie, dressed in her spring outfit, which is a flannel and a black skirt, and she just walks out to the front of the House of Crystal, and she checks the mailbox and looks a little bit disappointed. Then she walks back into the House of Crystal.
    GM: The light shining through the window catches in the prisms and glasswork pieces, creating this beautiful, beautiful array of light, dancing across the wall.
    Me: I want to "sell fortunes and good luck charms", so like, there's the dinging of this bell, and Sadie rushes over to a desk.
    Me (speaking as Sadie): How can I help you?
    GM (speaking as the customer): I'm having bad luck. Is there bad luck is there something you could give me to fix that?
    Me (speaking as Sadie): We've got some good luck charms here.
    Me: She takes a glass pendant that's in the shape of a crystal from behind the desk.
    Me (speaking as Sadie): These aren't very expensive. I could wrap this up for you if you'd like.
    GM (speaking as the customer): That would be perfect.
    Me: Sadie takes out some tissue paper, wraps it up, then puts it in a little brown bag she pulls out from underneath the cash register, and she just hands them the bag.
    GM: They hand you the money and just clasp the bag to their chest.
    GM (speaking as the customer): Thank you so much!
    Me (speaking as Sadie): It's nothing. Any time you need anything we're here. It's what we do.
    GM (speaking as the customer): Thank you!
    GM: And then the customer leaves.
    Me: I want to fade on that scene there.

    \n I tend to use the following terminology nowadays in most games (in Finnish because I play mostly in Finnish):

    "Hahmo", 'character' - One of the persons in the fiction of the game.
    "Hahmopelaaja", 'character player' - One of the players who have a player character
    "Pelaajahahmo", 'player character' - One of the characters with a dedicated player
    "Sivuhahmo", 'supporting character' - One of the characters who aren't player characters
    "Minun hahmoni", 'my character' - Me or somebody else referring to their player character

    While the distinction between saying "I" or "my character" exists in Finnish roleplaying, interestingly I don't think that we have a more informal word for the character than "hahmo" - so we use the same word for "my guy" and "player character", unlike what English-speakers seem to do.

    \n I'd agree with Emma that "my guy" suggests more of a game-piece attitude towards the character (whether video game or otherwise). I also hear it as a bit self-deprecating, as if to say that my character is not so special or important, so I won't bother you with the details of who it is.

    Thinking on my own usage, I think the only times I've used terms like "my guy" commonly is when running mini dungeon playtests with a friend, in which case one of us would create a little party of several nameless characters knowing that they would be killed by traps or retired rich within a couple hours. Then we would talk freely about "Let me get my guys [character sheets]" or "Which one of your dudes has the light?" or "My thief tries the door."

    For any ongoing game, I am used to hearing people talk in first person or refer to their character by name. I don't think I have even encountered "my fighter" or "my dwarf" in a serious game. For telling a story to someone who wasn't there, I expect to hear first person, "I totally destroyed the gazebo!", maybe with an introduction like, "I play this paladin in Ben's game. Last week we met a gazebo, and I totally destroyed it (even though we were only level 4)!"

    One point of usage that I have noticed differences in is the way GMs refer to the NPCs and monsters. I sometimes hear a GM say something like, "Now it's my turn," or "Let's see if I [the ogre] can hit you." Whereas I would normally say, "Now it's the monsters' turn" and "Let's see if the ogre can hit you."

    \n Here's an example, Emma. Assume this is within a challenge-based game.

    Suppose the GM makes up some challenge for the players to face, such as a dragon with a pile of gold. But then she doesn't let the players actually face the problem. Instead, she describes to them how a mysterious archer comes and fells the dragon with a single arrow.

    Here, the GM is breaking the rules of the game in order to show off how cool her own NPC is. If the PCs had killed the dragon within the rules of the game, we would say that they were "winning" in the sense of overcoming a worthy challenge. But it is meaningless for the GM to overcome the challenge of the dragon because the GM has unlimited narrative power. So if the GM is focused on making her character "win", that can be harmful to the other players' enjoyment of the game. It is taking away the players' chance to engage with the challenge.

    Now suppose the GM did this over and over, every time the players faced a challenge. The mysterious archer always shows up and saves the day. In that case, you would worry the GM was identifying too strongly with the NPC. She is abdicating her role as the GM, which is to play the whole world and create challenges for the PCs, and instead focusing on showing off this one character.

    \n That's it in a nutshell.

    The 'Mary Sue' NPC is often somehow better than he or she ought to be in the context of the game's setting, a 1st Level Wizard who can cast fireballs to use a pretty crude example. It is not down to the campaign style (such as a Martial Arts campaign where peasants all suddenly turn out to be black-belt martial artists) but simply because the GM loves and identifies with 'his' NPC so much that he wants her to shine brighter than the PCs.

    The GM wants the PCs to fawn over this wonderful NPC, but naturally the opposite generally happens. He wants the NPC to be able to overcome the obstacles that the PCs cannot and to avoid harm. That is why I mention 'winning'.

    In a more storytelling environment, imagine the NPC that somehow manages to grab the story and make it about them. The NPC that the PCs find themselves having to react to all the time yet no amount of pushing on their part seems to make the NPC react to them. This is the Hollywood hero who is at the centre of everything, comes up with the winning plans and yet manages to emerge both unscathed and in a stronger position at the end of the game. This is the hero that means the PCs are the bit-players and supporting cast and not the ones in the spotlight.

    \n To my way of thinking, it depends how you think when the NPC is under threat.

    \n I think it's quite different in practice. "How can Lady Anne manage to avoid being assassinated?" might involve, for example, her hiring a bodyguard. What if one of the PCs masquerades as a bodyguard for hire? Now we have a plot.

    \n The first case is the GM thinking like the NPC, taking into account the situation as present in the game, to save herself from assassination. That is empathic.

    The second case is the NPC being saved by the GM using all the tools at his disposal. That is acting against the game's covenant which states that the GM is the arbiter and not the opponent.

    The GM has, after all, all manner of ways to cheat the run of events. Paul_T has mentioned a few of them: lying about dice rolls and presenting or introducing factors that were not present until they were convenient to thwart the assassination.

    Imagine a novel in which Lady Anne has just been described as wandering alone in her rose garden. A would-be assassin lines up a crossbow and waits for the perfect moment before send off a bolt that is heading directly for Lady Anne's heart.

    In the first Case I presented above, what are the realistic ways that Lady Anne will survive? It has been stated that she is alone, the rose garden has little cover and the assassin has stated that he is waiting for the perfect shot. In that instance I would expect the plot to follow with Lady Anne hit, probably fatally wounded.

    In the second Case, the novel might suddenly introduce a hidden armour plate Lady Anne wears in her corset but has never been mentioned before and is not in her description, a herd of cows suddenly appear from nowhere to block her from sight, or she is pushed aside by a bodyguard who has apparently appeared from nowhere.

    The second case leaves you feel cheated, it is simply using a god-like deux ex-machina. Yes, the writer/GM can alter things to his own whims, but that doesn't mean it is the 'fair' way to do things. A suspension of disbelief only stretches so far.

      1. A character loses HP if monsters make their attack rolls
      2. A character can have some non-lethal consequence if they lose enough HP, but not die
      3. A character can\u2019t die if they are a main character
