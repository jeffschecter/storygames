What place, if any, does 1st person immersion have in story games?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nMongrel_GM \n\n\n
\n
\n\nAugust 2017 \n in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n A debate came up at gaming group last night. Essentially I held forth that 1st person immersion is a viable style for story games, with the huge advantage that the PCs' desires and goals are what drive the drama and narrative. The other view was that immersion is equally deep in a 3rd person director mode, and that the story emerges best because players put the needs of the story first, while focusing on the desires of their PC.

PC desires focus, approached with different technique.

I almost always approach my PCs from a 1st person immersion approach. Getting into character is one of the things I enjoy when role playing. Yes, acting. "What would I do in this situation?" is key in making decisions for my characters. For me, this is where much of the verisimilitude in a game is created/preserved. On a 50/50 choice I'll go with the dramatic story choice, sure. But sometimes my PC will play it safe, or try to get their way, and that could be at odds with the desires of the other PCs. Sometimes that could be at odds with the desires of the other players.

Does this 1st person immersion approach have a place in all story games? Some story games? In no well-played story games?

Fiasco, which I perceive to be the industry standard, is an interesting case in point. I've played it three times. It has some amazing things in setup that it accomplishes with ease. Characters I've played: a self-serving alpha male who would rather die than appear weak, a youthful keyboardist with artistic aspirations who has just joined a band of old rockers, and a bored London trophy wife with a checkered past. Each of these games had a setting, but no particular direction for the action inherent in the playset. In every case a zany story emerged. 2 of the 3 games evolved into small time burglaries with some characters involved in self-destructive side stories. The 3rd game involved kidnapping, revenge, and attempted extortion.

In all three games my PCs were swept into the action against their will (In 2 of the 3 games my PCs fought back in their own way when that seemed like what they would do.) Is it a fluke that my PCs were swept in each time? Maybe that was a result of approaching the game in 1st person immersion mode. Was it a good or a bad thing? A neutral thing? Were my fellow players trying to have their PCs lose? Or follow self-destructive goals? Which PCs brought more verisimilitude to the story? Is that even the goal of Fiasco? Was I throwing a wrench in the gears by trying to keep my PC's agency intact?

If I need to provide more details in order to form an opinion, let me know.

Last night's play test involved a Game of Throne's inspired scenario. My opinion is that, in the case of a political intrigue story, the story is best served by players using 1st person immersion and pushing to achieve their ends (rather than focusing on rising action and climax). Plotting and backstabbing ensue. In my opinion the story emerged as PCs tried to "win." But perhaps I perceived that my fellow players were playing as I was when in fact they were using "3rd person immersion." I think that last night the most decisive moments of the story came about when the players were heavily advocating for their PCs--advocating being something we associate more closely with 1st person immersion. But maybe that's just what I thought I saw.

Insights?

    \n First of all, I think it would be really helpful to sharpen the terminology a bit. You speak of "immersion", but some of the features you describe, such as "making decisions from the viewpoint of my character" are not exclusive to character immersion - there are other player-character relationships that may be relevant, and probably are. Consider the following list of common relationships that a player can have with their character in roleplaying games:

    Immersive play: The player primarily focuses on experiencing their character vicariously. When the character is excited, the player is excited, and the player knows that their character is excited by realizing that they are excited themselves, in a certain sort of second-hand manner. The player is not primarily focused on expressing their character of manipulating the story, because they're focused on experiencing their character; the main interaction on the player's part towards the other players is acting as their character.

    Pawn play: The player utilizes their character as a pawn in the game, that they manipulate to achieve their goal. Often the goal is winning or creating a great story, but it could be something like having your pawn act realistically just as well. The other people's characters are different from your own in that you're the one who has the right to control this pawn, not them.

    Advocacy play: The player's job is to understand their character as a fictional person, express that personality powerfully to the other players as an audience, and direct the character in making choices according to their best interests. The player's essentially a sort of lawyer or representative who makes sure that their character gets heard as a central character of the game.

    (I'm not saying that these names are any better than some other names for this stuff - I'm just giving names to different behaviors so you'll understand my next question.)

    So, my question in light of this terminological field is as follows: when you characterize your own play as "1st person immersion", would you say that you're doing "immersive play" or "advocacy play" as per the above terminology? You described your play-style as very focused on your own character, and that it is important to make decisions from the character's viewpoint (as opposed to considering the whole story), but that could conceivably describe either of those two different approaches!

    The reason I'm asking is that whether your play style is immersive or advocative changes the answer entirely: while there are a wide variety of games termed "story games" (it's not a strictly defined concept at all), the vast majority of them are not really suited to immersive play in the sense that rpg theory generally understands the term. In the meantime, advocative play is the intended play mode for most story games; the ones where you're supposed to use your character as a pawn to "craft a better story" are overall a clear minority.

    In summation: story games do not support actual character immersion well, but they generally expect you to express your character and defend their position zealously. So they're not mostly very immersive in the strong sense of the word, but most are 1s-person games in the sense that they assume that you'll be playing to advocate for your character, not to undermine them.

    (If you're wondering, the reason why your average story game doesn't want the players to throw their own characters under the buss is that the game derives most of its drama and tension from the struggles of the protagonist characters, and these struggles are largely expressed by the players while they advocate for their characters. If a player treats their own character as a worthless pawn, the game generally gets colder and the story less interesting; players should like their own characters and have empathy for their travails, so other players would as well, and that way everybody would be engaged by the developing story.)

    \n Huh, doing character motivated scene framing really is hardcore immersionism, I have to say. Generally speaking (can't claim that every game works like this) it's very much not what GMless story games try to do, though; even the ones that ask the players to advocate strongly for their characters will usually assume that scene framing is done with the flow of the story in mind, rather than framing "what my character would want to happen".

    But anyway, yeah, my personal opinion on this is that most story games are intended and built with the assumption that "GM tasks" are conducted from a "pawn stance" of sorts, so as to facilitate a well-constructed story, while character play is conducted from an advocacy viewpoint: your task is to express your character. The overall goal is to create meaningful stories, often with special emphasis on empathizing with the protagonist.

    (An arbitrary example of a game with this structure would be Apocalypse World: the GM is expected to make their choices with dramatic coordination in mind, so as to give the player characters maximum opportunities to engage with the setting and each other in various genre-appropriate situations. Meanwhile, the players are relieve of any and all dramatic requirements except that of running a coherent, interesting dramatic protagonist. One could say that the GM is playing with a pawn-ish relationship to the characters he plays, while the players are advocates for their characters. By default the game does not assume anybody to immerse.)

    So I guess that, regarding your discussion with your gaming group, I disagree with both sides: I don't think that character immersion per se is what these kinds of games generally intend or expect, but I don't think that they expect you to disregard the interests of your own character either. What they mostly expect is that you frame scenes and do other GMing tasks to facilitate character showcasing, and that you play your character in a way that is compelling to the audience (which consists of the entire group, of course).

    What I expect from these particular foibles (I have some experience with this kind of game, played with different people) is that pawning out (ignoring what your own character wants) leads to artificial and cold storytelling, as the players do not engage with the supposed protagonist; on the other hand, immersion play generally prefers a slower and more passive kind of game participation, which tends to make immersive players less compatible with story games. I can handle both at the gaming table, and they're not nearly the worst sorts of player preferences to have to deal with, but both are slightly problematic if the player is inflexible and unwilling to switch modes where necessary.

    The most well-matched player for most story games in my experience is one who is happy to treat the game basically as a show wrestling match: your character is the wrestler, you personally think that they're awesome, and your task is to "put him over" with the audience by being noisy and interesting and cohesive with your gimmicks and the character's story. The character is put into all sorts of dramatic difficulties and struggles during the game for the same reason that wrestlers wrestle, so that the audience gets to vicariously experience those difficulties, triumphs and defeats. A player who understands this will naturally have an advocatory relationship with their character: they show the character strongly to the other players, but they are also fine with waiting for their turn, and they don't mind their character taking a fall if that's the way the game goes - fronting for the character is a task, in service to an engine of drama, the players don't ultimately identify with their characters any more than a manager identifies with a wrestler.

    But of course that's just the most general of generalities; not only are particular games different from each other, but many games can also be successfully adapted for different sorts of play. I don't doubt it if you get good results with character immersion in story games, even if that's not quite what most of them are trying to do. I've myself played highly successful sessions with players who strongly prefer to stick to the character perspective in all of their game interactions.

    \n Once upon a time we used to talk of "flow experience" as an alternate terminology to "immersion". It's useful in that while "immersion" is usually understood to be specifically about a deep adoption of a fictional person, "flow" can be anything you focus into so deeply that it becomes effortless and dominates your entire attention. So "immersion" basically one possible type of flow experience, but there are others as well. Many immersion theorists of the old school would say that e.g. Chess cannot have immersion, while it evidently and obviously may have flow experiences for a focused player.

    This particular concept became useful in discussions of immersion, as people started arguing about whether you can only immerse in a character - some people reported immersive experiences in setting or even the mechanical process of play, for example. This was sort of a big deal in the traditional Nordic theoretical framework, which tended to privilege immersion quite heavily as a goal and purpose of roleplaying; if you happened to be one of the gamers who wasn't really into deep character identification, co-opting the concept of immersion was important for being able to say that your play also had value - you were also immersed in whatever it was you were doing, it wasn't just shallow posing void of emotional depth.

    It used to be part of the standard Forgite theoretical wisdom that "immersion" is just a different name for "I really viscerally like this thing that I'm doing", and therefore not nearly as useful as a theoretical concept as Nordic theory held at the time. As with all things, some people agreed, while others disagreed. The subjective nature of the phenomenon makes it difficult to observe immersion and make theoretical conclusions about it.

    Anyway, that came to mind because of how Jeff characterized his excitement at playing a story game as "immersion". As fine a term as any, of course, as long as we aren't terribly committed to figuring out the fine details of the psychology of immersion and whether self-reported immersion experiences really are immersion and so on.

    \n Mongrel said:
    "I strongly agree with that. And that was the crux of the disagreement with my gaming group: advocate first for your PC or advocate first for the story."

    You should always play towards what your character wants. But that is different than advocating for them to "win" at the cost of the story. When I say that the story is most important this includes advocating for your characters wants; this is what drives the conflict and tension and "pawning out" would be horrible for the story. However, if you're advocating for your character at the cost of the story overall, I would say you're not doing it right. If you put the story first, you will typically be playing your character in the right way because doing so is the necessity for the story to be good. However, as I said above, there can be certain times when sacrificing your character for the good of the story is very satisfying, but generally this type of play should be rare and should be implemented after advocating for your player during most of the game and having a good reason to sacrifice your character at a moment of climax in the later stages of the game (and if it makes the story really, really good). I personally think that people advocate for their characters naturally, and that by stating that the story comes first in a GMless collaborative Story game, it helps to make players share the story, and build upon each other's ideas. Many people are accustomed to playing RPGs to "win" and it's very hard for them to make that perspective shift. Basically, you should both advocate for your character and the story, but people more often forget to advocate for the story, so that is why I use the mantra "story first." Also, in Penelope's Loom telling a good, cohesive story is the main aim of the game. Again, this doesn't mean that you shouldn't advocate for your player, but you also shouldn't make "winning" your main goal.

    Basically, you should both advocate for your character and the story, but people more often forget to advocate for the story, so that is why I use the mantra "story first."

    \nFor what it's worth, I think that's a totally understandable pedagogical choice to make. You can read the first paragraph or two of the Zombie Cinema rules to see what I thought was the pedagogically most needful thing to emphasize about the nature of the game. ZC is precisely a vanilla Forgite drama game, as "story game" as they come, so I was trying to encapsulate this exact conversation in those sentences.

    It's an intricate tangle, and I've seen designers I respect immensely come down on different sides of the issue. A certain sort of story game - the forgite-style narrativist game - can be constructed to accomodate either character advocacy or looser story priorities, and most of them have quite some flexibility in this regard.

    Although I've seen how people forget to respect the story, just as you say, I've also seen the opposite often enough. Adverse phenomena like storyboarding (attempting to pre-plot the game's scenes before they are played) and player collusion (sacrificing genuine dramatic concerns for the sake of perceived group unity) can result.

    Much of the pedagogical history of narrativist drama games at the Forge was characterized by trying to teach people these two things at the same time. If you're interested, reading Ron Edwards's narrativism essay might prove enlightening: his theory on the nature of drama - adopted from the playwright Lajos Egri - is basically that empathetic character interest is equivalent to good drama, and therefore your roleplaying should strive to reach a good story through uncompromising character play. This never meant bringing your character's concerns into out-of-character decision-making, and it never meant "wanting to win", but it does mean that, insofar as Ron is correct about the nature of drama, there should never be a genuine reason for your character to act out of character just to "save the story". The story is, according to Egri and Ron, an account of what this protagonistic person does, so anything that forces them to act out of character can only weaken the story.

    Of course this sort of drama theory only works if the game accomodates it: player characters need to actually, factually be protagonists of the story in the dramatic sense, and the game needs to pace and coordinate itself in some sensible manner so those characters actually get into position to make story-relevant decisions during play. Still, those sorts of requirements haven't proven so hard to achieve now that we know what to look for in game design, as can be seen by the great number of games that work precisely like this.

    \n One way to throw a rope over the divide is to ask the currently-active character - using the Character's name, not the Player's - "What do you want to see/have happen?" ...and use their answer as the basis for the resolution of an action attempt.     I don't Player much, I'm usually the GM, but this is a position I try run my games from; I do this in order to escalate/maintain/protect the Players' "Immersion Stance" as much as possible.

    BTW, I often find that, when running a group (as opposed to a single Player), typically one Player at the table tends to be the "primary immerser" at any given point in time, while the others fall into more ancillary or "Advocacy" roles. I feel this is because the group as a whole is conscious not so much of the relative levels of immersion, but rather of the ascendancy of a particular desire to the current gestalt, the narrative center of the story at the moment.

    From that premise, it doesn't matter that a player plays their PC to "win" their goal if the goal is self-defeating. Things are probably going to end badly for that PC, he/she is not going to "beat" the other PCs.

    It also follows that for the fellow players at a table it could be indistinguishable which play style the tragic PC's player uses. 1st person immersive, and trying to reach their unwise goal? Advocating and thinking more in 3rd person terms to direct the PC towards a tragic end?

    It may all come down to player skill. If the tragedy contributes to the group story, the fellow players may never know what the process/style was in the head of the tragic PC's player.

    So to answer your question, yes! It is about my mental process as I engage in 1st person immersion. The more I am able to engage in dialogue, interact with other PCs, get the cadence and style of speech down until it feels natural (not usually 2nd nature in one-shots alas)--as I get to that point, the PC starts feeling like a real person. I don't have to ask as frequently "what would my PC do next?" because I just kind of know what I'll do next. And if I don't know, it's because my PC is troubled/confused and he/she doesn't know what to do next. For me it's a great source of verisimilitude.

    I don't know if we need to distinguish between the two styles if we already know what works best for each of us personally. I guess it is something that newer gamers might ask about. I guess I would teach by going through examples of approaching game play with either style.

    But really it's so "inside one's head" that as far as day-to-day game play goes, only each player will distinguish in their own head what they are doing. If the results are good, the other players at the table reap the rewards.
