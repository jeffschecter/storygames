    Having considered this for a bit, I think my issue is this: as a reader, I'm quite capable of taking a point you make in a paragraph and connecting it to earlier ideas; the signal of language is linear, but the comprehension of it is not. However, this kind of re-arrangement (when what you're describing isn't actually a flow, but merely a web of ideas, like pretty much any text) kind of limits my ability to make connections to the ones you've provided.

    \n

    I'm not sure how much of this is my particular patterns of thought, of course. I teach, and so I have to connect disparate ideas and tolerate\u2014and generate\u2014tangents for and from my students. So I'm used to taking a linear signal and making a web of things in my head. I suspect every speaker is, but I don't know that.

    \n I like it.

    The first (uncoded) one is fun (in a gamey kind of way), but I don't think it is fun enough to support being the chosen format for a blog. The second (color coded) one is a lot better, since it is more obvious what the main points in the conversation are (and thus the tangents are fun extras instead of reading hindrances). If I were you, though, I'd use colored boxes (such as "main conversation boxes are red, second one are blue, tangents are gray, highlighed conclusions and stuff are yellow" or something like that) instead of the colored thingies.

    When I write stuff for my own blog, I tend to include hiperlinks all the time, in exactly that frame of mind. ("This is what I want to say. This other stuff is related and interesting, but they are tangents. So if you want to learn about them, follow the link. Else, keep reading and ignore them. Optional stuff they are.")

    Of course, that comes from a guy who spends hours surfing through wikipedia articles and tangents for fun.
