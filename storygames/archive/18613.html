\n Hi to all. I've been away from this board for something like three years (time goes so fast), but I'm back now and I need your help.
First of all, I'd like to put things into context (you'll see, I think it is important) and I have a couple of questions.
You might (or mostly not) remember me with my project, Avalanche, a multi-plots calendar based campaign.
So, what had happened ?

Fall 2010 : finishing it by myself
For your information, Avalanche is not only a campaign, it is also a tool : a web based engine made to manage plots (content as adventures, not system like rolling dice or character creation). In fall 2010, the campaign was almost finished, but I couldn't find any programmer to help with the website. In my early days, I had been a developer (I was then a project manager), so I decided to take the job myself. That was a lot, but a lot of job. It took me around three months to develop the "engine". I was then working on the "main website" : the website that would present the product, that is there to sell. I was almost done and Avalanche would finally see the light of day and get "out there".
And then, by that time, I quit my job as a project manager.

Winter 2011 : the harsh truth
I didn't know exactly for what kind of job I was looking for, and, by chance, someone offered me to get into a partnership and have my own business, a "web agency". I was really happy about this, but I also thought that would be great for Avalanche. And then, I discovered the harsh truth, having access to professional skills of marketing and ergonomics :
- the "user interface" of my engine was crap.
- the design was non functional.
- the "main website" was just plain terrible.

Avalanche could be good as a game, a product, a campaign ... but it didn't really matter : no one would be able to use my interface without massive help, and I just didn't have the skills to sell it.
But still, the good news : I now had access to professionals !!!

Winter 2011 - Spring 2013 : developing the web agency
Starting and building a company is not an easy thing. We kept Avalanche in mind, but that wasn't our priority : we were developing the web agency. Starting as two free lancers, we are now, two years later, almost five employees and we have our own office, in Montreal. What services do we offer ? Here's an idea :
- web marketing (adwords, SEO, PPC) and strategy (which channel to use, for instance Youtube/newsletter/Facebook)
- production of content
- optimization of websites (UX tests, A/B testing)
- conception of websites (business architecture and analysis, human-machine interface, design)
- we also work in mobile environment
- game design and game layer (yes, we develop mobile games for instance)

I guess that's the kind of stuff I need for Avalanche. And that's what we're going to do.
Important note : a web agency works in what we call a B2B environment (business to business). We don't have customers ourselves ; we work with companies who have customers themselves. We see Avalanche as our first B2C (business to customers) product.

Summer 2013 - let's "kickstart" it
Avalanche, cycles-rpg, whatever ... it is now branded under "ExiStanc3". Also, we have a logo now. And, we've moved from "trying to sell it" to "trying to raise money" to produce it. That said, it is not really about the money. KickStarter offers in fact, a lot more than that (I guess that is the purpose of another discussion).
I've been working a lot lately around KickStarter and we've come with some strategies.

Important note : this site, and its members (you), are not my direct customers. I guess I would define this site as a "media" (you need medias in your kickstarter strategy) for the rpg aspect of my campaign. Sites where my customers might be (say ENWorld) will be address later. And just a final note : I truly believe, in the context of a kickstarter campaign that we have to appeal beyond the sole "role players".

Questions
In three years, lots had also happen in the indie publisher community ; the forge has ended, for instance, KickStarter is there.
I intend to publish an article on this site about our early steps in our kickstarter campaign : we developed a landing page and decided to drive traffic and manage our community on Facebook. So, I'm going to explain you why we did it and how. I hope this will interest you.
Otherwise :
- is this site still a good place to talk about those matters ?
- where is the community so far about kickstarter ? I've seen Luke Crane doing it and also some other projects, but I'm more talking about "collective intelligence".
- what kind of exposure/interest do kickstarter campaign about rpg projects do get in the community ?
- are there other communities (i.e. sites) I should be addressing that would be defined as media ?
- are there some resources out there that would give a list of non-media site I should be targeting ?
- what was the overall impact of the closure of the forge ?

Important note : my concerns are not about publishing but about marketing.

Direct promotion
Here's my landing page : www.existanc3.com
Here's my Facebook page : Existanc3 on Facebook

We're starting this very slow. We gladly appreciate any help. Thanks for your time.

    \n I've supported a few RPG crowdfunding projects, although I've never run one, so I don't know how helpful these remarks will be.

    Just from being a long-time lurker and sometimes-poster to these forums, it seems to me that there are some folks around here who are extremely savvy when it comes to crowdfunding.

    There have been some extremely successful campaigns, and some terrific flops. In general, the trend seems to be:
    + The more you tell people about your game, before and during the campaign, the more likely they are to contribute. (For example, I think your assumptions regarding what product info the customer needs to see are on the right track.)
    + The more detail you can provide regarding your budget, your schedule, and other nitty-gritty details, the more likely they are to contribute.

    Don't underestimate that last point. Crowdfunders are both customers, and venture capitalists, both at the same time. So they need to know all about your business model and your delivery plan.

    If you have a cool idea that's worth getting excited about, and you can prove you won't disappoint, people will get excited!

    As for other places to promote, you might consider the RPGNet forums. And Google+ communities seem to be pretty active.

    Hope this helps!

    \n I feel like I'm new enough around here that I often don't feel comfortable answering questions along the lines of "what are things like around here?" But just to echo Adam on one very important point:\n
    I think you're smart to really map out how to approach a Kickstarter. A bunch of people have written helpful guides to successfully Kickstarting a project. Find them and grok them. Realize that too much success can bankrupt you, if you haven't planned for it. Consider shipping costs up front (and international rates are much higher now). Map out your stretch goals in advance so you can react to the pace of your Kickstarter.
    \nThis is very important. A simple place to start might be to check out Fred Hicks's Google+ feed, as I've noticed he's been posting some helpful links and commentary related to Kickstarter since the FATE Core/Accelerated crowdfunding campaign. Issues of scaling your production are especially important; I've read some people suggest that your stretch goals should always be to improve production values rather than adding additional products, or even that you should put a hard cap on the numbers of physical products you sell, as you can jeopardize your entire campaign if your manufacturers/printers can't keep up with an order ten times the size of the one you first proposed to them. (And don't forget that international shipping costs are monstrous.)
