\n Let's say you're super excited about a kick-ass game that's set in a time and place it would be unreasonable to expect anyone to know much about, like, I dunno, Hanover in 1724. How much information do you want about the time, place, and people to be included in the game? You have four options - please choose the one closest to your broad preference!

1. Nothing. I have Wikipedia, fool.
2. Just what can be conveyed by system - for example, the trait "Feral child adopted by King George of England" on a pre-made character.
3. A paragraph about likely topics of interest. Maybe a list of names or something.
4. A whole lot - like #3, but assuming many topics, with a bibliography and cool lists of stuff and period handouts.

Just curious.

    \n 4, for sure. But the kind of stuff I want is less political history or even typical social history, but instead stuff that directly impacts play: how do people think about the world, at this time? What are they excited about, what are they afraid of? What kind of tech to they have, and what's still sci-fi to them? How far do they travel, how fast do they communicate? If they're in trouble, can they expect the police to help them? How does playing in this time differ from a game set in modern times? What are the important and interesting ways in which the reality of this time period differs from the vague fantasy of it I've gotten from TV and half-remembered high school classes?

    \n If I'm going to be playing, I want #3; give me a short (~1 page, 2 at the absolute most) cheat-sheet that just tells me the things I absolutely positively have to know in order to really get the setting. Too little information = I don't know what I'm doing. Too much information = why the fuck are you telling me so many useless things?

    If I'm going to be running the game, I want #4, so that I can use it to steer my own research (assuming I feel like doing any), and then pick and choose what I'm going to put into the #3-style cheat-sheet that I'm going to type up and hand out to the players.


    ...actually, I should probably expand on that first answer a bit. I want my #3 to be provided by the person who's going to be running the game, not by the person who wrote the game manual. The writer has no idea what kind of game we're going to be playing or what sorts of things we're going to be focusing on, and therefore cannot possibly come up with a useful 1-page summary of important information. So my desire from the game's writer would be to provide #4 -- as much information as possible -- but to spend some time thinking about how a GM is going to build #3 from that section, and maybe give some helpful pointers in that direction.

    \n I don't want history. I want atmosphere. That's why we're playing an historical setting, right? We don't want realistic depictions, we want the mood and aesthetic evoked (partly by our stereotypes and preconceptions) to explore.
    Dogs in the Vineyard is the work par excellence for this for, while the setting is largely fictional, a list of, for example, what establishments would be part of a town as compared to a village describes all the play-group needs to construct a solid sense of place.
    So, 3 is my selection. But it's gotta be the best damn list you ever wrote.

    If I was writing a game about WWI trench warfare I might include:
    1. Soldier's inventory.
    2. Soldier's diet (/Officer's diet)
    3. Soldier's routine (weekly line-rotations and watch-changes).
    (4. DM section including a list of historical missions/orders)
    Bam. Done.

    \n What I don't want: In the only game of 1920's Cthulhu I ever ran, I got totally knocked out of the story by the question "Are there paper towels in 1929?" I said there were and the PCs cleaned up the mess, but the damage to my flow was done.

    It turns out that the Keeper's Compendium even uses that very thing as an example of a question that will come up that you shouldn't let affect your game. But I couldn't help it! It totally tripped me up. Every game after that was set in the present (or in a cyberpunk future) to avoid the knotty paper-towel-existence problem.

    So if you write a historical adventure where there's a possibility that the PCs will need to clean up a crime scene in 1929, please let us know whether we'll be using bleach and paper towels, or lye-soap and rags, or stone knives and bearskins, or whatever. Thanks! Period cleaning-product ads would be a superb piece of background art to throw into the layout.

    (For the curious: no.)

    \n Somewhere between 3 and 4. More than a paragraph, less than an encyclopedia. I want to know who the major NPCs are, both in the particular adventure (if applicable) and in the parts of the setting the game is likely to take place in or branch out to. I want to know what kinds of skills and character abilities are likely to be present. I want to know if there's any oddities (time travelers? ninjas?). Essentially, let me know anything that I might need to run the game and that the average person, or at least the average member of the target audience, is unlikely to know. If it's historical or fairly generic, let me know where the details of the setting differ from generic or known history, at least enough to avoid assumption clash. If it's SFnal, I need a bit more, though not huge amounts more; a small to medium-sized book should be sufficient. Think Star Frontiers Alpha Dawn plus Knight Hawks.

    It's also going to depend on how long the game is likely to last. If it's a one shot, #2 is likely to be enough. Not more than #3. if it's intended to be a full setting in which to play long running campaign games, then give me 4.
