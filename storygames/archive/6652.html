\n
\n So Saturday, lessee, I produced an impromptu PrimeTime Adventures game, "Beth's Undergrund," a sort of Cheers meets Addams Family, though that scarcely does it justice. We had Gran'pa John (Willem), the 1,000 year old crusty (both literally and figuratively) ghoul, the fixture of the establishment, the "soul of the bar" (yes, that was one of his edges). He's obsessed with bringing down civilization but more than that, really, he's the ever-grumbling but underneath-it-all (in that ghoul way) sweet old guy who provides mentoring and guidance to all those young ghouls who "just don't get it."
\n
\nWe had our celebrity drop-in of the week (Rebecca, she forewarned us she had to leave before the game ended, though thankfully she was able to be in 90% of it), playing the upwardly-mobility-obsessed ("chasing a dream that can never be true," as my notes recorded it) Gwenda, the witch bartendress serving all sorts of concoctions (literally, name your poison), including many people don't ask for.
\n
\nChris playing Carrie Darklight, a very close-to-normal if extremely naive/rather clueless but sweet heart of gold 19 year old college sophomore occultist-wannabe whose finally "found people who understand her" (yes, this cast of ghouls, witches, and such!), a brand new waitress.
\n
\nJackson played Derek Batfeeder, an impulsive young musician who made a hasty deal with the devil to be a "darkwave virtuoso," without thinking through there's such as virtualy no fans of the genre; his deal has turned him into a young ghoul and now he's coping with the biggest mistake of his life and how it haunts him, a depressed (but in that goth I-like-it way) barfly at Beth's Underground.
\n
\nLast but not least, Jennifer played Zoe Martin, a fresh thrall to a vampire, a woman who thought she'd be turned into a vampire of course but instead was turned into a ghoul and, really worse than that, the ever-put-upon slave for all of her lord Victor's petty requests (such as getting his dry cleaning); she's trying to get over her disappointments after trying for the dreams of the glamorous vampire life as well as deal with her love-hate relationship with her dark (and very petty) lord (who does have great regard for her but buries it under a veil of vampiric imperiousness).
\n
\nWillem's play really did form a nice anchor for the show, for the other players, and I won't forget the hilarious scene where he teaches Jackson's Derek about how to make like you're hit by a car backing out of a garage so as to fool the driver into stopping (if not getting out) and thereby being eaten. Everyone really contributed the right elements for the show to move along and to have the right character balance; there were some nice family scenes, especially the scene where Carrie bonds with Gran'pa in the cemetary (Carrie's personal set), and Gwenda, smoking nearby (her on smoke break was her personal set), walking over to quietly hand Carrie a book of REAL magic potions and also providing a nice bonding moment as she gives a sentence (sadly, I didn't get it down exactly) to the young Carrie showing her interest in helping the young woman on the path to real magic.
\n
\nI think one of my favorite running gags in the show centered around Zoe's relationship with Victor's ashes (he got staked but his demonic soul continued to command her from hell! don't worry, he got better...), there were lots of jokes by all and well-played moments by Jennifer in how Zoe "dabbled" with his ashes, including some ash-snifffing (nothing like the erotica of snorting a few grains of the remains of your vampire lord!).
\n
\nI'm sorry Rebecca had to go, but the climax was a great moment of Seinfeld-like convergence as Gran'pa John's and Derek's pursuit of human prey collided with Zoe's hunt, aided by a not-really-getting-this Carrie's assistance, for the right mortal coil to restore Victor into.
\n
\nOn the other hand, Carl mentioned in passing about the high individual card getting narration although whoever gets the most reds succeeds, and somehow I just spaced that when we played, so duh moment for me, and players, sorry about that! But we were all pretty cooperative, as I recall, in narration, so I don't think this "mattered" TOO much at least in how we played it (there was a lot of "here's an idea" and "does that sound good?"), but I .do want to mention/admit this to the players as well as remind myself, and surely this had some impact in that there were successes I had that I narrated that someone else should have (though my side of course still had to have been successful). But it was equal at least in that we were consistent. Still, sorry, folks.
\n

    \n One of the highlights of the session for me was when VIctor had fired the entire staff and replaced them with his poor beleaguered retainer,
    and while she scampered about inside, the rest of the cast sat morosely across the street, watching the door. After a moment, in a move Wilson described as "barely getting past the sponsors and causing a nightmare for the legal department", willem's foe (whom he created), a Lummi Shaman and Gwenda, the bartender, combined forces to open the magically barred door.

    The shaman threw some teeth at the door, a black cat ran by and was somehow immolated. And Gwenda called up her voodoo teacher, a drag queen from new orelans, to cast a spell over her cellphone. i was totally floored, this is the kind of stuff hits are made of. half of the audience turned off the set immediately and the other half told everyone they knew and assured the five-year contract. and this was rebecca's first game, minus a "bad experience" d&d game from years ago. amazing!

    no worries for the rules hiccup, wilson. i thought it went down as smooth as the love potion gwenda gave to victor to have her fall in love with zoe but misfired and caused him to fall for derek. ha!
