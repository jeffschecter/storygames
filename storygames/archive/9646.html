    \n The question is what you're doing with your exploration (aka 'roleplaying'). If the activity is undertaken for its own sake, that's S. If the activity is used as a form of social 'competition', that's gamism. And if it's used to exercise moral/ethical/anthropological judgment, it's N. The idea that roleplayers do all of these things, and that system can help facilitate one or more of them, ought to be uncontroversial, and does not depend on the idea of conflict. What is more controversial is whether rulesets and/or sustained instances of gaming ('campaigns') that facilitate one of these three over the other two are more fun to play. In particular, the issue of 'exploration of character' discussed in the other thread shows that disentangling S and N can be very difficult in practice, and the kind of play that Ron has sometimes castigated as "Ouija Board Narrativism" in particular may be more functional in some instances than his essay acknowledges.

    \n I don't consider it a huge deal when people have definitions of the CA families that don't match the Big Model, because as I might not have made clear enough, I consider CA to be pretty compromised as a thing. That said, I think we make more progress when we have a problem with what it is saying than when we have a problem with something it's not saying.

    Roleplayers engage in exploration in different ways; as I said, that is uncontroversial in the extreme. CA isn't about how people do exploration; it's about which conceptions of what-gaming-even-is lead to games that break up, or might as well break up. That's the only definition of Creative Agenda that's ever been coherent (in a non-jargony sense) enough to engage with.

    Definitions of G, N, and S are a different deal. *AHEM* According to the Big Model:\n
      \n
    • Narrativist play is play in which all players are empowered to address premise - where "premise" is defined per Lajos Egri as a particular form of ideological question - as a part of their in-game actions. That's not tied specifically to "moral/ethical/anthropological judgment," although it will more than likely include some.
      • \n
    • Gamist play is play in which challenge is socially paramount - challenge isn't exactly the same as competition (challenge is one mode of competition, essentially the non-sneaky mode).
      • \n
    • Simulationist play is... well, people agree on this one the least often, but it certainly isn't defined precisely by exploration-for-its-own-sake. (I happen to be partial at the moment to Vincent's definition of "peer-appreciated wish fulfillment," but to get the full nuance of that it really helps to read those recent Right-To-Dream threads at lumpley.com. Oh god, now I'm shunting people off to read some thread on another site, like a proper Forge Twunt.)
      • \n
    \nI don't want to be the resident pissy pedant - really! - and maybe I'm just indulging my Internet twitchiness in a non-admirable way right now, but this is as succinctly as I know how to put them.

    And truthfully, yeah, this stuff is less important than the latter question of Sean's, of whether it is really more effective to target a design at just one of the (alleged) CA families.

    \n Look, here's one more thing. Exploration is what you do when you roleplay, it's imagining stuff. It takes place within a certain real-world social environment.
    \n
    \nWhere is that exploration leading? Exploration itself is fun, for a lot of people. (Myst. Sim City. 'Secrets' in Tekumel and Glorantha, or just going off the edge of the hex map and finding out what's there.) So there's no craziness about exporation feeding back into exploration, at all, RPing itself wouldn't work as a hobby if there wasn't some enjoyment in this.
    \n
    \nPeople who want to focus on that enjoyment and not get distracted with G or N are focusing on S. It's a little like, you can have ice cream with fruit, or you can have ice cream with chocolate syrup, or you can just have ice cream. We're the group who doesn't want any toppings on our ice cream, thank you. We're S-focused.
    \n
    \nCall of Cthulhu, historically speaking, was the seminal game for isolating G. That game communicated an approach in which going for G in the usual way was completely frustrated by the ruleset. So this separated the G from the anti-G, out of the incoherence which constituted many higher-concept D&D;, Runequest, etc. campaigns at the time. (If your little part of the RPG world in 1980 was different from my little part, fine. This was how we perceived it and we weren't alone. Maybe there's a different example that works for you?)

(The idea that every player has to be empowered to do this individually for it to be N is wrong - that's an important principle of one kind of functional N design favored by some of the Forge designers in the time of the Big Model's construction, but it's not the only way for an RPG group to 'address premise' (another barf word for me).)     1.) The episode's first example was a poor choice IMO, and very out-of-scope with the discussion that followed it. That Gankagok game clearly failed because of issues with the procedures. Most of it was you explaining to the players how things are "supposed" to go, and then lots of awkward fumbling about trying to drive conflict without a GM. The issues were all technical - I mean, seriously, when you get to the point where you have to ask the question "do you want to roleplay some more or roll dice now" (I know, it uses cards), you're far away from any problems that have to do with creative agenda.

    So it seemed ill-conceived to me to start talking about GNS after that, and my sense of disconnection moving from the example was strong.

    2.) The second example was good, showing a pretty clear investment in Gamist priorities, but the discussion about Muy Macho and "fiction-based" Gamism unnecessarily muddied up the waters, because the example afterward didn't actually reflect what was described. There's no indication that the fiction in the third snippet was being used as an arena of competition/challenge at all, or that status among the players was being determined by who was putting the "best" fictional content forward, or that anyone even cared about any of that.

    In fact, what *was* evident to me was a fairly consistent firing off of Narrativist payoffs - the "ooohs" and "ahhhs" surrounding the murderous action and its consequences, indicating a pretty active sense of moral judgment among the players. The decision seemed to land as something that had visceral, human meaning to the participants, and their reactions showed it. No muddied waters there, no maybe-Gamism, just straight-up Nar.

    3.) The fourth example pretty much continued the trend of Narrativist priority and emphasis - I read it as pretty clear in this one that the players are rendering judgment on their characters and that there's more going on than just reacting to an expectation of "correct" output or trying to "win" in some way or another.

    Anna's reactions specifically speak to this - her moral judgment as a player is all over that snippet... "Of course, douchewaffle is going to get a happy ending..." etc etc., and the passion with which she was willing to pursue murder herself at the end. I felt that as far as she was concerned, that dude *deserved* to die.

    Now, a question: did Anna not have a good time at the game because of her emotional response? It seemed to me from the snippet that she was very invested in the game and fully engaged with it. I've read books where what happens frustrates me or makes me angry, and I generally find those to be extremely good books because of the level of engagement they provide.

    4.) The Trail example doesn't really give any clear indication of what game content the players are invested in, and so can't really be used for a discussion about creative agenda at that table. It's clear that the choice to take the Stability damage got a reaction, but it's not clear why. You'd need to look at a longer example.

    ***

    Re: this thread...

    First of all, talking about creative agenda outside a group context is pretty much useless. By definition, individuals don't "play Sim" or "play Gamist", just like there's no actual such thing as a "Narrativist game design". The shorthand exists because it gets tiresome to write "plays in a way that might be highly endorsed in a Gamist environment" or "is ideal for supporting a Narrativist agenda at the table" over and over again. Historically, taking that shorthand literally has muddied up the discussion a lot more than I'd like.

    So, if you pull off the awezume combo of phat moves and wipe out the big orc leader, and no one else cares, and no one else ever cares, Gamism is simply not happening at that table. The social emphasis and support has to be there as a trend, a pattern of behavior in the group. That's what CA clash is - other people failing to support and engage the stuff that turns you on, and you failing to support and engage the stuff that turns other people on.

    So asking something like, "Well, what about the fact that you can read a premise into my actions that I'm not trying to put forward?" is pretty much a red herring - what you intend or don't intend is immaterial. Better questions to ask are, "Does it matter to anyone when my character makes a momentous decision? Does it affect anyone on a human level, as players? Do people get emotional about those moments? Do we, as a group, tend to plug into that a lot? Do they care if I make a decision that's "wrong" for the character, or is that meaningful to them somehow?" Those are the kinds of questions that help lead you to identifying a shared agenda (or lack of shared agenda) in a particular group.

    That's why the Trail snippet from the podcast falls flat. There's really just not enough there for you to get a sense of what everyone's invested in and emphasizing socially. The roleplaying almost seems ancillary to the conflict - in a couple of places, the mechanical action was only retroactively justified by narration, and no one really reacted to it; it seemed just to be necessary to explain the rolling.

    Likewise, Anna's emotional state at the end of the fourth snippet doesn't really tell you anything about CA - she could say, "I was upset because I identified with my character" or "I was upset because the story didn't go the way I wanted" and this wouldn't be useful information one way or the other. What *would* be useful is looking at everyone's level of investment in that particular instance, trying to figure out what it was they were engaging with, and examining how they expressed it... and then, mapping that to behavioral trends in that group.

    That's how you tell. You look at what game content people are supporting when they react to stuff and are engaged, why they have those reactions, and (most importantly) what trends emerge in that support over time.

    Anyway, thanks for reading my lengthy ramble.

    \nI attended a really interesting lecture at Knutepunkt by Johanna Koljonen about how to leverage the fuzziness of the boundary between player and character (both in designing scenarios and characters for others to play and in getting ready to play your own character). "Filling the hollow man with stupid," was one of the principles I remember, illustrated by a story about non-German-speaking Finnish larpers playing Nazi soldiers whose limited German vocabularies--"Waffen haben. Cigaretten haben"--made it easier to play grunts in a WWII larp. But an underlying assumption of the lecture was exactly as Zac suggests: the challenge is to stay in character, to live in character, and it is that more than anything else that defines "success" or "fun." That seemed really different to me, but perhaps I'm just provincial. So call it Immersionism, or call it Simulationism oriented around Exploration of Character and instantiated in a particular suite of Techniques. The former course emphasizes the diversity of imaginative interests that people bring to play; the latter emphasizes how different orientations to the fiction and varying game-mechanical procedures can produce widely different experiences of play even when people are generally agreed on what the point of playing is in the first place.
