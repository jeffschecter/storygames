    \n 1. The thing you're missing is "fictional positioning," which is a core concept. You can only make moves when you have the positioning in the fiction of the game to do so. Like Johnstone says, if you have a magic dragon-killing sword, maybe you can just attack a dragon with a normal move. Otherwise, you're kinda fucked. When you say: "I do [move]" the GM is supposed to ask, "Okay, how do you do that?" which is partially to make sure that makes sense in the fiction. Sometimes you have to do a few things (get into position, obtain an item, sneak somewhere) before you can make the move you want, and sometimes that involves making other moves first (like Defy Danger in DW or Act Under Fire in AW).

    2. Practically it's because you only print out one of every playbook when you prep for the game. There's also niche protection stuff. But there are AW-based games where having multiple playbooks of the same time is totally fine, or even ones where there's only one playbook. The Regiment and World of Dungeons are both examples.

    3. If you want to do it the easy way, leave it to the players to order their hirelings around. Otherwise, they're less likely to risk their necks for no reason, and kinda skulk in the background. But you can make moves against them when you get to make a GM move. Or you can write impulses for them ("Braktar is arrogant and brash") and then, when there's a quiet moment in the game or the PCs are arguing amongst themselves, you look down at your hirelings and have them act on their impulses (Braktar does something arrogant and brash).

    4. NPCs don't use the same rules as PCs. They do whatever they do and aren't limited by spells-per-day or anything like that. They don't have levels anyway. They just do what it fictionally makes sense for them to do.

    \n Well, like John mentioned, in DW the fictional triggers have to be strictly met. Otherwise the game devolves into a D&D-esque papercut match, where you just hack-and-slash.     \nIt sounds like you're asking how to make an ogre "tougher" than a goblin, when the to-hit roll for each is the same. Is that right?

    If so, here's some concrete ways:

    It's tougher to get close to an ogre. Weapon ranges set up the fiction for Hack and Slash and the like, and the ogre's got reach on you (most likely) so good luck getting in there with your sword. It'll most likely be Defying Danger to get in close past its long limbs.

    An ogre's moves are way tougher. When you get a 7-9 to Hack and Slash a goblin and it gets to counter attack it'll probably stab you or maybe poison you. An ogre might fling you across the room and smash you into a wall.

    An ogre, being a big tough thing, will likely have more creatures supporting (or controlling) it. A goblin might have a few goblin friends to watch his back, but an ogre might have a pet worg, some enslaved orcs, and so on.

    The fictional description of each probably effects Hack and Slash too. Maybe you as the GM describe the ogre with thick skin like old leather, tough and stiff. That's probably a reasonable fictional basis for saying that hitting it with a stick isn't really Hack and Slashing. On the other hand a goblin's probably kind of squishy, so smacking it with a quarterstaff is an attack.

    And, on top of that, there's just the stats of each. One attack (through Hack and Slash, Volley, or just dealing damage because of the fiction) is fairly likely to kill a goblin. That's much less likely for an ogre, which means it has more time to do bad stuff to you.\n
    Can we feel free to ignore the *when you do it, you do it* when we feel it's appropriate? How would that break the game?
    \nI'm probably biased, but I'd say don't ignore it.

    What you should do (and this may be what you're getting at) is clarify the fiction. Sometimes swinging your weapon at something isn't Hack and Slash. There are examples in the book of it not being Hack and Slash because the creature you're attacking can't defend itself (so you jkust deal your damage) or because there's no chance of actually harming it (so the GM just describes the outcome).

    Once you start ignoring the fictional triggers the game gets a little loose and formless. The moves (player moves and GM moves) all reflect what the game is about in pretty core ways.

      \n In AW you ask questions and then use those answers to make the player's lives interesting.
      For example, in an AW game at PAX a couple of weeks ago, I started by asking each player small details about where they lived. At one point I realized, 'woah, everyone individually decided to live underground' so I asked them, "why do you all live underground?"
      there was a silence as people thought about it so I prompted again, "is it because of the screamers and the shadows?"
      "yeah, totally" they all agreed.

      One of the players was a soldier, left in stasis since before the apocalypse, so the screamers and shadows turned into unmanned drones, also from before the apocalypse.

      In DW I recommend this same approach, don't decide your werewolves are immune to normal weapons, don't even decide there are werewolves. Just follow the characters around in their normal lives, find out what they are afraid of, then turn those fears into monsters.

      Ask the warrior, why do you carry around a sword, what do you need to defend yourself from?
      Ask the thief, why did you teach yourself to hide in shadows, what are you hiding from?

      When you put the creative decisions in the hands of the players you get a level of buy in that is uniquely satisfying.
