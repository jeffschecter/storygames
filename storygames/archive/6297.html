\n At the dawn of the criticism era there was a pile-on with regards to In A Wicked Age being a half-written game. At the time this wasn't the central point of the arguments, but given a bit of time I'm still very happy with the text and my experiences with it have been very positive. Here's my take:

In A Wicked Age is not written like a textbook; it's written as a conversation. The text takes more than one read. At 32 half-legal pages, practically a pamphlet - if it's all done in one pass then that's one damned expensive pamphlet.

As a reader, if you approach it like it's a procedural document, or a textbook, or like other games you've got on your shelf, you're going to miss the beauty of the text. If you leaf through it, pick it over, and re-read it, then you'll see the conversation - and it's very tightly packed. The first read gets you into play; after that, the text (i.e. continued conversation) improves that play.

The major criticism I've seen leveled is that you only can go halfway to the full game experience with a straight, single read through of the nook. If you do that, play just one session, and don't come back to the text, you'll end up disappointed. I don't think this is because the text is flawed, but because the ideal play for the game is very specific. Getting from any given starting point (indie, trad, neophyte) to that fine-tuned play isn't something you can really expect to do in a single session. But over multiple sessions, you go back to the conversation in the text and bam - there's dozens of things that help you refine your play. The mechanical procedures are only part of what the game is. As you return to it, the game tells you how to tighten up your conflicts, improve your scene framing, establish and vary your rhythm, and know how to spot resolution. Doing that right with In A Wicked Age's mechanics isn't something that's going to happen instantly - it will happen if you play, return to the text, and really absorb how it relates to what you did at the table. The text is an extended conversation with Vincent about both how to play In A Wicked Age, and how to play it extremely well.

If that means that instead of returning to the text, you went to the internet, that's OK, but you're missing out. For me, the text has supported that conversation better than the internet browsing did. If enough people play the game and post about it, that might reverse, but again, the text gets you into play, and then helps you improve that play.

    \n Ryan, I completely agree. Unlike a lot of people, I didn't have much trouble figuring out how the game was supposed to be played from reading the text -- and I like the way the text is presented, as well. The 'author voice' that Vincent used for IaWA lends focus to the flavor of the game. It's very different from the 'chatty' style he used for Dogs in the Vineyard, and I think that may have thrown off some of his fans. The writing in IaWA is very spare, very lean. In Dogs, Vincent tells you how to do something, then tells you why you should. In IaWA, he just tells you how. The 'designers notes' aren't there in the running text -- they aren't there at all. It may or may not be intentional on his part, but I think that style lends the text some of the atmosphere of the less-introspective, more visceral-action 'wicked age': Characters in a wicked age don't examine their motivations, they act on their drives and desires! That style of writing also cuts down on the word count, page count, and production costs, which I believe were conscious considerations by Vincent for this project.

    ...

    That having been said, I did have some misunderstandings about the mechanics, where things were not explained clearly enough. In order to create that spare, focused textual style, Vincent leaves some things unsaid, and sometimes I jumped to different conclusions than the ones I was supposed to jump to. That's where the Internet helped out a lot: Here in various threads, and on the Lumpley forum at the Forge, Vincent answered my questions and got my though processes lined up with his intentions.

    Some examples:
    I initially thought that returning NPCs carried over their damage the way PCs do. They don't: the GM alwasys assigns their full dice at the start of any chapter when they appear.
    I initially thought that a character could use more than one Particular Strength for a roll. They cannot -- and there's a good design reason for that, once Vincent explained it.
    I initially though that it was possible to strike off more than one appearance on the Owe List for advantage dice for one conflict. It isn't -- for much the same reason.
    I didn't have any trouble with advantage dice in multi-person conflict, but many people did, all based on one phrase in the rules, where it says 'pass the advantage die'. That implied to many people that in multi-person conflicts, there is still only one advantage die to be gained from successes. Nope; everyone who gained advantage gets a die, and no one ever has a different total against one opponent than against another.
    There was also some confusion about what constituted 'having bigger dice' when there were three dice on one side and two on the other. Vincent provided the 'persnickety rule for bigger dice': When the biggest die of the two die set is bigger than the biggest die of the three die set, if the two die set 'surrounds' the three die set, (meaning that the biggest is bigger and the smallest is smaller), then the three die set is bigger. If the sizes 'overlap', then the two die set is bigger.

    In most of these cases, jumping to the wrong conclusion about what the rules meant won't break the game (at least not for a while), but it's always nice for me to know that I'm playing the game the way the author plays it himself. I've compiled a set of Vincent's quotes from the various Q & A threads, and keep a printout in the back of my rulebook. It's a quick-and-dirty FAQ for the game until an official one becomes available.

    \nIaWA FAQ (Collected by DainXB from The Forge and Story Games threads)

    Complete Set of Consequences
    Exhausted; reducing directly and with violence.
    Injured; reducing covertly and for others.
    Humiliated; reducing for myself and with love.

    For any given application of the Anthology Engine, select all that apply. I don't like humiliated for the Wicked Age, so didn't include it, but it's an official option in the larger ruleset.
    -- Vincent

    NPCs
    NPCs can recur if the Oracle brings them in again. In the rules, "The unwitting husband of a serpent-demoness" comes up and is grabbed by Mekha's player as describing Mekha in that chapter. If no player picks a particular suggested character role, the GM could do the same thing to an NPC in order to bring that NPC back. "The unwitting husband of a serpent-demoness is your former mentor from Chapter One, Tullius!"

    No NPC can ever have a particular strength of greater significance than 2, because NPCs do not go through the advancement process. At the beginning of the chapter, assign all NPCs their dice. Recurring, not recurring, it doesn't matter. They all get a fresh set, damage never carries over for them.
    -- Vincent (& others)


    Buying Advantage Dice in Round One
    But either way, and this rule is plain not in the book, buying an advantage die for the first round means you add its sides (6) to your biggest die's for deciding who's rolling bigger dice: d10+d6 d8 is WAY bigger than d12 d10 (although d6+d6 d4 isn't). For purposes of who goes on the owe list, d12 d10 +advantage counts as d18 d10.
    -- Vincent

    Particular Strengths Significance
    An increase in a particular strength's significance is specific to the character who increases it, and new characters starting with Chapter 3 can have either existing or newly-made particular strengths of significance 2. But occasionally you'll have a particular strength where, because of how it's defined and described, if it gets bumped up for one person it OBVIOUSLY gets bumped up for everyone who has it. In those cases, go with what's obvious. If my character has a PStr of "the dog-spirit guards my life", and someone else has the same PStr and it's noted as the same dog-spirit, then it should be upgraded for both (the dogspirit leveled), but otherwise no.
    -- Vincent (& others)

    Particular Strengths
    Q: Far Reaching, p.20: The example uses exorcism and spirits, so the core aspect of this Strength being something you could "not normally inflict" is about not normally "in the fiction" (e.g., in this fiction spirits are not normally affected by mundane actions) as opposed to not normally "in the rules"? It doesn't let you do something the rules say you can't do otherwise (e.g., come into a conflict the rules say you are out of).
    A: Yes. A nice, easy example is a network of spies and agents - it's far-reaching because you can act through it in places that you aren't. Notice that if a demon goes to possess someone, they both totally roll dice over it, but someone standing nearby doesn't. With exorcism, someone standing nearby does get to roll dice - just like with a network of spies and agents you get to roll dice in a fight on a city street even though your character is up in her palace.

    Q: Does defining a Far Reaching strength effectively mean also indirectly defining an aspect of the game reality. Without Exorcism defined as ability to counter possession, which you otherwise can't, it's not clear that anyone couldn't try to drive out a spirit. Defining something as Far Reaching is a way of saying, "in this game, you can only do that thing with a Strength that says so".
    A: I wouldn't recommend that you draw any attention to this in play. It just happens, no need to attend to it.

    Q: Possession itself as a form of attack by a spirit seems a candidate for a Strength, with the Far Reaching benefit since "taking control of another" in that way would usually be "beyond what you can normally inflict".
    A: The corresponding subtlety here is that, by not making it a particular strength, we've established that it's something that spirits can normally do. Again, I wouldn't pay any explicit attention to this in play, just go with it.
    -- Vincent

    \nRolling Dice In Conflicts
    Q: As I understand it once you've chosen forms for a conflict you are locked in. Does the same go for Particular Strengths? If I think of a way to bring in my P.Str halfway through a conflict can I roll it for the latter rounds?
    A: By default, you roll the same dice forward, with the winner of the last round adding an advantage die in this next round. If you want to do something else, you have to both agree to it - and either of you can end negotiation simply by demanding the default.
    If you're like, "hey, instead of rolling this advantage die, how about I bring in my exorcism?" I'll be like "sure," every time. Easy - no particular strength is as good as an advantage die. But if you're like, "hey, in addition to rolling this advantage die, how about I also bring in my exorcism?" I'll be like, "pff. No."
    Or maybe I'll be like, "okay, but I'm switching to directly with violence," and you'll be like, "sure," and so that's what we'll do.
    -- Vincent

    Advantage Dice
    Q: Is it correct that after the first exchange in an action sequence (when awarding of owe list is made), you can strike your name from owe list in that same action sequence to use in the 2rd and 3rd exchanges (if it goes that far)?
    A: Yes.

    Q: Suppose A is challenged in round 1 by B. A wins and and gains an advantage. Now C challenges A in the same round. Does A get his recently won advantage die to add to the roll, and if not, why?
    A: No. Roll the advantage die you won this round at the beginning of next round, not before. (I waffled a lot about this rule, but there it is.)

    Q: Suppose A wins the challenge from C and gains an advantage. In round 2, does A get two advantage dice, since he won the the advantage twice in the previous round?
    A: No. You can have only one advantage die from winning.

    Q: Can you strike multiple entries of your character's name from the We Owe list to give yourself multiple advantage dice for a roll?
    A: No, just one.

    Q: Advantage dice gained by using the We Owe list last until the end of the conflict, but what about those gained from winning the advantage?
    A: One round only. But, like, if you won the advantage again, you don't have to throw the die back in the bowl and fish it out again, you can just hang onto it.

    Q: Imagine a sword fight. In round 1, A gets an advantage over B, B gets no advantage, C gets an advantage over A. In round 2, does A count her advantage die against B, but not against C?
    A: When you roll an advantage die, add it to your highest die. It doesn't matter who you're comparing it with - A never has a different roll vs B than she does vs C. (In the sword fight, A and C both roll advantage dice in round 2. Imagine for instance that A is using her advantage over B to keep B between her and C. She doesn't have the advantage over C, but she can use her advantage over B to neutralize C's advantage over her.)
    -- Vincent (& others)

    Consequential Damage to NPC Forms
    Q: When a particular strength is consequential vs an NPC form, Do we lower the highest dice? Do we lower both dice? Does the victor chose which dice to lower?
    A: A consequential particular strength will reduce one die in that form. The book doesn't specify, but it'd be most consistent with the rest of the rules for the winner to choose which die.
    -- Vincent

    Persnickety Rule for Determining Bigger Dice
    For 3-dice vs 3-dice, compare high dice, 2nd dice break ties, 3rd dice break ties.
    Want persnickety 3-dice vs 2-dice rules for who's rolling bigger dice?
    This rule is only for when the biggest of the three is smaller than the biggest of the two. d12 d6 d4 is obviously bigger than d10 d8.
    If the two dice *surround* the three dice, the three dice are bigger. Examples: d12 d4 -vs- d10 d10 d6; d10 d6 -vs- d8 d8 d8. The three dice are bigger than the two dice.
    If the two dice and the three dice *overlap*, the two dice are bigger. Examples: d12 d10 -vs- d10 d10 d10; d10 d8 -vs- d10 d6 d4. Believe it or not, the two dice are bigger than the three dice.
    (Also, I'm not 100% that this rule is correct for every single possible case. However, when it's wrong, the two sets of dice will be for all practical purposes equal. Example: is d10 d8 really bigger dice than d10 d6 d6? Maybe it's not, I didn't check. However, either way, it's within a point or two of 50-50, so who cares.)
    -- Vincent

    Terminology
    I wish I'd called them action sequences, formally and explicitly. That'd be more clear. Oh well, I'll change it for the second printing.
    -- Vincent

Or the opposite problem. As an example it can only clarify the portion of the rule use that works for the situation in question. So, again, good as clarification if there's a question from the main rule. But doesn't tell you anything about the general case.
