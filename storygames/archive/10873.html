    \n by "their game", do you mean "the game you are publishing" or "the game you are playing"?

    Assuming by your examples that its game you are publishing, it really depends on the product.

    Ribbon Drive, my definitions of success were:
    -when I tell sexy, intelligent girls about it, they want to play
    -when I show sexy, intelligent girls the physical product, they want to play even more.
    -sharing mix CDs with sexy, intelligent girls.
    -to impress my friends and family.
    -to receive lots of compliments and popularity from designers and publishers that I admire.

    With other games, I've had other goals. These goals largely speak to where I am at in my life. The game content functions similarly.

    \n
    \n My measure of success is that the game fulfills the sales goals I set when planning the game's print run and marketing. So if it's just a free game I put up somewhere, it pretty much fulfills its goals automatically, and everything that comes of it is bonus. But if I print a thousand copies of a game, I want to sell those copies within some time-frame, which gives me a fine measure of the game's success.

    \n For us we initially defined success for Diaspora as, "sell as many copies to others as we bought for ourselves." This actually set the bar fairly high because we bought a bunch for ourselves. Once we cleared that, we set the bar at, "hear about positive play experiences from people we don't know." When that happened, we changed it to, "pays for our scotch bill during development."

    That last one is crazy. Never happen. It's important to not peak too early.

    \n For a game I have designed (in no particular order):

    1. If the players have fun
    2. If a six year old can read the rules and play and have fun (or a group of six year olds)
    3. If I can keep the game fairly simple

    That's it for now.

    For a game I play (same):

    1. If I have fun
    2. If there is a mechanic that simplifies game play
    3. If I want to play it multiple times

    Making money is second to FUN. If you aren't having fun, there is no reason to continue.

    \n\nNovember 2009 \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n My games have to break even on the economical side of things. If I have spent actual money on producing the game I want that back some time in the future. (Not counting hours spent...) If I couldn't make my money back I wouldn't bother with polishing the games for general consumption.

    But that is not really success for me, just something I want from games that I make available to the general public.

    Success is seeing bright eyes and smiling faces on players when they come back after having played my games at a con, or over-hearing conversations in hallways saying that the game/scenario was cool.

    Success is reading AP reports on web forums.

    Success is getting that notification email from Lulu that says that someone was interested enough in my game to order it.

    Success is hearing players in the Indie games lounge say stuff like "OH! YOU'RE THE GUY THAT WROTE THAT GAME! It's a cool game!"
