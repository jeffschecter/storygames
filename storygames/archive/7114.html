\n Hi All,

After some discussions with Ron, I've decided to write a book about playing Sorcerer.

Play Sorcerer will be a compliment guide to Ron Edwards\u2019 Sorcerer.

The tools and techniques within Sorcerer do a bang up job of moving the narrative through twists, turns, moral tension and emotional payoff worthy of a good story. I loved the game since I first encountered it, and after tooling around with it for about seven years, I love it even more.

However, the rules are sometimes obscure. Some items have been left unclear. Little explanation has been offered as to how all the pieces fit together. And a great deal of thought has gone into the game since it was published almost a decade ago.

Play Sorcerer will take all the thinking and playing I\u2019ve done with the game and break out all the rules in a clear and easy to use manner. The book will explain both procedures and the reasons for the procedures (for example, Kickers, Bangs, Bonus Dice and others), how they all work together to a greater effect.

Moreover, the text will be a large essay on the act of social storytelling using roleplaying games in general.

The book will be approximately 150+ pages in a 6\xd79 format, released at first as a PDF.

I have established a collection for $1500 at Fundable.com to make sure there is interest for the project before I begin.

Anyone donating to this fund will automatically receive a PDF copy of the book upon its completion. (Email addresses are automatically stored when donations are made at Fundable, and I will simply go down the list and mail out the PDFs).

Thank you for your time and interest on this project.

I've set up a blog where I'll be writing about how the project is going at playsorcerer.wordpress.com

Donations to the fund can be made at Fundable.

If anyone has any questions about the book or items they want discussed, I'd love to talk about them here.

    \n Wow! Thanks for all the support guys!

    Paul, you ask a really good question. I see Play Sorcerer won't be reproducing any text, nor will I be doing a point by point coverage of the game as written. The dice rolling in Sorcerer provides very strange and awesome effects in play -- but when you first read the rules you think, "Oh, okay, I compare the top values to the opponent's top values and we see how has the highest unique value. Okay, Got it." Which really isn't getting it. So while I won't be explaining how you roll the dice, there will be several pages devoted to how the dice rolling mechanic works, what its effect on the game is, how to approach it as players.

    In other words, Sorcerer is the rule book that comes with the XBox 360 case, and Play Sorcerer is the glossy mag on the rack at the gamestore that breaks out all the stuff that's in the game and how the pieces all fit together.

    It's also a very personal project, of course. Some of it can't help but be, "This is how I paint, and here's why."

    As for the other books, yes many but not all subjects covered in those books will be covered in this book. I don't think you can really play Sorcerer without being informed by those books, and this book must address many of the concepts in those books as well.

    \n Hi Mark,

    Excellent question.

    In a word: no. Any books I print and sell will be sold at a mark up... of about $10. Funders won't have to pay that mark up, since they are printing at cost. (In other words, you're not paying my fee twice.) Of course, Funders can also print off as many as they want. Whether there's going to be a black market demand for the book, or whether they'll make great stocking stuffers, I don't know.

    There is also the PDF, which is emailed to all Funders for free. In fact, there will be two versions of the PDF. This won't be a big deal to some, but I'm trying -- I hope -- something new and interesting on this front. There will be the typical PDF that looks like the book. And then there will be the second version, which will be designed to be read and used on a computer screen. The page size will take into account that computer screens are horizontal, and that PDF page numbers usually bear no resemblance to the content's page numbers in a typical PDF. This second version will be paginated so that the PDF page numbers actually match the text pages so page references actually work properly.

    It will be a bit of work to set up this second version, but I think it'll be worth it. I'm trying to think forward on this front, and I see this as a place where books are going. I'm experimenting to see how best to set this up. And, again, this screen designed PDF is free for all funders.

    In other news we're almost 50% funded.

    \n So, I sat down to start working on the chapter outlines from my notes... and found that Jesse (who is behind the project) asked a question on the Play Sorcerer blog.

    So I thought I'd kick off the editorial discussion with my answer.

    Jesse asked:\n
    Christopher,

    I\u2019m curious about whether you\u2019re intending to more or less preserve the progression of the supplements. On The Forge Ron recently reminded someone that a Relationship Map as defined in the Sorcerer\u2019s Soul is an advanced technique with a very specific purpose that is not at all a requirement of *basic* Sorcerer play.

    Do you plan on breaking those layers out and addressing how each layer builds on the previous one? Or is it going to be more a comprehensive approach assuming the reader is at least passingly familiar with the concepts across ALL supplements and understands where those devisions already exist?

    Jesse
    \nI thought I'd reply here in a post.

    Jesse,

    This is will be one of those spots where Ron and I disagree. (If there was no disagreement of any kind between us then I suppose I'd never have had the inspiration to write the book!)

    I don't believe the Relationship Map is advanced. In fact, once I translated into my own way of looking at it, it's very simple. It takes work, but in execution it is simple.

    More importantly, if you don't give Players the tool of the Relationship Map, what are they left with? How do they prep? Many methods of play prep are simply at odds with what Sorcerer requires to play. They simply will not function.

    And if not those, what? Draw from Primetime Adventures? Dogs in the Vineyard? Without giving a clue to someone picking up the game as to how to get ready to play the players are thrown back on their own devices. Some will hit, some will miss. But why not give them a technique that works wonderfully well for the game right off the bat?

    Is the Relationship Map the only prep one could use for a Sorcerer game? Of course not. Not by a long shot. But it is so smart and so useful that it is is an excellent choice.

    The Game Master is going to have to prep somehow. In my view he might as well be handed the best tool available.

    So, that's my specific response to your specific example. In general, Play Sorcerer will be use a similar methodology for what goes into the book and what doesn't. Is there something in the book or supplements that serves as a (in my view) a baseline for playing the game? Then I'm discussing it in Play Sorcerer.

    That means pulling nuggets out of Sorcerer & Sword and Sorcerer & Soul and explicating. I mentioned this in passing to Ron, but I think most of the material in Sex & Sorcerer won't be touched on in Player Sorcerer. This, I think, is the advanced stuff. It will certainly inform the game, but it adds a great do the thematic layers of the content. It isn't part of what I consider "the bedrock" of game play.

    And here's what I mean by the "bedrock":

    You know "The Anatomy of Authored Roleplaying" essay in Sorcerer & Sword? As far as I'm concerned almost every rule and tool and technique found in the books is in service to Authored Roleplaying. That is the activity of the game -- just as every game (Football, Risus, Go) has a specific activity. Play Sorcerer is all about explicating how the rules, tools and techniques of the game work, how they work together, and how they bring this activity about.

    So, no... no sequence through the supplements at all. I see it more like each section will be about gears that interlock with each other as they turn: Bonus Dice, Relationship Maps, Demon Needs, and all of it. Some are gears within gears, others are very big and move many other gears. But its how these gears all work in concert to make the game do the thing it does.

    Making sure the text actually does what I'm claiming it is going to do, that is, explains how different rules and tools and techniques influence each other, I think will be one of the greatest challenges of the book. But it's very exciting to know I'll be giving it a try.

    \n Hi Richie,

    \n
    \n So, I tried to organize my notes into something really clean and slick.... and failed. I've learned over the years that there's outlining, and then there's the words hitting the page. Only in the latter does the clarity really arrive.

    Having said that, I did want to dump out the items I'll be addressing. So what I'm giving you is a bunch of outline notes gathered from across pages in my notebook. It's pretty sparse... but, again, I haven't started writing the book yet.

    It's important to know that organization of the book is still gelling. Some times that look like they might only be a subhead of half a page in the "outline" below might end up being ten pages in length. But I did want to offer up where my brain was going with all this:\n
      \n
    • Creating Socially
    • Trusting the Creative Act
    • Limiting Infinite Choices: The Chaos Theory of Improvised Storytelling
    • Players at the Table as Audience AND Creators
    • The Components of Setting: Demons, Lore, etc...; The GM\u2019s Role; The Players\u2019 Role
    • The Components of a Character: Attributes, Descriptors, Price Goals, NPCs, Places, Lore... (The Front of the character sheet; the back of the Character sheet)
    • The Kicker: what makes a Kickerl; how to use it; how it defines the game session; Kicker resolutions
    • Bangs: What makes a Bang; how to use them; what isn\u2019t a Bang; screwing with Players isn\u2019t what Bangs are about.
    • Conflict \u2013 what Conflict is, what conflict isn\u2019t; how to spot it, how to use it
    • Using the Dice in Conflict: when to use the Dice; how they produce twists and turns; how the wonkiness of the resolution is a metaphor for how the game plays
    • Bonus Dice: how they work, what they mean for the game; how to use them effectively; Bonus Dice as social magnet during play
    • Demons: How they function with the PCs
    • Demons: How the GM uses them
    • Demons: Abilities and how to use them for effective play
    • Demons: Needs and Desires and why they matter to the game play; Demons as major NPCS because of RELATIONSHIP to PCs, not abilities
    • Humanity: how it works, why it matters to play, what it produces
    • Lore: How to set up effective Lore; how to bring Lore into Play
    • Sorcery from both Sorcerer and Sorcerer & Sword: narrative and game implications of the activity for characters and players
    • Sorcerer: An Intense Roleplaying Game \u2013 what the heck that\u2019s about and why it matters
    • The Setting: Lore, Demons, Humanity and more.... ; explain how each works by itself and how they all work together
    • Inspirations for setting; digging into the setting
    • The Relationship Map: how it works; why it works; a point of focus for play NOT a simulation of social interaction
    • NPCs: building them; using them
    • Backstory: NPCs, McGuffins; Narrative focus is vital for Players
    • The PCs drive the story forward: how this works, why it works, the great effects it produces; GM: learning to toss bangs rather than build plot; Plot is what happened, story is what\u2019s
    • happening; the back and forth of PCs and NPCs
    • Narrative Context: anything worth doing in Sorcerer only happens within a Sorcerer story
    • Notes on notekeeping: how to organize all these characters and story elements!
    • The GM\u2019s Prep Work: what you do and why you should trust it; Prepping before the first meeting; prepping after the first meeting; prepping before each session
    • Playing the Game: kibitzing, laughing, freaking out; stepping back, checking in, retrofitting story elements; taking time to clarify what just happened or what people want; encouraging the
    • Players to go to town in their choices and descriptions
    • Knowing when to stop: lines, veils, closing out a session, ending a story
      • \n

    \n Hi Alan,

    It's already buried in the list (The Back of the Character Sheet), under Creating Characters.

    I designed a new Sorcerer Character sheet on a 8.5" x 11" sheet of paper which puts the "back" of the sheet on the front right in the middle of the character sheet. Because I think it's that important.


    Hi Dave,

    It's not particularly a mystery -- this social storytelling thing. A lot of games do it right now -- In A Wicked Age, Dogs in the Vineyard, Primetime Adventures and so on. The idea is that a group of people get together and make up a story.... you know, socially.

    I simply want to be more explicit about this -- what it means, how to do it, pitfalls and wonderful opportunities. At the base level it means that the players don't gether to experience the "GM's Story," but instead are making up a story together. At it's best, in my view, it's making what I call "Stories for a Very Small Tribe" -- the tribe being all the players gathered at the table (including the GM). This means that the story created by this social experience resonates not only with cool stuff, but with ideas and themes that matter to the individuals. So that you end up with a story that reflects what THIS group cares about.

      \n So.... True Story:

      CK was in my very first attempt at running Sorcerer ever. I had just revealed that his character's dead son was running around as a ghost (see Undead rules in Sorcerer & Sword). CK gets this very deep serious look on his face for a moment then bursts forth with, "Double Doors!" complete with wild spreading arm gesture. It struck me both just from the sheer sudden outburst but also because of its near perfect embodiment of my setting in such simple terms.

      That was almost 10 years ago and over that time I've repeated that moment to a few people.

      Last year Laura was in a Dogs in the Vineyard game at our local con. In it are three USC college students she's never met before and she's never seen around the con before. I'm not sure exactly what in the game prompted it but all of sudden all THREE of them in unison burst out with, "DOUBLE DOORS" complete with the hand gestures.

      So yeah, CK is a bit of a local gaming cultural legend. Just thought I'd share.
