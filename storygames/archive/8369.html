\n Last week during our Sunday game one of my players whipped out something he found while digging through his old books at his parents house. It was a formula he used to figure optimal sail sizes for boats according to GURPS: Vehicles.
\n
\nI wish I had a scan of this formula, because it was a real beast. My friend had created it in high school, back when Trig and Calculus were *fun* for him. (heh, they were never fun for me, but he's one of those Math dudes)
\n
\nAnyway that got us to talking: what's the most mathematically intensive RPG out there? Is it GURPS Vehicles? Old School Traveler? Chivalry & Sorcery?
\n
\nI don't know because any of those would have had me running the other direction with all due haste. I implore you, storygamers, to think about where we've been to appreciate where we are! What's the most mathematically intense RPG in existence?

    \n My bet would be "Powers and Perils", which was apparently created for this purpose. Since many early RPG developers were insurance actuaries, many of their formulas got used. P&P has factorials and other complex mathematical operations that boggled the mind - all in the name of producing realism, of course.

    Linkage here.

    The most annoying game of that sort to me is, of course, Rolemaster and its related games. All that overhead and it still creates the unrealistic stories of gore and such where every battle ends in trauma so severe that the healer must apprently work very hard to erase their effects almost entirely in but a few hours...

    ...Rolemaster should in actuality be more like a mystical version of House, MD. It was blood porn, and the inclusion of the "69" crits was an additional, immature addition to the whole mess.

    \n If you want math-intensive, you must check out Alternate Realities, a generic rules set written by some guys with really strong math skills. I was at a conference years ago with one of the authors, but didn't realize he was a gamer until much later. I only saw him present a paper, and he was scary smart.

    Anyway, this has to be seen to be believed; they're calculating derivatives in the sidebars in order to sketch out the various possible physical models underlying the particular reality you want to play in.

    Open the Primary Reality Guide and you'll see what I mean.

    \n I'd say Traveller and GURPS were even except that GURPS beats Traveller out due to insistence on using Imperial units, meaning that an already ludicrous calculation is made several times more difficult due to needing to be dealt with in pounds, inches and other units that make no sense. That might not matter so much to you Americans or others from backwards countries, but my technical education was all metric, so as far as I am concerned engineering should always be done in units that come in units that convert into tens wherever possible.

    \n The Star Frontiers Knight Hawks spaceship design rules were pretty complex, with many similarities to Traveller.

    SPI (I beleive) had a crazy sci-fi RPG called Universe with some out of control math. The formal for the number of hexes you can travle by jetpack based on the planetary conditions, gravity, and your jetpack skill included a square root. That's the only square root I've ever seen in an RPG.

    The game also had a wild matrix where your environmental survival skill was written in a box on a chart that looked sort of like the inside of a slightly defomred crossword puzzle. You wrote your skill in the square corresponding the environment of the world you were born on (say, arctic tundra, or temperate forest). The numbers then decreased as they spiralled outward giving your effective skill an any terrain. If your home terrain was space station, zero G, which was in a sort of curlicue off the top of the chart, then you ability to survive in, for example, an equatorial jungle on the lower right, would almost certainly be a negative number.

    I have not yet seen an RPG that uses imaginary numbers.

    Posted By: kevin.weiserAnyway that got us to talking: what's the most mathematically intensive RPG out there? Is it GURPS Vehicles? Old School Traveler? Chivalry & Sorcery?
    \nI had a lot of lonely fun with 2nd edition C&S when I was a kid, and I would say that chargen for magic-using characters was some of the most intense math I've seen. The main reason being that you had to enchant a focus as part of pre-play background, and doing so involved collecting various materials determined by the PC's birth sign, which each had their own innate magical resistance that needed to be overcome in the focus-building process. So, you'd know that you had X hundred days of pre-play life to account for, and you could enchant a given amount of each kind of material per day depending on your level, and you were continuously leveling up during those X hundred days, so your speed of enchantment would increase as the X hundred days wore on, meaning that the time it took to enchant each type of material varied over the course of the pre-play period... so you'd have to sort of map out how many days the PC spent at each level to see how much enchanting was occurring during those period so you could see if you had your focus done by the time play began.

    Basically, a lot of needless insanity just to say, "My wizard begins the game with a magic wand that actually lets him use his class powers."

    Also, I have dim memories of FGU's other number cruncher, Space Opera, *requiring* Trig in order to handle 3D spaceship combat. And chargen, of course, had lots of multiplying by decimals and such.

    Otherwise... I'd say that HERO certainly gets pretty intense. I can't see ever making PCs without the Hero Designer software (which is actually one of the better chargen apps out there, for any system). And doing division at the table to figure out Move Bys and Move Throughs was always fun... as was trying to figure out how to add damage to attacks (which takes up *four pages* in the current rulebook).

    I know it's not nice to condemn other people's fun, but damn... I'd like to think we know better nowadays. My affection for HERO will probably never die, but I really don't see the point of these kinds of RPGs anymore.

    \n Personally, I loved that in Aysle. I don't remember rolling for each node, but the chart itself I thought was very cool, showing how the wizards of Aysle saw the world as working. I also liked that the limited number of paths "forced" some kinds of spells to go certain ways. For example, transmutation required True Knowledge... but the only way to work that in, if you were transmuting something from a living form to an unliving material, was through Darkness. Another interesting addition was in making magic items -- to place a spell in something, you had to incorporate the material of the item in the path. Thus, to make a wooden wand have a spell in it, you had to incorporate "Wood" into the spell's path.

    There was a similar gem in the Orrorsh book for TORG, where the spell creation system for it was laid out. Most games would have taken the existing spell creation system they had and adapted it. Instead, TORG's writers created a completely different system from that of Aysle. Orrorsh's system focused on "Occult Events", which could be broken down into "subevents" using "occult props". Putting more subevents and props in made the rolls you had to do easier -- but at the same time generated plot for the GM, since the PCs would have to get the props they needed... or the NPC would have to get them.

    As I recall, there was yet a third magic system in another of the TORG setting books, but I don't remember which one, or the details.

    \nCold Iron was probably the most math intensive RPG in play that I have regularly played. Every resolution roll involved a lookup on a table that represented the normal distribution. Spell casters used exponential and logarithmic calculations to determine current mana (spell) points. I wrote a program to compute magic item costs using formulas I have developed.

    The most intensive math I've ever done for a game was for AD&D. I tried to derive a formula to determine how a fireball expanded to fill a constricted space. To do this, since I had not yet had multiple-integration in calculus yet, I actually managed to derive it for myself. I think I only ever managed to have a workable formula for the fireball to be constrained in one dimension. I could compute the radius of expansion if the fireball was centered in a sufficiently large room that it was only ever constrained by the floor and/or ceiling, given an arbitrary ceiling height and height off the floor of the blast center.

    Although the math is simple, GURPS and Hero character generation was certainly annoyingly mathematical. We constantly found we couldn't get the same point total twice from adding up all the individual point costs.

    Chivalry and Sorcery (1st edition is all that I actually have experience with) certainly had intensive rules for spells, but I never considered it all that mathematical.

    \n I just had a flashback to a game I once playtested, but I don't think ever got produced. It was a science fiction game with skill lists that went on for pages. Skills were percentile-based, of course, and calculating the build point cost of each percentage point was just a few taps on a calculator. Then, you were given three lumps of build points to buy skills. They totaled 10,000 points...with costs calculated to 2 decimal places effectively making it 1,000,000 build points. So, while it wasn't the worst math I'd seen in a game, the sheer scale of it deserves mention.
