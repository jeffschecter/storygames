So I've been taking them for granted for a long time, and then I thought, these are horrible things.

As far as I can tell, a character sheet is where we keep information that the other players do not need to know, interact with, or have authority over. (Yes, I don't consider the Polaris device a character sheet.)

Why do we have things that the other players don't need to know, interact with, or have authority over? How does that improve the gaming experience? Board games don't have character sheets. I think they are onto something.

    Drawing an analogy to your design tendencies here. You design, in part, to create structures so that you can do certain types of things without thinking about and remembering them all the time.     Jared's right. Although I'll say more than that, now that I have time to write about it.

    Contrary to Judson Lester's experience, I found *The Design of Everyday Things* to be very useful in my thinking about roleplaying games. So much so that I plan on writing some essays about an RPG design philosophy based on those concepts.

    There were a couple of those concepts that seemed to have direct bearing on the discussion of character sheets. One is the difference between knowledge in the head and knowledge in the world. Knowledge in the head, for any particular task, is slower than knowledge in the world, since you have to recall the specific relevant fact you want to use, but users with a lot of knowledge in the head on how to do these tasks can be more flexible and creative in their actions. Knowledge in the world, on the other hand, can enable users to perform necessary tasks with very little thought, if the interface to their tools/equipment is designed right.

    That's what a character sheet is: an interface to the system. It can be a mnemonic device or a repository of secrets, but these are neither necessary nor primary. The character sheet tells you (or should tell you) how to play the game, what's important to know. If you see a list of +1/+2/+3 modifiers with names on your character sheet, the naturaldesire is to add those to something. If you see some kind of tracker made of checkboxes or circles, you know you will be marking them off, using up the resource they represent.

    We've occasionally talked about the relevance of board and card game design to roleplaying games. The reason why board and card games are easier to play is because they have a lot more knowledge in the world, stuff printed on the board or on the cards. That's all a character sheet is, another way to put knowledge into the world. It's just that most character sheets aren't designed very well.

    A couple good design examples: *My Life With Master* puts the victory conditions on the character sheet, so you know what you are playing towards. *Sorcerer* has the nice wound tracker along the side that you can use without erasing, plus it has the back of the sheet, which tells you and the GM what the focus of play will be.

    Shared maps are really just character sheets, even if people are loathe to call them such, because of the connotations of "character sheet". I've got a game or two in the works that use shared maps. My task, as a designer, is to make the rules of play and the use of the shared map mesh in such a way that the map will tell the players what they can do, so that they don't have to look up rules in a book (although, perversely, I am also making the book an artifact of play, so certain things *will* be looked up.) I will also have traditional character sheets, but there's no rule about keeping character sheets secret. It's more like a set of resources that players can bring to bear on the activity taking place on the map.

    If you have a game rule that you think can be better expressed as playing cards, poker chips, or something else, you should certainly do so. But don't discount character sheets; there's a hell of a lot of room for better design in this area. I find the whole concept of designing character sheets the way you would design a dashboard for a car or the interface for a computer game an exciting, inspiring idea.

    Posted By: Jason Morningstar They are an artifact of the retention/retrieval problem, and there are lots of other ways to address that.
    I was talking with Dev and Ben recently about Primetime Adventures' approach to retention/retrieval, which is both unadulterated genius and maddening to me as a designer and player.

    The metagame of PTA gets you in the mode of telling stories "like a TV show." You figure out a premise, you choose main characters, you divy up screen presence, etc. On top of this, the game book provides a ton of suggestions about how TV shows are structured and what an episode might look like and similar things.

    Then play begins.

    And the system does very little to support or encourage players in telling stories in the style of serial TV drama. Instead, it relies mostly on the primordial understanding of how television works that's been indoctrinated into all of us by years of staring at the boob-tube. The game tells you that "this is a TV show" and players respond accordingly, producing play that feels like a TV show.

    In setting things up this way, Matt avoided having extensive mechanics that structured episode plots or framed scenes or established pacing or set endgame (or episode ending) conditions or did any number of things that haven't quite made it into the gaming vocabulary yet (framing specific "camera shots," deciding which characters are in a given scene, managing subplots, etc). He cut through all that with the swift sword that is -- basically -- freeform. The players share an understanding of what play is supposed to look like and, sure enough, that's the kind of play that results. Booyeah, the essence of freeform and a good example of a game being more of a practice than a specific set of rules.

    The maddening part, for me, is trying to figure out what is actually going on in PTA play, since so much of it seems to be based on our collective understanding of television. I totally want to develop techniques that help us structure scenes and sessions, but PTA does this largely by calling on us to rely on our instincts. This is amazing, in that PTA could operate totally without character sheets and dice and a GM and still be completely effective, I think. There is no retention/retrieval problem, because it asks you to use knowledge THAT YOU ALREADY HAVE. You're not asked to memorize a bunch of rules or stats or new setting material. I wish more games would make similar moves to draw on our collective knowledge, aside from the traditional approach of relying on genre conventions.

      I just had a flashback and remembered that during our first AD&D campaigns we didn't even use official character sheets!

      It was en vogue to use a hand-scribbled personal version (on graph paper or similar). Neither of them were pieces of art but differently-coloured writings, doodles ("my sword looks like this"), and sketchy dungeon plans in the margins did lend a certain kind of character (pun intended) to the sheets that today's neatly fillid-in, sterile photoshopped and inDesignded record sheets lack.
      But then, there were those pioneers of the PC age who couldn't stand "imperfect", DIY sheets and used their creations of nine-needle-printed character sheets.

      The strangest creation I saw (not in my own games, alas):
      A silk-screened polystyrene board (not unlike a clipboard), about 3 mm thick, laminated with an adhesive clear PVC foil, for use with overhead markers. A GM (a random encounter in a game store) made these for his whole game group (and it was mandatory that they use them).
