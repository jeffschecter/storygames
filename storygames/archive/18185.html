    Hi pigeon, since you already have the floor, how about giving the rest of us the elevator pitch about the game? I like the title...
    \nSure!

    Dog Eat Dog is a game of colonialism and its consequences. As a group, you work together to describe one of the hundreds of small islands in the Pacific Ocean, defining the customs of the natives and the mores of the outsiders arriving to claim it. One player then assumes the role of the Occupation force, playing their capable military, their quisling government, and whatever jaded tourists and shrewd businessmen are interested in a not quite pacified territory. All the others play individual Natives, each trying in their own ways to come to terms with the new regime. The game begins when the war ends. Through a series of scenes, you play out the inevitably conflicted relationship between the two parties, deciding what the colonizers do to maintain control, which natives assimilate and which run amok, and who ends up owning the island in the end.

    The mechanics of Dog Eat Dog are very simple and straightforward -- a few tokens, a couple of dice, and some pens and paper are all you need. The rules are designed to keep people paying attention to every scene and to force them to make difficult and real choices between assimilation and resistance.\n
    Also, if you're still around, could you let me know how many players are needed for the game? Could it be played between two people?
    \nI've never tried it with two. The rules should work, but it might not be very much fun, unfortunately -- you wouldn't get any interplay between the Natives. On the other hand, it might work the same as ever. It definitely works with three.

    I'm curious about book art, but that certainly isn't make-or-break for me. I dig your cover! Might be nice to get the image from the artist and feature it more prominently at some point.

    The one important thing I'm still fuzzy on is play length. Best in 2 hrs? Best in 4? Best in multiple sessions? Doable as one-shot or multi?
    \nThere isn't any art in the book, aside from I think a couple maps of islands in the Pacific. It's also not a campaign-friendly game at all, everything gets firmly wrapped up in one session.

    I've played four times now, and I'd say it usually runs between 2-4 hours, usually at one extreme or the other-- more players means it runs longer, but about half my games have ended shockingly quickly as the Natives assimilate the Occupation's values and drain all its tokens, while the other half were all struggles to the bitter end as Occupation and Natives trade every token back and forth, dancing on the line between insubordination and rebellion.

    I strongly recommend this game, it's been dynamite every time I've played and it's very good at what it does. It is a very short book, but that just means all the fat has been pared out of it, and it's full of useful advice and clarifications.
