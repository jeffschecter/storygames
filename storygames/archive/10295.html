\n Long story short, last night I got together with a group of friends to play a session of D&D 4E. Three of the six players were new to the game, the others had played only once or twice. We were continuing along with the first Scales of War module, Rescue at Rivenroar. The PCs had a map to the castle ruins and were following it, but didnt realize the map took them through a Kruthik nest (basically bug-like, bladed lizards who can burrow and who cut you if you stand too close).

So I draw out the map, they set up their pieces, the battle starts. About halfway through, maybe a little further, one of the players gets up and leaves. Walks out. The other players are still going, so one of them just takes over the character actions. The combat lasts a while, but everyone seems engaged and we keep playing.

The one player comes back right as the last Kruthik is being taken down. He is grumbling about the fight, so I wrap things up quickly, put the game stuff away, and we all start to talk. The conversation turns heated on an unrelated topic, and I can tell the guy is pissed, but I dont want to start something in front of everyone else. Eventually we all just leave (it was his house).

Later I call him and proceed to have one of the worst fights ever with him on the phone. Though there apparently were other issues, he told me that the game sucked and it was my fault, that I put them up against to tough of opponents, made no attempt to have any role-playing and that I was a terrible DM.

I told him he didn't even attempt to roleplay and that he walked out without telling me what the issue was.

Needless to say, it was a very bad end to what I thought was an otherwise ok game. Now I have no desire to run anything for this guy, who also said other people who stayed afterwards agreed with him and were pissed at me for not making the game fun for them.

So I am kinda left scratching my head over what to do next, what I did wrong and how this could have turned out differently. I have never had a game where we played through a whole session without issue and then was berated for the game not being what the players thought it should be.

Not really a question here. Just looking for discussion and to vent a bit.

ME

    \n Unless a monster gets tougher in some way when Bloodied, indicating some possible rage/desperation mode, many of them should be looking for an exit as soon as or not long after they're Bloodied. I'm always surprised to hear that a combat continues on to the bitter end after the victor has pretty much been determined. That's pretty much the dullest part of any fight and you should foreshorten it most of the time. That provides a similar effect to the house rule you're considering, but without the hit point math.

    With the PHB2, WotC added Expertise feats to the game, providing a +1/tier to-hit bonus at the cost of a feat. They did this to make the game less "whiffy," a trend which increases at the Paragon tier and then again at Epic. Without Expertise, PCs hit less often as they level towards 30.

    I determined to disallow Expertise feats in my game; they are dull and they are so good that every PC wants them, representing a "feat tax" and so every PC effectively has one less feat to differentiate their vision of their character. But I then levied a -2/-3/-4 to all opponent defenses (by tier), to achieve the same effect and then some. PCs hit more often as a result and the combat proceeds more quickly. I also improved opponent damage by +2/+3/+4 for each attack (by tier) to make opposition more scary without adding "swingy" critical house-rules. The extra damage means I might typically have one or two fewer baddies on the field which makes the combat faster and has a similar effect as reducing hitpoints. I'm pretty happy with these changes and they are among my few house rules.

    \n Just some helpful hints that helped in my game.

    If you do action narration, make it two-step:

    1. Ask the player to state what his character does
    (roll the dice)
    2. Ask the player to describe the outcome of his action, including vivid description (or do it yourself, if you narrate)

    Nevernevernever allow a player to narrate the action before the dice roll, like "I flip around on the balls of my feet, twist my dagger holding his blade, and shove the other up towards his lower intestine". The player spends a lot of creative energy describing awesome actions, with a positive outcome clearly in mind, and then the dice fail to support their narration and the energy drains away. A few of these whiffs and anyone's session will start sucking. However, it's actually fun to describe a failure sometimes, but never fun to expect a success on a cool Encounter or Daily power and end up having to narrate a crappy "miss" result.

    Also, all the modules I've seen are made of suck and fail (someone, please, prove me wrong!). I've successfully harvested individual elements from them on occasion, but I've never read a module I that seemed gripping or interesting from a story perspective in any way. Make the players tell you what motivates their characters by announcing Quests, writing magic item shopping lists, or just plain telling you - then craft or steal meaningful encounters to put in their way.

    Now, whether you used the above techniques or not, you shouldn't have to put up with passive-aggressive BS like what you're describing.
