\n It strikes me that there are actually three roles to resolution, and that the standard two-role (protagonist, antagonist) setup is, in mathematical terms, a degenerate case.
\n
\nThere's a protagonist, an antagonist, and then there's the character with something at stake. In many cases, the person with something at stake is either the protagonist, the antagonist, or both ... but there are genres that might be best suited to make that a non-standard case (perhaps even one that gives penalties).
\n
\nIt'd be sort of neat to have a player who wants to get into a fight on some subject, flailing around to find some third party who cares enough to put something of themselves at stake. Apart from emulating some fiction, it sounds like it would be an interesting social dynamic.

    Posted By: 1of3Tony, you're post is quite abstract. Could you give an example from such a genre?
    In Ranma 1/2, for example, the shoujo structure is wedded to very shonen, very explicit conflicts: to wit, Ranma always wants to get into martial arts fights. But these fights, on their own, do not matter. If he wins? Great. He reinforces his notion that he's the toughest and the strongest. If he loses? Doesn't matter. He makes up some excuse for why he lost, and reinforces his notion that he's the toughest and the strongest.

    Despite all his bluster, Ranma has no stakes in the fights he starts.

    Often, though, bystanders have heavy stakes in the fight. Most of the time it's fairly blatant "Akane will date/marry/clean-the-house-of me, your adversary, unless you can defeat me in battle!", though the formula is applied to many different stakes-holders, broadly and with gusto, episode after episode after episode.

    So there's a situation where, for instance, you have three participants in the fight: Ranma and Kuno fighting over Akane. How does Akane's role work? How much control does she have over how big the stakes are to her? If she gets to say (for instance) "No, it's no big deal ... if Kuno wants to go out with me that bad, I don't really mind," how does that impact both Kuno and Ranma, and what they get out of the fight? If, contrariwise she says (as she often does) "DEATH FIRST!" then how does that impact Kuno and Ranma?

    And then, of course, abstract that back into less explicit conflicts: If Eddie is being convinced to go to the dance with Veronica, but Rachel's really the one with stakes in the conflict (not that SHE wants to go with Eddie ... that would be wierd, they're-just-friends, but ... but ... he's better than that bitch Veronica) then how does that conflict play out?

    \n Mike S,

    It sounds like multiple people could each have one or more of those three roles. I read it as saying: Everyone is a protagonist, an antagonist, and/or someone with something at stake.


    So Thunderman is fighting an epic duel with Dr. Sin, and Ellen Mincy's life hangs in the balance. And the Nameless Crowd also have a huge stake in the fight. And Goonboy, the rival superhero who despises Thunderman, also has a stake in the fight between Thunderman and Dr. Sin.

    But Goonboy and Thunderman are also fighting, in an undercut-the-other-hero way, and Dr. Sin has a stake in that fight (he is trying to help Goonboy win, because Goonboy is the lesser threat to him).

    And Ellen Mincy is a political activist who is struggling to get President Rupert impeached. And so she's fighting a fight against the Nameless Crowd, and President Rupert has something at stake in that fight.

    And President Rupert is fighting the Nameless Crowd for their ongoing loyalty as his first Presidential term nears an end and he looks to run again, and he has a stake in his own fight.
