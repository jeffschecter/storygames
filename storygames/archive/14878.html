    \n Cthulhu Dark is easy in 2 hours. Spend no more than 10 minutes on character creation: create a few rough links between characters.

    At first, give them space to explore, interact, roleplay. With 1 hour to go, ensure they're investigating, on the track of the main mystery. With 30 minutes to go, start the final scene.

    Choose a simple mystery. I've run The Dying Of St Margaret's in 2 hours. You're telling a simple ghost story. At any time, you can skip a few scenes and go straight to The Horror That Is Responsible For Everything.

    I played two hour slots in Games on Demand and enjoyed them! They're good for trying out a game quickly. I don't necessarily want to invest four hours in a game I haven't heard of, but two hours might be cool.

    \n Hey John,

    My advice is to scale back expectations a bit and keep it punchy. Show off the system but don't be too ambitious. Take Dungeon World - part of the fun is making dudes, which takes 20 minutes, so do that and give some backstory at the same time. Then have a simple adventure prepped - mine was basically four rooms, three encounters, with some wiggle room on either end. Here are my notes. For DW you want to show them character generation, how moves work, what leveling up feels like and what the iconic bits for each class feel like. 1-2-3 done. Obviously it is a one shot so shower them with cool stuff and accelerate leveling.

    I think this advice can be applied to any game that normally takes longer - identify the important and cool bits, and find ways to highlight that stuff reliably while multi-tasking.

    \n I think first see what the players want. Fr'ex, Morgan Ellis was prepared to run a four hour game on Sunday, but at least one of the players couldn't stay that long. So, the game ran about two and a half hours. I can see how it would have run in a four hour slot, and that would have been fine. But, the two and a half hour version was also good. It's the sort of game where some scenes can be cut or not, and either way, the game will be fine. I'm not quite sure how to describe writing a game that can go two or four hours beyond "Make it modular. Use the bits you absolutely need, and use the others if there's time and it makes sense."

    I mean, you might think if the game could run in two hours, that the rest would be padding, or if the game works at four hours, there's nothing to cut for a two hour run. But, this doesn't seem to be the case.

    One of my four hour games, a Laundry game I genericed into, ran two, maybe two and a half hours. The GM only took one ticket from me (if it had run the full four hours, he would have taken two, and he stated at the beginning that he expected it to run short), and on pondering, I decided that if I'd been someone buying the ticket in advance, I would have been fine with that. The event ticket guarantees a spot if you show and the game goes off.

    I like Official Slots of four hours, and I don't mind if the game runs short so long as it's the right length -- long enough to cover the essentials, short enough to be interesting. For something like Apocalypse World or Burning Empires, you're going to have to pick what you showcase carefully, as you can't run a full game. I could see a sequence of two hour games, each going on to the next step, allowing people to play continuously or not, as they see fit.

    \n For games of the "go out into the world and have adventure encounters" variety (Mouse Guard, Trail of Cthulhu, Apocalypse World, Dogs in the Vineyard, D&D, etc), you build towards a single big encounter, and you build as simply as possible.

    Beginning: They already know what they want, but they just need to figure out how to get there.
    Middle: Something complicates matters, and damages their resources. The stakes are raised.
    End: Now they encounter the thing.

    Apocalypse World:
    Rourke's taken Litmus hostage. You all want Litmus safe, for the reason listed on your sheet: Litmus is your lover; Litmus is your boss; You want the satisfaction of killing Litmus yourself.
    Rourke's tower is 20 miles away, and you don't have vehicles.
    The chopper's gang has vehicles, if you can get them somehow.
    Along the way, someone trips a landmine. You've now got a badly wounded brainer on your hands.
    Now you fight Rourke.

    Have a tight situation map. Give people their motivation.
    Make it obvious what they're gunning for, and make that the only big conflict.
    Put one or two roadblocks in the way, and don't make these big combat encounters - make them hard choices that resolve quickly.
    Done.

    \n Note that "satisfactorily" and "finished" aren't the same. Especially if you go in saying "we're going to play, have fun, and probably end on a cliffhanger. everyone cool with that?", you can do a lot in two hours with a lot of fun and without needing to force closure. (If you get closure, cool, but it's not necessary if everyone buys into the idea that you might not have it.)

    \n When I do "Indie by Storm" (6 games in 4 hours), I carefully set expectations that the pace will be insane, that we will only touch on each game (for 30-35 minutes), and that they will be left wanting more (and here's where they can buy these games).

    Next to that, two hours for one game feels positively luxurious. Just do half as much stuff as you would in a four hour game.

    Also, prep lots. If chargens are appropriate, make them. Make short summaries of game procedures for the players so they can follow along and don't get stuck on the rules. Avoid the painful 30-minute chat session at the beginning of the slot: that's one-fourth of a 2-hour slot! Start on time and let the laggards figure out how to catch up.

    \n Can you elaborate? I'm curious at the different in our experiences.

    In my games, I worked hard to set the expectation of a fast-paced demo. I told players at the start that we would just get a taste of each game. They knew going in that we'd probably leave a lot of unfinished business.

    In addition, I worked to find a more natural stopping place. It was still a cliffhanger ending, but it was either an /obvious/ one where everyone nodded at me knowingly that I'd chosen to end the game there, or it was because we'd hit the 45 minute mark and everyone had agreed that we were gonna do 6 games in 4 hours or whatever.

    I've done demo-style games that were less frenetic -- typically two games in four hours -- and I think the players felt that the games were too short. So there's definitely something going on there.

    Posted By: Mr. TeapotStartin media res, and simply say "Here's what your motivation is. Your character already did X that got you to this point, where the real action begins."
    \nWhen I did this in Spirit of the Century games, I would do it like this.

    Me: "So, we have our characters, yeah? And we'll figure out skills & other aspects & maybe ad hoc stunts in play, right?"

    Players: "Yep!"

    Me: "Cool."

    PAUSE

    Me: [Suddenly yelling] "Your plane's under fire! What do you do?!?"

    When a player says "Uh, what?" I would respond with "A pulp adventurer is standing around contemplating your existence? Who is doing something!"

    Player: "I fire back!"

    Me: "Hell yes you do!" etc.

    I would slip in details (and allow them to also slip in details) with each action beat, treating it like the opening sequence of Star Wars minus the fucking opening crawl.
