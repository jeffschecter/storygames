\n Jeff Rients over at Jeff's Gameblog has posted a cool idea -- award experience points for wandering your game world.

Now, the first thing that pops to my head is that this is more-or-less the same as giving every character the key of Explorer, but the fact that you can vary the rewards from location-to-location adds another level of steering.

I'm sure more erudite theory-heads than I will put it into better language, but I really love the idea of giving extra rewards just for going places especially if the players are in on it. Rather than just randomly dishing out rewards, you are saying "look, this place is cool, go there and I'll make it worth your while". It steers the characters without having to be obvious or egregious. At the same time, if players want to tie locations to their respective backstories, this is a really easy way to add zest. The locations need not even exist in a traditional sense beforehand. "Hey, Thomas, isn't your character from Wayne's Hollow?" "Yep." "Cool, it's 500xp/one advance/5 training points per character if you guys go there." "Really? Because I had an idea for my character's family..."

    \n I've started planning a new D&D 4E game inspired heavily by the Fenian cycle. In those stories, wandering around in the woods plays a huge part, so I wanted to make that part of the game. So, we'll have a lot of the game going on inside this uber skill challenge, inspired in part by the "Wilderness Travel" suggestions Mike Mearls made in Dungeon (Part 1, Part 2). You have a map; when you make the check, you move in the direction you intend. When you don't, you move at a slight angle. So, you can get yourself horribly lost.

    I grabbed a map of Cormanthor, divided it into a grid, and I had my map\u2014to scale, no less. And since Ed Greenwood has charted this forest out in Tolkien-esque detail, I even have different forest regions, color-coded, with different DC's and different random encounter tables. See ,because once you accumulate three failures, something happens. You run into someone or something interesting. It goes on the map; so, if you run across a random troll's cave, the cave remains there and you can return to it later.

    This adds a nice extra element. Each location on the map can net you some XP. Give each location a level, and going there gives you the XP equal to one standard monster of that level.\n

    \nThis kind of thing gives the GM a way to flag content he is interested in. TSOY and Burning Wheel have some kind of GM flag mechanic (key scenes in TSOY, deeds artha in Burning Wheel). The value it has is that it ties into the feedback mechanism of the game so there is mechanical support for "doing cool things."

    I'm not sure you have to explicitly say up front "hey, if you do this, it's worth 10 points." GM excitement for something can be signaled in many ways, a cool name may be all you need. The mechanical reward is then confirmation, and completes the feedback circle. I think all that needs to be clear up front is what the mechanical reward looks like, and the GM making it clear that he has things he's excited about, and then of course the GM has to start flagging them by being genuinely excited, and giving in fiction reasons for the PCs to engage his "cool thing."

The basic premise (as my Solar System booklet has it) is that only things prepared by the GM in advance are worth xp when encountered in the game. This encourages the GM to prep (can't give out xp rewards without doing it) and it also encourages the players to explore prepped material instead of haring off to other directions. There's also no need to calculate the rewards in advance, as they're automatically set by the nature of the prep.

    Well, possibly you do, that's a matter for you. I don't though.

    Fruitful void. You mention D&D, that depends which edition. Early editions have no rules for exploration, the game was where the rules weren't. Later editions (3e and after) follow a fundamentally different design ethic, where the rules are intended to govern what happens in play. For early editions, that assumption reflects a fundamental lack of understanding of the game's design goals.

    The idea that mechanics should actively support the focus of play is simply a preference, not a rule of good design. Edit: Not a rule for all sorts of rpgs rather, for games that seek to actively promote story now, it probably is a rule of good design. But that's only one kind of gaming among many.

    But as for D&D, I play and sometimes run a game of Swords and Wizardry, based on the original D&D rules. The idea that when we explore we stop playing D&D, interrupt it, then go back to D&D later is just so off I struggle to follow it.

    Besides, why are you assuming the game is only where the game engine is? I don't think that necessarily follows at all. Again, fruitful voids. Also, some games are built with the assumption that certain forms of social contract and group fiat will be used in certain situations, those are mechanics, they're just not written down (which I grant, with D&D in particular, led to a lot of later confusion as people picked up the books blind, but I'm not in the business of defending early designers' failures to state what they viewed as obvious but in fact wasn't).

    \n Well, that's a problem with the setup, yeah. What you want is to have characters that are highly motivated to explore and engage with exploration. If you don't have that, no exploration game will ever work in the history of ever. Let me give you an example from a Maztica campaign I ran (koff koff) years ago.

    Maztica is a pseudo "New World" for the Forgotten Realms. It includes Mayan-a-like, Aztec-a-like and other native-culture-a-likes on a rich continent far from the main Forgotten Realms continent. Several kings and nations have funded expeditions there and even small colonies. Heck, Check it out for yourself, it's the first download and completely free.

    So I decided, this is badass, this is totally new, nobody else in my group has this, let's have an exploration game. Here's what I suggested at the beginning:

    * Your characters all have some motivation to explore and/or exploit Maztica. Share that motivation with everyone.
    * Your characters will all be sent by a powerful Amnian noble family to explore and report back. Scientific or magical discoveries will enhance their prestige, discoveries of gold and silver will enhance their pocketbooks. They prefer the latter but the exact mixture will be in your hands. Don't come back with nothing but plants and animals, don't come back with nothing but gold. They intend a well-rounded exploration.

    I also gave them a summary of Maztica that was about four sentences long and said "That's what you know for sure, there's a ton of other rumors, but you have no idea if they're bullshit or not."

    The characters we got were:

    A ranger guide and trailblazer who was always looking out for the best place to build or improve roads
    A half-wild-elf wizard proto-naturalist who was constantly having the ranger trap little beasties for him and had a huge chest full of plant samples
    A cleric of battle who wanted to find out what the natives worshiped and report back on their suitableness for missionary work

    And another one I can't remember. I think a paladin who suspected a demon or devil had fled his sect for the New World several decades previous and wanted to find them and send them back to hellllll.

    In this way the answers to those questions were not a "lecture" (I still don't understand how you would even deliver a lecture, like, at all, when playing an exploration game), they were vitally important to the characters.

    Now, D&D's an action game, so it was an action exploration game, with lots of chases and fights and magic and stuff, but there's no question it was an exploration game.
