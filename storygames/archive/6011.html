Posted By: hoogI've had a couple of instances where I really dreaded the result of dice-rolls

like when the life of an NPC I really liked was on the line

you know the player is going to roll the dice, and you know that there's a good chance that the horrible horrible stakes that have been clearly spelled out are going to happen

maybe it's closer to helplessness than to terror, but it can still get pretty intense
\n(From here. If you haven't posted already, please consider doing so.)

I'm going to phrase this question to JC directly, but if you have a similar experience or insight, feel free to post as well.

JC, helplessness is definitely a form of terror. Do you get the feeling, though, that when dreading a dice roll you're actually dreading an outcome in a fiction you're immersed in? Or are you dreading the dice roll for the same reason you'd dread the outcome of a dice roll in Monopoly that will likely land your little hat in jail?

In other words, are you feeling dread on a purely system/gamist/"real" level, or do you actually feel some fraction of the burden of the fictional participants in the conflict?

Follow-up question - regardless of your answer - is the dread a "good" kind of dread, where if things turn out not in your favour you still walk away feeling satisfied to have been a part of something pretty scary? Or a "bad" kind of dread where if the dice roll fails, you walk away upset at the outcome? This is, again, a reality/fiction dichotomy, I think: if something bad happens in a horror movie you're watching, it's satisfying. If something bad happens to you in real life, it's not satisfying.

Finally, how do you think the tension of the dice roll terror can be best replicated? What factors go into making it happen?

    Posted By: lachekIn other words, are you feeling dread on a purely system/gamist/"real" level, or do you actually feel some fraction of the burden of the fictional participants in the conflict?
    \nI had a moment in a game where my character did something difficult and heroic to save his companions' lives and then had to make a nearly impossible roll to survive himself. On the fictional level, I was definitely feeling the dread: "I'm probably going to die, and I don't even know if they made it out or not."

    On the "real" level, though, I was feeling anything but dread: "My guy's probably going to die! To save his friends! Who don't even know he thinks of them as friends! Awesome!"

    Of course, then the GM totally ruined the moment by cheating to keep my character alive, but that's a different issue altogether.
