\n Can't believe I didn't think of this before: what about perishable advantage dice (stationary or otherwise). Advantage dice that stick around or pile up can get to be a drag, so why not entice people to use them up in a blaze of glory?

Instead of defining particular advantages as either lasting or perishable (which bumps my "too complex" bone), in each conflict players could decide if they want to burn any advantages they have in exchange for more dice in that conflict -- probably double the normal dice for the advantage or something like that.

Players would be required to narrate how the advantage was used up in the all-out attempt: they fire every bullet out of the pistol and then throw it at the mummy, hack the fire axe into the crab-monster's shell so hard it gets stuck and yanked out of their hands, or (for example) crash their snowcone cart into a rampaging zombie elephant (peace out Jackson!). Win or lose, that advantage is gone.

- Normally ramping up the number of dice could really break the system, but in GC once you hit a certain number of dice your odds aren't really getting much better. And one conflict can only hurt the menace a little bit no matter what.

- It could encourage players to cash in the big stationary advantages for huge dice, which is probably dramatically better -- you should only try to lure the monster into the electrified fence once (shorting it out in a big kablooie) not over and over again to whittle it down -- boring!

- Less annoying accumulated inventory and looting bodies, since anyone with 2 conditions is going to absolutely double-down and try to save their own lives. Never die with bullets!

- Could also lead to interesting selfish situations: if someone is at 2 conditions they might double-down on big advantages that the other characters would rather save for a better time. Nothing like blowing the electric fence to try to save yourself (doubling down on 3 dice and destroying it) when the menace isn't even at 8 dice yet and is immune...

    John: Ben's idea doesn't change the one-shot-ness of GC. in fact, by burning out some of the advantage dice (stuff the protagonists use), it supports it. there is a different tracker for the antagonist (or whatever form the opposition takes), called Menace dice, which gradually grow from one to eight, and then are depleted again as the protagonists beat "it" in contests. (each roll beaten = -1 Menace die). those menace dice are only subtracted after their number has grown to eight, however- beating the menace in contests before that just means that your character isn't injured (which, you'll admit, is still valuable)
    You'll see that mucking with the advantage dice in any way won't affect the Menace dice.
    And what ben is suggesting, in my opinion, offers a great opportunity to push the game further into immediacy and past stagnation (in my experience, the plague of too-long running games.)

    \n I dig this. Definitely has a lot of potential in late-game confrontations with the menace, especially of the "This ends here!" final fight variety. Yeah, Ripley can pull out her constructobot suit, but then it falls out of the airlock.

    My only worry would be it unbalancing the not-really-balanced resources between the menace and the PCs. Like you say, if your character has 2 conditions, players are going to feel additional pressure to burn up their resources to save the characters' skins, whether it makes dramatic sense or no. For groups that have firmly grasped "all competition in GC is fake," that might not be much of a problem. But my sense is that most groups (who've only played the game, at most, once or twice) are only hanging on to that idea with one hand, trying not to let go. An additional incentive to strategize and try to survive at all costs might tip things in a non-optimal direction, even if, like you say, it wouldn't necessarily do much, mechanically.

    So, yeah, definitely give it a whirl and let me know how it goes. I'm thinking that the Gamma Edition might have a page or two in the back that's like "optional bonus rules that you should probably invoke a couple of times a game," including stuff like this and your "outrun the bear" sacrifice rule and ideas on how to destroy or transform parts of the map. Honestly, I think that kind of thing should be more common in game design: rules that can be invoked somewhat arbitrarily in cool situations, but would harm the game if people used them all the time. We should be able to trust players to not overdo it and ruin their own fun, yeah? Just like learning to fight effectively in a combat system, there's something to be said for helping people learn how to approach pacing and scene framing through play and some general advice, not strict mechanical guidelines.

    Posted By: Jonathan WaltonAn additional incentive to strategize and try to survive at all costs might tip things in a non-optimal direction, even if, like you say, it wouldn't necessarily do much, mechanically.
    \nI agree, that's a potential danger. Character-centric play is the big potential pitfall of Geiger Counter (also why I advocate going back to not having a 1-to-1 survivor/player ratio).

    Other than clearing out advantages, I like that (just like the "I don't have to outrun the bear..." idea) it gives the players something to decide about the conflict. Otherwise it's pretty passive -- once the director puts you in the fight you just roll and see your fate. I think the player should have have input into how hard the character fights to live. It lets the players shape the drama even after the director has framed the conflict ("this scene we don't run, we crash the car into it!"). Even if the character is going to die anyway, you can choose to go out in blaze of glory by burning your advantages. But you're right that doesn't mean the knee jerk answer should always be "blaze of glory!"

    I'm also hoping that both these rules encourage players to think about their individual character motivations in the scene rather than automatically becoming a unified front. Combined with the outrun the bear option you get some hard choices -- does my guy want to trigger the electric fence now even if it strands the other survivors on the far side with the menace? (go solo instead of grouping, burn the advantage, hope to be the high roller) One bastard with a nice doubled-down advantage can beat the rolls of a bunch of other teamed characters. Hopefully all those temptations push conflict between the characters.

    There's also something satisfying about getting to decide your character _isn't_ a bastard and doesn't betray the others (possibly even sacrificing themselves with the bear option to save everyone else).

    ...

    Can I just say that Geiger Counter: Gamma has a lovely ring to it? It's the irradiated edition!

    John, as Ben points out above, Geiger Counter really _is_ about the characters, what happens to them, what kind of people they are. But it's not about the characters _surviving_ necessarily, which is a kind of investment that people aren't really used to in roleplaying games. You're supposed to care about the characters but also knowingly put them in harm's way and have them do selfish things that doom them all. The game really wants the players to look at the big picture: some folks have to die in order for other folks to live. If anything, that's the theme that might be able to use some additional mechanical support, though I'm not sure yet how to do that. Perhaps advantage dice only appear on the board once people start taking conditions? And the groups of 2-3 dice only appear with character deaths? Something like that might work.\n
    Posted By: Number6intheVillageYou can't just rely on hoping that players will choose actions that make for a better story.
    \nI think I disagree with this. As long as players don't have clear mechanical incentives do something against the general direction of the story, they can be counted on to do things that make the story better (for them). I'm optimistic in that regard.
