    \n I was always fond of the way Conspiracy X made the players test their own psychic powers to use their character's (that is, you used an actual set of Zener cards and had to guess the symbol to have the PC's power work, and got more guesses as the character became more psychically powerful), but I think the current, Unisystem-based edition ditched that. Also the rest of the actual game system was kind of clunky.

    In general, I don't think most RPGs treat "psychic powers" any differently than any other kind of special power, so pretty much any game with an effects-based power system (HERO, Wild Talents, etc) would do. What about the psychics genre appeals to you? The Chronicle/Akira/Carrie "uncontrolled telekinetic fury of the unsettled mind" bit, the Dead Zone/classic seance style "fragile people that see too much" thing, Scanners/X-Men "the future of humanity in a world that's not yet ready" or something different?

    Hmm, now that I think about it, the common link in most of those stories is the power controlling the user, or at least having uncontrollable/undesirable side effects. Psi*Run does that well (but it's more suited for one-shots), but this is also bringing Don't Rest Your Head to mind... and it's already about dreams, which is pretty close to the whole collective unconscious/psychic potential thing. Bliss Stage is also close to that territory, though all the "action" takes place in dreams...

    \n Hmm. I think they come at the same basic idea via slightly different methods.

    Psi*Run uses Otherkind dice (as does Bliss Stage, actually), the gist of which is you get a die for each relevant factor in the roll, and then have to assign your successes and failures among those factors. So pretty much everything is a two-edged sword, and to use a resource is to also risk harm to it, and very often your rolls aren't so much about pure success and failure as deciding which losses are more or less acceptable to you; "I'm fine with failing this mission if it means my uncle doesn't get put in the line of fire". Or vice versa.

    I'm less familiar with DRYH, but the way I recall it is having several different die pools all rolled together, and different things happen depending on which die pool rolled the highest overall, right? So you have the same concept of "the final result is somewhat unpredictable and out of your control", but if only one pool can be treated as dominant, you don't get the "trouble spiking out along multiple paths on every roll" thing I think is interesting about Otherkind. On the other hand, cutting down on those multiple possible results of every roll might make the game run a little smoother, since you don't have to wrack your brain to make sense of multiple crises every time.
