    \n Hmmm. A couple things based on my experiences with playing PTA:

    Gamist play: As you surmise, it doesn't offer a lot of support for a Gamist CA. But it's pretty upfront about it; I know people who have refused to play it out-of-hand because it clearly doesn't offer what they're looking for.

    Simulationist play: This... this is tricky, I think. PTA does sorta suggest that it supports a Simulationist CA, but I'm not actually sure that's true. I mean, here's my take on what Simulationist PTA would actually look like:


    Director: We see, from above, a shot of the crime scene, with evidence markers scattered about...

    Player: How'd we get the budget for a crane shot here?

    Player 2: We picked up that sweet advertising from Nike and Gatorade, remember?

    Player: Oh yeah. Hey, did we roll to see if it was raining during the shoot? We're still saving money by shooting in Vancouver, aren't we?

    Director: Yep, I rolled that and it's overcast but not actually raining.

    Player 3: I can't believe I spent 6 hours in makeup for this.

    Director: Hey, it's not my fault your out-of-wedlock pregnancy is in full rotation on TMZ and The National Enquirer. How are those paparazzi rolls going, anyway?


    Now, I think a sufficiently motivated playgroup could pull that off with PTA. I think it'd be awkward, but achievable.


    Narrativist play: This is generally where PTA comes into its own. Most reports of failure with PTA I've seen have been failures to sufficiently engage with the Spotlight Character's Issue. The ruleset is perhaps not as explicit here as it could be, but I've seen a fair amount of subsequent advice from the author on this subject.

    A lot of other storygames with strong Narrativist support provide the Premise of the game right in the tin. PTA requires the group as a whole and also each individual character to generate their own Premises, if they're aiming toward a Narrativist CA.


    So that's my thoughts on PTA. My thoughts on your mode of writing... hrm. I can appreciate you're operating within a limit here. I guess I think I'd be more interested in seeing those words spent on a narrow, deep analysis than on a broad, shallow analysis. If it takes you 750 words to talk about Fan Mail, I'm okay with that as an audience member.

    Anyway, I just wanted to note that Roger's depiction of "simulationistic PTA" is probably a bit exaggerated. There's no reason you couldn't do sim-PTA without delving into the television metaphor any deeper than you do in nar-PTA. In fact, I've had the sort of aggravatingly simulationistic play in Universalis that I imagine sim-PTA would become. The feature I'm thinking of is absolute reign of the genre trope, to such an extent that all contributions to the game are judged on the basis of genre accord. Player characters are stock types, scenes are stock scenes and episode content is ruled not by character issues or character arc, but rather those issues and arc are deliberately chosen to not challenge received wisdom of the genre. Quality of play is measured based on how flawlessly the players manage to duplicate the tropes of the genre. You could basically do PTA like this almost without changing the game text; the only bit that would be absolutely contrary would be defining and resolving conflicts, which in the rules as written almost necessarily requires staking things that genre celebration wouldn't want to stake. Even that could be made to work if the players were careful to specifically choose Issues that are actually allowed and expected within the genre instead of choosing ones they care about themselves.

    In substance I concur with Roger, though: PTA is not very interesting if you don't buy into the character issues, and buying into them as a creative participant is pretty much narrativism on the go. It's not a game that would necessarily hamper other CAs that much, but it is supremely minimalistic in many ways: there is nothing there for players who won't buy into the character issues and care about how they're resolved. Basically, any freeformer group used to ignoring game text intention and mechanics will find that PTA is no worse for any CA than any other game they might care to play.

    \n For more positive genre-sim experiences, I'll refer to Dead of Night - that game has never let me down in this regard. I think that the reason for why DoN works so well has to do with the nature of the game's set-up, which encourages the GM to be clever in small ways in affirming and celebrating the genre, and with the tense, organic order of play when the game is actually executed based on that GM prep. It is definitely creative, but that creativity is directed at showing off this cool monster you made, basically. Nothing genre-breaking, nothing narrativistic, just a poetic notion of monstrosity embedded into a first-person's view of a horror movie conceit.

    I do agree with David in that the "absolute reign of the genre trope" is probably not that common in practical experience. That is, usually people don't uphold strict genre standards for their play over personal aesthetic judgment. Still, I've seen enough of this to know that it does exist, usually in groups that find coherent Exploration difficult. For instance, I've met anime gamers (the sort who play exclusively BESM or that newer similar game, the name of which I forget) who will basically be happy with a roleplaying session as long as the content transforms effortlessly into a genre-faithful post-play story - no interest in the procedure and interaction of the actual play itself, really. Might be that this phenomenon has something to do with bitter desperation: you've been trying to get together that perfect MERP campaign for so long that you've set aside any creative goals aside from enforcing that genre feel. I was a lot like this in my youth, in fact, now that I think of it. The foremost concern in many of our games was about "playing right", which meant playing to the genre and not fucking it up with teenager miscommunications and differing cultural experiences. Call of Cthulhu, for instance - that was basically just a couple of us Lovecraft fans trying to bash the rest of the group into playing the way Lovecraft would've wanted.

    I'm totally open to the idea that when taken to the extremes this sort of genre-fidelity is just a sign of inability in forming a functional SIS, and therefore it lacks a coherent creative agenda to begin with. I'm pretty sure that less extreme forms of this sort of thing are genuine simulationism - I can see almost all the steps in my own infrequent sim gaming of various sorts.

    \n Jason: an occasion of play is not simulationistic merely because it exists. That is, even if we can describe an occasion of roleplaying as simulating/emulating some type of static fiction, that does not determine anything about the Creative Agenda of the effort. The fact that the game simulates (intentionally using the confusing word here, wait for the punchline) television just describes an useful systematic framework the players use to shape and communicate the fiction. What makes a game simulationistic or narrativistic or in general something in CA terms is the underlying purpose in the creation of that fiction.

    What makes Primetime Adventures powerfully narrativism-facilitating is that its procedures are reliable in seeking out interesting angles on the character Issues and allowing players to address them via character choices. I'm saying that creative commitment to the Issues is necessary for narrativism because that's the narrativistic pay-off in its naked form: thematic choices in a story are only thematic when you care about them, and you'll only care about them in PTA if you have what I call "creative commitment" to the Issues that underlie the story and the choices. By "creative commitment" or "creative buy-in" I mean that you're actually interested in the Issue, considerate of the difficulty a protagonist might have in handling it and eager to see it established in a fictional framework, developed and ultimately resolved based on that development.

    In comparison, that whole head-space is not really present in simulationism: I could see players choosing Issues in a simulationistic game as well, but those choices wouldn't be based on personal interest and appreciation of the Issue's philosophical appeal - rather, you'd choose Issues that are appropriate to the character's role in the story framework you're developing. For example, you'd pick "Authority" as your cop character's Issue because you know that somebody is supposed to be a loose cannon in the cop show you're making. You don't have a personal commitment to figuring out this authority thing, you just know that somebody needs to struggle with authority in a real cop show, so you put in the requisite amount of struggling and use that as a springboard for appreciating the forms and style of a cop show. You might well even enjoy roleplaying the struggle - in fact, it is very common for simulationism that you do enjoy depicting a struggle with authority, alcoholism or whatever problems characters happen to have. However, that enjoyment comes from getting to act out and interpret - celebrate - the process of unfolding a piece of fiction in accord with your aesthetic goals, not from being personally invested in and sympathetic of the difficult choices the character faces.

    And yes, "simulationism" is a potentially misleading word, just like "narrativism" and "gamism". The great majority of people using these words think that "gamism" means crunch-heavy, "simulationism" means realist and "narrativism" means story-pertaining. That's just tough shit, not much for me to do about it. Probably that sort of terminological arbitrariness is just not a big deal for me because this is a foreign language to me to begin with; every word in English is just as arbitrary for me insofar as emotional connection goes.

    \n What Vernon's getting at is that your average genre-faithful simulationistic set-up will include things like getting chewed by the Sarge because that sort of thing happens in this type of story, and not because of the actual consequences the character "really" risks. The player in a game like this won't expect his character to actually get the boot from the police force - after all, Dirty Harry gets complaints, but he doesn't get fired, so why should I? In contrast, it would be typical of a narrativist mind-set to get inside the head of the cop character by really making career disaster a real possibility; the reason for having the scene in the first place, engaging game mechanics, being invested in it would be that the character's career is really at stake here, and we're really getting inside the character's skin by finding out whether the player chooses to try to make reparations, or whether the character's sense of justice really makes him little more than a vigilante with a badge.

    In a nutshell: a strongly narrativist game would include the scene where your cop is threatened by losing his job as a set-up for a situation where the character has to choose between his job and his sense of justice or whatever. A strongly simulationist game would include the scene because it is an expected part of the development of events in this type of story. Presumably any CA might also skip the scene altogether on the premise that you don't generally play scenes without any point at all. The CA can tell us why a scene exists as part of the transcript of play.

    I should note that I'm not describing all narrativism and all simulationism here. There certainly are forms of both sim and nar where the nature of a daredevil-cop-gets-a-reprimand scene would be the reverse of the above. For instance, if the game was My Life with Master or similar, it'd be clear that losing the job would be outside the bounds of the story space - the GM would just figure out some nasty and immoral punishment for the character, who certainly wouldn't get the option to quit; thus the very nature of the scene transforms from conflict background to arbitrarily evil consequences for prior failure. Similarly a naturalist-realist simulationistic game would likely let the character lose his job and then perhaps switch to a new character, depending on the campaign's direction. It all depends on the methodology actually, practically utilized in a given game to get a handle on the Creative Agenda.

    The potentially confusing part here is that it's pretty arbitrary to decide that a narrativist or simulationist game "has to be" more or less dramatic (in the Trifold sense) than the other. It's just that in this particular discussion context we're talking about dramatic games, and ritual processes are a typical technical part of dramatic genre-sim: we play through the motions typical of the genre because we enjoy playing through them, and we're relying on those motions to reliably keep the game on the rails. In this type of game a player would be quite fair in getting annoyed if the GM declared that his daredevil cop buddy character actually gets fired and sued and removed from the campaign for doing something typical of the genre, such as being a bit too liberal with the interrogation technique. The whole point of living the dream in genre-sim is to be able to trust that as long as Spock speaks the Spock lines and Kirk speaks the Kirk lines everything will come to be well in the end. Messing with that by bringing in out-of-genre consequences is a breach of trust.
