\n So I think Matthijs has raised a very interesting topic of discussion. I think that most of us are toeing the same party line, that recommending your friends games is perfectly okay. There are a few dissenting voices, which is perfectly okay.
\n
\nThe point of this thread is different than that other one. The point of this thread is to tell us about your friends' great games.
\n
\nFriend, for the definition of this post, is someone whom you have had more than cursory contact with. Someone you have interacted with only in passing on the message boards, for instance, is not a friend. As such, most of you are not my friends for the purposes of this thread. Do not recommend any game that you love to distraction if the person who made it is not your friend by this definition.
\n
\nWhen you tell us how great your friend's game is, tell us why you love it. Tell us what it does that makes it great. Posts in this thread should be for that purpose, or for asking for more information about a game.
\n
\nI'll kick this off with two great games made by friends of mine that I have regrettably not played in years.
\n
\nFastlane, by Alexander Cherry: This game is great because it took a silly concept and turned it into a tight, well executed game that has managed to reliably produce powerful and fun situations either in extended play or in one-shots. It's daring, asking gamers to go way beyond what they're used to when it comes to accouterments. That, by itself, makes me love this game.
\n
\nSitting down to play it during playtesting, and a couple times since has made me love it more. I love the fact that luck allowed me to have more mechanical narrative power than the GM. I love the fact that the same luck of the spin brought another player up enough to kick my ass, the very same dude I hammered badly earlier in the game when he'd refused to owe my character favors. The flexibility of the concept of favors is amazing. I hacked a computer by humbling it to owe me a favor. How fucking cool is that? I called in favors to disable security alarms, open doors, and delay a self-destruct sequence.
\n
\nThe mechanics make for convoluted situations of debts, bought loyalties and shifting alliances. This shit happened with players used to the party mentality as naturally as if we'd been playing this way for years. The game, while completely without setting, keeps that signature feel whether you're playing sci-fi on a Las Vegas planet, or gritty superheroes.
\n
\nThe Riddle of Steel, by Jacob Norwood: I loved this game from minute one of doing any research. It's a big, unapologetically crunchy game with traditional mechanics. The combat system is a fun game all by itself. I spent hours dueling with another fan of the game, just putting the combat system through it's paces. What I like about this is that you can just play the game, and the mechanics describe what happens in the fiction. When I stab someone in the chest, it's not an abstract subtraction of wound points. The mechanical description is that I just stabbed someone in the fucking chest. A rapier entering my throat and going up into my brain will kill my ass. (and did, on at least two occasions, while dueling against Julianos Vainsteel)
\n
\nThen you bring in the concept of SAs. I was skeptical at first. I've been conditioned to be skeptical about mechanical implementations of personality mechanics. But this one was different than what I've seen before. Foremost, it's a carrot, not a stick. I mean, sure.. There's some talk about using SAs punitively, but Jake admitted that he's never used it, and recommends against it. What it comes down to is the, to me, at that time, revolutionary concept of deciding what is important to you about your character, GOING for it, and being rewarded for doing that, and only for doing that.
\n
\nSo you've got a combination of a great crunchy combat system, and mechanics that reward you for playing your character to the hilt and without apology. What's not to love about that?

    \nDuty & Honour

    At Gen Con 2007, there I was on the Play Collective stand. A bloke from Newcastle (in England) comes up and asks if he can talk about his game. The chap was Neil Gow and his game was Duty & Honour. His description of what he wanted the game to be was instantly captivating: it would be stories of the Peninsular Wars, told in the style of the Sharpe novels. Great! Now, Neil was a bit unsure about how to develop and produce the game.

    Fast forward.

    Now, it's late 2008 and Duty & Honour is out there in public, brandishing a sabre and rallying to Wellingtons flag! I love this game for two main reasons:

    1) It captures wonderfully the feeling of derring-do you get in the Sharpe books. It also manages to handle a military game, with all the complexities of rank, orders, missions and so forth, in a way that enhances the roleplaying and makes it all fun and exciting.

    2) I've been lucky enough to watch D&H grow from an idea into a published book and give my own tiny bit of help along the way. Neil has done a great job and, moreover, he's a credit to himself and the principles of mutualism.

    Covenant

    I would also draw your attention an underappreciated game that has been out for a couple of years now. Covenant by Matt Machell provided probably the single best gaming experience I had in 2007. The popular notion of the 1990s conspiracy RPG is turned on its head, where the conspiracy has failed and now you, as members of that selfsame conspiracy, have to struggle with the reality of the situation, to come to terms with what is and isn't true. The way that Covenant sets the game up is really key to what makes it great. Cell creation is deceptively simple but feeds into the characters in a way that means right from the off, you have a whole web of relationships, intrigues, friendships and rivalries. A great game that gets too little attention.

    \n Thanks for finding the positive in that thread, Lance.

    I keep saying I love my friend Anna Kreider's Polaris hack, Thou Art But A Warrior, so I'll continue to flack for it here. TABAW is everything I like and nothing I don't. It "corrects" all the things that make Polaris a less than perfect fit for me - it's rooted in something I can relate to and learn about (The Reconquista!), it's populated with characters I can appreciate and care about (Muslim Iberia!), and it has both an index and table of contents. It does all this while leaving room for wonder, and fantasy, and the will of God to manifest itself in terrible and awe-inspiring ways. So for me the really cool, fun stuff about Polaris (the rotating division of authority, the phrases and compromise built into resolving conflicts, the built-in tragic arc) is retained and even amplified by telling an essentially human story. Plus (like Polaris) the art's great.

    \n So, some games from people that I've had crazy sex with on an oil rig in an electric storm after we saved each others' lives during the war:

    - A Trip Through Time Seen Through The Eyes of a Fir Tree (Lasse Lundin), where players are big trees, talking slowly and making whooshing noises. Very much an experience; very little a game.

    - L\xf8ken Committee to Evaluate Cultural Grants (H\xe5ken Lid), where players are bureaucrats in the Committee, discussing relevant questions - first, of course, cultural grants; but later questions can be \u201cWhy doesn\u2019t anyone want to make love to me?\u201d or \u201cIs there life after death?\u201d. All questions are discussed in the same detached, bureaucratic tone. Wonderful.

    - Itras By (Ole Peder Gi\xe6ver + Martin Bull Gudmundsen), because of the open setting ("find a section in the book that you don't like and cross it out, then write a new one yourself"), the community effort surrounding it (collaborative design, setting wiki), the chance cards ("time stops", "play this scene as a musical"), the action/resolution cards ("yes, but...", "no, and...")

    - Muu (Tomas M\xf8rkrid), a game of empathic creatures with a collective identity ("Muu is Muu"), who are afraid of crows and love strawberries and snuggling.

    Sadly, none are available in English at the moment, but we can always hope. In the meanwhile, there are lots of good games over at Norwegian Style, which is essentially a blog devoted to the concept "My friends' games are great!"

    \n I live with Judd. His Sorcerer supplement, Dictionary of Mu, is brutal dusty poetry. Check the entry on Friend for one of my favorite gathering of words ever. The "add your own entries to the Dictionary" mechanic is something that needs to be, in some form, in ever single game. I love collaborative worldbuilding, and tying it into play is heavenly. I haven't seen 1st Quest since I helped playtest it way back in the early stages of getting to know him, but if it's gotten any better I'll have trouble keeping my pants on the next time I play it. Reversing Keys so that you hand out XP to people who engage your character is just a brilliant and natural addon to the TSOY system.

    I watched Rob's son while he went to a wedding. Misspent Youth has been fun every single time I played it. Neotraditionalist Inuit youths, and dystopic fast food wageslaves were the two games I've been in. I really want to play a full run of it. The ways your traits change into "Selling Out" is tragic and perfect. You can fight the power, but you can't keep yourself from eventually becoming it.

    I've slept on Russell's couch and had my feet attacked by his cat. He's showed me more Zero Punctuation episodes while I've sat on his couch than I've watched of my own volition. His game Contract Work is still in the shop, but it fills a niche that I really want to be filled. Get a target, work backwards through the hit, and work through to the end - what went right and what went wrong? Who's become a liability?

    I have more friends with games but I'll limit it to the people that I feel closest to that design games.
