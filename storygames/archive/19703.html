\n Locally, we're organizing a "Nano RPG Day". So we're basically trying out a lot of RPGs in various 2 hour sessions.
I would like to ask you for suitable RPGs (nano, micro, rules-lite, one-shot, whatever...) that may be interesting and worthwhile for trying out.

I know there are tons of them. So, to keep it simple:
- Please recommend up to 3 of your favorite nano/micro/lite etc. RPGs/games/one-shots for a 2hour session
- optional: additionally post one of your own creation

    \nWhat You Wish For is very good. It's my new default system for short, intense roleplaying. I play it with Dixit cards for better effect.

    Argentin is a very nice larp about the minions of a losing evil overlord. Players partially create their characters answering a questionnaire. Just discard most of the mechanics and it plays as well.

    All the President's Zombies works very well too.

    PS: for a bonus game, just in case you end quickly: never tried The Mask but always wanted to. It sounds very creepy.

    \n Silly games are great as icebreakers. It's no coincident that I'm making over-the-top games that brings out laughter. It's great for building a group feeling before continuing to games with more serious topics.

    If @Simon_Pettersson released his two page game Drakar och bananer (eng. Dragons and Bananas - guess what that game focuses on), then I could translate it into English ... possibly after a half of bottle of wine, like when I translated Dr Magnethands (same type of game) into Swedish.

    I can also mention my two page parlor game The Murder of Mr. Crow, which is played in 90-120 minutes.\n
    I have called you all here to shed some light on the murder of Mr. Crow.
    However, this is not a time for tears, but a time to discover the truth.
    Look around you\u2014in this room right now is Mr. Crow\u2019s murderer!
    You are about to find out who did the deed.
    \nThe fun lays in getting the notion of having a murder plot be created and fleshed out through improvised dialogues. To realize afterwards what you all have created together.

    [edit] DaveyJJ have also reskinned the game to England during the post-war.

    \n There are lots of games I can run in two hours, if I keep focused and keep the scenes short. GM familiarity and energy are important as well.

    In the past, I've run Fiasco, InSpectres and Do: Pilgrims of the Flying Temple in that time frame. For Inspectres and Do, that gives enough time to create characters and run at least two adventures. Fiasco often goes a little longer, but if you aggressively edit scenes and have a small group and pay attention to time, you should be fine.

    \n I've run Microscope games in two hours. Also, if murder mystery is your thing, you can finish a session of Graham Walmsley's brilliant 1930s A Taste for Murder in that span of time, including a tea break (though we push the game into a 1950s, post-war setting in Britain).

    Rickard's mystery game mentioned above is also good. We modified it to the same genre (1950s post-war Britain) because we're fans of that specific setting and don't use animal-driven last names, but the mechanics are inventive and work very well. Probably the most succinct murder RPG I've found, and I'm always on the look-out for those.

    \n BeePeeGee. Well, it isn't as such. But there is the assumption that the players will form pairs consisting of a witch and a cat.

    One such pair and a GM is to me the minimum group for WQ. And such a game is doable in 2 hours.

    Two such pairs makes for GM+4, and a group that is familiar with the system can do a 2 hour game easily. I'd be hesitant over doing it with beginners though. Once the the time lost in chargen, explaining and answering questions has been subtracted there would not be enough time left to run a good size adventure with sufficient spotlight for every player in a two hour slot. At three hours It should not be a problem.

    The game itself however is brilliantly suited for large groups. The pair mechanism essentially halves the strain on the GM who usually address either the witches, the cats or one pair. 8+GM games are both feasible and enjoyable. Pairs not involved in the current spotlight often interact internally, cats making demands of their witches etc.

    Once I ended up with five players at the table, I solved it by giving one of the witches twin cats as familiar. It worked OK, but I usually push another game if the players don't make even pairs.
