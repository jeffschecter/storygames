    \n I posted my thoughts about a MLP:FiM Apocalypse World hack on the AW forums, but for you all, here they are:

    Look, you have 6 playbooks:

    -Twilight Sparkle
    -Pinkie Pie
    -Rainbow Dash
    -Apple Jack
    -Rareity
    -Fluttershy

    The GM in this game is Princess Celestia, since she controls pretty much everything in Equestria. But when the ponies enter the Everfree Forest...control leaves Princess Celestia's hands real quick, and the game takes over. That's an aside, though!

    There's not much advancement: cartoon characters don't change their inherent natures or abilities much. I still want to keep the experience system, though, because it works well, is fun, and goads players into action. One choice for an advancement is to switch characters next session (episode). I imagine a lot of character switching, with not everypony in every episode. Maybe some small advancements as well, like stat boosts or one new move.

    Princess Celestia's prep is pretty different. Maybe organized like a front, but a whole new one every episode. This will probably be where the hardest design work is done.

    I also think: less stats. Maybe three per character (+ Friendship/Hx), with five total stats, so each character is missing two core stats and thus some core Moves. My instinct is to simplify, and make the characters very distinct to reward switching characters and trying out new things.

    Obviously, stats will be renamed, basic moves will be retooled/changed, and new playbooks written up.

    This is simply a post of curiosity. I want to know what you all think about this (you'll probably have to know MLP:FiM to parse this post, but hopefully not the game. Ideally it'll be playable by young girls, and aimed at family play and pony geek brony play). I'm going to give it my best shot, anyway, until I decide AW isn't the right engine and do something else, or give up on it, or playtest the hell out of it and make it a real thing.

    I suppose it is just too easy to suggest Capes for the job. It doesn't even need any mechanical hackery to do the setting, just a proper Comics Code, one which is even not that far off the default one. The idea of folks only buying into conflicts they find interesting pays off straight in.

    \n

    Likewise, Happy Birthday, Robot! is kind of too easy to suggest, but telling stories with themes and settings you've decided on with your group beforehand works out surprisingly well. Minor hackery: All pre-established setting characters and locations are free words. (More reason to call-back to established things.)

    \n

    But while we're talking Daniel Solis games, Do: Pilgrims of the Flying Temple looks to be eminently twistable to reflect a series of characters with self-revealing personality-laden names who go wrestle with others' problems. If anything might bring it up short, its that Do characters evolve and change relatively rapidly while MLP:FiS characters -- pointedly -- don't. Only minor hackery necessary, though, if you wanted that to be different.

    \n

    I'd be very tempted to try and do something with the MLP-verse using Microscope, but I fear that'd rapidly go well beyond the scope of the show ... sort of by necessity. That established, think of the sheer level of electric epic you could get from introducing the Legacy: "Queen Celestia's Unicorn Horn and Wings."

    \n

    If I wanted to pick a game to just outright straight-up hack hard on a system for MLP, I'd be terribly tempted to go in with hammer and tongs on Remember Tomorrow. Why? Because its a wonderful system I think more needs to be done with and it would seem to crank out some crazy, over the top intertwined stories. Beyond that? No good reason at all save sheer perversity and the fact that no one seems to ever talk about it.

    \n

    Oh, yes, MLP seems a perfect setting for a Solipsist character, but that way lies madness. No, really, madness.

    \n

    (Pretty much anything to get away from the constant references to Apocalypse World or Dogs in the Vinyard, not only for the reasons others have suggested but also because sometimes it feels like SG is as obsessed with only having two answers as RPGNet is with only answering Savage Worlds or Exalted. I like both AW and DitV, but it sometimes seems like SG forgets that they have a hundred other games that are actually pretty darn compelling.)

    I don't think the ponies are different in the kinds of intricate ways that D&D/AW style stats imply. I'd rather use Puppetland; everypony gets a set of "are, can, can't" statements from their pony type, and then you add three to each list as you like, but they're probably related to the particular pony's personality and/or cutie mark.

    \n

    Everypony can talk, and move, and think, and pick things up, and do other basic actions. Everypony also has a job/art/talent, except baby ponies who are still in school.

    \n

    Earth ponies are: Strong and solid, professional, social, part of a big family.
    \nEarth ponies can: Do a good (job), excel physically, run fast, rule over the elements of the earth when they work together, buck powerfully with their hooves.
    \nEarth ponies can't: Fly, do unicorn magic, walk on clouds, hold more than one thing without using saddlebags.

    \n

    Unicorns are: Gentle and clever, solitary (but not reclusive), trained in (art).
    \nUnicorns can: (Do art) with unicorn magic, move around things within a few yards with unicorn magic, make a light with their horns, poke things, learn a few new magic tricks.
    \nUnicorns can't: Fly, rule the earth, walk on clouds, control the weather, jump long distances.

    \n

    Pegasus ponies are: Quick, light and energetic, focused, good at working in teams.
    \nPegasus ponies can: Fly with their wings, run very fast, walk on clouds, show off their (talent), control the weather when they work together, move silently if they don't have to touch the ground, hover.
    \nPegasus ponies can't: Rule the earth, use unicorn magic, do jobs where they need to be heavy, strong, or steady.

    \n I've finished a first version of a hack for Mythender. I haven't had an opportunity to play it yet though, so progress has stopped.

    There are six basic pony types, with six categories of cutie marks, you then pick which element of harmony you most closely represent. There are then a couple questions you answer based on the choices you made.

    The overall theme is pitting individuality against group membership. You can do things that empower the group, giving your friends additional resources, preventing the groups combined relationship from being overcome... or you can do things that show off and grow your individuality, making you more potent at overcoming obstacles (and drawing from group resources). I think it'll do a decent job of imitating the 2-part episodes.
