\n I've been leading InSpectres for years now in conventions, and This Is Pulp last year, where the whole experience depends on the group rather than just the game master. It's been fun, creative and different every time we've played. One thing I noticed when playing collaborate storytelling games like Archipelago, While the World Ends, Svart av kval - vit av lust, InSpectres and Fiasco is that instead of depending on one person, the game experience depends on the whole group. But after a while - lets say a half year - with a single group, the difference that I felt was so thrilling from conventions has disappeared. People tend to do or come up with almost the same things over and over again. I'm sure that I'm not the only that have one noticed this..?

On a related note. A few years ago, I burned myself out as a game master. I started to improvise more and more loosely until it finally burned out all my imagination. I wanted to be different every time but after a while, I recognized patterns in my own imagination and it all felt the same. It got boring. I was so fed up with roleplaying games that I couldn't even think of playing it for a half year. I took a step back and started to plan out the scenarios more (and helped developed the fish tank model during that time). I took inspiration from different sources and got new input to my imagination.

Now while playing collaborate storytelling games in my group, I'm starting to loose the spark once again, just as when I felt burned out as a game master. It's always improv and I feel that my creative spark has faded. Is it really so that you only got a certain creative space? I wonder how to avoid that in the kind of storytelling games that I'm playing.

    Rickard, can you pin down what you are getting "too much of the same" of in your improv gaming? I find that helpful. The same kinds of characters? The same settings? The same types of event sequences and story arcs?
    \nThis is a good question. A question that is hard to answer. A lot of answers have been about me, but the trouble I'm having is also with the group. As I pointed before, I'm tired of the "Frame a scene, create a conflict, resolve it, frame a new scene" structure, but that's not the whole picture. I can play games with my group that have other kinds of structures and it still wears me out, but like I said in the first post, I have no trouble playing InSpectres on conventions. I know what I will bring to the table, so the surprise for me will be what the others will do.

    When it comes to creating conflicts, the group usually falls back on somebody being killed, framed, raped or there's been a secret which involves incest or several people being with one person. Instead of getting a strong emotion from the twist, it's more of a "Oh, and here's the incest again. I was waiting for that".

    When it comes to playing, I want to bring my kind of fantasy to the table. I don't know how many times I've inserted an ape king in This Is Pulp on conventions, because it will be a new thing for the new players. Play Unsafe tells us "Do the obvious thing" but that only works for a while. You can see this in the American version of Whose Line Is It Anyway (an improv show I love), where you start to recognize the jokes after a while. This happens in the group as well, where we try to (naturally) escalate our imaginative story elements from one session to another - even if we're playing two different games - so we don't repeat ourselves. This means that stranger and stranger will be inserted in the story which means that we sometimes can't hold the story together because of those elements. Play Unsafe tells us to be dull and boring, but it's the same thing there. Dull and boring becomes repetitive after a while with the same group.

    Storytelling games that we've played a lot seems to fall together because we wants to avoid to repeat ourselves, but strangely enough, also becomes boring to me (not the group) because we have the same kind of conflicts in the game. That's why I've started to question "conflicts" in roleplaying games and instead started to lean against "uncertainty" in stories. But the thing is, this is not something I feel in traditional games with a GM. Why is that? That when just one person have prepared a story, it becomes more interesting for me than if the whole group of people I normally play with improvises a story together.

    \n Because the best stories were never created by committee.

    Eventually in a group, no matter what kind of game you play, everyone learns each other's "moves". This means that you have increased familiarity and that's fun and comforting, but you almost completely lose the ability to surprise each other. The way to beat this is to scramble up the group, add some new people, or maybe have everyone else play in a group that doesn't include anyone else for a while. Then you'll find yourself doing things differently and when you come back you will bring with you some new exciting stuff.

    \n Rickard, I feel like I've experienced a similar thing! A need for conflict arises and is identified, someone responds in a way that works well, and enjoyable conflict ensues, but then subsequently the process becomes predictable. I think in collaborative improv, though the nature of a conflict might be surprising, the timing never is. You never just find yourself in conflict unexpectedly. No one has the authority to smack the rest of the group with "nope, no conflict, the NPC does what you want" and then later "boom, suddenly this happens, you must deal with it now!" the way a trad GM can.

    I wonder if this could be overcome by every player in an improv game bringing some Sorcerer-style "bangs" to the table? Encouraging individual, outside inspiration, as opposed to socially responsive crowd-pleasing? Or some other system of competing for narration rights?

    A few more specific thoughts:\n
    When it comes to creating conflicts, the group usually falls back on somebody being killed, framed, raped or there's been a secret which involves incest or several people being with one person.
    These sound to me like they describe a very small range of genres and tones. Do you have the option to be subtler, or more grandiose? Can you do farcical pettiness, kid-friendly naivete, or super-heroic world-saving? Or is your group not interested in that sort of variety?\n
    we try to (naturally) escalate our imaginative story elements from one session to another - even if we're playing two different games - so we don't repeat ourselves. This means that stranger and stranger will be inserted in the story which means that we sometimes can't hold the story together because of those elements.
    I think I've experienced this most when it's "let's make a conflict for us, the players" time, and least when it's deep character study time. If you have the time to really establish who a character is and what they care about, such that everyone's interested in that, then conflict becomes much more about specific context (what was the character's mood when the bad news arrived?) and less about the broad strokes (murder? incest? something really strange?).

    I had a Sorcerer game where the nerdy NPC my character picked on decided he wanted to follow my example and become a sorcerer. In some contexts, that might have been boring, or hilarious. But for my character, it was a big psychological turning point, because he'd been trying so hard to justify the demonic stuff he'd been doing, that now he had to see if he really was proud of his path enough to lead another down it.

It has nothing to do with what we talk about above and below, but it's something worth noticing.\n

It worked in the beginning but now it's fallen apart and becoming clich\xe9. To be able to play out more mundane things, like the ones you gave examples of, I think it has to be more grounded early in the game. Otherwise, it will burn out your imagination.
