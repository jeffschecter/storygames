\n Over in Stuff to Watch, J. Walton mentions
\n
\n
the "proceduralization" of roleplaying (which honestly, I think the recent indie games movement can take a fair amount of credit for)
\n
\n
\nThis seems to me like one of many points of overlap between indie games and the OSR. One of the big aha moments for me in engaging with Moldvay's Basic D&D; with New York Red Box was the very explicit statement of the procedures running through wandering monster (or random room stocking) > surprise > reaction > if combat, initiative > fighting > morale > pursuit and evasion; recently procedures have been proving really powerful for me in roleplaying with kids. Over at Legends & Labyrinths, George Strayton has written up procedures for month, day, and turn sequences (link to PDF) that very neatly formulate & codify all this tradition's procedures in one document.
\n
\nWhat are some indie games examples of proceduralized roleplaying? What can we learn from them?

    \n You mean stuff like having a set number of scenes during a game, sometimes with specific instructions for each/some/all of those scenes? And related procedures that you find in GM-less games (or whatever you want to call them) where certain GM responsibilities are handled by the game rules or distributed to all players?

    In Shock: and Mars Colony you play through a (mostly) set number of scenes and then see where you've gotten to at the end. You can still do whatever you want in those scenes, but you have to say up-front what you want to get out of them, and you roll your dice to resolve the scene as opposed to specific actions within it.

    In Burning Empires there's a scene economy, you can only play a certain number of certain types of scenes before you have to roll to see which side of the war is winning. There's different types of scenes, so if you used up all your opportunities to roll dice early, you have to wait while the other players get their spotlight time.

    In The Shab al-Hiri Roach and Grey Ranks there is a sort-of "set list" of scenes you play through that follow a timeline and require certain elements, though there is lots of freedom beyond that (especially in the Roach). Both of these also feature dice for scene resolution, not task resolution.

    In Spione, players all take turns framing scenes round-robin style, and you're encouraged to build towards "flashpoint" (a conflict or crisis) and then you go to cards to resolve the situation.

    Um, there's the ritualized phrases in Polaris, too.

    (caveat: I might not be accurately representing each of these games, with such abbreviated summaries.)

    \nPerfect, Unrevised follows a strict set of procedures. There are scene cycles, composed of 4 scene types (Crime, Discovery, Retribution, Reflection), and they loop. The entirety of the game is held within those 4 scene types. Of them, dice are rolled in Discovery and Retribution. These are tests that define the scope and contents of the scene.

    The variables that make that tight framework interesting are: character motivation mattering, mechanical options during every scene, different types of Discovery Scenes (Capture, Establishing a Hold) and Retribution Scenes (Conditioning, Interrogation, Creating Guilt), a focal antagonistic relationship that gets more interesting and complicated as the cycle repeats.

    \n Im thinking whether it has a GM or not, you can still detail procedural stuff to help people play the game the way you think it ought to be.

    There is a famous software book called Design Patterns, which is nothing more than a list of a couple of dozen generalized solutions to common programming problems, that people who have a lot of experience with programming have found to be the best approach.

    I reckon the same thing for an RPG design. You the designer, presumably have an idea of how your game should be played, and on playtesting have found that certain approaches to certain fictional situations work best for your vision and with your ruleset. Listing those general fictional situations and the procedure for dealing with them would be proceduralization, wouldnt it?

"GM describes world -> state what you are doing -> resolve action" is pretty central to almost all RPGs, certainly trad ones.
    \nThat's definitely true, but it got lost almost immediately. I played D&D for decades, starting in 1980, without ever having a firm explicit grasp on its procedural cycles, until the old-school renaissance caused me to discover (I didn't play Moldvay Basic back in the day) and appreciate what they were and why they were awesome. Exposure to indie approaches, e.g. Play Unsafe, definitely helped me understand how going through a set of steps & rolling dice for each one wasn't going to have to produce the meaningless & dull wandering monster encounters that I heard badmouthed for most of my D&D life - either because the people slagging these procedures hadn't really tried them for themselves (lots of key old-school ideas, like XP for gold, got similarly rejected out of hand without understanding what they were there to do), or because they'd experienced these procedures in the hands of a DM who wasn't good at/sympathetic to improv.

    I think that D&D lost sight of its procedures in part because of bloat - all the same systems are in the AD&D DMG I grew up with, but they're hard to pick out from all the extra verbiage that's not in Basic D&D. Also in part because D&D increasingly started trying to become a go-anywhere, do-anything game of epic fantasy; the procedures in Moldvay or Legends & Labyrinths work because they assume that dungeon- or hex-crawling to find treasure is going to be the activity of play you'll want to proceduralize (which isn't to say that other stuff won't happen, just that it's not assumed to need the same structure).

    Note: I don't think anyone in the OSR knows what callers are for; I've had 15 players at the table for OD&D and not felt like callers would be helpful. There might well be a wargaming situation they come from - maybe where one side has many sub-commanders and one person is in charge of gathering up all their orders and transmitting them to the referee - but I don't know about it if so. If David Wesely is at Gen Con again this year I'll ask him about it.
