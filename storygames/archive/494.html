Playing Passive

urbanpagan
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games
This is a new thread started out of the Playing Across Gender Lines thread.

I've tried many times to play passive characters and none have ever seemed fulfilling. Sometimes, people I've played with for long periods of time have asked me if I was 'okay'. "You're not doing anything. Are you alright? Do you feel well?" I've seen many other players at the same tables as I have sat at, play a passive character and then upon reflection after the game, most (if not all) have talked about how they disliked their characters in one way or another.

I begin to wonder... is it that passive characters are counter productive in role playing games? As I see it, the whole point in playing a PC in a game is to do things. Things maybe you can't do in real life. Things you've always wanted to do. Etc., etc.

Some people have told me that I have a 'strong personality'. Could that be part of it? I'm not normally passive in my real life thus it's hard for me to play one? Yet I have no troubles playing a man, a child, a senior citizen, an elf, and orc, or what have. (These are all things I'm not as well.)

Am I wrong? Am I missing something? Any thoughts?

Lisa P

    I think it's hard to play a passive PC, because while you can have a passive protagonist, in a game your experiences are defined by what you're putting into the play. If you passivity is manifested as nothing happening (or indeed, if activity is manifest by changing nothing) there's nothing there to experience or enjoy. If passivity is manifested as more subtle actions, or explicitly making a show of buckling to or folding to potential activity, or by showing your steadfast willingness to stay passive in spite of large changes, then there's something interesting to see.

    I think I had this with a player in my Dogs game, although I think it went well. Her player had to make a very difficult and traumatic choice early on, and later player her character as largely shell-shocked and quiet; she had her own concept of how she wanted to take her character, and it was this. I'm not sure if she herself felt engaged during this time (I was concerned at times) but this recently resolved as her character more or less "broke", nearly screaming her aggravation and disillusionment and making a shocking choice*. So, in this case: I'm not sure if her passivity was well done, or simply gave her the space to push her character where she wanted.

    Conflict = Character + Desire + Obstacle

    One kind of passive is lack of character goals and desires. If this is the case, no conflict ever 'sticks' to this character, and they are, in very real terms, not doing anything significant.

    Another kind of passive is a lack of character initiative, where they do nothing to overcome the obstacles that separate them from their desires. If this is the case, the conflict is never addressed. Again, you're not doing anything significant.

    However there is a difference between a lack of character initiative, where the character does absolutely nothing, and a lack of positive character initiative, where the character does not do anything that overcomes the obstacle, but they do other things that may drive them further from their desire. In this case, the character is addressing conflict -- just not in a positive way for them -- and there's still something significant happening.

    There is also a big difference between playing passively and playing a passive character. A passive character can be a really interesting thing to toy with, especially when the character is the PCof an active player that is helping to push or pull the game and the other characters into making life difficult for the passive PC. Few characters remain passive in ongoing turmoil and still survive, or at least survive in play. Where those boundaries are, how they are met and surpassed, and the cause for the passivity in the first place can all be dynamic locuses of conflict and play despite the character's passivity.

    However, a passive player playing a passive character may just not be contributing anything to the story, or dynamic of the game..

    Yeah, when playing a passive character, you have to really emphasize whatever contribution you are making. My group loves hearing internal monologues and detailed descriptions of what other PCs are doing (or in your case, not doing), so that can be done. Some games are really good at emphasizing this, some not so good. Some GMs, myself included, can get sloppy even when the game is good at emphasizing it. For example, MURPG is a game organized into Panels (turns), everyone gets the same number of Panels. It makes it pretty easy for a passive character to be actively played, you will get the same amount of chances to do (or emote, or whatnot) things that everyone else does. However, a group can still neuter this rule by saying "What are you, the character, doing? Nothing? Okay, next person..." instead of remembering, oh yeah, the real question is "What are you, the player, contributing to the game this time around the table?"
