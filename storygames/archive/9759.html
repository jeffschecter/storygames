    \n For me, I'm highly prone to being a spotlight hog. It's terrible, awful, and jerky of me. I think it arises out of an impatience to have something happening all the time in a game. When there's silence or pondering, it tends to drive me nuts. On the other hand, I acknowledge that this is really fucking annoying behavior. It's shitty of me.

    When games have things that draw your attention to spotlight sharing (Primetime Adventures's spotlight mechanics, Burning Empires's scene economy, etc.), that helps me a hell of a lot. If there's something sitting in front of me, reminding me to defer to someone else, I tend to do pretty good.

    As for what I'm doing to fix this, it's really fucking hard. I almost need a therapist for it, I think. It's hard to recognize when I'm doing it. I sometimes ask people before a game starts to let me know if I'm doing it. I dunno. I'm kinda stuck.

    \n I wreck the most emotionally intense scenes with a joke or a non-sequitur.

    It's a problem I've had for a long time. My first creative writing course as an undergrad was structured so the class publicly discussed and criticized everyone's writings without knowing which student was the author of what. But when my first story was being discussed, and with some pretty powerful compliments, I couldn't stop giggling. So yeah, it was obvious to everyone who the author was. It was a bizarre, uncontrollable emotional reaction. I actually wrapped my spiral notebook around my head trying to control it, and managed to hold myself to a sort-of mad, squelched giggling.

    I dunno. I somehow can't handle intense emotional reactions to authorship. Jokes and non-sequiturs let the intensity out of the situation, sometimes to the great irritation of others. But my unconscious brain must be more scared of my own uncontrollable reactions than the consequences of violating the social contract.

    Blech.

    \n Losing character sheets.

    Seriously, because I also tend to demand that I hold onto the group's character sheets when I'm running a game -- so I don't lose my sheet, I lose other peoples'.     \n I criticize and nay-say plans, both in- and out-of-character. I can't help that my brain is wired to see problems and complications and dangerous vulnerabilities, but at least I can try to keep the misery of it safely locked up inside my own skull where it won't ruin anyone else's fun.

    Posted By: Accounting for Taste
    I'm trying to correct the habit by just shutting the fuck up when other people make plans nowadays.
    \nMaybe this will help:

    Listen to the plan.

    Think of three glaring holes, to you. STOP THERE.

    Bring them to the players, in the game.
    "It occurs to you, Lord Axestar, that rerouting the ship's power will certainly put the security on alert. How do you plan to get around that?"
    And so on. Just for those three. If more holes appear after, so be it.

    Roll from there: Does the plan fail or succeed? If it succeeds due to the die roll, then they lucked out, open holes in plan were luckily closed, perhaps by events out of their control. If the plan fails due to the roll, then its time to bring in the complication due to other holes spotted, and see how they deal with them.

    -Andy

    \n Star Player Syndrome: It's something I do when I play, that I usually like players to do when I GM that, taken too far, becomes assholic. I'm the guy with the strongly motivated character who's ready to take the game by the throat and go, go, go. The problem with this is twofold: If the GM has things in mind, I'll balk and refuse to go that direction unless it's what I/my character want. This one is more of a style clash than anything... There's some assholism in trying to force the players into your story, but that doesn't mean that digging my heels in and refusing to the point where the GM is getting frustrated and distressed isn't assholic too. The second problem with this is the one that many others have mentioned: I can run over the contributions of other players. I've made attempts to curb this by deciding, up front, not to be a Star Player for a game. I purposely refuse to take the spotlight so that other players can take the lead. This can backfire by going too far though, because many times, other players want some focus time, but they don't necessarily want to take the lead. It's also less fun for me; I have distinctly less engagement with the story when I'm not going balls-to-the-wall.
    \n
    \nPiss-poor Prep/Improv ratio: I cannot seem to get this right. If I make a concerted effort to prep, I usually prep a bunch of stuff I end up not using, or I try to hard to use it, and it seems forced. The same is true when I use other people's prep, like when I run D&D modules. I either go completely off the rails, or slavishly follow the text. More commonly, I'm too improvisational. Ask me the name of a just-introduced NPC, and I'm likely to ask YOU their name, or come up with something cliched or boring. Take that, and extrapolate it out to other areas of the game, and you can see why it becomes a problem. What's behind this door? "Uh, I dunno, what's behind the door? Uh, I mean, it's.. just a room. Totally unexceptional." Normally, this would be fine, as I've always been really good at improvising once I get into the swing of things, but lets go into my next problem...
    \n
    \nI've lost my touch. I don't get complaints, but I feel like I don't play or GM as well as I used to. As a GM, my descriptions are usually flat, sparse and boring. My characterizations are as well. I used to be able to get into the most minor character and make them come alive for their brief moment on screen, and my major NPCs were usually pretty three dimensional by the time we got well into a game. (though a disproportional number of my characters speak with some variation of a British accent...) I could see the action in my mind, and describe it vividly. I could see the possibilities branching out from a particular action and pick the one that seemed the most appropriate, to the point where players were constantly thinking I'd planned for that course of action from the beginning.
    \n
    \nI connect this last problem both with my lack of regular gaming the last few years, and, oddly, indie/small-press games. I don't blame the games.. I blame the mindset I've gotten into with them. I've become less focused on the fiction, and more focused on the mechanisms of play. I don't really know how I've managed to slip that way. I don't know how to fix it.

      \n I'm really liking the direction this thread is going, and I think the context is now right for me to throw my hat into the ring.

      So, I actually fo' realz kind of have the problem Clinton describes. :-) I mean, I don't generally do accents, and I don't make the other players cry, but I do tend to have a certain level of mastery of RPGs. Actually, a lot of what I'm talking about relates to what people are saying about mechanics interfering with the processes of play: this doesn't happen to me. Mechanics are easy for me, almost trivially so except with the most complex games' tactics. (What I mean is, even though I will almost understand how mechanics work right away, I won't necessarily have mastery over how to, say, win conflicts, quite so quickly, but still faster than most other gamers.) So I have no problem switching between thinking about what to do mechanically and thinking from an in-character perspective. I'm really good at weaving connections between the two*.

      I get incredibly frustrated when the other players\u2014including the GM, often\u2014don't grok mechanics as quickly as I do, and stumble with them, and fail to connect them to the fiction. It's a bad habit I've had all my gaming life, and it's certainly true that one of the major appeals of indie games, to me, is the expectation of sticking to the rules-as-written rather than relying on GM fiat. But it's "assholic" (I like that word!) because it causes to be impatient and rude and obnoxious.

      As I mentioned in the DexCon play report thread, I actually walked from a game last week, and it was all due to player skill differences. I felt like a total asshole, but I just wasn't enjoying the game at all. What can/should I do differently? I mean, GMing helps with this, just like teaching helps me not be impatient with people who are bad at math. (If you're my classmate, I'm going to get impatient with you if you can't keep up with me; if I'm your teacher, then I'm going to be as patient as I can and work with you till you get it.) But I don't want to GM all the time.

      I should mention, many other games I played at DexCon were excellent, generally when about 50% or more of the people at the table were at similar levels of skills to me. David Berg and I discussed this briefly during a break from InSpectres, actually. I referred to having "vision." He and I both sat down at the table and within a minute or two had really distinctive, funny, genre-appropriate characters ready to go.

      It's similarly possible to have "vision" for what to do when framing a scene, or a clear sense of how or in what direction a scene should go. It's something I almost never lack for, especially when I'm playing, but often when I'm GMing too. It has to do with the mechanical thing, but it's more than that. "Imagination" might also be a word for it, and, dare I say it, is one of the reason why sometimes people new to the hobby are actually easier for me to play with than old hats\u2014they are naturally imaginative, and have no preconceived notions about how mechanics work. But in order to play with them I am usually GMing.

      Mind you, inexperienced does not necessarily mean low in the kind of skills I'm talking about. (See the DexCon thread for discussion about DoubleKing's first time participating in any sort of LARP-like thingy. Part of the reason that was a successful game is that the other players aside from him were all pretty experienced and "visionary" in the sense that I mean it. But Erik, his protestations to the contrary notwithstanding, is a pretty naturally-gifted roleplayer in terms of having a good sense of vision and direction.)

      I *guess* what I'm talking about in this whole rambly post is a version of "spotlight-stealing," as discussed by Rob and others in this thread, but it's not quite that. I mean, I'm really not a spotlight-hog, and I'm perfectly happy to just contribute to what the other players are doing. It's just that all too often I'm the only one with any idea of what to do, mechanically and fictionally. And yes, I frustrate easily.

      Arrrgh.


      Matt

      *By the way, for those who are having trouble with this issue, I really recommend being incredibly strict with yourself about the idea of what's called "fiction first." You can't do *anything* mechanical without justifying it in the fiction first.

      \n I can be bad at bridging the social gap between internal vision for my character and expression of them in play. Sometimes I'll push too hard to show everyone a character they may not be all that interested in. Other times I'll do the opposite, thinking, "my character wouldn't care or do anything here" and becoming detached. It's really hard for me to modify my vision for greater compatibility with the play of the moment while play is going on. I need a break, and when I get one, I've occasionally asked, "Hey, is there some way I could change my guy to get more into this? I was gonna be comic relief, but that's not the vibe here." Perhaps I could be more proactive about looking for moments to request breaks for this purpose.

      As a GM, I under-prepare. The result of this is that all my mental power during play is directed toward creating situation rather than coloring it. Which sucks because I love color. I initially tried to remedy this by generating a short color list, but I've found that I never remember to look at it while I'm doing my usual situation creation. So I've tried to do more prep. It's not that I hate prep or anything, I just find it a particularly easy thing to procrastinate on.\n
      Posted By: Robert Bohlwhen I notice I'm doing it, or that a certain person hasn't had the spotlight in a while, I say, "Hey I feel like we haven't seen enough of X. Can we see more of him?"
      This is the glue that has made a lot of my groups work at cons. Also, suggestions of ways for the quieter players to contribute. "Oh crap! Nice reveal! I wonder how Ted's character would take that, in light of his last scene?" This can help Ted if he's having trouble finding material.

      \n Hm. I'm not quite sure how to bring other players to the same level, but that's the right answer.
      \n
      \nI'm a decent Regency-style dancer, after many years, but one problem I have is the dance version of the reverse of Clinton's problem. When I get a lackluster partner, which is entirely likely at an sf con, I dumb down my dancing. This is completely the wrong thing to do, and I know it. And, it's not the guy's fault -- whoever this guy is, he is someone who is taking a deep breath and going to a dance, risking looking like an idiot, and knowing darn well he isn't likely to pick up someone. He's taking a big risk for a small reward, the reward of being in the dance.
      \n
      \nWhat I need to do -- what the correct thing to do in an rpg -- is to find a way to make the other guy awesome. I'm not good enough to do that yet. I know guys who are. Years ago, when I was a lot less good at the waltz, one guy danced with me, and the whole time I was dancing with him, I didn't misstep and I felt great. He made me look good.
      \n
      \nSo, how do folks do this in an rpg? How do you make people who aren't the strongest role players out there, but who are taking a deep breath and wading in, improve in play and feel good about the whole experience?
      \n
      \nSo far, I've heard one tactic that works a lot of the time -- turning to someone else and saying, "So, what does your PC think of this?" / turning to the table and saying, "I think we need a scene with X person now."
      \n
      \nAny other tactics?

      \n As a GM, I try to be the Captain of the Story too much. Makes the control freak in me giggle with glee, but disappoints characters a lot and leads to railroading. Both of which I hate as a player.

      I'm curbing that by shutting up a lot more. I have a couple of groups that I play with and one of them I had despaired of ever doing a story game with. But, I began running Cat and found that they really leaped to the fore when I just shut up and threw obstacles at them from time to time. And, as usual, it was their ideas that fed into the obstacle creation. I guess for me it is following outline instead of plot that solves problem 1.

      Problem 2: I am also on the spotlight train. I find that when I'm gaming with a new group or a new system, I try to drive the game along - like I'm doing the GM a favor. But, let's face it, it's fun to have your own adventure show with a group of Scoobies supporting you!

      I'm using the 'shutting up' tactic for this one as well. One way to quell the freak is to make it shut up and watch. Also, recognizing that this is partially a trust issue, I have decided to trust my fellow gamers and the GM more and more. Having quality players and GMs has helped this IMMENSELY! Finally, I keep repeating to myself that this is an ensemble effort. And it is. Good games come from everyone at the table. You all know this; I just needed to get into writing so I could see it for myself.

      Thanks!
