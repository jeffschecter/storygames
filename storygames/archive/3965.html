\n I almost didn't go to GenCon this year because my GenCon buddy dropped out, but I did, and I'm glad I did. It was perhaps my best GenCon yet.

I didn't play a lot of games: a huge 8-player Beast Hunters game run by Andy--we hunted the beast to death--and a game of Blood & Bronze with James, Andy, Alexander and my wife Ami--Alexander crushed us all.

I enjoyed seeing friends I made earlier this year at Dreamation and Camp Nerdly. Got to have dinner with jenskot and Terry! Saw Judd and got to talk a little classic Battletech with Jeff. But best of all I made some new friends! The standouts: I got to meet Daniel Solis--who knew such a talented guy could be so approachable and sincere--we have to play a game next year! And I met some great guys from down under: Lon and Scott. We kept running into each other at demos and the Forge booth, so we decided to just become friends. We played a quick card game and spent the rest of the night talking about differences in gaming above and below the equator.

I had a fantastic time! I hope you did too. Tell me all about it.

    \n My Gen Con was very satisfying. On a general note, I felt like I made a lot more personal connections, e.g., dinner with Daniel, Fred, Rob, and several others, picking Ron's brain on a big project I am working on, etc. As usual, I was often too tired or suffered bad timing, so I wasn't able to get into the after-hours gaming like I wanted to.

    As far as gaming goes, I only managed to crank out 5 or so demos of Passages. The first two days, I spent 90% of my time preparing for my Dawning Star VIP Game to benefit charity. Saturday I had to run an actual event; and I left early on Sunday. But I did have one direct-from-demo purchase, which is always very satisfying.

    VIP Charity Game: This was a success by every measure. I was able to score two overstuffed bags of swag for the players/attendees. I'll have a full list of donees in my full press release, but I do want to mention S-G folks: Gregor gave me a copy of Best Friends; the Evil Hat guys donated a copy of SotC (that our VIP, JD Wiker claimed for himself), and Julia donated a copy of Steal Away Jordan. When she offered, I was hesitant; candidly, I couldn't see a big crowd of d20 sci-fi fans going for serious story-gaming about slavery. I am happy to report that JD's SO happens to be a history professor and was very excited about it. The event itself went off without a hitch. Everyone showed up on time. The game itself ran smoothly and was a lot of fun. The food was great andcheaper than I had planned for.

    The best part of the game was something I yoinked from someone here (Jason?) about taking real-time donations to influence the game. We stuck a big empty bowl in the middle of the table. 20 minutes in, the party split up, and I dropped $20 to commemorate the event. It started slowly, but the money really started flying. Players were throwing in bills to confirm crits, increase save DCs on their tear-gas grenades, and generally avoid death. At one point, the PCs entered the elevator, and one of the attendees dropped in $5 to make it play bad elevator music. As I hoped, the attendees (many being friends of some of the players) were dropping money in to fuck with the PCs or help out the evil vaasi, and the PCs were dropping money in to boost themselves back up. We had donations from $2 to give the wolf player fleas to $60 to "summon" a vaasi war mech. And Lee Hammock is, hands down, the best GM on the planet. He rolled with it all and gave all the players their money's worth.

    I lied. The best part was that, all told (including a modest personal donation to make it a nice even number), I was able to raise $2,500 to benefit the Shriner's Hospitals for Children. My tagline for the event was "A Good Time, and a Good Cause." It certainly lived up to that billing.

    \n Jared told me that your even numbered Gen Cons are better than your odd numbered ones, and he's right so far. I had a blast. It was really nice to not have the pressure of selling a new game. My booth activity was more focused on the IPR side and on helping out the new booth folks, which was cool. Meeting new people is always cool, but I also like to try and develop my relationships with friends that I only see at cons. So I did a lot of that, I think, and I really liked it. To my new friends, it was great to meet you and I look forward to interacting with you more, over the intarwebs and in person when I get to see you! To my older friends, I'm really glad I got to spend more time with you, it really made the con meaningful for me.

    I'll have some more in-depth reflections on the ol' bloggy, and some pictures! But in breif:

    GAMES PLAYED:

    - Beast Hunter Squadron (Thurs Nite). The squadron was cool, but I was so exhausted that after I got eaten I started dropping off. It made me want to play more Beast Hunters tho, so that was cool. Many kudos to Andy for stepping up and wrangling our squadron!

    - Darkpages (Fri Nite). This is Jared's dark comics game, if you haven't been paying attention. I ran a playtest for an intimidating set (Jared, Jason, Dave, Thor, Mike), and it was really, really productive, I thought. A lot of fun in the "joy of fixing things" sense.

    - Roanoke (Sat Nite). Kevin ran it for me, Clint Krause (the designer), Eric Boyd, Jason Morningstar and Matt Snyder. Our characters were great, and we a lot of really fun moments. I think the hardcore story-gamers of us had some trouble, cuz Wushu comes from a totally different design aesthetic, but I'm really glad we played. And I had a ton of fun playing with these people - we need to play more, all of you!

    - Darkpages again (Sat Nite). Alexander demanded a game, so I sat down with him and Kevin to do some Darkpages-ness. We made cool characters and ran through some of the new stuff from the last nights playtest, but by then it was 2 in the morning and we were all falling over exhausted. It was nice for me to solidify some of the changes in my head, tho, and again, a solid group of friends to play with.

    - Acts of Evil (Sun Nite). Ron, Julia, JRS, Matt Gandy(right?), and Jason. We started procedurally and all made characters, and then realized that there's a GM in this game! So Ron ran and grabbed Gregor, and he GMed for us. We got through a round of scenes, which showed us all how incredibly intricate and well-imagined this game is already, and then had a really productive discussion about addressing some of the work that still needs to be done on it.

    For next time: Smaller groups to play games with. While I had a good time in all of my games, I was really missing the dynamics of 3 or 4 people who are all rarin' to go with a game. My resolution for next year is to not play a game in groups larger than 5, or maybe even 4. Not because I don't love you all, because I do! But I think, for me, play will be more creatively satisfying, as opposed to the socially satisfying play that a large group usually means for me.

    Anyway. You're all rock stars.

Though there were some rocky moments--the playtest John Kim, Mike Holmes and John Wilson and I did of Jess Hammer's game never really jelled, but it did teach me the rules and inspire me to come home and play with my home peeps now that I've got a sense of it. And after that, all the games I was in were fun and inspiring: *Super* (hero) Models in Best Friends with Gregor, Julia, John Wilson, James and who was on my left? Angelika's divine posing still convulses me with laughter.

    Zombies with James, Anna, Willow and Tom--I wrote that up at the Forge. Dogs with many of the Playcollective on the last night: good down home killing of a bad seed, with the lesbian dog doing the honors to save him from the damnation of suicide while not being able to accept forgiveness from the wholesome dog I was playing. Then, the experimental game adapted on the spot from Jonathan Walton's Avatar mechanics which James, Mike Majcher, JW and I used to explore some sweet realist issues spun from the base of people we knew not too well.

    But the best thing of all for me was many stolen conversations with friends new and old: talking to Paul Tevis about improv and acting after demoing his fantastic little game A Penny for your Thoughts; spontaneous interviews with James, Amy, Mark, Jillian and Jeff at various times about styles of larp in the US and Canada; talking game theory with Ben for 15 minutes at the GoD table and getting to hear how his games like Adventures in the Land of 1000 Kings were bringing him closer to home by approaching the games from the people who play them more and more; talking to Ron about Jeep form and the Forge and putting into words how different and yet how similar our approaches are; connecting with the poor, exhausted Jason Morningstar about the same topic after the booths were broken down, with him asking me to defend my position; having a conversation on the last day with Star that made me realize that, yes, I do have to write the live action version of Under my Skin, the polyamory game that completes the trilogy of BtI and StM; and talking with Vincent on the way home about how far we've come as a community.

    There was a theme there for me. I left Ropecon with new horizons opened up for me, and found them continued and filled by the many amazing people at GenCon with similar experiences and stories to tell.

      \n I somehow only came upon this thread earlier today, so I hope you'll forgive me for making a post now (I spent another week or so in NYC after GC 07, which explains it).

      Wednesday morning, I walked around the convention-space, trying to fit map locations to actual locations, to facilitate faster movement between games.
      I then talked to some Canadian L5R guys, and since we were all TCG players, we took out the free Warcraft TCG decks we received in the care package and played games for a couple of hours.

      Wednesday night, finding some random strangers at the Embassy Suites lobby and playing german board-games for several hours, then having trouble finding my way to the Diana Jones award because I'm really bad at orienteering, till a kindly desk person took a map and drew the proper route for me.

      Waking up Thursday morning and seeing a 'stranger' asleep on a sofa-couch. That was Andrew, my WW playing friend from Boston.

      Passing Joshua A.C. Newman in the hallway one day, I tell him, "Joshua?" after he passes me, he turns around, does a double-take, hugs me, and then goes on on his busy way

      Going to True Dungeon, I think we accidentally got the Hardcore dungeon by accident. Some of the questions, like "Month's stones" totally didn't make sense to me. We don't follow that here. Anyway, it was cute how they had a girl in a skimpy medusa episode, so if any players looked at her their character was petrified. The final Beholder monster was left with _1 HP_ when time ran out. I scored several "criticals", a shame I was using a 1d4 staff, and that it was immune to criticals, heh.
      Afterwards Ewen, Mike, their friends and I went to a restaurant (The Ram, perhaps? It had a Privateer Press theme to it), where our waitress was really cute, but we waited about 50+ minutes (just to get our orders taken, perhaps?), and none of the orders arrived exactly right, or to the right person. We didn't tip.

      Going to dinner with the indie alley people, but there were like 15 of us, and the restaurant couldn't accomodate us all. So I went with TonyLB and had food at a Mexican place, I think the rest of the group ended up not waiting the full time for the table there eventually. I found out TonyLB is Jewish.

      There was a time I went to dinner with Harper, Snyder, Czege, Danielle, Wilson. I've talked of my water-caused nausea (I can feel the water going down in my body, and I dislike their taste), I've mentioned how I've got big balls, that I've known someone who looked like he were Paul's long-lost (younger) brother, so we've discussed some heritage stuff. It was an uproariously fun conversation. The restaurant we went to did have room for more indie folks, as we were about 20 people there too, but split across several tables.

      Finding out how far the Union Station was when I needed to get there, and thinking, "Damn, I'm STILL not there?!" for my Unhallowed Metropolis game. The system wasn't too exciting, but I did end up killing a werewolf with one super-shot of my sniper-rifle. It was a small room, with too many tables, and one group kept shouting, they were excited, and disruptive (we couldn't hear the GM speak). Dan Davenport was also at the table.

      Getting a demo of Blood and Bronze from Japes, and then The Hero's Banner from Tim, and asking Kenneth Hite why he was following me around.

      Playing a demo game of the Samurai edition of L5R, understanding it much better than the edition I've played back about 2000, and being pleasantly surprised at getting to keep the starter set I've played in the demo. I was going to buy it anyway.

      Playing in a playtest of Misery Bubblegum with Tony, Alexander Cherry and Danielle Lewon. We had fun with the setting we've created, and we all seemed to share the same mind-set, as at one point in our (magical?) high-school game, when you needed to have an "Aww" moment, I've just on the spot narrated how the school's ghosts said "Awww" as two characters kissed, taking a page from one of the better points in Harry Potter, the other players liking it, and having created ghosts in the setting.

      Using my big brass balls and winning a game of Mechaton with either 1 or 2 white dice left, and nothing else. Mike Holmes also sat at the table. That was the only Games on Demand game I've played.

      Full Light, Full Steam game ran by Jonathan Walton, when we all took too long to come up with character names, I made the suggestion that made a lot of sense and cut down on player/character name confusion, we'd just use our real names as our characters names. Jonathan became Joanna, and I had to remind people "Guy" is a proper English name (Guy Fawkes).
      We had a camel chase at the end of the game, a star-faring kind of game

      Coming late to a WH40K game (well, after the other players), sitting down, listening to one player and/or his character whine/whine/whine, told him that I'd eject him from the shuttle's hatch if he wouldn't put a sock in it. He played the Psyker, naturally. All the other players (half of them I suspect didn't speak a word aside from stating actions in combat) cheered me on, especially later as he presented himself to the NPC we had to get information from and making an ass out of himself and us. I stepped in and two critical persuasion type rolls later I have all the information we need. It turned out I had the "Face" character
      A cute moment was when we got to a major NPC, I turn to the GM and ask him, before we can do anything IC. "Does he outrank us? Do we know it?" and he goes "Ooooh, yes." and I turn to the Psyker and go, "Behave."
      I dunno why, it's always the Psyker/Wizard players in games I play that do it, heh.
      Talking to the GM later and the day after when I ran across him on the pacing of the game. I've had to leave because I've had a game after, the game ended up running 30 minutes extra, they ended up changing it a bit.

      Seeing the dealers' hall, seriously. I usually finish my romp through the dealers' hall in Israeli conventions in no more than thirty minutes, this? It probably took longer just to walk all the aisles blindly.

      Seeing people sitting with the Weapons of the Gods companion and nodding thoughtfully, at the social combat rules or mass combat, or whatever it was, including many people here.

      Sitting next to Keith Senkowski and bugging him. He ended up fabreezing me when I ruffled his hair
