\n Hi folks,

Does anyone know of a tabletop RPG that utilizes JRPG styled interactions between combat and a simple static board/grid?

To get an idea of what I mean, think of the Hex system in later Wild ARMs games and the element fields in Chrono Cross.

I ask because I am currently designing my own JRPG styled TRPG and was wondering if the ground has already been covered before (in English). I know there exists games that use simple range/movement systems like MECHA and Agon. Those sorts of suggestions would also be helpful.

To give folks an idea as to what I'm doing... There exists a Battle Board that is divided into 13 zones arrayed to look like a FF style crystal (this idea was taken directly from MECHA). Combatants move across these zones like how you'd expect in a SRPG, differing attacks have different ranges. Each zone is randomly given an Element by picking a bead out of a bag. Each bead is labeled 1 ~ 3. That level is applied to a specific zone by rolling a die. Monsters use/eat the levels to activate special effects, PCs gather the levels to cast spells and activate special powers.

    \n I have two examples which are maybe only partially relevant, but I think are at least interesting enough to mention.

    Spellbound Kingdoms doesn't really emphasize tactical movement between zones in combat per se... but the way combat works, is that every school of swordsmanship/martial arts/magic etc is treated as its own map of interconnected nodes, each of which represents a discrete fighting maneuver or spell. You have one or more designated starting points, and then every time you act you move a space through the "map", performing different attacks along the way. Typically the most effective or devastating moves are several nodes deep, and have several paths to get there, and many moves have map-affecting properties like resetting you to the start position after use, or resetting someone else to theirs.

    Ryuutama (coming in English in the future) has a battle system almost literally taken from a JRPG, Final Fantasy 1 to be specific; the extremely abstract combat map is baddies on one side, PCs on the other, with a front and back row per side. Characters in the back row can't be attacked by enemies in the front row, but back row characters with bows can attack front row characters. Characters can move between their rows, but that's the extent of the tactical movement. If there are no characters in a given front row, all the back row characters are now in the front row. If you've ever played a pre-polygon Final Fantasy, this should be very familiar.

    \n Spellbound Kingdoms is great. It's a bizarre mixture of all sorts of ingenious storytelling and improv-oriented rules, coupled with this great, innovative combat system Joe described above, a bunch of "take d&d and get straight to the good parts" rules (like super-fast leveling), and large swatches of beautiful madness (e.g. the setting, races, and character classes). It's not super well-edited, but it's quite brilliant. I'm really not sure why it hasn't gotten more play around here.

    \n Yeah, Spellbound Kingdoms is kind of an amazing game. It deserves a lot more talk than it gets-- it's got the same zany anything-goes kitchen-sinkiness that people love about, say, Rifts or Synnibar, but it's actually pretty fun to engage with on a mechanical level and there's some genuine innovation here and there. Not all of its experiments are unambiguous successes, but it is absolutely a labor of love and it's hard not to respond to that.\n
    I've been looking forward to Ryuutama ever since Andy mentioned it in the initial TBZ Kickstarter thread. I'm very interested to see how that game plays out. I feel like the combat won't have much emphasis or tactical depth so I wonder if I'll learn much for myself. We'll see, I s'pose.
    \nYeah, Ryuutama is not a game that emphasizes combat (really, traveling around is the real "combat" of the game, since most of your HP damage will probably come from exhausting yourself hiking), but there are one or two wrinkles; I particularly like how your initiative result doubles as your defense value, and characters can use an action to feint and reduce an enemy's initative, or refocus to reroll theirs. Your general health going into the fight (your "condition", a separate value from the actual persistent HP count) also determines how much physical damage you can take before dying, so there's definitely room for circumstances to make fights distinct.
