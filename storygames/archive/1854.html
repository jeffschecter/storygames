    If you're interested this is my 30 second description of the Big Model I've been giving to people lately that seems to help.

    I contribute X to the game. And in response to X you contribute Y. In recognition of this we smile and nod to each other. The thing we're similing and nodding about? That's Creative Agenda. Also X and Y are not atomic elements of play but rather a string of moments over time resulting in the mutual appreciation I describe.

    If, on the other hand, we end up arguing then there's a Creative Agenda disconnect.

    Would this work, as an attempt at explaining some of the big concepts in the Big Model?

    "There's this hobby called role-playing games, where friends get together to imagine things together.

    "In order for friends to get together and talk, there needs to be some baseline understandings about how that's going to happen. Where are you meeting? Who pays for food? What level of politeness is required? What's off-limits? A lot of this understanding goes unspoken, but it's important, because if you violate it often enough, or badly enough, you can damage the real-world relationships of the participants and hurt peoples' feelings.

    "When you're imagining things with other people, it's possible for there to be misunderstandings or conflicts about the imaginary content. One of the defining features of the RPG hobby is that groups are supposed to work out a process for smoothing over these moments. The process might be pretty ad hoc rulings on the part of one participant; it might involve consensus of all involved; it might involve devotion to a specific set of written texts. A lot of this procedure is influenced by, and influences in turn, the 'social contract' discussed in the prior paragraph.

    "People get different things out of the hobby; playing an RPG can satisfy different people in different ways. How well a game, or your group, satisfies you as a player is hard to measure on a moment-to-moment basis, because it's so transitory. But looking at things over the long run, you might be able to see an agenda at work in your own play, or in others'. (This refers to how you feel about the shared imagination stuff, as distinct from the real-world social stuff going on.)

    "When everyone is playing a game together, and they're all on the same page with regard to their 'creative agendas,' and the system they're relying upon to smooth over misunderstandings/disputes doesn't screw up the achieving of that agenda, that's a real groovy spot. For any given agenda, there are a whole raft of techniques you can use to help get there."

    That's kind of simple-minded, and maybe too wordy, but it's my understanding. Have I made any errors, O Sages of Story Games?

    My general thought is that people who don't role-play, but who might be interested or are at least curious about something that interests you, need no such explanation at all. In all of their social activities, both the social contract and the equivalent of a CA already exists (without the "C" if it's not a creative endeavor). It's expected, desired, sought, and in fact required.

    I also think people get a little too formal in their understanding of "social contract." It's not an actual legalized document or set of explicit agreements. It's all the social priorities and relationships that operate in that group, whether overtly stated or not, recognized or not, or whatever. Any group of people interacting to do something, together, have a social contract. The question is what it is for that group at that time, and how that affects the stuff they are doing.

    Your writeup looks too formulaic and directive to me. It's as if you're saying, "in order to play, you all must first agree upon a social contract," whereas the Big Model's point is, "if you're playing, then you're necessarily doing so in the context of a social contract."

    Best, Ron

    If I get it, the Big Model is supposed to describe what's going on in a game session, at least in the real world. So long as everyone's having fun and enjoying themselves, who cares how it works? But if it starts to stink and make strange noises, you've got to pop the hood and see what's wrong.

    1. People gotta hang out, which means having decent social skills
    2. People gotta imagine stuff as a group, which means some kind of process
    3. You gots to get paid, in terms of what you enjoy about this hobby
    4. There are techniques to help you get paid.

    Looking at some old games post-hoc, it's clear that I hated S. and R.'s behavior in the real world, so it violated (1), and there was no way to play with them any more. So I walked, and created my own campaign. The process was adequate (2), but didn't help me get paid (3), so I ended up going to the Forge and learning new techniques (4), which helped me enjoy imagining stuff with my friends more.
