    I don't think there are many of these. Oh, RPGs that are intended to run for just a few sessions that, in some cases, happen to go only one? Like My Life With Master? Those are common (and I'd recommend that one). But ones intended to be one-shots? Those are talked about quite a lot, but rarely actually created.

    That is, I think there are some, but the only one I can think of off the top of my head is SOAP (which is a "one-hour" RPG, start to end, including chargen). I can recommend SOAP, too.

    So, are you looking for a game meant to only play as a one-shot? Or are you looking for a short game that could be played as a one-shot?

    Mike

    Honestly, while PTA is great for longer campaigns, I've had hands-down drop-dead success with it as one-shots, when we all come up with a premise, get into it, and do some roleplaying.

    And I'm serious: I've been in sessions with total PTA newbies, and in the first 30-50 minutes after coming up with the show we want to do, and the characters, and get past the system explanation, the actual in-character roleplaying begins... and hot damn if I haven't had some incredible one-shots at cons and the like.

    It's a little disappointing sometimes, because I wish I could play more of that world through the game than the one 4-hour slot. But then I can always just run a series at home with my weekly gaming group (and I've had great success running seasons of shows, including ones set in Rome, a time travel game, and a firefly-like show).

    -Andy

    Coming of Age does this quite well, all you need to do is set the pace = 1 (since pace is approximately the desired number of sessions).

    The finished version will have about a dozen setting sheets - one to two pages that seed titles, opposition, and antagonists for a specific setting concept. I'm not sure what could support pick up and play more, other than pre-generated characters.

    As far as other games, I find Inspectres and octaNe to work well as quick one shots. In general I find some of the games geared as one shots to be too long to suffice. I'm usually looking at a 2-3 hour session at most. A focused 4-hour block is just something that doesn't happen in my gaming life.

    Full Light, Full Steam has one-shot rules. One shots work a little better with pregen characters and a pre-prepared situation, but if you push it, you can create characters, create a situation, and play through that situation in one sitting. Maybe with a meal in there, if it's an evening's entertainment.

    I find Dogs does this well, too: in a four-hour timeslot, you can run Accomplishments and one shortish town.

    As for games specifically designed to be played in one sitting... there aren't many published, this is true. However, I think we will see that change this year. The Game Chef 2006 entries will be getting finalized (of which Crime and Punishment is a member), and there has been a lot of talk about short-form games -- talk that I think will be turning into finished products soon. My own Sons of Liberty is slated to be out at the end of the year, and it is 100% the result of lots and lots of discussion about making games easy to pick-up-and-play.

    January 2007
    A lot of games that are often played as one-shots sometimes work find as one-shots, but you're missing the boat if you don't play them longer-term. For instance, MLWM should be tried for multiple sessions. Inspectres loses out on a whole game dynamic if you don't play it serially. Prime Time adventures, you miss out on the ebb and flow of screen time.

    These are all games where the designer has said that they weren't designed to be played as one-shots. That doesn't mean you can't make it work. But I think you can probably make most any game into a one-shot - heck, Luke even runs demos of Burning Empires, despite the fact that it's clearly got rules for length.

    My point is that we can go on and on with pointing out games with which we've had fun using them for one-shots. But let's all try to stick to games that are, as the poster has asked, primarily for one-shots, or which really do somehow lend themselves to one-shots due to some design consideration.

    Doesn't that sound a bit more useful? I dunno, maybe it's just me.

    Mike
