    \n In A Wicked Age is one of my favorite games, and your group would like it's collaborative world building. In my experience about \u2153 to \xbd the play time is spent on creating the characters and scenario, and it's just as much fun as the roleplaying.

    The default setting is not vanilla fantasy; it feels more Middle Eastern (part Babylonian, part 1001 Nights), although it's not intended to be any specific historical time. You can entirely change the setting just by using different "oracles", the tables that map playing cards to evocative statements. There are a bunch of oracles at Abulafia for many different genres.

    The GM role is pretty minimal. You don't have to make up the scenario, you just play the NPCs and do some scene-setting. My group played IAWA for a few months and rotated the GM role each session.

    The one thing I will say against IAWA is that the combat/conflict resolution -- the part where you roll dice -- is weird. I found it complex and unintuitive at first, but after a while it went smoothly.

    I like Fate Accelerated too. It has a more trad GM role. You could buy one of the cheap Fate Worlds books and run that -- each one is $5 and includes a pretty interesting setting, the necessary extra rules, and a sample scenario. I think some have pregen PC characters, but I'm not sure.

    \n What about the Blades in the Dark Quickstart?

    It's technically 96 pages, but everything after page 38 or so is either playbooks, setting reference material, or random tables. And it includes a starting point scenario so the GM doesn't have to come up with much of anything. It's also pretty far from "Generic fantasy", honestly hewing closer to "steampunk".

    Fate Accelerated is short, but honestly, I feel it provides very little help the GM get going. If you don't already know how to GM, I'm not sure it's going to give you what you need.

    Another good choice, IMHO, is InSpectres, since it's short-ish, has a clearly defined subject - which FAE does not - and is, honestly, kinda "lightly GM'd" in that players get to add a bunch of stuff to the game on successful rolls.

    Puppetland is definitely an interesting choice. I haven't actually played/run it, but I think it hits all the requirements. Though I think the new version is much longer than 50 pages.

    This is a problem, really. Most "small" games range in the 100-150 pages range. Below that, you tend to get 'mingames' that run 10-20 pages or so, but which don't teach GMing because they don't have room.
