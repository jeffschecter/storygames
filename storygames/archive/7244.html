    \n I had a similar gamer in my group. Pretty much the best advice I have is to treat him like you would any other gamer in this situation. Talk to them, see what the issue is, and try to address it as best you can. Just remember that he is not his diagnosed medical condition. It can be tempting to blame things on the disorder that have nothing to do with it.

    On the specific instance of shutting down though, ask him what he wants the group to do when that happens. My wife would occasionally shut down due to a mix of depression or exhaustion. Sometimes she just needed a short break. Other times she would bow out for the night and her character got NPCd or sidelined for a bit. Good luck balancing the needs of your group with the needs of your players.

    \n I just see a lot of issues all tangled here.

    First, you have this friends who has a diagnosed bipolar disorder, one of the most severe and dangerous forms of mental illness. Make sure your friend gets treatment.

    Next, there's his behavior in your game. He's still responsible for that. He was fine with the prodding and poking before, and now he's not. As Rich says, he isn't his disease, but his disease isn't a crutch either. I'd talk to him alone and see what's up. It might not even be a bipolar thing. Maybe he just had a headache that day.

    Last, how do you want to prioritize this stuff. I am not suggesting there's any one right answer. Do you kick your friend out of the group if he doesn't mesh well? even if it pushes him further downward? even if it loses you a friend? Do you let him stay in the group? even if it makes your games suck occasionally? more than occasionally? even if it drives off other players? even if it destroys this game for you? Where do you draw the line?

    \n Be sure the rest of your play group knows and understands the scope of the player's issue. They'll be, hopefully, a lot more forgiving if they understand their play isn't what is generating weirdness at the table.

    I have a player with fairly mild for-real Asperger's who plays with us, only occassionally. I forewarn my players, and they're cool with it. His weirdness trigger is ritual: we need to follow the rules and do things in a particular order, or he gets pretty agitated. But everyone gets that that's what is going on.

    \n Ryan,

    You mention that the two character's in question with the interpersonal drama are played by BPG and BPG's flatmate, yeh? And you mention that you felt that BPG's flatmate might have been going at it kind of hard to make up for stuff going on outside the game, yeh? And that BPG hasn't been his usual self recently away from the table either? This sounds to me like a totally out of game problem. Which means I wouldn't try to tackle it with in game solutions (either of the create an in-game fictional element variety, or of the guys knock it off at the table rule variety), I think these guys need to be spoken with away from the table, in an honest, intimate fashion.

    Now, I don't know them or your group, it might mean speaking with them separately, separately first and then together, or together right off the bat, or whatever, but since what you're seeing involves both of them, I strongly suggest speaking with both of them.

    I do know that mental illness is something that's not accepted in western society and that people living with mental illness often get their diseases treated lightly (oh, it's not a real disease, it's not even like a broken leg or anything) and is often perceived as a sign of weakness (maybe if you just tried harder), so it's definitely tricky to talk to someone about it because they are often so used to it (and their lives coping with it) being trivialized.

    As Adam (and Tony and Richard) pointed out, when talking to BPG, make sure not to commit the fundamental attribution error. He isn't his disease, his behaviour that day may have to do with external issues as much as it may have to do with internal issues (i.e. his shutting down could have been for any number of reasons). Same goes for BPG's flatmate, I notice that you figure his actions may have been prompted from the external reason of BPG's recent behaviour, but it's possible that the reason for him could have been something completely different as well.

    And finally, after all is said and done, I also recommend talking to everyone else about it. Depending on how BPG and his flatmate feel about it (key key key), this can either be done with them there, without them there, individually, or as a group. See what they think, but I think it might be a little odd if everyone felt uncomfortable about what happened, and then the next time you all get together everyone does their best to pretend nothing happened.

    EDIT: I also realize that what I'm suggesting is really hard to do. Honest intimate conversation is not easy, it's much harder than any in-game solution ever would be.
