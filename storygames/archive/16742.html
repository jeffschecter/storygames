\n I tried to pay attention to how combat is handled in some adventure genres where combat is central. if we think about anime, quite often combat can be seen as a long "dialogue" between the main character and an Antagonist, where the protagonist gets initially beaten up, but slowly but surely finds a secret weakness that allows him to win in the end. This is done via a process of hypothesis and experimentation by interacting with the antagonist.

If we see this on the party level, an old school game where you get to understand the ecology and the secrets of a monster before you are able to bring it down, follows a similar structure.

How do you think this can be modelled? In a way it reminds me of a code-cracking puzzle game (mastermind anyone?) where you must take risks and make sacrifices to gain any piece of information by running small -potentially deadly- experiments.

A monster could start out all powerful and be described with a network of advantages and disadvantages and clues that have to be uncovered by expending resources. (Oh no the monster can fly! /advantage/ .. hey but it can only move vertically.. we can use this to trap it /disadvantage/ )

Is there any system that does this well already?

    \n Puzzles tend to have ONE correct solution, at least from my perspective, so i don't find this a good paralell.

    The usual old-school big bad monster adventure from my experience consist of 3 stages. First one being investigation, as in finding the antagonist strong and weak sides. Second being preparation, where you gather needed resources and finally the execution. Note, you may have those stages repeat, when the "party" can't find the resources needed and needs to find another way, or has failed their attempt in defeating the antagonist. I do not know of a system, that does all 3 in an interesting way. Most focus either on combat or investigation.

    \n In Monster of the Week, the only way to kill a monster permanently is to kill it using its weakness -- otherwise it keeps coming back. In the rules as written, weaknesses are found by doing research, as that matches the genre, but it isn't hard to imagine circumstances where that research could actually be in combat.

    In Dragon World, you can pick up clues about opponents in combat the result of moves, clues can be used to find McGuffins (such as magic swords, special spells, etc.), and McGuffins are needed to defeat Bosses. This is actually pretty close to what you're talking about, which is not surprising as Dragon World is an anime-inspired game.

    \n This technique was eluded to in Esoterrorists, made explicit in Esoterror Factbook, and is central to conflict in Night's Black Agents. For example, creatures in Esoterorists have a Special Means of Dispatch - the creature's specific weakness. But in additition to that, you can spend investigative points tactically (tactical fact finding benefits) so you could use architecture to spot a weakness in the ceiling, biology or natural history to find a weak spot, etc. In Night's Black Agents, this is what thriller chaes are all about - using electronic surveillance or cop talk to throw of the cops or urban survival to get a short cut. I've also used this idea in GUMSHOE Fantasy - and you can push quite far in a player-facing direction.

    \n To be clear, the GUMSHOE approach isn't the OP's in its pure form. There is still an element of randomness. With dark horror, as Peter describes, you may not be able to defeat the creature without the Special Means of Dispatch, and in Trail of Cthulhu, the information might require you to make a terrible moral choice.

    Swavomeer - GUMSHOE handles Stage 2 (preparation) rather neatly - you can prepare, but you also have Preparedness, which is an ability which effectively lets you retcon things other prep into existence. I've found in Night's Black Agents that this curtails the players' built in desire to spend too long planning.

    UserClone - GUMSHOE Fantasy is something I'm writing just for fun to play in-house because it combines my abiding love of AD&D (and in particular my well-worn setting) with all the GUMSHOE stuff I like.

    I'd really like to see what Cthulhu Dark could do with this, and see a mod of GUMSHOE which is entirely investigation based.

    \n Digs in the Vineyard was used to emulate D&D a few years back by a friend of mine. He did essentially what you're describing above, with the dungeon being mapped as one "conflict" and each "trap/room/puzzle" was just a round, where the party had to "see and raise" to stay in. Worked out a lot like a battle conversation, expecially because in DitV you grab items from your inventory (like information, or Relationships, or Traits, or sometimes physical objects like lock-picks, etc) to add dice to your pool before you respond.

    But where it might be weak is that certain items don't merit higher values or higher dice or more potency against certain threats. Holy water, for instance, would have no more effect (in Dogs in the Vineyard) against a Vampire than against a stubborn child, without some careful handling of the rules and themes. You could codify some side-rules to represent advantages to know a weakness, or to exploit a network, to grant them more power or more serious Fallout (wounds in DitV, essentially). Otherwise, it's essentially possible to overcome most threats without doing that legwork, and it takes the wind out of your idea.

    Still, the back and forth is something you might want to emulate, if I understand you correctly. But I've heard a lot of good stuff about Gumshoe too. Sounds like some cool ideas from there as well.

    Good luck.

    \n Sure Ivan. We have 4 questionable heroes, and a load of evil goblin-like creatures attacking a small town. In the battle you roll a dice, narrates the result, and possibly you are wounded in the process. Each of the pre-written characters have 4 flashback scenes, all focusing on a shaded and painful past, and each of these scene have a mechanical healing effect if you play them out/narrates them (the player decides). You can activate the scenes as a "response", so you can't be killed before all the scenes have been used, unless you choose not to activate a scene and die instead. So in order for the character to be killed, you have to know his past and his dark secrets. However, the mechanics are pretty much the same as usual.

    I bet you can use this method to tell the battle-story you are looking for. Each time you "kill" the monster, it can recharge its hit points, by giving you information on how to really kill it.
