Rewards, Meaning and Push/Pull

TonyLB
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Story Games

So, brief summary of my take on Push/Pull: It is defined in large part by what people choose to see as important. Everything is (on some level) an assertion of authority, so a Push is not just that, it is an assertion of authority where the assertion is viewed as important. Likewise, a Pull is a solicitation of input where the input is viewed as important.

To my mind a fair rule of thumb is that Push happens when you view your own input as important and Pull happens when you view other people's input as important.

Now, combine with what we know (from game design) about reward systems: Reward systems can radically change what people focus their attention on. They can change what they consider important.

These two seem (to me) to be a very fruitful area for discussion. My theory is that rewards given to a player for what they do will promote recognition of Push, and rewards given to a player player for what other people do will promote recognition of Pull.

For instance, players in Burning Wheel are rewarded for hitting their own Beliefs.

By comparison, players in Primetime Adventures are rewarded when other players enjoy their narrations.

Does this seem to hold up with the intuitive sense that people have about Push/Pull? Do these rewards, for instance, seem to correlate with the sort of social dynamics that people associate with the two terms?

    Levi Kornelsen wrote:
    By my lights, Capes has some king-hell push/pull stuff. I make conflicts, and try to make them ones you want in on, and that's where the reward is?

    Well, certainly that's my take on it, yeah.

    I am very interested in the way that rewards can draw people not only to do a thing ("If you use a longsword and you're an elf you get +1 to attack") but a whole sequence of things ("If you use stealth then you can flank your opponents, and if you're already flanking your opponents then you can take an attack of opportunity, and if you take ten attacks of opportunity then you charge your Ring of Thiefiness, and if your Ring of Thiefiness is charged then you can use it to walk through walls"). Under that sort of schema, you do things not directly for the reward, but (game-theory-wise) to establish a condition ("flanking") that gets you closer to an end-state in which you can receive the reward.

    This gets really interesting when the sequence is made up both of things that you do and things that other players do. A single reward can lead you to assign importance both to your own actions and to the actions of others, in an interweaving that (I think) draws Push and Pull very closely together. And, incidentally, I think it's fun and functional.
