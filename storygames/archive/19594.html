\n I'm usually a taker from these conversations but I suppose I could try being a giver....once.....maybe.

I have found that there really is no end to the ways you can apply Apocalypse World's many mechanics to improve your gaming, I then got to thinking solo gaming was the only thing that seemed had not been improved by this gem of a system. Then I thought about it some more. AW and it's many hacks teaches us if anything that complex things become simple when thought of as moves so I decided as an individual with far too many games and not enough people to play them with to write on a series of index cards labeled with MC moves that fit the setting of the game I was playing at the time (which happened to be Technoir for those curious) and draw one for each time I made a particularly bad roll or just got stumped as to how I was suppose to progress and used these moves in context to further drive the plot.

Just wanted to share this see if anyone had any thoughts on this idea, I'm aware people do similar things with solo games but I like this system and would like to try and refine it.
(side note: To add meaning to the moves I usually rolled a Story Cube or two which helped add depth as to what each event meant when there didn't seem to be any good context for the move)

    \n So what's the purpose of solo play with the AW engine? Just curious. When I partake in solo play, it's for gamist satisfaction \u2013 experiencing a mechanical system and seeing if I survive or lose and looking for surprises.     \n In Squaring the Circle, I remove the MC by having bad outcomes produce Complications, Antagonists, Risks, or Debts (CARDs) to add to a House of CARDs, or at least shaking your House of CARDs. In either case, you roll at +6 less the number of CARDs you have, to see if your House comes crashing downing bringing all the imminent trouble all at once. The twist is at the end of the conference (i.e. the session) if your House is still standing you get the number of CARDs as XP. I do the same thing with theorems (i.e. projects or endeavours) which naturally replace Fronts* with something player-facing.

    What this thread reminds me of is that when you design a GMless / MCless game it can be worth checking if it is capable of solo play. I've discovered functional solo play hidden in some of my past designs, perhaps Squaring the Circle has it too.

    *It occurs to me that in drawing a hard move it may be worth incorporating the most relevant Fronts into the mix too. A separate deck makes sense to me, as you'll want hard moves to relate to the situation and not necessarily be equally distributed over all the possible Fronts.

    \n I feel those MC Attitudes should be more tightly pegged to the state of the game.
    Or at least modifiers. "+2 if you're nearly dead through no fault of your own" kinda thing.

    Of course, if we're really talking about automating the MC, the model would be much more complex; this still relies on you to make a judgment call.

    Addressing the whole topic in a general way: I've done tons of solo play with systems that weren't built for it. You will always have to make up impromptu rules for yourself and follow your own set of "ethical standards". One of my favorite techniques is the "binary tree" where you think of two broad classes of answer, roll 50-50 (high or low) to see which one it is, then split that into two more specific answers, again roll 50-50 again, do this as many times as needed til you have your final answer. Example: Say you've decided that you need to "take away their stuff". Which item will you take? Let's see... Weapons or Resources? (roll=low) Weapons. The gun or the knife? (roll=high) The Knife. Okay, the PC lost his knife.

    \n A simple thing I learned from Eero which is similar to the binary tree, but feels different:

    What's the first thing that pops into your head? Flip a coin: 50% chance that this is what happens.

    If it doesn't happen, you have to come up with something new. Now test it again with another coin flip.

    Continue until you get a result.
