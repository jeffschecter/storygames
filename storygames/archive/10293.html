    \nSomeone who works for Barnes and Nobles received an early copy of DMG 2. Highlights:
    \n
    \n"Skill challenge chapter begins with a recap of the new skill challenge rules along with the new table listing the DCs. It is followed by an example of play. They spend the next two pages laying out the ground rules of designing a fun challenge, which are the same as Mearls' first article on skill challenges, though there has been some refinement. This is followed by six more pages of guidelines on how to build fun challenges, including various ways you can fold, mutilate, and spindle the basic structure. They provide guidelines on how to incorporate ritual and power usage into the challenge. They specifically address the 'one person makes a check and everyone else assists' issue, and talk about ways to get the entire party involved. Finally, they spend an entire page on failing skill challenges, and how to make even failure fun.
    \n
    \nThe second half of the chapter consists of example skill challenges, drawn from the cream of the crop from other sources, and a few original ones as well. Most of these can be tweaked to fit a variety of situations, and their basic structure can be used as the basis for countless more."
    \n
    \n"It has some of the best practical advice on running a game that I've read since DMG 1. You know when a book has phrases like 'shared authorship' and 'cooperative narrative' in it on a regular basis, you're in for a treat."

    \n [2] Mouse Guard --> D&D\n
    Posted By: ValamirWow...the idea that making failure interesting is still a mind blowing event for some (many?) after 10+years of indie gaming effort really reflects just how low our penetration really is and how far ahead of the mainstream curve we really are.
    \nTotally agree. My experience at Gencon would confirm this.

    I don't remember the exact number but I believe people at Wizards claim that there are around 6 million people playing RPGs in North America (I could be way off on this number but it is in the millions).

    30,000 people attend Gencon. En World membership is 83,870. RPG.NET membership is 36,295. The sales for books like DMG 1 & 2 (where ideas for story based play are promoted) are much lower than PHB sales. Story Games represents a small percentage of people playing RPGs. But people who talk about RPGs online or attend cons also tends to be a minority percentage of the overall base (although much, much larger that the Story Games percentage). I've spoken to our local game stores and they've indicated a large portion of their customers never go to cons or spend much time online talking about RPGs.

    Additionally, the sales for DMG 2 tend to be lower than DMG 1 which tend to be lower than PHB 1. I knew a lot of people who were very excited for DMG 2 for 3E but couldn't get many players to buy into the ideas it presented. I would have love to seen how the D&D culture would have been affected if the advice in the DMG and rules like skill challenges and stunts were actually included in the PHB. That being said, I am excited and will buy DMG 2. I almost wish they released an alternative D&D. Tactical D&D and Story D&D.       \n I just posted this over at RPG.net, but since I've seen questions here before about reading PDFs on the iPhone, and I didn't find any mention of GoodReader when I searched the archives, I thought I'd share it here as well.

      I picked up GoodReader about 2 weeks ago, and I am absolutely beside myself with how well it works. Unlike every other reader I've tried, GoodReader uses it's own display engine, rather than using the iPhone OS's built-in PDF reader, and it runs circles around the competition. At this point, I'd actually rather read PDFs on my phone than on my laptop.

      In my opinion, here are the things that make GoodReader excellent:\n
        \n
      • It doesn't attempt to reflow the PDFs. I've used apps that do this, but because of the way the PDF format stores text, it's very hard to get sensible results. Text, especially columnar text and sidebars, is often rendered out of order. RPG texts are loaded with columns and sidebars, and tend to get butchered. By using other methods to ensure readability, GoodReader neatly sidesteps this problem.
        • \n
      • It loads the PDF into memory one page at a time. This is probably the most important point to me; the iPhone's built in PDF engine loads the entire document into memory. As a result, page navigation and manipulation (dragging, zooming) are laggy and extremely sluggish. Larger PDFs often cause the iPhone's built-in reader to crash. GoodReader, in contrast, has never crashed on me (knock wood), and moving around on the page is smooth and lightning fast. Zooming does cause momentary hesitation while the page is re-rendered, but at least for me, it's nowhere near long enough to be annoying.
        • \n
      • It has an easy interface for page navigation. Tapping near the edge of the screen moves the focus of the display one screen-width (or height) left, right, up or down, depending on which edge you touched. When you reach the bottom of the page, tapping the bottom causes the next page to load. There's a brief pause while it loads and renders the next page, but as with zooming, the pause is brief enough not to be annoying.
        In practice, then, reading a columnar RPG PDF looks like this:\n
          \n
        1. Zoom the text until the screen is displaying one column-width of text.
          2. \n
        2. As you reach the end of the currently displayed text, tap the bottom of the screen to scroll down one screen's worth of text.
          3. \n
        3. When you reach the bottom of the page, tap the right side of the screen to move to the next column, then tap the top of the screen to scroll back to the top of the page.
          4. \n
        4. When you reach the bottom of the last column on the page, tap the bottom of the screen to load the next page. It will remember your current zoom level, so that if the layout is reasonably consistent page to page, you won't have to adjust the zoom over and over again.
          5. \n
        \n
      • It has solid bookmarking capabilities. While it doesn't do anything earth-shattering here, the bookmarking functionality is more than adequate for my purposes. When adding a bookmark, you can title it descriptively, and the individual bookmarks remember location within the page and zoom level, allowing you to bookmark key sidebars or tables. You can bookmark multiple locations on the same page. Unfortunately, the app does not support annotations.
        • \n
      • It's easy to get PDFs onto the phone. GoodReader uses WebDAV to allow you to mount its file store as a network drive. To put files onto the phone, start GoodReader, put it in filesharing mode, and drag the PDFs into the appropriate folder in Explorer. If you don't have Wi-Fi, you can apparently transfer files over USB using a free third-party app, but I haven't tried that. You can also download files directly from the internet using an internal browser, but I haven't tried that either.
        • \n
      \nSince buying GoodReader about 10 days ago, I've read close to 500 pages of RPG material using the app, and I've been really happy with the experience. With the navigation tools it provides, and the convenience of being able to read in bed/on the john/etc., I now prefer reading PDFs on my phone to reading them at my desk.

      I'm also really surprised to find that, while hardcopies of the book are still unquestionably superior for use as reference at the table, when just reading through the manual, I actually prefer reading on my phone. I own a lot of my collection in both print and PDF, and even when I have the hardcopy right on hand, I now find myself choosing to read on the phone. While I've been reading novels using other ebook formats for a long time now, I never thought that PDFs would manage to woo me away from hardcopy game books.

      GoodReader is apparently on sale right now for $.99. It is well worth checking out.

      And before anyone asks, no, I am not affiliated with the makers of this program in any way. I just really like it.

      Posted By: Jason Morningstar[1] Marc that's great. Has your gaming influenced other improv-related stuff or vice-versa? I know you had a D&D themed show, too.
      \nAbsolutely. We did have a WoW-ish based show called Guilds of Steel, and there was talk a year or two back about doing a Lovecrafty-type thing based on our experience with the Roach, but it never came about. Play-wise, there's a bit of crossover here and there (in a Play Unsafe kind of way), which I think has really enriched things, from both directions. As far as games go, my first (very amateur) attempt at a story game design for one of the Ronnies was strongly influenced by an improv exercise (the Story Spine), and I've got a bunch of notes towards turning one of the other long-form narrative formats we did last year (Cochise!, the improvised 70s cop show) into an RPG, but that's in the very early stages.

      There's been some cross-pollination in the social circles - some of my gaming buddies taking improv classes, and dragging some other improvisers into playing some games - which has been pretty fun, as well. The two things are so close, in my mind, that I'm constantly surprised that there isn't more of an overlap.

      \n [3 games are math] Actually, although I can't defend this particular guy, a lot of scientific computation work goes into finding approximations that are <O(N^2) for problems with poly-time exact solutions. O(N^2) stops being tractable somewhere around the terascale regime. Some of the coolest real-world bits of CS I've ever seen are PAC-stable ways to get machine learning problems approximated in log-linear or linear time. Smart people make careers out of this, some of them not even on Wall Street.
