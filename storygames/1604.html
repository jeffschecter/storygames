Character Algebra: ( Quirk * Conflict ) = Trait

Judson_Lester
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Story Games
Over in Tony's Focus on conflict thread, there was this confusion that Tony cared about conflict. That's not what he was after, which is cool, even if it does feel like kind of a common problem around these parts recently. So, I was getting something interesting out of the tangents that were spinning off of that discussion in the misapprehension that we were on topic, and I wanted to start them over over here.

So, my contention is that any characteristic of a character that doesn't contribute to a literary style conflict (i.e. not a CR setup and resolution, but an actually nascent plot arc) is a mere quirk, something that distinguishes the character a little bit, but not part of what we care about in the character. This relates in a very strong way to the idea of using up the conflict in a character, and to Tony's question about what makes these traits something you care about.

I'd further contend that the reverse is true: that a mere quirk can become a fully fledged character trait if it's involved in a conflict.

Of course, my squirminess in this at present is this: the only solid distinction between a quirk and a trait would be whether they are or could be (either by inherent quality, or systemically) involved in a conflict. I don't think that's the real definition, to be honest, but that's as close to a formal definition as I can come right now. A second squirm is this: what do I mean by "involved in a conflict?" I want to say that the conflict arises because of the interaction between the trait in question and a second trait, local to the character in question or elsewhere, but I suspect flexibility here will be a virtue.

Finally, the operation I'm alluding to in the title there is convolution, a signal processing concept I'm gleefully abusing for the annoyance of the other engineers in the audience.

So that's my position on traits and conflict. Dispute? Agreement? Difference of opinion?

  • neelk
    October 2006
    I think I disagree with you. I'll try to lay out how I think things work, and we can compare. I'll deliberately avoid using any of the terminology you set up, to avoid name collisions.

    So, each character has a set of distinctive features. Any particular feature can become apparent to the audience only through contrast with other characters. You can't have a character seem brave if every character is brave, or wise if every character is wise. Bravery is revealed when there are cowards to contrast it against, and wisdom only when it can reprove folly. This is because we can only judge a character within his or her context, and if everyone is exactly the same there's no scale we can use to understand the intensity of a particular trait.

    Now, contrast does not have any necessary connection with the conflict in a story. You can use contrast to reveal similarities between the protagonist and the antagonist just as easily as differences. For example, suppose the *tagonists are a Jedi knight and a Sith lord, and we see them continue to duel on an exploding starship, even as the ordinary soldiers around them run for their lives from the pyrotechnic CGI effects. The conflict is between the two Force-wielders, but the contrast in this scene is between them and the mooks -- we see that the depth of rivalry between these two is deeper than even the human desire for self-preservation. The stormtroopers and rebels don't even need names, as long as we can imagine them as average human beings with a typical desire to live.

      October 2006 edited October 2006
      I came to this idea with the notion that you could build a pretty robust system around the idea of picking two traits (from the same or different characters) and developing a conflict between them.
      Well, consider that I wrote a whole game system that's pretty much nothing but this concept called Synthesis. Very simple Hegelian stuff, one trait is the Thesis, another the Antithesis, and the product of the conflict the Synthesis. Here's an old version:

      http://www.swcp.com/~tquid/synthesis.html

      (If you want the newer version join Indie Netgaming and get it from the file section there).

      So, yeah, I think it's more than doable. :-)

      That said, I think that it's a somewhat simplistic model, and that conflicts are potentially much more multivariate in form. In discussing bangs (which are essentially about setting up conflicts), I've said that the most obvious form is what I call the "Dilemma Bang." In this sort of conflict, the character has a choice between two obvious choices. Usually when you create the conflict from two "quirks," this is what results.

      For example, the most obvious way to build a conflict is to have two character relationbships, and have the characters related to have opposing goals. Character A wants X, and Character B wants Y, and X and Y are mutually exclusive, and only the PC can decide. In which case he's deciding which relationship to support, and which to dissapoint.

      But, again, this is just the most obvious way to go. Also obviously you can create more choices simply by adding in more traits. A third character in the above mix means at least a third choice. And, actually, can mean more choices if it's possible to select two of three in a solution.

      But less obviously you can create conflicts from only one trait, put into opposition with itself. For example, if you take the example of the character being a great swordsman, he could be given a decision where he has to choose between accepting either a well fashioned sword, or the training of a great master. So we can see that the options provided to one trait can be many-to-one, not just one-to-one. This is dependent on situation creation, instead of selection of traits.

      Also there are cases where situation may impact a large number of traits all at once in a non-specific way. The example I usually give here is dropping a lot of wealth on the character suddenly. Does his "Charitable" kick in, and he gives it to charity. Does "swordsman" get selected, and he buys a great sword? Or the training of a great master? Does he give it to his family? Does he split the wealth, and give some to each? How much to each?

      This sort of conflict is often more satisfying than the dilemma, because it allows a wider range of expression. This is the one-to-many situation - one situation element bringing in many traits.

      Then there are composites. Help Character A and feel good about oneself, or help Character B, and get a lot of wealth and figure out how to spend it. A basic dilemma, but with one choice bringing in the one-to-many case.

      There's nothing wrong with very simple, straightforward dilemmas. They're often very powerful. And, in fact, there are often more choices than may at first seem to present themselves (like the classic "walk away", "won't choose," "delay," or "none of the above" choices). And they're easy to come up with. But for advanced conflict creation, look for situation to drop on characters that have more complex decisions.

      Mike
