    I'm not particularly concerned with people who are already happy about how their games work. I wrote the game because I couldn't get something I wanted and I figured I could get it. The text is flawed, to be sure, but even Ron, who opened that floodgate, played what sounds like a really awesome game right after his accusations of skullduggery. He's holding the game to a very high standard, which is cool; it's the standard I set for myself when I designed it, and it hasn't met it because of my writing.     Posted by: Joshua A.C. NewmanHeh. I meant your impression of what a Shock is, not what the game's like.
    \nCheh. Say so lah.

    At any rate, my earlier impression of Shock? I have no idea now. It's stuck vaguely as "story element" in my memory, but I wasn't sure what kind of story element. Alexander William's post actually clarified a lot of things, at any rate.

    So... a Shock is in essence an element of the fictional setting, often a difference between the fictional setting and the real world (examples include tentacle monsters, cloning, superluminal space travel, the Force, god-like transcendent minds, dragons, jaunting, fantasy pseudo-feudalism, other dimensions, a hostile spirit world encroaching upon an oblivious humanity, extensive body modification, magic).

    Another element in character creation is an Issue, which is an idea that the players and GM will want to explore (examples would include Libertarianism, Worker's Rights, Alcoholism, Drug Use, Abortions, The Post-Modern Condition, Personal Responsibility, The Cost of Celebrity, Civil Rights, Gay Rights, Sexuality, Individuality, Self Deception/Groupthink).

    In the Shock: system, characters are formed in the junction area between Shock: and Issue, of which I'm not exactly sure how.

    In the Shock: system, characters are formed in the junction area between Shock: and Issue, of which I'm not exactly sure how.

    \n

    "How" is like this: you choose it, then make something up about the Protagonist in that spot. In the above example, you might say, "OK, so the government wants every birth or abortion registered and I'm a pregnant 14 year old, which is illegal. My Antagonist is a Fertility Agent." Then the person to your left says, "OK, I'm a Fertility Agent, and the Agency hired me after I successfully hid my own pregnancy and childbearing. They were so impressed with my evasive talents, they hired me." Since you're both building these characters that are colliding together over this thing, "Teen Pregnancy" and "OOWG" are going to factor hugely.

    \n

    It's something that happens in play. It doesn't need a score or anything; it's because you agree to make a story about that. Along the way, people often incidentally address other issues, too, which is great. Sometimes the secondary issues wind up being more important to people. I don't care about that so long as they're making the SF they want. It's not like I'm going to scowl at people if the issues they wind up addressing aren't on the Grid.
