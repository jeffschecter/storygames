\n It's time for a little experiment.

I've recorded four episodes of Have Games, Will Travel: For A Few Games More, and I'm holding them for ransom. The episodes, which run about an hour in total, cover:\n
    \n
  • Self-reflection on play preferences in RPGs,
    • \n
  • Flexibility and false choice in wargame design,
    • \n
  • The worker placement mechanic in board games, and
    • \n
  • Jeepform games.
    • \n
\nIf the ransom is met by October 17, I will release the episodes one at a time at one week intervals. If not, I will delete them. It's as simple as that. For more on the Ransom Model of release, have a look at this article. If you've got other questions, feel free to ask on the Gameslinger Enterprises forums.

Thanks!

--Paul

    \n As I wrote somewhere else in response to the same criticism that Jason offered, from an end-user standpoint, there is no functional difference between the Patronage model (the one where payment comes before work, as in Play Sorcerer) and the Ransom model (as Paul is using here, the one where the work is created before payment is secured). In both cases, as far as the end-user is concerned, there is no product until the money is raised.

    End-users will never see the destruction in the case of non-raising of funds, so it is functionally identical to it having never been produced at all. And if the funds are raised, then the Ransom model actually pays off for the end-user faster (and likely bearing greater resemblance to what was promised) than the Patronage model.

    What I do find very interesting is that if the money isn't raised, no one gets burned in the Patronage model, but the producer gets burned in the Ransom model (because their work goes to waste).

    \n Moreno, it's why I said functional difference. Because there is no functional difference for the end-user between either model.     \n Now we're in pedantry, but I, too, thought Ransom Model meant a sample of something was offered, but the final product was not written until it was paid for. That said, I think Patronage Model is a smarter/more accurate term (with Ransom Model meaning this approach of completing and then threatening destruction).

    As for "feelings" on the matter: I hope to use Patronage Model to fund production of future GLASS supplements, so I'm keen to see it work. The issue of immediate gratification is, to me, sort of moot--if I had Patronage in hand, I'd be quick to produce (I'm paid--I'd consider that as being on a deadline; and I'd publish my intended deadline, with a disclaimer that I reserve the right to extend it if an emergency keeps me from working). Further, in general, it seems too me that fans rabid enough to pay in advance will wait (viz Magic Burner pre-orders and the month+ wait to receive after paying in full). In general, such a pre-pay model SHOULD lead to work that's valued by the customer base (or higher-quality printing, for those who use pre-order to fund small-press runs). And you end up with a free product for all, and a well-compensated author. that's a win-win, in my book.

    As for the "New Ransom Model" (this), I'm kind of with Jason. "Nobody move of the nigger gets it" (Richard Pryor, Blazing Saddles) tends to just make me say, "Fine, shoot yourself." It feels... manipulative, I guess? I don't want to see something creative destroyed, but I don't want to be taxed to avoid that. What's wrong with a Donations Model--tell folks, "Dig it, I can't do this anymore without some donations to offset costs--chip in, and I'll say 'stop' when I've got enough to move forward for X months/weeks/whatever"? That has some of the aspects of Patronage Model above, but without a direct link to a given product; it's more like the literal meaning of "patronage," as in 'supporting the author no matter his produce.'

    In short, you can't ransom something to a person with little interest in it (and, frankly, podcasts are a dime a dozen, these days--no offense). And to presume to do so could have the opposite effect that you desire (viz my "fine, shoot yourself" point). In essence, you're attempting to apply leverage when you have none (or very little, if we speak of your fans). And leveraging strangers is just... well, icky. Leveraging your die-hard fans is... exploitative? I just can't see a good angle to it (and yet, you're apparently gonna hit your mark).

    -----
    One thing worth commenting upon, too, is the time limit. Why? Why not just let it be known and say, "when I hit $X, I release to public domain" and let the money trickle in? To create a sense of urgency, I suppose? For my own Patronage thing for GLASS, I doubt that I'll ever have a time limit--rather, I'd let the ebbs and flows of interests in various genres build up the coffers until one peaked, and then put that out ASAP. In other words, I'd present several options (sort of like you have, but you don't treat them as independent) and then say, "OK, start donating--each one that hits $X gets written ASAP, with first-hit, first-written policy, and a lag time of Y weeks to write it." That way folks know that another project could trump theirs (i.e. if they're really impatient, just don't donate to any project other than the lead one) and that, eventually, all projects will be completed, assuming a steady trickle of donations that eventually tops-up all Patronage requirement.

    Hehe... I'm gonna call it The Mutual Fund Model. Or maybe The Buffet Model (we don't put out new mac and cheese until the old mac and cheese is mostly empty).

    \n The time limit feature is interesting because of the Bystander Effect and Diffusion of Responsibility. Phenomenons that lead me to believe that without the time limit (or another vector) people would count on others to fund the project as opposed to funding it themselves.

    However, there are alternate methods as well. Play Sorcerer raised funds and offered perks to those who gave money. There was a limit on how much money Christopher Kubasik was willing to accept, so only those funders who gave money before that limit was reached would get the perks. Which I suspect acted as an incentive to donate money as quickly as possible. Functionally there was no time limit on Play Sorcerer, and the money was raised rapidly.
