\n When I was in college my then-girlfriend decided to run a game using GURPS Riverworld \u2014 which was an excellent supplement, by the way \u2014 and in which all of the players would assume the roles of real historical figures. This isn't too far from the way the books play out, if you've read them, so there was nothing particularly revolutionary about the concept.

I played Ernest Hemingway. Another player took on the role of Machiavelli. It went around the table like that. The game was a total disaster, and part of it was due (I think) directly to the fact that we were trying to be these real people and it interfered with the game-y things we were called upon to do.

Just this afternoon I was looking at an (apparently terrible) Xbox 360 game called Rogue Warrior. In it, you take on the role of Richard Marcinko, founder of Seal Team Six and an all-around badass. Because my brain is wired in strange ways, this game got me thinking back to that Riverworld game. I find that I don't really want to step into the shoes of a real person to do game-type stuff. It's can be beneath the person in question or totally out of character or just plain discomfiting because you feel a duty to that person to hew as closely to their actual self as possible.

Given my experience, I'd rather play someone in the mold of a real person. Not Ernest Hemingway, but a writer of the Lost Generation with a penchant for manly pursuits. Not Dick Marcinko, but a stone-hard Navy SEAL capable of taking out whole units of enemy on his own. These may be outsized caricatures of the original person, but that's okay because we're only using the person as a stepping-off point, not as a model.

Or am I being too sensitive to the whole responsibility thing? I mean, it is just a game and so we're free to take as many liberties as we want. Right? I dunno.

    I'm with Matthijs. Riverworld has Mark Twain in it, but he's not real Mark Twain. I'm sure Mark Twain couldn't possibly have been all the character that carry his name, even ignoring the logistical impossibilities.

    \n

    I played James Bond in a game of Hot War once. Obviously, he'd failed (he'd just been in Cuba, stopping the Cuban Missile Crisis, and emerged from a submarine, only to find the UK as it is in Hot War), and was certain the SPECTRE was behind it all. He was confident as ever that sleeping with the scientist, saying witty things, having a tuxedo, and being a classist dick would save the world again, when clearly, he was wrong, and was violently out of his element. I don't see why playing a historical figure would be any different. I'd feel differently and more cautious if it was a living person, now that I think about it. But history, fame, and fiction are amazingly similar processes.

    Here's the thing: the difference between "playing Ernest Hemmingway" and "playing a fictional character modelled after Ernest Hemmingway" is, well, the name of the character. Because even if you're playing "Ernest Hemmingway", you're really playing a fictionalized version of him. No matter how much historical research you do, you're not Hemmingway and Hemmingway was never whisked away to a magic planet where people go after they die (that I know of). You've already made a fictionalized version of him.

    I agree with what everyone's said, and would only add that it's the same principle that applied when you use a 'real' place as the SETTING for your game, which seems to be a pretty easy thing for most gamers to wrap their heads around.

    I think it'd be really tough to run a Riverworld game with folks who haven't read the books, certainly... If you get the idea that the characters in those high-adventure pulp SF novels are supposed to be anything but sketches of those actual people, you'll run into trouble, especially when you consider the metaphysical baseball bat to core beliefs that waking up in an afterlife created by aliens necessitates. There's no reason on Earth why the Heminway who ate a revolver might nit end up a radically different guy when he's had some time to actually reflect on that act.

    Slightly related to the OP...

    At a recent con I was running Don't Rest Your Head and a guy I sort of knew from a couple years ago sat down at my table. During character generation, he reveals that the character he's playing was... Richard Rogers, obsessive podcaster and gamer. It was really uncomfortable for me. I should have said something, but I didn't. I decided to run with it and put my concerns to the side.

    Then he set the opening scene, the answer to What Just Happened to You? with "I just found my computer trashed when I got home". As I set the scene, I pretty much indirectly refused to describe my own home and put it back on the player. I did the same with every scene and prop he pulled in until we hit the Mad City.

    As the game went on, he took his character into some cool directions (his Madness power was "I can interview anything!") and he never openly tried to mock me or anything... but it really, really broke my brain then squicked me.
