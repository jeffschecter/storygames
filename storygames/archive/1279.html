    I don't think it's a matter of good or bad. It's a matter of whether you're resolving intent as well as action.

    CR: You state your intent (and possibly actions). There is an identifiable conflict, i.e., someone (or something) works against your intent. You go through the CR system, which might be one roll (e.g., scene resolution) or several (e.g., HeroQuest's extended conflicts). Your intent will be resolved one way or another. No trying the same thing again.

    Example: I want to find incriminating evidence on you. We go through the system. Either I find the evidence, or I do not. Once we agree to have a conflict about it, however, both are possibilities (the GM can't say, "You win the conflict but there's no evidence"--you cannot violate the intent I won).

    TR: You state a task that a character performs. You go through the TR system, which might be one roll or multiple (but tends to be one). Your task will be resolved. The intent may or may not be resolved.

    Example: I want to crack this particular safe. My intent might be to get evidence, or I may just be curious what's in there. If I succeed, the safe's open. If not, it's not. Can I try again? Maybe, maybe not. If I get it open, there's no guarantee that my *intent* is satisfied. Maybe there's evidence, maybe not. I can succeed at all my rolls, yet never have an actual chance to satisfy my intent.

    Ergo: CR allows you to avoid frustrating your intent. But TR allows you to be surprised, play sandbox with a predetermined environment, etc.

    Actually, I think Tony's post is slightly confusing. CR/TR has nothing to do with players disagreeing.

    Conflict Resolution is used when two or more characters within the created fiction want something and those somethings conflict.
    Task Resolution simple resolves any probalistic action within the world WITHOUT resolving whether or not the character gets what he wants.

    The Classic Example:

    Jack the thief wants to get the diamond his rival Baron Ducardi wishes to use to propose to their mutual love interest Princess Irene.

    Using Task Resolution we roll to see if Jack sneaks past the guards, to see if Jack can locate the treasure room, to see if jack can pick the lock on the door, to see if Jack can spot and disarm the trap in the room, etc.

    See how none of these things individually decides if Jack gets the diamond or not? This works just fine if the GM has a map and every lock, door, trap and guard between Jack and the diamond planned out. First this is a lot of work for the GM but also in certain styles of play where the GM is being more foot loose it can be used to thinly disguise GM fiat. See, the GM can simply keep inventing more and more tasks for Jack to do until Jack fails one if for whatever reason the GM doesn't want Jack to get the diamond.

    Conflict Resolution on the other hand resolves the important issue in one go. See the conflict here is between Jack who wants the diamond and the Baron who doesn't want Jack to have the diamond. So we roll Jack's Theiving Skill vs. the Baron's Leadership skill (or whatever best respresents the Baron's command over his estate) and if Jack wins then he's gotten through and gets the diamond. If the Baron's Leadership roll wins they we say that Jack was caught by a guard or that a trap dumped him down into the dungeon, or whatever best makes sense from the context.

    A Note On Scale:

    Some people think this means that Conflicts are large on Tasks are small, but that's not the case. Let's reward this example slightly.

    Jack wants to woo the Princess Irene who doesn't think very much of him. Jack knows the Baron has a diamond (and assume in this case that the Baron is just a bit of setting color and not every interesting on his own).

    So, first we roll to see if Jack can steal the diamond from the Baron as above. But given THIS context that's Task Resolution. Because whether or not Jack steals the diamond doesn't resolve whether Jack successfully woos the Princess or not which is the conflict at hand.

    Similarly Conflicts can be very small.

    Jack now kicks in the Baron's bedroom door because the Baron's henchmen have kidnapped the Princess Irene. The Baron goes for his sword and Jack goes to kick over the table the sword is on. That's a conflict. What happens exactly to the sword? We roll, it's settled. Now the Baron decides to make a break for the door and Jack wants to leap in his way. Again, that's a conflict, we roll and that issue is settled. Next the Baron lunges bare fisted at Jack and Jack wants to wrestle him to the ground? We roll. Who gets hit? Who gets tackled? While the Baron is on the ground Jack tries to get the Baron to tell him where the Princess is. Again we roll and so on.

    The point being that at each stage we are resolving conflicts between what the characters want and the actions they are taking to get them. A key concept is that it's generally a bad idea simply to keep repeating conflicts. Once the issue of the sword it's settled it's settled, the sword is either taken or it isn't. No do-overs. Simillarly once the issue of whether the Baron can escape the room is settled, it's settled. No constant repeating of attempts to escape.

    Does that help?

    Conflict resolution is simply a method of resolution that concerns itself with the "intents", "goals", "objectives" or "interests" of the characters. As Jesse said, the scale of the conflict isn't important to the principles of Conflict Resolution, and if somebody had a "too-big" goal of "Kill the Dragon, save the Princess & become King of all Men" or an out-of-genre "I want to find an atomic bomb in the Dragon's pile of treasure" goal or they wanted something that is obviously out of genre or scale for the game, you can call bullshit (just as you would with Task Resolution). So from now on, I'm assuming that all the goals are reasonable and at the scale of the game system.

    So, a conflict requires two or more "characters"* who have goals that are incompatible or would interfere with each other. That's a "conflict of interest" and that's when you get out the dice (or cards, or whatever). If there is no "conflict of interest", you don't roll. (Which is the origins of "Say Yes or Roll Dice.") It means you only get the system out for when things matter.

    Up front, everybody should make clear what the various goals for each of characters are, which should make it pretty clear who is in conflict with who. It also has the effect of knowing what is riding on the outcome of the dice before they are thrown (If my goal is "get onto the ship before it sails" and your goal is "throw your character in jail" we all know the potential consequences of these rolls are), and I find that increases tension at the table.**

    Then the dice come out and that determines who gets their goal and who doesn't. Note that this step can be quick & simple, real crunchy, or anything you like. It can be one dice roll, or many, but it always has a definite end point. It can also generate any other side effects that might fall out of the conflict as well, like Fallout in Dogs in the Vineyard. What really matters here though, is that this step mechanically determines which characters achieve their goals and which ones don't.

    These results then need to be adhered to by all the participants when describing the outcome and effect of the conflict, rather than any kind of GM fiat as Jesse pointed out. Also note that this particular conflict has now been resolved, so unless circumstances change significantly, there cannot be another conflict over the same thing between the same characters again. (This is pretty much where "Let it Ride" in The Burning Wheel comes from)

    Task resolution systems are much more concerned with the "How" than the "Why". There will still be conflicts in these games (otherwise the game will be tragically dull) and they will have to get resolved somehow (otherwise the game won't go anywhere), but the system doesn't really help the GM in that regard. Instead, the GM has to determine how the success or failure of the tasks the characters perform helps towards resolving the actual conflict by looking at the situation and coming up with something reasonable based on those tasks. Now he can ask the players for their intents and so on, but this is just fitting a social-level Conflict Resolution system around the book's Task Resolution core; and that (to me) is a lot of work when you can just get games which support Conflict Resolution anyway.

    * "characters" don't actually have to be, y'know, actual people. A cliff could well be a "character" in this sense with the goal of "dash climbers onto the jagged rocks below". It's just easier to think of it as happening between people first, and then extend that to inanimate, but dramatic, dangers in the fiction as well.

    ** And as a hint I've just recently realised, goals that are "stay", "keep" or "prevent" are really dull. Much better to always have 'active' goals like. "Capture all intruders" rather than "Keep intruders out" for trivial example. It means that whatever the outcome of the dice, something new & interesting happens.

    And more to the point, it firms up my rabid agreement with Adam that a "pejorative mental shorthand" is not only not a real definition, but I think what most of us have in mind as far as what "task resolution" means.

    The discussion of resolution mechanics begins with childhood games like Cops and Robbers, where resolution comes down to "I hit you!"/"Noyadiddent!" "Task resolution" mechanics build from there by saying: we'll let the mechanic decide and agree to abide by the decision. But what that does is elevate the argument: the question becomes not "did that bullet hit you" but "how many times may I fire upon you?" - if I can fire enough rounds, I'll hit eventually. But we're still just bickering like kids about whether you succeed or fail, we're just pretending the argument is about whether I can shoot again or not.

    Functional play introduces into the system some limit on repeated jeopardy, and by so doing become a form of conflict resolution. As soon as we say "if you survive 3 attacks, you get what you want" we're establishing ad hoc conflict resolution. Codifying that into the rule set is gravy (not to say it wasn't amazingly cool gravy, but gravy all the same).

    One thing we set aside by simply putting the whole conflict up and resolving it within the rule set are the story telling potentials that task rolls give us. Mostly, these are non-conflicts that have dramatic effect. I'm considering specifically the ability to call for false Listen checks (in other words, there's nothing to hear) in Call of Cthulhu or order to drive home to the players that their characters are trying not to be caught.

    So, a few points of this musing: we think ill of task resolution since our experience is that it amounts to little more than bickering with dice added for color. But we can build functional conflict resolution out of task resolution, and there are useful effects of doing so. And that task and conflict resolution are not actually mutually exclusive alternatives, but have a more complicated relationship that is (not strictly) hierarchal.

    Task Resolution resolves the success of actions. Conflict Resolution resolves the success of goals. "Pure" TR says nothing about goals; it's up to the group to link resolved actions to goals. Pure CR says nothing about actions; it's up to the group to link resolved goals to actions.

    For really simple, concrete goals, such as goals where only one action is required and it's obvious what it is, Task and Conflict Resolution look the same. e.g. if your goal is "Hit this guy with my sword," there's no significant difference whether you use TR or CR.

    For some goals, even though they're pretty specific, it's not immediately obvious that an action will accomplish the goal. Let's say instead of "Hit this guy with my sword," your goal was "Give this guy a scar with my sword." It's quite possible to take the action (hit the guy with your sword) but not accomplish the goal (give him a scar). Now, maybe the game system has written rules linking the action to the goal, e.g. "If you roll a natural 20 on your attack, the guy gets scarred." If the written rules don't say anything, the people at the table will come up with some way to decide whether the action accomplishes the goal. e.g. your GM might say "Yeah, if you hit him, he gets scarred," or make up the natural-20-equals-scar rule, or say "No way can you scar him" (if your GM is lame). At this level, there's still not much of a difference between TR and CR, maybe just a bit of on-the-fly decision-making.

    When goals are fairly abstract, or require a lot of actions, the difference between TR and CR becomes much more obvious. e.g. if your goal is "Make this guy fall in love me."     Task resolution is between you and an imaginary object. Conflict resolution is between your interests and someone else's interests. It might help if we note that all games have both task resolution and conflict resolution.

    • D&D 3e task resolution: roll d20 + modifiers to beat a target number
    • D&D 3e confilict resolution (combat): reduce opponent to 0 hp

    • D&D 3e confilict resolution (non-combat): accomplish a chain of tasks determined by GM

    • GURPS task resolution: roll 3d6 below skill + modifiers

    • GURPS confilict resolution: same as D&D for most part, but some social conflicts are resolved with reaction rolls

    • Sorcerer task resolution: Drama resolution (you said it, it happens, assuming you have the right to say it)

    • Sorcerer conflict resolution: roll dice pool to beat opponent's highest die roll

    A large number of indie games use Drama for task resolution. Most mainstream games use the chain of tasks (essentially Karma resolution) for conflict resolution.

The Buffy RPG does similar with it's research rolls. i.e. You want to find out about a particular monster. You don't say where you're going or what you're checking, you just roll and if you succeed then you find somewhere about the information. The mechanic doesn't specify it.

    For example, one case of abstraction is in combat mechanics. For example, in Tunnels & Trolls, combat is highly abstract. You don't know what a given person is doing to attack -- they just roll their dice and you can add totals. D&D has a much lesser abstraction. You specify the weapons used, though little else. GURPS is even less abstract, specifying maneuver (like thrust vs swing and so forth).

    However, note that ultimately the group is going to restrict the level of abstraction for the game -- but it could vary highly. For example, a character might have the motivation that he wants to be a famous hero whose deeds are sung of throughout the land. If you're playing a large-scale, generational game -- the player might say "Look, let's roll to see if he gets to be a famous hero. I don't want to have to specify the tasks out to accomplish that goal." That works fine. However, not everyone will want that level of abstraction. Sometimes they might want to play out the individual quests along the way to that ultimate goal.

    Sometimes they might want to play out the individual quests that lead there. However, this means that the PC could go on a quest, and it doesn't make him a famous hero.     September 2006 edited September 2006
    As I was saying over on the RPGSite, this is how I see the procedural difference bewteen TR & CR:

    In a 'pure' TR system, this is what I see happening:
    1. A player wants X to happen ("I want to get the King to give my church money", "I want to kill that Orc"). He can keep it secret if he wants.
    2. The player 'translates' his desire into an appropriate (series) of tasks for his character - let's call that task Y ("I'll persuade him", "I hit him"). He has to tell the GM this.
    3. In order to perform Y, something in the mechanics determines the chances of success of the character to do Y.
    4. The mechanic is applied and determines if the character does Y or not.
    5. The GM* describes what happened, taking into account the results of Y and the current situation. Or, if Y is failed, the GM* comes up with the consequences of failure (which could be "nothing happens", or it could be "take 10d6 falling damage", or whatever) and applies them. (You can see this as translating the effects of action Y back into "did X happen", if you like)
      * Subnote: This can just be GM skills, or it can be GM + specific rules, like Bluff checks in D&D 3.5 or whatever.
    6. Depending on how good the translation from X->Y->X went, the player may or may not have got his original X, even if he succeeded. In the case of failure, the consequences are not always known to the player when he started his action by telling the GM 'Y'.
    In CR, there is less translation:
    1. A player wants X to happen ("I want to get the King to give my church money", "I want to kill the Orc"). He has to tell the GM this.
    2. Another character must want to prevent or interfere with X by wanting Z ("I want to stay as rich as possible", "I want to kill him right back"). If not, we are done, the player gets X and somebody describes what happened.
    3. In order to obtain X against Z, something in the mechanics determines the chances of success of the character to obtain X against Z.
    4. The mechanic is applied and determines if the player gets X or not, as well as if the other guy gets his Z or not.
    5. The GM (or sometimes players, depending on system) describes what happened, taking into account the results of X & Z (and any other restrictions that steps 3 & 4 might apply).
    Does that make any sense?

      What's fascinating to me here is that there's so many criteria for TR vs. CR being espoused.

      There's:
      1. Model of intent: TR is how (what you're attempting), CR is why (what you want to accomplish)
      2. Repeatability: TR you can try again, CR not.
      3. Type of opposition: TR is versus world, CR is versus characters.
      4. Level of detail: TR is narrow focus, CR is a higher view.
      Did I miss any? Really, I think we can natter on and on because we're trying to explain this distinction from at least 4 different directions, and I think there's two important points to take away.

      First of all, Fred wins: All games have Conflict Resolution. It might not be in the rules, but to advance a plot, or even just have stuff happen, your game has to resolve conflict. So trying to categorize games as CR vs. TR is a false dichotomy.

      Second, and this is up front contentious: only #1 there is a valid distinction. Conflict resolution is about following up on intent, task resolution is about determining ability. Many games indicate that you can't re-try task checks, and many games do allow re-tries of conflicts (consider how many times you've seen a sentence like "you can't try to get your intention again unless the situation changes." The "cliff is a character" idea only exists to support #3 - if you aren't trying to support the oppositional criteria, you don't need it. And level of detail is difficult enough to argue that it doesn't really need to be refuted.

      That's task resolution. True, but not the answer to the question.
