    \n I talk a bit about how we used storyboarding (without thinking about it or calling it that) last night in our D&D game to frame a flashback scene where two characters met the first time.

    Storyboarding was useful there because we knew how things really turn out (no one dies, these two end up trusting each other to some degree, and Mary leaves her farm to come to the city) but not the specific details. Daniel and Susan worked out a lot of stuff in director stance via metagame talk, then role-played out bits and pieces inside that framework to "color it in." It worked because it was an event from the past and finding out what happened wasn't the issue; the issue was finding out HOW it happened.

    \n The most important thing about 'storyboarding' as it has recently been used is that it is a perjorative term used by people trying to describe an in-game phenomenon that they don't like. It is not used constructively; it is used to identify an unwanted behavior. As yet, nobody has really got behind the technique to advocate for its actual utility, if any (although Ralph seems to be sliding towards that as a devil's advocate). A lot of 'storyboarding' discussions remind me of the old Beeg Horseshoe discussions where folks argued whether or not Simulationism actually existed. It's an incredibly one-sided discussion of questionable value.

    As far as I'm concerned, players complain about storyboarding when their fellow players scene-frame past the stuff that they wanted to play through. In other words, when there is an unspoken disagreement about which parts of the fiction are significant and which parts can safely be glossed over. It can and should be remedied by saying, "Can we stop? I'd like to play this part out." Turn the unspoken disagreement into a spoken discussion. It's really that simple.

    To my eyes, "story-boarding" is the same phenomenon as the "pre-narration" problem in PTA and similar games, and we've been talking about that since, when?... '05 or so?     \n I made a theory-ish post in the other thread about why I think story-boarding causes problems. I'll go ahead and quote myself here:\n
    My definition involves a shift in the way IIEE and the general procedural nature of play works. This is going to take some explaining:

    I believe that improvisation and "composition" (aka "normal" authorship) represents two distinct modes of the creation. As such, each mode presents its own set of advantages and disadvantages. Traditional RPG play is inherently improvisational. In fact, it seems structured to maximize the strengths and minimize the weaknesses of improvisation.

    Now, I would argue that the immediacy of the fiction that we experience when role-playing---stuff like the heightened sense of character identification---is largely a byproduct of improvisational play. This is where my understanding of "story-boarding" comes from.

    Normal play is built "sequentially" or "procedurally", one event on top of the last. I would call story-boarding those times when players step off to the side and develop multiple events all in unison, and then pop them back into the sequence. In other words, they hit the "improvisational pause" button, and begin engaging in "compositional" play.

    In the big picture, this is a perfectly fine technique, but there is a trade-off that players must be aware of. The players gain the advantages of "composition", but they lose the advantages of improvisation. And since the "immediacy" of the fiction is one of those things gained by improvisation, it's one of the things players lose by switching modes.

    And that seems to explain what people are complaining about with "story-boarding".

    \nI think this "if everybody knows what happens, frame past it" idea is part of the mechanism that causes the unsatisfying play for some people. There is frequently more value to playing out a scene than just determining a plot branch. There is the experience of feeling the scene happen. However, if people believe that they aren't going to learn anything new from a scene they aren't motivated to actually play it out, because it instinctively looks like it will be wasted effort. Since the scene isn't actually played out it feels to some people like there's a gap in the story, since they didn't experience it in-the-moment, and they stop finding the story engaging. It would be like an author leaving out the text of a few chapters and just giving the outline of what happens. The audience can't engage with an outline.

    I think the problem stems from "do two players disagree about the plot?" being a poor proxy for the question "is seeing this scene important to the story?".

      \n To me the problems under discussion have clear parallels with Ye Olde Schoole Railroading in its various forms.

      Skipping over scenes "where there is no conflict" seems unfun the same way skipping from one setpiece to the next is.

      Pre-scripting scenes tightly seems unfun the same way being overruled by the GM (be that via "you can't do that", or illusionist techniques) is -- or having your character seized by the GM.

      Etc.

      It seems to me that the issues are

      A. failing to see that scenes without character conflicts are necessary to highlight those conflicts. If we see a character only under duress, we don't really know what it means. How does the character deal with the duress afterwards? How is the character different when kicking it back?

      B. failing to allow meaningful immediate in-character play. It's cool to have shared narrative powers, but if that leads not being able to act freely in-character something is being missed out.

      I don't think these are failures of storyboarding, or issues of bad scene framing. I think these are sins on their own -- which both bad scene framing and bad storyboarding can result in.

      To me they seem like the results of focusing too much on the author/director stance and narrative powers to the detriment of playing in-character -- be that in-character play immersionist or not. I believe classic bad railroading stems from similar overfocus: classic GM advice focuses far too much on GM's powers and responsibility to make the game (story) happen, and not enough on the role of the game as a vehicle for in-character play -- the core activity from which the story emerges.

      \n Adam, since I wasn't there, and since I'm still not sure the exact level of detail your players provided, I'm not sure whether or not I would actually call what you guys did "storyboarding".

      I think a distinction should be drawn between "outlining" a scene/act and the phenomenon we're now calling "storyboarding". [Gawd, this is like the fourth time I've said this.] I believe "storyboarding" is more complicated than just outlining a scene. IMO, it's very significant *what exactly* is being outlined, and towards what end.

      In most examples of "outlining" that I'm aware of---which might include The Mountain Witch---the outline is used to simply set up a situation for play. If I say, "in this scene the two lovers meet, and it should end with some sort of hint that they are falling in love," that's not saying what's going to happen in the scene. In other words, in that sort of play the end of the scene isn't important, it's all the interactions in the middle that's important. The players are still creating the actual story, they're just following a preset dramatic arch.

      This is like a chord progression in a jazz song. The structure ensures there's some sort of musical movement, but it doesn't outline what the actual melody/harmony/etc is going to be.

      I believe the storyboarding issue is different. In those cases, the players are actually outlining the dramatic events that are meant to be important. So when they sit down to play the scene or whatever, they're no longer actually creating a story, they're just coloring or detailing the (psuedo-) script.

      Do you follow that distinction I'm trying to make? It's a very subjective standard---which is hard to discuss online since we lack the greater context of play---but I believe that's the thing that distinguishes between "fun" and "unfun".

      Adam, if you're following what I'm saying, would you still call your experience "storyboarding" or just "outlining"?
