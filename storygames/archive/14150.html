\n Every time I run or play In A Wicked Age, I get a huge desire to run or play a hacked version of Vampire: the Requiem. I don't know why exactly, but it's time to give in to my desire.

Mechanics/Resolution Hacks

1) The first hack is using "Say yes or roll dice" from DitV. V:tr is chock full of "to do X roll these traits against these other traits" and while the section about the main resolution mechanics says the GM can ignore the need to roll dice at her whim, I would much rather have the resolution mechanic come up only when someone at the table has a "Oh no you fucking don't" moment.

2) Vitae (blood) from feeding on mortals is capped based on the intensity of the relationship you have with the victim. A complete stranger will only ever give you a single Vitae, two if you kill them in the process of feeding. An animal will only ever give you one Vitae. If you have a relationship with the vessel, you'll be able to get more and more. Whether that relationship is friendly or antagonistic, the Vampire will only get the most out of feeding if the vessel has a strong connection to the Vampire.

3) A Vampire can only suffer final death by Diablerie. Even if it's body is completely destroyed by a nuclear blast, the vampire will eventually reconstitute itself. If you want to destroy another Vampire for ever, you have to feed on them until there is absolutely nothing left-- their very essence is consumed and united with their assailant. The main purpose of this house rule is to emphasize the "hell is other people" thematic statement that is often circumvented in V:tR by shooting people in the face.

4) Blood Potency caps Humanity. Your Humanity + Blood Potency can never be more than 10. If you want to be powerful in the ways of being monstrous, you're going to be messed up. Furthermore, every derangement you suffer from Humanity loss immediately raises your Blood Potency by one. If this causes further humanity loss, you don't check for derangement again. So the fastest way to become a powerful vampire is to act monstrously. And if you want the points that fuel your vampire powers (Vitae) you're going to have to feed off of those you have relationships with.

5) Keys from TSoY for XP. A very, very common V:tR hack talked about in different places. Basically it's there to ensure characterization. The V:tR XP system is so bolted on after the fact that many GMs running the game just give arbitrary XP anyway.

Procedural Hacks

1) City Burning much like the first session of Burning Empires/Wheel or Mouseguard. Or the city creation of Dresden Files. Willow's excellent blog posts about it:
http://willowrants.wordpress.com/2006/11/05/burning-vampires/
http://willowrants.wordpress.com/2006/11/06/burning-vampires-part-ii/

2) Relationship creation as part of Character creation. The whole point of Burning a city is to make an environment you interact with and since the relationships allow you to feed, they'll have to be explicitly stated.

3) Kicker creation after relationship creation. Pretty much how it works in Sorcerer as the City Burning session and character creation will occur at the same session. So everyone will have the same sort of collaborative self-propelling into action. The main reason is that as the GM, I don't want to have "my story" whatsoever.

4) Explicit shared scene framing. V:tR already has the scene as a mechanical unit of time. So this dial is being turned up to 11 and scene framing rights will go to any participant who loses a conflict. So if you get beaten down, out maneuvered or otherwise lose something you thought was worth fighting for, the next scene is framed by you. If it's a NPC, then it's the GM's turn. Times I've tried this before has led to a real victimization-revenge cycle.

Some of this needs to be fleshed out a bit more, but anyone have any thoughts or ideas so far?

    The #1 thing I found improved our games of Vampire was completely ignoring the "Humanity" rules. I dunno how Requiem does it, but in old Vampire they were pretty bad, and weirdly seemed to make humanity less important in the game. By having no rules for it at all, I found it made the players much more aware that there was nothing stopping them from sliding into inhumanity. Because there was no convenient gauge to tell you how human you were, it made us all judge the characters much more by their actions. It is far more terrifying to know that you can be as bad as you like, and there will be absolutely no consequences.

    There's some neat stuff you can do with Keys and Vampire. Here are some I used:

    Some Keys for Vampire:

    Key of the Monster:
    1 XP: Frighten someone.
    2 XP: Hurt someone.
    5 XP: Harm someone who cares about you.
    Buyoff: Fall in love.

    Key of the Guardian Angel:
    1 XP: Every scene you spend with a chosen human.
    2 XP: Save your chosen human from danger.
    5 XP: Significantly help your chosen human, at considerable cost to yourself.
    Buyoff: Reveal to your chosen human that you're a vampire.

    Key of the Blood Doll:
    1 XP: Every time a chosen vampire feeds from you.
    3 XP: Every time the chosen vampire feeds from you and leaves you weakened.
    Buyoff: Refuse to be fed on by your chosen Vampire.

    Key of the Beast:
    1 XP: Every time you frenzy.
    3 XP: Every time you frenzy and harm a friend.
    Buyoff: Frenzy and kill a friend.

    Key of Control:
    1 XP: Every scene in which you resist frenzy.
    3 XP: Every scene you resist frenzy against considerable odds.
    Buyoff: Voluntarily frenzy.

    Key of Repentance:
    1 XP: Every time you protect a human from harm.
    2 XP: Every time you suffer to prevent harm to a human.
    5 XP: Prevent harm to a human even though it nearly kills you.
    Buyoff: Feed on a human when it's not absolutely necessary.

    Key of the Puppet-Master:
    1 XP: Every time someone does your bidding.
    2 XP: Every time someone does your bidding, without knowing it.
    5 XP: Every time someone very powerful does your bidding, without knowing it.
    Buyoff: Reveal your plans to those who would stop you.

    Key of the Lurker:
    1 XP: Every scene in which you learn a secret.
    2 XP: Every scene in which you learn a secret, without anyone knowing.
    5 XP: Learn a secret with significant ramifications for powerful people.
    Buyoff: Reveal a secret without getting anything in return.

    Key of the Architect:
    1 XP: Every scene in which you work on a long-term project.
    3 XP: Every time you make a significant step towards completing your project.
    Buyoff: Complete the project, or abandon it.

    Key of the Anachronism:
    1 XP: Use technology from your own time in favor of a superior modern equivalent.
    3 XP: Spend considerable resources to do something in the manner appropriate to your own time.
    Buyoff: Use a modern equivalent when something from your own time will do the job.

    The "no rules for humanity" thing only works if you have players coming to the table interested in the humanity of their characters.     \n The reason why the Humanity system as written is good for morality-centered games is because players have no input in it. It really is the primary link to the vampire-story's Victorian roots. Anyone can justify anything to anyone else if you give them enough time, or put them in a tight enough vise, and vampires in Requiem are in a ridiculous vise and have infinite time. The key to making the system work is to really read how you gain Humanity back after a loss. It's remorse and making amends, the latter drives the game forward.

    This is also why in the oWoD, the Path of Whatever I Was Going To Do Anyway is both funny, and really something to avoid.

    \n I'm kind of looking for a humanity death spiral though. Where if you wrong someone and then make amends, you're building the intensity of the relationship with them, making them prime candidates for feeding, which is a supernatural victimization of a mortal, which might cause degeneration...

    In the humanity system as is, there are some problems with having a low humanity, but generally speaking, you can let it get really low before you have to worry about it. The rules even say that most Kindred stabilize their humanity at around 4. And they're right. You can hang out pretty much forever at humanity three through five without giving any thought to ever going higher.

    And if you do decide to go higher, system as is just encourages you to have the occasional contrived remorse/amends scene where you spend XP (you'd rather spend on stuff you'd actually want) just so you don't lose your character.

    I know it's not supposed to be a contrived scene where you just spend some reward points to make a problem go away, but that's what the system actually does in play unless you really take the time to force it to be different. At it's core, it's a tax on your reward points that you have to pay or you can't play that character any more.

    It's simply not a mechanic I want to fight in order to get what I want out of it. I would much rather have Kickers (and bangs) and the seeking of hitting Keys drive play forward.

    EDIT -- As for the Victorian fiction thing, you're right. They often include a very objective take on morality and the 7 deadly sins and humanity/degeneration fits with that. I guess I want a game more like vampire novels had they been written by Jean Paul Sartre in the late 1930s/early 40s. The Wall or No Exit, but with vampires.

    EDIT 2 -- That all said, if anyone has any ideas how to make the existing humanity rules work and work well (other than me as the GM insisting on people roleplaying a guilt scene to my satisfaction like the rules on p183 suggest) I'm all ears.

    \nIt's not supposed to be perfunctory. But the procedure spelled out on page 183-184 of V:tr is pretty weak. The player announces her desire to buy Humanity and the GM assesses the character recent actions in terms of their morality and says yes or no to the request.

I find "mental health problems are caused by immorality" to be distastefully prejudicial towards those facing mental health issues. So I need to rework Derangements as well to be something else entirely. Like mandatory key replacements with keys rewarding further inhumanity.

      \n An update:

      It's been a year and I've played two mini-series of this hack.

      In the first series, it started when a vampire got an XP line for their Key Vampric Degeneration that gave them an XP for every time they fed off of a mortal another vampire has a relationship with. The player chose the 14 year old daughter of another PC, who was a recently turned vampire as their primary target. The player took almost every opportunity they could to have a scene where she could simultaneously get XP for hurting the girl and her character's other keys. The other players started framing scenes to counter her vampire's attacks both in the short and long term. Including preparing a burial site, chains and a lot of cement to put the attacker down. It did end with the vampire being chained up and buried alive with wet cement. But not without first implanting in the girl's mind that the love of her life was trapped beneath the concrete and that she must free him in five years (his player saw his demise coming and she made preparations accordingly).

      The second series was a continuation of the first and started with the girl jack-hammering the concrete away. The vampire she freed immediately voluntarily frenzied, fed on her and killed her. Characters had been modified a bit (it was 5 years) and pretty much everyone had geared up for serious player-vs-player play. They all degenerated very quickly as they went after each other's mortal friends and family. It was like they all decided that the antagonist from the first series was the most fun and all went for it. The antagonist vampire from the first series ended up pretending to have kidnapped the girl and claimed he was holding her as a hostage to keep her mother at bay while he reestablished himself and made alliances with the rapidly degenerating vampires. The series ended with the vampire mother of the child finding her corpse and discovering she was dead all along.

      I've since come to the conclusion that nothing about the play that we enjoyed came from the rules set I started with (Vampire: The Requiem) other than some of the setting elements. Pretty much any task or conflict resolution system could have worked just fine. So it looks like the next time I run this, I'll probably go with Strands of Fate + Keys rather than a V:tR hack.
