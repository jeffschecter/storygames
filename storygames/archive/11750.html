\n I've made three posts in the last three days that are directly inspired by video games [1] [2] [3], and that got me thinking about the larger role of video games among the folks here. Surely I can't be the only one who's played a game and said, "Yeah, I want to do that."

One of the biggies for me has been trying to replicate the sneaky, stabby, fun-type stuff that one gets from the best of the Tenchu series. Thankfully FigureFour wrote Ninja Game, which scratches that particular itch pretty darned well. It doesn't do much on the roleplaying side (it's primarily tactical), but considering the way the video games play out this is perfectly acceptable.

I have heard of people making games inspired by such diverse sources as the Mega Man series and roguelikes. So I guess my question is what have you done, or what do you wish to do, in roleplaying that's directly inspired by video games?

    \nASCII @HACK! is a roguelike "RPG" (in quotes because it's really more of a strategy game with RPG color). It's broken, in its current form, and I mean to rewrite it... one day soon.

    I mainly though about a mash-up of story game techniques: DitV Traits, Universalis Facts, maximal player agency, "GMless" (actually has x-1 GM-types, where X is the number of players). It... doesn't work as yet, though mainly because it has too many moving parts, in an effort to include most of the RL tropes. The next (final?) version will be far more streamlined.
    -----
    In the past, I tried to build up a world-building and strategy game called Of Gods and Men, which was to simulate Populus or Black & White. It never got much past the resource management system design--like ASCII @HACK, I couldn't find the way to personalize and encourage acting, amongst all the mechanical calculations. I gave it up for dead, and it sits in some notebook on my shelf to this day.
    -----
    Aside from that, I'm not generally "inspired" by computer games. Most do what they do so well that, if I enjoy them, I just want to let the PC do all the grunt work and, you know, PLAY the computer game. I'm far more often inspired by books or movies....

    Marshall, Baroque Horror would be more appropriate.

    \n

    Castlevania's defining quality, especially in Symphony of the Night, is baroque excess. The architecture is endless rococo designs and labyrinths. At no point are the games frightening or horrific, but they have a surplus of horror elements all struggling for attention. The gameplay itself reflects this, with a million ways to progress, a million possible metagames you could play within the scope of SotN.

    \n

    2D cutscenes of course take after stage plays, whereas 3d ones take after movies. On a 2d sidescrolling plain, you lose dynamism of movement. The player-audience doesn't move, nor does an invisible camera move the viewpoint of the audience. Look into non-fighty mechanics that recreate campy over the top stageplays.

    \n I saw the link up there to the Legend-of-Zelda-style maps and sprites you could use, which were really cool.

    But I always thought Legend of Zelda did something which would be really hard to do in a tabletop RPG. I mean, the actual combat with weapons, plus running around and exploring and whatnot is easy enough, but one of the main themes running through all the games in this series was that it's not just about running around hitting monsters with more and more powerful swords. It was about fighting your way through a maze till you found the map, and using that till you found the compass, and using that to help you find the boomerang, which would let you hit the switch that would open the door and fight the monster for which the boomerang was a necessary tool.

    Then once back in the real world, the boomerang would let you do things and go places that you couldn't do before, even picking up hearts and coins for you and bringing them back to your pocket without you having to bother to pick them up. Every dungeon had some sort of neat tool that would let you do something new and interesting, and you had to explore to get it. Almost everything you could get in the game was actually sought and found that way, even extra hearts. Coins collected from monsters you killed were very useful for buying potions and stuff, but the only actual new ability they could buy for you was bombs, I think. There was no actual experience mechanic.

    Actually, come to think of it, Metroid had this aspect too -- you didn't have to kill every monster on the screen, only the ones that were trying to prevent you from getting the ball-rolling power or whatever. Exploring was half the fun, but if you were playing for a second time and you knew where to look, you could just run over and get it.

    I would love to see an RPG that somehow gave you new abilities through exploration rather than number of monsters killed. Also, it would be neat if each new ability you got was primarily a new functionality rather than just a bigger sword or a hotter fireball.

    Posted By: TulpaMarshall, Baroque Horror would be more appropriate.Castlevania's defining quality, especially in Symphony of the Night, is baroque excess. The architecture is endless rococo designs and labyrinths. At no point are the games frightening or horrific, but they have a surplus of horror elements all struggling for attention. The gameplay itself reflects this, with a million ways to progress, a million possible metagames you could play within the scope of SotN.2D cutscenes of course take after stage plays, whereas 3d ones take after movies. On a 2d sidescrolling plain, you lose dynamism of movement. The player-audience doesn't move, nor does an invisible camera move the viewpoint of the audience. Look into non-fighty mechanics that recreate campy over the top stageplays.

    I say "opera" because of the emotional content of the games. It's very over-the-top, melodramatic, over-wrought, and, well, operatic.
