    So, I do use an interview process - or, more accurately, I did, back when I actually had room for more people in our games. These days we've actually got a waiting list!

    When we were interviewing, we did a three-step process.

    First, we posted on a bunch of New York-area gaming boards saying "Hey, we're starting a game, email us!" In our post, we asked people to send us an email describing their interest in our game and their favorite character moment from a past game. We got about 40 responses, of which 30 were clearly not relevant. Half of them ignored our stated information about the game we were running (highly political, relationship-oriented) and the other half were just totally inappropriate - asking to bring in characters from other systems, using "I have a +2 sword" or the equivalent as their character description, and so on and so forth. Plus there were the two "WTF, a female GM?" emails, but those were just rude.

    Anyhow, we emailed the 10 (or so) people we thought were reasonable fits and interviewed the 5 who got back to us. This basically involved us going to hang out with them in person, hearing their gaming stories, talking to them about what we wanted to do with the game, etc. Of these, we actually really liked all of them, so they formed the core of our group.

    Step three was actually having the players meet each other. For our first round of players (of whom we would eventually lose two and add several more) we basically got them all together to create characters and said, "If there's someone here who really rubs you the wrong way, talk to us." Fortunately everyone seemed to get along fine! For later additions to the group, the prospective would come to play a session with the group. Anyone who got to that point was someone that we, the two GMs, thought we could work with - but we wanted to give our players a chance to veto.

    After that first session, if no current player objected, you were in!

    Socially, I think this has made us a really tight group. One of our number just got married, with everyone else in attendance and several of us actually participating in the ceremony. (One of our players is a minister and performed the marriage, which was neat!) We developed a high level of in-game trust very quickly because we knew we were all on the same page, and that helped us develop our out-of-game friendships, even though none of us knew each other (except for me and my partner) before we got together to game.

    Our most interesting story regarding this is the guy who contacted us when we had no space in our game . . . and waited for six months until we had an opening so that we could interview him. This guy is now my best friend.

    --Jess

If we all enjoy playing together, if I'm cool enough to play with them and they're cool enough to play with me, we'll keep playing. It's pretty informal and gut level, really. I don't have any "date games," per se. If I say, "Wanna play Sorcerer with me?" or "Wanna play a game of Universalis?" and they say, "Sure!", we're good.     I interview people before I play with them, if at all possible. Especially if I'm going to invite them to play in my home. I'm way past the naivete of cultivating meaningful relationships with all my gamer colleagues, but I still screen them.

    It's not harsh -- I basically ask them out to coffee or a light meal, though all my gaming buddies (and my wife, a non-gamer, but their hostess) are welcome to join us. I just ask what they expect to get out of play, what they've played before, and how they'd feel about games that we've played or want to play. And I let them know my house/hospitality rules.

    And even so, some people will be accommodating in the interview, and find out that we really do what we say we will, and that doesn't make them happy. They'll leave amicably and voluntarily, and are never heard from again.

    And even so, some people will join the group, bring other great (lasting) players in, and then explode in a fiery cataclysm when they inanely attempt to fully enforce copyright on their character simply because I attempt to protect my own game writing before publication. I need their contribution like I need a hole in the head. They get kicked out, and harbor an irrational, unrecoverable resentment. I shrug and move on.

    And even so, some people come back to gaming forums and post a long diatribe about how insulting, irrational, and idiotic I and my wife were during the interview. After one of my other gaming buddies notices the very one-sided multi-page thread, and figures out who the poster is frothing about, he gives me the heads up, and I call the person out. I get a half-assed apology that amounts to "I'm sorry I got caught," and the person requests that the mods delete the thread. Fortunately, I'm smart enough to download most of the posts before they're lost forever. I harbor resentment, and make sure that I and anyone I know never crosses paths with the individual (or his wife). They're never heard from again.

    Bottom line: I screen people because I want myself and the rest of my players to be happy. I have requirements because I think that gaming is not priority one, and because I've been burned when I don't enforce those requirements.
    I abide by my own requirements, OR I DON"T GAME.
    If I follow my own rules, and I invite you into my home, you follow them too, if you have no sensible reason to reject my rules. Or you leave. Very simple.

    None of this applies when I game out of my home. All bets are off, because I'm on someone else's turf, AND I CAN ALWAYS LEAVE.

    After family and job necessities, I am in complete control of my gaming enjoyment.

      Hey Larry

      Sorry, I was out of town for Easter. Plus, I'm a little cagey about my requirements these days, which is why I don't jam them in everyone's faces.

      But not cagey enough to ignore your question :) Here's a few of my criteria:

      1) Personal life first, gaming second.
      There are a bunch of corollaries here, and completely subjective distinctions based on how I've been burned in the past.
      For example, I ask that my players be gainfully employed, at least to the point that they have a steady income and their own transportation to and from my home. I've made exceptions for full-time students, but dedicated students, however well meaning, are unreliable. Their (correct) priority is their education.

      2) We're more than just gamers.
      Show respect by showing up on time. Break bread with us ($X buy-in for home cooked meals or pizza/pop purchases). Converse about things beyond just gaming, so we can all actually learn stuff about the real world as well as each other. It's useful for us as human beings as well as to create games that we're all insterested in.

      3) Know what we want. Play what we can.
      Role-playing is our primary goal. But real life intrudes. Long-term games are possible, but unlikely. Players may occasionally be unable to attend. We'll play LOTS of one-offs or board games.
      There are many other places to play d20. I already know how to play d20. I want to try new (not necessarily indie) stuff. So unless someone's got a really good pitch or a great track record, d20 games are out.

      I've stepped out of groups I created or helped create because I've lost my job, or they switched to a long-term game when I wanted to focus on shorter-term games, or I could no longer afford the time commitment, or I simply didn't like the game. My withdrawal in all cases is completely my choice with no animosity involved (on my end).

      I'll have conversations with people about these rules, and acknowledge that there are points at which they break down. But it won't change my mind, because the rules are there to improve the quality of my gaming experience. It's not about anyone else.

      If it was, they'd do the work of forming their own gaming group.
