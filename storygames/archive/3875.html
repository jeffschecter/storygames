\n Soooo ... I had this idea for a way that a player could play in an RPG, and the idea was this: Maybe that player would NOT play the role of any speaking character whatsoever. Instead, they would be in complete and uncontested control of describing anything and everything inanimate, non-sentient, etc. The hallways, the sky, the books, the cows, the freshmen. Things without any personality or self-awareness.
\n
\nHaving had the idea, I expanded upon it and then last night I played it.
\n
\nFor ways to draw cards, I chose the "Spooky" Mood:
\n
\n
DRAW A CARD WHEN YOU DESCRIBE:
\n- Noises of unknown origin
\n- Inadequate lighting
\n- Overly elaborate growth or construction
\n
\n... and the "Mystery" genre ...\n
\n
DRAW A CARD WHEN YOU INTRODUCE:
\n- The truth, obviously concealed
\n- A new truth that seemingly contradicts an old one
\n- One of these things just doesn't belong
\n
\nNow I'll freely admit that I missed playing NPCs (Sydney played most of them, with Jen and Eric having occasional rights if their character wasn't in the scene). I felt like my hands were tied behind my back ... I really could have pushed the "Mystery" angle much harder with just a LITTLE time handling some NPCs ... urrrghh!
\n
\nBUT ....
\n
\nWow, I found myself really excited by the focus on describing the scenery. Really, really, excited. Like, unholy glee. I knew in advance that there was some fun to be had there, but once I started doing only that thing, I suddenly realized what a huge and spectacular gold-mine of fun is to be had there.
\n
\nAlong with my control over the inanimate, I also got to tell people where each scene was set. So Eric decided that the three students were studying together for a trigonometry test coming up. I then set the scene in the crypt library of the school, and dwelt upon the 40 watt bulbs danging on extension cords from the ceiling (inadequate lighting, take a card!) and the vaulting columns with roccocco patterns carved into them (overly elaborate constructiion, take a card!) in which books were jammed seemingly at random.
\n
\nThey eventually went looking for some sort of trig quick-guide, and I switched over to setting the Mystery. Amidst the heavy books was wedged a trapper-keeper folder (one of these things just doesn't belong, take a card!). It was filled with notes, but of obviously different eras ... some of them were on yellowed paper, some much newer. Each of them had a place marked off on the paper for a name, and on each page someone had blacked out the name with a big, solid block of sharpie-ink (the truth, obviously concealed, take a card).
\n
\nAnd I'm sitting here telling people this, and they're like ... "Uh ... damn. That's creepy. Is there any way we can see what the name was?"
\n
\nAnd naturally I say "Well, it looks like if you looked at the paper against a really bright light you might be able to read it, but it's so dim down here." And I take a card.
\n
\nIt. Was. So. Easy.
\n
\nMy favorite moment:\n
\n
Jen is describing something (in her temporary role as school nurse) and she says to Eric "Okay, Ms. Mercury sits Aidan down at her desk ... 'You just stay here a moment, and ... ' "
\n
\n"What does the desk look like?" Eric asks Jen.
\n
\n"It's a plain metal ... whoops!" Jen replies, "Not my job, I forgot. Tony, what does the desk look like?"
\n
\n"Oooh!" I say, suddenly coming on point. "Hrm ... it's a heavy, metal-frame desk, but instead of the clean lines of the 1950s, this is all carved pewter-work with some sort of wierd, damscened linoleum for the top. There are big heavy drawers and each of them has a great big skull carving with cadeuceus snakes twining around it for both handle and keyhole. Oh, and in the corner of the desk-top, someone years ago clearly used a pencil to meticulously carve 'J.K. + R. R.' with a big black heart around it."
\n
\nI can't remember the last time I spouted descriptions like that. It's not that I don't enjoy it, it's just ... with Flags and Bangs and R-Maps and playing the NPCs and challenging the PCs and balancing this that and the other thing, who has time to think about graffitti on a school desk?
\n
\nI did feel like I got more traction from the Mood than I did from the Genre. Following the formula for the mood very quickly ... well, set the mood. Following the formula for the genre really required a reason to get the players pushing against that pattern, trying to solve the mystery. Not quite sure what to do about that ... if I had NPCs I could have pushed, but if I had NPCs then I'd have more stuff competing for my attention :-( Maybe the keys on the genre just aren't written correctly.

The game incentives come entirely from hitting your own "stuff" and not at all from hitting other people's "stuff". That does seem to put the setting guy at a disadvantage.     Same thing had occurred to me this afternoon in a different context (notably ... the guy who is supposed to have tons of NPCs fitting into the pattern of his dysfunction is the one who plays the NPCs? No, no, no ... Czege's principle tells me, rightly, that this is boring)

    I have this suspicion that a variant of the game's opinion mechanic may be the right way to reward people for hitting other folks's Keys.

    Like, if I hit the "Noise of indeterminate origin" key then I draw a card. If somebody says "What was that? Did you hear that?" then I can (at my option) give them one of my cards and draw another. Thus, I am able to refine my limited hand (toward better combos) while simultaneously bribing other people.


    SIDE NOTE: This works 100% better with the "dysfunctional worldview" character types ... they need NPCs to be hitting their keys, but (as Sydney rightly pointed out) it's not all that fun to play those NPCs yourself. So you give the other people the right to bring in NPCs, and their motive to do so is to hit the dysfunctional character's keys (by playing into their dysfunction).
