\n I've got several in the works, but what I am wanting mostly at the moment is to design a game that lets me do "Sector General" style stories.

The general synopsis is "Hospital for a Galactic Federation who solves all sorts of alien problems.." its a bit more complex of course and deals with psychology, physiology, first contact, and more.

I've no idea where to start, or what kind of mechanics would power it. Merely, that at some point I will find a way.

Main Inspiration: Sector General Novels by James White.

Secondary: Med Ship, MASH, Babylon 5, ER, St. Elsewhere.

Now a huge note: Sector General being written by an Irish man who lived through the troubles of Catholic/Protestant violence and more, the books were non-violent, almost completely so. It would be a very different game.

The need for a skill system that creates medical drama, divides medical skills finely, and has no violence is a challenge. Well, maybe I'll be inspired some day. Anyone have suggestions? More reading?

    \n Jason Morningstar's game of medical interventions, Medical Hospital has been talked about here before, but not in a while.

    I wrote a game called Koenig Hospital which was loosely based on Lars von Trier's The Kingdom (the original not the US remake). The game starts with the creation of a relationship map. There are dice but these decide on elements to be included in conflict resolution, not the outcome of the conflict itself which is decided by the player (there are also Graham based mechanics as I wrote it for him). Two of the dice have set elements but the third is created at the start of the game by the players and is colour specific, depending on what kind of game they're playing. We played it once and it seemed to work.

    What kind of conflict occurs in Sector General? I've only read one story. It was something about being attracted to weird aliens a surgeon was operating on. The solution to the problem was to put a buxom nurse on the staff to keep his mind off the alien.

    \n What level will the medical challenges be? Individual patients or species? Looking at vaccination the problem of the individual can be solved by treating everyone but looking at antibiotics the solution for the individual is the problem for everyone (drug resistance). One of the greatest medical inventions in the history of humanity is water toilets. Also not living in poverty (except for polio where living in poverty protects you). In general practice 95% of diagnosis is made through anamnesis (as one of my professors said on a lecture today: when you start taking tests you open the gateway to hell and when you're in hell the only way out is deeper in). When talking to an alien species they might have vastly different views on what causes their disease so that tool is out. While folklore is sometimes good you should never underestimate how retarded and dangerous some lore is. I read about one stone age level tribe in south america who equated health to a good appetite and the anthropologist spoke with a man who was feeling sick and was terrified because his appetite was gone. His solution was eating as much as he could and a few days later he died (was constipated) and the tribe thought it was very strange that he died so suddenly, he had been eating so well!

    Medical care, especially intensive care and trauma care, involves a great deal of violence directed towards a patient. A proper CPR should break a few ribs: if the patient is old and frail is will break all the ribs which is why the choice to not resussitate old patients is made: saving their life will only postpone their death a few days at most at the cost of great suffering for the patient. X-rays give cancer and can give permanent brain damage to children. Endoscopy is unpleasant and carries a small risk of injury. All surgery carries risks in form of infection and being put to sleep is also dangerous especially if you're already sick. Some surgeries risk other forms of permanent damage such a severed nerves (hoarse voice a complication of thyroid surgery, mastectomy risks giving "angel wings" that is protruding scapula). Even every day pain killers bought over counter (NSAID) can cause hidden but lethal hemorrhage or kidney failure.

    What tone is that book? If you want a pessimistic classic (on which Scrubs is based) I recommend The House of God.

    \n I'll second Paul Czege's suggestion of Mutant City Blues for a system, or basically any of the Gumshoe-based games. That, I think, works best for anything where you need to investigate something, solve problems, and still have a little bit of everything else. Use the combat mechanics to represent, instead of actual combat, interdepartmental or administerial red-tape and other such conflicts. The young, up-and-coming doctor butting heads with the head of surgery, or the somewhat unconventional genius doctor working his way around the draconian systems of the otherwise conservative hospital administration.

    Also, for further reading (I don't think I saw this listed), the Star Trek graphic novel of Leonard McCoy: Frontier Doctor.

    \n A few thoughts:

    The conflict in MASH is not doctors versus injuries, it's doctors versus absurdity. Sometimes they can save patients, sometimes they can't. That's not the issue in MASH, the issue is the army wants to do something stupid, and one doctor won't go along with it and the others that try get themselves in knots.

    The conflict in House is not doctors versus disease, it's doctors versus the unknown. The patient is lying, the tests are misleading, things are getting worse, faster than they expected. For House-a-likes, investigative games like Gumshoe might be a good fit.

    I think the key element to making the game work is identifying what is really going on. Since you're not a space doctor, you won't be Accurately Portraying Medical Decisionmaking. Instead, you should identify what the real conflict is and how the medical stuff feeds into/reinforces that.

    I have not read the books, but I suggest that if you're already looking at ER as secondary inspiration, you think about its story structure. Crucially, I don't think that the medical element of ER is all that important. It is at best an intrusive backdrop. You could just as easily plant the same characters on a covered wagon on the Oregon Trail and you'd have the same stories.

    \n

    That's because they are about personal drama, on a small scale, exacerbated by medical issues. Maybe Qtang needs more money for his department but the admins don't think it's worth it. That's an ongoing problem, and you bring it to a head by turning that problem into a patient: someone is brought into Qtang's department and they cannot afford to treat him. He will die if the department doesn't get its funding. If he dies, that's ok; that just means that some dude died, and the department is decisively not getting that money for some time.

    \n

    Now you feed this into another problem: Qtang fires one of his doctors so he can afford supplies, and gets in trouble because that doctor was the director's ... alien relative of some kind ... and so now the board is gunning for HIS job.

    \n

    That's what ER (and for that matter House) look like to me anyway.
