    PS - I'm not actually trying to be pedantic here. I think that TV, like other mediums, has lessons to teach us, but the lesson of "What is a good story" is one that puts us firmly in the subjective camp of the shows that excite the speaker. As such, I'm genuinely interested in _other_ lessons that shows can teach, and maybe those they can't.

    As an example, I liked The West Wing. It had strong characters, fun politics and exceptionally good dialog. However, most of that was because Sorkin can write, and excepting the lesson of "be creative" there are no real tricks or gimmicks that I could steal from West Wing to make my game more like that.

    In contrast, Law and Order, whether it's a good show or not, makes use of aggressive scene framing in such a strong way that it's virtually a signature of the show. Watch a few episodes, and you know that the signature sound means "Stuff happens, and now we're back at the good parts". If I wanted to explain scene framing to a player, it would be a powerful, useful example.

    Now, we can (and maybe should) turn this all on its ear and ask how we can pull from _other_ sources to emulate those shows we like. Wilderness of Mirrors is a great example of a game designed to capture certain TV spy tropes, and there are gimmicks and tricks I can use to make a West Wing-like game, but they come from sources other than West Wing. But even that requires some thought about what it is we really want to hit.

    So I worry I sound like I'm busting on folks, but I'm really not. I _believe_ that these shows are awesome but what I really want ttoto know is how they can make my games better, or how I can run a game that is awesome the same way.

    Law & Order
    Hell yeah about the scene framing. So well done. Also, the NPC info-dump. That show teaches you how to "reveal the town in play" as Vincent says in DitV. Even when a witness seems to clam up, the cops still get information that moves the show forward. It's an investigation procedural, with surprises and tension, but no brick walls stalling the momentum.

    Next time you run an investigation, ask yourself, "How can I make this more like Law & Order?"

    Lost
    One of the best ensemble casts ever. It is definitely the ur-PTA show. Spotlight eps, flashbacks, plot scenes vs. character scenes (and how they are usually both). It's not so much a gaming aid as it is a proof of concept for a style of storytelling that some games strive for. Battlestar Galactica is doing it, too.

    But here's a lesson: When you have a mystery in your game, LET IT BE SOLVED. Lost is doing this, but is taking its time (which I like). For another excellent example of this principle, see Veronica Mars (especially season one).

    Deadwood
    Another killer ensemble cast, which is great for learning how to divvy up spotlight time among PCs. But Deadwood has an even better lesson for us: The PCs don't have to all be on the same "side" and they don't all have to be "good." The mix of personas on this show can make for a great PC group, just boiling over with awesome conflicts all the time.

    Band of Brothers
    Survival is not a core quality of a protagonist.

    And I have to second The Sandbaggers because it's one of the best shows ever and few people know about it. Go watch the DVDs!

    The Wire. Great cast, great writing, great drama. Watch it to learn scene framing. There's nothing extra. Get to the point of the scene, or drive it towards the point (and sometimes the point is simply to reveal something important about a character), and then f'ing call "SCENE!"

If you're about to run a political campaign, a cyberpunk campaign, or you want to have both your heroes, villains, and shades of grey all have BIG, JACKED UP human flaws that just make you want to rip your hair out with pathos, watch this. Season liberally with "F*CK YEAH!" moments.

    Veronica Mars. Season 1 of this show is almost perfect television. It's also the perfect campaign. Every episode is its own perfect story, and advances the major plot along just a little. Give your players 22 episodes that each contain clues or red herrings about the overall mystery so their interest is maintained there, but hit the human interest/relationship angles as well as this show did, and have fun (humor) along the way. Your players will love you at least as much as Joss Whedon loves this show.

  • Rob Donoghue
    November 2006
    M*A*S*H is also a great example of the power of a default activity that is both critically important and not important at all, which is to say, "incoming wounded".

    There would _always_ be wounded coming in, but to make it a situation where you would call for a medicine roll (or whatever) every time it happened would entirely miss the point. Instead, you can assume the doctors would do well, on average, and only draw attention to the one offs. The tough decisions (Should this kid lose his leg?), points of competition (Who will be chief surgeon?) or the odd situations (This kid needs to be circumcised, and we have no rabbi) might pull in the mechanics, but the regular event will not. Additionally, the regular event provides a cadence for the rest of play that can put the real challenges in place. How long can you do this before you're too tired to function, and how do you balance the risk of making a mistake against the certainty of what happens if you stop working on it. Awesome stuff.

    Theoretically any medical drama could illustrate the same thing, but I'm uncertain any could do it better.

    One other thing about MASH is that the specifics of the situation (Medical + war) allowed for a fantastic illustration of how to handle NPCs. Most NPCs were transient, though some were potentially recurring, and some could be quite awesome, but there was no guarantee that a given NPC would live to the end of an episode. That uncertainty meant that NPCs were excellent ways to bring in issues that would be potentially game-ending for player characters. If you give a player character a tough moral decision (Do the right thing, or be able to go home) then it might be awesome to play, but if he picks "go home" then you've pretty well ended the game. Giving that same choice to an NPC and making it something for the players to bounce off of, kick around, and hi-light their own biases and issues can be powerful too, but with less chance of disruption and more likelihood of the choice feeling meaningful.

    So yeah, good call.

    -Rob D.

    Stargate SG-1. (Yes, I know it's already a game.) The players have a gimmick that constantly thrusts them into new situations. Their past decisions come back to bite them in big ways. They grow steadily in power and knowledge. A beloved adversary is never dead, only out of sight for a while. The huge, world-threatening plot can be put on hold for a while for smaller, character-focused adventures -- they'll get back to it, eventually. Practically every episode has at least one bit that screams "Adventure seed! I'm an adventure seed, right here! Look at me!"

    Hell, I started watching the show because I was having so much fun thinking about stealing game plots from it.
