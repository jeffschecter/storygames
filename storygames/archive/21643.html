\n Something that has been floating in the back of my mind for awhile, something subtle and fundamental to the games that I think really excite me and really opened my eyes in terms of moving past established norms and showing what's possible.

I tried to explain it to a friend the other day, I think the concept from a player point of view is the fundamental idea that the rules don't specify what it is you can do. You can do whatever you want that makes sense within the fiction. The rules only say "hey when this certain thing happens it triggers this mechanic"

I just think this is so important for cultivating the type of gameing that excites me. And in games where the inverse is true, I think this "the rules don't say you can do that" methodology contributes so much towards turning a lot of people away from the hobby.

I'm not sure if there's a more precise term for it. It's just something that stuck with me.

    \n For sure I'm not trying to demonize games that people really love to play (I got a lot of joy out of them too) only trying to articulate how in my experience design in games encourages fundamentally different approaches to play. In this case when does the fiction and the mechanics interact but more specifically as a player of a game from where do I start when making an active decision for a character I'm role playing.

    For the D&D Pathfinder example, you would definitely approach a class with the idea in mind that my class abilities are what my character can do, that's it. (Now that may not be strictly true but I definitely think it's how a lot of people approach it and the game encourages that through design I think)

    Versus something like apocalypse world where you do have a Playbook that resembles a class but the elements that make up that Playbook are largely fictional triggers that correspond to archetypal genre situations as well as tightly focused character drama prompts.

    When I first started designing my apocalypse World hack one of the things I had to stop doing was thinking of lots and lots of abilities to be listed. I had to ask myself the question "couldn't they already do this?" Or even, "is this element driving anything fictionally or being driven by something fictionally?" Stuff on a Playbook isn't only stuff you're going to be doing per say, it's more like a que to the GM and the player of the types of conflicts they're interested in.

    This may not be a profound realization for other people but for me it was a big deal a big paradigm shift in thinking and I still can't fully articulated the way that feels right.

    \n For me, it's a compound. The rules are like a vehicle. Some parts are where the players stand, what they can do. Some parts are the frame, limiting what is possible. (Some parts are the windshield, pointing to where they can go. And I add handles and levers here and there.)
    For my game I wanted no frame and no windshield, but I was taught there should be, evenminimalistic ones. Like : pay attention to each other, respect time of speech, etc.
    But still, a lot of techniques I don't speak of in the rules, leaving for the players to find out. Because it's not my job to tell the players what to do with the game once they can fly it.

    \n I think the OP is right, but that his examples aren't very good. It's also probably a 'softer' phenomenon than the post implies.

    This is not simply a "culture of play" thing, or rather, it is, but that culture flows directly from the rules in a lot of cases, but not necessarily in a clear "the rules say this" kind of way. Oftentimes it flows from the things the rules imply instead.

    A lot D&Dfinder style games have a lot of rules for the penalties and difficulties inherent in doing stuff. So when a GM who has read a lot of rules about "Okay, doing this is a -2, and doing that is a DC 15 athletics check." and nothing much about giving bonuses outside the scope of what the rules say, you end up with the player who says "Can I jump up onto the table, grab the chandelier rope, swing across the room and slash my sword at the Duke?" and the GM who responds, "Well, yes, that'll be a Jump check (DC 12), an athletics check (DC 15) and give you -2 to hit." So the player responds with "I guess I'll just use my feat that lets me charge without provoking opportunity attacks" because the rules have implicitly told him that while he COULD do the other thing, doing the thing that the rules EXPLICITLY tells him how to do produces better results.

    So it's not necessarily that rules tell you that you CAN'T do but rather that they make it more advantageous to only "do what's on your character sheet." because those levers give you clear defined "permission" and feedback, while the rest of the game seems to have been designed to make everything difficult.
