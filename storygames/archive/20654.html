\n A couple friends and I just finished a game of The Quiet Year, after multiple sessions (we talk too much). This was their first experience playing any kind of TTRPG, and they definitely enjoyed it. After we finished Summer, my roommate mentioned to me that he thought (based off the description I gave them initially) that TQY involved creating the world, and afterwards exploring it as characters. It's not quite what TQY is all about, but I thought it sounded like a great idea, and I've been on the hunt for a proper, relatively-rules-lite system ever since.

I think I'm running with Apocalypse World. I was initially hesitant, because one of my players is extremely math-averse, but the arithmetic in AW is pretty gentle, TQY has an implicit-though-unseen apocalyptic event as its finale, and the classes seem like they might slot in well with the dangerous, weird-fantasy world we ended up making in TQY.

Have any of you tried something like this, where you explore a world through multiple systems, or create a world with one system and use it as your setting in others?

If you can think of a better system (or PbtA hack) for the setting, I'd love any suggestions. Quick description: our community was clustered around the one remaining tower of a ruined castle. Our abundance was lore, which manifested as our relationship with the nearby forest; it demanded absolute nonviolence in exchange for its mystic protection (we assume this is how we bested the Jackals). The other tower lay torn in two in the lava pools a day's ride away from town, which we think held most of our history. By the end of the game, a suicide cult (about half the town) pacified the forest semi-permanently with their lives, a small crew of mechanized soldiers could be seen emerging from the east, we were in peace talks with the Jackals, an earthquake made a rift through the main path in town, and a dead sea serpent had poisoned the water supply.

    \n You can change Apocalypse World to not be so numbers-focused like this:
    First of all, get fudge dice, you know the ones that have an equal number of pluses, minuses and blanks. Also known as fate dice.

    Then change the playbooks and basic move sheets as follows:

    When I refer to "the ladder" below, I'm talking about this list:
    Unflinchingly
    Intensely
    Very
    Kind of
    A little bit
    Not really that
    Far from
    The least

    Unbeknownst to the players, but known to you, the converter, this scale is:
    Unflinchingly: +4
    Intensely: +3
    Very: +2
    Kind of: +1
    A little bit: 0
    Not really that: -1
    Far from: -2
    The least: -3

    Do not show those numbers to your players. I'm not kidding; do not let them start taking about +3 and -2 and stuff.


    Then make new playbooks such that:

    Choose one set:
    \u2022 Cool+1 Hard=0 Hot+1 Sharp+2 Weird-1
    \u2022 Cool+1 Hard+1 Hot=0 Sharp+2 Weird-1
    \u2022 Cool-1 Hard+1 Hot=0 Sharp+2 Weird+1
    \u2022 Cool+2 Hard=0 Hot-1 Sharp+2 Weird-1

    Becomes this:

    Choose one set:
    \u2022 Kind of cool, a little bit hard, kind of hot, very sharp, not really that weird.
    \u2022 Kind of cool, kind of hard, a little bit hot, very sharp, not really that weird.
    \u2022 Not really that cool, kind of hard, a little bit hot, very sharp, kind of weird.
    \u2022 Very cool, a little bit hard, not really that hot, very sharp, not really that weird.

    Teach your players how to roll like this (I'm serious, follow this procedure, it'll become fast after a while and you'll use the scale in your head after a while, but be careful about shortcutting it in the beginning):
    Let's say you have to roll to act coolly and that you are a little bit cool.
    Put your finger "A little bit". Roll four of the dice. Remove any + - pair because they cancel out. Then for each remaining +, move your finger up once, and for each -, move your finger down once.
    Let's say you rolled ++_-. The minus and the + cancel out, leaving +. Move your finger up once on the scale for the remaining +. It ends on "Kind of" You acted kind of coolly.

    Before you teach them that (but I wanted to explain it to you, the converter, first), change the moves so that this:


    DO SOMETHING UNDER FIRE
    When you do something under fire, or dig in to endure fire,
    roll+cool. On a 10+, you do it. On a 7\u20139, you flinch, hesitate, or
    stall: the MC can offer you a worse outcome, a hard bargain, or
    an ugly choice.
    becomes

    DO SOMETHING UNDER FIRE
    When you do something under fire, or dig in to endure fire, roll to
    act coolly. If you act very coolly or cooler, you can do it. If you
    act kind of, or a little bit cool, you flinch, hesitate, or stall: the
    MC can offer you a worse outcome, a hard bargain, or an ugly choice.

    (Uh, maybe that phrasing needs a little work.)
    Anyway, the point is to change all 7-9 results to "Kind of" and "A little bit", all 6- results to "Not really that" and lower, and all 10+ results to "Very" and higher.
    It's a lot of work converting or redoing all the playbooks but once that's done, your players won't see any numbers any more and you'll be happily in "word land".

    Put that ladder right on every playbook near the stats, or on laminated cards. Use the version that does not have the numbers. I'm serious. Do not show it.

    I've worked on the probabilities and they match pretty well from the start and across the advancements. You'll get pretty close to the same percentages of successes, misses, and 7-9:s.

    @systemtwin, not the same thing but, for example\u2026 me & my friends once created a desert "frontier planet" space colony setting playing Archipelago II, which I later wrote down as a setting document for Anima Prime. I then proceeded to use it more than once when demonstrating AP at local game conventions.

    Archipelago and AP are very different role-playing games from each other. The former encourages creating your world as you go (or exploring a known literary, non-game world), the latter requires a setting document as the starting point for creating characters. The former is dice-less and impro-y, the latter is crunchy and fight-y with heaping piles of dice. And Archipelago is meant to be played over much fewer sessions than AP. What they have in common is just some incredibly baseline features: being "fantasy" of a sort (but only in the broadest, non-restrictive sense), a strong focus on the player characters (similar to what you find in AW) and an emphasis on working together to build a story with everyone's contribution being equally important.

    \n If you use some kind AW-derived rules-set, you should be aware that there is often mood and genre railroading.

    A game about world creation and exploration can be many things, you can encounter horrific things one day,and joy and rainbows the other. But usually the design of moves is geared towards forcing you into certain choices that one author thinks are genre adequate, railroading you into certain possibilties and denying others.

    Maybe that is exactly what you want. Just be aware of this from the start.

    I was just interested in how you used the perocedures of TQY to create something not intended by the autor, which sounds rather cool. There is no need to use "dream askew" to funnel you back to community and scarcity.

    \n@systemtwin, I've wanted to do this many times -- take the scenarios and characters created in a zoomed-out game like Microscope and then run them through a second game to delve into the details. So cool that you're actually gonna do it!

    As for system, I think PbtA is fine, but apocalyptica is inextricably embedded in AW, and I sure wouldn't want to turn the characters you describe into pieces of that. But if you look through the playbooks (and especially their moves) and decide, "Yes, our characters should be that!" then I think you're set; go for it.

    Personally I would use Primetime Adventures. Quiet Year was basically your PtA pitch session, and now you're ready to just start framing scenes and playing. Just note down a core issue and a few other details about each character, grab a deck of cards and a stack of tokens, and start delving into character arcs. There's no math at all, and it'll leave the tone of your setting unchanged. If you'd rather not focus on what makes the characters tick and addressing their personal crises, though, then maybe not; PtA is built around that. Also has fanmail, which some people love and some people hate.

    \nI don't know what you mean by "opposing sexualities", but I do think you're worrying way too much about a non-issue!

    First, playing a character of a different gender, romantic orientation, age range or ethnicity, etc., from oneself represents no hurdle at all in most role-playing games \u2014 which are about adventure, action, community politics, problem-solving, etc., and not strictly about being one gender, age, ethnicity, etc. As long as people like their characters, they'll be OK! What about all the human teenagers playing the role of centuries-old vampires or elf wizards?

    Second, when the game is about being a certain way, and it's being distributed around the Internet as a role-playing game, you can probably trust the game to include tools to make players fit into their characters shoes, because that's probably the point of that game existing: easing players into a different point of view.

    Third, a "queer enclave" like introduced in DA is, by definition, an inclusive world. It doesn't oppose anything really, it allows for everything and everyone. It's the "society intact" which excludes, which tells you you have to be a certain specific way to fit.\n
    I need something where the rules can bleed into normal interactions like in AW, where there's pre-existing character entanglements, but with a slightly lower level of violence or at least a slightly lower expectation of violence.

    \n Some thoughts on this:

    1. I think it's an excellent idea, though, like the others here, I've never really managed to pull this off to the extent that I wanted. For years now, I've wanted to play out a specific scenario/adventure using three or four different systems, simply as a gaming experiment. I think it would be very interesting!

    I *have* done successfully in a more limited way with Monsterhearts and Fiasco with this playset:

    It All Ends in Tears

    Lots of fun! (And great for beginners.)

    2. If you want to use AW, you're bound to inject certain dynamics and fictional content, since the game is all built on inspiring fictional colour.

    However, if you have a "gentler" apocalypse, you could probably do that by picking out all the "non-combat" playbooks. There aren't too many, but if you have access to all the more out-there playbooks - e.g. here - you can pick out a set which suits your game.

    Dream Askew seems like a solid choice, as well.

    3. Since your starting game/session is going to generate a lot of material for you, you're probably best off if you pick a system which is fairly "bland" or "generic", to accomodate all the elements you've already created, instead of trying to supplant them.

    A few suggestions:

    * The Shadow of Yesterday/The Solar System is an excellent "generic" ruleset for character-driven roleplaying, an indie favourite.
    * If your group is fine with just a generic resolution mechanic, consider Vincent Baker's "Otherkind dice". It's not a full game with support, though.
    * If you want a more intense/dramatic game, you could use the rules from Dogs in the Vineyard - take out the setting stuff and just use the conflict mechanic. Would suit a post-apocalyptic setting quite well.
    * Primetime Adventures could work, too.

    Sandra, nice AW hack! I like it. I had a whole Fudge hack using Adverbs I wrote up long ago; this reminds me of it quite a bit. Using adverbs instead of adjectives allows you to use the "ladder" in written or spoken English much more easily, so we're on the same page here!
