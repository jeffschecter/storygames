\n Over in this thread, Valamir was asking some tough questions about whether folks were really getting feedback on their playtests, when the playtesters were enthusiastic players, already pretty familiar with S-G style techniques.

I'm pulling my tangential comment in that thread over here to open up conversation on it more specifically.

Basically, my question was this:

When you write up a game text, are you making sure you're covering everything that actually goes into making the game work in practice, and are you organizing it so there is the right amount of emphasis on those things, so that someone getting that text cold can actually reproduce similar results?

( The Lumpley Principle, to my understanding, is that Sytem is actually everything that goes into making the game work, including unwritten procedures and social understandings. That is, not just the rules as written, but also rules not used/modified and other understandings. That stuff is the Actual System).

The question came up for me recently when I began asking about a game that I'd bought and would like to run, but haven't yet. I discovered after asking some questions at the game's yahoo group that in practice, successful convention games normally involved quite a bit that wasn't really emphasized in the rules text, and that the game itself was often modified fairly heavily even by the author.

So what about it folks? How do you record and organize and emphasize what is actually important to successful play of your game, and have you run into circumstances where a rules text was somewhat or ver much at odds with succesful actual play?

    \n This is definitely on my mind!

    Fiasco relies heavily on players engaging in a certain way that I hammer again and again in the rules. Each rules chunk ends with a "Things to look for" section, with advice and suggestions, like:

    Here's an important point - your guy may not be in trouble. The game's central dilemma may not be about your character at all - maybe he's a distant relative, or a do-gooder, or just collateral damage. If it turns out your guy isn't in a jam as the game begins, focus on making the jam more intense until it touches him, too. Trust me, eventually it will.

    So I'm using that to describe a style of play beyond the procedures, after they are laid out. Making this explicit was, I believe, Ralph Mazza's suggestion and it is a great one.

    \n There are so many things that can get covered this way. The dynamics you need, or can expect to get, between the players is one of the hardest to put your fingers on sometimes.

    Our group played 3:16 recently, and the mechanics of that game put the characters at odds with one another--you have very different goals depending on what your rank is. This means that you can easily do some moves which are true to the character, but which dick over the other players. If folks aren't expecting this, hard feelings can ensue. Heads ups about this kind of player/character overlap and conflict might help set the expectations, but then it does seem obvious from reading the directives for the character ranks!

    Another cautionary tale here, I've seen this happen many times too--when mentioning a neat aspect of a game that arises from play (having lots of guns in Dogs has consequence for the actions you take in character) at the start of the game can prime people to do something to avoid, or court, that effect. But it can throw other things out of wack. The player I'm thinking of heard this about the game (I shot my mouth off) then chose to have no gun whatsoever, and had some issues dealing with normal things that came up in the game. Not that that's a bad thing in Dogs, but I wish I'd allowed them to go in with no expectations and learn for themselves what the game communicated to them.

    I realize this is a bit contradictory. Do we set expectations or not?

    I like Jason's example. It points out potential problems, and then gives a simple directive for how to allow the game to correct itself. Not raising expectations too high, I think, but giving another avenue for action if things are not quite right.

    \n To my mind, playtesting with a variety of general system style assumptions and ensuring robustness of the underlying system is the step past ensuring that the critical non-mechanical system of the designer's vision is clearly communicated to the players.

    There is a reason why for Coming of Age I've learned that I need to stress: action by action continuity, that losing is fun, that Titles should be specific (and even a little silly), but Truths should be broader and applicable to almost anything. Because these are the places where the non-mechanical system needs to come in or the game will be less fun.

    I've used examples to offer some of the dice handling and other tricks that arisen from dozens of games but ultimately, I've seen quite a few groups of players drift the rest of the game mechanics and still have just as much fun with the game. Because combined with the core rules (which are simple enough), the underlying dynamics of the game are insensitive to a variety of other changes in the social assumptions. The fun, as it were, serves as an attractor, and that brings the game into focus, even when players have added some fairly interesting rule constraints to their playtest.

    But admittedly, that's because my publication philosophy is that it isn't enough to design a game that plays well if the instructions are followed. I want to publish a game that plays well even if players don't do everything as instructed, in short a game that is actually robust. And I don't see how you can do that while ignoring the non-mechanical parts of the system.

    - Mendel S.

    \n In the actual case I was thinking of ( Astounding Tales by Howard Whitehouse) there was some actual very key stuff that was never discussed at length or emphasized, and which I only discovered after questioning some folks on the yahoo group for the game. Without that info, I had a very different impression (or at least a very vague one) about how the games being played at conventions ( which were getting great feedback reports) were actually accomplished. Here are just a few things I found out:

    1) Despite the fact that these games are regularly being played as one-off participation games at conventions, that wasn't really any involved discussion of putting one together at all.

    2) The games are generally run by the author with up to a dozen players, plus helpers.

    3) The author tends to run a sort of short interview of the players before the game to decide how he assigns the characters. This is important as the characters tend to be based on either very store sterotypes or fictional pulp or cinema characters.

    4) The "bad guys" players are informed that the Good guys must eventually win the scenario, and are then given some advice on "hamming up" the bad guys. IOW, Bad Guy Players are both players and a sort of mini GM/adversity generator.

    5) Players will tend to each control a team of characters. This actually flies directly in the face of stuff discussed in the book. They'll also be assigned pregens almost always, despite several slightly different chargen methods given in the rules.

    Anyway, this along with some other tidbits are all things not really discussed at length, that clearly make a huge difference to me. Now I have a better actual idea of how to put one of these games together.

    Now, how much of S-Ger/Dirty Hippy Gamer Elite stuff really needs to be up front in the text? What about stuff like Stealth GMing (which I actually consider kinda core to a lot of S-Gs and RPGs)? When is it ijportant to talk about concepts like Player Knowledge versus Character knowledge? Or setting good stakes? Or going for the throat in scene cutting?

    Ideally, yes. (The snarky answer: yes, that's called writing.)

    This is the hard part of writing games: it's one thing to put together rules that work; it's quite another to write instructions that make those rules transmissible to other groups. And there's another layer that hasn't been mentioned: in nearly any given gaming group, not all the players will have read the book. You have to write the text not only to transmit the rules, but to empower those who read the rules to transmit them to those who haven't.

    \n Short answer: Yes, thoroughly.
    Longer answer: Within the procedures of play themselves. The manuscript I'm working on describes each atomic piece of the game (Characters, Basic rules, Sets, Conflicts etc) in its own short, couple-of-pages section. Each section begins with a few paragraphs of introduction of the concept, in which I present the abstract notion of the piece and cover how it is intended to work in play: what sorts of information about the fiction the player can glean from it, how it's meant to be used strategically, how you may use this piece to interact with others, and so on. I suspect that in the end I'll still have some "Lumpley Principle" articles to cover, and these will get their own sections of equal importance to the others.
    What I'm avoiding is talking too much about social dynamics, because in this game it'd be mostly noise. "What to do if Jane disagrees with your input" or "What to do if Bob punches you in the nose" or "Who should bring the snacks" will not be covered at all. The chapter in the 4E DMG on player types and how to cater to their needs and desires is nice for D&D, with long campaigns and a large player base with disparate notions of what an RPG is, but is completely unsuitable for this game. Similarly, the bits of advice on physical game setting and snacks in IAWA is really nice and suitable for that game. This game simply assumes that everyone is largely on the same page, and the procedures of play does not give any wiggle room to introduce drift, house rules or free-forming to suit other or competing agendas.

    \n You know what else? Beginning composers (I taught some composition basics to two high-schoolers this past year, and also took a composition class that had some first-time composers in it) tend not to put in dynamics, tempo, articulation, etc. in their scores. They only write the notes. They have to be reminded, over and over again, to do so.

    I think the analogous situation is completely true for beginning game designers as well.

    And even more advanced designers will face complex questions surrounding these issues, as demonstrated upthread. Sometimes you actually do need to trust your players/musicians, and give them *less* information. In fact, this is *especially* true in jazz, which I know is often likened to indie games roleplay.
