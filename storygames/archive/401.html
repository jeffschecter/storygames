Okay this may not be entirely kosher, since Game Chef judging is still in progress, but I thought I'd do a preliminary list of the games from this year's Game Chef competition that have caught my interest. Since the competition is judged on a variety of criteria beyond just "does it make you want to play?" I don't see it being too big an issue.

Also note that this is based on some light skimming, except for my first item. Closer inspection over the next few days will probably change my impressions.
  1. Play Right!
    by Michael S. Miller
    Holy shit! I want to play this right now! I've generally been cool to the whole "put stuff down on index cards in the middle of the table" idea, but Play Right! makes this work for me. Maybe it's because you start from pre-existing source material or something.

    The mechanics look realiy cool too.

    I was hooked from the moment I read the first paragraph, and read the whole pdf, on-screen, in one sitting. If this doesn't get published, it'll be a crime.
  2. Our Guadian Devils
    by Jason A. Petrasko
    Probably the new winner in the non-existent "Coolest Game I'll Never get the Rest of my Group to try" competition. This thing is steeped in cool imagery and symbolism. Something for the Jungians to drool over.
  3. Our Steel, the King's Law
    by Joshua BishopRoby
    I haven't dug into this one too much, but I like the gamist-narrativist hybrid, and the general concept of pseudo-medieval cops bashing criminal syndicate heads.
  4. Crime and Punishment
    by Moyra Tarkington (this is Mo of Sin Aesthetics, right?)
    Law & Order the RPG, except you're not playing the cops, you're playing the show's writers! I love the original L&O (the rest of the franchise is okay, but not great), and I think this game nails the whole police procedural TV genre nicely.
  5. The Holmes and Watson Committee
    by Troy M. Costick (seriously, guys, what's with all the middle initials?)
    Victorian detective fiction. Need I say more?
There are a couple of others, like Capital of the Eternal Century, The Glass Bead Game and The Many Deaths of Dr. Livingstone that have also caught my eye, but I can't give them a thumbs up or down until I go digging into them.

So that's my picks for most interesting games of Game Chef 2006. What are yours?

  • Brand_Robins
    March 2006
    Cage of Reason, Our Steel, Kings Law, and Crime and Punishment have been the ones that caught my eye the most so far.

    Cage of Reason because, damn man, how often do I get to live out my dreams of combining enlightenment philosophers with death-match wrestling? This is like someone stole part of my brain and then put it to good use rather than just daydreaming about it.

    Our Steel, Kings Law is a very solid gamist game with enough personality and roleplaying elements to make me interested in it as something more than a boardgame. Plus, I have a fondness for fencing mechanics (they made me buy Swashbuckler despite the fact that at the time I hated "rules lite" games) and the way Josh intigrated the fencing and criminal chasing looks like it will be fun (and challanging!) to play.

    Crime and Punishment I love because it is two hours of solid zing. The playtests we did were balls to the wall and didn't leave a spare moment for dilly dally. You have to think, you have to put yourself out there, you have to scene frame and be agressive, and it all goes down wonderfully. I also like the structure of it, because it is almost like a half-step between Host A Murder games and RPGs. It has the "do it yourself, put forward your own issues, make your own story" element of RPGs, but also has the "stick to the rules, follow the script" element of the Murder games. I have high hopes of getting non-gamers to play this one, because most folk I know can come up with good ideas and can play out good ideas -- they just have trouble doing both at once. Luckily now they don't have to.
