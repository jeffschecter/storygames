    I think the real issue at the core of most recent ashcan discussions and (as I was recently discussing with Vincent) the confusion of IAWA/Poison'd is about audiences: determining who the audience is for a particular ashcan/product/etc. and communicating effectively to that audience 1) what your ashcan/product/etc. is and 2) how they can best approach your ashcan/product/etc. Especially if this means that a given person probably shouldn't buy your ashcan/product/etc. or should be warned that it's not really targeted at them, but, sure, they can take a whack at it if they really want but they should probably know X, Y, and Z first, because that will ease the process. I definitely think there are cases where the idea of making a sale or getting as many copies of your game out there as possible overshadow the best interests of your game and the people who might potentially be interested in it.

    For example, Matthijs, the other two ashcans that you bought... what if you had the opportunity to play them right before or after you bought them? I realize this is probably less applicable to your situation, being across the ocean, but at an event like GenCon, it's certainly possible to plan to run your game at Games On Demand for people who seemed interested in your ashcan, which both 1) creates a stronger sense of investment in the process and gives you time to show what you have and talk about what's missing, and 2) lets interested people learn how to play the game, diminishing the effort and work that you talked about. I'm going to be offering free copies of Geiger Counter to folks who play the game with me at GenCon for exactly this reason, in an effort to find a different method of ashcan distribution that is more effective at transmitting my intentions.

    \n Thinking out loud...

    In an ideal world, would-be Ashcan buyers would get detailed informations about the stage of development, and so they would know if a book is at the "this don't work, help me!" stage or the "this work flawlessly for me and my friend, I am selling it as an ashcan because I am still not sure the text explain the game well" stage.

    In this world, game authors are not the best judges of their work, and people never read the fine print...

    So, maybe, creating categories of ashcan (maybe color-coded?) would be useful, I don't know, to differentiate between the two cases.

    "what do I get for buying your ashcan?" it's good question, but the answer can't be the same for every category. I don't think a rebate would be a good answer in every case (too complicated to use), but let's see the different cases...

    1) "this game work, I am searching for blind playtesters to affine the game text".
    What you get when you buy it: a good game that interest you, at a discount, in a "rare advanced edition" (maybe signed by the author?)
    Why you should playtest it: well, if the game interested you in the first place...
    Why should you give feedback to the author: Well, probably because you didn't understand the rules...
    What you get in return: the author explain to you the rules, and you are able to play the game you brought.
    At the end what this gave you that people who brought the final edition didn't get?: Well, maybe the final edition will never arrive, but you already have a playable game. You got to play it before anybody else, and you have a special edition (and you paid less). And a citation in the final book will be nice.
    (in case of a pdf edition, you could even get a free pdf of the final game text. The cost of a pdf usually isn't very different from the price of an ashcan. But even if you don't get this, you already have the game)

    2) "this game works, but not like I want. I want to get specific effect that this game still don't reliably give"
    In this case, you have to be so interested in this game development to want to play the unnfinited (but playable) edition.
    The difference with the previous case: it's not enough that the game author give you explanations about the rules. During the playtests he should give you the new rules during the developments (he can't really ask you to buy a chain of different developed ashcans, right?).
    What you get for yourself: you can contribute to the finishing touches on the rules, you can play the game (unfinished AND finished when the rules are finalized), you get, piece by piece, the final rules of the game (a final pdf with the complete rules would stil be nice, as a thank-you). The game at this stage should go to blind playtesting with DIFFERENT players (and this would mean publishing a different ashcan...?)

    3) "this game is mess, please help me"
    I think that nobody would knowingly publish an ashcan like this, but maybe they thought that they were in case (2) and discovered only afterwards that the rules really didn't work reliably.
    In this case, I think that as a customer (I am not talking about what the author SHOULD do. there is no "should" in this post, this is what I would DEMAND as a customer) I would like to get (1) a refund, especially if the game author drop the project, or (2) a "help me get a good game from this mess, and I will give you xxxxxx!" request from the author, with xxxx containing AT LEAST a free copy of the final game and a big than-you on the book, and maybe some other quirks (a special numbered edition, a promotional poster, a discount on other games from the same author, I don't know). And I should be able to say "thank you but I would prefer a refund"
    (this could be seen as harsh, but I really see refunds as the only thing that will be able to stop this run to publish broken and incomplete games. Something that give "weight" to the choice to don't wait for better playtest, responsibility to people who publish for sale, and anyway you could have published the playtest edition it for free if you were not sure...

    As I said, don't take this for a viable proposal, I was thinking out loud and there are probably a lot of holes in what I said. But I think that a refined and more thoughtful version of this would be very useful for both authors and customers.

    \n I think it all comes down to personal choice, really. In the end, that's the only measuring point.

    A few years ago (2004?), Mike Miller came out with With Great Power, a preview edition. It was basically a proto-ashcan. It was absolutely fully playable. He was still gathering feedback. And in the end, the game changed in a few ways. Still, the points are:

    1) It was not fully ready.
    2) It was fully playable. (and, IMO, fun as fuck. I was sold through private play before the con in like, seriously, 4 minutes)
    3) He was hoping for player feedback.
    4) The price was $10, with a refund if you purchased the full game when it came out.
    5) He made sure that people knew is was a preview edition, yadda yadda.

    There was a mildly raging debate back then whether or not this was a good idea.

    This debate reminds me very much of that debate.

    In both, very little was resolved through discussion.

    However, in both, many personal tales were told ("this is why I bought/didn't buy ashcan"), and that information became Data for The Machine.

    I hope that, going forward, people can focus less on "getting to the bottom" of whether this enterprise is a good idea or not, and simply share their own viewpoints as to whether they will be participating or not, and why.

    \n Here's my data points, to feed to the machine.

    * I believe in the ashcan process, because for all the numbers of people who don't have time or intent to leave feedback, it is obvious that there are some who do have the time or intent to leave feedback. It might be 1/10 of what the ashcan creators are hoping for, but I think they're out there, and this provides a good common ground. A service provided to a customer, who is in turn interested in that product. Capitalism: Yay.

    * Personally, I am not an ashcan buyer:
    -- I bought the With Great Power ashcan, because I was sold on it as a playable game. I had great interest in the subject matter and the rules. So that was a unique situation.

    * Last year I bought two ashcans: Acts of Evil and Galactic.
    -- I was interested in some of the other games, but not interested enough to buy, read, and give feedback before it was baked. But when it comes out "for realz", I plan on, just like every other product, giving it a look and seeing if I'm interested.
    -- Galactic I bought primarily because a good friend worked on it, and it was cheap. I heard that some pieces of it are awesome, and that some pieces are totally fucking broke, and I picked it up anyway. It's a combo of being very interested in the focus of the methodology of the game (the way the play happens), supporting a good friend's art, and giving it a read. Plus, it was like, what, $10? Not much of a commitment, so I was fine with that. I had no intention of playing it.
    -- Acts of Evil I got because it sounded fucking awesome, in terms of how it creates those kinds of stories. I actually intended to play it when I bought it. Unfortunately, the investment time to go through it to understand it (cause it's a very dense game, every paragraph necessary to read and remember, unlike most games where you can skip through "skill lists" and "play advice" and the like to get to the important things) meant that I didn't end up playing it. Therefore, I didn't give feedback. I don't regret the purchase, though, because what little of the game I could keep in my "brain RAM", was really cool and made me think about ways to run other games, even trad games, in the same sort of style that Acts of Evil does.
    -- I picked up nothing else, because of a combination of not being interested enough in the setting, the theme, the rules, and knowing that my time the next year would be at a premium.

    * This year, I plan on looking over the Ashcan booth thing again. But currently, I have no plans on buying any of them, for the reasons mentioned above. If something catches me off guard, I'll certainly look at it; but I know that from now until probably Spring 2009 I'll be busy with other projects, and playing other games, so I won't be able to give good feedback, or invest the time in learning the game.

    * I like it when folks make ashcans downloadable off their websites for free.
    -- And I'm very sympathetic to the stories of, "When it was on my website for free, no one said anything; When I started selling ashcans, all of a sudden I was getting feedback from folks".

    * The real measure of success, IMO, for this venture is, did the author get the feedback they wanted, and did the player enjoy playing the ashcan?
    -- I don't feel a line is needed to be drawn between releasing an ashcan and releasing a full game. Because who knows, someone might have a game that's 90% ready to go, but they find out that that last 10% makes the game entirely unplayable without starting over from scratch; perhaps in time the whole project has to be cast down and scrapped. BUT, if that decision came from seeing the play feedback coming in, then that was a success: The realization that the game wasn't ready, won't be ready, and isn't worth making ready. And the purchaser is out $5 instead of $25, or $30, and perhaps even a friendship forms; a single lotus blossoms in the field, etc.

    Thanks for listening to my own viewpoint. I'd like to hear more thoughts, coming from people's own viewpoints.

    -Andy

    \n Matthijs,

    I am possibly in the ashcan phase of development on two separate games. I will not be joining the Ashcan Front for two reasons:

    1. my GenCon attendance is a last-minute decision and still in doubt
    2. I am not sure my games are 90%, having had relatively little playtesting

    So with that in mind, I will answer your question for myself.

    If I were selling an Ashcan, I would not want you to purchase it given anything like a 95% chance that you wouldn't play it. As I understand the Ashcan model, you'd not be doing me any favors. At best, I would be charging the printing cost of the Ashcan, so you'd defray the costs on a single, cheaply-made book, which would, with the "price" of the work I've put into it, mean I'm selling for a net loss, no matter how you spin it, UNLESS I'm getting the return on the investment I'm looking for: Playtesting. So unless there's about a 70% chance that you'll actually play the game AND get back to me on it, do not buy my theoretical Ashcan. If there's not a 100% chance that you will READ the game and get back to me with your questions and comments, do not buy my theoretical Ashcan.

    Also to address Moreno's comments:

    If you purchase an Ashcan, I believe you are owed basically nothing. You've made a decision to spend your money on a product, exactly the same as if you'd decided to spend that money on a completed game, or a set of dice, or a miniature. What you are owed is this: The author's active help in understanding and playing his/her game, and feedback on your feedback. If you buy the game and never play it or even read it, then you are owed absolutely nothing. I think you should reasonably expect playtester credits in the finished version of the game and possibly a special thank you if your assistance was above and beyond. Anything beyond that, to include a discount/refund/free copy of the game in .pdf or hardcopy is entirely at the discretion of the author.

    Basically, the author is publishing at a loss a product, but the product is not a game, it's an experience and an opportunity: to playtest a game that interests you. If you're not interested in THAT product, then please.. Don't buy my Ashcan (when I have one).

    \n You know what's probably even harder than judging your chances of playing an ashcan? Determining it's state of completion in percentage. By some people's lights, my games may be that 90% and qualify as ashcans. But given my difficulty in finding gamers of any stripe, let alone those interested in independent games, I've not been able to do a lot of playtesting of my own, and no one's taken me up on playtesting from my .pdfs online. If I'd had more time to plan my GenCon attendance, I may have decided to take a shot at joining the Ashcan Front, judging that my games were *probably* 90%.

    I have played them, successfully. I've had a few readers give them an eyeball. The games as they exist in my head are *playable* and have produced fun. Whether or not they can consistently produce that fun without what I bring to the table as the creator, and whether there are any gaping holes right inside my blind-spots is the unknown. The existence of such could push my game from the 90% where I think they're at to a much, much lower number. Hence my stance: You get what you get when you pay for it, and are entitled to no more than what I said in my last post.

    This doesn't address someone who sells you a game they know is broken and well below 90% complete. That sort of person isn't being honest about the ashcan process, and probably wouldn't give you a refund anyhow. I don't think there's much point talking about those cases, personally.

    So to close and clarify my point about intent: Obviously you can't read the future any more than I can tell you about what's in my blind-spots about my game design. But you can make a judgement based on your current expectations about opportunity, time, and interest to decide whether you really think you're going to play the game. If you think you really are going to play it, then by all means, buy it. If the vagaries of time and circumstance end up preventing you from ever playing it... Well, that's the risk I took when I sold it to you, isn't it?

    For final clarification: I am not actually selling an ashcan at GenCon 2008. I'm pretty sure I'll be attending, and I will have one hard-copy each of my two ashcan-worthy games in case I can get in some pick-up games. The above is my stance if I were to be selling, and the stance I'll be assuming if I make any purchases of ashcans while I'm there.

    \n Ashcans have no set price. They operate under the same concept as any other creator-owned game. A designer decides how much he or she will sell the thing for. Prices tend to be between, I dunno, $8 and $15.

    Relatively few recent ashcans provide a discount for people who purchase and interact with early ashcan editions. Again, it's up to each designer to do so. I did not take that approach with my ashcan game 44, for example.

    Matthijs, you're being slightly masochistic! I mean that in good humor. Certainly, I as an ashcan front creator and as a proponent/founder of the Ashcan Front want you to engage with the design of a game. We want you to support the game and become the designers peer and communicate. We do not expect it. Nor do we scorn you if you 1) buy and don't feedback or 2) don't buy at all. Totally your option.

    I sold something like 20+ ashcans of 44 last year. I sure as heck didn't get 20 groups' feedback in return. I did not expect to. I received relatlively little feedback. This I expected.

    Also, it's been less than a year-to-date since the Ashcan Front even existed. Kevin Allen Jr. will release the first "graduate" at the Design Matters booth in his game Sweet Agatha. To the best of my knowledge, no one else has "finished" an ashcan game. In fact, I'm not sure anyone has completed an ashcan (as in, a regular ol' "rough draft" game that was NOT part of the Ashcan Front) in the last year or so anyway. Probably there were a couple I'm forgetting.

    I sense some hint that this is sorta bad. Again, it's well within both mine and Paul's expectations. Paul and I have specifically communicated that we never expected any games to be finished by this year. We expect that these games will meet two likely fates:

    1) The game will never be realized. Ryan Macklin has already announced this to be the case for his ashcan from last year, for example.

    2) The game will be "slow cooked" and released as a solid product in 1-2 years, perhaps more. My own game 44 will be published by the end of the year, for example.

    There are, of course, other options. But, we saw these as the most likely results. Importantly, I consider option #1 totally viable and a good sign that ashcans as a concept do what I, at least, would like them to do.

    Finally, I really like that there is conversation with many viewpoints going on about this. I like that because about 1-2 years ago no such conversation existed. As far as Paul Czege and I are concerned: Mission Accomplished. We wanted to make known to the creator-owned RPG scene that ashcan is one viable option that will help solve specific problems of designing and publishing your game. It is clear to me now that this is indeed viewed as a viable option. Fantastic!

    Does it mean it's good for everyone? Of course not. Does it mean it has its own flaws? Of course not. But, we think the good outweighs the bad. This has been a good thing for the hobby, as far as I can see.
