\n Without strong engagement with the situations of the fiction, immersion is worth nothing. This is why, I think, many designers have said "If I can come up with an engaging game, who cares if it's immersive? Lots of immersion-friendly games produced flat situations, and I don't wanna do that!"

So, taking engagement as a prerequisite, what is immersion good for on top of that?

It's good for giving importance to discoveries and learning.

It's good for adding adrenaline to chases and physical struggles.

It's good for sensory detail that you otherwise might not bother sharing or processing.

It's good for cultivating a flow state where you can play for long periods without conscious effort.

It's good for remembering later -- killing an orc yourself sticks in the mind better than using some avatar to kill an orc.

It's good for group unity, the same way a camping trip is.

It's good for rewarding big choices about life rather than little choices about a game.

It's good for etching your contributions into the permanent record of a world that will outlast this moment.

It's good for expanded character expression, like accents.

The experience is more vivid, the emotions are stronger, the sense that it matters is greater. Take your most fun non-immersive session ever, throw out the out-of-game jokes and banter, and multiple what's left by a thousand. I'm not saying that that's easy to achieve, but it is possible.

Immersion-lovers! What else is it good for? Come, for we must dazzle those who dwell in skepticism! Well, okay, we don't have to do that. But, hey, at least a few people have asked me out of curiosity, so there ya go.

    Posted By: David BergWithout strong engagement with the situations of the fiction, immersion is worthnothing.
    Without immersion you have no game. I've played lots of games, thousands, and nothing is as droll as players not accepting the premise for the game, playing it as if it was some kind of abstract procedure. It is not enough to make the moves; you have to make them with intent and engagement. That's immersion, wether you play a board game, a computer game or a role-playing game.

    Included in immersion in role-playing games is the feeling for your character. One strong premise of the role-playing is indeed that; the role-playing. You need to have some vestige of identification with your character, and some interest in making his/her moves in a way that brings intent and engagement to it.

    Not all immersion is as BIG as David makes it sound like in his flamboyant post; it certainly is not. But you need some of it; a player need to accept the premise of the game to really play it. Game-play devoid of contact with the premise is, in fact, a player not participating in the game. One player refusing to immerse can ruin the whole game-play. Players stating: "Oh, I don't care who wins" - in a board-game, is really the ones not immersed, and by saying such things they undermines the game for all. Players stating: "It's the same to me what my character do. What do you want him to do?" - in a role-playing game, is doing the same thing; undermining the game-play. I pity people like that. It is indeed a small investment to make; accepting the premise of a game you have accepted to play. So; a small immersion is always needed.

    But then; when you have seen the depths of really BIG immersion, as described by David above, you are seldom satisfied with anything less evocative, engulfing and easy-flowing.

    \n As has been the case in many of the threads you linked to, I have a problem with using the term immersion because I find it too high level - it encompasses several things. And those things are valued in different amounts by different folks.

    I am working on an article about player motives, which are both the disires that lead to a specific behavior, and the urge to have certain types of experiences at some point during a game event.

    This list was developed through the lens of live combat larping, so there are areas picked out that have minimal play in tabletop (like Exercise and Crafting, though Crafting is possible). And there may be some areas that need a bit more fleshing out (splitting terms, possibly) to relate to tabletop play.

    But here's the list currently:
    1. Audience - Experience a satisfying narrative.
    2. Catharsis - Experience emotions through the character.
    3. Comprehension - Figure things out. Solve problems and puzzles.
    4. Competition - Win at something, or at least enjoy the act of competing.
    5. Crafting - Create non-ephemeral things.
    6. Education \u2013 Take away new knowledge or understanding as a player.
    7. Embodiment \u2013Make decisions based on character background, knowledge, and motivation.
    8. Exercise - Enjoy physical activity.
    9. Exploration - Experience the fictional setting.
    10. Exhibition - Show off (costumes, props, acting chops, mad skillz, etc.) and get kudos.
    11. Fellowship - Enjoy time with friends (also includes flirting and such).
    12. Flow \u2013 Enjoy losing oneself in the moment.
    13. Leadership - Be important to the player community.
    14. Protagonist - Be important to the story.
    15. Spectacle - Experience the awesome stuff.
    16. Versatility - Have the right thing at the right time.

    This list is just a beginning section of a 30 page article that will be published this summer, if not sooner. But I think it gives a more granular way to look at things.

    Immersion consists of a collection of things. Usually it's some combination of Embodiment, Catharsis, and Flow.

    The Exploration motive centers around what, in some of those other threads, is called "setting immersion", and may or may not be experienced in conjunction with the others.

    The Flow motive is basically what you are calling engagement. And as you saw, when Flow is combined with other motives, some people call that immersion too. That's part of the problem I have with the term.

    Exactly what combination a person calls immersion depends on the person and, for immersionist proponents, usually corresponds with "good".

    I think that, more than anything, what you are excited about is flow - that transcendent state of heightened awareness, success, reduced sense of self. I think that is your main focus of motivation. Having reached that, all the other elements have a heightened impact.

    And I think that's true. From a state of flow, everything is better. But that doesn't mean everything comes from flow. Your list contains a whole bunch of other motives, which a given player, even one who enjoys Embodiment and Catharsis and Flow (ie. immersion) may or may not value.

    Your list with comments:
    So, taking engagement as a prerequisite, what is immersion good for on top of that?

    It's good for giving importance to discoveries and learning.
    --Exploration/Education
    It's good for adding adrenaline to chases and physical struggles.
    --This would be Exercise if it were larp. In TT, it falls under Catharsis.
    It's good for sensory detail that you otherwise might not bother sharing or processing.
    --Exploration
    It's good for cultivating a flow state where you can play for long periods without conscious effort.
    --Flow, of course
    It's good for remembering later -- killing an orc yourself sticks in the mind better than using some avatar to kill an orc.
    --This, I'm less sure about. In fact, it starts to scare me a bit. I think this may be an area where distancing yourself from deep immersion may be better than embracing it.
    It's good for group unity, the same way a camping trip is.
    --Fellowship
    It's good for rewarding big choices about life rather than little choices about a game.
    --Making choices feel big and important would come under Audience - significance is what differentiates "story stuff" from "everyday stuff". And some Protagonist.
    It's good for etching your contributions into the permanent record of a world that will outlast this moment.
    --Protagonist
    It's good for expanded character expression, like accents.
    --Mostly this is Exhibition

    Posted By: ValamirI'm interested in hearing what it is about RPGs that keeps people trying to use them for doing this thing that they're clearly only partially suited for doing at all.
    Depends on how you design the game. I've embarked on a design-endeavor that is singularly aimed at creating deeply immersive play. I have had different levels of success with three games of this kind, one of them really delivering the players into an experience unlike anything we've seen in other rpgs.

    To make the games work this way, I have use old and invented new storytelling- and dialogue-techniques, and have made them the driving force in the methods. There is next to none mechanics in these games, and I believe that is an important fact.

    Focusing my methods on techniques have made my "immersive games" end up far from the way modern american games are designed and played, and as far from classical games like D&D and Warhammer.

    And then; nowadays I'm testing out my new method for the old classical game Fabula, and have seen that it too seem to support a high degree of immersion. That has led me to plan a test-campaign with little action, and much focus on character and relationships, to test how well this classical method hold up to game-play aimed at deep immersion. If it do, I expect us to have a series of increasingly strong game-sessions, nothing much happening, other than us living the ordinary lives of the characters, and being completely overwhelmed by the force of the fiction we create.

    This does not sound very exciting to the majority of role-players out there, I know that ...
    - but it is indeed exciting to a designer like me ...
    - and it is indeed a kind of play that transport me, and players in my games, like no other game-play, to a place of magic.

    To conclude, Ralph; the reason role-playing games are only partially suited to doing the immersive thing, may be due to them being partially flawed designs. Or; role-playing games have not evolved enough to do this very effectively. We must expect games of the future to be much more efficient at dragging us (shouting and screaming, begging for mercy) into immersion. I do hope so!

    \n Our desire for adventure is insatiable! That is; as long as it's done in a reasonably safe and comfortable manner, of course. Role-playing games are ideal in this regard, as you have already remarked. I believe any rpg-adventure is better off with players having a feel for their characters, identifying with them in complex ways. That is the hallmark for immersive game-play. So there is no reason to let go of immersion in high drama and adventure, in my view.

    I have had success doing immersion in my classical fantasy role-playing game Fabula. It has served me for 16 years now, approximately 2500-3000 game-sessions. During that time I have made several methodic experiments, most of them aimed at strengthening immersion. It has been done by compiling simple tricks of the trade in most of these experiments, and in some I have invented techniques of more complex nature. Most of the tools I've used in this respect has been aimed at the players, forcing them to step into the shoes of their characters, by playing on instinctive reactions and quite normal emotional responses.

    Most players are very positive and open in respect to what a game may do for them, and that is a prerequisite, of course, for succeeding with such experiments. Having players following up on the lead of a game-method or a GM, and digging in deep, has been a very rewarding experience through the years. I highly recommend climbing out on a shaky limb when designing your games ...
    - and when leading a game-session ...
    - and absolutely when playing a character!
    Trust and curiosity is good!

    As for the "immersive games" of mine ...
    - they are all contemporary in setting, dealing with the lives of "ordinary" people (nothing ordinary about the game-play, mind you). I am sorry to say that none of them are translated to English.     Posted By: David BergA few years ago, I tried to stick in a mechanic to reward immersion-conducive play
    Years back I got involved in a discussion at the Forge, about the connotations of "system" and "mechanics". It was back when Forge-speak were evolving. I tried to argue the point that these phrases makes some kind of mind-frame from which designers are hard pressed to break out of. I did not succeed.

    I believe my point is valid though. Is is hard to think outside the "box" provided by the language we use. When no suitable terms exist, we have problems thinking in those terms.

    In this regard it is a case of the term "mechanics" dominating the field of thinking, closing it to thoughts on storyteller- and dialogue-techniques, and experiments with tools in that part of the game-method. There seem to be a lack of understanding amongst English-talking designers for the importance of working with techniques in a conscious way, making premeditated tools for the players to use. You have a tendency, like you here David, to work within mechanical parameters.

    What I find most conductive to immersion in role-playing games, is having the players talk as, act as, and feel as their characters. I need the player to adopt the frame of mind of his character, and his point of view. To make this happen, I need to work the player. That is; to regard him as a human being, with instincts and emotions like any other human, and apply my knowledge of humans in my dealings with him, either as a designer or as a game-master. I need to organize my knowledge of humans, make it into viable dialogue tools, and make them available to the players. And, as the players are blank on this when they come to my game, I need to tell them carefully what to do, and make sure my tools works when they do it. So my tools have to be well conceived, effective, and simple.

    One such simple tool:
    When the characters enter a crisis in the fiction; talk loud and fast!

    Explanation:
    - Loud noises influence the human body; we go into danger-mode, the body producing hormones that sharpen our senses and shorten our response time. This physical response is very conductive to our game-play in a crisis.
    - Fast talking influence us, and makes us produce stress hormones, which also is very conductive to our game-play in a crisis.

    Background:
    - Emotions originates in our bodies. We have a whole system of glands producing hormones. These are keyed to certain situations and sensory stimulations.
    - If the whole group (or a leader) follows up on a behavior, and the behavior is keyed to a specific situation, the group is likely to produce emotions that mirror the real emotions of said situation. This is equally true for fear, anger, sorrow, love and happiness, all the main emotions.
    - When you instruct the players to use certain behaviors in their game, the players themselves start producing some emotions that suits the situation, and thus they strengthen their identification with the characters, triggering a deeper immersion. You get more realistic characterization, more complex interaction, far more intensity, and a lot of serious fun!

    It's simple; go outside the box of "system" and "mechanics". Start thinking dialogue-techniques as an important field of design in your games.

    - that has never sounded smart to me.

    \n No, Dave, I have not done one single experiment with sound-effects or music in role-playing games. I don't like it. Fiddling with a remote is bad-stuff to me.     - and then: mouth shut, prolonged silence, listening ...

    Suspense by lack of action! Great fun! ;-)

    \n A couple of tips then, most of them known to you already, on how to have players induce emotions in each other:

    Fear: monotone voice (caressing a players underarm or chin while talking monotone), silence, sudden movements with arms or body, eyes staring intently at someone or somewhere else, standing silent behind a player, ticking finger insistently on table.

    Anger: standing up; leaning forward, shouting, shaking fists, hard handhold on the players upper arm (steering him), banging hands on the table to strengthen arguments, bellowing "STOP!" (when they want to roll the die), being "unfair" (life's not fair, and that triggers anger).

    Sorrow: low voice, hanging head, hands touching, sighs, tears, reaching out to other players, hand over hearth while saying the name of a dead character, doing new tasks while pondering loss and meaninglessness, quiet reminiscence, sudden flashbacks.

    Love: talking softly, laughing, touching, caressing the hand of a player while talking to him as his lover, laying your arm over someones shoulder, smiling intently.

    Joy: laughter, talking fast and with short breaths, including other with words and gestures, enthusiastically enforcing the words of others, saying "Yes! Yes! Yes!", doing silly things/plays/pranks (a sign of surplus energy).

    In addition: do some of the things you usually do when in a state, and it will go a long way to induce that state in you and others. And that "what you usually do" thing is important; use your instincts! Remove yourself from consciously thinking on these things when playing. It should be used there and then, in the spur of the moment, to be most effective.

    \n Hi David!

    No, I never alert my players to anything, except at cons; where I say beforehand if my games are for grown ups (which they most usually are, at cons).

    As for your players feeling confused; try not to overdo it. Indicate the stance, by bending your head a bit, looking up at times, saggin your body to underline it, but keeping up communication.
