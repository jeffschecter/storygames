    \n Recess was a success! We were 180+ people strong with 50+ new to Recess.

    NerdNYC is a nonprofit dedicated to helping people who live in NYC (or close by) play games and make friends. Many people exchanged contact information and I now have 2 more potential gaming groups! Mission accomplished. I couldn't be happier.

    I was a mad man and in addition to organizing I ran 4 games. 2 Mouse Guard and 2 Dogs in the Vineyard. 1 Dogs game was with a group entirely non-white, male/female mix, 3 of the players between 19-22 who primarily play World of Warcraft and dabble in the occasional RPG. One player asked, which is the DPS character type when choosing Dogs Backgrounds (the had never heard of Dogs but chose it because they heard it was a western ala the Red Dead Redemption video game). The game was fantastic although I almost lost my voice by the end. Overall we had a solid and varied mix of people. Many new to gaming. We have a policy that all games run at Recess require no previous experience and all rules are taught.

    In terms of popular games... Deathwatch was the winner. It filled instantly! Then was Mouse Guard and Dogs in the Vineyard. Then D&D 4 and Pathfinder. Then Villains & Vigilantes, In Nomine, All Flesh Must Be Eaten, BASH, anything Cthulhu or an Indy Game. Many joked Recess was the end of D&D 4E. Several attendees brought their entire D&D 4E book collections to our Swap Table. There were over 40 D&D 4E books given away for free. And the number of Pathfinder games run and filled was unstoppable The Swap Table itself was a sight to behold, someone brought every issue of Dragon magazine from 1980 to 2000.

    These were the games offered...

    RPGs: Dungeons & Dragons 4E Essentials (2), Pathfinder (6), Deathwatch, Star Wars D6, Call of Cthulhu, FATE!, Burning Wheel, Trail of Cthulhu (2), Mouse Guard (2), Apocalypse World, Dogs in the Vineyard (2), All Flesh Must Be Eaten, Basic Action SuperHeroes (2), In Nomine, Barbarians of Lemuria, Villains & Vigilantes (2), Dungeons & Dragons 3E, Cosmic Journey, Starships & Spacemen (Startrek), Aces and Eights, Paranoia, Wordplay, Dark Refuge, Delve, Fortune's Fool (2), Misspent Youth, Old School D&D (1), Lacuna, Lady Blackbird, Legend of the Five Rings, Spookybeans, My Life With Master, In a Wicked Age, Penny For Your Thoughts, Vampire

    Board / Card Games: Castle Ravenloft, Battlestar Galactica, Dominion, Agricola (2), Small World (2), Arkham Horror, Death Angel: the Space Hulk Card Game, BattleTech, Space Alert, Dune, Last Night On Earth, Dixit, Puzzle Strike, Steam, ESCAPE FROM ATLANTIS, FRESCO, Mississippi Queen, Battlestations, The Settlers of Catan, Ticket to Ride, High Frontier, The Merchant of Venus, Tales of the Arabian Nights, Sid Meier's Civilization, Caylus, Funny Friends, Founding Fathers, Wok Star

    We hold Recess 3 times a year (usually January, June, and September). I may have to organize a smaller event because I can't wait till June to get everyone together!

    \n
    \n I'd played AW before, and use its moves as inspiration for carousing in my OD&D; game, but this was my first time running it. One of the other players had read the rules, all the others just liked the description in the program:
    \n
    \n
    Winter on the Jersey Turnpike; 5 players maximum; Mature Only; Dark
    \n
    \nWe'll play to find out what happens when these elements collide: your hot, bad-ass, cool characters; your gang, holed up in the Vince Lombardi rest stop and toll booth; the Hog Patrol, who roam the roads; and the weird arrival of Jack Frost. How dark we make this, and how much we explore the mature only aspects of the Apocalypse World system, depends on us; I'm not pushing for that, just giving warning that it may emerge.
    \n
    \n
    \nI seeded these elements to help get it moving as a convention game. I don't think I'll try it again as a short-form thing with strangers.
    \n
    \nHere was how I described the experience in a thread about how to better describe games in the program to get people on the right page:
    \n
    \n
    I think it's important to distinguish between "players will pursue their PC's individual agendas in individual rotating-spotlight scenes" versus "players should find reasons to stick together and usually all be present in each scene," and between "conflict between PC goals is expected to drive the story" versus "players are expected to cooperate to achieve group goals."
    \n
    \nI suspect my Apocalypse World game had some issues due to unclear expectations in these regards (beginning with me - I strongly prefer the latter of each of those dyads, but decided not to push that in the desire to do AW-as-written which doesn't seem to encourage that approach). Some of the players didn't know anything about AW and even the one who'd read it was probably, like me, a little unclear; AW mostly eschews the dramaturge posture I associate with indie games for what-my-guy-does immersion and takes a more trad/OSR sandbox approach to story-later letting things brew up as they will, but the rotating spotlight and PC-NPC-PC triangles feel very un-trad to me (although I've certainly been in D&D; games where backstabbing was expected so that's why it's a good thing to flag.) I think that AW is like grunge; it's popular and revolutionary because it blends parts of the best of genres, but like when I saw Mudhoney play in a parking garage in Seattle in '90, the different genre audiences don't know how to play well together yet; punks were getting their arms broken because the metalheads weren't expecting to catch people when they dove off of speakers at them.
    \n
    \n
    \nOther things I've run at Recess have been:
    \n- OD&D;: consistently awesome, a good match between what I love and how I can get people into it in a convention format; here's one example
    \n- 4E: this was a trial version of a roller-coaster scenario I later ran at Gen Con; it was lots of fun and the orbiting planetoid represented by a soccer ball we blu-tacked minis to was a big hit, but there was some disconnect between my old-school "let the players do what they seem to be into" approach and the "move right to the point" expectations 4E had for Deliverator & maybe others.
    \n- Rogue Trader (the previous WH40K RPG): this kicked ass because in most games if you play a xenophobic fanatic whose response to anything is "purge it with holy fire!" you're doing it wrong; very satisfying to reverse that for once. The game system encourages us to go real wide-screen and command fleets, destroy worlds, etc; it didn't offer a lot of support for these mechanically but in four hours that wasn't much of a hindrance and it didn't get in the way much either.
    \n- Rifts: like Rogue Trader this one filled up real fast and had a lot of enthusiasm for people (like me) who'd been wanting to get their SPACE! GLITTER! BOY! on for like 20 years now. The rules did get in the way here, though; more here.

    \n I ran My Life With Master for the first time! (I had only played it once, with Adam Dray GMing!) I only had two players but it was great. It was a take-off of the movie Despicable Me. I brought little yellow minions plushies, which really made the game in my opinion. We decided at the outset that Gru was a frustrated geneticist trying to cure cancer. The "minions" were actually living tumors. It went downhill from there. By which I mean more and more into total twisted awesomeness.

    I also enjoyed all three games I played, including Pathfinder! It's definitely a game that I would play long term if I could get into the right group but would not want to GM.
