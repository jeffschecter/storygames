    \n Man, I actually kinda like Engle Matrix games. I recommend trying them out even if you're all hippy - I know they don't seem like much at first glance, and they might not be roleplaying games (execution depending), but there are some interesting old-yet-forgotten ideas in there. Being the referee is interesting, as your only job in the game is to keep the other players honest and invested in the fiction, without yourself creating any.

    What I'd like to see would be a solid, generic Engle Matrix publication that put it all into one book. As it is, the game is scattered into who knows how many products and web pages. Something that dealt with designing your own Engle Matrix scenarios and playing them successfully would serve the game well in reaching new audiences, I think.

    \n I've played an Engle Matrix game on the yahoo group that Chris runs. I found it a lot of fun and also a bit frustrating. The frustration came about because our characters were all competing for a goal (to be the next headmaster of a school of evil wizards) and it became hard for me to separate my enjoyment of the fiction we were generating from my enjoyment of my character's success.

    Because the referee is necessarily in the role of judging the likeliness (or worthiness) of your argument, it can feel uncomfortable at times - especially on the internet where subtext is impossible to communicate. So if I make an argument I think is cool or clever, and it's rated low, I'm left wondering why. And whether I should be building a logical case or writing actions that will delight the referee with their inventiveness. This was compounded slightly by the fact that, as I recall, Chris stated he would rate some arguments low or high just to create a more interesting story and pace the game correctly.

    This aspect made it for me a slightly unhappy case of neither fish nor fowl - a story game with competitive aspects where in theory clever actions will advance your case, but in truth it felt largely up to the referee as to whether I succeed or not (plus the randomness of dice).

    That probably overstates the case (and likely is the result of some sour grapes after my character snatched defeat from the jaws of victory) because I really did enjoy the game. And the simplicity of the mechanism is a delight. Heck, it even makes for interesting solo play. So definitely worth a try.

    \n You make the matrix explicit by writing it down - the same way that a lot of story games make traits and relationships explicit.

    What makes a matrix game a matrix game (the way I define it) is 1), an explicit matrix, and 2), the matrix is open to being extended by the players (not the ref - the ref is only there to keep the game within the originally agreed-upon grounds).

    \n I've only played Dracula, which I think has an abbreviated version of the rules or something.

    But it does have this nifty question system for setting scenes. When it's your turn, you pick a question from the list to use as a scene starter. Stuff like, "What happens to weaken your opponent?" or "How does Dracula get Mina to drink his blood?" and "What senseless act of violence happens next?"

    After all the questions have been dealt with in turn, you go to a set of final questions that end the game. It's a clever way to set up scene-framing for new roleplayers, and laying them out on a boardgame-like track helps, too.

    \n The problem comes when you have a competitive game rather than a cooperative one.

    That's when you need to do the following:

    Have an explicit matrix. (The game universe is built up of discrete terms in a matrix.)

    Use round-robin turns instead of simultaneous (so you don't have player's making arguments that just disappear.

    Differentiate between the narrative (which always takes place) and the effect in game terms (which may or may not).

    Give all arguments the same chance of having an effect (e.g., 50% - just to add a bit of randomness).

    Limit the effects (e.g., for a naval battle, no single argument can cause more than a medium damage result on a ship).

    Limit the Ref to only ruling on the admissibility of an argument (is it appropriate to the scenario?) and its effects (is it balanced?). If not, return the argument for revision.
