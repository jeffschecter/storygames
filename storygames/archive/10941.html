[D&D] Getting rid of the d20

\n
\n So, I've been playing D&D on-and-off for a few years now. And I've never really been comfortable with the "randominess" I feel is brought into the game by the 20-sided-die-for-almost-everything resolution system. Maybe it's because I spent my formative years playing bell-curve style initiatlized games, like GURPS and possibly FUDGE, but I think in the light of the 4th edition's "skill challenges", I have begun to see it as wholly at odds with what the rest of the game, mechanically speaking, seems to be on about.

Which is to say, that the entire rest of the experience in playing D&D seems to be about careful/lucky management of limited resources - drinking the potion, spending the action point, consuming a spell/power - with the lone exception of this oddly fundamental mechanic that everything else hinges around. In yet more other words, if the core fun of the game is choosing when to use that kick-ass spell power (or whatever) it seems to me that randomly deciding that I don't get the benefit of that core fun because of random bad luck is a sign of something acting at cross purposes to something else.

Worse, as far as I'm concerned, in these poorly constrained "skill challenge" events, I find that I, in the player's chair, often have little to no resources to spend in my attempt to influence the outcomes. Outside of a few classes' utility powers, I don't really see much in the game at large that's generally useful as this kind of resource. (In previous editions of the game, there were various home remedies for Hero Points or Action Points, that could be used to improve one's chances; 4th edition has official Action Points, but they're largely useless in a skill challenge event.)

I think I have a solution, and but I could desperately use some helpful advice. Maybe it shows that I've been playing a lot of Dominion lately, but this idea starting to percolate in my head some time after I read about Robin Laws' Poker Skill Challenges.

The next time I GM this game for people, I plan to replace the d20 with a small pack of cards, one pack of cards for each player. The pack of cards might be numbered, or use some kind of color-coding, but either way it would have cards for the numbers 1 through 20 (eg, color coding would mean: black suit 1-10, red suit 11-20). Each player would, at the start of the session, draw a small hand of cards, and play a card from their hand whenever they would normally roll a d20 (eg, attack rolls, skills, saves, etc.) (Powers that involve a re-roll would flip over the top card of the player's deck.) A la Cosmic Encounter, the player doesn't draw any new cards until all the cards in the current hand have been played.

My intent is to reduce (without entirely eliminating) moments of lopsided, unfun randomness while also providing the player with a new kind of "resource choice" mechanic. The hand of cards introduces an element of sacrificing future victory for victory now, and I like that this might produce "how much this victory is worth" kinds of tension -- a tension I think I largely lack when playing this game (certainly in the game I'm a player in right now, at least). I think that moving to a completely non-random element would do too much to reduce other fun elements of the game -- or, maybe, that the semi-antagonistic player-GM relationship we all tend to skirt when playing depends, at least a little, on a character occasionally being able to succeed well beyond estimation. And too many elements of the game hinge on things like critical hit rolls to be removed entirely without amounting to a dramatic change in the nature of the game.

I'm primarily interested in trying out various hand sizes, and am suspicious that an auspicious number lies somewhere between 3 and 7. I actually suspect that you'll want fewer cards with more players (and vice-versa) -- more players have more resources to bring to bear, and a greater ability to "aid another", or find another character with a better skill rank, or better hand, thus watering down any potential for tense decision making. It might also be interesting to try playing with a 40-card deck (numbered 1-20 twice), or to try removing a number of cards randomly, face down, from the deck before play.

Oh - finally - this is a player-focused mechanic. I think the GM should still roll dice, primarily because the GM, as narrator, already has a generous ability to constrain randomness. In other words, since the GM can always throw more problems / tougher problems at the players, I don't think she needs any particular help in this regard. Although it might be worth saying that I'm already a big fan of using Fantasy Oracle / Harrow / Everway -type resources in conjuring up new challenges.

Thoughts?

    \n Hey Joshua:

    > My intent is to reduce (without entirely eliminating) moments of lopsided, unfun randomness

    Another strategy is to not let the dice provoke unfun results -- to ensure that no matter what random outcome the d20 produces, there's a fun result flowing from it. I think that this is easier in skill challenges than in combat, because in combat the results of a failed check are dictated by the rules. But in skill challenges, the result of a check failure or challenge failure can be *anything* that either doesn't grant the roller's intent, or that grants the roller's intent in a complicated or perverted way.

    As a 4E DM, I always always try to have interesting failure outcomes for skill checks thought up before the roll, and if I can't think of one, then I'll generally just grant the player what they want. (edit: or I'll kibbutz with the table on what an interesting failure outcome might be. It's easier to be a DM when you've got a bunch of assistants helping you be evil! Some players may not be into this kinda thing tho.)

    Also echoing Mr. Stout'n: I've played Castle Falkenstein, which uses a similar card scheme, and we had to have a formal agreement at the table that people wouldn't slough off crap cards on nonsense / meaningless situations. Without that agreement, the game kinda degenerated. Now, this was easy in Falkenstein because we were all civilized ladies and gentlemen who welcomed structure and shunned churlishness. I don't know if D&D has the same social contract.

    I hope this doesn't come across as a dismissive threadcrap, and apologize for discussing your proposed mechanic so slightly. I'll think more about my Falk experience and if I come up with anything that might be helpful, I'll let ya know. Good luck!

    \n You can do your card trick without cards and keeping the d20. I have had my players roll several d20 rolls in advance. Usually around 10, but there's no reason you can't do more. For fun, I also give them an extra 1 and 20 on top of it (actually, the second time I offered just a 20, and they protested - they wanted the 1 as well!)

    They tend to use the good rolls first, and then mid-to late stage is spending the bad rolls - the good thing is they can toss them on at-wills or saving throws or use powers that have good secondary effects not reliant upon hitting a target, or decent miss results.

    When they run out of pre-determined rolls, they start using regular rolls.

    They love it. It also speeds play up by a good degree.

    Incidentally, I do the same thing except that I build up a lot more rolls and I've said that I'm not allowed to go out of order. I have to do straight down the list. This speeds it up incredibly for me. I love it too.

    \n On the card issue: I've used cards in Console:Sidearm, and having crap cards in your hand led to an interesting choice. Sometimes you wanted to "reload" and draw a new hand. Other times it was worth making that attack, even though you knew it would miss, because the cards played recently form a "team hand" and your lousy card might be improving that by making it a full house. There's still some randomness, but you have a choice of how to deal with it rather than a choice of how to recover from getting screwed by it.
