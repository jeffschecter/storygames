\n Hey everyone,

I've been hosting a regular Tuesday night game at my place for the past year. We started things off with the intent of playing a bunch of games, changing our game every two or three weeks, so that we could play all of the new and cool stuff that we'd bought. I really liked this because most of my gaming experiences have been of the fairly long, convoluted campaign variety and I've been wanting to mix that up. Also, I'm normally the guy that takes the GM role for the group as I like being the GM, so having a new game every couple of weeks keeps things fun and fresh for me.

Recently I've developed an itch for a certain kind of gaming experience. Our gaming table for Tuesday night tends to shift around a lot, with some players being able to show one week and then not being able to come the next. Because of this, and also because I really like the idea of it, I've been hunting around for a Done In One RPG.

For those not familiar with the term, Done In One is a phrase bandied about in the comic book community and is the name for stories that are done in one issue. Setup, character introduction, plot, finale, all banged out in twenty two pages. Continuity and development does happen over runs of the book, but someone can pick up just one issue and get a complete story out of it.

My idea of a Done In One RPG is something that would allow the play group to sit down and make up their characters, run through a complete adventure/story/thing, and reach a satisfying end in about three hours. For my group's needs the game would also be adventurous in nature and not have the kind of crunch that games like d20 and classic DnD have. I wouldn't mind there being some carry over of stories and ideas from session to session of the game, but to support the original intent of "Done In One" it would also have to be the kind of game that wouldn't fall apart if a character or two missed sessions.

I've tried running games like SotC and PTA, but they aren't fitting quite right. SotC has a long character creation system which I love far too much to feel comfortable gutting. PTA is the kind of game that I feel really requires there to be all of the players present at each session.

So, my question is simply, do any of you guys have any suggestions for games that currently exist that support this mode of play?

    \n I actually have run "complete" games of SotC, Don't Rest Your Head, and Truth & Justice in around two hours, including explaining the rules and doing character (and in some cases, world) creation. The key with those system is that rules mastery isn't needed for the players and the mechanics are flexible enough for me as a GM to handle it all.

    There are also some really neat games coming out that are meant for play in just one session. Paul Tevis' A Penny For My Thoughts (which is an ashcan and in development) is a game that takes around three hours for four people and is a game where you create characters as you play (as you play amnesiacs regaining your memory through a story-telling play process).

    Off-hand, I can't think of anything else. I'll be happy to chime in again if I do, though.

    \n I'll second The Shab-al-Hiri Roach; I've played it several times in a single evening and had a blast. Hero's Banner also worked well for us, although I'd probably recommend using the tweaks Tim suggests on his Web site to speed up playing demos or at cons if you want to get through a generation or two per three hour session.     \n It'll probably sound odd, but older TSR rpgs and skirmish minis games with campaign add-on rules.

    B/X D&D, especially with dungeon delves rather than knocking around the landscape is very fast and you can come or go from the dungeon as you choose.

    Star Frontiers, Gangbusters, certainly Marvel Super heroes, almost anything mission-oriented as opposed to wander-y.

    With the minis oriented stuff, you almost only do one shots unless you feel like re-arranging a bunch of tabletop layout throughout an evening. At the skirmish level of play, most games are through in 3 hours or less, a bunch determined by how many players and pieces are involved. GW's Mordheim and Necromunda ( free) and Legends of the Old West fit that model, as do the Rattrap Design games ( .45 Adventure, Gloire, and the soon-to-be released Flash Gordon/Buck Rogers style game).

    \n Polaris? One observation from Polaris play I've had is "Pacing goes as fast as the most impatient player (Heart of Mistaken) wants it to." If either the Heart or Mistaken wants really fast mini-stories (with a "finale") then they can push the pace up as fast as they want it to.

    One quirk however is some Polaris games have almost separate stories for each protagonist. So if you have an unstable group, the set of protagonists will change. This might be considered a good thing, in that hopefully people will have incentives to wrap up short arcs within a session.

    OTOH if by finale you mean "death or final resolution of a character" you might need to adjust the rate of xp gain.
