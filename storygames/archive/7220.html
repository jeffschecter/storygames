\n Hello Story Games,

I'm currently working on a project that revolves around road trip stories. I could use some movie recommendations.

I'm looking for movies:
*that are set on the road,
*where the characters encounter situations that make them rethink their goals and self-conception,
*ultimately resulting in letting go. Of control, of their goals, of the future. Whatever.
*The movie isn't a stupid Vegas road trip movie starring a bunch of well-polished frat boys. Unless, against all odds, it is.
*It can be a coming-of-age story, but doesn't have to be.
*It can be oddball and quirky, or serious and moving, or anything in between.
*It absolutely must feature a storyline where people find themselves suddenly in great appreciation of the present.
*It may be interlaced with some deep existential questions, but doesn't have to be.

Two movies that fit this bill magnificently, which are both awesome awesome awesome:

Wristcutters: a love story
An oddball trip through through the afterlife. Zia convinces the only friend he has in his post-suicide world to join him in seeking out his ex girlfriend. Along the way they pick up another girl and people learn the delicate art of letting go of what they think they want, in favour of what they have right in front of them. I saw this last week, and this is what made me decide that this project I'm working on must happen.

Everything is Illuminated
This movie is absolutely gorgeous. I just watched it about twenty minutes ago. I can't even begin to summarize it right now, but I can say that I found myself going "woah... fuck" under my breath throughout the last half, because it was just so unfathomably touching for me.

O Brother Where Art Thou fits the bill I think, too, but I need to watch it again.

    \n Just thought of another book that may have escaped notice, and it has the upside of actually being non-fiction. Killing Yourself to Live by Chuck Klosterman is the author's account of visiting places where rock musicians died. It muses somewhat on the nature of celebrity, but is also intertwined with the author's struggle to choose between women in his life. Klosterman's writing is always witty and quick, and his pop-culture observations are usually insightful.

    On the Road by Jack Kerouac probably also qualifies, but I have to admit to never being able to bring myself to read it. I read the first few pages, and then something more enticing grabs me.

      \n Okay, this may not qualify as they don't spend a lot of time and there isn't very much appreciation of the present BUT Withnail and I is excellent and applies in every other respect listed. It is both oddball/quirky and depressingly serious. It's the story of two out of work actors in the 60s trying to surive while on vacation, drinking themselves to death and trying to buy eels from poachers. They also rebuff the sexual advances of a wealthy former actor. It's absolutely one of the funniest movies in existence.

      The Bird People in China is an amazing road trip movie from gonzo-horror/children's fantasy/musical comedy director Takashi Miike. It's a mostly serious magical realist film about life in a small chinese village, from the perspective of the odd couple; average salaryman and yakuza thug. It's incredibly mature and one of the finest films of the genre. Watch it now, you won't regret it.

      Also by Miike is a film I haven't seen yet but has the promise of being an excellent road trip movie: Dead or Alive 2: Birds.

      Oh, can anime films count? If so: Night on the Galactic Railroad. I can't tell you anything about it but just watch it. You'll be floored. It's one of those things where watching it without preconceptions just makes the emotional impact just amazing.

      \n I'm hereby stating for public record that I don't find David Lynch creepy or weird.
      I don't think he makes movies to be beaten.
      They're just a bit more like dreams than they are your typical Hollywood storytelling.
      I saw Mulholland Drive in the theater with maybe 6 other people, 2 of which walked out before it was half over.
      Behind us sat a elderly pair of women(lesbian-spinsters?), and a twenty-something couple.
      They made it through the whole thing, I was amazed.
      I don't watch his movies with the intent of breaking them, I really don't think they're that tough to understand.
      They have to be viewed a bit more carefully, I guess. I don't know.
      I find escapism via violence films creepy.
      I'm sorry for the slight segway.
      The Cowboy represents pre-Hollywood.
