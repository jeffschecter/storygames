    I want to be clear what you are doing. Are you:

    • comparing each die result to a target number based on the task (old WoD)?
    • comparing each die result to a fixed target number, then comparing the number of successes to the task's target (new WoD)?
    • comparing the sum of the dice to a target number based on the task (sort of an inverse TFT)?

    Old WoD, new WoD, ORE, and other dice pool systems where the die results are interpretted individually produce a lot of detail, like degree of success, but are harder to predict. The Fantasy Trip, GURPS, and other dice sum systems produce fewer results, often only success/failure, but are much more predictable, so they are good for tactical challenge games.

  • jhkim

    I've got a series of articles on dice mechanics on my System Design Page.

    So I'm going to take up the opposing side for a bit. Dice pools have a few inherent problems. For one, they don't scale well. On the low side, you can't roll less than one die -- and there are often statistical anomalies in a few dice (like Storyteller's infamous rising botch chance). On the high side, the number of dice rolled becomes more and more unwieldy.

    They also have a minimum complexity. Single die rolls -- like the Unisystem, say -- can be much faster and simpler. For example, I'll note that few boardgames use dice pools (or dice other than d6s). It's an invention of generally more complex RPGs.

    Still, they do give a satisfying tactile feel, allow representation of more intricate probabilities, and can be at least straightforward if not as fast as single-die.

    Hi, Victor.

    Joe mentioned that Verge does something a little different with dice pools. It's similar to the system in Godlike, or so I gather. In Verge, you essentially roll a pool of d6es and grab the most commonly occurring result. So if you rolled 6654333211111 you'd have four 1's, which would give you 4 successes. You get to add more dice every exchange and there are mechanisms for picking up everything but the 1's (or whichever result you chose) and rerolling it and moving the 1's over to increase your successes.

    I like pools because there are more fiddly mechanics you can use: add dice, subtract dice, reroll dice, sort dice, "use" dice, and so on.

    No one's mentioned Dogs in the Vineyard yet, either. You roll a pool of different kinds of dice (like 6d6 + 3d8 + 1d10 + 2d6 and so on) and keep using traits to add more dice. But then you only use two dice at a time as a 'raise' or 'see.' It's extremely effective.

    I think the degree of skill is just a question of the range of skill compared to the size of the die -- it's not inherent in the roll, but in how you set your range of skill numbers for the system. So, for example, in D&D3/D20 there is a large variance on the die (1 to 20) compared to the range of skill (0 to 8 for starting characters). On the opposite end, though, you have a game like CORPS -- where skill goes from 1 to 10 or more, and the roll is from 0 to 5 (1d10/2, round down). In CORPS, there is a huge difference between the average result for skilled and unskilled.

    However, I guess there is a related issue here. Single-die systems have no bell curve -- so there's no tail to the distribution. That means that if you have high skill numbers compared to the die size (like in CORPS), you'll get a lot of auto-successes and auto-failures. A lot of people may find that unsatisfying, and possibly prefer to increase the range of the roll instead. Or you can just accept a lot of auto-results. (Incidentally, you can get more tails to the distribution by switching to 2d6 or 3d6, but that complicates the rolling a little.)

    Victor:

    Ah, so it looks like you are doing a sort of reverse-TFT mechanic. The Fantasy Trip (Steve Jackson's pre-GURPS RPG) had players roll a variable number of d6s and add the results to roll under one of three attributes: ST, DX or IQ. More diffult tasks rolled more dice, while talents subtracted dice.

    In contrast, you have the number of dice based on skill, rolling (under?) against the score of the challenge. Also, it sounds like you have contests between players resolved by each player rolling dice.

    Might I make a suggestion? Resolve everything resolved by dice roll vs. dice roll, instead of some things being dice roll vs. challenge rating. Compare the two situations:

    • Bruno the Badass Barbarian is fighting with Rigomundo Rapscallion. Bruno rolls 1 die + 1 for Ax + 1 for Berserker; Rigomundo rolls 1 die + 2 dice for Master Fencer. 3 dice vs. 3 dice, whoever has the highest sum wins.
    • Rigomundo tries to open a locked door and rolls 1 die + 1 for Lockpicking vs. the door's 1 die + 2 for Difficult Lock: 2 dice vs. 3 dice, highest sum wins.

    This has a couple advantages:

    • you're only using one mechanic instead of two;
    • setting challenge difficulties is less arbitrary and seems more organic;
    • you can do other tricks, like give an extra die to the one with better armor or faster movement.

    And best yet: you can speed up the computations you were worried about by discarding matching pairs of dice. I rolled a 6, you rolled a 6, so they cancel each other out; keep cancelling matching pairs until none of my dice match any of your dice. NOW compare my total to yours to see who wins.

    Judson,

    At that point, why not just a setting module for Sorcerer?

    Because Victor wants a dice sum instead of dice pool.

    I think there's no difference in simplicity between contested rolls and roll vs. target number, as far as the roll itself goes. However, how bonuses are calculated is another matter. In the method I proposed, each skill or advantage adds one die, unless you have a skill like "Master (whatever)", which adds two dice. This is simple to keep track of. If you were to substitute roll vs. target and make no other changes, you need to add +3 or +4 to the target number to get the same effect. This is actual math, although it's easy math, as opposed to mere counting dice in the contested rolls approach.

    Where roll vs. target number would be better is if Victor decides he wants more variability in difficulties, with some advantages granting only a +1 and others granting a +5 or +6. However, this means there will either be look-up tables to determine value of an advantage or arbitrary GM rulings. If that's what Victor wants for the feel of how the game is run, that's OK, but it's not simpler.

    Also, if all resolution uses single rolls vs. target number, then you need to calculate defense scores (using the +3/+4 per die rule) for every player character, to use in PC vs. PC conflicts. Actually, you'd need defense scores for every arena of conflict used in the game (physical, social, mental, and so on.) More complexity.

    Again, this might be what Victor wants. I'm just pointing out what these choices will mean.

  • talysman
    October 2006

    OH, and I guess I should add that whether this is a roll-over or roll-under system matters, if we're talking dice sum vs. target number. If it's roll-over, the number of dice a character of a given skill has determines what tasks that character automatically succeeds at. Plus, if you assume a character succeeds about 2/3rds of the time for a task that character would deem "ordinary difficulty", you're talking about a 5 or 6 challenge for characters who roll 2 dice, 8 or 9 for characters rolling 3 dice, and so on. What's important here is that the difference between the target and the maximum becomes larger and larger, which could be important if you want extra special effects to occur at 10 points over the target, 20 points over the target, and so on.

    If it's roll-under, you have a problem in that low numbers = more challenge, but greater mastery = higher minimum roll. This is why roll-under systems like GURPS and TFT roll under the skill instead of the challenge. It's probably not good to use a roll-under in Victor's proposed system, unless he wants to subtract 1 for every die rolled. Not really a good idea, unless he's adding situational bonuses to the challenge rating, making the challenge easier. No real benefit; I mainly mentioned this because the contested roll approach assumed a roll-over, which works fine for that approach.
