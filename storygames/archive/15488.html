Engage/Avoid

\n In some games, especially at conventions, I find myself playing with someone with a jarring play style. They'll be loud in quiet games, or prone to monologues, or attempting to GM games that are GMless.

There are two ways to deal with such people. You can ignore them, playing mainly with the people you like. Or you can engage them, attempting to find a way to work with them.

Note, especially, that these people aren't toxic. They've just got a different play style from the rest of the table. (And, sometimes, they're a little bit slow to realise that.)

Sometimes, it works if you engage these people. Sometimes, it works if you avoid them. What do you do? Have you got any examples?

    \n It's a tricky one for sure and one I've dealt with from time to time. I've had varying degrees of success with engaging and avoiding-ish. I think it depends why they're like that to a degree. If their energy up because it's their first Con game (or they're just excitable) I find engaging them and asking them questions about their character can make them, pause (and take a break from being loud). This pause can give the others a chance to roleplay and hopefully they'll get a better feeling for the appropriate tone.
    \n
    \nIf they're the type that just likes to dominate conversations, and this is just another outlet for them to display their awesomeness, I use a more direct approach. "That's a great idea but let's give the others a chance to say what they do. I've also (for shame) administered a beating from an NPC they've mouthed off to incapacitate them, in a situation where inexperienced players were teamed with an experienced loudmouth.
    \n
    \nAs I say though "varying degrees of success".

    \n I agree with my colleague Mr. Mannon; if you are facilitating you can sort of steer someone gently or clarify their interests, but the fact that this occasionally fails is the reason I stick to two hour games with total strangers.

    It's not my job to tell somebody they are playing wrong, because they aren't. Interestingly, I facilitated a game a few months ago where the participants said up front "we want to learn how to facilitate this game", and having that permission and expectation meant I got to be both a fellow player and an instructor, which was cool.

    \n I will call for a break. After the break, I will restate and reconfirm the game's goals, ask if there are any questions about those goals, give examples of what we don't want, and then repeat those goals. In many cases, the person in question has no idea they are being disruptive. Don't be subtle.

    If the person is still jarring but not toxic (breaking the games agreed upon goals, cheating, bullying other players, being sexist / racist / homophobic), I will try to make sure they get their fair share of spotlight time but no more. Which includes asking them not to talk over other players when it isn't their spotlight time. Sometimes, because time can feel relative, I will actually time the length of their spotlight scenes and let them know when it's time to wrap up the scene. Or I will write down "scene" on an index card, put it in the middle of the table, and say, "if anyone feels any scene is going too long, just lift this up and we will wrap up this scene in a minute or less."

    What character this person plays is also a factor. Get a sense of who these people are before anyone makes or chooses characters. Avoid giving this player power to steer the game's direction. Handing them a "leader" character can be a disaster (I try to avoid leader characters in general).

    The GM can recruit this player. During a break, tell them what you want to achieve and ask them if they want to help. If they say yes, tell them what they can do to help achieve certain goals. I've repeatedly seen the most jarring player becomes the most helpful depending on what role they play in the game and how clearly the expectations of that role are made.

    If I'm GMing, often I will sit as far away from the jarring player as possible and closest to the quietest players. That way it takes less effort for quiet players to engage the GM, and more effort for the person in question to engage.

    Clear game descriptions that set expectations can be helpful. It gives you something to point to if the game goes in bizarre directions.

    Before you start playing, socialize. Ask the players what games they like and dislike. Ask them why the chose to play this specific game. If this exchange of information reveals there may be problems, give players the option of an easy out. If this game doesn't fit their play style, you won't take it personally if they choose to step out now. You can offer to help get them into a different game. Ask your con organizers what to do in these situations. If someone wants to change games, who can help them?

    I could go on for pages so I will stop now!

    \n That's part of the socializing before we start playing. I'll ask questions to get a sense of who is quiet or loud, new to gaming or rules expert level 20, socially agile or has trouble picking up social cues and more. I have an informal script in my head of followup questions to confirm my initial observations depending on what I'm trying to assess as I try to navigate who I'm about to play with. A lot of it is related to my job hiring experience. I did some funny things that made HR proud.

    If I can, I won't pick my seat as GM till I get to know the players. Sometimes this is hard because the game requires I spend a lot of time setting up my physical area as GM. In those cases, I will find an excuse based on what characters people are playing to ask everyone to move where they sit. In some cases I will be very direct, "you 2 sit closer to me because you are the quietest and I have trouble hearing in this room."

    \n I once played Montsegur 1244 with two wonderful players, one annoying one and one traditional gamer who didn't adapt well. In that game, I avoided the last two, engaging them as little as possible. We had a great game! But it was a curiously polarised game.

    Another time, I played with a loud gentleman who began by making lots of jokes. My God, I thought, I'm not going to get on with you at all. But, instead of avoiding him, I engaged him: I made an effort to frame scenes with just me and him. It was great. We kind of compromised: his jokey energy became really positive and I became a bit more lively with him.

    \n Whenever I've got and energetic player in a party I try to put that energy to good use. I'm not so enthusiastic myself, so I use short bursts of emotion to change the focus of that player. To do so, the worst you can do is directly take actions against that player. Actively ignoring him is better than going against him (like using an npc to take him out of the game for a while) but is worse than being direct about his attitude. But giving that player acknoledgement for whatever he's doing right is gonna help you get more of that in the game from him.

    In the case an argument threatens to get out of control, I choose to let the dice decide, like: 1-2, you're right, 2-3, the other players get their way, 4-6 I pull out something else from my sleeve.Either way it goes, the argument is over, we can continue to play and nobody can be blamed but the dice.
