    \n About...hmmm...eight to four years ago, my answer would have been "mostly internet forums, typically rpg.net." Before that it would've been "from USENET posts." Before that, it would've been a blank look and a shrug, followed by "from the guys I play with, I guess."

    Right now, my answer is a blank look and a shrug, followed by "from the people I play with, I guess." Thereby demonstrating the recursive properties of life.

    I pay some attention to internet forums, but mostly in a detached "What's the buzz about today?" sort of manner. I skim it, and make a mental note that at some point some people on the internet were raving about this or that game, and I keep the name and a few details about it in my head. At this point my interest is definitely not grabbed, however.

    But if someone in my gaming group starts to rave about it (we've got one guy who pays far more attention to new releases than anyone else, and who actually goes to conventions and things like that), then I'll get interested, and whatever I gleaned from the forums earlier might help with that. Basically, I'm letting my friends (well, mostly ONE friend) vet new games on my behalf, and I'll get interested if HE gets interested.


    So to put this in some kind of marketing perspective for game designers, I suppose that the take-home lesson is: don't try to make me care. I'm not going to conventions and not attending demos at the local gaming store and not subscribing to gaming news sites/newsletters/mailing lists because I don't want to do any of those things. It is almost certainly a waste of your limited resources to try and find an advertising/marketing venue that reaches me. Do yourself a favor and pretend I don't even exist: act like I'm not even a potential customer, act like I am living on another planet from yours.

    Instead, use your resources to hook my friend. He's out there doing all the things you want your customer to be doing: he's reading the internet, he's going to cons, he's listening to podcasts. If he digs what you're doing, I'll hear about it. If he doesn't, I'll also hear about it. Target the gatekeepers of taste, in other words, the people that guys like me trust to filter out all the crap.

    (As far as what you'd have to do to get his interest, I'm sure I couldn't say. He likes good editing and games that went through a lot of playtesting...maybe you could focus on that.)

    \n I'll bite. Usually, here. I used to check out games in progress on the Forge, but having reached such an impasse on my own projects, I've found that following developing games doesn't hold the same savor.
    \n
    \nMostly, I don't go looking for new games, though. My ability to play the games I have is severely limited, so I don't really want to bring new games into the fold of games I may never play. Occasionally, something catches my eye. Once it's caught my eye, Actual Play posts are where it's at.
    \n
    \nIt's a similar thing to what happened at GenCon. 3:16 wasn't on my radar. I'd heard nothing about it, and I didn't look into it. Then I paused by the game my roommate (Matt Snyder) was playing in, and it caught my interest. I thereafter bought the game on testimonials, and confirmed my decision with actual play that evening.
    \n
    \nSo in summary: Online buzz helps, but it's people whose opinions I respect that typically get the look. Catchy names may get me to click a link, but pictures and sales patter do nothing until I've looked. Once I'm looking at a game, Actual Play clinches the sale, or shuts it down.
