  • TonyLB
    November 2006
    I've come around to having two distinct modes of play ... though I can mix and match between them.

    In games where I'm hugely empowered to do stuff as a player, I do stuff as a player. The game is about doing stuff as a player, wowing people and being wowed. I like those games. They appeal to my desire to create.

    In games where much of the creative power resides with the GM, I do stuff as a person in order to impact the GM. I no longer worry about consensual illusions like "what really would happen in this fictional world?" What's going to happen is what the GM chooses to have happen. If I want something cool to happen then I need to actively make the GM awesome. I can do this by supporting. I can do this by providing the GM with adversity ("You want your character to do ... what? But ... but ... !") but, fundamentally, that's what it's going to come down to. The GM is in a role of privileged performer, and my goal is to make them awesome so that we all benefit.

    Given that different mindset, I'm down with pretty much all of the old-skool gaming I used to love, just in a new way.

    - I have to roll to see if my protagonist trips on his way up some unimportant hill;
    - I have to stop contributing when my character is out of the picture;
    - every copper coin and ounce of weight must be counted, even though the only one who cares about that shit is the GM;
    - I have to make rolls without knowing if they have any chance of achieving the outcome I want;
    - I have to find the one and only solution to a prepared problem;
    - people don't know their own agenda and create false expectations.

    In other words, I enjoy playing traditional games with the right people and attitudes. But indie games provide more reliable fun with a wider variety of people, for me.

    Rule Zero means that there are no other rules. There's no reason for them to exist. They are the advice.

    Posted By: ShaneJackson
    Apparently, Robin Laws's forthcoming game, The Esoterrorists, makes exactly that assumption. Something along the lines of, "Here's the clue, now how do you interpret it, and what do you do about that?"

    OK, I didn't state this strongly enough: "Do you find the clue" is a totally broken question to ask so long as you've got protagonists in a story. It's completely ass. It's a non-question. Not finding the clues means that you don't get to play the game. No one plays that way. And in the rare instances where they do, no one shows up for next week's game night.

    Some questions that are functional and may be mutually incompatible, depending on the direction of the game:

    "Which clues do you find?"
    "Who do the clues point to?"
    "Who does the clue benefit?"
    "Who planted the clue?"
    "What does this clue say about the investigator?"

    These are all things worthy of investigation, worthy of taking the reins as a protagonist.

    Now, if you've got a competitive situation where the players are trying to solve the mystery, the question remains not if they find the clues, but whether they interpret them correctly. I would find it a totally interesting game if, when you did an investigation, the measure of success was not whether or not the solution was found, but whether or not a scapegoat was sacrificed, and you couldn't know the difference. The important thing would be that one player's solution was accepted. Whoever's favorite suspect went down, that guy got the XP or whatever. Set it in, I dunno, 17th c. Massachusetts so you don't have to worry about things like "due process" â€” otherwise, you have the Law & Order RPG (which would be pretty neat anyway).

    Rich Stokes said,
    Is it the game-ness that appeals to you? It sounds like you're enjoying the VS and conflict and getting a kick out of trying to "beat" the players. Or am I misinterpreting?

    Dogs in the Vineyard is often about how far you'll go for something you think is right when violence looms as an option. It's the GM's job to ask the question. If the GM can't push as hard as possible â€” perhaps because "as hard as possible" means infinite badness â€” the GM has to pull punches by an arbitrary amount. A GM pulling punches is depriving the player of input about their character because the GM is either setting up opposition to be knocked down, or is setting up opposition to knock down the Dog. Only by careful trial and error can the GM correctly perceive the amount of opposition to give unless there are rules governing what they can do. When those rules are there, you're making the statement against circumstances that you all see are real circumstances (the dice on the table, the fiction you're fighting for).

      Putting on my necromancer hat for a minute:

      Read this thread and experience a trip down the sweet tunnel of time past! Oh, wow.

      The pendulum has swung so far in the other direction, certain fads and trends dominating game design. I think some tools have been left by the wayside, and some others... we've just forgotten how big a deal they were when they first appeared, kind of like how we make fun of our parents for having once been excited about wireless radios or CD players.

      For me, at least, it brings back heady memories of an exciting, hopeful time.

      And, very oddly, makes me realize that I never played a "traditional" RPG again, since around the time of this thread. I just quit, and that was it: it was either new-wave games or no gaming at all from then on. Not a conscious decision, just the way it panned out!

      Interesting.

      Only two exceptions stick out in my own game experience since 2006 or so:

      * I played an OSR game once (White Box D&D). Is that "trad"? Not to me, but it certainly isn't a "hippie game" either. It was a good time, although 180 degrees different from the other gaming I had done. Reminded me of my first roleplaying experiences (to which it was very close in feel). Terrifying, slow, full of arguments, no coherent story or characters, and yet lots of fun nonetheless.

      * My experience playing and running Apocalypse World feels pretty close to a "trad" game, with the strong GM authority and all that kind of thing. Some the moves really help make it more fun for me than some of my bad "trad" experiences, though, as well as the mindset of the players. Still, I'm convinced that AW is a traditional game at heart, and it feels very similar in practice (as Vincent points out in the text: "This way of playing, for some of you, will be just like what you've been doing all along").
