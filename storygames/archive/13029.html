    1) Good range of mechanical pencils
    2) Over the top of the pencils something like Pigma Micron archival ink paper
    3) Good quality paper like Arches
    4) A good plastic eraser as well!!
    5) Eraserable colored pencils (if you want to rock the color) and/or watercolor pencils

    \n I used to draw maps by hand all the time in University. Nowhere as stylized as Tony Dowler's stuff, but I also had to fit a lot of detail. Nowadays, I just bite the bullet and do everything in Illustrator, but here's what I would use back in the day:

    -Very Hard Lead pencil - I want the lines to be as light as possible, while still being visible
    or
    -Non-photocopy blue pencil - which isn't always as easy to ink over, but is worth it
    -Either a fine-tip archival black pen, or a Sharpie (which, if you're going to scan it in, is the better choice since they're cheap and common)
    -For paper, I would typical use Bristol, since it gives a nice, smooth surface.

    When it comes to scanning, it really depends what kind of scanner you have available. Hopefully, it will provide a preview on-screen. You want to increase both brightness and contrast to make the pencil lines fade out, and otherwise to clean up the background, so only the black, finished lines remain. Also, if you intend on using these for publishing, make sure to scan at a fairly high resolution, (I typically stick with 1200 dpi...but that's only because I have a massive hard drive that can handle immense files). 600dpi should work for most people, although, you should consider that most printers can't actually print higher than 300 dpi... but still, the higher the initial resolution, the better the final product.

    ...if color is involved, well, that's for someone else to solve.

    EDIT:
    And when it comes to the actual drawing bit, start with a basic sketch of your map as a rough draft, and take your time when drawing everything out. Don't rush yourself, since a single mistake can screw up your whole map.
    Style-wise, you might want to take a look at some classic maps. I'm particularly fond of city maps from the 1600-1700s, as well as maps from China and Japan, which offer a certain depth, as well as interesting ways to denote certain features. Otherwise, how you draw maps is up to the individual.

    \n It's funny to be called out as an authority, because I have a really hard time scanning maps!

    For black and white, everything said above about drawing materials, techniques, and tools is excellent. Many scanning tools let you preview and adjust the drawing when you scan. Otherwise, you need an editing program to edit the scanned image.

    I recommend 300 DPI for general use. 100 DPI is good enough for Web. For professional printing, I recommend 600 DPI minimum. Some publishers even ask for 1200.

    With a black and white drawing, you\u2019re usually looking for a few things in the final result:
    -\tYour thinnest black lines should be black and not gray
    -\tYour densest black areas should be all black
    -\tThe white area of the page should be clean and white
    For a drawing with tones, like a pencil drawing, you want your darkest spot to be pure black, your lightest spot to be pure white, and everything else to be shades of grey.

    For a black and white line drawing, your best friends are brightness/contrast and balance/curves. Balance/curves works better, but brightness/contrast are more common tools and work just fine.

    Using brightness/contrast:
    First increase the brightness as far as I dare to wipe out some of the imperfections in the white areas. Then increase the contrast as far as you dare to make the demarcation between black and white crisp. Then go over the blackest areas in an editing program with a black brush. Manually edit any black or white areas that need special attention using a black and white paintbrush or pencil tool.
    Using balance/curves:
    Balance/curves lets you determine what counts as black and white in your final. It works better than using brightness and contrast. Often this tool has little black and white eyedropper icons on the buttons. First you\u2019re going to select a spot in your drawing as your black sample (black eyedropper tool). Pick a spot that looks gray in your scan but should be black and select it. Then, using the white eyedropper, find a spot that\u2019s light gray but should be white and select it. You may also be able to fine tune the curves manually. Manually edit any black or white areas that need special attention using a black and white paintbrush or pencil tool.

    If your drawing program has a burn tool, this all gets easier. Get the main black and white areas to look right using the tools above. Then use the burn tool on the thinnest lines (or even the whole drawing), to make the black really pop out before you do any manual editing.

    There are few gotchas to watch for:
    -\tHand-drawn black lines actually have a thin halo of gray progressing to white. This makes your lines look alive and natural. If the halo becomes to white, our lines can look stark and pixelated. If it becomes to black, your lines look fuzzy and unhappy. Aim for the middle.
    -\tBlack areas always have some white and grey specks in them. Don\u2019t ruin the rest of your drawing trying to get rid of them. Instead, fix them using a simple editing program.
    -\tDitto for the gray specks that show up in white areas.
    -\tYou might want to clean your scanner and monitor surfaces before you start and keep your paper clean.

    Drawings with shades of gray are trickier, but these techniques should still work. Color artwork is much harder. If anyone has tips, I would appreciate it! It never comes out right for me. Photography might be a better route for color work, depending on how subtle the color is and where you\u2019re using it.

    I hope that helps!

    Posted By: GrahamBrad, when you say "trace" it with Inkscape, how does that work? Is that a drawing tool within Inkscape or something? Does tracing the drawing take away the handdrawn look?
    \nThe trace function in Inkscape (and Illustrator for that matter) takes a digital image and tries to find the lines and shapes in it, converting it from a pixel-by-pixel representation to a set of curves which are stroked and/or filled. The parameters you supply to the trace function affect the detail that will be preserved (including "sketchiness") -- I sometimes use this to deliberately smooth sketchy work so I can start with coarse pencils and effectively do the inking with the trace. The essence of the hand-drawn style will remain to lesser or greater degree depending on these parameters.

    \nAs others have mentioned before: GIMP, another good one is Inkscape (a free SVG program). The advantage of SVG is that you can scale your maps up and down in size without ending up messing with individual pixels. If you have the money, a Bamboo Pen & Touch might be a good option as well, you can trace your maps onto the computer without having to go through the headache of scanning and tweaking to get a good print out.

    Other variants I have seen for maps is a customized Excel spreadsheet where the columns and rows had been configured into squares and then could be coloured in or marked in Nethack style for indicating different things. Open Office's Calc might be able to do this, you might also look at Open Office's Draw application, as well as some of the FOSS vector drawing applications. They are usually free, and often someone will have made a Windows binary available for use. If you use a *nix OS then you're laughing, since you can compile them easily enough if a binary isn't available for your flavour.

    For global maps do a search for the GURPS Space planet record sheet. You might find it useful as well. Final question, would you want a hex overlay or a grid?
