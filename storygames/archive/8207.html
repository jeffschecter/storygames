\n While I rather loathe them in the theater when used for action sequences, like in say Quantum of Solace (QoS), I'm beginning to wonder how you could create them in a roleplaying game. Its not really something that I'm sitting here to myself saying: "Hot damn! Lets get some of this in games!" Its a case of being plagued by the question of how you could accomplish the same feel. My impression of watching the fast cut jumble of scenes in the aforementioned QoS is that an element of confusion and focus on single moments of the action would work, but I don't have any clear ideas on how you could accomplish this with rules or mechanics.
\n
\nDamn my own curiosity! *thinks*
\nAny ideas?

    \n Well, what's conveyed in those shots is not enough information. In action scenes, say fights or car chases, players would only be able to use three word sentences. Grammar wouldn't matter at all. I'd be strict about this, going back and forth. Maybe allowing a player to spend some type of resource to say two three word sentences in a row or a six word sentence, you know, zooming in on something a bit longer.

    Here's a rough example:

    GM: You (James Bond) is driving down the road in his shiny new Aston Martin along the beach in the south of France. Suddenly Jason Bourne, driving a mini, hits him from behind!

    GM: I'll talk for Jason, and you be James.
    "ACTION!"

    PC: James spins car
    GM: Jason grinds gears
    PC: Gun from pocket
    GM: Get's Sophie's purse
    PC: Fires at windshield
    GM: Jason, Sophie duck
    PC: Car switches lane
    GM: Group of tourists
    PC: Looks down road
    GM Tourists crossing Road
    PC: Slams on brake
    GM: Jason not surprised
    PC: Bond behind Jason
    GM Controlled spin hands wheel sophie screaming (Spent resource here)

    Anyway, it goes on. As I was writing that, the three word structure actually helps a lot to inspire action. In other words, I had fun doing that. It might work.

    \n I think it could work without all the sentence fragments, and without inforcing it from above so much.

    I often time use a player cut, rather than a other device for quicking action, it feels fast paced because it is much faster paced. In an action scene when other players can cut , it cuts on length and tedious explanations/actions. Of course their is a fair ammount of back and forth, because of this rapid switching it feels exciting.

    that's what i use in my game.

    \n A Player Cut, When a player can cut into or finish a scene. generally accomplished by the player yelling cut and then starting the new and shiny or just calling it a rap as required.

    Blahblahblah magic item haggling, Cut, this is boring lets go to the next scene

    Bluhbluhbluhdiplomaticevent, Cut, while player2 is doing that I'm going to corner the boss's henchmen to provide support, blah blah blah Cut while they do that I'm going to sneak into the otherwise abandoned office, as both the boss and the henchmen is busy and bust out his safe ....

    and so on
