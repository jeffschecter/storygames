\n Based on what I've seen done with PBEM games so far (and based on the notion that no Player should be posting a third time if not all Players have responded once):

1. scenes with four or more PCs in them age badly, lose energy and cause the group to forget what they were doing a few rounds in.
2. require an etiquette guide to get coherent and fun play
3. never quite hold everyone's interest

So my thinking came around to GM traditions and posting media being the limiting factor.

Group scenes should cease being tactical, technical or meandering and start being strategic (summaries and drama and negotiations.)
I wondered if the crowd here would throw down with ideas that could change the dynamic a bit (like those business meetings that no one looks forward to, you know, the ones where less gets done the more people are present?)

I propose (these notions grabbed from business):
1. Every PC should arrive at a group scene with an agenda they need to accomplish. When complete, they should OOC the GM their agenda is done.
2. Every PC should look to deliver their agenda, then draw off other PCs to a small sidebar conversation if more specifics are needed.
3. Every Player should draw up some tidbits, rumors and "impressions" that PCs observing their PC closely might gain from the 'summary mode'. This makes group scenes layered and interesting, though brief and full of many new questions.

Admittedly, greater minds than I have tried to make this dynamic work.
What have you folks seen done to good effect?

    \n I've been a PBEM player for something like a decade, and nowadays I find myself slowly but inevitably shifting towards PBP games (play-by-post, boards/forums based games, or whatever you call 'em). It was unexpectedly easy to migrate from email/netmail (yes, we started in FidoNet when Earth was young) to something more organised: all the good things are still there, yet you get much more transparency and dynamics. The posts tend to be a bit shorter than those emails, but they don't have to be (an random real life example here - not that you can read it, but you can see the size).

    How about this: one of the games I am running right now has 10 (ten) players in it. They have not yet separated so in your words we only have scenes involving ten PCs and Lord don't make me count how many NPCs in them. Two more are getting their characters ready to join in. Maybe you should try it when you have a chance: it works for me, it might work for you.

    As for your proposed solution, it looks good and steady, yet I doubt it'll work for me because stating my character's agenda explicitly and solidly is the best way to ruin my game (learnt the hard way). Perhaps someone less of an immersionist would give you a different opinion.
