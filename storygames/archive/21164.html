\n Looking for some Fate Core experts. I'm at the point where I like some of the concepts of Fate, but the execution leaves me high and dry. The four actions really rub me the wrong way. They're an immersion-shattering experience that routinely ends up with conversations like:

Player: "I'm going to embarrass the Countess by sneering at her attire."

GM: "Are you trying to Create An Advantage to put an Aspect like Embarrassed on her, or are you Attacking her to take her out?"

Player: "Uhhh..."

Maybe it's from playing with people who don't "grok" Fate. Maybe I don't "grok" Fate. That's why I'm wondering how possible it would be to play with only Overcome actions, as with Burning Wheel's first 74 pages.

You'd have to alter the stress and consequences systems for this to work, of course.

    \n It can work perfectly well.
    I've built my own game system, FateLess, around such a thing.
    I only have Overcome and Create Advantage actions.
    There are no Attack or Defense actions, and no Challenges / Contests / Combats.

    You do not CHOOSE between the type of action.
    You describe your action and then LOOK at the fiction: if it's an action that could solve the obstacle/problem then and there it is an Overcome, otherwise it is a Create Advantage.
    Most of the times this is immediately obvious.
    The other times you are free to choose.

    It makes for a much faster, smoother and narrative game flow, which plays well with the rest of the gmLess structure and tweaks (no Stress, only Consequences, for example).

    The overall effect I could notice in all my games is that even though mechanically it is rare to see a PC "taken out", the players tend to play more to the fiction, to see threats as more real and threatening, and act accordingly.

    I've also noticed that mechanically the "back and forth" feeling of a prolonged conflict shifts away from the actual dice rolls (which are way less than in Fate) and focuses on the Aspect Invocations.

    \n You are right, but then i feel like the problem that VSK presents here did not originate from the presence of the CA action, but exactly from the mindset required by the Golden Rule.

    Decide what you\u2019re trying to accomplish first, then consult the rules to help you do it.

    This is the specular opposite of FateLess, where you first describe, then check which rule fits.

    Both approaches can work. But Fate requires much more system mastery, to pause and think about what the meaning of your PC's actions is, then to look up which rule helps you achieve that, then to translate your (now clear) intention into a fitting description.

    This is what disrupts VSK's game flow, as far as i can understand, making all CA actions (and Defend, and Attack) unwieldy and cumbersome.

    \u201cI want to pick up that rusty Corolla and wave it around threateningly\u201d

    If you instead play fiction first and only have Overcome and Advantage actions, the choice is dead easy: is waving the Corolla able to, on its own, to solve the problem?
    Yes = Overcome
    No = Advantage

    No mechanical mastery needed\u200b.
    And later, when you DO naturally acquire confidence with the rules, then you can (but again you don't need to) easily and quickly think: "I want an Advantage, so I will describe this instead of that".

    \nWe are saying the same thing
    Let me rephrase for clarity.
    Player describes an action of her PC.
    The question one asks is: is the described action capable, potentially, to overome the problem?

    Example...
    In a fist fight on a sandy beach, you want to physically overcome your opponent.
    Throwing kicks and punches could, if successful, achieve your goal. If you win you end up with a beaten down opponent.
    Throwing a handful of sand in his eyes will not, by itself, achieve your goal. If you win you end up with an opponent still standing and hostile, with sand in his eyes. Which, in and of itself, could be useful if you find a way to leverage it.

    This is what I meant. One action OBVIOUSLY has the potential to achieve your goal. The other OBVIOUSLY does not. You, as a player, choose nothing. You are not allowed to. The description is already done, and now you simply assess what is OBVIOUSLY what.

    What is OBVIOUS, though, changes depending on your circumstances and your INTENT, your goal for the action.
    Which is a different element of this equation.
    One example that for me is very important:\n
    2097 said:


    "I\u2019m going to embarrass the Countess by sneering at her attire.\u201d is first describing.

    \nTo me this is not just describing. And in my very personal game experience this is not what I've seen happen in the vast majority of occasions. Usually the "description" ends up being just this:
    "I sneer at the Countess attire"

    WHY are you doing that is often not stated openly, and even not clear in the player's head. There is no underlying strategy, no real aim... it's just roleplaying, a reaction to a fictional situation.
    Which leads to the following step:\n
    2097 said:


    \u201cAre you trying to Create An Advantage to put an Aspect like Embarrassed on her, or are you Attacking her to take her out?\u201d

    \nThis, as you also mentioned, leads to the classic "Uhhh..."
    Because by presenting the game flow in this fashion, the Fate system offers to the Player a "tactical" choice that might contradict or simply not represent what he had in mind when he made the original description.
    Now you have to ponder: why did I made such description? what could I get out of it? what SHOULD I get out of it? can I make the mechanical effect X or Y fit into it?

    In the Countess example you question the meaning of the action, and then the meaning of the meaning, and then the meaning of the meaning of the meaning XD
    1) Why do you sneer at her dress? --> to embarass her.
    2) Why do you embarass her? --> to either A=defeat her or to B=create an advantage
    3) What is most convenient/effective/fitting choice between A and B? --> Uhhh...

    It probably sounds worse than it actually is. Sorry for the dramatic representation. But it does highlight how/why so many players end up in the Uhhh situation.

    Now, this might look similar to how FateLess works on the surface. But I guarantee you that the difference is not trivial. FateLess used the same flow as Fate, in its early development stages, and encountered the same problem. But since the mechanic changed the problem almost vanished (tested on new groups that never played FateLess before, not on veterand that have internalized the system workings).

    Maybe it is just a difference in how the mechanics present the same concept, if you choose to look at it that way, but if so it surely is a very small detail with a darn big impact on the gameplay results :P
