    \n I'd say that--excepting the inconsistency in layout (easy to fix if you make a template, rather than layout "freeform")--these are fine designs. White-on-black italics is going to be tough to print well, but it's possible. Also, if you want that double-line border on the whites, then put it on the blacks. Basically, get a consistent format for all cards, and just swap out black for white (and vice versa) for the background coloration.

    You could try to put the die in the lower-left corner (or the dot in the lower-right) to make both visible in a fan spread, but I kind of like how the title and numbers are all visible in a stacked spread.

    Resolution is simple; set the art at the resolution which takes full advantage of the press's lines per inch (lpi). Or just overkill it and go for 1200 or 2400 dpi. Read about lip here: http://dx.sheridan.com/advisor/line_screen.html

    \n Unless you're short on hard drive space or memory when a file is opened, "bloat" is a non-issue, these days. And no matter how chunky your graphics, they still have edges (the mosaic tiles) which are rasterized and, as such, will pixellate if set at a low resolution and sent to print on a higher resolution machine or web press. Finally, unless you embed the text as text (vector art) not as a graphic (raster) and deliver to print as something which maintains that vector art (PDF, PNG, EPS) then every little stroke and curve will pixellate, too. This is particularly an issue with white-on-black text.

    1200 dpi is a good median between "perfect" 2400 resolution and "amateur" 600 dpi home printing.

    \n Ryan, something else to consider while you're still entertaining the form of the cards is: all of these solids are going to suck your home printer dry. You may want to produce an alternate version for your personal/immediate printing needs - while keeping the ink heavy originals for commercial printing. A simple gray dot halftone instead of black would ease up your ink demand, as would going to clear cells for the background information on your images.
