Blue Rose: inches from awesome

Clinton
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Story Games

Joshua BishopRoby asked me in another thread to explain why Blue Rose is great, so I'll give my thoughts on it.

The biggest problem with this game is organization. The really good reward system is hidden in the page before the skill lists. This is just plain dumb. Here's how it goes:

When you make a character, you choose a destiny and a "light nature" and "twilight nature", which are really "how does my character act as his or her best?" and "how does my character act at his or her worst?" When you use either nature to pursue your destiny, you get "Conviction." Conviction is like d20 PCP - for a moment, you can lift cars. You get use any feat for one round, or not die when you should, or bring down righteous skill rolls when you need to. It's good stuff.

This is not complex, but it's the core of narrativist roleplaying reward systems: tempt and reward. The GM's job with this system is to present two options whenever possible: (1) the really hard path to go forth morally on and be good and achieve your destiny, the path that will break you, probably and (2) the pretty easy path to go forth on, do some questionable stuff, and achieve your destiny. The question is "do the means justify the ends?"

This is, seriously, the core of almost every narrativist role-playing game. Sorcerer: "what will you do for power?" That's a specific application. The Riddle of Steel: "What is worth fighting and dying for?" Also a specific application.

So that's why Blue Rose is awesome to me. It's really easy-to-use narrativist role-playing.

Why is it not awesome? The setting is nerfed. There literally can be no internal conflict to fight hard to upload the ideals of Aldea against those who would change it, or make sacrifices to preserve other liberties or whatever. All the threats are external, really, and that's boring as heck to me. It is, however, easily fixed.

  • MRUnderwood
    August 2006
    I'm a big fan of Blue Rose for this as well as other reasons. The Light/Twilight Nature and Conviction systems, in my opinion, simultaneously model the genre conventions of romantic fantasy in presenting both selfless and selfish impulses for characters and by stating, through Conviction, that characters can achieve most anything if they dedicate themselves, displaying a capacity for Simulationist genre emulation as well.

    The Conviction mechanic is one that I place in the realm of 'dramatic currency' systems, such as Stunting in Exalted, Dramatic Editing in Adventure!, Mutants and Masterminds, Drama Dice in 7th Sea, and others.

    I say this because I presented a paper on this topic last year at the American Folklore Society, titled "Can I Get Change For That Plot Twist?: Dramatic Currency for Communal Narrative Shaping in Role-Playing Games" and dramatic currency systems are some of my favorite bits in many recent RPGs, as they allow for a change in the balance of narrative control/agency within a role-playing game, exploring the territory between a Iron-Clad Ruling GM and a GM-less game like Universalis, Capes, or Polaris.

    Getting back to Blue Rose, I'd agree mostly on the setting. It's built very shiny, with the Unenlightened Fundamentalists on one side and the Unmistakably Evil on another. I think Aldea needs to be in more desperate straits from an internal standpoint with petty infighting ala Exalted's Scarlet Dynasty, that or the antagonists need to be less unmistakably *wrong* from the perspective of the written material. (Though to be honest, I only read the Blue Rose main book and haven't more than skimmed the World of Aldea or Blue Rose Companion).

Andy and Underwood, I think you're oversimplifying the utopian nature of Aldea; it's a utopia, sure, but it's beset from all sides and struggles against corruption from within. (In fact that's my largest annoyance with the game: it should have made Knights of the Rose the default character concept and just run with it, rather than set up this big elaborate setting with one giant and obvious PC slot and let you "choose.")
