\n Paizo has announced it's new adventure path Carrion Crown which harkens back to the days of Ravenloft.

From the Paizo release:

Carrion Crown draws its inspiration from the storied pages of gothic horror literature and film. From the tradition of Dracula and Frankenstein's monster, werewolves and zombies, undead wizards and cosmic horror, we\u2019ve devised a campaign fraught with lurking dread and nefarious villains, crafted to test characters\u2019 bravery, wits, and skill. Your characters will want to stand fast against the terrors they encounter rather than flee at the sight of the first ghoul or ghost. The PCs aren\u2019t being set up as dupes strung along in a series of unsettling events, but rather as heroes thrown against terrors their resolve alone might best. And this campaign can be as dark as you want it to be: if you like a bit of extra moodiness in your game, there are plenty of opportunities to make extra props, turn down the lights, and do all you can to unnerve your players.

Edit: Wanted to add, you can get the Players Guide for free if you are registered at Paizo's site.

    \n marking a shift from specific devises to general ones can only be healthy for all types of gaming: particularly small gaming and indie development.\n

    This year, the amount of DSs and PSPs may be at an all-time low as people are simply playing games on their mobile phones or tablets. We no longer have to carry a dedicated gaming device to play a first-person shooter, real-time strategy game, or even a dungeon crawler, and many of us are moving on.

    ...These games can be both casual or hardcore, they usually cost under $10, and they play on a device that you're already packing.

    \n On happiness and game design...\n
    Monaco, which was initially his diversion, became a much better presence in his life. \u201cI made sure to work on one cool thing every day,\u201d he said. \u201cOne thing that made it happy, one thing that was awesome, and made the game better. I made sure I worked on one cool thing per day, and I made sure the game was better every day after I was done.\u201d

    \u201cI got much further because I was enjoying myself,\u201d he says. In terms of making the game better, one of the best things you can do is to \u201chave people playing your game from like day two,\u201d says Schatz.

    \u201cThere\u2019s two types of people you should have play your games, first is your advisors, and you can\u2019t have too many of those because you\u2019ll get conflicting information. For me very early on Dan Paladin from The Behemoth helped out,\u201d he said. \u201cHe kept the game from being more cerebral, which is what I tend to do, and made it more arcade and snappier.\u201d

    \u201cThe other kind you want is people who don\u2019t know shit about games,\u201d he joked. \u201cYou don\u2019t want their advice necessarily, but what you do want is their impressions. Their experience with the game, and their impressions are always right.\u201d Schatz asks them three questions: \u201cWhat did you like, what did you not like, and what confused you.\u201d Those things are always right every time, he says.
    \nI'll make the last game when i die (via Gamasutra)

    "A free online game that simulates health-research projects in a virtual world aims to give players a taste of what it is like to be a scientist. In Power of Research (www.powerofresearch.eu), launched on 23 February by Biolution, a life-sciences consultancy in Vienna, players imitate the life of a researcher by choosing an institute and research topic, preparing for experiments, applying for funding, seeking staff, publishing results, attending conferences and collaborating with others. They need to be good at negotiation and time management. As players improve, they can win awards or become an institute leader. The game was developed with a euro617,000 (US$849,000) grant from the European Commission (EC), and is based on actual institutes and projects in the EC's research funding programme."

      \n So I've been watching the awesome folks at Pinball Publishing for a long time. They're an eco-friendly print shop based in Portland and do really amazing work with 100% recycled materials and plant-based inks. A year ago, they launched a line called "scoutbooks," blank or lined mini-notebooks with custom covers, inspired -- as far as I can tell -- by Boy Scout merit badge pamphlets. Recently, they've upgraded the options and allow you to order bad-ass looking 32-page 3.5x5" saddle-stitched booklets with chipboard covers and custom interiors through their amazingly simple website. With 1-color covers, they end up being $2-3 a piece, and it's between $2-4 for two color covers, depending on how many you order. In my mind, these are PERFECT way to print short indie games in a way that looks nice and professional. I've been planning all along to use them to print Geiger Counter and Super Suit, and thought about hoarding this secret away so I could be the first to do this, but decided it was better to share.

      If anybody does get games printed through them, please let me know because I'm dying to find out how they turn out.

      Posted By: J. WaltonPaizo isworking on a boxed set version of Pathfinder. The meme / business model continues to spread.
      \nIt's the best way to get featured in the toy section of Barnes & Nobles or Target.

      Paizo has been brilliant with Pathfinder so far. The major drawback was Pathfinder caters to older D&D fans who fondly remember D&D 3E. There may not be a staggering number of people entering the hobby, but most new players aren't playing Pathfinder. They are playing D&D 4E.

      But with an intro Pathfinder Box set aimed at evolving what the new D&D Red Box started, they hope to make Pathfinder appealing to new players.
