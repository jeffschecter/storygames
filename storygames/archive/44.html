Ninegun Choir: Graphical Character Sheets

shreyas
January 2006 edited January 2006 in Story Games


So I was watching some CLAMP anime or other and someone cast a spell and you know one of those glowing pop-occult designs swirled around their feet, and I was like, "That there is a cool way to represent data." So I came up with this.

The characters are like Final Fantasy occult gun-girls, or gun-bishounen, with nineguns surrounded by spinning haloes and butterfly knives with red and cobalt fire... urban/Squaresoft/steampunk kids with a Biblical veneer. They've been fired from their jobs in Heaven and to get them back, they freelance for the angels.

But the character sheet is the important thing here - it doesn't need to be all prettified, but it's surprisingly compact and informative.

In the centre, your signature. When other character sheets refer to you, this is the symbol they use.

Around it, a celestial sphere: the Sun, the Moon, a Zodiac, perhaps the pentacle of Man or the cross of Earth. Each of these comes with a package of occult powers that helps you do your job - the numbered circles inside.

Farther out still, your battle map. Somehow, this reflects the way you behave - in a conflict, you move along the straight lines, each node providing you with a special ability. A node like the Mercury symbol, connected to one of your occult powers, is a trap or an instinct; the power activates whenever the conflict moves to that node.

In the outermost circle are your allies and enemies; if you must, you can flee from a conflict and foist it off onto one of their battle maps by following a curve.

Now I just need a system.

    Gun-angels are trying to get into the good graces of the Host, and thus employed for better jobs, by doing "dirty" jobs that the more exalted prefer to avoid - fighting the Adversary, reshaping Creation in the image of Heaven, and administering tests of faith.

    In this age, it's not considered appropriate for angels to come down from on high with declarations and swords of fire, so the tests the gun-angels perform involve arranging situations that tempt men to sin, or that permit sinners to change their tune, and judge their actions. In service of this, the gun-angels have powers, that assist them in examining less numinous properties of people, and that allow them to rapidly and unnoticeably rearrange their relationships.

    Because of the indirectness of this method, there is no definition for what constitutes a 'faithful' reaction; the gun-angels must also be the judges.

    Heaven isn't very attentive, so all it knows are whether you administered a test at all, your verdict, and whether it had excessive negative consequences.

    Since this is a very competitive field, gun-angels form rivalries, and often work to undermine another's tests, preventing the situation from occurring or setting circumstances around it that cause it to and in a mess.

    Yeah, but I'd like to see something more coordinated, where the players have some control over where and how this happens.

    Here's a quick system sketch which is totally non-functional on the sheet as it stands, but which is maybe suggestive of the type of thing that you could do.

    A player has three different dice on his sheet: d4, d6, d8. Each of them stands for something (Monkey Kung-Fu, Qi, Positioning. whatever).

    Each turn the player rolls 2d6 and uses the total to move and change one die.
    That die has to increase at least one, and move 'forward' at least one, but otherwise you can split the total in any way so long as it is all used. If the die goes past it's maximum it starts counting from the beginning (eg on a d6, 5+3=2). Each place on the sheet can only hold one of your dice.

    Stuff happens when your dice end up in the same place another character's. High dice 'win', but the exact result depends on what you die is, what their die is, and where you are.

    Mix with wacky effects for positions and players supporting and opposing each other, and maybe places you ahve to defend, and you're away.

    It's random, but not too random: you trade control over what the die reads with where it ends up. The higher a die is this round, the more likely it will be low next time. The different die sizes have different characters (d4 is never very high, but you can crank it back up quickly).

    Is that interesting?
