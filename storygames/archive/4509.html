\n I'm wondering if what I'm talking about is as controversial here as it is in d20 circles - because I'm starting to think that I have brain damage with the responses I get to these ideas.

\nIt seems like everybody gets on the Open Content / Creative Commons bandwagon for rules, but then as soon as it's another kind of creative material, like a setting, everyone clams up and can't see any reason to do it.     \n I think there's room for licensing to be a big help in defining a project in terms of its approach. Here's what I mean:
    \n
    \nd20 Monster books are often a hit because you can flip through the pages and read one, then think about adding it to a campaign.
    \n
    \nThis isn't just about the art: The ideas are compartmentalized. Most gamers won't open up a monster book and say "Quaggoths?! You can't use them in my game, they were made for Forgotten Realms!" There might be notes relating to other points but there are two things going for it. The punch comes right away, there's basically no initial investment on the part of the reader (i.e. you don't have to read my two hundred page treatise on humanoid evolution in the underdark), and it's easy to think "Hey, I can use this in my game."
    \n
    \nTo a lesser extent the same holds for races. But I think this appeal breaks down with things like countries or knighthoods and so on specifically because they're usually presented as inseparable parts of an interelated web of reams and reams of pages of a setting. There's often poor division between elements at that level - one idea flows into the next and so on, which is great if you're already a fan but not as good for mining ideas.
    \n
    \nThis is where I think the license can play a role. If you made monster-manual style entries for things like cultures, plots, and so on, knowing specifically that you're creating Open Content, you're saying in the most explicit way possible "Hey, here's something you can plug into your campaign. There's a few connected ideas here but you can use what you like and ditch the rest."
    \n
    \nPutting the document up under open terms is a big sign that says "this stuff is remix-friendly" right from the start, and that's significantly different even from an established setting.

    \n I'm planning on releasing my game in two parts: the system part, CC-Attribution, and the setting part, CC-Attribution-Noncommercial.

    Why? Basically because I want to encourage people to release other settings for the system for sale, but don't really expect anyone to want to do supplemental material for the setting. I see the setting as the "supplement" part itself already. Why would you supplement a supplement?

    The other aspect is, in a backwards kind of way, that keeping the license for the setting material noncommercial actually makes it more open. What I mean is, the setting material defines the tropical and subtropical regions of a supercontinent in some detail, but leaves most of the rest of the very large landmass deliberately vague, so that if your group wants to go off to the north or south and define whatever the hell they like as being there, they can. I assume that other people will come up with cool ideas that I'd never think of in a million years. But if they publish those ideas commercially, they become quasi-official and people may no longer feel as free to make up their own stuff, which might be equally cool or cooler.

    Am I making any sense at all?

    \n Ryan, I think your idea makes a lot of sense. I wonder if one of the things holding this sort of project back is mutual fear. The setting's creator may be nervous about explicitly letting other folks play in his sandbox, either because their work will be lousy and detract from the setting's interest, or might even be better and therefore draw attention away from the originator's work. Conversely, another author might be unwilling to attach his ideas to "someone else's" setting. Fans do this all the time, but then they're fans.

    For the consumer, there's the question of canon. That's never meant much to me, but for a lot of people who buy setting books, this is a big deal. With an Open Source setting, by definition you don't have any sort of line editor to keep things consistent and avoid contradictions, and it's hard to avoid them without it. The Glorantha experience is instructive here: the setting can be pretty confusing to get your head around, even with a single authority who can rule things in and out of bounds by fiat.

    EDIT: I totally get what you mean about depriving a setting of coolness by mapping it too closely. In my opinion, that's what White Wolf has done to the Exalted setting, by clogging up the setting to the point that it's just not fun for me any more.

    Ryan, what is it you think people would be doing with this open content if it existed?
    \n(I responded to the other part separately because it was a separate idea)

    Ever had the experience of being inspired by something, getting a campaign idea, and then decided to make the project "yours" / "original" / "suitable for public consumption"? I've had that experience when coming up with campaigns and working on short fiction many times. I go "wow, it would be cool if there was an alternate universe where THESE guys were actually working for THOSE guys!" and then immediately "Well, it would be cool but I could never get the rights. Heck, I couldn't even share it online without the danger of a Palladium-like cease-and-desist notice. Ah well, maybe I can make up something with the same themes..." You end up blotting out the thing that inspired the whole project in the first place.

    Essentially, there's no straightforward answer to your question - because so much depends on what goes into the pool and how it's presented. If I take my 9000 page campaign setting from 1982 with details from the celestial alignment of constellations over millenia to the consistency of manticore droppings, I am adding nothing. No one wants to wade through that crap. But modular ideas, worked together in a digestible format? That's what would be fertile ground, IMO.

    Speaking for myself, if there was a bunch of open content out there I'd be trying to make it into the digestible format if I saw it. But what would people do with open content in a digestible format? That's what opens the door. Write a short story? Design a campaign and then make it into a product? Have another community project that builds on the first? Make a boardgame off the same themes? Convince a few dozen writers to put in 500 words each to get in on the cool community thing? Hard to say.

    2) A commercial user has no interest in using derivative material from a setting unless it would attract people. Greyhawk Rulez, OGL Continent You Never Heard of Droolz. Something like Dawning Star might be interesting but at most, I'd sell to a subset of Dawning Star fans. This is just like the situation with licensed media games, where they don't draw in fans to be gamers, but draw a chunk of gamers who happen to be fans.

    (The third, unspoken niche is the hobby publisher, who wants to charge money but doesn't have a rational economic motive. As Ryan knows from meeting me, I don't think much of that sort of thing, but that's a topic for another thread. Let's just say that the hobby could do with more no-strings attached free stuff.)

    Now if you're talking single orc clans and strongholds and families such, you *do* see that in OGL material from time to time, but most d20 gaming uses the site adventure model. I don't care about the orc clan's customs and motives in a general sense, but in terms of where they camp in the dungeons, their tactics and gear.

    \nThere's definitely a spectrum here: At one end, there's professional authors, and at the other there's fans.

    But a lot of us fall in between those: That's the amateur hobby writer that wants to produce professional-quality material. Some of these are the "and want to get paid for it, kinda" folks that clog your market - but I find most of the motivation in this group is the fact that they want to share their ideas with others and be respected for doing so.

    That makes licensing important in two ways:

    One, since we're talking about a spectrum of creative people, we'd want to choose a licensing scheme that both sides could engage. What I see as the big problem is the thousand or so GMs that are right now squirreling away ridiculous hours on their campaigns - identifying the good and bad parts is easy, and yet all this work is getting repeated a thousand times over. You're right that many hobbyists don't value their own time - but I do, and I know I'm not alone.

    The other is respect. Imagine you're being asked to write 500 words (a novel-sized page) for a collective project. There's a difference between writing 500 words for a Star Trek community project and one which you "own" and just as much as any contributor. The ability to say "Yes, you could write a novel about things in here if you wanted later" is important to those who think "Maybe one day I'll dip my toe in the industry." I want to encourage those people to participate in a commons, and frankly there's less respect when building on properties you don't have the rights to - that's the difference between "fandom" and "authorship".

    As for the Sci-Fi's Greatest Hits example, the chances of getting in trouble for it are proportional to its popularity. The more popular it is the more likely a company owning the IP will notice and issue a cease and desist letter. Yes, that's being a jerk, but we've seen companies do this to other kinds of projects and if you're going to make this kind of time investment that possibility is a definite dampener on your project.

    For a group project like you're talking about, a central authority providing cohesion would obviously be important. This happens in open source projects too - benevolent dictators that take the reins of a project and decide what's in scope and out of scope for the next development phase. That's an eminently solveable problem. But I think that the project needs to be framed in the right way from the start: Licensing is part of that framing process.

    \n Clarifying my own position:

    First, this whole thread and the discussion of Open Content has been an attempt to test the waters over here because I do have a project in mind and whenever I propose it on the other side of the mountains of madness I just get blank stares. So in a sense this whole thread is preamble, but since the good-natured fencing with Malcolm has naturally drawn me out here's the pitch.

    There are reasons*, at this stage, to do a project like this:

    Small in scope,
    Systemless,
    Containing single-page
    Interrelated entries
    With Distinctive flavor
    In a pre-agreed format and context,
    Containing Capital "S" Situations
    That can be explored in multiple game systems,
    Is filtered through a central authority,
    With veto power,
    Then edited extensively, and finally
    Released under the OGL listing it as 100% OGL material, then
    Released again at the same moment with a Creative Commons Attribution license,
    For free.

    I have something that can provide a core for this, and if necessary can provide more than half of the text itself. From the perspective of a completed project there would be a lot more to talk about. I'm saying that to intentionally draw a parallel to the difference between discussing Actual Play experiences and blithering on the internet about games that we've never played until we die of radiation poisoning from the discount cathode-ray-tube monitors we use to feed our everpresent addiction to strawman arguments and flamewars, which I'm told is what got this community off its collective ass and into serious design discussions in the first place.

      \n I've recently had this dilema with my adaptation of the Mongoose RuneQuest SRD, which is coming out as an OGL game called SimpleQuest.
      \n
      \nOrginally the rules were going to be OGL but the example setting & adventure were not. The setting was something I had already worked up for a commericial adventure, and I had conflicting emotional attachments about making it OGL as well. However I was painfully aware that making it non-ogl when the rest of the book was OGL made no sense.
      \n
      \nSo eventually I decided to stop messing about and remove that setting from the book, and put another setting in its place, Fantasy Greece, which is OGL as well. I've set certain boundaries on the amount I write for it, it is meant to be a short evocative example setting after all, and by OGLing I've allowed other start up publishers to use something that will strike a chord with gamers so hopefully the fun will expand from there.
