\n Hi!

I want to make a list of broad concepts that are intended to be paired. Assume that rival Gods own the concepts (because they do) in an Icelandic saga sort of setting. Ideally I'd like them to be punchy enough that any two can be paired to create custom Gods, but failing that, I want to identify the most juicy possible pairings. Here's an example:

Peace and Freedom

This is a great pair because they are in both opposition and concordance. There's a lot of story meat in there. Will we submit to draconian rule to ensure our safety? Will we fight a bloody war to be free? Some say you can't have both at once. The relationship between them (and their representative Gods) will always be tumultuous. Here are some other concepts:

Beauty
Clan / Community
Courage
Destiny
Faith
Hearth / Family
Honor
Justice
Knowledge
Love
Truth
Wisdom

What are most interesting matches? What concepts am I missing? What are unusual, but productive, pairings? What are dead-end, flat pairings? Can the list be pared or expanded? Thanks for your thoughts.

    \n Jason -- I love games like this! Here's how I'd slice them. Not sure how Norsely the slicing is, but the logic is pretty straightforward in each case:

    Beauty vs. Wisdom. The realm of appearances is shallow; to see beyond appearances is wisdom.
    Clan/Community vs. Truth. Social conventions are mere polite fictions; only by leaving them behind do we approach the truth.
    Courage vs. Love. It takes courage to overcome what we fear; it takes love to transform fear into something else.
    Destiny vs. Hearth/Family. The pull of hearth and home must be resisted in order to pursue one's grander destiny.
    Faith vs. Knowledge. One may believe, or one may know.
    Honor vs. Justice. Honor is sheer self-regard; justice requires that we regard the rights of others.

    \n Maybe some context will help.

    The game I'm working on represents the world as a hnefatafl board, and players actually play a game as the Gods, and each board position is a physical locale in the world that characters can visit. So one player is some PC dude traveling about, but also plays Lady Freedom on a meta-level - and if her piece occupies the place he visits, it's all gravy for him. But if Lord Peace owns the spot, that's trouble, or at least ill-starred. And whoever is ascendent (or if both are absent early in the game) or if a piece has recently been captured there - well, that informs what's going on in that location, along with other factors. So the Gods are literally playing a game with the mortal world, and you play that out, and the Big Ideas they are fighting about color everything.

    \n I don't know if this is helpful, but one way to tackle generating the opposing positions is to play "exploding the dualism" (my name for a linguistic thing developed by the semiotician Algirdas Greimas who I'm always on about).

    It works like this. You start with a key term--let's pick one from your list: Clan, say. Clan is now the "simple positive term," and we take it to mean kin, family, and (more broadly) community, although this introduces a little slippage or ambiguity, but we can live with that if the people whose discourse we're trying to map can.

    Next, you identify the simple negative term--the "opposite" of Clan (i.e., "not Clan"). If someone is not clan, they are what? What would our people say? Would they call someone from outside their community a Stranger, an Outsider, or what? Say we like Stranger: This establishes the first of two semantic oppositions: Clan/Stranger.

    You use the first opposition to generate the second. The "complex positive term" is equivalent to "both Clan and Stranger"; the "negative complex term" is "neither Clan nor Stranger." And then you seek in the language of the target group concepts corresponding to those ideas: Perhaps "Ally" works for the former, and "Ancient Enemy" for the latter.

    So now starting from a single word you've generated a set of complementary oppositions, with Clan <-> Stranger corresponding to Ally <--> Ancient Enemy. One interesting thing is that one can usually see some sort of patterned variation in connotation when you layer the pairs. So Clan/Ally on the one hand and Stranger/Ancient Enemy on the other seem to mark a distinction between friend and threat, while Clan/Stranger operates at the level of the hearth or campfire and Ally/Ancient Enemy at the level of the battlefield (peace and war, that is to say).

    But those are not the only meanings available at the level of the complex! The Clan <-> Stranger opposition generates other complexities given other contexts, which may happen to be the domains of the other gods (just like Ally/Ancient Enemy can be seen as the complexity emerging from the effect of the God of War on the Goddess of the Hearth).

    So you could do something like this (or train players to do something like this) for the meta-level game, where the interaction of the gods' spheres of influence had the effect of shading the situation at the level of mortals. So if the Goddess of the Hearth intersects with the God of War, the situation involves the tension between what it means to be Clan or Ally (or between Stranger and Ancient Enemy). Whereas if the Goddess of the Hearth (Clan <-> Stranger) interacts with the Goddess of Beauty (Beauty <-> Ugliness), the situation involves the tension between, um, Familiarity and Strangeness.

    Have I been clear or opaque?

    Bill

    Greimas, Algirdas J. On Meaning. Trans. Paul J. Perron and Frank H. Collins. Theory and History of Literature. Minneapolis MN: University of Minnesota Press, 1987.
    Greimas, Algirdas J. Structural Semantics: An Attempt at a Method. Lincoln, NE: University of Nebraska Press, 1983.

    I, of course, rely on his interpreters and explainers...

    Budniakiewicz, Theresa. Fundamentals of Story Logic: Introduction to Greimassian Semiotics. Philadelphia, PA: John Benjamins, 1992.
    Schleifer, Ronald. A.J. Greimas and the Nature of Meaning: Linguistics, Semiotics, and Discourse Theory. Lincoln: University of Nebraska Press, 1987.

    ...and I've found it useful to look at places where his work has been applied:

    Jameson, Fredric. "After Armageddon: Character Systems in Dr. Bloodmoney." Science Fiction Studies, 1975. Vol. 2.

    In that context, let me (modestly) mention my own forthcoming piece:

    White, William J. "'Structuralist Alchemy' in Kim Stanley Robinson's Red Mars." Extrapolation, 2007. Vol 48.

    But if I had to pick one item from this list to go with, I'd probably pick Schleifer as the best introduction to and overview of Greimas.
