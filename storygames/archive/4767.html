\n One thing I really liked in Various Burning Games are their X-Burners. Not really the character burner,
but all these World Burners and Perilous Adventure burners. Which are really not much more than
bunch of oddly relevant sets of questions (except the character burner, but let's forget that for a little).
And DitV had it's town creation which made prepping great fun.

And to admit, using these within my gaming group has always been at least as fun as actually playing
the rest of the campaigns.

So my next silly Idea: Contribute-a-Burner-a-thon! Ie. Make a simple set (say about 5) of Relevant questions that
bring out a awesome from some idea, and name it like X - Burner and post it here.

My feeble starter:

The Lost Soul - Burner
.. Darkness lifts and you wake up remembering nothing.
* What do you see around yourself? Are you cold? Where are your clothes?
* Does it hurt? Are there any weird scars?
* Do you feel hunted? Afraid? Do you have a foreboding sense of doom?
* What is your contact point to the world? A memory? Some item? Photograph? Name scribbled on dirty paper? What?
* What next?

    \n I never got further than this point on my idea for a soldiers-in-Iraq game, but here are the questions I wanted to ask during character creation:

    1) Why are "We" in this war?
    2) Why are "They" in this war?
    3) Why am I in this war?

    My example character was going to be a hardened kid from the streets of Chicago who joined the Gangster Disciples and who was told to join the marines to learn urban warfare. That was his #3.

    Posted By: NobleBill White -- you're on the right path but let's not for get that a "burner" is a series of question meant to lead toward maximizing story potential. It's not just a series of random charts generating a profile.
    \nIf you say so. But I've seen the "world burner" in Burning Empires, and it looks to me like a series of options from which the group selects in order to determine the parameters of the setting. Don't get me wrong; it's still a series of questions, but they're multiple-choice rather than open-ended. So think of the tables I presented as just another way of asking questions: "What are the surface conditions like on this world? The inhabitants? The government?"

    If that were all, then my response would fail on the grounds that it doesn't drive toward story potential, which seems to be your complaint. But it's clear that you can't stop with the closed-set responses to the prompting questions: you have to create the little narrative description that tells you what that combination of responses means. At that point you've got a world you can land on.

    In short: don't let the format fool you. A "random" set of tables is as much a "burner" as anything else--if you're committed to making sense of the results as hooks toward story.

    \n The Burning Wheel game I ran at DexCon 10 had a Burner for it. I setup the game with the idea that the PC had may a deal with an evil power and it was pushing the limits of the deal. The PC's here leaders of a Village, it was their responsibly to fix the situation.

    At the table I asked:
    What is the evil monster?
    What was the deal?
    What does it hunger for?
    And here is a kicker the king will be here soon. Why is he coming?

    Mel White was nice enough to make a recording of it.
