\n How many copies of your game (you specifically) would you need to sell to break even at Gencon?

Assuming you tried to keep your expenses as low as possible. Shared booth, hotel, travel...

I've been going to Gencon or Origins for over a decade for pure pleasure, not profit. So I'm used to treating my expenses at Gencon as vacation money. I'm completely divorced from the realities of breaking even at Gencon. That being said, my understanding is that post Gencon, having a Gencon presence can boost sales just from the exposure Gencon gave you. So recouping costs may not be limited to the time spent at Gencon itself.

    \n For me, breaking even on Gen Con sales alone isn't a possibility under the current model. My sales were as good as I could have hoped. That covers essentially booth, flight, hotel and a little bit. That's nothing to sneeze at, but it doesn't touch printing prices. Once I've sold out my first print run (knock on wood), then my full trip and printing costs will be covered. That said, Gen Con exposure is proving to be pretty essential for achieving that goal.

    My goals are undoubtedly different from the goals of other people. My plans include turning games into a mildly profitable business, but it's meeting and hanging out with lots of great people, gaming, talking, and sharing ideas that really makes it worthwhile.

    \n It's hard to break even within the local four days of the convention. Not impossible (I've done it in past years ... didn't come even vaguely close this year) but hard.
    \n
    \nThe thing is, though, that the effects of GenCon are not limited to the four days of the convention.
    \n
    \n(a) Many games get a sales spike on-line after the convention, as people follow up on their exposure
    \n
    \n(b) GenCon is among the greatest hot-beds of development know-how ... I, for one, feel that I've gotten my full entry price and more based on how far I've advanced my next design during the convention and shortly thereafter.
    \n
    \nAll of which may very well be my rationalization of the simple fact that I want to sell at GenCon, and so I pretend to myself that it makes a world of business sense. I'll cop to that possibility too.

    \n The math's not that complicated, John. Say it costs at least $500 to be at GenCon, between hotel, booth, food, travel etc. If your book costs $20, that means you need to sell 25 copies to pay for the con, and probably several more to cover the print costs of your game. So 30 copies is about the minimum for breaking even, I think. Many games sell few copies than that, in the teens, and only a true "con success" sells 50 or more these days. Breaking even is difficult, but not impossible, especially if you have a new, anticipated product. Without either a new product or a highly anticipated one, it's very rare that people break even.

    \n Now, I'll grant, but we haven't attended Gen Con as an exhibitor so our numbers are somewhat dated.
    \n
    \nBack then a full booth would cost about $950 or so rather than $1600 or so now. Our magical break even, accounting for total trip expenses and cost of goods sold was around $2,400. We would usually achieve that, but not much more. These days, with price increases on booths, hotels, etc, I would guess that number to be about $3,500 at least.
    \n
    \nThe thing is, you also have to value your time. Attending Gen Con as an exhibitor as also a time sink. You've got several days before the con that you are preparing product to ship to the con, preparing booth signage, preparing support materials for gaming events, etc. You've got booth set up day and then 4 days of convention and at the least the day you come back to unpack and unwind. Counting pre time, that is at least 8 days done. 8 days that could be used to do something else for your business. Once you factor in your time, the amount of sales you need to make essentially doubles. For us, we ultimately decided the investment in exibiting had become not worth it and those 8 days could yield more profitable results if worked in other ways. Though I'm pretty sure, at some point, we will return to exhibit at Gen Con to see if we can generate the types of sales that will make it worthwhile year after year.

    \n For Bully Pulpit, we'd have to sell roughly 110 books to break even on all of our costs (not including personal costs, like food). That's roughly leveling the price of our various books and considering printing costs and IPR fees. Our actual sales were about half that. However, we generally think of our goals for Gen Con as a mix of sales, marketing, and reward for our work, so I'd consider our sales levels a success.

    Put another way, we're not likely to ever sell enough games at the Con to justify the expense of hotel, flights, badges, booth buy-in, etc. for the three of us, but our presence there does a lot for our marketing efforts (demos, meeting people, etc.). In addition, it's one of the few ways in which the business is profitable to us individually- sales of our games throughout the year help pay for us to be there, allowing us to meet new friends and play games.

    If we wanted to increase our profit, we'd probably only have one of us attend, if we came at all. We considered that for this year, but I'm really glad we changed our mind, because I had a lot of fun and it was good to have all three of us together in the same place.

    \n I sold 12 copies of Dread: The First Book of Pandemonium via IPR, and 4 copies to the Finnish Contingent (for fifty bucks). But since I spent a total of $285, and the Finns paid fifty up front, that leaves $235. 12 copies of Dread is $300, so if I get, say two hundred bucks from IPR, that's close enough to breaking even for me.
