\nCarolina Death Crawl is now available for purchase!

This is a weird, spooky Southern Gothic competitive roleplaying card game. If that does not fill you with excitement I simply don't know what's wrong with you. Get somebody to check your pulse, maybe you are dead - maybe you are a swamp ghost!

The rules are a free download and you can choose either a do-it-yourself PDF of the cards or a really nice print-on-demand poker deck, which is what I strongly recommend. The custom printed cards are beautiful and functional and come in a nice jewel case. The DIY file has the same elements in an easy to produce format and is a great choice for international customers wishing to avoid murderous shipping charges.

Here's an article about the history of Potter's raid, the historical event that forms the core of the game.

This is a unique game and I'd love to tell you more about it if you have questions.

    \n No, yeah, winning is a side effect but there is definitely a winner - exactly one character survives. You accrue cards that have fictional prompts on them and when you introduce those prompts into the game you get points for it. Only you know how many initially. At the end of each act everybody reveals their totals and the character of the player with the fewest points dies. So there's a tiny bit of strategy (do I hoard this prompt for act three? Have I introduced enough to be safe?) but the numbers are set up to deliver ugly surprises. "Playing to win" means introducing tons of cool stuff into the game and then suffering a horrible death later on. And death makes you a swamp ghost, which is another sort of winning.

    Oh! And the prompts are scaled, so the more it is going to change the arc of play, the more points it is worth. Character-based prompts favor the lowest-ranking soldiers. A simple bit of color is probably worth one point. "A gnarled cypress tree, and the woman you just hung from it" is worth five.

    Can you tell I love this game?

    \n More seriously, the really important piece of CDC that you don't want to lose in a hack is that the characters are not strangers in the hostile territory they must traverse to reach safety. It was, at one time, their home.

    So if you want fantasy, don't make it "elves returning to Elfheim through the land of the orcs" - make it "Orc mercenaries in the pay of the elves returning to Elfheim through their ancestral homeland."

    Making them complete outsiders is a little facile. Destroy, Disgrace and Kill will be too easy and it will degenerate into an orgy of ugliness.

    \n Finally got a chance to give this a go! I ordered the POD cards and found them to be of nice quality.
    Our fourth player revealed himself to be a cowardly deserter, stating that the source material didn't appeal to him (he then went on to lurk around in the background, helping the swamp ghost and playing NPCs when he saw how much fun we were having).
    We went with an action-adventure tone, though Southern Gothic elements crept in. Our protagonists were men of low character, and while some may have spoken of having strong moral worth, did precious little to portray it.
    Private Henry Comfort, a murderous skirmisher who did not bat an eye when circumstances led to him use school-children as a cover for an ambush on Nethercutt's Rangers. Taken out into the swamps and murdered by his Corporal, when he tried to help himself to aforementioned Corporal's loot.
    Corporal Isaac Maybank, a man of good position who saw the raid as a prime opportunity to collect some monies to aid his financial woes. Arrogant and happy to sacrifice his fellows, he drowned whilst crossing the Neuse River when he discovered his saddlebags of loot had been replaced by saddlebags of bricks.
    Bugler Pembroke Dunham, an oafish scoundrel who proved a continual disgrace to his uniform. Dogbit, snakebit and delerious, he made it to New Bern with no companions and some fine future prospects on account of all that loot he found in Maybank's saddlebags.

    Loads of fun. We were a bit shy on dropping the prompts in at the start but got into it a bit more as we went along. Looking foward to playing again.

    \n I played CDC on Google Hangouts yesterday afternoon with my RPGGeek.com buddies, one American facilitating the game for three Europeans! I wrote about the game here, but the most interesting bits:

    Our very first scene saw us raiding a house for clothes \u2013 it turned out to be Maybeck\u2019s house, and through a disastrous series of events his mother was killed by the Bugler. My corporal refused to stay long enough for Maybeck to bury his mother and the tone was set. I think almost the entire story flowed out of that one scene. At the end of the act, my corporal gave coverfire so the group could escape, only to get shot in the back by Maybeck who whispered \u201cNo grave for you\u201d.

    As a ghost, I got to mess with different scenes and add unearthly elements. My favorite bit was after they had left 300 freed slaves looking for help to their fate - in fact, they\u2019d even killed their leader to get them to leave! After that, they went into a church, where I narrated that they heard a negro spiritual faintly reverbing through the church and then sang fragments of it in a spooky voice (I don't know any negro spirituals off heart, so I went for Only remembered for what we have done, which is actually a WWI song, but whatever)

    That was fun to do, and gave a very creepy result. It was also fun private messaging the players will all kinds of guilty and negative thoughts and acts. I alluded to horrible things they did (\u201cHow can you come back here after what you did to her\u2026 she was just a girl!) and how they deserved death and were going to hell. Never gets old!

    On the third act, Jonna joined me for the ghost team (her character committed suicide) and together we rained down misery on the remaining two characters. In the end they had to crawl over a field of corpses to get to their fortress. One got shot by their own troops, and one made it to life at least another day.

    ***

    Wow, what a game! It\u2019s depressing, tense, exciting and definitely badwrongfun. I was a little depressed in the first act because I couldn\u2019t score any points, but it turned out being a ghost was great.

    I was also curious how far we would go, would our characters actually be raping and torturing? As it turns out we didn\u2019t, but that didn\u2019t make the game any less excting. Plus, it gave me leeway to hint at much further things as a ghost and push some boundaries.

    It\u2019s very interesting. It was still fun, but not funny. Something many other grim games (CoC, Warhammer 40k) don\u2019t manage. I was very impressed!

    I actually felt a bit... I don't know, not depressed but physically impressed by the game. A bit shell-shocked. Perhaps it was because I'd been standing as a ghost and was still recovering from a heavy cold, but it left me in a light daze the night and the day after. Powerful stuff.

    ***

    Making CDC suitable for online play was not that hard. Our facilitator Andrea had made a full set of cards for Roll20, the ghosts worked with private whispers, and when there were two ghosts the two remaining players took off their heardphones so we could coordinate. As a ghost I even replaced my webcam so I could play standing up! The only thing lost was the seating order and cushiness.

    Andrea doesn't want to make her Roll20 card setup public (it's the only component that is sold, after all), but is happy to share it with people who can present proof-of-purchase of CDC.
