\n Over in another thread about improv and gaming, Emily asked me this:\n
Paul's experience with starting a long-term soap opera sounded complex and well-structured. Will you systematically use specific techniques in the ongoing play, Paul? Or the workshopping?
\nI've been asked about this before, so I'm going to use this thread to answer specific questions about my experiences with the form.

The Setup: I do improv with Ventura Area TheatreSports. Earlier this year, we did a workshop with Joseph Limbaugh on Improvised Soap Opera. This is form he picked up at Dad's Garage Theatre in Atlanta and now directs at the Acme Comedy Theatre in LA. I like to call it "PTA without cards," though that's not quite accurate. It works like this: For some number of weeks (in our case, eight; for the Acme it's sixteen), you improvise a a forty-five minute episode of a soap opera each week. You have the same actors playing the same characters each episode (though often with cast members shuffling in and out as their schedules permit). Before the start of the run, you decide on the setting and some basic details of the cast (mostly names and relationships). Then, during each episode, at the end of each scene, the Director will frame the next one during the blackout. Something like "Tulsa confronts Silas about his drinking problem," or "Rooster discovers something unspeakable in the basement." When the lights come up, you play the scene. Repeat ad nauseum and end on a cliffhanger. During the final episode, resolve stuff.

Questions so far? Emily, I'll get to your specific questions once we've all gotten to the same page.

--Paul

    \n So, back to Emily's original questions.

    One of the challenges we have with this form as far as techniques go is that we have to unlearn what we have learned. As a primarily short-form oriented troupe, we tend to get in and get the job done as quickly as we can. The problem is, that's counter to the spirit of the genre we're trying to emulate. Soap operas move incredibly slowly. So, we have to fight our tendency to want to resolve things and in general slow things down.

    We also try not to introduce more details than we need to. Unless we need to know right now that my character's father fought in WWI, it shouldn't get mentioned. Too many details leads to an over-constrained mass that we can't all keep track of.

    The most important technique that we're working on (and this is related to the "don't resolve" stuff issue about) is listening for REALLY BIG OFFERS. Making them is important too, but it's vital that we don't move too quickly and miss things like this. If say to do someone, "I'd do anything to stop drinking," we both have to give that space. The audience should mentally be able to fill in the Dramatic Chord (as we're trying to do in our heads). And then we need to apply Dogs-style escalation to that promise. ("Anything? Well, then. . .")

    As far as workshopping the characters goes, we started by creating a relationship map and then playing short scenes to further define the nature of those relationships. For me, that work as all been with the goal of figuring out what my character wants from the other characters. That way, when I end up on stage with them during performances, I know what to make scenes about and how to push the action along.

    Does that answer your questions?

    --Paul

    \n And I completely forgot something that ties in with the discussion about status we're having over on Graham's blog.

    In a Soap Opera, the villains always win (until the last episode). If you want to play a sympathetic character, you have to lose all the time. The audience will always be on the side of the low-status characters, but in order to keep them on your side, you have to keep losing. This, as it turns out, is hard. My tendency in short-form is to play characters who start out low status but win in end, bring the high-status character low. In the Soap Opera, I'm playing a very low status character who has some rather undesirable character traits. However, as long as I keep losing, the audience will be on my side.

    \n Yep. And, well, it's quite simply far less terrifying than being required to produce output at a "realistic" pace. I'm personally pretty comfortable LARPing, for instance, but would probably get fop sweat (I have at times) if I had to produce in front of an audience. Much less to be expected to do it well.
    \n
    \nImprov, it seems to me, demands that you act at least passably. RPGs, in my experience, are rarely ever about acting well. It's not about if you portray the character in a believable fashion where we may actually associate you with the character, it's more about chosing the decisions that the character makes. Which is why, in many cases, it's fine to do this in third person narrative. The Tabletop RPG player is more akin, in most cases, to being an author of a book writing out the actions of the character, than being an actor. That is, it's more important that we relay what the character said than to ensure that the delivery was somehow done by the player in such a way as the other players find the player to seem to be like the character.
    \n
    \nWhich is cool, because it then allows us to play such a wild array of things successfully in this context.
    \n
    \nOf course, I'm biased when I think of my IRC game, because it is, in fact, written, and not acted at all. The players literally are, in fact, authors, and not actors.
    \n
    \nI think that this is important to understand: people equate first person acting with "role-playing." But that's usually juxtaposed with "combat" or "system play," if you will. But there's this third activity which is making and relaying decisions about what the character does, etc, that aren't precisely what you'd call "role-playing," nor are they noodling around with mechanics (though they may be informed by mechanics). This "narrating," for lack of a better term - it often involves things like negotiation or personal explanation, and other things than you'd consider narration - is just as important to play as the other two categories of activities. Except in cases where the group eschews it, such as these actually exist.
    \n
    \n(On a side note, there are lots of groups who claim that "role-playing" is their sumum bonum, meaning that they want all first-person portrayal. The claim is often that this is for immersion's sake or something. I think that often, however, what is occuring is a reaction to metagaming of the sort that they don't like. The point being that they may be throwing out good metagaming/narration techniques, just as a reaction to bad ones. Yah, Beeg Horseshoe.)
    \n
    \nUnless you intend to eschew this other activity, it's important to consider in your techniques and such that you might want to incorporate from improv or elsewhere, how they interact with this sort of play activity.
