\n I was reading about the interface of a cheeseburger, and came across this little gem.\n
Once you get to be market leader with a typically complicated interface, you actually have a good shot at keeping your users, as they\xe2\u20ac\u2122re that traumatized that they wouldn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t want to go through another painful learning process.
\nI thought back on my attempts to sell a friend of mine on anything that was not-D&D/d20, much less many discussions I've had with people about reluctance to play more than one RPG, and... bingo. It also made me think about some stuff I've read on Chris Chinn's defunct blog about dysfunctional groups clinging to their heavily house-ruled systems and tenuous group dynamic. I.e., the default assumption that it's always going to be that way, so why start the ordeal over again?

    \n "Upgrading" to D&D3 from AD&D 2nd ed. is not a bit less expensive in terms of study and money than trying out almost any other roleplaying game. However, the vast majority of people who stuck to AD&D because they were doing fine with that system and didn't see the point in buying or studying new books (*) has promptly switched to D&D3 and then to D&D 3.5.

    I don't think that it's really the overhead needed to learn new system mechanics which keeps people sticking to D&D. It's more a matter of being affectionate to the brand.


    (*) which would be a perfectly sound motivation, if only it was true.

    \n I feel like something Simon C. said here definitely applies:
    Posted By: Simon CI think Ryan's right that there's a perceptual problem with roleplaying games that makes people balk at getting into them. But I think that problem is shared by most gamers. We think of our hobby as this unique, cultish activity, where recruits must be initiated and indoctrinated before they can be trusted to play. That's simply not the case. We've got to stop thinking of our hobby as this unique snowflake that must be protected, and start thinking of it as just another way to have fun. Everyone likes fun (except Ryan Dancy).
    \nThe geeky cultish attachment definitely promotes conservatism.

    -- Alex
