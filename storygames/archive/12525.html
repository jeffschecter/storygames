\n D&D 4e has significantly removed the need for a healer in the party with everyone having access to healing surges and death saves, Guild Wars 2 in the MMO front is doing the same with removing the need for DPS, healer or tank roles, and having a similar mechanic to healing surges and death saves. 3e even went a bit in that direction allowing clerics to swap out prepared spells for heals. All basically an admission that people don't like playing healers.

The question I have is why? Medical dramas like ER are quite popular, hell MASH was too, and plenty of people want to be doctors so they can help people. It's a fantasy that plenty of people have. Also, in AD&D 2e, the Cleric was pretty much the most powerful class, up until 8th level or so (and a lot more gaming happened in those 8 levels than the remaining 12). Apparently nobody wanted to be the cleric though. Evidently, there's something wrong with the idea of being a healer in an RPG, and I can't figure out what it is.

    Posted By: PeterBBYou're playing a non-combat character in a combat-based game.
    \nWe could refine that statement a bit more, because there are certainly people who like playing non-combat characters in combat-based games.

    I suspect people don't like playing healers because healers are not an interesting non-combat character: where another non-combat type might be able to go off and do other things (a thief sneaking away to grab the loot during the fight, a diplomat going back to rally the villagers to rise up against the invaders, whatever), a healer has to stick around and...heal damage. Which is not a complicated game action, and does not call for creativity or innovation or anything more than being able to do some math (don't waste a cure serious when a cure medium will do).

    Healing is, in many games, a passive role. Other people go and do things, and then come to you to get more hit points so they can go back out and do more things. And yet many games treat it as an active role: the healer has to be there and make the roll or cast the spell for the healing to occur, which means that the healer can't be out doing other things at the same time. That kinda sucks, and I can't blame anyone for not wanting to take on that role in a game when they could be playing a character who has more freedom.


    And yeah, clerics in D&D were stupid powerful, but I think the reason why 3e let good-aligned clerics switch any spell for a healing spell on the fly was so that they could actually have a chance of using their other powers instead of just racking nothing but healing spells all the time, every time. I know back in AD&D2e, groups I played in had a firm expectation that a cleric would be providing healing, and if a cleric's player memorized other spells instead, it was considered a HUGE faux pas.

    \nI think your premise is secure for many rpgs and for 2nd and 3rd edition D&D, but not for D&D 4E at all!

    A party of say, three Strikers, a Defender, and a Leader (e.g. a Cleric) is significantly more powerful and resistant than a similar party with no such healer. With the exception of Second Wind actions, which you can only do once per encounter, 4E Healing Surges simply replace healing utility magic like healing potions and wands of cure light wounds that were always available between combats in previous editions. For real in-combat resilience you definitely want a Leader. And as far as death saves, if you want to just roll the dice on death saves w/o someone to assist you with curative powers, well, I'd guess either you're not as attached to your characters as I sometimes become, or your 4E DMs have been wearing kid-gloves more often than than any of mine. (or than I do when I run it)

    Further, the role name of "Leader" in 4E is not a simple re-branding of the classic healer role from previous editions. 2nd & 3rd edition D&D Clerics were exceptionally powerful to tempt people to play them because they were generally pretty dull to play, this because they're best spell and action resources in most situations was simply to heal someone - very passive. In 4E, because of minor actions (they can heal as a minor action and still do other things on their turn) and because many of the healing Powers are more aggressive and interesting than a vanilla heal, e.g. having to strike an enemy to heal your allies, 4E Leaders are much more interesting to play. None of the 4E groups I've played in for run for ever have had a sense of someone having to suck it up and take one for the team to play a healer. The most common party configuration has a pure Leader and a pseudo-Leader class as well like a Defender/Leader or Controller/Leader. And most of the encounters need every ounce of that healing ability....

    \n D&D4e combats tend to vary a lot in terms of how much you need healing, depending on the situation and the party's tactics. When things go well, you don't need to do much healing, and a Second Wind or two is enough to keep the party functioning. When things go badly, and especially when, say, a striker or controller runs off on their own, healing vastly improves the party's survivability. Although 4e does reduce the overall need for healing, unless you're really consistently effective you'll still want a leader of some description. And more importantly, 4e made it so that every leader character can heal *and* do other neat stuff. The base healing powers are all minor actions, and the special healing powers tend to have other interesting (and in not a few cases offensive) effects. For me playing a warlord in 4e is really rewarding, since I can get into the thick of things and beat up the bad guys, yet through healing and other helpful powers I get to help keep the entire party functioning.

    The thing about clerics in D&D is that their role in the game has changed and become muddied over time. As I understand it they were originally meant to be templars. They'd fight with armor and weapons not quite as good as the Fighting Man's, and then use a bunch of nifty non-combat holy magic to patch up the party, provide food, etc. between battles. But for most of the game's history clerics were the only healer/priest characters, and the notion that they were priests who inexplicably wear chainmail affected how they were designed in successive editions.

    \n Guild Wars 1 actually did a lot to make healers interesting, even in the face of healing itself not being very interesting: the game gave you lots of defensive spells that you could use to reduce damage before it came in, at a much better rate-of-return than healing damage afterward, if you could predict who was about to take a pounding and "pre-prot" them appropriately.

    This still caused a problem, though, because healing was fairly demanding (you have to keep track of the team's little red bars *and* watch out for incoming threats before they strike *and* defend yourself as the enemy's #1 target). Players spoke favorably of the tactics of playing a monk, but many found actually doing it to be a busy and frustrating experience. That and you're stuck getting the blame for the whole team's tactical shortcomings.
