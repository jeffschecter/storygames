\n Last week, to commemorate our current Castle Falkenstein campaign (in which there is a Texan Steampunk Inventor), as well as the finding of an excellent Texas-style Chili recipe, I decided to cook chili for the group to eat before the session started. We were going to sit down to eat chili and homemade bread at 7:00 while starting play.

7:00 rolled around, and I was still cubing the steak. 7:20 I had the ingredients ready to simmer, when I realized I misread the recipe, and that it required 2 hours to cook, not 20 minutes. So instead I put it in the pressure cooker for 20 mins to cook, then boiled it on high for a few mins to boil away the liquid and make it thick.

It was delicious. But it wasn't ready until about 2 hours later anyway because of the cooker, boiling and other random BS. By then, the session was as dead as a doornail. We talked about work, life, gaming and media for an hour before calling it a night and rescheduling to the following week (tonight, actually).

Anyone have tales of gaming failure?

-Andy

    \n I have a Classic Gaming Fail that I'm kinda ashamed to go into, but it's Wednesday and that's a Day of Healing, I hear.

    Descent, the board game. Three gamer men, two non-gamer women. Having been advised by husband of the wife to "just let her play the game, even if not optimal," I proceed to piss off the OTHER woman with advice. In my "defense," the wife was frequently either cheating or incapable of learning the rules "equip and declare your action at the start of the turn" and, thus, would randomly swap out equipment, start to plot out an Advance then actually shift it to a Battle with Fatigue-paid movement, etc.--she'd even, once, tried to change targets after a roll, seeing it to be a good roll wasted on a chump. Now, I won't say "cheating"--even when she "recovers three Health" by taking a 5-Health token and returning a 1-Health token to the pool (hey, subtraction is HARD in gaming!)--but I will say, "Damn, haven't you played this game, like, ten times?!? Learn Rule One, please, finally!"

    But I was advised to just roll with it and never mind if she got lost or made low-optimal plays. Yet, somehow, I couldn't remember to apply this to the other (very prickly) woman who'd only played once before. Now, she's not the world's most patient person, and she HAS only played once; but she (too, for some reason) couldn't seem to get her head around the four (woah, 4!) types of actions, nor that a Fatigue potion restores all Fatigue, but a Health potion only heals 3 Health (tough distinction, that one!).

    ANYWAY, in one clutch turn, she wants to Battle but spend a few Fatigue to get into range a bit more--well and god. She has 3 Fatigue. She spends one to drink a Fatigue potion (!) [that's the chess notion for "WTF kind of blunder is THAT shit?!?"] so that she can be at 5 Fatigue and take... 3 steps. I made the horrid, horrid (surely misogynistic!) comment of, "Wait, no, do it right--use up all but 1 Fatigue moving, THEN drink the potion to get it ALL back and keep moving on"--but she'd huffed and gotten up and fled to the bathroom (!) somewhere around the words, "all but 1."

    OK, smoke break; everyone just take five and chill. Right?

    Wrong. By the time the host and I had returned from taking five more minutes off our lives, she'd LEFT. Just, like, stormed out in a huff because I (or maybe anyone?) had presumed to show her an optimal way to use Fatigue... a technique which, in fact, is almost CRUCIAL to managing Descent resources, especially in tougher dungeons. [Frex, the grognards talk about "the range" of a hero all the time on the forums, and that range is, IIRC: 2 x Move + (2 x Fatigue -1).]

    ANYHOW... epic fail to teach, epic fail to help others be awesome, epic fail to have fun (though, in my defense, her storming out did more to dampen the fun than my advice to use Fatigue more efficiently).

    Lesson Learned: I won't advise ANYONE on a game--even in a cooperative one, when he or she is increasing the odds of us losing--unless asked. Nicely. Repeatedly. Better to lead by example and let him or her WANT to learn the nuances. For example, in that very game, I had a guy with two free movement and Cleave (attack again if a Melee attack causes an enemy death). On ONE turn, I managed to kill five enemies spread across a 3 x 5 area through VERY careful use of a Battle action, Fatigue, and my free movement--a group record. I will simply wait for someone to (a) observe the merits of learning the synergies of the rules or (b) ask me how to best-execute a given term.

    \n Wow, that's a harsh toke on the bong of gaming, David.
    when i'm teaching games with strategy components, i often ask before a new player begins their turn if they'd like advice during their turn, or if they just want to feel it out. that way there's not an immediate assumption that they're doing something "wrong". i've been bummed when i've NOT been taken under the wing of gamers who know the game better (and trounce me, because of)
    just saying "don't let one person who's obviously in past their comfort zone bar you from helping others-
    let her teach you better ways to ask if they want help."

    there are several games that i don't play to win, either; instead i play for the fun of the interaction.


    lots of my game sessions seem to turn up critical misses these days.

    i haven't played a solid session since the summer, in fact; last week was supposed to be my first week of my new game lab - i've got a long list of local folks who're vaguely interested in story games, i email them the details about the game to be played and they email back to reserve a spot. the spots filled up in a couple of hours, but when the golden day arrived, one email cancellation, two no-shows. the person who showed up was actually just dropping by the house to say hello and had forgotten about the gaming session (which, in fairness, he told me he would... goldfish-memory, we call him.)

    so, gf-m and i waited 45 minutes and then played a different, barely browned game which was surprisingly touching. not total fail, but it's hard to concentrate and enjoy oneself in the face of such staggering disappointment.

    The second game lab is tomorrow night. so far we've got one confirmed, one cancellation, and gf-m planning on stopping by.

    sigh.

    \n I ran Afraid at a convention. Because I'd described it in a horror-like way, lots of Call Of Cthulhu players signed up. In fact, the players were mostly members of the Kult of Keepers, an immensely experienced group of Cthulhu enthusiasts.

    We drank coffee instead and talked about things.

    That said, I cooked a Christmas dinner last night and still joined in the game at the end, so WIN.

    \n I remember the game session that made me decide to check out what the Forge had to say, and look into indie games, and, eventually, here.

    It was a GURPS Traveller game. The players were heavily into two aspects of gaming:

    1. No railroading. The characters were to be given free reign to do what they wanted. No "plot", no "villains", just seamless fiction. I think some people call this "sandbox" style.
    2. What happens in game is as close as possible to "what would really happen". If the game's not challenging enough, or too challenging, tough luck. It's more important that it's all logical and believable. The game system is the physics of the game world, etc.

    Generally, playing with them was fun. Just not very consistently. Well, this session was very much one of the less, uh, bad ones.

    The player characters are flying their small spacecraft around a system they're investigating. One of the planets in-system is described by the GM as a small, icy planet. Scanners report no interesting features: just a ball of ice.

    The players, of course, decide to investigate. The GM is all, "Are you sure? Like, there's not much down there, according to your sensors?" But the players really want to go look there (and there weren't really any better clues to what was on any of the other planets, anyway).

    We fly down to the iceball on a little lander craft, leaving the main ship in orbit, and roll to land. Critical failure; some failure chart reveals that the craft is crippled as it slams into the ice. Some vital part is damaged; the thing won't fly right now.

    So, there we are, twenty minutes into the session, crashed on an icy rock in the middle of nowhere (weeks of travel time from any other star system). Our craft is damaged, we don't have the means to repair it, and it is too damaged to take off again.

    "How much oxygen do we have?"

    About three hours, as had been established previously. Ironically, we had planned to play for another three hours. Well, we spent the next three hours doing everything we could think of. Sometimes the ideas warranted a roll (often with disastrous results); two of the PCs got into a brief fight. But no avail: there was no conceivable way to get off the planet.

    By the end of the session, tension between the players was at an all-time high, with the GM blaming the players for going there and not making some kind of back-up plan, and the players blaming the GM for a terrible waste of three and a half hours (as well as, potentially, the previous year and a half of the campaign we were playing). Everyone was livid by the end, and went home truly pissed off.

    These were intelligent, mature gamers in their 30s. I don`t know how they resolved the situation; I never played with them again (at least not in that campaign).

    Oddly enough, it doesn`t stand out in my mind as the worst GAME FAIL of all time. But that`s even worse in a way: I`m pretty sure none of them thought it was that unusual an event.

I'd played the game for a while, and in several different campaigns. In all that Alex played in, he played a sorcerer. He's always played them well, without being power-gamey or min-maxy. This campaign was no different. At the point in the campaign when the gaming fail occurred, I was pushing a sub-plot involving an old... acquaintance of my wife's (girlfriend at the time) character. I was attempting to enliven what was, to me, a boring series of sessions and to give her some spotlight time. For the latter, I've never been one to play favorites for my wife; Quite the opposite. If I don't make a point to focus on her sometimes, she often gets relegated to a supporting role. For the former, the players were attempting to set themselves up in the high society of a major city, when I was getting impatient to get back to the "adventure". So, my wife's character was a former assassin, who left her guild (and family) on bad terms. An old mentor of hers began poking around, making subtly threatening gestures and overtures. Then shit went south, in a quickfast hurry. Alex, seeing the assassin as nothing but a threat and side-track, managed to get a material link to him, and was thus able to cast a spell on him without line of sight. I did everything I could think of to stymie him, but his insistence began to get on my nerves. Finally, I blew up, and told him that no matter what he rolled, he'd fail.
      \n
      \nThis, obviously, didn't allow the game to continue smoothly. After calming down a bit, we discussed what had me so frustrated. Unless I broke the rules as I threatened, there was nothing I could do to keep him from rolling a few dice and blowing away a subplot that was intended to focus on another player. Rather than talk to him about it, I let it get to me to the point that things went sour. Adding to the frustration was my boredom with where the players were wanting to go, compared with where I wanted the story to go, and where I thought the players ALSO wanted to go.
      \n
      \nI think it can be honestly said that the incident killed our gaming group. I admitted to being a little burned out on GMing, and wanting to put the campaign on hiatus. This caused some resentment, because, contrary to what I perceived, everyone was really enjoying the game, except for me. They attempted to roll with the punch gamely enough. Alex continued to run games on alternate play days, and Zach even tried to run a game or two, but it wasn't but maybe another month of limping play sessions (some of which were still pretty fun) before life somehow interfered and we stopped playing entirely. We managed to get the group back together for one last game shortly before I rejoined the Army and moved to Kansas, but we've not played together since.
      \n
      \nThis is at least the second instance of me behaving badly that has caused problems in my gaming. It doesn't happen often, thankfully.

      \n About five years ago, my longtime gaming group found ourselves unable to play any of our regular campaigns due to a couple of key absences, and everyone was sitting around going "what to do, what to do?" I had just discovered the Forge and the Indie game scene, re4ad all the GNS essays, and purchased and read Primetime Adventures. Alluvasudden I had this vision of being the "brilliant Indie Design Guy" and Saving Gaming for the evening, so I said, "Hey, everyone! How about you all name one element you'd like to see in a game, and I'll try and whip up a quick game that we can play this evening?"

      Everyone said "sure, I guess" and I ended up with a game element list of: Spaceships, Wolves, Zombies, the skintight power suits from the manga/anime Gantz, and...porn.

      I looked at the list and thought, "what the fuck am I gonna do with these?" but my hands were tied, right? They gave their answers and I figured I had to accept them in good faith and do my best with them. So I retired to another room with a notebook for about 20 minutes, and you know what? I made a game. I made the best damn game I could in 20 minutes using those elements in earnest. The premise was that a massive colony ship in deep space had suffered system failures in many sectors, causing them to "go wild" and become the domain of feral beasts and escaped bio-experiments (wolves and zombies). There was a pocket of civilization in one sector which lived in decadent opulence, and an elite class performed orgiastic entertainments for the masses AND went out to the wild to keep the monsters at bay in skintight powersuits, in return getting to live out their lives in a lush pleasure-den. I made some rudimentary combat rules and some monster stats, and of course every character had an "Issue." It was supposed to evoke the pressures of soap-opera drama in a high-crisis, lethal lifestyle a la Strikeforce: Morituri or X-statix (but before X-Statix existed), while keeping the "porn" angle strictly in the background.

      Well, we didn't play. While I was designing the rules, one regular GM took a player into yet another room for a solo scene "just for a few minutes." I wanted to hold off starting until they returned, because of COURSE a thematic, focused Indie design requires total player buy-in and participation. We waited. As the solo scene drew out longer and longer, I got impatient and knocked on the door, asking if they were almost done. They were surprised that I was waiting for them, and said I should just start without them. I said I kinda needed everyone on board to start off on the same page. The GM said that she didn't really want to play in a game with a "porn" theme anyway, and the other guy said that yeah, he didn't really feel like playing. Frustrated, and with playtime rapidly waning, I left them to their devices.

      So I resolved to get started. The remaining three players heard my pitch. A couple started making up characters, but the one who suggested porn was like, "eh, I don't wanna play, it sounds too involved." The Gantz suit guy made a busty nymphomaniac with a "Chesty LaRue" or "Busty St. Claire" sorta name, who had an issue with men. The Zombie guy couldn't think of a character concept. I tried to help him, but nothing was coming together. By the time we were "ready" with one (joke) character, one player stuck for ideas, and a guy sitting in the corner palpably draining the room's creative energy with deliberate nonparticipation...it was too late. Like literally late in the evening, and me with no drive to continue. I just settled back and let the jokes about boobs, latex suits and lube continue to fly, and when the other two emerged from the other room (after 1, 1/12 hours, if I recall), they were surprised that we weren't playing. I went home utterly defeated and depressed, feeling rather icky about my friends' willingness to back me up.



      It taught me a lot about A) the disastrous effects of overenthuisiastic idealism, pinning all one's hopes on THIS THING going EXACTLY RIGHT, and The pure poison of uneven investment in the room, particularly a lack of reciprocity. The effort I was willing to expend to facilitate a good time for everyone was NOT equal to the effort they were willing to expend to support that. I didn't REALLY learn the lesson until 4 years later, when I finally left the group to find players who WERE creatively invested and reciprocal, but this was the start of that realization.

      Peace,
      -Joel
