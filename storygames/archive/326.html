    Dave, I'm going to be really rude and talk about Ben like he's not reading this, too.

    Ben does this thing where he says that everybody uses Conflict Resolution because everybody resolves conflicts, which can be a little confusing to folks who don't know what conflict resolution is in isolation from the other bits of a game. The most confusing part is that, technically speaking, Ben's more or less right.

    However, when most folks say "Conflict Resolution" they're talking about a set of explicit rules that governs the resolution of conflicts. Generally speaking we're talking about setting stakes and then players doing something (whether it's rolling, bidding, spending, whatever) to work towards that goal, and occasionally other players doing other things to frustrate the progress towards that goal. The stuff that the players are doing have a specific and non-interpretted (or only slightly interpretted) effect that increases the likelihood that the stakes are fulfilled. It's not the players doing stuff and the GM deciding if they did enough to get what they want; it's they players doing stuff and accruing points or working down the GM's dice pool or something that the GM can't just gloss over. Everything is out in the open.

    By contrast, the conflict resolution that "everybody uses" may be obfuscated, non-transparent, and determined by highly subjective judgment calls. Everybody uses conflict resolution, sure -- not everybody uses straightforward, transparent, and relatively fair conflict resolution. Most explicit rules for CR attempt to make things straightforward, transparent, and fair.

    So my "sell" for Conflict Resolution? Hey check it out, these rules are straightforward, transparent, and fair!

    Joshua, an important distinction between conflict and task resolution in practice, which may or may not break the generalized claims you've just made (summarizing Ben), is that conflict resolution entails a shared understanding of stakes. That is, fully realized, conflict resolution is a way of enforcing the meaning of any given outcome. If you wrestle me to the ground and I get up again afterward, task resolution says nothing about whether you "beat me". Conflict resolution is like you forcing me to say "uncle", thus symbolically creating a shared understanding.

    In short the very concept of conflict resolution as a universal property of RPGs depends on the assumption that the participants defer to each other in identifying "conflicts" and naming their outcomes. But task resolution operates on a far less social level--as I wrote elsewhere, it works on the level of facts, not ideas. (Yes, everything that goes on in an RPG is really an idea, not a fact. But that just goes back to issues of aesthetics. Rembrandt's The Night Watch didn't turn into "an abstract arrangement of paint on a canvas" after Piet Mondrian started painting.) Task resolution is thus far more conducive--and vulnerable--to the subjective/individual level of interpretation than conflict resolution.

    If anyone wants to look at this as more of a sliding scale than a strict breakdown, that's fine, too, as long as the distinction is preserved. I do think that many so-called conflict resolution systems take on task-resolution-like properties if viewed from a higher scale. E.g., if "Do we capture the pirate ship?" isn't linked mechanically to "Do we find the treasure?", then the overall goal of the adventure is still subject to freeform connection of events. By contrast, My Life With Master has very strongly enforced semantic linkages from one resolution to the next, all leading ulimately to Endgame.

    Perhaps my "what if you care" phrasing was too stark, or too subtle. The distinction I'm making is that what makes something task-resolution-y is that its significance is a matter of personal opinion or comprehension. (Significance in terms of "what it all means", not "does it mean anything at all?")

    Take the usual spiel about getting info from a safe. The issue isn't whether I crack open the safe or not. What's important is "do I get the info?"

    Sure. But then, what good does the info do me, if my ultimate goal is to capture the villain? Without a precise, transparent mechanical effect that shows I'm now one square closer to my goal, the significance of "getting the info" is deferred; it may be helping me, or it may just be a nice bit of color.

    In both D&D and Dogs, the strategizing is made possible because the conflict resolution is built on a foundation of atomic "moves". If the significance of all possible moves in either game's conflict resolution system were perfectly transparent, then they could all be calculated in advance yielding either an optimal result for the participants, a set of fixed probabilities based on optimal moves, or the determination that the outcome is really just a product of social negotiation.

    It's the lack of certainty in the relationship between atomic moves and larger goals that I'm calling task resolution. It may come about either because of the lack of a mechanical linkage enforcing significance, or because the linkage is complex enough to prevent certainty about significance. MLwM offers extremely transparent, mechanical linkages between events and therefore I reckon it has about as much strategic depth as tic tac toe. But that's not the point of the game, so it's not a problem.

    Fred, I'd like to draw your attention to the idea put forward by others that CR is about what the player wants as distinct from the character. If your goal is to erase the player/character distinction, then CR of the "say yes or roll dice" variety either implies antagonism (healthy for gamist play) or some sort of weird universe where my desire to climb Mt. Everest is sufficient to get me to the top (poison for varieties of immersion and/or "simulationist" play). The former because the GM is my opponent and will always call for dierolls; the latter because the GM doesn't really have a stake and will always give.

    (It might help to repeat what I said earlier, maybe in a different thread: I'm not sure task resolution has a place in story games. I'll amend that to "mechanical task resolution which isn't mechanically incorporated into conflict resolution".)
