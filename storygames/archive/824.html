I'm starting this topic hestantly, on the grounds that I want to post something like this in two or three _other_ discussions, and it doesn't really fit anywhere else. So. It seems to me that there are three[1] major vices of game designers (or at least those of us who think about game design.) I don't think I'm near the first to recognize them. As I see them, they are:

# **Theory Wankery** We're all quite prone to descend into "pure theory" which doesn't relate to anything in particular. But it's so much fun to toy with and argue about. Pity it doesn't get anything done. Not to say that no design theory is worthwhile; it's just very easy to find yourself floating.
# **Mechanical Noodling** Playing with dice probablities, the imagined or modelled fow of abstract game currency, even scene framing ideas. If they aren't going into a game (even just to keep them fresh until they're cannibalized) it's not productive.
# **Drawing Lines** Where does gamism leave off and narrativism pick up? When does a game go from being a board game to a role-playing game? When does sweet become salty? All are equally valid[2] questions. Certainly, we can define and talk about and contrast these qualities, but it's my belief that
while all of those concepts are real and useful, trying to find a hard line between any of them is a fools errand.

Did I miss any? (Making lists, right?)

fn1. At least!

fn2. In other words, not at all.

    Yeah Nate, I'd probably go as far to say that that's the only thing I'd consider a vice.

    Procrastination: Sure it's "bad", but fuck this is just a hobby anyway. So chatting, theorizing, thumb twiddling, or shelving it for months, while it may not get your game out, may be "bad" to the author but it's still acceptable.

    But designing without playing... I dunno, I can see designing hockey pucks without playing hockey, but I can't imagine buying a game from someone who doesn't play games.

    I thought that such a thing was extremely rare, but just the other day I read an interview with an independent RPG publisher who said (when asked what game's he's playing these days); "None; I don't have time to play games". (and no, it wasn't Jared!) That kinda... well... I dunno, just didn't sit right.

    The one thing that just about everyone on the indie side of the fence is guilty of at least partly is talking fondly about your upcoming game even though you're not really working on it in earnest. I dunno if that's a "sin" really, though, I think it's just enthusiasm.

    -Andy
