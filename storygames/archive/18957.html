\n Something came up in another thread, and I thought it would make an interesting discussion. The nature of most action dispute systems in gaming is such that you pass/fail an action based upon a skill, attribute, or pool of resources that your character (or you as a player) has. It came up that in a very specific genre that a interpretation of this is an uncaring or unfeeling universe that doesn't care how much or little you want something or what the implications of the failure/success mean; you just pass because you're fast enough or fail because you weren't.

There are a lot of systems out there that don't claim to be set in an uncaring or unsympathetic universe, though. Few games actually take into account that it is easier for a good character to pass because they have good karma or the favor of a good god more so than an evil one. I have seen this attempted a few times, but it hardly ever works out in an equal opportunity for both the good and evil characters, in my experience. Has this been yours?

Are they inevitably set in an uncaring world due to the black and white nature of rules, least the game system itself be unfair toward one side?

    \n Good deeds and bad deeds are the economy of a game I wrote up once. Everyone gets (basically) the IAWA stats and a few deeds to start the session/sequence/adventure/arc/whatever. So a "deed" is just like "for myself" and while you're holding onto that, you can spend it to do something significant for yourself.

    * If you do something without a deed being involved, that's cool, it's off-the-cuff.
    * If you do a good deed, you hand that deed to the person it helps, and they then have it to use later.
    * If you do a bad deed, you discard it forever.

    Good deeds always trump off-the-cuff deeds. Bad deeds always trump good deeds.

    It's not unfair exactly but it definitely enforces a particular narrative arc, in which the good guys get boned at first.

    \n I don't think I understand the premise here. Are we asking whether it is possible to have a game where good gods help good characters, without making the game mechanically biased towards good? Presumably this would be achieved without having equivalent evil gods as a counter-weight, and player characters may be good as well as evil, and the help of the good goods has some actual mechanical effect on play.

    I dunno, seems like either the requirements are inherently contradictory, or there are plenty of games that do this thing. I'm probably not understanding the issue.

    As for the thread title, I'm very much in for an "unfair" game, I suspect, although that'd again depend on what we understand by the term. Most of the games I play would be deemed deeply unfair by at least some other people, I suspect. Some games are unfair in that they treat PCs specially, either for or against them; others are unfair because they set players in no-win situations and demand them to make the best of it; yet others are unfair because some players do better than others, either randomly or by skill; and of course there are the games where a particular ideology, religion or civilization is disadvantaged against some other one. The list goes on.

    \n I think that when you've played exclusively in a certain mode (specifically, one where a character's ability to influence the world around them is the only way you can make sure you're an active participant), playing any game where your character might be a complete underdog sound incredibly scary.

    I remember experiencing this kind of culture of play many times, and certainly none of the people involved would ever have agreed to play a game like My Life with Master*. Enough time playing games where any lack of ability/power on the part of your character means that you can't be a meaningful participant (and either become a punching-bag or sit back and watch the others play while you can do nothing), and this becomes a very thoroughly entrenched mode of thinking about gaming.


    *: Although isn't MLwM actually ultimately rigged in favour of the protagonists, despite their crappy circumstances?
