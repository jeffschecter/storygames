\n I posted this monster over at RPG.net and thought I'd post it here too:

I had a blast as well.

I ran two sessions of Primetime Adventures, one of Sorcerer & Sword, played Grey Ranks, and Poison'd and Sci-Run, which are bothh in the Ash-can phase (meaning they're nice presentations of games that aren't done yet.)


Highlights:

I had a great time at everyone one of my games. Ian Noble facilitated the Grey Ranks games, and his delivery of the background material (the true story of the teen-age partisans in Poland trying to defeat the Nazis in WWII) really set an amazing tone that produced a really moving session. (I think, however, from what I heard, the next night's session was even cooler!)

The Shock game Judson Lester ran was a blast. I suspect he tweaked the rules a little, but all I can say is, although we didn't get to finish the story, the SciFi world we were building on the fly was becoming incredibly detailed and rich. I mean, we were making up a hard SF story on the fly. And all the SF elements were impacting the characters on a personal and plot level that sort of astounded me. We had humor and pathos and a real mess brewing. I loved it.

(From what I understand, we were more tightly focused on the improv elements than previous runs of the game. We had 5 players, 4 social issues and 2 SF Shocks. I think that by limiting the focus, the players were able to more easily stay FOCUSED and produced tighter results that went deeper with each scene.)


I loved my players in the Sorcerer & Sword game. Each of them had read it, but had never played before. The got completely into adding color and detail for the bonus dice. Scenes were growing tense and you could just see how the drama and stakes were going to be growing for each one of them. I had limited the attendance to 3 players and had to turn people away. But I'm glad I did this. It really let me focus appropriate attention on each player and let them, I think, dig into to the game, "get" how their input would be rewarded. My reaction was: This was like a perfect first session for a gaming group, we rock, and I can't wait to see where this goes over the next 5 or 8 sessions.


I loved the Sci-Run game Tom ran on the fly. Loved it. I can't speak to the mechanics: It's still an Ash-can, Tom had read the rules but ran it two minutes after I said, "Hey, can you run something?" and apparently it shares details with other games that I don't know.

I do know that we played a group of four people who wake up in these SF containers that washed up on the shore in Long Beach during a horrible storm. We have no memories, but we have special powers. The way the game works is that your character sheet is: "What do I see when I look in a mirror?" and four questions. The questions are like: "Why can I read other people's thoughts?" or "Why am I afraid of the color yellow?"

The framework of the story is that there are agents of a shadow organization after us. As we run, we roll dice and distribute them on a chart. The charts has boxes for "Escape" "Getting Hurt" "Memory" (or labels like that.) So you can choose to put better rolls in one box or another. If you put a good value in memory, the group helps you answer one of the questions on your character sheet. So, you're basically creating flashbacks that fill in the blanks for your character, the reason you have the powers, the agents that are after you. But, of course, you're also trying not to get killed or captured or have your sci powers that your slowly figuring out get out of control, so you never quite get what you want, and have to decide what you want most scene by scene. It was blast making up the answers to the questions.

    The first PtA game I ran was called "Cult of Personality" and was set in the compound of cult ruled by a woman who was the wife of the nominal cult leader. We established less of a TV series and more of a Showtime miniseries. It was CRAZY -- crazy, crazy fun. Everyone there had motives at different cross-purposes. One character who had all the right reasons to destroy the cult was using horrible cult brainwashing techniques to destroy the cult from the inside, another who only wanted respect was doing one horrible thing after another to get some love. It was a Jacobian blood bath, and as we closed on the last shot, one brain washed character was on his way to kill the cult's lawyer on behalf of the cult leader's wife, while a rogue cultist had brainwashed a completely screwed up kid and sent him to kill the wife herself.


    The second PtA session ended up being called "Last Rites." It was built around a kind of detective show, but set in an alternate time period. We started thinking about WW II england, then kicked around ancient rome, then thought about two detectives in a D&D fantasy world, and then settled on Oxford in the middle ages. (Let's be clear... The history part was just color -- no attempts at historical accuracy are implied.)

    My friend Eric, who is also a writer and just sold a TV show was there, as well as Matt, who only had played d20 before -- neither of them had never played [I]Primetime Adventures[/I] before.

    As the Producer (GM) I decided that I really wanted to focus on the character's Issues -- Issues being things that stumble the character up when he's trying to get things done. Matt didn't watch a lot of long form hour dramas (mostly sitcoms and shows like Futurama), but when I found out he loved [I]House[/I] I was all set, as I've watch enough of that show to take it apart and use pieces of it as examples for what we were needed to focus on for PtA. (For example, House's issue is his impatience with people -- it's the very quality that let's him do his job well, as he has no time for people lies -- but it also means he makes trouble for himself when trying to talk to people -- which he has to do for his job.)

    Eric created this old battle-loving knight, Lord Gregor, who was furious that after successfully waging war was sent by the king to become the "King's Justice" in Oxford instead of being sent off to a castle and continuing his love of war. Eric did this awesome job of describing this guy -- "All most like an ancient tree, he's so worn from battle, as he rides his horse toward the city walls."

    And Matt came up with Marcus, this engineering prodigy who usually pays more attention to his blue prints than his pretty girlfriend Jill. Marcus ends up pursuing a murderer when his Jill's brother -- a city guard -- is found brutally killed.

    I was really happy how we nailed the premise of the series in the first sequence. I framed an establishing shot of Lord Gregor riding with his retinue toward Oxford. He rides through the city -- not being a city guy at all, very disdainful. He gets to the large 'urban manor house' the king has bequeathed him. The servants are all running around, getting everything ready, then standing at attention in the front hall as Gregor enters. His Issue is Pride -- and he feels dissed by the king. He let's loose on the staff, telling them he's been slighted by the king and roaring his frustration at being removed from battle.

    I play the Conflict -- the king walks in behind him. Gregor, in the heat of the moment, lets loose his frustration at the king. After all he's done for him in war, how can he trap him in these city walls?

    The king takes him through a walk in the city: we do this montage sequence where the King shows him the streets -- the thieves, the homeless children, the wailing woman, the pickpocket, the men moving goods that look suspicious. The king says, "This city is at war. With itself. And I need my best general to drive this war to peace."

    Boom. A prideful general who doesn't want to be there is tasked with waging a war on crime in the city streets. Add in youthful and myopic Marcus who always sees the world as a mathematical problem to solve, and we're ready to go.

    We had all this stuff ready to go about the conflict between religion and the new, scientific point of view and stuff...

    Anyway... it was all great.

    At the end of our session Matt said, "I wish we could keep playing. I'd love to watch this show."

    And then I just got an email from Eric (who, again, just sold a pilot to CBS) and he said:

    I keep coming back to that pilot episode. What a truly engaging session that was. And after all our previous discussion that day, to have it culminate in an idea and characters that just practically sing "this is a TV show" -- well, damnit all, I wanna see that pilot now.

    I spent the weekend up making up strong stories on the fly with smart, funny creative people all weekend.

    Gateway rocked.

    CK

    \n Additional notes:

    I approached the con sessions I ran with this attitude: I'm not doing the full show. I may not get to a big ass climax. Instead, I'm going from beginning to end with character creation and then we're doing the game at a pace and style that is the pace and style I'd use for my friends. No rushing. No making sure to get to the last fight. No pre-gen characters for the Sorcerer game to get to the game sooner. We're playing the game (and I think in these games character creation is part of play), and that's it.

    The same held true for all the games I played in except Grey Ranks. In Grey Ranks we decided to play three chapters and we did it.

    This all seemed to work well. No one seemed frustrated at the end, and everyone only wanted to play more...

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarI'm extremely interested in how the Grey Ranks games went, obviously, both mechanically and thematically. Hopefully there will be some actual play from those.
    \nI didn't totally grok the mechanic that night, but I think we all got it enough to play. It seemed at least 2 of the 4 players had a damn good handle on it. BTW, in hindsight, I realize if I'd asked more questions, I would have understood the mechanic better and had even more fun. So, I can't give much insight mechanically. But it was still TOTALLY AWESOME!

    This is because, thematically it was amazing. For me, the personal scenes made it even more powerful. I'll share some of my favorite bits. I forget some of the character names (heh, but remember npc names, weird).

    In one scene, my character (16 year old male) was creeping up the stairs to the Nazi leisure werehouse, planning to knife the lone guard from behind. Most the other Nazi's were out enjoying a night at the theater. We were trying to break in and steal cigs and other goods to boost moral of the troops. I announced that I was so nervous, because I'd never killed a man, this close, with a knife. I'd only done killing at range with a rifle. We cut to a personal scene from years back - it was my first day on the job at the butchers shop. The butcher told me I had to kill the chickens. I did, but loathed it so much I came back the next day and said I really needed the job (and my family needed the money), but could no longer kill the chickens as it was too disturbing. The butcher drags him over to the chicken coop,forcefully puts a butcher knife in his hand, and while still holding on, stabs the knife into chicken after chicken, reminding him "this is your job", etc. I refused to kill any on my own, lost the conflict, and lost the job. Flash back to the mission, not wanting to fail, he drops the Nazi guard with stabs to the throat and kidneys.

    During the middle mission of the evening we rescued camp bound people locked in rail cars. At the end of the mission, one player detailed spotting his love, Helena (keep in mind, his symbol had to do with faith in Helena) sharing a cig with a Nazi guard. So in the third mission, he angrily confronted her, she broke down crying, relaying that she'd had to befriend and sleep with Nazis to get medicine for her dying mom. Holy crap! The player lost his conflict, and Helena still was not forgiven.

    It was depressing but extremely satisfying play. I constantly found myself muttering the "oh god, no"s and "oh fuck"s when things went down. I must play again.

    Thanks to the players who made it awesome! There were other great scenes, I'm sure some more will pop up here.

    \n Yeah. The Strategicons just keep on getting better. I'm beginning to get overwhelmed with all the stuff that I want to do but can't because it is all happening at once.

    Saturday morning was unlicensed Poison'd (which led me to flake on Colin's sounds-to-have-been-awesome Spirit of the Force game, and to poach players (CK, for one) from Josh BishopRoby's goes-without-saying-awesome Full Light Full Steam session). The dangers of not having enough clones of oneself, I guess.

    Saturday afernoon was the Psi-Run session CK talked about. I wasn't as impressed, (which is not at all a mark against Tom's running of it) but I had a good time all the same. It certainly made me want to read the ashcan I picked up at GenCon, though.

    Saturday night, I co-ran a reasonably ambitious homebrew LARP with Ryan McMullen based on Neverwhere that I'd been nervous about for the preceding week or so. Everything came off almost without a hitch, and it seemed like we entertained 17 players more than adequately, which was incredibly satisfying.

    Sunday morning we played all the scenarios of Executive Decision, with Holly facilitating. It was fun, and in retrospect I'd certainly pull it out for a one hour taste of role-playing for completely new people.

    Sunday afternoon I played in (and took second) in the very first live Arimaa tournament. I'm not just a role-player, I also board game, and I don't hear anyone talking up how good Strategicon is for that. Friends of mine run Protospiel West, which apparently went very well indeed, and I was sad to miss.

    Then, Sunday night, Shock: went really well, even with six players, two of them completely new to story games. It was more than a little cool to see the lights come on for them.

    And Monday I got to play Phoenicia with the above mentioned Protospiel gang, which was also cool, even if I got my ass handed to me.

    Honestly, though, if OrcCon continues the trend, I may spontaneously develop bilocation, just to keep up.

    \n Well, I guess I'll chime in now. Gateway 2007 completely rocked! I know I came to the IPR booth on Saturday planning to buy and I sure did. I bought Carry, Don't Rest Your Head, Mortal Coil, Mechaton: Giant Fighty Robots, Prime Time Adventures, Agon and With Great Power... I love all my shiny new books.

    As for the games I played (fair warning: geek out starts here) Fantastic! I started off in CK's PTA game Cult of Personality on Friday. This was my first PTA experience but once we got the rules down, it really flowed nicely from scene to scene. Murderous mayhem ensued, but the scene framing mechanics kept the game focussed on the show we were creating and the game never spiraled out of control into gonzo-land. Very rewarding, lots of surprises.

    Next, Josh's Dogs game, which he has described as adequate, but which was much better than that. I could tell Josh was tired and I think it did impact the game a little. Pacing didn't go quite as smoothly as the town he ran last con, but it was still very rewarding with very cool conflicts. Josh, you're being hyper-critical. Best quote was when we were thinking about marrying the Steward's daughter to a boy she loved. David (new to Dogs) says "Well, we don't want to go over the Steward's head." And Will and I respond "We are over the Steward's head."

    Saturday morning I ran Dogs in a new town that I created after reading Jason Morningstar's Fort Lemon Branch. I basically stole the woman who wants to be Steward idea and also the notion of a branch fleeing the East and arriving at a heathen trading post, then added lots of my own stuff. Tons of conflict with three putative branch leaders and a real power vacuum. The Dogs decided that the woman was the best bet and then went to stomp on the New Prophet of the King. It was lots of fun and I owe a debt of gratitude to Noble, Devilmaycare, Willh, Sarah and Ryan who came over to my house and playtested the game (it was my first time running Dogs) the week before Gateway. That session pointed out where things went pear shaped, so I could fix the holes before the main event. Thanks folks!

    I played in Holly's Polaris game that afternoon with Willh and Michael, who had never even heard of Polaris until Vasco pushed him into the game. It was my first time playing and the ritual phrasing took a little getting used to, but the game was very neat. It was neat seeing Michael struggle with the narrative control aspect of the game. I was really screwing his character over. Hey, I was his mistaken. And then it finally clicked and I could see it in his eyes that he had narrative control too. He didn't have to take my shit if he didn't want to. I wasn't his GM and he had as much free reign with his protagonist as I did.

    At 8 pm Colin ran the Burning Wheel con scenario "The Gift" about an elvish prince and his retinue that travel to the dwarf kingdom to honor a newly crowned Dwarvish prince and to forge an alliance that both sides need. The scenario is tightly woven with tensions running high between and among the elves and the dwarves. Suffice it to say that after many duels of wits and a combat between the elven sword singer and the dwarven battle warden, the dwarf prince accepted the elves' gift and became an "elf friend" by receiving the Song of Friendship from the mouth of the dying elven loremaster. The elven swordsinger, after pulling his blade out of the loremaster's torso, faded away into the West. And the elven prince returned home after losing both the most renowned elven champion and the King's loremaster, but successfully completing his mission of forging an alliance with those abrasive, greedy dwarfs.

    Midnight is the time for Colin's Don't Rest Your Head game (yes, I'm guilty of Bogarting the Colin), which is sick and twisted and completely awesome. My character was an orphan with the power to make things burn, who just wanted his freedom. He ended up escaping Mother Wenn's Ladies in Hating, destroying the Tacks Man and finally staying in the Mad City to join the ink stained News Boys in their nightly runs of anarchic destruction. He found his freedom in the Mad City, which is more than some find. If you ever get a chance to play when Colin runs Don't Rest Your Head, you must do it. I don't care how little sleep you're going to get.

    The next morning it was the Roach, another first for me. The game was loads of madcap fun, with 5 out of the 7 characters roached. Thanks to Jesse for facilitating a great game and playing one of the more memorable characters. I really enjoyed all the political back stabbing, interspersed with roach commands making people do weird things.

    Then came Colin's Spirit of the Force game. Last con I played the young jedi on a dark path and ended the session by reaching into the dark side to kill a Sith carrying two ancient glowing red light sabers. Then I lost to the dark side, taking the severe consequence of picking up the Sith blades Pain and Despair. So, this con I was all fired up to play this guy again, but when we got into the room there were these two kids who had obviously not seen SOTC or any story-games before and I thought they would enjoy being Luke, while I took the Han Solo-esque character. Let me just say the awesome went all the way to 11 and then some. We fought shuttles from speeding trains and flew speeders. We got on board an Imperial Star Destroyer and seized contol, sending it into its fellows in a fiery ball of explosive goodness. The final scene erupted with conflict galore. My character, suffering from paranoia due to toying with the dark side, pulls his gun on the young jedi and tells him to "Back away from my sister!" who happens to be the young fellow's paduan and his love. The force mystic in the group tries to snag my blaster and I plug him full of holes, but not before completely exhausting my fate points and having to call upon the dark side cuz I have the aspect, "I'll do anything to save my sister." I'm battling to get away from the dark force rippling through my body, but I'm out of points and totally vulnerable, until the guy I just blew away comes to my aid from the other side of the void and starts compelling my light side aspects. He saves me. And that's when my sister consumed by the dark side herself runs me through with her glowing Sith blade. I loved this game! And the kids finally grokked SOTC and ended the day with one killing the young jedi and heading off into the sunset with the girl.

I admit to the killing in order to piss off dad and lots of good stuff ensues, particularly a heartfelt jail house scene with my half sister Bianca.

    I have to say it, once again. Gateway 2007 was fantastic! Thank you Noble and Ptevis for your work in setting everything up. I'm jazzed for Orccon in Feb.
