  • Christian_Griffen
    April 2006
    My definition: A story is the narrative of a premise running its course.

    Definition of premise: there are actually a couple of different types. There's the "A leads to B" premise (such as, "Greed leads to suffering" or "Humility leads to redemption"), there's the "A vs. B" premise ("Fraternal love triumphs over romantic love"), and there's the situational premise ("War does x, y and z to people", or "Being locked up under stress does x, y and z to people").

    The premise running its course means, the narrative shows how that little sentence turns out to be true. In RPGs, it'll be framed as a question, becase you haven't actually decided how it'll turn out yet. But once you do, you get from the starting premise ("Loyalty vs. Greed") to the final premise when it's run its course ("Loyalty triumphs over greed").

    Notice that the premise is NOT universal. You're not saying that loyalty always triumphs over greed. You're saying that here, in this story, you're showing one instance in which loyalty triumphs over greed.

    That's why there need to be conclusions. The premise question needs to be answered.

    You can go on and on with the narrative, but if you're not setting out to explore and play out a premise, you're not focused on creating a story.

    Note again: This is my personal definition, your definition of "narrative" and "story" are most likely different.

    Along the lines of what JBR said, I'm going to be a troublemaker and introduce some different terms, shamelessly stolen from the narratological work of Mieke Bal.

    We can talk about what makes a story at three different levels: the levels of fabula, story and text.

    A fabula is what we might refer to as plot, or what I like to call "a sequence of events in a diegetic world." By this I mean that there is a sequence of events that happens in an imaginary yet logically consistent universe which we are capable of imagining and reasoning about. The reasoning-about part is important, by the way; that's what lets us determine things like whether a given sequence is possible or plausible.

    From a fabula, we construct a story. A story is a particular way that the events of the fabula are presented. For example, we could present the events of the fabula in order, or with flashbacks, or in reverse order (Memento, anyone?). We can change the amount of representational time that we give to different elements of the fabula, or create individualized characters from the archetypal ones of the fabula, and choose a point of view from which to present the story. A story is a series of specific narrative choices made about the fictional events. (And I would argue that even true events are made fictional by the choice to tell about them, much as Jack's been arguing with his 'shared imagining' approach.)

    Finally, there is the text - the way that the story is actually presented. Is it a movie, or a book, or is it performed as theater, or performed over the course of a role-playing game? What language is it written in? How well does the author convey the point of view and the characters and the other elements of the story?

    I'd also like to pick up Chris's distinction between "story" and "tools that construct a story." I'd agree that The Sims is not a story, but that players of the game can use it to construct a story. Similarly, most RPGs can be played as games, but can also be used as story-construction tools. It kind of depends on what you do with it.

    Stories can also contain other stories. I'd say that Buffy is a story. So is each season of Buffy. So are most of the Buffy episodes. Plus there are stories which aren't confined to the format of the text, but rather are woven within and between different episodes and different seasons.

    To address the "So what?" concern, I'll point out that Bal's work has profoundly affected my style as a GM, my work as a game designer and my academic work (though I suppose only the former two are relevant here). I build my games by inventing a diegetic world which I then try to make accessible to players, but work on ways that we can then construct a story which actually takes a stance regarding theme, point of view, time spent on events, etc. Finally, we also work on our ability to perform the stories that we're creating - working on our acting, our eloquence, and our improvisation skills as a way of dealing with this at the level of text.

    That's enough of a rant for now, but I hope it's interesting or at least useful.

    --Jess

      OK, here's a practical bit from me.

      To me, "story" is about closure. A story has a beginning and an end. If there is a beginning, then the ending will generally bring you back to that beginning, or resolve what was set up in the beginning. That gives the story a sense of completeness. Sometimes the beginning is somewhat unrelated, and instead the ending resolves something from more in the middle. But I think to be more story-like the beginning should be just as clear. Note that this sense of completeness is not always the best thing. Sometimes you want to give some sense of openness.

      How does this apply to story games? Well, story games are usually meandering because they aren't outlined in advance and they develop out of the interactions of multiple authors. So the beginning rarely sets up the ending. You can do it a bit in games with predetermined endings. So in My Life With Master, you can start with the Master doing something bad, and you know that it will end with the Master dying. On the other hand, that sort of predetermination is also pretty limiting.

      I try to do something similar in my Buffy episodes. There is generally an item or creature which is the central symbol of the episode. So if I start on that (or something related), then I can get definite closure. More often I resolve a key piece but don't have a snappy opening... and that's OK.

      Since Andy brought up the question of how our theoretical notions affect our play and our design, I figured I'd take a second to expand on some of the stuff I mentioned earlier.

      When I design a game, I try to get players to think about the events that are happening (the fabula) but also how they're going to present those events (the story). For example, in Decade I wanted to give the players motivation to come up with some events that they want to happen in a scene, by giving them particular emotions to portray and leaving it up to them what events will cause those emotions to play out. I decided to control some things about the story (i.e. that it all takes place in one hour) but leave the contents of the fabula up to the players.

      Oddly enough, this comes out in a very different way when I GM. I develop the notion of the fabula very strongly, particularly when it comes to a sense of things happening off-stage. While I rarely fiat what those off-stage things will be, it's key to my style of game that the players always feel that they're in a world that is larger than their individual acts. When I GM, the fabula is bigger than the story. I visualize it as a camera following the PCs through the world, zooming in on them but occasionally turning to frame something else, so that there's a sense of what the world of narrative possibility is like.

      As a player, these ideas have mostly made me very open to making story suggestions and game elements, even before I discovered the indie scene. I had the notion that a player might try to participate on all three levels of the story, not just the level of text ("let's act out the GM's story") or the level of story ("let's deal with these events that have happened") but also at the level of fabula ("let's invent some stuff that's in the world, even if it never comes up"). While it's hardly original, these notions have made me a strongly collaborative player (and GM, for that matter).

      I don't know whether these ideas will be useful to anyone else, but they've shaped my own style - admittedly in an incoherent way - and are continuing to shape it as I continue to learn and think and theorize.

      I'm also working on some of the things that Jack alluded to (cognitive aspects of storytelling), but it's a huge topic and I don't really have any conclusions that would be useful to share. You'll probably notice me bring up ideas about this in specific contexts, where it might be useful to us as designers, GMs and players - but I'm not yet ready to generalize. I'll save that for my dissertation.

      --Jess
