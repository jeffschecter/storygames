    \n I've wondered about "micropress" production, too, at time. Mainly because I make a lot of Icehouse games, and one or two of them actually require reference materials, which would fit a card or pocket-book format pretty well. Some of my thoughts:

    * Compilations - POD providers often have a minimum page count to even make a "book"--some do offer brochures, but even that leaves a "gap" in the 4 pg - 30 pg range. So there's the thought of getting together with other designers to compile several such games (i.e. need reference; can't just read once and play forever) into a single publication. The biggest problem with such a structure is income distribution and trust. And coordinating a common design with the other contributors (not mandatory for RPG layouts, of course).
    -- An offshoot of this would be a literal magazine that asked for submissions: "RPG Quarterly" or similar.

    * Filler-Heavy - I think of this when I think of games that require playing card (Roach) or games which destroy the playing aides (The Sea Worshippers; most frequent-update character sheets). SO, fine, the rules take up a whopping five pages. Provide thirty more pages of perforated play aides, from reference sheets for all players, to disposable character sheets, to even cards, counters, or tokens which must be cut out of a page (or are perfed) to play the game.

    * Single-Sheet - Nothing says "trivial fulfillment" like a trifold game on Letter-sized paper. Print, say, 100 copies at Kinkos (for a whopping $7 to $30) and fold them yourself (or hit up a local mass mailer to use their trifold machine for, like, twenty seconds). An order comes in through PayPal? Stuff one in a standard envelope, stamp it, print out the mailing label, and ship it out with your other mail, in you mailbox. Total cost per delivery: 50\xa2 to 90\xa2 plus a bit of time. Could sell for $2 to $4 each, easy.

    Anyhow, the thing about micropress games, ultimately, boils down to scaling problems, at all levels. No deals on small press runs (unless you do, like, tens of thousands); hard to find POD to handle it; and hard to command more than the cost of the paper it's printed on. (Hmmm... could an advertising-for-printing model shore up profits...?)

    * Free PDF with Donations - Put it out for free, but leave them to print if they want print. Ask for donations, and have a simple PayPal link for doing micropayments (i.e. down to $1). Other offshoots:
    -- Ad banners on support web site or forums.
    -- "Premium" services require a payment (e.g. $1 gets you a single-user-use password to the Downloads and Supplements pages).
