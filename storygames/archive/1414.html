Three Kinds of Conflict All At Once [FLFS]

Josh Roby
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Play Advice
I have this problem. Everything relates to my current project recently. I feel like I'm running at the mouth. If you're tired of hearing me babble about Full Light, Full Steam, man, I totally understand. But I'm going to babble about FLFS now.

So in Scenes without goals and conflicts, we were throwing the word 'conflict' around a lot and it became clear that we were talking about different things with the same word. Short version: some folks were talking about engaging the conflict resolution mechanic, some people were talking about conflicts of interest between players, and some folks were talking about the literary criticism definition of 'conflict' (that being character+desire+obstacle). And it occured to me that FLFS uses all three of those in relatively discrete procedures and might present a worthwhile example.

You roll dice in FLFS for two reasons. The first is to charge up your thematic battery. Basically, at any point in the game you can call for a roll for your own character and give yourself a disadvantage in line with your thematic battery (I'm a Rake, therefore I show up to my post hungover from the night before). You set stakes and stuff, roll, and stuff happens and it's likely that stuff happens poorly for you since you're at a disadvantage (I bang up the lander's hull trying to dock while hung over -- whoopsie). This is engaging the conflict resolution system but it is not trying to define the scene in terms of "the conflict". It's pure character exposition. (It is setting up the character for reincorporation later, though. We'll get to that soon.)

The second reason to roll dice, though, is to contest control of the narration. Basically, Judson is narrating that he takes control of the situation because he's just a badass like that, and I tell him "hell no, roll dice to back that shit up." He rolls his Leadership and Empathy or whatever's appropriate and if he wins, what he said comes to pass and he keeps going, and if he loses, he fumbles in some way and I tell him how it happens and take up the reins of narration. Alternately, I can jump in and roll myself for some other, totally disconnected thing that I might be doing in the same scene, and if I make my roll, whatever that is, I narrate my success and keep going. Narration is mine, now. So these die checks -- engaging the conflict resolution system -- are a relatively pure expression of conflicts of interests between players at the table.

Now, the situation which the PCs are presented with is built off of their input, either in terms of their thematic batteries or their campaign-building discussion in the first session, or in feedback between sessions. Basically, the GM keeps a running list of flags and uses them to directly inspire the conflicts (character+desire+obstacle) that the situation is built out of. This is totally lit crit conflict all the way.

Because it's, you know, a roleplaying game, the PCs are trying to address the situation (built out of lit crit conflicts), and they do that by engaging the conflict resolution system. (Short tangent: for the purposes of this thread I'm referring to it as "conflict resolution" but, as that is very confusing for the reasons outlined in this thread, in the book they're just called die checks and there's less confusion.) The PCs use the mechanics to grab narration from the GM so they can narrate their success/triumph/victory, which is adjudicating conflicts of interests between players at the table. The "conflict" mechanics do not resolve the lit crit conflict -- that would be pretty boring. The "conflict" mechanics resolve the conflicts of interest so that the players are able to address the lit crit conflicts that compose the situation.

Remember the first bit where players can engage the conflict resolution system in order to charge up their thematic batteries? This functions to make the characters fumble and struggle throughout the beginning of the game, revealing all their character faults and flaws. In other words, at the beginning of the movie Daniel-san gets his ass kicked. Because the situation is built off of those same thematic batteries, this creates a resonance between the characters and the lit crit conflicts, engaging them with the situation as they charge up their batteries. Then as the game moves into the latter half (actually the latter two-thirds, but whatever), the players can start discharging those thematic batteries, openning up a can of character suffering and hard lessons to whup batman's ass. Daniel-san wins the tournament. And how do they unleash that pent-up power? By engaging the conflict resolution system again, this time taking a bonus equal to the penalties taken beforehand. The resonance set up between the characters and the lit crit conflicts pay off in resolving those conflicts through reincorporation.

So there are definitely three different things one might call "conflicts" and they all interact all the damn time. However, the whole mess becomes clearer when you very carefully distinguish between which one is which. Well, it all becomes clearer to me. Am I making any sense whatsoever to you folks?

    I do indeed mean conflict of interest between the players, but in play it's rarely anything like hostile.

    First off, any check is also an opportunity to charge a thematic battery. If I'm narrating along about how I'm investigating a crime scene and you tell me I should roll, I can charge a level of Curious or Thorough or whatever. Since we're already hitting up the die system, I can also set higher stakes than I might normally be able to just narrate away. I can say, for instance, that my stakes are finding the clue we need. So with you challenging me to roll dice, you are giving me opportunities to develop character and forward the plot.

    It's also totally possible and totally legal for two players to face off with each other, roll dice, and both use the same die check to charge up their thematic batteries. If that doesn't immediately make sense to you, think of every buddy movie ever made.

    If you are rolling dice, your stakes are always within the purview of whoever's dice you're rolling. That means no "and then it rains" rolls, cause no character is controlling the weather (well, usually, at least). It is far more in the form of "and then I do this" and "back that up with a die roll, mate." On the other hand, I can call for a die check on you and if I'm not rolling dice against you ("you can't just jump that gorge so easily!"), I have more freedom in the stakes ("or you fall down the cliff") and I can take inspiration for those stakes off of the Set and Prop cogs in play (We're in the Artemis Lowlands, and that cog has the complication "Swept away in the rapids"). So you challenging me helps reinforce the sets and setting.

    Lastly, there's an influence from the setting -- one of those diffuse and hard-to-quantify elements of design -- in which we're all playing Victorian-era British naval officers. Players tend to default towards being very polite to each other, even if they are trying to undermine and subvert each other's plans. Rank is also always, always present, and in some games there is not only naval rank as well as social standing, family heritages, even feudal ranks, and those are at least given lip service if not explicitly supported. So while the Captain may be trying to keep the girl on board and the Chaplain wants to smuggle her off, it's this bizarrely polite conflict, with in-character doublethink "I know you're trying to do what I don't want you to, but we won't actually come out and say it, but we'll try and maneuver our way around each other to get what we want."

    Additionally, the conflicts of interests between characters are very explicit in the game, and are, yes, totally part of the lit crit conflicts set up in situation engineering. Situation engineering spits out conflicts and character foils based off of the character concepts in play, so the differences between the players is repeatedly emphasized. You've got your thematic batteries right there on your sheet, you're passing the scrip when you reference batteries on other sheets, and you're calling them out when you use them. So if I'm a Closet Socialist and you're an Wealthy Industrialist's Son, there is no hiding that we are often at odds. Given that the players are all there for an interesting story, it's very very common for them to gleefully tighten the screws on each others' thematic batteries (and pass the scrip doing it) when they call for die checks. (Playing my Closet Socialist, I will call for a check when you try to ignore and brush past the pauper children on the street, for instance.) I haven't ever seen a player get annoyed when such a check is called on them, since it's directly hitting his character concept.

    Also note that it's quite within the rules to just pass narration to another player when you're done narrating, and in actual play this happens a whole helluva lot more often than direction-by-dieroll.

That is, I've said many times about Hero Quest before, that the resolution system resolves outcomes of the conflict that comes as a result of a thematic decision, but that the thematic decision is the one to get into the conflict in the first place. Do I fight here, or do I run? Is it worth enough to me? When we get to the conflict resolution, we see why it's worth it to my character as the augments come out that demonstrate why I as a player see it as meet that my character turn and fight.

    So the resolution system supports the decision, but after the fact. The simple player decision of "what my character will do here," is the player solving the character's internal questions, and, thus, creating theme.

    What's the "conflict resolution" system for, then? To use Ron's term, it's a "springboard" for creativity in coming up with the next action, likely the next conflict. Oh, I won the fight? Now I have to deal with the big brother of the one I defeated. That establishes another question for the player to answer, but, again, it's as a result of the GM's interpretation of the outcome.

    This is a quite common cycle in these games. In none of them does the conflict resolution system decide the premise or lit crit conflict, because in these cases, those decisions are the "fruitful void" that the player is to fill. In Pendragon, on the other hand, such decisions are made by the system quite often (if not every time). If you take away the theme creating choices in a game like Trollbabe then...what do you have left for the player to do?

    That's not to say other models aren't possible. Just that I can't think of an example of one.

    I've had this discussion before with Vincent, and I remain unconvinced of his argument -- I think we're actually agreeing, but emphasizing different things. I do put my money where my mouth is, though. I define conflict in my published games as "disagreement between two players about 'what happens next'."

    Here's where I get radical:

    All conflicts are conflicts of interest between the players because the characters don't exist.

    Here's where I get less radical. It's not as extreme as it sounds. I stand by the above even when players are "cooperating." Basically, I view it like this:

    "I think X would be cool."
    "RAD, I think X+Y would be cool"
    "Ok, roll sucker!"

    -or-

    "I think X would be cool."
    "Neat, I think Y would be cool"
    "Ok, roll sucker!"

    The trick is that it can also go like this:

    "I think X would be cool."
    "Rad. I think X+Y would be cool."
    "Ok, no need to roll. X+Y happens. Neat."

    -or-

    "I think X would be cool."
    "Neat, I think Y would be cool"
    "Wait, no need to roll. Y is much better. That happens instead, ok?"
    "Ok. Maybe X next time, pal."

    I think the following is absurd:

    "Captain Insano wants X to happen! I'm not saying I do, I mean, I pretty much AGREE with everyone at the table. But, still, Captain Insano want's it! What can I do?!?"

    Because the appropriate reaction is this:

    "You jackass. Captain Insano doesn't exist. YOU made him up, so YOU want him to do that. Captain Insano doesn't 'actually' think that. He's YOU acting fictionally. Stop pretending otherwise, you're not going to hurt my feelings, silly."

    Consider, why do we put up with writers saying things like "Well, the book just wrote itself once I had the characters thought up"? That's nonsense. That's an artist being arty. He wrote the fucking book, ok? It's not as though the Almighty came down and typed it.

    Interestingly, the difference in role-playing is adversity. When a SINGLE PLAYER has two characters interacting, we find it pretty flat (i.e. un-fun) in actual play of a role-playing game. (Is this the Czege Principle? I can't remember.)

    We have no problem wrapping our brains around a writer pitting two characters in conflict in his/her stories. Why? Because the writer has his own system in his head and process in writing for how this gets resolved. And, by the time we read it, it's resolved. In role-playing, it's not so easy. Then, we have two or more human beings who must resolve between one another "what they want to happen next" in real time. Sometimes it's freeform, sometimes is structured, sometimes it's fiat. Whatever works, it's system.

    (I think we've argued about this before, Matt. But I think I have a clearer statement now, so I'll repeat myself.)

    Of course the characters exist -- you actually had to do something to make them up, right?

    Solid fictional characters -- like Hamlet, or Oedipus, or even Han Solo -- are completely real. They're not real in the way that the Dr. Pepper bottle on my desk is real, but they are real in the same way that the state of Arizona or the Pythagorean theorem is real. That is, a character is a set of ideas with a particular logic and coherence to them. This inner logic means that some facts will be true about an idea, and some won't be.

    If I tell you that Panama City is the capital of the Khazhakstan, I'm wrong -- even though Panama and Khazhakstan are just ideas. If I tell you that Shakespeare had Hamlet discover string theory, or that Hamlet was totally well-adjusted and had no hangups, I'd be wrong. And by wrong, I mean I'm saying something that is factually untrue, even though Hamlet is a totally made-up imaginary guy.

    This means that when a gamer says that Captain Insano wants to do something the player doesn't like, he can be right. The character has a logic to it, and you can't do otherwise without breaking that integrity.[*] This is actually a really, really good thing, for two main reasons. First, we as humans create meaning from the patterns we see, and having a pattern that strong is a sign the group is doing something profoundly right. Second, as an ongoing thing, having creative constraint is really helpful, and when the necessary constraints are arising integrally from the fiction you're making you're cooking with gas. (One of my telltales for whether a Dogs game has really gelled is when players start Giving even when the dice say they can win, just because the imaginative force of the narrative is too strong to ignore.)

    So what does it mean when a player says "Captain Insano wants X to happen! I'm not saying I do, I mean, I pretty much AGREE with everyone at the table. But, still, Captain Insano want's it! What can I do?!?", and what does it mean when the players decide to make Insano decide not-X anyway?

    What's going on is that the player is saying that the logic of the character inexorably pushes towards X. If we make Insano do not-X, we have a different character. We have a new character who is, coincidentally, also named Captain Insano. This change means that the players have to do some rethinking to figure out what the story actually means, because you need to figure out how and why Captain Insano-2 made the choices he did in the narrative. That reinterpretation work can be a drag on the game, since having a solid intuitive interpretation of the meaning of what has happened really helps figure out what to create next. The right next scene for the game will be different for Insano-1 and Insano-2.

    So "I don't want X but my character does" is really a case of "we have downsides whichever path we take -- either do X, which I don't want, or change the character to do not-X, and spend effort recontextualizing the story". Which choice you make really depends on the relative costs and benefits.

    [*] Yes, of course sometimes jerks use this line as a cover for bullying. Assume we're not dealing with a jerk.

    Matt, reading your response, I think we basically agree about how to run or play a game, but disagree over nomenclature.

    I think that trying to equate realness with tangibility does violence to the English language, and worse, it does so in a way that's likely to promote flamewars rather than understanding. Ideas are real things. English is real. Threats can be real. Freedom is real. Mathematics is real. The state of Kansas is real. That social aggression isn't just rude, it's bad strategy -- it's likely to get your fellow player to dig in his or her heels, which is exactly what you don't want at a moment that calls for flexibility.
