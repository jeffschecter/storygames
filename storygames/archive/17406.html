    \n You might be better off thinking of fronts as characters or groups of characters with distinct agendas. So, you have the demon in Gregor's head, the fleshy thing, maybe a gnoll leader somewhere, those Will O the Wisp creatures, the one city that was destroyed, the other city that was destroyed (these could be a front each, or they could fragment--maybe the city council from one city is a front, and the apocalyptic cult from the same city is another front?), the balrog that isn't possessing Gregor anymore, and Kratar the dwarfen god of secrets and stuff. And maybe some wizards opening up far realm rifts, too? So lots of stuff.

    Pick the ones you think will have the most impact on the PCs in the immediate future. Decide what they want, give them an agenda and impulses. Then decide on a rough plan for them. Write out a sequence of steps, actions they will be attempting to perform over the next little while. This isn't concrete, but it's what you imagine them doing if the PCs don't stop them. Have them reach their final goal in 5 or 6 steps, maybe. This is not just so you have ideas to draw from in play for the PCs to interact with, but also so these agendas can butt up against each other, creating new and interesting combinations for the PCs to interact with. It's not exactly like AW or DW, but I give some advice on this for a different game over here and here that you might find valuable. You may have to skip to about half-way into each post (down to "The Adventure" and "Flowers of Evil," respectively).

    Then you need some portents. This is where Call of Cthulhu adventures can be instructive (or, they were to me, anyway). In order to write a CoC mystery, you have a villain and probably a summoning or something. Then you scatter clues in all directions around this central secret, so that investigators should be able to figure out what is happening by tracking down say 40-70% of the total clues (this way you never have that "failed roll that stalls the game" problem). In DW, you can be even more proactive, though, because you can have portents that don't just sit around waiting to be found. Ask yourself: "How do the PCs find out about this front/character/scheme?" There's your portents! You should try and put them in different places if you can, so that which portents the PCs encounter depends on where they go and what decisions they make. Of course, you can always have the PCs friends track them down and be like "doods, shit got heavy!"

    In play, you could do something like this: write out each fronts steps on a small card or piece of paper and stack them up. Write some portents on each one. Then, when it's your turn, look over all the steps that are visible. Pick your favourite, and start describing the portents. Or, if the action would affect the PCs, jump right into it. When you're done, remove the step you just used, thus revealing a new one, and look at the steps you have visible now and think about what each front/character/group would do now. How would they react, based on how far along in their plan they are? If something jumps out, do that. If one of the visible steps looks like it should happen now, do that.

    \n Okay, some concrete ideas:

    What about the gnolls? They all got killed right? Balrog-Gregor mopped up the last of them it seems like?

    Okay, what about the gnoll leader who sent his sorcerer with some troops on a mission and they never came back? He -- ehm, no, these are hyenas -- she will want to know what went down. So you have:

    Gnoll Warlord Hunting Party.
    Agenda: To summon a great demon and bind it, to be used against the humans in the upcoming war.
    Impulses: To fight weaker enemies whenever possible. To scavenge the dead for food and gluttonously indulge. To drink, dance, and howl.

    Five-Step Plan:

    1. Investigate the scene. Gnolls come sniffing 'round the old observatory to find out what happened. They will make trouble with locals, raiding farms, molesting travellers (who are apparently pretty numerous now!), and might even fight a few far realms beasties.

    2. Establish a base of operations. The gnolls will take over some local fort or castle, and this is where they will attempt to summon and bind another demon (or the same demon if he's still wandering around causing trouble, or Gregor's demon...). This one will be pretty obvious. Their neighbours will know, and there might be survivors from the battle who escaped and are trying to drum up support to retake the fort/castle.

    3. Collect magical ingredients. For some reason, this includes blood, hair, nails, and stuff from the PCs! They were there and now the gnolls need some sympathetic tokens from them. They will try to just kill the PCs, and when that proves to be a Herculean task, they will settle for escaping with locks of hair, the blood smeared on their blades, and any possessions the PCs consider valuable. This battle or these tokens might show them that Gregor is possessed...

    4. Collect a demon or a sacrifice. They will attempt to capture the balrog, using magical circles, or kidnap Gregor, or they will try to abduct an appropriate sacrifice in the form of a very, very important human being (you know, like a princess or something, use your imagination). They will then bring their captive back to their base of operations, unless it's been destroyed, in which case they will have to try and set up a new ad hoc HQ.

    5. Bind a demon. If the gnolls get to this point and the PCs somehow miss it, portents are easy! The skies turn black with roily lightning-belching clouds. Dead crows fly in flocks, circling the ritual. The ground shakes, and monsters roam the roads, converging at the ritual, ready to witness the demon's fall, or serve it should it shake off the chains of sorcery.

    And there you go, agenda, plan, and portents, ready to get fucked up by the PCs agency. Depending on what happens, the gnolls will have to regroup, improvise, and adapt. If they suffer setbacks, they might have to go back to step 2 and start all over again. Also, they might come up against other characters or groups and make deals, or change parts of their plan. Also, the balrog might wipe them out or enslave them. Just because they plan to win, doesn't mean they will!

    \n I thought up another one. You said there's some wizards who want to open up more rifts to the far realms, right?

    Wizard Conspiracy!
    Agenda: Permanently meld this land with the far realms.
    Impulses: Dominate and control. Show off with magic. Lecture those who are ignorant.

    5 Step Plan:

    1. Join the apocalyptic cult. So, this cult was small, but then the city was destroyed. The apocalypse! So this cult is now recruiting, sending representatives wandering around to promote it. And there's rumours that all sorts of scoundrels and questionable people are joining. Their numbers are growing!

    2. Take over the apocalypse cult. Strange news in the rumour-mill! The cult has a new leader. Apparently the old leader was deposed via magical combat, in public no less! Maybe the PCs can witness this if they go check out the cult, and they can get caught up in the following riot!

    3. Build a temple. Kind-of similar to the gnolls HQ, but what they are really doing is erecting a lodestone to focus the energies of their sorcery. They will of course want someplace where they have political and military control of, which could be an already-existing settlement. Some people are bound to be unhappy about this!

    4. Build a perimeter. So they send teams out to erect smaller temples. All over the place, they go, stirring up trouble and conflict. Strange magical occurrences follow them around.

    5. Perform the ritual. Once their perimeter is set up, they will perform the ritual, and if the PCs don't stop them, there won't be any more rifts to the far realms, because this land will be merged with the far realms forever!!!! Dun dun dun!
