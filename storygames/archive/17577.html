    \n My limited experiance with it has told me that the most important part of playing Freemarket, or at least one of the most important parts, is setting buy-in. The game is basically what would happen if 4chan, Secondlife, and Tumblr existed in the physical realm. Strange things can or should happen constantly, thing like convincing people to give you their giant metal cupcake so you can turn it into a flying hot-rod.

    Mechanically, the PCs will need to work together if they want to come out ahead in Flow. This hadn't been totally clear while reading it, but doing challenges as a group is cruicial.

    I'm sure there's more, but that's what I got out of the time I played it. I didn't find it as scary as some others do.

    \n Here are a few things I plan to do next time I play:

    - Diversify Experiences during character creation. When it comes time to pile all the characters into a Challenge, I prefer the creative challenge of roping in tangentially-related skills over the creative challenge of roping in redundant skills. I also like to have a variety of starting points from which to choose and begin Challenges.

    - Make sure someone has Negotiation. Making and fulfilling contracts is both fun and beneficial.

    - Conceive a MRCZ that's actually offering something I think is valuable. Playing a cynical scam artist or idle fad-monger is less fun for me than an earnest craftsman or service provider. The more you can envision (and get excited about) a path to success for your MRCZ, the more engaging its ups and downs will be.

    - Suggest that the SuperUser start by coming up with an interesting client for the MRCZ's services.

    - Clarify to the group that the fictional scope of a Challenge will often be incredibly flexible. A Printing Challenge, for example, could be played as each character chipping in over a span of minutes, or over a span of months. My point is: when you're looking for ways to contribute to a Challenge, don't limit yourself.

    - Clarify to the group what's going on fictionally with the Efficiency stuff during Challenges. Basically, the Aggregate is aware of everything you ever do, and the more bang it sees you getting out of the station resources you spend, the more station resources it'll give you next time. Some of my Challenge decisions felt very artificial and mechanical until I figured that out, and it bugged me. That might just be me, though.

    Beyond that, what Thark said. Hope this helps! Let us know how it goes!

    \n We had a good time despite some time boxing issues.

    Our biggest problem was for all of us to come to terms with, and agree upon, the setting. I was the only one who had read the game and we must have spent over an hour talking back and forth about how stuff worked, the layout of the station, etc.

    The players ended up with a MRCZ that was working against the systems of FreeMarket. Personally I would have opted for creating rather than destroying but the path the players chose made for an entertaining game.

    The mechanics for conflicts worked out very smoothly and after a few goes at it the players were confident in how the system worked, so no problem there.

    As a Superuser I wanted to let the players and their goals guide the story but we started off really slowly and the players fel stranded, overwhelmed by the amount of choices available to them and for a moment paralyzed in trying to work out how to further their agendas. I ended up throwing some encounters with (in my oppinion) interesting characters and events and once they got the hang of what they were trying to achieve they cleverly used almost all of these seemingly random elements towards furthering their cause.

    The freedom and the possibilities of the setting were intimidating at first but next time I suspect the players will be prepared for the challenge!

    \n We also had a MRCZ that was into fad-mongering and breaking. I think a lot of players have this tendency to want to subvert Freemarket as soon as they encounter it, because they're too cynical for its utopianism or are interested in playing rebels against the system or hate Facebook or whatever. That's cool, but it makes for a somewhat short arc of play. I think it's better, as has been pointed out, to play it relatively earnestly first -- as a group trying to get by at an honest labor -- and then let group friction and jadedness build up over time, culminating later in the game. Also, I think it's important to realize that MRCZs kinda come and go. There's no reason to commit to the same one or even the same set of characters for the entire game. I almost wish the game gave you several characters, so you wouldn't feel so invested in a particular project or group.

    I also wish the game had a "random MRCZ generator" or something that made it easier to populate the station with craziness. Maybe use the insane tables from Refuge in Audacity? In general, I found the job of the Superuser to be pretty exhausting. The players and their MRCZ certainly drive the game, but it's up to you to play the rest of the station, including all the inhabitants, their various levels of Flow, their weird desires and hobbies, the other MRCZs, etc. And there's not a lot to help you track all that stuff and play it with any sort of integrity; I found I mostly just had to make it up.

    Also, map the station as you go! I wish there was a better tool for this too.

    \n David, there's nothing telling the Superuser to push hard against the PCs. You're just supposed to play the station with some degree of integrity to its concepts, I think, and let the users do whatever they do and meet whatever situations that causes. It's pure sandbox in the most real sense. I suspect that the jadedness and friction will build up over time mostly due to the inherent cynicism and dissatisfaction of the players, plus the limits of utopianism. You know how, like, in every utopian fiction ever, there's something fundamentally wrong or some freedom that has to perish in order for the utopia to work? Freemarket likes to say that doesn't happen there, but it's not strictly true (as becomes clearer the more you play the game) and the players are likely to sense injustice or feel trapped by the system even when it's set up to let people do whatever they want.

    \n A few things come to mind:

    1. The station is about Flow. If you rebel against the Flow economy, you can't really do anything.

    2. The station is also about the hierarchy of MRCZs. You pretty much have to work to improve your MRCZ's position, if you want better space, more resources, etc. And to do that, you have to play along with the Aggregate, rather than resisting it.

    3. Due to the ephemeral nature of everything on the station, it can be hard to feel like you've accomplished anything permanent and lasting (aside from moving your MRCZ up the hierarchy). Even if you do something huge, it's a very "what have you done for me lately?" kind of place. Continually coming up with newer and bigger schemes and puts pressure on the characters.

    4. Sometimes the social currency economy of the station can seem kinda petty and bitchy, just like Twitter, Facebook, or internet forums where you talk about roleplaying. Especially with frownies and the like, there's this pressure to do what the masses want from you.

    All of these are part of what gives Freemarket its unique flavor and provide structure to the insane freedom, but IME players and their characters keep bumping into them because it takes a while to really wrap your brain around how a society without death and limited resources actually works.

    \n Gotcha. We're seeing the same things, just parsing them differently. Maybe the game premise just puts me in an optimistic mindset! The way I look at it is:

    1. If you do your best to do cool stuff, and don't screw anyone over, your Flow will take care of itself (at least, enough to not get kicked off the station).

    2. Having worse space and fewer resources than the next MRCZ isn't a big deal. Moving up is nice, but there's no pressure to do so from anything but your own ego and ambition.

    3. All your accomplishments are ephemeral, but hey, that's the life of an artist! Once you touch someone with your work, that's forever a thing that happened, even if it isn't commoditized as a Long-Term Memory. And you don't need eternal adoration to pay the bills, so when your masterwork's forgotten, whatever; make a new one! Then go relax and have sex with androids or something. I suppose that with extended play, a lack of direction and progress might begin gnawing at me, but we're talking, like, dozens of sessions here...

    4. I've long since come to terms with the fact that the connection between what I think is cool and what my society values is weak and unpredictable. There are gonna be fads that I think are dumb, and things I love that get ignored; oh well. This unpredictability is what makes human communication endlessly interesting. As for "petty and bitchy", FreeMarket seems like a huge step up from the real world, in that the Aggregate does its best to give you Flow for actual value added. No friggin' Paris Hiltons. Unless I'm misremembering a mechanic about having high-Flow friends being good for your Flow...
