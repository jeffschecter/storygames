    \n I'm providing specific design guidelines to create scenarios in Walkabout. These aren't specific stories or adventures per se, but environments, a problem within that environment and the repercussions of that problem...any story within the game is an investigation where the players encounter the symptoms, and follow chains of cause and effect to the central problem in the area. The game is generally pretty sand-boxy, players can do whatever they want, but it's their general duty to correct problems in the world using whatever tools they have at their disposal.

    \n Many thanks everyone! Just from this thread I've got already tons on books an pages to look trough. I've been searching through all the RPGs I've got and I'm surprised to find that many on them doesn't present even the outlines of a proper method for creating adventures. Eclipse Phase, for example, has a beautiful design and tons and tons of inspiring material, the setting is quite complete and populated with plot hooks... and while the "Running the game" section contains all the secrets for the setting, it doesn't actually say much about how to prep that for the game. The PF corebook mostly says "as a GM you get to do X & Y" but again, doesn't say how (though the Gamemastering Guide is the book that really covers all that, that one is a must to play PF).

    As a designer, I understand why and how easily you can fall into the mistake of thinking that, if you know how to run the game and feel it's incredibly easy to, then, once they read your book, everybody else and their dog will be able to do the same. It's not true at all.

    I just found out why (design-wise, not color-wise) there are games that some people will understand, run and enjoy better than others. See, you've probably heard before of the Theory of Multiple Intelligences, but have you ever thought how deeply it impacts our hobby?

    I've often had trouble playing 3.5 and PF because of the amount of rules and fiddling with numbers I've gotta make there. I'm an artist, so I'm more visual/spatial oriented. Our gm feels at home with the system, since he's more logic/math oriented. On top of that, he works as an accountant and so he's used to memorizing legal stuff and relate it to those numbers, so in the end he actually relaxes playing PF, while I get a bit stressed about that part of the game.

    Anyway, enough rambling. If anyone is interested on discussing this further, I'll be glad to open another thread. Again, many thanks for your help!
