\n Okay, remember when I was talking about Savage Worlds, and pointed to a fan-made handout that said things like "if you are having a hard time hitting a dude, but you're smarter than him, try a trick maneuver, if you're hitting the dude but having a hard time damaging him, try an all out attack..." etc., basically giving play advice organized by frustrating situation you might find yourself in. I was saying "yes, more of that please", because explicitly saying "look, this can be frustrating, but here's some thing(s) you can try that will help you" makes it absolutely clear that it's not necessarily your fault if you don't grasp every part of the rules instantly and have brilliant contributions immediately. That's completely great and all games should do that, forever, the end, anyone who argues is wrong.

So I was reading my Smallville RPG PDF, in preparation for an upcoming game set in what I have insouciantly called "The Marvel Movie Trailer-verse" ("If they didn't think it was important enough to get it across in a three minute trailer, you don't have to know about it."), and I came across page 73.

A quick precis of the Smallville system. You roll a bunch of dice and keep the highest two, adding them together. You are guaranteed to be rolling at least two dice because you're always rolling at least a Value (Duty, Power, Glory, etc.) and a Relationship. You may have some bonuses or abilities or whatnot that allow you to roll more dice in certain situations, but you're always rolling those two.

Then on page 73, it says this (edited slightly for brevity):

"If a Lead player starts having trouble picking an appropriate Relationship to roll, that's...a signal that the player doesn't know why what's happening matters to his character. All Watchtower needs to do is drop in a line of dialogue from a Feature or a scrap of evidence that points the way to who he's fighting with...or for. But it's not all on Watchtower to make things work; a little dialogue or exposition from a Lead can make a die roll all about living up to a friend's expectations or proving that a rival's accusations aren't true.

If a Lead's Values aren't applying to a situation, why is he in that situation?...the story shouldn't be about things the Lead doesn't care about. Watchtower can...bring events back into line with, or perhaps directly challenging, the Lead's Values. The Lead player can start looking for ways to make the episode's events relate to what his character is all about."

(stands on chair) YES! YEEESSSS!

I was psyched as hell to read that, and I'm reading it out loud to eeeeeveryone I teach this game to.

Okay, so I have a question though.

Looking at the lifePathways thing, it looks like if I'm born Rich or Strange, then I increase a Resource by 1 in that step, but the only Resource I am able to have at that point is the Extra which I drew in in this step, right? So I draw a circle, write "Pepper Potts" in it, draw an arrow and write "childhood pal" on it, and write down "Pepper Potts (Socialize, Business): 2d4 on my sheet. Then I immediately cross that off and write 2d6 instead. I don't have any other Resources to increase at that point. Right?

    \n I just finished reading Smallville today. I really like the "how to run the game" advice, especially everything in the Scenes chapter (scene framing and telegraphic deserve special acknowledgment for their usefulness).

    I'm very curious to see how it works in play, the 9th season characters seemed like they had a lot to keep track of but then again... playing them might be like starting a game with a 20th level D&D character. Starting from 1st level and working your way up makes keeping track of everything easier. The same is probably true if you start with a season 1 character.

    Does anyone know if there is a flow chart breaking down the various conflict steps?

    The game is simple but there are many little details to keep track of, a 1 page summary would rock. For example, in contests (conflicts) the instigator acts by building their dice pool and rolling. The target can then give in for free before they build their dice pool and roll or stick it out and roll to react. If they want to give in after they roll, they pay a plot point to their attacker. You compare rolls and if one side rolls 5 over, they can stress the other person out of the conflict. Otherwise the person who rolled higher (but not 5 higher) can roll and inflict stress. If the inflicted stress bumps a Stress trait past a d12, then the person may be stressed out of a conflict (like if someone rolled 5 higher). Otherwise, after a person takes stress (but isn't stressed out), they can push forward with the conflict (the stress taken may give the other side bonus dice) or they can choose to stress themselves out to the point that they fall unconscious. Which means bad things can happen to them in the next scene (being captured) but in return they lower their stress traits and receive a plot point. Stressing out of the conflict doesn't necessarily mean losing, it just means you can't stop the other person (but you aren't forced to do anything specifically yourself). Giving in is when you lose lose! And if someone does give in, there is an optional rule where the winner can return the plot point spent to give in and then pay the loser an additional plot point to stress them even more (and when stress is rolled the GM's trouble resource pool is increased). There are also rules for aiding rolls, interfering with rolls (which has its own list of sub steps as you join the conflict), complications, automatically failing conflicts, spending plot points, and challenging your drives that all affect conflicts.

    That all said, it sounds like it plays very fast but I could see people miss an option without a reference.

    \n The bit about spent plot points going to the player you're opposing (on the condition that they can't spend them until after the contest is over) is also great; it's great fun seeing all those tokens scoot around the table.

    It's weird, I've had a few D&D games end terribly because one player couldn't resist backstabbing the others, and I've long been skeptical of Amber for much the same reasons, but for some reason the PvP in Smallville strikes me as downright enticing. Maybe because there's a clear structure to handle things mechanically, so you always have a chance to defend yourself from someone else's dickery instead of having to constantly monitor the offending character so they don't lay some kind of devastating ambush.

    The role of the GM in this game is also unusual-- I find myself almost having nothing to do, because once you frame a scene with two PCs and a reason for them to disagree, they kind of just make their own gravy. This also tends to result in a bloated Trouble Pool with seemingly little reason to use it-- I don't need to add more tension to a scene where the PCs are butting heads, do I? Adding to this, I find that Complications can often spawn follow-up scenes dealing with the fallout. This seems at odds with the book, which seems to assume the PCs will manage to tidily wrap up the plot caused by this episode's Wedges within one session instead of spiraling off into unforeseen territory...

    \n Damn it! I'm torn between running Mouse Guard, Apocalypse World, Blowback, and now Smallville next!

    Has anyone played multiple sessions yet? Do players spend a lot of time looking through all the options on their character sheets? Do people spend a lot of time looking for ways to bring in dice rather than going with what feels most natural that moment? I've seen this be an issue with Dogs Traits and Burning Wheel FORKs.

    In line with Jason's original post, there is so much great advice in this book!

    My favorite... IN MEDIAS RES (p51-52), it discussing framing a scene in the middle of the action which is fairly common advice in many "how to GM" sections. But it tackles a common issue with an elegant solution. What if by cutting to the middle of a scene you end up skipping something a player really wanted to do first? Simple! Hand them a plot point and then let them spend it at anytime to reveal his past preparations mid scene. You could even incorporate a super quick flashback showing how it happened. Brilliant! The book is full of these tricks. I especially like that the Sneaky trait lets you spend a plot point to reveal something you palmed from the previous scene.

      \n EXTRAS:

      Extras are pools of dice. When you roll each die, you spend it for the rest of the episode. So if I have 2d8, I can use both 2d8 in a single roll or split up those two dice and add 1d8 to two separate rolls. Instead of 2d8, think of it as a pool of 1d8, 1d8. When you roll those dice, you spend them. You can roll as many or as few as you want. They refresh at the start of the next episode. And the most important thing to remember\u2026 you add these dice to your final result\u2026 not to your general die pool. So they are very powerful!

      PLOT POINTS:

      In most situations, people rolling against each other don't roll at the same time*. The active player rolls and may spend plot points before and after the roll. But once they declare their result, they are done. Then the reacting player does the same thing. In most cases, there shouldn't be a back and forth escalating war of\u2026 well if you spend X plot points\u2026 I will spend X+2! Also keep in mind, when you spend plot points, you give them to your opposition. But any plot points you earn in a contest can't be spent in that same contest.

      *When you aren't sure who the active player is, you both roll and whomever has the highest result is the active player. This may be one of the few times spending plot points may run into what you're talking about.

      CHALLENGING TRAITS:

      The book says when you challenge a relationship or value, you roll triple its value but then step down its normal value for the rest of the episode. I'm not 100% sure but I think that means you only roll the tripled value once. In general, that should be powerful enough to blow away your opposition (especially if you spend plot points to keep more dice in your final result).

      \n jenskot: your revised summary is correct. Resources are like a "roll 2, keep 1" benefit to your result. Once you use the Resource, it drops to a single die. As you say, you can also keep the second result for that use of the Resource but then you're down to nothing.
      \n
      \nYou basically get two uses out of your Extra or Location for the episode. The first use is skewed toward a higher number on account of it being rolling two and keeping the highest. Note that if somebody else has the same Extra or Location as you, them using it also counts. So you can cheese off other players with the same Resources by getting in there before they do.
      \n
      \nChallenged dice, Useful Details, bonus dice from triggered Distinctions or Abilities, etc etc all stay until the end of a Contest or Test. So, in the case of the triple die, they're there until one of you gives in or stresses the other. Then they go away and the die is stepped back. In some cases it's almost wise to Give In to a Contest where somebody's challenged one of their Drives because then later you can always come back and try again against their weaker Value or Relationship.

      \n I like. The one thing I would clarify is although neither side has to do their telegraphed actions in a situation where nobody Gives In, there's still a winner and a loser. The winner achieves what they were trying to do, they just don't get the loser to play the part they wanted them to play.

      Example from my other thread:

      Roxy Rocket's robbing a bank. Superman's being Superman.

      Superman: "Stop your evilness and come away with me to jail!" Telegraphed action: Roxy surrenders. He rolls dice.
      Roxy: "Never, Superfool, you best help me escape or I'll kill this cute widdle puppy dog!" Telegraphed action: Superman helps her escape. She rolls dice.

      They go back and forth.

      Superman wins. Roxy doesn't have to surrender, but Superman's player, the GM and the group (including Roxy's player) come up with a suitable result based on what Superman's player wants and what each of them narrated back and forth during the conflict. A good example would be Superman blasting her with laser eyes ("They're not called laser eyes!") and knocking her out and carting her off to jail, saving the puppy dog. Another one might be Superman stopping the robbery and saving the puppy dog, but Roxy makes her escape. It's all about Superman's priorities at this point, and what kind of Stress is going to be inflicted on Roxy. Maybe he just flat punches her out and Injures her or maybe he laughs in her face and makes her Angry, or maybe he takes her out so fast that she is Insecure about her position as bandit queen of Metropolis' skies.

      OR

      Roxy wins. Superman doesn't have to help her escape, but Roxy's player, the GM and the group (including Superman's player) come up with a suitable result based on what Roxy wants. A good example would be Roxy escaping on her rocket and throwing the widdle puppy dog off, making Superman have to divert to save it, or maybe she sets off a smoke bomb and in the confusion makes her escape, or maybe her ray gun actually injures Superman and she gets away before he recovers. It's all about what Roxy wants at this point, and the Stress being inflicted on Superman. Maybe he's Angry because she tricked him, or Exhausted due to being hit with a ray gun, or Insecure because a civilian got hurt or killed, or whatever.

      I think a lot of people look at "neither side gets what they telegraphed" as neither side getting ANYTHING they want. Which is not right. Someone still gets what they want. They just don't get the other side to do what they want.

      Posted By: noclueThings happen, but Roxy hasn't escaped (I guess escaping without Superman's help would be kosher, because her goal was actually Superman helping her escape) and Superman hasn't stopped her Evilness .
      \nYep, that's exactly it. She's allowed to escape (without Superman helping) if she wins. Superman is allowed to stop her evilness (without her surrendering) if he wins. Or neither of those two things could happen and some completely different thing could happen, focused on the desires of the winning character. What we know DOESN'T happen is Superman doesn't help and Roxy doesn't surrender.

      Like, the example in the book is this:

      If Lois can walk up to Clark, telegraph "Tell me everything you know about this Superman guy", roll a shitload of dice, win, and he has to tell her everything, then there's no show.

      So what happens instead is Lois telegraphs "Tell me everything you know about this Superman guy" and Clark telegraphs "lay off the Superman story, you won't get anywhere" and they all roll dice at each other, and if Lois wins, maybe she pisses off Clark with her pestering and he lets something slip about another situation (Anger Stress) or maybe she wears him down over time and gets him to make out with her and while they're making out she steals his keys to the top secret Daily Planet file cabinet (Exhausted Stress) or if he wins, maybe he deflects her with jibes about her love life and she flounces off, missing the next big tip that comes in (Insecure Stress) or whatever. Winning has to mean something or else why win?

      What DOESN'T happen is that Clark doesn't tell Lois everything he knows about this Superman guy, and Lois doesn't lay off the Superman story.

      This is why telegraphing is NEVER "what I want to do" and is always "what I want the other guy to do". Superman doesn't telegraph "I want to stop Roxy". NO, that's wrong. He has to telegraph what he wants Roxy to do.
