\n I haven't been keeping up with what happens in the RPG scene lately. And when I say "lately," I mean "during the past six years". It was, for instance, a surprise to me that The Forge -- where I used to hang out a lot -- is closed.

But I'm back, I'm planning to stay, and I would like to catch up to what has been happening. What are the games that have made an impact in the last half decade?

My shelf contains the cool stuff from back then, games like My Life with Master, Dogs in the Vineyard, The Mountain Witch and Polaris. But nothing much that was published since then. (Though I recently bought a couple of Bundles of Holding, from which I'm now going to try 3:16.) Please tell me about what I've been missing!

    \n Welcome back, Victor!

    I'm going through all of my RPG .pdfs at the moment, now that I have a tablet to read them on. Here are some of the ones, so far, that I'm super-keen to play/run. No time to link to them, unfortunately, but they should be fairly googleable.

    - Don't Rest Your Head: insomniac-horror gaming with a fascinating resource economy (Related game: Itras By: a 1920s surrealist setting)
    - Durance: a space-prison game from the creator of Fiasco, and based on the Australian penal-colony experience
    - Lacuna (Part 1) Second Attempt: another surrealist setting with a dash of corporate paranoia. The situation is sort of Inception/The Matrix-esque.
    - Stalker: diceless scavenger-punk roleplaying in a city where the laws of physics have been shredded
    - Until We Sink: a lovely small scale LARP about guests on an island resort that is sinking in to the ocean
    - The Quiet Year: a gentle post-apocalyptic map-building game about how communities deal with political and environmental problems.

    I echo all of the above recommendations.

    \n Some weird suggestions.

    Montsegur 1244: if you take on the game with the intention of loosing yourself in the character, the combination of setting and characters will make the choice in the end pretty obvious: if you want to burn or live.

    A Thousand Years Under the Sun: build a world's history together with your friends.

    Nano-games of all sorts: roleplaying games that are no longer than 1-2 pages. What fascinates me with these games are their structure of how to explain roleplaying games.

    None of these games are my cup of tea (perhaps the nano-games), but you should really know about them.

    \n Smallville is a stealth dirty hippy indie game in superhero tv show camouflage, corrupting our youth with things like characters' values and relationships being as important as any superpowers (and superpowers are optional -- one point of its design is so that superpowered characters and mundane characters can co-exist without the former overshadowing the latter.) It's kinda sorta like Fate if aspects had different numeric values (or so it feels to me on reading it -- I haven't gotten to play it.)

    If you prefer to see the system abstracted away from the setting, check out the Cortex Plus Hacker's Guide, which includes the rules for the Cortex Plus Dramatic system (underlying Smallville), and its cousins, Cortex Plus Action (first seen in Leverage, a caper tv show), and Cortex Plus Heroic (first seen in Marvel Heroic Roleplaying.)

    (Edited to add parenthetical about superpowers being optional.)

    \n The biggest influences have been Fiasco (and anything from Jason Morningstar, really) and Apocalypse World for sure. I feel like both games have spawned new paradigms and have influenced a lot of what I've seen lately. There's also a big time Old School Renaissance movement going on: back-to-basics OD&D clones are a big thing (not so much around here, but in general it's definitely A Thing). Fate Core just came out and it's a very clean version of the system. I expect to see a lot coming out there.

    There's been TONS of great games beyond these, of course, but those are the biggest ones I immediately think of.

    \n One thing which was only cursorily mentioned (by Steve Hickey) are the Norwegian style games, including Matthijs Holter's Archipelago (now in its third release) and - my absolute favorite - Society of Dreamers, also by Matthijs Holter. The former is campaign-oriented (but viable for one-shots), the latter is one-shot oriented (though it can take a substantial amount of time, depending on your playstyle).

    Vincent Baker's works in-between DitV and AW are also worth checking (Poison'd, In a Wicked Age), as well as Meg Baker's works: 1001 Nights and, especially, Psi*Run.
    And what about Mouse Guard, sometimes described as "Burning Wheel light"?

    As for new funding/market strategies, the latest development, after the peak of the Kickstarter craze, is author-designers using Patreon to fund the making of small, free games: this includes Ben Lehman and Joe Mcdaldno (as well as, er, myself).

    \n Within the Apocalypse World family of games, I'm a big fan of Sagas of the Icelanders.

    I think it's important to note that when people port AW they're not only changing the genre, but often cutting away features that made, in my view, AW especially cool, and re-working play into something that make the purpose of play something different.

    An example: I think Dungeon World is a really smart game that takes the efficient resolution mechanic from AW and builds a terrific, efficient version of D&D. But all the stuff in AW about interpersonal relationships, characters being caught outside of their comfort zones when conflict arrives, what sort of person the character will be when all is said and done\u2026 all this is gone. I'm not faulting DW for not having those things. I'm saying it didn't want them and was right to take them out and the game works because they're not there.

    But since Victor is coming in cold, I think it's important to point out that the difference in games "Powered by the Apocalypse" goes far beyond the Color being created out of rolling 2d12.
