\n RPG Con where violence isn't the answer?

James @NerdNYC wrote:
It would be interesting to have a Recess theme where none of the adventures could be solved through violence.
\nMy first thought\u2026 awesome!

A con where in every game, you can NOT solve the game's problem with violence!

That doesn't mean there is no violence in the game. Maybe violence is the problem you are trying to solve. But violence can't solve the problem. It either fails or seemingly succeeds in the short term but makes the problem far worse in the future.

My second thought\u2026 will GMs volunteer? Will players come?

But you lose and gain people no matter what you do. Last Recess was video game themed which meant we lost several older regular male gamers and gained many more female teenagers. I suspect this would be similar but on a much more massive scale.

We could add a charity element where we help raise money for victims of violence.

What do you think?

    \n My first reaction is: isn't that already true in games like Polaris, Dogs in the Vineyard, Mouse Guard, Apocalypse World, etc.? In those games, violence never really "solves" anything, necessary, but there's still a lot of violence in those games because 1) not all the characters are really that interested in solving problems, 2) solving problems is hard and attempts can sometimes lead to misunderstandings and violence, and 3) according to the premises of those games, violence is easy, readily available at hand, and assumed to be prevelant.

    So what parts are you really suggesting be changed, for such a meetup?

    -- Are all the PCs interested in solving problems rather than seeking their own interests or causing trouble for others (getting revenge, etc.)?
    -- Are the problems solvable without things getting really ugly? What happens when folks try to solve problems and it doesn't work out?
    -- Is violence really difficult, unavailable, or just assumed to not be prevelent in the game?

    Finally, the other place where violence creeps in is as a consequence for failing or otherwise messing things up. So, is failure not an option in such games or does failure just never lead to violence, however bad the failure?

    \n Taking violence off the table is a stilted and dishonest path for exploring nonviolence.

    I think Apocalypse World is a really honest and exciting exploration of how violence plays a part in solving our problems. It does amazing and lucrative things in the short term. It can even help you win your goals. But it doesn't reduce the amount of problems that a community faces, and once you hit about 4 sessions of play you start to witness the ways in which your violence isn't making things better.

    The best way to explore nonviolent solutions in roleplaying games seems to be: put violence on the table, and be honest about how powerful it is. Put nonviolence on the table, and be honest about how difficult and slow-moving it is. Explore the many-faceted consequences of whatever actions the characters take. Be honest about the fact that there isn't a holy grail of conflict mediation. See what happens.

    \n I'm not on the right continent, but it's a great idea.

    I do sometimes get weary of how ubiquitous violence is in gaming. The average PC, almost irrespective of genre, has a kill count that if they really existed would make them famous for centuries. The psychological impact of having killed tens, perhaps hundreds of people is hard to picture.

    Violence is cool if it drives something else, if it means something. It's a sauce, but most games (including those I play and run) lather it on as if pouring ketchup through a firehose at everything from a hamburger to a sherry trifle.

    Personally I'd like to see violence in rpgs slapped down, beaten up, sucker-punched, kicked in, blown to pieces, stabbed, slashed, bludgeoned, garrotted, nuked from orbit, shot in the head, buckwheated, hung, drawn and quartered. I'd happily see violence take a critical to location 12.

    So yeah, great idea. I hope you guys do it.

    Posted By: jenskotPosted By: wyrmwoodI can see this being immensely frustrating if folks aren't seeing eye to eye on what constitutes violence. "What do you mean slapping him means that both our lives will be ruined, but it's OK if I just want to verbally abuse him over the next week till he attemps suicide?" A strawman perhaps, but assuming everone has the same expectations on what is violent is going to lead to such confusions. Especially since notions of what is violent behavior and what is not will likely be part of a player's identity.
    \nFrustrating but also eye opening! It would be interesting to see people's different reactions. The initial game might be bumpy but it could lead to fantastic conversations afterwards. Also, so many gamers find social conflict to be controversial, it would be curious just to see how that would be handled.

    This is also another good reason to also offer games that have no violent component at all as an easy alternative option.

    Part of my point is more that you need to frame this discussion / ambiguity or you'll end up with problems. Spontanteous communication of different perspectives may happen or you might just have people thinking other people are stupid, crazy, or abusive. Indeed the later can easily occur with an attempt to discuss.

    If individual games (even avowedly no violence games) don't clarify what they mean by violence or that this particular amiguity is to be explored (possibly to the deteriment of exploring the consequences of violence), you run the risk of different perspectives driving things apart.

    Also bear in mind you get a very different conversation if you talk about what is peaceful versus what is violent.

    \n It's also possible to inject anti-violence elements into otherwise straightforward RPG material, right?

    Dumb example: In my Dungeon World game at Origins, the paladin got set up as this raging dualism asshole who thought goblins were the embodiment of evil, a stain upon the land, the whole nine yards. He convinced the party that they were basically devils from hell. So when they decided to kidnap a pair of them to find out some goblin stuff, I gave them a mother and daughter who didn't really fit the stereotype and had their own very sympathetic agenda. The paladin still wanted to slaughter them, but everyone else was like, "dude, really? They are just like us. Let's find a better way." Which was nice. Later on there were big fights as usual, but this scene provided a lot of difficult choices for them to make related to the role of violence in their character's lives. They badly compromised their worthy mission by sparing those two goblins, which made a pretty cool statement.
