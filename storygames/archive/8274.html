\n I posed my question about the effects of mechanics on play on a couple of different forums:
\nhttp://www.story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=8268&page=1#Item_0
\nhttp://forum.rpg.net/showthread.php?t=428070
\nhttp://forum.rpg.net/showthread.php?t=428069
\nhttp://www.therpgsite.com/showthread.php?t=12882
\n And one common response (thought certainly not the majority) was something like this:
\n"I am not the rule book's bitch, the rules are my bitch"
\n Now, this is a quaint response and the first time I saw it, I dismissed it as someone not understanding the question. But the more "me too" responses I received, the more I thought about this phenomenon. The idea that some people are offended by the notion that an inanimate object (or the author through this paper proxy) can influence them or even somehow control their actions. Why are they so threatened by this concept? I still don't really know.
\n
\n When I think about this topic, to me, the answer is obvious, of course, mechanics influence me, the real question is, "how much?"
\n
\n Still, I wonder if these sorts of theories will always have an air of controversy so long as there are highly individualistic people out there?
\n What do you guys think?

    \n Incompatible hobbies. Okie dokie.

    I think there's a RPing hobby that's all about playing out long-standing rituals with old social groups that have a very thorough understanding (possibly not elucidated) of how they work together. The system matters only to provide a common touchstone: We're all agreeing to play HERO because we can all suss out the numbers and probabilities, and we know how to fudge and tweak to get somewhere comfortable. Priority 1 is continuity and institutional memory: Hey, remember that Rolemaster game we played where Galfor the Dwarf played "toss the kobold?" Mechanics are nearly irrelevant for these folks, and they're carrying out their rituals despite -- not because of -- the rules. Are they having "fun"? You'd have to ask them. The ritual is fulfilling some kind of need, probably social.

    There's an RPing hobby that's centered on buying books, reading, studying, posting to forums, and participating in the community in every way other than actually playing. This scene has always been big but the Internet is the great enabler. They're engaging their imagination on the page, not between players. Is this "really" roleplaying? I don't think the publishers mind. They're not harming anyone. I feel like this is the great silent majority of roleplaying.

    There's an RPing hobby that's all about playing the goddamned game. The rules are the first and best authority, which is a necessary prerequisite for the very concept of "system mastery": There has to be a system there to master. You may see collectors in this hobby, but they're all about playing. You'll almost certainly see long-standing groups, but they're all about playing. This hobby is, to me, most akin to a hardcore boardgaming scene. This is, to me, a fairly recent phenomenon (although I've seen this priority back quite a ways -- probably early White Wolf is when it started standing apart from the ritual players).

    Here's why they're incompatible.

    The ritual players have nothing to say to the collectors, because they collect games for a totally different reason. They're on a quest to find the game that best enables them to fulfill their ritual. They start out rules-heavy, probably, and move to rules-light because they don't want rules to "get in the way of the story."

    The ritual players have nothing to say to the "play the goddamned game" players for, hopefully, obvious reasons. The biggest incompatibility is that there's a conflict of interest between fulfilling the ritual of play and playing the game. Imagine going to a boardgaming convention and sitting down at a table for some Risk. Everyone else at the table knows each other, and they have a deep understanding that Mark always takes Europe because he has an agreement with Bill not to attack Asia. WTF?

    The collectors have nothing to say to the play-the-game folks because, again, there's a conflict of interest. The collectors are there to talk about the fiction, the mechanics, the industry, designer gossip, all this social stuff that surrounds gaming but isn't actually gaming. The play-the-game folks are there to play.

    Are there crossovers and edge cases? Of course. Nobody belongs to just one of these and I'm not talking identity politics. But based on a lot of years of participating, I don't see much meaningful interaction between them.

    This also leaves out big swaths of RPing that conveniently exist outside most people's understanding of the hobby. What do LARPers have to say to book collectors? What do fanfic writers have to say to play-the-game folks? Shit, what do MET LARPers have to say to SCA folk?

    As far as I'm concerned, D&D is somebody's houserules made official. Do lots of people dig it? Sure. Do lots of people play it hardcore-Rules-As-Written? Sure. I'm also pretty sure that just as many people (more?) are out there drifting the rules too.

    Sometimes that rule drift is intentional and sometimes it isn't. For example, lots of people whined and bitched about how "fast" advancement was in 3rd ed D&D compared to AD&D. Funny thing is, it really wasn't. See, a lot of people apparently missed that in AD&D you were supposed to award XP for gold/treasure as well as killing things. But plenty of "hardcore" GMs decided that was just way over the top so they houseruled it out. No XP for treasure. People show up and find this houserule in operation and think "that's the rules" and don't bother questioning it. 3rd Ed hits and folks are comparing a brand new system (Rules as Written) to their own drifted ways of running AD&D and there's a whole chunk of bitching right there.

    I can't speak for anyone else as to why they happen to treat rules the way they do. For me, it's because I don't feel obligated in any fashion to slavishly follow some chucklehead's idea of how I should have fun. I pick up a book and if it looks cool and seems to match the sort of thing I'm interested in, I'll get it. If I find something not working, I'll either see if other folks have had a similar issue or I'll just change it myself.

    Heck, here's one thing that can force rule changes awfully quick: Number of players.

    A game like In A Wicked Age? I picked it up and it seemed cool, but it's basically worthless to me. Why? Because right now if I want to run or play anything other than D&D or a White Wolf game, I have _one_ player.

    Most of these rpgs? They don't consider you having less than 4 players. And it's not like the indy/small press scene is much better. They _are_ better because the few games I know of that work for only 2 (or maybe 3) people come from the small press side in the first place. But it's not like anyone is really working to make their games friendly for small numbers of people.

    So almost from the get go, you're going to have to start ignoring things and changing things in order to have a "good" game. Oh well, I suppose if you want to play D&D by-the-book you can have your one player and have them play a second character and the GM can play a couple of NPCs to fill out the minimum of 4 that the game assumes. I've never seen folks really have fun like that though, and I certainly haven't bothered with it myself. I just change the rules until it works the way I want.

    I'm frankly puzzled by the insistence that the rules be adhered to in such a strict fashion. I mean, if it's some sort of rpg tournament game or something where scores are being kept and all that? Sure I get it. You're competing, so you need to have a clear standard of behavior and interaction with rules in order to be able to judge "who's the best". But sitting around the kitchen table? Folks I hang with over the years can be a pretty competitive lot, but we haven't been competing when we sit down to play an rpg.

    Heck, there haven't been that many strictly competitive rpgs done in the first place. AD&D came about in part because of tournament play. After that...no "major" (as in sold in a gaming store) rpg springs to mind other than Rune. At least until the indy/small press scene popped up. Everyone I've ever known that wanted to do the competition thing got that out of their system with some other game (Magic, videogames, boardgames, sports, drinking, whatever).

    Edit: I'll also note, I've moved around an awful lot over the last 20 years. So I don't have any of this "established group" thing going on. Every time I have to start over and find new people to hang with and new people to play with. Right now I'm in the process of moving to New Zealand, so there's a whole _other_ rpg culture I'm going to have to learn to interact with.

    I also don't self-identify as a "gamer". RPGs just happen to be something I do, like weight lifting, running, reading a book and all sorts of other stuff.

    \n I think the question is actually a pretty complex one you're answering. Things like how people got started in the hobby, the reason they've continued participating, heck, maybe even the rule set they started with and the style of the GM...

    Me, I started with Red Box D&D. A simple enough system. As time's gone on, I've played so many games I can't remember 'em all. I've learned that there's _always_ something that isn't covered by the rules. I actually learned that pretty quick.

    Since there's always going to be something not covered by the rules, it means that the rules are really only functioning as "guide posts". More complicated rulesets are simply setting up more posts for a GM to guide the game by, but the GM (and players) are still going to wind up venturing off the beaten track.

    For me, this meant not only that the rules are guideposts but also that there's a limit to the number of rules that are useful overall. As more and more rules accumulate, more and more things are "defined" but any one group is only going to be using a fraction of those rules in the first place at any given time.

    So I tend towards systems that are on the lighter side of the rules continuum. It means I and the players have less to try and hold in our heads at any given point in time, and if something comes up in the course of play, we can address it and move on.

    No one person (this can include the rulebook) is the sole arbiter of how we're going to have fun. As a group of people, we're already compatible. We're sitting down to play a game of a particular sort, so we're "compatible" there too.

    I guess it's that social contract thing. I expect to be able to work stuff out with the people at the table. The rulebook is helpful for keeping everyone on the same page, but if something's not working... we'll change it. Because what we're doing at the table isn't being created _by_ the rules, the rules are simply helping us to shape what takes place.

    Currently PDQ is my "go to" game system. I'm perfectly happy to play a variety of games, all the way up to Unisystem. After Unisystem, I consider the rules to be too heavy and don't have any interest in it. I'll _play_ something heavier than Unisystem, but I'm not going to bother learning the rules. Which means that someone else is going to have to carry the burden of me not knowing the rules. If that's fine with 'em, then it costs me nothing. Most people aren't down with that though.

    I expect people at the table to eventually have an idea what they're doing. If I tell 'em to make an attack roll or a skill roll, I don't expect to have to explain that process after 3 months of playing a game every week. After that? I'm pretty easy going. My rule of thumb is, if I don't know the rule I'll flip through the book. If I can't find the rule in a minute or less, I'm making something up. If I find the rule and it looks pointlessly complicated (Attacks of Opportunity to beat an often pounded drum), then I'm making something up. If a player has a suggestion for how to handle it and it sounds cool? We'll try it out.

    After the game, I'll evaluate how it turned out and make a decision about what to do in the future. Then I'll make a note of it on a sheet of paper (if I need to) and then let everyone know where I stand on things. I expect players to tell me if it works for them or not, and if it doesn't then we'll figure something else out.

    If I reach a point where it's just a hassle? I'll end the game. I did that back in November for the Witch Hunter: Invisible World game I was running. People were willing to try it because it looked similar enough to White Wolf games that there'd be a minimal barrier to entry in terms of system mastery was concerned. I ran the game for a while and just wasn't happy with the way things were going. I modified a few rules and it made it better but still wasn't really satisfying. So I sent an email that said, "Folks seem to be willing to play Witch Hunter, but they're not really _pumped_for the next game. And there's some stuff that just bogs down. So unless people are actively enjoying the game, I'm going to pull the plug." I knew one person wasn't into it and I only got a response from one person that said they were really grooving on it, so down the game went.

    "Damage for what?"

Unhappy, but too shy to really talk to the GM about it back then, I simply quit going to the game.

    So... yes to both questions, for me.

For the rest of us (and probably most people in the world) we need some help.

I don't mean "ignoring encumberance", I'm thinking more of a great example (can't find it) on RPGNet where they played InSpectres at a Con or something, and the dude running it ran it exactly like Ghostbusters: And all the "unique" things about the game, like the confessional scenes (and some other things, I forget; but mostly the confessionals) were discarded because the GM thought they were dumb.

    After which, the player complained that InSpectres wasn't fun, because it was like a watered-down version of Ghostbusters. When in truth it was like a watered-down version of Ghostbusters because the group (well, mostly the GM being an ass; the core issue was that it was a con game, rather than a game with friends somewhere) decided not to use any of the unique rules or playstyles the game is built upon.

    That cycle, the "try the game; discard/change major rules; have a sucky time; the time would have been at least bearably more interesting if those rules were followed; player/GM then complains about the *game* being unfun" thing, I think is retarded, and why I think rules mean something sometimes.

    Other than that, there's not much to say from my court. It comes down to the chaos of individual tables, people, relationships, then how the rules help them get their game on from there. I'm not that great with sweeping generalizations.

    \nI think Paul strikes to the heart of it. The real issue at work here isn't really anything to do with individuality or coarse language or being threatened or whatnot. The issue is responsibility.

    A game session might suck. It might be excellent. Either way, people are going to decide who is responsible.

    One group might say "Well, heck, these rules are just terrible. We all tried our best, but there's just nothing good in here."

    Another group might say "It's all Bob's fault. He was running the game, it's his game, and he failed in his duty to entertain us." Alternatively, "Wow, Bob is awesome. He's running an awesome game and the game is awesome and he is awesome."

    (Of course, these are two extremes, and lots of people fall somewhere in the spectrum.)

    I think what's actually in evidence here is the palpable disdain the people in Group #2 often have for the people in Group #1. When people have embraced this sense of duty, they take it very seriously. Any perceived attack against it, however slight, is taken very seriously.

    There's a whole mess of dynamics at play here; if you're going to try to unravel some of them, I wish you all the best.
