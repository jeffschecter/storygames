\n So board games have the same problem, when I say that I am going to my board games Meetup people often ask me, "what sort of board games do you play?"

I honestly don't know how to answer, because describing the games I play requires being familiar with a terminology that means nothing to someone uninitiated.

RPG's have a similar if not worse problem. If you want to tell someone you're playing something other than dungeons & dragons (not that there's anything wrong with that game) people look at you surprised not having known there are other RPGs.

How do you deal with this issue? What do you say to get people interested? (Or simply explain the experience in some way)

    \n Fair question, it's funny I think I find it easiest to explain it to folks who have never played any tabletop story game/Role playing game. For them I just go conceptual. Hey it's like organized make believe but we all agree on some rules to guide the story.

    But usually it's Trad gamers and boardgames.

    I personally usually just say "yeah there are other RPG's cool stuff on a spectrum of kind of like D&D games on one end and some very very different sorts of games on the other"

    That part is not so difficult, but I'm not sure where to send them to find out more info. Even the somewhat limited knowledge of the games I know it took quite a while to acquire.

    \n There isn\u2019t necessarily a good \u201cjumping off point\u201d for story games or indie RPGs.

    Your best bet might be Tabletop (there are good sessions of both Fiasco and Misspent Youth) and/or all the podcasts out there that do story game playthroughs.

    Some Kickstarter intro videos could be helpful, too! They tend to be brief and to the point.

    In the other hand, if you ARE asking about introducing and explaining:

    For trad gamers:

    \u201cI\u2019m interested in role playing games that have a more dynamic narrative; they are designed to worry less about winning fights and more about characters, their stories, and difficult personal decisions. Some of these games don\u2019t even have a GM, so everyone gets to contribute creatively to what\u2019s happening.\u201d

    For board gamers:

    \u201cThe games I enjoy are tabletop games, like board games, but instead of manipulating figures on a board, there\u2019s a strong component of imagined or fictional material. As a player, you\u2019re not limited to the actions the game has specified for you, but can try a nearly infinite number of options to solve problems or get ahead. It\u2019s like participating in making a tv show, movie, or novel, while also being a character within it.\u201d

    \n I've always found that storygames are much easier to sell non-gamers on (in big part because they're very much designed to be parseable by non-gamers, whereas trad presumes gaming experience, and because storygames use skills more commonly held by non-gamers than tactics and strategy) as long as the DnD comparison isn't made, or as long as it's immediately shut down if it's made.
    "Is this like DnD?"
    "No, not at all."
    I can imagine it would be harder with neo-trad stuff and other more orthodox indie stuff though, since the answer with some of those games to "Is this like DnD?" is "ehh, sort of". whereas the stuff I play is entirely unlike DnD.
