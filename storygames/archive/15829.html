\n Like the player-character in one of jenskot's "what would you do if..." experiments, I am in the happy position of helping design the backer rewards and bonus goals for the Dwimmermount Kickstarter announced today at Grognardia and Autarch.
\n
\nThe bonus goal I'd like to direct promotion toward is the donation of the original Dwimmermount materials to the public via PlaGMaDA (which is running an exciting Kickstarter of its own) and to scholars through the special collection at Texas A&M.; I'm hoping that James' example will help inspire the donation of other original gaming play-notes to be preserved digitally and as research artifacts. If you or someone you know has awesome stuff for the PlaGMaDA archive, why not contribute it now? And to encourage the Science Fiction Research Collection to continue its acquisition and curation of gaming items, positive mentions of the importance of their work go a long way.
\n
\nDirected promotion on backer rewards:
\nOne of the ones I think is going to be coolest, but also most conceptually challenging, is a play aid to teach the GM the situation of the factions of the dungeon - by showing how to draw them into a vinyl battlemat-like infographic of the dozen-level dungeon with wipe-erase marker - and then track how the players' exploration change the situation dynamically. What should that look like? What would you find most useful for running a megadungeon as the tentpole for a long-term campaign? Are there insights from play mats, boardgames, etc. that could make the design tight and elegant?
\n
\nUndirected on backer rewards:
\nExtrapolating from the rapid progress of Autarch's current Kickstarter and the fact that Grognardia is an order of magnitude bigger than anything else in the OSR, the Dwimmermount crowdfunding is going to be able to make all kinds of cool rewards possible. What would you like to see?

    \n Although it's slightly different, for the Bookhounds of London special edition I put together a pack for each of the lucky 100 subscribers. Bookhounds is a Trail of Cthulhu campaign frame set in the murky world of book dealers in 30s London. It was packed with a special edition of the Book of the Smoke, a guide to occult London, written from the point of view of a 30s occultist and containing no game or modern references, it looks like a 30s book. These came with a piece of 1930s ephemera, perhaps a small book or pamphlet, a masonic apron and in one case a microscope and slides. Then each one got two or three period postcards and some coins (some English, some foreign). Furthermore we commissioned an occult map of London which was cut up and distributed to European subscribers. US subscribers got an occult token. All of this was put in a rucksack stencilled with an occult symbol and the name of the author of the occult guide. There's a big thread on Yog-sothoth.com with photographs of the contents of many of the packs as the receivers try to understand the clues which point to the murder of the author of the book.

    In effect we've made each pack a period artifact and included everyone in a mystery.

    \n More options is always good; a great thing about Kickstarter at the macro and micro levels is that you can throw things out there to see if they appeal.

    David, it's as good to see you thought of the ones we did go with as it is that we also thought of reasons not to do those ones! Scaling up the top-level rewards just gets too expensive - we couldn't do the one where you get the Sultan + the maze because it wouldn't be affordable within the $10K cap imposed by Kickstarter.

    The creative input ones are a careful dance between letting people make their mark on the setting and the feeling that what we want to get is Dwimmermount as James originally envisioned it. That dance plays out really well when it involves actual play - we all love that the way elves are described will reflect him accomodating ideas his players brought to the table - so I've focused on opportunities to work actual play into the mix. Most visibly is the artist in residence program for the Adept illustration rewards; you don't have to say "would this art order really reflect this setting" because the artist is drawing something that did happen in a session - but that's also implied in the backer feedback from Neophyte on up. It's great when the person with the best idea for a place during their home session or at one of our events can post it at the forums and James will run with that as he's fleshing out his original GM notes with the memory of ideas that came up in his campaign. It's kind of artificial when the person who pays the right amount gets to name the place, and doesn't drive making the product better.

    GB Steve, I love that package! Kickstarter is also awesome because it lets you package together experiences and products - at the most basic level you're getting the experience of being in on the ground floor and backstage during rehearsal - and that is a great experience that's directly relevant to the game and what gamers like.

    Was there a feedback loop between the mystery and the product - like did you listen to see what people decided was the solution, and then write the best ideas into the book? (Oh how much better would shows like Lost be if they were crowdfunded and could reduce the delay in this loop; as we've said here about the Cracked article it's clear they are using the GM method of making it up as they go along, but handicapped by it being months too late to use the GM method of letting your players supply ideas when they think they're solving a pre-existing mystery.)

    \n With ACKS, we found that there weren't enough people who ordered softcover to support a traditional print run. The lesson (rightly or wrongly) was to focus on getting the economy of scale in the format that was less easy to do with customer-side POD. People who would have paid $25 but not $40 are still customers at the $10 level, and enabling them to do softcover POD makes it easier to handle shipping. Getting books overseas proves to be a major issue - in the 21st century gamers are surprisingly worldwide - so if we can work with a local equivalent of Lulu that's a big advantage.
