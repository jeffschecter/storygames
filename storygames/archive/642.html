Okay, so I can't hope to say everything I want to say about this topic in one post (especially since I have other things to do this evening), but an email conversation with Judd requires me to get this off my chest.

Judd and I exchanged a few messages about the "system" of freeform games. Judd said that there must be something going on there that governs how people interact with each other in such situations and that we should be able to basically codify that into actual rules and mechanics and mimick that kind of play. The recent discussion of Theatrix is also relevant here, because I suspect that's exactly what they tried to do. Doing some of that can be really cool and effective, like the way Ben and Shreyas formalized inter-player negotiation in Polaris and Mridangam, respectively.

However, formalizing the freeform "system" (if you can even call it that) of a specific group of people in a set of rules and mechanics loses one of the most crucial aspects of freeform, which is its ability to change and adapt to new situations, new players, or just the constantly changing tastes, priorities, and (dare I say it?) creative agendas of the players involved. Everything is arbitrary in a socially retricted way. You can't do anything, but you can do anything you can convince the other players is a good idea, which can be dramatically inconsistent with the way issues have been resolved before. Some systems, notably Universalis (but also Polaris, etc.) have tried to turn this kind of negotiation and convincing of your fellow players into part of the game, using inter-player conflict as fuel for inter-character conflict.

However, in the vast majority of freeform games I've been involved with, inter-player negotiation isn't viewed as something enjoyable or something that escalates tension. Instead, it is a royal pain, something to be avoided at all costs. You only stop and negotiate something if a player objects (which you are encouraged to do only when absolutely necessary) or when the narrative has become self-contradictory. So codifying something that most freeformers (again, in my experience) avoid like the plague and making it a crucial part of your system is neat and all, but, if your point is to replicate the advantages of freeform in a non-freeform game (and you might not care about that), you're barking up the completely wrong tree.

I think trying to turn freeform into a set of rules and mechanics (something that we already understand) as a way of grokking it usually tends to destroy freeform in the process, not allowing it's real nature to translate well. You may end up with a really cool ruleset, if you try such a thing, but it's not necessarily going to play or feel like freeform.

So how do we talk about freeform in a systematic fashion?

Within the rather different worlds of systems thinking, organizational structuring, communications theory, lingustics, and anthropology, there is the concept of a community of practice. This concept was first developed by Jean Lave and Etienne Wenger and is detailed in the following publications, if people want to check them out:

    Jean Lave and Etienne Wenger (1991) Situated Learning: Legitimate Peripheral Participation. Cambridge University Press.

    Wenger, Etienne. (1998) "Communities of Practice: Learning as a Social System," Systems Thinker. http://www.co-i-l.com/coil/knowledge-garden/cop/lss.shtml

    Wenger, Etienne. (1999) Communities of Practice: Learning, Meaning, and Identity. Cambridge University Press.

According to Wenger a community of practice is defined by three things:

1. Joint Enterprise
2. Mutual Engagement
3. Shared Repertoire

The simple version explaination of this is as follows:

A group of people come together to do something, whether it's to live together in harmony or to play Dungeons & Dragons. They are together because they have a shared purpose, a joint enterprise. Their joint enterprise need not be fully defined in the beginning. They may develop a sense of purpose over time and only slowly begin to uncover what they're all about. Also, their joint enterprise can shift or change as the relationships of group members and the ties binding them together evolve over time.

They are involved in communicating with each other in all sorts of ways. Vocal communication, body language, etc. And more than that, they are committed to communicating with one another because it is crucial to their shared purpose. You can't build a house with someone if you can't communicate with them. So you have to continually be engaged with other people in the group in an effort to understand them and to have them understand you.

A by-product of working together with a group of people on a project is that you developed regular patterns of behavior, a shared repertoire, which allows you to communicate more easily. You develop shorthand for certain communication acts. You develop the kinds of everyday ritual activities that Chris Lehrich is fascinated by. You develop what Neel Krishnaswami once dubbed a "shared symbolic language": you know that butterflies are the spirits of the dead and represent both death, the past, and the ancestors. All of these regular patterns of behavior are accumulated as a part of communicating and working together on something.

Over time, the shared repertiore becomes a kind of Status Quo, a language that outsiders have to learn how to speak if they want to communicate with members of the group and really be a part of the community and the joint enterprise. This is quite likely while some people don't feel comfortable on The Forge, long-running collaborative fanfic games, or other places where the existing community, over time, has developed an ideosyncratic way of communicating/behaving among themselves based on many, many previous conversations. The Status Quo is also a way of doing things, a set of fixed behavior patterns. Over time these become traditions and then, eventually, "the way things are done," even though, when they first started, they may have been an accident or a misunderstanding.

Of course, the shared repertiore doesn't have to become hegemonic or a kind of Status Quo if the people involved keep changing the "rules" or the overall goal, or changing situations call for different kinds of solutions or approaches. And so you can see various communities of practice evolving over the course of human history, from communities like Harvard or Cambridge to communities like RPGnet or the Forge to communities like your Friday night Shadowrun game. Even communities with a strong sense of Status Quo can change if enough social pressure is exerted by members to overturn habitual practices or change the overall shared enterprise.

Anyway, that's an introduction to the basic concept.

To return to my original point, I think most freeform games tend to operate as very clear and pure communities of practice. Every roleplaying group is a community of practice, but, when a group chooses to adopt an outside rules set as the basic for play, that can sometimes obscure the fact that they are actually making everything up, in a certain sense. Not every group plays D&D or Exalted the same way, with various rules or subsystems being emphasized or ignored by different people at different times. If you pay attention to what happens in a group, you can actually observe improvized practices become habitual, most clearly through "inside jokes" and the like, which are a clear example of shared repertoire. But the same thing happens with the way different rules are invoked or different situations are resolved.

When do you decide when you should roll for something and when you should just talk your way through it? Pay attention to the way this is determined, next time you start playing a new game with a new group. In the beginning, rolls may be called for on a somewhat inconsistant basis. But, after a few sessions, you should notice that everyone has a sense of when die rolls are needed and when they're not, based on the way you've handled them in previous sessions.

    Gah! I wrote a long response to you, Judd, but the internet ate it!

    Here, read Erick Wujick's very short article on dice vs. diceless play!
    http://www.indie-rpgs.com/articles/24/

    Your character approaches a ravine, you roll dice, you succeed! The GM says, "You leap over it!" Voila, dice-full play!

    Similarly, your character approaches a ravine. You say, "I leap over it easily!" The GM says, "What's your Aspect?" (You're playing Nobilis.) You say, "3." The GM says, "Hell yeah, you leap over it and probably do a triple lutz on your way over." Voila, diceless play! (The same example would work with negotiation in Polaris).

    Finally, your character approaches a ravine. You're playing Exalted. You say, "I jump over it easily and proceed on my way." One of the other players says, "Come on, you can do better than that! Where's the tension? Shouldn't you roll Dex + Athletics [dice!] or invoke a leaping Charm [diceless!]?" You say, well, the ravine doesn't really interest me. But I have a really cool idea for the wall of fire." The other player says, "Cool. What happens at the wall?" You continue playing. Voila, freeform!

    My point is this: you are playing freeform all the time in games. Whenever you say your character is doing something, no one objects, and you keep playing, that's freeform. Whenever you're brainstorming about what the next scene will be about: freeform. Whenever you're trying to negotiate a change to the way you're handling armor classes: freeform. When ever you handle anything without consulting mechanics or dice, that's freeform.

    And that's what Eliot's trying to say I think. Traditional play is just freeform with some additional stuff tacked on.

    To respond to Jess' anxious questions:

    One thing I really dig about the communities of practice stuff is that it gives us a way to talk about ALL roleplaying (not just tabletop, not just larp, not just online freeform) in a way that allows us to make generalizations about what people are doing that are still accurate. Everybody goes through the group socialization process. Everyone's involved in creating a negotiated space in which communication and play occurs. Etc.

    However, talking about things on such a meta-level, while great for analyzing the practice as a whole, is not necessarily going to make your play experience better. You might understand what's going on more clearly, but putting things into practice on the group level is going to require looking at your own community of practice, not on some meta-community that binds all of roleplaying together.

    So there's the huge sprawling community of practice that is ALL ROLEPLAYING.

    And there's the small, individual PLAY GROUPS.

    But in the middle, there are also other communities of practice. There is, for instance THE FORGE and STORY GAMES and PEOPLE WHO YAK ABOUT EXALTED ON RPGNET and PEOPLE WHO WORSHIP MONTE COOK and CHANGELING FANS all sorts of other medium-sized communities.

    And being a part of those communities of practice influences how we behave and conduct play in our own personal play groups. So I guess what I'm trying to say is that we can make generalizations at all sorts of levels, not just the super meta or the individual group.

    You might try to argue that "individual play groups which are heavily influenced by The Forge or are attempting to run Forge games of a certain era tend to exhibit these kinds of qualities." It's still a generalization, but it's based on the idea that, as a community of practice, various behaviors and tendencies of Forge participants have been normalized by their interactions. It's harder to be really sure of this when it comes to online communities who have talked about roleplaying but, in many cases, never actually played games together. But you could try to make a good case for it.

    Does that answer your question, Jess?
