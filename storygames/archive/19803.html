\n Does anyone else have a very strong gender bias when creating NPCs?

A player in my recent games has observed that the vast majority of my NPCs are male. Not just combatants, not just major actors, but authority figures, traders, passerby, victims... the works. I hadn't noticed this before, but it turns out its a pretty strong bias - if the "on screen" characters in a session are one-third female that's a very good session indeed. One-fifth is more usual.

To work on this, I'm developing tactics. Firstly, explicit goals that we can target and track:\n
    \n
  • \tI included good, strong female NPCs (i.e. there were some, without thinking about ratios. This is a kind of "starter target" that is easy and needs little book-keeping.)
  • \tAt least as many female as male NPCs came \u201con screen\u201ds
  • \tThe power balance of onscreen NPCs was roughly equal between the sexes
  • \tThe diversity of NPCs by age, role etc was comparable between the sexes (this is a classic one to mess up - easy to make only (a) young, attractive women plus a few (b) wizened old crones hunched over cauldrons)
  • The sexualisation/emphasis on the appearance of onscreen NPCs was roughly equal between the sexes
\nAnd some tactics I've tried or am about to try for achieving them:\n
    \n
  • Explict check in pre-session prep --- am I at, or above, gender parity in terms of prepared NPCs (including NPCs that predate this session - may need to compensate for past bias)
  • Have a "cull" of existing male NPCs - explicitly mark as "out of play for now", or even kill/incapacitate. Replace each with one or more women who fill a similar role.
  • Policy - all improvised-on-the-spot NPCs are women (to compensate for my tendency to conjure up men. This one is important for me as ~most of my NPCs start this way)
  • Be willing to consciously introduce e.g. five women in a row and make no comment on it (maybe no-one will notice... and if they do, would they have noticed five men in a row?)
  • Have a list of context-appropriate female name right there in front of you; leave the male names in the book or folder.
  • Don't make gender an explicit issue in the setting - don't make characters or cultures that are noticeably gender-biased
\n
Anyone else done something similar, or have any suggestions?

    \n I know this is true with my games as well, but it's intentional as a simulation element. In fact in our old west game female npc's are generally wives, teachers, missionary/nuns, or whores in keeping with that particular bit of history.

    Fact is, most societies are/have been patriarchal/male-dominated, so that leads to the most familiar realism element. In pure fantasy games (like our D&D), I greatly enjoyed purposefully crafting matriarchal races and cultures to get away from this. However there's still plenty of gender oppression in most societies.

    About the only games where it really doesn't figure in are superhero and sci-fi. Those games seem to be much better at creating gender neutral experiences.

    Of course, there are other things at work as well. I would wager that males tend to be more comfortable creating/using males. I'm also certain that some are just put off by the potential trap: "I want to be sure to include more female NPCs, so I'll make a 'rescue the damsel' game." Or far worse, to me at least, is the artificial forced balance that makes no sense within the narrative.

    So rather than worrying about it we do it like we try to do everything else in gaming - do whatever makes sense in the narrative and screw anyone that has a non-realism problem with it. 8-)

    Anyone have any tips for men looking to roleplay women?
    \nDepends heavily on the game for me. Some games look for more idealized literary depictions of humans with roots in various historical narrative genres, while others are all about your internal expression, and yet another kind want you to depict realistic, relatable types. Any of those types might be pretty inappropriate for a game that focuses heavily on something else.

    So yeah, my usual advice for anything at all: break it down analytically and act accordingly.

    More concretely, I've found that a solid baseline for almost all games I've played women in is to play the character as a human first, and then add some gender-appropriate superficial detail. Sometimes, where appropriate, the "superficial" detail may flower into an interesting and true to life fracture in the otherwise relatively gender-neutral characterization, while most of the time you'll at least be guaranteed a mild and game-appropriate portrayal, even if it so happens that nothing more comes out of the womanity of the character. I'm admittedly not much of a risk-taker in this regard; I've no need to go all drag queen until a game explicitly calls for it, so a relatively low-emphasis technique like this suits me fine. I'm not even much of a believer in gender essentialism, anyway, so playing women as if they were human suits me fine.

    For example, Zombie Cinema is a game where men end up playing female characters all the time, due to the requirements of the genre - it's a pretty drama-poor group (or perhaps one banking on a ho-yay subtext) that voluntarily makes a zombie movie with only male characters. At this point I wouldn't be surprised if half my experience roleplaying woman characters were in that game. My general experience is that it's not difficult to do a satisfying job on the role, either, by the above technique: just determine social class, politics, values, relationships and such for the character, and let the idea that the character is a woman do all the gender-related work for you. You do this by reminding the other players regularly about her femininity: do the same things you'd always do, except slip in some little mention about discarding your high heels before sprinting out and away from the zombies.

    This subtle, yet superficial, reinforcement of the mental imagery will then basically do all the interpretation work for you: you don't have to choose whether your character being female matters, and what it means, because you're merely representing her for the other players to react to. It's in fact the other players who decide whether they care, and what femininity will mean in this game; usually they do care, and often in Zombie Cinema we see machismo, feminism and romantic themes spark out as reaction to the relatively straightforward fact that there's a female protagonist in the story. Femininity here is something you impose upon the other players as something to react to, so it's not actually too important how you do the thespian side of it: as long as you express (my narrativism theory is all about expressing characters) the idea that the character is a woman strongly enough that the other players take note, they'll do the rest of the work. (Not that you can't do the expression ham-handedly, but that's why I suggest taking "she's just a human who happens to be a woman" as your baseline, and work from there - you'll avoid embarrassment and remain a salon-worthy contributor to the game.)

    Of course that same technique can be used the other way around, it's just that most of the time most of the players do not see anything remarkable in somebody being male, so they do not feel much need to react to it. That's what male-normativity in society and arts means, I guess. Play Maid, though, and you're suddenly in a game where it does indeed matter if your character is male, and it is indeed a good idea to sprinkle your contributions with minor flavour that reminds everybody of the fact.

    \n I'm running behind on this, but I *love* randomizing gender. It really gets you past your biases (and audience expectations) in a very simple, elegant way. (You'll be surprised at your own reaction to some of the choices it throws up at you, as well.)

    I used this in my game "The Silver Dragon's Tear":\n
    For each Personality, pick a position (e.g. mayor), role (e.g. Damsel), and roll one die for sex (1-2, male;
    3-4, female; 5-6, reroll: if the reroll yields a second 5-6 result, then the person\u2019s sex is ambiguous,
    somehow crossing expected gender roles, like a eunuch, a powerful female leader in an all-male
    religious order, or a man deemed \u201chonorary\u201d woman after he failed the Rites of Manhood).
    \nFor best effects, the order here is important: you don't say, "Ok, I've got a female character. What's her role?" Instead, you say, "Ok, the mafia's boss will be heading the meeting. Let's roll to see if it's a he, a she, or something less clear-cut."

I.e....

    "I'd love to come along, but I have my responsibilities. She gestures at the entire castle with a mesmerizing wave of her long fingers and gracefully picks up a goblet from a nearby tray-table."

    (Also, it's not like you didn't say your own controversial stuff: I know a lot of very good GMs who don't do voices at all. I use accents probably more than any GM I know. YMMV.)

    Anyway, more on topic, because I've done this gig before--hell, I made a world with strenuous gender equality (I kept careful check of who I had in positions of authority to make sure it was evenly split) with the exception of the throne, where there was a cultural preference for a Queen. Of course, my players described the world as a matriarchy, which seems to be a Greek word meaning gender equality.

    In the last year or so I've kit-bashed a couple of campaigns and in the process, expanded the roles of women. Slight spoilers for both below:

    a) Masks of Nyarlathotep: There are actually a few women in this, but practically none are developed to any great degree; I went about fixing that. First, I gender-flipped an important (but in the original text, MacGuffiny) NPC, Jackson Elias; making her an intrepid female reporter gave me a lot of insight into the character. (I was always going to try to get the PCs to be emotionally invested in Elias, but the gender flip helped me emotionally invest in the character.)

    Next was Hypatia Masters, who in the original text serves mostly as a literal vessel for body horror and is depicted for the most part as an airhead socialite. That wasn't going to stand, so instead I had her broker a deal with Nyarlathotep and become a powerful, near-immortal figure that could act as an ambiguous ally of the PCs.

    Finally, I pulled M'Weru off her mountain in Africa and made her an undercover asset, working with the PCs to manipulate her co-conspirators, and gave her a forceful personality and a lot of earned achievements to match.

    This in general can serve as an outline of how to included more female characters in a misogynist time period (such as the 1920s): make several memorable/important/powerful NPCs to serve as proxies. (I note in passing that I also included several interesting/powerful female NPCs not in the original text along the way.)

    b) Keep on the Borderlands: Standard Operating Procedure for me when I run anything I didn't write is to break down the motivations of the NPCs, as they are often poorly developed. Or, in the case of Keep, non-existent. The first thing I did was assign broad characteristics to each of the tribes in the Caves of Chaos, and give them named leaders. Of these, three--the kobolds, the orcs of Cave B, and the gnolls--got female leaders. The most important of these was the leader of the orcs--she was a crafty and experienced centurion of an orc military unit organized along the lines of a Roman century, with high competence and unit pride. She quickly became a favorite of the PCs.

    (The kobold queen got massacred early, and the gnolls didn't figure in too much, but when the barbarian PC killed the leader of the other orc tribe and became their chief, he discovered that the tribe was actually managed by the trio of wives who ceremonially married the current chieftain, whoever he was.)

    I made a couple of the other NPCs female as well. The leader of the keep isn't highly detailed, so I made her female, the highly respected Lady Belladonna. There's a mad hermit in the woods, which was a boring idea to me, so I made her a druid, one of the last remnants of the original inhabitants of the region. Probably in the end I had more male NPCs than female NPCs, but the memorable named ones tended to be female.

    This is more illustrative of what you can do in fantasy or scifi: just make a lot of NPCs female, and make sure to have them in places of importance.

    A word about portrayal: as with any kind of quasi-narrative, what's going to matter most is motivation, not any particular tics of the character. Yes, tics/quirks are a powerful way to differentiate the characters, especially if you aren't good at voices or other acting techniques. Yes, it helps to draw clear, almost caricatured portraits of NPCs. But what will make them work, what will make them characters in a narrative, is their motivation. You don't need to have a female NPC kick off her high heels (NB: many women never wear heels) unless that says something about her character in some way. You don't need to worry so much about mannerisms or voice. You do need to wonder about why she's doing what she's doing, and then if being a woman is important or influences that motivation, look to ways to express that. It's often not...as crucial as it might seem. In my examples, M'Weru and Hypatia were both influenced by having been born women in a repressive time, but that only mildly shaped their real motivations (power/the destruction of colonialism for M'Weru, immortality/politics among the Outer Beings for Hypatia.) In Keep, Burgash the orc and Belladonna the commander came from cultures where being a woman in authority was absolutely unremarkable, and so I treated it that way.

    So it goes.

      \n If we're going to talk about women's roles in history, it's important to give a thought to *how* and *why* they might have been left out of, say, a particular historical account.

      Outside of, say, a traveling military unit in a culture which didn't allow women any participation in war*, pretty much any human society is going to be roughly 50% men and 50% women.       Actually, on Rickard's second point, there are issues here with social status - realistic for there to be many women, not so realistic for them to occupy positions of high status. But "realistic fantasy" already bends historical reality quite a lot (with the magic, dragons etc) - why not bend it a little more and have gender equality too?
      \nI do not mean this as some sort of stealth-misogynist counterpoint, I'm just going to answer the question as presented. I feel this to be worthwhile because this objection is trotted out all the time, so apparently there are a huge number of people out there who genuinely don't understand why you might want to have dragons, yet not have utopian gender relations in the same work.

      The reason why somebody might draw the line at gender roles, yet not at dragons and magic, is that they may be going for the literary genre of "historical romanticism", which continues to live as what is commonly called "vanilla fantasy". This genre of fantasy is often sort of misrepresented as being "about" elves and dwarves and dragons and magic, when it's actually more about something that I like to characterize as a romantic longing for a mythical past when men were heroes, swords were swords and horses were transportation.

      This creative mission statement is behind a great amount of the canon of fantasy literature, and you can see the seamless transition from the genre of historical romance (Sir Walter Scott, Victor Hugo, etc.) to fantasy literature proper, with J.R.R. Tolkien at the height of the literary sensibility involved here. For literature (and gaming) in this mode, fantastic elements are there as poetic symbols that heighten appreciation of human qualities: the wizard is so wise that the wiseness seeps out as magic, and so on. Furthermore, the quasi-historical setting is there in an effort to strip down the human condition into something more basic and easier to perceive, for the purpose of transmitting various literary themes: if these people, living in this simple world, can make these kinds of choices, what does that tell about you, modern man?

      For this genre of fantasy a fundamental matter of human nature like gender is not primarily a political issue, you can't just overwrite what you consider the historical norm, the "natural order of things", simply because you want to make it more comfortable for players to take on roles in the fantasy setting. By doing so you would no longer be commenting on the relevant human condition of our past, as your world would then be merely wishful thinking rather than being a condensed perspective on what the world has historically been like. Saying that you gotta have gender equality for comfort here would be like saying that you gotta let every player character get assault rifles for comfort - it goes directly against the aesthetic goals, so either you scuttle the Plan regarding romantic historical fantasy, or you accept that this setting is going to feature some historical types of gender stereotyping.

      I should note that setting-fidelity to perceived historical human condition does not need to mean that player characters are particularly hassled about it; it's common to treat PCs equally regardless of gender or other human traits, even if they have to face a world that treats them whatever way. The Shadow of Yesterday is an example of a game that is utterly reliant on historical themes of oppression for its content, for example, and it generally encourages you to play women or ethnic minorities who are often in subservient positions in the fictional societies depicted in the game; it's just that your character is fortunate enough to have the means to be a hero who overcomes the generally oppressed condition of their ordinary class. Call of Cthulhu is another example of a game that is nigh pointless if you insist on not depicting historical social ills of the American society, which often causes tension between this creative drive for historical realism and the desire to acknowledge that women, ethnic and sexual minorities are welcome to play, even if within the game world these same minorities are often treated in rather inhuman ways.

      Also, it should be obvious that if you're not doing something like this - if your D&D setting is just an excuse to mix up some dragons and githyanki and beholders for a soap opera or slaughterfest or whatever - then there indeed is no good reason not to have a radically player-conforming setting, including idealistic notions about gender, ethnicity, religious freedom and other historical societal issues. My impression is that the reason why there is so much covert resistance to radical gender equality in fantasy gaming is that the historical romanticism is really common as a more or less well-understood theme, one that explains a lot about what people find appealing about fantasy in gaming and literature. I know that it's one of the central hooks for me, as regards being interested in fantasy gaming. I can remember how nigh-disgusted and disappointed I was with the magic torches that were introduced on the D&D equipment list in 4th edition, exactly because of the way the concept went directly against the thematic ideas of historical romanticism: if your fantasy world doesn't even have fear of the dark in a pre-electric society, then why the heck keep up with this swords-and-horses rodeo instead of just going full Star Trek with it? Since then, looking at my own D&D gaming, it's been very strongly oriented towards the ideals of historical fantasy, insofar as literary theming goes.

Perhaps this character is always stroking her shawl when she is nervous. That one always holds her nose up and keeps adjusting her glasses when they slip. Pick it up (or even just touch it) when you're speaking as that character, and a fictional persona starts to be associated with that prop, each time the character is "on screen".

      All of these are nice ways to get a distinct personality across without necessarily having to modulate your *voice* in an unnatural and unconvincing way. Let the players' imagination fill in the other aspects of the character, much as they do when you switch between roleplaying a wheezing goblin and a mighty ogre.       \n Actually in many ways exceptions really DO suggest an opposed rule, especially in the study of history.

      There are a few types of history that get recorded and then survive to be studied: huge events recorded by many, random things that don't matter much and so slip through cracks but are fragmented, events described by the power structure to reinforce their power, or events contrary to the power structure that survive because they're so unusual and important (at least to someone) that they're protected from eradication by those opposed (or at least cautious of) the power structure.

      It's the last that we're mostly talking about here. For instance, in a culture where men are expected to accomplish something and hold power a random man doing so in a not unique way is almost never purposefully recorded. It's not 'noteworthy' because it's the norm (as reinforced by bulk evidence of major events or incidental pieces). Conversely, in that same system a woman accomplishing something out of gender norm is taken note of by some and often some version of it therefore survives to be known to us in detail.

      When you combine a few of this fourth type with an overwhelming volume of the first three that show an opposing reality the ONLY logical extrapolation is that the contrary events are 'the exceptions that prove the rule'. If they weren't there would either be many, MANY more of them, or none at all.

      Now obviously this does break down somewhat the further back we go, and the more limited the source material. But for macro issues (such as women's legal and social status during a several hundred year period for which we have literally tens or hundreds of thousands of pieces of supporting evidence) the only deniers are those with mental issues or seeking to utilize a form of revisionist social engineering (usually for laudable goals).
