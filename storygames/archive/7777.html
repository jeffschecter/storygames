\n There's been a bit of talk about "stakes" (as in "conflict stakes") lately, and I'll to get some feedback from all the players and designers here:

It seems that stakes, having had their 15 minutes of fame, are now kind of looked down upon as being "so 2003". In a Wicked Age... is super popular, and there is much renewed interest in Sorcerer--both games do not have explicit stake-setting. Ron Edwards said demeaning things about stakes about a year or two ago, and made it clear that you could be way cool without them.

So, are stakes a thing of the past? What's good about them? If you're a designer creating an RPG, why might you choose to use set stakes in conflict resolution?

I can start:

Stakes can be really good for getting people's thought process out in the open. For a group struggling with conflict resolution, trying to identify which rolls are meaningful and which are not, or looking to make sure conflicts and rolls have meaningful consequences, this sort of open communication is really useful. Perhaps any game looking to use rolls or conflicts in specific ways or specific situations should recommend for the players to set explicit stakes, so they can all police each other and help each other identify those situations.

(Ah, but would the have More Fun if they dropped explicit stakes eventually?)

Clarity, on its own, is also a bonus. Knowing what to expect before you go into a conflict might create a greater sense of security in the players, which might be good for games looking to make the players feel safer or more comfortable.

Finally, stakes go well with garlic, or stuffed in vampires.

    So, because I'm apparently 6-18 months behind the cool kids, I went ahead and googled up some good thinking on the subject and came up with this.

    It reads to me -- again, just based on Kim's thing -- that the "problem" with stakes comes down to setting suitable stakes/outcomes/whatevers for the fiction, as well as what seems like the inevitable problem of unhappy parties on both sides of a conflict prenegotiating every goddamned thing before the dice ever hit the table.

    On the first part, it seems like it's been solved to some degree in some games. In BW, while you discuss Intent, any given Intent has to be resolveable with a specific character ability. So, like, "blowing up the world" isn't really a suitable intent because there's no "blow up the world" ability. But there are also reeeeealy loosey-goosey versions out there that don't have such a restriction. Just off the top of my head, you can pretty much toss any old conflict out there in Shock: and it comes down to a roll-off. In my own AP, it seemed like a non-issue. We were all aboard what would or would not be a suitable scope for the conflict.

    On the second part, that can seriously suck. I've got players in my groups who toss out a big stake (say, a broad outcome for the Duel of Wits -- a notable exception to the "every intent must be resolved with a skill" ethos in BW), wait for the other side to come back, get freaked out by the equally-big counterstake, then whittle down each side 'til you're left with an uninteresting competition. All the wheedling bleeds the tension out of the conflict and you're left with meh. I don't yet have a mechanical solution for that -- it seems to come down to trust between players.

    Funny that stakes are now a "debacle." Are they really?

    \n My only issue with stakes is pre-playing what will happen disconnected from what you are doing. In some stakes setting games I've seen, you completely skip the "how" you are trying to get what you want. Which to me is what makes what happens believable.

If I choose knife fighting, then your life is at stake. Then you describe how which determines the scope of what is at stake. If I choose to cut you in self defense to keep you back vs. plunging my knife into your eye or investigating who killed the victim vs. who was the last person in the room.     \n

    I'm still working on a better approach with this one. In movies, you often get some kind of foreshadowing scene that highlights what's at risk, right?     I can't speak for anyone else, but one of the reasons I like the Pre-Negotiated stakes is that a central goal for me is to have people interested and happy no matter which way the die-throw goes.

    Note, this has zero to do with characters being happy.

    I would call pre-negotiated stakes a sort of reverse CYOA Book approach. Remember those? You'd read a section, then choose between 2-3 outcomes, based on a little info from what you'd just read and the way the choice was described.

    Now, in that case, you didn'y know what you were getting, with the case that many times you came to an abrupt end (often either Boring-Happy or Unhappy-Suck), and ended up flipping back in your choices anyway.

    Pre-Negotiated stakes sorta reverses that by putting out agreeable outcomes ( again, agreeable to the players), then randomly deciding between each.

    The players then riff off where a given outcome has taken them to continue the game.

    Personally, I don't see the charm of games that produce results no one wanted. It seems to fly in the face of human-centered game design. I mean, I've got computer games and other pre-programmed game types for that kind of thing, and a ton of forms of passive entertainment.

    As far as I'm concerned, the purpose of an rpg's rules is to create the unwelcome and the unwanted in the game's fiction. The reason to play by rules is because you want the unwelcome and the unwanted - you want things that no vigorous creative agreement would ever create.
