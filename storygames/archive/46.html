[design] I made this game, but what am I saying?

DevP
January 2006 edited January 2006 in Story Games

So I made The Dance and the Dawn a while ago for an Iron Chef contest, and that was good. I feel about ready to start revising it again. But here's an odd thing I'm trying to figure out: what does the game really say?

I was driven in making the game by this idea of how I wanted the playstyle and aesthetics to be, but I'm not sure yet what kind of message the game is transmitting, and I feel like I'm too close to the game to figure it out. It's about how princesses end up wishing for princes to save them, but I was also hoping it would display how that kind of wishing can be really bad for you, and that's important to me because I personally really dislike that kind of trope in fairy tales, and what it does to the people who believe them.

So, I'm hoping there's some crypto-feminist or pseudo-empowering element in my game, but I can't quite tell. I'd love it if someone could read it and tell me what they see.

  • Mike_Holmes

    What Ben said.

    Your game makes no more statement about husband hunting being good than a wargame says that killing people is good. They are both simply interesting situations in which players make their own statements (which may or may not have much to do with the situation in question). Accepting playing the game does not mean accepting that the situation of the game makes for some eternal truth. Just that you find the situation interesting.

    Now, other games, games like Kill Puppies for Satan, do have a message. But they deliver them by constraining the effective ways in which the characters can act. In Dawn, it's not that it's a good choice to pursue a husband, it's the only choice. The game doesn't say, "see, look, getting a husband is the only way to go!" It says, "If we have some women looking for a husband, what's the best way to go?"

    Crucial difference. This is all about narrativism premise. That is, in Sorcerer, the game doesn't ask you, "Is it good to be a Sorcerer?" it assumes that everyone starts as one. The question in Sorcerer is "If one was a sorcerer, what would one do with that power?"

    Note that, further, as I read back in Dawn (just to be sure), I see that it's their "true love" that they're looking for. So it's not even really husband hunting from that perspective. In Cinderella, one could say it's about husband hunting, but does Cinderella go after the Prince for his money? No, the Sisty-Step Uglers do. Cinderella is in it for love.

    I think that makes it OK, from that POV, too. :-)
