    \n Filip,

    HotB is kinda like "A John Wick game post-Forge integration."

    That is to say, there are still big crunchy splats, over the top grotesquely purple prose, a setting much more about metal-opera than finess, some random weird shit, some random cool shit, and lots of GM advice about how you need to be a magician. However, there is also a system that determines narrative authority, shifts power around the table, and unifies resolution.

    How well it'll all work I can't say, but it is a different thing than the old games so far as system paradigm goes.

    \n For $5 it's hard not to justify picking the game up. I think it's got a lot of neat ideas in it and the core system seems to be really neat, though I don't think it's nearly as elegant as it could be. The wagers system is fantastic. John sort of neuters Aspects as presented in SotC, but goes on to say you can play with them un-neutered if you like. My biggest complaint about the game is John's writing style/voice. To me, it's the most off-putting writing I've ever read in an RPG (and this from a HUGE one-time fan of both L5R & 7th Sea). It's so off-putting that I'm not sure I'll ever finish actually reading the book. Certainly, that won't be the case for everyone.

    \n Daniel's right that the style and voice are unique. John states right up front that he's writing in the style of Ven literature - and goes on to describe aspects of that literature, and then makes no apology for the style (although the fact that he felt the need to describe it at all could be taken as an apology).
    \n
    \nThe only part of the writing that I objected to was the part of the introduction where John say's this is the anti D&D.; Not that I'm a D&D; fanboy - I've played more than my fair share - just that any comparisons of "how this game isn't like these other games over here" always seems cheapen the message of the game. My opinion is - let the work stand on it's own, and leave the comparisons for those reading/playing the game.
    \n
    \nIn terms of the system, I think the wagering mechanic reads like it's very clean - but it remains for actual play to highlight if the search and handling time is within tolerable limits (for me).

    \n John ran his preview adventure at OrcCon this year and it was definitely an awesome gaming experience. There's a lot of player narrative control built into the dice mechanics. I like the way players can choose to roll less dice in order to make wagers, that allow them to shape the fiction. The setting is evocative and I love that the fiction of the game is very consciously imitating the conventions of ven blood opera rather than trying to be realistic or simmy. So, the bad guys are pale and red eyed. The world is moody and dark, with love and death in the air. Art and fashion are important, love is always deadly, and you can rely on a noble's word, except when they are lying.\n

    \n Off the top of my head: you have 6 virtues (stats), but you can only ever have values in 5. Or maybe 5 and 4. The important part is that you always have one big "slice" of things that you simply are not good at, a hindrance.

    There are "kinda"-Aspects: they can be invoked and compelled in specific ways (instead of general ways as in Fate).

    There is a wager mechanic that allows you to reap bigger successes by using less dice than you could (IIRC): if you pass the test anyway, the extra dice become extra "story power".

    Oh, and there are "seasonal" actions that allow you to manage your kingdom/barony/whatever, wage war on a bigger scale, arrange marriages, and so on.

    I've only read the quick-intro pdf, but I think these are more-or-less correct.

    \n I've bought the pdf. It's five dollars. I've given five dollars to people who I did not know and expected nothing from in return. There's no question I'll give someone five dollars for a four-hundred-plus page roleplaying book about the political and emotional maneuverings of an ancient operatic race of escaped sorcerously-constructed slaves.

    Having read it, I certainly want to play it. I want to accumulate holdings. I want to be awesomely capable at somethings and awesomely doomed at others. I want to love and hate furiously.

    I understand that this is not what everyone wants, but if that sounds like you and you have five stinking bucks, then yeah, buy the damn thing.

    Before I read the book, I found the gimmick to be transparent and eye-roll worthy. Now, I'm not so sure. Reading a book that claims to be an attempt at authentically replicating something fictional has sucked me in, somehow...It's magical thinking, in a way. It's as if, before you ever start making a character, you're already being invited to roleplay yourself, in a world where scholars of the esoteric study the mysterious civilizations of Lemuria, Mu and Atlantis. I was quite struck when I recognized that impulse. Raised on Kenneth Hite and Grant Morrison and whoever else, I prefer my histories to be crazy rather than truthful (whenever they conflict, I mean). This is a book that lets you become cooler just by reading and thinking about it, by taking up the mantle of this alternate past. It reminds me a lot of Hite's talk of reality quakes, where sections of time subduct under a moment, just like tectonic plates at a fault line, and all of a sudden the past is different from what it used to be. Specifically, it reminds me of "pragmaclasts," artifacts from the subducted time-strata, "thrown up" by whatever forces are responsible for such things. Diaries in unknown languages. Records of wars that never happened. A roleplaying game based on a civilization that never existed.

    So yeah, I'm more in the "delighted" camp, lately.
