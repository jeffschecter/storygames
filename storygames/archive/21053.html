\n I'm curious if anyone has any familiarity with RPGs that feature a combat system with little to no randomization, relying instead on resource management, strategy comparison, or some other mechanism for resolving success/failure without rolling dice, drawing cards, etc.

For the purpose of this topic I'm specifically interested in games that have an actual resolution system, as opposed to story games that have a formless narrative combat.

I can think of two systems that come close.

Onyx Path/White Wolf's Burn Legend, which was a Street Fighter/Mortal Kombat-inspired setting hack for Exalted 2nd Edition and published in Shard of the Exalted Dream. This featured a clash resolution system where different techniques could auto-defeat certain other techniques based on category, resulting in a good deal of tactical technique picking, but would fallback to a "clash roll off" where an auto-defeat didn't occur.

Ben Lehman's Polaris has its spoken key phrase-based conflict which is a combination of player negotiation and resource management, but falls back, again, to a dice roll when the protagonist decides not to compromise.

Can anyone think of examples that don't rely on randomization at all? In particular, any that you particularly enjoy?

    \nDread. Uses a Jenga tower.

    Svart av kval, vit av lust. Collaborative storytelling with vampires. Participants that aren't in the scene can force characters in the scene to do something in the form of the vampire's Beast. If they refuse, they get a token. In a conflict, older vampires always win over younger vampires, unless the younger vampire unleashes the Beast. If the Beast is unleashed, anyone can take a token from the younger vampire to make that vampire fail.

    Once Upon a Time. Uses drawn cards that needs to be involved in the story. It's outgoing randomness (*roll*, "what do I do with this?"), instead of incoming randomness ("I want to do this", *roll*), so that's why I'm mention this game here.

    Sometimes in the future I will make a tactical combat oriented RPG without stochastic randomness (dice, cards, etc.). My first draft was fun, had more emergent complexity (depth) than D&D4 with only two pages of rules, but used dice.

    Other than that, I would like to see combat systems with inspiration from chess, Hive, or Domino.

    I've got a game that features no stochastic randomness that I'm currently testing in an early access called Deadpuncher (currently costs a small amount $2.99, not trying to do a salesman plug I swear). Inspired by Burn Legend and Yomi, it operates like this: characters have access to maneuvers from fighting styles they learn, essentially in a small deck-building exercise. At the start of each round they select a target and a maneuver, then compare maneuver choices. Opponents compare Sign tags to determine if one trumps the other, and if not they compare static stat-based Speeds to determine who succeeds or fails. Consequences from cards then occur, with a Risk-management system where you can opt to take a lower-level Injury immediately (and therefore keep your Risk low) or accept points of Risk being placed into a pool. Finisher maneuvers can convert the stored Risk into much greater Injuries, potentially ending the fight immediately.

    I'm still in the testing phase, and it's not exactly a tersely-worded system, but so far I feel like it's encouraging complex emergent strategies between opponents after they learn one another's styles (with each fighting style having its own ratio of tags and counters to consider) and allows for fairly fast round-by-round resolution (I put this together to playtest a combat engine for a moderately sized LARP in mind of theater-style LARPers who prefer a strategic combat system over a more narrative negotiation system).

    But having drank the kool aid, as it were, I'm definitely looking into other ways to handle combat that maintains strategic depth while removing the swing of die rolls and still allows for easy-to-memorize and quick resolution rules for each round.

    \nI've played Undying, which is all token-bidding. I didn't play very much, but I got a feel for the mechanics. I found them really fun! The specifics of Undying's economy kinda pushes you in the direction of a downward spiral for your Humanity score, but I'm pretty sure that's a feature, like Urban Shadows' Corruption spiral.     \nI found the key to Firebrands (or, rather, a possible key to it - if you're down on "playing to loose" already, the skirmish mini-game/subsystem will make immediate sense to you) is to understand most or all interactions between main characters as flirtation. I especially recommend having a swordfight early in your first game: the intimacy of it (just two people involved, their lives potentially at stake) helps drive home the point more easily than in a skirmish, with all those other named extras involved. However, a swordfight does include some randomness in the form of flip-a-coin options, unlike a skirmish.
