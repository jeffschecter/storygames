The End of Miss a Turn

\n
\n
\n
\n\nSimon_Rogers \n\n\n
\n
\n\nApril 2012 \n edited April 2012 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n There isn't much more irritating in a board game than miss a fricking turn, and in roleplaying games, it's pretty much the same, unless it's short. So, being stunned - unable to act, posessed and in a trance are all rather dull for the player concerned. I'm not saying there isn't a place for it, but I'm keen to make such things more interesting.

Clinton mentioned that Stunning is a right bastard in 13th Age, which prompted this post.

Someone pointed out (might have been Graham) that in GUMSHOE, that if your mental stability is below 0, then you can't make investigative spends which are the best way to have spotlights, narrative influence and find info to defeat your foes. Between 0 Stabilty and actual mental illness, there is a dull spot. So, I suggested that instead of being unable to use investigative abilities you have to make another Stability test when you want to use them - risking insanity. For GUMSHOE Fantasy, when characters were possessed, we gave them an internal mental landscape and let them fight their way out of it each round.

So, which games make you miss a turn, and what can we do about it? Perhaps you don't mind miss a turn?

    I agree that the risk is important. However, I think that in practice the risk is linked to the problem. If disablement means that the player doesn't have fun, then the GM and/or the group are generally going to adjust things so that it doesn't happen. So that means that the risk isn't real any more. The best way to make the risk real, then, is to make sure that the process as a whole is still fun.

    In terms of game design, I think the best way is to ensure that combats resolve quickly after the first character is taken out - because the alternative ways of keeping the player involved have not been very successful in my experience.

    The two old solutions that I know of are: (1) having back-up characters that the player can immediately switch to, or (2) temporarily switching to play the opponents, like Rafu suggests. Neither of these have been popular in my experience, and GMs more often fudge things to keep PCs active.

    (Rafu - could you say more about the design technology here? I can't think of other options implemented within tabletop RPGs. A number of larps have had "ghost" options for PCs as another choice.)

    \n Some of the games Graham mentions are very susceptible to players missing turns, they're just different about. The old D&D paradigm is about team-based play, and thus players are included in play automatically; missing a turn is a mechanical exception. These drama games, on the other hand, are scathingly performance-based: make a character the other players don't get nor appreciate, and see how your degree of inclusion plummets. It's very easy to position your character in the fiction in a way that leaves you out of the entire fight (or other intense scene) in a way that is rare in the basic D&D set-up. You don't even need to be a very bad player, you just need to be a bit unlucky. In D&D the character concepts and activities are at least so constrained that you can sit dumb for the entire session and still be fully included in the fights.

    But yes, there are some games that have come so close to eliminating player-sidelining as to make no difference. Games with formal turn-order and formal spotlight-distribution like Fiasco or Zombie Cinema, for example, don't really have anything you could fairly call "missing your turn", unless you include things like the player wasting his turn because he doesn't have enough skill to play. Primetime Adventures... generally, games that do formal spotlight distribution don't really get turn-missing, as the system mandates the same amount of attention and important choices to each player.

    Whether this is somehow generally superior in comparison to games without missed turns is, of course, a complex question of rpg theory. I would be inclined to say no, if only because many games derive their zest from the creative risks involved in the choices the players make. TSoY, for example, is totally a game that I specifically want to play now and then because it provides no creative safety rails, making for an exciting ride. Create a dramatically sucky character in that game and you will get less turns and less interesting choices during those turns than some other player.
