\n This is an old discussion, and may easily end up in old bickerings. Work with me here, please, and lets try to attack the theme with a new twist ... (and leave any personal soreness out of it)
Posted By: HexabolicI shed a few tears at a Werewolf game, of all things. Some powerful drama worked out between a dying elder of the sept and the truculent warder. Excellent stuff, not at all what I expected from that game.
The sad thing about Werewolf and Vampire, is that these games have a theme that may blow your brains out, but instead the games work to dull most of their players.

I know there are thousands of players out there, who have made their World of Darkness a vivid thing. Those of them reading this will have to forgive me for being so unfair when ignoring their ability to make these games right. Forgive my unfair (and partly unfounded) generalization, please. Let it ride, for the sake of discussion.

I am sad for the many storytellers and player-groups who have failed to make these games into the jewels they should be, and sad for my own experience with them. I came to these games as an adult and experienced player, but with great expectations all the same, and found nothing but odd leadership and strangely placid players. So these games failed to deliver me, and had made my more WoD-experienced co-players into something I barely recognized as interactive participants in a game.

- From the way these games present themselves, I would expect playing them to bring you, the player, to the brink of tears. I would expect them to engage most players profoundly, and to produce meaningful drama of high quality.

- From the way these games are played, however, I do not expect them to bring me anywhere. I can see how players fall for the cool content, and how many of them fail to see that the coolness of it is hollow, as the method leaves the storyteller impotent, and fails to place the players in the eye of the storm.

I love these legends still, and would like to have a discussion on how to make the legends right in role-playing games. I will do this is two separate threads, one on the vampire and one on the werewolf, if there are enough interest for it on the forum. So here's my agenda for this thread:

- How do you design for interesting vampire-play?
- How do you support the players in the conflicts of the vampire?
- Do you have an example of this done right (game or session), and thoughts on why?

- How do you make potent player-character vampires?
I'm talking potent as in playable, captivating, emotionally challenging and thought-provoking ...

    \n I think a big part of the horror in VtM, which, I think is a potent thing to experience as a player through the vehicle of your character, is the sheer hopelessness of your condition. Its weird, but a good Vampire game, using the World of Darkness is more Manchurian Candidate than The Hunger. I think the reason for this is that the Vampire experience becomes more diluted more and more each time you play, each time you familiarize yourself with what your about to experience. Horror, like humor, works best when you can keep your audience (even if your audience is yourself) off guard.

    How do you design for interesting vampire-play?

    You make the nature of vampirism and the PC's relationship to vampirism a mystery up front. Establish a world with a feeling or safety, consistency and meaning and then introduce the vampire, or vampirism as the cancerous other that they are. This works with whatever version of vampires your going for. The sex that vampires represent should poisonous self destructive sex, so should the power and the violence that they bring. Never make them pretty, never make them nice but always make them dangerous and irresistible.

    How do you support the players in the conflict of the vampire?

    Give them room to breath and think about the consequences of their characters actions. When they are the most comfortable, speed things up, push things along, force hands, rock boats, when they are the least comfortable, let it sit. Let the monstrosity and the otherness sink in. Talk about uncomfortable things before and after actual play. Get them to the edge of the creepy pool and urge them to dive in.

    Do you have an example of this done right?

    Yes, over and over again. The first time I introduced some of my high school friends to role playing we a version of vampire that turned out to be an extended prelude. The only common and predefined elements that we agreed on were that all of the PC's were cops and that at some point, some time in the future, they would become vampires. It moved between slow establishing action to punctuated violence and a gradual building of the realization that these creatures, these very evil, unsettling creatures, had their cold dead fingers in every single pie in sight. How do you react as a cop when you realize that your on the payroll of a monster and that the slightest resistance on your part means not death, but worse than death, blood addiction, cannibalistic, the murder of your family and loved ones.

    It played out like an edgy cop drama mixed with taught psychological thriller and we never once stuck our tongues in out cheeks or stopped long enough to let the vampire become ridiculous or predictable or cliche, and that, is why I think it worked.

    The problem with VtM, I think, for a lot of people (forgiving for the moment its difficulties with probabilities, consistent presentation, etc...) is that a lot of people looked at the Gothic punk world and laughed at it. They might have liked and even thought it was cool but they brought with them a level of self awareness that turns the monstrous other into just another Halloween costume or dated movie reference.

    How do you make potent player-character vampires?
    Find that space between hollow and passionate. Take something about yourself, some way that you can be cruel, some way that you can be "inhuman" and exaggerate it or twist it until it becomes truly monstrous. Be willing to play the living dead, the hopeless and to cling on for dear life to anything that might keep you alive a little longer. Read Wraith: the Oblivion which did so many things right that Vampire should have done from the beginning. Never laugh at yourself, never take anything for granted about what being a vampire means. I mean, sit down for half an hour and try, just try, to imagine what it would be like to murder someone for their blood. The blood that you'll drink like a starving man eats food while their corpse grows cold beside you.

    In other words, if you want a game that does vampires and not the romance of vampires (which to me are two very different beasts) you have to take how horrible these things would be if they were real seriously.

    \n How about this.
    I like the theme of the personal horror while your humanity slips away, but instead of having an indicator that tells you how much left before you turn into an NPC, I'd work it this way.

    You get relationships, people you care for and who make you human. They give you a mechanical bonus, they nurture you, help you, protect you. They keep you warm from the cold that creeps from the inside of your heart.
    The more you act heinously, the more you put your relationships at risk, the more grasp the beast has on you.
    The beast's grasp might translate into compulsive scenes. The GM (if any) may call a scene, tell the scene's theme like in "how you kidnap and slaughter an innocent" and then it is up to the player controlling the vampire to frame such scene.
    Such scenes should harm relationships first.

    Maybe losing relationships earns you darkness tokens that can be spent by others to call in complusory scenes. or something like that.

    Some solid buy in is going to be needed, mind you. Kinda inevitable.

    But avoiding giving the characters powers that are only useful to fight would surely ensure that players turn the game into a hack and slash.

    To be honest, I'd do completely without any mechanics concerning such "vampire powers" because they can perfectly work as mere color. They're hardly the point about being a vampire: the point is "struggling with the beast within which threatens to eat away what remains of your humanity".

    And this is a possible approach, the one closer to what the premise of WoD Vampire was supposed to have.

    Great suggestions from everyone, I would return to the books and point out something that I didn't grasp how great it was at the time, which is Themes and Tones. These are very simple organizing principles around which you build your campaign. Think them up and stick with them as far as you can. If your theme is Disappointment, be sure that every NPC is disappointed in something or has disappointed someone or has expectations for something that will never happen, and so on. Soon the players will be telling their servants, "You have failed me again." and sighing heavily. They'll get it.

    Another thing is to emphasize consequences (and determine them using the Theme and Tone). And not just bad, tragic consequences either. If you are constantly hammering them with how awful it all is, they will feel it is pointless to do anything, and they will do nothing. Put them in an unwinnable trap like the WoD's Camarilla and they will battle like devious animals to achieve something virtuous. (This is why the Humanity system as written is good.) If doing good is impossible, or seems so, your game will go nowhere, not because they won't choose to be monsters, but because if that's the only option, it's not really a choice. So yes, come up with some tragic consequences for them getting involved in (by choice or not) situations, but also think about making amends. It's a long eternity, someone will come up with something decent to do at some point just by chance!\n

    \n My expedient, at the age of eighteen, was to tell the players to make themselves (and each other) as characters and then have them become vampires. Shortly thereafter I moved to a 'freeform' game, where I was just picking issues that I knew were provocative and personal. It was emotionally sophomoric and simple but it did provoke an incredibly engaging game. The wish fulfillment was juxtaposed against personal consequences, and the tension between was was desirable and what was unacceptable between the players was very fruitful.

    I used completely typical techniques, most of them recommended by the VtM book. After a few sessions I had a very large number of players, and would take them aside individually or in groups, assign them characters and ask how they felt about the issues being raised by the actions surrounding the 'protagonists'. Then I would connect them to the issues and other characters in play, and sort of just lob situations and referee, and from time to time cut to expository scenes. It ended up being slightly LARP-ish (unfortunately I didn't know what that was at the time), and theatrical. It also facilitated high engagement, even as the audience.

    I am sad to say that the rules, like Strength and Humanity and Fortitude and Brujah and dice pools, were only an initial point of reference and in the first few sessions when I was still using them were a real drag; however, a lot of the suggestions for approaching play were at least inspirational and often useful.

    Posted By: d.andersonI am sad to say that the rules, like Strength and Humanity and Fortitude and Brujah and dice pools, were only an initial point of reference and in the first few sessions when I was still using them were a real drag; however, a lot of the suggestions for approaching play were at least inspirational and often useful.
    Dice pools are a drag, even in games of today. Heavy mechanics in general are a drag, as they tend to come in the way of efficient dialogue-techniques. There's little horror, and little of other moods too, to be had with halting dialogue-technique.

    To support themes dependent on shared moods, like horror and romantics, you need a game-method with good balance between conflict-mechanics and dialogue-techniques, and to have that you need fast and dynamic mechanics. Mechanics of old, like those in Vampire, are too heavy to function well in such games. They are as flexible as a tank in a dense forest; you will get through it, but the forest is wasted. The faster mechanics makes it easier to vary the pace of the game-lay, and to shift focus between active and reflective scenes. And you get to follow up on inspired dialogue in a much more efficient way. Such flexibility is of great importance, in classical role-playing games (the ones with the GM/player-divide), when dealing with moods and deeper character-play.

    And if the game is lacking in this department, you are most often better off leaving the mechanics altogether, and go free-form.

    \n The "heavy" die pool mechanics (the scare quotes are because, by the standards of its day, Vampire's die pool mechanics were extremely light and flexible) did have one important advantage, which was that it made it diabolically clear how superior the PCs were to most humans and how inferior they were in the power hierarchy of monsters above them. Just saying that you punch someone with Satanic strength, or rolling one die with "Satanic strength" written next to it does not have the same impact as rolling 7 dice and counting up all those lovely 10s, then watching the GM roll his 4 dice and smile wanly. It had its disadvantages, of course, but "heavy" mechanics are often quite solid and add to the "My Verisimilitude! (TM)" of the situation. Nobody just said you were a monster, the dice would prove it for you fairly reliably.

    \n I absolutely agree, and I think we are meant to be more titillated than horrified by Interview with A Vampire (this especially becomes even more true in the sequels.)

      \n I think Vampire the Masquerade made a huge mistake by moving the entire focus into vampire society. Everyone's a vampire and mortals are either enemies or pawns. And the primary focus of the game is all about interactions between vampires. BORING!

      If you look at vamp lit, almost all of it, from the more horror-focused Dracula to the more romance-focused post-Rice stories, focuses on the interactions of humans and vampires. Sure, the Rice stories had a lot of inter-vampire story, but the frame story was an interaction with a mortal. And other later examples like Twilight are all about vamp/mortal interactions.

      Vampires only matter as characters in the way that they interact with mortals. And if that interaction is minimized, leaving the mortals as puny extras, then you might as well don the capes and call it a supers game.

      The story in a vampire game is not in whether or not a vamp can punch out a mortal. It's about whether or not he wants to. Cause that mortal is the brother of his tragic love. Or a well-loved townie who will be noticed if he goes missing.

      You can give the vampires all sorts of powers. Just as long as you give them tons of compelling reasons not to use them. And make those decisions and consequences the CONSTANT focus of the game.

      Basic Sorcerer playstyle. It's not about whether or not you can get what you want. It's about what you are willing to risk to get it.

      And if you play up the weaknesses, you can do the "Clark likes Lana, but whe wears that kryptonite necklace" thing. The mortal chick love interest is devout and wears her rosary all the time.

      I've personally been working on a quick hack of rules centered around the hunger/alienation angle of vampires, one that I think I've far too often downplayed to focus on other aspects favored by my players.

      Basically: everything is a depleting resource.

      Every defined trait is a dice pool.

      Every time a background/relationship/trait *other* than the core vampire dice pool is used, it erodes.

      Every scene blood is lost.
      More so if the vampire pool was used.

      Between DM framed scenes, players can define and initiate short vignettes about how they try to salvage a world that slips through their fingers, which can - and often will - costs them more resources than those they saved.

      Or they can feed.

      Which, given how small is the blood pool, they will have to do at least once every gaming session. More often if they abuse their vampiric powers (obviously at the cost of a chance to replenish another trait).

      When a pool is fully depleted, it is gone.

      If, during a scene, a vampire's blood is depleted, another player gets to initiate/define in next vignette how the beast taking other made said vampire fuck up one resource.
