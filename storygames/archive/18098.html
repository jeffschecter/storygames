\n Hi there,

Disclaimer: this is the very first time that I'm trying to write and complete a RPG, so please, don't kick me too hard.

I've started to write a story-game called "Elevator RPG". It's a game about being stuck in a broken elevator.
I first thought about it like: "hey, the setting IS the game". If PC are stuck in this elevator, for a few minutes or hours, what would they do? Would they start to talk to each other? about what? What have they got in common? What is different? How will they react? Will they panic?

I'm not sure it's a good idea or not, but since the game is all about chatting in this broken elevator, there are no stats or dice. The player has just to write a more-or-less precise description of the PC on the character sheet. That and... the "twist". Each character has a Secret.
That's the key of the game...
I'm not completely satisfied by this mechanism, if you people have a better idea, please don't hesitate.

If you want to read (and please comment) the v0.1 draft.
For the geeky crowd, the whole source (Markdown files) of the game is available on its github repository ; you can fork it, make changes and submit changes if you want via "pull requests".

Thank you for your attention.

    \n I think this is a terrific idea for a game. Yes, the theme of characters trapped indefinitely in a small space isn\u2019t new, but, as Jason Morningstar says, you can make it distinguishable.     1.\tCheck out the entries in 1km1kt.net\u2019s Small Spaces comp. There\u2019s even one called Claustrophobia, which is obviously going to be the focus of a game set in a broken elevator (or \u2018lift\u2019, as we say on the other side of the pond).
    2.\tWatch a couple of movies with a similar focus, fr\u2019x Towering Inferno, the Poseidon Adventure, and the non-comedy Airplane.
    3.\tOne of the things you get in Airplane is the fact that most of the characters have an agenda of some kind, which is sort of like secrets, e.g. the sweaty middle-aged businessman has to get to the office to sign a lucrative deal with an important customer; the nervous, mousey young woman is engaged to be married, but is having doubts, and is going to a rendezvous with her fianc\xe9e to have it out with him; one guy stays very quiet throughout the whole ordeal, refusing to be drawn on who he is, what he does and what he\u2019s doing in the building. Etc.
    4.\tThe characters all have different resistances to stress, so they start to fold after different lengths of time and in different ways. One of the stressors could be the other characters\u2019 reactions to stress, fr\u2019x one of them starts to shout and scream for help, at which the sweaty businessman (SB) yells at him to pull himself together.
    5.\tNot sure how it would work diceless. One of the things I would want to include would be a resilience track, which the characters would progress along in the manner of the sanity track in CoC, something like totally strung-out\u2026\u2026\u2026.zen-like calm. But how to move them along it? First, I think they\u2019d all start on different points, so SB would come, as it were, pre-stressed, and would already be sweating and harassed-looking as he entered the lift.     6.\tOne by one each character should have a set-piece monologue, which could either be revealing their agenda, or simply how they\u2019re feeling at that moment.
    7.\tCharacters could build emotional alliances with each other, possibly against other characters.
    8.\tI\u2019d like it if there were an Epilogue, where each character would narrate where they are in six months\u2019 time.

    \n So the thing about an elevator game is this: you get almost no stimulus from the setting after the initial setup and you have no obvious way to add new elements from outside the character group (ie NPCs) after the initial setup.

    So you need to do one (or both) of 2 things:

    1) Have a way to generate lots of material to work with up front. Secrets. Opinions of other characters. Compelling reasons why they need to get out of the elevator quickly. Things like that. Fiasco-style pick lists are a good way to do this. Or come up with something else clever. You sort of have this, but I think that at the very least you need to provide lots more examples for inspiration.

    2) Have a way to retroactively introduce situation-changing material mid-game. Turns out 2 characters had a connection in the past but one of them doesn't realize it until the other suddenly reveals it mid-game. Somebody starts having chest pains and isn't sure if it's heartburn or a heart attack. Somebody thinks they smell smoke. Stuff like that.



    I like the idea of using cards. That would be a way to do #2. Maybe every 10 minutes, everyone gets a card - some of the cards are a big deal like the possible heart attack but some are more internal, slow-burn things.

    You could force-stack the cards so that the Rescued card can't show up until at least 3 or 4 rounds of other cards have come up.

    Also, the nice thing about using cards is that you could let players write their own cards, especially after they've played the game once. Heck, that could even be a semi-mandatory activity for end of game - write a new card if you have an idea and it'll go into the deck for next time.
