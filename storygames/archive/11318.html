\n I was interested to come across this passage recently:\n
Novitz, David (1996). "Disputes About Art." The Journal of Aesthetics and Art Criticism, 54(2), 153-163To some of us, the claim that role-playing games might properly be considered works of art seems an impertinence. We point to the melodramatic settings of such games, to the juvenile exploitation of horror and suspense that characterize them, and conclude that in view of this, they cannot possibly be art. Of themselves, however, melodrama, horror, and violence cannot disqualify role-playing games from being works of art. . . . Suppose that instead of Shadowrun, we decide to develop and play a role-playing game based on Jane Austen's Persuasion; a game of morals and manners that is sophisticated and, at its best, profound.

Even so, and despite its sophistication and its similarities to the novel, this role-playing game is unlikely ever to be played, and (as a result) is unlikely ever to be regarded as a work of art. In order to play it, adults would require a considerable amount of time; they would have to devise and remember a vast number of rules, and they would would have to bear in mind all of the complex, emotionally charged details of the game--something that would tax the intellectual energy of even the most imaginative, experienced, and sensitive players.
\nThe existence of an Austen-esque RPG doesn't necessarily invalidate the point Novitz is trying to get to--which is that role-playing games fulfill a need in the lives of their mostly adolescent male players having to do with the creation of "a space beyond the reach of adult condemnation" within which can be experienced "a sense of independence, freedom, and spontaneity" otherwise inaccessible--but if there were such a game, it wouldn't help him. Now, there is at least one Pride and Prejudice RPG site, although it's a Regency Era shared-world forum and not a tabletop game per se. Interestingly, its participants seem mostly to be women, as some of the rules of the forum seem to imply:\n

-Please do not change the color of your posts. It's okay if you want your character to always speak in pink but don't make the whole post pink.
\nBut is there a tabletop game based on the work of Jane Austen? Is anyone working on one?

    \n JayWalt, thanks for pointing me to this thread. I've thought about these questions a fair bit, and I hope the following jumble of thoughts makes sense:

    As JayWalt says, I got involved in StoryGaming a few years back when I set about creating a Jane Austen RPG. Curiously, my point of departure was very much that of David Novitz: I was supposing the existence of a Jane Austen RPG as a thought experiment while trying to make a point. The coincidence is suggestive: there's something about Jane Austen's fictions which makes them seem like an appropriate opposite to pulp adventures. Unlike Dr. No, though, my thought process led me to actually try to craft such a game.

    My first attempt was so horribly inflected by D&D assumptions as to be laughable in retrospect: skill lists, skill contests, etc. Since that time I've learned a lot by reading and playing stuff created in the early 2000s, like The Mountain Witch, Trollbabe, and Primetime Adventures. By far my favorite indie game writer is Vincent Baker. The game reports from DitV and Poison'd strike me as the perfect riposte to David Novitz's claim that rpgs cannot attain to art.

    This said, there's no question that these games place a considerable burden on the players, who must attain to a certain mastery of the material if they are to successfully enact a fiction appropriate to genre expectations. You can't play out a noir game if several of your players are unfamiliar with the requisite conventions. And this is a significant barrier to the development and play of an Austen game: I've yet to find a group to playtest that's sufficiently familiar with her work. "Why do I want to get an invitation to the ball, now?"

    And yet I think David Novitz overstates the importance of period detail. Details only matter insofar as the group decides that they matter. I wonder whether the enactment of an rpg fiction is a collaborative effort best compared to improv comedy or jazz. The players need to play together often and learn to trust one another. And, when they get it all just right the show's a hit, but the players are the only audience.

    A number of you have mentioned Philippe Tromeur's rpg of Wuthering Heights. It has always struck me as a parody of the Romantic Novel, rather than an earnest enactment of it. Throwing dice and consulting a table to find out your persona rather undercuts the dignity of the Romantic claim of selfhood. Contrast the earnestness of Vince Baker's character creation in Dogs, where you're encouraged to create traits for your character based on what "Pa" used to say. I mention this not to suggest that Tromeur's game isn't good (I've not played it, so I'm not sure), but rather to question whether it can be considered as an enactment of the Romantic novel\u2014a parody isn't an enactment but rather a way of generating laughter at the thought of seriously enacting.

    As to how one might create a Jane Austen rpg, Bill White suggests a "relationship map": that's an approach I've been considering. Jane Austen's characters focus intensely on their status in a highly fluid hierarchy. So actually tracking characters' status on some sort of board would force players to focus on the issue that their characters are supposed to be focused on. Yet the folks over at the Forge will warn of the danger of "trampling the game's fruitful void": when you create a mechanic you reduce it to numbers. If the game is about loyalty, don't create a loyalty stat, create a mechanic which encourages both loyalty and betrayal and set the characters free (Mountain Witch). If the game is about justice, don't create an alignment system, create characters endowed with a self-righteous sense of god's purpose and set them loose (Dogs in the Vineyard). So, to continue the parallel, if the game is about social status, don't create a social map, create a pyramid scheme. That is, rather than mapping society explicitly, create a mechanic which allows us to witness the social map in the movements of some social currency.

    \n Yeah, let's leave that aside, or for some other thread perhaps, or this thread is in danger of devolving into a maelstrom of arguments of the "nu-uh" type.

    I think there's something we must take into consideration here: the date of that article. It is now 14 years old. Note that our hobby has witnessed some astonishing revolutions in the past ten years. If you asked me in 1996 what roleplaying is my answer would be different than today.

    I'm in my last year of studying philosophy and comparative literature and I've been considering how to breach the subject of roleplaying to the literary academia. I think Chuck's comparison with jazz improvisation is quite accurate. It is a sort of literary free improvisation around a set of rules that limit our behaviour (just like instruments, musical scales etc give musicians something to work around). The problem is that RPG sessions rarely leave something behind, which is sort of a requirement of literary aesthetics: the written page.

    There will be a department (is that the right word in English?) of "Aesthetics of Sports" opening this year at our university, and in his "introductory" presentation, the professor talked about comparisons about a sports match and a theatrical play. The reduction of a play to its barest element that can exist is the text. The text will exist without an audience. Meanwhile in a cricket match, the barest element is the play itself. There's a lot to think about there, and I believe both areas are still largely unexplored.

    But back to Austen...saying there can't be an Austen RPG is preposterous. And I'd also contest the claim that it would take a lot from the players. In my personal opinion Dogs or My Life don't place bigger burdens on the players than say, GURPS or D&D. It's just a different kind of burden to which we are often not used to.

    \n Interesting point, somelady. What really defines Austen's work is her light but incisive satirical eye. I'm not sure that this could be translated into an rpg performance. It might make better sense to think of ourselves as creating a period romance rpg.

    Crucial elements, as I see them:
      \n
    • A conflict mechanic that foregrounds social status and romance
      • \n
    • Characters that care about buying clothing, organizing events, travel as short-term goals
      • \n
    • Characters that care about marriage and love and property as long-term goals\u2014in Austen's novels, it's possible to score a trifecta and win all three; later novelists often have plots that "discover" the impossibility of this quest, as Eliot in Middlemarch
      • \n
    • An ending where the good characters have good endings\u2014again, this is especially true of Austen, and less true of later writers
      • \n
    \nThe question in an Austen novel isn't how it will end, but how the characters will win through to that ending, given their current circumstances.

    Oh, and I second JayWalt's suggestion of a contest\u2014though a collaborative project would also be to my taste.

    \n In writing an Austen game, you'd have to make a choice about how you see Austen. It's something I find interesting about roleplaying games: when you write about a genre, you need to decide what it's about. So my postapocalyptic game, for example, will be different from yours, because we see different things as important in postapocalyptic fiction.

    So, Chuck, you've written what you think Austen's about. For someone else, it'll just be a straight romance, like a modern romantic comedy. Other people will pick up feminist themes; still others will place social issues right in the foreground. There are lots of possible Austen games. I find that rather exciting.

    There's a question, I think, of whether you can write an Austen game unless you understand the culture it comes from. I'd be suspicious of Austen games written by people who aren't from Britain. If that sounds harsh, imagine the criticism I'd get if I wrote a Tennessee Williams game. Perhaps a fairer way to phrase the criticism is: what does it mean to write an Austen game if you're unfamiliar with the culture? What are you trying to do?

    \n To be fair to Novitz, he's not really taking a position on whether or not RPGs are art; he's interested in disputes over whether or not something is art. He comes to this conclusion:\n
    In our culture at this time . . . we are willing to regard objects as works of art when once we understand the deeply humanizing role that they play in other lives. We do so . . . out of respect or moral consideration for other human beings, for at some level we recognize that a failure to accede to their classification is a failure to allow them to be heard; a failure to attend seriously to their values, aspirations, and projects.
    \nThis is actually a very sweet gesture, given that the article opens with an anecdote about arguing over whether or not RPGs are art with his then-14-year-old son. It amounts to David saying to his son Julian, "Thank you for showing me the way here," which is a nice thing for a father to say to a son.

    That said, I like the idea of a contest, but it should be about play rather than design, and be an exhibition rather than a contest. I also like Jonathan's idea of writing about it as a scholarly project; it would make a great conference panel. I'm imagining people framing up an Austenesque game using whatever system they prefer (including a new system), running it, and then reflecting on the experience and its connection to (and deviations from) her work. Maybe we could record the games and recruit an Austen scholar to listen to them, or at least those portions that were judged by participants to be particularly noteworthy.

    Graham's point about "understanding the culture" is well-taken, but it's not clear that just being a 21st century Briton absolutely qualifies one as an expert on Austen. The most one can say is that one is less distant from the target culture than others. I am generally suspicious of "standpoint epistemology" in any form, but others will disagree. It's like the scene in Lord of the Rings where Strider chides Bilbo for making up songs about Earendil in the house of Elrond.

    \n I, for one, would heartily get behind a Jane Austen RPG design challenge. I'll even toss in some Jane Austen themed artwork as a prize.

    This topic came up around our house in a big way when I was getting into Indie RPGs heaving about five years ago. My wife is a great Jane Austen fan who has given me a begrudging respect for Austen's work.     \n A whispered comment from Jason Morningstar reminds me of an idea I had a few months ago, one I'd love to flesh out more fully: why not run Pride and Prejudice as a simple skin on the Mountain Witch?

    Darcy, of course, is the witch, and Bingley the witch's majordomo. Like the samurai of Timothy Kleinert's game, the five Bennet sisters and their friends all have mastered the same skill set: conversation, sketching, cardplaying, etc. And they must rely on one another for help in overcoming the rivals and rakes that stand in their path.

    The physical challenges of Timothy Kleinert's game become social challenges in this game. In place of character death we'll have the sort of social death suffered by Lizzie's sister Lydia when she falls prey to the charms of Mr. Wickham (running off to live with him in sin, before finally marrying). In this sense, the physical combat of the Mountain Witch would be for this game an allegory of the moral suasion enacted by characters in Austen's novel.
