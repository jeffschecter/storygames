\n A few years back I played in a game set in the Antebellum South. During the course of the unfolding, Lovecraftian story, the PCs discovered a plantation where the white owners had all been slain and the slaves were all still alive. Given that we weren't playing a bunch of monster-hunters, and given further that we were all whites of the period (I was a military officer), it doesn't take much to see where we went from there: clearly the slaves killed the masters and were telling lies about the event.

The only problem we had was that the group seemed very, very invested in this idea of being contemporarily progressive in race terms, even if this made no sense from a story/character standpoint. Maybe there was some meta-thinking involved -- most GMs don't do slave-revolt scenarios, after all -- and therefore the initial premise was glossed over, I don't know, as I never played with these people again (convention game).

At any rate, I tried to make my portrayal as honest as possible, moving through the scenario and its revelations as the character would, not as Guy-Who's-Read-A-Lot-Of-Lovecraft would. And during the course of my character's coming around to the weird goings-on, he uttered the unspeakable. No, I didn't say the name of Hastur. I said... nigger.

Man, you could have heard a pin drop after that. I had five people staring at me like I had three heads for the rest of the game, all because I wanted to address the setting and the character in a genuine fashion. After a couple more uncomfortable hours, the story was complete and I was on my way, thinking mostly: "What the hell just happened there? Who sets a game in pre-Civil War Virginia with that kind of thing happening, a handful of white characters and acts shocked when bigotry enters the picture?"

Maybe people aren't used to letting the ugly out when they play. Maybe there's a comfortable distance between reality and gaming that has to exist in order for the average gamer (whatever that means) to remain comfortable. Maybe some players can't separate character from the person across the table. I have ideas, obviously, but I'd like to hear yours.

    \n Your maybes don't really matter, and are a bit insulting. It wasn't their fault. The problem is you crossed a line that sounds like it was in place for everyone else at the table, and you were out of sync. The solution is, before play, to start a discussion about where people's lines are, and it's also good for the GM to point out and make clear, that they will involve slaves which will bring up issues of race (and human rights, and sexual abuse, and torture, etc). Maybe everyone was fine with having bigotry enter the picture, but not with the N-word. Unless you have a talk, you won't know where the line is until you cross it.

    Regardless, it's poor form to make any sort of defense of crossing someone's line by saying "Well, it's what my character would do." The correct response is more like "Shit, I'm sorry, I was trying to be true to the period, I didn't stop to think that it would cause a problem. We obviously need to establish some boundries that's going to make sure everyone is comfortable, because I was working with different assumptions." That way, if you're interested in playing a bigot, or even just raising racial issues, or issues of any sort that's likely to push buttons, you'll know ahead of time. Some people have no problem roleplaying violence against people, but can't stand violence against animals. You can't know for sure where the lines are until you establish them out in the open.

    \n That's the thing, isn't it? This kind of thing is strongly governed by your out of game subject position, so it has personal implications.

When I was working on Manacle and Coin for Exalted, I tried to strike a balance with a sidebar that said, basically, "Virtually everyone who isn't a slave has no problem with the institution of slavery, but this doesn't mean you have to be like that.     Then the developer decided that fantasy libertarians would have emancipation commandos, which I suppose worked for him but I thought undercut the whole point I was trying to make. The fact that I don't like libertarianism and resented crypto-propaganda about it in the game probably makes me biased, though.

    I'm not trying to hijack this thread, but the SIS assumption is so that it needs to be kicked in the teeth before I can even *talk* about discrimination specific issues.

    Let us consider a model of individual participation:

    Identity?Rationale-Internal Narrative-Public Narrative

    The dashes represent components that have a transparent relationship to the player. Rationale is *why* you are forming the narrative ("My character would probably be racist and in the game setting, would say X"). Internal Narrative is the vision, Public Narrative is its expression.

    The ? separates Identity, the player's subject position (upbringing/culture/gender affiliation/etc.), which is is transparent in some places and not in others, depending on the person. It nevertheless is the basis the player uses for every subsequent step.

    In other words: Except to the extent that you cannot account for your Identity, the process is transparent for you. You know why you did it.

    Now, consider a process of reaction. "A" is the event/player being reacted to:

    Identity?Rationale-Internal Narrative-Public Narrative-Public Narrative A |? Internal Narrative A|? Rationale A|? Identity A

    | is the point where the other person does not know how one element follows to the next. I've put ? beside it because we can have:

    1) Situations where the element is not known |

    2) Situations where the element is known in part ?

    . . . and these are not stopping points. I might know someone's sense of Identity pretty well, but not how other stuff follows from it.

    These |? states are the source of a great deal of confusion, because each player has a far fuller awareness of their participation than anyone else's. When we think in terms of a faux-autonomous "space," we are drawing on our full participation cycle but only a fraction of the other person's. We may (by, for example, intimating the other person is "insecure") even use the false space to assume knowledge of elements of the other person's process to which we are not privy.

    The most vexing thing, when it comes to bigotry is this: In most psychologically healthy, ethical adults, bigotry is the result of reflexive expressions of Identity. You say annoying things because you were raised that way, you have unresolved issues or you come from a different culture, and the full process of expression comes from a place that is not transparent to you. Note that this is not a justification for saying annoying things; rather, it's incumbent upon the individual to explore their actions, correct them and acquire better habits.

    So consider this:

    1) Many people who have been discriminated against have experienced it in a reflexive form.

    2) These people do not have a guaranteed insight into your participation process.

    3) Therefore, they do not necessarily know whether your contribution is reflexive discrimination or a studied simulation with safe limits.

    The above post is culled from my personal notes on theory, and I'll add to it another concept: Interior Access. This is a bit of craft I developed a few years ago, but I doubt I invented it. Interior Access is play with a focus on creating transparency when we witness other people contribute. Here's what it is:

    1) It is an explicit right to interrogate the player about motives.

    2) The player being interrogated may not refer to firm system elements (getting bonuses, etc.) -- this analysis is about the player. It's personal.

    3) The player *may* refer to abstract game/narrative elements (what my character would do and why, what I want from the store), but the interrogator may choose to ask for information that's deeper than these constructions.

    4) The interrogator may not express an opinion until after completing the interrogation.

    5) The interrogator may not attach a value judgment to the player in response, but may express doubts and critiques of the player's contribution process.

    I developed Interior Access as a GMing technique, but it could be used without that position. It can be intense, because the player literally has nowhere to hide -- not behind system, setting, character or anything else. You don't use it all the time and it comes and goes, since everybody sits in the hot seat eventually and learns to own their own decisions, understand where other people are coming from and volunteer their interior process. Things will build up again, but you have IA to deal with it.

    I recommend it for issues like this because we get the right to learn about each other and understand what's going on. Critique stings less because it doesn't come out of left field -- you are *expected* to say something when the situation above occurs and run through IA.
