\n I know some of you were interested in the progress of Bmovie and how I was doing with getting an indie card game pinted.

After a little bit of a hiatus in communication from Avalon, I have been speaking to Lance about printing 'Revenge of the B-movie'. I now have a quote from them and it comes to $1528(\xc2\xa3790) for a 500 deck run with 100 of those sent to the states and 400 sent to my home address in the uk. That price includes the shipping.

I am now hoping to retail the game for about \xc2\xa310 a deck with a \xc2\xa35 - \xc2\xa36 retail price point. Hopefully this should be out by Dragonmeet, which is nice. I think for the states this will need to be around $15 rather than $20 but I was wondering what you guys think. The game is 60 cards, full colour, with tuck box and rules.

Break even for the run is going to be about 100 decks, dependent on where they are sold and I reckon I can make a profit of about $6k(\xc2\xa33k) on the run.

Cheers

Iain

    Posted By: ihmcallisterthis will need to be around $15 rather than $20
    \nFor a $20 card game, I'm expecting at least 110 cards in full color and they look totally awesome. Four color printing on front and back. It looks gorgeous.

    Gloom is in this category (it's on clear cards) and it has cards that look like this:



    ...while not in full color, it looks totally awesome.


    For a card game around $10-$12, I'm still expecting a 110 card game that looks good or a 55 card game that looks awesome.

    Bang! is in this category (110 cards, looks good) and it has cards that look like this:



    Dungeoneer is in this category as well, and this 55 card expansion has cards that look like this:




    As a purchaser of a card game, I'm not willing to cut a game the same amount of slack as I would a RPG designer just because the designer is doing a short print run or isn't a big company. If you're putting a $20 card game out there, it's got to have high production values and have the same shelf presence as other games at that price point. If I saw your game with 60 cards (and I have to ask, why such an odd number? Most printers use 55 card sheets. It seems like you're wasting resources you're paying for.), if I saw your game with 60 cards sitting on a game shelf in the same size box that a deck of regular playing cards come in, I'm probably not even going to look at it. Especially when I see the $14.95 price tag.

    My gut feeling is a card game that comes in a box the size of a deck of standard playing cards shouldn't be more than $7.99 retail.

    My friend has a game called High School Drama!. It comes in a smaller than standard size for board games. He was surprised to find that vendors and distributors at trade shows would come up and feel the heft of the box to determine if they'd carry it. If it felt heavy to them, it's a good game to carry at the $23 price point. If it was lighter -- as crazy as it sounds -- not as many people would have carried the game. This is why when I see Bang! (110 cards) in a box that's twice the size as a standard card deck, I'm thinking I'm getting a good deal for $10.95. If the Dodge City expansion came in a smaller box that was the actual size of the expansion deck (65 cards), I'd think that $10.95 would be a ripoff.

    \n Oh, whatever you do, make sure you get a proof copy before they print the final run. Don't just send your files to the printer and wait for a pallet to arrive at your house to find out your purples are too red, your pinks are oddly yellow, and the color separation on the cards that are supposed to look like parchment actually look like parchment and not colored like someone urinated on the cards*.

    Also leave room on the back of the box for UPC stickers. If you don't have space for that, you won't be able to get the game into retail stores. That is, if you're wanting B-Movie to wind up in retail outlets.


    * True story from a local designer that didn't get a proof copy. Except it wasn't the cards, it was the box.

    \n I'm on the other end of the spectrum from Thomas. I don't mind paying more to support independent card/board games. There are so few! I would gladly pay $15 - $20 for any size deck if I know the game is fun and I know the money is going to the designer. Those considerations outweigh all others for me.

    Also: Trying to compete with the big names of card game production in retail is a good way to lose a lot of money. It sounds like you have made sensible financial decisions so far, and ending up with a profit margin that is double your print cost will let you continue to print and sell your game. Don't drop below that price point!

    \n The quantity perception is a good point but totally bogus in a lot of cases.
