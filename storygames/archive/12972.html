\n So you can incentivize RP or tie it to mechanics ("Take an extra Hammer Die every time you act Romantically"), and this has an illustrious history of working for many games and groups. However, if you're not careful, you can end up in one of these failure conditions:

- The narration has nothing to do with the mechanic and loses some impact. (Parlor narration, right?)

- Players take the pure mechanical route. ("I always want Hammer Dice, so I'll always act Romantically")

- Flat narration to squeeze in a bit more of an obvious bonus. (Example: some Wushu play, when each extra Stunt die is so useful. "I kick him in the face (1), and in the face (+1), and in the face (+1)." This example is obviously too flat to be accepted; but in any case, this is a player forcing themselves to generate content, rather than being rewarded for content they wish to generate.)

Is there a line at which incentivized RP is no longer effective? Is this a factor of group preference and playstyle? What has worked for you?

    \n Situation 1:
    For me, the first problem is avoided with sensible design that ties the mechanic with the narration intrinsically. If the game is based on the the story of Hephaestus and his romances, hammer dice might indeed be sensibly linked to romantic behaviour and it would be logical. Joe the carpenter getting better at carpentry thanks to his girlfriend, not so much.

    Situation 2:
    Best solved by rewarding behaviours that are problematic. Give the hammer die only if your romance restricts your actions or causes you do do something foolish.

    Situation 3
    It all depends on sensible limits on the maximum incentive. I also tend to build "in a new fashion" or "in a creative fashion" in my incentive mechanics. I kick him in the face only works the first time, if at all.

    Posted By: DevPIs there a line at which incentivized RP is no longer effective?
    \nMechanistic "Hammer Dice for acting Romantically" mechanics that aim to program player behavior are often very dumb about social motivation. What happens with the intimacy/desperation/sincerity dice in My Life with Master is that if your roleplaying for the bonus is weak, another player has the opportunity of delivering something interesting and different that takes the bonus away from you. It's maybe not a conscious thing, but people absolutely use this to keep others from phoning it in.

    \n Lots of different devices work for me depending on the game.

    One I really like I think of as a soft incentive. In one regard the whole point of the scene is to get a buff for a future battle. But because you're already in the scene and you choose it in preference to other explicit kinds of scenes your purpose is understood it feels more relaxed to me.

    It's the "Connection scenes" in Contenders. It doesn't feel stilted because the explicit purpose of the scene is to connect with your Connection and the scene begins and ends with that one goal. It's a few years old and I personally think it's hot tech, but it's pretty unexplored territory as far as use or hacking for other games.

    \nI use two different styles to get around that one.
    1) There is a catalog of different actions, and each needs to be activated, before you get to reuse an action. These actions are broadly described, but they make sure, that the player uses variation and it also reminds the player of what kinds of variations there (e.g. kick, head butt, punch, slap, hurt knuckles, nosebleed etc.).
    2) The player asks a another player to do the narration. It works quite well and is rather funny. E.g. I want to punch the opponent, but instead of me describing it, and I turn and ask another player to do it. This adds a whole new level of description and removes the repitive flatness.

    Posted By: DevPIs there a line at which incentivized RP is no longer effective? Is this a factor of group preference and playstyle?
    There is indeed a line where incentivized role-play is no longer effective.

    In games with less focus on realistic behavior and consequences, the incentivized role-play may always work well. You may fantasize any consequence within the parameters of the genre, and get away with it, at least as long as it drives the drama and feeds the imagination of the other players.

    In games with focus on realism, the effectiveness of incentives will vary dependent on theme:
    - Themes within the experience of most players, will lend themselves very well to such game-play; the players will intuitively know what to do, how to react and what consequences to expect. And the game will be better for it.
    - Themes outside the experience of the players, is not suited to incentivized game-play. The incentives may guide them on what to do, but are seldom effective in guiding them in how to react, and are far from giving good guidance on consequences. In games like these you need methodic tools (both narrative- and mechanical-tools) to help the players discover the consequences and conditions produced by the character-actions, and to help them into realistic reactions.

    War is one theme that demands methodic support to be played out in a realistic way. Most players have little or no knowledge of what a soldier goes through, and are ill-equipped to interpret realistic characters in conditions of war. The same is true for most other themes of violence too; most people dont do it, and don't experience it, so they just don't know what will hit/kill/shock/stun/break them in a fight, or what it will do to them after the actual violence is over.

    \n I have been doing this with 0 Hunters

    \nI have yet to hit a point where incentivized RP does not have the effect desired. In my game the incentive is based around character tropes (called types) if you play to your type, other players can then give you extra dice. there are limiting factors to make sure it's not abused.
    \n
    \nHowever I have found the challenge of playing to a character type to be difficult and rewarding enough in and of it's self, the reward serves as recognition for the effort.
    \n
    \nI think incentivized RP really hinges on how you set your goals and how player narration is framed. the goal to act romantically is going to produce some strange narration if it's framed in a scene about hacking a computer.
