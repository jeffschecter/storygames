\n
\n A bit of a sidetrack from "Games that actively support reincorporation" was the question of how to handle character weaknesses like Indiana Jones' fear of snakes.

So the first disadvantage mechanics (starting with Champions) assigned character weaknesses as part of character creation, and the mechanics like frequency rolls as well as GM advice encouraged the GM to bring character weaknesses into the game. In Forge parlance, these are pure flags -- i.e. the player takes them to communicate to the GM and other players what he is interested in. So if I take a Hunted, I'm saying that's the sort of enemy I would like -- and the mechanics require the GM to provide that enemy at a certain frequency rather than unrelated enemies. However, they can't be actively used by the player.

A number of later games like Theatrix (1993) and The Babylon Project (1997) introduced a variant mechanics where players got rewards when the weakness actually appeared in play. The latter has the advantage of being more automatically balancing, and it introduces an incentive for players to make the weakness appear.

In terms of play, though, this incentive seems tricky to me -- because it asks me to work against myself as a player. i.e. To portray my character well, I make the character try to avoid her weaknesses. However, I am encouraged to make out-of-character choices to thwart my character's desires and have her weaknesses preyed upon. I find that it is a tricky line to walk. This seems related to the Czege Principle, though not identical.

As a Spirit of the Century GM, I have a sheet of PC Aspects, and I go through them to regularly hit PCs with Compels from them. By SotC, players can suggest compels, but it is per se up to the GM to offer them -- and in practice usually I make the offers. The balance works well for me -- which is to say that I think I prefer avoiding weaknesses and having other people bring them up.

    \n The way that Aspects work in SOTC is as a device for regulating the flow of an in-game currency called Fate points. You pay Fate points for game-mechanical or narrative advantage; you earn Fate points for game-mechanical or narrative disadvantage. So, in the case of Indiana Jones:

    A self-compel:

    Indy's player (call him "Indie"): "Jock! There's a snake on the plane!" [holds up a Fate point to signal the self-compel]
    The GM: "That's just my pet snake Reggie." [acknowledges the self-compel by tossing "Indie" a Fate point]
    Indie: "I hate snakes Jock! I hate 'em!"
    The GM: "Oh, for Pete's sake, Indy, show a little backbone!" [makes a note: "Jock thinks Indy is weak-kneed"]

    But:

    "Snakes! Why did it have to be snakes?"
    "Asps! Vipers! Very dangerous -- you go first." [GM holds up a Fate point, signaling the compel]

    costs Indy a Fate point when his player says, "Okay, even though I'm afraid of snakes, I gulp hard and let them lower me into the chamber" and hands the GM a Fate token. Later, he can recoup that Fate point by self-compelling a game-mechanical penalty to his skills when dealing with the snakes, trading reduced effectiveness for more game-currency.

    If Indie'd said, "No way do I go down there until we get rid of the snakes!" then the GM would've handed over the Fate point.

    So the player isn't authoring his or her own adversity; there's still a negotiation between two players over what the character faces and how he or she responds

    \n I'm with John on this one. I think it may well be a preference thing, but I've thought about this one in depth. Basically, to keep going with the parallel, we want Indy to be threatened, but we also want him to win.
    \n
    \nThat's key, the character with whom we identify, we want to win. This is typical of RPG play (though, again, far from universal), and is how I almost always feel about my characters.
    \n
    \nWhich is not the same as saying that we're disappointed if the character fails. Well... this is difficult...
    \n
    \nWhen watching a horror movie, we "want" to be scared. When watching a tear-jerker, we "want" to be sad. These are "negative" emotions that we generally experience by sympathizing with the characters in the drama. We "want" them to fail, because that's part of horror or sadness, or just setting up victory later to be sweeter.
    \n
    \nBut we don't "root" for them to fail.
    \n
    \nWe don't shout, "Go in the temple, Indy, so you can find snakes in there!" We shout, "Go in the temple to find the Ark, and win... even if you have to face some snakes which you hate, which will prove how strong your desires are, or show how human you are!" That last part being even more tacit than the first.
    \n
    \nThe point is that we, as players, though we understand and like the downsides of the drama, are like the moviegoer cheering Indy on... advocates for the character's success.
    \n
    \nI choose that term very precisely. Yes, when cheering in the theatre, we don't rationally assume that our cheers and advocacy through good thoughts for the character will really help him win. But, like sympathizing with your favorite sports team, we still engage in this bit of sympathetic magic. By doing so, we make the success of the target feel like ours.
    \n
    \nIn a roleplaying game, this is made all the moreso true, because we actually can affect the character's chances for success. By, for instance, looking cleverly at the character's areas of effectiveness, and using them to maximal profit for the character.
    \n
    \nYes, we min-max for the character.
    \n
    \nNow the "problem" comes in when we then take on character victory or success as a measure of our own victory or success. If we can be accounted as having these positive effects on the character's outcomes, are we not showing off our own tactial prowess? And, if so, don't we likely have gamism? And therefore not narrativism or something else?
    \n
    \nWell...
    \n
    \nThis is where things get very, very tricky. I think that it is quite possible to both advocate for the character, and not to take credit for his success. Perhaps taught, in fact, by cheering poitlessly for Indy, we learn to try to modify the character's outcomes positively, but also expect no credit for doing so. Instead, the reward is getting a good story.
    \n
    \nBecause there is a flip-side to creation of conflict, and that's the creation of solutions, or at least potential solutions. This is the creative input of such a player. It is precisely, to me, the Czege Principle that we're talking about here. The reason that it's unfun to give us the role of creating the conflict and/or it's resolution, is because we then take on some of the "blame" for the character's loss. We lose that sympathetic magic that connects us to the character. We indeed become the "author" now, and are no longer the advocate for the character, but for the story.
    \n
    \nAnd this, for me at least, goes over the line. Oh, to be sure, I'll have my character do stupid things, or do things that are likely to result in trouble for him. But I do so expecting the GM to hammer my character. Not me.
    \n
    \nThis is why, in fact, Universalis (and games like it) are so different to me from "traditional" RPG play. I can, in fact, be the author of a character to the extent that I'm just creating a story - that's it's own sort of great fun. But there are times where I want to simply collaborate in the story, my role in the process only being that of beign the advocate for the character's success. With the GM or some other force being responsible for the forces that actually cause the negatives for the character. So that, like Indy, whom I did not write, I can sympathize more.
    \n
    \nNone of that is particularly rational behavior. But that doesn't move me at all on the subject.

    \n There is an additional thing about player flags/disadvantages that does not quite make them fit the setup/payback or reincorporation/heightening thing: They are introduced before play, during setting construction/character generation, and not as part of the fiction/story.
    \n
    \nSo when a player writes down on a character sheet "afraid of snakes, -10 points" and then the GM or player only brings it into the fiction/story in the climax/crisis, then it is not really playing its full potential.
    \n
    \nGoing back to the Indy example, the first snake in the plane is really not payback/reincorporation, it is actually setup/presentation.
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
