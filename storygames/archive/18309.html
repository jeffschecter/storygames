\n Okay so this is something that's been bugging me about the entire *World family of games.

I often see people claiming that in AW, 'say yes or roll dice' doesn't apply. 'To do it, do it' and 'if you do it, you do it' take its place. The MC is not the arbiter of whether what you want to do obviously just succeeds or if it warrants a roll. The moves do that for us, with their perfectly non-ambiguous (jk) fictional triggers. If you seize something by force, you roll to seize by force. Yes, even if the MC is all 'well it's much more interesting if you succeed and knock him over, what would even happen if you didn't get hold of that thing?'

'Say yes or roll the dice' was a concept that solved a problem for me \u2013 it states that if a conflict or action is not interesting, skip over it. 'If you do it, you do it' can, depending on the move trigger, bring back the rolls for uninteresting actions. The key point is 'depending on the move trigger'. In order to make a good move, the trigger must completely cover any and all situations where that action is interesting enough to warrant a roll, and no other situations.

When this works, it solves the same problem as 'say yes\u2026'. What happens when it doesn't make sense to fail, for example when attacking someone defenceless? Well, the Hack and slash move (from DW) says 'When you attack an enemy in melee\u2026' and a defenceless enemy isn't in melee, are they? 'When you seduce or manipulate someone', when the group has the same mental model of what manipulating means (which is hard to achieve), is the same. If you're just talking, we don't roll. When you've got the kind of leverage to call it manipulation, that's an interesting situation where we let the dice decide.

--

What made me post this is the discussion about Dungeon World's Spout Lore move over here. I can think of a lot of situations where it's maybe not impossible but at least very tricky for the GM to think of a good hard move on a miss. Situations that aren't interesting. Where I as a GM would like to go by 'say yes\u2026' and just give them whatever their accumulated knowledge ought to be; at least what I (given my prep about the world) consider to be common knowledge in the realm. But no \u2013 if we go strictly by 'if you do it, you do it', you have to roll.

For the combat example, this is built into the trigger nicely \u2013 situations where I as a GM would have just 'said yes' are pretty much covered by the qualifier 'in melee'. With spouting lore it's not that easy. There are way more situations where I would not have called for a roll if it were up to me, but that still go for that trigger. With a certain reading of the move, I completely understand the GM mentioned in that thread, who didn't let his players roll to Spout lore.

I'm not writing this to critique DW in particular. I think generally the basic moves of all published AW hacks I've played hold up pretty well. But this is something that I think deserves mention when people start creating new moves. If you're playing RAW (which maybe no-one does), the move has to make the 'say yes or roll dice' decision for you. When you write a move, the onus is on you to get that trigger right.

--

Maybe this 'say yes\u2026' vs 'to do it\u2026' is a false dichotomy; it's probably a bit of a sliding scale. But I still wonder \u2013 @lumpley, was it your intention with the AW engine that the triggers on the moves should (ideally) serve as the judgement for what warranted a roll? Normal caveats like 'it's your game, do as you like when you play' aside. If so, how much thought went into the triggers \u2013 did you have this in mind?

How about designers of other AW hacks \u2013 did you ever go over the basic moves to make sure they wouldn't trigger when no roll was actually needed?

--

'Go aggro' has been subject to quite some debate since AW was released, and I think it's because the trigger is vague. I mean, 'going aggro on someone' evokes a certain feeling but exactly what are you doing? I've definitely been in fights rolling 'seize by force', thought of meanings of 'aggro' (aggro range of monsters, being aggressive\u2026), and thought 'yeah, I'm aggro right now' (NB: I'm not a native speaker). I've seen advice going 'well, look at the 7-9 list. If none of those even make sense, you're not going aggro.' That's clever \u2013 interpreting the trigger so that it triggers when the move would give interesting results!

Maybe that's the solution. Don't care too much about getting the triggers right \u2013 define the move from what it does. 'When you do x, y or z happens'. Well, if I'm doing x and neither y or z seems plausible obviously the move isn't for this kind of x-situations.

Then again, advice for 'acting under fire' says 'If you really can't think of anything to do to the player on a 7-9, pass it up. They caught a lucky break.' (not exact wording). This supports the idea that if you're acting under fire, you roll the goddamn +cool, cause the move says so.

--

If you take as a rule that the moves determine what's an interesting situation to roll for, they also start informing the fiction in a subtler way. If we decide that the Spout lore move is part of our game, we have decided that 'consulting one's accumulated knowledge of a subject is potentially dangerous, every time'. It decides a priori (with no knowledge of the particular fiction) that in this story, when people start thinking about what they know, that's a dramatic situation. Just like Hack and slash decides that attacking someone in melee is. It's just, that one isn't so far from our frame of reference so we don't think of it that way.

On the other hand, if it's allowed to 'say yes' instead of rolling the dice, the moves become watered-down guidelines instead of hard rules. It's possible to attack someone and agree that yeah, this li'l kobold is not even a threat to you, don't bother rolling. That's not necessarily a bad thing \u2013 it's just a different mindset.

--

Obviously I make this sound like a bigger deal than it is. If you find yourselves in a session of someone's hack and realise that the move would have you roll for something completely uninteresting, with no interesting options for either outcome, I guess you'd just skip it. I'm still interested, however, in hearing designers' thoughts on this. I've only played AW, DW and Monsterhearts, so that's my frame of reference.

Is there a solid thought behind the idea that 'if you do it, you do it' should replace 'say yes or roll the dice'? (In games that use it of course, not in every game.) Or is that simply something I've made up?

Do you even get what I'm talking about here?

    \n The philosophy is: if you're not making one of the limited number of moves available (all of which have explicit fictional triggers), then you don't roll for it. But also, the AW system collapses down (in onion layers, as Vincent describes on Anyway) so that if you forget to roll for something, you're missing out on a part of the game, but it's no big deal; the game still functions.

    The core of AW is a conversation between the various players about what happens (totally functional, by itself). Then the GM's agendas, principles, say alwayses, and moves are layered on top of that (which creates a totally functional diceless system for play). Then the player's stats and moves and gear and other stuff are layered on top of that (creating the full system). But there's always the basic conversation and the diceless core functioning beneath the layer where dice are rolled.

    It's not a big problem if you don't roll dice when you're supposed to! The principles and other stuff will carry you through (though you may be missing some aspect of the game). But sometimes it's a problem to roll dice when you're not supposed to (i.e. when there's no fictional trigger), because then the stuff you're supposed to do afterwards may not make any sense.

    \n What follows is just my opinion, based on a combo of "what I find fun" and an attempt to enjoy the game for what it is, "rules as written":\n
    The question is, is that a case of:
    \u2013 The group (or MC) deciding on a case-by-case basis that this particular situation doesn't require a roll
    Yes. Sometimes the results and consequences will be obvious. With no uncertainty to resolve, don't roll. When there's uncertainty, roll. (Or, I should say, interesting uncertainty. If no one cares, also don't roll don't play through it, just skip ahead.)

    As for extreme likelihoods, like "I'd say there's a 1% chance this would work" or "I'd say there's a 99% chance this would work", I believe the recommended solution is to hand the GM the responsibility for honoring the fiction. So if I try to shoot an NPC point blank in the head while they're dazed, and I roll badly, the GM might narrate how I do in fact blow off their earlobe as they lurch to the side. If the group cares about the percentage discrepancy between the fiction (1%) and the dice (e.g. 42%), then either this isn't the game for you, or start adding in situational modifiers (+3 for being super easy).

    When the results are obvious but the consequences are not, I think you have the following option: roll fails, action succeeds, horrible consequences ensue. I blow the NPC's head off but am instantly shot by their enraged compatriot. But I might be botching a rule on that one.

    As for "what to do if the roll fails?", to me that sounds like a problem worth solving rather than dodging, because I'd expect it'll come up in situations where we really want to roll. It's basically a GM skill, in my opinion. Learn the principles, look over the hard moves, and be creative. Personally, I think "During the time you spent doing that, bad stuff occurred" is easy to throw into random situations.

    \n@David_Berg : yes, "success"+ a hard move on a failed roll is totally a valid *World move, and sometimes necessary, in my experience. Those have been some of my favourite moments in play, actually.

    I'm reading this and looking forward to some more responses.

    My experience with these games is that the OP's dilemma is a very real one. I consider it a flaw in the design of these games (AW and relatives, which I enjoy a great deal): sometimes it's really not clear which is the better way to go. It says, "if you do it, do it", but sometimes it makes more sense to apply other principles (like "say yes or roll the dice", which isn't an AW rule, but still often useful in a game of AW).

    The best solution I can think of is mentioned above: some combination of "follow the MC principles" (e.g. "is this making the PCs' lives interesting?"), and "let's play it a few different ways and see which is most fun for our group".

    It's better if you look at the rules and procedures as a painter's palette of paints and brushes. Which one you use at any given time is a question of art or skill, based on the effect you want.

    Someone's sneaking up on an enemy, and they want to stab them in the back.

    It sounds like we should roll "go aggro" here, but actually it would be more fun for everyone if we treat it as an "act under fire" roll. And maybe the next time the MC simply decides to "look through crosshairs" and "inflict harm": the target is dead, no roll needed.

    There's room there for different interpretations, and that's a huge part of where an MC's art and skill comes into play.

    The best advice I've seen for deciding which to do if you're really not sure is as follows:

    A. Keep asking questions about the fiction. Establish exactly what's happening, how fast, when, what it smells like.

    Chances are good you'll pretty see why a certain move is better than another, based on something someone playing says.

    For instance, maybe the player says, "I'm terrified that the guard dogs around the corner will hear me struggling with the sentry!"

    Cool, then I as an MC would probably choose to "inflict harm" (the sentry's dead! blood everywhere!) and ask the player to roll to act under fire, to see if she can take out the sentry without attracting the attention of the guard dogs.

    B. Whenever you're not sure what to do as an MC, it's your turn to talk and everyone is looking at you.

    This means: time to make an MC move. Look at your list and pick something. "Announce future badness" and "offer an opportunity" are very good general-purpose moves for such situations (along with asking questions, as mentioned above).

    When people start playing AW there are often these moments where the MC gets an itch, like, "Oh, man, I really feel the need to roll some dice right now."

    But none of the moves match. What do you do?

    Make an MC move.
