\n One of the things I'm finding with trying to design rpg's is that if I playtest them with vets they always seem to work and then if I playtest them with rookies they crash and burn.
A problem that bit me recently was one with narration rights - That's Drama's narration rights are nothing new ... they're basically Misery Bubblegum ... and it seems to me any game that gives the players inviolable narration rights and combines that with character advocacy could exhibit this. (The Pool, Inspectres, Wilderness of Mirrors, In a Wicked Age, Archipelago)

Frex:

The Pool - you've written some statement about your character and what they want, like "I want to find my father's killer." You make a roll and get your MoV. So you say, "... and then my father's killer walks into the room and confesses and I (forgive/kill) him"

Misery Bubblegum - you have your Dream - maybe it's to escape with one of the other PC's to a better life. So you play the high card and nobody can trump you. So you narrate "...and I convince Joanna to come with me and we escape to a better life."

Inspectres - you're investigating something - but instead of giving yourself a small clue with your narration rights, you say "...and then the evil mastermind comes out and says, 'You have gotten too close to discovering my evil plan!' but he slips on a banana peel and we capture him."

Wilderness of Mirrors - say your mission is to save the discovered mole. you win narration rights and say "...and then we find a secret passage that bypasses all the dangers we spent an hour designing and it leads right to the cell where they're holding the mole, and we escort him out."

In a Wicked Age - you've got your best interest, let's say it's to get pregnant by the harvest god. You're in a conflict. They challenge "I stab you in the eye" and you answer in a way that they don't get what they want but they have the advantage, so you say, "I duck and escape into the woods, where I meet and get impregnated by the harvest god, but you catch up with me in the act, and are standing over me with the knife as he sows his seed..."

Archipelago - you've got your destiny "will I become king?". you get to frame your own scene, so you say, "All right, it's my coronation ceremony, and the high priest is putting the crown upon my head ..."

Usually, when playing with vets, these problems never come up ... that's been my experience, anyway. Why is that? (I have some theories but I'd like to throw it open first.)

Some of these games, if rookies to narration rights pick up the game, then the problems reveal. Not because the rookies are being dicks: it's just because they think that's how they're supposed to play, or maybe they're used to games where you win, or maybe they're testing the system. Some of the games continue to work anyway even with rookies testing the limits. Why do some of the games work - and others not? (Again, I have some theories, but ... )

    \n Simulationism.

    Plain and simple. We're playing "The Mountain Witch." We all know the genre, and we're actively trying to emulate it. Nobody spontaneously says in act one "I know O-yanma is supposed to meet me here, by this twisted tree, because my Dark Fate is telling me so, but I've had a change of heart, and now I kill him. Anyone want to join up with the dice?"

    Because such play is antithetical to the genre.

    Most games don't bother with rules to lock down your play, because they assume a playstyle or they assume a set of genre tropes. I doubt there are many authors out there who would write down all their assumptions, or even care to.

    Take Puppetland, for instance. It's wide open and the players are free to narrate almost anything, but in Puppetland there's a game-mechanics sponsored feedback channel to tell players they're out of the genre. When they cuss or swear or murder or do things that aren't in line with a children's puppet show, they get a black mark on their character sheet. Fill that sheet up, and that puppet won't come back next session.
    But there aren't any rules to handle what to do with the puppet until the end of this session. Are they free to go on a murdrous rampage? Swear until everyone's ears bleed? Violotae the nearest sheep puppet in an orgy of debauchery?
    Nothing in the rules says you can't, and indeed, there isn't even any game-mechanic feedback to prevent you.

    The underlying assumption that keeps everyone going is the genre you're emulating. It's all the agreement that the group has about how to use the rules of the game. Without wich, all games are teetering on the edge of madness and ruin.

    But hey, sometimes I like a little risk, myself.

    As for the Rookies / Vets thing, I find most often that it falls along people sympathetic to the theme. Run an anime game for Western gunslingers, and they short-cut the game and shoot holes in it. Run a sci-fi game for ren faire geeks, and they do the same.

    Until the underlying themes appeal to the player, (or until you find a player who is willing to look for the theme and enjoy it, no matter what it is) you're going to find people who shoot their load early and skip the foreplay. Don't burn yourself out.

    Hope this helps. Feels a little ranty to me, so I apologize if that's the way it sounds to you.

    Misery Bubblegum - you have your Dream - maybe it's to escape with one of the other PC's to a better life. So you play the high card and nobody can trump you. So you narrate "...and I convince Joanna to come with me and we escape to a better life."
    \nMisery Bubblegum bounds in your narration rights with the constraint of "what happens next". So, if it's the scene where you and Joanna are having that discussion, and you win, absolutely you can narrate, "...and I convince her to come with me." That escaping to a better life thing? Off the table, because it's not "next" - you'd need more scenes to resolve it.

    But if it's not that scene, and instead it's a scene about who ends up getting embarrassed at prom, your rights don't extend far enough to give you that option in the first place.

    The issue at hand seems to be that you're equating "inviolable" with "unconstrained". In every one of the example games you mentioned, constraints exist on your narration rights - sometimes enforced by the rules, sometimes enforced by the social contract. Could some games do better at defining where the boundaries are? Maybe. But that's probably out of scope for the thread.

    \n I'll answer for the games on your list that I have some familiarity with:

    In A Wicked Age: At first glance there's nothing to prevent that per se, but it might be straining the intent of the action and answer rules a bit. The book says "in your answer, you have to say what happens and what [your character] does, that prevents [your opponent] from carrying out his action, but leaves [your character] at the disadvantage." So, technically, the example did say "what happens and what I do," but adding on a rider (not just "I escape and run into the woods" but "I escape and run into the woods and have AN ENTIRE SEX SCENE, which you come in at the end of") might be dirty pool. Or it might not. Depends on the group.I don't think IAWA has any stipulations on how big a span of time "say what happens" covers, but I expect people's default assumptions will be that it's limited to actions within the scene.

    But that probably doesn't get at your central question. for that we have to look at the Best Interests themselves. When you create BEs you don't just say whatever. For players, you "look at other characters and guess where they're going to be strong. Cast your character's best interests against them there." For the GM, you "ruthlessly threaten the players' characters, where they're weakest." So all the BEs will be aimed at other characters, and consequently other players. So, "impregnated by the Harvest God?" That's a BE that you aimed at a character. If the Harvest God, is a PC or NPC, they're going to have some agency in the matter. It's out of bounds to say what they do. If the player of that character is willing to impregnate you, then great! Otherwise you've got to get in conflicts, negotiate, etc. If by some chance the God isn't a character--and this is REALLY an outlier, because it requires someone, a priest maybe, for your BE to be set against, and requires the GM to say "nah, I guess i'll pass on playing the Harvest God"--then great! You can say you got impregnated by the Harvest God! Good for you! Unless the dagger-person in the example was specifically trying to stop you from getting impregnated by the harvest god, then yes, you win. Nothing wrong with that; it's how the game works. There's nothing wrong with that; it's how the game works. (and you're apparently about to be stabbed, which is ALSO how the game works!)

    Archipelago This one is really easy. You set the scene that way, and someone says "Try a different way" and explains that getting crowned right off is pretty unsatisfying, and the Destiny would be more interesting if we were to progress toward it gradually. If you still insist, "no, I really want to begin with the Coronation," then someone says "that might not be so easy!" and you've got to draw a card for a "Yes, and" "No, but" etc outcome, which may get you crowned king or may not, and at the very least will take your story in a new direction.

    It's also possible that some player might have Ownership of Monarchy, or politics, or ceremony, or whatever, and be able to say "no, the way coronations work is, you've first got to..."

    Archipelago is great for this. It has explicit tools for adjusting play based on individual sensibilites and expectations, and getting on the same page with tone and content.



    So, in the first example, IAWA works with this kind of input because A) there are some barriers the rules throws up to prevent it happening, and if you manage to overcome or avoid those barriers, it's fine to do it! "Winning" Best Interests early and often is just standard, on-spec IAWA play.

    In the second example, Archipelago works with this kind of input because it has tools for the players to refine best practices as they play, until they arrive at something that works for everyone.

    The thing you're worrying about, Jamie--players describing victory too soon--seems tangled up in another issue, which is just players describing TOO MUCH at a go. That is often an issue with green players (Experienced RPG folks can err in the opposite direction, assuming that the ONLY thing the can or should describe is "I swing at him."). If it matters for a particular game or group, it probably bears having a talk about how small or large an increment of narration should be. it's an issue that will hopefully grow smaller and smaller as game texts refine their clarity about what players do moment-to-moment.

    The bottom line for me is simply being true to the fiction's integrity. I think that's an underlying assumption in most roleplaying games, though the text doesn't always address it clearly. Most of your examples border on meaningless because they lack a context of what fictional events led up to the narration. "You make a roll and get your MoV. So you say, '... and then my father's killer walks into the room and confesses and I (forgive/kill) him.'" Uh, ok...but what was the roll for? What were you trying to achieve? What was happening in general, what were other characters doing? If the roll's completely unrelated to your father, if no scene-setting or character actions remotely suggests that your father might appear...then maybe you're not being true to the integrity of the fiction. Again, with the IAWA example: what was the conflict about? Why is he trying to stab you? Was there anything in the story thus far that suggests that a forest tryst with the Harvest God might be around the corner?

    I think gamers generally expect only fiction-appropriate actions, but often look to the rules to provide this, by quantifying the range of fiction-appropriate actions and walling off areas that are inappropriate. So when they see a game deliberately leave those walls out, they think "oh, anything goes," and can almost feel a DUTY to narrate nonsensical shit that even they don't enjoy. That said, though, I think this problem is rare in practice. I've seldom seen it. Generally, I mostly see people describing what makes sense to the situation, with occasional excesses.

    Like I said, game texts aren't always clear on this point. If I may toot my own horn, the Dreaming Crucible has the rule Say What You See, which invites players to treat the fiction with integrity and describe what they really see, not what they think would be coolest and most clever. It's gone well. I will admit, it helps that Crucible is a Faerie journey game, so it encourages a bit of wild and sweeping narration. And I also explicitly wall off the end goal by saying it doesn't happen until a resource is depleted, so there's that, too.

    \n All roleplaying games have to deal with this, the absolute narrative authority thing is a red herring. Either the players will priorize the robustness of the fiction to some degree in the first place, in which case that's the level of robustness you can rely on for the game, or they won't, in which case they will break the game in whatever manner they're allowed to. Even with something like D&D they will constantly have their character try impossible things, so stupid and insipid that the GM can only shake his head and ignore it to make the game go on.

    This is not to say that individual games can't make it easier to avoid such abuse and make it more obvious so it's easier to spot and call. Teaching methodology is also useful: if you can make players understand the underpinning form of the game better, then they'll be less likely to play against the spirit of the game. Thus it's not entirely correct to say that this is only a problem for jerks: it's true that this will always be a problem for jerks no matter the quality of the game, but we can only make sure that honest players can coordinate towards good play by planning the game well in pedagogical terms.

    Zombie Cinema is a game with both "unlimited" narration rights and character advocacy. The game text explains to you that your mission as advocate is to express your character's interests and determine how the character strives towards his interests. It goes on to state that sometimes the player is not advocating, but rather has the task of narrating events to their conclusion. These are two separate roles the player switches between as necessary - this should be pretty clear to us all here, what I mean by saying that you're not supposed to sublimate your character's interests into the narration duties you sometimes have: the fact that your character wants somebody dead has nothing whatsoever to do with you the player, you're well able to narrate for us for a minute without letting your character's interests get in the way. This is true for all games in this tradition, beginning with Dust Devils.

    Notably Zombie Cinema does not have any actual mechanics or much in the way of structure to help avoid abuse of the above: if you miss the distinction the rules make due to preconceived notions or laziness, or if you choose to ignore it, the game is pretty helpless about it. The most likely way for the other players to intercept your abuse of the narrative powers will be when you overstep the purpose of that narrative role and start extending it from the current conflict to unrelated events in the story: here there is a good chance that another player will interrupt and ask you to keep your narration to the topic at hand, your character's victory in conflict. This is, however, merely treating the symptoms: the disease is the fact that you're mixing up your separate roles as a co-narrator and as a character advocate, and the fact that your narration is biased and pointless is merely a symptom of this misunderstanding. The problem will be corrected once you as a player truly accept that you have not only the privilege of immersing in your character, but also the duty of being a fair and level-minded co-author occasionally, when the game requires a coordinating hand.

    Also, I've definitely seen the first scene climax in Mountain Witch.     \n There's something there, Ralph, and you're not wrong. But I'm not sure you're right, either.

    Explicit procedure only takes you so far. At some point, you've got to make the game your own and take responsibility for it.

    The crappy thing about a lot of pre-Forge game design was that it was made up of a bunch of contradictory, mashed-together techniques, shibboleths, and rhetoric and a massive, massive set of assumptions about play culture and motivations. You could not make it work without doing what JD talks about - picking and choosing and customizing until you had something that suited you and your group. If you tried to follow the text, it would suck most of the time.

    The big advancement that came from blowing up that paradigm was "put in only the rules that you need, that serve your game's purpose, AND NO MORE." Getting the cruft and the assumed culture-of-play out, and destroying the assumption that all RPGs were basically D&D in drag, freed people to design and play in new ways without having to already be brilliant editors and adaptors of other people's crummy texts.

    But the big bad thing that came from that was re-introducing our own forms of cruft, in the form of games that prescribed TOO MUCH. Narrow, delicate little things that would work right for just this one thing, and would blow up if you didn't want to do exactly that. Designs got increasingly baroque as they tried to take all the judgment calls and - when you get down to it - creativity out of play.

    I think games like The Pool - incomplete, fragile, but not trying to be any more than that - are the most usefully eye-opening games for understanding where the next steps in building better games is. It's not in spelling everything out in some perfect text that will communicate to any - any! - potential player exactly how to get the same results that the designer intends. It's in creating the minimum necessary set of instructions that will enable a group with functional playing-together skills to get fun out of it.... and in isolating and teaching those playing-together skills as a thing that ANY game can benefit from.

    I have no problem with the idea that a fair proportion of the games we are seeing now are "for experienced story-gamers" and assume that set of skills are at least basically present. What's missing is the organized effort to share those skills outside the confines of specific games, and a solid set of entry-level games that teach those skills in a way that is portable rather than intimately tied to the mechanics and structures of one game.
