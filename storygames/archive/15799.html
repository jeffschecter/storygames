    \n It's conservative ideological underpinnings at work. Pretty simple. It's not like fantasy worlds are proofed for internal logic before publication.

    Or, for a more detailed literary theory answer: fantasy stories are usually written with themes and aesthetics that might be termed romantic or even atavistic. The genre is a continuation of historical romance, and it's a deliberate throw-back and refusal to embrace modernism in art. For this reason fantasy worlds tend to harken back to a mythic past when men were real men, women were real women and monsters were real monsters. Breaking archetypes is a powerful tool in this context, and often fresh new takes in the genre are "fresh" because they simply refute genre assumptions and represent a woman as a warrior hero or whatever.

    Looking at gender-switching in this light, it just might be the most fundamental part of human identity, and therefore a part that is almost impossible to mess with while simultaneously preserving and affirming the idyllic medieval reality of your average fantasy story. It's just not a very natural thing for people to write about when they're trying their hardest to work in what is essentially a lyrical form: the author is trying to impress upon you how beautiful the princess is or how brave the swordsman, so having weirdness like gender ambiguity mixed in there is just about the last thing in his mind. Occasionally it happens, but it's going to be massively transgressive: in traditional fantasy the hermafrodite will be a wizard or a witch, outside society and reproductive logic, very much an eldritch figure.

    Nothing in particular prevents one from doing transhumanism (which this amounts to) in a fantasy milieu, but the author's going to feel the pressure of expectations there: fantasy literature is where you write about ideals of beauty, so it's possibly going to look like you're unhealthily interested in alternative sexuality as an author. Of course that's surmountable, but apparently audiences, creative needs and cultural trends haven't yet crossed to make this happen in any great numbers.

    It might be indicative that the only fantasy novel that comes to mind at the moment that has significant trans-sexual content is Piers Anthony's Pornucopia, which is little more than an erotic picaresque itself. Can anybody name others?

    \n Funny you should mention that Ben. Up until the 19th century, so long as you reliable performed you gender, legally speaking, your sex was irrelevant. There are a number of British court cases to this effect, of women becoming men legally by simply adopting masculine decorum. It's not until the rise of biological sciences in the public sphere did matters of sex start to be tied so rigorously to matters of gender.

    So, in this respect most fantasy authors fail to reflect historical gender mores in their fiction. They typically project into the imagined past social arrangements that weren't well established until the 20th century.

    For further reading, I suggest Foucault's the History of Sexuality, Vol. 1 and Fausto-Sterling's Sexing the Body.

      \n If sci-fi is partially about "what do you think the future will be like?" I would say that mostly, fantasy is partially about "what do you think the past was like?" at least on a metaphorical or symbolic level. And most people would not say that the past was characterized by more fluid gender than the present. One might make the case that gender was less binary in this "past" compared to ~the English-speaking world 1850-1950 when the idea of fantasy as a market genre was being consolidated, but "less binary" and "more fluid" are not the same concept. when you combine that with the idea that, i would argue, "fantasy" tends to be more about ideas in a symbolic way than about the logical implications of a give idea, I don't think there's reason to be surprised that gender fluidity isn't a more pervasive theme in English-language fantasy writing.
      \n
      \nSo you get gender change as something that may happen significantly - look at Tip's transformation back into Ozma in Marvelous Land of Oz, or mythical examples like Tiresias, who is male, then female, then male, and bears a prophetic daughter while female, and gains supernormal insight himself(herself) due to the consequences of this path of change. Orlando is the subject of a novel because he, she is unusual in his, her fluidity of gender.
      \n
      \nAlso, saying that gender fluidity would be the default way of thinking about gender suggests that polymorphism and transformation depicted in that fantasy world is itself so pervasive compared to gender transition in the writer's culture that it would naturally follow that it would become the assumption - Is the experience of being transformed more central to most people's experience in that world that they define themselves as protean in regards to any aspect of change, not just gender? How much does King Arthur's change in social class lead to a sense of fluidity in class in the world he lives in?       \n
      \nBy all means, write and game fantasies that deal with these ideas. I don't think it's surprising that they aren't a default given that fantasy exists.

      \n I think there's also this issue:

      In order for a story to be accessible to readers, there need to be familiar baselines to which we can align our expectations.

      Like, if you have transgressive gender identities, temporary and shifting sexes, groundbreaking thoughts on magic, newly invented races, fantastical new cultures, strange otherworld physics, bizarre cuisines prepared with invented ingredients, etc, etc, etc... (many) readers can't juggle all of those difficult moving parts at once. There's no familiarity with which to establish traction.

      People need some familiarity. It builds a baseline and helps underscore why and how the unfamiliar is important. An author (especially a speculative fiction author) needs to provide some stable ground, even if only to subvert that stable ground later on.

      The bummer is that conservative social relations & gender roles are almost always part of that "familiar ground," and magic and invented cultures are almost always part of that "exciting unfamiliar." A good writer is someone who chooses their unfamiliar carefully, in a way that both serves their story and explores new literary ground.

Transhumanism just carries with it a whole extra set of reasons why we should see it there.

      Posted By: anansigirlBut, if we're just talking aboutperforminggender, a man can perform as a woman with no physical change at all. Right?
      \nSure, absolutely!

      But since (a) fluid gender performativity is complicated, and (b) writing complicated stuff is hard, and (c) a lot of folks don't get it... it's understandable that it doesn't show up more widely in most literature (assuming that it doesn't show up very widely in most literature, which I don't know if that's a true thing or not). But with transhumanism, writers are intentionally and specifically unpacking what it means to be a human and to break down traditional definitions of humanhood, gender fluidity becomes a thing that ought to be super prevalent. Partially because transhumanism's biological fluidity would provoke new thinking on gender, but more importantly because a genre that seeks to explore complicated ideas about body and self and transition simply can't do so effectively while holding to constructs like fixed gender binary.
