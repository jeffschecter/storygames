    \n Difficult, especially with the expense of text-messaging. Of course, one could use computer-based free messages, but then why not use email? Other problems are the lack of descriptive width to the play proceedings and the weak user interface of writing text messages.

    If I tried something of this sort, it'd be with some sort of game where the players have a lot of shared context within which lots of meaning can be compressed into short messages. It's not a roleplaying game, but perhaps I'd try Diplomacy - moves can be proposed and submitted by text message easily enough.

    On the other hand, I wrote something remotely like this a couple of years ago here. (City Spooks is the relevant one.) It's still not quite roleplaying, but at least it's a game, and I imagine it could be implemented with a GM instead of anonymous server processes. The text messages would still only be a method of communication on the move, though, instead of the primary medium. Tricky to make them the central means of play.

    \n Cool idea, David!

    Albert, I was thinking the same thing about Twitter myself. The same character limit as SMS, but accessible on the web and mobile.

    But the medium is tough because it lends itself to quick, declarative content. So your rules might want to hone in on that. If you could avoid dice rolling or somehow incorporate the mechanic into the messages, that would be even better.

    Dave M, I think the tough part about SMS vs. IRC is that most phones don't keep an easy-to-read history of SMS messages like the iPhone does, for instance. Also, IRC is great for multiple users, and SMS tends to be more person-to-person.

    But if you used Twitter, you could create a filter for the game, like #DitV. And have all the players subscribe to that filter.

    If you are into using PERL (I am not!), you could probably create a program to scrub entries for certain parameters (i.e. '1d6' or 2d8') and use a RNG to roll for you. But that's...like...pretty crazy and I'll have no part of it.

    \n Ritual phrases are words that players in certain games say at moments as instructed by the rules of the game. For example, the most prominent example of this is Polaris - in that game you say certain words when you start a session or when you frame a scene to set the mood, a bit like how a fairy-tale is started with "A long time ago in a land far, far away...". Polaris also features a conflict resolution system that is based on key phrases - you can say stuff like "It was not meant to be", and that has a rules-mechanical effect on how the game proceeds.

    \n Cell phone text message gaming?

    Okay, I do this with friends occasionally.
    The key is it needs to be systemless. Form dictates that all answers be short, fairly free-standing (which usually means singular-action-oriented), and play logically off of the previous posts. There is only ever one or two characters, there is only ever one plot thread.

    So, the other day Kaleigh (a non-gamer, save one session one time) messages me:
    "You step into the cave, and immediately a cool breeze makes your spine jump"

    The system is that I have 140 characters to respond, and I can abbreviate any words I want so long as my meaning is still communicated. In this case, form is sufficient system.

    I reply by asking:
    "What do I see in this fell cave?"
    "the bones of three hundred..."
    "I search for the relic. Is it amidst these bones?"
    "Yes u pick it up. Its shimmering"
    "I whisper to it the prophecy that I have taken to memory"
    "It trembles and cracks. U drop the bone to the cave floor. And then..."

    As something I'm posting outside of text messages, it's sorta lame, perhaps. As a slowly unwinding, small-scoped story that gets unfolded through little snippets of text every 5-10 minutes... it's so rad.

    \n Here's something I just made up!

    Grab some people. Choose some to be explorers and some to be advisers. Explorers take on the role of people exploring some intriguing location -- a haunted mansion, an ancient cave, an abandoned warehouse, and so on. The advisers sit back at home, telling the explorers what to do.

    In play, explorers discuss amongst themselves what they see and what their characters are doing. They text to advisers (in character) with what they've done, what they're seeing, any advice they need, etc. The advisers shoot back messages with nice advice -- which door they should go through, what the sound they're hearing might be, or what happens when the moon is full.

    Play ends when the explorers either die horribly or reunite with the advisers.

    \n It's funny... over at the RPG.net LARP forums, we were talking a while back about using cell phone apps to handle task resolution in parlor LARP (non-contact; not boffer or Airsoft stuff). A simple app would not only have your PC's abilities and stats and what-not, but it would also be able to use Bluetooth to detect others "in range" on which you could activate an ability. So folks are in a firefight, they just click to "shoot gun" and it prompts for a target, from a list of those in range.

    This isn't QUITE "RPG by text message," but it makes for a much cooler LARP system, in that game play would be WAY sped up and it would avoid a lot of handling errors (innocent or not) like failing to spend mana or blood for an ability use.

    Anyhow... I guess my whole point might be that, rather than come up with a TT RPG that can play via TM, why not develop an RPG that runs in an app by default, using IR, Bluetooth, or even (falling back to) TM to handle inter-character conflicts? Design for the medium of play, rather than hack something ffor a different medium into a (slow) mode of play. Make sense?
