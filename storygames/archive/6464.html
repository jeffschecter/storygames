\n I dunno, probably!

Bully Pulpit Games is a primary sponsor this year and I want to get the word out about the Forge booth at Gen Con - it's a fantastic resource, the commitment is low, and it's a great way to get your game out there. I am particularly interested in seeing new games and new faces this year. It is, in my experience anyway, a uniformly great experience. Some of the booth's most energetic boosters have moved on to create shops of their own across the aisle, but the Forge booth remains a weird icon and Gen Con destination for furious demos and interesting games like yours.

Anyway, there's a thread over at The Forge with more details, but we can talk about it here as well. People who have participated in the Forge booth - is it worthwhile? Is it scary? Is it fun? Is it expensive? Tell us of your experiences as a first-time designer/publisher.

    \n My initial experience with the Forge booth was as an outside observer, and from that perspective, it can seem a bit daunting. However, working with such an excited bunch of folks (the ropers and demo runners, the great IPR crew) and watching your games leave the shelf in someone's hands is really rewarding, and a great way to focus your time at the Con.

    One thing that some new folks may not know is that the Forge booth, as a general rule, really focuses on getting attention and sales for new games. So if you sign on, you can expect Ron and all the other old-timers to spend the first few days learning your games, running your demos, and generally making your booth experience worthwhile. On Saturday and Sunday, folks will likely focus on their own games more, but by then you should be well into the swing of things.

    \n It's two groups in one booth with common goals. Technically the "Forge booth" is a jointly-sponsored booth, paid for by Adept Press (Ron), IPR, and a few other companies. IPR handles all of the sales details, letting the publishers focus on talking to folks and demoing games. Last year the space was physically split into a retail area (display racks and a register table) and demo space, which worked well.

    \n Being part of the Forge Booth was one of the best experiences of my life. Or certainly up there, anyway. The camaraderie of helping other folks run and sell their games is amazing. I learned a tremendous amount about my own games as well, and what goes into getting other people excited about them. I made great friendships through the booth, and continue to benefit from the help and attention that my games got from being a part of it.

    I can't recommend it highly enough. This is the way I would want to launch my games.

    \n The Forge Booth at Gen Con is fun and it illustrates how you can get together with a few friends and get yourself a cost effective booth at Gen Con, make it work and be fun. Its boot camp, its indie RPG pollen, its a place where I made friends, its a place where I found cool games.

    The foam tile floor, the round tables, the always-growing shelves, the pre-opening speeches...its a neat thing.

    My favorite moment of the Forge booth ever is probably Ron, after talking to Kevin about his game and non-verbal communication, gave his close of the day speech in cavement grunts and gestures. And everyone got it and started to grunt back, banging tables and cavemanning it up.

    Good times.

    Also...money.
