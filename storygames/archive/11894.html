    \n Post a list here. If it's good and cheap and you can bring it to Ropecon, I'll take it off your hands Or somebody else will, my point is that you might consider selling the stuff as a block to whoever wants to pay for it - you'll get less money than by dribbling it in pieces, but you'll also get rid of the stuff quicker and easier. A piecemeal sale is likely to end up with the experts skimming all the good stuff and leaving you sitting on a box full of d20 sourcebooks or whatever Sturgeon bulk you have in there. I don't know about your living situation, but my own experience is that I'd be happier if I just carried all that extra stuff to an used book store and gave it away for whatever pennies they'd be willing to pay; I'll make more money working the day job than worrying overmuch about the logistics of selling my used junk.

    If you want to make money on it, you might also consider splitting the collection into thematic packages, perhaps filling it a bit, and then selling it in considered pieces. This'll have the benefit of increasing the value in comparison to single items. For example, if you're going to sell a random Pendragon sourcebook (to pick a game I'm idly interested in), I'll pay you a couple of euros and postage for it - 20% of cover price or so, likely, because I'm not that invested. But if you can give me the Complete Pendragon Collection with every single book for it (or some reasonably logical subset), then I'm going to pay you double or triple that, or even more than the cover price if the collection you're selling would be arduous to complete by clicking around at Amazon. This strategy targets well-off people who have stopped collecting stuff themselves long since and are today more likely to buy a finished collection to peruse when they get an itch for some topic. Fellow collectors will complain and refuse to pay you for the entire collection just to get the one book they're lacking, but that's back in the dribbling territory: let them take the stuff that interests them and you'll again be sitting on the stuff nobody by definition wants.

    \n Depends on where you live. In my area (Portland, OR), there's a local bookstore chain called Powells. I ended up weeding out nearly 300 books from my game collection when I bought a house, and sold half of those to Powells. Got about $500 for my efforts. It isn't much, but since a lot of them were old, out of date, or obscure, it was actually a pretty good haul. The rest of them I ended up giving to friends who would enjoy them more (like my entire collection of World of Darkness and RIFTS). Still, sometimes worth checking with independent book stores. Sometimes even local game stores will sell your items on commission.
