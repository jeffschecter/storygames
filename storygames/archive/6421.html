    Posted By: Graham W3. Leisure Games, London
    \nHeh, I kind of did that over HERE a few years ago.

    For me, I like product reviews, but I'd love to see them done in a new way:

    1) A review that is written as if from a member of the fantasy/other world that the game is about: "The author I believe portrays the complexities and the history of Seattle in a fair enough light. However, Shadowrun's focus on the more violent aspects of our everyday life is a little offputting. It's not like all of us know what company Ares is, or that everyone carries a gun. OK, well maybe I do on both counts, but I'm a gradeschool teacher in the Dwarven barrio. Having said that, though, seeing my husband appear in the NPC notes as a major player was very entertaining. In truth, he's actually not all that menacing..." etc

    2) A review in the form of an actual tabletop play session. Challenging, yes.

    3) A review in the form of a panel, like how Ebert and Roper review movies, but with maybe 3 or more people throwing their thoughts on the product past each other, and having a dialogue. I'd like to see this done visually, but might be costly.

    4) More reviews of gaming accessories.

    5) Reviews of good locales to throw game sessions publibly ("The Starbucks off of I-55"; "The It's A Grind off of High House Road"; "The Pan Pan Diner in Durham"; "The Orange County Library"; etc)

    6) Reviews of snack items that gamers might not otherwise notice (in other words, not the Snickers, Mtn Dew and Hostess Cakes, but other sorts of snacks as well that you might have to dig around a little to find).

    -Andy

    \n Game design contests. Which ones matter? Which ones are lame?

    Established play groups. Review for entertainment value, psychodrama, mutual support, creativity.

    Theory blogs. But not the theory! General content quality, layout, ease of navigation, frequency of updates. The must-readability of the site.

    Game fiction. JUST the fiction. Bonus points for not slashing your wrists after the first three.

    Posted By: Quintin StoneReview posts in this thread.
    \nIn reference to the self-referentiality of forum posts on Story Games - and, indeed, on a large fraction of all forums and newsgroups - Stone suggests that readers of the thread review posts in the very same thread. This opens up many possibilities for those interested in meta-debate: Reviewing Stone's post is an obvious first step, but what about reviewing the review itself? Not to mention reviewing answers to the review post, which might easily appear. On more bellicose forums, this might easily degenerate into flamewars; however, in the zen-like context of Story Games, the creative one-upmanship could well create inter-thread spin-offs well worth the read. Only time will show. Meanwhile, Stone's very concise post can be seen as the quintessence of forum meta humor: An intellectual exercise for those who like that sort of thing, but ultimately probably a derailment of the thread - in spirit, at least. Within the context of Walmsley's first post, it's clear that what is aimed for in the thread is a more eclectic assortment of phenomena, and - notably - phenomena mostly situated outside the Story Games environment. (While Richeson is a part of that environment, one can assume that his mention in Walmsley's post was mainly based on his presence as a reviewer on RPG.net).

    It's hard to know whether to recommend Stone's post. Since he's started only two threads so far at Story Games, it's hard to put his post in the proper context. How, exactly, was it meant? At what level of meta-humor is he operating? However, since such a short post in itself can clearly incite the reader to serious reflection on a variety of subjects, and the post itself is deceptively easy to read, we recommend it to all but the most jaded of internet forum users.

    \n Subcapitalist textual theory implies that the establishment is capable of social comment. If one examines dialectic theory, one is faced with a choice: either accept the postsemiotic paradigm of expression or conclude that the goal of the artist is significant, but only if the premise of dialectic theory is invalid. In a sense, Lyotard suggests the use of the postsemiotic paradigm of expression to analyse and modify class. If structural desublimation holds, we have to choose between subcapitalist textual theory and the subtextual paradigm of expression. But the subject is interpolated into a postsemiotic paradigm of expression that includes narrativity as a totality.

    For those reasons, the previous review cannot to be recommended to anyone, ever.
