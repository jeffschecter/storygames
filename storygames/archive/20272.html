    \n I was one of the original "founders" (supporting members) of rpg.stackexchange.com. I have the t-shirt to prove it.

    People ask questions. Other people supply answers.

    People upvote or downvote questions and answers. Votes are a weird sort of social currency, and there are scoreboards for members who have lots of strongly-upvoted questions and answers and such.

    The person who asked the question can designate any single answer--even if it's not the highest voted answer--as the Accepted Answer. It gets marked as such.

    A person can place a "bounty" on any question (own or someone else's). Like "I put 50 points on the question: 'What is the OSR?'" After a certain amount of time, whoever gets the Accepted Answer will receive the bounty. Yay, points!

    There's a fair amount of indie game stuff on there.

    I was very, very active on RPG StackExchange when it first came out and was consistently in the top five or so members, scorewise. The politics about how to run it (and what were valid types of questions and answers) annoyed me. I had a few frustrating run-ins with other members. I stopped participating. I'm sure they moved on just fine without me.

    \n The site has a very specific culture. Unless one first lurks attentively or reads the FAQ / help center (and even if one does that), one is likely to make a few social mistakes to start with.


    The site is good for technical questions. This includes abstract technical questions, like: As a GM, how do I choose what options to select in the ritual move in Dungeon world?

    The site can accommodate extremely specific game recommendation questions, but they are treated with suspicion, so treat with care.

    One can ask about social problems. The good answer will always be "talk to the people involved", with small possibility of useful personal experiences.

    One can ask about history of the hobby.

    One can ask answerable questions about existing bodies of rpg theory, but my intuition is that it would be socially risky.

    "How to accomplish this or that as a GM/facilitator" is likely to work, as long as "this or that" is sufficiently narrow or well-defined. Treat with care.


    Main rules are: Ask a question that can be answered from authoritative sources or personal experience; answer questions based on those and with references when available. Stay strictly on topic. Don't start or continue discussions.


    Use RSS feeds to follow interesting tags or at least ignore non-interesting tags to stay sane. It is okay to answer old questions, so browsing interesting tags might be helpful.

    Entering the site means entering a new community. One should be humble and learn the social norms if one wants to find a place there. Follow the community carefully, participate on meta, and ask when you don't understand how things work. Complaining and insulting are not very fruitful.
