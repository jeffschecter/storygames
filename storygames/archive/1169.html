    Mike Mearls, in his interview on (I think) Treasure Tables, mentions that he runs D&D once a week at lunch at work. Each game lasts an hour. There is little attention paid towards plot. He just presents the players with a tactical combat situation and they fight.

    Simplify your dungeons way down. Don't bother with "town." Don't make the players map or choose which way they go more than once an hour. This stuff is not interesting. Railroad the shit out of it until you get to a tactical encounter. Let them fight and have fun. If folks level up, they should do that as prep between games.

    I experimented with fast-play D&D at this year's AdamCon. We played through Return to Keep on the Borderlands, or whatever the remake is called. I converted it to 3.5 on-the-fly. I gave triple normal XP for encounters. I worked hard to get characters into action quickly. Characters were 3rd level by the end of a 5-hour play session and the players had a blast.

    A hack I came up with: have the pregen characters come with some character descriptions in the form of 3 one-word traits, 2 instincts and 1 belief. This isn't like setting up for thematic kickers or anything, just fuel for color and a guideline about how to play your character. I feel like that breakdown of characteristics is pretty bite-sized and easily processed. (If you're a chaotic-neutral dwarf with an axe, you may still choose different tactics depending on whether your trait is "Patient" or "Bloodthirsty".)
