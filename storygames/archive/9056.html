\n How necessary to the enjoyment of long-term multi-session recurring play is the idea of Character Advancement? meaning, the idea that over time, you mechanically increase your character's viability within the game system.

What can be done to remove it, yet keep the mechanical interest of the players? I've mentioned this idea to some local gamers, most of which have outright scoffed at the notion. It seems that "leveling up" (so to speak) has become a crucial part of the long-term play process. But what can be done instead? What methods are available to suitably substitute for "advancement" in a game campaign?

I ask, because I've been toying with some new proto-ideas for a game - a game which I want to be able to facilitate enjoyable multi-session and even long-term play. But the more I think about it, the traditional concept of mechanical character advancement just doesn't fit well within my vision of this game. However, I fear that without it, I might lose the interest of some of the players.

What do you suggest, SG? What other rewards, tangible or otherwise, can be offered?

    \n Short answer: yes. It's a common approach to LARP play, actually, so that new players aren't totally gimpy compared to experienced players, which creates a two-speed game (low-level content and high-level content must be provided, every game).

    Longer answer: you might be confusing "advancement" with "reward." I don't think you can successfully remove reward for play from long-term play. That reward, however, needn't be a mechanical change in character efficacy (and the commensurate inflation of opposition efficacy--the arms race). For instance, a reward could be allowing the player more authority over the game world or play situations. Again, this approach is common in LARP--players become "lords" or "barons" or otherwise enjoy benefits of rank that don't provide efficacy but might provide a better cabin at the campsite, or a say in out-of-game plot planning. Another way to constrain advancement is to provide it as one-shot gains rather than persistent ability, or provide item rewards which (typically) can't all be used at once--flexibility, not additional efficacy. Another way might be to provide "experience," but it only applies to a subsequent character (an "heir").

    Basically... read the Experience section in GLASS, for a variety of ways to constrain advancement or out-right eliminate it.

    \n I like change, too, and agree that for any long-term game, you NEED change of some sort to be a part of it. Otherwise, why bother making it long-term in the first place?

    But I also like advancement, for several reasons:

    - There are plenty of games where character creation limits how competent a PC can be at the outset, and that frequently makes long-term goals seem ridiculous without advancement. I can make a character who wants to be king of the world, but if he starts off barely qualified to run a Dairy Queen and cannot improve his skills significantly, then he comes off as delusional and sad rather than ambitious.

    - In new or unfamiliar systems, advancement gives a handy way to ease into things. Learning a new trick every other session gives you time to familiarize yourself with them and really understand how they work in a way that isn't true when you sit down in the first session and have forty tricks at your disposal right away.

    - Advancement is one of the things that I can play with when we're not playing the game. We get together on Saturday, we have a good time, it's awesome; then on Tuesday over lunch I can idly skim the game book on my own and think "What would be a good choice for spending those XPs?" The fact is, advancement is fun. It's fun to do, it's fun to think about.

    - Advancement lets me fix my mistakes organically and make a story out of that. We start playing a game, and up until the first session I think "This character's going to be awesome to play." Then I find out that no, he isn't. He doesn't quite fit the game, or the thing that I thought we were going to do a lot of turns out to practically never happen, or the thing I thought he would never have to do is the thing he ends up doing all the damn time. And I could turn around and say, "Okay, screw this character, I'll just make a new one," but what if we went to some effort at the start of the game tying all the PCs together? Now it's not only my own work that's been wasted, it's the work everyone did, and that's not cool. But if there's a chance that I can have fun playing the guy who starts off in over his head but eventually learns the ropes, I can do that without having to make a big deal out of it. Just funnel all those advancement points into learning to be a fighter rather than a lover, or whatever.

    - Advancement lets the game itself change, for the same reasons. We could bop around solving crimes for the FBI or what have you, and then transition into running our own detective agency following up on paranormal experiences, and then turn to charitable work to improve our hometown, and all the time keep the same characters without having to do periodic major rewrites ("okay, so she was a fingerprinting expert, but in this scenario she'll need to know how to exorcise demons, and in the next one she'll need to be able to hang drywall..."). Instead we can all fit ourselves into new niches as we go along -- learn on-the-job, as it were.


    ...which I guess puts me in the skeptical "I don't know what other rewards you can offer to make up for no advancement" camp. You can do one-step-forward-one-step-back advancement, where people can get better at some things at the cost of getting worse at others, but that's obviously not a perfect solution for anything. You can offer more ethereal types of advancement (more friends, more history, etc.), but that doesn't hit all the right notes, either.

    To be honest, I'm more familiar with no advancement in short-term games. There, it's not a big deal; you can have everyone start out with mad skills and unholy power if you want to do a "let's take over the world" scenario. You can stick with systems that everyone knows well so that no one has to be eased into anything. You can make sure that the characters fit in with what the game is about and not have to worry about how things might change. But that's not where the appeal of a long-term game is, I think, and I don't know what can be used to fill the gap there.

    It's an interesting question. If it IS taboo to take advancement out of long-term games, maybe it's for a good reason?

    \n It's worth noting that, in many long-term games, characters actually get weaker over time. Call of Cthulhu comes to mind, with the ever-diminishing SAN score. Also, some games that support a kind of "tragic warrior eventually brought down by the weight of the world" motif -- Burning Wheel, Riddle of Steel, and Warhammer Fantasy come to mind -- have the character taking on more and more penalties as the game progresses, such that they are eventually not able to heal them all. It's like getting to the high levels of Tetris, basically, where blocks start coming faster than you can get rid of them. As such, even if your abilities increase, you still have this weakening process occurring at the same time, which may eventually kill you.

    Also, freeform games have been going on forever without any mechanical changes or advancement. My sense of those is that the players become invested directly in the fiction and characters, skipping any investment in the mechanics or becoming mechanically superior, and so are chomping at the bit to continue finding out what happens -- as if they were watching their favorite TV series -- without any mechanical reinforcement. But if you have a fiction and characters that really aren't that gripping, where the players are mostly invested in their own PC and becoming more badass, then that might not work.

    \n The important aspects of advancement are a) a sense of achievement (we went from point A to point , and b) a change in the landscape of play. I find I need to feel that accomplishments are measurable somehow. Achievement in the fiction is better for me than mechanical advantages, but if you can tie them together, that's even better. Second, for a long-term game to be successful, it really really helps when new stuff comes into play. This is as easily achieved by gaining new powers as it is by accumulating wounds, penalties, scars, and disadvantages--either way, the challenges you face and the options available to you are changing over time.

    Reward can also be had through the revelation of information ("I learned what X meant, and now that's a piece of information I can use to achieve goal Y") or the discovery of new things to explore and play with (for instance, moving on to explore a new world or country, discovering a new species, facing a new enemy).

    I'd say you need some form of reward and change in a long term game. But it certainly needn't be limited to mechanical bonuses and advantages.

    What does advancement mean to you?

    \n

    In D&D (at least the varieties I've played), it means increasing the number of tactical options while the number of tactical choices you have to make also increase.

    \n

    In GURPS (at least through 3rd ed.), it means pretty much that characters converge on similar stats as they find what's most useful, rubbing out their distinctions.

    \n

    In Prime Time Adventures, it means that you've resolved an Issue and can confront a different one, perhaps rewriting some stuff about the character concept to reflect the character in play.

    \n

    In none of them, ever, does it mean becoming more powerful mechanically, though they might involve that fictionally. Otherwise, the choices, tactical or moral, would be rendered moot. That's why, as you level in D&D, the monsters also get more powerful.

    \n

    Greg, there's no problem with discussing your own game. I don't know why people started apologizing for it all of a sudden.

    Posted By: NPCI ask, because I've been toying with some new proto-ideas for a game - a game which I want to be able to facilitate enjoyable multi-session and even long-term play. But the more I think about it, the traditional concept of mechanical character advancement just doesn't fit well within my vision of this game. However, I fear that without it, I might lose the interest of some of the players.
    If you design and take everything that seems right to you and toss it in favor of what unnamed "players" want, you are going to produce a crappy game. So there's that.

    If you design a game that fails to support the creative agenda of the specific players you are designing the game for, they will not enjoy it. In other words, if they roleplay to enjoy one-upping each other in power fantasies, then any game that is not about one-upping each other in a power fantasy is going to fall flat.

    But before all of that, I think the best route for you is to articulate what sort of game experience you hope to design. Don't worry about techniques (which is different from playing with ideas \u2014 just don't worry about it) until you know what your game is supposed to do. Once you know what your game is supposed to do, you can sell players on that, rather than the individual rules that the game is made of.

    \n "Illusory" advancement works well, though, when the setting can provide performance benchmarks for the illusion. At that point, mechanical advancement and world advancement go from shyly looking at each other in the hallways to madly groping each other under the bleachers.

    I mean, yeah, a party of adventurers in D&D is always going up against level-appropriate challenges, so getting new spells or a bump in attack bonus or a cooler sword just means they're keeping pace with the new beasties they're fighting. All gains in power and effectiveness are balanced out by how much more powerful and effective their opponents are. But in the context of the setting, the characters know that the stuff that made them pee their pants back when they were callow level 1 novices with nothing but cheap weapons, fifty feet of rope, and a 10' pole -- like, say, a gang of trolls -- are now something that they could slap down easily if they ever had to.

    Not that they will, since their days will be filled with dragons or liches or Demogorgon or what have you, but that's sort of the point: mechanically, the advantages of leveling up mean less in a traditional D&D game than the perception of advancement with respect to the setting does. Being bad enough dudes to treat trolls as a minor annoyance even when you don't actually meet any more trolls is cool. It can change who your characters are with respect to the game world, the same way that getting a title or a castle or a wicked-looking scar would. It's not an either/or deal, it's a package: you get the new mechanical toys and expanded challenges AND the new position/reputation within the gameworld all at once.

    \n Change is important for any roleplaying game. It's obviously important narratively, and it's important for the game aspect as well - players will have mments when they think "Hmm, my character isn't very good at what we're trying to do now" and will want to make decisions that alter that fact in the future. "Levelling up" is the most straightforward way to do this, and it's not purely illusory - if there are tasks in the rules that have set difficulty benchmarks (climbing a wall, swimming in armour, making some fantastic new armour to replce the stuff you had to shed in the middle of a lake) then players will notice the difference.
    \n
    \nThere's also an issue of verisimulitude - in the real world people do learn how to do new things or get better at their existing skills. It's weird if this is impossible in a game.
    \n
    \nHowever, I don't think that it's necessary for characters to get ever more powerful with no corresponding drawbacks. After all, in the real world there is some value to being young and energetic - octogenarians aren't the most capable people on the planet.
    \n
    \nYou could design a game in which players can "cash in" attribute points for XP that can be spent on skills. The exchange rate on this could be rather favourable (cashing in one point of an attribute allows you enough XP to buy several points of skills), and indeed, there could be attributes that exist purely for the sake of being cashed in. If characters have attributes like Naivety as well as purely positive attributes like Strength and Memory, then the players will start by reducing their Naivety to pay for skills that they want. Eventually, however, all of the negative attributes will be gone, and then the player will have to start sacrificing Memory or Health - showing the inevitable decline of middle and old age. You could even cash in unusuaed skills to show how some abilities are simply lost if unusued. Combined with a reasonable limit on how much you can cash in at once, and how fast you can acquire new skill points, I think it would be pretty cool.
    \n
    \nI think it would be a neat way to handle experience and learning in a game that uses a lifepath for character generation, or a "random but equal" chargen like REIGN's one-roll character creation. It's not going to work so well in point-buy, unless attributes like Naivety are cheaper than other attributes (and thus represent a viable longterm investment).

    \n There's a definite difference that a lot of commenters have pointed to between "advancement" and reward.

    You can play a long-term game with no mechanical advancement so long as everyone is still enjoying it. You could even play an existing system and just strip the experience points or equivalent out of it, so long as you and your players still found a reason to come back to the table every week.

    Let's say, hypothetically, I'm running some kind of game that's similar to Ars Magica, but without the experience system. There's still a lot of things the players can do that will keep them coming back, even without any mechanical change on their character sheet. They can research new spells, write grimoires, teach apprentices, have magical duels with neighbouring Covenants, make magical artifacts, negotiate trade with the Fair Folk, slay dragons to collect Tass, resist bullying from the local lord... and as long as the players see the accomplishment of the -goal- as something that's as important as "buying the next level of Rego magic", then they don't really need an advancement system.

    Experiment: At the start of the first session, hand out an index card to each player. Play the game as normal, and try and suss out what each character is doing and why. At the end of the session, have the player write one concrete goal, short- or long-term for their character on the card, and hand the card back to you.

    Run your next session in such a way that all the players have a chance to forward their character's goals. When a player feels that the goal is complete, then give them a big red pen and let them write COMPLETE on the goal card. Then give them two more blank ones.
