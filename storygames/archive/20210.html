\n Hi folks

for my current development on Directions Storyplaying System, I had put together these
Storyplaying Guiding Principles:
https://drive.google.com/file/d/0B0kstQO9Gc1dMUVtMDRRd182UGM/edit

Essentially, they are:
- Enjoy telling stories together
- Be generous
- Flow over form
- Take full responsibility for the game
- Invite the unexpected

So, nothing much surprising and along the line of a lot of indie/story games, I guess.
Just wanted to share it and welcome any feedback you have.

thanks
BeePeeGee

Be boring; say obvious things; something will grow out of that when people are starting to give each others' ideas a spin.

    Be transparent and share your intentions openly.
    \nOh, yeah! This is spot on! It's also connected to what you write later, namely ...\n
    ... and being open where the story may evolve in the future.
    \nThis also goes with what you said about don't plan ahead. I usually have a strive of where I want things to go, but I never plan specific moments (well, I do ... and I can always make it work, but ignore that). Do I want a sad story? A heroic one?     The thing I always have the most trouble with is inviting the unexpected. Obviously, the players in an RPG depend on the GM to provide this for them and the GM gets some unexpected things back, too.     For example, how Scene 4, Step 4 is Beth inspired to have Heather proclaim her love for Gerry?

    Are there are more specific principles that you use in actual play when to introduce something new? That would be useful for me, and perhaps others.

    \n Hi BeePeeGee,

    I largely agree with Rickard, with the caveat that brevity is good so you should probably limit such high-level orientation text to just the most important stuff.

    Much of what you've written is familiar to me, but the one part that stood out is "flow over form". That's a unique way to look at that dynamic, somewhere between freeform and Rule Zero. If there's any part that deserves highlighting or elaboration, this might be it. (Or, I might just be biased because I dig flow over form.)

    On that topic, I'd just like to throw out one way I look at it; compare and contrast as you see fit. The rules are there to help get you into the state of flow/immersion/total engagement with the fiction. Once you're in, then you don't need the game's formal structures (rules); use them only insofar as it seems natural and doesn't distract. At some point, the flow will wane -- at that point, go back to the rules again to fuel your next journey.

    \n Thanks for all the feedback, guys.

    Rickard: thanks for all the details. I will include your feedback in revisions.
    For what I have in mind, the guiding principles need only answer the "how" to interact and play with each other. Thus, some things you address are covered in the rules of the game as well as setting development. The rest will be covered later.

    ArtPatient: thanks for returning to my game play example. I have completely changed/refined quite a bit of the mechanics since then. Now, it has a better flow, focus on roleplaying (less "play before play").
    "Inviting the unexpected" is covered with specific mechanics using a designated "surprise meter" as well as game-specific random tables. (I'll post a draft some later time).

    David/Asif: Spot on. That's what I had in mind with "flow over form"! The Directions Storyplaying System use the rules as flexible building blocks or training wheels. You are free to modify/drop them as seem fit for your game/story.
    This is a much different attitude than i.e. Luke Crane's designs that seem to go "flow follows form", meaning: "stick exactly to the rules and you'll have a terrific Burning Wheel/Mouse Guard experience".

    And yes, brevity helpful, especially in the introductary sections. So, it's interesting what is redundant or can be left out.
