\n I want what other people don't (sometimes).

But if I want something you don't, my preference is to compromise to make the game fun for both of us.

If I suspect what I want is controversial, I'll ask permission first. But "yes" doesn't always mean "yes".

It may not 100% be my responsibility to know if yes is yes, ideally people will be truthful, but I want to ask permission in a way that gets me that truth. My goal isn't "get what I want". It's "would this be fun for you?"

How do you ask permission without pressure? Without persuasion?

Say I ask, "would it be cool if our characters are fighting to win Jack's affection?" Plenty of people who aren't interested will simply say no, or offer an alternative. But others find it difficult to say no. They may not want to come off negative. Or maybe they feel social pressure. Whatever the reason, they may agree without wanting to.

How do I ask, "would it be cool if our characters are fighting to win Jack's affection" without pressure? Or in a way that helps you feel comfortable to be truthful? Especially at conventions with strangers.

Of course there is the further complications of saying "yes" but changing your mind later. Or simply not understanding what you were saying "yes" to before the game started. But we can tackle those separately.

Rock,
John

    \n Looking at it in print I think I'd respond better to "would it be cool if our characters are fighting to win Jack's affection?" than to "would this be fun for you?" You didn't really suggest that as an option, but I wanted to point out that I like the idea that what's being queried is not me or my feelings but my aesthetic sense - as one player to another, do I think that this turn of events would fit the ideal way I imagine the game taking shape?

    Now of course in practice if what's really at stake is my emotions, that's going to color my response. But in a way it lets me save face if I can present it as an aesthetic decision: "no I don't think that makes sense" is easier to say than "no I will be enraged if anyone threatens me as a rival for Jack" or "no it squicks me out that you even think that about my character" or whatever.

    I've dealt with a number of people (men) in a roleplaying context where they act like even admitting they have feelings is seen as a sign of weakness, so they come up with false-logical pretexts to act on their emotions; "is it cool if?" presents the impersonal response as an option because "you" isn't even in the sentence.

    Hope we get the chance to play together at Recess and test this theory!

    \n I am 100% with Tavis. I think "Would it be cool if?" are the perfect words; beyond that, it's all about body language, facial expression, inflection, etc.

    If you really, really want to be sure not to be pushy, you can always raise pros and cons yourself, just to illustrate that either answer is acceptable. I sometimes do this when playing with shy strangers. It tends to go like:

    "Would it be cool if our characters are fighting to win Jack's affection? I mean, that might be a little too teenage melodrama... But, maybe we can do it right, and set up some tense intrigue! Or maybe that's hard, and it'll more likely come out silly. Should we give it a shot?"

    Players who don't have any better ideas will often say, "Okay, sure," just for the sake of moving forward. But hopefully it'll be a little warmer, fuzzier, "Okay, sure," than if they were simply ordered around. For a particularly unenthusiastic "Okay, sure," I might continue:

    "Well, maybe we can do something better. Any ideas? No? Well, maybe our characters are fighting to manipulate Jack, rather than seduce him? Is that cooler? Which one is better?"

    \n I've recently done some interviews to collect data for my bachelor paper. I hadn't done much of the sort before so I tried to read up on it a bit. Something that's often stressed is the value of avoiding questions with yes or no answers if you actually want to know what people think.

    I know it could feel like taking it a bit far (especially since the wording might make it sound like more of a big deal than it is), but asking "How would you feel about our characters are fighting to win Jack's affection?" makes it impossible to give the false, easy yes.

    Of course, it might lead to a false "Sure, that'd be cool", but maybe there'd also be enough hesitation before hand to signal that it's actually not that cool.

    Asking questions is tricky business.

    I know I'm not the greatest communicator, but I think I can safely assume John is generally tactful and respectful, both when asking questions and answering them. So perhaps that isn't enough. I do agree though, it is an essential component - a foundation on which to build.

    I think one problem is, many people think being "tactful and respectful" is "avoid confrontation and don't hurt their feelings", as John suggested. So they will compromise their own fun, because they worry that if they don't, they'll spoil other people's fun. There's a lot of second-guessing that can go on.

    The same situation crops up (probably a lot worse) when you're the GM and ask the same kind of questions as players. The extra authority granted by the GMing position in many games exarcebates things. So, whatever techniques you use as GMs to talk to players in ways that encourage them to be willing to open up might well work when you are a player too.
