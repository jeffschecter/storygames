\n This was mentioned in a couple of other threads and I figured it needed a thread of its own.

Maybe there is no universal answer, but what do folks think brings people to their Con games.

Obviously there is the advertising/blurb in the program, but what makes a good blurb?

Sometimes it seems that adding a promising setting/property will help a game fill. Serenity/Firefly, BSG...
At Dexcon I had two almost full tables from running a game set in Terry Pratchett's Discworld. Although in the genre/property celebration game, most players don't care about the system.
Unfortunately, the property thing only works sometime. I almost never get a full table for adventures set in the Brisco County Jr. world. Andrew's LARP set around the series "The Lost Room" was canceled due to low turn out (and my wife and I were two of the signed up players), but thats an amazing property for a LARP.

At the previous Dexcon or was it Dreamation, someone did a trifold/stapled handout with all the indie games and a brief description. I thought that was a good idea, but I'm not sure if it helped bring in players.

At Dexcon, WEGS was a tremendous amount of hack-n-slash fun, but for all the advertising Larry did he still ended up canceling about 1/2 his slots due to lack of players.

So, how do you get more players to come to the Awesome?

    \n I haven't surveyed players about why they play in one of my games. But I have some anecdotal examples. For Ganakagok, a player and her partner joined because she was drawn to the player character sheet (with the eskimo face) and then hooked by its description as 'a story game'.
    For a D&D game I ran,"Prisoner of Mankara", a player was drawn by the blurb description "Alexandre Dumas meets Neal Stephenson".
    And on at least three occasions, I've roped in people whose game folded so they were looking for something to do.
    I like the idea of having some kind of survey for the players--not just what they liked and disliked about the game but why they played in the first place.

    \n 1) Your reputation as a GM
    2) A cool game system that's all the buzz that people want to try
    3) Your blurb (and other possible ads you may have)
    4) Location! The location of your game room, poster, ad, etc. If you're off to the side vs. you're along a high traffic lane.
    5) Harnessing the grapevine, ie. talk/convince someone into coming to your table and he might bring a friend or two.

    All that said, I almost never play in con games (probably making me part of an odd minority). We only have one con (which is excellent, though) in Finland and I spend my time listening to the various panel discussions, guests of honor, wandering around or just talking to random people. So, I'm not talking from actual experience of running these things, but rather out of an understanding of marketing.

    Joachim Buchert

    \n I put up ads once around a local con (Strategicon). I grabbed some art off the internet, designed a flyer. Taped it to walls and pillars around the hotel. This was several years ago, long before the Indie Explosion scene. I got a table full of players who had never played before.

    I knew I was walking into a con with a game that wasn't particularly well known. I talked it up to people I met. And so on.

    I would say this technique embodied:
    a) an enthusiasm for the game
    b) a desire to share that enthusiasm

    Without getting into specific techniques (there will be a gazillion, many dependent on circumstance), letting those two notions guide your behavior will pay off greatly.

    I'd say that's at the core of it.

    Don't in any way wait for the thing you want to happen to happen. Brainstorm always, "What can I do to make this happen?"

    \n By the way --

    Different techniques and products will draw different kinds of people with different tastes.

    While I know many people are drawn by games from established media properties, like Firefly and Star Trek, I'm the kind of guy who'd probably going to pass. While this may not be accurate, my experience has been the game's having too much emphasis on loving the show, not enough on making up new stuff now.

    My only point is, whatever you're excited about doing, put that front and center. If you're playing Star Trek, you mention that because you want people to know, right? If you're playing a game about an on the fly collaborative story-telling experience, say that.

    You can't appeal to everyone. That's an impossible and insane goal. But you can appeal to the kinds of people who like what you like. What excites one person might turn off someone else, and that's fine.

    \n I'm not the best GM around, but my games usually fill up at conventions. My approach has been:

    - I always go for a game that is very different from the mass of other games posted, but not so outlandish that people can't figure out what it's about. Many story games are great for this.
    - When inspired, I try to use a movie or book setting that many people are interested in but that is not already covered by a licensed game. Examples have included Firefly (before the Serenity RPG was out), The League of Extraordinary Gentlemen (before the crappy movie), etc.
    - Most important, I always have a short catchy game blurb that (hopefully) makes people excited and curious.
    - When I'm feeling enterprising, I may link the blurb to a pre-con Website giving more info.

    I try to use simple systems that allow minimal explanation at the table. I normally have pre-gen characters ready unless the game also includes on-the-spot character creation (e.g., Wilderness of Mirrors.)

    \n First a question - Does your game have to limited to the role playing hall?
    \n
    \nI ask because it is nice sometimes to take a game into different crowds. One way to do that is to do up a version as a board game, another as a miniatures game and a third as a role play game. I've billed Engle Matrix Games as all of these at one time or another.
    \n
    \nWhen I started out doing Con games I went to miniatures conventions - so I called them miniatures games. When I first went to Gen Con (1991) that's how I billed them. The thing with miniatures games are that people will walk by and if they like your lay ot they will join. So to get this walk by traffic I made nicer stuff. When you do this though you have to pay attention to people walking past, make eye contact and invite them to ask questions. Some will join - those who don't have still heard the pitch.
    \n
    \nOf course having a good blurb is useful. At big cons people look at the titles and game system. Players who only came to play D+D are a lost cause - they won't be swayed by anything. The more adventurous types will scan titles. You need to catch their eyes. Zombies in Disneyland does that (though don't say that too loudly - the mouse police are very techy) Kill puppies for Satan does to. Movie tie ins and genre pieces like "Cthulhu" anything pull others. Historical pieces pull some. There really is no way to know what will hook but enthusiasm does matter. A brief dramatic description is better than something boring.
    \n
    \nThe one thing I had to learn by repeated failure was that the esoteric subjects I like are NOT popular. Nothing about 1848 revolutionaires attracts any attentions. Darn the unfairness of it all!

    \n Well... what gets people to my table is me asking them. That is, I don't have a "table" per se, we just grab whichever available table is nearest and seems most comfortable.
    \n
    \nAt Origins after 6PM that means one of the tables in the breezeway, for instance. At GenCon after 6PM it means a table in the lobby of the Embassy Suites.
    \n
    \nOpen gaming areas. Tables that just aren't being used. Gaming on Demand tables.
    \n
    \nSo, you're talking to folks, and you say, "Wanna do a playtest of Other World?" and folks reply, "Sure!"
    \n
    \nOr, if they're busy, I talk to sombody else who is not. Until we find a few players and play. It's pretty much 100% successful, if you don't account for the time you have to spend socializing. Which is fun anyhow. And, this way, I end up playing with folks I like, rather than a random selection of folks who think that HeroQuest is a Milton Bradly board game (which it is).

    \n I'm a strong believer in good blurbs. I'm a sucker for catchy descriptions!

    But so much is in the eye of the beholder. In the other thread, jim pinto gave examples of blurbs he's used, and he seemed to be surprised and disappointed that they didn't generate more interest at the convention. On the other hand, like others said in that thread, they read like vanilla fantasy to me. The difference is that the GM has an entire setting and story in his or her mind's eye, but the rest of us only have the meagre 200 or so characters allowed by the convention program.

    At Go Play NW, we had another example; one GM (you know who you are!) had trouble getting players for a homebrew that was probably very interesting, but was not promoted well. The description included some key no-no's: fantasy, homebrew, "20 years in the making." Instead of being advantages, in a description these become giant liabilities. Everyone hearing them flashes back to some weird GM they've know years ago, still living in his parents' basement and compulsively detailing his Tolkien-clone setting. Do I think this is what was being offered? Probably not, but neither was there anything to tell us what it was about the game that should wow us.

    I'm giving these examples not to point fingers at people, but to illustrate a very common problem at conventions. While a one-sentence zinger description is no guarantee that I'll have fun for four hours at the gaming table, a dull, confusing, intimidating, or clich\xc3\xa9 one is almost certainly going to be a turn-off unless I know the GM personally.

    \n Well, I actually wasn't imagining this for 365 days a year. Instead, I saw the three days of the con being like this.

    The concept of the scheduled block of time comes from the "tournament" -- using D&D (and children's) competitive rules for a group of strangers to meet on equal ground to overcome a series of neutral challenges contained in the module.

    This functions well for a scheduled block of Squad Leader, Risk, Monopoly.

    And it can work well to introduce people to games like Dogs in the Vineyard and son on.

    But unlike "tournament" style games, I'd argue that "'story-games" -- whatever those are -- are not about overcoming the challenges of the dungeon as a team, competing against other teams using the same numbered character stats and room descriptions. (Which, by the way, makes perfects sense for a Gamist, competitive event.)

    A game of Dogs or Primetime Adventures might best served by not being crammed at the table with strangers, but with people you'd like to play with. (I think of Ron's "band analogy" here.) The talking part isn't for talking shop, per se -- it's for finding out, "Hey, I'd like to make something with you."

    The difference between this and signing up for an event blind are huge. And, in my view, very appealing.

    \n I think the lounge and jamming group model works well for small story games events (the Go Play/Camp Nerdly events, game day events, etc.) or sub-events (e.g., setting up a story games lounge at a larger con, kind of like the RPGA groups except with story instead of stats) where you can expect about 25-75 people.

    But for even small conventions (e.g., ConQuest NW and Dragonflight in Seattle are in the 400-600 range, DunDraCon in the Bay Area is at 1,500), the scheduled blocks become necessary because of the information overload factor: if you don't have a way to figure out who and what are where when, you can spend your entire weekend just looking for games. Participants have usually paid good money to be there, and they need means to get in at least some of the games they're interested in.

    \n
    \nOne of the major problems at ICON is that gamers have to stare at a table of sheets with minor blurbs about the game, and then sign up their name for it. Many game slots go unfilled, and often times if a slot gets filled late, the game master has already left because of low turnouts earlier. (This happened to me twice this year.) Players signing up will pick games based on their name alone, and whether or not anyone else has signed up for it. This lets new games go unnoticed, or games with great masters and potential get lost in the shuffle of mediocrity. I think the best way to remedy this would be a tripod with a poster. Advertisement at the table itself is essential, and a little advertising goes a long way. Another good trick for keeping gamers attention, is to have a bag of candy. Something simple, like jolly ranchers, or some sort of mint. Many gamers smoke, and will get antsy if they don't have something orally fixating them. Often times this leads to quick breaks to go get munchies mid game. Something for the gamers to stick in their mouth, generally helps them focus on the task at hand rather than getting lost. Also non smokers love candy, and sugar highs help at cons.
