    But of course, Jason, you don't have to play them that way. That's the beauty of it.
    \n
    Yes, you can elect to play queer characters regardless of what playset you choose. Jason decided not to include a rule that says "No gay characters allowed." Stoked about that.

    But in talking about queer content in games, we can look beyond just (implied) permission. We can think about things like active representation, structural support, and narrative space. Fiasco doesn't really have queer representation, aside from a few fan-created playsets. Do the mechanics or the text of the game actively encourage people to disrupt their blank-slate assumptions (ie. that important characters will be white, able-bodied, straight, cisgender, English-speaking, else)? Aside from dimensions of class and criminality, I'd say no. I get the impression that lots of you might say yes, by virtue of the fact that so many people ended up adding queer characters to their games as a "natural fit." Cool.

    Where I think Fiasco does succeed in creating space for queer storytelling (aside from custom playsets, like aviatrix's brilliant stonewall one) is in the story structure it introduces. A story structure that lends itself easily to camp, or underdog dark comedy, or bleak tragedy about the dispossessed. Fiasco doesn't centre around stories where you level up, get the girl, and live happily ever after.

    You can pick up most games and slot an incidentally-queer character into them. It's a different thing to have a game that actually features queer representation. And it's a different thing again to have a game that creates space for telling stories about the arc and uncertainty of queer lives. Fiasco does some of these things

    To me, this thread and the other recent thread combine to make me angry because I don\u2019t always, when I sit down at a game table, expect to be asked bleed out my issues. To paraphrase AsIf in that thread.

    Regarding promotion or not of queer content in rpgs, I recall, in a Monsterhearts campaign I was MCing, a PC was protecting a (same gender) NPC from a hive queen. That NPC tried to kiss him. He (the PC) rejected him, but he had to roll to see if he emotionally hurt the other, or if he was affected somehow by the event. That was cool, and the game contained mechanics and advice to make stuff like that happen.

    In other campaign (Apocalypse World), were a friend was the MC and not me, my PC, a performer, used a move, rolled fine, and declared a gang leader (same gendered) to love him. The MC, then, said "uh, I hadn't imagined the gang leader to be gay. It will be better if we turn "love" into "is very intrigued/interested on you"". The campaign was short, but this happened in the 1st session, and by session 3 nothing had happened, and that was really frustrating.

    I've been thinking a lot about why, in one case, there is queer content in the game, albeit in the other it was vetoed by the GM. In AW, seduction has nothing to do with gender, but nonetheless the GM did what he did. In MH, turning on somebody has also nothing to do with gender, but there's a paragraph explicitly advising the MC about why you characters don't get to choose what turns them on in the game.

    I don't know if MCing MH, my friend would have done the same thing (protecting certain NPCs from "turn on") or not, but I believe conceptions about gender and sexuality in people's brains are sometimes really ingrained to be changed just by reading a paragraph of advice. An homo/transphobe (not the case of my friend, luckily) will deliberately disregard advice or queer content, or twist it in an insulting way. Or he will drop the game in favor of other ones.

    What I mean is, I love to see inclusion promoted in games, it's something that should be done in more games, but in the end it often takes way more than a text to change people's ideas on gender and sexuality.

      \n Paul T,

      It's a social justice concept, I see it getting used in a weird smattering of contexts (in discussing stand-up comedy, in politics, elsewhere).

      In essence: you can choose to punch up (at oppressors, at dominant narratives, at those in power, at injustice) or you can choose to kick down (at the already marginalized, at the powerless, at the dehumanized). The former is risky and worthwhile, the latter is easy and wretched.

      Jokes that challenge rape culture are punching up. Rape jokes are kicking down.

      Jokes that call out racism are punching up (see Hannibal Buress for an example of how to do that), racist jokes are kicking down.

      Being trans-identified, I end up quitting a lot of sitcoms because they kick down at trans people. The "straight guy pursues hot chick, finds out hot chick has a penis, gets disgusted while the laugh track has a field day" routine shows up in literally dozens of sitcoms and movies. The entire joke is "ha ha, trans women exist, isn't their existence shameful ha ha." That's kicking down.

      Punching up is using humour to challenge power. Kicking down is using humour to shame the marginalized.

      Actually, wait. One thing that Fiasco does to encourage punching up is to make sure all the characters are interconnected and have relatable needs. Kicking down tends to rely upon isolating and Othering people.

      (And to be clear: I am not attacking Fiasco. I think Fiasco is a good game. I'm just looking at it critically and wondering how best to support it at the table. I also don't think it's entirely the domain of a ruleset to create healthy play environments that are non-oppressive. That is a responsibility shared by hosts, social contracts, individual players, game texts, game structures, and wider cultural contexts.)
