\n I got to do a couple rounds of S/lay w/Me with my ladyfriend Carly. I thought the system was simple and enjoyable. We ran into a couple tricky bits, but I think that was a matter of system understanding and confidence.

I GM'ed for Carly as she played Gideon, a scholar, who went to the place where the clockwork war raged on still. It was a massive clockwork city, empty of all life. Clockwork people went about their days, clicking around and picking up metal groceries and sitting on metal couches reading metal newspapers. The game ended up way more steampunky than I'd thought when Carly drew a picture of Gideon and he was wearing a suit and a bowtie. Gideon met a crazy, lonely girl in the Clockwork City while he tried to unravel its mysteries, and then a metal creature with sharp blades for hands and telescoping legs pursued them through the city. It ended up killing the girl and Gideon shortly after.

Then it was my turn. I played Lukuls who was a skull-wearing savage who journeyed to the Lunar Citadel with his tribesmen to gain the blessing of the moon goddess as they made war on the Ursu tribe, who worshipped her enemies, the the Three Bears (haha that was accidental and not hilarious in the game). As he journeyed to the top of the Lunar Citadel, he heard his men shouting. He rushed to leave the temple but the priestesses would not let him out. He forced his way out and his men were gone, so he attackd the priestesses. At that moment the moon goddess revealed herself. Lukuls decided if she would not give him her blessing, he would take it. In a lucky shot he struck the helmet from her head and ran away with it. As she approached he smeared the helmet with his blood and whispered words of prayer to his snake god, Lyriss, and the moon goddess became trapped in the helmet.

I think in the second adventure, the monster was Lukuls.

We hope to play again soon.

    \n The clockwork city was cool. It was interesting to see our shared imaginings of it bumping into one another. I was describing this crazy clockwork battle in a city where all sorts of mechanical people run along tracks, and she's like, "So, Gideon gets off the train..." and I was like, "Trains stop here? And we're playing in a world with trains?" It was cool though. She keeps shifting the game towards steampunk where I'm like rawr sword and sorcery.

    We played again last night and Mirabelle went to the city built on a cemetary. It was a city built by a clan of sorcerers who'd fled their enemies and enacted rites of protection to keep themselves safe on the old necropolis of an ancient empire. Any who wished to live there under the protection of the sorcerers could do so, but they swore an oath that when they died they would pass into the service of the sorcerers until their bodies fell apart.

    Mirabelle, a woman with a bandaged arm and a bandaged eye, was there seeking Foust, an old student of her master's. She wanted revenge on her master and believed he could help her. When she arrived she met Anala, a young woman with long white hair who volunteered to act as her guide. She showed her some of the sights of the city, including the puppet show at the One-Eyed Vulture inn.

    That night Mirabelle awoke in her room in the inn and felt cold and pain clutching at her chest. She looked out the window and saw it rimmed with frost and a shrouded creature with glowing red eyes was looking in. She whispered a protective spell and it disappeared.

    The next day, on their way to see Foust, Anala fainted, her skin blue. The creature appeared again and gestured towards Mirabelle and her veins turned black. She felt cold and fatigue overtaking her. She fled with Anala, seeking to find Foust and made her way to some of the vulture statues in the city that protect the living against the angry dead. The creature followed and the guardian vulture tried to stop it but was shattered by a wave of the creature's hand. Mirabelle undid her bandages, though, revealing a clockwork eye and an arm of brass. She held her hand out and an aperture opened and she sucked the creature into it.

    Foust had been watching the whole time, but had been unsure if Mirabelle had been sent by his old master to kill him. When she revealed she was here to study under him, he gave her a flask to drink from that ridded her and Anala of whatever affliction the creature had given them. He opened a door in a rock nearby and Mirabelle followed him into it.

    Another good story! One thing I've noticed, which sounds like it might be different than how some others play, is that we don't play hard for the dice. We often make many, many moves where no monster or goal/lover dice are in play. The scene in the inn, or Anala's initial conversation with Mirabelle about the city. It could be we're not including forward-moving actions in our Goes, but it seems like we are. It seems to be working out well.

    \nIt happens when either the Hero player relentlessly pursues his Goal or the Love/Monster player relentlessly attacks with the Monster.

    When I played it, I played it with a player who is highly imaginative and extremely engaged in the game but needs work on his internal sense of pacing. When he was the Hero he basically tried to incorporate pursuit of his Goal into every Go thus racking up dice. If I wanted the game to be at all interesting I had to respond by similarly keeping pace with the Monster.

    When we switched rolls he had the Monster attack me on almost all of his Gos and so I similarly had to respond with bringing in my Goal on almost all of my Gos.

    The fiction still worked but I felt a similar want for a more wandering pace.
