    \n I was actually pretty fond of the OP, if you accept the slightly absurd premise (summing up anything non-trivial in 12 items), it's a good list. As has been pointed out, if you've played all of those, you can drop in on pretty much any gamer group and have some 'shared' background. It's also a pretty broad spectrum of what games are 'about,' running the gamut. Overall, if I was trying to provide a guide of pen-and-paper gaming in general my list would look a lot the same. I'd probably put MLWM on. Maybe Dogs, but knowing that it means kicking off something else is a pain.

    I guess how much you hate it depends on what you expected out of it. Note that it doesn't claim to be the best games (in fact it's pretty rough on some of them), and that the target audience (from what I've seen of Gnome Stew) has probably only played maybe 3 from the list, and may have never even heard of at least one or two. If he was trying to say they were the best games I'd have issues, but these are about giving glimpses into different ways of gaming, and it's about as good a list as I could do with the same constraints.

    \n My goal would be to showcase games with historical significance. The goal would be to pick games and game types that cover the range of historical game development so that the person familiar with this canon would then be proficient to discuss rpg development and history with others. I do this sort of thing all the time, really, considering that I play with teenagers, who are by and large pretty ignorant and in need of somebody explaining our heritage to them.
    • Old D&D (not AD&D) or Tunnels & Trolls to get a solid grounding on the first forms
    • Traveller or Runequest or some other second-gen game.
    • Hero, GURPS or some such
    • AD&D (any version) with Dragonlance, at least if historical understanding is the goal
    • Planescape or Vampire or any other '90s supplement wheel
    • Ars Magica
    • Call of Cthulhu
    • Pendragon
    • Paranoia
    • Sorcerer
    • Dust Devils or Mountain Witch
    • My Life with Master or Contenders or one of those things
    • Once Upon a Time, Under the Bed, Baron Munchausen or other liminal cases
    \nEach of the games on this list can be replaced pretty much nilly-willy, as my purpose here is not to pick the original innovative games, but the ones that represent the design currents strongly and in clear, unambiguous light. As is, the list should pretty much give anybody enough range to not only correctly place a game in its own decade design-wise, but also characterize 95% of the games they're going to encounter for their design influences.

    Still, a proper canon wouldn't be a dozen games wide; I'd go for something like 20-25 to properly list all the games a person should know to some degree if they're claiming expertise in roleplaying. It's probably under 40 games but definitely over 15.

    \n I pointed out GURPS and HERO and treated editions as separate games. I think there's worth in playing through them to see the evolution, and specifically at least one of GURPS and one of HERO to see how they're similar and different.     For instance, in Fantasy Hero, there's a shitload of evolution between the very first edition (a small standalone game based on a very cut down version of the whole system) and the current (a kitchen sink book that, is almost as big as all prior published Fantasy Hero material combined and doesn't even include the system).

    Ghostbusters is a must. Especially for the Story Games crowd. If you can, play the first edition. While you're playing it, consider both its publication date (1986) and how much of it is obvious nowadays but no one had ever thought of before then.

    \n I think Jason's approach to "what to play before you die" is absolutely fantastic. I'll offer my own take as well. My guideline is, "Games you should play to open your head to the many facets of roleplaying."

    * A high-immersive strict-IC LARP, maybe even with costumes (MET or boffer)

    * A high-crunch tactics-oriented game, especially if it features point-buy character creation (HERO, GURPS, etc.)

    * A high-authorship game that requires you get out of the pilot's cockpit of "your" character (Burning games, Shock:, etc.)

    * Dungeons & Dragons, one short campaign in each of the four major editions so you can sense the change in texture from generation to generation

    * A D&D knockoff, maybe even several of them. But only once or twice each. You'll both see why D&D works as well as it does, and pick up on the most common dissatisfactions with it that have driven so much of game design.

    * An adaptation of your favorite license, to suss out your own relationship with prior art

    * Something diceless but structured (Amber, Nobilis, etc.)

    * An RPG meant to be played and finished in a single evening

    * An RPG that only works throughout the life of a long campaign, and then actually play out that campaign

    * A high-GM-discretion RPG. Play it once to beat the GM. Play it once to collaborate with the GM. Play it once as if the GM weren't even there, deferring to your fellow players before going to a final authority.

      \n So, here is my list, such as it is:
      \n
      \n1. Play a D&D 4e game set in the current timeline of the World of Warcraft (as near as you can).
      \n
      \n2. Play a long term continuous world-building campaign. I suggest Burning Wheel, but the closest i've come to is either GURPS Traveller (~15 sessions and 2 or 3 characters) or Iron Heroes D&D (for 9 or 10 sessions with 1 character, but mostly seeing the inside of massive, history revealing - rather than setting-changing - dungeons. Though we were making up the history as we went.)
      \n
      \n3. Play an anthology-type game long enough to write a sourcebook. (a la In a Wicked Age)
      \n
      \n4. Play an anthology-type game for as long as possible using a non-anthology system, prefferably D&D.
      \n
      \n5. Play a real competitive player vs player game, like Paranoia or one of those indie survival horror games, some of them get pretty close.
      \n
      \n6. Play a game in early playtest! I did this with Michael Miller's By The Stars, and it was a really interesting experience, with the game changing radically each time. But most "indie gamers" probably have a lot of experience with this! So how about, playtest an early draft you didn't write yourself! ;)
      \n
      \n7. Go ahead and actually play a game with mechanics or theme that you wouldn't normally touch! I'm terrible about this, partly because i don't go to a lot of conventions, partly because i am a-feared. (I've done a confusing MET larp though. Once.) Og, anybody? Or maybe Orx?
      \n
      \n8. Definately play one each of the big evolutionary pathways of the mainstream rpg - D&D, GURPS, HERO, Storyteller, etc. Not just *have* played it - actually go back and start up a real game using these systems after having been fully exposed and experienced with indie gaming / story-gaming and their techniques. I did this with Hunter (2nd Ed.) a couple years ago, and it was a blast. It also put Storyteller's many faults into stark contrast. I'd love to do it again with GURPS, my old gaming standby.
      \n
      \n9. Play GUMSHOE. I haven't done this, but i'm intrigued. It looks like it shouldn't work, like the system doesn't do anything to help the creation of fun mysteries - but i'd love to see it fail (or succeed!) in action.
      \n
      \n10. Play a "company-level game", to use the specific term from REIGN. I haven't done this, not for real - but it really excites me, and i've been toying around with my own system for this sort of play. I think the REIGN rules themselves are too detached from the characters, and too "strategy-gamey" than they should be. It needs to be an RPG.
      \n
      \n11. Play a game designed for one specific razor-sharp Theme. Grey Ranks, carry, Steal Away Jordan, Shock:, Breaking the Ice come to mind...
      \n
      \n12. Play out the scenarios on Judd's list, if I can get all meta here. Though most of them are probably impossible, i'd think!

      \n I like the fact that several here includes larp in their list. My list is both games I have played, and not.

      1 - Until we sink ... (a groundbreaking design by Magnus Jacobson, narrow in theme, but THE most important game the last 10 years)
      2 - 13 at the table (a larp, a family dinner, social drama, 100% in character, simplistic and functional)
      3 - Prime Time Adventures (another important design, open up new venues of play and design)
      4 - World of Warcraft (my game group of 18 year olds is adamant about it: WoW's GREAT. I doubt it, but it's still important)
      5 - Muu (a game of the classical type, poetic fable, no conflicts in the ordinary sense, important design in the Norwegian tradition)
      6 - Pendragon (the idea of aging characters and playing a "dynasty" gives a scope not often visited in classical rpg's)
      7 - 1942 (one of the most successful and serious larps held in Norway, WWII-theme, but sry: not available)
      8 - Lady og Otto (a short story for 4 persons, by Frederik Berg Olsen, very strange and enticing, Danish scenario-tradition)
      9 - 3:16 - carnage amongst the stars (a new design, simplistic rules, good build up of drama, focused theme)
      10 - V\xe4gen and Quo Vadis (christian rpgs, Swedish design by Len Howard and Johan Anglemark)
      11 - L\xe6relyst (rpg for use with classes in school, Norwegian design by Rune Andersen)
      12 - RAVEN - claw and beak (I dare you to play it, a challenging read, never tested, my own design, recently posted on StoryGames)

      I have been careful not to include any of the "big" games. Warhammer, GURPS, Vampire and D&D is uninteresting in this context. I wish for players and playsmiths to play games that pushes them in new directions.
