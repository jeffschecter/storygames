    How do you define fairy tale games? I am going by TV Tropes:
    http://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/FairyTaleTropes

    Based on those tropes
    Golden Sky Stories has a fairy tale feel to it. lots of animals that become people
    Anima. It fits the cursed people that are not what they seem. Humans who were once fairies and other mythical creatures.
    Vampire, Werewolf and changling would also fall into that cursed people feel. Evil queen/king kinda thing with the Camarilla. Fairies, well thats what changling is all about.

    Generally though most fairy tales are in the story which would really be handled at the adventure level.

    D&D had two alice in wonderland modules.
    Paranoia had an alice through the mirror shades module.

    I am running a Pathfinder campaign with the twist that everyone is an anthropomorphic animal and that can't get any more fairy tale. Evil queen that cursed everyone, bad guys are evil animals and good guys are good animals, it works, it's funny, but it works.

    You might find a few story games that fit into a fairy tale. Those I am less familiar with.

    \n@Chaiboy My definition of fairy tale? Well that's a bit complicated, I guess: Marchen. Thanks for the list of titles. The D&D Alice adventures would be interesting to look at. The other games are using the themes and motifs of fairy tales, certainly, and perhaps are modern fairy tales, in fact; but they're a little outside of what I'm trying to do. I guess I'm looking for a more c.15th & 16th or earlier setting for recreating/re-telling famous fairy tales--provide the GM and players with a fairy tale setting and elements and let them re-tell or re-create their own version of a famous tale; or create a completely new story with the existing elements. What would you do, if you were Jack, Ivan, Vasilisa, or the Goose-girl, something like that.

    I want to design them as series of one-shot micro-games, so the adventure would be intertwined with the game itself, presented in media res.

    @mfisher\n
    I'm also trying to do a fairy tale adventure front for DW and also am coming up short with games that have already been made. So instead I just read a TON of fairy tales and fables
    \nI've read a ton of fair tales myself. I enjoy the genre. It's a little like reading fast fiction. I've been out of the role-playing/story-game scene for a long, long time. So, I'm also coming up short on game titles.\n
    Are you looking for mechanics or ideas?
    \nI have a game mechanic that I want to use. I'm looking for ways to solve the problem that most fairy tales only have one protagonist. I'd like to not make a solo-play game. I'm also not interested in having my game bleed into the fantasy genre. I'd like to see what has been done (well) already. I don't want to walk over the same ground as someone else. If there is a good game in existence already, that handles fairy tales, then I would just make supplemental material for that. I'm not dreaming of, or desiring to re-inventing the wheel.

    @snej Thanks for those titles and the blog article. I'll take a look.

    @zircher Thanks. I agree: I'm actually interested in Slavic fairy tales at the moment, but I've read widely. I'll be sure to check your link as well.

    \n FTs can still work with a group.

    Lost in the woods and find a seemingly safe place with a witch works with one or 4 easy.
    Someone cursed needing help.. works with a party.
    Save the princess... well yeah that can be tough since there is just the one princess.

    One thing that I notice fables do a lot is have the characters find items along the way that is perfect for the next part of the story. It can be a physical item or an item of information on some weakness of the enemy that they use to get past them.
