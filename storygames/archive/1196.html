Dictionaries of Mu and Other Tomes: How Do Books Present Information In Ways We Don't?

iago
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Story Games
So, right: Dictionary of Mu is awesome, and presents information as a dictionary. While it's not the only RPG to have done that, it's certainly one of the few.

What are other ways of presenting information in a book form (I'm less interested in other printed forms, though we can discuss that as well, since I'm looking for new ways to put *books* together as books) that haven't been explored much, if at all, when we narrow the field to role-playing games? Right now, I'd say the whole "instruction manual" form has been done and overdone.

* Dictionary and Encyclopedia are basically different quantities of information on a per entry basis.
* Some superhero RPGs have presented information at least partially in comic book form, but perhaps not fully (I'm hardly exposed to the full sweep of it).
* At another extreme, we have "untitled", though since that's more a collection of madman's notes, it skirts past the edge of what I'm going for when I'm talking about book form.

Stepping outside of books (though again, this is of limited value for my purposes, but I'm willing to keep that within the scope of the thread all the same), I have thoughts like

* That little two-sided card that has the rules for how to play texas hold 'em, that they slide into a pack of cards along with two jokers.
* Flash cards

With each thing discussed, I'm interested in seeing what it is that something offers that RPGs aren't doing especially well right now, as well as what additional challenges presenting an RPG in that form would entail.

For example:

Flash Cards: Upside - Allows information to be presented non-sequentially, and portions of a "book" to be shared around the table without only one person having hold of the entire book at a time. Downside - Confounds keeping things in sequence; you could lose the parts of the set; no easy "page reference".

That sorta gig. What's being done in the wider field of books, that ain't bein' done in RPGs at all?

    As I understand it, the Four Nations project that Shreyas, Thomas, and I are working on plans to present itself as an unfinished gazateer, a mottled assortment of maps and legends that need to be pieced together.

    Engel's monster manual, Pandoramicum, presented itself as the travel journal of a monk who wandered around looking at creatures. This was a riff on a lot of the White Wolf stuff, that emphasized a lot of first-person presentation of setting/history.

    There's the pre-modern tradition of offerings novels as journals, ala Frankenstein. Or a series of letters to someone. Or some other assortment of stories. Modern authors such as Italo Calvino (If on a winter's night a traveler...; Invisible Cities), David Mitchell (Cloud Atlas), Audrey Niffenegger (The Time Traveler's Wife), Milan Kundera (The Book of Laughter & Forgetting), Umberto Eco (everything he's ever written, basically) reuse this all the time, inventing new forms for the novel to take based on it's fragmentary and diverse early history. We could learn a lot from the variations on the novel.
