\n
\n I've been trying to get through my backlog of new indie games so I can finish updating my webstore - helps considerably in getting this stuff sold if I add them into the store, you know, instead of just sitting on them.

I just read through the rules of Misery Bubblegum, and man, this game is horribly written! The rules are split between two trifolds and a dozen cards randomly mixed in among the playing cards. I thought that Capes was a mess in the clarity department and surely just a fluke, but apparently, dear Tony, you need somebody to edit yourself and your crazy graphic design. Please do that for your next game, Capes is already causing me more support questions than most other games we retail combined.

In other news, the game itself seems to be in the top five of this year's new stuff, alongside Ron Edwards (I'm not giving him two spots for S/lay w/ Me and Trollbabe) and whatever else I decide will end up there. (I'd probably pick MSG, but apparently it was published last year without anybody telling me.) I'll have to play to know for sure, but the cards are sweet enough to cause diabetes, and I really like how the structure flirts with that of teen anime without actually using prescriptive rules to emulate the media. Once you get over the representation of the rules, they seem quite intuitive and playable, too - or I might just not be understanding them. I don't know, really, considering how spotty the text is, so I might just be projecting a good game into these rules shards. For example, I've yet to find any text that would definitely say that high-numbered cards beat low ones in conflict, which I presume must be the way it goes. It's just "Winning card wins the stakes." Real informative, Tony.

    It hasn't gotten into the revised rules yet, because I'm not quite sure where to put it.

    Because the card-printers I'm working with can make printed boxes that fit up to 72 cards (which the full MB deck exceeds) I am fairly seriously considering whether the right way to package the game is as a deck-box with the pink and blue (i.e. -essential-) cards, and then an included-but-physically-separate foil-pack with the phase cards, and rules/guidance stuff like the variant rules for phases, the 'What is your dream?' section, and maybe a startup scenario. That way there's a clear distinction: This is the stuff you can't play without, whereas this other stuff is meant to help but can be left to later if you just want to start with the lightest possible system.

But yes, I would suggest that with a large number of players around the table, the GM will probably do better (strategically) to use the non-phased rules, and work his butt off to spend cards quickly by challenging the players and drawing them into conflicts, then use his ability to call a draw when he can do so to advantage.\n
