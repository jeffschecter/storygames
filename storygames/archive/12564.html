    ...and you begin to play someone who grew up idolizing your past character.

    Generational roleplaying where decisions and loyalties hang in the balance!

    \nWhat you describe is The Shadow of Yesterday!

    Clinton R. Nixon, the guy who wrote it, says "TSOY is like a Weird Tales contribution written by Emily Bronte".

    Keys are the game's secret sauce; by choosing a Key, you are making a statement about what's interesting and important to you as a player. When you meet the conditions of a Key you have chosen, you are rewarded.

    So I want a guy deeply conflicted between family and duty? No problem.

    Key of the Guardian (Precocious little sister)
    Your character has a ward, someone who depends on her for security and protection.

    * Gain 1 XP every time this character is present in a scene with your character.
    * Gain 2 XP whenever your character has to make a decision that is influenced by them.
    * Gain 5 XP every time your character rescues them from harm.
    * Buyoff: Abandon your little sister.

    Key of the Mission (Break the pirate syndicate of Jagonath-3)
    Your character has been tasked with bringing the space pirates to justice and will stop at nothing to do it.

    * Gain 1 XP every time she takes action to complete this mission (2 XP if this action is successful.)
    * Gain 5 XP every time she takes action that completes a major part of this mission.
    * Buyoff: Abandon this mission.

    Result: Player-driven amazingness.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarWhat you describe isThe Shadow of Yesterday!
    \nYeah, I was thinking of linking this one, as well! It's an amazing game, and amazingly on point for what you want.

    The Shadow of Yesterday is set in a world called Near. Near is changing, crashing, dismantling itself. It's various cultures (an imperial state, a jungle mystic swamp culture, a gypsy nomads in the forest people, some others) are waning and changing.

    You have conflicted loyalties in a few ways: your people want you to cling to a heritage that you will likely outlive; and, you come from a distinct heritage and are typically thrown into a party comprised of people who didn't.

    So, while the mechanics facilitate this loyalty-conflict-and-indecision stuff beautifully, the setting does its share too!

    \n

    Dogs in the Vineyard also has special rules for family. If all the players and GM knew that the game was to be about family, then the GM would know to construct towns around it and the players would use their Relationship dice appropriately. (This is how I like to play 1-shots, at least: you tell the players before character creation that "This is a town about blood and sex" or "This is a town about money and family", so they construct characters who will mesh well with that. In this case, there's no reason for the GM to say it first, if there's time for everyone to know. This is just a thing I do for 1-shots at cons, when time is tight and executive decisions are welcome.)

    \n What you describe is Houses of the Blooded!

    HotB is about people who spend their lives arranging for people to owe them some kind of duty, and discharging the duties they've contracted. It provides several different avenues of mechanical support, including on-sheet relationship mapping, duty-related Aspects, mechanical bonuses for aligning with formal allies such as spouses, lieges, and lovers, and more stuff I'm probably forgetting. (HotB's main problem is, in my view, its extreme mechanical richness.)

    I also also endorse the suggestions of everyone above.

    \n

Personal renown is based on magical prowess (mainly in the form of spirit deals and construct creation), hunting ability (which is used as a metaphor for many things in life), and finally the mastery of a craft skill (which feeds back into family and society prestige). Magic needs to be an Art not a science. Family status is about your skill for gaining more family prestige in the empire (i.e. craft skill), how big your family is, a.     \n If you were to do this with TSOY, gifts would be portable Secrets (Imbuement if they have mechanical effects). Meaningful hunts require a hunter to Bring Down the Pain, which engages the gritty, lethal sub-system. Magic is your own hack of 3-Corner magic from the World of Near.     \n The way I handle gift giving in my Bronze/Iron Age-y game is that you have 'wealth points' that represent small, valuable, trade-able items in fiction. Mechanically, you spend wealth to increase your abilities (number of dice you can roll when using that thing), and you are limited in how many dice you can use per 'round' or 'exchange' or whatever by how many wealth you currently have. So it's useful both to spend and to hoard, hopefully creating some tension. Some of the other game mechanics are also based on player negotiations, usually involving trading wealth as in fiction gifts. Dunno if that goes anywhere near what you're talking about, Jason's modifications to TSOY sound like a really cool way to go, though.
