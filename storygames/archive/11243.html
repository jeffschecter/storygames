\n I think all stories are about a battle between light forces and dark forces. The hero battles the dark forces. At the end, the light wins, and the dark is banished.

Also, I think all roleplaying games are like this. If they're not, then they're not about story, they're about something else.

Could you ask me questions about this? I need people to ask questions like "Who wins, light or dark?" and "What about anti-heroes?" and "What about tragedies?".

I also need a few people* to say "Ah! What about this story, then? That doesn't fit your rigid structure, does it?". Then I will say "Actually it does, because of X". And we will argue for a while, until eventually I say "Oh, OK, that's an exception" and you WIN and I have useful information.

Anyway, please do ask questions, because it'll help me. I think all stories are about a battle between light forces and dark forces. Ask me questions about that, because it will really help me. Thanks!

    \n Hamlet follows the tragic pattern: the hero gives into his dark side; commits a dark act; and has to die. With tragic stories, it gets rather murkier, because the light and the dark are in the same person. But once you've gone over to the dark side, you must die. Dark is banished, because light must win.

    I tend to think Grimm is quite clear cut. Cinderella, the light figure, becomes queen, while the dark Sisters get their eyes pecked out. Red Riding Hood lives, while the wolf dies. Snow White becomes queen, while the wicked stepmother is forced to dance in red-hot shoes until she falls down dead. The morality is questionable, but the light figures (the ones the story sees as good) win and the dark ones are punished.

    With Japanese ghost stories, you probably win. I've played the Mountain Witch, but only once, and don't know it well enough to say much. It's entirely probable that everything I say comes from a Western and Christian tradition. I'm deliberately not trying to apply the idea to non-Western stories, because what I'll do, inevitably, is interpret those stories from a Western perspective, and that'll go very wrong.

    \n Let me define my terms a bit, before someone accuses me of cheating.

    The dark forces are most importantly selfish, power-grasping, greedy and uncaring. They are usually described as some combination of dark, ugly, bad-smelling, hidden, disguised, confusing, unnatural, deathly and sharp/jagged in shape.

    The light side, by contrast, is selfless, giving and caring. They are usually described as light, beautiful, pleasant, open, straightforward, natural, life-bringing and straight or curved in shape.

    In heroic epics, the two sides are embodied by distinct characters. In more complex stories, they can appear in the same person: so the hero struggles with a light and a dark side. If the dark side wins, the hero suffers a bad ending, probably death. If the light side wins, the hero has a happy ending.

    The ideas come from Christopher Booker's The Seven Basic Plots, which is very long and quite good. It's better at the start than the end.

    \n From the Greeks: The Odyssey. While Odysseus was heroic by the standards of his time and culture, he violates all of Graham's standards for Light - as well as the model of Christopher Booker, despite Booker's rather embarrassing attempt to shoehorn the story into his model. Odysseus whores and schemes his way back home, massacres a ton of guys, and then gets rewarded by Athena. Remember that his final victory over the suitors involved a great deal of treachery, scheming, and slaughtering the unarmed. He is an amoral schemer or morally reprobate time and time again, at least by the standards of Light, and he is presented as finding victory as a result of those qualities. He doesn't even accept negotiation or attempts to surrender. \n
    "If you are Ulysses," said he, "then what you have said is just. We have done much wrong on your lands and in your house. But Antinous who was the head and front of the offending lies low already. It was all his doing. It was not that he wanted to marry Penelope; he did not so much care about that; what he wanted was something quite different, and Jove has not vouchsafed it to him; he wanted to kill your son and to be chief man in Ithaca. Now, therefore, that he has met the death which was his due, spare the lives of your people. We will make everything good among ourselves, and pay you in full for all that we have eaten and drunk. Each one of us shall pay you a fine worth twenty oxen, and we will keep on giving you gold and bronze till your heart is softened. Until we have done this no one can complain of your being enraged against us."

    Ulysses again glared at him and said, "Though you should give me all that you have in the world both now and all that you ever shall have, I will not stay my hand till I have paid all of you in full. You must fight, or fly for your lives; and fly, not a man of you shall."

    Their hearts sank as they heard him, but Eurymachus again spoke saying:

    "My friends, this man will give us no quarter. He will stand where he is and shoot us down till he has killed every man among us. Let us then show fight; draw your swords, and hold up the tables to shield you from his arrows. Let us have at him with a rush, to drive him from the pavement and doorway: we can then get through into the town, and raise such an alarm as shall soon stay his shooting."

      \n Many moons ago I devised an RPG theory drawing from Booker's work. I called it the FUNnel Model
      Here's the bit on narrative - which speaks to the questions Graham asked above - how can we use these ideas in RPGs?\n
      Narrative: Once Upon a time

      The earliest known story is the Sumerian epic of Gilgamesh, which survives today over 5000
      years after it was first told in Mesopotamia. Surprisingly the plot structure follows the formula for a Hollywood blockbuster. This is more than mere coincidence, it shows that humanity has a universal need for and appreciation of stories. Intuitively, who doesn't enjoy a good story?

      In certain circles, there is much discussion of premise being the foundation of narrative.
      However, as Christopher Booker shows in his literary tour de force The Seven Basic Plots, there is in fact only one universal premise - Good vs Evil, Altruism vs Ego; Light vs Dark*.

      This is further supported by briefly considering three of what are commonly termed Narrativist role-playing games: Ron Edward\u2019s Sorcerer, Vincent D Baker\u2019s Dogs in the Vineyard and Paul Czege\u2019s My Life with Master.

      The heart of Sorcerer\u2019s premise is humanity - essentially how light or dark a character is.

      Dogs\u2019 main narrative thrust is how far do you go for your vision of what is right - essentially when do you cross the line into darkness (the genius of this game is that it will often be the
      drawing of a gun which represents movement into darkness).

      In MLWM the characters live under the shadow of the wholly dark master and only by performing enough good acts can they gain the power of light (represented as love) which allows them to overthrow the shackles of darkness.

      Narrative enjoyment comes from the fulfilment of the universal plot structure - darkness sowing the seeds of its own destruction to be finally overthrown by light. Of course there are
      stories in which light does not triumph, the Dark Inversion as Booker calls it. Interestingly these stories have only been written within the last couple of centuries (or older ones simply do not survive as memes) and are less psychologically satisfying since they conflict with our intuitive moral values. Hence Hollywood\u2019s obsession with (the more bankable) happy endings.

      This is also the problem with balancing gaming or exploration against narrative - it\u2019s all too possible that the dark powers will \u2018win\u2019.

      * This is a vastly simplified synopsis of Booker\u2019s work. I implore anyone with an interest in the narrative form to invest the necessary time to read The Seven Basic Plots as it is the most important book on narrative ever written.
      \nIn short good narrative oriented games already have the Light vs Dark struggle mechanised. If your fun is coming from gaming (competition), immersion or exploration then the light/dark conflict is less important.

      Seriously, though, I'm interested in structuralist accounts of narrative; one of the few scholarly successes I've achieved has been using Algirdas Greimas to read (English lit Ph.D.) Kim Stanley Robinson's Red Mars, which is a weird recursion, given that one of the characters in the trilogy uses Greimas's theory to analyze the other characters. Greimas extends Vladimir Propp's "morphology of the folktale" to come up with what he calls an "actantial model" in which characters are regarded as "actants" (functional roles) interacting with each other over their objects of desire, power, and communication. It's very neat, and I have notes for a game that uses those actantial roles game-mechanically to shape the fiction.

      Greimas is a hard slog, but I'm making it sound more complicated than it is.

      Sender ---> Object ---> Receiver
      ^
      |
      Helper ---> Hero <--- Opponent

      The hero wants or needs an object that is also a kind of message between some sender and receiver; he is aided and opposed by other actants (some of which may be internal to the hero). So, for example, Luke [hero] wants to "become a Jedi" [object], aided by Ben Kenobi and Yoda and everyone else and opposed by Darth Vader and the Emperor and his own susceptibility to the Dark Side. In a larger sense, though, "becoming a Jedi" though is also a signal to the people of the Galaxy [receiver] from the numinous Force [sender] about the overthrow of the corrupt order of the Empire and the restoration of peace in the Galaxy, which is why we celebrate and sing "Yub yub."
