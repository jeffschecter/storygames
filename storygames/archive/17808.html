    \n I think the most important elements in the game are the trust mechanic and the Dark Fates. The different successes are a terrible hassle especially in combat: I'd keep how the trust affects the rolls, but ditch the success system and just interpret the die results freely and hand out wounds that feel appropriate. Not a beautiful hack, I admit.

    Also, push the players to foreshadow their Fates during the journey, I find that it adds to the payoff

    \n The concept of fishing for stuff from the players, really embrace it. Let players hint their fates with it.

    My favourite technique is to define a simple piece of scenery fairly generically, then ask a player to fill in a detail relevant to their character. So:

    There's a dead samurai nailed to the mountain rocks. Who is it?
    The witches fortress has an elaborately carved iron door depicting sins. One of the carving looks familiar to you - why?
    There's a shrine by the side of the road to an ancestor. Who and how do they relate to your character?
    A rider gallops past you, heading back from the witches fortress. Who is it?

    And so on.

    \n We played TMW out of the box in this session, but you might find some nice gems in this play report.

    The key for my games of TMW has always been pushing the trust issue HARD. The GMs job is to sow those seeds of mistrust and discontent. Take the time to make sure all of the players really understand what their individual Dark Fate means. I've found players who don't want to get it "wrong" will avoid bringing it up in play. Which sucks.

    \n A thing I've found is that players are often much more reticent than they need to be about revealing their Dark Fate. They feel like they'll give the game away if they reveal too much too early, so they clam up and only give out little bits.

    In my experience, it's much harder to guess other players' Dark Fates than you think it will be. Also, it works better if some of the Dark Fates are revealed before the final showdown. Everyone wants to save them for the big reveal at the end, but actually the showdown with the Witch works better if most or all of the Fates have been revealed.

    Not everything you throw out there is going to catch with one of the players. Just keep trying, and eventually one of them will pick up something you're putting down.

    ..
    The players, though, felt that introducing story elements of their own and tying them to their fates was hard and didn't really come naturally to them. ...
    \nIt exposes the core of the hobby. Right there. You can do anything... so what do you do?

    I told people to think of a character in a movie that they liked, or some story that they've been trying to write, or a character they played before that didn't get to achieve everything he or she wanted. Usually they just need a little narrowing and they'll start tossing ideas out. (Most of my players who clammed up were just not very familiar with the ronin genre, so they needed a little extra push to feel welcomed.)

    The most rewarding foreshadowing, and also the hardest to guess at sometimes, is the kind of foreshadowing that interests / conflicts with / ties in with other players. In a sense, I'd tell them to do what you're doing. Toss a bunch out there, see what people pick up on, and learn the language of the session.

    It's often easier to reincorporate all the foreshadowing into a (mostly) coherent story after the fact, even if the individual elements of foreshadowing don't seem related at the time. "Nono, I told you I was from Owari because I knew you would see it as a lie. I only meant to shield you from the truth..."

    It's very difficult to have a "fate" reveal if you've been stingy with the foreshadowing. Other players are going to tug at your story additions, and they are going to weave theirs into yours. If you only present a few things, then later in the game you'll feel too constrained. You will be forced to be "the guy that kept all his secrets and now it's too late." That's a fun character to play sometimes, but not if it's your only option.

    Lay out a wide base. Don't worry if people don't engage some of the things you say. Don't take it personal, and move on.

    ... again, that's just been my experience. I'm glad to hear people enjoyed this game! I'm not surprised at all to hear there was some stumbling early one. Don't fret. It's just a game that has to be done a few times.
