Yeah, yeah, I know, Immersion is one of those tar-baby words. Tough.

Here's my take on immersion: I feel immersed in a character when I feel the visceral responses in my own body, or at least shadows of them, that my character would be feeling.

Intense, risky, well-described, well-paced combat and action scenes get my adrenaline up.

Painful, angsty, wrenching scenes make me cry.

Et (best left unsaid) cetera.

That's what *I* mean by immersion.

As such, immersion depends on my intellectual and emotional state as much or more than it does on qualities of play.

    I love the "people do that all the time on TV" escape hatch (to a point, of course). I think the TV analogy is helpful for new gamers, and I remember thinking that they'd have a much easier time with games like Eden's Buffy and Angel rpgs. They don't need to worry, "Am I doing this right?" They know the characters.

    For me, immersion is sorta like flow. Wnen it's on maximum, I don't have to think; I just do.

    I don't have to worry about minimaxing and strategy. To the degree I do, immersion is threatened.

    That doesn't mean strategy is bad. It doesn't mean a good designer can't build it in to immersion. For me, the Dogs bidding system clicks. For my friend Beth, it so doesn't. It pulls her out of immersion. More accurately, she goes into an out of immersion in a way she finds distracting. For her, lack of immersion is better than this back and forth. She likes the Marvel Diceless game, whereas I find that has far too much strategy for me to either enjoy or be immersed.

    I don't have to worry about breaking the game or interfering with someone else's fun. To the degree I do, immersion is threatened.

    Again, not necessarily a bad. I should be paying enough attention to make sure we're all having fun. But, sometimes, I don't have to. We're all on the same page, all jazzed, probably immersed -- and it's great. And, if I'm thinking, "Hm, I've been hogging the limelight," well, better immersion be threatened than our fun.

    I don't have to worry about being in character. I don't ask, "Would my character do X?" I know. For best results, I know what to do that is in character and helps everyone increase their enjoyment of the game.

    Practice can help with this. One of the things Josh taught me was, "Pay attention. If you do, you can find an in-character reason for just about anything." Use this as a feature, not a bug.

    Physical reactions? Eh. If my character is worrying about money, starving, whatever, I really don't want to have physical reactions. I'd rather be like, "Cool!" when something awful is happening to my character than like, "Ouch. I, myself, am in pain." This is counter-immersive. Wanting that level of disengagement threatens immersion. And, that's okay, too. Other physical reactions? It depends, but I think I err on the side of less immersion.

    Of course, while I want to be like, "Cool!", I also want to be able to let the character freak out or whatever.

    -Lisa
