\n A man you have seen in the background a few times comes up to you in the pub and he says the following...

"I know you're into gaming... I heard some people talk about narrative gaming one time... I've only played D&D and a bit of Traveller. What's this narrative gaming mean? I didn't really understand it."

How do you answer?

I was in this situation and I didn't really know. It completely depends on the context in which he heard it (which he'd forgotten). An indie gamer friend gave one possible definition and I riffed off of that (so as not to seem ignorant of my own hobby). But I did say that it really depended on the context and I was struggling with it.

Are there any other words you can substitute into the sentence that maybe you would struggle with?

Edit: Just to clarify I'm not primarily asking what Narrative gaming means but highlighting the vocabulary gap that I see gamers struggle against.

    \n I try to just use neutral terms, and explain what I think are the most important (e.g. fun) differences for me -- e.g. for narrative stuff, I'd talk about distributed authority and collaborative creativity and the like -- even though narrative games don't necessarily need to possess those qualities, I just frame it in terms of "oh, the games that I like that people call narrative, this is what I like about them that you won't see in D&D or Traveller." I'd probably throw in "rules light," and then follow that with "but not all the games are rules light, and some of the games have rules for stuff you wouldn't expect in D&D, like player ownership of motifs" and then I'd talk about Annalise and Universalis or the like.

    If a story gamer asked me why I enjoyed Savage Worlds, I could talk about the enjoyment of discovering and exploring the world that someone else has spent time creating -- you could talk about this in terms of more reading a book than writing one together, but that's not really dismissive -- it's not like roleplaying necessarily has to be creative in order to be fun. There's also an aspect of "us against them" and competition and a sense of urgency that is present in a different way than it is in more collaborative games. And again -- it's not like this is true for all gamist style gaming, but it's the part that appeals to me and that I can describe with some degree of passion, that sort of deal.

    Anyway, ultimately, it's a fairly huge question, I just frame it by saying "these are the aspects that I like of what you just asked me about," and I follow it up with "but, just like D&D, lots of people have lots of definitions on just what playing a game means."

    \n I'd answer like this:\n

    When groups play together, they either all agree on what's important for play, or they don't. If they all have different ideas of what makes a game awesome, then no one will be happy all the time and possibly, someone will be unhappy a big part of the time.

    I don't know where you heard about "narrative gaming," so I'm not sure what those people were talking about. However, there's a term called "Narrativist" gaming, sometimes called "Story Now" gaming, and its one kind of play to aim for. Like, imagine you're playing some old version of D&D or something and the cleric refuses to heal your fighter because you tortured the orc, and the cleric's player is making a point about his RL feelings about torture. But you're playing it more for the challenge of survival and you're all into optimization and making the best tactical moves. Maybe an occasional moral statement is fine in that game, but if you never play the way the cleric's player does, he's not getting what he wants most out of the game, and if the cleric's player never cares about tactics and optimization ("so what if we all died? it just goes to prove my point about man's inhumanity to man!"), you're gonna get frustrated eventually.

    Or at least, you know, not have the best game you could have.

    Narrativism is more about playing to tell a *meaningful* story, even if you all die. It's more complicated than that, but that's the gist of it.
    \nWell, actually, I'd start with, "Are you sure you want to know? It's really complicated and I might be able to explain it without a lot of jargon, but maybe not. It's a specific kind of RPG play, but if you're having fun already, who cares?"

    \n Totally not a technical/formal answer, but here's how I'd answer someone who asked me this question and had played only D&D/Traveller:\n
    Narrative gaming is when you play games like D&D or Traveller, but with a focus on storytelling rather than "winning." Some games encourage this better than others, but in the end, it all comes down to what everybody playing buys into.
    \nAnd if I had time to elaborate....\n
    For example: I once ran a game where the characters were trying to get to the bottom of a mystery. When they tracked down a person of interest while he was trespassing in someone else's home, one of the players said, "I shoot him in the leg." When everybody else looked at him like he was crazy, he said, "What? We're never going to see these people again."

    Everybody was looking at him like he was crazy because they were playing it like it's a story, like their characters were real people. This guy was playing it like a game to be won, where nobody bats an eye if you torture someone for information in an innocent family's home if that's just the quickest way to achieve your objective. (And it's never been clear to me whether he meant "our characters will never see these people again" or "we [the players] will never see these people again because the game will be over soon," but I suspect the latter.) This kind of shows the distinction between playing RPGs as "narrative gaming" versus what some players might actually consider more "traditional." In games like that, you're allowed to act like a sociopath as long as your job title is "hero."

    One definition of Narrative is a string of connected events. I don't generally like extremes but they can be useful for illustration purposes. On the far left, a story game engages Characters with other characters, with plot, even with themselves (Man against Nature, Man against Himself, etc). On the far right, a "traditional" RPG engages with the rules. At the back of the AD+D DM Guide their is a random dungeon generator. You roll to determine passages and rooms and you roll to determine traps and monsters (and, by extension, loot). The dungeon ends when every passage and room is closed off (no more exits).

    "I know you're into gaming... I heard some people talk about conflict resolution one time... I've only played D&D and a bit of Traveller. What's this conflict resolution mean? I didn't really understand it."

    I'm reading a really good book at the moment called Characteristics of Games and it's about defining characteristics that board games, sports and computer games have. (And even a lot of the subject matter there has not been looked at a lot.) I think that a lot of the definitions that the roleplaying game community has for the characteristics of RPGs are quite controversial due to their prescriptive nature (rather than descriptive).

    My own loosey goosey definition of a narrative game is one in which the game itself asks "why?" with respect to choices the players make and the answers matter to the rules of the game.

    In my mind the traditional game is the physics simulator, rules are interpreted by the players and they check to see if they offend the physics simulator. Maybe some rule that is story-game-like won't offend the physics simulator like say a fate point currency as you can say that it represents a full on effort or something. The interpretation of some rules will offend the physics simulator like say, the Player's Turn in Mouse Guard. If the physics simulator is sufficiently offended the game ceases to be traditional.

    But this isn't the most important subject to this topic. Why can we all spend so much effort on definitions and not get anywhere? Sometimes you tell a guy that D&D isn't the best system to use for a modern political game, and he hears "D&D is a bad system and you are a bad gamer: I personally attack your gamer credentials and do not value you as a person!" That happens just when somebody has asked you a question about what a word means in the pub. I just wanted to talk about gaming.


    You want to know what I think? (Oh boy, this'll get me taken off the christmas card lists!)

    A big subset of gamers put too much of their own identities in identifying as a particular kind of gamer. Gamers are active, they expend effort to play and it's really cool. It's deeply investing just by doing it and the player has a great time in a sheltered little community of groups.

    But this guy wants to talk to someone else outside of the community and tries to share opinions and ideas, which are all formed by highly personal anecdotes and experiences, and so the person lacks the perspective and lexicon to do so effectively. This reinforces the unique identity of the guy to terms that change meaning from person to person.

    When this guy is met with someone who challenges these opinions then it routes back to the experiences and onto the identity. Effectively, the person becomes upset because a difference of opinion has directly attacked self-identification. Which makes every little discussion inherently personal. And then we get folks offended over nothing.

    It's like everyone starts off broken the moment they declare themselves to be a roleplayer.

    Let's make up jargon to separate us. Let's shout "system matters!". I have always preferred "The play's the thing".
    \nThe term "roleplaying game" exists because some people in the very active miniatures war gaming hobby noticed that this little D&D miniatures war game ( and others like it) was really doing something quite a bit different from the "classic/traditional" approaches to miniatures gaming.

    Some people loved what it was doing. Some people hated what it was doing. They all agreed though that it was different enough that some means was needed to tag that kind of game and that kind of play to differentiate it from that parent trunk of the gaming hobby.

    So someone suggested Roleplaying Game. It was a placeholder term and nothing more, but it stuck. And people in the hobby kept it because some people wanted to kick out those sorts of games from the minis gaming hobby, while others wanted to feel elite, and not at all like those old fogies playing the old sorts of games.

    You're seeing the same kind of thing here. Same situation, same response.

    On the upside, that split, indicated by the change in jargon, ends up leading to RPGs truly diversifying in mechanics and playstyle, going beyond being just a single figure miniatures campaign game with a tradition of talking like your character every now and again.
