\n
\n I've been fascinated by the Indie Promotions Suck! thread and I wanted to tease something out of it but I didn't want to derail the direction the conversation was going at the time so I've taken the liberty of spinning this one off.
\n
\nIn that thread Judd said he wanted to see more things that said "Here are tangible and workable suggestions to make indie RPG marketing suck less..." and later I touched on 'Game Champions' and later we talked about podcasts as a way to provide promotion for games. We also talked about how being enthusiastic about your game was the basis of honest 'good' promotion. This got me thinking ...
\n
\nI think we all agreed that being genuinely enthusiastic about your games helps get them noticed. However it strikes me that getting someone ELSE to be genuinely enthusiastic about your game actually might have more value. After all you have a vested interest in liking your own product whereas someone else is far less likely to have that same vested interest. It offers a degree of validation.
\n
\nAnd that got me thinking about who would be the people who would carry the most 'weight' in that validation process. Whose opinion when saying 'Guys, I've read this and played a session or two and believe me, this game rocks HARD' is going to carry the most weight.
\n
\nNow in the real world we would court these people - I regularly offer free club passes to the students who I have identified as the 'social leaders' of larger groups of students so that I can tap into that mentality. I think thats a little dirty for the community feel that happens on sites like this and it indeed could make people start second-guessing the motives of people who are being pleasant with them or asking them advice about their game ... are they just fishing for a plug if I like it?!
\n
\nWhich lead me onto podcasts and the influence they have. I listen regularly to Sons of Kryos, Durham 3, HGWT, Voice of the Revolution, Independence Insurgency and Masterplan (whisper me if I am missing a gem out there...!) and I get a LOT of my detailed pre-purchase knowledge of games from this avenue. In fact, a lot of these guys were high on my mental list of 'game champions' because of the nature of their medium.
\n
\nSo I think this presents a conundrum... we have people who can add substantially to the promotional bang of your product but we also have a community feel that we don't want to be shilling stuff to them just because they can do that?
\n
\nI guess my question is to the podcasters themselves - guys, what's the best way to get our games promoted on your shows?
\n
\nNeil

    \n I began my podcast-listening addiction by seeking out information on games about which I'd heard good things on the internet. A slippery slope indeed ("Burning Wheel leads to The Sons of Kryos; The Sons of Kryos leads to Primetime Adventures; Primetime Adventures leads to Have Games, Will Travel; Have Games, Will Travel leads... to sleep deprivation").
    \n
    \nAs a consumer of podcasts and potential consumer of games, actual play recordings are pure, digital-streamed gold. I bought Don't Rest Your Head after listening to The Gamemaster Show and Spirit of the Century after Mel White's Virtual Play. Paul Tevis' AP recording of Polaris has it high on my list but also informed me enough to talk to my group and find out that they are into the setting but that the story-telling rules intimidate them (and therefore I seek individuals who would be willing to give it a whirl).
    \n
    \nInterview shows are fun and a great insight into the creative process but the participants in the discussion are, by necessity, informed and the listener (and potential consumer) is commonly uninformed. I guess a better way to say it is interviews sell the creator(s), not necessarily the game.
    \n
    \nReview shows are the best way for a listener to get snippets of insight into many different games. It's the most direct form of new media marketing for publishers, but it is uncontrolled marketing.
    \n
    \nOne of the most remarkable phenomenons that makes an impact on listeners (well, me at least) is the way podcasters are fricken' jazzed after great games at a convention. HG,WT and SoK both talk up con play experiences that make me need to get that game. Star Wars, Episode L sold me on PTA and Burning Taft sold me on Mortal Coil (I think that's right...). Try to corner podcasters at cons and ply them with food, drink and a great game session and you've got it made. :)
    \n
    \nI hope this contributes to the discussion.

    \n When I was kicking off the Sons of Liberty playtest, I recorded a handful of little, 30-second and 60-second audio blurbs advertising the playtest and inviting folks to give it a spin. Then I mailed every podcaster I could find contact information for and said, "Hey, I'm doing a playtest, I have these audio spots, if you'd like to help me out, can you drop one in your podcast?" I made sure to keep those spots short, punchy, and funny. I made them as easy to get and use as possible. I'd say about ten podcasts ran one of the spots, and I saw four or five additional playtests crop up (and these are actual playtests, not just downloads of the rules doc) and send me feedback.

    Now, I'm not sure if the podcasting nation really wants to be deluged with 30-second spots emailed to them. In fact, there's a very real reason I did it for the playtest and not for the release -- I find folks are way more willing to help you design a game than they are willing to help you sell a game. I mean, you're basically asking for free advertising. I don't know if any podcasters would be willing to run actual ads for released games, with or without compensation (run my ad and I'll send you a book?). Alternately, one of the queries turned into an interview on the podcast, and that's also a good thing.

    Still and all, at least for the playtest, I found this to be a very worthwhile strategy.

    \nI'm in the minority here, because I don't see my show as one that promotes games. Sure, I suppose it does, but it's very much not why I do my show or the purpose of talking with my guests. Of course, I also don't see why people who aren't designers listen to my show, but plenty do, so I'll just accept that they see something in my show that I don't.

    To answer your question, I'll talk about games if it's a design I know of and see as a positive example of some particular goal or execution, and I'll talk about games with my guests if they can relate talking about their games to a focused advice topic. I take the stance that if you hear about a game on my show, you've probably also heard about it elsewhere, so you've probably already heard about why you might be interested in it. That frees me up to talk designer-to-designer with someone and problems designers face -- and hopefully that promotes the person, not just the game.

    \n For my two podcasts specifically:

    1- The Gamer Traveler: If your game is about travel, features an aspect of travel, or is travel-friendly, I will mention it at least, review it if I get a chance, and perhaps even bring you in for an interview. Likewise, if you have a game that matches one of the locations I feature, or one of the themes of a particular location, I'll mention it and perhaps review it if I can. It's pretty much about being able to tie it to the niche that my show covers.

    2- The Digital Front: I do interviews with e-publishers here, and that includes pretty much all the folks here with PDFs at IPR et al. I already did one with Fred and look forward to doing more (once I get my but in gear). The angle is very specific, however. Aside from the interview, we run a review section for an e-product, but it is a straightforward review. Occasionally I'll insert a personal opinion after the review, especially if I have a strong opinion on the game (positive one, we don't badmouth anyone, preferring to just not do a review). This one is more industry-y in tone, so it doesn't have a lot of that fanboy-ism that drives sales, but if it helps, we're here to help.

    \n My group supplies some of the content for RPGMP3.com which is (currently) a site for so-called "actual play" recordings. It's a niche of a niche, of course, and not everyone wants to listen to other people playing games, but we dip into quite a few story games and we've had very good feedback from listeners. For one thing we're not looking to tell people how to improve their gaming, we're just fumbling our way through new systems and old, having a laugh and making the sort of crappy jokes you'll hear at a thousand gaming tables.

    Perhaps because RPGMP3 began with D&D as its focus a lot of the listeners have very little experience of story games and "small press" releases, so it's been interesting introducing these guys to new options. The fact that so many gamers seem to know almost nothing of even the biggest indie games does suggest that there are worlds to be conquered in terms of advertising and communication, but we don't actively promote anything. Reviews are informal, in-game mostly, so in our case there's little extra that a designer could really do to push his product via our recordings except perhaps posting to the forum. Game designers can't be everywhere, of course, and posting on every forum just to chase an extra sale or two is likely to be exhausting, but sometimes it's nice to know that people are willing to say hello.
