\n Airk, here is an other part of the puzzle piece in the communication mismatch between us.

Two games:

Game 1. To find a trap you roll two dice and add them your Sharp. 7-9 some result, 10+ some other result etc.

Game 2. To find a trap, you say what you do. If you push on the red plate with your shovel, you'll see that it's on stiff hinges and would fall if you stepped there.


In Game 1, improvise all you want. There might or mightn't some meta rule in place for how many traps you can offer.

In Game 2, interacting with the prep is the resolution system.

There are also games that historically started as having only mechanics from those two categories ("1/6 to hear through door" and "If you step on the plate you step on the plate") but has merged to include variable "difficulty classes". "To find a trap you roll a d20, add your Perception, and try to overcome a value, and that value varies from trap to trap." Arguably these systems have the same problems with improvisation as Game 2 while enjoying less of the "tangibility" benefits of Game 2.

My statement. The referee can't make up traps as they go and still be a referee. There needs to be "hat separation" in order for this particular resolution system to work.

Now, can the trap in the room be invented while the characters are in the hallway but before they enter the room? I guess.

This article is part of "the mirror argument". The mirror's effect being set before the players even step in the room is part of the resolution system for that particular game.

Games that use other resolution systems, like PbtA, are not bound to this.

    \n No, the disconnect is that you are (again) maintaining that improvising requires you to make up stuff when the players start interacting with it, instead of making stuff up before that.

    You can totally improvise with method #2 (as you "guess") if you decide, as the PCs open the door into that segment of hallway or room or whatever, that there is a red plate covering a pit trap or whatever. You are just doing it in a fashion that allows you give the PCs useful information and be prepared to present it as part of a coherent environment instead of saying "Yeah...uhm.... you push the plate and...erm... it's actually lid to a ...a...box built into the floor!" or worse "You enter the room, and uh... there's a... red plate on the floor and you... step on it..."

    You can't safely improvise this stuff in the exact moment, but improvising in the exact moment is the GMing equivalent of running out in front of the train to lay tracks. It's much more sensible to have a least a 100 meter headstart.

    \n I'm going to make a comment here, and please let me know if it's helpful or useful. I'll butt out afterwards, if it's not welcome.

    My experience has been that this is one of those places where the idea of "creative agenda" (or any similar centering/aligning of priorities, whatever you want to call it) is the key to cutting through this kind of debate, both in discussion and in actual play. Having an aligned creative priority gives a group a way to get through such debates and keep making their game as fun and as productive as possible.

    Focusing entirely on techniques of play is good, but you can get into the kind of argument we're seeing here: for instance, whether it's acceptable to invent something 10 seconds before they come into play, but not 2 seconds before. This all seems a bit silly to me, and to see people getting heated or upset over it (presumably because they feel like their preferred "fun" is being attacked, a natural human reaction) is really unfortunate.

    Of COURSE, in any given game there are things which are prepped and things which are improvised. There's simply no way to play without improvisation at all.

    So, how do we know when it's OK to do so, and when it's undesirable or suboptimal?

    Looking purely at approaches and techniques only gets you so far, and can eventually bring you to a point where you're so attached to the techniques that they become overwhelming (for example, if you become convinced that the only way to have a fun game is to prep EVERYTHING, or that you shouldn't prep because improvisation is more flexible, or whatever other absolute stance).

    I find that focusing on some kind of creative goal or guiding principle can help us all take a breath and relax, and then align our process with that goal.

    For example, in Eero's "challenge-based adventuring" paradigm, we adopt a fairly explicit "Gamist agenda": the whole purpose of the game is to challenge the players' cleverness, wits, and ability to judge risks vs. the odds provided by the game itself. We create fiction (sometimes very intense, detailed fiction!), characters, fictional environments, secrets, enemies, and so forth, all in service to this goal.

    This kind of insight about the game you're playing immediately allows you to cut through a debate like this, because you can safely consider and discuss any given choice in play and hold it up against the object of our game.

    This shows us immediately why it's satisfying to decide what the effects of the mirror are before the PCs even encounter it (because it means more of their earlier choices are lucky guesses or smart risk-taking, and they pay off - or not - appropriately), but why it's totally fine to improvise a dungeon layout, adding a slave pit to a corner (because we're concerned about the characters' ability to defeat the slavers, and the pit is equally advantageous as it is disadvantageous - it's just another tactical feature which a clever combatant might use to their advantage, if they can).

    More importantly, it allows us to see why, given a slightly different setup or different player goals, the exact opposite might be true. If improvising the slave pit makes a challenge significantly harder or allows the players to circumvent it altogether, it was an unwelcome addition to the game; we have failed to engage in the challenge fairly.

    On the other hand, if revealing the mirror turns out to be entirely irrelevant to the challenges we're dealing with, simply improvising something might be entirely acceptable and even welcome.

    We can negotiate these things in real time, as well. That allows us to troubleshoot difficult situations smoothly and without conflict, by embracing our joint commitment to creating and resolving challenges impartially. For instance, I might say, "I haven't determined yet what the effects of the mirror are. Are you committed to the act of pulling off the canvas? If so, I'm going to roll on this random effects table here. Any objections to that?" (And we might negotiate over which random table gets used, if necessary.) Perhaps I already had an idea, but I hadn't settled on it, so I could say, "It's clearly either a demon-trapping mirror, or a scrying mirror. Roll 50/50 to see which it is; if it IS a demon-trapping mirror, however, there will also be a 2-in-6 chance it's currently containing one." This then becomes a precedent for handling such things in the future, as well.

    Similarly, I might say, "It occurs to me that a dungeon like this definitely *should* have a slave pit. However, adding it to the layout of the dungeon might make it significantly harder or easier to reach your goals. How should we handle this?" The players might agree to let me think about for a while, because we're all really committed to this particular challenge, and we don't want to sacrifice the integrity of the dungeon map. Then we might spend some more time on other concerns for the rest of the session ("We need to sort out our provision situation, anyway..."), and agree to get back to it next week.

    Or we might agree to randomize it somehow, or bow to the judgement of a player knowledgeable about this kind of thing - "Hey, Julie, you started this whole 'slaver dungeon' thing when the campaign began. Where would a slave pit typically be, in this kind of dungeon?" Or we might try some other operation entirely, like adding it in the logical place, but then making a roll to decide where the characters are - to see how advantageous their position is relative to this new addition. A successful Dungeon-delving roll might allow them to choose freely, as though they had known about it before they got to this latest crossroads, whereas a failure might mean they were clueless and wandered past it earlier, missing out on the opportunity.

    The point is that the clear guidance of a creative agenda or game object allows us to consider this kind of challenge together and come up with a solution. We know that we want to create interesting challenges and resolve them as impartially as possible, we know that we value certain information over other information (like the concept of "fog of war"), and we know that we want the fiction to remain as coherent as possible (or whatever our actual priorities are in the game; these are just examples). With that kind of collaborative focus on a creative goal, these hiccups no longer form an obstacle we can't easily overcome.

    \n How do you explain Eero's success, then? (Or, more relevantly, all the people who have been inspired by his writeups here and have started games based on them after reading?)

    For many people - including myself - his Step On Up view of D&D (which I'm using as a shorthand for a whole lot of material, though, and direction) produced precisely the same lightbulb moment you experienced with the "mirror" in your D&D game. Suddenly the game makes sense.

    I suspect - but cannot be sure, of course - that people who play in this style but can't see the usefulness of a "Step On Up" mindset are attaching all kinds of other associations to the idea, and those include stuff they don't like in gaming, so they reject the label. I strongly suspect that if they were to play together, they would see that they're doing the same thing, after all, and almost certainly have a great time.

    The shorthand is that the players are trying to be clever and to take risks and to pull off meaningful successes. We create a tangible and believable fictional environment for them to set the parameters for these meaningful challenges and successes. The GM uses prep and principled improvisation to set up challenges and then steps back and resolves them as impartially as possible (as an arbiter or referee), which, in turn, enables the players to engage with the challenges in whatever way they like and to enjoy facing them, with a legitimate sense of danger and real outcomes at stake.

    The combination makes play feel "real" - the effect is one where the players are set against solid fictional challenges, and outcomes are determined according to what seems believable - "what would happen" - and successes pulled off by the characters feel incredibly meaningful, imparting a real sense of accomplishment.

    We all know that the stunt we pulled with the mirror had real impact, because it was a logical outcome of prior decisions (GM's prep + character actions + circumstances of play), and no one can gainsay it. If the scene framing is good and clear, if challenges appear balanced (as in frighteningly difficult some times and suspiciously easy now and then), if our choices and efforts do change something, if consequences narrated feel reasonable, if the whole process creates exciting/shocking/hilarious moments and everybody is having fun, then the world feels alive and we are okay with/prefer not to look behind the DMs screen and risk breaking the illusion. I mean, even if we knew for sure / found out everything was prepped, that doesn't add anything (for us) to the experience, it just tells us something about the GM persona.

    Going back to my GM experience, I have lots of memory issues. I can't memorize my prep unless I prepare like if I'll have an exam next day. And make really concise and complete notes (it's another way to help me memorize stuff) which is something I hate about 5e: I have to keep interrupting myself to check my prep and remember what was supposed to be in that room, then I have to think a way to properly narrate that. And then look at the players faces and recite it by memory while trying to infuse it with the proper emotions I want to transmit about the place! Arrrrgh!, it's so slow and frustrating that the most enjoyable moments are either when I remember the prep and/or when I fill the blanks with Impro. Which in D&D I end up regretting because when I check up the rules and numbers it turns out the great scene and challenge I just framed is totally unbalanced and the players will die without a real chance of changing anything because of all this.

    I mean, okay, players can tolerate a few moments of prep checking and rules searching, I do delegate things, they do help me with that, but roleplaying is a conversation which I'm taking forever to answer with 5e, so I can't blame them when they pick up their smartphones while I'm desperately searching for the proper way to keep the conversation going. Brrrr, it kinda reminds me of the worst dates I had when I was a teen, when I look at it like that!

    That's why I'm most comfortable and feel the world more tangible when conversation is fluid, when I can keep all players on the edges of their seat and I don't have to worry about the session as if it were an exam, nor about juggling lots of numbers on my mind while trying to come up with an interesting engagin character. And specially when I don't need to stop everything to make sure I'm being fair. I love 5e but it still seems not to be my thing anyway, so I'm most probably returning to the design table for the next system I'll use for our upcoming campaign.

      How I see it overall:
      (My "theoretical framework", if you care, is as defined in 1st edition AW - I haven't carefully read 2nd edition yet - which I believe to be the state-of-the-art Forge textbook. Thus, RPGs as conversations, turns which don't feel like turns, and simultaneously RPGs as games, with an object you make moves towards. That kind of stuff.)\n
      • In strictly Forgite terms, the SIS is by definition shared: a social, conversational thing. There is no committing to it except by saying something aloud, for everybody to hear, of course. That's your move, and as it changes the state of the "board", you can't take it back. Note, though, how this just means "that's the object of Forge theory" rather than "nothing else matters". Of course there's more which matters! Here, we're discussing something which does matter to game-play but actually happens before all the SIS stuff.
        • \n
      • It is entirely possible to mentally commit to your move, in advance of actually making (speaking) it. As everybody appears to agree upon in this thread, this isn't just desirable on a GM's part, but it can in fact be required for a satisfying, functional game to happen, depending on what its object is. How far in advance depends on the object of the game and other techniques. Accordingly, @2097's enjoyment of the magical mirror scenario depended on knowing that the GM had already committed to the mirror's properties before the bullywugs uncovered it, even if - technically speaking - that's the moment the GM made their move.
        • \n
      • A significant peculiarity of games with a "traditional" GM role, compared to games where everyone is on more equal footing, is that - unlike other players - a GM has a lot of stuff to mentally juggle, including committing to moves they won't be able to visibly make until later, or they might never be able to make, making the trapped mirror scenario somewhat typical. Another peculiarity of many (though not all) such games is that other players tend to phrase their moves as implicit or explicit questions, with the GM or group consensus needed to validate them as "final" moves, while the GM's moves are self-validating. Sum up those and it follows that it takes significant mental discipline for a GM to distinguish between their already-committed-to but not yet implemented moves and mere musings for possible future moves, and to honor their own commitments... It may feel futile at times, as everything is happening within their own mind only.
        • \n
      • A special case of the above is when a "traditional" GM is decidedly committed to an outcome and, in order to achieve that, treats anything else as comparatively noncommittal. A GM who's committed to the PCs surviving the fight, for example, might make the mirror magical and cursed/trapped on the spot specifically in order to kill some bullywugs and give the PCs a break. As long as this doesn't contradict any previous move (actual move, as in publicly manipulating the SIS: like, a PC magic-user casting Detect Magic and the GM telling them the mirror isn't magical) this doesn't even count as cheating: it's well within the scope of the GM's legal moves. Depending on the game, it may run against some GMing principle, though, or be a breach of social contract.
        • \n
      \nWhat I'd most like to point out is: according to the above not all prep is the same. There are different kinds of prep, namely:\n
      \n
      Noncommittal prep
      You prepare a toolbox to use during the game. Lists of names, items and stuff are just an example such tool. Writing random event tables can be seen as an instance of noncommittal prep too: commitment to an event only happens when you roll on the table and make a move accordingly, to start disclosing what the new event is.
      \n
      Committal prep
      When you write something down, you commit to it. It feels to you like a game move you're making and failing to honor that commitment would be like taking back a move. In AW (1e) Fronts, it's an act of commitment to write down a Stakes question: you commit to only finding out the answer through certain methods which aren't entirely under your exclusive control, as opposite to just dictating the answer by fiat.
      \n
      \nNoncommittal prep doesn't have to be sketchy, nor does Committal prep have to be detailed and exhaustive.
      I'm not sure a "middle ground" is even possible, but I do believe mixed prep to be common - where some elements are mere tools while others have been committed to in advance.
