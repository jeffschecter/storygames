\n So here's a problem I've run into many times with beginners. The player has chosen to establish, and begins the scene, which moves along at a decent clip, getting to a resolution in narrative before the group has had a chance to choose a resolution die. Any advice on how to slow it down, or avoid the issue?

    \n My advice is to adjudicate in retrospect. No harm, fair play. This probably gives the establish-er a little too much resolve-y power if it happens all the time, but once in a while it is no big deal. If you are having fun and the brisk pace suits your group, it is on the resolvers to pay closer attention the the arc of the scene and head it off at the pass.

    Slowing your scenes down, if that's what you want, is a matter of intent and communication. Ask more questions, enjoy the details a little more, model the behavior you want to see.

    \n A simple trick to do just that is to step out of the scene, in narration.

    Even if Judy's character establishes this scene with Mark, and Judy runs the scene (intentionally or otherwise) to it's conclusion without consulting the group, the thing is, the scene isn't actually resolved. Make that clear.

    No need to be a dick about it. Show don't tell. If it's a good ending and y'all agree with it, grab that white die and add "And Judy smiles and walks outside to light a cigarette." If it's a bad ending and you all think it should be so, grab that black die and cut to the news reporter just outside trying to get past the police barricade.

    The only time I've seen this behavior be a bad thing is when a player runs roughshod over the input of other people because they're trying to "get away" with something. Take a pause and talk that over, if you can, or just capitalize on that dark cloud that's hanging over their apparent "happy" ending to twist it against their character. Make it clear that the game's still gonna be fun even if things happen to their character that aren't good or that they wouldn't want to happen to themselves.

    As a side point, in my games we're getting to the point where we grab a die mid-scene, most often mid-sentence, and just make it clear to everyone without saying anything. It lets us stay in character and still accept the influence of the group on the scene. Try grabbing a die in the middle, even if it seem premature the first few times. That way people get in to the habit of listening to the group's feedback.

    \n My target tends to be to push the dice across when the purpose of the scene is obvious and let the player see that and resolve in the direction chosen, if that's a little early then I've no problem with that, it gives the scene a bit of time to justify the resolution.

    If players were getting to the resolution point early all the time I'd just are sure the colour had been picked even quicker and probably throw in a reminder that the dice colour being chosen is the marker for scene end to be coming up. If it needs reinforcing I might state 'it ends well/badly for your character' when the colour is chosen to remind that an apparently positive scene can still have negative repercussions and vice versa.

    \n To me, it is prescriptive, though the direction it takes is influenced by the narrative. But from my view, this is where the 'game' aspect begins to form, and what makes it so interesting, that the resolution is out of the hands of someone, regardless of where the scene is going. Whoever sets up does not get to pick where it ends up, whether that be the table or the player. And that is some FUN stuff.

    \n Yeah, regardless of what happens in the scene, it's not decided until the die is chosen by whoever gets to resolve that scene. The course might seem very obvious and in that case the choice either confirms that direction or turns it on it's heels. "Yeah, this is all going really well for you but... [black die] it's not going to be that easy."

    I've seen some games where people select the die before the scene even starts, which isn't how I do it, and seems odd. It can still work but I think it takes some of the tension and possible surprise out of the scene.
