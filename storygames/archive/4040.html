\n Way too long ago, LA Gamers sat down to play test Business Solutions, being
that that's part of the mission of the group, and Jason asked.

The scary thing is how solid this game plays. If you take away only one thing
from this play test report, it should be this: Business Solutions produces a
surprisingly consistent mood, just a little comedic, without having the
no-rails wackiness that's a common result of play without a GM. That in and
of itself was surprisingly cool.

Precis on the game: Three players make 6 repairmen, in pairs of junior and
senior. Six scenes, where one player presents a business, one player plays
his senior, the other the junior, and they go out to fix a copy machine. In
the course of the scene, one of the repairmen will also run into someone they
know, and play out some difficulty in that relationship. There's a real
commerce in mojo cards, that at the end of the game (the All Hands meeting)
will result in one character being promoted, and his first job being to fire
the one with the least good mojo. Really fine single session role playing.

Characters: \n
    \n
  • Mark: Sylvester Leroi, lead patronizing old-timer in need of
    crime; Dylan Opal, trainee irritating hipster in need of slack
  • Josh: Jessica Jones, lead unsavory geek in need of lunacy; Auggie
    Penderson, trainee bitter nerd in need of power.
  • Judson: Bruce, lead clueless creep in need of betrayal; Aaron, trainee
    earnest noob in need of loyalty
\nThe clients were Diamond Jee's, a jewelry store in a bad neighborhood,
Interstate Gravel, which was very dusty, and Bridgewater animal control and
correctional facility.

Sadly, the only relationship I have noted is Sylvester's grasping buddy, who I
distinctly remember as being an old army money who wanted to borrow a little
more money to cover gambling debts. I do remember a consistant quality level
about the various relationships, and it all worked well.

Specific quibbles we had over the course of play:\n
    \n
  • We'd considered the possibility of doing a switchback to select
    initial needs, so that everyone gets a fair pick from the list.
  • There was the suggestion of there being some kind of random tables to
    suggest ideas for businesses
  • There was a question about when exactly the relationship type is chosen.
    We tended to select it just after the initial call, so that the repairmen
    could discuss the relationship on the drive over.
  • While the text wasn't clear, we played that when dividing mojo after a
    success, it was not an option to simply discard the third card, which seemed
    in line with the general pressure to make a deal or lose everything.
  • Likewise, anywhere that the rules require a player to give up a un-used
    card, we weren't sure what to do in the event of being currently void of
    cards. We played it as lucky: no further loss.
  • We have a vague note about "what's at stake" regarding relationship
    challenges. As it was, we sort of intuited reasonable in-fiction outcomes,
    but if there was one place where we weren't exactly sure how to proceed, this
    was it.
\nIt would be a shame that Business Solutions has languished so long, except
that it was playing bridesmaid to Grey Ranks, which forgives many sins indeed.

I'll count on Mark and Josh to fill in details I might have missed.

    \nThis is just musing in the moment, and maybe I just have Acts of Evil on my mind, but part of what Paul's going for there is a development of NPCs into real characters - and his method is to continue to toy with their core tension over time. No idea whatsoever yet how well it works, but it suggested that (if it doesn't strain credibility) some sort of reuse of characters might flesh them out a bit. Nothing says my humiliating relative can't be your grasping old flame, but at present, nothing says they ought to be. I don't know what that suggests about relationship challenges - but knowing that some characters will recur might give an added impact to success or failure with them: "Your mom likes me better than you."
