Remember this?

\n

THEY BECAME FLESH is a tabletop role-playing game about fallen angels, right after the Fall. It is a game with two Game Masters, who control God and Humanity while players take on the roles of the Fallen\u2014 angels who loved humankind best and were punished by God for defending Adam and Eve. The Fallen are pushed and manipulated by God and Humanity, and every action leads them to one of three options: forsaking humanity and the Fallen to become Angels, forsaking God to become human, or forsaking both in the name of brotherhood.

\n
\n

Well, Elizabeth, Jonathan Walton, and I are bringing that project to completion, and we still need your help. We've reached our goal for the text and the War in Heaven supplement, THE KEYS OF HELL AND DEATH. We'd like to offer custom dice as well, and if we hit $6,000, we can make it happen! Take a look at the Kickstarter if you're interested.

    I am having a hard time with the idea of the angels being cast out because they have compassion for humans. This has me scratching my head. How detrimental to the game would it be to change that stat from compassion to passion or obsession? And then the age old question of "so why can angels question God at all?" remains a mystery and theme during play that you struggle around.

    Does any of this make sense, or am I just too stuck in my perceptions to enjoy this game? Thanks for making it.

    \n Hey John! I will try to explain my theological explanations for the (obviously alternate!) mythology the game is based on, and give you some idea of what the game dynamic is, so that you can come up with new ways to skin it that make you less uncomfortable. And I get where you're coming from; as I explained in brief on my blog, the whole game dynamic and the relationships between the Fallen, God, and Humanity are based on my experiences as a minister. This is not an anti-Christian game, just as I do not think Vincent would describe Dogs In The Vineyard as an anti-Mormon game. Instead, this is a game that helps me make sense of my own past relationship with Christianity. If that makes sense.

    That said! In the world that is THEY BECAME FLESH:

    - Angels can choose free will, but Humans are born with free will.
    - God does not lack compassion when he casts the Humans out or casts out the Fallen; it is the age-old tightrope of Grace Vs. Free Will. Those who question Heaven cannot live there; to allow them to live there would require God to take the angels' ability to choose free will away.
    - God is neither good nor bad, and neither is Lucifer, at least at the beginning of the game. You go into it simply with what you know of their actions: God cast the Humans out of Eden, Lucifer objected and was cast from Heaven. Like with the Fallen themselves, their "rightness" and "wrongness" is established through play.
    - No one "is evil" or "is good." Everyone has agendas. This is true especially for PCs: if you want to go in and play "oooh I'm so eeevil," I can't stop you. But I can say that I personally like the game best when you don't go into it with a preconceived idea of wanting to play a redemption story, or a transubstantiation story, or the story of how Lucifer became the MOST EVIL or anything\u2014 when your character just does what they think is best and the chips fall where they may. But that may be heavily influenced by the way I approach life.

    So with that said, my suggestion would be to be the God GM and make your God do good? But the book will never tell you who is right or who is wrong. That is up for your group to explore and decide together through play and the actions you choose.

    I hope that helps!

    \n The big influences for this game were The Prophecy trilogy starring Christopher Walken, and listening over and over again to My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy by Kanye West when I did the initial 24-hour draft.
