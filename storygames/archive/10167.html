    \nNathan, I'm not a scholar or a student of narratology, but I can try to explain how I think actants work in the context of a story. Actants seem to be roles possessed by characters. These actants depends on their relationship to other characters' actants and the situation in which they are relating. For example, one character can manifest different actants as we see in the earliest Star Wars movie: Princess Leia is first an Object to be rescued, then a Helper during her escape (it's really more of an assisted escape than a rescue) and later becomes a Sender when she orders the attack on the Death Star. Of course, I may be wrong in my interpretation.

    \n Note that Greimas's use of the word "actant" is different from Latour's, so take care not to conflate them. Greimas understands actant to be the structural role within a story played by the character in a narrative (and so "actant" is a term of art within a narratological grammar); Latour understands an actant to be the product of binding together humans, objects, and texts in the real world (and so "actant" is an actor-network, a "cyborg" held together by various mechanisms of control).\n

    \nYes, this. When you look at a character sheet, you can conceive of it as a network of properties and relations. What if they were all fundamentally contestable, either within the fiction or within the game?

    In my notes, I have an idea for a science-fiction game based on Latourian sociology of science. It would be sub-titled "technoscientific space opera" and the point of play would be to create more potent and durable actor-networks involving your focal character; conflict would be a matter of accommodating the resistance of other actor-networks. The mechanics would thus involve "subverting" the elements on other character sheets and shoring up the ones on your own.

    EDIT: Adam Dray's "Verge" actually instantiates this: you have no character sheet. Instead, there's a network of characters, organizations, and objects connected by semantic and relational ties. To win a conflict, you draw in dice from the things to which you're connected. If he adds in "texts" (i.e., cultural elements), then he's created the first Latourian story-game.

    \n Homeworld Project seems to fit in with some of these ideas. The web of relationships which is described by the character grains and the reflections of those grains in the wider world act as a background for more nuanced interactions. Manifests seem to resemble the idea of the gun in your hand, by producing a person, event, or other thing of explicit importance to the action which is directly associated with one aspect of a character (or a specific reflection).

    For example, during my Star Wars: a New Hope Homeworld Project game, our Jedi Master will sometimes manifest his light saber, transforming it from a simple accessory to being an extension of his Mastery and an entity in the action in its own right. If I, while GMing the Dark Side, "destroyed" it, and hence removed it from the action, I could return it by manifesting it from the Dark Side - turning his own weapon against him or to egg him on to commit some atrocity in violation of his pacifism. This would be a sinister moment indeed since Dark Side is the reflection of his Jedi Master grain.

    \nHow ANT (Actor-Network Theory) and the concept of actant can help in story games?
    \n
    \nI will try to put in few words what I understand about it (not very much, and just about latourian concepts) and tell how it can be useful to explore games like Radiance or Shock with this example:
    \n
    \n When we put a concept in nature (or take part of it to make a tool), we try to do it in way to make more easy to work with it. Imagine to walk in a forest without a GPS or a map? The map is not the forest but it is a material translation of it, enough to make you out of the forest, find gold, or a rare species (the legend is the function of the map, how it symbolises certain aspects of nature)
    \nMaps, theories, microscopes, flowcharts, structures etc. are hybrids (a mix of nature and culture, material world and knowledge), they put their mark in our lives, they affect us.
    \n
    \nThe concrete bridge is not just matter (we have hours of human work there, maps,imagination, ideology, dreams, computer programs...). The bridge can affect the dynamics of a city, circulation of people and cars (it plays this role in this network) but if you take the same bridge and you are studying the suicide rates, it can be a different actant, or affects humans in another way. The power the bridge has in a certain network, make it a more powerful actant, a less powerful actant or no actant at all.To know it, you have to follow the actant the way it is interesting to go in a network.

    Posted By: MJGrahamDoes this mean that characters and relationship maps are the same thing? Or am I completely off track?
    \nOnly if the relationship map encompasses humans (as individuals and collectives), objects, and "texts" (considered broadly: culture, ideology, beliefs), like Adam's game Verge. But you can do it a different way: Imagine a character sheet where the right to make or remove entries upon it was entirely contestable within the fiction of the game. Your concern in play is the strength and durability of the ties you create to the other pieces of your "actor-network," because you need them in order to get things done. But your nerves may fail you, your friends may betray you, your tools may break down, and your beliefs may lead you astray.
