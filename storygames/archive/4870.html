    \n I've experienced some of this. A possible reason comes to mind:

    Imagine that Player A just framed a scene. Now it's the next player's turn. Player B might be hesitant to scene frame and narrate something that Player A introduced in the last scene. Why?

    -Player B might not be into whatever Player A introduced, and might want to focus on a different part of the imaginary space.

    -Player B might feel like he or she _has_ to narrate something different, so as not to feel like a plagiarist--you gotta come up with something new and different, or you're just copying the previous player!

    -Player B might feel like they're stepping on some of Player A's territory by doing that. For instance, if Player A did a great job roleplaying the Duke of Ingham (as an NPC), Player B might hesitate to introduce Duke Ingham because he or she feels like they couldn't roleplay that NPC as well as Player A just did--they wouldn't be doing the Duke justice..Or, if Player A introduced a mysterious chest, Player B might assume the player had something in mind, and thus hesitates to create their own "contents", preferring to leave it up to Player A to reveal that mystery.

    -It seems "safer" to stick to your own, new material than risk warping or corrupting another player's fictional material and getting into an argument or creating bad feelings.

    -Finally, everyone's got some ideas. With your turn to frame only coming around so often, by the time you're up you might be itching to introduce your idea and make your creative input. Following up on the last player's contribution would mean losing the chance to introduce your own new material.

    Any of those sound familiar?

    \n The reasons could be many and I think the answer is likely in the details.

    1) I think the "producing player" is less likely to have his PC in that scene. So, if the number of players is limited and one player is producing, there is less chance of multiple PCs interacting in the same scene.

    2) The knowledge is shared. In the traditional RPGs, there is a lot of "you know this but your PC doesn't because he wasn't there" or whatever. When I play games like PTA, the knowledge is more easily shared and it is frequently assumed that a PC not in the scene may use the knowledge acquired by a different PC as long as it makes sense and is good for the story ("you have a good excuse"). This could lead to a more dynamic plot and even though the players are not in the same scene, they are interacting in a way. If I think of my traditional games, even when the players are together, they are doing separated things and interacting much more with the environment and NPCs rather than within the group.

    3) If your session time is limited, I think it tends to exacerbate the other points.

    4) It could have to do with the style of the game/plot. If you play a game where there was a murder and some or all of the players are trying to find out who the assassin was and at the same time some or all of them are among the suspects (with the real possibility that the PC was the murderer), there is no way they wouldn't interact quite a bit.

    5) I don't think it has to do with the fact that the game was designed to play with a GM. The way I see it, PTA is much friendlier for multiple characters in a scene than, for example, Polaris. In PTA if your PC is included in a scene initiated by another PC, even if your screen presence is lower, you can participate in the conflict to attempt your agenda. While in Polaris, only the heart and the mistaken have an active participation in the conflict.

    6) I think that unless you play with the intent of increasing PC-to-PC in-scene interaction, the tendency you noticed will persist. On the other hand, I don't think the current status means that the PCs/players are playing several different games. I think the interaction is happening at a different level.

    I like your method of rotating the producer. One concern: there needs to be trust as the producer has no incentive in holding back the budget. If he/she uses a high number of tokens to increase the difficulty, this means that the future producers will have less budget and there is more fanmail available to the players including him (or her).

    \n What I find interesting in various distributed authority/no-low/rotating GM systems are system features that encourage Players to reincorporate something created by other Players (weave story together) as well as bring other Players into their scenes -- such as rules tactics that reward inter-Player (rather than Player-NPC) conflict or cooperation, that provide reasons to "get PC's into the same scene", that create resolution systems where a PC needs help from other PCs.

    Traditional Gamist play has a very simple "the adventure is designed to almost kill the 4 of us working together, we start wandering off on our own we're toast" premise for cohesion then supported by the character class concept's "distributed PC specializations that need each other" approach to creating PC-vehicles for Players.

    That doesn't mean there is a "bubble" even in this gamist play as each player has their own "cool background for PC etc." that the system often doesn't cause to be "talked and walked" outside the Player's head. In my experience, ask players of a traditional D&D group to describe "what their characters did in the 6 months between the last adventure and the kick off for this one" and they will explode out in as many different directions of solitary activity as there are players.

    \n Hmm, if I'm understanding bubble-play term correctly, I've observed the same phenomenon in Polaris. That is, we tend to have almost-completely-separate story arcs for each protagonist. Sure, there's some cross-over, but I'd say 80% of our scenes are single protagonist only.

    One set of exceptions involved a very sharp hierarchy: one character was the General, another was a Full Knight, and the third was a Squire to the Full Knight. When multiple characters were present, the subordinate would fade into the background as a supporting character.

    Another exception resulted in a major in-character argument between knights. I was playing the non-scene protagonist, and I started to feel like the scene was becoming about my protagonist. Since it was the other players "turn" to have a scene about him, this made me uncomfortable, so I removed my character from the scene by having him storm off.

    I think it has something to do with pro-active agendas. Polaris knights tend to be highly resourceful, driven, and pro-active entities. When multiple such people get together, they either share an agenda, or they don't. If they share an agenda, one person tends to naturally become the leader. If they don't share an agenda, they tend to get into conflict with each other. It's possible to get shared agenda with equality, but its a knife-edge case.

    With GMed games, I've been thinking about how one would handle a game with multiple pro-active players - where every player is trying to push an agenda. again, either the agendas line up, in which case one might as well have one leader (one proactive player, every other player reactive), or they don't line up, and the game focus changes to PC versus PC conflicts.

    Basically, I'm starting to think something like "There can only be one hero" at any given moment of time. So one solution is to rotate who gets to be the hero from scene to scene. But if some characters are inherently heroic, then this amounts to rotating the characters.

    We did some experiments with this in an otherwise traditional game. I made two characters - a very high status character and a very low status character. The other player had a medium status character. We then rotated scenes. In one scene I played my high-status character, and dominated the agenda. Then I switched to the low status character, and the other player dominated the agenda. My scenes focused more on big-picture politics; his on small-focus skulduggery. They were about the same overall plot, but even here it was kinda like two separate "bubbles"
