\n In his Shadowrun thread, Jenskot's been tinkering with rules to craft the optimal experience for a group of players with diverse wants. He's pointed out how his players' ideas of immersion, realism, fairness, style, adventure, sci-fi and spy fiction often clash. One player wants to revel in realistic tech details while another wants to do ninja-movie back-flips; the first guy bores the second, and the second takes the first out of his immersive groove.

This got me thinking about two things: pre-game orientation, and supporting creative rights.

Pre-game orientation
Most groups I've played with do a good job of establishing the facts and general tone of the gameworld. Most groups I've played with do an awful job of establishing how that fiction is created by play. Does a fictional event happen because it's plausible, because it's cool, because it's meaningful, or what? When do we assume it, summarize it, or play through it in detail? I've found that a little pre-game discussion of these sorts of things goes a long way. Sometimes the game lays down the law, sometimes the GM lays down the law, but often enough there's space for discussion and consensus/compromise.

Once these agreements have been formed, I've found it extremely useful to refer back to them early in play as players are getting the hang of the game. "Remember, we don't care about that unless it's developing the character," or, "Remember, we're not following adventure fiction here, if that trap would realistically kill you, it will kill you." Asserting those things during play is infinitely easier if they've been previously agreed upon.

Supporting creative rights
I was discussing Right to Dream with Ron Edwards, and I was surprised by how simple and vital that "right" turned out to be. It's basically the right to contribute meaningfully to the fiction. The scary thing is that, in many games, this doesn't happen! I'm not talking about players telling each other "shut up" or "you can't do that" per se; there's a much wider range where, sure, I can play my character, but I can't use him to do anything that anyone else really cares about.

I don't have an all-purpose system for establishing a space in which everyone knows what sort of contributions matter to the group and is primed to make them. I think it rightly varies from game to game. But I do have an all-purpose philosophy that I've found effective in helping everyone to contribute:

1) Care about how the fiction is created by play (see above). If you see someone playing in a way that clashes with your understanding of this, speak up, and figure out who's right for this game.

2) Care about others' interest in your contributions. I think most of us do this just fine. If you say something that you really thought would get a reaction, and no one cares, then be wary. The second this becomes a pattern, stop play to clarify. "Guys, I thought we liked X! Is that wrong? If so, I'm going to play my guy differently!"

3) Care about your interest in others' contributions. Maybe I've been lucky, but I don't feel like I ever play with people who are just boring human beings with nothing to add to play. So, if you're talking, and I'm bored, something is going wrong. Again, if it becomes a pattern, stop play and address it. Non-diplomatic version: "Dude, no one cares about that! We're interested in X." Diplomatic version: "I'm missing what you're going for with this. Would you mind explaining to me how this relates to X? Then I'll be able to respond better."

Obviously, these all work best if there's a pre-game agreement on X. But even if there isn't, addressing disconnects can help forge one.

If my vision of Shadowrun play is a reality simulation where we care about clever tactics solving challenging missions, and your version is a power fantasy where we use badass gear to kick butt and take names, action-movie style, it's in both of our best interests to figure this out early on. I don't necessarily mean stopping play for a twenty minute abstract discussion. Simply saying, "Why are you spouting cliches while you shoot guards?" and hearing, "Cuz I'm fucking Bruce Willis, beeotch!" can be a first step toward that larger understanding.

Another way I'd phrase my philosophy: if you want the game to go a certain way, speak up for that. If you're happy whichever way it goes, find out what the other players want to do, so you can do that. If nothing's been agreed upon, then you must treat your vision (and everyone else's too) as provisional, until an agreement has been established through play. No one can give me the right to be Bruce Willis except the play group.

    I'll tell you the best I can offer from my own experience. I have an odd sample, though. Mostly, I GM for people I know well, or play at cons with other design enthusiasts, or playtest with various folks.

    Most of the veteran roleplayers I game with enjoy thinking about design. They may not have patience for tons of pre-play discussion, but are happy to have mid-play asides to troubleshoot. The tough part isn't willingness, it's awareness in the moment. But as long as one player is aware, that's enough.

    Most of the noobs that I introduce totally strange RPGs to are happy to be led. Though a lengthy pre-game intro spiel may strain their attention spans somewhat, they'll usually soak it up pretty well, in the interest of learning how to play.

    As for folks who are set in their ways: When a player insists that something must be done a certain way, I ask them why, stressing the positives. What's cool about that, what's your favorite aspect? Once I know where they're coming from, I'm usually happy to go, "Okay, group, let's try to do it this guy's way!" But, when I truly believe that it won't work for the game, I will say so in extremely certain terms: "I'd love to do that with you in a D&D game, but in this game, we are doing something completely different. If you decide it's not for you, fair enough; but for now, try it our way."

    \nBrainstorm time:

    Every player starts each session with two tokens of each type. (Example: the types are Puzzles, Relationships, Realism, and Violence. The game has instructed the players on the precise meanings of these categories -- Puzzles mean actual challenges to player cleverness; Realism refers to real life, not to any genre of fiction; etc.)

    Before play, they each hand the GM one token, indicating what they'd like to see in that session.

    So now the GM has a pile of tokens that everyone can see. Play begins. In the early part of the game, we start slow, setting up the situation, and no tokens are played.

    At some point, the players get to a "What next?" juncture. They're about to open a new door, or talk to a new NPC, or take some sort of step forward in the fiction. The GM picks up all the tokens, hides them, selects one, holds out his closed hand with the chosen token concealed inside. He then narrates the new room or NPC up until a clear sense has been conveyed, and then opens his hand. "Yeah, she's speaking in riddles, and here indeed is your Puzzle token."

    The GM discards that token. It's out of the game. He then places all the other tokens back on the table.

    The first player to the GM's left gives one of his 7 remaining tokens to the GM's pile.

    This process repeats for each "What next?" juncture, with the token-adding player moving clockwise around the table, so everyone gets a turn.
