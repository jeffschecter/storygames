    \n Oh! This one I can answer, it's at the begininng of the age of monsters section. It explains that wandering monsters encountering make no rolls, they simply kill at a I kill one thing per unit of my population until my people are dead or it's are. Basically it's handled by which group's turn it is when the encounter happens. If a delving group encounters a wandering monster group, you do the roll a d6, adding population, and whichever's higher is considered the victor. If the wandering monster group wins, it kills as many of the invaders as it has population, either all wandering monsters dying, or all invaders, (lots of times, both). If it's the wandering monster's turn, it automagically does the kill one per population thing.
    \n
    \nTook me a while to understand these rules, and I do understand the 'adjust so it's fun' unspoken rule, I do this alot with 'where the die lands' cause sometimes it just lands too close to another, I use where the dice falls as a guide, but usually feel free to adjust it about a finger or so in any direction so that it looks good. After Tony affirming Albert's statement on my post, the real reason I care about the rules is cause it provides an interesting randomizer to my own creativity. I guess it should be evident when almost every table has a 'make something up' option. But, yar, the wandering monster rules are right at the beginning of the age of monsters, not in the wandering monsters section.

    \n So, I roll for a custom wandering monster. I feel there's not enough loot in the dungeon, so I say they're Gold Golems. Kill them, and they turn into treasure.

    Two of these show up in enemy occupied areas: one spawns right in the Dragon's Horde and is quickly put down, putting Gagaan Hit at 5 treasure. The other spawns in the Ant caves.

    The Dragon fights the ants, and they fend him off. He relocates to his old lair, mourning the loss of his precious vault, and leaving the golem corpse behind. The ants run into deadly ooze, and some demons, but manage to regain much of their numbers. About a third of the map is Ant Territory, though they only have four units. The Skeletons and Derro just kind of sit around.

    The Kingdom of Kingshamford sends three more knights into the dungeon. These run into Gagaan Hit, who relocated within their reach. Three knights are an equal match for a dragon, and they are all slain, a vast hoard ripe for the pickings.

    \n That was awesome! I was so disappointed that Gagaan Hit bit the dust. When I played I fudged to keep my Dragon alive; no way he was going to fall prey to a mere Minotaur!

    This game confirms my feeling that HtHaD has a tendency to go overboard in the final stages, with too many dramatic and interesting events ("Metaplot"?) taking place before an actual player group ever lays a 10-foot pole on the Dungeon. Practically everything that went down in the Age of Villainy (and a few in the Age of Monsters) would have been great situations for real PCs to confront. I mean, reclaiming a Sacred relic from the Plague Pits? Awesome. Or reclaiming it from the Pool of the Thought Lords? Double awesome!

    A related issue: the more history you generate, the more of an overwhelming tangle it may be to the players when they encounter it. Like, the Dwarves built the Hall then the Drow moved in then the slaves revolted then the Drow died out then the Ants moved in then the Ants drove out the Derro then the Thought Lords ruled the Ants then the. . .etc. I guess two ways to alleviate that are to (A) simply pick and choose what history actually "happened" or ever comes to light, and/or to (B) break the chunks of history up into many separate adventures.

    I think the real art in HtHaD lies in knowing exactly when to quit. I'd say the second you get a specific situation that grabs you, put it to bed and call some friends.
