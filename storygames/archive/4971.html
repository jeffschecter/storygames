\n Has anyone done anything like this? I would be happy to organize it or if someone more experienced in these things did it I would participate.

So here's my idea. An artist does four digest sized Black&Whites. The participating designers then choose (or are given) the plates to design a game around.

Not only is the art fast and focused and the games designed with vision and consitancy but you've given artists and designers exposure working together (on a limited scale).

Prize pools should be in the mix is all involved can put a little something in the mix. An artist/designer would win as a pair and thus expected to work as a pair. You can't do your own game (although I am certain really clever and talented people could way cheat)

I would certainly participate.

Thoughts?

    \n As a spitball on the wall... why even have a "contest," per se? Why not, instead, make it a sort of "match-up" or "square dance" or "secret-artist-Santa" thing?

    Basically, any artist who wants to play submits... whatever: color or not, done in an hour or in a week, whatever. Then, each submission set is randomly allocated to a designer who expresses interest. ...And there's the rub, I guess: we'd want designers to be committed to producing a salable game (oh, god, don't make me define that, in the harsh glare of the angry industry thread) because the artists' compensation would be exposure in that game. Maybe, then, we'd want "design Teams" to drive projects home...?

    Hmmm... or maybe just collect and then display/post all the sets of art and have any and all interested designers propose game pitches, associated with one (or more, if possible aesthetically) sets? Then, each artist would decide which of the proposed project to which to attach their art. The drawback there is that some artists might not have any proposals, which would be disappointing (but, hey! that's incentive to really make evocative, inspiring art!). That way, an artist who is propositioned for a project has a pretty good chance of being seen in said product, as the winning designers will want to run with their ideas (presumably); and if they drop the ball after X months, the artist can just go down the list of losing proposals, seeking a designer who will finish a project.

    Yeah... I like that: everyone's reward is exposure, each artist gets to choose the "best" game for his or her art, and each designer gets to choose the "best" art for his or her game/product (might be a setting book or whatever).

    Thoughts?

    \n Things I think might be good:
    1. All products of this contest are free. The art is free to use, and any games that come out of the contest must be distributed as free products. I think avoiding the insane publishing argument is the best tack to take. Asking someone to participate in something fun for free isn't immoral.

    2. I agree with Bent that there should be enough designers interested that each artist's work will be guaranteed to be used.

    3. I think it would be fun if the artist's participation were in two stages. The first would be the initial 1 or 2 illustrations to kickstart the design process, but perhaps as the design comes along, the artist could do 1 or 2 more illustrations at the request of, or working with, the designer? I see this having two nice outcomes:\n
      \n
    1. A designer could learn a little bit about art directing, feeling out what an artist's strengths are and using those skills to their fullest. Even if the artist is working in a style that may not be the designer's first choice.
      2. \n
    2. The flow of information should be both ways, with the artist perhaps acting as a sounding board for the designers ideas of how art can influence the game. Ideally this would give the artist some insight into the design process, and the demands of content.
      3. \n
    \nAnyway, those are my ideas.

    Posted By: Remi2. I agree with Bent that there should be enough designers interested that each artist's work will be guaranteed to be used.
    \nThat's utterly irrelevant, if you take the approach I describe above.

    1) X artists put up whatever they are willing to give away, for self-promotion, in whatever style (color, BW, sketch, paint) or base 2D medium (scanned art, CGI, rendered).
    2) Y designers come up with pitches--of any length and detail level--and associate them with a given artist's set of work. Basically, the designers "apply" to be the one given rights to use the art.

    # Artists < # Designers doesn't matter: only one designer (or team) per artists submission will win rights to use it.
    As I noted above, # Artists > # Designers could lead to disappointed artists, unless some designers submit pitches to more than one artist to make up the difference.

    The whole point being that everyone is opt-in, the best artists will have their pick of designs, and the best designers will have awesome art as the very seed of a new game. And players get X new free games (assuming no artists bail out, after seeing all pitches for their art). Win, win, win.

    Give it some serious thought. There's no prize with which to deal (that's built-in: exposure); you don't really need an organizer (artists can just post threads with their art, designers post pitches in those threads); the only "judges" are the artists, the only thing "judged" is willing submissions; and the outcome is resolved individually, to each artist's (and, thus, designer's) tastes. Heck, it needn't even be a timed or "scheduled" thing: as artists put together packages, they can post them and then give some kind of time limit during which designers must post pitches... hell, or not, if they're willing to sit on their art indefinitely, awaiting an inspired designer. This "contest" could instead become a "practice."

    \n I'm going to use Remi's points, but I am replying to all who concurred. For what it's worth, Remi, I totally agree with your points 1 (all free, for all) and 3 (once matched, artists and designers work on as much additional art as the artist is willing to give).\n
    1) Exposure isn't a prize.
    \nUh... I guess we can't get into a "uh-huh!", "nu-uh!" debate; but as a (former) graphic artists, I can promise you that four ~$20 pieces (call it $100) is a pittance for anyone who doesn't need or wouldn't benefit from exposure (i.e. any popular or professional artists). That sum would hire me for less than two hours. Unless were talking about Denny Dent, not many high-quality artists can even finish one decent color piece in less than two hours, let alone four or more pieces (i.e. enough to make more than a token impact on an RPG book).

    Of course, I am now squarely in the cross-hairs of folks whose techniques or styles allow them to produce at a much higher rate, who are well-exposed, or who would be doing this to help *designers* rather than themselves: good on 'em, I say. They are a valuable minority.

    But that brings me to a significant question: What's the aim or agenda of this endeavor? I felt the opt-in-submission + designer-application process made a very balanced endeavor which ultimately would maximally serve the interests of matched artists and designers; what's the point of a "competition" which will end up with winners and losers (unless SG has some alt-definition for competition that I do not yet know)?\n
    2) I'm uncomfortable making this into choosing sides for dodgeball, but with people's art. Asking people to contribute work should result in a designer at least attempting to use that work.
    \nOk, I can say this is a bit absurd. I could do a few (very amateurish) comic style drawings and be somehow guaranteed that a designer submits a work to me? That's definitely not a competition; it's a stroke-fest, with the designers (mostly) as the gripping hand.\n
    3) This is a personal thing, but I think it would be very healthy if there were a greater breadth of styles and techniques beyond the ones generally on display in RPGs. I believe your suggestion would result in those artists who cleave most closely to the accepted paradigm of RPG art would be the ones with the most offers, with quirkier or more personal work being ignored.
    \nAgain, I gotta say absurd, particularly in light of the SG/indie scene. If we ran this on RPG.net, sure, the "cut abs and big tits" art would be raise a ruckus.

    But in this forum, I'd expect any and all artists to be considered, and those with evocative styles will... uh, evoke interest, period. In fact, I am surprised that you'd show so little faith in this scene, to presume that folks here (and folks drawn here, say, from The Forge) would be dismissive of quality art just because it didn't fit some "RPG paradigm." I seriously doubt it, though I don't know most folks here except by their work and posted (or implied) ideologies.\n
    I would prefer to give the designers at least a small opportunity to stretch aesthetically, rather than just pick over whatever piece most fits their preconceptions of 'good art'.
    \nAll the more reason to open up the format of the endeavor to an opt-in, take-applications style, rather than run some competition where some (most?) of the artists will be sent packing ("not RPG style") or some (most?) of the designers are press-ganged into wrapping a game around art they don't particularly dig.

    \n I wasn't wanting a dust-up, either; and I really didn't think we had one going--it's not a "fight" until there's blood split. No one escalated to Physical.

    And don't forget the (implicit, explicit? optional, required?) follow-up potential, once there's an artist+designer match-up: The artist can then work more organically with their designer, providing (say) more art or marginalia or book design elements (graphics) or iconography or whatever. This would provide great practical, professional experience with coordination, communication, and direction.

    My only real "sticking points" have been the notion of competition--with the implication of losers--and the locus of decision-making and judging: external to those risking time and pride, or squarely in their hands.

    Think of it like match-making versus speed-dating. The former trusts some presumably knowledgeable authority to thrust two folks together to make a match-up, and it hopes that the match-up becomes wonderful and productive. Conversely, speed-dating places a "buffet" of options before people who self-select to be judged (and judge), and it lets the participants' have (mutual) authority to get together or not.

    Which practice would we rather endorse?

      \n Okay, here's my proposal.

      6) The winners are congratulated.

      7) People go on to make awesome games with awesome art.
