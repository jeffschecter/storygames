\n Building on Luke's definition here\n
Posted By: Luke Wheel"[An RPG is] A game in which a player advocates the goals, priorities and survival (or doom) of a persona who, in operation of the game's mechanics, is confronted with one or more ethical choices."
\nOne way of looking at this is to treat the condition of the persona being confronted by one or more ethical choices as an indicator of how much we can relate to that persona as an entity in the game.

And that is, I believe, a very good question - especially as it gets more nuanced than just is the game a RPG or not:

How do we distinguish between game entities that people tend to simply manipulate (pawns) and ones which people attribute a degree of person-hood or identity (persons)? And what levels might we explore between them?

What can we do as designers and players to take advantage of this distinction? Can we help ensure that our games and our characters have the right amount of person-hood? Can we learn solve the problems that seem to plague secondary character mechanics or how to make more abstract persona (like nations or conspiracies) feel like persons during play?

    \n Guy, what I'd point to is that not all characters in all books are perceived as having person-hood and not all characters in all books are perceived as not having person-hood. So there is something similar going on there. And perhaps something to further draw upon?

    Of note, I'm not at all convinced that you need to control a character to attribute it person-hood.


    Lance, if it makes it easier, think in terms of making things (either as players or as designers) in games which have the potential to be attributed person-hood. Avoid the silly thought experiments about RPG sociopaths who perceive everything as simply entities to be manipulated, and perhaps instead think about how we increase that potential.

    Uniqueness seems a start, but is that really necessary or really telling? After all, after a short while the events of play will tend to distinguish nearly anything. Although perhaps the truly indistinguishable is of little hope.

    In any case, the fact that it is the only red poker chip in the game may be unique, but does that, combined with anything less than pathological willingness turn the chip into a person?



    I suspect there is some resemblance required at the core. Literally a cognitive tripping of our self-models that causes us to treat something which we might know on some level isn't like a person, as a person. It's analogous to what gets activated when we look at a tree and sense an emotion, as though we've assembled a sort of facial expression.

    But resemblance can't be enough, because we spend our time interacting with people all the time all too often cannot afford to really, fully perceive them as persons. Its simply too much mental and emotional effort. Indeed, you've probably felt change happen at some point when you've deepened your appreciation for someones' person-hood. Not that you didn't, intellectually know it before, just that you didn't fully let yourself perceive it. Somehow that change happens for some things during RPGs, and not for others.

    The question is how can we better handle how that change happens?

    \n A phrase from Stephen King's Dark Tower series was floating in my head as I was reading and writing last night. That phrase is "She has her own face". This in turn brings back snippets of Heinlein's Stranger in a Strange Land, which uses a remarkably similar phrase more than once.
    \n
    \nIn both, there is a character alien to our world. To this character, all the people seem homogeneous, a faceless crowd.. Except here and there, there's a character who, even when a minor player in the tale, stands out. This character isn't nameless goon #3, or "Uh.. Karen, her name is Karen, yeah." In the books, the character is shown to be someone that the author cares about, even if you see them for a few pages and never again.
    \n
    \nI believe there's a similar aesthetic value in anime. I'm not a really big fan of anime, but it was once explained to me that anime characters are always given distinctive appearances, elements of which may or may not be explained in the fiction. They're also frequently given certain personality traits, which may or may not be exaggerated. Examples include Dragon Ball Z, where you've got Krilin with the dots on his forehead, and Yamcha with the scar on his cheek. Another character appears to be wearing clown makeup, and Bulma's hair is blue. Another anime series which I'm somewhat familiar with is Wolf's Rain. Kiba has these vibrant blue eyes, Tsume has an x-shaped scar on his chest in both human and wolf guise, and his eyes are golden even as a human. Toboe wears the bracelets in both guises, and Hige wears a collar. Personality-wise they're distinctive, and while there's depth to their characters, they fall into readily identifiable roles that make them stand apart from the other characters.
    \n
    \nDoes that better explain what I mean by unique?

    \n You're facing off against five orcs. They all look alike and they all have the same stats. Just before the combat starts, the GM describes one of them as looking nervous and scared.

    Person!

    Maybe it has something to do with the thing having an intent/emotion/goal? Nameless goons all wanting to kill you. Okay. We don't know why; they just seem to be placed there by the GM's intent, or maybe by their chief or something. Then one of them has an emotion of her own. Suddenly she's a step towards personhood, yeah? (And now I gave her a gender, adding more uniqueness, too.)

    Even more subtle: You're facing off against five orcs. They all look alike and they all have the same stats. Just before the combat starts, the GM describes one of them as looking furious and angry.

    Step towards personhood?

    EDIT: Not saying it's the ONLY requirement (or is it?), but a neccessary component.

    Simon, it seems like what you're describing is that the game element should have (or be perceived to have) the potential for person-like autonomy? Although you seem to be coming at this in terms of innate qualities and to abuse Luke's definition an effects based description perhaps ascribes the elements as faced with meaningful decisions.


    In general it might be best to avoid binary examples in favor of increasing the potential that a particular game element is perceived as person-like.

    Also, I'm especially intrigued by game elements that aren't strictly person-like in terms of the fiction. Say you are running a fantasy game or designing one, and you want to fulfill a goal of making the Magic seem like a presence to the players - like an actual person in the fiction of the game?

    Does the existence of meaningful decisions or emotions of the Magic suffice? Or do we need to literally embody the magic, to make the metaphor into a reality for the effect to be achieved?
