    With Great Power... is actually designed to do story arcs leading to a crisis, radical transformation of the heroes (which can be death), and then resolution. I've never played it so I don't know how well it works in practice, but it seems much better suited to your objective than Capes, which I have played, and which is all about pitching out cool ideas that the other people will run with and make story off of, and then taking their cool ideas and running with them in return: It's essentially uncontrollable by anyone participating. You can throw your favorite themes in there, but it's entirely up to your fellow-players whether they ignore them or emphasize them -- talking about the game you want to run BEFORE starting is always a good thing!

    Sorcerer is another game with a definite "count-down" mechanic, namely your character's Humanity score potentially falling to zero, but that's very dark and not easily translated to "age and die." Again, I've not played.

    And otherwise I'm just blanking, honestly. "Indie" games almost never support a specific group agenda ("We grow old and die," "We save the Kingdom," "We fall in love") unless they're specifically hardwired for that agenda in the way the rules are designed.

    Nate,

    Fred is right in that Capes games are impossible to control in the traditional GM-Player sense. You can guide the story in the direction you want but every other player at the table has equal power to resist or assist that guidance. The key is to get them to buy into the direction you want so that they want to go that way too. There are lots of way to do this and some of them are a little counter-intuitive until you get comfortable with how the game works in practice.

    I disagree with Fred that Capes is as complex a game as WGP. In terms of rules density, Capes is a simpler game. There really aren't that many mechanics to learn and all the mechanics are fairly simple. However, in application the game can become very complex. It's like Diplomacy (if you are familiar with that game) in that there are a few simple rules but when you get into the game and start applying them the situations can become complex. In that sense, Capes is probably more complex than WGP.

    Frankly, I've played both games and neither of them are very complicated. Capes can be difficult to figure out from simply reading the book if you've never played an RPG similar to it. I actually had to sit down with someone who knew what they were doing and play a session before I grokked the rules but once I did that I almost didn't need the rules except as a refresher. Playing a scene or two is all it really takes to "get" the game. Don't despair.

    Wow. You get sick for a few hours, and everyone makes you ... uh ... superfluous.

    At a glance, I believe I endorse all of the advice given here.

    Now if you really want to make things happen, though, there's a mechanism I don't think people have pointed out to you: Events.

    Most of the time, people play Goals ("Doctor Doom defeats the Fantastic Four!") because they're very hard to veto and therefore very easy to write. But then, maybe you get the plot-element you wanted (the defeat) and maybe you don't.

    If you want to get at least Stan Lee levels of plotting you can do that by playing predominantly Events. It means you need to think harder. Like, for instance, in most groups you're not going to get away with "Event: Doctor Doom defeats the Fantastic Four" but you almost certainly could get away with "Event: Doctor Doom receives iron-clad evidence that the Fantastic Four has been defeated."

    Playing an event like that lets you say "Hey, I'd really like to at least have a lushly described moment where Doom and the audience are convinced of the Fantastic Four's defeat. If you guys win the conflict then, yeah, it was all a clever deception on Reed's part, because he's hugely smart that way. That's cool (though I'll fight to get the genuine defeat). As long as I get that moment then I'll be okay either way."

    JD: For a Capes-Lite interpretation? Yeah. Anybody who wants to outright oppose the goal should be on the other side.
