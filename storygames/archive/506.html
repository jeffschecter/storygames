How do you want your character to die?

TonyLB
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games

No, seriously ... when you drive your character toward death, how do you want it to happen?

These days I want my character to die as he lived. I don't really care about the consequences (did his death have meaning, do people care) but I do want to make sure that if he was a big fat honking coward then he dies because he meets the monster he can't outrun, whereas if he's a big dumb barbarian he dies because he hits the monster that he can't outfight. Switching those around would be goofy.

I used to want my character deaths to be big and meaningful. I wanted the character to be deeply misunderstood, and to have everyone hate him, and then he would give his life in one spectacular act of redeeming heroism, and everyone would gather around his body and lament that they had not realized what a spectacular hero he always was. Oh, and also, for the women, that they hadn't gone on dates with him.

Yeah, it's a little disturbing in retrospect.

I genuinely wonder whether I'd have been in trouble if anyone had ever acceded to my requests that they view my character suicides as admirable and redeeming. Luckily for me they mostly said "Damn, Tony! Why'd you try to shield my fighter from the dragon-flame with your body? I've got a ring of fire resistance, and your mage had ... what ... two hit points? You're an idiot!"

    I have a very hard time thinking about the deaths of my characters. Not because it's unacceptable to me for my characters to die, but because...character death is an emotional blind spot? I don't find character death very interesting. Not my own, not those of others.

    The "I want to go out in a blaze of glory"/"I want to go out in a way which drives home to my survivor friends how wonderful me and my sacrifice were" were just never very interesting roleplaying moments to me. And "how would my character approach death...I don't think my characters think about it themselves. And I'm not sure I'm wired to think about it, except as a series of reactions in the moment. That is to say, I can play one of my characters dying, but it'll be a surprise to them and a surprise to me, or the logic of the moment will play out with little or no philosophical input from me.

    My characters, and the characters in the games I play, very very rarely die. I honestly cannot remember the last time one of my characters died in a game, and I can't remember the last time anyone died in a game I played, and in fact, I cannot remember any deaths in games at all. I mean, ever. In all my roleplaying. Don't remember the deaths. They don't impact my memory, if they happened. I remember some deaths as narrative/status/game mechanic transitions for the character (character is now a ghost, keeps playing, character is resurrected as a cyborg keeps playing, character reincarnates as a dog, etc), but that's about it.

    This only applies literally to death itself . I remember people taking big risks that lead to changes to their character (physical disfigurment, insanity, loss of precious objects, severe alteration/destruction or relationships or social status), but not deaths. Does my brain just close off those files and eventually empty the recycle bin on them? I don't know. I don't know. If I substitute "death" for "action which brings large change, probably painful", then the answer is I'm pretty utilitarian. I take the action, I make the choice, because it's the thing that's necessary at the moment, and perhaps because it will hurt me less to make the choice than for the burden of it to fall on someone else.

    Some gamers I've known, or at least read about, obviously see character death as a potential dramatic pinnacle, or at least a logical aspect of character existence to consider and in some cases embrace, but for whatever reason the concept has never gotten it's hooks into me emotionally, not in a roleplaying context anyway. Projecting into a theoretical future, I reckon my characters either die of old age, or they die of cancer or heart disease, or they die in auto accidents, or they die in battle, they die in the various typical ways death happens. "But that is another story", as it were, not the one I'm reading/writing/hearing around the campfire.

    I'm not a fan of character death. Like Vaxalon, I like to think of my characters keeping bees in Sussex. I don't mind having my characters beaten, maimed, humiliated, whatever - but I don't like for them to die.

    That said, there are two kinds of character death I can really get behind.

    The first is the death that comes from the character's own story. I want my character flaws to catch up with me in a very Greek-tragedy sense. Whether the character is cruel, arrogant, thoughtless, lazy, whatever, that's what should get them in the end. Their death should flow out of their inner nature.

    The second is the no-good-reason tragedy, a character who has everything to live for but, well, doesn't get the chance. I played a character once who, early in the game, promised her life to the gods for a chance to bring her lover back from the dead. She spent the rest of the game knowing she was going to die, it was just a question of when the god in question was going to call her chips in. Playing someone facing a heartbreaking doom was, well, heartbreaking for me too.

    In my current long-term Ars game, I think we'll be playing my character all the way to her death, whenever that is. I'm hoping for a deathbed-with-descendats-gathered scene, but as my GM is evil I suspect I'll get something very different (but highly satisfying nonetheless!). So perhaps there'll be a third type of character death I discover I like, once we get there.

    --Jess

    As an aside, this was a question asked of players at the beginning of character generation for 7th Sea. I always thought it was kind of brilliant because, well, I love Dumas, and the death is as important as anything else for a Musketeer. The answer was just some useful fodder for the GM - it gave him something to foreshadow and tease the player with, and it worked out pretty damn well.

In a swashbuckling game it should have flare, and in a cyberpunk game, it should hardly matter at all, but it should end things.
