\n I agree--the title is weird. But it's something that's got my mind abuzz a bit since reading through Pathfinder for a game that's starting up soon.

The most engaging element of the system, for me, is the fact that it follows Magic: the Gathering's "Golden Rule" (the same rule also followed by CCGs): "specific cards trump the general rules of play". The entire game is premised around the fact that there's a relatively simple set of core rules, and a large number of specific options which bend those rules in different ways. Playing Magic means that you can find ways in which those rules combo, and that's where the fun of the game is--in building and then playing out a deck designed to synergize and defeat an opponent. It's one of the reasons, I think, that I hopped into messing around with CCGs--you bring together a diverse collection of abilities that click into a cohesive "engine" that does what you want it to.

It's also cool, because so long as everyone's on the same page, you can play a CCG at a high-tier competitive level, but you can also play it on a casual level that still makes use of fun combos--it's just not completely optimized.

The way I see it, 3.5 and Pathfinder scratch that same itch, but allow for more character flexibility, and for the introduction of a story element. They let you build a deck that has character and story, not limited to the narrow goals of a card game, and that's pretty cool in my mind. I definitely notice that 4th Edition cut down on the "intricate combo" aspect, in favor of giving character options more raw power. Being effective is no longer a matter of finding the right tricks, but rather of finding the right bonuses to make you effective. It's an interesting shift, not objectively worse, just not as much to my preference.

(On the other hand, 3.P's major flaw is that the "combos" which define many caster classes overshadow the other combos and render them redundant. That's its biggest flaw, and it's a pretty big one, and that's the one thing I'd love to see fixed about it. A lot of this observation is along the lines of "3.P is theoretically like this, but in reality...it falls short of the ideal".)

Mythender is the only indie game I've actually played which delves into the concept, with various Gifts giving you different abilities, letting you stack and combo them together for better effect. I hear Anima Prime is a bit similar in that regard.

Any other games out there I should know about? For this purpose, I'm not so much interested in something like Fate; I love Fate, but Stunts are meant to be taken in isolation, ways of giving you small bonuses in specific situations. It's very good at character diversification and specialization, but that's not really the niche I'm wondering about.

    \n Huh, isn't The Shadow of Yesterday the obvious example here? It's basically the game that pushes this principle through a couple of envelopes in the service of dramatic storytelling. That's at least why I'm fascinated with the game, that it takes the 3rd ed. D&D principle of localized rules (localized into individual character builds, specifically) and lets players loose to do whatever with it. Most importantly: unlike 3rd edition D&D in its fruitless quest for "game balance", TSoY mandates that it is up to the campaign to discover where the game balance lies: whichever combination of the legos proves supreme, whether it be half-ogres with spiked chains or whatever else, that's the story we are telling now, how the half-ogre and his chain saved the world. It helps immensely that there are no "builds" in the game, so that whatever discoveries you make about combining various rules, it'll necessarily happen at least mostly in play, through play, for play, instead of being a vapid exercise in armchair wish-fulfillment. (Yes, I have Opinions about 3rd edition D&D...)

    In a more general vein, almost all games that feature localized subsystems necessarily play by the rule that specific-trumps-general; it's just CCGs where an idiot-proof rules-writing paradigm has taken root. I mean, I haven't heard anybody suggest that e.g. FATE characters shouldn't be able to use their special knacks because those knacks break the rules. Of course following the "Golden Rule" doesn't mean that these games have too much in the way of combo friendliness; in that regard I can't think of a game better than TSoY.

    \n This topic is of interest to me, but I feel like 4e is being treated as kind of a straw man here. All about "finding the right bonuses"? Have any of you ever even played a controller, or (especially) a defender? Or built an effective party with two or even three defenders? I mean, I guess if you have, and that's how you played it, it would not be very satisfying. But there's quite a bit more to 4e than stacking +x, +y, +z to damage or whatever.

    It's true there are unexplored design frontiers of 4e. Powers that make use of counters never existed, for example, but there's no reason they couldn't.

    But I think, really, to some extent we're comparing apples and oranges. 4e is cooperative, MtG is competitive. 4e is designed with a view to making sure everyone has a fair chance to participate. A lot of effort went in to ensuring there are no crazy out-of-control game-breaking Pun-Pun type combos, and PCs of the same level are all generally within a certain margin of effectiveness in their roles. At the same time, the goal of 4e encounters is very simple: get the enemies down to 0hp as quickly as possible, keep your own side above 0hp as long as possible. The strategic fun is all about how you do that as a team, and what happens before you get there.

    MtG is designed with a view to making sure players can sometimes win, sometimes lose, and that they can get better at winning by building a smarter deck. It's totally okay for there to be some super-powerful combos, as long as they can sometimes be countered or disrupted. A player should be able to completely dominate sometimes, as long as the opponent can feel like it's fair, like if he got better at the game, or had a better deck, or even slightly better luck of the draw, he might have had a shot. (Sorry if I'm talking out my buttinski here\u2014I have only a little experience with MtG, and more with 4e.)

    So, I mean, I think it would be helpful to evaluate games partly with a view to those sorts of considerations, and whether their use of power combos contributed effectively to an overall view of design and gameplay.
