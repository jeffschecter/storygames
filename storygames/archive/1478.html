    Jack, you're agreeing with something that Thomas didn't say.

    Let's start with definitions.

    The Forge provisional glossary defines the "Reward System" thus:

    a) The personal and social gratification derived from role-playing, a feature of Creative Agenda. (b) In-game changes, usually to a player-character, a feature of System and Character. (c) As a subset to (b), improvement to one or more of the character's Components. Typically, the term refers to how (a) is facilitated by (b).

    You may not like this definition, but my guess is that this is where Adam is coming from.

    First and foremost, the reward system is founded in the gratification of play. To facilitate this feeling, most games allow for players to earn the ability to make changes to the game when they accomplish things in the game that are in accordance with the creative agenda. Usually, this involves changes to the PC (commonly known as 'experience') but it doesn't have to. For example, in Ray Winninger's Underground, players can earn the right to improve the standard of living (measured in a half dozen different ways) of the community where the PC's live. This is a good reward mechanism because RWU is intended to be a simulationist game about social problems.

    Some games don't have reward systems. Traveller (the old old black box version) didn't have a strong reward mechanism. Gamma World first edition had one but it was very, very slow. It's no coincidence that when new editions were made of these games, they were given reward systems.

    If your game doesn't have a reward system, then it is missing a significant tool that games use to reinforce the creative agenda. If you aren't using that tool, or replacing it with something at least as effective, then yes, I can see how the game would have problems.

    To Jack: No, games don't need reward systems. Baron Munchausen doesn't. Pantheon doesn't. They have rewards, but those aren't reward systems because they don't feed back into the game world (Shared Imagined Space). It should be noted, however, that both of these games are designed to be played in a single short session (an hour or two) and there's no character continuity whatsoever; each game is totally separate from the one before. They don't fall flat because they have a different mechanism, a "winning system" if you will, to replace it.

    When I was a child, I used to play with my grandmother's dominoes. I'd build buildings out of them, knock them down, slide them around... she didn't like that at all. She wasn't so much worried about what happened to them... dominoes are easily replaced... but she didn't consider it to be a "game" and it wasn't... I had reduced the dominoes to a toy. She called this activity "dummy-noes" and refused to have anything to do with it. There was really no room for interaction or challenge in the way I was playing. I haven't read FateClock, but I suspect that may be what is going on.

    You both are, of course, permitted to disagree with my opinion. I'm not trying to convince either of you; I'm merely trying to amplify Adam's comment about reward systems by explaining my own understanding of the topic. I'm sure other people will have a different take on what Adam meant.

    To distinguish between "game" & "toy", I like Greg Costikyan's defintions in his excellent 1994 essay, "I Have No Words and I Must Design". In that, he says that games have goals, toys do not.
    I Have No Words and I Must DesignAccording to Will Wright, his Sim City is not a game at all, but a toy. Wright offers a ball as an illuminating comparison: It offers many interesting behaviors, which you may explore. You can bounce it, twirl it, throw it, dribble it. And, if you wish, you may use it in a game: soccer, or basketball, or whatever. But the game is not intrinsic in the toy; it is a set of player-defined objectives overlaid on the toy.
    The reward system, as I understand it, is a way of directing goals & objectives to the players so that the rest of the rules (the 'toy' part) can be used in a game-like fashion, and thus, maintain longterm interest.
    I Have No Words and I Must Design"But what about roleplaying games?" [...] they have goals; lots of them, you get to pick. Rack up the old experience points. Or fulfill the quest your friendly GM has just inflicted on you. Or rebuild the Imperium and stave off civilization's final collapse. Or strive toward spiritual perfection. Whatever.

    If, for some reason, your player characters don't have a goal, they'll find one right quick. Otherwise, they'll have nothing better to do but sit around the tavern and grouse about how boring the game is. Until you get pissed off and have a bunch of orcs show up and try to beat their heads in.

    Jack: Traveller was an excellent game. I played a lot of it, too, and long after other games had surpassed it in sophistication.

    It is fairly easy to let a reward system creep into Traveller. The way it happened in our game was in terms of money, power, and scope. Our characters started off as a "troubleshooter" team, taking contracts with various employers to handle problems that their internal elements were not prepared for. Later, we acquired our own ship and started on a career as privateers in a nasty little war on the outskirts of the Empire. Gradually we were able to amass quite a fortune by selling prize ships at a discount to our patrons, and decided that it was time to settle down. We went to a number of oppressively governed planets and smuggled out a few hundred like-minded families, and then brought them out to an uninhabited world on the edge of civilization to found a small colony. Gradually the colony grew, but word eventually got out about where we were and the planets our colonists had come from banded together to send a warfleet to forcibly return their citizens. Diplomatic and military adventures ensued, which eventually resulted in getting our colony recognized as a member world of the Empire, and as such protected from the kind of unsubtle conquest our enemies were pursuing. At that point we declared victory and started something else.

    None of the changes that happened to our characters were bought with experience points, but there very definitely was a reward process going on - you could see it on the character sheet as "Cr" started to be replaced by "KCr" and then "MCr".

    God, I loved that game.

    Anyways, my point is that when there isn't a reward mechanic built into a game, players will very often find one. In a game where wealth and gear are important definitions of who a character is and what he can do (like in Trav) the accumulation of wealth and gear will usually become a reward mechanic.

    It might be helpful for people to consider my distinction between reward system and reward mechanic. I did introduce these terms to internet discourse about role-playing, so I might as well weigh in.

    The former is a reality - a feature of play. Without it, people don't continue to play. It's about people, communication, pleasure, and stuff like that. You tell me what you and the others did when it was clear to everyone that you wanted to keep playing, or to play again, and there it is.

    The latter is a ritualized procedure of some kind, or in plain language, a rule. Rules like this are only reward mechanics if they express or reinforce the reward system.

    Seems to me that keeping these concepts distinct solves any of the questions raised so far. No, Tomas, you don't need reward mechanics. I certainly never said you did. It also seems to me, though, that identifying their lack as somehow purer than their presence is uncritical romanticism.

    Adam's saying that a particular design doesn't have reward mechanics - and for him, that means the reward system is less accessible, or in this case wholly inaccessible. That's a fair point, I think. If you don't have reward mechanics which match the intended reward systems, you've forced anyone who's not exactly in tune with you, personally, to guess. A lot of people aren't going to have the time or inclination to guess.

    If that's what you want, then that's OK too. I see nothing worth debating.
