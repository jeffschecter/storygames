    For me, the two really useful points to take away from this chapter are:

    1. Reincorporation is really satisfying. For example, if the players find a crystal scroll at the start of the game, it's really satisfying if that scroll solves the problem at the end of the game. It doesn't matter whether it makes sense or not (well, not much).

    2. Quiet bits (platforms) are really useful in a story. There's a temptation to make stories big and full of conflict (Bam! Conflict. Bam! Another conflict. etc etc). But the quiet bits, where nothing much seems to happen but you establish lots of colour, are important too.

    Graham

    That's very true. Interesting.

    In a way, foreshadowing is a special case of reincorporation. With foreshadowing, you're setting up something you know will be reincorporated later ("You see a black rook overhead, which you recognise as an omen the King will die.").

    I think reincorporation is slightly wider. It gives you the power to set up something unspecific ("You see a black rook overhead, which you recognise as an omen.") and then reincorporate it later in a satisfying way ("As the King dies, the rook comes to perch by him, as if his death was fated."). Even though when you introduced the rook, you didn't know quite how you'd reincorporate it.

    In the Risus Companion, breaking routines is used as a scenario design technique. You split up a routine into several sub-routines, and then break every single one of them.I don't remember the exact example, but the principle goes something like this:

    Routine:
    Order pizza

    Sub-routines:
    Go to phone. BUT on the way to the phone, you're attacked by vampire buffalo.
    Call pizza parlor. BUT there's a mess-up, and your call is transferred to the local Mafia branch instead.
    Speak to pizza parlor people and order pizza. BUT they try to sell you their Daily Special, which requires you to answer a quiz and take a quick time travel trip.
    Wait for pizza . BUT while you wait, there's shooting in the neighboring flat, and you have to find out who did it.
    Pay for pizza. BUT you don't have any money, and the pizza guy tries to take your computer as payment instead.
    Eat pizza. BUT it starts talking to you.
