\n What is it, if anything, that would attract you to a unique, deep setting game?

By "deep" I mean a game whose setting has a lot of back story, historical and cultural detail, and all of this detail should be at least minimally known and/or understood by the players and GM. The depth has to do with the amount of published detail that you are expected to "account" for in some fashion.

By "unique" I mean the setting is unique to that game, with no outside references or other media to refer to except those that might come after the game is released (e.g. Dragonlance novels). It may be reminiscent of something else, but it is different enough that your knowledge of other settings may be more of a hindrance than a help. A setting is more unique, I would argue, if there are specific rule mechanics that are tied into the setting, such as non-generic magic or skill systems, or cultural elements such as character classes.

(Forgive me if there are other, customary terms around here for these concepts. I haven't been involved with Story Games for a long time.)

Classic examples of unique, deep settings, I think, would be Glorantha (Rune/Heroquest), Talislanta, World of Darkness, Planescape, Castle Falkenstein. More recent examples might be Eclipse Phase, Numenera, Ashen Stars.

Unique, deep settings have been the rule in the past and probably still are for the majority of role-players. But they have a lot of challenges. Just the time commitment of actually reading all the material can be a challenge. Also, the more detailed and unique the setting, the more likely there will be at least one element that you and/or some of the other players in your group don't enjoy. Also, let's face it, as a game designer there is always the chance that your much beloved setting, which you have lashed like an anchor to the raft of your basic rules-set, will sink in the face of potential customer apathy, never to be seen again. The halls of RPGGeek are littered with such artifacts.

What makes you want to play such a game? What makes you avoid it? What are their advantages and disadvantages?

Extra credit question: As an RPG designer (both potential or actual), what criteria would you use to decide that your unique, deep setting has something special to offer, and is worth committing to paper/pdf and trying to sell to others?

    What is it, if anything, that would attract you to a unique, deep setting game?
    \nDiscovery.

    If the players are given objectives which reward delving into the GM's setting, the cycle of GM: "I made up a cool thing!" -> player: "I discovered a cool thing!" is fantastic. It's one of my favorite things in roleplaying.

    Note, though, that "there is a deep setting" is not the most important part of this equation. A discovery game can survive a setting that isn't quite deep enough a lot more easily than it can survive a player agenda that isn't pointed at the setting. But if the basic equation is in place, then I find deep settings rewarding in ways that less deep settings are not.

    - secrets can be teased
    - gleaned bits of knowledge can be related to each other
    - puzzles can be assembled
    - "Aha!" moments
    - feeling that one's actions have consequence, being woven into a tapestry that persists beyond this moment
    - feeling of realism (if you're into that)
    - feeling that the setting is alive and active (does some of GM's job for them!)
    - thoroughly-established environment for player action
    - characters deeply attached to their world, with emergent matters of interest beyond specific plot points

    Many of those things could be true in games with barely any setting at all. But in my experience, deep setting games are the best on most of these fronts.

    P.S. I just realized, I led off with the assumption that the GM created the setting. So, I'll clarify: in situations where the GM bought the setting, the GM's fun is not in showing off "here's what I made" but rather "here's how I used the stuff others made". So a good deep setting for sale had better be full of ways for the GM to make it their own.

    P.P.S. Re: "unique setting" -- discovery is best when it feels new (as opposed to "I've done this before").

    \n The same thing that makes me play any other game: good game design. A fun, awesome game to play. If your awesome game happens to also have a detailed setting attached to it, the quality of the game increases the odds that I will take your setting seriously and consider using it. This will be doubly true when it turns out that the excellent game you have made is excellent in part because it is intricately tied to the things that make your setting interesting and unique.

    If your game is not interesting to play, and does not have mechanics and interactions and broadly-defined 'system' that works well and appeals to me, then it's not going to matter at all what setting is like -- I'm not going to play the game. That's true whether the setting is deep and unique or flat and derivative.

    If the setting turns out to be great in of itself, then I might buy the game and then adapt the setting to some other set of rules, but I probably won't play it -- at least not past the point where I've figured out if I like the game you've used to implement it.

    As to the original question, I have always been attracted to these deep settings, but in actual play, it's best when I don't get caught up in most of the detail.     \nI don't remember ever reading those threads! There have been some sandbox threads though, I learned about RuneQuest in one of them. For big, detailed settings, I have two things I think are important and I've probably posted all this stuff at some point before:

    One is a conceptual framework that governs the whole setting, which has nothing to do with aesthetic (and specifically visual aesthetic) but has everything to do with situation and events. Like playing Planescape but keeping the theme of great power politics always in mind--so everything gets filtered through that lens of meaning. This is actually my go-to concept for Planescape style fantasy, and probably the reason why I immediately recognized Dark Heart of the Dreamer as the post-modern immigrant diaspora experience turned into a fantasy game where you play characters sort of like Fafhrd and the Grey Mouser.

    Two is actual details that can be used in play. If a book's description of villain is "he is very smart" I don't care, give me some actual schemes of his that are smart. Visual descriptions are fine and everything, but a setting really needs to be situations that the PCs can get into so that you can do the role-playing thing and not the book club discussion group thing (assuming you showed up to a role-playing game).

    This is another important thing: Knowledge about a detailed setting, to me, is the same as knowledge about history (and, like Ken Hite is wont to say, the real world is the most detailed setting you can play in). If you use your own knowledge to invalidate the contributions to the game made by other players, it doesn't matter that they're not living up to your sense of verisimilitude, you're a dick. If you use your knowledge to make the game better, by giving other players details they can use and ignoring things that only you care are wrong, then you are a good player! So if you are writing a detailed setting, I think you should keep that in mind and have your setting help out with that.

    As a designer, I have two considerations for everything: How much fun and how much money. I have a limited amount of time and effort I can spend on projects that don't give me some of both.

    Classic examples of unique, deep settings, I think, would be Glorantha (Rune/Heroquest), Talislanta, World of Darkness, Planescape, Castle Falkenstein. More recent examples might be Eclipse Phase, Numenera, Ashen Stars.

    Unique, deep settings have been the rule in the past and probably still are for the majority of role-players. But they have a lot of challenges. Just the time commitment of actually reading all the material can be a challenge. Also, the more detailed and unique the setting, the more likely there will be at least one element that you and/or some of the other players in your group don't enjoy. Also, let's face it, as a game designer there is always the chance that your much beloved setting, which you have lashed like an anchor to the raft of your basic rules-set, will sink in the face of potential customer apathy, never to be seen again. The halls of RPGGeek are littered with such artifacts.

    What makes you want to play such a game? What makes you avoid it? What are their advantages and disadvantages?

    Extra credit question: As an RPG designer (both potential or actual), what criteria would you use to decide that your unique, deep setting has something special to offer, and is worth committing to paper/pdf and trying to sell to others?
    \nThe key for me with any deep setting is whether it is interesting enough in itself to learn about. Regardless of whether the setting is from real life, a comic, a television series, or an RPG - the key question is whether it is fun to learn about. For example, if a game is set in 1630s London, and I have to read a bunch about 1630s London, then I'm probably fine because that is material that I enjoy reading. Likewise, if a game is set in media that I enjoy, like Star Trek - then I'm probably fine - because I'm happy to re-watch some Star Trek episodes. If it is set in media that I don't enjoy, then I would tend to avoid it.

    For example, I've been a fan of Harn as a deep setting, because I was interested in the setting as written. I've considered Castle Falkenstein as well, though not the other examples you listed.

    The obvious disadvantage is that there is a higher barrier to entry. The advantage is that one has more meaningful objects to create with.

    There is a common criticism of deep settings that one is consuming the settings instead of creating. So, for example, if someone writes a novel set in real established history, it is less creative than writing a fantasy novel where the author makes everything up. I think this is completely off base. If I am running a game set in history, then we are still creating - the creation adds onto the already established details like what people wear, what people eat, and so forth. Likewise, if I set my game in Harn, we are concentrating on more subtle details - like how this individual worships Agrik, rather than what the religion is.

    \n There's an aspect of Castle Falkenstein (and also of Blue Rose -- and Star Trek, come to think of it) that makes me want to run it a particular way, although I've never actually done so (or run any of these three)). That is that I want to look at some of the assumptions and undercut them.

    In Castle Falkenstein, it occurred to me that we're getting pretty much one guy's viewpoint, and he's a guy from our world who fell in with one particular crowd of heavy hitters. What if we look at the world from the point of view of someone (I was thinking a woman, but the gender swap isn't necessary) who wound up on the streets, and not with the cool folks? With Blue Rose, I was interested in the neighboring country that's a theocracy and looked on with such condescension by the good guy country -- which also happens to have better land and better luck, or in a campaign about the less than shiny people who have to keep the country running, the ones who do the dirty work so the rest of the good guy country can pretend it doesn't exist.

    This isn't the only thing that draws me to settings. I'd be fine with a shiny game of Castle Falkenstein or Blue Rose. I loved exploring Glorantha when a friend who'd been gaming for at least as long as there had been a published version of the world agreed to run HeroQuest. I've had a couple of amazing convention games of Pendragon.

    But, there is definitely an attraction for me of "Okay, so what's the part we're not being told?" or, for something based on a show like Star Trek, "What if the script writers didn't insist on certain types of stupidity or amnesia (like things that are useful in one episode and then are never dealt with again)?" The latter is also "How _we'd_ do it better", which is fun.
