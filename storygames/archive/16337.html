\n So a lot of us went to Solmukohta in Finland all in order to get horrendously sick and discuss role-playing theory with like-minded, similarly obsessed people from around the world. It was mostly a larp conference, but a strong sympathy for tabletop (specifically indie tabletop) could be discerned if you talked to anybody.

The roll call from Story Games was:

Myself
Emily Care Boss
Epidiah Ravachol
Rafu (Raffaele Manzo)
Alex Fradera
Jason Morningstar (+ Autumn Winters)
John H. Kim
Matthijs Holter
Nathan Hook
(Anyone else I'm missing?)

There were several talks and discussions that resonated specifically with issues of game design that are in dialog with our own, and this thread can serve as a site of our collective brain dump.

In no particular order, thoughts I had concerned...

... far-right, militaristic tabletop RPGs (Price of Freedom, RECON)
... establishment of a mid-level theory of tabletop RPGs, as Eirik Fatland has done for larp (and David Bordwell has done for film, Brian McHale for literature, Ian Bogost for games, etc.)
... explicit technical game instructions (the new American way post-Apocalypse World) vs. highly functional social consensus (the Nordic freeform way)
... establishing a regular forum to run Danish-style games in the United States, linked up with a regular convention that many of us already attend.

    \n I'm interested in making more explicit the minimal conditions for organising a short room larp.

    My motivation is that I came away wanting to make this happen off my own back, and that seems doable to me in a way I hadn't considered before. At the same time, I recognise that the medium contains some pitfalls that aren't found in tabletop (for instance, GM hesitation about how to rule a decision is far more problematic in a real-time multiplayer context). Until very recently I'd always conceived of larp as a medium that demanded expertise and experience to work at all. Now I wonder if the bar is somewhat lower than that, given a group who are not paying to play but just keen to squeeze fun out of it.

    It would be really useful if the larp community had placed a lens over their games to give attention to ease of running, especially for those more familiar with tabletop, and framed some guidance for new entrants. Is freeform far easier than plotted? Does heavy use of NPCs demand greater play fluency than one might expect? That sort of thing.

    For what it's worth, I think that I could now run Playing with Intent within my social network (granted that it contains a lot of improvisers).

    \n Such a rule of thumb isn't necessary, but it does help. Especially in the case of repeatable mini-larps with a central story arc. The running of those benefits a lot from having played the game, from knowing the script thoroughly, and from having some idea what various things players may end up doing during it.

    1942: The Police is an excellent example of such, and I envy those who got to play it under Sergey's expert guidance. I've co-run it twice with very good GMs, but never played it myself. In contrast, my Tribunal has intentionally been written so that it does need much familiarity, and can be picked up even by people with no larp experience. While dealing with very similar topics, the two have almost totally different story-construction systems (and thus produce quite dissimilar experiences), and that's why their level of required active game-mastering (and thus familiarity with the game) are very different.

    \nI think there's one very clear methodological/tehnical reason for this: In a tabletop session all the players and the GM are present all the time, and everyone here's what other people are saying and how they're constructing the situation/world/characters/mood/etc. But a larp can be distributed over a huge area of land and time, with some players never even meeting each other. So if you're looking for a coherent world, this is the way you have to approach it.

    Simple problems can arise for example, when some players set a house on fire. How can you communicate this to other players in a smooth way? Or somebody deciding that they're living in a monotheistic culture and somebody else simultaneously deciding that it's actually polytheistic or atheistic.

    This topic, the construction of diegesis (or even diegeses), is talked about quite a bit, especially in some of the texts by Markus Montola and Jaakko Stenros.


    Another very important reason is that designing the world is often part of designing the larp in the first place. As with tabletops. To leave it up to the players would often be fairly pointless.


    This is not to say this hasn't been experimented with, by setting larps in abstract environments or directly telling the players to create the world with the game masters, and other experiments. I think these topics are mentioned in States of Play in the articles talking about the Valve larp and the Palestinian larp project

    \n

GREAT... but don't appropriate their terms thinking you've found a shortcut.       Posted By: Alex FFor those in the know, is is a worthwhile thing to consider? Or would such an experience be too "tip of the iceberg" to be worth the investment (given there will be other cool things to do)?
      Check the RPG.net LARP forums for that area (there's a sticky thread for game searching). Alternately, you can check Shade's LARP List, but it's currency is patchy.

      Generally, however, US (non-convention) LARPs are long-running, whether boffer or parlor--that is: full action with or without additional systems, or system simulated, including freeform 'systems'. Finding a one-off outside of a convention could be quite tricky.

      That said, contact some of the long-running game hosts and see if they could let you participate in an NPC capacity, or as a "cameo" character. Pitch yourself well (i.e., a scholar from the Land of Jeepform, wanting to learn the "US Way") and you probably won't be charged to play one or two events--charges usually go toward paperwork and maintenance of character data, and you shouldn't require that. Note that most parlor LARPs are one or two evenings a week (if not 24/7), while boffer LARPs tend to run Friday night to Sunday afternoon. The latter might charge you a nominal fee no matter what, as they might have venue charges to offset.

      Go for it, I say! Sure, there's tons of stuff to do in Chicago... but aren't you a Serious LARPer\xae?!?

      Posted By: lizziestarkI think Emily's assessment of the difference between Nordic scenarios and Nordic larp is spot on -- to me they seem like two ends of a sliding spectrum (or at least, this was how Juhana Petterson explained it to me) -- whereas stateside, at least, I'd become accustomed to thinking of tabletop in opposition to larp. Adding to the confusion is this tradition of physically playing out scenes during "tabletop" games, which for me further blurs the line between tabletop and larp, making freeform scenarios seem like parlor larps.
      \nI'd say tabletop (saying "My character does this") and larp (doing it) are two methods you can use while roleplaying, but not by far the only ones. Using miniatures to represent complex tactical situations would be a third fairly common one in tabletop, and the groups I play in often use small cards with NPC's faces to represent complex social situations. In my experimental indie RPG The Hand of Gulliver the Man-Mountain (PDF) the players use their fingers to represent their characters and the back of the GM's hand is the location.

      That's the scale in tabletops. And sometimes you get stuff mixed up, and when people are talking out of character, you assume it's in character or they want to take something back later and you can retcon or fastforward etc.

      In my experience, larps are usually much more rigid in that the aim is to play your character from beginning to end with no breaks and no changes in playing style and never going out of character (unless someone's in real danger or something). This has also been experimented on, of course, but I'd say a bit less than with tabletops, since it can be very disruptive. One great thing I remember was the three-day larp Hamlet in 2003 in Stockholm, where the ordinary playing would stop every now and then, and all the characters would gather around one NPC/actor who would perform one of the famous monologues. This would represent each character's inner monologue, so we were essentially watching our own thoughts being performed at us. After the monologue the normal playing would resume, but some characters might be changed by their interpretation of the monologue.

      Posted By: catty_bigAlso, what's the indie scene like in Nordic countries? Specifically, how do the more trad gamers react to it?
      \nIf you're specifically referring to gamers who only (or almost only) play Forge games and subscribe to that philosophy, I'd say it's very small. There's a few actives in Finland, but not many.

      However, all games published in Finland (not sure of the other Nordic countries) would be indie in some sense. Many are self-published or by small publishers, most are stand-alones with no supplements, and typically each has their own game mechanics designed for that particular game.

      That said, I think most tabletop games played over here are based on D&D, GURPS, WHFRP, WoD and other big American games. It's difficult to guess how the majority of gamers (those who don't discuss their games on forums, magazines or cons) play, but it seems like most of the active ones are heavily influenced by ideas from Nordic Larp, Forge/US indie, jeepform, OSR, and random sources (art theory, TED talks, AR, web apps, performance art...) even when basing their campaign on more traditional sources. (Sometimes the influx of new ideas is, of course, preceded by misunderstandings, wars between factions and hating all the new stuff.)

      Posted By: Emily CareIt was a revelation to find out the history of Fastaval scenarios wrt numbers of players. Initially (or earlier) they were written for 4 players. They they went up to 5, now commonly include 6.
      \nDuring the 90ies, the organisers requested that scenarios had 6 player characters. Due to the tradition of having one group per room and a typical Fastaval location offering at most 30 rooms suitable for play, having more players per group meant having more people playing. This strict requirement lead to the concept of the 6th PC which did not fit in the fiction. The requirement of 6 players per group has long since been abandoned. In more recent years, the expectation that a session would play for 6 hours has also been abandoned, opening for shorter and longer games. A typical Fastaval game today is for 4 players, one GM and plays for 4 hours. If it is meaningful at all to talk about such thing as a typical Fastaval game.

      \n Freeform is rather nebulous in meaning. Just like the creative agenda terms are. Some people define them a specific way. Others use them to mean something similar but different.

      Usually freeform means that a game is extremely light on structured mechanics. No hit points. No objects to trade. No structured jeepform techniques like monologue and bird-in-ear. Lots of talking in character.

      What else it means depends on who is saying the word to whom.

      Sometimes it means the GMs don't do much after the game starts. Other times it means that conflict is resolved by GM fiat, so the GMs actually have a lot to do.

      Sometimes it means that there's a super duper simple conflict resolution mechanic that resolves anything that can't be resolved by just talking. Like one round of rock-paper-scissors and that's it.

      Sometimes it means that players can set scenes and move the action wherever they want. Sometimes it means that the game is closed within a set location so this doesn't happen. Sometimes it means GMs have to be called over to set a scene that's different from the default location.

      It means different things to different groups. I'm pretty sure I haven't listed all the differences, but that's a fair chunk of them from the top of my head.

      It's easier to identify things that are not freeform:

      Pretty much everything about one of the 1000+ player fantasy live combat larps in Europe is not freeform. High mapping of player action to character action. High mapping of physical space to game world space. Low PC creative input outside the limits of being their character and doing what the character would do (you can't, for example, invent a new country off the top of your head just by talking about it - setting documentation of done by the GMs). Very structured mechanics interactions - if you touch someone with a foam sword that does hit point damage to their character, for example. High mapping of costume cues to character information - if someone has a mouse half-mask on their face and fur glued to the back of their hands, you can assume they are from the race of mouse people - if they don't have any indicators like that, you can assume they aren't.
