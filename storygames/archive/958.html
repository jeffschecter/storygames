The Vaxalon Principle: The more creativity an activity requires...

Vaxalon
July 2006 edited July 2006 in Play Advice
"The more creativity an activity requires, the more creativity its participants will display when they engage in it."

This may seem a little tautologous, but it's not...

So here's how I came to this conclusion:

Last Valentine's day, I decided to write a sonnet for my wife... a Spenserian sonnet, no less (they're more rigorous than Shakespearean sonnets). The rhyme scheme is rigid, the meter is ironclad. They are NOT easy to write. I worked on it all month.

It came out GREAT. The best poem I ever wrote, by far. Everything else was just doggerel. The enforced rigor required me to stretch my abilities further, and the work came out better in the long run. Could I have done as good with a free verse poem? Sure... but the medium doesn't REQUIRE as much, so it doesn't push me to stretch.

I looked at RPG's in this light.

The more requirements you put on contributions to the SIS, the harder it is to achieve, but the better the results when you fulfill it.

    I don't entirely agree with this.

    I think constraints are good up to a point. Sonnets are a good example: there's just enough constraint to fire your creativity.

    But too many constraints stop you being creative. Let's say I ask you to write a sonnet including the words "bear", "rose", "dice", "conference", "office", "document", set in Renaissance Italy with one central female character, who's in love with a man who's unattainable because of a difference in social status. You might do it, but it won't be very creative. It'll be an exercise in getting it to fit.

    (On this note, doyce, were your 56 words actually creative? Don't get me wrong, I'm impressed, but did the constraints actually fire your creativity? It sounds, to me, like an exercise in getting in to fit.)

    In other words, I don't agree that "the more requirements you put on contributions to the SIS...the better the results when you fulfill it". I agree that some requirements will give better results; but I don't think the results then get better as you add more requirements.

    Graham

    Might be a hard title to claim, Fred, when people have been expounding this theory for ages in terms of RPGs. I think I recal Erick Wujcik writing an article for Dragon magazine or somesuch in the 80's in which he talked about the positive and negative effects of constraints and where the line should be drawn (as a response to Amber). That's just the earliest I can recall. I'm sure that people were discussing it right out of the gate, with ideas like whether or not players should be allowed to affect combat rolls by using creative tactics that are not suggested by the text ("look, more freedom = more fun!")

    The notion that constraint is important is a response to the opposite notion that freedom is better for RPGs - which is how freeform got started, when taken to it's logical extent ("I don't need Rooolz to play!")

    The following is a link to a post with more of my own thoughts about this: http://www.indie-rpgs.com/forum/index.php?topic=2450.msg23932#msg23932

    Anyhow, I think that which level of constraint works to be most entertaining for a particular individual is, well, pretty individual. Hence why there are so many levels ranging from Complete Freeform (all elements including setting are created on the spot) to "Your Love For Me is Way Outta Line" level constraint (where one player has to play a dead sea-sponge on display in an office who simply does running commentary on play with small mechanical effects). All are enjoyable to somebody.

    Further, it also comes down to what particular sorts of constraints people like. Some people can't stand the idea of social mechanics, for instance, because they feel that it constrains their character responses in terms of things that they prefer be free. While those same people might at the same time think that combat should not be allowed at all in their game (I'm thinking of some LARPS, as my example). So you can't simply say "I like constraint" or "I like freedom" you have to indicate where you like freedom and constraint.

    And, when all is said and done, the set of constraints that you have in play, which determine how each player can add to the SIS is, well, The Lumpley Principle. So this makes a good correllary. System is the constraints on how the SIS is formed. The selection of constraints has effects on how enjoyable play is. The latter part could also be labeled Creative Agenda.

    Mike
