\n I learned...

- playing Storming the Wizard's Tower, I learned that a) it's a Gamist game, since the point of play was chiefly to beat bad guys - I didn't actually know that we were going on a good ol' dungeon crawl: since I had relationship traits and stuff, I assumed things. Granted, we chewed the scenery so much that we kept a running tally of truly salient quotes ("The concerns of lesser men are as but feathers to their superiors", f'rinstance), but yeah - totally weirdly, characterization and all that were a big part of play, but only sort of supported by the game mechanics. Granted, Storming just drips Color, and pushes GMs to design settings that are full of the stuff, but strictly speaking, that's a sort of secondary agenda or something.
b) I can have fun with Gamist games - as somebody who had always thought that only something like Donjon could be sufficiently co-creative to make me happy Stepping On Up, I was really surprised to see a well-executed, fun, colorful Gamist game take place, with me a part of it! That's not to say there aren't tweaks and suggestions I'd make (poor wizards!), but the core mechanic was really solid (and not just 'cause I, a warrior, threw double handfuls of dice all the time ^_^)
c) It's likely one may have no idea how a game is going to "look" until you've sat through a sufficient period of play. The phenomenon of "the game text feels unfinished" seems to be a pretty sweet thing to have, actually, as it shows a certain elegance, perhaps (or just bad editing, if it's Hero Wars ^_^) in the rules.

- playtesting my game La Familia (click here for rules), I learned that it's possible for my design to support short-play (3.5 hours, all told), and smaller groups (3 people), but this seems to almost automatically push aside certain parts of play. Only one protagonist's Grudges saw any screen time, some implied non-tagonists never got so much as a cameo (the Bishop of Milan, for one), and Clan Treasures, those grand and mighty assets of the major families, only saw screen time as props, not actually useful devices.
I was extremely pleased that the core mechanic worked as beautifully as I could have hoped - it was both a group experience, and a meaningful one, to come up with Complications for each conflict roll, and said Complications drove play in new directions, fueled by communal creativity, as I'd hoped. Shyeah!
It occurs to me that Pips (each Pip lets you modify one die result by one, up or down) may actually be redundant, or at least need to be more carefully used - we've all probably seen the ugliness that comes from a tiny pool of character traits, when one or two traits are suddenly useful for bloody everything. I'm looking at Buddha's use of his "Scheming" trait, although it might've been Joe's, actually. Anyway, since Clan Treasures grant Pips, but they are very story-ful and can be taken away, it occurs to me that they really need to overshadow trait-Pips, so as to keep the pot stirring. Again, I'd have to actually run a game that included Clan Treasures more in order to be certain.
So - a multi-session storyline, more people at once, etc., will be what I look for next. With any luck, I can drum up some interest at the Strat AND break the Curse of the One-Shot, at least for a little bit.

And... go!

    \n I finally got to play Montsegur. I learned how making a few procedural things really rigidly set in stone, and leaving everything else to the players, can make a really fantastic diceless role-playing experience. It reminded me of Emily's jeepy Under My Skin, except the direction for scenes was coming from the rules.

    I played Pandemic, too! It was my first cooperative board game experience and it rocked. I have always wanted to play a cooperative board game and I suspected that some must exist somewhere. It shook my world a bit. I want to write RPGs with strategic board game components now.

    I also learned that I don't see these cool people nearly enough. An hour travel isn't too much, I think. Trick is finding time to hang out with some of them more often. (Dan Eison, dude, you live in my neighborhood, so we must get our game on now.)

    \n Although I had an awesome time at both games I played in, the most food for thought from Ice Station Nerdly was actually from a conversation I had afterwards.

    I played Montsegur back at Dreamation in January. Raimond was my protagonist, and I wasn't really sure what to do with him for the first half of the game or so. This often happens when I play characters with high levels of political/social power who don't really want to leave their throne room, and in games without in-your-face opposition. So, after the game at Nerdly, I asked Dan what Raimond's player did with their scenes, especially in the early game. "Well, mostly Raimond indulged his worst instincts and made a lot of trouble for himself and everyone else." So obvious, and yet, not really something I'd considered. In games with a tragic bent like Montsegur, I tend to treat my characters as charges for me to guide and protect as best as I can through the disasters to come. Next time, however, if I'm not sure what to do I'll try having my character massively screw up.
