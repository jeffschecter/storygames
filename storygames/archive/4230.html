\n In this thread, Matthijs asked the various game designers around here if they expected people to play their games more than once. I'd like to ask a related question, but from a different perspective and posed it to players of said games. But first a little background...

Back in early February my group and I sat down to play Polaris. One of our number wasn't able to make it for our regular PTA game that week and it corresponded perfectly to the four players needed to play Polaris. What unfolded over the course of two sessions (yes we broke the rules!) was a story of family, revenge and tragedy, as the sons and daughter of Al Shain destroyed themselves under the subtle machinations of their fellow Knight Al Athfar. It was full of emotion and revelation as the protagonists uncovered not only the mystery of sins committed by their patriarch, but also each other. Needless to say it all ended in a Jacobean bloodbath, with which we were all very satisfied.

I consider it the most enjoyable role-playing experience I've ever had, to the point that at the time I said to myself and then the rest of the group, "this is why game."

However, the interesting thing is this: all the participants in that game, including myself, have all expressed that they have no desired to play Polaris again. Not because the subject matter was too disturbing or anything like that -- to the contrary. We simply feel that first story was so awesomely epic that we wouldn't be able to help but compare any further play to it.

I personally have a desire to see how the Zeal/Weariness, Novice to Veteran progression plays out as per the rules, but I'm overcome by the sentiments I described above.

Anyone else every have a similar problem with their favorite games?

    \n Huge problems with games where the setting is the game, like Kult. Once the great secrets are revealed, why go back to it? Sure, Kult asks questions rather than gives answers, and you can adjust the answers and have a different setting and a new game experience, but there's still an aversion to go there.

    Human drama, on the other hand, never gets old. I can't imagine having this problem with PTA or Sorcerer, for example, because they are both toolkits for telling stories with certain common threads but in potentially completely different contexts and settings. Same with D&D.

    I don't know Polaris that well, but from what I gather it is very setting-strong (which is good). For me, I could see that leading to a "we've now told the Ultimate Story" syndrome, where some big questions have been answered and the setting used to its fullest. It doesn't mean it's exhausted, but it means that the participants may not be up to the challenge of trying to tell an even better one.

    ...cut to 2, 3, 5 years later:

    ANDY: "Damn, that Polaris game was a lot of fun. I've got some cool new ideas, and my blood is boiling again. Anyone want to throw down?"

    It's in the same way a director or actor, artist or musician says "I will never do X again", only to do it when the time is right.

    The time may be never right.

    But given time, it could be that, once enough time passes (and I'm not talking weeks, I'm talking years), you'll find yourself wanting to give it another shot.
