\n I'd like to try something out with you, the good people of Story Games. Inspired by a few things Remi has said about how he runs series creation in Primetime Adventures, I'm noodling over a setting-building sort of engine and I want to give its basic ideas a test drive.

Basically, I want to have anybody who wants to participate help to create and develop a setting, and use this thread to do so. In short form, this will be a guided riffing activity, with everybody riffing off of the ideas posted before them. The idea is that the setting that emerges will hopefully be something that folks would enjoy using as the backdrop for a relatively long-form game.

So if you'd like to participate, here's how it works:

1. Turns: You may not make two consecutive posts. Let somebody else go before you go again.
2. Quoting: Your post must quote a portion of a prior post, and then respond to it starting with either "Yes, and..." or "Yes, but..." As of Phase Two, you must quote two portions of two prior posts, with your contribution tying them together.
2a. Yes And... posts add details that push an idea or theme forward. If the grand vizier was scheming in the first post, your Yes And post may introduce his conspirators.
2b. Yes But... posts qualify or challenge an idea or theme put forward by a prior post. The Yes But to the grand vizier could be that he is horrified at the excesses of the Shah and seeks to replace him with a noble and just government.
3. Be concise: Posts should not be any longer than ten lines or so.
4. Cross Posts: Occasionally we'll get some cross-posts that are both addressing the same topic. If this happens to you and your posts hits the forum second (or third), it is your responsibility to either remove your post or edit it so it no longer conflicts. You might want to throw in an "Under Revision" flag at the top of it first so other players don't quote off of that post.
5. Phases: The game will proceed in phases. The first phase works exactly as described in points #1-4. Later phases will amend, modify, or add new wrinkles. I will occasionally post "END OF PHASE AT 4PM PST" or something. Get your posts in before that time and then I'll post the next phase's changes. Play then continues. (Disclosure: I will use this to fine-tune and poke at the procedure as we go.)

Again, just to belabor the point, the goal in this thread is NOT to tell a story. The goal in this thread is to elaborate a conflict-ridden situation in a conflict-ridden setting that is just aching for some protagonists to come and start knocking shit over. Think of it as an exercise in creating unbalanced situations.

What follows are three 'starter posts' to serve as examples and as seeds, and then I encourage anybody and everybody to jump in!

When human colonists came here fleeing the collapse of the Pax Galacti, they crash-landed their starcraft into the northern pole, where temperatures were closer to tolerable. Many years have past since then, and a rough civilization has developed along the coasts of the Polar Sea. These city-states and "empires" huddle close to the cool waters, trading and fighting amongst each other, and keeping their backs to the oppressive heat of the deserts to the south.

    The city-state of Tripoli is often recognized as the keystone of Hyperborean civilization -- especially by the citizens of Tripoli. Situated on a peninsula that juts out into the Polar Sea, Tripoli boasts many technological relics of planetfall, many of which are still operational. The most lucrative among them, however, is the canal across the peninsula, dug out by the last exhaust of a ship's plasma drive. The canal serves as a trading link between city-states on either side of the peninsula -- and drives all that trade directly through Tripoli.

    Unknown to the refugees at the north pole, another fleet of ships arrived in the Arel system after them. Weakened by war, disfigured by core breaches, the barbarians saw the colonists at the north pole and knew that, if they landed, they would be shunned or driven away or simply slaughtered. They turned, instead, to the rugged south pole. There, without the benefit of a large body of water and on rocky, bitter land, they founded their own colony... and told their children of the idle, fat, worthless people far to the north, who had all the blessings of the gods and refused to share. Thus was born the Myth of the North, and soon the Cult of the South grew powerful among them, plotting and planning, and learning the secrets of the vast desert that held them back from the riches of the North.

    \nYes, and...

    The Phreaks come out at Night...
    Hyperborea has very little remaining indigenous animal life, the majority being small, unobtrusive, herbivores that eke out a meager existence among the desert's sparse flora. The Phreaks are a major exception to this rule. The huge nocturnal creatures seem to be omnivorous, feasting on plants, animals, and carrion in equal measure. Phreaks resemble nothing so much as demonic cockroaches. Their leathery, blood red faces, are set on vaguely leonine heads atop long necks. Their round lamprey mouths are ringed with spidery pedipalps, which they use for feeding, and their multiple eyes look uncomfortably goatish. Phreaks can maneuver their long, squat bodies very rapidly on ten mulitisegmented chitinous legs, and can even rear up on their hindquarters to effect a centaurlike pose. They have always shunned human contact, but now the slow expansion of the colonies places ever stronger pressure on Phreak society. During the latest Night several incursions of Phreaks into the smaller towns have lead to bloodshed on both sides of the conflict.

    \nYes, and...

    At the height of the League War*, with Tripoli under seige from without and riven by dissent within, the then-ruling Doge, Vicente abu-Bakr al-Dhimmi, cultivated a special corps of agents to combat the agents of the rival religious factions. After the conflict, this corps was institutionalized as the Confraternity for Extraordinary Reconciliation. Reporting directly to the Doge rather than to the Council, this body's members are known formally as Confraternes Extraordinary, semiformally as Reconciliators, and informally as "the Doge's Dogs." These "Dogs" have for all practical purposes the powers of judge, jury, and executioner, and a wide mandate to investigate potential disorder in the many fractious, often religiously ghettoized districts of Tripoli itself and in the neighboring client states. It is their responsibility to maintain Tripoli's fragile peace against agents of the Catholic hierarchy, Anabaptist rabble-rousers, mysterious Wanderers, rampaging Phreaks, and the ordinary, ugly hatreds of the citizenry.

    * The carefully nondenominational name for the conflict preferred by Tripolitan authorities; it is called "the Tripolitan Crusade" by most Catholics and "the War of the Catholic League" by most Anabaptists.
