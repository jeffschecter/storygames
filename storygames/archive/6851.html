\n One of the things that bothers me about RPG discussions is that they usually focus on content rather than process and purpose. People talk about wanting to promote a certain tone or atmosphere or gathering tropes of a genre or dissecting the structure of certain kinds of stories. But all of that isn\u2019t very useful because the actual game at hand is defined by its processes and procedures which is one of the biggest misunderstandings about The Big Model, it\u2019s about *process* and not about content.

At Nerdly Beach Party Paul Tevis and Ryan Maclin came up with this idea of Totemic Gaming. The idea being that you knew you were playing a game because the game book was on the table, even though the ACTUAL procedures of play differed vastly from those presented in the game text. The primary example was Totemic GURPS where players narrated stuff and \u201coccasionally rolled 3d6.\u201d Paul goes so far to say in his pod cast, \u201cYou had people show up to these games who had never played GURPS\u2026 and still haven\u2019t.\u201d I think actively recognizing this is VITAL for honest play and good design. Identify the game you are ACTUALLY playing and don\u2019t just point to the Totem on the table.

Let\u2019s take a look at my personal preferences.

I really like Ron\u2019s adaptation of Ergri\u2019s dramatic narrative ideas to RPGs. I like play which presents a Premise (a question about real world human behavior) and through play produces Theme (a localized answer to that question). It\u2019s very important to me that the processes of play prevent any one person from controlling the Theme. I like play processes which allow Theme to arise from ephemeral contributions of different players as processed via the system. Consider the Humanity mechanic in Sorcerer. You can be the biggest bastard but still get away with it or you might be a saint and still be damned, all on the turn of the dice. You can\u2019t force any of the four big outcomes listed in Sorcerer\u2019s Soul. They arise from play itself.

I really like Meg Baker\u2019s distinction between No One Gets Hurt vs. I Will Not Abandon You. I used to very much be a No One Gets Hurt player and have to actively fight those social defense techniques. Play is most rewarding for me when everyone at the table expresses a little vulnerability and we allow our fears and limits to be pushed and tested. A successful game is one in which I learn something about myself or my fellow players as real people. I think it says a lot about me that after playing so much Dirty Secrets and Spione that I now consider Sorcerer, My Life with Master and Dogs in the Vineyard to be my \u201clight\u201d games.

As a technique I really enjoy players (including the GM) as character advocates (however those characters are distributed). This is because I think it\u2019s one of the most sure fire ways to achieve the above two preferences. You can invest a character with those personal fears, limits and vulnerabilities and then pitting them against each other as mitigated by system prevents anyone person from dominating Theme. This is really my personal definition of \u201cimmersion,\u201d genuinely feeling for my characters and playing to those feelings.

All combined I even have a term for my personal play preferences. I call it, \u201cPlaying Passionately\u201d because it\u2019s all about forgetting \u201cthe\u201d story as an uber-structure and investing in the here and now fiction on a personal and emotional level and then taking action on those feelings. Forget how things \u201cshould\u201d go. Forget even where you \u201cwant\u201d things to go. Invest, act and re-evaluate based on outcome.

Now here\u2019s the thing, since I think this is a fun and enjoyable thing I often speak as an advocate for this process. If all you give me in a discussion is content: tone, atmosphere, genre tropes, plot devices, etc then the answer I give you is going to be rooted in the above because the above can be applied to any kind of content and I like to encourage people to try it out.

However, if people thought more critically about the actual processes and purpose of play like Paul and Ryan identifying Totemic Gaming then we could be more helpful with one another and designers could be more mindful of their designs (as I also advocate that design should be a selfish act and indeed you\u2019ll see that the most successful designers are selfish). Articulate how and why you play, and design (if you design) to do that more efficiently.

    \n Okay, to lay out my play preferences:
    I would like to follow the rules, because I want to beat those rules. To divert from the rules means I wont be beating them, I'll be beating something else. Those rules are my target and I want to keep shooting them until they are crushed. This means I want to actually finish an RPG game - I know this is anathema on pretty much every RPG forum I've ever been on, so really I don't belong. Here I am. By finish I aim toward some end condition - top level has always struck me as an end condition in D&D for example. This is also usually why I have trouble reading new indie RPG's, because while they present a million options, I don't see any ending that I'd use those options to get towards. That's usually because the designer just intended exploration of options for explorations sake, rather than in getting toward an ending.

    Once I have completed a game, it's a trophy to me. Sure, people might challenge me to games of it latter, and my skill at it might become rusty over time. But at some point in my personal history I beat the game. And to a degree I expect that other roleplayers recognise this accomplishement. In that respect I am somewhat deluded - most don't give a crap about any of that, even though they would invite me into their game. Typically they are not aware anyone could have expectations different from their own - for the longest time I was that way myself.

    One of the biggest problems though, is that beating a game means mastering all circumstances within it. But most RPG's have massive currency leaks, where the GM can call on unlimited elephants or unlimited gold to land on you. Many would and have said (including Ron Edwards) 'Oh, that'd be rubbish play. A GM shouldn't resort to that' or such like. This is scrub ruling ( http://www.sirlin.net/Features/feature_PlayToWinPart1.htm ). Unlimited resources is another circumstance to master. The irony is, the way of mastering it is pretty much identical to scrub ruling. The only means possible to really control it is social manipulation "Oh, that's a cheap move, GM! You shouldn't do that!" and suchlike. And presumably, since there is no other means of overcoming unlimited resources, this method is within the boundaries of the challenge (though this is merely an assumption). You might like to think of it as hacking your GM, like one might hack a computer. Though on the other hand, this situation has given me piercing insight into the ways social politics work.

    The problem I have with writing up my own game(s) is that I have not finished this game of hacking. I dislike currency leaks - they lead to all sorts of murk. Murk which I could master, as any circumstance. And given the rabidness of most roleplayers to push their murk, it seems like they are acting the opponent. But my estimate is, and this is just an estimate, that if I fully mastered this, the other person would acknowledge my victory not because they see it, but because they have, to a degree, become a puppet extension of me. Which would mean no one would hail my victory. Your probably thinking 'That's wacked out to think you could do that!'. As I said, it's an estimate of what it would take, rather than what might actually be possible. This is what concerns me about currency leaks, is that this is the only way to master them. And yet I don't know if that's true, it's just an estimate. So I'm still in love with this battle and all it's subtle intricacies. I don't know if it could only end in an abhorent way. So I have no solid moral reason to throw it all away (and the years of my life dedicated to it), and the currency leak games are so damn engaging it distracts me from writing games which have no currency leaks/have less complicated circumstances to master. Why make something less when there's such a wonderfully complex game already there?

    Well, that's helped me typing it out, but I imagine it's perhaps quite shocking in some ways. But if asked, this is me!

I should have to work some for it, maybe even work a lot. When I propose something interesting I don't want the GM or the game rules or whatever authority there is to just say, "Awesome! That happens." The second that happens the thing I proposed becomes completely uninteresting. Don't get me wrong. I don't want to be constantly blocked from making fictional contributions. I just want there to be procedures in place for me to make those contributions where I have to contend against appropriate opposition for it to happen.

    My personal preference are procedures that are simple in concept but complex in execution. Like chess. Rules need to make it so that there is never an optimal way to attack and issue. Every attack (application of the rules to a situation) should have an appropriate response that counters it and makes it no longer optimal. Once again like chess. In chess there is no single optimal strategy that will give you the advantage every time. There's always a counter if your opponent can intuit what your plans are.

    If I can't have simple concept and complex execution, I want a systems that has lots of interconnected parts and complex interactions within it. Games that give me processes to play with and manipulate in order to get what I want. If every interaction with the system happens the same way over and over and over, then I'm bored.

    All of that stuff up there doesn't mean I can't enjoy playing lots of different games and styles. It's just my preference, especially for long sustained play. I've played and enjoyed PTA but I don't know that I could sustain an interest in the game for more than 3-5 sessions in a time. The system just seems a little to simplistic to hold my attention. It's still a great game to pull out and insert between other games for something a little different. I like Burning Wheel. Lots of parts to manipulate and some decent strategy elements. Also, it constantly feels like the fiction is pushing against me when playing BW. I have to work for just about everything.

When players can feel like they lost (where "lost" means failed to be an effective advocate for their character)...that they as a player "let their character down"...then you see passion, creative curveballs, clever ideas, and players willing to betray other player's characters to ensure the end they want for their character. I need to play with players who find creative wrestling fun and don't shy away from being encroached on, or encroaching others. I don't have much patience with players who view their characters and their vision as being sacred and protected, I need them to be willing to go to the mat defending their vision and thus being willing to lose it, because its the creative wrangling that's fun for me.

    \n I'm an omnivore, I guess, because there's several different ways I like to play.

    One way is pretty much identical to Jesse's "Play Passionately," with perhaps a clarification that I want it to confront me with things that scare me, that I didn't even know I was scared of, and I want to find out that I'm capable of overcoming that fear. Not only to I want the game to surprise me, but I want to surprise myself and my friends with what I'm capable of creating and responding to.

    Another way is like model trains. Y'know, setting up all those little pieces and watching with pride and wonderment as it moves basically on it's own. Now add onto that the ability for players and the system to move those pieces and introduce new ones on-the-fly, and then we get to see what consequences it had. This one also revolves around surprise; we often end up in places that no one could have anticipated. Sometimes everyone puts the train set together and fucks with it as a group, sometimes the GM (usually me) puts the train set together and lets the players fuck with it, then the GM fucks with the parts that the players interjected. Sometimes there's a story, and sometimes there's not, and sometimes there's a story on accident, but that's not important; what matters in this mode is fiddling with all the pieces then seeing what happens. (Train wrecks are fun, by the way, but most fun when you didn't expect them.)

    A third way is a little something I like to call the Way of the Disco Samurai. I use the word "disco" here because, to me, it connotes a certain flamboyance and radically ramped-up machismo or machisma (depending on your sex or preferred gender role). I use the word "samurai" because this is about battle, the contest, and because it's about battle taken to the point of something artistic and transcendent. In some ways, this is like Playing to Win, because everyone plays to the top of their ability--to do less would be a dishonesty and a dishonor to your opponent and the universe at large! But, in other ways, it's not, because you refrain from certain tactics, because you have certain expectations of yourself and each other, and because the arena is clearly defined and focused. The most important expectation here is the artfulness of your maneuvers. It's gotta look good. If you defeat me by dropping unlimited elephants on me, then bullshit. You have won nothing. There is no art in that. If you defeat me in a way that is clever and beautiful and legitimately more skilled than I was, then I will say with my last breath as I spew pressurized blood, "At last, I get to see the Shoruya Horse-Killing Technique," and die with a smile on my face, just like in the samurai movies. To battle is to ask yourself who you are. To attack is to express yourself through your blade. To win is to prove your power to your opponent, to yourself, and to the universe at large. To lose is to have the opportunity to learn something. I want to battle you, to attack you with everything I have, and I want you to do the same. And, you know what? I hope I lose, because then I have the opportunity to learn.

    Edit: I want to add that I use competition in all of these modes as an engine to drive things into action. But only in the Way of the Disco Samurai is there real loss (i.e., something at stake), although in all three modes a loss is also a gain.

      \n OK, sorry to break up the great Cheese Monkey Debates, but I'm-a take a shot at this:

      I think "Jesse's "Play passionately" describes me pretty well, but I'm going to write this without referencing or rereading his original post, to try to express this in my own words.

      Most of all I'm looking for emotional engagement around the table. This means driving toward character goals pretty aggressively, not just ambling around and kindasorta hoping to eventually get to the good stuff. Characters in motion. Characters that care about something, and are willing to take action to get it, and who are supported by the rules, the GM and the other players in doing so. "Supporting" may mean "vigorously opposing," but not simply shutting down--"nah, that doesn't work," "you can't do that," etc. Also, while aggressive, forward motion is the rule, downtime is cool as well. Having a rhythm to the story is good, but interminable campaigns that never seem to go anywhere or where PCs are bufeted by the whims of fate, are not.

      Also note that "emotional engagement" doesn't have to mean "feeling what the character feels." It DOES mean being invested in what the character feels, just like a good story that draws you in.

      So that's "Purpose," now for "Process":

      I prefer procedures that are tied tightly to narrative, meaning I want to be able to attach a fictional description to more or less every instance of rules interaction. A bit or two like Fanmail is fine, but the majority of mechanics should have a fictional component welded to them. And not just tacked on implicitly! Like, each mechanical operation should actually ad something to the story, to the shared description of "what's happening." Just understanding as a group that rerolling your dice represents a surge of heroic determination (or whatever) isn't enough; I want an actual, engaged and excited description of how our bloodied hero rises to his feet, rage burning in his eyes, as orchestra music swells!

      This tends to mean a fairly light level of crunch, though it varies with the ruleset and how well it bolsters narrative. But by and large, if there are to many fiddly bits it becomes hard to meld them to fictional input and the process bogs down. I guess whatever actual level of detail is present, the important thing is for the rules to be light on their feet.

      Some specific mechanical biases: I dig rules that stat out more than physical and mental capableness, expanding into areas like the character's personality, what he cares about, destiny, and the like. Sometimes even eschewing capability tracking altogether, though I prefer a blend. What it boils down to is tracking what matters to the story we're telling, not "emulating the world" or "conforming to realism" or any such thing. I also like a ruleset that lets me handle any number of special circumstances, crazy stunts, or unusual powers, without piling on a fuck-ton of special case rules. That way we're using genuine creativity instead of rote memorization to create memorable moments of awesome.

      I also want fairly aggressive scene framing with an emphasis on knowing where to end. I tire of endless navelgazing conversations that trail on and on, or mission planning that obsessively nails down every last little detail and possible wrinkle. I want conversations to be more like a movie or a book, where the exchange ends on the most dramatic note, instead of trickling out until everyone's sure they have nothing more to say. (Which of course precludes playstyles where getting the plan just right is important or you'll get screwed. I need both the plan and its results to be handled loosely.)

      And finally, I want authority-apportioning that's fairly egalitarian, freeing for all and burdensome to none. This doesn't mean GMless necessarily (though it can), but a GMed game should allow the GM to be just one more player, bouncing stuff off the others and riffing off what she receives. She's another player with a specific set of authority and responsibility, sure, but that responsibility is more "hey, here's some stuff that should work well with your characters' shit" and less 'tis is what is in the world, whether you ever really interact with it and whether it ever allows your characters' goals and issues to be relevant." The GM's responsibility is to whip up a bunch of rough ideas and old them loosely, adjusting in play to player choices and spontaneous developments. Of course, it goes without saying that the ruleset absolutely must support this-see "light on its feet," above.

      I think that covers it. I'll confess that I'm not totally confident in my description, mostly since this represents an ideal I'm searching for, as opposed to what I consistently get in my regular play. More than a few items above constitute a sort of wish-list: I've encountered it here and there, but in my regular group, not so much.
