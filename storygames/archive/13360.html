\n Hey everyone.

I'm considering using "pay whatever you think it's worth" as a payment model an upcoming game. Basically, send me any amount of money*, and you'd receive the game in an electronic form. Over a certain donation, basically printing+shipping+$1, I will also mail you a physical copy of the game. I'm also considering having a recommended donation, which is what I'd normally charge for the book.

For example, let's assume that $8 is the cut-off for a print version and $20 is the recommended donation.

Would this make you more interested in buying the game? Less interested?

Let's say that you were interested in buying the game. How much do you think you'd pay? Why?

* Or an e-mail explaining why you want the game but can't send money.

    \n I'd likely be less interested, but that's largely because I have this retail sideline that colors my viewpoint and somewhat controls which games I'll buy - these fancy payment models tend to fuck up retail, and especially the sort of long-term boutique sales model we have. I'd be disinclined to invest in a half dozen copies of the game as a retail investment, say, as it would be more difficult to price them fairly and sell them when the publisher has a fancy direct sales plan. As strange as it is, the fact that I'd be disinclined to buy a half dozen might cause me to not buy a personal copy, either, as I tend to get enough games for my personal needs out of the retail thing.

    Setting that aside, considering the game as something to buy personally... I'd say that the model would have no positive nor negative aspects for me, really. Price points concern me little when I make my buying decisions (not rich, but I buy anything so rarely for myself that price does not become a concern when I do), and the extra flexibility I'd get in choosing my payment would be offset by the uncertainty I'd get from buying something where the seller does not show confidence in their product by pricing it themselves. So it'd be more or less even. As for price-point, I would either do your recommended donation or the minimum donation, depending largely on your wording for the deal; if I felt obligated or virtuous for doing the recommended number, I'd go for that without a second thought, but if I felt annoyed by the pressure or indifferent towards you or your product, then I'd do the minimum (or just pirate the game, actually).

    I'll also add that a variable payment model is interesting not because of demographical tendencies that favour it, but because of the extra profit it stirs out of multiple marginal demographics: you can get small amounts of money out of the large number of people who are only minimally interested in your product, and you can get extra out of the solid hardcore audience, thus optimizing your profit for several audiences. It seems to me that this is best achieved by encouraging the customers to self-categorize themselves efficiently: allow the big spender to buy an extra-expensive package, allow the marginally curious to buy the extra-cheap package and so on.

    Considering the above, you might want to set up a third, premium level of payment, exactly because you want to enable people to categorize themselves: if I were excited about your game and it was clearly limited out of retail circulation to not confuse me, I well might spring for a premium option such as a hardcover color version of the game or some such, making extra profit for you. Word it right and I well might have more motivation for this than I'd have for the options you list; it's a fact that the difference between spending $1 and $50 is less of a hurdle for me than the basic issue of whether I'm interested in buying something in the first place, what with the credit card hassles and getting the package out of the post office and all that noise. Make it more exciting and worthwhile for me by offering more value, and I'll be perversely more likely to make the spend.

    \nBased on the pricing examples above, if I was convinced to buy I'd probably go for $10 on the printed version. That's an easy round number in my "money I don't have to think about before spending" level. It's also a price that I tend to associate with being a really good deal: not just a good bargain, but also inherently more likable (because a book that would be awful at $50 and disappointing at $25 becomes pretty good at $10). I'm not saying I wouldn't pay $20 for the right book, but it would actually be a decision to do it...I'd have to think about what else I could be using that money for instead, whether I'm likely to actually play the game in the next few months, etc., because my natural tendency is to ask "Do I really need this?" before buying anything that costs more than a nice lunch. Below that pricepoint, I'm less likely to ask questions and more willing to get off the fence.

    I'm not likely to spend anything at all on the electronic version unless there's something that appeals to me on a different level. For example, if the PDF was extraordinarily good (formatted to be read easily on a laptop screen, searchable, good bookmarks, etc.) and I really liked the game, I might want to throw some money in to try and encourage that level of quality in other PDFs. If you were in the midst of a serious financial crisis, I might throw some money in as basically a donation to keep you from being evicted or starving or going without chemotherapy or whatever. My gut-level instinctive purchase point would probably start at $5 and under no circumstances go higher than $10, because paying more than that for even an extraordinarily good PDF is just plain crazy.

    \n Points of reference:

    1.) I used to sell Ribbon Drive for $30. Now I sell it as "pay cost + whatever you want." The most common payment is for $30. There are some who pay $20, and many who pay $24-26. In general, I've felt like people have responded to my offer with sincere generosity.

    2.) I've been spending a lot of energy this week helping my coop radio station run their membership drive. A membership at the station is on a wide sliding scale - you can pay anywhere from $25-80 for a yearly membership. People tend towards memberships at the higher end of that scale - $50-60, at least.

    I think that sliding-scale pricing tends to work well. Lots of people will tend towards the higher end of the scale. Others will be delighted to get the thing at a rate that they can afford. I've never felt snubbed by this system, personally.

    Then again, I live in a hippy community where sliding-scale prices are actually fairly normal.

    \n Hmm, I actually like the idea of later payment, it seems like less of a bother to me. That is, just give me the game in exchange for my email address, and promise to write me about it in a week or so. Then when you write (an automated form letter is OK, the point is just to remind me about my "purchase"), you can ask me what I liked about the game, and then I can give you some feedback and tell you how much I'm going to pay you - nothing if I didn't like it, your suggested price if I found it worthwhile, or the extra deluxe package price if I really got excited by the game. (Or any other payment of course, but it's a good idea to give me some guidelines just to make it less of a bother for me to try to figure out what you're expecting.)

    For some reason the above procedure seems more likely to make me actually initiate the process instead of just not doing it. This is probably because I don't need to make the actually rather difficult judgment of how much to pay you at the beginning of the process; once I have to make the choice I've already checked out the product and formed an opinion on whether you're a poor fool, a talented author or my new favourite rock star designer.

    Yes, this might well be my strategy if I wanted to go with variable pricing. I especially like the idea that anybody who wants the game is sort of forced into a correspondence with me, ensuring that I at least get a lot of apologizing letters about how the game didn't really fly with my audience. Besides, a solid customer database is a beautiful thing that comes out of this arrangement without any extra fuzz.

    (Admittedly giving out my email address is a bit of a threshold, so if I wanted to have no threshold at all, I might even try to somehow get rid of that limitation. Still, I'm confident that good production values and an interesting spiel will convince the likely customer to take that much of a leap - if not, then they wouldn't be paying you for the game, either.)

    \n Hmm, combining retail with pay-what-you-want is certainly an interesting issue - I wonder if it's possible? The simplest solution would be to have a minimum payment that corresponds with recommended sales price, of course. Then a person could buy the book out of a store, give the store-keeper his slice of profit, and then perhaps continue into the Internet to donate more to the author. In practice you wouldn't see many people doing this, of course; part of the psychology of buying is that you own the thing fair and square after the purchase, after all.

    In theory nothing prevents using the shopkeeper as a middleman, of course, if he wants the hassle of discretionary pricing for himself as well. Fraud is a danger, obviously, but aside from that there's no reason why you couldn't just split the incoming money 50/50 or whatever with the shopkeeper. I for one would be loathe to mess with this sort of thing as a retailer unless it became common practice or the game was very special due to the work of setting up a discretionary payment product in the systems.

      \n And to inject more economics into this: the reason variable pricing works is because different people value a dollar differently.

      When I was a graduate student, I was lucky to have $20 in disposable income in a week. Spending all my discretionary income in one go on a game book was serious fucking business.

      When I was a highly-paid software engineer. I generally had a lot of money left at the end of the month. Spending $20 on a game was trivial. Spending $50 on a game was something I could do on an impulse.

      So if we're talking about exchanging value for value, an indie designer who charged graduate-school me $10 for a book should charge software-engineer me closer to $100 for that book, because software-engineer me has more discretionary dollars than graduate-school me and thus values them less.
