\n
\n
\n
\n\nDavid Artman \n\n\n
\n
\n\nOctober 2009 \n edited October 2009 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n I'm just curious as to what folks' average session length is. NOT waiting to meetup, NOT blathering or gossiping or sorting out food for a half hour until settling down. I mean actual play periods (including reasonable, necessary breaks of course).

I ask because I'm finding that, as I age my gaming group ages, a "session" is becoming, like, MAYBE three hours. I barely get my groove on in that time, unless I do some warm up on my own in advance. Cons typically have a four-hour session standard, but at least 15 minutes of that is waiting or wrapping up--I've always felt rushed, and like the game was ended... not FINISHED.

Back In The Day, a six-hour session was nominal for us, with some all-nighter eight-hour or ten-hour sessions on weekends. We'd never have considered treying to do a James Bond module in four hours, or a D&D crawl in three. (It took Champions to bring us to the "Holy fuck, ONE combat took us the WHOLE evening?!?")

Feel free to give the whys and wherefores for your particular session length, too--I'm more interesting in mitigating factors than a mere poll. What can I do to make these old farts get and stay focused for longer than a Lord of the Rings movie?!?

    \n For my current pickup D&D group, we play between 5 and 6 hours, but I would say about 1.5 of that is getting everybody organized, socializing, snack breaks, etc. So call it 3.5-4.5 hours of actual play. Which is just barely enough to get a bare-bones, 3-encounter + connecting scenes/"rp" adventure in. I should add that this is a MONTHLY session.

    I don't think there's much that can be done to improve the effective amount of play your group can fit into a given amount of time - in my experience, that is almost entirely down to 2 things - the native task-focus of the group's least focused member, and the complexity of choices offered by the game. There's pretty much only trade-offs rather than pure improvement available in tweaking your complexity, though. I run about as tight a ship as one can in 4E, and we still feel rushed often.

    For other games, it does vary, but not as much as you might think.

    \nThis is interesting to me, because that's actually how I prefer to play... but my current crowd of players either can't (prior commitments) or won't (love the long session for its own sake) hear of it. It's been years since a regularly recurring short game session was really the norm for me.

    What makes this interesting to me, though, is that I offload the creative energy burden by playing prep-heavier, more traditional "GM proposes, players dispose" games in this environment. I can distribute the creative energy I need across multiple shorter bursts across several weeks, and come to that long session ready to put on a show for 4 hours. Meanwhile, players tend to check in and check out over the course of the session (and the creative expectations on them aren't as high), so we manage these long sessions energy-wise fairly well.

    I'd rather play the short, prep-low, everybody-engaged, creative-at-the-table style. But under the strictures of the format I have, we adapt. I wonder if this correlates in any way to the actual development and popularity of the "trad game" model?

    \n My Monday night group plays for about two to two and a half hours, and spends only about fifteen to twenty minutes in chit-chat around that. Players arrive at or shortly after 1900h, and are usually out the door by 2200h.

    My Wednesday night group actually plays for probably about 90 minutes to two hours, and there's much more time spent on chit-chat before, during, and after. Players gather from 1945h to 2015h or so, and we typically wrap up at 2230h to 2300h.

    I'm much happier with the Monday night scheduling -- it's just as fun, and there's jollity and some socializing, but it's typically within the play context. The Wednesday night group has been together for decades (almost three of them), and the chit-chat to play ratio varies with the people who actually attend (some attendees are much more prone to digression and socializing than others).

    That said, there are other reasons that I continue to attend Wednesday evening (the taste in games is more adventurous, there's a long standing comfort and awareness of people's tastes and behaviours, and there's a fair number of folks with decent GMing skills in the group).

    \n My response is a bit dated, as I've actually recently moved and so I'm not gaming right now at all ( trying to get a new group together amongst old friends here).

    My experiences gaming out in Oakland started to show me a pattern of my likes and dislikes and areas of what I considered success.

    First, actual time of play was going to last for no more than four hours max, with various pre-, post-, and midgame breaks. Often actual play time was shorter.

    Second, campaign style play was not happening in any reasonable sense. Whatever I was playing really needed to have some kind of wrap up within 1-3 sessions, with 1-2 being a better bet, and one-session optimal.

    Third, the game almost always needed to be playable with around 3-4 players. One-shots could be bigger.

    Those limitations have decisively changed my interests in games, my own personal design thinking, game night organizing, and buying patterns.

    \n I'd say our time spent actually playing the game is at least 2 hours and never more than 4. 2.5 to 3 hours is a good session length. There's about a half-hour to an hours' worth of socializing on either side of there, too.

    And like others have said, I think this is perfect. I wouldn't want to play longer than that. I don't want to do the old high school thing where we'd start playing on Saturday afternoon and come stumbling home on Monday morning. I have other things I need to do and other things I want to do, and I can have just as much fun (and maybe even more) in a 3-hour session as I ever had in one of those lost weekends.


    I tend to agree that good prep makes long sessions better...but I'd also add that good prep makes short sessions better, too. There's really no substitute for preparation; even your off-the-cuff improvisation is better if you've taken time before the game begins to prepare something. It gets your head in the right space (what David Artman was referring to as doing some "warm up on my own in advance", I think), and if you come up with something usable before you play it'll be that much more awesome when you actually use it (because you'll be able to edit, pare down, polish, and generally improve your idea).

    But then, I'm kind of a low-prep apostate. I try to prep even for low-prep games, because I like the results so much better.
