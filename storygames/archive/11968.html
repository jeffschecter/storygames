    \n So you take your lineart and seperate it out onto it's own layer. (How I do this is a bit of an archaic technique, maybe somebody has an easy way...)
    1) Duplicate your image so that you have two identical layers. Then select the bottom layer and delete so that you have your image on the top layer and your bottom layer is pure white.
    2) Channels -> Select All (top layer)
    3) Delete
    4) Your lineart will look mostly gone. Now lock transparency on your top layer and use a large black brush over the whole image.
    5) Voila. you now how have separated lineart. From there it's super easy to either change the hue or to brush colors on top with the transparency still locked.

    (I hope this makes sense. I typed this totally from memory)

    \n There are a ton of places that will allow you to license already-created imagery inexpensively, and you can apply whatever processing techniques to that art that you desire. Most of these places don't sell pencil sketches, though.

    iStockphoto has the best royalty-free collection of vector art on the internet-- biggest, highest-quality. That said, it may not have what you're looking for, since vectors tend to not be sketchy, and they don't really cater to the anime/manga market as much as they could. Which brings me to something no one's mentioned yet: to find good, inexpensive art, you may need to keep an open mind when it comes to styles and techniques. The less specific your needs, the more likely you are that someone can fill them.

    I'm working on a full-color, anime-styled revision of It's Complicated for release in May, and I got all the art I needed for the book for $30. It wasn't what I was thinking when I went looking for art, but I just browsed iStock until something tickled my fancy. Then I saw all the ways I could use the art I was purchasing, and boom! There you go. Everyone I've shown the in-progress layout to loves the art. Everyone is impressed by how inexpensive it was.

    \n I just thought of something else I wanted to mention...

    So I did some illustration for Sons of Liberty because I was super excited about the subject matter. Thing is, Josh Roby's requests for each image were, like, insane. One illustration I did, Josh's request went something like this: (paraphrased) Ethan Allen and his group of revolutionary moose riders in the woods of Pennsylvania inciting a bunch of Mohawks to rebellion. WTF? It's still one of the craziest projects I've done, which is awesome because it's also one of the most fun projects I've done. Thing is? After I was done I added up all the time spent and figured out that I made somewhere between 1 and 2 dollars an hour. And I was okay with that. I made a little money, and I got to have a lot of fun.

    I just wanted to mention this because, well, it's easy for non-artists to forget that art doesn't happen in a vaccuum. There's a lot of time, effort, and energy that goes into each piece we produce, and just as you wouldn't want to sell a game book for only a quarter's profit neither do we want to be under-compensated for our time. It's easy to look at a professional illustration and go 'they want HOW MUCH'? But what separates the pros from the hobbyists is that they actually get to put a fair market value on their work.

    Elizabeth's suggestion about stock art is a good one. Also, there are hobbyists like me out there who illustrate games that we're interested in supporting for discounted rates. Maybe if you got the game out there and got people playing it you might be able to make a connection. (It's actually how I wound up doing last-minute illustration for Kagematsu...)
