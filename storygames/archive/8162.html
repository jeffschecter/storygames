    \n Okay, I have read the English translation. It has clever bits, but I do not see how it is ground-breaking or important.

    Here's what I think is clever.\n
      \n
    1. It explains what players do better than a lot of other RPGs. The general advice paragraph in "Concept", the "How to Speak?" section, and the "The other characters" section are well done.
    2. Three of the six pages of rules are on cards (15 events, 11 characters). This makes the game rather replayable, as players will draw different combinations of characters and events every time they play.
    \nHere's what I think is lacking or problematic.\n
      \n
    1. Perhaps it's just a matter of taste, but I find the whole surreal-but-serious thing to be too strange to play. The fisherman is dead. The island is sinking. Someone stabbed a seagull in the head with a needle. But some people like that, so let's move on.
    2. Inelegance. The game requires a die but it's actually only used to select a player on certain rules on cards. Easier: just put everyone's character card in a pile, shuffle, select one.
    3. There's not much game here. Maybe that's a feature, but I want more structure. I want more mechanics (not necessarily crunch) to make the game do what it's supposed to do. The rules boil down to: pick a character card, then take turns drawing event cards and talking in character. There's a little more about facts and lying, and the event cards do lend to the surreality that seems to be the goal of the game, but I'm left feeling like there's a lot missing here.
    4. Specifically, there's no resolution system. I don't mean a "who wins this conflict" because there's really no conflict in the game (and that is at least interesting -- at worst, problematic; I suppose there might be conflict between characters, but the game offers no way to resolve these other than narration on top of narration). The game lacks a procedural resolution system. At any point, I can say anything at all and it becomes a fact of narration, so long as it doesn't contradict anything else already in the fiction. There is no way to say, "No, that didn't happen." It's basically like a live improv show: yes, and...
    \nIs any of that ground-breaking? I don't see a single thing. I've seen short experimental games that did similar things for many years. Hell, I wrote a two-player hostage negotiation game that has no resolution system, but it has more structure. I'm not saying I'm clever. I'm saying that lots of people have been experimenting with stuff like this for a while. When did Magnus write this? If you say 1996, then I might agree it's ground-breaking. If you say 2005, then I'm not seeing it.

    Tomas, please take this discussion as both a challenge and also as a polite debate. You made quite a bold claim ("THE most important game the last 10 years") and I want to talk about it. I don't see it. I'm willing to listen and consider other views here. I'm trying to be open-minded.

    \n Adam: any analysis of a game based on reading only is flawed. You may read a game and state that it does not appeal to you, but that's it.

    Until we sink ... (original: Inntil vi synker ...) reads as a very simple game. However: it is very carefully written, in a language that supports the drama the players are meant to be creating. And the method makes you play this game different than classical role-playing games, with greater ease and a more effective focus, without shedding the principle of "one player - one character".

    Robert: the writing support the strangeness of the drama, and the way it is supposed to play out. I am not sure if the translation does the same, but I hope so. I don't think Magnus have any objections to "silly", but the game is better suited to a low-key surrealism, in my view. I would recommend playing it with 4 or 5 players though.

    The game is simple. It solves the conundrum of making effective GM-less games based on cards. And it has a higher than average replay-value for these kind of games (games with set theme).

    Shortly after playing the game for the first time, I tested the method in a design of a political farce: No danger, the plane is crashing! It created a totally different kind of game, but the method functioned very well.

    Magnus made a new game on the same method too: In the Belly of the Whalefish. That is another gem, an high adventure-game more fun to play than "In a Wicked Age", in my view. And again it shows that the method is very strong.

    Then I tested the method in a social-realistic drama: Autumn of Life. Once again the method functioned, and once again it created a new kind of drama. This time the method facilitated one of the strongest dramas I have ever experienced in roleplaying games, a game on death and friendship.

    From this testing and these inspired designs I gather that the game is indeed a jewel of extraordinary brilliance! I seldom do praise games other than my own, but Until we sink ... deserves every scrap of praise I can muster for it. After this game there is no turning back for me as a designer: I have to make games in this format!

    I do not care much for the theme of the game, but that doesn't come into the equation. With the risk of being boring: this game is THE most important game-design the last decade!

    You may find the game, translated, in the blog N\xf8rwegian Style, here: Until we sink ....

    Have a nice day!

    \n About the method and conflict negotiation: One thing that's easy to miss is that the game is about the characters' conversations - not their actions. In each scene, all the characters are gathered on the porch/veranda/thingy, and players talk in character. Something like this:

    Matthijs: "So, dr. Higginsworth - what do you think is the reason for the strange noises behind the barn this morning?"
    Per: "I think it must be a wild moose!"
    Matthijs: "Hum. Hum. Doctor, have you been at the rum?"
    Per: "I might have been, if it hadn't mysteriously disappeared during the night. Don't act as if you didn't know!"

    Which means that the question about conflict negotiation procedures is kind of... well, beside the point. The characters don't really try to make things happen - they talk about things that have happened, and sometimes try to convince each other of their version of the truth.

    The atmosphere is an important part of the game. It's pretty subtle, and the game works best (IMO) when played subtly - although the advice about starting your character as a bit of a caricature is useful.

    I'm not going to claim it's the most ground-breaking game around; however, it's very well written, and does what it aims to do in subtle, interesting an emergent ways. Good vibes.

    Tomas, lots of activities let us play a role. I don't think that's sufficient for calling something a role-playing game. I think "role-playing activity" is a superset of "role-playing game." I'm not even making an argument about "game," a part of the original definition that I think the hobby has long left behind. I'm just saying that what we have called RPGs for the last 30 years have certain qualities that set them apart from therapeutic role-play exercises, improv exercises and acting, and freeform make-believe. I'm happy to admit that the current crop of products published in the last five years has stressed and strained the traditional RPG definition (and raised the blood pressure of the RPG Pundit as a result, I'm sure) and that Until We Sink might be just another game pushing that envelope further.

If Until We Sink is billing itself as guided make-believe or constrained improv, I wouldn't disagree. I also agree that the text looks and feels like an RPG but only the way a car without tires looks and feels like a car.     Kaare, I absolutely don't exclude the Lumpley Principle. The problem is that the language uses "game" to mean too many things. In this particular instance, I meant a printed game text. I don't think it excludes the LP to say that a text must do the things that the LP-System must also do. I think if a text fails to do those things, it is not a RPG, but the group can add those things in the social contract and make it an RPG.

    Is Until We Sink billing itself as "Instant RPG! Just add IIEE!"? I don't think so. I don't see in the text where it is trying "to guide these preset norms by its gentle mix of rules and f\xf8ringer," unless you mean that the reader is to make some pretty wide logical jumps by reading the text and guessing what the designer would do. I don't doubt that one can't play a RPG with this text, once one adds some necessary pieces, but I don't think it's complete, as written.

I was headed that way in my last post, where I used the analogy of a vehicle. "This is clearly a car, but it is missing wheels! How am I to drive it? Do I carry it, or slide it on the ground, or is it actually meant to float in the water, or do I just need to find some wheels and attach them?" In the case of Until We Sink, the game actually is a car that doesn't need wheels because it's constrained so much by the "only dialog" requirement. To strain the analogy even further, the players just sit inside the car and talk, so they don't need to drive it at all. But it's still a car, and it's complete enough for its own sake.

    Nonetheless, I still see very little here that is ground-breaking in the RPG design field. Perhaps it is ground-breaking in Scandinavian circles but not globally (and this is a forum with global representation). The game hasn't really solved any problems, except by constraining them away. Instead of improving the technology of automobile wheels, it's simply removed the wheels and said that the car doesn't have to drive. This feels to me like a regression to improv and make-believe without adding anything new in the design arena. You can't rediscover an older thing and call it innovative.
