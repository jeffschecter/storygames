    January 2007 edited January 2007
    Let's take a look at everything that Sandy said:
    Indie

    Indie games are games designed and written but not necessarily intended to be played. This definition is, admitably, somewhat controversial, but I feel it best characterizes the indie movement.

    While many designers hope their game is played, their goal in creating an indie game is to craft a strong work.

    This is in contrast to a high concept game, which is designed for sales, or a homebrew, which is publication of the game they're playing. An indie game is, first and foremost, an author's creation for their specific purpose.

    Indie reflects a design attitude focused on making the best game, not the most accessible or saleable game. Yet oddly enough, accessibility can be one of the creator's self-applied design goals, leading to a categorical cross-over with the marketable 'high concept' games.

    Similarly, an indie designer may design a game with the purpose of reaching an audience not yet served--designing a game for young kids, for example. This is a high-concept game (designing a game to appeal to a specific niche), despite their indie pedigree. So the categories of 'high concept' and 'indie' are not far apart, and certainly not a function of market cap.

    The mechanics for an indie game are deeply tied into the setting or, if the game is setting-free, the mechanics themselves are the game. The game is often read and critiqued by others, again without actual play being required.

    There is also a small school of indie game design that takes an existing game and reworks it. I consider that 'homebrew indie', a hybrid owing more to indie game design than the publishing dreams of homebrew.
    This:
    **Indie games are games designed and written but not necessarily intended to be played.**

    Is the only part of that that I don't agree with, but with a little rewording could probably understand where he's coming from. I agree with the rest of his statements pretty much 100%. Aside from that one "not intended to be played" bit, are people finding other parts of that characterization that are wrong in their book? Cause for the way he's breaking it down (and for some reason using "Indie" to mean something other than "Independently Published") doesn't really rub me the wrong way at all.

    EDIT: Forgot this part:
    The game is often read and critiqued by others, again without actual play being required. Yeah, here it's pretty clear that he's talking solely about Gamechef and 24HourRPG, which Sandy has participated in (both of them, actually!). I don't mind those activities being labeled as he did so here ('critiqued, not requiring actual play', etc), but from there to "On the Shelf" either means:

    1) You playtested the fuck out of it, and probably revised about 70-95% of it, or else
    2) You rushed it to the stands as-is, and it kinda sucks.

    Do I do think there's a bit of being uninformed with thinking that games that have come out of the Forge (or Most of them, anyway) were generated along the same lines as a "critiqued and unplayed" game like those in the 24 Hour RPG or Game Chef contest. I'd say that he's not doing it maliciously/deliberately.

    More objectively, part of me is wondering if he was using his article to show how "A Faery's Tale" somehow isn't 'indie', as a lot of those "rec an Indie game" usually sees a couple recs for that game. But anyway, that's a side note.

    Honestly, though, more than ever it's kinda showing me how recently the whole concept of using words like "indie" (and the definition of Indie I still vehemently recognize as "Creator Owned and Produced") is just too full of holes, air and/or crap to be really meaningful anymore.

    I don't have any better word to take its place. "Lesser Known Games"? "Small Run Games"? "Thematic Roleplaying?" "Story Games*"?I like the concept of Self Produced and all (and will probably keep using that term over Indie), but when it comes to classifying games, "Indie" has for better or worse built up this whole group of notions around it.

    A lot of things are changing. I wonder what the landscape of terms is going to look like in a year or two.

    -Andy

    * incidentally, "Story Games" as an actual reference to RPGs has been something that never quite settled right for me. It was just meant to be the name of this site, originally. But hey, I couldn't find any better term, and it is kinda catchy.

    It strikes me that Sandy is confusing the following:

    Indie games don't sell a lot to gamers.

    Indie games aren't played a lot by gamers.

    From About the Forge, circa 2000:
    What is an independent role-playing game? Our main criterion is that the game is owned by its author, or creator-owned. We don't care what its physical format is - it can be:
    * a book in the game store

    The Forge is not only a place for role-playing game authors, though. It's here for anyone interested in discovering new games, having better role-playing experiences, or discussing role-playing game theory.
    From Indie RPG Awards FAQ #1a, circa 2002:
    What is an "Indie" Role Playing Game (RPG)?

    "Indie" is short for Independent, meaning, "independently created and owned". There is no ontological definition for "Independent RPG", but we came up with a definition for the purpose of these awards:

    * A game where the creator is the person who has written at least 50% of the actual game content.
    * A game where the creator has full control of content and publishing. They can say, "I'd like to print 200 more copies" or "I'll drop the price by 50%", "I think this game needs more ninjas..." and do so without asking permission or checking with anyone else.
    * A game where the creator is the publisher: They alone are responsible for paying all the costs of printing, art, advertising, etc. They also have full executive receipt and full control over the disbursement of profits.

    Here is a quick-and-dirty guide. First, check the copyright. If the copyright goes to the author of the game, odds are high that it's an indie RPG. If the copyright goes to a company, and that company is run by the game's author (or the company consists solely of the author), odds are high that it's an indie RPG.
    These two aren't entirely consistent, but they're pretty close, and I think they've got a fair amount of momentum and enthusiasm behind them.

      I feel like I'm pulling rank or something, but...

      Merwin, when I took and implemented Jared and Clinton's idea for Iron Game Chef, and actuallly made it an event, I had certain criteria for the winner. First, if you read my reviews of those games in those three events, you'll see that I'm being quite flip about the whole thing, and that it's not really very serious.

      Second, and even more telling, one of the scoring criteria is "Completeness." Where a ten means "theoretically it might be playable as written." I don't think I ever gave out a ten in that category. Because everyone realizes that even if the game has a set of rules that seems to cover most stuff...even if a really experienced GM could maybe run the game as written by filling in the blanks a lot...that doesn't equate to the game being really complete as written.

      In point of fact, at one point we were considering publishing a book of some of the entries (had it in the works), and were going to subtitle it "Half-baked designs." Meaning that in the foreword that I wrote for it, it explained quite clearly that these designs were not complete, but just ideas for games as people had come up with them. Very much a caveat emptor thing saying that people should only play these games at their own risk. That we weren't publishing them saying that they were ready to play.

      Now, I didn't write the 24 hour game text, Andy did, I think? Anyhow, I think by "complete" here, they mean, "Has all the elements that you'd expect to see in a RPG." Like "theoretically playable." But that's a far cry from "I'd publish it in this format."

      Here's the thing. Ask any of the 24 hour game designers whether or not they feel that the games as "completed" (there are actually very, very few that have achieved the goal), are worthy of being published in the form that they exist in when "finished" at the end of 24 hours. Ask Phil Reed if he did any work on his entries before actually publishing them. He did. Lots.

      So...nobody running the contests expects publishable games as a result...the designers don't expect that the results will be publishable for play... moreover, other than as the results of the contest as a PDF to be reviewed, they don't publish them.

      If we've given the impression that this is what these contests are about, then... I guess we owe you an apology or something. But I don't think that this has ever been the intent. These contests are intended to get people to stretch their creative muscles, and, perhaps, to come up with some proto-designs that might eventually be worked into a format (including playtesting) that will make them worthy of being published.
