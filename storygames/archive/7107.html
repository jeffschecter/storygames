    \n Exception-based rules systems lay out a basic/generic/universal way things work, and then individual characters buy feats/powers/stunts/googlybears that allow them to break those basic rules in slight ways.

    For instance: In general, roll d20 and add Strength to hit. Paladins, however, can roll d20 and add Charisma to hit when they have a certain power.

    \n Brian, I haven't read new WoD, so I dunno on that count.

    Burning Wheel is modular instead of exception-based \u2014 you use new rules because the table has decided to use those rules. Or to put it a different way, everybody can FORK; it's just that in any situation, some characters will have more pertinent FORKs than others.     TSOY Secrets, Paul, I think fit the bill, although it's now been a long long time since I read or played the game. Roughly speaking, Secrets let you do things that other people can't, right? The question would hinge on whether the only "rule" that Secrets breaks is "you can't do that without a Secret." If that's the case, then Secrets just add new powers and new rules; they don't create exceptions to existing rules.

    As for what I'm looking for \u2014 not really sure. More examples, more data. I'm intrigued as to whether D&D actually can claim a relatively unique design perspective, since it's ALL exception-based stuff, not just the powers labeled with magnifying glasses and stop signs.

    \n Not all games with exceptions are exceptions-based games. I think a useful litmus test is whether or not, in a given interaction with the system, you are more than 50% likely to invoke an exception rather than one of the base rules.

    Magic: The Gathering is the canonical one, and most CCGs that I know of are. Most of GMT's card-driven war games (The Napoleonic Wars, Paths of Glory, Twilight Struggle, etc.) are close, given how often you play cards for their Events (which break the rules in some way) rather than using them for Operations Points (which use the base rules). Feats were the exceptional part of D&D 3e, though I'm not sure I'd call it exceptions-based in the way 4e is. Ditto for Exalted's Charms, though I've not played Exalted so I can't say for sure. I can't think of any other RPGs that come close.

    Games that use cards are the most easily implemented exception-based games. You've got to write those exceptions somewhere, are cards are an obvious place.
