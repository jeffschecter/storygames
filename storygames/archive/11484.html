    \n Yeah, there's a lot of Grey Zone games.

    Spione is one. Two players are in charge of the Principles. But part of the game involves other players narrating what these Principles do. The Principle Players have veto rights. But that's all. It's a veto, not sole control. All other characters in the story are up for grabs all the time by anyone including the Principle Players.

    Dirty Secrets is similar in that the Investigator is controlled by one player and everyone else shares the rest of the cast. It's legal though to narrate what the Investigator does again that player has veto.

    S/lay w/ Me has kind of an odd narration trading thing. You control the Hero and I control the Monster and Lover and we both control everything else. However due to the nature of the game I HAVE to have some control over your character because I can't just say, "I embrace you." I have to give the consequence so I have to say, "I embrace you feeling you relax and acquiesce to my grasp."

    Capes has true character sharing. At the start each scene everyone selects from the pool what character they want to play. There's an optional rule however that a character can be declared Spotlight which means that only the player who created him can ever choose to play that character. But it's an optional rule.

    \n In Polaris, you don't share protagonists. But often secondary characters wind up getting played by multiple people since different protagonists might have different relationships with the same secondary character. This can be especially interesting when, say, one protagonist is a given secondary character's enemy and that secondary character is also the beloved of a different protagonist. You wind up with secondary characters that feel more three-dimensional because you're getting multiple perspectives.

    \n A number of games with scene framing allow the framing players to take over other players' characters at the beginning of a scene, too. Fiasco pops to mind, since my copy is sitting, minty-fresh and new, on my desk; if it's my scene and I choose to establish, then I begin by saying something like "OK, Bob, our dudes in the brothel billiard parlor, and my guy catches Cowboy Bradley cheating at pool. He's been looking for an excuse to put that guy in the ground ever since the incident at the old claim." And I just made Bob's character a cheater, and if everyone agrees that it would be awesome, a victim.

    Showdown, a two-player game forthcoming from Seth Ben-Ezra, has a more radical scheme. Most turns evenly split control of both characters between both players. But it goes deeper; I know terrible, pathetic truths about your character that neither you nor he does, and if I win one of the rolls each turn, I get to reveal it, and reveal a piece of his self-image as a cultivated lie or a delusion.
