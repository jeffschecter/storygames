\n
\n I used to like dice, but slowly over the years I have discovered that I like them less and less. These days I want dice (including any random element) to stay out of the way.
\n
\nI never want dice to tell me my characters action did not happen. (Whiff)
\n
\nI never want dice to tell me that the skilled swordswoman lost to a noob.
\n
\nI never want dice to tell me that my character feels or thinks or does something other than what I want my character to feel or think or do.
\n
\nI never want dice to break an interesting tactical encounter turning it into a cakewalk or a slaughter.
\n
\nI never want dice to make fearsome characters less impressive since they will eventually hit a string of bad luck and lose a fight they would normally easily win.
\n
\nI never want dice to slow the game to a crawl, especially if cross referencing tables or multiple roles to determine one thing come up.
\n
\nI never want dice to roll in a situation where the result does not matter.
\n
\nI never want dice to reward PCs for out of character, behavior, out of genre behavior, or poor tactical decisions.
\n
\nI never want dice to provide nonsensical broken results.
\n
\nI never want dice rolling put into a game unless it serves some clear purpose that the rules could not handle without dice.
\n
\nUnderstand that I have seen every item on this list in actual play on multiple occasions over the years.
\n
\nI know that some games do manage to avoid all these pitfalls. (Congratulations to the designers and gms who make that happen.) Even then I still often get the feeling that the dice slow the pace of the game, distract from the story, distract from the role playing, and make the game slightly less fun.
\n
\nDoes anybody else here feel this way about dice some or all of the time?

    \n I think that, in a lot of your examples above (which are great exercises in When Dice Fail, I have been on both sides of each of your examples above), it comes down to mostly the GM failing in using the dice.

    Basically, if you imagine a hammer and a nail, what you are seeing isn't the finely nailed-in hammer, it's the hammer-user not using it correctly. Whiffing, nonsensical results, result not mattering, slowing things to a crawl (this can depend on the game), swordsman noob (this is whiffing again, just skinned differently), etc. The ones that stand out are:

    >> I never want dice to tell me that my character feels or thinks or does something other than what I want my character to feel or think or do.

    This might vary from person to person. I love controlling my character, but I do like from time to time to be surprised, not by only what they DO (holy crap, I succeeded at that difficult thing/I failed at that easy thing), but by how they feel and the like. Makes for interesting RP challenges IMO. With moderation, of course.

    >> I never want dice to break an interesting tactical encounter turning it into a cakewalk or a slaughter.
    >> I never want dice to make fearsome characters less impressive since they will eventually hit a string of bad luck and lose a fight they would normally easily win.

    Both these are kind of the same, but I actually love these... as long as the system sucks such that they happen *all the time*. When they happen rarely, that's the kind of epic stuff that "table tales" are made of.

    Judd has a memorable story of his samurai in L5R, in the first adventure (first encounter?) getting some bum dice rolls, and *brutally dying in the first combat*. That event (once he made up a new character) actually became a central event in the ongoing campaign IIRC.

    But again, that was a very memorable one-time result, not "this random crap happens all the time in the system".

    But all the other things you list can be chocked to Bad GM Decisions. I know, I've made them all at some point or other, some even recently! And I have of course been on the other side of it as a player as well.

    -Andy

    \n I feel that way but mainly with things like war games. Luckily I've found some great games and haven't experienced it in a long time, starting with octaNe and leading right up to Burning Wheel and Apocalypse World which I'm playing right now and have yet to feel like I've been made a chump by.

    \nNow how is the mechanics of Lady Blackbird set up? Was that a matter of luck or are the odds such that failure is a really off chance?

    This is kind of just theory to explain after-the-fact what I saw happen,

    Your pool dice let you choose how many dice you're going to roll. What I saw at the table was people would always grow their pool to 2x (or often 2x+1) the difficulty - so their expected value was success. But what are their chances at that point? At this point, I think we intuitively guess our odds are about 50-50, but really they're 65-35. (If I'm doing the math right. It still doesn't explain what I saw at the table - I guess they just had a night of really good luck, or maybe there were a couple more missed rolls I've forgotten about.)
    And when you run out of dice you have a refreshment scene and get them back.

    Conversely, I think with The Pool when you grow your pool to 6 dice you intuitively expect almost a 100% chance of success, but it's really only 67%. (Huh, the same as the typical LB case.) So my players were almost never growing their Pool that big, and often trying things they had <50% at, hence all the disappointing and frustrating whiffs.

    In short, I think LB is more fun because your odds are better than intuition tells you they are, and the Pool is vice-versa.

    \nLorrraine, this is key, to me. I can't imagine why anyone would disagree with this concept.

    Basically, when the dice (or other randomizing elements) hit the table, it's because this, here, now, is a potential turning point in the story. We should all feel that not using dice in this exact moment would be a missed opportunity. We should all think that one outcome would be awesome, but man, that other outcome would also be so awesome. Only a randomizing element can decide for us, and in doing so, manipulate the various resources and flags and elements that set up the next awesome situation.

    Many - I'd hazard to say most - games are designed this way. I believe that these games are in the minority, but that a great deal of GMs have encountered such games and grown used to an absurd, punitive playstyle of calling for rolls (even though 4/5ths of the table is yawning) to maintain the illusion of a physics-based game world rather than a drama-based story world.

    If I were you I'd check out Amber, for an example of a totally diceless game with drama-based resolution, and Apocalypse World, for an example of a totally dice-filled game where the dice drive the drama forward and the book tells the GM (=MC) exactly when rolls are appropriate and when they are not.

    No, I just mean that "mechanic" doesn't imply "randomness" any more than it implies "go clockwise" or "take three coins".

    \n

    We use "randomness" to mean "things complex beyond our ability to understand them". If you want a process that represents something else, do something else.

    \n

    So, let's say we have a system where I summon magic powers, but I don't know how well they'll work. I throw a thunderbolt, then roll to see how effective it is \u2014 let's say it's the number of guys in the army besieging my city who are killed by the lightning, with proportionate effects for morale.

    \n

    Now, let's say we have a different system where I summon magic powers, and I get them by making bargains with natural spirits. I then choose to use Coercion or Trade, my choice, and accept the consequences, as determined by the person playing the spirit, constrained by the effects of Coercion and Trade. So let's say I choose "Trade", and the spirit has desires, and I have ways to fulfill those desires; and the spirit has lightning, and, I desire to throw it. The spirit might say, "I'll throw all the lightning you want, as hard as you want, if you give me all your children," and I might say, "Just make the army burn, and I'll marry you myself." At any point, I can Coerce, and the spirit gains me as an enemy. I probably don't want to be a storm spirit's enemy, but I get what I want right now.

    \n

    In both cases, we have an uncertain outcome, we have an effect we want, and we have a way to determine it. One uses dice, the other uses negotiation and pursuit of character desires.
