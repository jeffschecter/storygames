I'm writing MAG (Massive Action Game -- this is a placeholder title, of course) for Sony. It's a PS3 game that was announced yesterday. I've been keeping quiet about this project for way too long, so forgive me, but I've got to scream this from the rooftops and so forth. Details here.

Anyhow, to maybe give this thread a purpose of some kind, wouldn't it be hilarious/awesome if there was a 256-person story game? Or at least a Massive Story Game. Imagine an entire con where every single person was involved in the narration of an epic story, the War and Peace of story games. Like MAG, it would have to be organized meticulously into small groups that work towards the same goal.

I guess in a situation like that, documenting the experience of each 'cluster' of players would be crucial, in order to illuminate the impact that the players had on the overall narrative (and to establish the scope of said narrative, since each group of players would only be exposed to a small piece of it during gameplay)...

    \n At least in my (smaller-scale) experience, the fundamental trick is to embed unfolding shifts into multiple characters: Like, when Adam gets together with Bob and Charles, they can realize that together they have all the pieces of a puzzle that lets them get into the Dour Chambers ... but that's something they're only likely to even think to ask about after the big undead fight on the first evening, when folks start wondering "Hey, why were all of those bodies dressed in the finery described in local legends of a fallen empire in a city of fire?" That way, releases of small pieces of information can (gradually) change the entire situation of the game to something new and unexpected, all by chain reaction.

    Basically, you break your Bangs up into multiple stages, individually inert, and then much of the fun of the game can occur without GM (or even staff) assistance, as people jiggle up against each other and occasionally explode.

    Metaphorically, that is.

    \n The thing I've noted about online gaming with a fair number of people, is that experience overwrites experience. No matter how much gore or blades or shiny graphics you have, memory is quickly overwritten. Because everyone just plays over and over. If you were to just play once then had to wait a week before playing again, you'd no doubt remember alot of the little scenes and situations. In effect, you'd actually remember a story.

    But when you play over and over again, story is obliterated by sheer weight of sensory input.

    This is kind of why people like their 'headshots' stats and 'bullets fired' stats and so on. Things they would never remember, but when they look at them, they have a sense of history. Of story.

    I think games need to, and would benefit, from actual recording events more than that, and making reviews of those recordings actually a part of play (like perhaps after a review, an optional quiz on whether you or your buddy threw a grenade or used C4...answer right and you get some extra credits or something). Commercially, think of it this way - the more people remember the good times in a game, the more they think that game is good. And any sequel or subscription that comes with it.

    \n The overall player base for NERO is well over 1000, as is The Camarilla. The old days of Killer on campus could get into the hundreds easily; and there's at least two and maybe four 100+ per monthly event LARPs within the North Carolina borders.

    As for 1000-player EVENTS in the US (southern)... not so far--the Norwegians and English (and French?) got us beat hands down on massive gaming. However, the LARP Cruise might hit those numbers, if not all in the same storyline/genre:
    Gamer Adventures

    But I know Raf means "nar/indie/dirty-hippie game" when he says "story game", not any old RPG (which is what it means, per Clinton's coining), with (or without) any resolution system. As opposed to "traditional" game styles, like NERO (boffer, full speed combat and projectiles) or The Cam (step-lock, round by round resolution).

    I think massive NARisnness would only really happen either in an ARG (alternate reality game) or on some kind of fic forum like Skotos. It's a population-coordination and -communication thing (logistics aside; just talking game play). The fact that NAR sort of demands a lot of player authority and a clear communication of the SIS exacerbates common problems in less NARish games that want to disseminate "plot."

    Get 1000 people in four major State Park campsites and you still won't get NAR without them all being HIGHLY coordinated--like, come check in at the Game Desk every hour, on the hour-sort of thing. Or radios. Or something.

    Hehe... I wonder if I could get a Titanic LARP running on that LARP Cruise, or would it be in bad taste (yes, I am seriously considering attending--$400 is chicken scratch; even accounting for airfare, that's a CHEAP week's vacation).

We have a close, albeit small (relative to RPG Open) community; and we are WAY more serious and commited to design/coordination/helping each other out than shit storms (usually--a few of us can become... vehement.. at times, but what else is now?). I promise you'll be enlightened (judging by the rather-basic line of questioning here) and welcome to contribute NARdly notions and Nordic questions (quite a few of 'em post regularly, as do Swiss, English, and Parts further afield).
