\n I have friends who work at Marvel and DC comics and they claim a common belief is "most people don't know how to read comics."
\n
\nPossibly hyperbole but they claim they've done tests where many non-comic readers can't follow which bubble or panel they should read next in an average comic book.
\n
\nIs there's something similar in RPGs?
\n
\nSomething basic we take for granted that non gamers have trouble doing, something that shouldn't require practice. For example, to play Chess well, I need practice to get down the strategy. But even if I don't understand the tactics, I can still play. In an RPG, someone can be shy, feeling uncreative, not grasp the basic strategy, and not want to speak in character and still play. If I don't know which bubble or panel to read next, I can't read a comic.

    \n A few thoughts:

    First of all, chess is a great example, because you can learn how all the pieces move and be able to work out what your options are at any given point and still not know how to play chess. The checkmate is a very weird instance of the chess rules. Checkmate is not like anything else in chess. RPGs have a lot of segments that are different too. D&D3's combat system is not integrated into the rest of the system, you can learn the skill system and how to make decisions for your character and still (Edit:) not know how to do combat. (Where did the end of that sentence go?)

    Secondly, some comics are easier to read than others. Archie comics are friggin' designed by computer to be mega-easy to read.

    Third, it is not a mistake to make a RPG that you need to develop skill in to play successfully. The dismissal of "good GMing" in things like the original System Matters essay is a good (enormously wrongheaded) example. GMing D&D is a lot of fun to learn how to do, even if you may not be perfect in every way after you finish reading the rulebook. It's a rewarding skill to develop, just like chess or playing a musical instrument. It is not a mistake to develop a game that requires you to speak in character! It is not a mistake to develop a game that requires you to be creative! Yes, such a game will be a failure for people who don't like those things, or can't do them but so what?

    Posted By: JDCorleyThe dismissal of "good GMing" in things like the original System Matters essay is a good (enormously wrongheaded) example.
    \n(Disclaimer: 1. It's been ages since I read that essay. 2. I always take stuff Ron said and interpret it the way I like and I don't care much for What He Really Meant.)
    I don't think he ever dismisses "good GMing", that would be silly. He dismisses the common phrase "Bob is a great GM, he can even make FATAL fun!" which implies that a) Bob's great GMing techniques saving the game aren't system b) that all games are the same and rules don't matter because hey, we've got Bob. Etc. etc. But whatever.

    In the context of this thread I'd say that one thing that people commonly can't do is communicate and set expectations properly. Which is a people problem, not a RPG problem. (Is not knowing how to read comics a problem of functional literacy or a comics problem?)
    \n
    Posted By: TylerTWhen you think about other manuals in daily life, cook books, car repair, all of these are step by step
    \nProbably worth pointing out, for the sake of the example, that most people who know how to cook practically never look at cookbooks. Maybe just to find a new recipe or refresh your memory a bit, but it's all tacit knowledge, improvisation and application of acquired skill, taste, sense, not step-by-step following of instructions.

    Like with AW Fronts. They have a pretty rigid procedure and instructions about how to set them up but what they really are is a guideline for organizing your inspirational material to help you make shit up during the game. Same with the Principles and Moves. You're not supposed to go down the list at the table, you're supposed to internalize it and then wing it.

    -
    Compared to comics I don't think there's one general thing that people fail to do at RPGs (except maybe the communication thing above), because RPGs are too diverse and interactive. I do think however that most RPG books are terrible at explaining the game because they do it from an insider position, with tons of baggage and blind assumptions and then go on preaching about "what is roleplaying" to "outsiders" or "begginers" and often just make a mess of it.

    I've seen complete newbs try roleplaying and absolutely shine at it, because it's actually an extremely natural activity. You just have to present it right, instead of making it esoteric and strange and underground - a sin that is on the side of gamers themselves (ourselves).

    So I'd like to reverse the position: Anyone can roleplay. They might not find it fun or a worthwhile investment of their time and energy, but they can. It's us gamers who made roleplaying full of "doing it wrong" and "you can't".
