\n First, let me just link this right off the bat: Truth, Inc.
\n
\nSince this is some direction promotion and I'm having a blast with this playtest so far, lets do that too! In short:
\nIts about playing special people in a tech-rocking future dominated by a sprawling company that knows everything (maybe) and their butt-kicking drama as said company tries to pidgeon hole them into a set destiny. Pretty much you could add the subtitle: Where shit hits the fan. You have the option of playing an employee of Truth, Inc. - a citizen with an interest in Truth, Inc. - or a fugitive wanted by Truth, Inc. So the company is always at the center of things, at least at the start of play. The game runs on three tenets of play: The characters are cool motherfuckers, its the goddamn future, and the question shits on everyone.
\n
\nOk, with that out of the way the interesting part of the playtest experience for me has been matters of trust. The game group I'm playing this with is my normal group and we also play a handful of rather traddy stuff like Exalted, Shadowrun 4th, and DnD 4E (various people GM and so on). My experiences with those games has been a bit different than Truth, Inc. in regards to trust. The trust is always there, I think it has to be in order to play a game with people. I'm talking about multiple levels here, like you trust in the system to do what it says and you know it can do as much as you trust a fellow player to treat you with respect or observe some part of the fiction.
\n
\nThe contrast here for me is while the trust is there (or not) in these other games it was always buried under whatever other issue propped up with a particular rule or part of the fiction under discussion. Playing Truth, Inc. its been much more in the open and the gaming has improved because of that. A big big part of Truth is the fact that the shit is going to hit the fan, and the Admin (GM) is going to totally screw the character over at some point. Its a part of the game, and in fact its how you earn reward levels which either lead to improvement or can be spent to refresh various character energizing pools.
\n
\nWe just finished the 3rd playtest session up yesterday and now finally I can see the game coming together in a working way and it looks great. The players are aware their character is going to get screwed over and even embracing it, and they trust me as the Admin to make sure its going to be cool. I've even had a couple players decide that even though they could spend reward levels to save themselves from it, they were just going to run with it and see what shakes loose (also earning more reward levels in the process). We had so many laughs and enjoyment over the last session (and the earlier ones but not as prevalent) that I just can't wait to play again and I think this is obviously what we are all looking for in our games.
\n
\nSo now I'm wondering a lot. Has anyone else has any similar experiences in their gaming? Does this make sense to anyone but me?

    What counts here as "cool"? Is that a measure of colorful portrayal? Of dramatic climax? Of thematic depth? Of pure "how much can I possibly screw with you?" glee?

If I heard a "you're gonna get screwed, and here's why that's fun" pitch, and opted to play, then trust becomes easy.

    \n David, right.

    This is exactly what I was talking about. You've got two types of trust going on, trust in the person and trust in the system to do what it promises (what you bought into). Obviously there is overlap, since the players are executing the rules of the game and realizing the system that way.

    Whats cool? Oh man, I totally dropped one of those "this is so clear to me, but doesn't me crap to you" words here. My bad. So ok, talking about cool in this context. Here is what I think is cool, prioritized from most important to last:\n
      \n
    1. The vocal players think its fun (can't help that the silent players don't express).
      2. \n
    2. The fiction itself has some engaging, interesting detail.
      3. \n
    3. The events open doors for more drama or thematic content.
      4. \n
    \nSo this doesn't include the "screwed" angle at all, I'm thinking broader than that in my preferences/style. I'm not sure how much of this can be enforced by a system, though advice and direction can be given.

    I guess I'm kind of saying here that certain games, certain styles of play, really bring the matter of trust right up front. My experiences with a lot of traditional games has been that the trust is slowly built up over time as the game wears on and things develop, and something more dramatic really pushes it right at the start.
