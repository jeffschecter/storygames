    \n A different cultural spin: an Italian POV on some of the cards.

    The 2 of spades: bad luck, worthlessness, a denied love affair (from the game Briscola, part of common parlance/culture)

    The Queen of Hearts: passion, love, strong woman

    The Queen of Spades: bad luck

    The seven of diamonds: good luck (from the game Scopa)

    The joker: "the fool" in Italian, or rather "la matta", "the she-fool" (the face of the joker is always male, but "card" is a female noun in Italian).


    It helps that the suits are very often referred-to in their Napolitan name (even when not using napolitan cards), so "hearts, swords, clubs, gold coins/money" (the tarot ones, basically), so they tend to have pretty colorful implied meanings.

    \n Just to throw out some historical perspective, part of the significance of the Ace of Spades traces to the taxing of playing card decks. In the Brittish tradition, the Ace of Spades was hand stamped to show that the duty tax had been paid for it. The law also required that that card have the name of the printing house on it. So the Ace of Spades usually got a lot of design time spent on it.

    There's also an interesting story about it's significance in the Vietnam war on the wiki page. http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ace_of_spades

    Also, it was possibly the origin of the Black Spot concept from Treasure Island. Used by pirates to signify judgement.
    http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Spot_(Treasure_Island)

    \n Hey folks,

    So it seems that the Ace of Spades is a no-brainer, as is the Queen of Hearts. Of the other suggestions, the ones I like are Jack of Diamonds, King of Clubs, and Two of Clubs. Googling and Wikipedia finds these associations:

    Jack of Diamonds (looked into because of Kit's suggestion of the con-artist thing)

    In the "historical French" style, the Jack of Diamonds is represented by Hector, who was, according to Wikipedia, Troy's greatest warrior and, "... Homer places Hector as the very noblest of all the heroes in the Iliad: he is both peace-loving and brave, thoughtful as well as bold, a good son, husband and father, and without darker motives."

    There's apparently a folk song called Jack O'Diamonds (some lyrics here). It sounds to me a lot like a song about addiction, someone who can't see that his addiction is ruining his life or prevents him from living his life, but there's also this sense that he values something most people don't, near the end.

    I can't seem to find any new age consensus, just people saying "here's my take." If I'm unaware of some tarot-like meaning to the card, please let me know.

    King of Diamonds (Ashley's trove of stuff)

    In the "historical French" style mentioned before, this card is Julius Caesar.

    Lots of strip clubs and jewelry places.

    Tarot.com seems to say that it depicts someone wealthy and happy. Meh.

    Two of Clubs (The only club mentioned with any meaning really, by Jeff)

    Urban Dictionary suggests that showing it to a young lady is a request to see her breasts.

    That's about all I can find, but it does seem to be the lowliest, meanest card in the deck. I like it if one of the special effect cards had a very low value if the rest have a high one.

    The way it looks now, I like the Ace of Spades, the Queen of Hearts, the Jack of Diamonds, and the two of Clubs. I'd have all 4 of the royal cards if I went for the King of Clubs, so looking at that:

    King of Clubs (process of elimination)

    According to the Wikipedia article, it sounds like an outwardly happy and prosperous person who is inwardly miserable, which kinda can work.

    The "historical French" figure was Alexander the Great.

    --

    Any other thoughts? I'll also be using both Jokers. I almost didn't, then Eppy looked at me like I was an idiot.

    \n Regarding clubs in particular:

    The suit is named from the Roman suit (as you know from having looked at Roman/Spanish suiting), but the icon comes from the German suits, specifically the suit "acorns." (The icon in the French-based Anglo-American decks is now an oak leaf, which is derived in obvious ways from the acorn). So I (completely idiosyncratically) have always thought of clubs as an optimistic suit, since the icon (which is for me the most salient feature of the suit) represents possibility, new growth, and all those other things that we think about regarding seeds. In line with this, I find that the Queen evokes fertility, womanhood, fecundity; the King, a being fully integrated into and a peace with nature (a hermit-lord, of sorts); and especially the Ace, which is the card of genesis: it is the singular, original spark from which all else grows (the Ur-seed).

    I doubt that these would be useful for your game about stand-up comics, but I have dutifully recorded my views about which cards are flavorful (and what connotations they have). I like the idea of using a low card like the two, and it certainly has strong connotations (especially for those of us who are addicted to Hearts). I look forward to seeing the completed game-- sounds fascinating, and I love card-based mechanics!
