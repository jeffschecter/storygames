OK, so I have this idea that I am very excited about. I want to build a game called Medical Hospital, which will be all about medicine. And hospitals. The core conceit is that it will be composed of, say, ten minigames - the surgery minigame, the romance minigame, the political minigame, the trauma minigame, the diagnostic minigame, whatever. They will be modular and extensible and use the same systems and currency. So players can combine the comedy/romance/diagnostic to run a light, fast Scrubs analog, or surgery/trauma/drama/politics to run a more serious game, or throw them all in the mix for a weird melange. Maybe there is a supernatural minigame to run Kingdom Hospital/Riget. Whatever suits the mood and tastes of the group - more minigames means more complex sessions. Some of the minigames will be explicitly RPG-like, and others will incorporate other elements. So far I've only worked up the surgery and trauma games and tested them a bit. In a vacuum they are a bit silly, but I have a feeling that once characters (the surgeon, his assistant, etc) are layered over the physical manipulations, they'll be gold.

So the idea for the surgery minigame is that you are performing an actual procedure. You have a letter-sized print out of your patient's guts with instructions. Office supplies are your tools - scissors = scalpel, paper clips = hemostats and clamps, post-its = sponges, adhesive tape = sutures. Each time you invoke a peripheral element (clamp, sponge, suture) as a surgeon, your assistant checks for complications, which may involve the patient's condition or may involve the surgeon's relationships somehow. Still fuzzy on how this works, but it will tie into other minigames.

Trauma is also fun - in this case, you use the same guts images, but actually rip, tear, or puncture them. The surgeon is then timed as she debrides the wound, stops any bleeding, and patches it up. Multiple injuries? Teams of surgeons? Multiple procedures? Strictly awesome. One of my tests is a brain tumor, which involves a craniotomy first to gain access to the brain. My friend Joel suggested printing some procedures involving bones on heavy cardboard, requiring massive scissors or coping saws. There is a lot of creativity possible in creating challenges.

Here are some pictures of my wife Autumn performing her first appendectomy, along with a largish .pdf of my test procedures. One exciting thing is that there is a huge library of public domain medical imagery, so players will be able to create, remix, and share procedures. Anyway, tell me what you think about this idea.

    Some thoughts on how to use cards in Medical Hospital. Still not thinking about the currency. The important thing is that the minigames are tied to scenes, and are very brief. Card values vary wildly based on the minigame in play and a character's personal suit (this isn't fully baked). Anyway:

    CARD HANDLING AND RESOLUTION

    Medical Hospital uses hands of seven cards. There are three basic ways these cards can be used, and these methods vary by scene. Players may retain cards, face down, from scene to scene, but they will always draw up to seven at the beginning of a new scene. Whether freshly drawn cards are face up or face down will depend on the particular scene, but retained cards are always face down.

    Each scene minigame ends after all seven cards have been exhausted, or a procedure has been completed, or a single conflict has been resolved by comparing hands.

    Retaining cards from scene to scene can make tactical sense, because specific cards and combinations will be a direct benefit in specific future minigames (retaining any three red cards, for example, makes all but the most complex surgery risk-free).

    There are three core games: The color game, the sequence game, and the matching game. Each has two variations.

    THE COLOR GAME
    Flip over cards at specific play junctures. One color card has no effect, and the other color has a negative effect.

    Surgery
    Surgery minigame, you draw up to seven cards and the nurse flips one card every time you reach a junction in the procedure. Red is good, black is a complication. You can choose to draw from your "held" cards instead. No single procedure can have more than seven junctions. Multiple simultaneous procedures are possible. Number cards are procedural/physical complications, and face cards are interpersonal complications guided by suit. Aces are both.

    Trauma
    Trauma minigame, you draw up to seven cards and the nurse flips one card immediately, and another card every ten seconds. Black is good, red is a complication. You can choose to draw from your "held" cards instead. If surgery isn't finished in 60 seconds, the nurse adds an automatic physical complication every ten seconds. Number cards are procedural/physical complications, and face cards are interpersonal complications guided by suit. Aces are both.

    THE SEQUENCE GAME
    Direct conflict between players. Each puts forward a hand of two or more cards to resolve the conflict. Cards in numeric sequence - regardless of suit - are added together. A player without at least two cards in sequence automatically loses. Aces are wild cards, taking the place of any number card but having no numeric value themselves. Face cards can be played prior to conflict resolution as "narrative points" somehow related to suit, and when played they are exchanged for a random card drawn from the deck.

    Comedy
    The high total wins the conflict, making high-number cards valuable.

    Drama
    The low total wins the conflict, making low-number cards valuable.

    THE MATCHING GAME
    Card features - either suits or numbers and faces - are matched to determine the winner of a conflict. More matches is better.

    Diagnosis
    Match suits. Your personal suit gains a bonus (maybe card count doubles, maybe a +2 or something). Maybe suits are ranked clubs/spades/diamonds/hearts, maybe not...

    Romance
    Match numbers/faces. Higher numbers, and face cards, are better. Face cards can be used in some narrative way as an alternative, maybe to block another player. Aces are wild.
