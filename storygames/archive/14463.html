    \n I'm not an expert in RET but I am a psychotherapist and use similar ideas (though I'm more a structural family theroay cognitive behavioral therapist).

    The basic idea is that the way we think about situations defines what they are to us (regardless of objective reality). We often predict negative - it seems "more realistic" to many people. The goal of therapy is to help people begin to see adversity in other ways. Reframe the meaning of what is going on.

    I do this with myself all the time. I feel a feeling in my throat or stomach and recognize that is it excitement rather than call it fear or anxiety. I accept that it is uncomfortable but I also recognize that is is very temporary (as are all feelings/emotions/sensations). I maximize my pleasure not by denying negative uncomfortableness but by accepting it immediately and letting it pass. It works!

    I'm not immediately seeing how it player into gaming except that how we understand things greatly affects the idea of rewards. For instance - while I've not played Dogs in the Vinyard - I have a strategy of play that might be very annoying to the GM and other players. I don't like violence so I would come into a town and accept loss after loss rather than escalate situationsl. Each interaction would give me more ideas about what was happening in the town. When I felt I knew what was going on I would call the towns people to an open meeting. I would ask who I thought was responsible to come forward and without warning I would shoot them No escalation - just sudden execution. Then I would explain what God showed me was going on in their town and give them a stern warning to fly straight. Because I would have been seen as weak by the towns people by then they would not see it coming. The shock would thus be greater and the lesson to be wary of the Devil all the greater because you cannot judge based on surface information. As word spread of my justice people would begin to fear my coming so the terror of the dogs would spread far and wide. I would be practicing real terrorism.

    This is my thought based on my revulsion to playing a religious policeman who is judge jury and executioner. I know from years of study of policing that this kind of absolute power leads to the worst kind of corruption. Accepting defeat in all matters except the last would be the humbling antidote to the power. Gandhi with a gun.

    So I've run this scenario through my head and realize that it is based off of my political believes and prejudices and that they really shouldn't come into games so I would recognize the beliefs and change my behavior in the game so as not to screw things up for the rest of the players. Which in RET land would lead me to different consequences.

    Chris Engle

    \n Call of Cthulhu sanity checks?

    I ran a series of CoC games back in the mid 80's that were some of the best games I've ever run. I'd lead the players into dark holes and then make them start making spot hidden rolls. Each success would bring another small of terrifying observation. I got them to the point where they didn't want to pass their checks. They didn't want to see anymore. They had to stop looking to save what little was left of their minds. Then I'd put them in "Saw" like delimas. "I only want one. Only one of you has to decide. Pick one..." That was a very dark period of my life and it came out creatively in those games. I don't want to go back there again.

    Todd

    You're right that Engle Matrix games are like RET. I use a version of the game in therapy: "Bob's Brain". The players are Bob's brain. I often run the scenario "Let's get Bob in trouble." I give them an openning problem (cherry picked from what I know about the people in the group) to start it off. Then we go around the room adding to the story with the occasional counter-argument so they can see self talk fighting it out. Some times all the thought battle it out for victory so they can see a person deeply conflicted. It is fun but mainly it is theraputic so I've never run it at hobby conventions for normal audiences.

    \n Funny thought - you could totally implement cognitive distortions through some sort of lifepath system.

    Here's why: you need to understand that cognitive distortions are actually an evolutionary imperative. Second by second and day by day, we get hit with way more stimuli that we can process through our sensorial memory, and the short term memory gets hit by way more data than we can actually store and retrieve efficiently from our long term memory. As such, we need to rely on categorizations, generalizations, maxims and other shortcuts in order to (1) choose what stimuli to process through attention and which to ignore and (2) burn information in our long term memory in a way that's effective in terms of storage and retrieval. Mind you, these are not conscious processes, but they happening nonetheless: we just don't have the hardware to do otherwise.

    I'm not sure exactly how one would go about this, but considering that cognitive distortions arise and get replaced based on what stimuli is important to process and what information is important to store in a phase of your life, you could certainly track their evolution through some kind of lifepath thingie.

    \n Lifepaths are the way to go if you want to develop a character with a rich backstory, or want a character deep-enough to use in a long campaign.

    But I tend toward games where backstory is just a rough sketch, and it's pretty clear which personality-bits will be highlighted in a single session or a single arc of maybe 2,3,4 episodes.

    Which would makes it even easier to simply tag a character with 'emotional reasoning' or 'jumping to conclusions' and then watch them flail.

    Halfway between the lifepath and the one-shot trait, would be to assign cognitive distortions as Fallout.
