\n For something a little different, this is a 'gunboat' version of diplomacy. In 'Gunboat', there is no communication between the players and no press. It is interesting playing 'Diplomacy' when there is (perhaps) no diplomacy, There is communication, though, through various signaling that I think is learned as the game goes on.
I think gunboat games help develop an understanding of the rules and tactics of Diplomacy. I find gunboat more 'soothing' than regular games. Because there are no agreements, there are no betrayals. Players don't have to be worried about false agreements--either as a perpetrator or a victim!
These can take some time though...the last gunboat game I played lasted four months and covered 1901 to 1922.
I won't play but I will try to make regular updates here. If you play, I think posting here is ok but don't reveal your country and don't use this forum as a substitute for negotiations.
The link is here Gunboat. The password is sgleague.

    "Gunboat diplomacy" is TR-type speaking softly and carrying a big stick. It refers to the (real-world) practice of a nation sending a cruiser or two to a potential trouble spot to let the locals and/or interlopers know that you're prepared to use force if things don't settle down (or stay quiet). I seem to recall that the Kaiser was notorious for it--ah yes, Wikipedia tells me that the Agadir Crisis in Morocco was a notable example of gunboat diplomacy, as was the Yangtze River Patrol in China.

    The metaphor for the game Diplomacy is clear; it's where you let your troops do the talking.
