\n I'm just wondering, how much is too much. My original outline for Hearts & Souls 2E (if you aren't familiar with 1E, its a superhero game where success is based in part on the ability to monologue, and 2E adds banter)
The more I write for it though, the more I feel I need to write--explanation of superhero tropes, the setting (people asked for that, a lot of them.) GM help, on top of rules and so on.

So when and where do you stop?

    \n As simple as possible but no simpler.

    Which means you and the editor take periodic looks at it and answer this: is this possible to use coherently and completely for common use cases?

    If not, then add or rejigger stuff.
    If yes, then strip it down.
    Repeat until you meet in the middle.

    Let\u2019s say some gadget is just a black box with either one or two buttons. The buttons, in the case of two, are different sizes and colors and look different. The buttons, in either device, may be labeled.

    Which of the two is most usable? Well, that depends on how many things you need the gadget to do.
    If it commonly needs to do two things, it\u2019s simpler to have two buttons than to have some weird \u201cpress the button three times then hold it\u201d scheme.
    If it only needs to do one common thing, then yes, one button is simpler. But only then.

    This is why I\u2019ve been a proponent of Fate Core rather than Fate Accelerated Edition.

    It explains things more clearly and in more detail. The player-facing parts are very well summarized in the cross-referenced quickstart section in the back.
    The GM-facing parts are much stricter and more better in FC than in FAE.

    \n There are so many ways in which you can go about this; a lot of it depends on your target audience and what knowledge of theirs you rely upon. Are you making this game for avid comic readers or people who've only ever seen a superhero movie or two? The second thing to consider is the focus of the experience you're trying to provide. Is it fine if they meander through superheroland aimlessly, enjoying the scenery? Or do you want them to have a specific experience that's like pages out of a certain kind/era/writer's type of comic?

    Once you have your design scope nailed down, you can think about how to bring this about: which parts to promote, provoke, provide, or prevent. That then determines what you need to have in your document.

    \n I'm going take a technical route with this answer, so sorry if it's dry. It's the philosophy I work by.

    When I was taking art classes, one of the rules they pounded into my head is, "Form follows function". On organic things, every piece (muscle, bone, etc) that makes it up generally has a particular function. You can also apply this to machines. Every part in a machine (well, a good machine) is there for a particular reason, and helps the entire thing do what it's supposed to do. Everything interacts, and is all relevant.

    In stories (or RPGs) your Form takes shape in the way of rules, setting, characters, etc. The Function of the game can be somewhat likened (in my head at least) to the Concept and Setting of your game. In other words, what is it that your game is trying to achieve with it's Form? So the question: When to stop?

    I try to look at concept and setting first and foremost. Then I try to go back to it as much as possible. Everything I write should relate, somehow, back to those. In my current project, I found myself in the same predicament, and I thought back to 'form follows function'. Everything I've written after that point loops back to something else that's of importance. Even characters that would seem (to players) to be of minor importance have a meaning that bolsters the concept and setting. Some things were removed or changed to help come back to the original concept as well.

    Once you have enough Form to support your Function (remembering to keep things tight and relevant), your concept should be able to stand on it's own legs. Everything else is icing on the carefully-prepared cake you've created. As long as what your writing adds to the narrative in a way that's relevant to the players, I say absolutely add it.

    If not, you can always use it for fiction based on your game! (This is my plan for all of the extra stuff I write/think about that just doesn't seem to be appropriate for the game text)
