\n Hello everybody,

I'm working on a game design meant specifically for playing coming-of-age stories in the "high schoolers transported to fantasy world" sub-genre. Think Chronicles of Narnia/Vision of Escaflowne/Twelve Kingdoms/El-Hazard/Labyrinth. (Maybe even The Wizard of Oz, though it's a bit of a stretch).

I'm currently stuck on coming up with the basics of the resolution system, and I'm hoping that someone might give me some suggestions that will get me moving in the right direction. See, I've got some fairly specific ideas about what sort of character traits should play into the resolution system, what kind of dilemmas it should force, and what player behaviors it should reward, but I'm having trouble turning all that into an actual dice mechanic.

I originally threw out this idea on my blog, in this post:

http://www.evilbrainjono.net/cgi-bin/blog/showblog.cgi?permalink=491&showcomments=true

Since then I've done a lot more thinking about it. Here's what the game's about:

Central to the theme and premise of the game is that your characters start as basically blank slates, and you discover who they are through the process of play. I want rules that encourage players to try on a persona for their character, play around with it, then throw it away and transform their character into something else. At the same time, I want rules that encourage players to have their characters invest in the fantasy world, in the sense of seeing a conflict, making a decision that one side is right and the other is wrong, putting a stake in the ground and being willing to fight for it. And then, later on, perhaps to change their mind and switch sides, as the complexity of the situation becomes more apparent and they think more about what their values are. Eventually you help save, or help destroy, the fantasy world, and return to Earth, or not.

I'm hoping the process of transforming your character's identity, supported and encouraged by appropriate mechanics, will 1. emulate somewhat the process by which teenagers become adults, 2. be fertile ground for various thematic statements by players, and 3. be a lot of fun.

I'm thinking I want PCs to start with little more than a personal goal, some relationships, and a flaw/weakness/insecurity or two (the kind of thing that GURPS would call a "mental disadvantage"). The personal goal is something related to the high-school world that you want, but your insecurities are preventing you from achieving it. (Simple example: I want a girlfriend, but I'm super awkward around girls. Better example: I want to earn parental approval by getting into a good college, but I go out of my way to piss off my teachers cuz I have a problem with authority figures.) Playing out your weaknesses, eventually overcoming them ("buying off the disadvantage" as it were), or else accepting them as part of your personality that you're OK with, would be a major part of the game.
At the end of the story, when you've returned to the real world (or not) and get some sort of epilogue for dealing with your original goal. For example, is it easily achievable thanks to your newfound maturity and moral fiber? Or have you abandoned it completely as something childish you're no longer interested in? Or have you decided that the people you met in the fantasy world were so much more important to you than your original goal that you choose never to return at all?

(I'm thinking that maybe the strength of your connection back to your high-school world vs. the strength of your connection to the fantasy realm is important enough that it may benefit from specific mechanical reinforcement of some kind of trade-off. Sacrifice your memories of your homeworld in order to get your wishes granted, a la Bastian in the second half of The Neverending Story?)

    \n I've also got an idea that each player rolls up a "report card" for their charcter with grades in various subjects, which serve as an aid to brainstorming the character's personality and also provide some meager academic skills to start with, which will be intentionally ill-suited to the fantasy world, though still usable. ("I have an A in Music, so I'm gonna try to charm the Queen with this song we've been practicing for the graduation ceremony...") This idea might be overly cute and I'm willing to drop it if it doesn't fit in with the rest of the game. The idea of grades in specific subjects sort of naturally suggests a skills-based task-resolution system, which is probably not what I want. On the other hand, it could be made to fit into a generalized Trait-based kind of conflict-resolution system, where traits relevant to the conflict at hand give you different amounts/types of dice like in Dogs, and it's simply a rule that skills from your Report Card are a smaller size of dice, and therefore ultimately less significant than, the size of dice you get for things like character goals and relationships and other things that are more personally meaningful like that.

    I also have to decide: if Math and Social Studies are specifically recognized by the game as numerical attributes that all players have, then is there an exhaustive list of other, fantasy-world-appropriate skills that can be learned by characters after spending some time there, like Horseback Riding or what have you? Thing is, I'm hoping this game can use collaborative setting-creation techniques for the fantasy world, and therefore no one skill list will cover it, and I'm not particularly interested in making one anyway. So I think I'm really leaning towards the generalized Traits sort of thing.

    Really important part that I haven't gotten to yet: virtues and character classes. This being a fantasy world, it has an absolute moral compass, in that righteous behavior gives you mythic power. I'm thinking that traditional fantasy Character Classes, or some kind of mix-matchable ability sets, like "healer", "knight", "mage who blows stuff up", etc. etc. can be earned by the characters once they enter the fantasy world, and that each character class has some associated virtues (an idea used to good effect in, for example, Ultima IV). That is, every character would have scores in things like "Bravery", "Honesty", "Compassion", etc. etc. All scores would start at zero -- your high schoolers are blank slates, remember -- and go up and down in response to how you play your character. Perform some courageous deeds, your Bravery goes up, the Knight class opens up, you can claim it and then have Useful Abilities. With me so far?

    Now, remember I said that part of growing up in this game is about trying out different identities. The character classes, being archetypal identities, are an expression of that idea. So it should be really easy for your character to use a class for a while, but then choose to emphasize a different virtue, and switch to a different class. Mechanically, class-switching should be easily done and encouraged rather than punished.


    Subtle design points here: rather than a specific Bravery score being required to become a Knight, maybe it's that the Knight abilities are like "gain a number of bonus dice equal to your bravery whenever you put yourself in harm to protect someone else", or whatever. So that anyone can pick up the Knight class at any time, but it doesn't *do* anything unless you have a positive Bravery score. I guess this depends on what you need to do *in the fiction* to change character class, which I haven't thought about yet. It's also related to the question of "do I gain bonus dice based on my virtues in the absence of a character class ability that says I do?". I'm thinking the answer is "no" -- the character classes are more interesting if they are the one and only way to translate virtue scores into effectiveness. Finally, I'm thinking that if there are all these character classes with various powers, I'm going to need to have a certain amount of mechanical complexity in the resolution system, so that classes can be differentiated from each other mechanically, with abilities that do mechanically different things. In other words, a resolution system like Primetime Adventures where you resolve the whole scene's conflict in one go wouldn't be appropriate for this game, since it would give character class abilities nothing to latch on to.

    \n So now we've got your goals, your insecurities, your report-card grades, and potentially your relationships (haven't thought much about those) and your character-class abilities all as input to the resolution system. We have virtue score changes as a reward mechanic that plays into classes and therefore into effectiveness. (I'm pretty sure that virtue score changes depend entirely on the contents of your role-playing and not on any other mechanic. I.E. the game rules couldn't tell you when you had forfeited a point of Honesty; only the GM, or another player, analyzing your character's behavior, could tell you that.) There probably also needs to be some kind of fortune-in-the-middle mechanic where I can see that I'm unhappy with a die roll and pay out of some limited resource, or risk some penalty, or escalate the conflict stakes, or whatever, in order to get myself a re-roll or whatever.

    Now, at this point, I'm not quite happy with how this is shaping up, because the virtues are too one-sided. It's a bit boring, both game-wise and fiction-wise, if the rules always encourage you to play to increase your virtues. I.E. high Compassion helps you be a better healer, healing people shows compassion, self-reinforcing cycle, you are rewarded for a one-dimensional character who simply demonstrates more and more compassion over time and nothing else.

    The GM could counteract this tendency by specifically introducing choices that pit one virtue against another, where to gain in one is to lose in the other, but there's only so much you can do with that before it feels contrived. So I'm thinking the game should also provide some mechanical temptation to go down in virtue scales. My first idea is that if you behaved in an evil way, contrary to a virtue, you could get a spectacular bunch of bonus dice for that one action, but lose a point of the virtue permanently. You'd be trading long-term for short-term effectiveness. ("Evil is shortsighted"?)

    But maybe a better idea is that the *opposite* of virtues are... also virtues. For instance, if the opposite of Honesty is Cunning, such that decreasing Honesty below zero results in a positive Cunning score, then there could be a whole selection of Evil Character Classes that draw from the evil sides of the virtues. The Cunning character could earn classes like Thief or Asassin. Now, see, that strikes me as pretty cool, especially if it's a Significant Thing in the fiction to be an evil class -- maybe the Evil Empire that's threatening the fantasy world comes along and offers your character his or her greatest desire in exchange for serving them. Encourage people to go to the darkside, the way Edmund does (temporarily) in The Lion, the Witch, and the Wardrobe. This makes for a lot of cool story possibilities: betrayal, redemption, split loyalties, etc etc. The coming-of-age story strikes me as more true, with a bigger range of dramatic statements, if some of the teenagers spend some time being evil and tearing up the fantasy world and putting peaceful woodland creatures to the sword in exchange for cool powers, and then maybe come to regret their actions and reform... or maybe don't.

    The "evil classes" idea probably doesn't mesh so well with the "evil is shortsighted" bonus dice idea, so we might have to throw out the former, even though I like its simplicity.

    \n A final possibility is that each Virtue is not so much Good vs. Evil as it is two opposing points of view, either of which is potentially valid. This immediately changes the feel of the fantasy world from epic Manichean to ambiguous shades-of-gray. This might be more appropriate for a game about teenagers, since challenging conventional morality is a pretty important part of growing through that teenage stage. Especially in a world without your parents or teachers, it's going to be all about defining your own morality, seeing what results that leads to, then reconsidering and perhaps changing the principles you're choosing to live by. A more ambiguous set of values might work better for such a game, although it's at risk of losing some of that high-fantasy frission. (instead of "Oh no! He's gone over to the Dark Side!" it becomes "Oh no! He's gone over to the Cynical Side!". Doesn't have the same ring.)

    But in that vein, here's some potential virtue scales where the left side is a sort of classical fairy-tale virtue, and the right side is a rebellion against it which might be considered as a virtue in its own right, though a more antiheroic one.

    Honesty <- 0 -> Cunning
    Bravery <- 0 -> Discretion
    Peace <- 0 -> Violence
    Cooperation <- 0 -> Individualism
    Mercy <- 0 -> Judgement
    Purity <- 0 -> Curiosity

    The prevailing "good" culture of the fantasy world might *define* the quality on the right side as Evil, but it's not objectively evil, which might make it a more appealing choice for a player character. In this type of world, the game is less about the difficulty of walking the straight+narrow path, and more about choosing which of the opposing sides on an issue appeals to your character more.

    There probably still needs to be something in the mechanics that pushes you back towards the center of those scales, where you have the opportunity to rethink your values and change Character Classes. It needs that in order to counteract the tendency to just get more and more extreme in one particular direction to get more powerful with a single class. So I'm back to the idea that you can wager points from your virtues as a positioning mechanism, i.e. "I rolled lousy, I'll risk a point of Cunning to re-roll that..." and it works the same whether you're on the positive or negative side of the scale, i.e. if you lose it you move one step closer to zero. But what the heck does that represent in the fiction? I think I just counteracted the idea that changes in your virtue scales should only come from role-playing.

    Bah! This is hard. And I'm no closer to figuring out a dice mechanic that does all the things I want.

    Sorry for the super-long first post. Thanks for reading, and let me know if you have any ideas. Thanks a lot!

    \n Heya Jono,

    Cool idea.

    Various thoughts to use or ignore as you see fit:

    1. We played a game called EPICS a few years ago that was a "define your character through play" system. What I learned from the experience was that with sketchy characters, players go into the game with specific ideas for their development, and that as GM, if you give adversity that doesn't match what they need for defining their character the way they want to, they'll ignore it. If they don't ignore it, then it's just because they're being a good sport, and the result will be that their character doesn't develop how they were hoping, and they lose interest in the game over time.

    2. I don't think identity formation is about trying on classes. Perhaps this is more for the 11-13 yr old age range, but I think identity formation is about adopting role models and trying out their identities. "When my brother talks to adults, people listen." And then I adopt the body language and demeanor of my brother as best I can, and see if I can't get people to listen to me. If I fail, perhaps more than once, then I'm defined as someone who people don't listen to. Perhaps this creates insecurities.

    3. So, if I were designing it, I'd do something with trait definition via the adoption of role models like that, and then I'd introduce colorful NPCs into the mix. "Prince Guaifenesin was glorious in battle against the witch king." And then we see how well I manage to achieve success in battle.

    4. The high school age range is less honest, in my mind, more about image creation than identity formation, more about creating image to cover up enduring insecurities than about identity. I think the 11-13 age range works better for Narnia, etc.

    5. So, conceptually, I'd have Identity defined by a character's efforts to adopt traits via choosing of role models. That is, Identity would be sourced externally to the character. Insecurities, however, would be directly associated with the character. And then there would be Things I Must Do. "You, are the chosen one. You must retrieve the crystal sword from the caves of flesh eaters and use it to slay the behemoth." "You must bring the rival kings together for the conclave or the fae will be so offended as to withhold their magic from the realm for a thousand years." "You must pass Algebra or you can't be a cheerleader." The Things I Must Do would be oppposed to the Insecurities in some powerful way, so the game becomes about navigating both of them by choosing role models, adopting traits, and defining an identity.

    6. So, I think it would resolve the problem we had with EPICS, because letting the player define some component of Insecurities and/or Things I Must Do will give the GM what he needs for creating adversity and still put the players in the position of defining their characters through play.

    Paul

    You asked:\n
    Anyway... what I'm more interested in, is knowing what your interest in these stories is so I can better understand what you are looking for?
    \nThat's a really good question. I can tell you they're my favorite fantasy sub-genre, without being able to articulate exactly why. Part of it is that culture shock is interesting to me, therefore fish-out-of-water stories are interesting, and confronting fantasy tropes from the viewpoint of a citizen of the modern world especially so because of the contrasting expectations. Part of it is that the protagonist comes from a situation familiar to the audience, has a level of knowledge of the world equivalent to that of the audience (zero) and therefore serves as a viewpoint character who is really easy to identify with.

    But the biggest part of it gets right down into the deep magic of why humans tell fantasy stories at all. I'm no expert on the subject, but I feel like it has something to do with connecting the epic/mythological to the personal. This kind of connection is present in different ways in different genres; for example, think about superhero stories, where the hero exists (as his secret identiy) in the world of mundane, personal problems which the viewer can relate to, but also (as his hero identity) embodies a mythic archetype locked in an epic struggle. Quest-style fantasy is about taking a journey starting from the mundane/personal world (e.g. The Shire) into an epic, mythological world where actions like throwing a ring into the lava have cosmic repercussions. Etc.

    And back to the genre in question: look at Labyrinth (the bestest movie ever!!) where Sarah's personal issues (I hate my brother! Also I whine about things being unfair because I refuse to take responsibility for anything I do!) sorta flow into the world of the labyrinth, and then what happens in the labyrinth flows back into her personal issues (realizing the goblin king has no power over her, etc).

    So that's what I like about these kind of stories. And so I want to play a game where I can explore that connection: how do the choices I make in this mythological space flow back into my personal life and affect what sort of person I grow up to be? There's lots of fantasy RPGs, but I've never seen one designed specifically to address this particular sub-genre.

    I've also wanted, for a while, to do a treatment of the fantasy-character-class idea where, instead of just picking one to play, you have to earn your way into them. i.e. to take on the power of a mythic archetype (the barbarian, the necromancer, whatever) you first have to act in accordance with the values associated with that archetype (prove your cunning before you can become the Thief, etc.) In other words, a character's fantasy-world effectiveness is a symbolic function of behavior and moral chioces, which I think fits in well with the other stuff I want to do in this game. But I could be wrong; this might be an idea that I'm better off shelving for a different game.

    \n Hituro said:\n
    What sort of mechanical systems do you like? What gets closest to the feeling that you want to evoke from this game? Do you want to be resolving tasks, or scenes, or conflicts, or goals?
    \nHmmmm, good questions. I would say that I wanted to resolve conflicts, but after reading the thread about The Slippery Slope of Stakes I'm not so sure I even know what conflict resolution means anymore.

    I like fortune-in-the-middle; I like systems where a player can say "This action right here is so important to me that I'm going to burn some limited resource in order to give it a better chance to succeed." I like systems where you chuck down some dice and then realize that you didn't roll well enough to get all the things you wanted, so you might have to make a sudden decision about what the most important thing is, and sacrifice the rest.

    I don't want to resolve a whole scene at once, because I like it when there's some back-and-forth first, with players escalating and narrating increasingly crazy or extreme actions and not knowing how it's going to turn out until the very end. I like how escalation mechanics can be a cattle prod to the creative part of the brain ("I gotta think of something to top that?!?"). I like systems where certain things on a character sheet can only be used once per scene or once per encounter or whatever, because that prods me into getting creative to find ways to use everything on my sheet that I haven't used yet.

    Also, I don't like the conflict resolution to be totally abstract to the point where you could play it as a standalone dice game without reference to the fiction at all. Sometimes it seems like indie designs skirt close to that edge. I prefer the actions in the fiction to determine what you get to roll, the outcome of the roll to determine a change in the fiction, and the change in the fiction to determine what the next valid range of actions could be. At the same time, I hate having to pause play to look stuff up, so I want it simple/generic enough to be able to handle any situation without reference to specific charts and tables of modifiers or whatever.

    (Yeah, I know, I'm greedy. I want it all.)

    What's closest to the feeling I want to evoke from this game? Hmmm. I guess I want, primarily, a feeling of fun and adventure, where you get to use cool powers and pull off cool stunts and be heroic and all that, but with an undercurrent of "your choices have serious repercussions", such that dilemmas are frequently coming up in play ("Here's your best friend, who has become a Black Knight and joined the Evil Empire's side. He's challenging you to a duel. What are you gonna do?" -- OK, not the most original example, but you get the idea) and what you choose has serious implications not only for the resolution of the situation but also on your character's long-term development.

    \n Hi Marshall,

    I remember reading about the Otherkind dice a while back (though I forgot that's what they were called), and so that system has been kicking around in my head for a while.

    I'm starting to grope toward something that feels like it might work, built around a variation of the Otherkind dice. I'll throw out the gist of it here, then develop it more later...

    Skills, goals, relationships, virtues, and other traits all have a number and type of dice assigned to them.
    Skills are the weakest, so they use the smallest die size. An A in Science might give you 4d6, but those are still merely d6, while relationships, goals, and earned virtues might use d8s and d10s. Details to be worked out.

    When a conflict starts, each side identifies what it's trying to do. Each one of these becomes a "category". It might be helpful to mark the categories on paper. So, I'm trying to lead the giant away from the marketplace; the giant is trying to smoosh me. We draw two circles on a piece of paper and label one "lead away" and the other "smoosh".

    I roll in all the dice that are relevant to how I'm trying to do what I'm doing. If it's relevant to my personal goal, I get those goal dice. If I'm acting in accordance with a virtue in which I already have points, I get to roll in those dice too. And so on. What virtue, if any, my action corresponds to is preferably judged by a player without a stake in the conflict. Stabbing the giant in the toe to get his attention might be Violence, while telling the giant that I know where he can find an entire buffalo to eat might be Honesty. (With sufficient creativity, I can probably figure out a way to use just about any virtue for any conflict. Encouraging creativity is good.)

    My insecurities/mental flaws have dice too. If I have an insecurity that's relevant to this conflict, I give those dice to the giant to roll against me. (And I get an experience point or a reroll chip or some other bennie each time I get screwed like this.)

    The dice all get rolled. Each side chooses one of their dice to put into each of the circles on the paper. (If it's player vs. NPC, the GM puts the dice out first, then the player. If it's a PC vs PC conflict, each side chooses secretly, then all are revealed at once. Maybe?)

    Highest die in each category wins the category. Someone narrates what happened to get us to this point. (Highest individual die gets the narration? Or perhaps the narration rights are a third, implicit category which always exists?)

    The conflict isn't over yet. Whoever is not happy with the results as they stand can start a follow-up conflict by narrating whatever they're doing next, to get more dice. The GM can spend chips out of some kind of "threat pool" to introduce new threats or complications into the scene, which give the GM more dice and/or introduce new categories under threat.

    A player doesn't have threat chips, but can allow a new category to be threatened in order to roll in another trait which hasn't yet been used in this scene. For instance, if I have a relationship with Sue The Giant Intelligent Eagle that I haven't yet used in this scene, I can narrate how Sue is helping me, get the dice for that relationship, but I have to introduce a new "Sue gets hurt" threat category, to which each side assigns a die.

    All dice already rolled stand as they are. If I'm currently losing in one category, but in my new roll I get a die high enough to beat my opponent's die, I can swap the new die in there and discard the old one.

    I can't roll in a trait which I've already rolled in this scene. Say Peace <- -> Violence is one of the scales, and I've already used Violence: I can't get more dice by comitting more violence. I have to think of something different to try. I am allowed to use the opposite of a trait that I've already used, so I can try to use Peace: I get a bunch of dice, but in so doing I've sacrificed one of my points of Violence. Maybe I get one die, of the highest die size, for each point that I previously had in Violence. (So the more I was invested in using any one virtue to solve problems, the more of a reward I get if I reconsider that investment and give up some of my power to try the opposite approach.) It would work in exactly the same way if I was going the other direction, from peace to violence. This option would always be available as a kind of last resort; it's a small character-transformation moment each time it happens.

    The process of adding new threatened categories and rolling in new dice can go back and forth several times, until both sides accept the result as it stands. The owner of the highest die in each category decides what the resolution of that category is.
