\n I was thinking about the direction that TSR was taking the hobby before it was bought out by WotC. 2nd Edition had already moved quite a bit away from 1st Edition and BECMI/RC in terms of putting more things in the players hands, and various supplement books also supported players quite well. Their 2 spin off games, SAGA and Alternity, explicitly and implicitly supported player empowerment in their core mechanics. SAGA had it set up so that players determine actions for everything, both attack and defense, so everything is centered on them. In Alternity, it wasn't explicitly stated, but having a roll under system with a situation die modifier also gave players control. Rather than AD&D where you'd roll to hit and then say "I hit AC 3 or better, is it a hit?" or d20 where you roll and say "I rolled a 25, does it hit?", you could go straight to rolling d20+situation die, comparing the result and saying "I hit for 7 damage".

They're different approaches to player empowerment, SAGA lets the players control any task resolution, and Alternity gives the players enough information from the results to narrate the outcome, without having to wait for confirmation or permission. I find this to be more empowering than fate/action points, which usually amount to a slight number boost or re-roll, but don't change anything else.

What I'm curious about, is what other systems have either implicit or explicit assumptions in their base mechanics that give players more control over the narrative, and not just through having a limited resource to introduce details into the game, but throughout the game having them tell the story of the action happening while the GM has more of a setting the scene role.

    \n I'm not sure that what you're defining there (which I've always heard it awkwardly phrased as "player-facing rolls") is really about player empowerment, but:

    * Unisystem games (Buffy, Angel), when dealing with non-important characters, are entirely player-facing. Players roll their attacks and try to beat a goon's (mostly) static defense number - players roll their defense and try to beat a goon's (mostly) static attack number.

    * Icons - same thing, but with Fate dice.

    The reason I'm not really sure that it's about empowerment is because it's quite possible in all these systems (at least the ones I know about) for the GM to set the target number very high (for a big challenge) or very low (for an easier go). I think it's more about reducing the randomness and making players more able to estimate what their characters can do - 1d20 -1d20 has a much more significant potential range of outcomes than 1d20.

    \n Where I'm seeing the player empowerment is that they don't need to ask the GM for permission to do something, or for confirmation if it worked (some of that does come in with SAGA, but you know that easy is 4, average is 8, hard is 12, crazy is 16, etc, so you can estimate and usually pick a card that succeeds, so there's rarely any doubt about if you succeed or not, and if you fail it probably wasn't that important to you). If you take D20, there's very little difference between having the DM do all the rolls and having the player do the rolls, since the DM adjudicates anyway. If you have a system where the players know immediately that they've succeeded or not, there's the option to do something like rolling to attack, seeing that you hit and dealing the damage, and there's really no room for vetoeing it.

    Charlie: (rolls dice) "Ragnar swings his axe and buries the blade deep in the Ogre's flesh for 13 damage."

    vs

    Charlie: (rolls dice) "I got a 19, does that hit the ogre?"

    In the first example, Charlie's actually telling part of the story, in the second one, the GM is telling the whole story, with some occasional input from the players. It's rather like the difference between being on stage doing the improv and having a director who sets the scenes, and being in the audience and occasionally called upon to give some suggestions which the actors will work with. Audience with input vs creating a story.

    \n Okay. I think The Pool was, for many people, a seminal game in this regard. The other examples are all a little more subtle; this one is really in-your-face.

    That game works like this:

    When a situation comes up that requires a roll, the player rolls (never the GM). Either player or GM can call for a roll; neither can veto the other in this respect.

    A roll consists of rolling a pool of six-sided dice. You must roll at least one "1" to succeed. This means that the only determiner of the difficulty is the number of dice rolled.

    However, it is the player who decides how many dice to roll: s/he has a pool of dice and simply picks a number of them to roll. (In the original game, the GM can modify this slightly by adding a couple of dice, but it doesn't make a huge difference, and many people and many variants ignore that rule in any case.)

    So as a player, when you want to accomplish something, can decide when you want to roll, and then effectively set the difficulty by choosing how many dice you will roll.

    On top of that, when you succeed in a roll you can choose to gain another die *or* you can choose to narrate the outcome: describe *how* you succeed. This means that if you care enough about something in play, you can ask to roll, roll all your dice, and then describe exactly how it goes down.

    When I first read The Pool, it shocked me, because up until then I'd never seen a game in which it was impossible for a GM to railroad the players (my friends still didn't believe it was possible, even after I told them about this game). It works due to a clever risk-balancing mechanic which effectively entices you to roll smaller numbers of dice (especially in some of the variants), since you risk losing any dice you roll.

    I hope that helps. The Pool is often cited (and with good reason) as one of the seminal games of the indie/story game movement, and has been a powerful influence on many, many game designs.

    \n If you know the TN or or the AC or whatever, up front than you can narrate what happens after you roll pretty effectively. In D&D combat, at least, there's a fixed number to roll that if you're familiar with the system you can almost always guess at effectively.

    The important thing in D20 isn't whose rolling the dice, but what the player chooses to roll for, and how they build their characters up front. Also, the dearth of rules in D20 is empowering in a sense because it lets players no explicitly how hard things are and what they need to do to reliably accomplish various effects, this is very different than SAGA, which, iirc, only has explicit rules for combat and spell casting and in the case of spell casting there's a lot of GM judgement calls as to where a particular effect falls on the various charts and tables.

    Rule 0/house rules aside, the players have a very fixed set of abilities and expectations in D20, which I also see as a (different) form of player empowerment.

    \nThe reason he pointed out Alternaity as different, was because when the player rolls his dice, he does not need any further input from the GM. He can roll the dice, know he has succeeded (in fact, in a reversal, the GM may have to ask him if he has succeeded, not the other way around), and immediately go to rolling damage or tell the GM that the NPC is mortally injured and needs to make a roll to stay conscious, or whatever.

    For combat:
    Unisystem: Player declares attack, Player rolls dice, adds skill, reports result. The GM tells him it's a success, checks a table for how much he has succeeded, and tells him what damage to roll or whatever.
    Alternity: Player declares action, GM tells him difficulty, Player rolls dice, compares to skill, finds result, reports that and rolls damage. GM then applies armour if any, and reports if enemy is still standing.

    Notice how the GM can't then fudge the result to turn a success into a failure. He can stack the deck by reporting a high difficulty, but it is transparent when he does this.
