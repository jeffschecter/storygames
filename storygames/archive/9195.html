    \n I've played a couple of times with a table full of non-gamers, and each time it went very well, so don't worry on that score. My advice to you is my advice to everybody - start out strong and model good behavior in your first scene.

    Use the "I love you and you hate me, I hate you and you love me" thing to kickstart a conflict. Bring in a Luminary and do your best to ruin the guy you hate. Establish some piece of backstory while you are doing it. Explain what you are doing as you do it.

    Vigorously takes sides in the first round. Be really big about supporting one player and building a faction to take down another. Drop dice in every scene, just to model being constantly involved.

    Another good trick for somebody who is creatively stuck is to encourage them to frame a scene in which they can include their Expertise and both Enthusiasms. You cannot have a boring scene with both Enthusiasms in play.

    In one game I intimated in my first scene that there was a Pemberton Rod and Gun Club, and that its preferred quarry was the bottom fifth of the undergraduate class. The PC I hated was an Assistant Professor, new to campus, so I framed a scene around his Rod and Gun Club initiation.

    One last suggestion - unless you are spreading it across two nights, consider trimming a couple of scenes, since things will probably go a little slowly. Just take a vote and drop one active scene and one more sedentary scene. Leave the Luminaries as they are and end on an active scene.

    Have fun!

    \n 1b, 2c.

    I like Roach, but I find it difficult with players who aren't used to that sort of game. It really requires some sophisticated storytelling skills. Get everyone used to using Director stance: narrating stuff apart from just their character. Similarly, they need to be driving towards conflict, while being prepared to enjoy losing. Most importantly, it's not really a competitive game. The competitiveness is an illusion, although a fun one.

    On the horror aspect: I personally think that it's important not to drive the horror up too soon. Keep it creepy and character-based towards the start, then go mad towards the end.

    I find Roach requires a lot of energy. Breaks between events are good. If you find the group is running out of creative ideas, take a longer break and resume as necessary.

    Graham

    \n 1b - once there's a clear conflict, make sure you know who is on which side and see if more roleplaying needs to happen. After it is resolved, there's probably more roleplay and narration to be had as well.

    2c - roll the dice when the uncertain outcome is going to halt the scene unless it is resolved. Be alert for this point so nobody ends up spinning their wheels.

    3c - stake-setting can conform to your local play culture just fine. I prefer B but have played A, and even both in the same game.
