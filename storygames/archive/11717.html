\n Hi,

If I commission artwork for my game before I sort out the layout (or, more accurately, before I arrange for someone else to sort out the layout), will that cause me any problems? I know what size I want my book to be (6x9 paperback) and most of the art will be free-floating in the sense that it won't have to match up to a particular point in the text. This suggests to me that I should be OK, but I'm very much aware that I Know Nothing here. So, is there a correct sequence to do this in, or should it be done simultaneously, or what?

Thanks for the help,

    \n Nah, it should be fine. Make sure you get black and white art at 600 DPI. And always commission it from the artist at a bigger size than you plan to use. For example, if you're planning on using 2" vignettes, make sure you tell the artist that you need 4" pieces.

    \n More important than any of that is having a game that's actually done and ready to be put into layout. I speak from personal experience -- and those of others -- having commissioned hundreds of dollars of artwork (probably in the thousands by now) for game products that have never actually materialized.

    Do you have a final text? If not, it may be too early to do a real layout with art. Bang something reasonably attractive together and use it for playtesting. Use whatever art you want, since nobody's going to sue you for a "personal use" PDF. Check out Ghost/Echo as an example (psst. John didn't license that art, as far as I know).

    If you do have a game that's totally done, playtested, and ready for final layout... why haven't I heard of it? You should get on that marketing thing.

    \n Mark, it sounds like you may be a step ahead of this, but for anyone else who reads this: I've done pretty much what Jonathon suggested and been pretty happy with the results (see a draft here). Ghost/Echo is what a skilled designer can do for a playtest doc, my example is what a complete non-designer can manage.

    You can make a pretty decent looking doc using non-pro tools and public domain art. The PDF there was produced in Pages, using open source fonts and public domain images. It's not great, it was one of my first attempts at layout, but it at least has some visual appeal.
