Conflict inside the skin

TonyLB
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Play Advice

So I suspect we're all pretty cool with the idea of the following sequence: You play a knight. You want him to climb a high, slick tower. He tries and fails. He gets ropes, tries and fails. Finally he gives up, and goes and does a quest to help people. Upon this quest he gains a map that shows him the secret path to climb the tower. He returns and tries to climb up the high, slick tower and succeeds. Yay!

I also suspect that some folks may not be as comfortable with the idea of the following sequence: You play a high school student. You want him to admit his love to a girl in his class. He tries and fails. He manages to find time alone with her when some friends are out at a theme park, and fails. Finally he gives up and goes and helps his friend with other emotional issues. Doing this he gains insight into his own fears and how they were holding him back. He returns and tries to admit his feelings to the girl and succeeds. Yay!

So ... how come this is so difficult?

Is it that it violates our sense that we are the character ... since those internal constraints that stop the character don't exist in us, the player?

Is it that it violates our suspension of disbelief, because while we're willing to give a system the benefit of the doubt about whether a guy can climb a tower, we're sure we know whether a guy can talk to a girl?

Is it because it robs the players of choices, by telling them that they simply cannot have their character do certain things?

Is it because it parcels out authority to narrate in a way with which we are not familiar (specifically not giving one person absolute rights over everything that happens inside their character's skin)?

Or is it something else entirely. What the hell's going on here, anyway?

    I actually think that the "how" of emotional failure is key to whether players are willing to accept it. I know that I would feel very differently about the following two examples:

    Player: So, I sit down next to her and say, "Hey, I like you." (Much abstracted, okay? )
    GM: You stammer and blush. The words just won't come out, and she's looking at you like you're nuts.

    Player: So, I sit down next to her and say, "Hey, I like you."
    GM: Actually, you can't do that. You're too shy to tell her that.

    In the former example, I feel that I, as the player, have a choice of how to react to my inability. I can choose how I feel about it, where it's coming from, etc. I have a variety of interesting ways to react to the problem. In the latter case, I'm being told exactly how I feel about things. My behavior is being constrained in much less interesting ways. I don't mind emotional conflicts for my character that are introduced by other players or the GM, but they have to be introduced as conflicts - not as fiat about how I'm going to play back to it.

    Consider the comparable examples with the tower:

    Player: So, I try to climb the tower. Check out my cool rope!
    GM: Sure, but your rope breaks. What next?

    Player: So, I try to climb the tower. Check out my cool rope!
    GM: You're too afraid to try to climb the tower. What next?

    One empowers the player to respond, the other constrains their possible responses, not just on the scale of "What next?" but possibly in the long term of the game.

    As a player and GM, I probably would never phrase my stakes in terms of system rather than story - I'd say something like "I want to make her heart beat faster when she thinks of me," and then take care of the mechanical effects outside the scene if and when they came up, but that's a minor detail.

    Actually, I'm not at all sure that it is. The system is a means of communication, of the player and GM agreeing on what is established and what is free to narrate. If you don't have that communication channel, it makes it harder for the GM to cede narration to the player. Imagine, for instance, this exchange:

    Player: I sit down with her and say "I like you."
    GM: And you're trying to achieve?
    Player: I want her heart to beat faster when she thinks of me.
    GM: Okay ... and we roll and ... not going to happen. He fails. Why is that?
    Player: Because he's such a calming presence that their deep and abiding love isn't about her heart beating faster ... she feels so comfortable with him that it calms her and gives her peace.
    GM: Yeah, y'know what? No. That's not a failure. I'll narrate you a failure, since you clearly can't be trusted to do it right.

    That exchange suffers from a lack of communication between the two people, and therefore from an inability for the player to confidently narrate something that will be satisfying to the GM.

    Now you can open the communications channels wider, even without what people think of as "system" (though, of course ... it is ... it's all system) by more clearly defining your results before hand ("Okay, if you win her heart beats faster, if you lose ... what?")

    And now, having run off on a tangent, I'm going to bend it back around and make it a side-trip ... hopefully even one of value. I think that the question of making sure that both sides are satisfied with the outcome is another of the hard things in playing inside the skin. Some of us (well, some of you ... not me) shy away from carefully considering the impact of failing a conflict inside the skin.

    Specifically: How is the young man who fails to say anything to the girl worse off than he was before the encounter? I mean ... we all know that he is, right? That feeling stings. But it's hard to pull it out and think about it. Is he so hurt that he won't be able to even approach her tomorrow? Does he give up? Or is he all the more obsessed with expressing himself, filled with ardor and zeal? And if the player has a choice of which of these paths he pursues, what are the consequences of one choice over another?

    All these things are hard to quantify and agree on, compared to (for instance) "You fell off the tower. Your ankle is twisted. So, you still can run, but it's going to hurt like hell and do lasting damage if you do."
