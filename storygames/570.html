How does the GM advance?

TonyLB
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games

No, I'm not talking about any social-contract dreck like "By becoming a more skilled GM" or "By earning the trust of the players." Damned hippies.

In many games, as I'm playing, my character becomes a more effective instrument of my righteous awesomeness. My fledgling little 1st level mage becomes an unstoppable 20th level juggernaut of mystic might. He becomes a better tool for me to do the things I want to do. I, as a player, make this happen. It is my reward for being cool, and it draws me forward.

I know of no games where the GM is similarly rewarded. Where are the mechanics that will let me advance my world from one where the biggest real threat is a band of goblins (and everything else is "far away") to one where elder dragons flap in to have tea and subtly threaten genocide against the mud-races?

Why must the poor GM, selfless martyr that she is, be deprived of the simple pleasure of advancement?

    Brand and John Harper both mentioned Rune -- but for those who aren't familiar with it.

    Rune is a 2001 game by Robin Laws, a game of fantasy Viking action based on the computer game from Human Head Studios. Both players and "runners" (i.e. the person running a given encounter) gain victory points. But Runners don't get Victory points purely for beating the heroes. They get victory points for:
    • "Almost Down, But Not Quite Out"
      Taking a hero almost entirely down without killing them.
    • "Chortle at the Misfortunes of Others"
      Player failures at specific rolls.
    • "When Terrain comes into Play"
      When terrain or traps get used either way in a fight (max of four times for 80 points).
    • "Proxy Score"
      Half points for the runner's own PC score (controlled by another player during the encounter)
    Note that the runner is the person controlling the scenario, which could be pre-packaged or designed by someone else. If he's also the designer and he got points from each of the other categories, then he gets an extra bonus. So the runner gets blackjack points of a sort. He wants to heavily wound but not kill the heroes, and wants to drag in terrain up to four times.

    Bear in mind that I haven't played it either, so take what I say with a grain of salt. But I've got my copy on the shelf by me at the moment, so...

    Victory points can only be spent to increase your own character's abilities. However, when a majority of the group of players passes certain thresholds of total victory points, then the designer gets extra points to build encounters with.

    Note that you yourself get victory points by running an encounter. So increasing your victory points helps increase the number of encounter build points you'll get next time -- but you have to get a little under half the other players to cross the threshold with you.

    For example, suppose there are five players. I'm GM/"runner" for this encounter. If me and two other players all pass 500 total victory points in this encounter, then for my next encounter I'll get 50 more encounter points.

    Okay, I was trying to whisper this, but that's tricky to notice on this board. Anyway ....

    The players would get a general amount of Exp for participating, but then they would gain extra depending on their actions. There also may have been some sort of bonus for "going it alone" against a monster, I don't quite remember (we played a bunch of homebrews back then).

    Okay, you are describing absolutely classic positive feedback here. A 2nd level fighter is going to get the general amount of XP and nothing else. If he tries for something more (and particularly for "going it alone" bonusses) he dies. So, because he's the underdog, he gets the bare minimum of resources. This tends to make him more the underdog as time goes on.

    Likewise, the 5th level person gets the bare minimum, plus achievement, plus going it alone. He skyrockets away. Again, very characteristic of positive feedback.

    Consider, by comparison, this sort of system: Everybody gets XPs for every monster killed by the party ... doesn't matter who killed it. You was there. You get nothing for anything less than your level killed by the party. You get 100 points for any monster of your level killed by the party, 200 for your level +1, 400 for your level +2, etc., etc.

    So, your players go against 3 4th level monsters, 3 5th and 1 7th.
    Your 5th level characters get (3 x 0) + (3 x 100) + (1 x 400) = 700 XPs.
    Your 2nd level characters get (3 * 400) + (3 * 800) + (1 * 3200) = 6800 XPs.

    That way, the underdogs actually get rewarded more than the powerful. With those numbers, it doesn't take very long (at all!) before everybody's very, very close to the same levels again. That's negative feedback in action.

    Okay, this is wandering into very negative territory (thanks largely to YOU, Jack ... sheesh ... "because it was boring" ... Mr. Helpful). I'd like to focus it down a little and see if we can go from theoretical agreement that this could work to talking about the craft of how it would work.

    So ... advancement in many games is by increasing the player's power to impact the game. The player's power to impact the game is a bundle of things which are often called "the character."

    This is totally not a representation of one person and their abilities. That's a historical artifact that colors people's perception to the point where they say downright stupid things like "Well, the strength of the world-wide Templar Order is a character ability of Sir Frederick," when any sensible person would say "Uh ... aren't there a few more people in the Templar Order than just Sir Frederick?"

    I got no problem with that. There's no dysfunction that I can detect. People want to (a) give players vast powers to effect the story ("Templar Order") and (b) anthropomorphize those powers into the shape of one particular avatar ("Sir Frederick"). They seem to achieve both goals with no more than a small mental sleight of hand, and I think that's cool.

    But it does put us in a sort of strange position when we try to visualize the bundle of things that represent the GM's input and control over the game. We don't (usually) have a handy face to slap over that bundle to anthropomorphize it ... and so a lot of our ideas of advancement get left out in the cold.

    So: can the world advance as a bundle of narrative levers? Can the GM be given progressively more powers as his bundle of levers increases?

    And, also, what kind of choices would she face along the way? If I'm advancing my Mage, I make some decisions: do I seek out magic items in ancient tombs (making a gadget-laden James-Bond-esque mage?) or do I spend time at the books (making a potent scholar with access to many personal spells) or do I branch into some sort of specialty path (illusionist, necromancer, multi-class)? All of these result in different bundles of narrative levers for me down the road.

    If I'm a GM, advancing my World ... do I establish relationship maps between the principals, or do I bulk up on threats and adversity, or do I branch off into some specialty path (political intrigue, epic destiny, comedy)? All of these could (with the right system support) result in different bundles of narrative levers for me down the road.

However, it took a turn and I do want to kick in a little bit with a "middlin'" mechanic I put together for MURPG. Now, for those of you who aren't one of the nine people who bought the Marvel Universe RPG, this game is played with "stones" of effort, you put stones in the action boxes on your sheet that your character is doing and those are compared to a target number or the stones of their opponent.

      One thing I have always tried to do in comic book games is to simulate the rising and falling action of the badguys. That is, no matter how wuss the villain or how brazenly idiotic their plan, in the first segment of the plot arc, they're winning, they can do nothing wrong, the heroes are falling all over themselves just to keep things hanging on. Once the heroes gather some information, have some personal crises, and so on, they start to win, and by the end, no contingency plan, however masterful, can save the villain, no matter how powerful.

      In Adventure!, I did this by giving a boatload of Inspiration to the villains, but not allowing them to regenerate it, as the heroes did.

      In MURPG, with the stone mechanic, I implemented the following rule:

      There are two Drama Pools, one for the Villains, and one for the Heroes. (Note that non-villainous NPCs cannot draw on any Drama Pools.) All stones in Drama Pools are red stones.

      The Heroes' Drama Pool starts with one stone for every player character.

      The Villains' Drama Pool starts with one stone for every player character, plus one stone for every rank of EITHER: Wealth, Intelligence or Influence that the main villain of the scenario has. If there is a team of villains, they must pick a single stat from a single villain. (Ed. note - I wanted the masterminds to have a longer arc than the brutes, after all their contingency plans are what make things last so long in comic book arcs).

      The Drama Pool provides extra red stones to any Action at any time. The normal "cap" of the Action Number still applies, and you cannot use more red stones from your Drama Pool than you are using from your own Energy Pool. (Put another way, at least half of the red stones you are putting in the Action must be from your own Energy Pool.) (Ed. note - I wanted it to be a boost but not to completely get rid of the underlying fatigue/injury/effort system.)

      When Villains spend a stone out of their Drama Pool, one is added to the Heroes' Drama Pool. (The reverse is not true.) (Ed. note: Thus, the Heroes' pool is constantly being replenished as villains beat on them.)

      When a Hero faces a Limitation, a red stone is added to the Drama Pool. (Ed. note: "Oh no, everyone hates mutants...I guess I'll go beat up evil chicks with bad hair...!")

      When a Hero takes blue stone damage, a red stone is added to the Drama Pool. (Only one stone is added no matter how many stones of damage the Hero suffers.)

      Drama Pools are restarted after every Mission.

      Hope this helps to show a "middle path" whereby this sort of thing can be added to "regular" systems.
