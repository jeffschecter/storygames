    \n Everway used a form of Tarot for its resolution; after declaring your intent, you drew a card and then interpreted whether that led to a positive or negative outcome, depending on the name of the card, the art printed on it and the cleverness you displayed in linking the former to your narrative.

    Psychosis used Tarot mostly for determining the shifting nature of the illusions that covered the true nature of whatever your actual situation was, in a way similar to the above, e.g. the illusions was whatever the card was interpreted as. Its been a long time since I played it, but I think it also used the Minor Arcana to resolve conflicts, based on comparing the rank of the card to a target... my memory is hazy.

    Most recently, Hell 4 Leather (the Joe Prince one) also uses Tarot as an inspirational story-telling device, as in the above two games, but also uses the Minor Arcana for minor conflict resolutions and a special 'Death' draw at the end of every scene to see if vengeance is enacted on one of the Fool's victims.

    In all cases, the use of Tarot cards focuses more on the imagery & meaning of the cards than any precise mechanical effects, so they largely depend on the creative spontaneity of those playing the game and an agreement on what is fair & reasonable within the social contract of the group. Beyond that, task/conflict resolution usually comes down to using draws or plays from the Minor Arcana to meet some target number... or again, just interpreting the imagery on the cards to determine success & failure.

    \n This topic was actually going to be the second of my "Unexploited Resource" entries for my blog.

    Lots of games use tarot for it's symbolism (including many of the games mentioned so far in the thread)...

    ...most games that use Tarot for their symbolism (whether a game-specific custom tarot, or a general form), don't use it as an actual resolution mechanism (including many of the games mentioned so far in the thread).

    My own game that's languishing in development hell, "Brigaki Djili", specifically uses Tarot Cards as character traits. Offering the upright reading as a beneficial aspect of a characters personality, while the reversed meaning indicates a detrimental aspect that always accompanies this trait. Players build up their characters over the course of a game using a five card spread.

    \n David Berg's game "Delve" uses a modified Tarot deck (I think he makes it himself) to design adventures. And then the characters/players can make a Tarot "reading", which gives them vague clues about the adventure, in a reverse of the process. That's pretty cool because it's both real-world and in the fiction, too: your characters are actually drawing those same cards that you're drawing at the table to divine some clues about their mystery.

    \n I'm going to vouch again for Joe Prince's Hell 4 Leather. I had a chance to play it at Conpulsion 2010. The Tarot resolution system is elegant, involving four layers of interpretation.

    Layer 1: Scene framing. Six of the major arcana are used to start six specific acts in the game, each linked in identity to the card used. These are fixed. That is, they happen in the same way in every game.

    Layer 2: Characters. At the start of every scene, all but one player is dealt a character card from a fixed subset of the major arcana. Players play these characters during the scene and may opt to hold onto them for the following scene. These characters are interpreted by the player who holds them.

    Layer 3: Conflict resolution. When any of the Characters conflict during a scene, a card is drawn to determine the outcome. The interpretation is done by a neutral party.

    Layer 4: Driving mechanic. During a scene, the chief protagonist (a character fixed to one player) may opt to kill one of the other characters. This is done by shuffling some of the major arcana, laying them out on the table and then attempting to pick out the Death card from the available cards. Very moody and tense.
