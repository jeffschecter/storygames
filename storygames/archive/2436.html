Okay, so over in The Great Divide thread, Matt suggests that there is this divide between two RPG markets that have historically been conflated: those who enjoy creation and challenging old forms, and those who enjoy partaking and celebrating in the forms created by others.

Those who enjoy partaking and celebrating content and doing so in an interactive medium used to worship their (creative-type) GMs and buy lots and lots of game supplements, but now they are better and more conveniently serviced by Massively Multiplayer Online games, such as World of Warcraft or Xbox Live.

Creative types, by contrast, are attracted to indie and story games. Indie and story games allow all of the participants to be creative collaboratively. The players serve as their own audience for the creative endeavor.

Assuming that the far larger market of partake-and-celebrate folks continues to drain away out of the RPG hobby, we are left with just the create-and-challenge folks. If we are to make a market out of just them, rather than assuming they and their partake-and-celebrate friends play together, weâ€™re looking at a pretty fundamental shift in the market. Itâ€™s much smaller, first of all, and more spread out, but these are logistical concerns â€“ large ones, but relatively straightforward.

The question that comes to me, though, is whether or not you can really build a market of creative people who have no audience but each other. Is six people getting together to collaborate on something that no one else will ever see going to be fulfilling enough to keep those six people participating in the hobby (with or without staying in the same group)? Or does a fundamental piece of human creativity require an audience to show it to?

We can talk in abstract all day long, but if this participants=audience thing does work, one assumes that it occurs elsewhere, somewhere. I asked the question: what other communities practice highly creative, highly collaborative hobbies in which there is no audience outside of the participants? Have any of these communities lasted more than a single generation, thereby implying that the activity itself, rather than the friendships of participants, was the core of the community?

So far, we have:
Garage Bands with no intent on ever playing for an audience. I am skeptical of their existence, but Iâ€™d like to hear more.
Poetry Slams in which the entire audience is assumed to be stepping up to the mike at some point during the night.
The Society for Creative Anachronism, which is in its second (and sometimes third) generation, has people make period clothing, dress up, act funny, play-fight, cook period food, and camp.

There may be other examples. Iâ€™d love to hear them.

The question for this thread is: is this the shape of RPGs to come? What lessons can we learn from these examples? What changes will need to be made to RPGs to approximate these cultural phenomena?

Seriously, the common thread I see between all three examples thus far is sex. Status within the community figures into the latter two. Food, too. Another common denominator is expression of self, which is actually downplayed in most RPGs.

    Seriously, though, I think this is part and parcel with the Web 2.0 phenomenon and the increased culture of participation that's slowly growing worldwide. Weinberger's Small Pieces Loosely Joined makes some good points about it which I talk about here. Expression of self, participation, authenticity, engagement, exploration are the themes I see coming out.

    Is this the shape of RPGs to come? I believe and hope so.

    What lessons can we learn?
    * That it's still important to have people with high levels of creativity to create spaces for people with lower levels of creativity to participate meaningfully.
    * That you never know what you'll get.
    * That it's essential, in fact, not to define what you mustn't get, apart from what is minimally necessary to keep the whole thing from melting down in mutual acrimony.
    * That what you get won't be anything like perfect, but it will be real.
    * That the process is the important thing, not the product, if there even is a product.
    * That we're more than just consumers.

    What changes need to be made to RPGs to approximate this?
    * The GM needs to fade into the background and become a facilitator, but probably not disappear entirely except in groups where everyone is reasonably experienced at being creative for themselves.
    * There need to be multiple "ways in" to engage with the material from different angles and in different ways, to suit people's differing levels of commitment and ability as well as their various ways of thinking.
    * Building on past material that is time-tested and possibly familiar is a good start, alongside the innovation (I just this moment realized that I have Awesome-o-fied the rosary).
    * You can assemble things from diverse sources and they'll just work together, if you expect them to.

    I haven't explored all the methods of collaboration that might be happening in the Sims community, but here's what I know about:

    1. those able to create custom content (skins, eyes, clothing, object recolors, and hacked content like hair) sometimes work in artistic teams to make themed sets, or take requests from the rest of the community that can't create satisfactory custom content; the rest of the community uses that custom content in stories.     3. the community creates challenges that everyone can participate in, either by uploading a particularly difficult house setup or by describing rules you have to abide by and score yourself on, like the Ethiopian Challenge (no refrigerators or other food-preparation objects on the lot, no electric items, no buying dinner at restaurants.)     Bryan's got a good point. Creativity can mean different things. When indie designers talk about creativity, it seems to me that it's something very high-end and personal. But if you think about, say, ordinary people playing a pickup game of basketball or football or something, there's a kind of creativity there, too. It's the unexpected way people build on what's already happened. If you call that first kind of creativity "imagination", I think you can see why Ryan Dancey said that the majority of roleplayers don't care about imagination. They care about enjoying an established world and, indirectly, the second kind of creativity, which I would call "spontaneity".

    I get the feeling that some of us get too hung up on the interpersonal part of roleplaying. These people we're talking about, the ones who drop out of roleplaying because it's easier to play World of Warcraft, they feel the same way about interpersonal communication as they do about imagination: yeah, if it happens, it's good, but that's not the main reason they play the game. If either interpersonal communication or imagination were their main goal, they have other more direct ways of meeting that goal. They play WoW because they can log on at any time and play even if they don't meet up with anyone else. I imagine it's the same with Second Life (it's synchronous, sure, but you can also just explore, anytime you feel like it.) It's certainly the case with The Sims, which has interpersonal communication, but that's not necessary... and, in fact, The Sims Online (which had the missing synchronous communication) failed.

    Most of the pastimes and hobbies we've been talking about that "don't have an audience other than the participants" can actually have an accidental audience, it's just not the main point of the activity.

    I've been expounding a theory that comes from some stuff that Chris Lerich posted over at The Forge a couple of years back. Here's the short version. Before books (before the European Church, really) we told stories to each other in order to transmit values. Shaman's telling stories, or just father's to sons, whatever. With books and centralized religion, you get one version of a story told over and over, and no creative input from the reader, nor feedback. It's a one way transmission process. And I'm not going to be judgmental about that either - to each their own.

    But I think that the urge to be a shaman, or at least to transmit values orally through "story" is a strong urge. And that we've accidentally rediscovered it through RPGs. So Mike's association with his "decentralized religion" is spot on. It's all about sharing your values with people who are empowered to provide instant feedback in a social way. With an audience in a theater, you may get applause or boos, but you don't have a social context for that feedback. You don't know that it's Brand telling you that something is wrong, and so you don't know why. That's key to this personal format.

    I'd almost go so far as to say that it's a superior form, but that would be my bias showing. Let's just say that nothing fits the particular goals in question like a RPG does.

    Oh, and BTW, if you've ever wondered why RPG discussions about mode of play and such can become so heated? It's because they are precisely the same thing as discussions about religion. It's questioning how somebody does their value transmission in play.

    Mike

We can take it as assumed, and then look at the particular derivative need in question. We have to eat, too, in order to live to reproduce, but that doesn't make eating the same as playing RPGs.
