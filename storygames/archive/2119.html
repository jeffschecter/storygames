      hi Nate,

      I'm glad the book put a decent amount of focus on the politics & policy of a crisis situation

      but I had a few problems with the way it was done

      first, since the book is basically a survivalist fantasy writ large scale, it has many of the paranoid assumptions of that genre: the smug satisfaction of the prepared sparing little sympathy for the fools who weren't ready as quickly, the loving attention to weaponry, the approval for the strategic sacrifice of hundreds of millions of people to the threat.

      second, it has the fascist lite line that existing governments and institutions are weak and corrupt and will fail under stress - that they need to be reconfigured under strong leaders and shocked back into any kind of functioning.

      third, it follows that weird assumption that the best way to ensure international cooperation is a good old external alien threat: the r. reagan space invaders theory of global fellowship

      fourth, the book's political heroes are A. an apartheid apologist, B. Colin Powell, and C. the British Royal family (A. and B. get 'assists' from someone who may be Mandela and Howard Dean to make it safely bipartisan.). Somehow these three fairly conservative and inflexible institutions are the ones that adapt and lead the world, which could be meant as irony, who knows.

      fifth, the book was published during an unpopular war during an era in which it is harder to dehumanize the enemy than during some previous conflicts. And it presents the perfect enemy in the sense that they are no longer human by definition- they must be wiped out entirely. Now it's not the author's fault that there's a real war on, but the parallel sometimes crept into my mind when I was reading it.

      I think those are the kinds of things i was referring to

      I recommend the book
