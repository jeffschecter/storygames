    \n But no working RPG system that I know of was designed to solve social situations\u2026
    (As a designer, I never considered that a valid aim, actually, but that's not the point.)

    What you describe is (I know you know) a mismatch of expectations regarding what the game is and what the game is for, and especially about what your role (GM) actually means in the game.
    People show up to be entertained, by you. Which wouldn't be half bad if those people at least knew how to be a good audience, but oftentimes they don't\u2026 People are not generally trained or socialized to be a respectful, grateful audience to their entertainers: instead, they generally act needy and entitled. Mostly, those who overcome this are only those who learn to master the specific art at hand and become entertainers themselves.

    Damn if I don't get this feeling all the time when I invite people over for actual dinner, not RPGs! The best dining guests are those who can cook themselves: you can see they understand, they appreciate your work and enjoy the meal - or, if they don't like it, at least they're willing to either give genuinely helpful advice or shut the fuck up/make small talk about something else entirely, right?
    Whereas, the irritating kind of guest (always somebody who can't cook) doesn't even bother to ask what you made for them: they make requests. There's the people who eat like three things in the world, full stop, and they fully expect you to make two of those three dishes for them: they won't try anything else. There's the people who always add salt or other condiments before tasting. And then there's the people who liked what you cooked them when they first visited and expect you to make the same exact thing again, and again \u2014 which might be sort-of flattering once, but it gets old fast. Mostly, these people don't even realize they're being annoying, and the crazy thing is - according to contemporary etiquette - I can't tell them. Telling them equals being all snooty and snobbish about food (huge taboo! who would ever want to sound like a hipster?), impolite to my very own guests\u2026 'Cause it's all just a matter of tastes, right? And all tastes are equal, or something.

    All of the above: not a metaphor for something else. Which I guess can be enlightening, in a way.

    \n Sandra,

    I'm not going to comment on the social situation just now - it's a fairly complicated thing, clearly - other than to say that, generally, it's always good to put your foot down. (In the same way you're doing it in-game: "Hey, here's how it works. If you show up late and don't help cook, you're on your own next time. Cool?" Now the decision is no longer on your head, you've all agreed to the consequences. And everyone will respect and like you for that. People, in general, like dealing with people who are assertive and stand up for themselves - it's easier! And yet for some reason we often fear to do that. I don't know if that's your case, but I thought I'd mention it.)

    However:

    The general frustration you're describing, and particularly the feeling you're talking about with playing Everway, is a very, very familiar one.

    I'm fairly sure that this feeling was probably the primary impetus behind the Forge and the Forge designers' attempts at redefining gaming. "Let's create systems which apportion social pressure better, so we can all have fun without playing these weird social struggle games!"

    And if I'm wrong about that... well, it was still one of the MAJOR driving forces behind the whole movement.

    I remember those discussions where Ron laid out the responsibilities of the GM, and pointed out how often things like "organizing the session", "inviting the players", "cleaning the space", "prepping for the game", "providing snacks", and so forth appeared to just be assumed to come with "tell the story", "make all the important calls in-game", "know the rules inside-and-out", and, essentially, "be responsible for the group having fun" (as you point out in your Everway experiences). That's a heavy burden (even just one of those would be), and no wonder "GM burnout" was a very common topic.

    I think the whole OSR approach (sandbox, let the dice fall where they may, principled GMing, etc) which you seem to be gravitating towards is one pretty good solution to the problem of "being responsible for the fun", and shifting that to the group itself.

    Don't be afraid to do that with the social aspects, too. Maybe someone else can host the game, for instance? I'd even imagine that if your friends are wealthy enough to go out for dinner each time you play, they wouldn't mind all chipping in a few dollars to buy you dinner in exchange for all the work you do GMing the game. Lots of options to consider here, in any case. No need to continue a lifestyle which is clearly no fun for you; play with the variables - it's a game, it should be relaxing and a good time!

    \n Every case of RPG or GM burnout I've experienced has been people and/or schedule burnout, as everything here pretty well corroborates.

    The problem is that life takes priority, so truly good solutions are often a sacrifice of the game. When our GM kept falling asleep, for example, we shifted to playing more board games.
    Sometimes it works, though. When going out to eat was expensive and cut into our gaming time, we just told everyone to arrive fed. The only reason we had developed the habit of going out to eat together was that it had become a sort of "intrigue time" that was an extension of a particular campaign with a lot of scheming. But those discussions had withered with subsequent, different games; it just took a while for us to realize it was hampering us. We also had the added benefit of not having to deal with each other at our hungriest. That was one of the best changes we ever made.

    One thing, however, that none of my groups have ever survived: scheduling. Just going from weekly to every other week stunted the hell out of one group's games and investment.
    It's possible the biggest reason for some of these problems is actually scheduling - for example, it's possible the reason these numerous duties have fallen to you is because if you didn't do them, nobody else's schedules would allow them to participate. I don't know if that's true, but I've almost always found that when I felt burnout creeping up, I realized it was because without that burnout-inducing burden, the game couldn't work.

    Opening up communication is the big step, but it's possible that communication will only make it clearer that things need to be a certain way or not at all. So don't let that new era of communication make you a victim of the pressure (internal or external) to keep a group, schedule, or particular game intact.

    \n I might add (because I find the hosting customs and strategies an interesting topic) that I've seen a pretty wide range of arrangements of the practical stuff related to the cultural salon that is the rpg session over the years. Three things that I've been particularly fond of, and that may not be as common as they should:
    * Playing at a suitable restaurant or pub is fun because you get fed and watered to the extent you feel like, nobody needs to clean up afterwards, and the game segues naturally to other social activities before or after. It's not absolutely superior to a private affair due to higher average expense and venue distractions, but for certain types of game and group it is the golden ticket.
    * Having a "campaign secretary" who takes care of all the logistics instead of the GM is so very, very functional; it is unfortunate that gamers seem to have some cultural misconceptions that often prevent "ordinary " non-GM players from committing to a campaign to a degree where they're willing to host, negotiate schedules and deal with catering. When I've managed to appoint a secretary, whether I was GMing myself or being that secretary, it's always been a very worthwhile and natural distribution of duties. Nowadays my first thought about a major campaign plan concerns whether I could find an equally motivated co-pilot on whom I could hoist the secretarial duties
    * Further splitting off hosting from catering, particularly when the GM is hosting (which can be easiest for everybody when playing something where the GM has 40 kg of stuff they use for the game) is a very good idea. I love it when players bring food when I'm hosting, and I love to cook for the group when I'm being a player and have nothing else to do for the game; I get to show off some weird recipe, be generous, be a good example for others, get everybody fed and so on and so forth. In our summer Paranoia game I've gotten the players into a habit of bringing food by bribing them with perversity points, which has worked well for everybody concerned in the affair.

    A remarkable dislike is that I've found that I'm not very fond of the common habit of bringing a take-away dinner to the game when you arrive. I get it that it's convenient to grab something on the way and then eat it on the premises, but I don't find it ideal from the perspective of the group - I think that we can do better, and doing better involves ensuring that everybody gets fed in an elegant manner, without people having to be annoyed with your food wrappers floating around, or eye your delicious sandwich enviously because they forgot to bring their own, or wait for you to finish your pizza before the game can actually start. At times this can develop into a sort of a covert one-man war between me and the habit of coming to the game table fed, with delicious and consistently available food offerings as the weapons of choice - the war goal is to get people committed to the idea that the salon logistics provide the feed, they don't need to bring their own burger doggy bag

    (For clarity, because I sound like I advocate a secret underground cooking conspiracy, I should say that I have nothing against not mixing food into gaming at all per se - I'm cool with a group that decides that the game starts after dinner, with everybody eating at home before arriving. In practice this is apparently a very rare arrangement in the busy urban landscape, and it soon degenerates into take-away dinners eaten on the premises, at which point the cooking knives come out and I start advocating for a communal dinner arrangement - if we're eating here anyway, might as well do something interesting and convenient that gets everybody fed instead of just the 40% who brought their own.)

    The take-away from my experiences is, I think, that the more you can spread out the salon-hosting duties and particularly separate them from creative duties, the better; having them all pile up on the GM is, I think, an unnatural and immature state of affairs that has to do with cultural habits (treating the GM as a dancing bear, there for your enjoyment, to be specific) that we might desire to discard going forward. It is possible and easy (if the will is there) to separate the secretariat, hosting and catering, so I heartily recommend looking into the various possibilities for any group that feels even the slight bit mature (in the sense of having been playing together a while, I mean) - if you think that the other players actually like being there and being involved, that's the right time to start speculating about maybe splitting up the workload. It's not the answer for all groups (for example, when the GM is clearly the senior partner and leader of the group as part of e.g. a school gaming club), but for a social circle of peers it's just plain weird to not spread out the duties and ask everybody for some degree of commitment to the common affair.

    You'll note that I use the term "salon" a lot in this context. The reason is simply that I find much to be admirable in the old European custom of the cultural salon. For example, consider this: in the cultural dynamics of the salon the host was rarely the intellectual centerpiece of the salon; rather, the host would arrange for interesting people to come in and provide the entertainment. The ideal of separating hosting from GMing in tabletop gaming arises out of the same social and practical concerns.

    \n I used to play in a group a couple of years ago at the home of a player who always cooked for the whole group. Our most busy group member sometimes arrived after dinner, because he had to pick up his kids at school, but I always made an effort to join the dinner. I'm vegan, and even though I offered to bring my own stuff or help out cooking, this player always prepared (delicious) vegan food on his own for the whole group. I guess it helps that he's vegetarian, but the whole group enjoyed his cooking.

    A bonus was that when you have kids, as I have, the opportunities to meet people just to socialize are a lot more constrained, so this was a great outlet. During dinner we would discuss anything, both RPG related or work related or whatever, but never the current game. That made the switch from just hanging out to playing the current game very clear.

    I tried to carry this over to my Whitehack group (no player overlap with the previous group) when we started playing, but we play at a player's office, so we had to eat out. That meant we could either go to a regular restaurant and I could order what's available off-menu (a vegetarian pizza without cheese, pasta and tomato sauce, or something), or we could go to a vegan restaurant where I would feel responsible for the other players' experience.

    Some people find vegetarian or vegan food lacking, and I didn't want to enforce this on everyone each time we met. The setup now is that people can meet and eat before the game if they want to, but then they schedule it themselves on our mailing list. I usually show up after dinner to just play, mostly because I don't find eating in a loud restaurant nearly as relaxing as eating at someone's home, and because of the vegan options. So we have two or three players who sometimes eat together before the game and two players who never do it. Sometimes people bring food to the game and eat at the start of the session, but since the arrangement is that you're supposed to arrive fed, I don't think anyone watches these people longingly. People usually bring a sandwich or something quick.

    Most of my gaming groups haven't had a fixed schedule. In high school and at the university I would play as often as possible. After graduation it would go a couple of weeks after a session until someone got restless and started to schedule the next session. Numerous emails and Doodles later we would find a time two weeks away when everyone was available at the same time. It was not ideal. Doodle-like scheduling is far better than plain emails though.

    In my current Whitehack group we've always played every other Thursday, and it's been perfect. I was very inspired by West Marches when I set it up, and exactly like you do, when players are missing their characters just disappear for a session and we come up with an explanation when they get back. Usually their character will guard the horses outside of the dungeon, or wander the already explored corridors or something. In my game the players themselves divide both XP and gold between the characters, and missing players usually get something, but not as much as the players attending.

    The thing with the two-week schedule is that even when I feel some burnout after a session that didn't go as well as I wanted - perhaps I messed up some part of the pre-written module or made a judgment call I regret, two weeks is long enough for me to miss the game and look forward to it again. I also enjoy that the game just "appears" in my calendar at the start of the week and I can look forward to it without having to get people to reply and divulge their schedules.

    I imagine being in a situation where player A, B, and C can play on one day, and player B, C, and D can play on another is stressful. How do you decide if player A or D get to play, if you're available to play both days? We've had players who wanted to join our game, but said that Thursdays wouldn't work, which meant they automatically disqualified themselves. I didn't have to try to find a date to suit them as well as the rest of the group, which was nice.

    The only real problem with our current setup is that the come-if-you-can attitude makes the number of players fluctuate. At the moment, if everyone attends, we're five players plus GM. Last session three players had other stuff to do, and the final two didn't want to risk killing their characters by taking on the module on their own, so they cancelled. I sometimes suggest GM something else if we're down to two players, but now we've specified that we'll play Dungeon World (I haven't played yet, want to try it!) or B/X if we're down to two players. We've also recruited a new player, so now the risk is instead six players each session, which is the upper limit for me.

    The above is a description of things that has happened to me and how I've tried to solve it, so please take what you can from it. I don't want to give you specific suggestions on how to handle your situation, because I don't know enough about it.

    \n I always treat gaming gatherings at other people's houses the way I would treat any dinner party: it's rude to show up empty-handed. Whenever my friends are hosting a game, I show up with something to share with the group, and generally the kinds of things that I think they'll all enjoy.

    The Sunday Afternoon Game

    My friend runs a biweekly (Sunday afternoon) Pathfinder campaign. We have about six players, and generally one or two of us can't make it, but three or four is sufficient for most games.

    People never show up empty-handed for this group.

    Of the group, three are often dieting. Two of them do low-carb. Another is diabetic. So what happened? People started bringing healthier snacks: cheese and meats, nuts, guacamole dip, and so on (generally, stuff everyone there could enjoy). Yeah, sure, there's still the usual junk food here and there, but no one feels left out.

    The GM does feed us, but he always does a crock-pot dish that's easy and cheap. He's stated that he's happy to pay for the food since he hates to travel and some of us are driving a good distance to get there (it's 31 miles/50 km for me) and gas is expensive.

    The Tuesday Evening Game

    We play a different biweekly game at his house (rotation of different indie games). Only a few players in common with the Sunday game. I tend to GM these nights.

    Because it's a Tuesday evening, the host has already eaten before the game but a few of us come right from work. We order pizza or Chinese delivery. Sometimes it's only me, or a few of us. Sometimes I just cover the food for one of the people who has a smaller budget. It all works out and isn't a "thing."

    We often do show up empty-handed for this. It's a shorter game, and we know that if we're hungry, the players should handle calling for food to be delivered.

    This group has been together longer than the Sunday one. In the Tuesday group, we have all known each other for at least 8 years now and we've gotten to be more than just gaming friends. I think that's probably part of why we're more relaxed about showing up empty-handed, though we'd stop doing that if any of us ever sensed it was an issue. None of us want to take advantage of our host.

    Mind you, I hosted the Tuesday group for many years before we switched to the current host's house.

    Friction

    The one point of continual friction with this group is the GMing duties. The idea of the Tuesday group is "we try out an indie games for a session or three, then switch to a new one." That was fun for a while, but I'm really feeling the burn-out around learning and prepping new games--especially when I prep a game and they all go "Nah" after they've tacitly agreed through prior discussion that we'll play it.

    I actually got to the point where I told them I was done GMing and it was someone else's turn. Like I said, we are close friends and we can talk about stuff like this. Everyone agreed that it was unfair for me to own all the GMing responsibility. Two of the others agreed to run games. Both flaked out. We cancelled a bunch of sessions as a result.

    I love GMing. I just hate constantly switching to a new game. Especially if it's literally a NEW GAME that I just bought, have to read and understand, and have to run cold. That puts a really huge burden on me. Every so often, I have a new game I'm itching to run, so I pitch it to them, but I don't want to do this every two weeks.

    Now I tell them what I'm willing to run. This late summer, I told them I was willing to run Sorcerer, gave them a pitch for a short campaign of 3-5 sessions, and they agreed. I've run (and played) Sorcerer before but needed to refresh my memory. Mostly, though, it's what interests me the most at this time.

    If they flake or lose interest, there would be no game unless someone else steps up to the plate. Luckily, they seem to be into it.

    We never did a thing with food in our group. If someone wants to eat, they bring something to eat. I never even considered that this could be an issue.
    \nYeah, our group did the whole "let's eat together!" thing for a little while years back, but it's such a hassle that we stopped. There just wasn't a good enough way of doing it to make it worthwhile; someone inevitably ends up shouldering more of the burden (labor, cost, organization, whatever) than someone else, which can be fine for a little while but eventually burns them out.

    And since our typical game session is only about 2-3 hours at most anyway, it's not like anyone's going to be wracked with hunger pangs at the halfway point. You just eat before you come over to play, or if you're coming straight from work you can grab something quick along the way and eat it before play starts. Just getting together to play something is special enough, we don't need to break bread together (symbolically or otherwise) to make it an occasion.

    \n Accounting points out something I've noticed as well, namely that the particular social arrangements of the play space tend to come and go; we're not talking about something you settle on for life. If I had to pin a schedule on it, in my experience we're talking about cycles of like 3-6 months at a go; basically, a given campaign or campaign season goes along with a given specific arrangement of who does what, and then there's an inevitable shuffle for the sake of variety if not because the player base shifts or the game moves to a different place.

    Food has been more common in the specific arrangements I've seen over the last decade than not, though. The most minimalistic crews tend to be proge hipster gamers (all sorts of weird diets compared to your more conservatively geeky adventure gamer, if I'm allowed a peculiar generalization), but even those will usually settle in a pattern of bringing fruit and nuts and flavoured mineral water as a bare baseline to a nominally foodless game night; sort of replaces the traditional junk food option of chips and Coke, which otherwise seems pretty ubiquitous - I'd have to specifically forbid it to prevent those things from materializing

    The key to making the collective catering work is basically that you need somebody in the group to enjoy hosting and/or cooking so that it doesn't feel like a chore so much as an interesting way to enrich the social dimensions of the experience. It's a common hobby - if I was a family man with more reasons to cook, I probably would characterize it as an actual hobby myself, for example - so it's no wonder that gaming groups with some sort of catering arrangements are pretty common. Sort of like how it is common to play music at a sit-together, because many people like music, too.
