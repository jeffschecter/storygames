Formulae, pacing, landmarks and conflicts

\n
\n
\n
\n\nTonyLB \n\n\n
\n
\n\nSeptember 2007 \n edited September 2007 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n I'm an increasing fan of formulaic stories ... ones where you know roughly that X, then Y, then Z are going to happen. Why? Because of my experience that they are reliably satisfying. Not risky, not avant-garde, but fun every time.
\n
\nHere's one thing about formulae: They provide consensus for narration ... almost authority, in fact. When the young farm-boy has refused the call to adventure once, and some terrible thing happened, and then he's called a second time ... well, it's time for him to go. Nobody questions "Hey, wouldn't he be scared or something?" No, he could start out scared, but the act of indulging his cowardice, followed by further trauma, means that now he's heroic.
\n
\nI mean, seriously, how does that make sense? But that's okay. It doesn't have to make sense, it's the formula. We accept it because we're trained to accept it, and I think that's GREAT. The point is to get the boy on the road to adventure, so whatever makes that believable is okay with me.
\n
\nThe more I think about it, the more I think that pacing is just a general type of formula, operating in the same way. If the shy girl plucked up the courage to confess her love in the first scene with the new kid, well, I might be unhappy accepting that. But if she agonizes, and moons over him, and then manages to pluck up her courage after a talk with a wise older sister ... well then, that's cool. The pacing acts in the same way as the formula above: it helps me to accept the end result.
\n
\nThere are destinations that I can only enter happily after being primed by a certain type of journey.
\n
\nDoes this make sense, so far? I have a little mini-sorta-rant to follow this, on landmarks and conflicts, but the stuff above seems sufficiently unintuitive that I thought I'd give people a chance to squawk "WTF?" before proceeding to use it as a foundation.

    \n Okay, so ... on to my little mini-rant.
    \n
    \nWe don't know every step of the kind of journey we need. Indeed, this is the benefit of a formula: It is a MAP ... a reduction of the vastness of the possible journeys we could take into a few explicit landmarks that we can use to guide our steps. It tells us what to look at, in order to lead us step by step along the path we want to take.
    \n
    \nTo use the farm-boy example: We look to the mountain "Call to heroism" and we walk until we are past that, then we decide to travel toward a different peak, "Refusal of Call." That puts us within sight of "Tragic Consequences" and we trek over that way, and then we find that we can see "The Call reiterated" off in the distance, and when we trek over toward there, and walk into a place called "Heroism" we think "Yeah, it's right that we got here. We've done the journey."
    \n
    \nNow what many good gaming systems will do is that they will hang rules on these landmarks, to make them more explicit. They won't tell you "Yeah, okay, you gotta look at this 'Call to Heroism' thing, because it's important," but they'll say "Okay, you've been Called. Flip your potential card sideways." That attracts attention to the moment in a different way.
    \n
    \nSo game systems can help us point out landmarks in the story, yes? Explicit rules always draw attention to their objects, and drawing attention to the landmarks makes it easier for people to navigate. That's a strong technique for getting people in synch with the formula.
    \n
    \nNot feeling the mini-rant heat, yet? Okay, let me lay it out: Conflicts are very bad landmarks for many kinds of stories.
    \n
    \nThe shy girl trying to pluck up her courage? That story is very poorly served by concentrating on conflicts. I'm not saying that conflicts aren't there. My experience is that people who want to dissect a story in terms of its conflicts can always find them. I do not doubt for an instant that conflicts are there, part of the landscape.
    \n
    \nBut it's like using mountain peaks as landmarks when you're trekking through a deep forest. In order to see those landmarks you have to climb up above the canopy, and take yourself almost entirely out of the forest you're travelling through, just to get your bearings. In order to navigate the story of the shy girl by using conflicts as landmarks, you have to re-envision her embarrassed blush, and her stuttering retreat, as part of some violent throw-down between two aspects of her inner self. You have to re-envision her talk with her older sister as the sister trying to beat sense into her against powerful opposition.
    \n
    \nThat's a wierd, stand-on-your-head way of looking at what's happening in such a story. It's quite as wierd as being in a forest and ignoring the waterfall that you've just come upon (which would be the ideal landmark) in order to climb a tree and look at the mountain peaks in the distance. It's using a hugely unintuitive set of landmarks ... and while they may well do the job, they don't make it easy.
    \n
    \nAnd yet ... they're the only landmarks for which we have rules-craft. Conflict resolution is very well explored territory, rules-wise.
    \n
    \nThis is a great thing if you're trying to build a system for stories where conflicts are good landmarks. It rocks on toast for action-adventure. But it's a problem of having only a hammer, and seeing the whole world as full of nails, if you try to build a system where conflicts are poor landmarks. If we wanted to hang rules on some other set of landmarks, how would that even work?
    \n
    \nI'm building such a system, and I find myself immensely hampered by my constant temptation to build a conflict resolution system into it. I'm increasingly certain that conflicts are the wrong landmarks to guide people into the formula of the stories I want to emulate, but I don't know how to hang rules on the right landmarks, or even how to identify them.
    \n
    \nAnd that's my mini-rant. How dare people highly develop a technique that does something other than what I'm trying to do right now? The ARROGANCE! :-)

    While any of those choices can be presented in the context of a conflict to put it into effect, only Challenges really call out that you must intrinsically have conflict and choices are not just about "to engage or not", "this or that" and "what to do with stakes won" but also should be crunchy enough to let you show your Gamist legs. However, if the Fork choices aren't "in a bit of conflict" that creates predictability.

    I think if you don't have conflict, you need some other means of creating "suspense" and "unpredictability of what follows". I suggest those two dynamics are important to the play experience. Even if it's just "you don't know what value choice the character will make and it's suspenseful waiting to see the choice". Particularly if players know each other and have played over time, if you know "oh Ted always takes it this way", you need uncertainty if Ted is successful to keep it interesting and that likely comes from conflict, no?

    Posted By: Mark WYa know, if you take stakes out of the picture, narration trading does about cover it.
    Well, I love narration trading, but there are other things you could do.

    For instance, something I'm now considering is this: To reveal a truth about your character, you place a card, face-up on the table. If nobody can best that card then you reveal that truth to exactly the audience that you intended (or perhaps you must reveal it to everyone, unless the intended confidant makes the effort to beat the card). However, if another player can best the card then you reveal that truth to their character. If the GM bests the card then the truth is revealed, but only to the audience (the real-world ones, beyond the fourth wall). In any case, if the truth is not revealed to everybody then it is now a Secret. A Secret cannot be revealed by the character it is about (unless they win a second reveal specifically for that purpose), but may be revealed at any time by the player who won it.

    That's only secondarily about narration ... the thing being revealed about the character is the same in any case, because really we all want the reveal to happen somehow or other. But the consequences of the reveal are nicely up for grabs.

    \n The first thing to do is to get rid of the intuition that conflict is necessarily the subtext of violence. Once you do that, you see how arbitrary it is to think of some events being conflicts and other events that arise from narrative tension as "not-conflicts." Conflict is a state of tension between narrative elements that is resolved (really, transformed) by events in the narrative. That's it. If you let Junior High English dominate how you're going to talk about conflict then yes, you'll devolve into "(Hu)Man Versus . . . ," you'll reflexively anthropomorphize the ". . ." and return to the violence-subtext. Since violence is a central part of the history of RPGs it is difficult to ward off that tendency, but that doesn't mean you're correct.

    In short, it doesn't make any sense you say something is "not about conflict." It's like saying your version of water doesn't have any Hydrogen. But the folk-definition of conflict used in most RPGs (and elsewhere) is overly narrow.

    There's an excellent reason why games are concerned about conflict: These are aspects of the narrative that can be transformed/resolved through a game system, wheras in other modes an author simply decides. There are game systems for non-conflict aspects, certainly. They're things like character height/weight/eye colour charts, character advancement and the like. Systems used to determine aspects of the environment and set-dressing also qualify. But none of these things actually cause the plot to unfold *in of themselves* (Your PC's green eyes may cause her to be treated differently, but that's the intersection between that detail and something else).

    One thing you can do is make conflict much broader and more global, instead of instanced in typical RPG terms. This could resolve itself like Plot Coupon/quest fantasy. Pick up they things/get the conditions that you need and the global conflict is resolved. You could in fact get each player to map their own landmarks, then weave them together collaboratively. We all want to beat Sauron, but this requires a minimum of maybe 10 steps per player (it's an epic, after all). These steps are automatically achievable; the rules simply structure them. Committee for the Exploration of Mysteries has ideas to mine here in terms of pre-play design.

    \n So, Malcolm pretty effectively outlined my thoughts on what conflict is, why most games definitions of it are a bit too fighty, and why it _is_ an essential part of story, so right off the bat, let me open with a "yep, that."

    I'll even elaborate to say that, yes, absolutely there needs to be context for conflict for it to be satisfying. You _technically_ can have a story without conflict, or a conflict without context, but in both cases you're going to be talking about a pretty bloodless mechanical exercise, so I definitely want both.

    To my mind, it's easy to point to the necessity of conflict - is there more than one way the game could turn out? If yes, then there are conflicting ideas at work. If no, then, well, I'm not entirely certain what game I'm playing.

    But that is a very strict definition, and it ignores the fact that "conflict" is weighed down with adversarial connotations. Consider the example Mike cites from improv - given on the context I have, I have a suspicion that they meant a much less hoity-toity definition of conflict, something more like "Either I am right and you are wrong, or I am wrong and you are right, and that is the only way it can go." When two people come into something with different ideas, then reasonably move to a compromise, was there a conflict? Well, yes (because there were conflicting ideas, and they were resolved) and no (because the two parties did not conflict _with each other_ to resolve this).

    It's a pretty headache-inducing distinction, to be totally frank. But having made it, let me toss out the question - I obviously am unclear on how conflict is being used throughout these thread, so in more concrete terms, what are we removing, and what are we leaving behind?

    -Rob D.

    PS -And, for the record, if you'd asked me a week ago, I would have been adamant that conflict (by the hoity toity definition) is necessary for a game to be satisfying for the same reason it's necessary for fiction to be satisfying. Recent observations have left me slightly less certain of that position, though I'm still pretty firmly in that camp.

      \n Cool! So, I'd like to (perhaps prematurely in your scheme of things) start extrapolating principles out of the examples into the larger space.

      Firstly, what we're talking about is kind of a personal plot arc or character arc, like "hero refuses call, hero accepts call, hero rises to challenge, hero gets overconfident and fails, hero comes through in the clinch" or "boy meets girl, boy gets girl, boy loses girl, boy gets girl back", or "bitter character holds back from commitment, bitter character is confronted with the power of love, bitter character risks accepting love", or... well, there are lots of them, and we recognize them when we see them.

      I might have a little barn-raising where people suggest some at some point. I have a use for them.

      Secondly, not all of these steps are conflicts, but all the ones I can think of are in some way related (or relatable) to conflicts. Some are changes of personal perspective which influence what someone will get into conflicts about; some are avoidance of conflict (which tells us something about the character); some serve to prefigure the conflict; some to make the conflict, when it comes, more poignant; some give resources (or, I suppose, take away resources) which could be used in later conflicts. I'd welcome suggestions of examples that have nothing to do with conflicts whatsoever, but I can't myself come up with any offhand.

      Third, many of them (as you've alluded to) don't have unknown outcomes, at least not at the meta level - the character may be unaware of the outcome, but as a story-savvy audience, we know that the first two sons of the king are going to fail because they were rude to the old lady/fox, and the third son will succeed because he was helpful.

      There's no need for a resolution system for these ones. We know what's going to happen and we narrate it. Maybe the way in which we narrate it becomes significant in some way in the long term, but what we're looking at here is not a conflict because it's not a matter of "it could go either way". We might even say going in, "Oh, I know how this one goes." That's what makes it a formula.

      I mean, shy girl meets attractive boy, roll dice to see if she talks to him? I don't think so. Of course she doesn't talk to him. She's Shy Girl. But maybe something about the way she doesn't talk to him, or the way her player describes the scene, becomes significant and can be reincorporated later on when she does talk to him (which, of course, she eventually does).

      Posted By: MikeRMI mean, shy girl meets attractive boy, roll dice to see if she talks to him? I don't think so. Of course she doesn't talk to him. She's Shy Girl.       I find this in a fair number of story-games, actually: Sometimes the closer you get to the climax of a story, particularly a long-running story, the more scenes there are that need to be there for the story to be satisfying, but for which every outcome is predetermined. I think it would be cool to have meaningful rules stuff still happening, so that people are still playing the game. I just don't know that it ever needs to feed back into the fiction. Maybe it's just rules stuff to keep the game entertaining, and the scenes spotlighted.

      Example: End of The Princess Bride: Wesley has saved Buttercup, defeated Humperdink, all the tension is over, all the plots have been resolved ... all that remains is for the two lovers to ride off and kiss against a stunning sunset. And yet, the movie provides a moving portrayal of just how incomplete the story would be if the book were closed, the story stopped, the last scene left off. You need the scene. It's part of the story.

      Is this making any sense?

      \n Sorry, late to the scene here.

      As Emily has pointed out, there are a few designs in the pipe (Sign In Stranger, Jared & Luke's Project Donut, my 3-4 chakra-based games) that try to make a manipulatable resource out of, basically, whatever happens in the game or, more often, whatever happens in a particular scene. I don't know where the other designers are drawing their inspiration from, but, for me, my thinking about this was spurred by the lauded-and-forgotten Marvel Universe, in which characters measured XP in "lines," which were brief sentences that recorded what happened to them.

      The cool thing about this, in my mind, is that it doesn't really matter what happens in a given scene. You don't have to pick out the core conflict. You just write down what happened, either a general overview of the scene (or other subdivision of the game experience) or a description that focuses on what was important to your character. Since I can't talk about the Donut and am not sure what Emily's doing with Sign In Stranger right now, I'll give an example from the game I just finished a draft of:

      In Mwaantaangaand, your character might be pursuing the Agenda: "Find my dead daughter in the underworld of Mpemba and apologize for failing to protect her." And, in a scene about that Agenda, your character might get lost amidst the twisted streets of the City of Chalk and come to the realization that they are completely and truly alone, both physically (separated from the other members of their special ops unit) and emotionally (they have not connected with anyone since their daughter died). So you could record "I Am Truly Alone" for that scene, marking a significant moment in the story, but one in which there may not really be a conflict.

      So, yeah, I think this is totally possible and there are already games that are beginning to do this, though more techniques and implementation methods would totally be awesome. Is that the kind of thing you're thinking about, Tony? Or am I misunderstanding your overall goal here?

      P.S. Bliss Stage's "scene labelling" technique is also a great example of this (great point, Mark!), even though that works primarily orally with the outcome of the scene only being recorded as far as its mechanical result goes.

      \n One thing I was (am?) working on for my d20 Cabbages and Kings adaptation (why yes, I am adapting a 1904 romantic comedy novel to d20, why do you ask?) was having a deck of plot cards with 1904 romantic comedy formulae on them, which would be laid out in each 'chapter' of the story to define what happens next. Long stories would have a lot of cards, short ones only a few. One would be kept in reserve to be the "end twist", since we are talking about O. Henry here.

      So for example you'd have:

      Mistaken Identity -> Change in Financial Circumstances -> Something Returns To Normalcy -> Change In Target Of Affection -> End Twist: A Secret Revealed as one particular story arc.

      The good thing is that there's not that many different sorts of 'turns' in a 1904 romantic comedy. The difficult part is that in order to reduce them to a card, I had to generalize them so much that they really didn't provide enough limitation for them to provide a lot of guidance to players - not to mention that they're highly reliant on initial setup, which also could be highly kooky.
