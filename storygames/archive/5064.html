\n OK, this is my first post in a little while. I'm just coming back to roleplaying after some time, hoping to be part of a game over the holidays. But a couple of different threads have got me thinking: Rob Donoghue makes an excellent observation on White Wolf's Changeling court system and Don Corcoran's Wizard setting burner.

So combining these two, I was thinking about questions for creating characters that are dealing with some sort of lose or trauma (hence the "Dead Inside" in the subject even though I haven't read the book yet).

My first crack at them was good, I thought. But I found something interesting, the four stages worked better with the Changeling structure backwards.

Depression: What was your old life like?
Bargaining: What did you do in Arcadia?
Anger: How did you escape?
Denial: What do you want now?

But looking over Don's thread, I realized that my questions lacked punch. My second pass tried to make them more personal.

What will you never let be taken from you again?
What will you never do again just to survive?
What will you never let happen to someone else again?
What will you never tell someone else again?

I'm probably never going to use these ideas, but it's bugging me that the questions aren't awesome enough. What can I do to make them better?

    \n I really like question-based character generation, but your second batch doesn't make me feel like I'd know what my character would necessarily do. You could take your first set and focus them more directly, like (not having read Changeling):

    Depression: Tell us why you can't leave your old life behind.
    Bargaining: What is the strongest memory you have of your time in Arcadia?
    Anger: What did it cost you to escape?
    Denial: What is your first impulse to do now?

    I really like the questions in Don't Rest Your Head:
    * What's been keeping you awake?
    * What just happened to you?
    * What's on the surface?
    * What lies underneath?
    * What's your path?
    Each with a good couple paragraphs unpacking that question some regarding what to think about and why it matters.

    \n You know, I think the main issue is what was I planning on doing with these questions.

    Last night, I wrote up a whole system using Tarot cards where the main point of the game was mechanically playing the Sequels (the point being that the character can't "win" scenes because the character must move backwards for the story to move forward). The questions dictated the issues of scenes that the character would go through.

    But the more I think about it, the less I want to build a whole new game. More likely just looking for a way to get flags from the players through pointed questions. Thank you all for your help clarifying this issue for me.

    \nTo be honest, even since I've played Don't Rest Your Head and Burning Empires, I've been incorporating flagging elements from them into other games without caring about mechanical tie-ins.

    I've had players for other games tell me their three BW-style Beliefs and Instincts. I've asked questions of my own characters to tease them out a bit more. It's a pretty powerful tool that can be used in any game, as long as you're okay with the tie-in being person/group-based rather than system-based.
