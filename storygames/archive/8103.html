\n I'm working on my heroic action fantasy game again, and here's my weird idea.

Note: A d\xa7 is a 6-sided die with faces 0 through 5. 2d\xa7 runs 0-10 with an average result of 5, just like 2d6 runs 2-12 with an average of 7.

Right now, in my game, simple task resolution, you roll 2d\xa7 and add a stat and circumstance modifiers against the GM's target number. The GM, when setting target numbers for NPCs, always uses 5. PCs win a tie. This is lopsided in favor of the PCs because of that tie.

Here's what I'm considering. The GM doesn't add 5 to the NPCs, instead he adds zero. The GM wins a tie, the PCs roll just 1d\xa7. So if the PC has the advantage already, they just succeed, no roll. But if the odds are even or against the PC, then they have to roll.

Rolling a 0 would always cause mechanical "damage" to the PC, but not rolling at all wouldn't do anything.

Now this is lopsided between PCs and NPCs, and I'm OK with that, because the PCs are heroic protagonists.

Say Jim is a PC, and Todd and Paul are NPCs. Jim has Swordsman 4, Todd has Swordsman 2, Paul has Swordsman 6.

Jim and Paul fight. Jim hurts Paul on a 3 or better, but Paul hurts Jim on a 0, 1, or 2.
Jim and Todd fight. Jim hurts Todd. No rolling.

Todd can't win against Jim unless he can get some other advantages against Jim.

Is this universe stupid or just very encouraging of heroes?

    \n Reminds me of Mook rules in other games (now, D&D 4E). But you have a sliding scale of mookdom, as reflected by the PC's base stat. Kinda clever, actually--better than the binary mookness of D&D 4E.

    As for taste... well, I like to roll, and I like systems which give even the mookiest mook a chance for a lucky shot that affects the hero. Why even narrate the mook, if it's a no-roll situation and the PC will always beat him? Perhaps it can add to the narrative (man, look at that "hero" eviscerate all those farmers with pitchforks!), but it does precisely nothing for the encounter challenge (per design).

    I dunno... just spitballing, here, but what about something like "exploding zeros"? The PC must roll, but only if a zero is rolled does it matter--the PC rolls AGAIN, and if they get another zero on that die, then they suffer the mechanical harm. Now, if THAT reduces a stat enough to de-mookify the mook, then we got us a proper fight--the mook found an Achilles Heel and, now, is not so mookly anymore and fights on a level field (albeit probably STILL at a serious disadvantage).

    (Yes, I set a personal challenge to use mook a different way every time!)

    \n I'm going to agree with David on this one. If the mook isn't a challenge (albeit a minor one) why bother having him there? Even minions in D&D 4E pose a threat. They aren't automatically hit on each attack, but they do automatically die if they're hit.

    Personally, I also like unified mechanics, so if I'm rolling 2d6 for static checks, I should be rolling 2d6 for opposed checks as well. Also, switching from 2d6 to 1d6 in these cases removes the bell curve that multiple dice create, and instead you'll have an equal chance of achieving any result.

    I don't really see the problem with the original way of doing it. It's fast and simple. Depending on the amount of rolling in the game, I'd even consider actually rolling for the NPCs as well, but I understand the reasoning for just using +5 to make gameplay smoother.
