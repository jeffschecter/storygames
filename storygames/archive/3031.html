\n Here's a little exercise to see whether a rpg/story game produces 'experimental' fiction.

Not that this is NOT a test of whether a game is itself experimental in terms of pushing the boundaries of traditional rpgs. That's an interesting but seperate matter. In fact I suspect that a lot of more 'experimental' games may tend to generate more 'mainstream' fiction. One more caveat: I'm not implying that experimental is good or bad.

These scales are meant to be a very rough measure of whether the story or play experience generated by the game would qualify as experimental fiction. For my purposes, I'm defining experimental in contrast to some strong tendencies of mainstream literary fiction of the past 30-40 years:

* mimesis
* realism
* intentional theme
* middle range of human experience
*character/plot driven
* meaning
* consistent style [edited to add based on Bryan's comment]

Add up the points you get from these 7 categories. The higher the score, the more experimental the story is.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------

0 pts if the story imitates life so that you are swept up in the story and identify with the heroes. 'Psychological realism' is just as important in this case as settings detail.
1 pt if its in the middle
2 points if the story is structured to interrupt itself and remind you that it is a story, is a game.

0 pts if the story is set in the real world.
1 pt if it is somewhere in the middle
2 pts if the story includes fantastic elements

0 pts if the story has an intentional theme
1 pt if it is somewhere in the middle
2 pts if the story does not have a pre-planned theme

0 pts if the story focuses on the middle range of human experience, ordinary daily life
1 pt if it is somewhere in the middle
2 pts if the story focuses on the extremes of human behavior, such as violence

0 pts if the story is organized around characters and a plot
1 pt if it is somewhere in the middle
2 pts if it the story is organized around the exploration of details of the physical, sensory world

0 pts if the story has a recognizable meaning
1 pt if it is somewhere in the middle
2 pts if the story is absurdist

0 points if the story is told mainly in one style and with one voice
1 pt if it is somewhere in the middle
2 pts if the story is told with multiple literary techniques and with multiple (and potentially contradictory) voices

-----------------------------------

so, for example, I'd rank D&D (at least as I play it), as getting 8 points on this scale, so the story from a D&D campaign is marginally closer to experimental than mainstream. (0 from the meaning category, 1 pt from the mimesis, daily life, style/voice, and character/plot scale, 2 from the realism & intentional theme categories).

    \n Scores for Beast Hunters:

    * mimesis
    2 points. Story is not the focus, the challenge to the players is. Therefore, strong game elements, distinct challenge moments, etc.

    * realism
    2 points. Magical beasts, tribal hunters, tattoos of power...

    * intentional theme
    2 points. The game is not thematic, it's a challenge game.

    * middle range of human experience
    2 points. Special people (beast hunters) in a violent environment going on extraordinary missions.

    *character/plot driven
    1 point. This one is tough. It's definitely character-focused and plot-focused through the adventure structure, but the sensory details are important for the challenge aspects. Partially depends on prep work and play style as well.

    * meaning
    0 points. Very clear structure of what happens, nothing absurd about it.

    * consistent style
    0 points. Both roles (Challenger and Hunter) have their pre-determined "voices".

    Total: 9 points.
