    \nNo idea, except that people have a tendency towards being wrong. (That is, we humans more often hold inexact or incorrect beliefs than correct ones when it comes to complex conclusions about the nature of the world. Probably has something to do with our cognitive capabilities being optimized not for general logic and stochastics, but rather for simple observable cause-and-effect. Socrates being the wisest of the Greeks and all that.)

    I myself do not hold this particular mistaken impression (which is not to say that I don't hold others), for the same reason you have: I regularly get complex emergence out of complex systems. Heck, TSoY is basically predicated on this very phenomenon, it'd be no different than say Heroquest-except-clumsier if it didn't have the capability to reliably produce emergent phenomena that strike through the entire campaign from deceptively simple mechanical conceits.

    If I had to guess at where this idea might come from, it could be that it's easier to not get emergence from a certain type of hand-holding, over-complex game design. If the game is written in the spirit of discouraging independent invention, and instead it provides minute procedural solutions to every possible game state, then there obviously isn't room for discovery of emergent behaviour. Has more to do with the ethos of the group (adopted from a game text or not) than with the complexity of the rules system, of course.

    \nHere's the Wikipedia article on the topic. The short of it is that "emergence" in systems theory (as in, cross-discipline general science of how systems work) is the name for the quality of having undesigned (non-intentional, that is) properties. A feature of the system "emerges" from the known and directly observable bits. Something being emergent is of course somewhat subjective, as the line between "designed" and "emergent" properties lies wherever you think that "obvious" or "explicit" ends.

    This is obviously important, and a very common, universal quality of various systems. The most important single example is probably mathematics, which may be defined as the discipline of figuring out the emergent properties (called "results") of various arbitrary axiomatic sets applied to equally arbitrary structures.

    \n Typically emergent play refers to relatively complex play aspects that emerge from the interaction of simpler aspects through the course of play. Dwarf fortress is a good example from computer games. The actual elements are very simple, but the end result is complex and unpredictable.

    \n Adam, maybe it would be helpful (for understanding the idea of "non-emergent roleplaying game") to observe simpler games instead, ones where emergence is much less usual. Examples of such are games like Under the Bed, My Life with Master, Zombie Cinema, Fiasco, 1001 Nights. All of those games have less emergence (measured in the amount of unexpected directions and nature that play can and does take) than a complex, wide game that can be utilized in a multitude of ways. Are you familiar with any game of that sort?

    I have played games like the above a lot, and I can definitely say that emergent content is not a guarantee in roleplaying games, particularly in ones such as those. This is, of course, a subjective matter: I don't think that rpg content is "emergent" in any interesting way when it is expected and measured by the game design in advance. For example, merely inventing an argument about whether to run or fight in Zombie Cinema is not "emergent content", but rather the bare minimum to be able to play the game, and it is much easier to manage to play with such pro-forma content all the way through than it is in e.g. GURPS, which falls down and dies if there's nobody putting work into it. To reach emergence in a simple and procedural game like ZC, you need to push further and discover how rules and creative intent work off each other to produce something unique and unexpected.

    My point is not that this is some sort of surefire recipe for getting "non-emergent" play, of course, simply because I really don't see the term itself as objective in any way. It's like calling some game yellow: I guess it has some sort of yellow-ish bits here and there, but ultimately we can only discuss the degree of yellowness it has. Same for emergent content or behaviour: is it emergent when the players of My Life with Master decide to play a Dracula scenario? The rulebook doesn't tell you to do exactly that, so maybe it is? On the other hand, it's exactly following procedure, and clearly the designer anticipated the eventuality, the game makes request on you to add your own creativity in this very spot, and the play never gets out of the prescribed envelope, so what's emergent about that, in the practical sense of the term, any more than about any decision in any game? There's no correct answer, we can merely say that some systems have more interesting emergent properties than others, and we pretty much merely mean that they have a bunch of properties that are not self-evident at first. Explain a system better, and the amount of emergence is actually reduced, as behaviors we would have classified as emergent are now considered obvious. "Features" instead of "emergent features".

    Maybe one could get at a certain sort of game design elegance by observing how much set-up one needs to produce what amount of complexity, and we might call that particularly emergent game design. For example, Go and Game of Life are often used as examples of highly emergent game design for the simple reason that they have unexpectedly simple procedural rules for the complexity of the interactions the rules imply. In other words, there is a lot of systemic complexity implied beyond the obvious you pick up by reading the rules, and this is called "emergent complexity". It's still subjective because the complexity is there whether we observe it or not, and we only call it emergent when we fail to immediately observe it, but at least we can make some difference between a simple game and a complex one: you would presumably expect less implicit complexity from a simple game than a complex one, because you're focusing on the ratio of observed complexity vs. actual complexity, instead of merely the question of whether there is any emergent content (most systems do - only the simplest systems are so obvious that humans grasp them entirely and immediately all at once), or how much of it there is.

    (If we were merely focusing on the absolute amount of emergent content, one would assume that a more complex game would always be the "more emergent": of course you're going to have more features that only become evident in extended operation when the system is more complex and therefore has more states, operations and combinations available. This "linear amount of emergence" is not as useful a concept as "elegance", or "ratio of implicit to overt complexity", though, for many purposes.)

    All this is of course not to say that there's any particular disagreement here about the topic - emergence can be achieved in heavy games as well, as I said up near the beginning. I guess Joe just heard something from somewhere and wanted us to affirm that we don't think that way, or something? Internet is full of stupid ideas, I hear, and we certainly can discuss them even if we don't particularly disagree with anybody about them.

    \n Fruitful Void is related, yeah. One might say that Fruitful Void is the place that the game ideally delineates for emergent (undesigned) behaviours to occur. It's the place where the rpg designer leaves us to figure out the answer ourselves, so it's pretty much emergent by definition.

    I do agree that all rpgs have emergence, but then I also think that all systems have emergence as long as you have a dumb enough observer, so ultimately I think that it's fair to talk about some games being more or less emergent than others. I mean, I totally think that I get less emergent behaviour in Zombie Cinema than in Shadow of Yesterday, to name two games from opposite poles that I know well. Dust Devils is just about in between the two. At one end of the continuum I get games that may only surprise with an unusual dice distribution or the particular creativity brought by the players, while at the other end there are games with sufficient freedom of process to have almost any aspect of the game (aside from creative agenda, I suppose) to become more and more emergent, and less and less tied down to the hints provided by the rulebook.

    Regarding games (as opposed to systems in general), I might hazard that "emerging behaviour" is something that is necessarily possible as long as the game has not been solved. Regarding play (as opposed to games with victory conditions), the "system" we're observing is really just the creative matrix of human behaviour, so of course it's going to be immensely complex, with massive potential for emergence. Because roleplaying games are by definition (my definition, anyway) first "play" and only occasionally "game", it's no wonder if they all involve emergence as an outcome of play. To design a roleplaying game with no emergence, we would have to be able to design all ways a player might relate to the game.

    \n I think that emergent gameplay requires you to draw on pre-existing rules.

    In a rules-lite game, those rules come from a source external to the game: the common sense and shared genre assumptions of the players.

    In a rules-heavy game, those rules are coded into the game, and must be learned.

    For someone who hasn't learned the rules well, a rules-heavy game is a barrier to emergent gameplay. For someone who has, though, the consistency of the rules lends to stronger emergent gameplay, because you involve the rules in fresh ways, and you can have the satisfaction of knowing that certain things were, in fact, required and in line with the rest of the game.

    EDIT: To sum up better...

    Rules-heavy games have less of a barrier to creating emergent play, in one sense, because rules-lite games require you to evaluate and create rules around doing crazy things. In a rules-heavy game, the rules suggest those crazy things as you tinker with them.

    By learning a game, you begin to see rules as tools instead of as requirements.
