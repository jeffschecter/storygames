\n So, we had our first session of Acts of Evil, and I see a lot of promise to this game. Look for a real session report soonish, but probably not until after Gateway.

So, LA Gamers representin'. Mark was GM, Josh, Colin and I were players.

In the meantime, I have burning curiosities about how the game is meant to work, in the hopes that we can play it without drifting too far. (First and biggest criticism: the text is opaque. The systems look solid, and I think every question we had was answered by the text, but damned if it didn't take us a solid, uninterrupted 90 minutes to understand the 9 equations enough to make characters. We have ideas for play aids, and layout and stuff.)

Okay, in some particular order:\n
    \n
  1. PC Nemesises Can a PC become the Nemesis of another PC? Especially with Conmittance, where you could be suggesting fetishization into your nemesis visage, it seemed more than a little feasible. Strategically it's interesting because as your potential Nemesis, I don't want to resist you.
    2. \n
  2. Slayings Can I narrate the casual murder of otherwise unimportant NPCs? Our ultimate reading was that technically, NPCs narrated in as scenery were pretty much player created Nobodies, and so real NPCs, and so would require an outright Slaying to kill, although that seemed weird in the context of evil cultists. We realized after the fact that the NPC who did die was probably, yes, a Nobody, and that the Slaying rule probably meant that any of us could have demanded that Josh use a successful resolution to kill her, but none of us cared, so we let the killing go. Honto or drift?
    3. \n
  3. Transendence and Terrenes Mark's explanation, which we let lie, was that Terrenes are assumed to be Earthly locations, and as such, Temporal transendance can reach all the other Terrenes, but Cosmic can meet other Cosmic transcendants but not reach other Terrenes. Correct?
    4. \n
  4. Conflicts not with requested NPC (hypothetically) I call for a scene with a Teacher. Mark frames the dinner party example (and maybe the example treats on this question) from the book, with a Teacher and many Rivals. Can I go from a successful Teacher Resolution, and use my newfound Clarity to resolve against a Rival? Can I take a lonely scene with just a Teacher, and turn from a successful resolution to a resolution with a Nobody that I narrate into the scene? How much of that it kosher?
    5. \n
  5. GM's Role Finally, what's the GM trying to accomplish? I got a very "you know, GMmy kind of things" vibe from the book, but maybe there's a better explanation.
    6. \n
\nLet me go into more detail on the last question. The GM's powers give him the ability to severely monkey-wrench the strategies of the players, since if I call for a scene with a Rival, I might get an NPC, I might get a weaker PC, or I could get thrown up against the leader, who could have double my power. It seemed like part of the strategy for requesting scenes involves the GM presenting a sort of evil genius to be played against - I carefully construct a situation where there's only the one Victim that I want, so that I know what I'm getting when I call for a Victim scene - and some of it requires that the GM be enabling. Mark played several NPCs as incredibly solicitous, in an effort to goad us to higher action (dismally unsuccessful), which wound up being completely counter our strategic intentions of finding conflicts to roll against.

And really, in a lot of ways, the stance the GM takes colors a lot of the rest of my questions. For example, if the GM twists scene requests as much as possible, then I have an incentive to kill off difficult NPCs, and the other players have an incentive to require me to Slay them. If terrenes are less reachable, they have more of an incentive to stand in the way, since the difficulty only exists for their opponent.

Like I said, most of these questions have answers that can be teased from text, but it's difficult and uncertain work.

    \n Paul,

    That kind of helps. Maybe I (and we as table) need to chew on that idea some more, but maybe it'll help both of us if I frame the question a little differently.

    As a player, the forward looking, drunk on special effects comes through clearly. And it comes through because I can look at my character sheet, and the 9 equations, and see that (Aha! I need more Aspect, and to get there I need more Power, so:) "I'm calling for a scene with a Victim." And I prepare to chase down poor Galena in the form of a 500 pound white wolf.

    But in the GMs chair, there's less clarity. Mark was doing a great job at taking requests for Teachers and Nobodies and coming up with brand new characters, for the most part. I think, maybe, that the lizard men resulted from an envy of our special effect budget.

    But! Between the choice of existing characters, and the way NPCs are portrayed, the GM has some huge strategic tools. Given a call for a Nobody, does he invent one, or present the Agency 4/Purpose 6 near-protagonist? Does the amount of Power that the calling PC has influence that decision? In the scene, should he withhold conflict until presented with the over the top special effects he wants to see?

    As players, we're not sure how to read the GMs part in our machinations. Sure, we can go to someone else's favorite victim (or just the ones in the home Terrene just after Transcending time) and give them a whopping agency. But we can't guarantee which Victims the GM will present to the other Occultists. We know what our opponents are striving for, the same way we can look across a chessboard and infer what Black wants.

    So, the GM has the biggest strategic tools, and he's a complete wild card, even to himself. "Focus on the present moment" is a start to giving him a direction, but I'd like to hear something more.

    I don't think that a mechanical pressure to choose NPCs is required, but some sort of goals that those choices serve might be. As an example: "to keep the competition going: drag back the leader, make things easier on the back of the pack" or "you're own malicious entertainment at the expense of your players" would both be fine goals to pursue, and neither need be universal across tables.

    Is any of that making sense?
