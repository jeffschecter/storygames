    \n It's A4, 126 pages, with a spiral binding. We did this to make it easy for teachers to copy pages from the book, and to let them leave it open on the desk while reading, taking notes etc.

    The basic design philosophy for the book has been: Make it easy for teachers to use. This goes for system design, book layout, text, the physical book... everything. It's been a very interesting and enlightening experience for me; we've been able to cut away so much baggage and just focus on what actually works, what people need. Very refreshing to work with a role-playing game for people with no role-playing experience!

    \n Couple of minor points, really--would take all of a half hour (in Photoshop/GIMP), if the text layers are preserved.

    1) Is "Rune Andersens" supposed to be possessive ("Rune Andersen's" or "Rune Andersens's")? Might be a grammar rule difference of which I am not aware, though....
    2) "De hoye landen" seems too 'small' in relation to the rest of the cover space--I'd scale up and widen it so that it was closer to 75% of the cover width, not ~50% like now.
    3) Also, that line is the only one with no effects on it (the white drop 'shadow' or transparent boxes on the other text), which gives is a sort of 'flat' look compared to other text on the cover. It looks like it's 'punched out' of the cover, or that it's supposed to be a white drop 'shadow' and the original main font layer is missing. Consider, maybe, a "douple drop shadow" which results in a black outline with a white outline around that, to better-parallel the credit line below it (see #4 below).
    4) Conversely, "Skrevet an Matthijs Holter" seem too 'busy' relative to the other lines--the white drop 'shadow' is fairly heavy, for the overall font size. I'd scale the text size up in a similar ratio to the scale-up of the main title, while keeping the thickness of the drop 'shadow' the same, which should give the black text better overall weight, make the white 'shadow' less dominant, and make the line closer to 50% of the cover width.
    5) #2-#4 above all would generally expand the text further down the cover, overlapping some more of the stumpy, tan tree limb above the couple. This is a Good Thing, as that limb almost dominates the composition--I literally feel my eyes stumble or lock on that limb, after reading the title and credit lines, before continuing down to the 'real' content of the couple. The scaling suggested in #2-#4 would also 'use up' a lot more of the (somewhat static and dull) big brown limbs/trunks/whatever at the top of the page... which seem to me to be there to give a 'background' with minimal busyness to the titling in the first place (a Good Thing).
    6) The bottom box is just a BIT closer to the margin than the top box, which gives the impression of error, not intent. I'd either equalize that (same distance for both from their respective margins) or I'd further emphasize the intentional difference (by pulling the top box and title and credit lines down, say, a 1/4").
    7) Similarly, I'd shift the title and credit lines up or down a bit (after scaling above), to equalize their spacing OR to make intentional spacing variation more obvious and 'artful'.

    Summary: monkey more with your font effects, sizes, and spacing relative to each other and the page. This is very much a 'feel' process--hard to explain, easy to show, obvious when it's all in balance.

    \n I, too, thought about colorizing some of the text--the orange is a good call, Justin; would balance across the page nicely--but that could make for a lot more effects work to keep the color from fading into the background illustration. Not a big deal... but not "a half hour" of fiddling, anymore. I'd be looking at bevel effects (instead of drop shadows), some gradient work (to avoid overwhelming the illustration), maybe a lighting effect render or what-not. Just colorizing to orange, with maybe a drop shadow/outline, could end up being two steps back.

    Anyhow... fontography and design balance are definitely art forms, hard to just say "do FOO and you're golden." If they're tight for time, I'd focus on what simple effects can better balance the existing white/black text, rather than head off afield on color work and it's additional effects needs.

    That said, yeah... the box transparency needs to be fiddled--I think it could look better either MORE transparent or solid. I would NOT eliminate the box, just because that makes for still-more font effect needs which could either get schitzo-looking or overwhelm the art (if all text is rendered the same). Also, the boxes sort of imply an "imprint" or "publisher" "stamp"--very common in book designs, particularly if in a series (ex: Ziff Davis or Houghton Mifflin).
