\nClassic Story Engine returns with the Plus Edition featuring new organization, new options, and new genre plug-ins. Designed to run story-based games from the ground up, Story Engine focuses on scenes rather than turns, making crucial moments seem like both dramatic and cinematic events. Its adjective-based characters help weave a story around them, while innovative mechanics adapt seamlessly to any genre, providing fluid and fun games.
\n
\nNow available in downloadable PDF, with the option to also Pre-Order the Softcover. Order Now!
\n
\nStory Engine was originally published by Hubris Games as a universal, scene-driven roleplaying system in 1999, with a Revised Edition released in 2001. Its first debut was in 1996 as the rules system for the Maelstrom Storytelling game. Cut to 2011 and Precis Intermedia breathes new life into this highly-acclaimed roleplaying game, making it more accessible while also introducing a few new twists.
\n
\nIncorporating one of the first e-publishing RPG companies, Precis Intermedia is known for its diverse line-up of clear and concise games. Precis Intermedia's current game lines include the ENnie Award-winning Ancient Odysseys for fantasy gaming; ENnie Award-nominated Two-Fisted Tales for thrilling pulp action; Brutes, New World Disorder, Warcosm, and A Fistfull of Miniatures for miniatures gaming; HardNova 2, Coyote Trail, EarthAD 2, Mean Streets, and Ghostories for low-cost, ready-to-play entertainment; Active Exploits Diceless for universal diceless roleplaying with that familiar 'dice' feel; Story Engine for scene and adjective-based, collaborative roleplaying; and more.     \n I guess part of the problem is actually a scene's relationship to the dice in general. It's not explained well in the rules. Do you set up a scene and goals and then roll the dice and play out the scene based on the results? Do you play out a scene until there's some kind of turning point and then establish goals and parameters and then roll, playing out the conclusion?

    If the former, there's no time for a quick-take. If the latter, how do you know that the potential quick-take is something that "would not normally be possible during the larger scene" (whatever that even means)?

    And then there's the definition of a quick-take itself. What makes something "not [...] possible during the larger scene"? And the examples are no help (to me). If enemy planes are defending a zeppelin, taking out the lead plane sounds like it would be high on my anti-zeppelin to-do list, not something impossible to accomplish during the scene.

    And then it goes on to say that you can use a quick-take to guarantee some action "\u2014instead of relying on winning the overall scene to accomplish the action", which suggests that a quick-take is something that actually is possible during the larger scene.

    It's all very messy and confusing and it makes me sad because it has held a dysfunctional place in my heart since forever.

    \n I think it's important to put Story Engine in context. It was one of the first (the first?) game to really formalize scene resolution as opposed to the ever-prevalent task resolution. Most scenes are resolved with a fortune in the middle dice mechanic. Quick Takes are really a cheat, a throw-back to task resolution.

    Quick Takes allow you to add more detail when you have a particularly important/cool action that you want to resolve with dice. Even within the context of a scene, you can "zoom in" to a particular task, roll the dice, resolve that action, and then zoom back out to finish resolving the overall scene. Or you can use a Quick Take outside of scene resolution, when you want to resolve one particular action quickly without going through the entire scene resolution process.

    Quick Takes are optional, but you can think of them generally as the system's task resolution mechanics.
