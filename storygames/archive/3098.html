\n So, would an enlightened soul be able to give me an example of how a social conflict, composure stress and all, would work in SotC? I'm particularly interested in how one focused on Deceit would work. E.g., someone is trying to bluff someone else. What kinds of maneuvers would be used, and what needs to happen for one side to win? A consequence, or taken out? And what is the bluff-ee doing? Are they retaliating somehow and inflicting stress on the bluff-er?

And Cat and Mouse... I'm not sure I understand how that would work.

Thanks in advance.

    \n Maybe this example will be useful.
    PCs are Andrew Mellon, his daughter Ailsa, son-in-law David, and son Paul. The NPC is Admiral Wrangel. The location is Skokloster Castle, the Wrangel ancestral home, which now show signs of disrepair [Scene aspects are: medieval castle; in need of maintenance; sold off family treasures]

    Wrangel has a map the PCs want, but he won\xe2\u20ac\u2122t sell it. From an earlier assess, the PCs know that Wrangel loves to gamble, though, so a high stakes poker game is set up.
    David, with \xe2\u20ac\u02dcSuperb\xe2\u20ac\u2122 (+5) Gambling skill will play against Wrangel, also a 'Superb' gambler. The other PCs watch the game.
    Wrangel\xe2\u20ac\u2122s Resolve is Fair (+2); his Composure stress track is 6.

    As the poker game begins, let\xe2\u20ac\u2122s say the initiative is Wrangel, then Ailsa, Paul, Andrew Mellon, and David. The PCs have 5 Fate Points each.
    Wrangel starts off with a straight Gambling skill roll. I\xe2\u20ac\u2122d envision this as at least the first hand of cards, perhaps more. David\xe2\u20ac\u2122s defense is his own Gambling skill roll. Let\xe2\u20ac\u2122s say David is also a \xe2\u20ac\u02dcSuperb\xe2\u20ac\u2122 Gambler, with a composure stress track of 7 due to high Resolve. .
    Wrangel rolls a (-1), resulting in a 4 \xe2\u20ac\u02dcGreat\xe2\u20ac\u2122. The GM tags his aspect \xe2\u20ac\u02dcwill bet the farm\xe2\u20ac\u2122 and spends one of Wrangel\xe2\u20ac\u2122s Fate Points (let\xe2\u20ac\u2122s say he has two left) to add +2 to the roll; final result is +6.
    David defends\xe2\u20ac\u201dhis roll is +1; combined with his skill of 5 results is +6, equaling Wrangel\xe2\u20ac\u2122s roll and preventing any composure loss.
    Now it\xe2\u20ac\u2122s the players\xe2\u20ac\u2122 turn.
    Ailsa, a beautiful young woman, clings to her husband\xe2\u20ac\u2122s side and proves a distraction to Wrangel. Her player is attempting a maneuver to add the aspect \xe2\u20ac\u02dcdistracted\xe2\u20ac\u2122 on Wrangel. She rolls her \xe2\u20ac\u02dcGreat\xe2\u20ac\u2122,+4, Rapport against Wrangel\xe2\u20ac\u2122s Fair (+2) Resolve. Ailsa\xe2\u20ac\u2122s initial score after her roll is +5; Wrangel\xe2\u20ac\u2122s is +4 (he rolled 2). Because Ailsa achieved at least one success, Wrangel is \xe2\u20ac\u02dcdistracted\xe2\u20ac\u2122.
    Paul Mellon goes next. Paul wants to see what Wrangel\xe2\u20ac\u2122s playing style or comments during play reveal about himself. He uses Empathy to \xe2\u20ac\u02dcread\xe2\u20ac\u2122 Wrangel. Paul\xe2\u20ac\u2122s Empathy is Great \xe2\u20ac\u02dc+4\xe2\u20ac\u2122. Wrangel will defend with Rapport, which is Good \xe2\u20ac\u02dc+3\xe2\u20ac\u2122. Paul\xe2\u20ac\u2122s roll is -2, which is disappointing, so Paul spends a fate point to tag his own aspect, \xe2\u20ac\u02dcthe old college try\xe2\u20ac\u2122 to reroll. The new roll is +1, totaling +5. Paul spends another fate point to tag his aspect of \xe2\u20ac\u02dcthe old boy network\xe2\u20ac\u2122 (interpreting it to mean he\xe2\u20ac\u2122s able to build camraderie with Wrangel) for a total result of +7. Wrangel\xe2\u20ac\u2122s defense roll is -1, modified to +2. Paul successfully reads one of Wrangel\xe2\u20ac\u2122s aspects\xe2\u20ac\u201dthe GM reveals that Wrangel is \xe2\u20ac\u02dcselling Swedish ore to the Nazis\xe2\u20ac\u2122.
    Andrew Mellon is next. Recognizing that Wrangel will not bet the map unless forced to, Mellon wants to raise the stakes of the game beyond Wrangel\xe2\u20ac\u2122s immediate ability to cover, and place on Wrangel the aspect \xe2\u20ac\u02dcout of cash\xe2\u20ac\u2122. To do this Mellon uses his Superb Resources (+5), added to his roll of +2, totaling +7. Wrangel\xe2\u20ac\u2122s own Resources are Fair +2; his resources roll adds 2 more, totaling +4. Mellon successfully raises the stakes and adds the aspect \xe2\u20ac\u02dcout of cash\xe2\u20ac\u2122 to Wrangel.
    Next is David\xe2\u20ac\u2122s turn. David wants to defeat Wrangel at the card game. But a straight Gambling contest would be opposed by Wrangel\xe2\u20ac\u2122s own gambling skill\xe2\u20ac\u201dwhich is Superb. So David will attempt to bluff Wrangel. David will use Great \xe2\u20ac\u02dc+4\xe2\u20ac\u2122 Deceit, combined with Superb \xe2\u20ac\u02dc+5\xe2\u20ac\u2122Gambling. The gambling skill will add +1 to David\xe2\u20ac\u2122s bluff (Deceit)[combining skills p.63]. Wrangel defends against the bluff using Empathy, which is Average (+1). David rolls 0. But then he\xe2\u20ac\u2122ll freely tag Wrangel\xe2\u20ac\u2122s \xe2\u20ac\u02dcdistracted\xe2\u20ac\u2122 and \xe2\u20ac\u02dcout of cash\xe2\u20ac\u2122 and \xe2\u20ac\u02dcselling ore to the Nazis\xe2\u20ac\u2122 for an additional +6 [these are free because it is the first time in the scene these new aspects are being tagged p. 41]\xe2\u20ac\u201dbringing that last aspect into play by implying Mellon\xe2\u20ac\u2122s businesses would buy ore at a good price\xe2\u20ac\u201dand spend two fate points to tag his own aspects \xe2\u20ac\u02dccan\xe2\u20ac\u2122t resist a bet\xe2\u20ac\u2122 and \xe2\u20ac\u02dccurrent mistress\xe2\u20ac\u2122 (indicating Wrangel\xe2\u20ac\u2122s wife is a former lover) for an additional +4; totaling 15 (including the +1 for Gambling). Wrangel\xe2\u20ac\u2122s defense roll is -1, modified to 0. Wrangel will spend a fate point to tag his aspect \xe2\u20ac\u02dcwill bet the farm\xe2\u20ac\u2122, and another fate point to tag an aspect \xe2\u20ac\u02dcno one likes a cheater\xe2\u20ac\u2122, raising his result to +4. The net difference is 11, which is significantly more than Wrangel\xe2\u20ac\u2122s 6 composure track, so Wrangel marks off his stress 6 box and he must take a consequence. An appropriate consequence could be something like \xe2\u20ac\u02dcWrangel owes David Bruce a fortune\xe2\u20ac\u2122 or \xe2\u20ac\u02dcWrangel puts the map in the pot\xe2\u20ac\u2122. Wrangel could also offer a concession\xe2\u20ac\u201dperhaps offering the map in lieu of owing any money, or offering the map in exchange for a lucrative business deal with the Mellon financial empire.

    At this point, if the GM decides that Wrangel doesn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t concede or simply puts the map in the pot, the card game would continue. The PCs would have to continue the game to gain another consequence. They might even have to force a \xe2\u20ac\u02dctaken out\xe2\u20ac\u2122 result on Wrangel to then narrate that Wrangel does, in fact, give up the map\xe2\u20ac\u201dand probably a lot more as well, as the taken out result allows the victors to describe the loser\xe2\u20ac\u2122s fate. The PCs could continue to \xe2\u20ac\u02dcgang up\xe2\u20ac\u2122 on Wrangel\xe2\u20ac\u201dwith some PCs using maneuvers to apply aspects on Wrangel that then one of the PCs uses to make a big hit. The trick is not to run out of fate points!

    \n Actually the book says you can freetag only an aspect per action (the other free tags still available for other characters or for subsequent actions).

    But it's a very widespread houserule (and endorsed by Rob, iirc) to allow the so called "freetag stacking" in order to encourage the kind of teamplay action where the group puts a number of aspects on a big evil guy, and then one of them lands a mighty blow tagging all those in one go. This way it's also simpler to go straight to Consequences.

    \n The book's prohibition against multiple free tags was put down while we were still uncertain if stacked free-tags would be system-wrecking. But the community has come out overwhelmingly in favor of stacking, based on some post-publication experiences we've had, and folks "voting" with their house rules, so it's something I'm a big fan of. (I thought I might be a fan of it, but wasn't sure; Rob needed more play experiences than we were going to have prior to publication to know one way or the other. It's also very much a dial to twiddle depending on your genre conventions.)
