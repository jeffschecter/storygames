My game, Your game, Our game

\n
\n
\n
\n\nTonyLB \n\n\n
\n
\n\nOctober 2007 \n edited October 2007 in Play Advice
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n Here's something I've been pondering lately: Each person in most RPGs is, in some sense, playing two games simultaneously.
\n
\nOn the one hand, they are playing a game which is solitaire, all about them. This game is often limited to their character sheet and their own numbers. It is solitary and often fiercely private.
\n
\nOn the other hand, they are playing a game which is all open, all out front, all joint with the other players. It's often on an entirely different set of resources (and often contains vastly more of the fiction of the game).
\n
\nThe cross-over inputs between these two games are sometimes remarkably few. I'm sitting there watching my fighter's hit points dwindle away under Balrog attack, and when a lightning bolt arches in from the character sheet of our mage, I'm like "Holy shit! Deus ex machina! Suddenly it's a whole new ball-game!"
\n
\nThe cross-over inputs are sometimes remarkably tightly intertwined ... to the point where I would argue that large portions of the character sheet of all players become part of "Our game" rather than their private property. "C'mon, c'mon! You can see my hit-points here, people! Gimme some artillery support. And Matt, I know you've got the spell slots to fast-cast a Cure Major, so don't sit there hauling away with your dinky little d8 mace when you've got a front-line fighter about to drop in his boots!"
\n
\nThis is without even getting into the fact that some people's private game is an entirely different game with entirely different rules ... yeah, Mr. GM, I'm lookin' at you.
\n
\nThat's my thought for the morning. Not quite sure how to capitalize on it in design, but I think there should be a way.

    \n A great example of the GM's game, in the sense that I think Tony is talking about:

    The Cruel Fortunes in Poison'd

    The GM resources / constraints which Vincent provides are literally game-like: in order to bring certain effects into play you have to create the right set of predefined other fortunes. Some you can start with, others you can bring into play via the Prizes mechanics, and a few are only available in a preset group of circumstances. The players, in this case, have the ability to see what the GM can do, but they can only intervene in very limited ways. (E.g., they can bring the fortune Malcontentment into play.)

    The players instead have access to Opportunities, a set of options that the players have access to collectively, with the character elected Captain possessing certain privileges in controlling these options--though the other players have an investment and influence over those choices.

    Players can then create mutual bonds between subgroups by means of Bargains, and each has some partial control over their own character. The more I think about it, the different parts of the system break down into different sub-games with different participants, in exactly the way Tony suggests.

    \n Play is what people do. Games are the constructed activity that arise out of play.
