\n I\u2019ve just finished a playtest draft of Bad Family, a comedy game where everyone plays a star in an animated sit-com about a family having the worst day of their lives.

It\u2019s a game for four to six players, and it\u2019s designed so you can play an episode in about an hour (or just over).

To play it, you should be enthusiastically interested in family sitcoms (like The Simpsons, Family Guy, and Modern Family) and / or relationships between family members and stories about dysfunctional families (like The Royal Tennenbaums, Breaking Bad, or pretty much any TV show ever).


Where can I get it?
You can download Bad Family here: Bad Family .pdf (from dropbox)

It\u2019s a game in playtest at the moment, and if you play it I\u2019d be keen for feedback.


What do I like about it?
- It\u2019s funny: scenes are resolved using a best-case vs worst-case scenario mechanic that has always produced spiraling chaos every time I\u2019ve played

- I\u2019ve deliberately designed Bad Family to be a very simple game: it\u2019s almost just a structure for telling a fun story with your friends

- The second half of the rules are written as a script for someone to read to the other players, guiding them through three introductory rounds that teach you how to play the game. I\u2019d never written something like that before, and it was fun and challenging

- Socially, I\u2019ve tried to design some of the procedures in the game so that the players get to know more about each other as they play.


Short version of the rules
You choose a character to play from a list of Family Members, and decide what your Family Member wants for this episode.

You\u2019ll all take turns being the Star of a scene. As the Star, you describe how your Family Member tries to get what she wants.

During the scene, all the other players do the following:
- ask leading questions about how you're trying to achieve your goal
- pretend to be guest stars who appear in the scene
- suggest things that could happen (which the Star can accept or ignore)
- play the audience, responding with laughs and 'awwws' just like a studio audience or laugh track.

But everyone else also competes to introduce something that could go horribly wrong in the Star's scene. If things do go wrong, the person who introduced the worst-case scenario gets closer to winning the game.


Origin story
I\u2019ve been working on Bad Family since 2004 \u2013 it\u2019s the first game I seriously sat down to write after visiting the Forge for the first time.

That first version went through three rounds of playtesting at local cons and with friends, and it became quite a complicated game. The feedback I received caused me to put it on the shelf for about three years.

Obviously my sub-conscious was mulling over it: two and a half months ago I got hit with an epiphany about how to simplify the game. Whereas the original version had a game board and various types of tokens and points you had to track, in this new version players have to collect three cards (from an ordinary deck) to win.


Who am I?
I'm a New Zealand based writer and script-writer. I wrote and directed a feature film comedy called \u2018hopeless\u2019, and co-created its 26-part sit-com spin-off, Love Bites. (These are terrible titles, and I can tell you some stories about working with production companies and networks some time.)

I edited Mike Sands' Apocalypse World hack, Monster of the Week. I\u2019ve written quite a few games for game competitions. My next project will be rewriting Left Coast, a game about sci-fi authors in 1960s California, which had quite a bit of playtesting and editing feedback over the last six months.


Anyway, hope you enjoy Bad Family. Again, you can download the game here: Bad Family .pdf (from dropbox)

I'll be pretty busy over the next couple of weeks, but if you've got questions I'm happy to answer them when I can.

    It's been an odd game when I've playtested it. One-shots tend to go a little crazy with stuff happening like:

    - a dad trying to impress Wayne Gretsky by bench-pressing a dolphin
    - an office rivalry escalating to the point where someone needs to defuse a nuclear bomb
    - a small child who wants to confront her fears about climbing a tree being blocked by the tree coming to life and mocking her.

    But the game I've played in longest (about eight episodes now) has gotten a lot darker. It's a family that idolises the oldest son, Alex, who's perfect at everything (school, music, goes to Harvard, loved by the community) but is always off-screen. The legend of Alex has warped the entire family who are either trying to live up to his reputation, define themselves as separate people, being stalked by people who love Alex, or (in the case of the dad) are so focused on Alex that he neglects and forgets the rest of his children.

    It's been really fun to watch, and we're now at the point where a lot of the characters have acknowledged that they're screwed up and they want to get better and be a better family ... but the game won't let them.

    I've (finally) finished a more thorough read-through of this. It's looking really good! The rewrite since the last version has been time well-spent, I think. It's got a clear agenda, and rules that look like they'll consistently deliver on that.

    I do have a few questions though.

    In "Star" scenes, I'm not exactly clear on what play looks like up until the point when someone comes up with a "worst case scenario". The text makes it sound like the Star player is describing the scene, and the other players are asking questions and drawing out more material. The example of play, on the other hand, makes it seem like the other players are describing things happening in the scene which challenge the Star character. For what it's worth, I think the play as described in the examples seems more natural and fun. I wonder about whether it would feel like the Star player was providing their own antagonism otherwise.

    The "Knock" player gets to take some cards and give them out and suchlike. I wonder if this rule is strictly needed. What purpose do you see it serving?

    The "Worst Case Scenario" is really the heart of the game, and I think you've done some smart things to really make that mechanic central in every scene. It seems though that half the time, people are coming up with these great worst-case scenarios, but nothing comes of them. Is there a way of making red card narrations incorporate elements from the worst-case? Like, "everything described in the worst-case scenario happens, but it works out to the good of the Star character". You could almost have a table (an option for each card suit?) "The opposite of what was described happens instead" etc.

    On that subject, I think it would be helpful to clarify the difference between the interjections the players make before "The Knock" and the content of the Knock itself. It seems like before the knock, players are saying things which present obstacles but also opportunities for the Star character to go after their Want. The Knock, on the other hand, is a definite description of the character utterly failing to get what they want. This is a case where I think maybe you rely overly on advice, where a hard rule might serve better.

    That's all I've got. I really like how pared-down and minimal this game has become. It's a neat structure that I think will deliver on the promise of hilarious and quick games.

    \n Good point! My hope with the game is that the rules contribute to setting up a tension between 'being selfish' and 'being altruistic': I'm trying to make the rules create the social dynamics of a real family.

    You're definitely trying to grab as many cards as you can, so there's a real reluctance to give them away. However, in Family scenes (where the whole family gathers and their problems intersect), you gather cards if someone decides that your Family Member has helped them the most during their entire experience of playing Bad Family to date (not just the previous round of scenes; it includes in all of the previous episodes, too).

    I'm trying to create a gamble - that a bit of altruism now (giving your cards away in Star scenes) will help you a lot, later.

    But I have to keep playtesting whether this works (it's seemed to in my most recent games), whether the balance of risk and reward is right (I'm totally not sure about this) or whether the whole trade-off is just a bit too complicated for the simple game I want to write.

    What I really liked
    \xd7 The language and the structure of the text. It feels like you talk to people that haven't played roleplaying games before, and I like it.     \xd7 The theme, where you use tropes to easily make people understand what the game is about. Name drop a few TV series, have a fixed number of family members that everybody can recognize, and the players are ready to go.

    \n Hi Steve,

    The rule might say something like:

    "Players other than the Star, you can describe anything you like happening in the scene, you can introduce new characters, have them say and do whatever you like, and invent anything you want about the world and the people in it. But:
    1) You can't say anything about what the Star character does, thinks, or feels. That's up to the Star.
    2) You can't decide whether the Star gets or doesn't get their goal. You can throw obstacles or opportunities in their way, even things that make it very difficult or very easy to get what they want, but ultimately, what you describe must leave open the question of whether the Star gets their goal. If there's doubt about this, ask the other players if what you're describing leaves the goal open.

    The Knock: When you knock, you can describe something happening in the scene, as normal, but the rules are slightly different. Instead of describing something happening, you invent a "Worst Case Scenario", something terrible that MIGHT happen. The rules for these are slightly different:
    1) You can't introduce any new characters [I just made this up now, but it's maybe a good idea].
    2) What you describe must irrevocably and unambiguously prevent the Star from getting their goal."

    Does that make sense to you? I think I'm mostly describing what the rules already suggest you do, I'm just making explicit rules which someone can point to in play.

    \n Hey Steve!

    We got some time over after a Fiasco session (a game that hasn't really lifted for me), and I suggested that we finally should try Bad Family. Said and done: the game was on. I had forgotten most of the parts of the game so I played the game with my nose in my pamphlet. Should try to read it one more time before I bring it to another party this weekend.

    I used the tag lines for the game, telling the participants that we were going to play a game where we all were going to be a part of a animated dysfunctional family. I name dropped the list of TV series from the book, and handed out the character sheets. I gave Simpsons and Family Guy some extra weight, because I thought most people had seen those shows.

    Me and five other players started to choose from the list of the family members. I thought it took a little to much time to ask about in what order everybody was born, and it would be faster to just hand out the names and let the participants choose in clockwise order, starting from the player on my left. On the other side, the talk about who is the youngest in the family follows the theme of the game. Some of the participants had also just met each other for the first or second time so it's a kind of bonding as well to know if one player is the youngest, that another one had three half-siblings and things like that. So I ended up liking the idea of who chooses the family member first.

    I continued explaining that all members will have a goal in the episode but only two will reach their goals. I gave some suggestions from my mind on what kind of goals that could exist to show that what the member's wanted could be anything from going out with the dog to start a nuclear plant. Four of the players (including me) knew directly what their characters should want to achieve on this session. I started to ask questions to the two remaining players, that wasn't really sure what their characters wanted. I asked why they choose those members and said that they could take anything in the everyday life or something that they wanted IRL. Finally, I handed out the list of the suggested Wants.

    We used the following family mambers:
    • Alex Jones: oldest child. Wanted to do drugs.
      • \n
    • Jamie Jones: middle child. Wanted to attend to a school party.
      • \n
    • River Jones: youngest child (can't really be the youngest, because there's an infant in the family, but hey...). Wanted to eat ice cream.
      • \n
    • Dick Jones: father. Wanted to go out with the dog.
      • \n
    • Bry Jones: lodger and family friend. Wanted to kill the cat.
      • \n
    • Annabel Jones: grandmother (on the fathers side). Wanted to throw Bry out of the house.
    \nWhen all this was done and the names (and who they were) written down on a piece of paper, I continued explaining that we were going to take turns and play out scenes where one of the member were the star (The direct translation of "star scene" into Swedish didn't sound that well, so I used "spotlight moment" instead). I started with the player to my right - I didn't really read out anything from my printed pamphlet - and asked the standard questions: "Who are you?" - "What do you want?" - "Where are you?" - "What hinders you from reaching what you want?". The first scene was about River Jones, who was grounded so she couldn't get to the fridge where all the ice cream were. After the regular questions, I told everyone that they could continue to ask questions, and I continued showing how by asking leading questions to River. After a while I knocked and explained how the rules worked for that.

    It went on like this - on my turn (because I was the next person) - I introduced the flip by giving away my black card from the first knock. I introduces a Family scene when everybody had done one star scene but no cards was given out on the knock. To cut a long story short, the cat was shoved off the roof by Bry, and Annabel threw Bry out of the house. It took a little more than one hour to play, and we kept the story around the chosen family members - we did mentioned the mum being away but that was all. Everything went on smoothly and the players - where two haven't played this kind of games before - thought it was really easy to understand (especially compared to Fiasco), mostly because the game is based on a well known trope. The theme of the game made it easy to get inspiration for scenes, and the thing with always asking questions gave both pushed the player in the star scene and was easy to understand. We usually went from asking questions to play out scenes between family members which came naturally because this episode took part in and around the house. Two players pointed out that they liked that the names was non-gender specific.

    When we played out the end scenes, we discovered that one player - one of the most silent ones - didn't get any cards. She lost both knocks in her star scenes and never knocked in another players scene. I dunno if that's a problem or not, but another player had five cards (needed three to win), mostly because he knocked even if he had already reached his goal. We established on the family tree that Alex - who didn't get any dope but instead smoked an embalmed ferret - had created a meth lab, I said that James became the coolest kid in school when she finally attended to the school party (even if he didn't reach what he wanted - a declared something for another player's family member), and grandma Annabel got someone to help her at home.

    ---

    I think this will be an instant buy if you ever publish it. People thought it was very similar to Fiasco, but easier - mostly when it comes to the structures. No one said that they liked the competitive part, and thought it would ruin more than help the game if we had cared about it. One player - one of the more quiet ones - said several times that he wanted to play the game again, and I will bring this game to a couple of the Swedish roleplaying conventions and gatherings. Perhaps even play it with some other gamers + alcohol this weekend. Who knows?

    Cheers!
