\n I hope I'm not leaving a bad taste in anyone's mouth when I invite those of you who enjoyed the original versions of D&Dto think back on those memories.

I'm hard pressed to describe explicitly what I'm talking about, but I'll point you at this list for inspiration:
http://robert.infogami.com/On_old_school_dungeons

I'm curious about what positive elements of those early experiences can be replicated. Obviously we can't be 12 again in our parent's basement, but I'd like to recapture some of the simple pleasure of encountering some wild and new challenge in the depth of the earth. The mix of danger, oddity, and Gygaxian (or other) humor.

So, what are your thoughts in regards to this? Are there distinct and identifiable ways to create that Classic D&D feel to a dungeon or adventure?

I ask that you restrain your urge for sarcasm if possible (I know it's tempting). Thanks.

    \n I've been going back through some old school modules for levels 1-3 recently and one thing I've noticed is that there are only like a dozen different monsters or so and they show up everywhere. And, they inevitably attack you.

    Poison needles are on like every chest as the trap of preference.

    Most treasure can be found a) in a belt pouch b)in the mattress/sleeping straw or c) in crates or barrels, possibly under them.

    Consistency of setting is not desirable: This week it's LotR inspired fantasy, next it's Indiana Jones, a week later you're suddenly in a Sinbad the Sailor flick. Don't even question it.

    \n\ngladen \n\n\n

    \n\nApril 2007 \n

    \n Finally! Something I can contribute to on a positive level...
    \n
    \nThe original D&D had a certain element that most games I experience these days are missing entirely. For lack of a better term, and not desiring to apply a psuedo-scientific term to it, I call that element the cool factor.
    \n
    \nIt wasn't about convulted and multi-level storylines; it wasn't about a slick running game system, it wasn't about theory, or doing something different...D&D was about having some fun.
    \n
    \n"You mean I get to be this really cool Conan guy and go hack apart monsters and haul away their loot?"
    \n
    \nThe main point that I've danced around here is that although D&D was a cumbersome system, it still had a large helping of coolness to it. The game itself, when broken down to it's ingedients lacked cohesiveness and followed no discernable logic; and the idea of the dungeon crawl simply seemed to be an extension of that lack of logic.
    \n
    \nHowever, the mechanics were simple enough that a 12 year old could easily grasp them, the need for cohesion and logic was deeply overshadowed by the fact that it was fun to play, and the core-line monsters were nasty enough to be villains, but somehow endearing enough to be fun to fight in a dungeon near you.
    \n
    \nSomehow, someway, all of this twaddle thrown together became a very fun game. The fact that it was so fun is what made it cool. It didn't matter that one had inject their own background logic into why the classes had such restricitons. It didn't matter that elves got every advantage under the stars AND could live forever (well close enough). It didn't matter that the 80 or so common beasties were pretty much the same thing over and over again with a different line-art sketch as the picture. What mattered was that it was fun.
    \n
    \nThe fond memories we harbor of our basement antics, munching on chips and downing beverages whilst we played are what we recall. So we look to the atmosphere to recreate that cool factor....but that isn't it.
    \n
    \nWithout the need for subplots (or perhaps even a plot at times), and without the need for realism, we all mamaged to dive into it with a full suspension of disbelief and enjoy ourselves.
    \n
    \nThat abstract and nearly impossible to define element that made the game so enjoyable is what I have called the cool-factor since I started reviewing games, and then later began to create them.
    \n
    \nFun is what made D&D cool for us when we were younger, and it is unfortunate that so many designers have forgotten to make their games fun to play, as well as everything else. And oddly, I find that most games that attempt to incorporate that cool-factor into them (most especially if they are in a fantasy setting) are usually scorned and labelled as "heartbreakers".
    \n
    \nIf you're looking to recreate that feeling from the old D&D, it is not the system itself, nor the trite and cliched scenarios, it was th e'cool-factor'. That elusive element that cannot be mechanized into a game, nor can rules be made to enforce it. There is something about a game, any kind of game, that just makes it fun to play; in and of itself. That is the cool-factor. I've scrapped many a completed game system, because although it was original, or alothough it was unique, and although it may have been logical and as fun yo play as Game X, or Y; it just lacked the cool-factor.
    \n
    \nTo recreate the cool factor, you simply need to forget about all of the tripe about each and every detail of the game, stop breaking it down to a molecular level to the point wher the very thought processes behind a mechanic or rule in the game has some jargon attached to it (that is contantly being debated), and just concentrate on whether or not it's going to be cool.
    \n
    \nIf a game is strong in and of iteself, and the mechanics and system are solid enough to be logical and hold up to game play, then it stands to reason that thew system should withstand a pint or two of cool.
    \n
    \nWhen one stops worrying about the convulution of the rules, and what applies when, or how to make the system withstand attacks from the critics of the opposing gaming camps and begins to enjoy putting cool things into the game for the sake of being fun, then the cool will flow from your games, and that reverie of yesteryear should turn into the phone calls from your peers asking about moving next week's scheduled session to tomorrow night.

    \n Kesher, that's an interesting thread. I'm actually pretty skeptical that the megadungeons they're waxing nostalgic about are producing fun play experiences for anyone except the Dungeon Master. But this little gem on page six was pretty good:\n
    Playing off of these ideas, and having been recently working through the inevitable complaints regarding ecology that crop up when proselytizing the megadungeon, I've been generating an argument called "How the goblins became human" (with abject apologies to Ignatiev). At some point, goblins stopped being bogeymen in the dark, waiting to snatch unwary children or lone travellers, and simply became a separate species in a pluralistic monster world. The original three books and Moldvay/Cook (my preference) don't speak of females, young, or cultural practices. They simply posit goblins as, well, goblins, and give some information on them fighting better when a chieftain sort is present. I love this presentation, as it gels with the idea of nightmarish, macabre figures in the shadows populating your dungeon, and not the run of the mill humanoid lair, with eating chambers, sleeping chambers, quarters for the ruler and his mate, etc. It's a question of genre to me--in this case, folklore and fairy tale versus National Geographic in a fantasy world. One keeps such beings mysterious and fresh, the other tends to normalize them into boredom.

    Three goblins in a room. What are they doing? What does it matter? They are simply lying in wait for hapless folk because they are goblins, and that's a powerful image to me. Don't get caught unawares, or they'll snatch you in the dark.
    \nMan, the "National Geographic" handling of humanoid races in D&D is an assumption I've never really questioned, but it does kinda stink for a couple reasons: a) it makes the monsters seem less scary, and b) leads to morally disquieting comparisons to various aboriginal/tribal peoples in the real world.

    Man, the "National Geographic" handling of humanoid races in D&D . . . leads to morally disquieting comparisons to various aboriginal/tribal peoples in the real world.
    \nYeah, no kidding. I was recently toying with doing a D&D-ish game set in the European Middle Ages, and I deliberately wanted to exclude goblins and just use humans as the people to be chopped down without mercy. The more I began thinking about it, though, the more troubling it became. Essentially the entire D&D experience--and most of the fantasy novels and canonical literature it's based upon--is founded upon identifying a Racial, Religious, or Ethnic Other, essentializing that Other as Pure Evil, and then slaughtering all of them in a righteous frenzy.

    Whatever happened to Tynes's "PowerKill"? Because that was essentially an attempt to make the same point. It's cool when you descend into a dungeon, kill goblins, and take their stuff; it's profoundly uncool if you burst into a housing project, kill the people who live there, and loot their corpses. What's the difference?\n
    We could go with something radical and not built so much for a Dungeon Crawl, just to see how an indie game or a non-D&D would break a classic dungeon and recreate it into coolness.

    \n HIya Vaxalon! (& everyone else)
    \nYes, I agree that what I was saying might, on the surface, sound like mantra of "system doesn't matter", but that is actually very far from my point. How about I paraphrase your paraphrasing and directly state that system isn't all that matters.
    \n
    \nA coherent, and playable system is, IMHO, very important to an enjoyable game; however I have found that there are certain abstract, perhaps undefinable, elements that might make up for system shoprtcomings, poor cohesion, bad logic, or any other factors. D&D was a rare bird. Let's face it, if it were published today \\, as it was in the original issue, it would be the laughing stock of the RPG world. However, aside from a haphazard system, mechanics that were neither totally roleplaying nor entirely tactical (kind of an ill-mixed melange of those), and (as has been pointed out) creatures with names taken from mythology and literature and boiled down to the lowest archetypical common denominator, there was still some abstract there that made it fun to play.
    \n
    \nSo does system matter? I vote a giant Hell Yes on that. Games are more than system. They are more than theme. they are more the sum of the text. I lok at a game as a rules system, mechanics, spirit, heart, theme, and soul. Whilst some of those are concrete things that can be systematically debated and measured, some are entirely subjective, or abstract.
    \n
    \nYou asked me if I could give you specific examples of games that I felt the designers had forgotten to make fun. Yes, I could, but my opniion on such matters was (I felt) not germaine to the discussion, nor called for. It is undeniably true that ALL game designers want to make a fun game; however I have found that so many of them are so formulaic that they seem like so much generic twaddle.
    \n
    \nTo edify my statement, I shant throw a burning doo-doo bag on the porch of these excellent game designers' works, but rather ask you this: How many movies, in your life, have you seen that you thought really truly sucked? Now do you think that any filmmaker sets out to make a bad movie?
    \n
    \nI've seen a plethora of games that were strong in the concrete and lacking in the abstract. I've seen just as many of the converse. To nmake a truly wonderful game, it must be a great system, and it must also find a niche in the spirit and theme, and all other abstracts.
    \n
    \nThat tiny target/goal, like finding the heart of your ex-wife in the courtroom, is the cool factor. I have yet to be able to pinpoint exactly what it is, or to define it in stoic and sanitary terms, but I can read a game manual, play a game, or look at my own stuff, and let you know if it is cool or not.
    \n
    \nAnd I LOVE your comment about "to make a game cool, you simply make it cool" (please forgive me if that comment is prickish, or confrontational, it is not meant to be, I truly love that comment). You may feel free to call me any four-letter word of your choosing, and I won't be bothered about it. Here's my answer. If you feel the need to break down what I said into hyperbole, then by all means, my answer is a resounding yes. No game will ever be successful if it isn't cool! What is cool, you might ask; I can't put it into some convenient definition.
    \n
    \nIf I could, I imagine that I would be much more successful than the the paultry 5-10K per year that my works bring me.
    \n
    \nWhat I was intending to communicate was that so many game designers get far too involved making sure that their pops are banging and that their game fits into either the forgeian camp, or the chaos oppositionists. I was trying to relate how so many game designers dedicate their rules to making each and every detail a laboratory sanitary aboslute, that they lose track of the primary goal; which is to make a game that is not only fun, but that they want to play again and again.
    \n
    \nTo be honest, I don't concern myself with too much with game design theory, nor do I concern myself overly with those that claim that game design theory is so much piffle disguised as profundity. I concern myself with whether or not I want to play a game, and whether or not people want to play my games.
    \n
    \nIt is almost impossible for me to put a defining finger on what makes any game that way, so I simply call it the cool factor. A cool game is one that your players call you up en-masse and ask if you can move the next game date forward from next Saturday to tonight. I cool game is one where the players who come for a 3-4 hour game session, end up going to the gorcery store at 7 AM to get enough food toi cook breakfast for everyone. A cool game is one where you have to move the furniture out of your living room and create a giant play-table for all of the extra players that have become almost fanatical regulars.
    \n
    \nD&D was like that; and some other games are like that as well. Is it the system? No, not entirely. Is it the GM? No not entirely. What is it that makes a game grow beyond a set of mechanics into something that people enjoy? That, my friend is the cool factor. I can't give set of instructions for it (i've had far more misses than hits), I can't even give you a list of qualities that cool games have. I can only notice when a game has all of the factors that will make it cool.
    \n
    \nSo to make a game cool you simply need to make it cool? YES!!!! What about games inspires your passion, your love? What excitement and joy can be conveyed? That is cool!
    \n
    \nI merely meant to state that so many game designers attempt to emulate a formula or a pattern, and their games suffer from it, because the end product has no heart, no soul, no spirit.
    \n
    \nAnd I never meant to intimate that one should disregard designing a strong system in order to make a game cool. I was merely implying that a solid system is not a cool game until the breath of life is injected into it by it somehow attaining that indescribabloe cool-factor as well.
    \n
    \nAnd you cited Dogs In The Vineyard as being broken down to the molecular level with everything having a reason for being (In a very Chic French manner). True. Very true. I do own the game, and we do play it occassionally. It's a great game, and I apologize if I ruffled your feathers. I wasn't pointing any fingers at all, just spouting my observations from the past few decades.
    \n
    \nThe RPG world is very similar to the Video Game world right now. A used game cube system is selling for less than a used NIntendo NES. IN fact, the Yobo company, and others, are marketing their clone systems to America right now. Now why is it that a good running NES is goung for $80 on th eopen market when a used Playstation 2 is going for $70? The PS2 has thousands of times more memory, roughly 63KL more colors, hundreds of times the resolution, and all sorts of bells and whistles. The fact remains that those old and outmoded NES games were simply much more cool. There ius a certain something, that is hard to define, that makes Maqrio World more desirable to play than SOCOM 12, the apology.
    \n
    \nNow is Dogs the rule, or the exception? I am not the one to judge, the public is. I think it's cool, a rare gem amongst so much crap. I also believe, firmly, in fact so much that I stake my livelihood upon it, that there is so much more to a game than just the system, just the spirit, just the heart. All things must blend together, and create a whole that keep people coming back for more.
    \n
    \nGladen

    \nI'm not sure the hallmark of old school gaming was to ignore the system - or not totally. It was only somewhat later, as people started to look for different experiences, and people started to want "story" that ignoring the rules became more prevalent.
    \n
    \nAnd sure, you will never recapture that experience of first playing D&D.; But it's actually quite easy to recapture a lot of the old school feel. Just by playing the game. It didn't last very long because of conflicting commitments, but I recently started an AD&D; 1e based campaign (admitedly using some house rules I had developed in college), and it was quite fun.     \n

I think there is room for a rehashed AD&D; 1e style game that provided a streamlined game, got rid of some of the worst of the troublesome rules (spells and such), but stayed close to AD&D.; The same for any other old school game that was successfull. There's also room for things like OSRIC whose purpose is not strictly to replace and improve on the game, but to breathe new life into the old game.
    \n

      Larry, I'll let Buzz speak for himself if he thinks I'm misrepresenting him, thanks.

      I see I owe Buzz a retraction and apology since he's at least played some TFT, though I don't know how he can then conclude that the point of the game was to ignore the rules. My point was that all of these old school games, save AD&D1e, have anything from moderately to very clear rules for the things they cover; AD&D1e is a mess of course but even so this was (often? generally?) handled not by ignoring rules but by paying enough attention to them to, effectively, edit them into a usable form. Granted this then generally served as the basis for extending the mechanics--as I argued elsewhere, things like the "dropped lantern table" in Runequest are more like fossilized evidence of that process in action, than prescriptive rules.

      That deserves a bit of explanation. Most RPGs of the era consisted of two or three unified core systems, combat, magic, and skills. Sometimes all three were integrated, sometimes not. But those rules were basically complete and playable. Other situations were handled on an ad-hoc basis, and when something was both important and occurred frequently enough, someone would make up additional rules for it. Like aerial combat, or ships, or supply rules, whatever. But both these things, and the other stuff that was handled less formally, had as their baseline the core mechanics. So lanterns dropping in the midst of combat was a frequent enough occurrence that people decided it'd be more fun to resolve the effect of same by rolling on a table, than by simply ignoring the effect or deciding arbitrarily.

      However, whatever you used to handle those sorts of peripheral issues (and they were peripheral, in the sort of old school I'm talking about), the core systems weren't there to be ignored, rather the peripheral stuff was largely a method of framing the use of the core systems, to enhance meaning (or "investment"), generate variety, and set up a sort of dialog between the purely mechanical aspects of the game and the socially-negotiated fiction. It's a bit like crystallization of a material around a "seed"; though not as deterministic as the chemical process, the game as whole grows out of the kernel of the "hard rules" and is affected by the structure of the latter even when the game's scope becomes quite large.

      \n Okay, I think we're just approaching this from different angles, which was compounded by your use of a phrase that sounded to me like an all-too-common buzzword (no pun intended).

      This thread seems to be in its last stages but I'd suggest that anyone addressing "recreating the old school feel" needs to begin by thinking about what that "feel" means to them. For me it had very little to do with the quality of the rules. It's true AD&D was a mess, but that didn't impact actual play for me because I'd already been playing white box D&D, which although written in even worse fractured prose, was simpler and thus made it easer to grasp the "core concepts" (also informed by experience with board wargaming). So coming from there, and especially as we transitioned in stages since AD&D books didn't come out all at once, it was easy to treat AD&D as a bunch of optional rules, but nevertheless to have those rules pretty much nailed down before play.

      What I took "ignoring the rules" as, and I think a few others did, too, is the oft-seen canard that older games run on "rule zero" or "GM fiat". All I wish to say about that is, all RPGs employ a great deal of discretion; formally redistributing authority over what happens in the game world doesn't change that, and still allows the participants to depart from the rules as far as they like without breaking them, technically. (If you play D&D and never roll the dice because you never get into a fight or spring a trap, you're following the rules. If you get in a fight and the GM doesn't keep track of the monsters' hit points, simply declaring them dead after enough tension has built up, the GM is breaking the rules. That's my standard, basically.)

      So what is "old school dungeon feel" to me, if it doesn't mean houseruling or fudging? To me, it's basically a game where the emphasis is on exploration (requiring actual player skill at mapping), puzzle-solving, and combat, usually without excessive concern about plausibility, morality, or any character-centric issues. The idea is to go in, kill things, take stuff, and get out alive. Being tempted by a few magic items that might do weird shit to you (often based on a die roll) also goes with the territory, as does the prospect of a few weird spectacles just thrown in for atmospherics. In fact it wouldn't be too much of a stretch to describe the old school dungeon as Las Vegas with monsters.
