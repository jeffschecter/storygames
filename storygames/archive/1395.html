I'm working up templates for various types of conflicts for Misery Bubblegum. Internal, External and Relationship. I've hit the point where ideas have stopped flowing like water for me. I could use a kick-start, if anyone finds themselves brimming with inspiration.

My basic idea is to get things that are specific enough to inspire people to customize them, but general enough to make such customization possible. My standard is that the element should be equally workable as a main plot-point in Buffy, Veronica Mars or Battlestar Galactica ... so nothing that's specifically science-fictiony or fantasy, and also nothing that couldn't be science fictiony or fantasy with appropriate customization.

    MUAHAHA! It's all part of my diabolical plan, young David. Next ... the butterscotch!

    To generalize a bit here: There are things you can say about another person (like "You look fine the way you are") that have the potential to be conflicts. Then there are things you can say about another person (like "You worry too much about how you look") that are, already, conflicts.

    And ... uh ... yeah. I had a point there at some point. Oh yeah! I remember!

    The first ones, the "might be conflicts" give a real cool payoff when you combine them with something else and actually make them a conflict. "You look fine the way you are" combined with "I don't trust you" is fun waiting to happen. But, the "might be conflicts" also have higher possibilities of turning up as complete duds. "You look fine the way you are" combined with "Why am I so lazy?" has a great potential for being 'meh'.

    By contrast, the second ones, the automatic conflicts, give a reasonable payoff no matter what you do with them, but they don't drive you to combine them with other conflicts. You can sort of say "Yeah, these two things, 'I hate myself and deserve to die,' and 'Your world is under attack' are both present in this scene, and both important. Interact with each other? Uh ... no. But they're important!"

    When I manage, by luck as much as anything else, to make one that is (a) obviously, unavoidably a conflict but (b) cannot by played until it is placed in the context of another conflict, then I start getting excited. But at this point I'm not clear how I do that ... it sometimes clicks, but I don't have a conscious grasp on the process.
