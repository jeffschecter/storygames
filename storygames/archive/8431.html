\n Ok, so I've been reading this Star Wars thread, and there's an interesting argument there about whether a game saying "the GM should make a determination" is inherently incomplete, or as I'm reading the discussion, just plain bad.

I've been playing and reading a lot of D&D retro-clones lately, as well as the Holmes Basic Set, and GM determination is everywhere in there. Exploring why people enjoy this play, I keep seeing this as a positive thing: the game is loose enough that anything you can think of to do, the GM can quickly make a call on and move on.

I'm more convinced over time that for a certain style of play, GM determination is powerful mojo. However, I've seen a lot of negativity towards "GM fiat" as unfair and unfun.

So, what's the deal? Is there a good place for this is a modern game?

    \n So I'm often the GM, and will take this from that angle for a moment.

    There are times where I've got no problem using my judgment to resolve conflicts and such. Really, in most games it happens at some level anyway, so making it explicit and focused isn't an inherently problematic issue for me. And hell, there is something really fun in that old school mode of the having the players wow you so much that you just have them win.

    But there are also a lot of times, especially when playing in more difficult games with more subtle themes, where I don't want to have the buck stop at me. When the group as a whole is trying to work around a tough theme, I don't want to have final say because it makes it too easy for the game to become about my theme alone. Or when there is a particularly difficult call with lots of status and enjoyment on the line it can be cumbersome and socially awkward to always have to be the one who makes the final call.

    There are times where always having to be The Decider is just annoying. Where it exhausts my creativity and makes me feel on the spot all the time. In those times I like a game that has some way of avoiding that.

    So really, my answer is going to be contextual and contingent. (Damn me and my relativism!) There is nothing wrong with GM determination in general, I'm just glad that I have games where I don't have to do it all the time.

    I think what I like most is games with telescoping and collapsing parts, really. So that in the same game with the same mechanical system I can sometimes make the call and sometimes not. Those games are cool.

    As a player... actually, as a player my answer is much the same. Different reasons, sure, but still the same outcome.

    \n Absolutely usable and important, I've been researching this issue myself recently. My preliminary conclusion is that the problem is in game texts focusing too much on rules over method - the text tells you that the GM makes the call, but it does not really enforce the notion that the GM has a method and purpose in how and why he makes that call. At best you have a GMing chapter as some sort of cryptological secret, sacred text the GM is supposed to learn outside game time and then apply, but at least I have great difficult getting anything out of your average, self-contradictory, vague GM text. It's like trying to use the Bible to make calls on how other people should... well, yeah, that's that analogue.

    This is also why Internet discussions of these GM calls are not usually very useful. One person tells you how the game sucks because his GM made a bad call, then another guy says that he'd never make that call in this game because obviously you're supposed to GM it this other way... and they're both right. The game sucks if you GM it badly, and rules if you don't, and it's not really something you can pin on the game unless you start demanding that games actually have real GM methodology to offer, the kind where you can really point to the text and say that a given GM is playing against the instructions.

    The real answer to this issue, I feel, is to confess that almost all old GM texts are bad, bad texts that often do more to hinder than help running a real coherent game. Game writers have tried to cater to all imaginable styles of play with vague rules and even more vague GMing instructions (including instructions on how to use those rules), which has proliferated into a game culture that doesn't believe in texts in the first place, and with good reason. The only way to redeem the powerful GM (or any kind of open-ended player decision-making, really) is to accept that it's not enough to just say that the GM "makes a determination". You also have to say why and how he makes it. Something like having the GM decide whether monsters appear or you character manages to climb that cliff, to pick a couple of examples, are utterly unproblematic situations if the whole group is crystal clear on why and how the GM is making those choices.

    So yes, method - explore that more, and learn to write about it. I might define method as "rules you apply in your head to your own decisions only". I imagine that it's easy to see how having robust method obviously makes a powerful GM unproblematic. And how a GM who's just running randomly from moment to moment, prone to reacting adversely to what others do, might be a problem.

    \n To clarify the above in a punchy manner: if your game says that the GM decides how a NPC reacts to a player's roleplaying, say, then why not also continue that sentence by explaining what the GM bases that decision on? Words don't cost that much. My belief is that primitive roleplaying texts we might explore to find these "GM decides" rules don't tell you the reasons for that decision either because the designer had such a narrow perspective that he never realized one might get confused about this, or because he was explicitly trying to avoid this thorny and difficult issue on which he simply didn't have design tools. There were plenty of "great GMs" running around in the halcyon days, but they were all running different games and with different methods, so it would have taken more bravery than designers had to be explicit about the why and how of GM decision-making.

    Solar System has many places where there is GM decision-making in the game. One of the things I did in my rewrite of the game was to make an explicit list of the points in the game where the GM will need to make decisions, and also why and how those decisions should be made. I also did the same for players. There simply aren't any decisions in the Solar System for which I don't provide a method for making that decision - a player may, if he wants, read the text carefully and make all decisions in the game based on the designer-intended method. Or if there are some spots where I failed to provide method (and I have a vague memory of being stumped about some point of GMing duties in there somewhere), then I say that it's a too difficult topic to deal with now, and has to be left for individuals to solve or hash out in the forums.

    \n David: there is a simple and valid reason for having the GM decide some things, and it's basically the same reason you have for having a player decide on some things. That reason is that the game will have more unexpected, powerful input by not running everything through the consensus-making procedure. If we want a game to be genuine interaction at all instead of just being the result of such interaction in the form of a concensus process, we have to accept that some players are going to make some decisions without taking input from others. If you expect everybody to have a say in everything all the time, then that process is your game and any dice rolling or whatnot will just be an afterthought.

    This is a familiar point to me because that's what my brother, an avid D&D gamer, tries to do with D&D as a sort of bastardization of Forgean game design principles. I think he's backed out on the worst of it, but at one point his adventures were vague affairs where he'd engage the players into a discussion of whether they'd like to have wolves attack the party, or whether they'd rather skip this essentially meaningless random encounter and head straight for the next plot point. I can assure you that increasing the amount of consensus decisions at the expense of the GM doing his job did not improve the overall game experience, as those authoritative backstory/setting-calls made by the GM are essential to not having the players get confused and start fighting over storymaking power. After all, why concentrate on having your character face the challenge of the hungry wolves when you can just face the challenge of voting the challenge down even before it's introduced into the game...

    \n Eero,

    I fully agree that seeing the whole thing as a process rather than a single instance of agency would be helpful. I also think that understanding of game texts as teaching texts is woefully neglected.

    I mean, neither of those things is easy to do. Understanding process is difficult, both for the person trying to convey and the one being conveyed to. I think a lot of "indie" games mechanize their process so heavily because in crystallizing it into formal game procedures they hope to side-step the necessity of conveying a lot of the underlying structure. Worth noting, they often aren't successful. Many Indie games work, at some level, because folks understand the process from other Indie games and a culture of play shared through Cons and online talk and the like. Folks who don't understand the still necessary but unmechanized processes often have trouble getting satisfactory play.

    The other difficulty is that process at the level you're talking about is often heavily socially contingent. A process that works for one group and set of social and aesthetic considerations may well not work for another group, and unlike where mechanical processes go wrong it can be very difficult to figure out where the disconnect is. Thus when the process fails, it tends to result in cascading failure that can be difficult to fix.

    So when I read this thread and look over at Tony's "better" thread and Jonathan's "structured freeform" thread, it makes me think that we're starting to look at designing to that level. That is, that we're starting to look for ways to design games such that they enable at the social process level, rather than at the mechanical interaction level. Historically I think there have been some strands of design that have tried to use mechanical interaction to replace social procedure, with mixed results, and that questions like the one Clinton is asking are because we're starting to look beyond that.

    (Edit: The Morningstar brings up a similar point. Just because the GM isn't doing the process, doesn't mean someone isn't. The mechanics may help, but in the end the social procedure still has to be processed.)

    \n I'm religiously anti-Fiat. So, when I'm GMing, if something comes up that I have no rules to cover, I'll make something up, but I'll do two things that trad texts don't emphasize sufficiently:

    1) Keep the player(s) involved (at least in making a roll). "OK, well, make an Elvish check, difficulty 3."

    2. Have a consequence in mind. Failure and success on the roll need to look different. (This is something the GMing chapter of Spirit of the Century nails; that chapter in general is an amazingly beautiful bit of writing about gaming at the process level for GMs.) Sometimes I'll even say ahead of time what the consequence of failure will be, so that the players can know if it's worth it to expend resources like Drama points on the roll.

    Bad fiat GMing is also not about situational authority, which does rightly belong to the GM in most GM'd games (even in indie games with a GM, it mostly belongs to the GM even if the players have some of it)\u2014you're damn right the GM gets to say when wolves attack the party! IME, bad fiat GMing is about arbitrarily ignoring the rules, usually because one of the players (aka me) knows them better. I'm not talking about house-ruling, which can be OK when given sufficient warning, I'm talking about flat-out ignoring the results of a die roll (aka "fudging"). I've never known that to improve a game.

    \n This may or may not be controversal.

    A big part of the GM's duties historically have been to prevent abuse. We all know the bad-GM horror stories, but really a big part of being a good GM is making sure the social interaction works: the shy player gets some spotlight, the deliberate thinker get the time they need to process, the domineering Alpha personality is kept in check, and so on.

    An interesting chicken or the egg question for me is whether critical thinking about game play and design leads to socially responsible players or whether socially responsible players are naturally more inclined to think critically about game play and design. Either way, I think the broader indie community has a pretty high ratio of socially responsible players who don't need a GM as social traffic cop / babysitter. That lack of need means, I think, that a GM is less important and that we are (as a broad generalization) pretty well able to handle the social dynamics of GM-less play. I think that makes this community more inclined to see GM-less play as easily doable / preferable.

    I think it also means that seeing less value in some of the very important good things a GM can bring, we tend to focus more on the bad things a GM can bring, thus inculcating a negative bias.

    Combine this with one of the core (and I mean REALLY core / fundamental) realizations to come out of the indie design community over the last several years -- that the duties of the GM can be broken up and distributed and are not required to all be vested in a single person -- and I think we've created an environment where the default position is (at best) GM's are not necessary; or even "bad".

    I think there's plenty of room for indie designers to turn our attention to the oft maligned GM and see what interesting tricks we can make it do. I wouldn't be at all surprised if, in the process, we recreate a role that would be intimately familiar to the really old, old skoolers.

    \n So, as I said, for me there was a very tangible, very practical difference. Here's the situation from our session. Three characters in ancient Egypt are hiding in a temple from a plague of locusts summoned by priests of Seth. They hear screams outside--an old priest hasn't made it to safety and is being eaten by locusts. Now they can decide to charge out and try to save the guy, risking their own hides, or they can stay inside figuring it's too late already. They all three run outside. Now it's up to me, the GM, to tell them what the consequence is. My mind thinks the following:

    1. On what basis do I decide to hurt their characters and how badly? Is it based on their descriptions of their actions? On simulating what would really happen? On dramatic story flow? Do I even have enough information to make this decision? Is my information the same as the players', to the degree that they'd agree with the assessment?

    2. Do I hurt all three equally, even though that's unrealistic and could break suspension of disbelief? Does it matter that one of the players is my wife? How would the other two feel if their characters got hurt but hers didn't, even if I say it's based on my assessment, not our relationship? Since this is an online game where I haven't met the other two players face-to-face, will they understand my reasoning? (And no, I don't want to discuss either working these things out through lengthy dialogue, again and again, or changing my medium of play or my players involved--I want to discuss this specific situation at this specific time.)

    3. Do I have to explain myself and my decision? What if a pattern evolves over time that looks like preference even if I didn't intend it to?

    4. What if they weren't aware of the actual risk involved? Telling them, "There's a good chance you may get hurt," is much more vague than telling them, "I'm going to roll a D10; the higher the worse; up to getting your leg gnawed off."

    5. Whose responsibility is it if the leg of a character gets gnawed off? If they go for it based on GM fiat, the decision is up to me. I have to make it, and I have to justify it, to myself and to them. If I have a die and I tell them BEFOREHAND what the exact risk is and thereby imbue the dice with that power, the decision and responsibility is now theirs. This is a very important difference for me, especially when playing with people who are not my closest friends and whom I can't discuss everything with before and after the game due to constraints (con games, online games, etc.).

    Negotiation only helps this if we were both on the same footing: see IAWA. If I as the GM have to negotiate every outcome, it's not only tedious and time-consuming, it's putting me and my decisions on the spot all the time.

    Now, outcome-setting could work if you mean that we agree beforehand what the result would be. But then, that would require lots of back-and-forth negotiation about the situation and the consequences. And I'd just like to have a quick and smooth way of saying, "This is your risk, take it or leave it."

    This is not some hypothetical I'm making up to make an argument. I'm just sharing exactly how I felt about this issue in a real play situation.

      \n Hey David,

      Let's give each other the benefit of the doubt here. We're not here to argue with each other, but to learn from one another. Apparently you've had one kind of experience in your roleplaying history whereas Brad had a different one. Can we take that as a basis for conversation?

      I personally, for example, come from a social science background. When we talk about roles, I'm reminded that there are different kinds of roles and that they are created differently in different groups. But also, "roles" are a social science construct, part of a model to understand society.

      When we look at roleplaying, different groups have roles that are partially based on the games they play, but also on many other factors, including their relationships among each other. We can maybe look at those differences and try to see how we've got different experiences.

      For one, there are games without one central GM. Universalis, Polaris, Capes, and other games all work well by either distributing the responsibilities of a GM or "rotating" them within a game session.

      Also, the accountability, in my groups, always has been to all other players. The GM (me, most of the time) didn't have a special role there. If we want to play a detective game and one of the players is on a space trip, all of the other players pull him back to reality. We're all responsible for playing together. The GM never had a special role to play there.

      Finally, just because someone is the GM doesn't mean they have more social power. What if the whole group disagrees with the GM's ruling and says, "We don't accept that as true. We're going to do it this other way here." Who's got the power now, the single individual GM or the group who can play amongst themselves? The rules cannot force anyone to do anything.

      So, I see several options: you can have a strong GM by agreement of the players; you can have players that self-regulate or regulate each other (this works, frex, in freeform gaming I've experienced); you can distribute or rotate GM functions; and probably more.
