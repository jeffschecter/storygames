    I think the system is woven through the entire outcome. However, there was something very powerful in Frank's choice at the beginning of the game to take on an enormous load of Bad Medicine to give game-mechanical oomph to his narrative of being a cursed youth. That meant that when Frank involved his character in another player's turn, that huge burden became available to me as the GM as a resource in my favor to call upon to gain control of the narration. Eventually, Frank would wave off any invitation to "help out" during another player's turn to forestall such use on my part. Essentially, the mechanics provided a narrative explanation for Frank's character's self-imposed exile! And it was his act of self-ostracism that drove the rest of the story.

    But there were other nice moments. I liked that Lisa's card for her final fate was the same as she had drawn for her situation during the turn, nicely signifying the stasis that her moon-avatar character had found himself in.

    And I liked that, at the end, the "older characters" (Don's burning prophet and Shawn's crazy grandmother) were using the mechanics to determine who'd get narration rights for the final fate of the world, rather than more personal goals.

    So there were lots of places where the game-rules intersected with the production of the game-fiction. It was really fun to watch.
