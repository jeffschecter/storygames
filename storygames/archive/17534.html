    \n Here's a non-railroaded chokepoint:

    The evil monster is threatening the village. In one week's game time, the village will be destroyed and the module over.

    It's one weakness is the Mysterious Ring.

    To find the Mysterious Ring in the Lost Swamps, the characters must make a Swamp Navigation check.

    (Swamp Navigation checks in this system can only be re-rolled once every IC month after you get new maps or after a rainstorm.)

    The characters fail their Swamp Navigation check.

    At this point the module is over.

    Nobody has railroaded anything - the characters could very well have decided to face off against a monster that has no weaknesses and all get killed, or they could run off to some other village, or ignore the quest entirely, but the module does indeed have a chokepoint.

    if there's really nothing much in another village or nothing much to engage with if you just ignore the quest, then it's just railroading. In such a case it's a classic 'everywhere is boring and nothing happens, except along this track I've set up'
    \nYeah, that's kind of the gold standard for railroading.

    IMO, chokepoints are a single place where proceeding in a particular direction (solving the mystery, defeating the bad guy, opening the tomb, whatever) relies on one and only one thing, and if the PCs fail at that thing, they can NEVER go that direction. It's kind of a bad thing, in the sense that most people like to be successful and a strong chokepoint can take that away from them with no room for appeals, alternate approaches, clever solutions, etc.. If you're playing with people who enjoy succeeding so much that they will make multiple attempts to succeed and/or come at difficult problems with as much cunning and lateral thinking as they can bring to bear on it, then you should really consider how you can make strong chokepoints flexible enough to accommodate that; sticking to your "no, you failed your Library Use, so you never figure this out, so you lose" guns is a bad call for that group. But if they don't care whether they win or lose, it's probably okay to let them lose because they boned one roll.

    Railroading to me is when no matter what the players want to do, they always end up at the same place (which I think is often fine, as long as it's an interesting place and how they got there seems appropriate/plausible to the players), or they discover that everything else is always boring/uneventful/spiteful until they get back on the train and do as they're told. Choosing to go left or right on the road and always ending up at the goblin village is railroading, but not bad; it's a road, the choice was largely arbitrary, there isn't much to bitch about there. Choosing to turn around and go back home and finding that the bridges have all collapsed behind you and the rivers are suddenly swollen and also made of lava and teams of dragons are incinerating anything that tries to fly away because GOD DAMN IT you are going down the road to that goblin village OR ELSE? Bad.

    So yeah, I think you can have chokepoints without railroading. Imagine a chokepoint like JDCorley's "gotta have The Mysterious Ring" example. As written, you have one and only one way to get that ring: make your Swamp Navigation check. But if the module just said "the PCs need The Mysterious Ring; it is in the Swamp Temple," that might change things. There might be lots of ways to find a Swamp Temple using other resources besides wading hip-deep in sludge, depending on the game and the PCs. It's still a chokepoint -- if they want to win, they MUST get to the Swamp Temple, there is no other way to get The Mysterious Ring -- but HOW they get there doesn't have to be railroaded at all. They could even, as JDCorley points out, decide on their own to lose ("Screw that village, it sucked anyway, let's go be pirates somewhere tropical").

    It'd be railroading if the GM then forced them to go to the Swamp Temple anyway ("Your pirate ship was sucked up by a tornado and dropped into a swamp! Roll Swamp Navigation now!"), I guess.

    \n Railroading is obviously a contextual concept. If we're merely looking at a single scenario, then a chokepoint or railroading or whatever is measured against that module. If we're looking at a campaign, then things are completely different.

    This actually is extremely important in actual play. Many of the adventure modules I've used in our D&D sandbox have been somewhat linear in nature, involving chokepoints. This has not devolved into railroading in any meaningful sense, however, because the general structure of the sandbox campaign uplifts and supports us: players who find a given adventure too difficult or not rewarding enough or whatever have a viable, realistic ability to just walk away and consider this particular adventure lost. I emphasize viability here: it's not a theoretical fringe failure condition to declare that the characters retreat from this adventure, it's something that happens all the time and is a key strategic choice.

    Few campaigns, however, have a full-blown sandbox backup for the adventure modules that are used. Are you really OK as a GM if the players decide 15 minutes into the adventure that they'd rather skip this one? Did you bring a backup? How much work do you lose as a sunk cost if the players discard this adventure? It seems to me that most prewritten adventure modules are used in a context where the module is at least tonight the only show available. Often it's also a show selected by the GM (as opposed to consensual negotiation as our D&D campaign). These things matter, they're basically the difference between a railroad and a dynamic play experience.

    To pick an example, Jim Raggi's adventures are often pretty chokepoint-heavy, at least compared to many other old school D&D authors. However, these cruel adventures with only one way into the dungeon or inescapable death traps work really well with the kind of strategic backup our campaign provides. I can imagine how absolutely frustrating something like Death Frost Doom or Grinding Gear could be for a player if you didn't get to make the choice to enter the adventure yourself, if you didn't get to select your approach vector, if you didn't set your own victory conditions, and so on. However, with players involved in all these elements and more, the linear dungeon becomes just a cruel choice instead of a sadistic exercise in powerlessness.

    I'm curious, but how can you have choke points while not railroading?
    \nIt can also just be bad design or a mistake.

    A choke point exists at any point where the PCs want to accomplish something and there ends up being only one way to do it. Choke points generally represent bad scenario design because it is so easy for them to get clogged (preventing the PCs from accomplishing whatever it is they want to accomplish). When a clog occurs, it can be solved by railroading the PCs through it. But that's not the only way to deal with a clog: You can also say, "Sorry, Mitt, the only road to the White House is by winning the election. You didn't do that, so you don't get to be President." Or you can become permissive as your players begin looking for alternative paths around (or through) the clog.

    Any given mystery scenario (like Masks of Nyarlathotep) necessarily starts with the assumption that the PCs are interested in solving the mystery. (That doesn't mean you need to railroad them into wanting to solve the mystery; it just means that if they engage with a mystery scenario, one can assume they want to solve it.) If the only way to solve the mystery is to find a specific clue, then you've got a pretty serious choke point that can clog very easily (and clog with quick-setting cement when it does).

    It's easy to say, "Well, that's not a problem as long as nobody cares about completing the scenario." But the players do care. That's why there's a problem: Once the drain is filled with cement, the water starts rising and the group starts floundering. It doesn't make for an entertaining or interesting session of gaming. And saying "they should just get out of the sink" doesn't actually solve the problem in actual practice.\n
    One of the best bits of basic GMing advice I've gotten (and given) over the years regarding chokepoints was to have at least one idea about how the PCs could get past it (two or three, even), but to not be so attached to those ideas that you won't be able to recognize a clever, workable solution just because you didn't think of it first.
    \nYears of practical knowledge suggest think of three ways and be open to more. The advantages of prepping three is that: (a) The scenario is robust; (b) you're not particularly wedded to any one of them; and (c) in prepping those three you've probably built up enough detail that it becomes a lot easier to deal with unexpected angles.
