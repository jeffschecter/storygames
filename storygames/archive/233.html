  • Matt Snyder
    February 2006 edited February 2006
    My god! I have so much to say about typography I don't know where to begin. I started typing a little guide to serif, sans serif, and other unusual faces, and I realized I'd eventually be typing it furiously in a little shack in Montana. Instead, just go to the bookstore or library and get The Elements of Typographic Style by Robert Bringhurst. Or, just ask me or Joshua Newman for some type advice. He and I both love to talk about this stuff!

    So much of the gaming scene's typography is, well, puke. But, that said, I'm a huge stickler for type, and I think about it way to much. Most books get by quite admirably. Yeah, that weird feeling of identifying a font on a sign or something like that in real life? Every single day of my life. "Oh, that's such-and-such," I say looking at the grocery cart handle. Mrs. Snyder replies, "You're a dork." I return a volley, "Hey, you CHOSE to marry me!"

    I'm a fan of: Fontcraft That site has some wonderful faces that are very suitable to game geeks everywhere. I buy fonts from them frequently.

    If you buy a font from Adobe, you can't go wrong. They are the best. They are also, usually, the most expensive. 'Dems the breaks.

    Here are a couple lists to help folks:

    Matt's list of 10 superb serif faces
    These are good for your main text in a book. Very readable stuff.
    1. Adobe Caslon (other non-Adobe Caslon versions are availabla)
    2. Bembo - My favorite font!
    3. Bodoni
    4. Centaur - Careful! It's delicate, and hard to read small.
    5. Dante
    6. Galliard
    7. Adobe Garamond (other non-Adobe Garamond versions are available)
    8. Minion
    9. Sabon
    10. Times (Yes! Times, that much maligned Microsoft Word font is actually good!)
    Matt's list of 10 superb sans-serif faces
    Great for sidebars, titling, captions, and other treatment. Not as good for main text, although some can work well in this capacity.
    1. Avenir
    2. Eurostile (Great for a science-fiction-y look)
    3. ITC Franklin Gothic (other versions available, I believe)
    4. Helvetica (Another often-used but awesome typeface. Lots of versatile variations.)
    5. ITC Legacy Sans
    6. Myriad
    7. ITC Officina Sans (And ITC Officina Serif is a great companion, see also: InSpectres)
    8. Optima (The Sorcerer font for all of the inside text)
    9. Syntax
    10. Univers (Big type family, lots of versatility)
    P.S. I hate Arial because Helvetica actually has more subtlety and identity, if you can believe it. When in doubt, use Helvetica if you can, I say.

    Matt's list of 10 other quirky, cool faces
    These are good as decorative or titling, usually. Some work in short blocks well, but I don't recommend any for pages and pages of reading.
    1. Blue Island
    2. Caliban (Good script font -- Go Burning Wheel!)
    3. Copperplate Gothic
    4. Fusaka (A fun Asian-techie looking face)
    5. Galahad (Get Medieval on yo' ass)
    6. Magnesium MVB
    7. The Rotis family (Rotis Sans Serif, Rotis Semi Sans, Rotis Semi Serif, and Rotis Serif)
    8. Sanvito
    9. Trajan (Awesome capital letter titling face)
    10. Zapf Dingbats (dig a bit and you'll probably find a bullet character you can use. Also good for boxes or cirlces, like on a character sheet for example.)
    You know, after compiling these lists, I reminded myself how much fonts can go in and out of style. That's often why I like to use tried-and-true Adobe fonts, because I know they're not going to go out of style. They've been around for decades (in some cases centuries!). Case in point: Abaddon. It's the font used for the Sorcerer logo. It's actually pretty cool, but I feel so burned out out it! So it goes.

  • Jason_Morningstar
    February 2006
    Hi John, it's off-topic because it has nothing directly to do with typography.

    Projects like SiFR irritate me because they are predicated by the underlying assumption that the Web is just like a car dealership brochure. The fact that this assumption causes endless hardship for the people I deal with daily (people who are blind, visually impaired, have motor function issues, etc) is really secondary to the superset of general usability, which takes a huge hit whenever designers decide they know what's best for presentation when offering electronic content. Flash (designed by visual designers for visual designers) is the flaming sword of the brochure crowd. SiFR may or may not be functionally accessible, but it is another example of the thirst for control that is antithetical to the fundamental nature of the Web.

    These remarks may come off as inflammatory, but please read charitably. I care a lot about this topic (maybe too much!) and don't mean to insult anyone.

    I'd start by following the rules of the medium. You wouldn't design a brochure for a Minnetonka Chevy dealership on A4 paper (or whatever) and you shouldn't build pages that don't validate. Building valid pages is an excellent start, because it provides some necessary constraints that will make your pages more usable, just like a Chevy ad built to American standards will fit in the envelope when it is done.

    I don't know what you are getting at with usability and subtext. I think a usable page can only help draw out meaning. If your goal is so specific that the tools available to some guy with a legacy machine in Angola can't communicate it, you're working in the wrong medium. (Again, my bias is showing!)

    On "Font Mastery"

    People are conflating two abilities in those they term font masters (gurus, , ninjas, whatever cool-ass-hip title you like to use, etc.)

    1) The ability to "Know" a font by looking at it. And, related, knowing shloads of fonts to suggest for those in need at the drop of the hat.

    2) The ability to use a font well in a design.

    I claim relatively very little of skill #1. I like to think I have some of skill #2, and keep practicing to make it better.

    And now, an anology.

    You want someone to draw you a picture.

    1) You approach someone who has Every Paint Known To Man. Colors? Man, this guy has fucking colors. He has more shades of red than you've ever heard of. This guy shits in more colors than you've ever dreamed of. Colors OUT THE ASS! He uses them all the time, and they're in something called the "Crayola 128."

    2) Over here is a schlump with only about 2 shades of red. He only has a lousy "Crayola 12." But, his reds are red, and his blues are blue. And, huh, he really seems to know how and when to mix 'em up. He even shades 'em together. Neat. You ask him if he knows what "Burnt Sienna" means. You explain it, because he doesn't. He shrugs and keeps drawing with this brown piece of wax. Nice work, by the way.
