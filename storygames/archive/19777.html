Update: I came up with an idea a couple of years later: HFrez

\n----

I like the board game The Resistance, but I wish to add some more RPG elements to it. No dice or stats, the game part of it is perfect, but some random flavor tables.
A name list
A list of principles (as per Baker's Coherence and Contradictions, or PbtA games)
A list of missions
A list of roles in that mission

To get lots of variety, the mission table could be a few parts, like: "you need to [table a] the [table b] in order to [table c]".
The roles have me the most stumped. If we know someone is wheel man and some one is face man, it'd be obvious who stabbed the mission. The roles need to be parallel and vital.

The name list is the easy part, but I kind of wanted to crowd source the other lists from you guys. Any ideas?

    \n I did this recently when running a Resistance larp:
    https://www.facebook.com/events/1447538115511025/

    The players created the nature of the Empire, the Resistance, their own characters, why they joined the Resistance. They also came up with an oath of the Resistance which they repeated when they came together to choose a team and any time the argument got too heated. They also had to come up with their own voting system (to work out whether a team was approved).

    They had about a dozen targets to select from, each with a prescribed number of team members on, providing enough of each type to fit any configuration of number of players.

    Each target had an Empire description (what the Empire said it was) and a Resistance description (what the Resistance said it was) and then detailed the roles that were needed for any mission on the target.

    There were ten roles in all, each could be filled by any player, but obviously a character with a particular background could (and did) use that as a reason why they should fill a role with a particular skill.

    The roles were:
    Assault
    Communication
    Crowd Control
    Demolitions
    Driver
    Honey Trap
    Infiltration
    Lockbreaker
    Security
    Undercover

    Each character also had a personal connection to one of the targets, sometimes a friend or relative who worked there, which also gave them a reason to argue to be on the mission.

    I can post the target descriptions here if they'd be of interest.

    \n Do you have a copy of Battlestar Galactica? You could run through the deck for ideas on different types of missions or crises. We used to role play that game all the time, though it is far more complex than The Resistance. So here's a few basic thoughts:

    1. Deck of missions/crises that lend context.
    2. Assign each character a role that simply describes their general skills but does not limit game play.
    3. After success or failure of the mission is determined, all players on the mission go into another room to discuss what happened. They then return and each give individual accounts of how the mission went. (Could add some fun to the discussion about who is a traitor!)

    \n I don't find the idea of simply attaching fictional roles to the players all that compelling, personally. (After all, you're going to figure out that your friend Ben is a traitor because of the way he votes, and his body language, not because the fiction says he's a former government employee!)

    But what about doing it the other way around?

    Let's say each mission has a specific objective, independent of other missions. One mission means the Resistance finds the Death Star plans, the other means they manage to keep the Empire from finding planet Hoth. That kind of stuff.

    Every role/player has a specific objective or impact on a mission. Maybe if Chew-D2 comes on the mission, he always creates a lot of Imperial casualties, because he's trigger-happy. So we know that any mission involving him means a lot of people are dead.

    That way, a certain set of characters on a certain set of missions means a developing story: "We managed to get the Death Star plans, but because Chew-D2 was on the mission, there was a wild massacre. However, a little droid was on the mission, who is good at information science. So maybe he's got the plans now?" "Yeah, that sounds about right!"

    You could also make fiction-specific "betrayal" cards. Instead of just generic sabotage, you'd have a whole bunch with different *types* of sabotage (e.g. "last-minute defection", "vital equipment sabotaged", etc.), and each player could pick an appropriate type of sabotage from the deck. That way, you could even try to guess which character might be using a certain type of sabotage.

    I like the board game The Resistance, but I wish to add some more RPG elements to it.
    \nI would remove the game part because that would interfere with "characterization". Turn all character card up so everyone knows who are the traitors. Play the game out like Battlestar Galactica. Have a few guidelines for scenes to be included.

    The scenes in the first act should focus on establishing relations, hierarchy and professions on the ship. The game could have lists of examples.

    The second act focuses on the mission and each person that is included in the mission. The leader could for example be forced to include as many people to the left as required for each mission. Instead of plot cards, the game can provide a deck of cards with suggestions of things that happens. The participants turns up 4-5 of those cards, and the characters on the missions select each one of these cards. Scenes are framed around the events of these cards. A card could for example change a character card to the opposite (red to blue or vice versa). Also, why does the leadership change?

    The third act happens when either side gets three tokens. An epilogue is played out with any number of scenes to tie everything together. How are the people affected by the betrayal? What relations will be in focus? Will anyone survive? What will the consequences be for the people. Try to include at least two characters in each scene.

    I left some things out, like who frames the scene, who got a higher hierarchy when it comes to deciding any "arguments", who the red and blue cards are representing, how the scene framing changes if a red role is the leader, and so on.

    \u2022 Keeping the resistance game play but adding more flavor
    \u2022 Creating a story game around the resistance structure but prioritizing the story

    \n I definitely also think that in the vanilla Resistance, you have a better setup for roleplaying, because the first team votes are frequently a bit "blind". Avalon fixes this problem by adding roles for the "good" side that have more information, but I think this could work to your advantage! If everyone adopts a persona, then you start the early rounds by looking to players' characterizations for reasons to downvote their team. (It's a bit like how in Mafia/Werewolf, when you're playing with casuals, people get killed off because they look at you funny or something.)

    Hmm. I wonder if the game would break if you extended the number of missions to 7. Because that'd mean you have more leeway for voting based on characterization, and not just on an analysis of "well, you were on that mission when it failed, so..."

    I'd even go so far to say that it might actually foster the paranoia if you operated under the assumption that when the Resistance members run a mission, they part ways, each do their part, and then separately come back to base and compare notes. Which means that they're discovering what happened at the mission at the same time that everyone else did. Which means that the spies have to have explanations ready for what went wrong.

    So, to sum up, here's how I see the flow going.\n
      \n
    • The Team Leader chooses their team, as different members lobby to be put on the team.
      • \n
    • Players vote on whether the team is good; once the team passes, move on to the next step.
      • \n
    • The Team Leader explains the plan to their members (and to the rest of the Resistance), assigning specific jobs to Resistance members, and then they go on the mission.
      • \n
    • Execution of the mission, and everyone "comes back to base" to check and see if it passed.
      • \n
    • If it failed, go into a round of "okay, who didn't take care of their part?"
      • \n
    \nThe "who didn't take care of their part?" doesn't have to be conclusive, and I think it contributes to the paranoia aspect more if it isn't conclusive. You'll never know 100% (until you start narrowing things down).

    \n You could produce "playsets" in the form of specific missions, grouped together by the required number of characters on the mission.

    It would be EXTRA fun if the Imperial spy on the mission could choose a specific "failure" card, hoping to implicate another member of the team...

    Something like:\n

    Mission: Steal the plans to the Death Star from the Evil Empire Research Facility. (For four agents.)

    Requires:
    * One agent to shut down the forcefield generator (infiltration/sabotage).
    * One agent to distract the sentries just outside the Access Tower.
    * One agent to destroy the Secondary Data Banks, which may house backup copies of the plans.
    * One agent to transport the plans out of the Facility and off-planet.

    Possible complications:
    * The Empire has a new security failsafe attached to the Facility. No one knows exactly how it operates, but it is rumoured to have something to do with ancient sorcery.

    Success: The rebels escape with the complete plans for the Death Star. (Mission: Infiltrate Death Star Construction becomes available.)
    Failure: The plans are stolen. However, the following sabotage cards are available:

    * The explosives used to destroy the Secondary Data Banks failed to explode. (The Empire still has backup copies of all the plans.)
    * The agent distracting the sentries was strip-searched and questioned. (Some secret about the Alliance's plans may have been revealed.)
    * One of the agents appears to have been possessed at some point during the mission, and has strange gaps in his/her memory.
    * etc.

    [The players each take one in secret before the mission, and any spy can, of course, throw one in to sabotage the mission.]
    \nThe team leader could choose a mission to undertake before voting for the agents involved (or the missions could be grouped in a pack to start with - i.e., here are the five missions required to secure the rebel alliance's escape from Hoth, in this order).

    Each player would lobby for being a good fit for a particular job, and, when the mission is completed, have to defend their character's success or failure in the mission.

    This could be pretty entertaining, although you'd need some pretty hardcore fans to write all those playsets.
