Push, Pull, and Probe

wyrmwood
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Story Games
Recently, I had a chance to play Bill White's Ganokagok, which was quite fun. While playing I discovered that a certain social aspect of how I play was coming to the forefront, something I can sum up best with the question: "May I make a suggestion?" Essentially, I passively offer an idea, keeping it as neutral from the authority side as possible. And this question is not merely rhetorical, I wait for a response or acknowledgment that suggestions are welcome. I've done the same thing while running and playing games like Inspectres and octaNe, which also clearly define narration rights. But I've also done it while running RPGs which have a strong GM presence, specifically distinct from the GM authority.

Sometimes I escalate the suggestions with an offer of system assistance, which makes that social strategy strongly resemble the idea of pull, but this is atypical. Much more rarely, I will use my own authority to make the suggestion occur, resembling push. More usually, the acceptance or rejection of the suggestion (or even the offer of a suggestion) is a way for me to better understand what the other player wants. In essence, probing (perhaps there is a better word) is a social strategy focused on information and building trust, rather than distributing authority.

In my experience, probing is very useful, for more than just mildly influencing play. Its also an effective way to build the level of understanding for pull, or to keep push and pull from causing problems. But what personally interests me about probing is that it lies distinctly outside of (lumpley principle) system, it does not distribute authority, instead it affects it indirectly, acting as part of a meta-system as it were.

To my mind, probing is useful, but appears neglected in design and play. Is this truly the case? Should it play a more prominent role? How can we think design and play to enhance the use and utility of probing?
