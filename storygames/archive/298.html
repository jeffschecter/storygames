Is your game short? Well, is it?

Nir Shiffer
March 2006 edited March 2006 in Story Games
I like long games. Those long campaigns lasting years, where you really get to know the characters and the world, and care about them, to the point of stuff happening in the game causes a significant emotional impact in the players? Those. I like overarching plots, and unepxected reversals on long-established "knowledge". I like investing in the game outside the sessions, whether it be post-production or setting enrichment, and I feel stupid doing it if we're just going to toss the game out in a month.

It's been claimed before that the open ended game, or "endless game", is usually marketed as a default setting in "mainstream" RPGs, and yet so few people ever really play years-long games (I couldn't find a link, if someone could hook us up...). I wonder if this is really so, and would love to hear from people here - how long are your games, usually? How long do you prefer them?

Which leads us neatly to our own beloved Indie games. The vast majority of them are geared toward the mini-campaign length, usually clocking between four and ten sessions. This, I assume, is not coincidental. Why did you design your game to last 6-8 sessions?

And lastly - what do you think would differentiate the designs of games intended to be played over six session versus those geared for fifty (40-50 sessions is the mean average for a weekly game lasting a year)?

    1) How long are your games, usually? How long do you prefer them?

    The longest game I've been in is a D&D 3/3.5 game that has stretched over about 4 years, with irregular play. We started senior year of high school, and then all of us players scattered for college, so we only get to play when we come back for break. Our characters started at level 4 and are now all in the 30's. And there's fun stuff about being powerful, and famous--my character is a bard with Epic Leadership and therefore has over a hundred level 1 followers, plus lots of unofficial fans/worshippers. (She is somewhat chagrined at the "worshippers" part.) And we have a town that we've cultivated from a small fort guarding a pass to a bustling city. That stuff is fun.

    Most of my games have been much shorter. I've been in several short-running D&D games that petered out quickly. I was in a schoolyear-long Deadlands campaign, but stopped going about 3/4 of the way through due to, um, boredom.

    I've found, more recently, that I crave resolution of some sort. I'm in a Mountain Witch game, and I'm looking forward to getting to the part where we all kill the Witch and/or die trying. Also, I've found so many new games that I want to try out, that allocating time for a 4-year campaign for any of them means that I will never get to all of them.

    Additionally, I'm in college, and I have no clue when I'll get to see some of the people I play with after we all graduate. And we're all seniors, busy with thesis or equivalents thereof, so we don't get to play every week. This makes a long-running campaign less than ideal, because I doubt we'll ever get to finish it.

    2) Why did you design *your* game to last 6-8 sessions?

    I have two game designs in the works. One is a 24-hour RPG that needs playtest and revision, and that has a limited arc. It's about children learning to experiment and have confidence in themselves in the safe space of a magical land. The obvious end to this is when the children have all learned an important lesson, because it means their stories are done.

    My other game is the Fantasy Heartbreaker I'm working on. That one I want to work well with one-shot, short term, and long-term play. One-shot and short-term are my highest priorities; I'm experimenting with limiting GM resources, and possibly supporting rotating GMs. Also, I think there need to be more games that can be played by people in my position, with irregular gaming schedules and moving around.

    3) What differentiates the designs of short-term versus long-term games?

    One of the main things is the existence of an end-point. Mountain Witch, Polaris, My Life With Master--they all have specific endgame conditions for the characters/story. Also, those endgames are obviously reachable within a smallish number of sessions.

    Admittedly, I think there's frequently a conflation of the concepts "has a specified end" and "is for short-term play". The Shadow of Yesterday supports long-term play, but characters stop being playable when they transcend.

    I think that long term games really need a way to keep producing interesting things for the players. D&D has its newer and tougher monsters with different abilities. TSOY has Keys. Mountain Witch, on the other hand, is really all about the Dark Fates and Trust mechanics; they produce a situation that *has* to come to a climax.

    -Selene

    "What differentiates the designs of short-term versus long-term games?"

    I just wanted to add that some of the indie games ostensibly designed for short-term play actually *can* handle long-term play, if the group's interest is there. The text of Sorcerer tells you that when you resolve a Kicker, you can pick a new one and keep going. A PTA series can continue indefinitely, with people picking new Issues and a new story arc. I've been playing with PTA variant that allows characters to pick up and arc Issues on the fly during play, and then choose from their Issues for the upcoming episode both to determine Screen Presence and to act as a flag for the Producer.

    So the distinction may not be as far off as it seems. Selene said most of what I would have said - one of the keys is how well the design accounts for the incorporation of new, relevant material into play. If you want to use some of Johnstone's Impro mojo, you can look at it as how well the design supports the regular cycle of breaking of routines in play and reincorporating material that "defines" that play.

    I've often thought that the petering out of campaigns over time is a result of not enough of one or the other of those two things happening on a regular basis.

    There's So Many Goddamned Games.

    There's So Many Goddamned Games, Redux.

    Someone once mentioned that the gaming industry is hard to crack, because you are effectively competing for time with other games. I agree with that assessment. So pretty much any game I work on will be optimized for 3-6 sessions, have playable options for 1-2 sessions, and yet can be played longer without breaking too bad. I don't have the hubris to consider that people should be playing my games for three or more months straight when they could be playing other great games (board games, even), which is an often very conscious decision some folks have to make: What games do we play next, and at the which other games' expense do we play those games?

Not necessarily because they're "deeper" or "richer", but let's put it this way: After a hard week of work, it's nice to settle into a game world that's familiar and relatively stress/focus-free. "Playing Hard" with a huge string of 2-3 session games can be a little tiring, I think, if you're in a strictly casual gaming group.

    I spent many years chasing the long campaign. It was the ideal, the apogee, the apotheosis of gaming: the one campaign that stretched across years and generated stories that you and your friends told among each other forever.

    I never got there.

    So, given the realities of my gamelife, I make short games. I in fact make a lot of one-session games. My "big project" FLFS can be played long-term -- but it's episodic, and each session is a different situation, with little-to-no carryover between sessions.

    In fact I think the episodic game is an important distinction to add in there. There are short games that set up a situation and resolve it in a handful of sessions, there are long games that do the same over many many sessions, and there are games that set up a series of situations that are resolved in serial order. There is actually a very big difference between the last two -- it's the difference between feature film and television, or short stories and novels.

    3) And lastly - what do you think would differentiate the designs of games intended to be played over six session versus those geared for fifty (40-50 sessions is the mean average for a weekly game lasting a year)?

    Many games get more unwieldy over time. Dice pool systems often mean that you're rolling more and more dice. For example, Dogs starts out as a bucketful-of-dice game, and you add dice pretty quickly with each session. Other games accumulate complex options. D&D only adds a level every few sessions, but you add on much more complication and options with each level. For a long-term campaign, steady escalation which doesn't add many more dice or complications is good. Rather than adding more feats, upgrade lesser feats to more powerful ones. Add to bonuses rather than number of dice.

    Within the fiction, you need a stable state to return to, or well-defined arcs which end.

    Yeah, so dig how it works in Shock:

    You finish a story, whether it's an episode or a larger thing. Let's assume that your Protagonist is alive at the end.

    You cash out your resources, turn them into a generic resource. Then you write down a character sketch for your next character. Now here's the important part: it might be the same dude. It could be John Carter or Conan or Flash Gordon, one story on.

    ... or it could be someone else entirely. You want to confront a different Shock than your Protag was designed to confront? Cool! Start with someone else. You get the same resources, either way.

    You aren't penalized for ending the story, not even really in the time it takes to start a new character; that only takes a minute. Pick a spot on the Grid that's interesting, write down two numbers, thee phrases and a name, and you're ready to go.

    You have to end stories; the mechanics demand it. It doesn't mean the end of your character (necessarily), it doesn't mean the end of your participation in the game (ever), it doesn't even mean you won't have a character by the next time it's your turn (uh, unless you're playing with only one other player).

    What it does mean is that you have to do stuff that matters to you right now to play the game. Smash the space ship. Have a philosophical argument with an android. Gamble on the future of humanity. Do what you need to do and see what happens.

    You do this for three or four sessions in a row, each time, getting closer to addressing your Story Goal. Then you throw down about the thing you thought really mattered a couple of sessions back, now with the full knowledge that's come over the last several sessions.

    What happens, that's the ending.

    When I run, I tend to run short, one session stories, even in the confines of a larger game.

    OTOH, Cthulhupunk was Lisa Padol's very successful set of two successive 6ish year campaigns (set in the same world, with many of the same characters). A key to its success was that while Lisa tended to run one or two stories at a time (the second campain, +20, had more or less two sets of characters), she'd interweave characters intended to be important in a later situation into the current one, thus allowing for both resolution and continuity -- rather like a serial radio play (only with more continuity).     I believe there's another a big advantage that short games have---a short game allows a player to play for broke in a way they can't in a long campaign, except maybe under unusual circumstances.

    In a long game, you can't play a real jerk of a character that will literally steal and betray and maybe even kill the characters at every available opportunity without fear that it will ruin somebody else's fun. I mean, it's no fun to have the character you have been playing for nine months backstabbed because he looked at another PC funny, or even to have that magical sword you spent 6 sessions searching for stolen and pawned for whore money.

    But in a short game, that all is possible. That's why The Mountain Witch is a short game. A game where every character is likely to betray at least one other PC isn't going to last too long. I like to brag that the game has a 50% PC mortality rate, either by suicide or PC-PC violence.

    (The downside to style is exactly that it won't last too long, but oh well, it's fun while it lasts.)
