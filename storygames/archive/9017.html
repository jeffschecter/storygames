\n I just got posted my interview with Monte Cook.

Near the end he made a comment: "If I was gonna write an indie game, I would play off that all the time, that kind of fast-and-loose play, and rewarding someone for cool roleplaying with a mechanic that then empowers them to do more cool roleplaying, which is really what Hero Points are. I would do that up and down, that would be the game"

And then Later:

"Like I said, i'd like to play more off of less ridged mechanics and more rewarding player creativity, which is really hard to do as a GameMaster, it's really hard to run that game. You know, because it's so much more narrative and so much more off the cuff, but if you can teach somebody to do that, I think it's a really rewarding way to play.:

Earlier on in the interview he said he hadn't gotten much of a chance to follow the indie RPG scene, he's read Burning Empires and that's about it.

I really enjoyed this interview, and a lot of what I heard leads me to think Monte has the beating heart of a Storygamer down in there somewhere. I was thinking of sending him a PDF of *one* story game. Saying something like "I know you don't have a lot of time, but here. Here's an example of the type of play you spoke of but haven't been able to codify."

My current vote: In A Wicked Age

It's short, it's easy, it's fantasy. So what do you think Story Games people? What ONE game should I send to him?

    \n If I had to choose, it would be between PTA and Mouse Guard. Mouse Guard is more current and a great read. In terms of GM roles that aren't too alienating, Mouse Guard is a good fit. It also encompasses a great deal of the techniques used in modern story games and presents them in the best game instruction text I've ever read. IAWA is a great game in play but the text may not convey the concepts as clearly. I would suggest reading Mouse Guard and playing IAWA. If playing isn't possible, go with Mouse Guard.

    \n Hmm, as others have implied, I think you're going to have to choose between a game that contains the type of mechanics that Cook is talking about, and a game that reads well and clearly communicates what "story-gaming" is all about. I can't think of a game that does both well (though I haven't read any of BW books, including Mouse Guard).

    PTA seems the obvious choice as far as mechanics are concerned. Universalis probably also fits the bill, though Uni' might seem a little too "foreign" to someone who isn't familiar with indie games. (There are actually a ton of games that "[reward] someone for cool roleplaying with a mechanic that then empowers them to do more cool roleplaying", but most aren't as obvious about it as PTA or Uni'.)

    If you're going for just a good read... Dogs is still probably one of, if not THE best written indie game book.

    If you just want a game that's easy to play, there's a bunch of games you could give him. I think that's where most of the suggestions in this discussion fall.
