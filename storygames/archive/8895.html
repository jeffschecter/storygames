    \n This is a cool topic. I don't know much about the machinations behind the current decision but I can give you some insight into the challenges that led to the decision.

    Basically, the Camarilla is a "global chronicle" - it is a LARP that basically anyone can join by creating a character in accordance with published rules, organizing a game and paying a fee. Then, your character can basically be taken to any other "venue" where it's permitted by the local folks and everything that's happened in your game has happened in theirs. This is so incredibly great. The mysterious and awesome events of your game are shared not in blog posts or in actual play posts, but in-character with all the inconsistent narration and confusion that entails.

    However, there's an inherent problem to this approach - the World of Darkness games, even the relatively limited ones, are extremely broad. A Vampire game can be very political, or it can be action-packed, it can be quirky, sad, dramatic or bombastic. That is a good thing about Vampire, not a bad thing! But there's no question it creates tension. Not only would Mr. Darcy not jump a sports car off an exploding ramp, he would merely look sadly upon someone who had.

    Normally this tension is not resolved. In practice, if people want to play in a game long-term, they make a character for that game and play it in the style of that game with the other characters. In the short-term, it's exploited for yuks at best and some eyes are rolled at worst.

    But the other challenge the Camarilla faces isn't breadth, it's depth. Because a game can go for many, many years, it becomes increasingly difficult to slot in new players at the "normal starting character"'s power level while still providing interesting material for those who have a massive pile of experience. And it is very discouraging to new people. You start to wonder, often, why you are here, when someone already fills the niche you wanted and does it better than you possibly ever could. (And you'll never catch them, since you're all gaining experience at more or less the same rate!)

    The idea of resetting character stats, and/or re-doing how experience is handled, is one way of addressing (some of) these problems (partly).

    I've actually kicked around the idea of a Camarilla venue, but:

    A) it would be small,
    it would be limited in time,
    and C) it would probably be a mortals game based around detectives and police officers of various stripes, which would make crossover play ridiculously stupid...

    all of which make Camarilla stuff completely useless. I suppose it's my griefer's instinct alone that leads me to think flinging a venue like this into the Camarilla would be great.

    \n Yes, despite my cynicism, I rather like the idea of the Camarilla. When I was a member of a similar organisation, One World By Night, I could travel anywhere in the US and be fairly assured of a bed for the night and some roleplaying. It was rather pleasant.

    Like you say, the difficulty is that the older characters are hugely powerful, with power to beat down the newer ones. There's then a disincentive to abandon your old character, because you'd lose all that power. So the idea of a reset, to level the playing field, is actually rather a good one.

    Ironically, a Camarilla-style organisation might work better with indie games, because there's less emphasis on character advancement (in terms of New Powers). A national Shadow Of Yesterday organisation, for example, might work rather well.

    \n I've been in an out of the Camarilla over the years. I kept going back hoping that the social aspect of the game would make up for how horrible it is to deal with the bureacracy of the Camarilla as well as certain other problems. The best way to understand the Camarilla is that it is structured like a cult. If you're new, you're getting dumped on, both in game and out. If you've passed the tests, gathered the special knowledge and ascended the ranks, then you shall be blessed with rewards, respect, status, positions and responsibilities (and bureacracy, a lot of bureacracy).

    If they're trying to reset experience without changing the story, that tells me a lot. Over the past couple of years they started handing out buckets of experience points with which to make stronger characters. This sounds like it may be a method of balancing new characters with old, but it worked out the opposite. Established players (whether they were new or old characters) got a lot of xp, while newer players got less for their (obviously newer) characters. This may seem unfair, and it is, but I can speak from experience that you want power imbalances in a larp. It's just a really ugly way to achieve it, and perceived unfairness was probably part of why they're changing things. But if I had to bet money on anything, I'd bet that whatever they're changing it to will go awry in a year or two anyways.

    What's going to really go badly is continuity. When character powers suddenly change due to an experience reset, well, you're probably all familiar with how it sounds when the crew encounters the fifth "only thing in the universe that creates tachyons." It's worse than that. I ran a mortal game after the new rules-set came out, and we had to go through an approval process to do anything that might impact upon or be visible in non-mortal games that occured in the same city. So, when a character wanted to do graffiti or paste up posters, I had to submit official requests for permission to let it happen in-game. Sound lame? Yes, yes it was, and it was in the name of protecting continuity. The more you wanted to do, the more you ran up against Camarilla bureacracy.

    The only regret I have was not attending a convention. I'd like to see one of the big larp conventions of 1500+ people, but I'd show up for the con, play, then run screaming, lest I get dragged back into the rest of it.
