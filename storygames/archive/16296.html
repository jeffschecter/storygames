\n
\nI know some of these are derogatory in some respects, but I have played with all of these types of people and even though that's their style of play it doesn't always mean a negative experience, well at least for me, it could be though. Do you guys have others?
\n
\nRules Lawyer: Will stop a game just to look up a rule or more importantly play the "it's covered in the rules this way" during a session. Even if it breaks the flow of a game.
\n
\nTwink/MinMaxer: Used the rules to maximize effectiveness of character for game mechanic's reasons.
\n
\nThe Book: The one player you can reliably expect not only know what the rule is but where to find it if she doesn't know the exact details of the rule. Usually has her role-playing book with little tabs all over it.
\n
\nFudgemister: The player who doesn't trust the GM to be fair with the game. Will fudge his dice, use his outside game knowledge either subtly or not to keep his character alive.
\n
\nThe RPist: Will play the character's flaws to the detriment of himself and the story line. Doesn't mind a good death for his character and enjoys the hell out of getting work form his GM to make the story move forward.
\n
\nTag-along: Comes to games cause its "cool" but falls asleep during the session, has to be woken up for either combats or anything special.
\n
\nSide-traker: That guy who's read all the source books and novels and then cuts in with "Wouldn't it be cool if Bla came in." Or "Bla Bla has this...." and you spend the next 10 minutes wanting them to be quite so you can get on with the game. (These ones annoy me most of all.)

    \n I think it's far more useful to classify behaviours rather than people.

    People are complex, changeable, and diverse. What they do in one game will be different from what they do in another. Categorising people is reductive, often controversial, and pathologises play in a way that I find unconvincing.

    On the other hand, there are recognisable patterns of behaviour that appear from time to time in play, and understanding these behaviours strikes me as a very useful thing indeed. By focusing on the behaviours, rather than the people, I think you open up a far wider set of options for understanding, encouraging or discouraging, mitigating or enhancing the behaviours.

    \n The Martyr: Throws her character into danger to make the story more interesting for others, even if it means character death or other removal from play results.

    The Scene Maker: Usually a GM, but will get up from the table to paint scenes, show full body language, and may even set up a LARP-style vignette.

    The Noble Politician: Will do whatever it takes to further her character's agenda, even if that means subverting or ignoring the character's previously established personality and morality.

    The Improviser: Freely and happily contributes story, character, and setting details to keep the roleplaying moving, trusting the group to accept the details even though they weren't previously established. Does accept details in return when others do it, even in cases where her own character is filled out with detail.

    The Hulk: Loves to play angry characters of few words, with a dangerous and violent personality. often dramtically increases tensions, especially in group parlays with opponents, but never goes too far to derail the game.

    The Challenger: Has a keen eye for potential conflicts and tense moments and pushes things to increase dramatic tension, or dives into interesting conflicts for the good of the story. Often a GM.

    \n I just posted this in another thread, but I'll put it in here as well, because it's relevant.

    This is a list of motives I've observed players make decisions based on. The vast majority of the negative behaviors you've identified in this thread represent players frustrated with a lack of opportunity to satisfy these motives productively.

    (Note: This list was developed primarily through observing live combat larp players, which is why you have things like Exercise in the list. But it works well for tabletop if you allow for that.)

    Motives:
    Audience - Experience a satisfying narrative
    Catharsis - Experience emotions through the character
    Comprehension - Figure things out. Solve problems and puzzles.
    Competition - Win at something, or at least enjoy the act of competing.
    Crafting - Create non-ephemeral things (costumes, props, documents, etc.)
    Education - Take away new knowledge or understanding as a player.
    Embodiment - Make decisions based on character background, knowledge, and motivation.
    Exercise - Enjoy physical activity and movement.
    Exploration - Experience the fictional setting.
    Exhibition - Show off (costumes, props, acting chops, mad skillz, etc.) and get kudos.
    Fellowship - Enjoy time with friends (also includes flirting and such).
    Flow - Enjoy losing oneself in the moment.
    Leadership - Be important to the player community.
    Protagonist - Be important to the story and impact the game world.
    Spectacle - Experience the awesome stuff (pretty costumes, elaborate sets, funny NPCs, etc.)
    Versatility - Collect important things (spells, lore, favors, etc.) and have the right thing at the right time.


    I think recognizing that players have these desires, and that they are perfectly reasonable things to want out of a game experience, can lead to a productive discussion with your players. They can also be used to identify what players are looking for and guide session planning.

    These are the short versions of the motive descriptions. I have a 20 page article that goes into more detail.

    \n
    \nOk so lets move round the grid from Combat Focused through Power Gamer to Tactical Focused
    \n
    \nThe Hulk (aka combat wombat) I place this player close to the Combat Focused line but deep in the power game quadrant. Most Hulks need to have a good level of knowledge about the game while still being able to focus on powering up the characters.
    \n
    \nThe Book moving closer to the middle of the arc through Power Gamers, but close into inner circle of where it's less difficult for people to make the move over the line of gaming. I place the book here because usually these players tend to be more about the game overall, trying to make the game flow fast and by the rule.
    \n
    \nThe Noble Politician, moving deeper into the grid of power gamers but almost in line on the same arc with the Book is where I think you'd find the Politician, They are more selfish about their motives of power gaming. The rules are there to benefit them.
    \n
    \nRules Lawyer, continuing down closer to the Tactical Focused line you'll find the Rules Lawyer, pretty much below the Politician. These players tend to want to play the game fairly at an individual level, this means if it good for them it's good for you.
    \n
    \nThe Line of Tactical Focused:
    \nWelcome to Fudgemister land, a place where balance is important. If this player feels you are putting them at a tactical disadvantage and doesn't trust you to be fair they will use their considerable knowledge of the game and skills as a character actor to fix this issue
    \n
    \nSide-traker, Moving from Tactical Focused towards the Story Focused just outside the white circle maybe about 4 o'clock is our Side-tracker, using his character actor skill to opportunistically stop the flow of the game giving them either time or just to derail the play the game dynamics change or stop.
    \n
    \nExoticist, deep deep in the far right hand corner is the Exoticist, someone who's whole play is based deeply in their enjoyment of Character Acting. This can be detrimental storyline if the player doesn't account for everyone else.
    \n
    \nThe Rpist, crossing the line of Story Focused into Storytellers we find the Rpist at about 7 o'clock about halfway down the grid. Plays the game for the story now always versatile enough deep play a character but understands the need for their play to move with the story.
    \n
    \n
    \nThe Scene Maker, deep in the Storytellers about 8 o'clock is the Scene Maker with a good balance of Strategic and Story focus to ensure very memorable scenes, though sometimes not rules consistent.
    \n
    \nThe Martyr moving close to the Strategic Focus line half way from the edge of the grid and the white inner circle you find the Martyr, a player willing to sacrifice for the over aching storyline and giving the players enjoyment as well as themselves.
    \n
    \nThe Improviser found in the inner circle very close to the Strategic Focused line so close that they can easily move over the lines to be what is needed for the game.
    \n
    \nMoving over the Strategic Focused line into the Thinkers we find The Challenger not too far mirrored from the Martyr. With a deep understanding of the game and loads of thought the Challenger bring his vision of the role-playing games base premise to the story.
    \n
    \nTwink/MinMaxer, last but not least on my little list sitting in the centre of the Thinkers grid is the Twink, master of his math, and games rules that are so grey they can use them to get the maximum out of their characters.

    \nThe horizontal axis is Strategic Focused to Tactical Focused.
    \n"Strategic" means "a perspective larger than the immediate future and surroundings".
    \n"Tactical" means "a perspective limited to the immediate future and the immediate surroundings."
    \n"Invade the Normandy beaches on D-Day" is Strategic. "Take out that bunker before the machine gun kills us all!" is Tactical.
    \nThe vertical axis is Combat Focused to Story Focused.
    \n"Combat Focused" means "Conflict resolution is interesting to me." "Story Focused" means "The world and the interaction of the characters is intersting to me."
    \n"Kick this door down and kill anything that moves" is Combat focused. "This door has the Mark of Malvena; the space within should be safe for us to rest in" is Story focused.
    \nThis creates four quadrents. Starting in the upper left and moving clockwise, label these quadrents Thinkers, Power Gamers, Character Actors, andStorytellers
    \n
    \nA Thinker
    \nIs a player who most enjoys the game when it delivers Strategic/Combat Focus. This kind of person is likely to enjoy min-maxing a character, spending hours out of game to find every conceivable advantage available in the system to deliver maximum damage from behind maximum protection, even if the min-maxing produces results that are seemingly illogical/impossible. This kind of person wants to solve puzzles and can keep track of long chains of facts and clues.
    \nA Power Gamer
    \nIs a player who most enjoys the game when it delivers a Tactical/Combat Focus. This kind of person is likely to enjoy playing a character that has a minimum of personality (often, this kind of person plays a character that is simply an extension of the player). This kind of player enjoys short, intense gaming experiences. The consequences of a failed action are minimized for this player, who will roll up a new character and return to the fray without much thought for the storyline implications of that action.
    \nCharacter Actor
    \nIs a player who most enjoys the game when it delivers a Tactical/Story Focus. This kind of person is likely to enjoy the act of theater; using voice, posture, props, etc. to express a character's actions and dialog. This player will have a character that makes sub-optimal choices (from an external perspective) to ensure that the character's actions are "correct" from the perspective of the character's motivations, ethics, and knowledge.
    \nA Storyteller
    \nIs a player who most enjoys the game when it delivers a Strategic/Story Focus. This kind of person finds enjoyment from the logical progression of the narrative of the scenario. There should be a beginning, a middle and an end. Characters should develop over time in reaction to their experiences. This player will look for a non-rules answer to inconsistencies or anachronisms in the game experience.
    \nThere is a fifth type of player, who does not express a preference along any of the four axis. This person is a "basic roleplayer", who finds enjoyment from strategy, tactics, combat and story in rough equilibrium.

    \nThat's a big part of why I phrased the motives as specifically being things that produce observable behaviors. Sometimes others can see our motives more clearly than we can see in ourselves.

    I can identify players for whom, for example, Exhibition is a huge motive. But because having such a motive is often considered vanity, society encourages people to not acknowledge and embrace the fact that they enjoy such things. And that is why I focus heavily on presenting all the motives in a positive manner.

    All the motives, when channelled and encouraged properly, can bring TONS of good stuff to a game. I played a tabletop game with a player who loved to play characters with very specific accents and mannerisms, sometimes funny and sometimes serious, depending on the game. He played a troll decker in a Shadowrun game that used a speech mannerism based on the main character from Sling Blade and had his decking port hidden up his nose. I laughed so hard when he spoke that my sides hurt for days after we played. And we all had a great time. It's an excellent example of Exhibition being used to entertain the rest of the group. The player probably didn't consciously acknowledge that he was showing off his funny characterization and playing for laughs - he's a pretty humble guy and the idea of "showing off" would appall him. But by giving an appropriate amount of free reign for it to happen, the GM gave him the opportunity to satisfy his motive and gave the rest of the table the opportunity to be thoroughly entertained.
