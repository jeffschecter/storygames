\n Over in the promo corner of RPGNet, I saw this post which made me wonder.\n
Spectrum Games is pleased to announce the imminent release of Urban Manhunt, a non-collectible card game that brings to life a violent death sport of the early 22nd century....This game is definitely an homage to the near-future movies of the 1970s and \u201880s \u2013 The Running Man, Escape From New York, The Warriors, etc. I wanted it to be bleak and dystopian
\nIt's an indie game about brutal manhunts and it's coming out next month. So what's the big deal? Even if you were the most avid design follower, you wouldn't have a clue who I am, but this month I wrote a game about brutal manhunts for Nathan Paoletta's Two Games One Name competition. After I posted up news of the beta release on RPGNet, I noticed the Urban Manhunt topic quoted above, by Cynthia Celeste Miller (Slasher Flick). It had been posted just 6 hours after mine.

Two games of the same theme announced just hours between one another? Seems unlikely, but I've heard it happens in many creative genres, from books, to computer games and even to film (Capote/Infamous). Does this kind of thing happen a lot? When it does happen does it mean anything?

I'm more interested in the coincidence that had the last month of my life devoted to an indie game concept that was broadcast on the same day as I broadcast my own version.     \n >> Parallel Evolution?

    That's what it's normally called, yeah.

    Another big example (for J-geeks) is Castle Falkenstein vs (the Japanese RPG) Tokyo Nova. Both games were the first time in their respective countries that anyone tried publishing a complete RPG where the central mechanic was purely based on trump/playing cards, no dice etc, which was revolutionary at the time. It just so happens that even though the use of the cards is a little different, and the settings are different, the games were released within a few months of each other (less than a year, with Tokyo Nova coming out first), leading otaku game conspiracists over on 2ch and local hobby stories to be all "Well it's obvious that R Talsorian's Mike Pondsmith stole the idea from us; remember at the time he was releasing anime-themed games like DBZ and Bubblegum Crisis, so he must have been in contact with Japanese publishers and developers who knew of (then "Ascii", before "FEAR") upcoming project, and that's where he took the idea for what would become Castle Falkenstein..."

    ...when of course most rational folks, even in the mini-realms of RPG fandom, simply understand as a textbook example of parallel evolution.

    \n Falkenstein? What a game. I remember buying it in Gaelcon 95 (or was it 96). I met Mike Pondsmith. He made a big impression, with his deep American voice and charming serenity. He was the coolest roleplayer I'd ever known, though I was comparing him to the proto-nerds at the convention. I couldn't, at the time, imagine anyone like him ripping anything off. And besides, the book must have been in layout for at least a few months (did you see the artwork in that thing? With two textures of paper and everything?).

    And yes, cards in roleplaying games were a big deal. I remember being blown away, and having to go to a 'normal' shop to get some playing cards as the gaming stores didn't stock them.
