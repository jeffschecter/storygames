    Ron, I've been in that boat too, many times. I do think it's a problem of rules complexity, though. A light rule set doesn't require as much investment to learn, and you can reasonably expect everyone to catch onto the mechanics of Risus in half an hour or so. As a GM, you may be called upon to adjudicate the rules, which I think is fair. Granted, anyone could take on this role, but it's not unreasonable to thrust this onto the GM.

    On the other hand, you cannot reasonably expect players to understand anything but the basics of a 5-book run of GURPS (okay, perhaps also the specialized rules dealing with their own niche). You also cannot reasonably expect a GM to have 5 books of rules in their head. Thus, in my experience, you end up with three possible modes of play:\n
      \n
    • Only use the basic roll-3d6-under-your-skill for everything
      • \n
    • Flip through the book for five minutes every time someone tries to do something
      • \n
    • The GM fudges up some rule combo from tidbits she recalls in that particular instance
      • \n
    \nTo me, that makes GURPS useful as an engine for sim play, whether you have hardcore players who want to flip the rule book every five minutes, or players who don't give a crap about the rules and want the GM to act as a blackbox representation of the game world.

    It makes GURPS useless to me as an engine for any other type of play, where I claim that players must be able to know enough of the rules to be able to use them to serve their personal creative goals. For example, if you can't grasp the intricacies of d20 combat you will not achieve victories in D&D. If you can't grasp how Crew dice work in Matt Wilson's Galactic, you won't understand how to use them to push for an address of the central conceit of the game.

    I'm not sure if that's even controversial.

    David, in the referenced thread, I do mention the idea of the GM being the one who owns all the rule books. So yes, for that reason and for the other ones you mention, I agree that it's an understandable pattern. I guess I would argue it's a pattern I often find destructive, though. The GM has investment in the game and tries (with varying results) to make it rub off on players. The players who don't share the same degree of investment are unable to learn the complex rules. In effect, those players are limited to sim-exploratory agendas.

    If you've played the game for a very long time, especially if the rule set hasn't changed drastically in that time, it is entirely possible that everyone has a good grasp of the rules by now and can use them fluidly and competently to serve their creative goals. In that case, good for you.

    \nRight. Your two points are intertwined very closely.

    My claim that only Sim play can be successful in GURPS play is based on the three play methods I outlined above. David/Hituro and Jason/JDCorley adds a fourth, which is that one can actually learn to use the system to serve other agendas quite well after extensive play with the same group of people, and that's fine. I'm positing that a game system which can only be played non-Simmy with success after 5+ years of practice should either only be used for Sim play, or had poor design assumptions from the get-go.

    Interestingly, two of the remaining three play methods correspond directly to two of the three methods for resolving situations the rules "do not cover":

    Roll 3d6 for everything = success rolls
    Fudge together some rule in the moment = random rolls

    What remains is "take time to look up every rule" and "arbitrary fiat", which seem like exact opposites of each other, but are quite related - they simply shift the role of GM and rules in the GM/rules-complex closer to one side or the other. So I claim that there are really two ways to play GURPS, assuming you do not have intimate knowledge of the system (where system is defined via the Lumpley Principle) only arrived at through extended, continuous play with the same group:

    On the one hand, you could look up every rule and apply it in meticulous detail, with the GM having final rules arbitration privilege. This appeals, generally, to sim-style wargamer peeps. Note that the agenda here is not the "game" in "wargamer" but rather the simmy and exploratory aspect of wargames. I'm not a wargamer, but this playstyle would be my preference between the two.

    On the other hand, you could simply state your character's actions with the understanding that the GM, acting as a complex world operating according to variables you cannot hope to comprehend, will spit back the results. You, the player, do not give a crap whether the GM is juggling calculus in his head in order to arrive at the conclusions, or whether she simply hand-waves it - all you know is that on a roll of 6, X happens. The less you know about the process, the better. This appeals to players who want to explore their character and the world in actor-stance, which is also a very simmy agenda.

    \n Renato, my claim is that even if the GURPS rules advised the GM never to fudge a roll and always look up every rule before using it, the resulting play from a player standpoint would be near identical, except slower. That's why I'm reluctant to use terms like "bad GM advice".     And Tommi, yes. You are absolutely right. In a group where everyone has a comprehensive understanding of System (including social contract, division of narrative power, rules etc) it is possible to achieve playstyles other than Sim using GURPS. Much like, if you have a comprehensive understanding of chainsaws, you can use one to sculpt chess pieces or carve chicken.

    Far as I see it, there are three problems with doing Nar play in GURPS.

    One is tied to GM advice and Moreno's point - if the player is not the author in control of how premise is addressed, since they do not know the parameters of the rules they use, Nar play cannot occur. Narratives which in retrospect appear as though they address a premise can be produced, but they will be expert-authored rather than generated dynamically through play and those who participated know the difference.

    Second, the (base) rules are clearly written with logical consistency in mind, as opposed to rules which are game-able or dramatic, and as such are useless for play other than Sim without some major tinkering, by adding drama points or instant CP awards or what have you.

    Third, the sheer complexity of the rules (without doing some major rule-slashing like Jason) make grand-picture odds-calculation very difficult.

    Example tying all three together: authoring something as straightforward as "our sad anti-hero retreats to his favourite sleazy bar, gets boozed up and finds himself a cheap lay for the night" might get complicated by things like Reaction and Influence rolls, both of which draw their variables from dozens of system attributes. If the GM finds it amusing, she may arbitrarily decide there's a 1/10 chance to get an arbitrarily chosen STD, or that a bar fight breaks out. As a result, you have no more authorial ability over the fictional world than you do in the real one. That's great fun if your intent is to "live the dream" - less so if you're trying to stay focused on a theme or make a statement.

    Yes, some given GURPS GM might just say "okay" and move on to the next person, but the unspoken justification for not applying the rules is not "because I understand you were saying something important with that and I don't want to mess with it", rather "because it doesn't matter to the story, so let's sweep that under the rug and move on".

      \nWaiting for Ben Lehman

      Alpha Playtest Version

      The game requires approx. three to six players, all present at the same location. Designate one player as "The Victim." Designate another player who is not present, with or without his/her prior consent, as "Ben Lehman."

      "The Victim" crafts a statement, about any topic whatsoever, that "Ben Lehman" wants to stab him to death over. It should not be immediately clear why. The "Victim" calls "Ben Lehman" and relates this statement to him, invites him to come stab him to death, then hangs up.

      Play then proceeds to the Victim's left. every player in turn makes an argumentative point based on previous statements. If the argument is on the main topic of the Victim's opening statement (hereafter called the "Original Post" or "OP"), s/he gets a Logic Point. If the argument is on a side topic or digression, s/he gets a Personality Point. The next player can then try to rebut the previous argument. All players but the Rebutter and the the Rebuttee vote, and if the Rebutter wins the vote, s/he takes the Point away from the Rebuttee.

      Note that whether an argument is "On Topic" or not is generally unclear, in the absence of "Ben L:ehman" to clear it up. The Arguer declares whether s/he is taking a LP or PP. Any other player can challenge, and the other players vote. If the Arguer is overruled, s/he takes the other kind of point.

      The Victim has double votes toward Rebuttals, and no votes toward On/Off Topic.

      Endgame:
      When Ben Lehman arrives, he then tells the Victim why he wanted to stab him to death. He then mimes doing so, with or without a prop. All arguers whose points coincide with Ben Lehman's recieve 5 additional points, 10 if "Ben Lehman" is the actual Ben Lehman.

      Player with the most Logic Poings or Personality Points (PPs win in case of tie) wins the internet!

      \n To sum up my take on this thread:

      I think I had a good core thought or two, somewhere up in that mess, but they were weakly supported and quite possibly flawed. By refusing to adjust my position to address other experiences, primarily from Jason and later from Tommi, I failed to convey those thoughts properly and confused myself with theory.

      Here are those thoughts:

      I wholeheartedly believe that buffet-style rulesets like GURPS or HERO primarily caters to Sim agendas, where the GM is tasked with the (impossible?) job of representing The World so that players can concentrate on playing Their Characters. When applied with a heavy dose of GM fiat, it becomes a tool the GM uses as s/he wishes to represent the world accurately, rather than a tool used by the players to empower their choices in that world. As such, the level of rules complexity is largely unimportant as long as everyone involved recognizes the rules are the GM's domain.

      I was wrong in saying one could not play with Gamist or Narrativist agendas using such systems. After all, they are highly complex and buffet-style, so one should be able to strip off bits catering to a pure Sim agenda and arrive at a system perfectly capable of any kind of play. Whether I think this is good game design or not is a separate concern.

      Whether one can actually successfully play with Gamist or Narrativist agendas without good knowledge of the rules is a more interesting question. The Amber throne wars example is neat, but I'd have to say I think the rules in this case are largely social and unwritten rather than mechanical and written, and discovering the rules are in fact part of playing the game. Nar play is more difficult to justify, but the examples I've seen so far all revolve around the GM explaining the rules and laying out all the options as the game is being played, which does not necessarily constitute an example of the players not knowing the rules.

      Frankly, I can see arguments either way, and I'm not as convinced as I used to be. A game written with a Nar mindset could well achieve Nar play without the players knowing how all the bits work together ahead of time. Exhibit A, Dogs in the Vineyard - the game seems to be about gun-toting holy warriors until you start playing the game, taking Fallout, questioning the Faith, being driven out of town, etc. Exhibit B, Sorcerer - the game seems to be about bad-ass demonologists until you start messing with those Bangs and realize your Humanity is dropping, fast. If you never put your Crew at risk when you're playing Galactic because you don't think a single d8 is really going to matter that much, you will not see the Crew character grow and you won't see the central premise of Galactic in action.

      It's more complicated than I first thought, and I appreciate the input of everyone who has helped me get to that realization.
