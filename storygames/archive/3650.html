    Posted By: Matt_Snyder1) What technology do you use and/or recommend? I found an article that suggests a decent mic ($50 at least), a power source and a mixing board. None of which I know too much about. Is this even necessary, especially if I'll be doing talking mainly over Skype or something comparable? (i.e. won't be doing much in the way of local interviews at my computer).
    \nWhat I have is a mixing board, two mics, a USB audio bridge & a laptop. Really, all this is way overkill for what I normally do, and you can totally get away with doing a podcast with the headset you have -- especially if you're not sure how much money you want to throw into this.

    If you want to get into convention recording, it gets more complicated from my understanding, since you'll want multiple mics, and that's where you'll need a mixing board and something beyond gaming headsets.\n
    2) Can you recommend something for recording at conventions? What works? Handhelds? Portable "studios"?
    \nPaul Tevis is fond of his iRiver. I have my laptop & board, but while it's portable it's not very mobile -- I can bring it with me and set up somewhere, but I can't roam with it.\n
    3) What have you found to be the best online telephony service? Is Skype indeed the way to go?
    \nI pretty much just use Skype for Master Plan\n
    4) Do you have any technical advice on recording and/or editing? Resources?
    \nLoads, but no one point sticks out as a quick answer. Audacity is free on the PC, and I hear Mac folks use GarageBand, so the entry level for editing software is really low.\n
    5) What do you find makes your podcast work well? Interviews? Reviews? Regularity? I'd love to hear some top suggestions of how to make your content compelling.
    \nWithout getting too deep (as I'm prone to elsewhere) into the topic, it's all about knowing who your target audience is. My target audience enjoy the interviews, but they also want me to keep talking about my own experiences & efforts. I can give you top suggestions for how to make your content compelling to my audience, but I don't know how much that'll help you.

    Regularity is something my listeners have complained about -- Master Plan doesn't come out regularly enough. It comes out when I have time to make it, and I'm trying to make more time, but life has a way of interrupting those plans.

    I am firmly not a review podcast, because that's not the intent of my show. When it comes to the interviews, I try to keep them about the subject of game design, usually about one or two specific topics, and edit heavily to keep things going. The interviews might drift around some, but the end result is (ideally) something focused.

    I'll also say that working to keep my show under 30 minutes is a big deal, and most folks seem to like it. The only complaints I get on that front is "I wish you'd go longer so you could go into more detail." My philosophy on podcasting is: Leave the audience wanting more is better than leaving them wanting less.

    I should also point out that, if you can get away from the booth for some of them, there will be some podcaster panels at GenCon on Friday & Saturday. I'm on some of the ones on Friday.

    \n Matt, Ryan pretty much answered your questions. If you're using Skype, 'Okay' is about as good as you can hope for. If you're happy with how you sound, the $40 headset is fine. Audacity is also available for the Mac, if you don't want to be locked into Apple's system (GarageBand has been super-reliable and easy to use, so I stick with it).

    Daniel: I've never recorded a game, so I don't really know. Plunking a dedicated microphone in front of everyone probably isn't an option (it's not for us, not least of which because it would be annoying). You need a omnidirectional mic, but cheap ones sound like crap, just recording a room. I honestly don't know. If a recorder outputs an audio file, you can import it into GarageBand. Maybe rent some wireless lavaliers and a mixing board?

    Ryan: It's fine if you want to share our conversation. Oh! I have a mic suggestion for you, that will solve your background noise problems. The Sterling Audio ST-55 has -10db roll-off switch. Basically when you turn it on, the microphone ignores soft background noise. You sacrifice a little bit of detail, but it removes a lot of 'mung' from the background. In addition, the ST-55 is a great-sounding mic. Maybe a little more spendy than you want, but I found one used, and I really love it.

    Posted By: RemiHey Ryan,
    Pretty much I was getting lots of noise from one of my two remaining inputs on my mixer. I tried all three mics (which were quiet on the other line) on the input and ended up getting the same noise from each one. So it's the mixer.

    If you're asking how to get rid of line noise? For me the answer is to get a mixer that has XLR inputs. That should help a lot. Making those the only analog connection in my setup should help as well. I'm looking at a Alesis 8-channel USB mixer for this purpose.

    Does that help? Seriously, unless you're planning on buying a multi-thousand dollar condenser, there's no reason to sink a fortune into equipment. You're going to end up putting it up as a low-quality mp3, anyway, so just record in a relatively quiet room, with no fans blowing on your mic and you should be fine. I think where people run into problems is that they have so much background noise and such that it makes it difficult to hear them.

    Posted By: Matt_Snyder

    1) What technology do you use and/or recommend? I found an article that suggests a decent mic ($50 at least), a power source and a mixing board. None of which I know too much about. Is this even necessary, especially if I'll be doing talking mainly over Skype or something comparable? (i.e. won't be doing much in the way of local interviews at my computer).

    \nI'm embarrassed to admit that I have only spent money on Skype Recorder. Other than that, I use nothing more than the hardware I already had and free software to record, edit, and produce the Godzilla Gaming Podcast. I probably owe it to everyone listening to the show to get some better gear, but what I've got works and that's all I really need it to do. For me anyway, more expensive, albeit higher quality, equipment, would be a luxury I can't really afford right now.

    Recording platform: Mac OS 10.4 with built-in mike (and iSight when I use my MacBook - video conferencing with Cam makes it SO much easier)

    Recording software: When Cam and I were physically in the same location, I recorded with Audacity. Now I use Skype with Skype Recorder.

    Editing software: I do the basic editing with Audacity, then convert them to MP3s and cut the show together with Garage Band. For balancing sound levels I use Levelator.

    Hosting: One word - Libsyn

    You can spend a lot or a little. You can do a great podcast either way. :-)\n
    2) Can you recommend something for recording at conventions? What works? Handhelds? Portable "studios"?
    \nI haven't done this so I can't make any recommendations here.\n
    3) What have you found to be the best online telephony service? Is Skype indeed the way to go?
    \nI find it adequate for my purposes. After trying several jury rigged set-ups I went with Skype and those frustrations went away. If you're recording with Skype, I recommend spending the $30 or so to get Skype Recorder, which also made my interview recording headaches go away.\n
    4) Do you have any technical advice on recording and/or editing? Resources?
    \nSee previous sources cited. There is a hell of a lot more advice and technical know-how (not to mention equipment and software) for podcasting than there was just two years ago. When I started GGP in January last year, I was stumbling in the dark, but it's much easier now.\n
    5) What do you find makes your podcast work well? Interviews? Reviews? Regularity? I'd love to hear some top suggestions of how to make your content compelling.
    \nI'd have to say it's the chemistry I have with my podcasting partner, Cam. I really appreciate the work solo podcasters like Paul Tevis, Ryan Macklin, and Sam Chupp do, because going solo and making it interesting is very hard to pull off. Our approach is to try and make it like an open discussion, even though we can't talk to our listeners in realtime. What we do on air is just us, with no fancy script (though we do use an outline of the segments, and points we want to bring up).

    Doing a general gaming advice show is pretty tough I think - the gaming audience that listens to podcasts is very savvy, and there are a lot of shows that already do general advice. On the GGP, Cam and I just try to talk about what interests us about what we're doing, some gaming self-analysis, and simply discussing what's on our minds about the games we play. This seems to be a big part of the appeal of the show. We do occasional interviews, but the core of our show is two lifelong gamers talking about games. If we went away from that, we wouldn't have anything.

    We try as much as we can to bring our "international" perspective into things (how much we actually can do that is another matter, but!) Cam is from Australia, I'm from the States, and we both lived in Japan for many years (Cam is still there). Aside from this giving us a rather unique segment (Gaming in J-Land, aka, Mike and Cam Eat Something Weird On Air...) I hope it helps color our commentary somewhat.

    As for reviews, our twist is that we look at "old" (10 years +) games and new story games - the "bookends" so to speak. This lets us look at where the hobby we love has come from and where it's going.

    I guess what I'm saying is, look at your assets and figure out what you have that is unique, and work with that. You (Matt) have done game design and self publishing , so that's one thing (two things!) You did Dust Devils, and that's another. I don't know what else you might have up your sleeve but those first two sound like something I'd like to hear about!

    As far timing goes, set a schedule you are comfortable with. We do a monthly, 90-minute show. If we tried for once a week or even every other week we couldn't do it. Our schedules just don't allow it and frankly, I don't think we could keep it fresh doing it every week. But others do, so it's a question of what you've got and are willing to give. At any rate, our listeners seem satisfied with our show length, and given the monthly schedule, I think that's just fine. Most will tell you if the content is quality, they don't care much how long the show is.

    The main thing is, have fun! Don't sweat the format, make your own. You've got something to say and podcasting is great way to bring other people into your conversation!

    If you take the podcasting plunge, good luck! Looking forward to your show!

    Now, back to editing our Luke Crane special show! ;-)

    \n When I started, I too read through many posts that got into audiophile territory. Once the model numbers started flying around (and no slight to those of you that can sling 'em) I was lost. I wanted to know, "How do I do a frikkin' podcast??"

    The steps to hearing yourself yammering from your computer speakers are fairly straightforward:

    Pre-production

    1. Conceptualize your show. Decide what you're on about, how long it'll be, how often you'll put it out, any themes, features, interviews, etc.

    2. Outline or script what you want to talk about (I prefer a loose outline).

    3. Get some recording software for your computer. I use Audacity (for editing as well, but Garage Band can do the same things and the interface is better). Set it up so you can record yourself.

    4. Plug in and set up your mike(s) to run with your recording software (or worry not if the mike is built in). Initially, things like bit rates and such will confuse the hell out of you (they still confuse me). Don't sweat 'em the first time around, just get the damned thing recording!

    Recording

    5. Press record. Talk. Enjoy yourself. Press pause occasionally and take a break and get your bearings. Don't worry about being perfect - that's what editing is for! I find a good segment length is 10 - 15 minutes. If you go longer than this, break it up (in post) with a bumper or promo at the halfway point of the segment.

    Post-Production

    6. If your show files are not automatically recorded as MP3 files, convert them. Most audio software can do this. Audacity saves files as it's own file type, and you must convert them to MP3 (or WAV files).

    7. Edit - Put the files into your editing software and get to work. I go through and take out all the "um's," "ah's" stuttering, blabbering, car noises, etc. Some people don't - it's up to you (but I do appreciate a smooth cut). Cut out extraneous bits, stuff that isn't immediate to the conversation can go, tangential blathering can go - you can save an average of 10-15% (or more) of the show's total time by selective editing.

    8. Assemble the show - I use Garage Band because it has all the nifty FX and it's easy to drop segments in and move them around.

    9. Intro and outro music - Download some royalty-free music somewhere (there's a lot out there for podcasters) and cut it into an intro and outro file. You can mix voice tracks into it to make an appropriate intro. Save this file and reuse/refine it.

    10. Download some fellow podcasters bumpers and promos. Hit their websites or the Goblin Broadcast Network for these. Drop these into your podcast between segments.

    11. If you've cut the show together with something like Garage Band, you create a final mixdown (that combines all your various segments, bumpers, FX, and music into a single track). Garage Band imports this directly to iTunes, and once it's there (in iTunes), you need to convert the file to MP3 again. When finished you've got your show.

    Release

    12. If you've made it this far and have a completed show, you need hosting. Libsyn is my recommendation. For $5/month, you get up to 100mb of storage, unlimited archive space, a blog for the show, and support. It's set up specifically for podcasting and it is very simple to use.

    13. Post your show - Posting to Libsyn is easy. Include your show notes on the blog provided.

    14. Voila - You're a podcaster!

    Of course, if you want you can make a website and forum for your show and what have you, but these are the basic steps to simply getting a show out there. Flashy podcasting gear and all that are for later!

    \n As format goes, if it's just you behind the microphone, keep the show short and play short bumpers between segments. With only one voice on the recording, your voice will eventually fade into white noise. Short bumpers and different audio cues help to break up the audio landscape and keep the listener focused. Take a listen to how Have Games, Will Travel is structured: short segments with short bumpers. If you've got two or more hosts, the show can be longer without your audience's interest waning.

    Take a look at a month's worth of a serialized syndicated comic strip like For Better or For Worse. You'll see that the characters in the strip -- even if sitting in the same coffee shop having the same conversation -- use each other's names much more often than if that was an actual conversation. The constant use of names lets the first time and casual reader know who is whom in a comic strip.

    Just like this comic strip example, if you've got multiple hosts in your podcast, you want to make certain you keep using your co-host's name in conversation. Far too many hour-long podcasts introduce multiple co-hosts at the beginning of the show, but because the hosts know each other and can look at one another when speaking, they don't use their names after the intro. When a listener e-mails back and wants to discuss something said on your podcast, knowing who's who really helps the listener.

I edit the show content itself into it, and run through it in "native" format, making any edits there. Once done, I'll render it out to m4a, import into iTunes and reburn it as an mp3, which I then upload to our server using fetch.

    For completeness sake, I'll also note that our theme song(s) were written and performed by Finn Kisch and recorded and mixed on a MacBook using a Yamaha 01X board, Digital Performer 5.1 and Reason 3.

    In general, with editing, mastering, show notes and links, and posting, I find post-production runs about 2 hours per show, give or take. My experience with Garage band has been mixed - it works, but lordy, is it ever buggy.
