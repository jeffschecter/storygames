\n There are lots of interesting ideas in this thread on games for the Star Wars setting. But that setting has been so game-fied and re-used as to make fit many different styles of play (although some better than others).

But what I'd like to hear is what games would you use if you were playing the original three Star Wars movies - not as they are, but as they might have been? Imagine you've heard almost nothing, about Star Wars, and instead your group decides to play a space opera with a smuggler named Han Solo, a princess named Leia, and wanna-be pilot named Luke Skywalker, and a smart talking robot named R2D2. What game would turn that starting point into something that could have the themes and events in the original three movies?

Extra points if the game actively supports those themes and the cinematic space opera action, rather than simply not getting in the way.

- Mendel S.

    \nWell, it's an interesting distinction. When someone to me says, "What system would you use to do this setting?" my brain tends to lock up.

    But this time around we're focusing on "a space opera with a smuggler named Han Solo, a princess named Leia, and wanna-be pilot named Luke Skywalker, and a smart talking robot named R2D2."

    It's just me, I know, but modeling fictional environments boggles me. Characters in motion, I know how to do. And for me the distinction between the two requests is huge. (But, again, that's me.)


    My go to game for this would be HeroQuest. (I'm only familiar with the last edition, not the upcoming one.)

    The Traits allow all sorts of cool things to happen in terms of personality (Luke: IMPATIENT 17), relationships (Luke: IN LOVE WITH PRINCESS 22), occupations (PRINCESS 22, SMUGGLER 24), home cultures (FURTHEST PLACE FROM THE CENTER OF THE UNIVERSE 17... or whatever the line is...) and so on.

    Jedi powers could either as an umbrella skill or breaking it out into unique abilities (telekinesis, mind control, so on). All the crazy light saber stuff is abstracted enough to be a snap, allowing whatever color players want to add.

    The two resolution systems mean that blowing away a group of Storm Troopers shouldn't take up too much time....

    But if things go wrong you can break out the bidding system, and every time you swing across a chasm in a huge Death Star or SWOOSH between some asteroids to lose a TIE Fighter you're committing an action for the next round. Because the resolution system is so elastic you can move across all sorts of tactics to try to win the day all in one fight: "Luke, I am your father..." is just one more attempt to dominate a scappy kid, along with bashing with him objects hurled by use of the Force, or trying to knock his light saber from his hands.

    Super-heroic craziness is supported by the Hero Points. The Points can also be used to cement objects to a character (light sabers, for example), or cement improvements one might make to objects (like a beat up freighters named Millenium Falcon.)

    The game also supports the gathering of our Heroes into a Hero Band as they team up, which gives them advantages directly in the mechanics.

    New connections can be added as Traits as needed. (Luke: FATHER 21) and so on.

    The game won't get bogged down in lots of minutia about how things "really" work ("Okay, so how much damage does an X-Wing Fighter do against the Death Star's hull?") since the focus of the game is PCs in conflict with something else and that's it. ("Okay, extended contest. Luke, you're coming up on the first laser cannon of your run. Here we go....")

    It even handles Luke's HeroQuest on Dagobah, when he battles his father and finds only himself.

    Heroic deeds and over the top action inspired by passion, loyalties and personality with a system that supports moment by moment addition of color while still robustly handling resolution mechanically.

    All in all, it seems like the perfect go-to for me.

    \nI'm going to be a dick one step further.

    We like certain games because the offer up a focus of one kind or another in play -- emphasis some things, de-emphasizing others, giving us fiddly bits we like fiddling with (which is why we like them).

    My thesis -- absolutely documented in labs from here to Norway -- is that when we watch source material (Star Wars, Buffy, whatever) we're going to be drawing from it the things we would want to model or play that kind of matches up with the kind of things offered up by games we like.

    That said, I love Sorcerer and Primetime Adventures, and I don't think either would work well for doing Star Wars. But I do have games I'd reach for first to play Star Wars (like HeroQuest) which is still one of the games I'd reach for first in general.

    So, when we say, "This game does this movie well," we're saying, "I'm going to be doing the thing I like do with this game with this movie."
