    \n So! We ended up being an RPG-group-sized gang - Alex, Anders, Jason, Frederik and myself. (Graham popped in for a minute, but had to leave because of technical problems).

    We talked for about an hour. I talked a bit about the history & genesis of the game, the ideas behind the different design elements, and what people have been doing with it. Alex pitched in a lot of questions, Jason contributed his experiences, Anders & Frederik commented via the chat box (no audio).

    It was a pretty good format, I think. The technology isn't quite like being together in the same room - but it's a very different experience from discussing online. You can see people's reactions, it's easy to throw something in, etc. However, some subtleties of interaction are lost - there's a half-second lag when you talk, so interruptions and reactions are slightly off. Also, everyone's pretty much stuck in the same position, face towards the camera.

    I'd definitely want to do something similar again. I think it would be great for:
    - Designing a game together. You can talk, throw ideas around, take notes on the side, discuss examples etc.
    - Discussing a specific topic. In that case, you might want a moderator. (I'm thinking about what it would be like to see different Story Gamers discuss racism and appropriation via video chat. Would it be interesting, enlightening, or just horrible?)

    \n It was a really good experience, and felt like a really constructive use of time online. Not quite chatting in person, but an adequate facsimile.

    The issue of lag was minimised by the fact that you (Matthijs) were the de factor conversation leader and therefore hub of the conversation, meaning you could easily play a facilitator role as much input was directed from us (spokes) to you (hub). The downside of this is that it means less direct interaction - I don't think that Jason and I had much direct conversation, for instance - but you still get indirect channels through video (nodding + minor verbal affirmations) and the thread of the conversation can still be picked up and continue.

    I'd be interested in more free-form conversation (eg the game design example); I definitely think that a facilitator role is a useful one in most situations with a distributed group, in the interests of maintaining flow while including a balance of voices.
