    Well, let's back it up a step to see what the definition of Fetish is so as not to go all over the place:

    Fetish
    1. an object regarded with awe as being the embodiment or habitation of a potent spirit or as having magical potency.
    2. any object, idea, etc., eliciting unquestioning reverence, respect, or devotion: to make a fetish of high grades.
    3. Psychology. any object or nongenital part of the body that causes a habitual erotic response or fixation.
    and

    Fetishism
    1. belief in or use of fetishes.
    2. Psychiatry. the compulsive use of some object, or part of the body, as a stimulus in the course of attaining sexual gratification, as a shoe, a lock of hair, or underclothes.
    3. blind devotion: a fetishism of sacrifice to one's children.
    Well, the obvious connotations after reading the actual definition lead me to think that, yeah, saying "fetish", "fetishism" and "fetishization" (which isn't really a word, but it's not hard to see it as 'the process of creating a fetish') pretty much is pejorative.

    The only things I can think of that involve gaming and fetishism are the following:

    1) That dice, or character sheets, or combat manuvers, etc must be used in a roleplaying experience, thought by some players and designers. (the definition of fetish as eliciting unquestioning devotion) And yeah, it happens sometimes. Other times, it's not really "fetish", it's just "strong preference".

    2) The real fetish of believing that spirits or luck-gremlins or whatever inhabit dice. There's a lot of funny gaming stories out there about microwaving or drilling holes into "bad dice", leaving dice naturally set on the face you want them to come up the most to "train them", etc.

    In either case, I'm not seeing any practical application to game design or play here. Unless you create a game that makes people punish their bad dice and the like.

    Since we're starting with basic terminology here, I'll recommend Walter Benjamin's seminal essay on Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction .

    In particular, his concept of "aura".

    Wikipedia defines aura as: the sense of awe and reverence one presumably experienced in the presence of unique works of art. According to Benjamin, this aura inheres not in the object itself but rather in external attributes such as its known line of ownership, its restricted exhibition, its publicized authenticity, or its cultural value. Aura is thus indicative of art's traditional association with primitive, feudal, or bourgeois structures of power and its further association with magic and (religious or secular) ritual. With the advent of art's mechanical reproducibility, and the development of forms of art (such as film) in which there is no actual original, the experience of art could be freed from place and ritual and instead brought under the gaze and control of a mass audience, leading to a shattering of the aura. "For the first time in world history," Benjamin wrote, "mechanical reproduction emancipates the work of art from its parasitical dependence on ritual.


    Of course, mechanical reproduction begat the "limited edition", autographed copy, rare variant cover. Reinstating re-instating aura to otherwise-less-than-unique fetish objects.

    Let's see... In trying to work on an RPG inspired by the Superflat art movement -- which uses deconstructions of otaku culture to examine Japanese culture in general and the Japanese's relationship with art in particular -- I've been seeing some parallels between the two geek cultures, especially when comparing the Akiba-kei otaku and hardcore traditional D&D gamers. Some day I (or possibly someone smarter than me) needs to write an essay about this. Anyway.

    What I would call the core activities for otaku -- watching anime, reading manga, playing video games -- are somewhat similar to RPGs in that they require a relatively limited investment in physical goods, and those that are actually required are mostly 2-D content and the means to enjoy them. Most fans go quite a bit beyond their basic needs, however. You don't need lots of sourcebooks, minis, and sparkly dice to play D&D, any more than you'd need to buy figures of Rei and Asuka in Chinese dresses holding pistols in order to sit down and watch Evangelion. The amount of time and money one can spend on these subdivisions of the base hobby is enormous, sometimes enough to dwarf the original in the first place. (I really need to spend more time roleplaying and less time on message boards about roleplaying...) Most don't complain about this though; they actively embrace it.

    Part of what supports this is manufacturers playing to people who like having tidy shelves of neat stuff, the fetishization of the physical objects of a mostly mental hobby. Geek subcultures also highly prize arcane knowledge, however. For otaku there is a sort of evolving canon of necessary otaku knowledge -- if you want to be a true otaku, you've got to know your Gundam -- which is in turn reinforced by creative works with a marked tendency for referencing the canon (like in Keroro Gunsou, how Keroro is always building Gundam models, or Nakagomi's penchant for dressing up as Char in Akihabara@DEEP). I don't think it's by accident that it's games like D&D and Exalted, which encourage and reward the accumulation of arcane knowledge, that struck a chord with geeks.

    Evangelion is interesting from this perspective, since it very deliberately has a lot of this kind of technobabble and intrigue going on, but it very deliberately goes nowhere. And despite being very artistic, it was a mega-hit that saved Studio Gainax from going out of business, and they've been relentless in merchandising it any way they can over the past 10 years. Where some creators -- like the people behind Cowboy Bebop, or Bill Watterson -- shy away from excessive commoditization of their work, Gainax, being created by and for fans, embraces it.


    I was going to start a thread about this when I got my thoughts together more, but I really want to see what'll happen if we try to deconstruct D&D the way that Superflat deconstructs otaku culture. I'm not sure what would come of it, but that "FARP" game that never got released (about a society founded on ironclad misinterpretations of the PHB), Drowning & Falling, and the owlbear meme that swept through here a while back all point in that general direction.

    That's all I've got for now.

    Huh. It's interesting that Curly is basic pointing at an earlier version of the institutional theory of art, which describes creative works as socially constructed things that only really have meaning when you have an "art world" (an audience) that knows how to appreciate them in the appropriate fashion. Walter Benjamin was unfortunately wrong though, since people still attach high importance to objects, ownership, and structures of power even when creative works can be mass produced. I think, to get at this, we need a real good analysis of roleplaying's artworld, our fandom, our market (which are all closely related ideas).

    I know my brother and I are really interested in the recent movement towards promoting "comics as literature" instead of "comics as collectibles." The comic collectors' market, which dominated in the late 80's and most of the 90's, didn't really care so much about the content of comics as long as you could buy them for cover price and, ideally, sell them down the road for cash. But how much does WILDCATS #1 sell for nowadays? The bottom dropped out and the comics market took a dive. But if you're more interested in reading comics (because, hopefully, they're actually entertaining) rather than just owning them, things work differently. Heck, you don't have to own them at all. Comic libraries become a real possibility. The whole approach is different.

    Roleplaying games are slightly different, sure. There isn't a huge market for a first printing of the Player's Handbook. But there's still this strong emphasis on collectibility and owning books, dice, miniatures, cards, and other related stuff. There's this huge emphasis on the "industry" and publishers and the new book that's coming out and all the commercial aspects of the hobby that are not related to roleplaying at all. THAT, to me, is fetishization, all this time spent focusing on something that's not really related to roleplaying. Imagine what things would be like if it were more about "roleplaying as activity" instead of "roleplaying as publishing." You wouldn't feel the need to buy things in order to play. Whole swaths of things would become irrelevant. And that, in my mind, is a community that I could get much more excited about.

    Andy, I don't find "fetishism" to be a perjorative. I use it meaning the second "Fetish" definition and the third "Fetishism" definition. To avoid the hassle of scrolling back up, those are:
    Posted By: Andy

    Psychiatry has appropriated fetishes to refer to sexual matters; we don't have to. The key thing for me is when a gamer invests a ton of import into something, often because it is lionized in game texts, without doing any research on the subject or gathering empirical data. At that point, we have people attributing "unquestioning reverence" to these ideas and objects, propped up by a "blind devotion" that their attributions are correct.

    As example, I offer up: the katana. Oh, the katana is the best sword in the world, and should always have the highest damage rating in the game, and they're so freaking cool my character is going to weild two of them at the same time. All of which doesn't even begin to touch on eastern fetishism and all the gamer wanks in the world who think that bushido is really cool when in fact it's a restrictive nightmare of an ideology that fashions you into a tool of despots and prevents you from being a human being.

    I think fetishism is a big problem in gaming, inasmuch as gaming has "big problems." Why do I think it's a big problem? Cause it gets in the way of the role-taking and meaning-creating process that's core to what I appreciate in roleplaying. If the katana, and whoever wields a katana, are always and automatically the best choice, then what's the point of trying on different roles or creating different meanings that aren't backed up by martial power?
