\n
Ben Lehman, in I Looked at a Table Full of Games
There's a strong reward for having an only-partially functional game text.

Why is this?

Imagine two text, functional text, and semi-functional text. What's the process.

Functional text:
1) Pick up text. Read it. Get excited.
2) Find some friends. Get them excited.
3) Play. Have fun.
4) Play again.

Semi-functional text:
1) Pick up text. Read it. Get excited but confused.
2) Post to the forum of the designer. Have involved conversation about the game.
3) Find some friends. Get them excited.
4) Play. Be more confused.
5) More internet conversations with the designer.
6) Play again.
7) More internet conversations with the designer.
8) Play again. Have fun.
9) Write on the internet about how now you get it and man is it awesome. Maybe phrase it in a stuck up "difficult to learn games" way that looks down your nose at the people playing the first game.

Note that the reward, in terms of exposure, goes to the semi-coherent text.

I've noticed this with Bliss Stage. Despite serious, serious editing errors (like a discrepancy about Bliss from broken relationships, and a missing rule about starting age), the game text teaches you in exhaustive and slow detail about how to play the game. Thus: zero posting about it.

Fortunately for me, my target audience isn't y'all, so this isn't a big deal. But if you're designing not for profit, but for social cred on Story Games? Oh, man, a semi-coherent text is the way to go. And I'd give that advice to anyone.
\nI think Ben is spot-on here. I think this community or communities ends up rewarding games that are semi-functional more than games that are fully-functional. And wording is important, here: this community ends up doing that rewarding, not with it being anybody's intention to do so. Just like how nobody really wants to shoot the Steward, but they almost always do.

So the question is: how do we fix this reward cycle?

    \n So I think good critical reviews will help. We've been over that before.

    I also think that people who really talk passionately and positively about games they really enjoy is also good. Like, many of Judd's AP posts serve as an example here. Judd doesn't say a game is brilliant, (or doesn't just say is) -- he talks about what the game did in play, why he likes that, what it did, and often times what else he would like to do or do better.

    We don't have to chop games to reduce the cycle (in fact, that may be counter productive, for the reasons of Drama I shall speak of below). What we may want to do is be positive about games that we've actually had good experiences with and speak of them grounded in those experiences. Not to, say, assume that PTA is the game for everyone (it isn't), but to talk about what our PTA game was like and why we think that was, so that others can take out of that what they want to.

    At that point, one would hope, it may become possible to tell the games people are talking about because they really love them vs the games people are talking about because they are the RPG.net/Storygames darling of the month.

    I think that cutting the Drama is also useful.

    Because man, the thing that gets the most social reward on the interent is having some drama. Love a game, hate a game, tell everyone else how they should or should not play and BOOM. Drama.

    Tell everyone their favorite game is crap. Drama!

    Tell everyone the game they didn't like is brilliant and they just don't get it. Drama!

    Pose problems, even problems that are real but are subtle and multidimensional, as though they're simple and have clear answers that involve everyone else changing. Drama!

    And oh my god, will you ever get so much attention for that drama.

    \n As Ben also mentioned the "rush to publication" thing gets talked about over and over and over again. But the thing is, nothing ever quite gets done about it. It's a rare game that is not slow-cooked to perfection. The technology is now here to make printing a book almost an afterthought. That doesn't mean it should be an afterthought. It's like how cheap public transportation makes it easy to get to the other side of town -- you should still have a reason for going to the other side of town before you get on the bus.

    From my angle right now, I think the problem is exposure. We have crappy tools for gaining exposure when a game is in development (forum posts, playtests, sneak peeks at art), and we have quick and easy tools for gaining exposure when a game is in print (POD publishing, the Forge booth, GenCon). We aren't even capable of taking away the quick and easy tools, so the question becomes how do we make getting exposure in development easier?

    Here's one idea: some central repository for games-in-development. In my head, I'm thinking of a website where designers can upload their games for public review. Maybe have some ability to have discussion there, but that's not really the point. I'm not saying there should be a new destination site; I'm saying there should be a service that makes it quick and easy to make games-in-development available. At the outset, a simple FTP server is a first step, so that any thread anywhere (here, on Knife Fight, at RPG.net, in blogs, in emails) can link to the rules document. But we could go a step beyond that: it should be trivial (I say ignorantly) to set up an automatic process for any PDF that is uploaded to be split up into its individual pages, too (or make it a pull-based server-side whatsit, technical people weigh in!). Then we can not just link to the rules document, but we can link to the specific page of the rules document we're talking about.

    What I'd like to stay away from, though, is saying things like "We really need to all just pay more attention to games in development" sort of pie-in-the-sky stuff. It's not going to happen; these things have been said before. The fact of the matter is, paying attention to games in development is difficult, unrewarding, and often frustrating. If we want to change how much attention goes that way, we need to make it easy, rewarding, and fun to do so. To take the central repository as an example, it doesn't directly make people pay more attention -- but it makes it lots easier for people to do so.

    \n From where I'm at, this is ultimately a conversation about the zone of mediocrity.



    So let's combine that with Ben's First and Second Cases.

    I think Ben's first case is more vulnerable to falling into the zone of mediocrity than the second case is.

    Reasonably perfect, well-designed games that don't strongly stimulate the love/hate response are not going to cause much discussion. I don't think that the first case games produce excitement so much as they produce contentment: ah, that was a good meal. My tummy is full. Moving along.

    The second case is more likely to provoke a stronger reaction, and stronger reactions are more likely to put the game outside of the zone of mediocrity. Outside the zone of mediocrity, more conversation is due to happen.

    Here's another observation:

    I think the first case falls into the zone also because a well-designed game often does not promote a sense of ownership. A broken game -- the second case -- actually gives me a greater opportunity to feel like I can take ownership of it because there are problems to fix; I become more of an owner of the property by examining what's broken and suggesting potential fixes.

    Thus I conclude that if the first case wants to co-opt the out-of-zone mindshare that the second case gets, the first case needs to be better at promoting a sense of ownership. Give people fiddly bits to configure those games with. Don't give one good solution to the problems your well-designed game fixes; give three good solutions, and thereby build in a need for folks to discuss which solution they think works best for them, and why.

    Embed reasons for talking about the game, in the game. Which is not as simple as just building the best game you possibly can. Plenty of excellent stuff isn't worth talking about at all (and often it doesn't sell as well as it could, as a result).

    Ben's second case is currently winning the "something to talk about" game because something broken or incomplete is a very cheap, easy way to create something worth talking about. It's not a great, nor even necessarily a good, way to do it -- but it's easy, and you can (and often do) stumble into doing it without intending to.

    The first case stuff can only overcome this by being more worthy of conversation for good reasons. And that's harder work than producing something broken. That's just flat out not going to change, so I tend to view this as a burden on the producers of the first-case stuff.

    Producing something excellent is not enough. Producing something remarkable -- that is, something that's worth making a remark upon -- is what you're truly going for here.

    Seth Godin has written an entire book about this. I recommend everyone who's looking to get a game out there and noticed give it a read (I believe it's a follow-up to his Purple Cow book, but I haven't read that one yet, just this one.)

    \nYeah, though we can't take credit for it entirely. Fate's community was born out of the Fudge community, and Fudge has always had an ethic -- a mandate, even -- that its players customize the system to fit the needs for the game they're playing. So when Fate spun off from Fudge (inasmuch as it "spun off" at all), it took that ethic of -- wait for it -- player ownership of the game right along with it. What Fate did in the form we originally released, though, was what Fudge was missing: we didn't just say "here's a toolkit, build a game", we said "here's a bunch of pre-fabricated options that you can plug together to build yourself a Fate implementation of your own, rapidly". We didn't say "here's the one way to build this out", though, and I think that's important too.\n
    Posted By: Josh Bonobo RobyWhat do we do with games that are fun and complete, but not going into print?
    \nThat is really fucking hard, man. The big issue I see there is that print gives you a physical artifact, which connects through the primitive-brain's idea that what you can touch and physically handle and see is real and important, and purchase increases your emotional, gut sense of valuation for the game.

    So the challenge for free or at least not-in-print games is to figure out how to communicate on a gut, visceral, emotional level with the stuff that ISN'T an intellectual response to the game.

    Possibilities include:

    - Take advantage of the idea that we were an oral culture long before we were a print one, and make sure to give people ways to connect to the game that doesn't put it in competition with the mindshare of a print game: e.g., record and circulate short audio recordings that teach the game; work on getting some podcasters excited about those games (podcasters are alpha gamers, so this pays off in many ways)

    - Create tools and experiences for the game that transcend what print is even capable of: web-widgets and whatnot (see: abulafia) to enhance the experience of interacting with the non-print game.

    - Other stuff I'm not thinking of.

    Posted By: Josh Bonobo Roby
    Here's another part of the problem, from my perspective: the community does not give significant social rewards for games which are completed and published outside of print. Right now, there is little in the way of genuine alternatives to print publication. Walton and Shreyas are the flagbearers, here. What do we do with games that are fun and complete, but not going into print? How do we get them more attention, how do we get them more play (and thus more attention), and how do we incorporate them into the creative collaboration stew from which the next generation of games is derived?
    \nTalking about myself (I can't presume what could get OTHERS to play free games), these games should be:
    1) in a printable format (a good-looking pdf that would not bleed my ink cartridges dry) to read and look at
    2) look like a full game (with example, explanations, presentation, etc), not like a conflict resolution system.
    3) something that I would want to play if I saw it on a game shop shelf.
    4) not something to playtest to find out if it works

    I PREFERS to buy pdfs, so it's not the format the barrier: it's the way these games often look perennially half-finished, or they lack text that explain how to play, etc.

    Some real-life examples:

    - The Pool: I always wanted to try running this game, but I get the impression that to be able to run it well I would have to read years of forge threads with explanations and advices. (I played it at a convention as a player, though)
    - Bacchanal: it's a good-looking pdf: check. it look (and it's considered) like a complete game, not a work in progress: check. So I downloaded it. When I did read a thread of advices on running it at the Forge, we played it.

    It looks like there is a lot of bandwidth going to recognizing faults that have gone unrecognized, or spoken about quietly, which is great. I'd still like to look for means to change how the system works so these things don't get ignored, or ideally, these faults get baked into new games less frequently.

    So far, we have:
    1) a web service to host playtest documents to make them easy to reference and easy to disseminate.
    2) ground reviews in actual play
    3) "Only one thread about a given game on the frontpage at any given time."
    4) Guidelines for turning a PDF into a usable booklet
    5) Give your games means to provoke a sense of ownership in its players/playtesters
    6) Borrow solutions from online gaming on fora and chatrooms and whatnot
    7) Make sure your PDF-only game still looks good, not like an RTF document.
    8) Consider sending out physical copies for playtests to encourage sense of ownership.

    I'm sure there's more...
