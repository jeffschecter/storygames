Give it (the information) to me straight: knowledge checks.

\n
\n
\n
\n\nValyrianSteelKatana \n\n\n
\n
\n\nAugust 2017 \n in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n Almost all knowledge checks are handled terribly in every single game I've encountered. They routinely suffer from pitfalls deriving from information being needlessly "gated" behind a knowledge check.

1. Player characters that are supposed to be knowledgeable fail routine knowledge checks.
2. Player characters that are not supposed to be knowledgeable succeed on esoteric knowledge checks.
3. Player characters fail critical knowledge checks for a scenario, grinding the game to a halt.
4. Knowledge checks are so overused that "bookworm" characters are marginalized.
5. Knowledge checks that result in "bad information" are ludicrous, far worse than "you don't know anything." (Why is the character who specializes in being a loremaster and thus mostly likely to make knowledge tests also the most likely to give out bad information? Argh!)

Frustrating, to say the least. Problems that can be mitigated with judicious GMing, of course, but I'd much rather a system work with me than against me.

Moreover, I come across the issue of players requesting to make knowledge checks. "Can I roll History to learn about X?" This phrasing is rather poor because it muddies what the character is trying to accomplish. The question is more accurately phrased:

"X is something that I find interesting / important / potentially beneficial, and I would like to use my character's History skill to redirect the focus of the game onto X?"

Or something along those lines. I'm struggling to reconcile this narrative request with the more traditional elements of gaming. Players who have invested resources into making their characters knowledgeable should be provided with some sort of mechanical incentive for doing so, otherwise you run into issues with "dump stats" (if you're playing D&D).

The closest thing I've seen to making this functional is Fate Core with the Lore skill and discovering/creating an Aspect. Unfortunately, Fate has a number of flaws with the system that make it less enjoyable for my players and myself than D&D, and I positively loathe Aspect-fishing in this regard.

Mouseguard (and Torchbearer and Burning Wheel) all have elements of making knowledge useful via Wises, but that doesn't exactly translate well to D&D. (I'd be fine with a player taking Orc-wise and taking a +2 bonus vs. orcs, but that doesn't resolve my issues with knowledge skills like History, Arcana, Religion, etc.).

I'd be interested in hearing how people handle this conundrum.

    \n I'd say this is one place where rules come after the fictional facts: Is the character knowlegeable? if she is, how common is the subject? For our group* , if it makes sense that the character should know no roll is needed, you handle the information and that's it. The act of the player remembering and arguing that she's got a related skill, background, bond, class, species, etc is enough to trigger the GM response.

    Rolls are only called when remembering the information is difficult. Like, the character is about to be mauled to death or doing something else while trying to remember this crucial fact, or has to remember before a certain amount of time. If this element of challenge isn't present, the only other excuse is that the knowledge is incredibly rare even for the character, in which case fail means different things for a knowledgeable and a non-knowledgeable character.

    If a knowledgeable character fails the roll it means that she doesn't know but knows who does -a perfect excuse for a miniquest. If a non-knowledgeable character fails the roll then it's reasonable that she spouts the wrong information and the rest of the characters may roll against whatever the result was to see if they believe it (well, our group is okay with this rule even when it means they all believe the wrong thing, but that's just our group)

    3.5 is a bit more explicit about knowledge rolls Check where it suggests a different DC for different types of questions. 4e is even a bit more explicit about what you could get when you beat different DCs. Hope anything here helps, good luck!

    \n Yep, this is a problem. Two hacks can improve the situation a bit, though they don't solve it:

    1) Use "Say Yes or Roll the Dice" and "Let it Ride" hard, as DM.

    2) Make most knowledge checks be passive. In 5e, for example, even first level characters will routinely make DC 10 checks, and some can hit 15. Virtually all knowledge DCs will be in this range.     I'd at least use these to improve things. Something more substantive is necessary to really solve the problem. For example, I sometimes use On Mighty Thews' knowledge/magic check idea in my DIY DnD game (one stat is Knowledge, and my game is ability check heavy).

    \n For traditional sandbox gaming (by traditional I mean: rules set mostly simulates a fictional reality), I simply don't see the problem. When player asks if their character knows something:

    1. Does pretty much everyone know it? If yes, the character also knows it, or if the game system supports it and there are consequences to failing, ask for a ludicrously easy dice roll. (Ludicrously easy dice roll might DC 0 in most D&D contexts which use ability modifiers or DC around 5 in Eero-D&D.)

    2. If pretty much anyone could know it, but it is by no means obvious anyone happens to, then consider: how likely would it be for a median person to know the answer to the question? Then set a difficulty for the check which makes that probability work out roughly correctly.
    So if around half of median citizens should know it, the D&D DC would be 10 (11 to be exact, but 5 % here or there), Eero-D&D DC would be 20, or you would call for a D20 roll below the attribute value. In Burning wheel, you would ask for an obstacle 1 wise test.
    Or, if you want only 1/5 of median people to know the answer, then the D&D DC would be around 17 (or 27 for Eero-D&D) and the Burning wheel wise obstacle would be 1 or 2, but the next criteria might be more meaningful.

    3. If so few common people would know the answer that it is not a meaningful metric, then use a different calibration point; how many professionals would succeed, or how many experts would succeed, etc.
    So, if you want a professional to succeed with probability 1/4, the D&D 3 difficulty would be DC 20 (professional has a +5 bonus) and the Burning wheel obstacle might be 3 for a wise or academic skill.

    4. Alternatively, you can first figure out what kind of people would have a 1/2 chance of success, check what would be their skill level, and reverse-engineer the difficulty from that.
    If you want a master to have 1/2 chance of success, then in D&D 3 the difficulty might be a bit more than 20 (expert rolls at +10, but remember take 10). In Burning wheel, a master would have exponent 7 skill, so the obstacle would be 4.

    This all is just the typical way in which you handle skill check difficulty in traditional games, right? It requires some familiarity with the probabilities of the dice rolling scheme and a feel for what kind of ability and skill values are normal, expert or master level, but after that, it should mostly just work.

Maybe ask for a skill check which tells how much of what they just told is true. Not all players enjoy this, however, as it forces some to change perspective from playing their character to authoring the world.

If they still fail too often, then they don't know as much as they thought they did, the dice rolling mechanics are a poor fit for your understanding of expertise, or you are setting the probabilities wrong. I would be very interested in traditional roleplaying rules systems where this fails significantly. Maybe D&D 5 with the quite limited variability in proficiency bonus?

    2. On esoteric checks, calibrate the difficulty according to the success probability of experts. This should lead to a reasonable outcome, and if not, see above.

    \nThis already looks almost like a list of solutions.
    1. If a character is really supposed to know, then don't roll: they just know.
    2. If a character is really not supposed to know, then don't roll: they just don't know.
    3. If a piece of knowledge is crucial to move the story forward, then don't roll: maybe make the characters work for it, but eventually find a way to handle the info to them.
    4. Don't overuse knowledge checks.
    5. Give wrong information only if it makes sense: most often if I don't know something, I don't know it wrong: I don't know it at all. Or maybe I can guess, but then I know that I'm not sure. It's very difficult to be positively sure to know something, but it's wrong.

    An example: we find a strange ancient artefact, do I know when and where it was crafted?
    If I'm not an archaeologist/historian/scholar, then I have no clue at all: no roll.
    If I'm a "generic" archaeologist/etc., then I may attempt an educated guess: roll and possibly get it right, or wrong, or have an idea where to find more info. In any case, I know that it's just a guess.
    If I'm an archaeologist/etc. specialised in that period/culture and that's my bread and butter, then I know exactly where it comes from: no roll.

    \n One could almost have knowledge checks be for a character to declare something he believes is true, and the check itself determines if it is true.

    e.g., "I want to know if the fey are vulnerable to anything," is more of a statement that, "I want the fey to be vulnerable to something so that I can use it against them." The answer here being "cold iron" or what have you.

    PLAYER: "Are the fey vulnerable to anything?"
    GM: "Everything has a weakness. What do you think they're vulnerable to?"
    PLAYER: "I think they're vulnerable to cold iron."

    Then test your knowledge skill to see if this is true. Maybe they're vulnerable to cold iron, maybe these fey are different, and they're actually resistant to cold iron. But this rams headfirst into a potential issue: what if the fey really are vulnerable to cold iron in the GM's prep?

    1. Change the prep. (Undesirable.)
    2. Tell the player he doesn't know for certain. (Undesirable.)

    \n I do pretty much what Thanuir describes when it comes to D&D. Works for us. Because I try to be very proactive as a GM with interesting and useful trivia, it's fun to play characters who know a lot of stuff; the more you know, the more tools you have for resolving difficulties when something inevitably goes wrong.

    It should be noted that the D&D conception of knowledge is very cinematic. The purpose of knowledge checks is to either be dreadfully ignorant of some important trivia, or get to expose it smartly and benefit by the knowledge. A highly random check works for these purposes well, because we're not asking whether your character is competent in theology in general; we're asking whether they happen to be able to recognize this obscure scriptural quote at the drop of the hat. Maybe they can (being an expert in the field certainly does improve your odds), or maybe the nigh-illiterate moron happens to get it simply because it's from their favourite book in the Bible. The checks signify as much relationship between "skill" and "success" as hitting in combat does: sometimes you hit, sometimes you miss, and that happens to everybody.

    This cinematic conception of knowledge as an ability to dredge up relevant trivia is a bad fit for certain types of knowledge-encompassing challenges. There are various traditional schemes for making these things work, such as defaulting to averages and rolling longer series of checks to reduce the randomness. Depends on the precise game and its framework what one would do, precisely.

    One simple observation: there are only two common creative motives I know of for not telling the players everything interesting. Unless a given game is supposed to be challenging or immersive, there is never a reason to "gate" information. Of course a game may still wish to determine whether a character is ironically ignorant (that is, the players know something the characters don't), and knowledge checks can come in that way, but it's a pretty different thing when it's not the players who are kept ignorant.

    \n I'd say that there are ways to make the tool less of a balancing act and even into a reliable one, but they require specific definitions of the social contract that no design up to date actually knows how to deal with.

    From the examples you compare mountain-witching to, all there exudes all-out competition; while the mountain-witching trick itself requires a quite toned down inter-player and GM-player competition at worst and more collaboration at best. This stage can be reached only when both the GM and the players go out of their comfort zone and actually have a good time creating stuff together by building over each other input.

    By accepting other's input you make them feel validated, while full blocking others only ruins the fun, so after a while players get the idea and start respecting each other's input a bit more, thus turning the tool into a fully reliable one.

    But then again, it's a different form of fun, it requires someone taking the first step, enough trust to go with it and for everyone to go out of their comfort zone. Our group was able to give this step easier because we had enough bad experiences with competition-stance play that it made sense for us to try it. So if you really know enough your group and they certainly have tons of fun with competition or other forms of fun, then by all means, it is a tool and a situation to avoid. Otherwise, it's as good as anything.

    \n I agree with Thanuir's first post: read the fiction first and roll or not as it dictates, and there's no trouble. The trouble comes from using the knowledge check as a button the players can push (or the GM can require them to push). Using any mechanic in this fashion is risky if the group is hoping to play more fiction-first (e.g. the OSR style I know you like, VSKatana).

    As for letting the players invent the truth a successful roll reveals, that's stepping firmly outside that OSR idiom. If your players like exploring an imagined world that feels real and tangible to them, don't do this. If a concrete and persistent world is not a priority, then sure, rope in everyone's imagination!

    If the goal is to give players bang for their buck, define the scope of knowledge skills before the game begins. The GM should be up front about what sorts of knowledge skills, if any, they're prepared to support. Some GMs don't have much room between "everyone knows this for free" and "no one knows this" -- knowledge skills are useless in their games. Other GMs don't run adventures where knowledge really matters; checks can answer questions, but usually without conferring any advantage. Then there are lots of different types of GM orientations -- a GM running an epic metaplot may reward lore-style checks, while a GM running a puzzle dungeon may reward engineering-style, practical knowledge checks.

    If I were designing to support knowledge checks, I'd designate a few spheres of knowledge, then instruct the GM to pick which of those will be in play before character creation, then let players choose among those, then compel the GM to make them matter in play.

    \n I think that the D&D approach to Knowledge checks, as outlined in this thread, can be made to work. But it also makes me yawn a bit - it's limited, it's arguably not the best tool for the job in this case.

    The discussion here is all about "does the character know X?", and the tool is a binary random roll. I'm not sure that's the right approach. As a designer, I'd want to be think less about that, and more about the kind of conversation I want the players to have at the table. (Making sure that it fits the type of game you're trying to play, of course.)

    If you have access to it, I'd take a look at Vincent Baker's draft of Amazons; it has a bunch of "moves" or procedures which mirror knowledge checks to some extent, but do so in a nice way.

    I think that instead of "does the character know fact X?" you might consider exploring more interesting or nuanced questions like:

    * "Is the character's knowledge about X sufficient for this situation?"
    * "Are there meaningful gaps in the character's knowledge of X?"
    * "Is the character's knowledge of X practically relevant here, in this situation?"
    * "Does the character's knowledge of X simplify the situation or complicate it?"
    * "Is the character's knowledge of X dangerous, or helpful, or both?"
    * "Does the character know how to remedy the gaps in their knowledge - where to get the answers to their questions about X?" (e.g. what is the next step in solving this?)
    * "Does the character's knowledge of X come from a reliable source, or a biased one?"
    * "How difficult might the character's known facts be to verify?"

    Etc.

    At least, that's what I'd be doing if I were designing some "Knowledge check" rules for a game.

    (Somewhat notably, this approach can be used regardless of whether your interest in "Knowledge checks" includes player authorship of world details or not; they can work either way.)

    \n Great discussion here, and great to have the comments and input of Eero and Dave, who have both gamed in this style a LOT.

    I still feel that a binary "yes/no" random check is not the best way to handle character knowledge, for what it's worth, however. It's not the most interesting, nor the most fruitful procedure. It tends towards "you know everything" or "you know nothing", neither of which are always particularly useful for roleplaying, nor ideally suited to create player choice.

    (Also, unless I am mistaken, this thread is not purely about OSR-style "challenge the player" games, which means that broadening the discussion might be useful as well.)

    My proposal was to deal with more nuanced applications of "Knowledge" checks:\n
    Thanuir said:


    This approach reduces the role of player skill, but makes knowledge skills more reliably useful (or at least impactful).

    \nThat's a pretty interesting reaction. It seems to me that leaving "do you know everything or nothing?" to a single random die roll is the ultimate in "reducing the role of player skill".

    I'd be much more interested in an approach where Knowledge skills give you something to work with... but it's your ability to turn that to your advantage (as a player) which is being tested.

    For instance, some D&D-type games might have a challenge which looks like this:

    * There is a magical curse affecting a certain area, which the players wish to enter.

    Rolling once to see whether the characters know how to dispel the curse seems like the least possibly interesting way to handle the situation.

    Learning that the curse was created by a certain type of creature/sorcerer, cast in a particular "magical language", subject to the phases of the moon, described in incomplete form by the scholars of such-and-such a place, addressed in a book carried by the scribe, but by people unfamiliar with its entire effects, or likely partially negated by holy magic (but only by someone pure of heart) is much more interesting.

    Some people use Knowledge checks this way already (intuitively, or from experience), but I think encoding it into a ruleset might be really fruitful.

    For instance, imagine coming face-to-face with a dangerous enchantment or sorcery.

    * If you have the appropriate Knowledge, the GM will tell you the name of the sorcery and something about its nature.

    * Now, roll your skill/ability (or perform some other procedure), and the possible outcomes are things like:

    Great roll - You know what steps could be taken to dispel this sorcery or transform it.

    Excellent roll - You know where the information on how to dispel or transform this sorcery are to be found, and reliably.

    Typical roll - You know that it can be dispelled, but to do so, you must expose yourself to it.

    Or perhaps - You have information on the sorcery, but you doubt the veracity of the source or its completeness.

    On a poor roll - You have a vague sense of what it might be, and you know how to learn more details about what it is, but to do so, you must give it the means to act, which means spreading outward from its current location.

    On a terrible roll - You are aware of a few costly ways to protect someone from it, but not how reliable they are.

    That's a (sloppy) example of how you might build in a subsystem for dealing with "Knowledge" skills in a way which presents interesting chances to the players instead of simply throwing all the information in their lap.

    Aesthetically, in an OSR context, such information might be represented in Random Tables, with Knowledge checks giving access to different tables, perhaps (if you wanted to present it in a "familiar guise" to that particular tradition of game design/play culture). But those specifics don't matter all that much - that's just presentation.

    \n I implement the effect you describe, Paul, by detaching the information from its purpose and letting the players construct or reconstruct what they will. This is how it works:
    1) Have a situation in play, and an interesting setting (or lacking that, interesting imagination).
    2) Allow the players to ask questions and seek answers via various maneuvers, such as consulting allies, remembering useful information themselves, seeking in libraries or whatever finds their fancy.
    3) Establish success by whatever means. Often some dice rolls are involved. Get a sense for the depth and completeness of the knowledge gained.
    4) Answer the questions with interesting knowledge. More success gives you more facts or less well-known facts, but it's all interesting and pertinent to the question.
    5) Allow the players to apply this knowledge in useful ways.

    The distinction between steps 4 and 5 in there is crucial: the GM does not provide useful information, he merely develops and improvises an interesting setting. "Interesting" means thematically compelling and tactically actionable. Greater effort at information gathering gets you more of the "interesting" information, which may either improve your chances of figuring out something useful, or it may simply confuse the issue with multiple red herrings, depending on the situation.

    I find that enacting this teleological detachment makes research, exploration and knowledge aspects of D&D actually interesting, because that whole realm of play is now relegated into a sort of GM story hour that the players can tap at will, for idle entertainment or lethal curiousity, as occasion warrants. The GM does not distinguish between important or non-important information, because the information has not been construed as such in advance: only the way the players may decide to apply the information in play determines its value.

    This does, of course, mean that I technically speaking do not know if a given problem has an useful solution or not. For example, one of the adventurers in our campaign got afflicted with what they thought was vampirism last fall; I did not have a solution or a cure in mind, and when research was engaged in, I responded strictly with diegetic beliefs and folklore that the characters in the setting might know; I showcased the setting, letting the players piece together what they considered a sensible course of action. Because the illness in question never was actual vampirism, I never had reason to determine whether their cure would have been efficacious, but if that knowledge were needed, I would have determined it only after the fact, rather than having a ready-made solution in mind in advance.

    It's one of those weird brain disjunctions that I find useful to practice in D&D refereeing; helps maintain overall hygiene of the play process if the GM leaves plan-making and puzzle-piecing to the players.

Your level 6 knowledge of removing the vile worm curse also implies level 5 knowledge of removing other curses, level 4 knowledge about curses in general, level 3 knowledge about magic in general and level 2 knowledge about pretty much anything relevant to your culture. So, just define the top level knowledge your character has and you are good to go.

    Example knowledge skills: student of magic (4), vagrant (1), archmage of destruction with a focus on effects of fireballs on local fauna (6), child (0).

    ValyrianSteelKatana said:


    1. Player characters that are supposed to be knowledgeable fail routine knowledge checks.
    2. Player characters that are not supposed to be knowledgeable succeed on esoteric knowledge checks.
    3. Player characters fail critical knowledge checks for a scenario, grinding the game to a halt.
    4. Knowledge checks are so overused that "bookworm" characters are marginalized.
    5. Knowledge checks that result in "bad information" are ludicrous, far worse than "you don't know anything." (Why is the character who specializes in being a loremaster and thus mostly likely to make knowledge tests also the most likely to give out bad information? Argh!)

    \nPoints 1. and 2. together make me wonder if there's difficulty-setting or randomization problem. Point #1 doesn't strike me as too odd, I'm a smart cookie on lots of subjects and I'm constantly faced with gaps in my knowledge. d20+small modifier is enormously swingy, is that the issue? (Rolling 3d in Burning Wheel is never going to hit Ob 5 unless you dump a bunch of Artha.)

    I don't design scenarios with "must pass" knowledge checks. The "must pass" knowledge check is a bit like the "must win" fight. The approach I much prefer is when players propose that they might know something (maybe even something specific) as a way of trying to get leverage. Confront the players with a problem that you don't know how they're going to solve, and they can suggest they might know the bishop, or that they understand how sewers under the cathedral were recently maintained, or how the church gets its laundry done.

    I suppose the difference between a fight and a knowledge check is the latter is a single, atomic action. You can win a fight but come out badly beaten up; there's not really much equivalent with knowledge checks. GUMSHOE makes each knowledge area a resource; you spend points to be smart about architecture in a way that will plausibly help you.

    A similar approach is to externalize players' 'knowledge resources'. The bookworms have books which have lore points. (This works in fantasy settings, less well when you're an modern, well-educated expert.)

    I think it's useful to have a palette of 'near miss' options. You have a vague idea, but you're aware there are gaps in your knowledge. You learned Thabian's Rule for this situation, but it always struck you as a little bit simplistic. You don't know, but you know who does. You know (or you knew) but your knowledge is out of date - something has moved, or changed.

    One pattern that I'm now a little suspicious of is the knowledge check as a way of filling in exposition. In small quantities it's fine, but the wished-for moment where players have an in-character realization about background information seems unsuited to role-playing. The most extreme form of this is when players are supposed to have a realization about an inconsistency between two separate pieces of exposition. ("Wait, this can't be a real Sveylan temple, the engravings are all wrong!")
