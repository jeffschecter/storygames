\n At the moment this is just notes and junk. But I hope to push out a prototype in a few days.\n
You're a teenager. You have to go to school, do your homework, make friends, keep friends, and survive the slings and arrows of bullies of all kinds. Then, one day, you get bit by some radioactive bug and suddenly you're climbing walls and dodging spitballs.

Or maybe you're a gynoid, a super fighting robot made to look like a girl. Defending the planet from disaster, but longing for a normal life with friends, school, and work. Then you get that chance and it's so much more complicated than your Saved by the Bell video tapes led you to believe!

Or maybe you just suffered a trauma and picked up a bow and arrow to maybe get your life under control again. You use your uncanny talents to keep innocents safe, because you never want anybody to go through what you did.

You're a teenager, but you're a superhero too. You use your great power to fulfill your great responsibility -- whatever that means to you. You do your homework, fight assholes in power armor, and defeat power hungry engineers with death rays. Though that's not going to be easy with cliques, money struggles, and straining friendships all around.
\nSo, teenage superheroes in the way of The Spectacular Spider-Man animated series, but with a dash of Runaways darkness and even some Powerpuff Girls and My Life as a Teenage Robot strangeness.

At this point the game looks to be primarily a mashup of Prime Time Adventures, Spookybeans, and a couple bits from Apocalypse World. I'm mostly just trying to make the superhero game that I would want to play.

The game revolves around framing scenes, rolling for narration rights when taking risks, and gathering points to spend on defining details in the "cliffhanger" end-of-act and end-of-episode scenes. Each session is meant to be like an episode in a dramatic cartoon serial, like a Greg Weisman series.

    \n It's kind of a response to my experiences with Monsterhearts and Smallville. It was originally a Monsterhearts hack, but after the afternoon that I spent scribbling things out it ended up drifting a lot.

    What I have so far is pretty rough, but here's a snapshot of what a typical session looks like.

    The GM comes to the table with a villainous plot that ties into their season-long arc. This is basically a bite-sized Danger in AW speak. They push this when they get narration rights and set up intermissions. The players come with their PCs and ideas for where they want to take them as well as characters in the world that they want to see.

    Play is divided between acts, scenes, and intermissions. There are four acts in an episode and each PC gets one scene per act. Intermissions are details inserted between scenes, and help connect things together. The main currency of the game is tokens, and those can be spent to insert details into scenes or intermissions as they're happening, or when you aren't the focus character (in this case the token goes to the focus character, otherwise it goes into the ether).

    When it's your turn to be the center of the scene, you say what the scene is about and who it includes. You establish the set dressing and all that. Then, when your character does something risky, the GM and the scene's focus player roll dice. The GM spends dice from their pool (I'm not sure how they get dice into the pool yet) and the player picks up a die for each character trait that they want to leverage in the action. Odd numbers are successes, evens are duds. Whoever has the most successes gets to narrate the outcome.

    When you win narration rights, you get full control over the outcome of your action. When you bring certain things into your narration (a super-villain crashes the scene, your powers put you in a spot, real life puts you in a spot, etc) you gain a token.

    In between scenes, the GM may interrupt with an intermission and frame a mini-scene around an NPC. Other players can spend tokens to interrupt with details and reveals and insert characters. If anybody inserts something to do with a thing on your character sheet, you gain a token too. Every episode begins on an intermission and every act ends on one, too. Tokens gained on the very last intermission roll over to the next episode, but not any gained before.

    I'm not sure it's going to be quite HBO by default. They veer to much into shock value and tits for my tastes. This is more directly related to the tone of certain comics. Like, "Brian K Vaughn's X-Men Evolution" or "Mark Waid's Static Shock" (or even just the original Static run).

    Here's how I'm imagining XP and advancement working out...

    The game will work on a pretty traditional structure. PTA style scene setup flows into a standard Apocalypse World "say what you do, the GM reacts, moves trigger and snowball" sort of deal.

    When you roll a 6 or less, gain 1 XP. When somebody spends a string on you and you accept, gain 1 XP. When a move tells you to, gain XP.

    The MC has the hard moves, give them a new condition & advance a condition. The Defy Danger & Shut Someone Down moves also

    When you gain a new condition, write it on your sheet. Each condition has 6 boxes and an escape clause. The GM will tell you what you need to doto get rid of it if you ask on a successful Peer Into the Cosmic Storm roll. When the GM chooses to advance a condition, you check off a box and it gets worse. Mark XP at each condition's third box. If you reach the sixth box you get 1 XP, and whatever the condition is leading to happens.

    On... let's say 6 XP, you erase it all and take an advance. The advances for The Mask (Peter Parker/Spider-Man, Kamala Khan/Ms. Marvel, Kurt Wagner/Nightcrawler) are...\n
    • Gain +1 Savvy... and somebody really likes you, and wants you to know it
    • Gain +1 Cool... and you get positive attention from another clique
    • Gain +1 Bang... and a villain is involved with somebody close to you
    • Gain +1 Cosmic... and you get an opportunity to pursue your passion, with a catch
    • Gain another Mask move... and you gain an obvious physical change. Choose another option from your physical changes look options
    • Gain another Mask move... and you team up with an older hero
    • Gain another Mask move... and one of your classmates becomes wrapped up in a villain's plans
    • Gain another Mask move... and you meet somebody from the future
    • Gain a move from another playbook... and you gain a secretive ally
    • Gain a move from another playbook... and somebody has declared themselves your arch nemesis

    That's alright. I like talking about this stuff. Though some of this may be a bit stream of consciousness.

    My main frame of reference for Peter's narrative is the Raimi movies, the Weismann cartoon, and some bits of the Ultimate comics.

    Okay so, the thing about Peter Parker is that he's bitten by the super-spider, and then he exploits it. Does the wrestling thing (we see how that turns out), meets girls (hel-looo, Black Cat!), and basically just dives into the superhero thing, complete with a Spider-Signal on his belt. In the Ultimate continuity, his reaction to first climbing a wall and sticking to his ceiling is a whispered, "Cool!"

    Now, he has to keep this a secret. Otherwise, well, he gets dissected or something. But he's not pressured to. It's completely his choice, because he feels like this is the responsible thing to do or whatever.

    Seems legit.

    And, sure, it complicates his life. But overall, his story is about Peter growing up and becoming a man. He's all about consequences and coming to terms with responsibility.\n

    Compare to Miles

    \nMiles is bitten by the super-spider and he's... well, reluctant would be putting it lightly. He's terrified. He hides it away, buries it deep, and just tries to live with it. He doesn't even consider the superhero thing until Peter is dead already, and even then he needs coaxing. He gets into a prestigious prep school, by lottery, and his first thought is, "What about the kids who didn't get in?"

    The first thing he says when he climbs up his bedroom wall and sticks to his ceiling is, "Oh no."

    If Spider-Man, the story, is a puberty metaphor -- a stand-in tale for the changes that your body, mind, and roles go through during your teenage years -- then what boy grows facial hair and dreads it? Somebody who isn't a boy at all.

    And then, when he does begin to embrace it, people are telling Miles what they think he is at every turn. SHIELD wants to keep him under supervision, Prowler is turning him into his enforcer, his father is unintentionally saying that he wouldn't love Miles if he had super powers, thus shoving him deeper into the closet -- this conversation in particular sealed it. So many trans kids have had this conversation before.

    If we treat his costumed identity as his transgender identity, and his secret identity as his closet then this seems like exactly what Brian Micheal Bendis is signalling so strongly in this scene, and that especially fits in with the careful flipping around of the Spider-Man formula in Miles's initial arcs.

    Miles's story is all about identity, figuring out who he is and developing into somebody who can live his live with integrity. "What is the responsible thing?" is a part of that, but figuring out exactly who he needs to be, who he needs to be responsible to (including himself), is the main thrust.

    His own self-conception -- this idea of who Miles Morales is, who Spider-Man is, and where they meet -- is constantly changing. He's constantly transforming into a different person, entirely fluid to the effects of the world around him but doing so in a way that, by the end, keeps his essential "Miles-ness," that kid who asked "What about the kids who didn't get in?"

    And he goes through hell for it, sure. But for all the pain, he's all the more Miles in the end.

    \n My experience in Monsterhearts was that all the ingredients were there for coming of age content, but such content took a back seat to discovering and showing off each character's status quo and response to the crisis of the moment. Transformative, maybe, but formative, no. I think this is the product of shared creativity in general, multi-protagonist casts in particular, and a certain thread of story-focused RPG design on top of that.

    Like, when I'm making my character in Sorcerer, first I figure out roughly who they are and what they can do, and then I figure out their present quandary, a quandary which must (as per the rules) hinge on other people. I'm probably going to invent a quandary that is going to allow my character to put who-they-are-now to the test, and the other people involved are probably going to be antagonists or otherwise active agents who keep the struggle external. "I have just acquired sorcerous powers and bound my first demon and I'm not sure who I am now," is not really supported in the way that "I use demonic powers for X and am in conflict with people/person Y" is. The crises of play are supposed to make you (as your character) choose, and in so doing, reveal things about yourself -- maybe who you are now, maybe who you've been all along, whatever. So identity exploration is there, but it's the "maybe" and the "whatever" that keep it from being a focus of player attention, especially re: changing identity.

    I think this is a very logical way to approach drama, and Apocalypse World and Monsterhearts proceed similarly. We have our characters and they are cool and we are eager to show and explore their coolness. Then the GM comes up with some people who are trying to kill them, take their stuff, or otherwise threaten what they value. Go! By the time we really know who your character is now, we've probably spent a lot of time forging and changing relationships between the PCs, rolling dice, killing stuff, planning what to do next, etc. Probably not a lot of quiet moments where one PC gets to fill up some thought bubbles with their internal issues.

    If I were going to use a current system for an exploration of identity-formation, I'd probably use Primetime Adventures, which puts an "issue" front and center as the core reason to care about the character. Whether they're good at ninjitsu or mind-control or whatever is pretty clearly secondary, and the GM's adversity is supposed to be tailored specifically to their "issue" (something Sorcerer also does, and in that respect I like it more than MH). A PtA game where every "issue" was "coming to grips with powers" might be a blast, or perhaps it might be necessary to specify further to properly inspire GMs -- "coming to grips with not being normal" or "coming to grips with being able to kill with impunity" or whatever quandary a given character's powers present.

    Maybe that's still not enough, though. Maybe we need some "before" time, getting to know who the characters were before any powers entered the picture. This sort of prologue play is something I often lobby for in various games and it's a tough sell! Few gamers I know have enough spare gaming time to conquer the "get to the point" urge.

    Brainstorm: what I'd do is pitch a game to players by saying "powers will happen to you!" And, just like anything else that happens to you, you don't get to choose them. So, when you're investing that time and thought and emotion in creating a character, you're creating a person who has to be interesting for reasons other than their powers and their escaped arch enemy. The char-gen guidance would be about making someone who already has things they care about and goals they're invested in (maybe as a PtA-style "issue") before any powers enter the picture. The first few hours (sessions?) would be about these people and these issues and pursuits. And then -- BAM! The GM gives this PC wings, and this PC laser eyes, and this PC invisibility, and... what now, y'all?

    My inspirational reference would certainly not be Avengers, probably not even X-Men, but rather the movie Chronicle, about 3 friends who stumble on a superpower source and proceed to fuck up their lives with their newfound abilities.

    Of course, if the characters in the game want to play Avengers, then cool, we can have capes and heroism and other supers trappings, that's up to them -- but we're still playing for the purpose of exploring their "issues", and the GM is pushing on those, whether its with funny-costumed villains or with overdue loans and family illness.

    I realize I've wandered off the "as metaphor" part of growing-up heroes and taken it in a pretty explicit direction, but that's where I was inspired to go by your description of Miles Morales (whose story I have not (yet!) read).

    My "hack" was really just a custom character creation move, to establish some focus on a teenager's life and problems (rather than going to the supernatural and inter-player conflicts as the focus of play from the very first step):\n
    High School is Complicated, Dude

    Once you've chosen your Skin, stats, moves, and so on, take a look at this list of problems:

    * My academics are really suffering: I'm in danger of flunking some important classes, or worse.
    * My family, my home, or my life outside school is a dysfunctional mess, unstable, hurtful, jarring.
    * I'm unpopular, shunned, forced out of the circle or group I desperately wish I could be a part of.
    * My romantic life is a disaster, or non-existent. Something just happened which has left me lonely and heartbroken.
    * I'm in some serious trouble with the authorities: either the school administration, the local police, or both.

    First, choose two of these and cross them out. These are definitely not problems for you right now. Very much the opposite, in fact.

    Second, choose one more and write this beside it:

    "Yeah, maybe that's kind of true. But it doesn't bother me too much; I don't really care right now."

    This is something that could be a problem for you later, but not just yet.

    You can make it a less serious problem, too, if you want.

    The remaining two, though, those are trouble. They occupy your mind, they're constantly staring you in the face. Tell us how and why.

    At least one other player character is involved in each of your problems. For each problem, choose another character and then pick one:

    * They're helping you fix the problem
    * You look up to them as your model for how you wish your life would go
    * They're the source of the problem

    Of course, use your backstories and Strings to inform which of these three you choose.

    \n I'm sorry to bring up Worm again, but it has kind of a neat take on this. Superpowers manifest when a person goes through some kind of trauma (although for some, it's remarkably minor trauma, like being cast out of a popular group at school), and the powers tend to be related in some way to what happened. People who are hurt physically often develop physical/dangerous powers, for example.

    However, it's possible (but rare) to go through a second traumatic moment and develop further powers (which, rather, tend to change or diversify the powers you have, not in a particularly well-understand way, because it's so rare*).

    It would be very interesting to have a mechanic like this in place - as the character achieves certain goals or suffers certain defeats, their powers change (maybe not for the better, depending on how optimistic or tragic you want this to be - perhaps powers that are more destructive tend to get stronger with time, whereas constructive, benign powers like healing tend to get weaker, encouraging certain excesses in their use over time). This can be built into a mechanic which allows feedback from the player to the GM/group and back as the game develops.

    Changing playbooks (Skins) in Monsterhearts accomplishes this to some degree, but I think it could be done much better than that.

    I also really *like* the idea of authoring a difficult situation for a very human character and then being given an unknown power or set of powers (outside of my control).

    On one hand, there's the curiosity/fun of discovering what the powers are (you might not know without experimenting - are you brave enough to injure yourself to see if you are invulnerable or if you have healing powers?). On the other hand, there is the interesting fun of having to deal with a difficult situation (e.g. your marriage is falling apart) with an entirely unsuitable super power (the ability to spit acid and poisons).

    Both sound pretty cool to me.


    *: In Worm, superpowers are somewhat well-understood, largely due to social media and internet speculation. Kids study superheroes in high school, and are made to write essays on relevant issues (or even particularly famous or infamous superheroes) for sociology class or speculative papers for physics class and so forth.

    \n The playtest didn't go great. We started late, setting generation took longer than expected (though we ended up doing a single triangle matrix between us), and two of the three PCs need some major reworking. This is probably one of those games that needs playbooks.

    I am working on two different games right now that work on sort of cyclical narratives. "Freaks!" works on (short form) television seasons where the big conflict changes each season and World of Dreamside works on long form hero's journey stuff, like Chrono Trigger and Cucumber Quest.

    So, one mechanic that I'm playing with to help push this in each case is milestones from Marvel Heroic Roleplaying. Each PC gets 2 per season, and they go like this...

    [It describes a conflict of identity.]
    Gain 1XP when you [do a thing to establish an issue].
    Gain 5XP when you [take some generalized setback].
    Gain 10XP and discard this milestone when you [make a choice between two identities].

    I'm still working out what XP is spent on, but it might include temporary bonus dice, changing traits, and other stuff like that.
