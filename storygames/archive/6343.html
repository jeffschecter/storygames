    \n I admit I find the answers so far on this helpful but not conclusive however that is likely just the way I read them.

    The main point is that there is no "persuade someone to change their mind". That's conversation, that's talking, that's "pure roleplaying", there's not dice on that, also as a Player you have final say on what your PC's mind thinks about something (though you are heavily motivated to compromise as shown in your actions etc. when negotiated).

    There is "force someone". As long as your fiction/your table agrees "yup, that's force" then the mechanism used is fine. You force them to give it over by winning the argument in court (assuming they didn't then decide to keep it and become an outlaw), you force them to stop pursuing the princess's hand by tripping them, mocking them and making them look a fool (assuming they didn't want to be the fool pursuing princess), you use such an abusive, harsh, horrible argument or such a seductive, sexy, make-yourself-wanton move (Sahu Sheen in the text) that you don't care they agree because they're crying, torn up, disgraced or you don't care they give in to seduction against their "better self, social mores etc" and will hate themselves the next day, and your table agrees these are forms of force then you can dice for them.

    Note, however that the dice tell you who wins. It's still for players (including GM) to decide what happens. Most "force" does actually leave a choice if sometimes an unattractive one. I point a gun at your head and say give me your money, you have a choice. You don't, I still have a choice too. This is where negotiation etc. comes in. I win my seductive dice as the lusty spirit, the PC can still say "I take damage, I don't negotiate what you want happening in the fiction", and narration proceeds to incorporate that (the seducer as winner describes what the exhaustion is like, and perhaps the lusty spirit goes on to another conflict and wins again, ultimately just how important is it to the player not to give in to the seduction, important enough to be knocked out of chapter, to die? Cool, we have learned something about that character).

    PS - re above, I agree that lies and deception can be seen as force. They deny true choice, don't care about what the subject really wants or thinks.
