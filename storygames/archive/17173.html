\n (By diceless I mean "fortune-less". Using cards or something doesn't count.)

In particular, I'm interested in how diceless games deal with two things:

1) The injection of surprising things
2) Adjudicating uncertain situations

I'm interested both in what solutions you know of, and also what you see as the strengths and weaknesses of those solutions. I don't think I've ever played a diceless game, and I've only read a few, so suggested reading is also good.

    \n To my knowledge there are three ways diceless games handle challenges.

    1. Simple stats, You're either better than the other guy or you're not.
    2. Resource distribution, you have a certain amount of points you can spend on doing things. Sometimes certain behaviors can get you more points during the game.
    3. Many choices with limited changes, I'm only aware of SPF doing this though.

    \n The diceless (resource-based) game I have the most experience with is Golden Sky Stories (a.k.a Yuyake Koyake), a Japanese RPG I translated. In GSS PCs have connections to and from each other and NPCs, and at the start of each scene your connections grant you points of Wonder and Feelings. You spend Wonder to use special powers and Feelings for attribute checks. (And during play everyone gives Dreams, kind of like PTA Fan Mail, to reward good roleplaying, which you then use between scenes to improve connections.)

    The thing I've found is that this resource-based approach creates a really different dynamic, which works well in GSS because it's a heartwarming game about magical animals helping people with everyday problems. The game has rules for opposed checks (basically a bidding war with Feelings), but I've never once used them in play. Instead as the GM I'll say things like, "You need an Animal result of 6 to climb that tree," and the player will sometimes choose to fail in order to adorably fall down or something.

    To me the big issue with resource-based mechanics is that they change the basic dynamic from "Do you think you'll be lucky enough to succeed?" to "How much do you want to spend to succeed?" or "How much do you think you need to spend to succeed?" When players are using resources on things where they want to succeed, they'll naturally spend them according to how much they prioritize success, and usually hoard them otherwise. In GSS the overarching goal is more to build friendships and such, so success and failure at any given action are a step removed from that.

    \n Puppetland.

    You bid what you can and cannot do into the scene. Transgressive actions lose you resources. You can never regain resources. Once all 16 are lost, your character is not yours any longer.

    It winds up being more like a game of chess. Admittedly, the focus is not on the "competition" inherent in picking "cool powers" to beat other puppets. So there's not "win" condition, if that's what you're asking?

    It's difficult to see Puppetland as a competitive game, and more of an exhausting impromptu performance, where the rules are known in advance.

    \n Nobilis, my favorite diceless system, is pretty straightforward.

    Johnzo's got the right of it for question 1): your surprises come from the other players' (or GM's) choices. When I run the game I specifically try to set up situations where I can't predict how the PCs will react, and seeing what they do is part of my fun as the GM.

    For 2), there's pretty clear rules on what it takes to accomplish any given action, if your relevant attribute isn't high enough, you need to spend miracle points to do it. Once you're familiar with the system it's almost effortless to play. That said, you can occasionally have a hammer-nail type problem: "Well, I've got Domain 4 (fire), so I'm going to solve all my problems by creating Fire!" Also, some players are really reluctant to spend miracle points, because "what if I need them later?" and end up short-changing their character's potential, and other players burn through them almost instantly and end up feeling sad and underpowered until they earn a refresh.

    \n For the most part these games are "resource-management" as @Onix stated above me. You may have attributes of a specific value and then a pool of points to distribute for an increase. You then compare your attribute + points allocated (0+).

    In Nobilis which is the diceless game that I know best if you're performing a "miracle" (action) you state the level of the miracle. If the level is greater than the character's attribute level you add in miracle points to make up the difference. If the value is greater than the opponents attribute+miracle points then your action succeeds to which you and the GM negotiate how it affected the narrative.

    \n Peter, I think you'd like Polaris a lot, actually. We should play!

    I think most diceless systems tend assign authority in specific ways, in order to adjudicate uncertain outcomes. Sometimes (like in Amber), your fixed stats pretty much tell you who's going to win any given confrontation, but you are encouraged and allowed to create fictional circumstances that give you an advantage over someone with a higher stat, with the GM as the gatekeeper of whether it's "enough" or not.

    In Polaris, each player has a role with authority over certain things (the Heart controls their character, the Mistaken controls Demons), and then the ritual phrases are more fine-grained "switches" for authority over the final outcome (making bargains, essentially).

    For me, the specificity of who has authority over what, and under which circumstances, is pretty important for fortune-less play.

    2) Adjudicating uncertain situations
    \nLets look at what dice do in uncertain situations. The rules system identifies some situations as uncertain, defines a range of possible solutions and the fall of the dice provides a chose one of those solutions to the game.

    But in reality any situation in uncertain in games. Noting is certain. If we wanted any situation to end with carrots, it can. The game has no certainty in it own, only the certainty we as players want to give it. What dice really does is to when we think it applies, present a limited range of conclusion to a limited set of situations. The dice might dicided what happens if you decide to attack someone, but every moment of the game is uncertain, since you could have chosen to do something else during any moment of the game.

    So most uncertain situations in any games are always handled without a dice. You just decide what happens.

    Sometimce a dice based rule system helps define what it possible. Or it seems that way. Deciding and coming up with a range of possible and interesting solutions to situations is always a player skill, not something that comes from the dice. The system or the dice can suggest a solution in an abstract way, but incorporating that abstract thing into the game is always something that the players do. Dice are mute, they give you numerical result. Not story. The story comes from interpreting the numbers.

    So what you do with dice to do is often is to represent that a situation is uncertain and create an emotion in the player (not the character) of that the situation in uncertain, and without their control. And without any one else control. You can archive the same sort of emotion, by creating a game environment where everyone can put something into the story, but no one is in complete control of it.

    \n If you're talking about unpleasant uncertainties, players are human beings and we do have a kind of darkness inside. If you tell even the sweetest little girl "it's your turn to think up something nasty that happens to me because of what I just did" do you really think she's not going to come up with something, if not outwardly horrific, at least disturbingly cruel?

    As for adjudication, resource allocation is often key. You kind of have to think of it as an economy, a story you "buy" successes with one bit at a time.

    Do you WANT to make something happen in the story? Spend a resource (or undergo a minor character change.)
    Universalis does this, too, but the other systems here are more advanced and a bit more focused on the emotional impact of changes (which give a lot of depth to the story.)

    If you want to up the excitement, you can do an Amber-style bidding war for task resolution, but I've found that games where you simply "buy" a better outcome have plenty of opportunities for set back, disaster and reversals.

    \nRibbon Drive is a diceless game. It's also an example of structured freeform, in my opinion.

    The only randomization is that prior to play, you randomly determine the order to play the mix CDs that everyone brought. Scene framing and scene tone are influenced by the music playing at the time, though only insomuch as the players choose to keen their ears and listen.

    The game isn't about determining whether the characters succeed, but instead about whether the characters change their minds (about their future). The point of conflict is internal and self-driven.

    Surprises and unexpected twists occur all the time - partially triggered by the music playing, partially because games always do this. See Jason's comment upthread: "There is nothing more surprising than six people making stuff up together."

    \n In Silver & White, the situation starts out being totally effed up. You're a bunch of kids wandering the neighborhood and discover your photography teacher dead and an impossible machine and strange photographs in his basement. And GO! The game starts you there and just follows where you go. Plus, there's a lot of implicit structure and themes in the descriptive text you read out-loud to the group before play begins. That helps built the vibe and create a shared sense of what the game's about, one of which is weird adolescent tensions. There are also rules for narrative control of certain elements that keep things strange and interesting and prevent you from ever feeling like you know what's going on.

    In Metrofinal, the world is ending and everything is growing more and more surreal as the distinctions between things breaks down. You are a powerful agent of fate, but you are constrained by the structure of the board, turn-taking rules, and the fact that other players often determine the results of your actions. Plus, only certain characters are able to cause things to progress at certain locations, which is a roadblock and forces characters to do things they may not normally have considered.

    Honestly, most of the more successful diceless and GM-less games I know do that thing you're talking about by creating reasons for the players to not be on the same page about something, and then empowering them to explore those differences through play. These can be different objectives, different interpretations of the same thing, different characters, etc. Juxtapose incongruities!

    P.S. The best games with dice do the same thing. Dice don't create problems by themselves, they do it through how they are interpretted into the fiction by players. Diceless games do the same thing, just without dice.

    \n I have done rules for a game specifically to be done by email, or forum posts among 2 or 3 players. Because of the nature of this format, I didn't want to use the traditional randomizers, and instead wanted to create randomizers that were player controlled.

    So, the core of the game revolves around offers of ideas for the next player in the form of Chapter Titles. What happens is this: after you write a chapter of what your character is up to, you offer 5 Chapter Titles for the next player, who must select one, and who then must write his chapter using the Chapter Title concept wrapped into his chapter some way.

    There is also an additional reward system for players who balance their chapters between having good and bad outcomes for their characters, since there is no ability to have "refereeing by the other players" to keep a player from just writing his character to get what he wants all the time. So, the system allows that to happen, if the player wishes, but is not rewarded for doing so.

    \n To build on what Jason's saying: it depends.

    If you're writing a 2-player game and both the players are supposed to be "on the same side" (like it's a buddy-cop game or something), then the structures and mechanics of the game (including dice, but they're not necessarily required) will have to do some of the heavy lifting to complicate and resist the collaboration of the players. Alternately, if the players take more antagonistic or less collaborative roles (say, in Murderous Ghosts), then the system doesn't have to step in to play that role as much.

    It's a conversation, right? And the rules are there to mediate the conversation. So what you need the rules to do depends on what things the players are handling on their own and what things the mechanics have to step in and facilitate.

    \n I like Polaris' resolution. That game have realized that the other players can create as much randomness as dice.

    There is a karma resolution based system about intriguing vampires that have been started to spread at Swedish conventions. A game that I'm really fond of nowadays. It's called Svart av kval, vit av l\xf6gn (Black of Despair, White of Lie). The only bad thing is that the game just exist by word of mouth. The players are playing one vampire each (it's kinda GM less) and they all got one goal each that want to fulfill during the game. During a scene, any player may give a beast command to another player. The Beast in a vampire is always trying to show the violent side of the vampire or try to humiliate the human side. The commands can be "Tear that person apart", "Run through the wall", "Show your physical strength", "Take him as yours", "Crawl in the dust", "Be her slave", "Betray your brother" etc. The player who doesn't obey the beast takes a token. You may have as many tokens are you like. If the player have a token and obeys the beast, a token is removed.

    Anyway, when you do something to someone else, the list of who succeeds is as follows:

    The Beast
    Vampire hunters or human mobs
    Elderly vampire
    Younger vampire
    Human
    The Beast rejected

    So a younger vampire will always loose against an older vampire. You may always release The Beast to win the challenge, but you have to overcome the problem in a violent way. That's the only way you may win over older vampires and vampire hunters. If you got a token while releasing the beast, any player - including yourself - may take the token, which means that the beast don't want to help you.

    In the end, this nifty system creates a pattern where younger vampires must follow a much bloodier path to get what they want ... and they if they want to follow it, it's a question of what they will do to reach their goal.

    I really never seen the point with karma resolution, but I adore this system.

    To my knowledge there are three ways diceless games handle challenges.

    \n All this discussion about diceless has inspired me to create an example of a diceless combat contest mechanic for a sandbox dungeon-crawl.
    The purpose would be to invoke more dread than bad dice rolls can.

    Example, PC versus monster combat: Compare stats chosen for the contest and secretly bid points from their Deadly pools. Secretly choose whether attacking or defending, then reveal both secrets. Higher attacker scores a wound. Maybe include rules for bonus points from environment use, equipment or combat skills.

    Players can discern whether a monster's stat is higher or lower, but not the exact value until after the creature's first attack. Even then, they still don't know the size of the monster's Deadly Pool until they've encountered it enough times to learn. But it should match the perceived dangerous appearance of the beast and/or the perceived danger of the area it's discovered in. The GM shouldn't be a dick and have the same monster in two different encounters possess different stats or Deadly Pools (maybe a small random variance is fine -- or variance is a stat itself. Some monsters are known to vary wildly from creature to creature. Astute Monster Scholars can learn which ones and maybe know the tell-tale signs that give away its physiological capabilities).
