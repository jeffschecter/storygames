How to critique

\n
\n
\n
\n\nJoshua A.C. Newman \n\n\n
\n
\n\nFebruary 2008 \n edited February 2008 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n

OK, listen, people. Critique is something you learn how to do. It's got System and everything. I learned how to do this studying art in college and you might have too. It's a powerful tool, but if you misuse it, it makes you an asshole, whichever side of the crit you're on. So here's how to maximize the helpful and minimize the ass.

\n
    \n
  1. There is exactly one reason to give criticism: you are helping the creator make their thing better.
    2. \n
  2. No one cares if you like it. That's not what this is about.
    3. \n
  3. Seriously! "I like it" or "I don't like it" is hollow grandstanding and self-importance. Cut it out.
    4. \n
  4. Tell the creator the effect something has on you. "A conflict took 20 minutes." "I don't understand what the difference is between these resources." Stuff like that. Don't tell the creator what to do. Ever. You can suggest solutions if you've thought about them, but be completely prepared to offer them and let them fall.
    5. \n
\n

Conversely, the creator has to follow these rules:

\n
    \n
  1. Listen to every piece of criticism. Even the most ignorant, stupid asshole might give you a good idea if he says enough stupid shit.
    2. \n
  2. Don't do what people tell you to do. Do what you need to do to have the effect on them you want.
    3. \n
  3. Don't stay in love with your ideas if they're stupid. If everyone's telling you that something isn't working, it isn't working. Maybe you need to change how it works, maybe you need to change what you want, or maybe you need to scrap that idea altogether.
    4. \n
  4. Never, ever defend a decision. If someone asks you why you did something, tell them. But you're not here to defend your work, nor are you here to explain everything to your critics. If they don't understand, then either they don't need to understand or you need to make it clearer in your work. Explaining it standing in front of your work getting angry isn't going to make the work better.
    5. \n
  5. Thank people. They're a resource for you to improve.
    6. \n
\n

If the critic is coming from a position other than helping the creator, it damages the interaction. If the creator isn't willing to listen, it sinks it altogether. So make sure, when you're giving criticism, that the creator wants critique. And when you're the creator, make sure that when you're asking for critique, it's what you're asking for.

\n

And if you're publishing, you're asking for it. So make sure that you've asked among people you trust first, wringing all the critique you can out of them first. Because the public is filled with the stupid assholes who test your rule 1.

    \n Right on, Joshua.

    In the old days, we had a thriving culture of highly useful criticism. It was Actual Play. People posted about their sessions and described what worked and what didn't. Designers would post about their play and ask for advice on how to fix or deal with a problem they encountered. After a game was published, players would post about their play and places where the rules seemed problematic or didn't support the group.

    It led to discussions based on concrete examples and real situations. It was grounded in respect because it was always about playing the game and learning to make that play better and more fun.

    I think it's the best way to have a public, frank discussion of a game, warts and all, and also make that discussion useful to someone.

    I've done critique as performance. It sucks, and it is *so hard* to avoid. I think that we all have the urge to be sparkling, funny and witty. These impulses did not add value to my critiques, ever.

    I swore never to ever confuse critique and performance after I attended a large (70+ writers) paying workshop with a bitch-diva NYC editor. She would crack jokes, sing little songs, and torment her customers with long ambling digressions before crushing them with a casual six-word dismissal. If she got a chuckle or a laugh -- which she did often, because the room wanted to love her and impress her -- she fucking preened. Her critiques were so furiously self-serving that I suspect they resulted in actual genital damage. It was sickening. It was not critique; it was critoradectomy.

    I should note that I don't think anyone here is an asshole of that magnitude. I'm just relating a formative experience.

I love reading designer and playtest notes. But I think that John's creator-first-last-and-only attitude is less risky. And ultimately, it should be left to the creator to decide how much of the public they want to engage with their work at the various stages of its completion.
