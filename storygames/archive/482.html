The Soul-Sucking Seduction of d20.

Kuma
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games
So, my brother ran was at an auction and ran across a lot (as in 'an (collective) item for auction' - not 'a bunch of') of gaming stuff. He bought it for a song and got a crapload of Battletech/Mech Clix stuff, which is why he bid on it. There was also a shitload of D&D stuff - including rather recent material.

He doesn't play d20, so he sent it to me.

And so for about the last week, I've been paging through book after book of D&D material - Forgotten Realms stuff, various sourcebooks. At first I was appreciative of the windfall (I mean, about $600 worth of free gaming books - who wouldn't be!), but I wasn't sure that I'd ever crack them open.

And slowly, ever so slowly, I've been getting hooked.

I mean, I have a lot of very transformative ideas - integrating board games and RPGs, open gaming frameworks, creating a lingua franca of game design. But I mean - what about just making a d20 sourcebook about ... I dunno ... umber hulks? What about bringing all of this Story Games stuff back to d20 and changing the discussion there? What about Levi's Perfect20, or maybe a step further?

Or maybe just wallowing in the sheer d20ness of it all?

Am I losing my soul, or am I finding Nirvana? It's so hard to tell.

    First of all, I've had problems with the entire concept of d20 to begin with, from multiple angles. It's basically a risk-soak for WotC, but at the same time it refrains from being a truly open/copyleft architecture. I saw and worked with a lot of people who got burned badly in the d20 bubble - plus, I believe that d20 has only reinforced the very VERY fucked up three-tier AAA-only distro system.

    But that's neither here nor there.

    I loves me some D&D - I always wind up going back to it, no matter how far afield I go with other ideas. But if you're going to stand up a game and say 'this is mainstream gaming', d20 is it. And there are a lot of things that are wrong (IMHO) with that model - at its core, its consumerist, its emotionally unchallenging ...

    So that makes the case that maybe a lot of what goes on here at the fringe needs to come back to the mass of gamerhood that makes up almost 80% of the market. That you can change the game from the inside out. But is that what I need to spend my time doing?

    It's not guilt, really - its just a question of priority and, to some extent, artistic integrity.

    D&D is a strategy and tactical game that rewards knowing trivia of the setting and the mechanics of the system. That's what it does, and it does it with great greatness.

    The main function of the system, near as I can tell (and I'd like more insight into this from people who like it and have played it more than I), is to 1) give you an opportunity to make strategic decisions, 2) put you in a tactical situation that gains or loses you resources for the next strategic decision point. Failure means starting from scratch, success means getting more resources and doing it again.

    If that's what you want, it's the tool for the job. I totally understand that some people like it, and I think I understand why they like it. I get the strategic and tactical thrill from other venues that I find more satisfying, and I find the trivia stuff weak compared to material that we can create in the course of play.
  • JDCorley
    April 2006
    D20 is one of those systems that gets out of the way for everything except combat, although Spycraft's chase system (and later, other additional systems for things like investigations and searches) shows that it's certainly extensible to make cool mini-games in a lot of different areas.

    I think there's an element of gambling to the bog-standard D20 combat minigame, too. In other words, the complexity is such that you just don't know if you can do it or not and you might or you might not, you don't know until you roll the dice. The challenge rating system is an attempt to be sure that there's a range of potential challenges that you can count on seeing and do what you can to increase your odds of success. Gambling is often overlooked as a component of traditional play, I think. There's a reason that millions and millions of people go and gamble despite losing a lot. Gambling is FUN. And while there are some tactics and strategy involved in gambling challenges, it's a very different sort than a chess game. That's why very strategic and tactically minded people can get frustrated with D20 mini-games, the element of gambling.

    But it also is a very open-ended framework - and I think that there's a lot of room for expansion and reconfiguration - particularly in the story-game direction. At its core, D&D is just Stat + Skill (+Feat), with a lot of derived statistics (saving throws, BAB), within a level structure. The value judgements about D&D are mainly baggage beyond that.

    Surely there's a whole universe of room for working within that structure, and hooking into a crapload of Stuff (the accessory books) that you don't have to re-invent the wheel. I mean, the core books that I have almost reach my knee - that's a masive pool of resources to tap for raw material. The idea is to tap it in an active Situationy way instead of on the 1" square map.

    You nailed it, Kuma, that's the great challenge of the d20 glut - nailing down the fenceposts. People who whine "it's so tedious leafing through 100 books to find the right feat!" are missing the obvious answer - don't leaf through 100 books. Get what you need and make it yours, all yours. Even if you have a group of books that form a "line", there's never any need to incorporate everything. The same thing that is true for the old World of Darkness books is true of the d20 mountain of material - it's all where you say "stop, that's all I need for right now".
