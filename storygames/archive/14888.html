\n So I'm in a group that has these parameters:

4-6 people, attendance generally up in the air until people show up
2 hours of play time, one night, weekly

These aren't going to change. This makes a lot of games really difficult to enjoy and I want to brainstorm ways of managing them given our constraints.

Given our situation, a few general questions:

1. What games are a perfect fit? (We already play a lot of Fiasco, which was essentially custom made for this group)
2. How can we adapt ourselves or the games to enjoy Apocalypse World, TechNoir, Dresden Files or your favorite game?

If you have a group with similarly awkward or weird parameters, feel free to lay out your situation and ask for ideas here as well.

    \n Mist-Robed Gate, Fiasco, Geiger Counter, and Microscope were all designed specifically for one-shot short-session GM-less pick-up games with larger numbers of players and are, off the top of my head, about the only games that I can think of like that. Maybe Montsegur 1244 too. Microscope also supports multi-session play better than the other two. I expect we'll keep seeing more games like these.

    In my experience, two hours is fine for Apocalypse World if it's a continuing session, though it doesn't give you much time to play after the initial setup, if you're just starting out. It'll stretch out the pacing of the campaign, but that's cool. If you have more than 4 players, I might split into two sub-groups, though, or try to get someone to help co-MC the thing. At five and six players, AW (or any game really) slows down a lot.

    If you already have characters, you can do Mouse Guard in two hours too, especially if you hew closely to the explicit guidelines about having a few challenges/fights and then shifting immediately to the player turn. Historically, I've been more drift-y with it and run it more open, but it's stated way to play is dense and fast. Having more characters in Mouse Guard is slightly easier because the way the fight rules group PC actions together.

    \n Mouse Guard - The low to no-prep aspect was why our group started with this game in the first place. Basically, we had last minute cancellations or no shows so the GM (of our Deadlands game) wouldn't run anything and we'd play a board game instead. Finally, I said that we would always have Mouse Guard as a fall back and it has since taken over.

    Advantages - Codified Gm/Player turn helps things move along. Being patrol/mission based means that you can quickly come up with a self contained scenario to engage the players that showed up. Beliefs/Instincts/Goals create meaningful play for the characters.

    You can also lift a lot of these hard-wired things and use them in other games (I'm pretty much sold on the mission for short sessions)

    Disadvantages - It really helps if everyone makes characters together.

    \n I like the idea of very short Basic D&D (or other stripped down dungeon crawl game) dungeons. Start right at the entrance with a brief descriptor of how and why the PCs got there. Then, Go! Two hours to finish it.

    Rinse. Repeat.

    Set up a forum for the population of players to do in-town purchasing, learn about new dungeons, etc. A sort of PbP / real life game hybrid.

    \n Apocalypse World -
    You might be able to do this if players are comfortable with skipping ahead in time greatly between sessions.

    If the Brainer, the Hardholder and the Battlebabe are present... You start with, "So, there's this bad dude named Rosco, and you've kidnapped him. He stole like a truckload worth of hash oil, and that's how your hold makes barter every week. We open in a dingy interrogation room."

    If the Brainer, Driver and Gunlugger are present... You start with, "So, the brainer's done some psychic location mojo to pinpoint the rebel base. Driver, you've gotta get everyone to that place while dodging sentry fire. Gunlugger, you need to get Carlita's head."

    In short: look at who you've got. Quickly figure out what kind of situation would be awesome for precisely those characters. Frame aggressively to arrive at said situation. Introduce new NPCs and circumstances and threats as needed. Progress the "metaplot" storyline only as much as these immediate situations allow. Allow a single situation & its fallout to comprise the whole session. "This is the session where we blow up the bridge, and keep Ricky's men out of town."

    \n Maybe I'm missing something, but it strikes me that any game that is played as discrete, episodic sessions with an assumed ensemble will work. The 'issue' you face can be thought of as having two failure points: serialized plots and inconsistent cast. You can't fix the latter, so work on the former. Just about any game can be paced to episodic play (though some don't 'shine' as much that way). Supers, in particular, but 'crawl of the week' (as mentioned above) also works. Fight the urge to play epics and focus on mission-style play.

    I also suggest trying to make it three hours, if possible. Two strikes me as too little to warm up, get into rythem, and have even one solid peak moment.

    On more practical matters, cut systems ruthlessly, say yes, play the hub not the spokes, etc. Try simultaneous actions, and trust players to be legit (I'm thinking of D&D, now, and similar turn-based resolution games), only going back to turns as a conclusion nears (e.g., the killing blow).

    Also, if you end up stuck in the middle, and next session someone doesn't show, just 'ghost' them: run them on autopilot. Works best if the players all provide 'scripts' of a sort for when they aren't there (like a long list of BW Instincts).

    I won't bother naming a slew of games that have short play times; you know them.

    \n I had a lot of success with Burning Empires and playing through one maneuver. It usually took us about 2.5 hours, but that was also online.

    Really any game with scene framing could be done like this since you can control how much is going to happen.

    If you want more traditional combat elements in the game something like DnD Encounters is a good approach actually. Just plan for 1-2 story hooks and 1 encounter.
