\n Hey, folks. My wife and I have been kicking around the idea of a Dune-based game and while both of us like the idea I could use a little help from the hive mind.

Basically my wife will take the role of a Fremen woman, circa 10,156 (35 years before Dune), the daughter of a naib. The Fremen culture is pretty much male-dominated, but I hope that by placing her one step removed from authority we can have a decent excuse for her to engage in activities that might normally be restricted to men and thereby open up the story possibilities a bit.

The question is: what to do?

The classic situation, and the one I'll open with, is Harkonnen mercs straying into the sietch's territory and an escalating conflict between the Baron's forces and the fedaykin. This will be good for some high action and doesn't require much to start: the Harkonnens show up and trouble ensues. But what then? When the lasguns and crysknives have been put away, what kind of stuff would you do?

    What does she need that only the Harkonnens have? (Other than their water. Maybe a family member held hostage? Maybe even her dad, while she rules the seitch as regent? What if the dude's gone a long time and the power of the seitch is with her and the Sayyadina? How does that effect the 'male dominated' society?)

    \n

    What the the Harkonnens need that she has? (Other than for her to die as a painful example. Maybe she knows of secret spice sands that the sandcrawlers can't get to. Or maybe Feyd wants her as a concubine because she would be a good hostage against her seitch? Maybe she, herself is a prophetic Sayyadina and she knows things about the future of Arakkis that he might be interested in, particularly if it might give him a hand up against his uncle and brother.)

    \n

    The most important thing to me is that you fuck the canon in the ear. Use the parts that are fun and evocative, then make it your own. Introduce House Bettrio, who would be the third most powerful of houses, if the Atreides and Harkonnen weren't tying each other up. Bring in Avrin, the cousin to Feyd and Rabban, who's been slowly poisoning Rabban into stupidity. And so forth.

    \n Also, even without the off-worlders: the Fremen weren't a unified army. They're a bunch of people, just as prone to squabbling, backbiting, forbidden lusts and occasional errors in judgment as any other bunch. So.....who in their band resents her position? who has she rejected in the past? who has been stealing food? who has petitioned for her hand in marriage three times already and now her father is weakening? While you're at it, throw some random sandstorms and a lost child into it. And where did this little metal plate come from, with the writing that nobody can read? Further afield, the towns and villages of Arrakis hold many distant cousins -- cousins who have decided to move back out to the desert to be with their 'real people'. The band thinks that these new people are idiots and the band is right -- but is she going to watch the villagers die in front of her? The Fremen were also seeded early on by the Missionaria Protectivat (the prophecies that allow visiting Bene Gesserit to control them), but many of the prophecies mutated -- and some turned out to be right, anyway. Throw in some visions, a mad prophet, and a contradictory religious text...

    ...y'know, I think I'm going to go run this one.

    \n Joshua, Lachlan, those are all good ideas and I'm going to steal from your suggestions shamelessly. It's true that I've kept canon in mind while deciding what can or cannot be done in terms of the overarching story, and maybe I ought to toss it out the same way I plan to in a Star Wars game I'm going to run later this year.

    As an example of what's been holding me back: I feel constrained by the Fremen concept of Ichwan Bedwine, the brotherhood that all Fremen share and use as a way of guaranteeing peace between sietches. I don't want to ignore it because if I start ignoring Fremen traditions entirely then it kind of stops being a game about Fremen, but I can't take it as an ironclad rule because it kills conflict, which in turn kills story.

    \nIchwan Bedwine is a colonial-era invention of an oppressed people. It is only invoked when it has utility - when unity of the Fremen peoples is deemed desirable. The very concept is only defined in relation to outsiders, non-Fremen. It would be extremely na\xefve to think that the Fremen would live in peace among themselves without an external threat holding them together.

    That is to say, I can't see an abstract ideological concept like that getting in the way of the realpolitic. And should a character in the story disagree, well, that's conflict, too. Every society has its idealists, they're the spice of the political debate. Just compare to calls of Christian unity throughout the centuries, for example.
