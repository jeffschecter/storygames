    \n Just finished reading the first Harry Dresden book yesterday so allow me to reccomend the potion recipe used: you need one thing for each of the senses. That limits the amount of ingredients, though it doesn't limit too much how many things a sandwich can do.

    -Edit-
    This thread gave me another idea for a system I'm working on, I'm wondering if it could make playing the wizard a bit more interesting. The system is card based, so the wizard will have a deck with spell components that includes:

    -elements, each one in a separate card, like water, air, fire, etc.
    -spell ranges, one card for ray, another for area. By default the spell will be only personal or touch. Up to three cards could be used to boost the spell range (three rays for more distance, a mix for a spell that has an area effect once it reaches its target)
    -components that modify the permanence of the spell (otherwise the effect is instantaneous) like summon creature (lasts until the creature is defeated or the end of the turn) potion/rune (spells can be prepared with time and unleashed in the right time), etc.
    -effects (being the default move or damage) like inmobilize, charm, poison, etc.

    the rest of the spell variables can be determined by the caster level, like the amount of cards that can be used in a single turn (so a wizar could take more turns to prepare a powerful/complicate spell) , the top limit of the spell before it goes out of control (so no, you can't prepare a nuke on first level even if you take a lifetime to prepare it), and so.

    I've read about modular magic systems and RPGs with cards... how many times has this been tried before and why isn't being used more?

    \n In my magic system, the players construct spells by stringing syllables together and shouting them out with a gesture to invoke their effects. When this happens, the other players all immediately try to duplicate the act. The power level of the spell is determined by how many of the other players get it right -- the more that do, the weaker it is. But the players who are able to mimic it, mark experience and get to tell the GM what they think the spell was supposed to do based on the caster's performance
