Explicitly game-worthy films. Not necessarily good or important films, but films that either illuminate common gaming tropes or suggest whole untapped genres of tabletop play. Also: links and rationale. For example:

Atanarjuat: Ganakagok is the Atanarjuat RPG, basically. This is my favorite film ever. Ever ever? Ever ever ever. Two and a half hours of bloody, beautiful Inuit legend, complete with witches and demons, revenge killings, and the best chase scene in the history of Inuktitut cinema. Screaming with authenticity.

Idi I Smotri (Come and See): For anyone playing at war. Do you think war movies (or war games) necessarily glorify war? This film will erase that notion. Alternately fascinating and stomach-turning. There's an intensity of rage here that is almost impossible to understand.

Tremors: A clever and silly monster movie that plays out as if written by gamers. Maybe it was.

    BLADE 2, the best of the Blade series and IMO one of the best Action movies ever.

    Together they form a tight combo for high-action high-tension games:
    xXx tells me as a GM not to worry too much about form and "saying no". If the player wants to have their character talk with the Big Bad Guy out of the blue, or infiltrate the super-secure base with little prep, I won't cock-block them with insurmountable obstacles, but rather look for cool opportunities. Namely inspiring is the scene in xXx where Vin Diesel's character (who remembers the name? I've seen it like six times, and I sure as heck don't!) gets the "lowdown" on the bad guy in the corner from the police agent; and while the police agent drones on about his background and connections, Vin Diesel just walks over to the guy and starts talking to him, hanging out, introducing himself, etc. It fits the theme of the movie well, but more than that it was eye-opening for what can happen when the players do "rash" (but not intentionally disruptive) stuff, and cool things that can happen when they're not blocked.

    And in Blade 2, it showed me how to run awesome chase and fight scenes. I have very little desire to play in a game where the fight scenes don't go down like Blade 2. Unless it's like Historical or something. If my guy can do "Kung Fu", then you better believe he's gonna go all Wesley Snipes all over some mook ass.

    I call chop-socky movies, but I'll stick to ones easily available in English, which influenced the development of martial arts games.

    The Karate Kid (1984) - Introduced America to martial arts films. The Rocky of karate.

    Big Trouble in Little China (1986) - Every roleplaying game involving Asian-y elements and pseudo-mysticism and kung fu is indebted to this John Carpenter picture. An early attempt to make an American film the Hong Kong way. Feng Shui is the Big Trouble roleplaying game.

    Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles (1990) - Yes. Hugely influential on the folks current roleplaying. This Hollywood movie just capped of a landmark run by the more successful cartoon.

    Under Siege (1992) - Lordy, Steven Seagal. Pretty important and beloved by thousands.

    Rumble in the Bronx (1995) - Jackie Chan has much better movies, but this was the first one to get a major release in US theaters and make him a household name.

    Fist of Legend (1994) - The best Jet Li movie until Fearless (which is a prequel to it). Introduced the West to Jet (when it was released several years later) and also master fight choreographer Yuen Wuping, who would work on...

    The Matrix (1999) - Come on. The Wachowski Brothers are the ultimate gamer dorks. Martial arts films were only beloved by middle schoolers, uber-nerds, and martial arts jocks before this brought them into the mainstream.

    Crouching Tiger Hidden Dragon (2000) and Hero (2002) introduced wuxia to the West and are still the only wuxia movies most gamers have ever seen. These TWO FILMS define a entire genre for many, many people. That's pretty crazy.

    Volcano High (2001) should be required viewing for anyone doing a "we look really cool and dramatic all the time" martial arts game. The movie is composed of 90% Cool. There's a version out that was remixed by the RZA and dubbed by a bunch of hip-hop stars for MTV, but flip the disc over and watch the original first. Interestingly, the recent prep-school witchboys movie The Covenant, which I found surprisingly entertaining, shows serious Volcano influence.

    Ong Bak (2003) - The return of movies in the early Jackie Chan "stunts all the time!" style, but minus the comedy and plus Thai kickboxing. Tony Jaa has fairly successfully marketed himself as the next big martial arts star.

    Kung Fu Hustle (2004) - Stephen Chow has been making movies for years and years, but the success of Shaolin Soccer (mostly in non-US countries that actually care about "football") enabled him to pitch a big budget movie targeted at international audiences (much like Ang Lee and Zhang Yimou before him) and the result is this flick.

    This list totally ignores the samurai-ninja genre, because I know much less about that.

    For crime dramas, there are a handful of movies I always turn to:

    Heat: Watch the unfolding brutality that drags everyone down into damnation. Brutal and explosive view of crime in LA and the destruction that it brings to everyone that touches it. Also, possibly the best realistic running gun battle in movie history.

    LA Confidential: While it isn't the novel, this book of corruption and sex in 30's LA is a near perfect example of how to weave stories of loyalty, power, ambition, and the law.

    The French Connection: Though it hasn't aged as gracefully as it might have, the ugly cop and pretty villian make for a classic combination in a film that gets the down n gritty in a way that only 70s movies can.

    And joining the list, despite some reservations on my part:

    The Departed: Who lives, who dies, and who deserves it? What does deserving it even mean? When you're looking down the barrel of a gun does it matter if you're a cop or if you're a criminal?

    Finally, for the genere of "normal man meets bad ass criminal" I highly suggest watching the following two moveis on the same night (or in close order):

    Collateral: A look at what makes a man and the role of violence and self-direction in that definition. Plus it has Tom Cruise not sucking in a movie.

    Man on a Train: What happens when an aging bank robber wanting out of crime and an aging literature professor wanting out of mundanity meet? Can they get off the rails before their choices reduce them to meaninglesness?


    And I'll also cheat and add the whole series of The Shield and the first 5 or so season of Law and Order -- even though they're TV and not movies.

    In keeping with my pet theory that collaborative storytelling rewards broad brush-strokes over nuance, I'd say the "old Hollywood" genre mixing seen in Hong Kong movies is worth paying attention to. Your horror movie switches to slapstick comedy, and then involves a police caper? That's entertainment!

    Vampire Effect - great Hong Kong vampire flick. These are not the traditional "hopping vampires" -- these are World of Darkness type vampires. Who kick ass at martial arts. Also, lots of wry "Buffy"-style humor. (Boys/vampires sure are a lot of trouble. But they're kinda cute.)

    Shaun of the Dead is a fine example of how to make mundane issues like friendship and relationships lively and palatable by couching them in a fantasy setup.

    It's beyond me why Westerns and police dramas aren't huger in gaming. I mean, what games did you play as a kid, frequently cited in so many "What is role-playing" texts? Cops & Robbers, and (the very un-PC) Cowboys & Indians, of course.

    The gun choreography in The Way of the Gun is pretty slick, with everyone paying attention to where the guns are being pointed as though they're, you know, actually lethal weapons that will quickly end your life, so maybe you better not be a hero. (The realism kinda breaks down near the end, though.) It's an aspect of firearms that's never really modelled in RPG rules, excepting possibly Dogs in a way.

    Anything with Steve McQueen in it can be mined as a source of stuff for manly cool protagonists to be doing.

    Chronicles of Riddick is the best D&D adventure evar. Zombie space nazis? Fuck yeah! I have no idea why a glossy D20 game based on this did not hit the shelves, it seems like a pretty obvious license.

    I do loathe Blade 2 with passion, though. CGI garbage.
