    \n 1.) I've played with 2, 3, 4, and 5. For me, the two-player game feels really personal. There's space to learn what the player sitting opposite you really cares about and wants in a community. Playing with three or four feels similar to me: moments of discovery and surprise, factions slowly emerging, truces hard-won, a map full of weird ideas. Playing with five feels unfocused to me, with too much downtime. That's why I list the game as 2-4 players: because I find 5-6 boring at the table.

    2.) I think the second question you ask here is the important one: is it even necessary to worry about this? If it didn't hamper your experience, then don't worry about it. If it felt like a flat note and a detraction, then the easiest way is to pause the game and say, "Hey, I notice you're only starting projects. That's an okay thing to do, but remember that this isn't a puzzle to solve, it's a community to explore" Or pause and re-read a section of the rules that seems relevant (like the part on domesticating the situation).

    3.) Usually I see 8-12 named characters by the end, and 3-4 tend to have developed personalities and represent larger trends/factions in the community. Your game sounds like it definitely sat on the low-end of characterization, but that's also totally fine. Did you find yourself wishing for more humanization/personalization? Did issues that the community faced lack traction? Named characters primarily serve to make the community's issues seem more real.

    \n 1.
    It plays great with three.

    I've played with six, and followed Mcdaldno's advice in a previous thread: taught them all the rules at the start, and then split into two groups of three players. At the end of summer, we swapped a player between the groups and had them take a faction of their community with them over to the other group.

    2.
    I think it's possible and reasonable to 'play to win' with the Quiet Year - to try and get the best outcome for your community. I've certainly had a community hit 'win' - in that I've felt they've done as much as possible to prepare for the coming of winter and the Frost Shepherds as they could. Starting projects to end scarcities seems like a reasonable response.

    I wouldn't want to play like that all the time - I think I prefer exploring a different facet of how communities work in each game I play.

    Contempt isn't about them, it's about you. The trigger for Contempt is whenever you feel something -- namely, alienated from or disrespected by the actions and decisions of the community -- not when you feel something about someone else. Obviously, the actions of the community (and therefore your fellow players) are going to trigger most of those feelings, but you aren't taking Contempt "against" someone, or even against a specific action. It's not called Dissent, after all.
