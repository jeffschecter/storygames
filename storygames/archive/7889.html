\n As the topic says: What skills are important to being a good game master? There might be differences in lumping or dividing them, but gimme your opinions. Things like:

* Pacing
* Engaging the players
* Reading the players
* Reincorporation

Is it possible to divide GM skill into these kinds of subdivisions? What I'm looking for is things to look for in trying to improve one's GM ability. Things to look for, and ask myself "How good am I at X?", and then maybe devising techniques to improve my X.

It's a very general question, I guess, and I'm not sure where I'm going with this, so there are few wrong answers here.

    \n Here's the statement of ethics from the International Association of Facilitators, which is business-oriented but you can translate into GM-like skills:

    # Teach them to fish: helping people "do it themselves"
    # When the true leader leads the people say, "We did it ourselves"
    # Facilitators are substantially neutral to content discussions
    # Facilitators are outcome oriented
    # Facilitators Plan before Doing
    # Facilitators use proper models and methods
    # Facilitators work to understand factors driving groups and individuals
    # Facilitators promote participation by all
    # Facilitators encourage collaborative decision-making when appropriate
    # Facilitators capture the results
    # Facilitators manage the climate and space of the event
    # Facilitators manage the resistance
    # Facilitators provide and request timely and effective feedback
    # Facilitators seek to understand and manage themselves

    \n Here are some things that I try to do:\n
      \n
    • Incorporating character flags
    • Using dilemmas rather than unilemmas (you can either do this or die...)
    • Guiding the story along a narrative structure. Not railroading, since I only improvise as a GM, but I'm pretty familiar with narrative structure from studying creative writing. I'll try to create choices that will move us toward the next plot point, even if I don't know what it is.
    • Encouraging player authorship

    \n + Crowd control!

    Seriously, being able to control a crowd's dynamics: let the quiet people speak up, be forceful enough to get people back on track when they are distracted. I used to be really shy, and GMing helped me learn to take control of social situations and be a "leader".

    + A good speaking voice (proper support, if you know what that means, enunciation, clarity)

    + Being able to communicate and generate excitement and enthusiasm. You know, smile, get people excited about stuff, portray it in a positive light.

    Also, and I don't know what to call this, but being able to keep your own thoughts organized. You have to know what to keep, what ideas to throw out, and how to make it all come across in a clear way, while keeping track of it yourself. A good example is making sure you have some simple mannerisms, speech patterns, props, or whatever else that allow the players to tell your NPCs apart.

    \n Oh, one more:

    I'm sure everyone is thinking "scene framing". But I'll add to that:

    Having a good sense of timing for *when* to frame and end scenes. Dramatic timing, you know? Like, when do you cut from this storyline to this other one? When does such and such character enter the scene?

    For instance, cutting a scene after a particular phrase, one-liner, or revelation lends weight to that moment. It resonates in our minds as the last thing that happened. I'm adding this as advice to my latest game project:

    If a player something cool or memorable, consider cutting the scene right there. It'll make that player look even cooler.

    \n All excellent suggestions. I'm thinking, is it possible to lump all of this into a more manageable number of piles and put stickers on them? Here's an example of how to do it.

    Creativity: Making shit up that is both relevant and interesting, in prep but more importantly in improvisation.
    Structure: Storytelling structure and dramatic timing.
    Delivery: Voice, acting out, description, putting on a show.
    Communication: Making sure everyone is on the same page. Both listening to and speaking to the players, and facilitating communication all around.
    Enthusiasm: Building energy, making the players invest in the fiction and delivering on that investment. Making everyone care.

    Can what's been said already fit into these categories? Can you think of important skills that do not? Do you have better categories? Is the whole category thing a stupid idea?

    My thought with the categories is to help me classify different techniques. For example, I can classify Simon C's idea on building culturally rich NPCs that we saw recently (too lazy to link it) as a Creativity technique, and Paul T's "selling failures" technique as an Enthusiasm technique. Many things would be in several categories, but that's okay.
