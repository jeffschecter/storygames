Misery Bubblegum: What's the GM's polestar?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nTonyLB \n\n\n
\n
\n\nJuly 2008 \n edited July 2008 in Game Design Help
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n In doing solo playtesting (i.e. I play all roles and work through the rules) I've come upon a big question for Misery Bubblegum, my sad-sweet shoujo game. It goes basically like this: Incidental plot elements like a chase scene, or a rivalry, or a quest, are added during play by the players playing cards in a way that will let them reward the GM for developing those story elements. That's all well and good, but when you remove the need to start out the game with a map of those incidentals, what's left?
\n
\nPlayers can also build up little plot elements about themselves ... setting up (for instance) a hang-up about self-doubt that other folks can recognize and work to, BUT they build that on top of a foundation of who their character is, and how they feel: That basic, unchanging foundation provides a pole-star for them to orient around, which makes it much easier for them to bring out the other plot elements in a coherent framework. Right now, the GM is totally lacking that, and to be honest I don't even know quite where to start on providing it.
\n
\nFor those who want (hugely) more information, the whole solo playtest is available here, but I don't think it's required reading.

    \n Jason: What I'm actually crying about is that the game isn't hitting on all cylinders, because it is not easy enough for the GM to contribute all of the stuff that he's needed for.
    \n
    \nLike with jazz, it doesn't really matter to the drummer what the saxophonist plays: The drummer's fine with responding to pretty much anything. However, having a saxophonist walk up to the front of the stage, look out at the audience, cough, and stand there not playing ... that's not cool. It means that the band doesn't get to fire on all cylinders.
    \n
    \nLikewise, Misery Bubblegum as it currently stands creates nice character-driven stories, but they're not layered with enough of the external urgency and shocks that the GM should provide, simply because that part of the GMs job is so much of an empty page that it's easier to abdicate entirely and work with the character-driven stuff, where inspiration is plentiful.

    \n
    \nMy understanding right now is that players:
    \n
    \n- build B-level plot elements
    \n- I'm going to potentially wrongly assume that plot elements are situations, characters, issues, and conflicts or conflict seeds
    \n- but these plot elements are not selected randomly
    \n- they select appropriate plot elements based on who their character is
    \n- for example, let's introduce X NPC because they nicely reflect everything I don't want my character to become
    \n
    \nGM lacks this framework to work with. And the GM is responsible for bringing in A-level plot elements. So what framework could they use?
    \n
    \nI suggested episode themes.
    \n
    \nFor me, a theme is a statement (aspect, moral, message, lesson) or question about life, society, or human nature.
    \n
    \nI've GMed games where the theme was, "you don't have to grow up to be like your parents."
    \n
    \nSo all my A-level conflicts revolved around this idea. Put the characters into conflicts that there parents were in and have them make a choice informed by their parent's decisions, lives, and consequences. Have villains that pop up who are the way they are because of their parents. I'll even have the pacing reflect this with scenes with adult themes being purposely slower and more methodical. In general, I like to have two themes so that it doesn't become obvious. And I try not to make the theme absolute. Maybe players want to be like their parents. Just like the players introduce NPCs to hit their issues, I introduce A-level conflicts to hit on my themes.
    \n
    \nI don't want to go any further in case this is way off track.

    \n John: A couple different mechanisms, depending on which card.
    \n
    \nPERSONALITY CARD: This is a card chosen from a separate deck, one such card per character, unchanging during the game. It has three general behaviors (for Vain, for instance: Lying about yourself, Admiring yourself, Pushing someone else out of the spotlight). Any other player, when they notice the character doing one of these things, may award them a card. One card per scene only (it is placed face down on the Personality card, and picked up at the end of the scene).
    \n
    \nROLE CARD: Again, a card chosen from a separate deck, one per character. It has three general beliefs (for Bookworm: Knowing more means understanding better, Everything should be planned out, Structure is always good). When a player has their character act motivated by one of those beliefs, they may themselves place a card on top of the Role card. As with Personality, one card per scene.
    \n
    \nHANGUP: A card that gets drawn from the deck, and then played by a non-GM player as an investment (rather than for its substantial conflict-resolving power): It describes a hangup that prevents the character from taking action (for instance, Self-Restraint). When another player notices the character not acting because of that hangup, they can give them a card. One per scene.
    \n
    \nPLOT ELEMENT, GM: A card that gets drawn from the deck, and then played as a bribe to the GM (rather than for its small conflict-resolving power): It describes a type of behavior the character can engage in (for "Chase", for instance: Run away). Any time that the player has their character do that, they can give the GM a card. One per scene.
    \n
    \nPLOT ELEMENT, PC: A card that gets drawn from the deck, and then played as a bribe to other players (rather than for its medium conflict-resolving power): It describes a type of behavior those characters can engage in (for "Prejudice," for instance: Challenge your assumptions about them). Any time that another player has their character do that, the player who placed the card can give that other player a card. One per scene.
    \n
    \n
    \nSo, put another way: The GM can get cards through whatever their baseline equivalent of Personality and Role is, or through GM-specific plot elements on other players' characters. PCs can get cards (a) directly through playing to their role, (b) through someone noticing that they played to their personality, (c) through someone noticing that they were restrained by their hangup, (d) through another player noticing that the first has played to their PC plot element.
    \n
    \n
    \nMaking any sense, or is it too intricate to see without cards on the table?
