\n Hey there, I'm formulating an essay for a class on how games handle historical content in different ways and how their methods are either effective in transmitting that history or how (and why) they deviate, and then where that overlaps with the goals of history instruction. For reference, I'm gathering a bunch of games with historical context to look through. My list has Sagas of the Icelanders (my main source for a few reasons), Montsegur 1244, Grey Ranks, Last Train Out of Warsaw, Dog Eat Dog, and if I'm pushing it I can probably talk about Durance's historical metaphor, though that might step outside of the scope of this essay.

So what am I missing? Because I'm positive there's more. The easier to access the better, and simpler or more direct the presence in a historical context is ideal, but it's cool if it's more about archetypes (or stereotypes) and play experience than accuracy.
In particular, I'm extra interested in anything that interacts with the world of medieval Scandinavia or Viking culture, hence why Sagas is my largest source. So Viking stuff that still ignores the rest of the historical context of Scandinavia is still of interest to me.

(also, no need to limit yourself to tabletop stuff - if you know any video games that tackle the Scandinavian/Viking world, or a thinly-veiled version of it a la Skyrim, that interests me too)

Thanks in advance!

    \n In my school's 7th grade civics class we roleplayed the Constitutional Convention. There were no real "mechanics" as such, and only a few "key issues" that we'd be deciding over the 2 days we did it. I played Benjamin Franklin. The main thing it taught us is that they had to be wheeler-dealers and couldn't "just not have slavery" and still have a 13-state Republic. It was pretty cool, I absolutely agree it can be a solid educational tool!

    I'm doing an American Horror Story-esque World of Darkness horror anthology in a fictional city throughout American history: A Vampire game in 1921, a Hunters game in 1963 so far.

    \n There's Call of Cthulhu, which has material for many different time periods. Scenarios and campaigns vary wildly in every area, from time period to quality to how they deal with the historical context. Other games in this genre (Lovecraftian) include Trail of Cthulhu, which officially focuses on the 1930s and is getting a book about the Surrealists. Bill White's done scenarios for Trail -- at least one of which he's also run with Cthulhu Dark -- where part of the draw is playing historical figures, from Robert Heinlein to Gary Gygax to Hillary Rodham.
