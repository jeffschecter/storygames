This is true for Juiced Rider, and some other imagined/planned projects. I am toying with the idea that you don't get to role-play all the time, however and whenever you like, and that system comes to the fore when you disagree with someone or want to get something which you might not get and it has an impact on the story.

I want it to be a game, and in a game, all activities you may do are part of the scripted options. In D&D, both 0ed and 3ed, there is no description of role-playing, and the know-how of the activity is actually transmitted by players.
In my "RPGs" (sometimes "Story-Games"), you can only Role-play when it is said that you are free to. There are specific scenes/bits of the action where it is said you may role-play.

How do you feel about that?

(If you're wondering, I'm currently working on Juiced Rider where this occurs)

    \nA lot depends upon (a) how fun the non-RP stuff is and (b) how natural the transitions back and forth are.
    \n
    \nIf the non-RP stuff is boring make-work then you've got a boring game, and I'll desperately want to inject fun into it by RPing. Then the restriction will chafe, but bad!
    \n
    \nIf the non-RP stuff is fun, but it inspires me to RP right then, right there ... well then, the restriction will chafe, but not so badly.
    \n
    \nBut if the non-RP stuff has its own fun rhythm, and I can get involved in that and not be missing the RP ... and then just at the point where I get inspired to RP something, the system tells me that it's my turn to do so? That would be SPECTACULAR.
    \n
    \nSo ... y'know ... no pressure :-)

    \n Switching between modes require a lot of effort from me. If I have to switch into roleplaying, it had best be for a tangible benefit, not just "OK, you can RP now".

    This is best exemplified, for me, in computer land. When I MUSHed - all RP, all the time - I RPed. Living through your character was the point of the game, not to get more powerful or explore new areas. When I MUDed, I played it as any CRPG - killed things and took their stuff, speed walking into the next area to do the same thing, occasionally scripting it so I could walk away and have my toon kill stuff for me (where such things were allowed). I now play Progress Quest for that.

    The point is, when I tried to play on MUDs that "endorsed" RP, or on "roleplaying" MMORPG servers, I rarely RPed. There was no tangible in-game benefit for me doing so - it was strictly filler between dungeon raids or rat farming. Yet I love highly immersive roleplaying. I just need a point to it.

    Give me a reason for RP, and I'll play it. It's not a bad idea. Rationing opportunities for RP may even provide highly focused and concentrated RP when the table turns and you are allowed, but I think for that to happen there needs to be high stakes involved.

    \n I'm not at all clear on what Guy means.

    I know for our group, some types of "not roleplaying" are usually considered to be good fun, like planning out the secret headquarters of their superhero team, or magic item design/creation in D&D3.5, or writing up little index cards with plot seeds/ideas on them at the start of (or between) sessions. And at the same time, some types of "not roleplaying" have made our group totally miserable -- asking a player to narrate a success in PTA, fiddly accounting details in D&D (keeping track of food/water, for example), or creating a character in a system like Champions.

    So I think maybe we're with TonyLB, in that we enjoy non-RP stuff when it has a fun rhythm that wouldn't be much improved by RP (or at least not improved to the point where not roleplaying it feels like we're missing a great opportunity to have fun), and we hate non-RP stuff when it seems boring or pointless or simply doesn't make up for the loss of fun RP opportunities.


    ...but maybe I'm missing Guy's point, I can't tell. I think I need an example to really understand what he's trying to get at with this question.
