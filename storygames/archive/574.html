Generic American settings

Graham
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games
I like settings I can engage with. Usually, that means settings that are familiar to me (cities, farms, universities) or ones that spark my imagination (moon bases, castles).

Now, many American game designers use contemporary American settings for their game: high schools, malls, American highways, distinctively American suburbs. Usually, the setting is barely detailed, because it's assumed that American readers will be familiar with it.

Indeed, I often get the feeling that the designer is using a generic American setting as a sort of default. He's assuming that readers know the setting, so he doesn't need to detail it.

I can't engage with these settings. They're neither familiar to me nor interesting. So I don't buy those games.

And I find this a great shame. By giving me a generic setting, the designer's missed a huge opportunity to give me a richer one. What kind of American highway is it? Is it dusty and endless or modern and soulless? What kind of high school? Bitchy or gritty?

Note that what I'm not saying here is that I feel "excluded" (as per a recent thread). I'm not saying: make your settings less American so that they're accessible to me.

What I'm saying is: if you use an American setting, describe it to me. Don't assume I know what it's like: tell me. Make it rich enough so I can play with it.

Graham

    Fantastic. Useful stuff.

    You've made me realise that my first post was confused. With retrospect, I realise I was blending two arguments together.

    The first argument was something like:

    1. If you use a American setting without describing it, I won't understand it and probably won't buy the game

    ...which is fair enough, I think, but not a very deep point.

    The second argument is, I think, more interesting:

    2. Generic settings are generally bad for a game

    By a generic setting, I mean (as JD suggests) a bland, homogenised, media-generated stereotype setting. They're mostly American settings like suburbs, high schools and cities, but you might even include generic warzones and generic English country houses.

    What I'm not talking about are open settings: settings which leave gaps to fill in. So, for example, I'd say that My Life With Master has an open setting (there's gaps to fill in) but not a generic one (it's not set in a bland, generic medieval Europe).

    In fact, I think that (contrary to my first post) a generic setting can be detailed or undetailed. You can have an undetailed generic setting ("A typical American high school") or you can have a detailed generic setting ("A high school. The kids run up and down the corridors. There's a guy in glasses getting beaten up and some cheerleaders that everyone wants to talk to"). They're both rather bland.

    The reason I brought this up was because, reading through various Game Chef and Ronnies entries, I kept seeing these generic settings.

    So my argument is this: generic settings get used a lot, especially by new game designers (of whom I'm one). They're generally a bad thing: they make for games with little staying power, which you'd play once, but not again. I think that, if we want to encourage games which go on to be published, we should dissuade people from generic settings.

    (Joshua and Rob said pretty much the same thing but better and shorter.)

Let's see if I can explain why:

    Police procedurals are really a third generation child of a fiction legacy, televised. The grandaddy of the procedural is the Detective of Great Skill: Holmes of course (or Poirot, or a dozen others, if you will), and others like him, who stood apart, magical and untouched, with a vast intelligence and keen insight, a mystical sense of perception.

    Readers were to stand stand alongside Watson and marvel at, not identify with, his abilities. This eventually paved the way for the hardboiled hardhitting detective novels, who had vast, accentuated minds, took great risks, and although like Holmes, they stood outside of regular law enforcement and regular society as a Private Dick, instead, his world was dangerous and compelling, secretive and sexy, and in a complete rebellion to the mannered detectives that preceeded him. This was intended to show realism, but ended up hyperaccentuated.

    So then down the road comes the procedural, that takes the inaccessibility out of the crime. The police are real people caught in real situations. They are hampered by bureaucracy, daunted by the criminals, they are both the defenders of and the victim of the public, and yet still perservering. The allure of the procedural drama is placed firmly on the line between the complexities of the case and emotional resonance of the detectives. TRhe best procedurals pendulum back and forth above that line, which is why the political and emotional intensity meters are set the way they are.

    Pushing it there every time would be counterintuitive to the draw of the genre... see?

    I wonder if looking at it on two axes may be helpful. I'm thinking about the level of detail provided by a written setting, and the degree of idiosyncracy it incorporates. This would lead to 4 poles:

    1. Detail Low, Idiosyncracy Low: Generic setting. Setting emerges from what the players bring to the table, but without guidance or a firm vision there may be a risk of tending to the lowest common denominator - safe options that are less likely to clash with the game, and that everyone has a good grasp on (because of their ubiquity in culture). However, a group confident with the game system and with their own vision are going to be able to wrap it around this fairly straightforwardly, as there is little 'getting in the way'.
    2. Detail Low, Idiosyncracy High: I would put Dogs in this category: lots is left unsaid, but you have elements (coats, ceremony, mormonism) that are very evocative, and so interact with individual and group schemas to produce an emergent sense of setting, directed to some extent by those elements. But these elements suppress some possible settings just as they offer up others, and this may dismay some who find it difficult to play as these elements (more mormons than coats, from internet chatter) get in the way of where they want to go.
    3. Detail High, Idiosyncracy Low: Concerned mainly with the mapping out of relationships between setting elements, in terms of distances, partnerships, and so on, to give some kind of predictable framework; the kind of work that John Kim has said he likes to be provided with by a game setting (hence I imagine one such setting might be Harn).
    4. Detail High, Idiosyncracy High: Concerned mainly with throwing crazy ideas at the reader to get the juice flowing, as well as the mapping provided by the previous. Possibly something like Exalted? I dunno. Anything that provided me with the stuff that I got from reading China Mieville, say - a million tit-bits that left me wanting to know more.

    People will prefer some approaches over others for certain games. I would prefer a generic setting for a supers game because I have a palette of
    super-stuff that I want to smear this way and that. A spacefaring game, I want some ideas, but once you get me started I'll be happy riffing off of them. The third option is less my taste, but would make sense where the characters were very compelling and I wanted a complex structure for them to explore and alter. The fourth I would probably read and then use the snippets that most jazzed me, zooming into the bits that I thought were cool. My current short-duration gaming taste shapes these preferences to a large extent. Possibly other people will have a single flavour that they always go for.
