\n Dear reader,

Whether you\u2019re new to Story Games or just looking for new things to read, this is a great place to start. Be sure to take a look at the Welcome to Story Games thread, as well as the \u201c(re)introduce yourself\u201d thread, and consider saying \u201chello\u201d there whenever you feel ready.

Meanwhile, in this thread you\u2019ll find a great variety of material to read, old and new. You can always click through to the Best of Story Games category to find a handful of good things to read, but in this thread you\u2019ll find a greater variety that\u2019s constantly being updated, as well as a larger variety of opinions. There are lots of great discussions that don\u2019t necessarily belong in Best of Story Games but are still full of great material.

To everyone participating:

In this thread, post a link to a favourite thread or two, and tell us what makes it interesting to you. Try to give enough context and information for the reader to have a good sense of why they might want to read that particular thread, and what excites you about its content.

Thanks!

    \n Occasionally we have been known to dissect a particular game, creating discussions and guides to getting the most out of it. Some people consider the very first "story game" RPG (at least consciously so) to be Ron Edwards's Sorcerer.

    However, the game is notoriously "difficult" for some people, who tend to struggle with it before "getting it". Nevertheless, those people often turn into Sorcerer's greatest fans. About a decade ago, Jesse Burneko wrote a series of threads which serve as a guide to understanding and playing that game, in great depth:\n
    [Practice: Sorcerer] All in one place

    \nD&D, the classic RPG at the root of the hobby, has always received a lot of discussion here. Of particular interest is hearing how people have made the game their own. Several posters have formulated their specific way of running and playing D&D at their table, in great detail, both in terms of specific techniques and in terms of broader philosophical and aesthetic approaches.

    These are great to investigate - whether you're looking for a whole new way to play D&D or just to borrow a house rule or two.

    @2097 has been developing a variety of house rules for 5e, to the point of starting to refer to her house rules as "2097e". These include procedures for all kinds of things, like who the monsters attack in combat (a sort of "monster AI", to aid in objectivity and let the GM just adjudicate outcomes). If you follow the links, you'll find a variety of discussions, house rules, and experiments:\n
    D&D House Rules [2097e]
    \n@Deliverator has his own version and style of running D&D5e, with, among other things, an experience system inspired, in part, by Dungeon World:\n
    5e the Deliverator Way
    \n@Adam_Dray posted in great detail about his own "City of Brass/Mirrorrim" campaign, which involves a number of really unusual choices, from the particulars of the setting, to the detailed play agenda, to new races and rules modifications. Deals with social issues (like poverty!) in an urban setting in a most unusual way, and you can see the evolution and development from a novel concept for a con game to a megadungeon campaign in this series of threads:\n
    Epic, Amazing Thing: D&D 5e City of Brass
    \n
    Adam Dray's City of Brass/Mirrorrim
    \n
    Mirrorrim: Player's Guide, Free Preview 5e
    \nThere have been a number of threads dealing with how to run 4th Edition, as well. (Here are a few examples:)

    http://www.story-games.com/forums/discussion/7879/ganakagok-ified-d-d-4e
    http://www.story-games.com/forums/discussion/13680/story-gaming-d-d-4e
    http://www.story-games.com/forums/discussion/16138/d-d4e-big-next-time-gadget-techniques-for-fictionful-4e

    Perhaps the most interesting, though, is this treatment of The Chronicles of Prydain as a heavily structured D&D campaign, by @Eero_Tuovinen :\n
    [D&D 4th] Some notes on my Chronicles of Prydain microcampaign
    \n
    [D&D 4th] Chronicles of Prydain Campaign Notes
    \nEero has also written a lot about his idea of "primitive D&D", which is, in many respects, an approach to "old-school" or "OSR-style" D&D, but, in other ways, breaks from the OSR tradition. His thoughts have spawned a number of discussions on the nature of "challenge-based adventure" play. You can see how that all got started here:\n
    Let's ask Eero about Primitive D&D
    \n
    Writing up Eero's Primordial D&D
    \nWe have also discussed D&D as a larger phenomenon, with particular attention to the online game/show Critical Role - a controversial but very interesting discussion, as I struggle with the idea of a new following for this classic game inspired by watching other people play online:\n
    Critical Role and the Rise of D&D
