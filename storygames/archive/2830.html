\n Pick a game designer and challenge them to write a particular sort of game. Say why.

It's understood that challenges may be:

a. Addressed to someone, here, who might read it or
b. Just a wish (e.g. "I challenge you, Gary Gygax, to make a roleplaying game of sexual love!")

Oh, and I think you should be able to request a challenge. I'd like a challenge, please.

I'll start.

Troy Costisick! I've seen you write many games set in locations you don't know: Victorian London, Medieval France. I challenge you to make a game set in a location you know intimately!

Graham

      Fatal Flaw

      A RPG for outside play for 3-8 players.

      Find a small area outside, preferablly, with some activity near you. There should be a small expanse of dirt between you - which must be soft enough to be drawn in by a stick. Check for insects before sitting, as you'll be sitting there for a little while.

      You will need one long, fairly sturdy stick (for drawing), and each player needs a handful of stones - the more unique looking, the better. Draw a circle in the dirt among you.

      Prophecy, or Finding Your Character

      Each player rests, listening and watching the world around them. Wait for a moment or a event around you. When you find one of your liking, place a stone on your edge of the circle. Name your character aloud and describe the event you witnessed. Tell how your character has been chosen by fate as the only one who can perform some great deed, and how this prophetic event has shown that.

      Then take the stick and draw an arc containing the stone you just placed between it and the other lines drawn. Then tell of a weakness of your hero, some mundane or ordinary thing they cannot or must not do.

      When all but one player has placed a stone in this way, the last player takes the stick and describes the opening scene for the other characters. This player is now Destiny, the others are the Heroes.

      Perils, or Your Achievements

      Each scene destiny tells of a problem facing the heroes on their journey to achieve their prophecies. Destiny then passes the stick to a hero with the fewest stones placed, that hero may then describe how this is one of their great deeds, pointing with the stick to the stone representing it. The player may then describe how the hero overcomes this obtacle, and how another peril takes its place.

      Alternatively, if this obstacle does not match any of the hero's deeds, then the hero may place a stone near his or her other stones and encircle it. You cannot place a stone within the enclosure of any other stones when you do this. Describe the deed you have gained and a new weakness in some mundane or ordinary situation. Otherwise this is treated as above.

      Lastly, you may pass the stick to another hero with more stones than you have placed. That hero then has chance to respond.

      When a new peril is described, the hero passes the stick to a hero with more stones then they possess. They must respond to this obstacle as above. If there is no such hero, the stick passes back to destiny.

      Inner Weakness

      If hero ever describes her or his self performing an act which violates a weakness, then another player may point this out and cause that hero to lose the stone on which the weakness was based, that player also loses the deed for that stone. No stones may be placed in that enclosure again.

      For example, if Zhengul, as foretold by a bird's caw, had a deed of slaying the giant, and a weakness of cooking, then if Zhengul, let's say, made a soup to poison the great beast of the South, then another player could point out he has committed his weakness, and Zhengul would lose the stone, and his ability to slay giants.

      Journey's End

      If a stone cannot be placed during any peril, then a player may point this out. If, by the time the stick returns to destiny, no stones are placed, then the journey comes to its end. At this point destiny describes the final scene by which each hero will face their destiny. The stick is then passed around.

      Each hero who retains their original stone succeeds against their foretold peril. Each which does not, must either describe how another deed has changed the prophecy - for example, befriending the giant, rather than slaying it - otherwise, that hero has their failure described.

      When the stick returns to destiny, she or he describes an epilogue scene, and passes the stick back the other way, letting each hero describe a vingette about what happened to their character after the foretold deed. When the stick returns once more to destiny, the game ends.

      \nJohn Harper, for the record... the game you challenged me to write is so dope so far. It's called "Vessel". Basically, I took "trials" in the most literal way possible.

      The game is about a group of religious peeps who, during a long trip, get ambushed by a godless mob. Will they hold to their faith even against death? Can they be convinced to lay down their beliefs? Can they pit the godless ones against themselves? And can they escape?

      The game allows you to shape the religious order, the vehicle, the demands, the mob leader and the mob chorus. In some games, small details will turn the tide. In others, only volume and violence will.

      Things that the game can do:

      -A group of Empire cultists are flying in a private helicopter from New York to New Second. The helicopter is shot down and falls from the sky, where a group of scavengers swarm them. Can they reason with these post-apocalyptic aggressors? Can they spread the word of Empire and order?

      -A witch-hunter inquisition carriage is stopped dead in its tracks, the horses either killed or let free. The families of several "confirmed witches" (imprisoned but not yet dead) descend upon the carriage. Will the hunters renounce their work? Will they free the witches to save their lives?       \nKingdoms: A Little Game about Generations

      Each player needs a Diary. You can have any kind of diary you want. You\xe2\u20ac\u2122ll also need correspondence. Envelopes, blank pages. Again, you can have anything you want.

      The Narrator (GM) also needs a Journal of some kind.

      In the game, you play a young prince or princess destined to inherit a kingdom. Just not yet. Define your character with a set of adjectives. You get five. Three Good, two Bad.

      Use your Diaries for in-game actions and your Correspondence for between game actions. The games take place at social events. The players meeting for parties and gatherings, keeping track of events with their Diaries.

      At the end of each event, each player reads his Diary out loud. The players then vote on which Journal is the best recounting of the event. That Diary is the \xe2\u20ac\u0153official\xe2\u20ac\x9d entry. Write it up in the Narrator\xe2\u20ac\u2122s Journal.

      For any Conflicts, players roll dice. You roll three dice for any appropriate Good adjectives and subtract a die for any appropriate Bad adjective. The high roll wins the Conflict and narrates what happens.

      No player may narrate another character\xe2\u20ac\u2122s death \xe2\u20ac\u201c only his own.

      Play progresses with each session representing a year. As time moves on, characters grow older. Eventually, characters pass away, leaving their children to play in their wake. (Or, in some cases, orphans.)

      As for drawing and erasing, you have to draw up your Kingdom, divided into Regions. Each Region has a geographical feature. As you add elements to your Kingdom, it becomes greater. Stronger. I have no idea what that means yet.

      That\xe2\u20ac\u2122s the game off the top of my head. I\xe2\u20ac\u2122d like to add more. I probably will. And will probably have it for sale at Gen Con.

      \nLet's see....\n
      Nighthawks
      When you and your group are eating at a restaurant, each member of the group picks a stranger somewhere in the restaurant to be their character. This can be the cook, a busser, patrons, or anyone else who happens to be in the restaurant at the time. It helps if the stranger is out of earshot of your table.

      Each player speaks as their character, putting words in their mouth or simply saying their inner thoughts out loud. Players should adjust their dialogue in response to their character's actions. If a character leaves the restaurant, is out of sight, or comes within earshot, let the other characters take the spotlight. This is essentially MST3k for the real world.

      Even if it's not obvious at first, each person at the restaurant is a story. A guy at the counter is secretly in love with the woman laughing over pancakes with her boyfriend in the booth across the room. The busser is a masked vigilante by night, stalking the streets and protecting the citizens from the criminal underworld. The owner of the restaurant is actually an ancient forgotten deity and the wait staff are her disciples.

      Each player's goal is to introduce their character, introduce a conflict the character is experiencing (possibly with another character), and ultimately bring that conflict to some resolution with the help of the other characters. The group wins if each character's conflict is resolved just before the characters leave the restaurant. If the character actually works at the restaurant, then it's enough that they go around a corner or into the kitchen.

      This game can also be played at parks, conventions, mall food courts or anywhere else lots of people are around, but you'll have to decide in advance when the game should end.
      \nNot so much a game as a slightly more formalized style of people-watching. I'm sure Ben Lehman would be able to come up with a cool dialogue-based system for it though.

      \n I think Matt is right and wrong.

      Right: We need to be clear about what we're taking seriously. The whole indie games pitch I've heard time and again is "this stuff is proven. the designers did a lot of rigorous playtesting in order to create a stellar game, and you can trust that it works. everything here is grounded in actual play, and everything is about creating a stellar story and worrying about what matters." If we say that shit and then go and put out a joke that was only written to impress Graham Walmsley or Jason Morningstar, with no clear division between those games we talk about and these ones... we do ourselves a disservice. People looking in don't know if they should take us seriously anymore. I don't know if I should take you guys seriously anymore.

      Wrong: You can go ahead and create whatever you want. The Morningstar is right in that you can create new things as a result of taking a joke seriously, or half-assing a serious premise. Doing whacked out shit can produce amazingly interesting food for thought, and can often reappear in future projects. F'rex, my BPG Owlbear Challenge game had a really awesome mechanic called Externalizing Threats, where each time a character dies that player introduces a new force of opposition to the dwindled party. I'd never have come up with that unless I'd thought to myself "Hey, I should create a game which mandates Total Party Kill".

      So, ultimate point? Be aware that although your Masterpiece and your Cock Waving Joke are very different in your own mind, someone who's taking a first look into indie games might not "get it". And be aware that indie games and the associated communities are trying to establish a certain reputation or mark, and it is one of thorough playtesting, focused design, and guaranteed results. Be aware that you shouldn't fuck with that. Unless your point is that you should, and if so, a pissing contest isn't the best place to make a point.

      Shit.

      I believe very, very strongly that this happened last year. It's why I'm collaborating with Paul to create the Ashcan Booth.       I am not suggesting you should not design stuff, even stuff that won't get published, in an effort to improve design skills. I'm for that. That seems to be a misreading of my point.

Because what's happening from where I'm sitting is that the online social scene is increasingly rewarding disingenuous excitement and false praise at the cost of honest, cricital feedback that will go much farther toward better design skils and particuarly better designed games and game products.

      Obviously, I have no power whatsoever to make anyone do anything, or have any special moral capacity to dictate what people should do. But, I also get to think "Huh, that person isn't as good a game designer as I thought. He/She seems to be more after the satisfaction of having their shit in print and impressing friends than making a fucking awesome playable game, realizing it's now as awesome as they thought, and then fixing it."
