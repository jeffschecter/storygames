\n I didn't want to derail this thread: http://www.story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=5332 with an in depth discussion of why "Conch Shell" mechanics don't sit well with me. To be clear "Conch Shell" mechanics are mechanics that simply parse our narration rights with no regard for in-fiction character goals. In the original thread John Wick was quoted regarding his Houses of Blooded project. It pointed out that the system does not resolve in-fiction character success or failure but rather simply establish who can say whether the character succeeds or fails. In that thread I express my distaste for such mechanics and people have asked me questions about this distaste and so I'm starting this thread to answer them.

This answer is a deeply personal one and may spill over into some dangerous metaphors. For a game to really work for me there needs to be a significant degree of social risk. This is why in Big Model terms Step On Up and Story Now work best for me. I gravitate towards Story Now because I prefer the social risk to concern personal value judgments and emotional vulnerability rather than skill or prowess. Also see Megs discussions about No One Gets Hurt vs. I Will Not Abandon You play. I used to be a No One Gets Hurt person but as my gaming tastes have refined I find myself striving more for I Will Not Abandon You.

That said, when I play I commit myself personally to the fiction on a deeply vulnerable level. I like to throw parts of myself out onto the table for evaluation and judgment from my fellow players. This is not the same as Actor Stance decision making or "immersion" as that word is usually used. I do this whether I'm a player with a single character or a GM with authority over multiple characters or whatever combination in between. Whatever "game units" I control I like to commit myself on a personal emotional level. Me, Jesse Burneko, am personally spread all over my contributions to the fiction, again in an intensely vulnerable way.

Now, I do this with full understanding that I might get hurt. And I prefer to play with people who are willing to get hurt back. I am most satisfied with a game when I walk away feeling beaten and broken but like I've grown closer to and have learned something personally about my fellow players. Josh Roby once accused me of enjoying RPGs as a, "venue for social violence." To which I replied if by "violence" he meant like two sparing buddies committed to beating the shit out of each other on the weekends, or fraternity hazing rituals, or consensual bedroom rape play between lovers, then yes, I enjoy RPGs as a "venue for social violence."

This is all because I find personal emotional catharsis fun. I remember a few months back people talking about how films like "Schindler's List" or "Requiem For a Dream" are "rewarding" but not "fun" and I found myself confused. No being negatively emotionally impacted is just as "fun" for me as being positively impacted. I enjoy the sensation of walking out of a movie theater furrowing my brow because what I just saw troubled or disturbed me.

So what has all this got to do with "conch shell" mechanics? I find that such mechanics inhibit emotional commitment to the fiction in the manner I enjoy. In the games I enjoy most adversity has to come from somewhere, and it has to be REAL unrelenting unforgiving adversity. If, we find ourselves in a scene where the adversity player is just as committed to my success as I am then I question his commitment to the source of adversity. What's so weak or lame about his adversity that he isn't fully emotionally committed to it? I invite you into my dungeon to play and lay out all the instruments and you come at me with a pillow fight. It's almost rude and insulting.

On the flip side if I'm so detached from play that failure is willfully acceptable to me then frankly the price of failure isn't painful enough and someone needs to jack the adversity up a notch. I enjoy unexpected devastating failure as a component of my play but just that -- when it's unexpected and devastating which it won't be if I've willfully chosen it.

In short I enjoy play when I'm passionately and personally committed to the success of my in-fiction game units which is not the same to me as "winning." Winning is a social thing among the players. In-fiction success of character agendas is something else. More importantly I want the other players including those responsible for antagonists, villains, or whatever adversity to play with an equal level of commitment and passion. To do otherwise is to prioritize other concerns above "Story Now."

I find that people who enjoy "conch shell" mechanics are often committed to "story structure" based on pre-play notions. They're looking forward to their guy getting his come uppence. Or they really want a tragedy or they can't wait for the villain to take a dive in the third act. I don't know or care about outcomes until they happen. I care about the in-the-moment, here and now, personal struggle of the characters and the choices they make and the actions that follow from it. So I see no reason to be committed to anything OTHER than following through on the relentless pursuit of their fictional passions.

Edited Note: I once used the metaphor of two fencers who agreed on the parameters of every exchange in advance. I said that the two fencers might produce a beautiful display of choreography that's enjoyable to watch but the two participants won't ever grow or learn anything about one another AS fencers.

Make sense?

Jesse

    \n For me constraint matters, but I think it may be less important than creation or information.

    For example: Trollbabe. When you hit the dice in Trollbabe everyone says what their character is trying. The dice then tell you things about what happens. In the end someone ends up with narrative authority, yes, but in the context of what happened in the free and clear, what happened with dice, re-rolls, and relationship usage. That system does something much more than just determine who gets to speak, and even does more than constraining whomever it is that speaks -- it informs, expands, and molds the story and the events. (I can give more detailed examples if they're wanted.)

    It also, as it happens, determines IC wins and loses, which is something that plays into Jesse's bit about real conflict, I think. You can say a lot about how the win or loss happens, but the win or loss rides on the dice.

    So Trollbabe is one of those games that has a "dice will show who narrates" thing -- but it isn't really about who narrates, the dice actually determine what happens and shape and further the story and drama by their use outside of the simple function of narration.

    \n Brand - Yes, absolutely. It reminds of what I describe as Hard Core Primetime Adventures in which certain rules about the High Card narration are taken to their logical extreme.

    1) Stakes are the character's in-fiction goal. The red card count determines if that goal is met or not. So in-fiction character success/failure is nailed down. Then the High Card narration rule kicks in.

    2) It is important to note that the consequences of failure are NOT negotiated up front. So when failure occurs High Card guy has carte blanche to hose the character in question.

    3) It is also important to note that HOW the character's goal is achieved in the case of success is ALSO not known upfront, which means that High Card guy has carte blanche to attach a price or complication to the character's success.

    In my opinion this is what makes getting the High Card in PtA so valuable. You can not take away or change the character's success or failure but you can spin doctor it all you want.

    Brand, when I say "constraint" I'm talking not about who is constrained from talking. I think you're misreading me significantly. I'm actually talking about how the authority (whoever that is) is constrained from what they must and must not say. That's really important not to confuse here.

    We're saying the same thing. Mechanics that are just narration trading don't resolve fictional events. And, resolving fictional events is constraint. That's what I'm talking about.

    ALSO! Please note that I want to toss every damn reference to "stakes" right out the window. It ranks right up there with me with conch trading! Somehow among our community, this idea evolved:

    "If I win, I get X, but if you win you get Y."

    It think this is poisonous to the spontanaeity of ideas in games. It ruins the point of introducing fortune by making it a binary choice at the end of declarations. It's silly.

    There's a reason INTENT is part of the IIEE concept. It's because intentions can be altered after the fortune is introduced. So, instead of the "if I win I get X, but if you win you get Y" you have something much more entertaining like, say:

    "I'm going after X. You're going after Y. Holy crap! Look at that roll! Ok, I'm definitely getting Z now too! Sweet!"

    Stakes ruins Fortune in the Middle, and a pox on its house I say!

    \n I think a great example of narrative rights being passed from player to player to push a spotlight effect without deprioritizing would be Psi Run. I'm sure it could be a passing-of-the-conch game, were the players uncooperative; but in each of us framing our actions within a scene, we've had a deeply immersive, powerfully emotional narrative play experience that's engrossing and enthralling. Part of that, I believe, is the distinct lack of mechanical merits and flaws within the character, merely a set of questions which each player must answer about themselves.

    We've hacked the ashcan somewhat, to where, instead of earning the right to narrate an answer to your own question, you earn the right to answer, either individually or as a whole, a plot question, such as "why are the Cabals of Maddra following us wherever we go?" or "Who is the man in the green sweater?" Sometimes, if the GM has a specific answer, he'll say "Hey, I've got that one right here;" or, sometimes it's more like "Hey, I have a possible answer to that one, but first I'd like to hear what you guys think is happening."

    It's not as caustic to the narrative as the reverse engineering mechanics found in a lot of detective games ("I think we're going to have the murderer be that guy *rolls* Yay!"), but still allows a modicum of back-loaded ominousness to play, where we as our characters can suddenly feel that ominous sense of doom as we learn of our fates; or it could just as easily get pulled back a notch, where that horror vibe of "Don't open the door! Ahhh!" takes place in our minds, because we know what our character will do, and have an inkling of what might happen.

    \nThis one is simple, really. In free and clear you have a goal set up, something your character wants. If you fail you do not get that thing. You can narrate hell and high water keeping you from getting it, but saying that you get it anyway or in any way, is against the rules.

    Player: Hmm... However, there is a large, flat shelf of granite sticking out of the side of the hill...
