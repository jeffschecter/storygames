    HERO whore powers, activate!

    The current edition of HERO can be had in two versions:

    * Sidekick is the basics of HERO in 128 pages. It's $14 now, iirc.
    * HERO System 5th Edition, Revised is the full monty; 500+ pages for $49 or so.

    If you long for the days of 3rd ed., Sidekick is probably right up your alley. If you want crunch that can choke goats and, literally, stop bullets*, you go for the full rulebook.

    The HERO System Combat Handbook is a nifty way to: a) collect all the various and variant combat rules published in many different supplements in one place, and 2) add detail in this area to your Sidekick-based game without shelling out for the big HERO rulebook and said supplements.

    One thing to keep in mind w/r/t the phone-book-ness of the main book is that the current edition is very much about being a thoroughly-explained toolkit. I.e., it's not so much that the book is a mass of cover-to-cover rules, but rather rules + suggested variants + extensive discussion of said rules designed to cover as many genres and tastes as humanly possible. HERO has almost become kinda FUDGE-like, hence the word "toolkit" on the cover of the big book.

    E.g., a typical supers game is going to use 100% of the big Powers chapter, but 50%-60% of the Combat and Skills chapters. Conversely, a gritty heroic game in the vein of Traveller is going to be all about the Skills and Combat chapters, while using the Powers chapter pretty minimally. Prepping a HERO game involves a lot of dial-setting and bolting components together.

    Anyway, if you want to stand in astonishment at page counts, take a look at the Ultimate line sometime. E.g., The Ultimate Skill is solely a book expanding the skill system, and it's over 300 pages.

    So, yeah. HERO is not for the heavy-book-fearing. It's a tinkerer's game for people who want to be able to go into pornographic detail mechanically, if they so choose. My immersion-loving HERO group freaking loves it to pieces.

    * Seriously, they did ballistics tests you can read about on their forums.

    Rules bloat, especially combat rules, is an interesting phenomena. One one hand, I think it's really good that these games systematically document so much. On the other hand, I think it's sad in a way. Often these systems have a certain internal logic that makes it easy to derive new rules from old rules in reaction to an unusual situation. About the third time that situation comes up, we start to wish the derived rule was written in the text. So we start adding to our house rules, or if we have creative control of the game, we add to the rules base for a later edition. But this bloat has a counter effect of reducing the actual players ability to accept and respond to creative input, and especially for games with tactical combat systems the game starts to look more and more like a wargame and less and less like an RPG.

    I think it would be interesting to work on how to document the logic of the system without having to enumerate every possible derivation. Perhaps the trouble is that not everyone is good at or enjoys making those derivations. And of course there's that urge to document the decisions you've made.

    Frank

    Posted By: ffilzRules bloat, especially combat rules, is an interesting phenomena.
    As a dedicated HEROphile, I have to take a little bit of issue with writing this off as "bloat." The extensiveness of HERO's combat system has less to do with adding more rules to cover the same subjects (i.e., making existing systems more complicated, which is what I think of when I hear "bloat"), but simply adding more subjects. I.e., presenting a bunch of options to tack on to default combat depending on what you're setting the dials for.

    An apt comparison might be to the 10th anniversary edition of FUDGE. It's a huge book, but that's because it's full of options.

    The book being discussed is basically the core combat rules plus a catalog of combat tweaks from various books. E.g., if I want to add X, Y, and Z options to make combat more lethal or "realistic," I can find them all in this book as opposed to looking through a bunch of separate books.
    Posted By: ffilzBut this bloat has a counter effect of reducing the actual players ability to accept and respond to creative input, and especially for games with tactical combat systems the game starts to look more and more like a wargame and less and less like an RPG.
    FWIW, I've found HERO responds pretty well, and doesn't feel all that wargame-y to me... at least the way we play it. (Ron Edwards has pointed out to me that his essay on D&D applies similarly to HERO.) E.g., I've found it easier to frame my ideas in terms of what seemed reasonable ("Okay, what I want to do is..."), as opposed to what fit the rules ("Hmm. I could Bull Rush, but I'll just Power Attack"). My experience with D&D 3.x has generally been the latter.

    That said (taking off my HEROphile hat), HERO could probably be a lot more streamlined than it is. Sections like adding damage to advantaged attacks makes my head hurt.

It's totally the opposite of the more typically indie, no-prep way of doing things. It's the slow build, the backstage work, that leads to the big reveal on opening night.
