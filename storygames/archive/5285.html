\n I need to find someone to bounce ideas off of for a game I'm working on. I have this idea for a one on one horror/haunted house rpg. It has a bidding mechanic to deal with the one big problem that plagues the genre: Why stay in the house? Now this is controlled easily enough in fiction. But in a rpg with player meta game knowledge, well it's harder to make them stay. I propose a Meta game solution. A system in where the player gains "earned" dice for allowing the GM to "describe" them into more peril.
\n
\nHave the player hear a noise in the basement. Open the basement door? Yes. Here is a die.
\n
\nDo you go down the stairs in the dark to the light switch at the bottom? Yes? Very good, here is a die.
\n
\nYou are standing in a pool of light on the bottom stair. You flip the light switch the light flares for a blinding moment, then burns out. But just for a second you thought you might have just seen....something there way back in the back by the old coal chute. Do you feel your way back there?.....and so on.
\n At any time during one of these bidding scenes the player can just say no. And it goes back to the normal flow of an rpg. The "earned" dice are very important in that they are used for two things" First they are useful as bonus dice. But also they can be used to buy cards during end game. I was going for a set number of scenes in a game. With a defined end game. with one of three possible outcomes. Escape, Insanity or Death. Perhaps using a blackjack type end game.....and that's all I got. Seems like a lot of Meta game in there, almost board game like. Oh and the game system is designed with semi-incompetent PC's in mind.
\nWell what do you think Sirs?

    \n I, too, wondered about the motivational element, being another player who will push any button just to see if it causes the base to self-destruct.

    What I thought of was to have an inherent risk (I'm thinking a random one) with *every* bonus-die-gaining question, action, or situation. So, for example, opening the door might release a foul stench that reduces the character's "Stomach" stat for the remainder of the scene; going down the dark stairs might result in a step breaking, reducing the character's "Flee!" stat until treated; flicking the light switch could provide just enough light for the monster's "Medusa Petrification Power" to cause a Polymorph Savings Throw. You get the idea.

    That way, the risk/reward isn't on the meta-game level of "the players are smart enough not to walk into my traps, so I have to pay them to be stupid." The risk is a chance of mechanical penalties in exchange for the consistent reward of bonus dice. I leave the percentage chance of penalties as an exercise for the reader, but any probability less than about 90% is going to be a good enough "deal" to a min-maxer that you ought to get some Huckleberries....

    \nPanicky guy

    \nGhostbuster (could be classic type or crazy ghost whisperer like the lady from Poltergeist)
    \nWhenever he wants to perform an action he rolls 2 dice, unless they are explaining the phenomenon, then he rolls 3 dice, unless their explanations make others lower there guard or stay in the house, then he rolls 5 dice

    \n Sounds like a cool idea for a one on one game, but I don't think that has to limit it to one character vs the house.

    A lot of horror movies, and most slasher flicks, have a group of people that get thrown into a situation. I think it would be cool if each of the player's "stats" was an individual character (ie. Dumb Jock, Nerd, Popular Girl). Each could be used for different tests, or limit your options/give bonuses for dealing with certain tasks.

    Let's say the task is "go down the creepy stairs into the basement". I could lead with the Jock whose Bravado would protect me from from fear tests, but it would also increase the chances of not noticing the weak stair that will break. If I choose to lead with a more cautious character, I'll notice the stair, but might lose my nerve.

    It would also give the opportunity for escalating tension. Each scene could end with the death of a character, allowing the endgame to be a classic "loan survivor vs the menace" moment.

    I don't know how much of that is useful, and how much diverges too much from your vision, but I hope it helps.

    \n Cool ideas here.

    At first blush, it seems to me you could have a complete game using blackjack as your sole resolution mechanic.

    The player's moves are:
    When you expose yourself to greater danger, take a hit.
    When you take a stand or dig in to endure what's coming, stay.
    If you bust, you are brutally slain. The House describes how.

    The House's moves are:
    When the spook rattles the chains, creaks the floorboards, moans, drips blood, or otherwise scares, taunts, or lures the player, take a hit.
    When the spook mounts a direct attack, stay.
    If you bust, the spook is destroyed, exorcized, or has its torment resolved. The player describes how.

    When House and player both stay:
    If the player wins, the PC escapes, but she's changed by the experience.
    If the House wins, the PC goes missing, and another character has to go in to find her....

    \n It's an interesting idea. I have notes for a B-movie game that I abandoned some time ago. Anyway, my focus here would be to emulate horror movies/novels.

    First thought - a definite end state in the end phase; that is to say: the game can't end until the third act ( or fourth or denouement or whatever). If a bad roll ends the game in the first act, it doesn't emulate the fiction.

    Second thought - the players should be rewarded for bad moves by the characters to simulate the tropes of the genre (" I investigate the noise outside", "I go down to the cellar by myself", etc.) These moves could be in the form of cards that suggest actions or attitudes/attributes (dauntless, stupid, flirty, drunk, etc). They might also trigger a Doom track that propels the game forward to the end-state. The more the characters do, the more "powerful"/plot immune they become but the quicker they reach the last act. A balancing act between gaining the resources needed while holding off the end-state.

    This says nothing about the mechanics of the game in regards to what the actual resources are or how they'll help; but we haven't addressed what type of game it is: rpg, board, card, hybrid, co-op, competitive, one character or many.

    I would like to see a system that accommodates the lone Lovecraftian scholar as well as the slasher teen blood fest. More replay value.
