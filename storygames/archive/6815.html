\n Hi All,

So my group has been trying to tackle the whiffness of 4E and we have been trying a couple of different house rules. We have tried allowing Action Points to be used as re-rolls, but there was some weirdness with that. John Harper suggested that we spend a healing surge for a re-roll. That worked pretty well, but some thing still felt off about it. Ultimately, I think the issue comes down to one of game balance. The below method should reduce the overall whiffness of the game and lead to more blood on all sides.

Lets look at the situation holistically, rather than piece meal.

Why use Healing Surges for re-rolls?
* The is sacrificing his survivability for effectiveness by exerting himself when it really matters.
* Re-rolls mean more damage is inflicted. This keeps the encounters shorter and bloodier.
* This preserves the roll of action points and prevents any confusion as to when Action Point powers are triggered.
* Reliable Powers can be re-rolled or they can miss and be used again later (as per normal).
* Only Paragon Classes with re-roll powers can get 2 re-rolls on the same attack.

Rules for PCs using Healing Surge Re-Rolls
* 2 Healing Surges - Re-roll a Daily Power
* 1 Healing Surge - Re-roll any other roll (including saving throws, skill checks, and damage.)
* +1 Healing Surge if you rolled a Natural 1 (just an extra penalty for really sucking)
* You must keep the result of the 2nd roll.
* Maximum 1 Healing Surge re-roll per roll.

To balance this out, the DM also gets re-rolls for his forces. Monsters have healing surges that they don\u2019t get to use during a fight, but they do have them. In addition, it just as frustrating for a DM to totally whiff with his awesome power or to have his heavy hitter totally stymied by failed saving throw.

Encounter Level _____________DM Re-Roll pool
* Equal to PCs Level________5 Re-rolls
* Per 1 less than PC level____+1 re-roll
* Per 1 more than PC level___-1 re-roll

Weaker monsters need a bit more luck and tougher monsters need a bit less luck.

Thus PC Level is 10.
Encounter Level 8___=___7 DM re-rolls
Encounter Level 10___=___5 DM re-rolls
Encounter Level 13___=___2 DM re-rolls

Rules for DM using Healing Surge Re-Rolls
* 2 Healing Surges - Re-roll a Power with a Recharge Roll
* 1 Healing Surge - Re-roll any other roll (including saving throws, skill checks, and damage.)
* +1 Healing Surge if you rolled a Natural 1 (just an extra penalty for really sucking)
* You must keep the result of the 2nd roll.
* Maximum 1 Healing Surge re-roll per roll.

This is the latest incarnation of the method that we are going to try. I am putting it out there for comments. We have not tried it in play yet.

    \n From what accounts I've read that refer to whiffyness*, on a regular basis it seems to be where you get your turn, and it's a roll to see if you have absolutely no effect on the game. Roll to see if your input is meaningless. Again and again. Every miss of a sword strike, for example, leaves no currency mark on the game what-so-ever.

    IF that's the core of the problem (and it might not be in this circumstance, but hey, I'm just working from what I know of 'whiff'), then rerolls are just delaying the problem - ie, you roll twice to see whether you have no effect, rather than once.

    My suggestion, which I've dabbled with sort of in some playtests, is to make the entirety of the roll count. One idea that springs to mind is that on a miss, the roll made on the D20 is XP earned (learn from your mistakes, if you want a causal reason). You roll a 4, you get 4xp (adjust number for level (keeping in mind handling time though)). And an idea I playtested once was that (this was with level 1 characters) that a miss still equals 1 point of damage. Though I suspect 1 point didn't feel much better than an entire miss, for my players.

    I've also considered having certain baddies designated as mooks** and on a miss, you still get to remove one from the board. On a hit you get to remove one and do damage to a regular monster. My concern (haven't tested this) is a matter of space, handling time, and what happens when I run out of mooks. Actually, thinking on that now, perhaps there could be a per round reinforcement number, for mooks - so X amount trickle in every round. Okay, so I'm rambling now so I'll just think aloud. Hmm, what if they fill up the room? Have a limit on the number of mooks who will pour in at a time? Gah, the monsters wouldn't hold back at a certain amount - but then again it's kind of simmy of me to think that way. Hmmm...

    * Haven't read any about 4E, just other games. Indeed I was rather surprised that with 4E I heard with some powers, if the attack is resisted, they still take some damage or some effect. It seemed like they were addressing the 'roll to see whether I'm wasting my time' whiff problem.

    ** Yes, I know 4E has mook rules. These guys would be even mookier. Seriously!

    \n What about this?

    If your roll fails to hit the AC or Defense or whatever, but exceeds 10 or 12 or something (adjust for gameplay, obviously), give a +1 or a +2 (adjust as needed) to the next guy or gal rolling since you're distracting the enemy but not really connecting.

    This won't preclude whiffs, but won't drain the players of their resources continuously for rolling just under the target numbers. Because, I believe, this is a death spiral. If you spend all of these healing surges and still can't connect, you won't last very long and you'll be forcing the party to rest more often and such.

    \n Here's another. If you whiff, you get a token. You can then turn in one five tokens for a future roll, after the roll has been made. You can only hold as many tokens as 5 + 1/2 your level, to keep players spending these things!

    Heroic Tier: +5
    Paragon Tier: +10
    Epic Tier: +15

    That way, if you would whiff anyway by spending tokens, you wouldn't spend them and just rack up another for the pile.

    Kind of the video game "angry meter" sort of thing. I used that style of mechanic for Fighter-D Alpha and it was pretty awesome.

    \n I think the goal is that something, in terms of game currency, happens on your turn. So, as one means of meeting that goal, the whiff bonus only happens on the first roll that fails during your turn. Whether your rolling a dozen times, once, or twice, only the first roll that misses grants the bonus.

    Alternatively if any of your attacks on your turn manage to hit/do damage, then even if all your other attacks whiffed, you get no wiff bonus. You had an effect on game currency, so no need for a compensatory currency to come into effect.


    Finally, the thing to remember is that this goal means a currency change - thats going to cause ripples through all other currencies. Indeed, that's the idea! That the player effects the game every single turn.

    How I see it is you weigh up "The player having an effect on the game economy every turn" Vs "Having a stable game economy". Which do you put first? That's up to each of us, really.


    On the anger meter, I don't think it meets the goal (as I understand the goal). It's a currency which makes you whiff less, rather than making every single roll count. The anger currency has no effect outside of making future hits more likely, thus it isn't having a flow on effect onto the rest of the games economies. Mind you, my XP idea almost has the same problem - it's effect wont be for a considerable time (leveling only, and crafting, if 4E still has XP costs for crafting).

    \nThat's a good point, but I think it's still wonky. Consider the ranger. The ranger has several powers that involve multiple attack rolls, so they'll be building up a lot of tokens. But the ranger also has a lot of single-attack-roll, high-damage powers. They'll be hitting with those a heck of a lot more than the poor fighter, who has relatively few multiple-attack powers.

    Here's how I'd do this, if I were going to (I haven't played the game yet, so I can't say if I think the whiff factor is too high - I want to believe that effective combinations of powers with flanking and buffs will mitigate that a lot):

    After rolling the dice for attack powers (to hit, but not damage, probably), the player can choose: a) the power takes effect using the dice as rolled, or b) the power has no effect (including miss effects) and you get a re-roll token. You can only do this with encounter and daily powers, not at-will powers. You can only spend one re-roll per roll, and any accumulated re-rolls are lost when you take an extended rest.

    That way, if a wizard gets a single hit on a minion with their fireball, they can trade that for a hit later. You can describe it as the power distracting the opponent, the character discovering some weakness in the opponent, or whatever. It's a little more fiddly, and introduces some redundant rolling, which is not ideal, but it does give the players a choice every round. Restricting it to daily and encounter powers is important, I think, because otherwise there'll be way too many re-rolls floating around, and it'll lead to odd tactics like powering-up against a minion, to get a bunch of re-rolls against a boss. Also, I think the misses really hurt the most with the encounter and daily powers, so that's where you need the mitigating factor.

    There's a minor problem with powers that have miss effects being less attractive (since you have to lose the miss effect for the re-roll) but I think that once people have a couple of re-rolls "banked", they'll opt for the miss effect, if it's at all useful.

    EDIT: Spending a re-roll means you have to re-roll all the dice used for the power. You can't hit with all but one of your attacks, and then re-roll the last one, and you can't miss with everything, and then only get to re-roll one.

    It probably is wise to take everything I say on this topic with a grain of salt. I'm coming from a background of a LOT of experience with 3.5, and pretty good familiarity with the rules of 4, but nothing beats knowing how the game feels in actual play.

    I wonder if part of the issue here is about wanting different things from the game? For me, the enjoyment of D&D since 3e. has been the mathematical challenge of it - the illusion of balance, even when such balance doesn't exist, and the fact that you're playing is what is supposedly a game that's robust against exploits and cheap moves. The rules say to me "Here are your limitations, now what crazy things can you do?" That applies to playing the game, as well as character creation.

    For that reason, any house rule I introduce has to be as robust as possible, and affect the balance between the classes as little as possible, or else I feel like I'm not accepting the limitations the game imposes. I'm especially wary of house rules in games like D&D, where character death is such a stong disincentive. I never want the player to be in a situation where they feel like the dodgy house-rule killed them, not the "real" game.
