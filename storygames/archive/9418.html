\n Just a little pondering and a hope to generate discussion.

What kind of approaches work to building a one-shot scenario-game?

Term usage:
Scenario-game
A game meant to be played in one session, with self-contained rules. Quite possibly, it will be played with at least some strangers ( as at a convention) or with your regular play group, but with no assumption of familiarity with the mechanics being used prior to meeting up for the session.

Self-contained rules
The mechanics for the thing are assumed to contain just the rules necessary to run the specific game in question, not any game set in the fictional game universe.

Scenario
The characters + situation have just enough back-story to show a general set-up, with the action begining at some sort of tipping point wherew those things begin to collide, and the play of the game itself has at least a broadly painted direction to it.

To bring this back to earth, I'll give a little context. A while back, I started a thread about building Braunstein-style games. I managed to build one, and I'll be throwing it out for testing tomorrow. At this point, I'm thinking that I really enjoyed the designing end of that, and have enjoyed playing some similar things, such as a one-shot Wodehouse inspired LARP.

I'd like to put together more of this kind of thing, and I'd also like to put together some kind of document talking about building this kind of thing.

I'd appreciate any feedback from folks who have built or played in similar games, especially regarding the following sorts of problems/issues:

- Presentation of background material and rules
-Dealing with time constraints, both in terms of initial start up and ending the game
-Insuring that characters + situation collide/interact rather than hare off wildly
-The role and duties of the designer/presenter of the game
-Building or stealing mechanics directly important to the specific game, without going to mechanics overload
