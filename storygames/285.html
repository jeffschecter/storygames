    I really think that it's generally for the best if a game (either in it's Platonic form, or as in "a game of Bridge") does best when it's clear whether the ultimate outcome will be that one player wins, and the players are all striving to be the winner, or the ultimate outcome isn't all that important and we're all just trying to have a good time.

    For the record, I don't think that the dichotomy there between cooperative/competitive. Arkham Horror is cooperative, because the players will all win or lose together. But the game's object is to win.

    Obviously, a reason we play games is to have fun, but I think there's a difference between games that present victory as the object and games that present fun as the object.

    And I think a large part of the difference is in the system of incentive the game presents. If the aim is victory, then you want to make plays that will win the game for you, and the game needs to make the moves that are "right" be winning moves. If the aim is just fun, then fun moves need to be encouraged, and strategic thinking shouldn't be rewarded.

    Hell, I'd go so far as to suggest that cooperation/competition are neither opposed nor orthogonal. I think it's very hard to suggest that I could take a hypothetical design and rerstructure it so that I could turn the cooperation dial without the competition dial moving as well, or even that I could predict easily how the two will relate in a particular design.

    In fact, the Game Designer's Third Disease (after Theory Wankery and the Orphan Mechanic Syndrome) is Metasystemics: the idea that the critical characteristics of a game can be related to each other in some sort of chart or model.

    By way of analogy, who among you would try to place foodstuffs on a Sweet vs. Salty table?

    And I think it's also salient that it's entirely reasonable to say, for example, that:

    • The Mountain Witch is cooperative in that we win by having fun and telling a kick ass story about the tense interplay between shifty ronin.

    • The Mountain Witch is competitive because we game (roll dice and spend points and call for aid) for the rights to narrate, and because ultimately we'll probably have all kinds of betrayal going on, and that works better if I want to win, in terms of accomplishing my Dark Fate and not getting killed and whatever.

    And both those statements are true at the same time and not at all contradictory. In fact, a lot of the cooperative aspects feed on the competitive ones, and vice versa.

    (And in terms of design study, I'd point to Colossal Arena, wherein the course of play, one forms tacit alliances with other players.)

    Jess, I think there's a tendency, when people begin to seriously dip their toes into writing about RPG stuff, for folks to try to create meta-categories for various games, players, or play styles. Ron's GNS is, of course, the most famous example, but there's also Mo's Push-Pull thing, the kind of categories you present here as Collaborative-Competative, and many, many others.

    And, as far as I can tell, breaking gaming up into categories is most often a tool for saying "I like play that's more like X and less like Y." Ron claims that GNS isn't supposed to do this, but part of his position was that there were already games that supported G & S, but not so much ones that supported N, so GNS ended up becoming "Go Narrativism!" And Mo's Push-Pull thing is similar. While claiming to not be a judgement on other play styles, Mo herself is clearly "Go Pull!" Likewise, you seem to obviously be "Go Collaborative!"

    So, I guess, I'm struggling with how to best help you out here. I think dividing gaming into things you really enjoy and things you don't enjoy as much can be incredible helpful. It focuses your own design goals. It lets you know what kind of games you should try to be a part of. It does all sorts of good stuff.

    But I think that categorization can become unhelpful, because categories begin constraining the way you think about things. Obviously, most play is a combination of collaborative and competative tendencies and you don't necessarily want to do all one or the other. You want to make the choices that make the most sense for your game, no matter what the meta-categories say.

    So, I guess, when you say, "Collaborative play is like X and I want to figure out how to support it better," I'm wary of labeling specific games and players, but I'm more than happy to talk about play techniques that I think support the kind of play that you seem to enjoy. Does that make sense?

    There seems to be a large number of unsatisified players who want play that supports a high degree of negotiation and interaction between players but also very little fiddling with meta-mechanics or resources or tactics or the like. They want some system support but also want to preserve some degree of immersion or don't want to break the fantasy to deal with mechanics or just don't really like fiddling with mechanics. And most of the Forge stuff and even the non-Forge indie stuff tends to create interesting story conflicts by encouraging inter-player conflicts using mechanics (Universalis, MLwM, and PTA being the clearest models here, but even including stuff like Dogs, Mountain Witch, Polaris, and Breaking the Ice).

    And, honestly, a large number of the players interested in high collaborative, low meta-mechanics play seem to be female and on the edge of both mainstream and indie roleplaying (the indie crowd, as different as it is from the mainstream, can still be a boys' club), so their interests aren't really being taken into account by recent design and theory work, unless it's being done by folks like you, Jess, or Mo, or Annie Rush, or Meg Baker, or Emily Care Boss.

    And, while most male designers really respect, enjoy, and want to support the kind of work the indie women are doing, I get the sense that most of the indie crowd aren't really interested in that kind of play or don't think that it's really possible to have some serious system that doesn't require messing with mechanics or fiddling with resources or creating inter-player conflicts through reward systems or whatever. This seems too much like freeform. Or like Amber. But when you drop something like Neel's Lexicon or Shreyas' Mridangam or my KKKKK on them, something that reframes things completely differently than other RPGs, they go "Oooo, that's neat," but it becomes a one-time thing, an experiment. Nobody is picking it up and running with it and really playing in this sandbox yet.

    Still, I think the interest in this kind of collaborative, low-impact play, potentially (and once we actually figure out how it really works), is HUGE and I sympathize because I often feel that way and have been trying to get at how to best support play in that style. Unfortunately, most of what I have to show for it are my 2-player experiments and not real solutions yet. But it's definitely something that I hope to continue working on.

    So that's a long response to say, basically, I don't think the style of play you are looking for has really been formalized in a game yet. There are games that do some of that and there are games in development that will hopefully do a lot more, but I don't think this area has had sufficient attention put to it yet. I've been trying and so have a few others, but this is not where most people's attention is right now (where is it? I don't really know).

    Maybe you'll be the one to really make it work.
