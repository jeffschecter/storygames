Hi,

\n

If you've been following any of Black Cadillacs' development over at the Forge, skip to the next paragraph. However, if you haven't, BC is my attempt at putting a very human spin on war. It's focused on giving the players, as human beings some sort of personal grasp on war -- maybe it's about why we fight them, maybe it's about what it costs; the players figure out the question as they play, and the question may vary wildly from player to player. What the game doesn't allow you to do is to be apathetic on the subject; you'll absolutely have to comment on war as a human being during play.

\n

I've always held that although it was possible to set a BC game in a fictional war, and that it would be immensely rewarding for some people, that I was not one of those people. Whenever I think of BC, I think of an historical context; I envision World War II, the Crusades, the Soviet intervention in Afghanistan, or even the current war in Iraq; I don't think of the Clone Wars, the subterranean war against the Matrix, the war against the Dominion, or even Barbarella fighting Duran-Duran.

\n

I need to emphasize here that I'm not judging the validity of any of these as settings for Black Cadillacs. I'm just pointing out that no fictional setting has ever lept up at me and said "play this".

\n

That all changed two nights ago at HoundCon. I'd just wrapped a fun session of Black Cadillacs, set in Vietnam. I'd just introduced two new players to the game, and we'd had a good time. It was late, but we were all jazzed; the three of us settled into a fun conversation about game design, my publishing options, and a mixed-bag feedback session.

\n

Meanwhile, the con's eponymous host wrapped up the card game that he'd been running at the adjacent table. He sauntered over and proceeded to blow my fragile little mind,

\n
\n

"Hey Darce, can you use this to do fictional stuff?"

\n

pause

\n

"Sure..."

\n

"Cool. Because I want to see attack ships on fire off the shoulder of Orion."

\n
\n

Oh. My. God.

\n

YES!
