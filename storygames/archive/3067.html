    \n I would not say that doing work for free is part of indie. Now for the caveats.

    Caveat #1: The primary writer and game designer is not being paid for his time, and will only get something even approaching adequate compensation per hour if the game is a pretty big hit. I spent literally hundreds of hours writing FLFS, and if I were freelancing I would have got a paycheck for those hours. As it is now, FLFS is like two hundred bucks over break-even. Doing the math, I've not been paid very well for my hours.

    Caveat #2: I do think that indie games are more likely to contain free work for the purposes of getting the worker's name out there. FLFS has art by Kirk Mitchell, for instance, who gave me art in exchange for a byline and no cash.

    Caveat #3: There are also the charity games that crop up in indie-land, like Story Games Names Project, which has donated art and layout (note: I'm the free layout, and I'm the reason it's been delayed for so long).

    Caveat #4: Proofing. There is a lot of proofreading-for-free in the indie game world, usually attached to playtesting (which is also done for free). In my day job, proofreading gets $12 an hour to start, and those costs are not visited onto indie games. Additionally, I've seen a lot of folks offer to do full-on copyedits for free because they want to see the game rock. I don't think that these free services are to boost the community, though: they're almost always to boost one specific book that people want to see do well.

    Indie isn't about free, it's about DIY. There has been a strong spirit of mutualism and communal support in the past. That doesn't mean that all work is for cash remuneration. Trade in kind is reasonably common.

    I've provided editing for Michael Miller, Nathan Paoletta, and Luke, of course.     I get compensated in at least two ways for every job I do, including Indie ones.

    \n

    One way is respect. If a project will increase the respect I receive, then good. That might be because something will be a portfolio piece or it might be because the person I'm working with will tell all their friends.

    \n

    Another is money. This is usually in the form of dollars, but is sometimes in the form of trade. It's not trade for a copy of the book \xe2\u20ac\u201d I get that anyway \xe2\u20ac\u201d it's trade for similar work. I'll trade my book design for Ben's editing, for instance. Or I'll do it for a cut of sales. That way, I'm getting paid either in professional services or with a respectable amount of money. I was paid for Dogs in the Vineyard in a pile of games from Gen Con, which had substantial monetary value. I wanted to get up to speed on game design myself and I wanted Vincent's taste to inform that.

    \n

    [Edit: The third is education. I have to be able to learn something from a project. Maybe I learn about the subject matter, or maybe the medium requires me to learn stuff.]

    \n

    The last is artistic integrity. That is, if I believe in the project, I'll do it, as long as I've got one of the others.

    \n

    Almost.

    \n

    I can't eat respect and integrity, unfortunately, so I usually charge something. If the person I'm working with can't afford me, I'll help them get up to speed themselves so they can get some books selling and then perhaps be able to pay the people they need for the next project.

    \n

    I think it's really detrimental to the community to have all the craftspeople burn out and resent each other. Finding ways to compensate each other is a badge of pride and, more importantly, it's sustainable.

He's gotten paid not a lick of actual cash for his efforts (just like Lydia, Rob, and myself haven't -- but the difference here is that Lydia/Rob/Fred hang out together in real life, while Lenny's squirrelled away in Vegas and wasn't a pre-existing member of our social circle).

And SOTC has been nicely high-profile and has acted as a great opportunity for Lenny to make connections and inroads in the gaming industry that he hadn't made before.

    I still feel guilty at times about the not-paying thing, and I've confessed it to Lenny on many an occasion. I feel guilty because I'm human, and I'm running a small press because I love games and I share that game-love with Lenny... and I like to think the friendship we built while working on SOTC is one we're both getting dividends on.

    I still feel like SOTC's been pretty profitable, even if we haven't chosen to spend those profits to pay folks for their time. With their support and permission, those profits have been put towards future projects. Make of that what you will. It's not a "boast".

      \n Just as an outside-in perspective on this thread:

      A lot of the relationships that were originally in place when the Forge (and along with it the 'indie' label) have changed radically since, mostly with the advent of d20 and the web (which happened to dovetail together nicely).

      A lot of ink has been spilled on the mainstream/indie divide, but when it comes right down to it (other than the Holy Duality of WotC and WW), the very nature of what 'mainstream' means is being wiped out.

      Back in the dim dark recesses of the 80s and 90s, a book like Dogs in the Vineyard would have been, quite literally, impossible. You had the three-tier distribution model, and if you didn't have the capital to buy into that structure, your game wasn't going to go very far. 'Mainstream' was any game company that did have the capital to participate, and 'indie' was everyone who didn't.

      With electronic distribution (and greater electronic readership), the web and PoD, the playing field has been leveled considerably, and with that flattening of distribution barriers, the line between mainstream and indie (which as GMS points out was a fallacy and an artifact of the 3-tier system, really) is going to vanish completely.

      Really, there needs to be an acknowledgment in the 'indie' community that what they do is no different than what the creator of Skyrealms of Jorune did - it's just that the power allocated to the publisher is vastly different these days - and it will continue to change.

      [Edit: There's also the issue of the content or style of indie games and the narrative bend of the Forge getting mixed into this heady brew, which doesn't make things any easier.]

      \n So shitty stuff happens all over the production side of gaming. Good stuff happens too.

      Once upon a time I worked with Dream Pod 9, and wrote a good chunk of Tribe 8 and occasional contribution bits of other games. Towards the end of my time writing for the company they got tight with money, and at one point asked me if I would accept not getting paid in order to "support the game." I told them no, they hired me as a professional, they were going to pay me as one. They did that time. The next time they said they wouldn't pay, flat out. I had a co-author's sister, who was a partner in a litigation and contract dispute specializing law firm, send them a letter on her letterhead. They paid me the next day and I never did another job for them.

      Not long after that they got a lot of the folks on the Tribe 8 mailing list to do the Tribe 8 Players Guide. The whole community chipped in and did it all (so far as I know) on a volunteer basis -- I don't even think they got comp copies. The book came out, made enough money (as nothing was paid for production) to do Tribe 8 2nd edition. This time some of the folks who did the Player's Guide got brought on with the expectation that this was a professional gig, not just volunteer stuff. (Or at least so I was given to understand.) But after the book had been produced and brought out none of them got paid. Not only that but many of them, who'd worked damn hard on it, got cut out at cons -- not allowed to run official demos or even man the Pod booth.

      There is no doubt in my brain that the Pod fucked people, used the spirit of community and fan-love and exploited people for their own profit. They were a business and they did not follow good business ethics.

      In more recent times Fred Hicks and I got into a brawl on the blog-o-swinesphere. The upshot of it is he ended up hiring me to edit a book for Evil Hat. His first question to me is what my rate was. I told him I wasn't going to charge him, because I wanted to do this one pro bono. He insisted he be able to pay me, and I shot him down. He finally said that I at least had to take comps of some product, and I rolled my eyes and agreed. He really, really tried to pay me, and wanted to pay me a fair price. That I shot him down was my decision and something I did for a combination of personal reasons.

      Really, doing a project for free because I want to support it is a choice I can make. If a friend was guilting me into I might feel taken advantage of -- but this wasn't that situation. This was my decision. And its in line with things I do outside RPGing anyway. My rates at editing and proofing and stuff are highly variable. If I'm proofing on an easy project for people I like, for example, my rate can be as low as $15 an hour. (Sometimes as low as $12 if I'm trying to pad out my CV in a particular area.) When I'm doing high end editing/graphic desing under pressure for law firms who are willing to pay me enough money that they don't have to treat me nicely, I get as much as $100 an hour. So what price I'm going to charge any given company for any given project is a highly variable thing. In this case I decided, on my own, that the price was nothing. And Fred just had to deal with that.

      See, this thing I'm doing with Fred, for me, was a hobby thing and a personal thing. So business ethics and practices were only involved because Fred wanted to involve them. As far as I was concerned this was a different thing.

      So the thing is that Malcolm is thinking about things in terms of this "thing" being a business, and following business ethics. Many of the Indie folks (not all) are thinking of it being a hobby they do with their friends, and so think of it in terms of personal ethics. Those two POVs are obviously going to have difference between them. Especially when folks are not always clear about what they are doing or why. Or at least when each individual may be clear but the "group" isn't because not every member of every group has the same agenda. I would not, for example, lump Green Ronin in with Louis Porter, or Wizards with Dream Pod 9. Nor will I automatically toss Clinton into the same pot as Evil Hat or Mob United. But because of the "indie/mainstream" binary we often end up doing that to each other.

      I also think that GMS does have one point worth thinking about -- it isn't fair to compare gross profits on a work done partly or fully by volunteer labor with net profits on a book for which all the labor is paid for. And while I don't see that happening out of everyone in the "indie community" every day, I have occasionally heard folks compare two different ventures that are as alike as apples and oranges in terms of net vs. gross as though it was the meaningful measure. Of course, this also brings up the always tricky issue of knowing who was paid for what. A lot of products out there that were done "professionally" didn't pay people after all, and I know a lot of "indie don't pay" folks who do pay people in various ways -- sometimes even with money (shock gasp!) in a professional manner (shock gasp!). Just because a book is at the Forge booth doesn't mean everything in it wasn't paid for at a professional rate, and just because its at a big name shiny booth doesn't mean it was paid for at all.

      For me it isn't. For me the freedom to set my own goals and decide if I want a given project to be professional or personal, and to chose what that means for me and how much I want to charge -- or not charge -- is the point.

      \n One of the theories behind Atomic Sock Monkey Press is that "everyone gets paid." Period.

      On Dead Inside, I did a mix of royalty (of net profit) and outright purchase of art/services.
      On MNPR:RPG, I purchased editing.
      On Truth & Justice, I purchased art and editing.
      On The Zorcerer of Zo, I went with all royalties (of net profit).

      For art that's purchased outright, I've paid $25 for quarter-page B&W illos, $50 for half-page B&W illos, and anywhere from $100 to $250 for color full page covers.

      For editing that's purchased outright, I've paid between $50 (for 64 page book) to $100 (for 128 page book).

      I think these numbers, while competitive (more-or-less) in RPG work, are fairly miniscule compared to other industries.

      The royalty percentages for ZoZ break down to 40% writing (me), 35% art (split between artists), 15% layout, and 10% editing. (There's one or two refinements/deviations in there, but that's how it basically shakes out.) The six month sales of ZoZ have led to royalty numbers for the participants that are roughly equivalent to the money that they'd have received if services had been purchased-outright. Which means that after this, they'll be effectively be paid more than the usual work-for-hire amounts, so long as the book continues to sell.

      When sales slacken on a product, I have offered to "buy-back" participants' royalty percentage with a flat final payment. This minimizes paperwork/royalty tracking as sales decrease, and puts a final lump payment into a particpant's hands.

      (Goodwill and networking benefits are also included in working with ASMP, either as a participant or licensee, though those are kinda ad hoc -- I try to draw attention and support my friends/participants work in other venues via ad swaps, blog mentions, Overviews, purchases of their products, comp copies of my products, general advice/insights, connecting person X to person/opportunity Y, etc.)

      Given the minimal margins of this industry, plus my own personal beliefs regarding "I do the job. I get paid.", I now believe that royalties (plus possible eventual royalty buyout) is the way to go for small -press RPG production. Participants are rewarded in all cases, and that reward is tied to the success of the product.

      While ASMP is by no means generating enough cash for me to do it full-time, it does generate a nice second income -- enough such that I could quit the second job I had at the bookstore over this past holiday season.

      \nI have to fall pretty strongly on the less forgiving side here. There are projects that are unquestionably "hobby" projects, but as soon as the distinction begins to blur, it's your responsibility as a publisher to act in a professional manner. If you're going to take work from someone at below "market" rates, it should be for one of two reasons: 1) They have the same deal as you do (ownership stake in project or the company that can turn into cash money down the road if you "take off") or 2) they're "family" (either real relatives or unquestionably close friends who are benefitting pretty directly from contributing unpaid to your success.)

      The situation Brand described upthread is the really unethical one -- offering "relationship capital" to people who don't currently possess it with you as a trade for their services. It's okay to "leech" off of someone who really is already a good, dear friend; it's not okay to bring someone into the circle in exchange for their unpaid or underpaid labor.
