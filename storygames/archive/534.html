What sorts of creative constraints do you like?

jhkim
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games
So I've recently started listening to Paul Tevis' "Have Games, Will Travel" on my commute. And his talk with Paul Czege made me think about interest in creative constraints. So two recent threads have brought up some people's problems with the constraints of a well-defined setting -- Shreyas' "Setting Without The Crap" and ross comment in the D20 thread.

So Paul's suggestion was that he liked creative constraint. By forcing people to work within the limitations of MLWM, it brought out their creativity. In general, I enjoy a well-defined setting (often modern or historical Earth) and I feel like it is a positive creative constraint to me. I can't just make up a new city or somesuch -- rather, I strive to work within what is known.

So it occurs to me that perhaps people prefer different types of creative constraint. So maybe for someone, it's good to lock down the sequence of scenes, but not good to lock down the setting. Maybe someone likes predefined situation, but dislikes pregenerated characters.

Going first, I think I'm pretty broad about my constraints, but I prefer them to be highly concrete. Maybe there's something more here, but I'm not sure. Taking a recently-mentioned example, I had a tough time coming up with stories in Baron Munchausen, because I felt like it wasn't constrained enough.

    I like narrative constraints, of which examples are few. I like being told "Okay, now your character must establish a relationship which pains them" and figuring out who or what to link my character to to accomplish that while still forwarding the story or ethic that I'm trying to produce with my character. This is why I lovelovelove Dogs fallout.

    I don't like tactical constraints in roleplaying. Being told, "Oh, you can't do X because of Y, so you'll have to figure out a different way" annoys the fuck out of me. Now, I'm all about that exact same thing elsewhere, in tactical wargames or boardgames, where I'm invested in the tactical situation, not the narrative situation. In RPGs, being told that my proposal cannot work because of the "reality" of the fiction kind of misses the point, in my mind -- if I'm not changing the reality of the fiction in play, I'm not playing.

    That said, a sort of 'middle road' where I might propose something and another player calls me on it and engages some sort of resolution system, especially one using the flags that we set up in building the game -- that's all good. "I'm not sure your hard-line patriot can let a traitor go like that. Roll your Duty to the Crown stat." That's good stuff.

    I'm really happy with the way The Roach funnels play while allowing crazy freedom:

    The game itself consists of six scenes, which you will chew through in about four hours. They are always the same six scenes, in the same order.

    You make four meaningful choices in defining your character. Each is selecting from a list of maybe a dozen options. It takes about a minute.

    In each scene you draw a card that tells you something you must do.

    So those are all pretty hard-core constraints - but that's absolutely it. It's enough to keep the game focused, but not enough to feel confining in my experience. To me it often feels like it amplifies or intensifies conflict to have hard and fast rules about the situation.

    Slight tangent...

    There's an idea in improvisation that a certain amount of constraint can spark creativity. For example: if I say "Think of a story, which has a rifle, a cloud and a bar of gold", then you'll probably think of something quite imaginative: more so than if I'd just said "Think of a story".

    On the other hand, if I'm too restrictive - "Think of a story set in a Elizabethan England involving a rifle which must never be fired in the story, a blue cloud and a bar of gold shaped like a small cottage" - then you won't think of anything good. Too much constraint shuts the creativity down.

    To answer John's question: I like to be constrained in the character that I play, because it forces me to play characters I wouldn't usually play.

    Graham
