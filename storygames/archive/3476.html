    Posted By: E.T.SmithEssentially, where is the story-game equivalent of the D&D Basic set, which told millions "this is how role-playing works" and was instrumental in giving RPG\xe2\u20ac\u2122s their early 1980\xe2\u20ac\u2122s boost?
    \nInterestingly, D&D doesn't even have the equivalent of the 1980s D&D Basic set anymore. The current basic box set is more of a board game than anything else and, judging from the number of confused newbies I've seen on the WotC boards, seems to do a pretty lousy job of introducing roleplaying concepts.

    The closest there seems to be to a good basic boxed set is "D&D for Dummies", but that's not really so much an introduction to the hobby as it is a book that tells you how to use the $100 worth of books you already bought.

    Honestly, I don't know how kids these days are expected to break into the hobby. The few under-twenty-somethings I've gamed with in recent years were either introduced to D&D by their gamer parents (which is freakin' awesome!) or somehow migrated over from WoW.

    What was cool about the 80s box set was that it represented an attempt to spread the hobby past its wargaming roots. You could buy the damn thing at toy stores, for crying out loud! That's something I'd really like to see today: a concerted effort to move roleplaying beyond geek culture.

    I can think of at least half a dozen bright, imaginative, creative people I've known over the years who probably would have found roleplaying to be an engaging and rewarding hobby--but, since they weren't into dragons, vampires, or space ships, there wasn't a game I could show them and say "here, you might like this." If there was a good Law & Order/CSI/Sopranos-style RPG, though, they'd have been all over it.

    Posted By: E.T.SmithWhat single, unified text do I turn to to explain to me relationship maps, scene-based resolution, flags and other such core concepts.
    \nY'Know, I was thinking about this last night as I was reading the rules to Legends of Alyria last night. Just to note I haven't played it yet, but am planning to in the next 2-3 months or so. But anyway, the game has you being preparation by creating a relationship map of various main characters. When you are done, you choose the characters on that map you want to play and stat them out. That's pretty hardcore "Story Gaming" there, and plus draws upon R-Maps to boot.

    The problem is that the text kinda assumes you know what an R-Map is (or can guess at what a good one looks like) and doesn't really show you step-by-step how to do it. There's a lot of other stuff that's sort of taken for granted there, too. But it's a solid example.

    And when I started thinking about Legends of Alyria, my mind wandered to two other games that will probably be released/in playable form/etc in the next year or so, which I totally recommend keeping an eye on (to see if you dig this style or not):

    Vincent Baker's Art, Grace and Guts. Like LoA: You roll on some tables, figure out What the Background is, What Will Happen, and Who is Involved. Then you choose characters from the list of Who is Involved, stat them out, and roleplay. At the end of the session, you check this list to see which characters Must be played in the next scenario, and which characters will be left behind. It's very clever. It smells like a gimmick at first, but it's a machine that produces cool stories.

    Clinton Nixon's Inuma. You create the setting together with your friends. Then, you create a pool of characters that populate the world. Finally, you go back, choose one, and play them in an open scenario. When you're done, you put those characters back in the box, then decide which ones to play next time.

    Please don't use this as an impetus to pester either author: :-) They'll be done when they're done. However, int he forms that I've played them, they are exemplary of what story games is all about: Maybe not all Flags and R-Maps, but definitely "Setting the Scope of the Story FIRST. Then make characters."

    \n This is something that has been on and off of my radar, so forgive me for prodding at the proposal a little.

    Core text by committee?
    If people outside the hobby are to sit down and read a piece of technical non-fiction about it, I believe they need to be guided by a personal voice. I certainly wouldn't have read the GNS essays when I returned to the hobby after 10 years away, if the text wasn't delivered via a voice that engaged me (in this case, Ron Edwards's). I believe that a genuine shift in the way you think about things, and remembrance of what you have experienced, benefits enormously from a voice, an individual, telling you these things.

    So, being a worrier, I have a bit of a worry that if half a dozen of us contributed to the Core Text, you wouldn't get that. Personal voice would be smoothed over in the service of consistency. Alternatively, voice would be piecemeal and flit from section to section. Those within the community might well enjoy those flashes of character - reading the Marginalia commentary in the Push 1 preview, I chuckled at exactly this - but that's a pleasure accorded by familiarity.

    An alternative, and one I would love to see, is a bunch of people having a crack at this independently, with the community/chums acting to support these individual visions. The story game movement has been very successful writing content using this praxis, and I really think it's the one to continue with.* I tried it over a year ago, more on the general overview than specific techniques, and although it's neither definitive, pinpoint accurate, or immune to controversy, I'm pretty sure it fulfilled my immediate needs, providing something to share with curious friends if my cup-of-tea pitch** has stirred them up.

    Game vs How-to
    I am absolutely certain that any Core Text must be interactive. I mean, absolutely. Just as a purely textual book about visual art could never act as an entry text to that medium, so should this contain hooks that demand doing, trying, shaping. I'm certain that Ben was not being flippant when suggesting the Pool, and agree that it or something like it should form part of any package that is trying to open up story gaming.

    Go you!
    My penultimate thought is that this is something that we could all really do with. I'm really heartened that so many people are enthusiastic about this; I was only energised to write my posts back when because my social groups were almost exclusively non-gamer at that time, and it pressed upon me to explain this new activity. Opening up the hobby = 100% awesome! I might be very well wrong in exhorting an authorial perspective***. I want to make sure that people are aware of the impact of approaching this kind of project in a particular way.

    Who, me? Yes, you!
    My ultimate thought is: if you want to write content for an intro to story games, consider where you are coming from. I wrote about my perspectives after 6 months of exposure to the games and a vestigial history of rpging, and it showed: in a bad way, that I was certainly oversimplifying or missing some of the variety of play due to ignorance, and in a good way, in that was fairly aware of what in gaming is novel and what is familiarity tilted on one side. If I try to improve it now - which I may still do - I must be aware that I may do harm as well as help the text, by making false assumptions or eliding what are complex issues, or worse still assume that more explanation is needed than the case.

    So, consider your experience! I'm not warning off the veterans by any means (though they should be extra cognizant of their position). I am inviting, urging the people reading who still consider all this malarkey pretty new to consider having a stab. If not now, then in 3,4 months or so. You are probably the gateway we all wish to be.


    *Hey, I'm not forgetting the awesome projects like cons, stickers, and books of names, where it really is a genuinely shared endeavour. They are great! Different to what we're talking about here, though.

      \n I'm glad that the Pool was helpful to you. It was helpful to me. It is also in, like, my top 10 fun games of all time.

      There seems to be an assumption here which I think is... very wrong-headed. The assumption is that there's this one bulk of techniques and all "indie games" use those techniques. This is wrong, and harmful. For instance, I'm demo-ing Polaris for some guys who are used to playing Dogs in the Vineyard. I go "all right, we've got to a conflict," and they go "cool, what's at stake is do I beat the demon."

      I go "wrong wrong wrong wrong I WILL GOUGE YOUR EYEBALLS OUT WITH A SPOON." There is no specific "at stake" in Polaris, there never was, and there never will be. Furthermore, if you graft in notions that you've picked up from Dogs in the Vineyard like "what's at stake is do I kill the demon", you'll kill all the interest and tension in the game dead immediately. And this really is a bigger problem than just these one guys I ran a demo for: I've seen people mutilate my poor game with it over and over. I've seen people mutilate Dogs by using PTA's stakes. I've seen people mutilate PTA by using passive GM assumptions from, I dunno where, Universalis I suppose.

      I can see where this comes from. There are two assumptions which I think a lot of people carry over from mainstream games. The first is that role-playing game rules are infinitely graftable: a rule which is fun in game A is necessarily just as fun when used in game B. This is true only inasmuch as your game is just a pile of rules and doesn't contain any rules which work together. The second is that all games basically use the same techniques. There's a lot more in common to between a good RIFTS GM and being a good GURPS GM than there is between being a good Dogs in the Vineyard GM and being a good Bliss Stage GM. In fact, if you try to GM Bliss Stage like Dogs in the Vineyard, you will screw yourself out of a good time, because the GM in each game has a totally different set of responsibilities.

      Let's take the Pool as an example, since it was linked earlier this thread. Go read it. See how, for instance, the monologue of victory rules mesh with the Pool size rules? If you ported (as many early failed attempts at the Pool children did) the monologue of victory rules straight into another role-playing game, without making them dependent on a resource pool, you totally change around what a monologue of victory means. Do you see that? Do you also see how it would be totally futile to, say, graft a Dogs' style town creation system into the pool? It makes no sense! Nothing happens!

      See how this makes defining shared terms tricky at best, harmful at worst?

      Protect your eyeballs!

      \n Hey, T-Boy --

      In terms of how I think as a designer (which is totally different from what a player would ever need to know to play a game), things like conflict resolution, scene framing, and so on are not techniques. They're questions.

      Example.

      Scene Framing is "who gets the rights to say what about what's going on in the fiction when we start it up?" This is totally different in Dogs (where it's non-existant and uses the traditional RPG technique of "leave it mostly up to the GM after she asks the players 'where are you going next?'") to PTA (where people request scenes in a formal manner and the GM then describes the action to her desires) to Polaris (where one of two players gets full rights to say whatever they want.)

      Every RPG has scene framing rules, or lacks them specifically.

      Example.

      Conflict Resolution is either "when two characters in the fiction come into conflict, what happens?" or "when two players come into conflict about the fiction, what happens?" And games have enormously varied answers to this, from "nothing special" (1001 nights) to "a complicated dice bidding process involving guns" (Dogs in the Vineyard) to "you roll the dice and then the GM makes something up based on the die rolls" (kill puppies for satan.) That's three different answers from the same household, to give you an idea of the diversity of answers here.

      Again, every RPG has conflict resolution rules, or lacks them specifically.

      There's nothing shared between these techniques. Looking for shared elements is actively confusing and harmful. It's much more productive to see how they differ.

      Posted By: Ben LehmanIn terms of how I think as a designer (which is totally different from what a player would ever need to know to play a game), things like conflict resolution, scene framing, and so on are not techniques. They're questions.
      \nYes, they are.

      When a group of players makes use of these techniques to alter a game to their liking, they are altering the design of the game. They are changing the answers to those questions. It is important that they recognize this, and that they make any alterations with this in mind. Because, to be plain, simply tossing in changes to one rule can have a "cascade" effect on other rules that are linked up by design and may not, at first glance, appear to be.

      Which means that if, f'rex, a given article on "Scene Framing" decribed a stack of different ways to frame a scene, mentioning each in the context of "a game that works this way", and went on to note things that each method does and doesn't work well with, and why...

      That'd be a damn sight more useful to such folks.

      I don't think that anyone can stop house-ruling from occuring, and protesting alterations to a game beyond saying "look, the designer is not responsible after this point" is bullshit.

      Does that mean that I think the designer has any responsibility to help people house-rule in constructive and useful ways? Absolutely not.

      But as a form of game support, that's cool stuff. Allowing that support to grow around a game - and even pointing it in a possibly fruitful directions - has given many well-reputed games a far more solid 'presence' than they might have otherwise had.
