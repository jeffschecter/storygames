\n A Poem for Mr. Morning is a new game I'm working on. It is a modern, contemporary game, set to the place you (the players) live. The players constitute the Morning-family; father and spouse at 50 in start of game, and children at 19-29. The game aims to investigate the fate of the family, from the celebration of the 50th anniversary of the father, and to his death by cancer 10-30 years later.

The game provide the players with pre-generated characters, and make use of the players first name as the first name of the character (to make it simple remembering and using the names, not to make them "play themselves" in the game). The characters got a handful of traits like; self-contempt, hard-working, poetic, sporty, etc. The traits are not set in a mechanical system, but used as inspirational elements in a method largely based on player intuition and interactive dialogue.

The game is played out with players alternating in setting scenes, by stating the season, the place, and which of the characters are present (you may set scenes with 2 to all characters present). The player setting the scene also is the one who may end it. The first scene is set by the game, with all characters present, and so is the last scene (the player of Mr. Morning got power to end these scenes). Each scene consist largely of a dialogue (with some descriptive comments), wherein the characters and their relationships are developed.

The players are quite free to steer the game as they see fit, within the framework of the method and the characters at hand. So this is largely a game building on the scenes feeding into new scenes, strengthening the relationships as play moves along, and making it all vibrate with more and more acuity. And all of it steered by the intuition of the players.\n
Sample scene:

Characters:
- Bent Erik (31, black-clad and rebellious rock-poet)
- Erlend (57, conventional family-man, and gay, stepfather of Bent Erik)

Scene set by player Erlend:
Erlend and Bent Erik in the living room, late autumn-night, in front of the telly.

Erlend: That was quite a film, eh?
Bent Erik: Oh, well ... jaa ...
Erlend (enthusiastic, clearly trying to bond with BE): So, what do you say about it? Makes Hollywood seem a bit bleak, eh?
Bent Erik: Maybe ...
Erlend (still lively): It's like; we have seen so many of these films now, and they really blows you mind! Makes you see film in a totally different way, eh?
Bent Erik (beginning to tire): Ja, it's been interesting.
Erlend: So, what do you say; ready for some more? Tonight? Got an English comedy-show for us to delve into ...
Bent Erik: English comedy?
Erlend: Ja, "All about Anderson". Great series! From the sixties.
Bent Erik (totally resigned now): Well, ok, we may see it ...
Erlend (smiling enthusiastically): Great!
(cuts through air with his hands, indicating the scene is over)
\nWe had first play-test yesterday. The game ended in tragedy, as Mr. Morning rejected his son trying to reach out to him (after years of quite open contempt), and then went down a spiral of self-loathing which ended in his bloody suicide (in the bathroom, cutting his wrists, found by his family while still alive, dying in their hands).

The game mostly did what I wanted it to do; to give the players a framework for creating their own version of the Morning-family fate, and for them to experience "the magic of being another ordinary human being".

I look forward to the new play-test this wednesday, with me as observer, and the planned blind-testing of it in the US (don't know the city) and in Alta (Norway).

Any comments on the game-theme are welcome!
- How do you feel about playing out contemporary human drama?

    \n Contemporary human drama: honestly, it depends on what type it is. I don't want to deal with the same issues in my entertainment that I deal with in my life. A game where a gay son tries to reconcile with a conservative father? That's not a roleplaying game, that's therapy.

    And I strongly dislike games that *tell* me to use my real-life experiences, for much the same reason. I don't find that it makes it easier; in fact, I find it much the opposite, because I'm likely to be playing with real pain rather than imaginary pain, and that's not something I care to do with relative strangers.

    Posted By: InaI really enjoy these kind of entertainment, but I don't think that playing 'contemporary drama' is fundamentally different than playing any other kind of drama.
    I really enjoy this kind of entertainment too, and really enjoy making games like this.

    Playing contemporary drama on common themes, though, is something that makes us bring our own experiences into the game, as a backdrop to our actions within it. Related experiences from real life will help us keep the interaction realistic, and this effect may be utilized by the game-smith. You do not have to, as a game-smith, feed the players so much of the fiction-world-basics in such games. So they do differ fro the more fantastic or strange genres. :-)\n

    LoL! Erlend was one of the play-testers yesterday (the one "Erlend" in my sample of play above), but I have never played his game "The Father" (at least; not to my recollection). I do not know if the games correlates in aiming at an uneasy feeling. My game is not singularly aimed at this, but uneasiness is a very obvious possibility with the elements given.\n
    Posted By: InaAnd if I may I'd like to ask you to tell me more about your game.
    Of course you may! I'm happy to answer questions about my brainchildren!\n
    Posted By: InaI see that the person to end the scene is the one that has also started it. But is there any particular condition for the scene to end?
    No. The player decides.\n
    Posted By: InaIs there a specific aim for the scene? Like \u2013 to reveal a new fact form the past, to strengthen or loosen the bonds between characters, to bring the situation to the point when something significant happens?
    The game gives you no advice on aiming any particular scene at anything. It gives you general advice about playing with a calm mindset, being positive and always emulate and extrapolate on the elements given by other players, even when these are details about your own character. And the games advice you to keep most of the scenes about everyday life, nothing spectacular. The details of the particular scenes are left to the players.

    One thing which came up during evaluation yesternight, was the option of advising players to intersperse the gameplay with some kind of "aimless scenes" (scenes with no correlation to the many plots that evolves during play), to vary the rhythm and give the players some respite from the intensity of the drama.\n
    Posted By: InaI'm asking about these, because setting the goal of the scene really helps me to understand, what the pace of the play should be and what kind of story are we going to create.
    I believe that by not helping you understand goals and story-arc, I will increase my chance of helping you delve into your character. I do this by writing gametext aimed at helping you focus on the feelings and conditions of your character, and aim at helping you, the player, to accept the unpredictability of fate. My aim is to have you come out the other side, with a kind of drama impossible to predict and difficult to understand, but totally recognizable and intensely satisfying.

    I prefer such a mode of play. Catering to and relying on the intuition of the players is all about this.

    I aim for simplistic design, to support complex drama.

    \n Second play-test;


    - five players (2 men and 3 women)
    - me observing on the side
    - 17 scenes all in all (including preset opening and end-scene)

    As with first play-test the first scenes was a bit lame; more chaotic than nervy, and players floundering about (due to weak game-text from my side).

    Further on the game started to produce scenes of beauty:
    - 7th scene: mother and daughter walking and talking in the park
    - 9th scene: mother leaving father at airport; going away alone, for 6 months; short farewell, tired mother
    - 11th scene: mother and daughter on cafe; mother has not seen father since she came home
    - 13th scene: father sitting alone in the night, on his veranda in Spain (simple and peaceful solo-scene)
    - 14th scene: father lost house in Spain, renting small room in family-home (which is owned by mother now)
    - 15th scene: bleak and coughing father walking from doctors with daughter, both silently afraid (might be cancer)
    - 16th scene: mother and two sibling discussing father (sick, lonely, needs someone). Mother not speaking to him.
    - 17th scene: father dying by cancer, family gathered around him, he weak and resigned; no more to live for, dies

    Two of the players did not get it; they ended up with much less emotional intensity and immersion than I aim for. Still both of them were part of some beautiful scenes and the source of some nice characterization (from my viewpoint, and from the viewpoint of other players). They fell victim to the intuitive method of the game, which was not communicated in the best of ways. These two players had intensity-arcs going the wrong way of the intended.\n
    Side-note: One of these players played my game "Lykke" (Happiness) last winter, and said this game had serious flaws in comparison to that game. I know that this game is more open, more dependent on the intuition of the players working in unison to create the drive of the drama. "Lykke" is equipped with a small mechanic to create the drive, so you get a lot more for free in that game. But this game is meant to be simpler in method, putting more responsibility on the players. So I will have to make this game the best it can be, without comparing it to other games.
    \nThree players had a stronger experience, though even them a bit short on both emotional intensity and immersion. They had intensity gradually climbing towards the end, as intended, but I would like to see an even deeper engagement in the game. One of these players was totally new to verbal role-playing games (had some larp-experience).

    Notes:
    - The game relies heavily on the players cooperating to frame scene, fill them, and finding the balance in their co-play, between invites and challenges. I have not addressed this point properly in the game-text. Will do!

    - Needs to underline the strong focus on PCs in the game. No NPCs are actually needed. Some statists will do nicely in some scenes, but only as narrated figures in the environment, not as active elements.

    - Needs to clarify that only two verbal modes are meant to be used in the game; dialogue in-character, 3rd person narrative for actions.

    - Underline the need to be short when setting a scene; no story-telling; only effective scene-framing! Go straight for interactive dialogue!

    Very useful game-session! Had lots of useful feedback from the players (a questionnaire and a discussion). Know more about what this game is intended to do now, and that makes me eager to write a new version (but I will wait for the report from US-play-testing ...).

    \n Third play-test;


    - five players (3 men and 2 women)
    - me participating
    - 11 scenes all in all (including preset opening and end-scene)

    This game started off quite nice, with tensions from the start, and then it heated up ...

    The scenes:
    - 2nd scene: Mr. Morning and his wife eating breakfast, very mellow and low-key
    - 3rd scene: 3 siblings driving go-cart, letting tensions show through roaring action on the field
    - 4th scene: Grandma dying, Robert the "black" talking to her, and father bailing out of the scene (tired). quarrel amongst the rest
    - 5th scene: Alejandra Morning talking with friend on the phone, about her "need to go away", Mr. Morning calling about his trousers
    - 6th scene: At the airport; Alejandra moving to Texas, tense farewell with husband, and "kind" son
    - 7th scene: Thanks-giving; "kind" son reading a poem, father destroying it by being drunk and miserable
    - 8th scene: Hike in the woods, but father bailing out, and being forced into a near breakdown (gets his pills, and flees, in the end)
    - 9th scene: Father and "black" son on the Detox-centre, father about to get released, calm, Robert talking about anger; father answering calmly
    - 10th scene: The daughter inviting her two brothers to help her in her new apartment, and them ending up in a fruitless quarrell
    - 11th scene: Alejandra Morning's 60th anniversary, in Texas, with a bunch of her new friends, great speaches from father and "kind" son, and then father falls over on the beach, and dies.
    - THE END -

    Strong experience for all!
    Clearly different character-stories:
    - Mom Alejandra getting a beautiful story about newfound freedom when escaping the family
    - Father Tomas going down as a bitter drunkard, and then raising from the ashes
    - Black son Robert being dark and cynical, but clear-sighted, and successful as a poet
    - Kind son David trying to mediate the chaotic family, while being stunted in ambitions, but redefining himself and finding happiness
    - Eldest sibling Veslem\xf8y working hard, trying to create more than her father and mother, and ending up loosing everything in her cramped materialism

    Notes:
    - The game needs a looser intro. Think I have the elements for doing that now.

    - The threshold of setting scenes in the game needs to be lowered even more. Will ponder that ...

    - Needs to lay out the options of "The Black Sibling" better. Robert played it very nicely tonight, and gave some great input on this. Thanks!

    - Must clarify the "poem-mechanic" of the game, so it is more of an option for the players (especially the kind and black sibling, who are both "poetic")

    Very strong and nice game-session! A narrative triumph! Touched us allk in lot of ways, with depth and beauty. Especially the small communications, un-said and half-said (which are hard to write into the scene-list), that filled the space between the characters, and fed into their future, were wonderfully rich this time.

    Had lots of useful feedback from the players (a good discussion, and answers to some pointed questions). Know more about how this game will become stronger now! I will do some changes to it in the coming weeks, and put it out there for blind-testing.
