Thinking about a layout contest

shreyas
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Story Games

In that other thread (I'll link later if no one beats me to it) I said that we don't do a lot of discussion in this community about the physical qualities of games, not in public places like this one at any rate.

So I'd like to propose a layout-and-design contest, in which the participants recieve a game text, in a plaintext format, and they are to do page layout and design for it.

I'm thinking about like January or February for this, once the dust has settled from other activities, although I think that since it's not going to call for people to do game-design, it's sort of a different-thing and could run at the same time as a game contest did.

So I have some ideas about how to run this---

  • When the date approaches, I'll be asking for text donations. I'll want to have a final set of like four texts of similar length, and each participant can choose which (or maybe 'choose from a pair') to lay out for the contest.
  • Contestants will have two weeks to work.
  • The final submission should include the following: Game with front cover and title page (in pdf format), Character sheet, Specs for maybe two illustrations. Maybe three.
  • I like Andy's rating model, gonna borrow it. However I think that participants will not rate designs based on the text they worked on.

I'd like to know about like, what I haven't thought of, particularly from such as Kevin and Andy who have run their own events.

    October 2006

    Okay. Here're some rules and constraints!

    You will recieve two games, from which you will choose one to lay out and design, as though for print publication. You're not actually laying out a full text, you're making a representative sample, an excerpt of sorts, so that we can get an idea of what the completed text will look like.

    You will submit:

    • A game text as pdf document.
    • The game text will include the full text you selected, plus a front cover.
    • You may optionally include a back cover, with back-cover copy of your own composition.
    • A character sheet, or other necessary/useful recordkeeping papers. All of these should be together in one separate document.
    • Specifications for illustrations. This includes three items; either three specific illustrations, or an art bible applicable to all illustrations in the text, and two specific illustrations.

    You may include:

    • Details of binding. If you don't then the default is assumed to be perfect-bound on the left, as a standard English book. If you choose a different binding, you must describe it and include a picture of what it should look like.
    • Details of paper.
    • Illustrations. These should simply be placed in the pdf where they belong. If you do include these, you're not exempt from the art spec, and if you get your illustrations from a publicly-accessible source, then include your source in the spec. If you don't include illustrations, then in your document, you should show where they would go, clearly, so that each position can be connected to a piece in the spec.
    • Suggestions for the author on how to improve the physical experience of the game, like, "You could make the table on page XX into a deck of custom cards instead," or "You can roll one pool of dice instead of three if you colour-code them." These must be separate from the main text.

    You may not:

    • Purchase art of any kind (page ornaments, typefaces, illustrations, etc.) for this project. If you already have art, then you may use it.
    • Materially alter the text. You can correct spelling and adjust phrasings to like make your paragraphs look pretty, but don't actually change how the game works.

    Your author can't afford, or simply won't pay for:

    • Die-cuts.
    • Full-colour books. You can use plates, but in this case the rest of your layout will be black-and-white. If you don't do four-colour plates, then you can have two spot colours for the whole document.
    • Colour and fancy hand binding at the same time. You can do Coptic stitch or Japanese stab binding or whatever you like, but then you are printing in black-and-white.

    That's all I can think of for the moment, trying to take into consideration all the questions raised upthread. The next thing is texts - as you see, the objects we'll be working with here are excerpts, and I'll cut them all down to the same length. Preferably, they will be texts that have not been made public before, so I'll talk with interested parties privately about that. Whisper or email.

  • Joshua A.C. Newman
    October 2006 edited October 2006

    Man. Luke's way better a book designer than I. I wish he was here.

    • Cleanness. How easily can it be seen? How well does peripheral information integrate (this is kinda my Big Thing. I love sidebars and other methods of 2Ding text). How easily can you find what you need? These are hard to do.

    • Shelf usability. Your book competes with other books on the rack at a store, and it competes with other games on your game shelf. It has to stand out in an appropriate way. It has to go through these phases on the store shelf:

    • 1- Grab the eye. This makes them look at it straight on, instead of it passing through your peripheral vision (which only sees contrast and movement, btw).

    • 2- Give a broad, iconic view of the content to interest your target audience. Guy in a space suit. Blood splashed over arcanely tooled leather. A superhero fucking a poodle. It has to communicate iconically with the audience you want to reach.

    • 3- Textual and other linguistic information that gives details that makes your audience want to open the book and see what it's all about.

    • 4- Beauty. Make someone want to touch it again and again, to leaf through it, and hey, then play it! Often!

    These happen in this order. Later ones can't interfere with earlier ones.

    4 there is kinda subjective, but there are tools that can push you in proven directions: the Golden Mean and Color theory are good ones.
