Naming the Boss: Traditional, Tasty, or Accurate?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nJason_Morningstar \n\n\n
\n
\n\nDecember 2009 \n edited December 2009 in Game Design Help
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n This is sort of dumb and played out as a topic, but I'd like your honest thoughts. So a poll! Choose one:

TRADITIONAL: You prefer Game Master, a title that is instantly understood based on years of general use.

TASTY: You prefer titles for this role that reflect the setting (Dealer, Superuser).

ACCURATE: You prefer titles for this role that are more reflective of what the person actually does (Guide, Facilitator).

Does the nature of the game influence your choice or do you have a universal preference?

I'm asking because I'm adding that role to a hack of a GMless game, so I have the luxury of approaching it with a clean slate. My instinct is to go for accurate over traditional or tasty. What do you think?

    \n Traditional. Everyone will call them a GM anyway, so call them a GM.

    I can occasionally go with Tasty titles. I like Freemarket's "Superuser" and Call of Cthulhu's "Keeper", for example. However, they can often be incredibly wanky: there are too many games that call the GM "The Company" or some such, even though people will rarely use that title.

    The "Accurate" category also smacks of author wank. However much you, as author, think it's accurate to call your Game Master a "Provoker" or "Ritual Leader", everyone else will understand that role as the GM.

    \n My preference is for Tasty. For example, in my game Slow Burn I've called the GM role the "Handler" as that's what they call the person who oversees or supervises intelligence operatives.

    The advantage to Tasty titles is that there's usually no significant pre-existing expectations attached to the title in context of the game; so you can define a Tasty title however you want to, with whatever role and responsibilities you need ti to have.

    It's true that out of habit, most will end up calling that role the GM anyway; but I still like the flavor it adds to the game.

    \n I find that I just end up calling it GM (not game master, but GM) no matter what it's called. Even with my own games (except with Trial & Terror, where I remain faithful to NYC).

    Which, I contend, is a vote for something other than GM. Those steeped in the tradition will call it what they like, no matter what you call it, and therefore there is no reason to pander to them. They know what to call it and don't need to you make up a name for it.

    It's those who want or need a name for it you should be catering to. And for them, there's no reason to stick to tradition. Tasty and accurate are far more useful in these cases.

    Confession: I only half believe that. In truth, I think the naming of the GM matters far more to the person writing the game than anyone else. It can help you find your voice and set the tone for your text. And it can surprise you. In Dread, I called the GM the host solely because I had no other abbreviations in the text and GM stood out like a sore thumb. Years later, Eero pointed out to me how appropriate that title was, as I had embraced the implications of the word and impressed upon GM the duties of a host, which definitely shaped the nature of the game for the better. By accident.

    Posted By: jchokeyWhat is actually at stake in this choice?
    \nI get the impression that this is the kind of question that's really only interesting to people who are writing a game; the people who are playing a game seem to pretty much call the job whatever it is they want to (and a lot of them just want to call it "GM" and be done with it).

    But I imagine that a game's author might want to invent another term just for the sake of inventing another term. Maybe just calling it "GM" feels like cheating, because it's an easy, obvious term that everyone already uses...while a "tasty" word would be theirs, all theirs! Maybe a new term makes them feel like their game has an extra-special mark of distinction. Hell, I don't know -- maybe they want a new term just because they're bored, or they want to see if they can make anyone say something like "Hollyhock God" with a straight face, or they honestly do think that they've come up with a name so cool that everyone will love it.

    And y'know, whatever their reason for wanting to call it something other than "GM," it's cool by me. It's their project, they should do what they want with it. They're the ones hunched over their keyboards writing the damn thing, I'm not going to say they shouldn't do what they want to with it.

    ...of course, I'm still going to call it "GM" when I sit down with my friends to play it, and not care at all about whatever fancy-shmancy new name the author came up with for the job. Because while I support the author's right to do what they want with their text, I also support my right to pretty much ignore anything in the text that I want to. Sorry, writers, that's just how this deal works: I buy your game and I get to do what I want with it, even if that's something you never wanted me to do.

    \n What's at stake is comprehensibility.

    Basically, there's a wide swath of stuff that can be called "GM tasks." If a role encompasses a chunk of this stuff, it can be called "GM." However, when there are additional tasks, or multiple roles each of which touch on GM tasks, things get more confusing. At some point (ex. Polaris) it becomes more confusing than not to maintain the GM language.

    The same is true of PC and NPC, by the by.

    This is something I struggle with in a lot of my games.

      \n I'm in favor of accurate, though apparently tasty at first glance. "Do" presently has two functional roles for players.

      When it's a player's turn, she is called the Storyteller because, more than likely, she'll be telling a little bit of a story in which her character does something awesome. This is very consciously not called role-playing, since it's assumed that the Storyteller is literally telling a story about the character, not playing the character herself.

      Meanwhile, the other players are called Troublemakers because, more than likely, it will be their job to introduce unforeseen complications and problems that arise out of the event the Storyteller just described. They introduce the Trouble that this character, or some other Storyteller's character, will have to overcome. This, in turn, may result in further problems that the Troublemakers will create as well.

      So, "Storyteller" and "Troublemaker" may be kinda frou-frou at first, but the titles very much describe what players do when they take on those duties in play.
