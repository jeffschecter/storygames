Is it wrong to love other people's misery?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nTonyLB \n\n\n
\n
\n\nOctober 2007 \n edited October 2007 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n I love fictional characters who are miserable and helpless in some vitally important way.
\n
\nNote: The "and helpless" bit is essential here. These aren't people who are miserable because they're causing their own misery. Those people are sort of annoying. The characters I love are morally blameless: there is nothing more that they could be doing to better their situation. The son who has done everything right but still can't please a demanding father, the geek at school who is constantly ridiculed by his peers, the captured princess held by an all-powerful wizard, that kind of thing. People with massive sadness beyond their control.
\n
\nI don't think that what I love about these characters is the suffering per se ... rather, I love to see the ways that they can rise above that suffering to do something important and valuable for others. The captured princess can't escape, but maybe she can find a wounded knight in the enchanted forest, and keep him hidden until she can nurse him back to health. HE can escape, even if she is powerless to do so. The idea that, even though her life is really hard, the princess can have the strength to worry about others ... I find that quite powerful.
\n
\nAnd yet, I can't deny that what that leads me to is telling stories about people who have far more suffering in their life than I do. I've heard people talk (with contempt) about the concept of "misery tourism" ... spoiled, pampered bourgeois who have never really suffered, playing at hardship in order to get a frisson of emotion and catharsis. That doesn't sound so nice.
\n
\nIs there a line there? Or are these the same thing?

    \n I think that there is a line that can be drawn, but it's a hard one to see. All stories have conflict in them, it's part of the definition. Stories exist to make statements about values (even really complex ones like "this sort of person is both horrible and yet somebody who we would otherwise admire.") To the extent that we use misery in such stories to train ourselves on how to act well, it's fine to use the misery in question as a force to move us to learn. In fact, that's why we have stories, and not just training. Who wants to really charge that hill, if they don't have to? But, if you haven't been trained to charge the hill, and trained from a moral/ethical standpoint, you probably won't charge the hill. You learn to charge the hill by watching John Wayne do it, and admiring him, whether or not he survives.
    \n
    \nAll religion is based in these sorts of stories from our holy texts. If Noah can weather 40 days and 40 nights, and still keep his faith, then we should too. If we feel his misery in reading about that, and then realize how righteous Noah is in his perseverance, and this moves us to have some fraction of Noah's strength of character, then that can't be a bad thing, can it?
    \n
    \nOn the other hand, the "misery tourist" isn't really interested in learning, but is really just so bored with their life that they read Noah's story just to feel the misery, and to learn nothing from it. To the extent that a "story" has little or no value statement for the observer to consider (even possibly to reject, if they decide that they don't agree with the author), it may well be misery tourism.
    \n
    \nAnd I'll be judgmental enough here to say that people who do this really need to examine their life more closely. As Shreyas points out, I don't think that they're hurting anyone else in most cases. But I think that they're hurting themselves. Sadness can have a purpose, but without purpose I think it's just a good road to depression.
    \n
    \nObviously, Tony, your examples are showing clear signs of values - you've gone out of your way to specify this - so I don't think you should worry.
    \n
    \nThis makes me think of the show Six Feet Under, which is about the saddest subject of all: Death. In fact it is nothing but episode after episode of dabbling in the misery of those who have lost people in their lives. It is a courageous endeavor, to me, in that this is a subject that is never, ever approached with this much honesty. For myself, I am incredibly lucky enough to have never lost anybody that I've loved. Not one person. But having watched Six Feet Under, though obviously such an experience can never compare to the real event, I hope that when I do lose somebody, that I can experience the event with at least a little more dignity or equanimity than I would have if I had not seen the show. It has made me think about something sad that I would otherwise have shut out, and I am the better for that.
    \n
    \nWhether or not you're learning, or just touring, is something that each individual is going to have to decide for themselves. And it's not really conscious much of the time, either (this is what makes it so powerful a learning tool), so discerning whether you're learning is not always easy. But if you make a conscious effort to do some evaluation of the message of the story, that's probably a good way to ensure that you're learning for certain, and not just livening things up in a sad way for yourself.
    \n
    \nThis is what works for me, anyhow.
    \n
    \nMike

    \nP.S. the "and helpless" part is the beginning of horror. There is something slightly horrific about the princess trapped in the tower. Horror stares straight into the eye of misery as hard as possible, and asks the hardest questions (what do you do if you have no good choice?). Horror movies that don't ask these hard questions really feel sick to me. Afficianadoes will say that these are the most "hardcore" of all, and what I think they're saying is that it's a challenge to endure them, given that they are all pain, and no gain.

    \n No, Tony, I'm not really sure why that's troubling to you. I could speculate that it might have something to do with the way you restated it sounding like I'm saying that Sim players are insane or something. But that would be a minsinterpretation. Basically I have a very, very low tolerance for what constitutes a "Thematic Statement," if we want to go with that term. That is, I think that players who are playing sim are actually usually doing so with a gamism or narrativism bent, but doing so in a way that masks that bent (doing so in order to preserve what they find most important in RPGs, which is the appearance of the "reality" of the created world... perhaps immersion by some definitions). This is my version of the Beeg Horseshoe.
    \n
    \nAs such, even the most simmy of players are either:
    \nA) Involved in sublimated Gamism, in which case they are learning from the misery how tough they are as tactitians. Or
    \nB) Involved in sublimated Narrativism, in which case they are learning from the misery what each other feels in terms of values.
    \n
    \nPut another way, I think that the "misery tourist" is probably rare, and, yes, may be at least neurotic (definitely not neccessarily psychotic). The bar is very low, in my mind. I think that most everyone who watches horror movies, or plays RPGs about sad stuff, or whatever, is actually doing it "right." The fact that you can even worry about it, to me, says that you can't possibly be one. The few that I've met would think it was odd even to consider the possibility that it was somehow internally damaging to themselves to be engaged in such activities.
    \n
    \nThis makes me recall one individual I knew who had a different, but possibly related attitude. He loves horror movies, because they make him laugh. The more horrific they are, the more the movie tries to make you feel for the character (especially if it's syrupy), the harder they make him laugh. I think for this individual that it's an overgrown defense mechanism or something. Interestingly, he claims not to know why he laughs at this stuff. But I would guess that he doesn't want to feel the horror, so instead of associating himself with the protagonists, he completely dissociates.
    \n
    \nAnd as Mel Brooks once said, "I get a papercut, that's tragedy, you fall down a manhole into a sewer and die, that's comedy." So in this individual's case, I think he doesn't want to learn anything "the hard way" by feeling the protagonist's plight - especially the helplessness - and so he finds these things to be comedic. The Misery Tourist doesn't want to learn anything, either, but they revel in the misery anyway, because they relish the misery itself.
    \n
    \nIf you're looking for a more stringent standard, one that says that even if I'm learning something that it's still exploitative... I dunno. Maybe. There's certainly the question of humility. If the person doing the learning isn't doing it respectfully that's potentially problematic, too. Are they really learning? Like if I think, "Gee, that stupid Princess, why doesn't she just escape herself," and that's my take-away from the example story in question, that might be problematic.
    \n
    \nBut if I'm genuinely learning in a humble way from the story, I think I'm pretty much beyond reproach.
    \n
    \nAs an example of actual play, I was at Origins last year and playing Conspiracy of Shadows with (amongst other people) Clinton. And he had his character do something so truely awful that I balked when he warned us prior to having his character do it that it was going to be vile and should not be taken to reflect on himself as something he'd condone. He probably didn't have to say that, given that we'd understand it implicitly, but it was so vile that he had to preface the act out of character, just in case.
    \n
    \nBut I took it in, and saw it in the light it was intended, and most certainly "enjoyed" it not as a vicarious thing, but in the spirit of the theme we'd created which was something like "Secrets Can Destroy a Family."
    \n
    \nAs a good counter-example, consider the cases of PC/PC rape that occur in some play, those that the player of the recipient reports being incredibly bad experiences. I can hypothesize about play that I could accept where my character is raped by another, if it's in the process of making some sort of important statement. There are potentially good reasons to have such an event occur. But one can sense if it's just the other player getting their kicks off of it, and it's beyond bad taste to being what I'd call an actual assault of sorts.
    \n
    \nThat might be beyond misery tourism in this case. To just reveling in causing somebody misery.

    Posted By: Paul BI'm not sure youcanmake a formula out of it, Tony. Do you have a particular subject in mind? Or is this all theoretical?
    Wellll ... lemme explain why I'm sketchy about drawing a line in that way, and maybe that will explain why I prefer to look for somewhat more objective criteria.

    Basically, it comes down to this: Your standards mean that anybody who cries outrage and offense is automatically and always correct. Doesn't matter if they have the real world experience ... it's not about their authority, or any of that jazz. The mere fact that they are offended means that the material was (ipso facto) offensive. This makes being offended into a massively powerful trump card in any social situation.

    That's a perverse incentive. I'm not particularly fond of perverse incentives. It's also a positive feedback loop: as more things become considered more offensive, it becomes easier and easier to view yet more things as offensive, because taking offense has been so wholly legitimized. I'm not particularly fond of positive feedback loops. I'm really, really not fond of combining the two.

    This isn't even vaguely hypothetical. I've seen this sort of pattern destroy real communities, both real-world and online, with alarming regularity.

    Can there be any practical application of the "don't offend anyone" way of drawing the line? I'm honestly skeptical. I think that the mathematics of the system mean that its use, no matter how well-intentioned, tends toward a death-spiral of intolerance and censorship.

    I want to respect people, I don't want to trivialize their experiences, but I think everyone's gotta draw that line based on their own judgment of the material and the history.
