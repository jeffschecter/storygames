\n Some time ago, I was watching the silent film fanedit of Phantom Menace and mocking it because the editor just didn\u2019t \u2018get\u2019 silent films. Half the screen time was spent on absurdly long-winded intertitles.

My thought was, what would a game be like where none of the players are allowed to communicate dialogue except through the medium of writing? They can communicate action as usual, but the only way their characters speak is by putting pen to paper or chalk to chalkboard.

The big questions: would you even consider playing this game, and how would it change the gameplay?

    \n Primitive: the characters cannot speak.
    Penny For My Thoughts: speaking and acting are equally restricted.
    Nightmare in Silence, from Blood Brothers 2, I am guessing = what I am talking about.

    My take is that by forcing players to divide their attention between actually playing the game, writing out brilliant dialogue, and reading what other players have written, dialogue becomes a resource to be managed. Therefore, I would expect this rule to cut out all of the idle chatter and bread-haggling and whatnot in favor of dialogue with real impact. On the other hand, unless the players converse amongst themselves to reach consensus, each character would be more likely to act independently. Overall, I think an intertitle rule would make for a very pulpy, high-action game. I think if I were designing such a game, I would want to reward players for using this rule to the best effect, similarly to the way most games reward action over talking.
