\n It seems ever since Deadlands came out there's been no way to game the western genre without including at least some weirdness, even if it's as mild as making the native spirits a real force. I'm wondering just why this trend came about and if it's worthwhile to even try reversing it.

Certainly there have been new weird western developments, like Dogs in the Vineyard, so I'm not laying all the blame at Deadlands' doorstep. The weird western subgenre had been around for decades before Deadlands even appeared on the scene. It just seems to be the thing that drove the weird western forward in the gaming realm.

The thing is, though I see the appeal in weird western roleplay, I have a stronger affinity for westerns played straight. No vampires, zombies, werewolves, Cthulhoid creatures from the outer dark... none of that. My ideal western game hews closer to the model established by classic (and not so classic) Italo-Westerns of the late '60s, early '70s. There's plenty of meat there, what with vicious outlaws, crooked landowners, bounty hunters, gamblers and gunfighters crossing each other's paths. Why does it seem to interest so few players? Or is the pull of magic and monsters just that much more powerful?

    \n The classic Western has several elements, which have been subverted so often that the subversions themselves are classics. Here are is my summary:

    * Authority is nonexistent except through the action of strong men. These men nay be heroes and villains, but the application of violence is the only legitimate authority and the only relevant action.

    As I say, there are plenty of subversions. Shane, a gunfighter, is almost paranoiacally respectful of a weaker rancher that he admires. Strong women appear from time to time to take their share of the bloodbath. As the West gets less wild, law replaces the vigilante and the vigilante often doesn't like it much, or only admires it grudgingly. And in some cases, the "strong" part of "strong men" is subverted by having a con artist or dandified "hero", often for comedic effect (Maverick, The Apple Dumpling Gang).

    There are other implications: the natives are irrelevant except insofar as they are hostile and may project violence that threatens the heroes. (EASILY and often subverted.)

    There's a bunch more, I might be able to think of them later, or others could chip in.

    I loooove Westerns.

    \n One common reason why genres get weird-i-fied is that it opens up additional character niches. It becomes easier to have a party of characters who each have their distinct spotlight. In Deadlands you can have your huckster, your priest, your mad scientist, and your gunslinger, and they all have a decent system-supported chance of shining during play. If you scale it back to have only 'realistic' characters, then you have to be more creative about finding easy ways to make sure that all of the characters are distinct. Note that much of the source material with westerns is about lone protagonists, or maybe groups of two or three.

    Now sure, you can have a game where everyone is a gunfighter and they're made distinct by their personalities, their ties to the community, their attitudes toward the natives, ad infinitum. I don't doubt that virtually anyone on this board could make that work. But if you're taking a bunch of D&D players and trying to get them to play a western game, and then tell them that everybody has to be a fighter... well, I think it's understandable why games layer on the supernatural or other weird vibes.
