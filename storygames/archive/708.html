"Inspired by this thread":http://story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=694, I was thinking about space opera again. Any ideas on how best to compromise between science (relativity, physics, etc.) and the fun aspects of space opera? Got any fiction or games that has done this well already?

Here's some features I'm used to:

* You can have a ship that can go between worlds for mercenary purposes.
* It can be staffed by a relatively small crew, and run as an independent operations. It is not prohibitively expensive. (In other words, analagous social and economic structures as seen in Firefly and Traveller.)
* Travel between worlds is feasible; travelling from A to B does not mean that you've moved 500 years into the future and everyone you know is dead.
* Cultural, you can treat a world of people as analagous to a single country or territory in our modern sensibility.

Of these, I definitely think the fourth is expendable (and should be expended) but I'm not sure about another good mental model of what other world will be like. As for the other three: I feel like they're pretty important to much of the space opera _feel_, but I'm really interested in alternatives.

    Another concept:

    You've got a Von Neumann machine rocketing through space. It shows up in a solar system, begins carving up asteroids/terraforming viable worlds, and then seeds it with DNA samples. (Actually, a Von Neumann probe could probably store the human genome, artificial wombs, and the sum total of human knowledge pretty easily. So, it could set up an actual colony of its own.)

    Then the probe builds a bunch of copies of itself, and sends them off to the nearby worlds. Within 300,000,000 years, it will have colonized the entire galaxy. This sounds real complicated, but all you gotta do is build one probe and send it off. The rest takes care of itself. (This is one version of the Fermi Paradox: if aliens exist, why aren't they here already?)

    Dev: jump gates aren't entirely bad science--but they'd almost certainly have to be a remnant of a precursor civilization.

    Kip Thorne's last chapter of "Black Holes and Baby Universes" works out the theory a little bit. You've got these things called wormholes, which are tiny little shortcuts through space. Wormholes generally exist on the quantum scale, and even then only for a super-tiny amount of time, because any amount of matter, like stray subatomic particles, passing through pinches the wormhole shut.

    But if you could thread the wormhole with strands of exotic matter with "negative mass," it would prop the wormhole open, and you could gradually widen it. There are two difficulties here: one, nobody knows what "negative mass" means or how we could make it; two, if we could make it, it would probably be super-difficult and super-expensive. By which I mean, we'd probably be so powerful that we can't conceive of meaningful human drama at that scale.

    But you could set up something like an ancient, long-vanished civilization that established those things as a transit network. It's not bad science, it's just crazily speculative.

    Heh@Matt...

    If you have enough engineering and energy to be able to throw ships around the solar system, you've got enough engineering and energy to terraform the moons of Jupiter.

    For example, let's say you wanted to warm up one of those icy Jovian moons and melt yourself water ocean. Simple!

    Jupiter is about five AU from the sun. That means that it gets about 1/25 as much sunlight as Earth.

    You take a huge quantity of aluminized mylar reflector, set it up in orbit around Jupiter so that it focuses the sunlight onto one half of the moon's surface, and in a few decades you get a nice warm water ocean.

    Ganymede's radius is roughly 2,600 KM. This means that the area of its cross-section is around 21 million km^2. You'll want a reflector with about 700 million square kilometers (7x10^8) because the stuff has a bolometric albedo around .9... you lose a little bit of heat in the reflection.

    Aluminized mylar under current industrial conditions is about 7 g/m^2, or about 7,000 kg per km^2. This means that the reflector would weigh around 5x10^12 kilograms.

    A modern oil supertanker has a mass of around 200 million kg (2x10^8). This means that all you need to do is have enough industrial capacity to build and ship the equivalent of 2.5x10^4 or 25,000 supertankers. That's not inconcievable.

    And if you want to do the same closer in, like in the Asteroids or Mars orbit, it's even easier.

    For FTL to work in a good SF it needs to have a set of laws, which while not necessarily compatible with physics express limitations on what that drive can do (much like magic in a good fantasy game). They you can explore the universe that emerges from the results of those limitations.

    Consider GDW's jump drive. Communication is lilmited to the speed of travel and the edge of Empire lies two years away from the fore. The result is to create something remniscent of the Roman or British empires where local governors must have power to make decisions but too much power leads to rebellious provinces.

    Larry Niven and Jerry Pournelle have a pretty good system with the Alderson Drive in their Future History/Mote In God's Eye books with a failry readable pseudo-science explanantion - gravitational fields distort the fabric of space creating canyons down which ships can travel at FTL speeds. Access points to hypersoace are limited to the edge of gravitional fields of stars - allowing space battles. The conciet of the Mote in God's Eye comes from the fact that the motie homeworlds jump point to the nearest system exits within a star.

    This is the mechanism of soft SF - put humans into a different system and see how their society would develop. It is stronger than soft SF but more open to adventure.

    The other rule of soft SF is you are only allowed a limited number of 'breakages' to what we understand now. Two is the usually quoted number, though they often seem to end up as a half-dozen. But limit yourself and define what is broken clearly.
