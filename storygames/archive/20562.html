\n I just put this together, and I don't know if it's useful to anyone, but I'll put it here in case anyone wishes to read it. It's mainly for myself to refer to, later.\n


1. The *World game basic procedure of play is a semi-structured conversation: we take turns talking (but not precisely so - there is no set order or tracking of how many times a given player may speak).

2. Players, when it's their turn, can either (or both) a) ask the MC questions about what their characters know or what they can sense, or b) say what their characters do, think, or feel.

3. When it's the MC's turn to talk, she makes a move (typically a soft move, to set up a harder move). [Using some variation of the AW list of moves, which is fairly universal.]

[Note: I'd consider the "workspace" move fundamental here, as well. Some variation on, "name the requirements, and ask if they wish to try another way", should cover it.]

4. The MC's choice of moves is guided by her Agenda, Always Say, and Principles. (Roughly, "Why", "How", and more specific constraints.)

5. Most play is mechanically "freeform" (within the constraints of the above four points). For exceptions, see below (6 through 10):

6. There are subsystems also called "moves", which are triggered in specific fictional situations. Some of these "moves" have an immediate and specific effect, described in their text. Most others involve dice, as described below (7 through 10):

7. Diced moves are for players only, not the MC. These moves involve rolling 2d6 and adding a stat (stats range from -2 to +3).

8. Outcomes are classified in three categories, 10+, 7-9, or a miss.

9. On a 10+ or 7-9, the player and/or character generally retain agency, often picking from lists. 7-9 is usually an unpleasant choice which brings complications into the situation; 10+ is usually unambiguously good. (Generally, the roll either accomplishes or does a good deal to advance the PC's agenda, in either case.)

10. On a miss, the MC makes an appropriate hard move, either based on the fictional situation or encoded in the move's description. (In comparison to the 7-9 and 10+ results, it's usually the case that the PC's immediate fate is no longer in the player's hands, until the MC has played their move and it's their turn to talk again.)

X. "Case-by-case" modifiers to the 2d6 roll are generally not used; it's considered more appropriate to adjust the stakes - risk harsher consequence on a miss (for increased threat/difficulty) or sweeter success (for decreased risk/difficulty) - instead.

I've marked the last one "X", because it's not really fundamental to any given *World game's design. However, it's a pretty strong part of the *World ethos, and true in the vast majority or *World games. For many people, it's a defining feature of *World games.

    \n I'd say your 7 and 8 are not important to me, and 9 only partly.

    It's true that many, probably most, PbtA games keep these conventions, but they are by no means necessary. E.g. Undying (which uses no dice at all), Dungeon World (DM rolls monster damage dice).

    For 9, I'd say that giving a lot of agency to the player about how moves resolve is a key factor, but the details about what rolls are good and bad is not.

    Also, I'd add moves snowballing. That is, whenever you resolve a move, things in the fiction change and move on - you don't get stuck trying the same thing again, or rolling and being in the same place you were before.

    \n Truly vital things (in order of importance) when fleshing out the skeleton:

    1. The particular Agenda, Always Say, Principles, and Moves for the MC.

    2. The details of move construction.
    * How they mediate the conversation, and who they give choices to.
    * How they drive forward a developing fictional situation.

    3. Lists of fictional content. Typically/traditionally, these are "playbooks" (and other lists, like threats, names, and so forth) which form the basis for the fictional content and colour of the game.

    (If you're writing a hack, I would start by dealing with these three things, in this order, and then start playtesting as early as possible.)

    \n I'd agree that those three things are what make an AW hack (though I think I've heard Vincent say that Playbooks aren't important, you could just make up characters with stats, skills, traits, etc.) but I don't always follow them in order.

    For example, the hack that's absorbing my time at the moment, Apps, is inspired by The Fades: that was the starting point in the process, but from there I went straight to roughing out a couple of Playbooks I wanted to see in that kind of setting. As I wrote the Playbooks, the shape they took told me what the basic moves had to be, e.g. if The Hushed was very good at going unnoticed, then there should be a basic move that enables that, so avoid conflict was born.

    As I created more Playbooks and moves, it became clearer to me what the game would be about, but at this point I still haven't written down my Agenda & Principles: they're there in the design fog, somewhere, but I haven't distilled them into actual written words yet.

    It's good to have a procedure, but sometimes you just have to write what's in your head, throw it into some kind of shape and hope that the pieces can be made to fit together without having to backtrack too much or even start over at the beginning.

    \n "Snowballing" doesn't always interfere with retrying because consequences of actions can take a different direction.
    For example, suppose a party of space marine PCs has the primary objective of killing a cyberdemon in a Doom adaptation.
    Marine 1 goes for a direct BFG hit from a distance, but he misses badly because a stray shot from some imps behind him hits him first.
    The MC's answer is that as a consequence of this miss the Cyberdemon remains undisturbed enough to shoot a rocket at Marine 2, who's closer and more exposed than Marine 1.
    At this point Marine 2 has a new item on his todo list (evading the rocket), but Marine 1 can shoot his BFG again (even if turning around to mow down the distracting imps might be smarter than trying again)

    \nI think you could still argue for snowballing as part of the skeleton, mostly as such:

    It's not just that the MC is told to make a move on a miss, but that the MC is told to make a move which escalates the situation and keeps the story going. That's the common factor behind the lists of MC moves in the best PbtA games. If you stop at "the MC makes a move on a miss", that move could be anything, including non-snowbally stuff like "you don't unlock the door; would you like to try again?". I would say that every PbtA game needs to use MC moves which change and evolve the situation, so that on a miss, things snowball.

    \n What about the settings?

    I noticed that the *W games I am familiar with (Monsterhearts, Dungeon World, and Apocalypse World), all seem to be \u201csoft settings incorporating player ideas supported by genre expectations and handouts\u201d.

    To unpack the above statement, imagine a line with hard settings (where everything in the setting already exists [hardwired] before the game starts) at one end. Near the other end of the line are the *W settings. Most of the *W setting doesn\u2019t exist before the game and much of the \u201cbuilding out\u201d of the setting is done by the players. The coherence of the setting (how it all fits together well) is assisted be strong genre expectations (everyone at the table has a good idea what could happen in the setting) and by what is written in the handouts (playbooks, common moves, etc.).

    Monsterhearts\u2019 genre is \u201cyoung adult urban fantasy\u201d such as \u201cBuffy\u201d, \u201cTwilight\u201d, or \u201cThe Vampire Diaries\u201d. Dungeon Worlds genre is what I would call \u201cD&D Fantasy\u201d. Although this genre is not as well known in popular culture, it is very familiar to the RPG set. AW\u2019s \u201cpost apocalypse wasteland\u201d is a little more open genre, but the handouts and the emphasis on scarcity help with the coherence.

    I am unsure if the settings of other *W games are of the same type, so I don\u2019t know if this idea is part of the skeleton.
