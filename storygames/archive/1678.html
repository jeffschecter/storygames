An epiphany about ADRP resolution

Vaxalon
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Play Advice
I just had an epiphany about the ADRP system, and why people like it.

Players don't see any difference.

D&D Player: "I rolled a fourteen, with my +2 sword that makes sixteen. Did I hit him?"
GM, looking down behind his GM screen, and pretending to check the AC: "Hm, yes."

ADRP Player: "My warfare is 32. I also have better armor and a horse. Can I beat him?"
GM, looking at his notes: "Hm, yes."

Players have much the same experience playing ADRP as they do with a GM who's fudging.

GM's like it better because they don't have to lie -- they're playing it by the book.

Just another reason why recruiting from existing hardcore ADRP fans makes no sense for my Amber LARP rules.

    Some Hardcore ADRGP player, and they exist, though their numbers are in question literally play _nothing else_ but the DRPG and variants on it. Anecdotally, this is frequently accompanied by assertions that other systems are too fiddly, complicated, or whatnot, and that they don't want to learn any new system.

    For these players I'd extend Fred's realization a tad and say that playing the Amber DRPG is functionally indentical to playing a game which you do not wish to understand. To wit, the guy who knows D&D is going to put some meaning in the roll, but the person who just wants to play their character and simply rolls what they're told when they're told could really be using any system, and if that's their goal, the ADRPG serves them very well indeed for the sheer amount of stuff they don't need to keep track of.

    This is certainly not all Amber players, but this particular segment is probably the hardest sell for Fred's ideas.

    I think that to some extent that's an issue with the nature of the playstyle it's trying to cater to. I'm not comfortable using the term illusionism, because I'm not 100% on the term, but I mean that whole "ask GM for permission, he tells you what happens" thing with a dose of "pay no attention to the man behind the curtain" GMing techniques. That style's pretty much incompatible with any kind of system that restricts GM authority.

    See I reckon that the core of Amber's system is that playstyle. The mechanics of the game - what looks like a system (highest stat wins) - is not the important bit of the game's system. It's a red herring. Just a tool to provide a baseline for GM judgements and (maybe) a handle for players without providing so much structure that it can challenge the GM's power.

    Being overruled by GM whim is the mechanics working as intended, because it trusts (requires!) the GM to be making the right decisions rather than (helping the GM by) telling them what those right decisions are. It's a minimal support structure designed to facilitate that play style that I'm not sure I should be calling illusionism. The meat of the system is the knowledge of RPGing that you bring from your prior experience, and all the rants and advice about what "real roleplaying" looks like and how to be a good GM in the book.
