\n Just a totally way early reminder that TusCon is coming up in a few months. Last year we had three SG members run games at the con. (Alexander ran two sessions of Fastlane, Jason ran True20 and Capes, and I ran Lacuna and PrimeTime Adventures.) If we can get more SG folk to show up, we'll totally take over the gaming room!

If you haven't been to TusCon before, it's a Very Small Sci-Fi Con With A Gaming Room. The dealer room had eight vendors, one selling a small section of games and RPG books (a few in the True20 line). The gaming venues weren't even mentioned in the program. When the gaming room was at it's busiest, they had six of fifteen tables with games running; five of them were RPGA events, one of them was was one of us running a game. A few RPGAers tested our non-D&D games, which was pretty cool.

This year, I'm thinking of running another game of PrimeTime Adventures and Dogs in the Vineyard. Maybe not Dogs. Definitely PTA.

    Posted By: Thomas DPosted By: Peter AronsonI've been preparing an OVA scenario
    \nOVA? What's that? I'll probably slap my forehead when you answer, but all I can think of is the anime term.
    That's because it's an anime RPG. The name stands for Open Versatile Anime, and the confusion is sort of deliberate. It was designed by Clay Gardner, and first published in 2003.

    Anyway, OVA is a cute little rules medium-light system, built around the Ricochet system, which has a point buy system where abilities scores and skills are treated exactly the same. It uses a fairly standard opposed rolls and roll vs fixed difficulty system using a modified dice pool where you choose the highest value, but add up duplicates. It has a nice assumption that characters are fairly competent, even when doing something they don't have specific skills for. The recommended GM'ing approach is fairly traditional, but it is a system that seems like it could be adapted for other styles of play.
