    \nThey meet on the magicstar server in channel #indierpgs
    \nI, personally, don't think that the IRC medium is better or worse for many games (cards are hard to do, but the dice roller on magicstar (and others) is exquisite). But, I do think that good gaming on IRC depends on the quality of the group. More so than FTF. Some of the issues I have seen are:
    \n1) Chat lag - People not sure if someone is AFK or typing something big or...
    \n2) Cross Chat - two people responding to the same narration out of order
    \n3) Attendance - It's hard to get people to show up on time...
    \n4) The "Anonymity" factor - sometimes people will say/do stuff in chat they would enver do FTF
    \n
    \nSome things that are really great
    \n1) Nobody "does voices" via text
    \n2) People can do longer, more flowery narration without getting interrupted, etc.
    \n3) With chat logging, you can easily keep a record fo what happened.
    \n4) People can play at more flexible times, since they don't have to show up anywhere, get dressed, etc...
    \n
    \n Good luck man!
    \nDave M

    \n I've played most flavors of White Wolf games on IRC, Exalted being the current (going on 4 years soon O.o) one. Both freeform (eh) and in dice-based weekly sessions (yay)
    HQ (same game as Dave!)
    The One Rule (mostly freeform)

    I keep thinking of other campaigns and then going "oh... no.. that was white wolf too..."

    My list of good/bad mirrors Dave's basically.

    I'd add to the issues:
    5) Complicated combat takes longer to resolve due to response times between people.
    6) Typing speed - some people are really really slow at typing. Either everyone waits for them (this is the nice way) or they get lost in the shuffle and whatever they were responding too has scrolled off the screen by the time they post.
    7) You can't split the pizza bill with people who aren't there. Some people consider it to not be a social activity without everyone being physically present in the room. (see also, Attendance)


    and the greatness:
    5) Ability to have IC and OOC channels - this is wonderful. You can discuss mechanics and make off-color comments without getting lost as to where you were in the game, or really interrupting the flow of IC stuff.
    6) The game fiction can be read back over easily to refresh memory, share with others, or just enjoy as a story.
    7) Dice scripts simplify/speed math (tells you how many successes automatically, etc.) and you can program new ones to do rather elaborate things if you are so inclined.
    8) I find immersion (by which I personally mean right now, 'getting into the character's headspace') much easier without having the actual people there to be dissonant with my mental image of their characters. Also I'm more confident in doing more description without feeling akward about it.

    \n Me and some other deviants have been playing Panty Explosion on IRC since June last year. 31 sessions in 4 arcs so far.

    The main problem I see with IRC-gaming is timing, something that's been pointed out above already (typing speeds and multiple replies to the same thing). I haven't really got a solution to this kind of thing; it's the same thing around a table - the group has to develope their sync over time - it's just that it's a lot easier when you can use eye contact and signal stuff through body language.

    With Panty Explosion that issue kind of goes away as soon as someone grabs the dice. Once you start a conflict everyone gets a chance to act - we've been using the alphabetical list over the characters that's automagically created by just being in the same IRC channel. Does someone wanna make a response action? Just wave your e-hand. Rinse, repeat.

    A strong point with using Panty Explosion for IRC-play is that everyone in the group can still be involved even though only a few of the characters are "on screen" in a particular scene: a soon as someone initiates a conflict another player will have to narrate (best friend narrates success, rival narrates faliure). This way no one is completely left out - just make sure the friend/rival-connections are evenly distributed, anything else would just be dumb.

    Finally; using a dice bot has proven extremely handy for this game where you have pools of dice that are drained and refreshed frequently. So... thumbs up for Panty Explosion online!

    \nI've played a couple of WoD games this way.

    The biggest problem is attendance and focus. I have no idea how to surmount them. Since it's online, people tend to act more casually - commiting with less forethought, dropping out with less warning. There's also distractions of food, bathroom breaks, family members around, etc. There's less of a "game space" for individuals, so interruptions occur more easily. Perhaps givign people concrete times in which to create a space to set aside might be good.

    Another issue is typing over each other. You can develop codes to use to state "I'm done now" or "I'm not done yet" like ellipses, with perhaps a two or three entry maximum (they can continue past, but others can interrupt). For action and task level games, it's a bit of a bear.

    I've only tried the WoD ones and although I think games like HQ, Polaris, and such might work better, the idea of gaming online just puts me in a "no gaming is better than bad gaming" state of mind. And I used to use IRC as a social tool, but RPGs online just didn't seem as social.

    \n Polaris works very well on IRC. I played about four sessions. The biggest obstacle was scheduling. We had West Coast players and someone in France in the same game, so we would get together when we could for a long session (like 6 hours). It just didn't work out.

    There never really was a problem with typing order or anything like that. The game was a lot of fun.

    We also ran a one-shot of Don't Rest Your Head -- that worked really well too. In that game we framed scenes aggressively and kept the scenes short. This seemed to help with the inherent slowness of play-by-chat.

    I don't mush as much as I used to -- it just feels too hard to commit to the time that seems to be expected. I'd really like a game where dropping in occasionally would work, because I like the sense of a persistent world and asynchronous play happening on a mush.

    For what it's worth a Spirit of the Century mush (Victorianesque Mars) is under development right now, and I have designs simmering for In a Wicked Age and The Shadow of Yesterday.
