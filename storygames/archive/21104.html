First, here are the relevant pages from Kutulu by Mikael Bergstr\xf6m [BTW, to pronounce his name, think "Strawberry" not "Bird", and make the "g" silent if you want, it's a very soft palatal approximant rather than a hard plosive] (CC-BY-4.0 2017, my [Sandra] translation):\n
Kutulu said:

To find oneself in fisticuffs, or\u2212God forbid\u2212an armed duel, is definitely something all civilized ladies and gentlemen with a modicum of self-preservation stay away from, to the greatest possible extent. Regrettably, it happens that the investigations the player characters undertake puts them in situations where there's no other choice.

When someone initiate force in any form, they have the initiative and acts first. Whoever is portraying that person state the action they intend to perform.[... Kutulu-specific skill names elided ...]

People who are attacked will want to perform a counter-attack or at the very least try to avoid being hit by the attack aimed toward them.

When attack and possible counter-attack or defense has been performed, the initiative passes to the next person who is yet to act or react. When all participants in the battle has been given an opportunity to act or react, the initiative is reset and everyone is yet again free to declare actions.

This means that the battle is divided into rounds, in which every participant shall get an apportunity to act. Because of that, the in-fiction length of these rounds vary, but it's seldom longer spans than a second or two.

Sometimes it can get chaotic when many participants wants to declare actions at once; at those times, it's the game masters role to as quickly as possible untangle the mess and decide who attacks whom.

The advantage of acting early and jump on the initiative is that it gives you some control over who your opponent will be in the battle. The disadvantage is that you, when doing so, lock your action in and become vulnerable to being attacked by another participant. Every participant can only perform one action or reaction per round.

A declared action can not be undone. If it turns out to be based on a misunderstanding of the situation, the player character is assumed to have made a corresponding miscalculation in the inescapably stressful nature of battle.

If a player hesitates too much this can be counted as they conceding their character's action for the present round.



Fig. 1. With graph paper the game master can easily keep track of who has acted. In the figure shown, Chester and "kultist 2" will get the opportunity to act before Patricia or "kultist 1" gets to act again.

\nSo, my thoughts on that... first, there's a remarkable degree of "coldness". To initative violence only has disadvantages; in Kutulu, you can only defend yourself against one enemy at a time and the others get free shots at you (and are likely to kill you, given the damage rules on the pages that follow). On the other hand, you lock up your opponent too and prevent them from damaging your comrades.

Second, it's not as easy to port this protocol to D&D as I first thought, because A. D&D has four action types (action, reaction, bonus action and movement -- this could be fixed by instead of one square, I'd use a 2\xd72 square, if I could find paper that looked like this: ) and B. in D&D, defending yourself doesn't cost an action. And if I'm going to keep using the "Players roll defense roll" variant, it'll get very chaotic, and C. D&D doesn't have the same coldness because there's not simultaneous "deal damage to each other"; you have to survive to be able to deal damage back.

But what can I salvage from it?\n
  1. The rule that who ever initiates force diegetically \u2014 whether NPC or PC \u2014 acts first. Yes, this leads to a "speak fast to win"-situation, unlike Kutulu which mechancially rewards being cold. Not sure how to fix.
    2. \n
  2. Very un-raw, but: if your action is "Dodge" you can do it "in advance", spending your action in advance to get advantage on all defense rolls.
    3. \n
  3. The rule that if you're attacked, you then can "reply" right away iff you have any main actions, bonus actions, or movement left. That could mean attacking someone else, disengaging, fleeing etc.
    4. \n
  4. The limit of one action, one bonus action, one reaction and one movement per round is sacrosanct; if you've already spent your action, you have to wait until the next round to spend another action. To track this, we have:
    5. \n
  5. A grid paper with the PCs listed in initiative score order, room for monsters to be listed underneath.
\n
In other words, this is the precendence hierarchy:\n
  1. The "first-talker" (can be PC or NPC) if they haven't acted in this turn yet.
    2. \n
  2. Anyone who has been attacked this round and haven't acted yet
    3. \n
  3. Who has the highest initiative score and hasn't acted yet
    4. \n
\n(Monsters of similar type can attack in groups. E.g. "Five of the skeletons attack you!")

Defending (i.e. just surviving the attack normally), you can (obv) do out of order (unlike in Kutulu) and it doesn't cost any actions (unlike Kutulu).
Dodging, you can do "ahead of time"
Reactions, you can spend out of order, once per round.

    \n Here is the rules doc I posted to them. Contains a lot of the same thinking and ideas as in this threads OP.     Messy Initiative System

    \nThis is nerdy and not something any player needs to understand. It's provided here for the Spikes & Mels only. If that's not you, just don't worry about this text, the turn will flow to you normally.

    OK, all gone? Just us rules nerds left? Good. Here's what I'm thinking:

    To keep track of the action economy, every one is listed on a grid in this order: First, super-bosses. Then, the PCs and their henches in initiative score order (normally 10 + dex mod, but things like Remarkable Athlete and Jack of All Trades increases the initiative score). Last, all the pretties in no particular order (they all have a line of their own). Their initiative score doesn't matter.



    The columns are the combat rounds. If you spend your action, move, and/or bonus action, that's marked with a forward slash. If you spend your reaction, that's marked with a backward slash. Once you've spent your action or your reaction, you have to wait until the next round to spend that type of action again. That's the action economy.

    Now, in what order does everyone get to act?
    Surprise rounds, which only matter when one side is hidden from sight and actively, sneakily ambushing the other, are handled first. That doesn't apply to normal "You're talking to someone, and suddenly they attack!", that doesn't trigger a surprise round. But sneakingly attacking from the shadows does. When the surprise round's done, the following applies to both PCs and pretties:\n
    1. First, who first diegetically initiated force? Who shot first, Han or Greedo? They get to act first. (Once an initiator has been selected for the fight, there's no use "talking fast" to get to act sooner. It's only making the very first blow that makes you "initiator".)
      2. \n
    2. Then, who's been attacked already this round? They get to act before anyone else.
      3. \n
    3. Third tiebreaker is list order, top first.
    \nPretties can attack as a group.

    Rolling a normal defense roll doesn't cost any action and you can do so whenever you're attacked, even if you've already spent your action.

    When defending, you can if you want spend your action, if you already have it, to dodge. You'll get advantage on the defense roll and all other defense rolls and dex saves for the rest of the round. If you dodge, make your movement and bonus actions right away.

    Now, those are the rules. I put them in a flowchart but when I did, it looked super complicated. But here it is, just to have a canon representation of my thinking.

    How this came about

    \nI've been wanting a more "conversation"-like initiative system for years, where things just "happen" without rolling up a list. And at first I tried colored disks and I tried a little circle and I've tried all sorts of things. At the con game this March I just had everyone sit in initiative score order and we went around the table. (Simple, but feels like a board game\u2014but maybe that's a positive thing? If this doesn't fly, that's what I'm going back to, just following the static initiative score always.)

    Then I read the game Kutulu, which inspired this system. There are a couple of differences. First, Kutulu doesn't have any reactions (or bonus actions) like 5e has, you get your one action and that's it.

    Second, in Kutulu, defending isn't free\u2014you can spend your full action to counter-attack. So if Alice attacks Bob and Bob counterattacks, they would roll simultaneously and the winner would deal damage to the other and the losers would deal no damage. And they've both spent their action for the round.

    So that means, in Kutulu, that if you get attacked by three skeletons, you can only defend against the first one, the rest get to do damage to you with no opposition. (But damage rolls have about a \u215b chance of rolling zero damage, depending on the skill level of the monster.)

    This also means, in Kutulu, that there is no benefit to quickly shouting "I shoot first!" because they will only counter-attack you back and then you can't defend for the rest of the round. Because of that, there's no need to have an "Initiative score" which I've kept from 5e, but moved it to a tiebreaker.


    Obviously I like 5e:s action economy, where defense is always free, more. Kutulu's system makes sense for it, because it's about scholars who are looking for clues and sometimes make a mistake and stumble into a monster nest. In 5e, people know what the hell they are doing so they can defend themselves.

    \n I designed and tried something like this hacking World of dungeons. After about 10 sessions with it I can attest that:

    -Going by who speaks first works perfectly fine. You see, in VtM, players who get highest initiative do go first but benefit from knowing everybody else's actions. Here is the same thing. Yes, the player going first acts first, but his actions create opportunities for the next and so on, balancing things beautifully in this mess.

    -I wouldn't mix it with 5e action economy. 5e is beautiful as it is in its pristine order and turns. This works best with a PbtA system. Messy turns call for messy actions, to do it do it and if you roll under the TN the GM makes a move. Rolled a 2? then the DM move affects EVERYONE (like an area attack, reinforcements arrive in 1d6 rounds, enviromental threat activates, etc) Does that mean PCs or NPCs get two actions on a round? More or less, it's actually like a soft threat, then a hard one and another soft threat, maaybe another hard one and that would be it. Without damage rolls it would look like:

    \n WM, the point of this system is to have the pristine turns and to keep the standard action econ, but in an order that's more likely to have response windows consolidated and to more often mesh better with typical diegesis.

    Greedo: I've been looking forward to this for a long time.
    Han: Yeah, I bet you have.
    Han, to DM: I shoot him under the table!
    DM: OK, AC 11.
    Han rolls.

    I wanted to encapsulate my thought process so that any question of "who acts first" is simultaneously strictly formalized while also having all of the appearance of being free-flowing, seamless\u2026

    Just "to do it, do it", but with "invisible rules" under the hood. Perfectly in line with my "Project Bathwater" design philosophy of strict rules that the DM has to obey but that are invisible in play. Also hopefully meshes well with finchian trapfinding and with my more formalized take on lawsian petitioner/granter talking.

    The goal is to have it look and sound very freeform but under the hood be very rules- & heuristics driven.

    Bergstr\xf6m's guidelines in Kutulu were an amazing catalyst. Weird that I haven't been able to figure any of this out on my own, it's been all Finch, Laws and now Bergstr\xf6m. But I take to it like water in the desert.

    Your counterexample neither has the action economy maintained nor the under-the-hood heuristics engine.

    \n I've done something kinda like this before in 5e! Used it for about a year-ish and it worked really well. I think it went like this:

    1. Whoever starts the fight, in-fiction, starts with the initiative.
    2. On your initiative, you take your whole turn as per normal, action + bonus + move.
    3. If you attacked / were aggressive to someone, initiative passes to that character.
    4. If you didn't try to harm anyone, initiative passes to whomever you like.
    5. If initiative would pass to someone who's already acted this round, they pass it to whomever they like.
    6. Once everyone's acted, a new round starts: this just means everyone's available to receive the initiative again, and they get their 1/round reaction back.

    \n I used the popcorn system for a game that ran about 2.5 years ago to 1.5 years ago. It was a kinda high-fantasy 5e. After that we played an Old School Dungeon Delving Post-Apoc 5e, which used "phases".

    The rules are here: Core Monsters Adventures Treasure

    The round is divided into four phases, which are resolved in order each round. Within each phase, all action occurs simultaneously.

    1. Magic & Miscellaneous
    2. Missiles
    3. Movement
    4. Melee

    Within each phase, everyone commits to actions they're taking that phase in the free-and-clear, and we all roll simultaneously. A lot goes unsaid; we rarely mention who our target is, players rarely mention what their hirelings are doing; they just roll the dice and announce hits.

    (To speed that process along, I usually announce monster AC, and assume they're focusing fire in the most efficient way possible. And we roll damage only for PCs, not hirelings and monsters.)

    Each person still gets the same Action, Bonus, Move, and Reaction each round.

    If you totally catch the other guy off guard, if you burst into action before he has a chance to respond, you can get a free surprise round, in which you can take a single action, bonus action, or move.

    It's super fast. I'll definitely use this again when I go back to this style of game. I gotta say it worked way better than either popcorn or default 5e initiative, for us.

    This game just concluded; we're playing board games & one-shots while I try to wrap my head around how to run a game like Silmenume's APs from the Forge circa '06.
