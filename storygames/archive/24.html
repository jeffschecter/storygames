Ah, but what can Modern RPGs learn from MMOs?

Andy
January 2006 edited January 2006 in Story Games
OK, so that's Rob's topic turned on its head, but still, it's something I think about every now and again. I've tried various MMOs (usually during their demo periods), and have stuck around for too long, they just aren't my thing in their current incarnation. Maybe one day. Anyway, after a binge session of the single player game Morrowind, and thinking about the popularity of MMOs, I've loosely tossed around some things that MMOs bring to the table that maybe our RPGs and Story Games could learn from.

MMOs: You can play ANY TIME you are inclined: Someone's gonna be online.
Face to face gaming, sadly not so. The instant gratification of being able to log in anytime can be effective countered by having powerful, fun sessions with friends once or a few times a month. The hold MMOs have is a strong one, but I think that with attempting to improve the play experience (through rules, styles of play, play advice, whattevah) people will hold RPGs up as "limited, but (hopefully) quality time". This isn't so much a "thing to learn from" as a "thing we're working against.

MMOs are fucking visual as fuck
I can describe what my supers character looks like, or I can build him in beautiful, exquisite detail with the CoH chargen engine. MMOs, with their graphics, really can make us feel the grandeur of a palace, the desolation of a desert or wasteland, the danger of fighting on the edge of a cliff, the rush of flying through the air.
What can we learn from this? I'd like to discuss.

MMOs are generally easy to get into
The rules for chargen and the underlying system can be understood pretty quickly for players of all ages. The tutorials that you go through in both online and offline games like the Final Fantasy series (where it literally coddles you through your first few battles, revealing more of the system as you go) is certainly a lesson for making a game: Perhaps don't reveal the entire system to the players on the first die roll, but rather have a structured series of events in the first gaming session which gradually expose various aspects of the system. Set it in stone, so that a New GM could run New Players through it, and have the same experience as people on the other side of the world with experienced GMs and Players.

Some MMOs focus on boring fucking shit
Like in Ultima Online, spending hours digging minerals to eventually sell or make dinky swords with of your own. Fuck that, that's not something we need to learn from... or is it?
First, I think it teaches us to keep Boring Shit to a minimum.
Second, I think it speaks to player authorship of the world. If some kids will spend 10 hours virtually digging iron out of a virtual cave wall just so that they can make a dagger with its own cool, unique custom look and feel, this "crafting" is just as powerful in face-to-face games. What if in D&D you let the player convert a small number of XP into building unique weapons or spells without all the messiness (finding a smith, making skill rolls, etc), after which you get to describe them to the group and use them to advantage in play. That sounds like a game that "crafters" would be inclined to play, more than a game where all the treasure is found or given.

Shared Experience
RPGA "Living XYZ" games are basically MMORPGs before MMOs were invented. Now, with the existence of MMOs, I dunno... Living D&D-based games feel like they're in the shadow of MMOs. However, through them, they can experience the same events together: John in Seattle and Mary in DC can both go through "The Evil Temple in Evermeet" and discuss it online.
Likewise, shared events and places between players is a source of conversation, sharing, discussion and fun.
Sometimes, just the setting and background provides that cross-group sharing: In Exalted, did your players visit Gem? Did they attack the Great Wood Temple? In Vampire, did Caine show up in YOUR game? etc
Sometimes, they are tied closer to the gameplay, like in Dogs and their towns (you can create your own on the fly, or take one of the many online and use it): In Dogs, did your dogs go to Whitemore? How about Hell's Hollow? How did they defeat the false priest?
Sometimes it's the scenarios themselves, like those one-shot RPGA events, or Luke's legendary demos. Who did YOU play in The Gift?
Finally, I see the shared experience really set off in games like Jason's THe Shab al-Hiri Roach, where there are 6 Set Events, and Set NPCs. That limitation is freedom, and whatsmore it becomes a major, major shared experience for ALL players of the game. What happened to Regina Sutton in Your game?

There's got to be more things that MMOs do that can make us think more about RPGs, and how to make them more effective at punching people in the face with accessible fun...

-Andy

    Another thing you have in MMOs is status as a real (and sometimes easily measurable) resource. Why do people spend real money to get crap in the game instead of playing the game to get it? Status. It's the Lame->Lords progression that people were talking about in the other thread. I can be cool now instead of being a schmuck for x levels...for a real dollar price.

    I think the lesson for tabletop is that people will often take the easiest road to a reward even if it is detrimental to their fun. One of the designers for City of Heroes once noted that if there was an MMO that gave you xp the fastest if you just did nothing but press the space bar for hours, 80% of the players would do only that and then complain that the game was boring...even if there was a ton of more fun stuff to do in the game.

    As a designer or GM, it is worthwhile to look at your game and see what you are rewarding and whether the behavoir that gets rewards also makes the game fun/better.

    MMOs utilize the downtime

    I was discussing with Chris Chinn how MMOs still have mechanically active stuff happening to your character while you're not logged in. WoW has an EP bonus, EVE Online lets your skills power up (eliminating the common level-grind), and Dofus lets you regain a hard-to-obtain resource if you log off while your character is in special areas. This makes good business sense for MMOs as it keeps members hooked, but it also helps in games intended for long-term play over several sessions.

    The key point is that it's not a reward for not playing, it's a reward for playing again. I think PTA does this in a story-game way through "Next Week On..." It gives you something to look forward to when you come back to the game.

    Looking at Andy's statements...

    MMOs: You can play ANY TIME you are inclined: Someone's gonna be online.
    This is actually a bigger issue I think as MMOs become an alternative for the busy professional thirty-something gamer who has a wife and kids and doesn't have a lot of freetime. Online gaming is just so much more convenient. However raiding with 39 other people, even on a Vent server, is nothing compared to the jokes and banter around a gaming table. As much as I hated the ads ran by WotC last year taking a none too subtle stab at WoW, they were right. Its better to be together even if it is a tad difficult. The payoff is that much better.

    MMOs are fucking visual as fuck
    I think this has a lot to do with consistency and ownership. Imagine a game where you are all playing Orcs? All WoW players will be able to spot an Orc or Tauren outpost a mile off because of the architecture (even that one as you enter Desolace that gets new players everytime...). So have the players define some of the visual aspects of the culture. Let them really get into it. Maybe have visual or textural reference - a piece of leather or some bark - so that you appeal to a number of senses. I'm not quite sure how you can capture the immense vistas of EVE Online with this method but you might be able to.

    MMOs are generally easy to get into
    I think a game that has an underlying single mechanic works well with this statement. Remember when (and I could be wrong here) D&D 2nd Ed had one roll where you had to roll high on the d20 and another where you had to roll low? THACO and Saves I believe. Thats just silly. One unified mechanic allows for a quick and simple introduction to the game whilst slowly unfurling the new stuff. Note this doesn't necessarily say that the game cannot have complexity.

    Some MMOs focus on boring fucking shit
    MMOs are time sinks and generally there will be a mechanism to reward those with lots of time for being online. They also become increasingly about minute incremental gains in power. So sometimes you can convert time into power. However there is the other phemonenon - the crafter - the person who just gets off on it. I believe that this attracts people because it allows them to have a fuller 'character' within the world. They aren't just a warrior for the Horde, they are one of a few skilled blacksmiths that can craft the UBERSWORD OF DOOM! That in itself give fame on any server. Maybe it's those extra bits that your characters do in a larger face to face rpg? The deeper things that round out the character?

    Shared Experience
    I think that MMOs give players a common language to work with and a common frame of reference. I've done it in these answers, using examples that really only other WoW players would understand. I've got dozens of casual acquaintances that I can barely remember the second name of but we can have intense conversations about druid PvP strategies or the correct way to tank Ragnaros. RPG experiences rarely have that common ground - moreover when this happens we rarely care. How many times have you seen people being mocked for sharing stories of their character or their game? The silly thing is that those people have unique tales to tell, whereas the MMO player will have a very narrow range of experiences at their disposal. I'm not sure how we could deliver that commonality of language to RPGs and retain their hetrogenous nature.


    I have a theory however, based on the experiences of my playgroup. We played 'traditional' rpgs for many years and then we played WoW a lot and then we turned to story games. Why? I believe that WoW does D&D better than D&D. It takes the essence of the wargame soul of D&D and rips it out and upgrades it. If you take that stuff out of D&D whats left? Character and story. Smaller, quicker games filled with character and story. Sound familiar?

    MMOs: You can play ANY TIME you are inclined: Someone's gonna be online.
    This is one of my design goals with the Year We All Died. Because you play characters that are mostly communicating with each other through phone calls, emails, blogging, text messaging or other forms or remote communication you don't need to be sitting right next to someone else to lay the game.     Two ones that are already kinda "duh" around here, but are exemplified well by anecdotes in MMORPG design:


    People will ask for crap mechanics they're familiar with; don't listen to them

    Not mechanics they like. Just mechanics they're used to. Resting and overlong recharge times used to be standard in every fantasy-themed MMOG. When the new wave of games rolled out, people actively bitched about not having those elements in games, even though most of them found the actual mechanics really boring. Then they played them for a while and stopped wanting those things.


    Follow the resources

    Take a careful look at the resources and currency in your game. Whatever is an effective way to get them is what a lot of folks will be doing. For example, if the best way to get rich or high-level in an MMOG is to do something mind-numbingly repetitive, people will do it and then tell you your game sucks because it's mind-numbingly repetitive, even if you gave them the option of doing something else less boring that would earn them a smaller reward. Similarly, if the most efficient way to get treasure in an MMOG is to grief other players (this is a popular trick in games that permit PvP and make you drop your loot when killed -- players can get hours' worth of farmed-up loot out of a single fight), that'll happen a ton, too. (Story-game example: I think most of the coolest TSOY Keys are the ones where going for the big 5-point reward or the buy-off results in cool dramatic stuff -- heck, all of them were basically designed with that goal in mind.)
