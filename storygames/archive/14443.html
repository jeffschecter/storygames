    \n I've used a track, with a counter for each Player Character and a counter for the pursuers. It shows the distance between players and pursuers.

    You can also flip between sneaking and chasing. On a failed sneaking roll, it becomes a chase. On a successful hiding roll, it becomes sneaky again.

    \n In the Jungle, you can't tell how far away your pursuers are, unless you stop, climb tall trees and look for encampment smoke around dusk or dawn. Any noise you might here could be your enemies getting closer, or monkeys or a new danger. Talking to the locals is very important but they might not be too friendly or truthful.

    I think using the Dark Continent ploy of having an empty map which you fill in as you go is a good think. You might know the start and end point, that some rivers are in the area but not their exact courses, whether hills end in gentle slops or cliffs.

    I'd have various set scene such as the rope bridge, the short-cut, the ruined temple. I'd encourage calculations of distance travelled for self and enemies, trying to guess the map ahead from what is known and how to take advantage of the terrain.

    \n A track as suggested above could help. You could also have some maneuver available. Stuff like 'diving into cover', 'making a hard right' etc. If you succeed at those meneuvers, you get a big advantage, or perhaps go into 'sneaky mode', where the pursuers are still combing the area but aren't exactly sure where you are (again, taking a cue from the above suggestion). If you fail, however, the pursuers get dangerously close.
    \nIf you're in a chase, you'd need some special meneuver to get into sneaky mode, carrying with it a risk of losing valuable distance. Perhaps you can't attempt those maneuvers unless you're at some distance from the pursuers. And when you're in sneaky mode, maybe you'd need to successfully make some consecutive hiding rolls for it to be over.
    \nMaybe if you fail your hiding roll by a little, one guy notices you - and you can try to, I don't know, shoot him dead before he manages to alert his buddies.
    \n
    \nIn the track, you could also have two markers for the pursuers. The first marker represents just the fastest and smartest fellows - if they catch up with you or find you, you can still fight them, and if you kill them quickly enough you can continue running away. The second marker represents everybody else - if that catches up with you, you're surrounded by a horde of people, and you're screwed.

    \n We really liked the Chase Sub-system in Empire of Dust. It did not give the players choices, however, as it's a completely random roll on a chart. Dependingo on the outcome you either gained 1 "point" towards escaping or 1 single round of combat occurred at a distance determined by the chart (short range, melee, long range, etc.).

    It was tense because it was random and you weren't sure if that hulking beast was going to get a turn to swipe you in melee. It did seem to take too long to escape though as the chances of rolling a point towards escape was somewhat small.

    \nI like this. So in effect you arent treating chasing as different from any other dramatic situation. That is - complications, decisions and consequences. If there are no complications or decisions, then the fastest party always wins. As a rule of thumb, I think its best to concentrate on the slowest party - they are the ones that should have to decide to escalate if they want to stay in the race.

    i.e. "Their legs/car/horse is faster than yours -- they are catching up/getting away ... what do you do!!!!?"

    I think it also depends on who is chasing who, you might ant to handle it differently. I mean, if the PCs are chasing an NPC, its usually going to advance the story if they catch him, but if they dont, its been much to do about nothing. whereas if the NPCs are chasing the PCs, it could be interesting either way. Generalisations of course.

    \n 'Old Timey Crowd'? No respect for your elders

    The 007 RPG is, to this day, one of my favorite games - largely because of the chase mechanics. It's a bidding system, where each of the parties ratchets up the difficulty of the maneuvers. James Bond 007's core mechanic is the Ease Factor, which determines how difficult a roll will be - chases involve each side bidding for ease factors, which decrease with each successive bid (kind of like Name That Tune). Once a side is no longer willing to push the ease factor lower, the winning side determines who goes first and both sides use the lowest bid ease factor.

    It's a remarkable system which perfectly captures the tension and fun of cinematic chases.

    \n For a d20 experimental game I ran years ago I cam up with one I really liked, but will likely need tweak for whatever system you may be using. But overall it works well and is easily adaptable to most games.

    Dudes in the chases have some sort of skill in it right? Ok that is how many d10's he will get. You can get extras for narration, car, help, etc. Roll those, they are now your pool.

    Take a deck of cards as is. Shuffle count off at least a number of cards equal to the characters skill rating. Modify as you see fit, but the number of cards presents the difficulty of the chase.

    You a come upon a turn, and ( draw a card, the value on the card is the number that they have to come up with out of their pool to keep up). The face cards create some sort of random effect. The chases is lost once they have lost by a certain amount, the chase is won if they make it through the deck.

    Face Card Effect Ideas
    Jack - Combat action (dude in the car pulls a gun on you)
    Queen - Did you lose him? ( something happens and you think you might have lost him, draw one card from the chase deck and one extra from the unused deck, take the higher of the two)
    King - Blow a tire (GM rolls a die, if there is a die of that value in the pool it is removed)

    Modifiers
    Wide Open streets ie desert (can fail by up to 10 before failure)
    Rural streets (can fail up to 5 before failure)[Failure amount also represents damage that your vehicle may take]
    Head Start advantage (sometimes you know he is going to run, adjust the deck as you see fit to reflect any advantages you may see)
