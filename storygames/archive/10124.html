    \n I had to look up what the 4E rules were for starvation and thirst. For reference, here they are:\n
    Posted By: 4E DMG page 159
    Starvation, Thirst, and Suffocation
    When deprived of food, water, or air, the rule of three
    applies. An adventurer can handle three weeks with-
    out food, three days without water, and three minutes
    without air outside of strenuous situations. After that,
    such deprivation is a significant test of a PCs\u2019 stamina.
    At the end of the time period (three weeks, three
    days, or three minutes), the character must succeed on
    a DC 20 endurance check. Success buys the character
    another day (if hungry or thirsty), or round (if unable
    to breathe). Then the check is repeated at DC 25, then
    at DC 30, and so on.
    When a character fails the check, he loses one
    healing surge and must continue to make checks. A
    character without healing surges who fails a check
    takes damage equal to his level.
    In strenuous situations, such as combat, going with-
    out air is much harder. A character holding his breath
    during underwater combat, for example, must make
    a DC 20 endurance check at the end of his turn in a
    round where he takes damage.
    As with environmental dangers, a character cannot
    regain healing surges lost to starvation, thirst, or suf-
    focation until he eats a meal, drinks, or gains access to
    air again, respectively.
    A character with 0 or fewer hit points who contin-
    ues to suffer from one of these effects keeps taking
    damage as described above until he dies or is rescued.
    \nNote that a character with 10 healing surges can thus go two weeks without water, even if they fail every endurance check. A real character will last longer.     \nI think you would be wrong.

    "At the end of the time period (three weeks, three
    days, or three minutes), the character must succeed on
    a DC 20 endurance check."

    This is what triggers the first check.

    "Success buys the character another day (if hungry or thirsty), or round (if unable
    to breathe)."

    This is what success grants you.

    "Then the check is repeated at DC 25, then at DC 30, and so on."

    This is what the DC does when you re-check and when you make that check. Since the immediately preceding statement specifies that "success buys the character another day," this is the implicit time period to re-check. Re-checking immediately after a success is contra-indicated.

    "When a character fails the check, he loses one healing surge and must continue to make checks."

    This is the consequence of failure. Since the time period for re-checking is one day, and since the failure block does not define a new duration, either explicitly or implicitly, we default to the existing re-check duration.

    The alternative that you present would also be outside the precedent of similar mechanics. Compare, for example, these rules for environmental dangers on the same page:

    "The endurance skill determines how well a char-
    acter can withstand such dangers. Every eight hours
    within an area of environmental danger, the character
    must succeed on an endurance check. Each time a
    character fails, he loses one healing surge. If a charac-
    ter has no healing surges left when he fails a check, he
    loses hit points equal to his level."

    Very similar mechanics here. Again, the time interval for checks is static - in this case, 8 hours. Failure imposes a discreet penalty.

    Posted By: FrankTrollmanFun facts:

    Your ratio of hit points to level is generally better at low levels than at high levels. So a 1st level Fighter with a Con of 12 will perish after 37 days of failed endurance tests (27 hp, 1 hp lost per day). A 5th level Fighter with a Con of 12 perishes after only 21 failed Endurance tests (51 hp, 5 hp lost per day).

    The actual tests are basically meaningless in terms of how long you can stay outside. Our 5th level character dies after 21 failures and has a +8 to his Endurance tests. But every time he succeeds, the DC goes up by 5. If it goes up by 5 twice, he can't make the tests any more. The first one he can succeed at he succeeds at on a 12+, so he'll probably get it in the first 2 or 3 attempts, leaving him 18 or more tries to get a 17+ - which he will almost certainly get. So practically speaking, what is going to happen is that some time during the three weeks before he expires after having gone 3 days without water he will succeed on two of those Endurance tests. That extends his test days to 23 and he won't be able to succeed on any subsequent tests. Why it was necessary to roll 23 d20s to determine which two days during the 23 day ordeal were the two days that his condition did not worsen is completely beyond me. It's so many dice rolls that it might as well be deterministic. Seriously, it's a 7 out of 1000 test that you die in only 22 days, is that really worth rolling 23 d20s to determine?

      \n So, Psionics. Back in the AD&D glory days, I even picked up the Psionics handbook, but it was never as just-freakin-cool as the Dark Sun Psionics.

      Here's how I think about it:

      Everyone has the chance to have some kind of crazy mind power, even if it's totally unrelated to the rest of their life. Something about being out in the sun for your ENTIRE LIFE makes you go a little crazy, and that crazyness unlocks something in your brain. And then, when it does, there's this elaborate series of metaphor (IRON TOWER! THE DRAGON!) for all the attacks and defenses. The color made it really visceral, to me - two dudes, locked in mental combat, staring at each other but also off in their metaphorical mind-realm. Awesome.

      Also, psychometabolism. Change your body with your mind. How Athas is that?

      I always wanted to play a psychometabolic halfling. That would be rad.
