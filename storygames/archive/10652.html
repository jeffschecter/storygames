\n Split from Art, Games, and Money\n
Posted By: joepubThis Is Not A Question You Asked But I Want To Talk About It.

Hm, instead of answering this I'll say something I've been thinking for a while, in terms of game pricing:

When I consider the value of a game, I consider the value of other pastimes I enjoy.
I could buy Agon ($20) and probably play 1-2 sessions over the next year, or I could go see several punk shows.
I could buy Agon ($20), or I could go watch a movie in theatres and buy popcorn and twizzlers.
I could buy Agon ($20), or I could rent the entire third season of Dexter and watch it.

I don't own Agon. I just finished the second disc of Dexter, Season Three. I'll rent the next disc soon.

For myself, as a consumer, I've started to think about game prices in terms of what I could be doing with that money otherwise.
Games are often priced higher than, say... the door price of going to a poetry slam ($5).
Since I do performance poetry, I'm not losing out on that creative-participation-within-a-community-of-creators aspect.
For me, a $40 game has to demonstrate itself to be more socially and creatively rewarding than 8 poetry slams.
It also has to demonstrate itself to be more socially and emotionally rewarding than 4-8 concerts.
It also has to demonstrate itself to be more entertaining than renting 13 movies from Applause Video, and watching 4 in theatres.

That's something going through my head when I think about charging for my own games, and when I think about buying others' games.

    \n Another problem with crunching the numbers this way is that it assumes a dollar has one one-thousandth the value of one thousand dollars. That is, that the price tag can be infinitely divided and carry equal weight in total.

    For example, for your disposable income, you probably have a barrier below which the price is just not an issue at all. If I see something that I want and it costs five bucks, I get it. Some people will set this high and some low. Items below this bar are basically free, psychologically: a $40 game is not up against 8 poetry slams (for me) because that's 8 separate effectively free instances. I'm doing those anyway. Buying the game doesn't take them away from me. If I had to buy all 8 tickets up front, it would be.

    Tack on the fact that quality and mood are relevant. A poetry slam when I'm not into it, is worse than worthless. A really bad movie alone is not worth anything. A bad movie on opening night with great friends is worth way more than the ticket price, but the next day would be worthless. So I'm also buying risk rather than entertainment, and have to weigh the chances I'll enjoy myself as well as how much I'll enjoy myself against the dollar value.

    Short version: cash comparison is way too simplistic for me. Doesn't bear on the problem at all, in fact, most of the time.

    \n I've reached the point where I have a lot of personal restrictions that a game needs to meet.

    Briefly, the game needs to meet criteria similar to what I'd ask for from a boardgame:

    It needs to play in 1-4 hours of actual game time and in that time reach a relatively clear point of completion.

    It needs to be playable by not-me people who are not interested in spending more than 15 minutes learning rules or doing set up pre-play. ( I'm willing to put a limited amount more pre-play time in, but probably not much more time than the game will last, unless the other prep is something I was going to do anyway).

    It needs to be playable with 3-6 participants without too many rules mods with variable size games. If rules mods are required for play outside of optimal numbers, they should be clearly stated.

    How well a game does those things determines how much I'm willing to pay for it.

    \n I don't consciously compare the price of an RPG book to the price for other entertainment, but it's definitely in the back of my head when I'm hefting a book in my hand and thinking about whether I want to carry it over to the cash register or not. This was true before the economic crunch, but it's REALLY true now: I relentlessly examine all of my entertainment purchases and whenever possible opt to save my money instead.

    Generally, I buy a book when:

    1. ...it's ridiculously cheap; $20 is as much as I am willing to spend for a big, fat all-in-one rulebook or a complete set of rulebooks. Supplements or thinner books I tend to top out on at around $8, PDFs are even less than that (because I think they're a pain in the ass to work with). I'm willing to wait to find a used copy. I'm willing to wait for a new version to come out, too, which is why I have a D&D 3.5e collection consisting of books I got for $12 or less.

    2. ...I know for a fact that I'm going to be using it in at least one campaign and am reasonably sure that I will be using it in another one at some point. I don't buy books on spec anymore. I don't buy books just to have them on my shelf. If I'm not going to be playing the game for at least two months' worth of sessions, I don't want the book.

    Looking at that and figuring a 3-hour session as more or less the standard for our games, it's looking like I'm putting the value of a typical fat RPG book at approximately eighty-three cents per hour of gaming. Which is definitely an entertainment bargain: clearly I am one thrifty, canny little shopper! Toss in a little food money on top of that and it's still cheaper than movies and concerts!

    (But like Paul B says, scarcity has to figure into this somewhere, too; I'll pay more money to see a concert gladly because it may be the only time I'll get to see that artist perform live for several years, whereas I know that even if I don't get a new RPG book, our group will still have something fun to play on Saturday.)

    Posted By: joepubI consider games as existing within the same realm as poetry, movies and concerts, in terms of my spending habits.
    Right... and, further, you seem to consider a game's contribution to your life very discretely. A movie is two hours, plus maybe jack-jawing about it with friends another hour or two, total. A game is... how do you REALLY measure that? A game could do all (or some) of these:
    * Provide one session of entertainment and never be opened again.
    * Help me pass Throne Time with interesting reading.
    * Surprise me with a technique that I can maybe appropriate for some project of mine (I've bought every LARP manual I have seen, due to this factor alone).
    * Provide entertainment AGAIN, years down the road, with a-whole-nother posse, maybe my kids (odds looking slimmer every decade), maybe at the retirement home.
    * Give me a setting... or art... or a particular cool NPC... to use in a totally unrelated game, or maybe even an unrelated-to-gaming manner (e.g. fiction writing).

    This is why the Givens Principle works better for me than some sort of "Relative Purchase" metric: I can, usually, SEE right there, in my sweaty hands, what the book/product brings to me; and it's pretty easy to imagine more down the road. So all I have to decide is, "Hmm... could I make that myself? Is this just another fantasy heartbreaker, or a Mage variant/re-skin? Can I do this already with HERO or Universalis or MET splatbooks or by strapping BITs to a game I already own (etc)?" That stuff is easier for me to (somewhat intuitively, of course) judge than "Is this book worth the 'teenth I won't be able to smoke because I bought it?"

    Tomorrow, I'll be sober again... and I'll still not have that cool book. Conversely, I might run a killer game with it for several sessions... being blazed up by co-players each time. How can I measure hypotheticals?

    going to a poetry slam.
    renting a new release.
    going to a punk show.
    buying a poetry chapbook.

    It'd be crass to think in terms of "I can get eight of those units for the cost of this $40 RPG", sure.
    But it's totally not crass to think "I can have these amazing experiences for a trivial, psychologically free amount... so why pay way more for this other thing?"
    (recognizing that thing here is more than just a book, or just a game session)

    Further, for $40 I can:
    get a bus to Seattle and back.
    take my girlfriend to the movies.
    Buy a couple movies.
    Buy a couple books.
    watch a play.

    As a result, most games aren't worth their price tag + the time it takes to read them.

    \n I view RPG value in three ways.

    1) A contribution to a cause that I am interested in. My money fuels an industry that I care about. This is especially important with indie games. I will buy a game and feel AWESOME about knowing that someone like myself is making money off of it.

Reading it, playing it, thinking about how it will affect other games or my view of the hobby in general. Even if I never play a game (look at my shelf - there are maybe half, if that, that i've actually played) it contributes to the fun of every other game I _am_ running by merit of getting me to think about it.

    3) My shelf gets bigger. I am covetous by nature and having MORE books makes the Collection a bigger, more awesome, more satisfying thing. This isn't RPG specific, but I will complete a "set" just so that I can feel that I have accomplished something. *laugh*

    \n Reaction,

    I was mostly meaning time in the sense that I have a limited amount of weeknights, and of that number I allot 1-2 to spending time with friends.

    A roleplaying game session takes up 1/2 of my social time with friends.

    Further, scheduling games is tricky for many. Specifically, there were courses that I didn't take this semester, so that I had a schedule that was flexible enough to set up games.

    So, for scheduling + actually committing time to playing... there is a significant investment.

    Now, actually reading the games...
    Yes, there is an investment of time, and several kinds of reward (fun, lasting impressions, new knowledge).
    Polaris is really fun to read. Ben has written it in such a way that I feel like the time I invest in the book is justified, in and of reading the book.

    Reading the text of Sorcerer is a net minus for me, meaning the play experience has that much more work to do in impressing me.

    So... yeah.
    What I mean to say is this:
    I've stopped evaluating roleplaying games (as products and as experiences) solely against their own insular market.
    Because they compete with the time, energy and money that I could be sinking into other creative and emotionally fulfilling endeavors.

    As a result, I buy less games.
    Also as a result, I cherish the games that I do enjoy all the more.

    I've started to think about games in the context of how they impact my life.
    That's led to realizations like, "Hey, Polaris is one of the most important things I own."
