\n I know we've talked about this before, at least Andy and I have, but I'd love to get people thinking out loud about this.

My wife Autumn is reading a book about the history of the shag dance, a history which is quite colorful and exciting and filled with conflict and drama. Forbidden love, killer music, changing times, and a cool dance you can do while holding your drink.

"That'd be a neat setting for a game," I say.

"Yeah," she says, "and you could resolve conflicts by shagging.*"

So how would this work? Extend the notion to any non-random, social, physical activity that you use to functionally sort things out in the fiction. How does the take-your-pants-off-animal-lawyer-Dracula game work? I'd love to hear of other non-theoretical examples.

*UK residents, go ahead and get it out of your system now; I get it, it's hilarious, shag is slang for fuck, ha ha, let's assume you've already added your pithy comment pointing this out!

    \n The nice (?) thing about the shag is that it is only sorta a partner dance - it's very male-centric. The female partner just mirrors, does an occasional twirl, while the guy goes bananas. So you could have two guys dancing solo at each other pretty easily, I think. I just found out that Remi's in-laws are old-skool original beach shaggers, so maybe I'll get a chance to pick their brains at some point.

    I'm still not seeing how this becomes a game tool, though. "Precisely duplicate these moves" is not fun. "Use these moves to communicate" could be fun. "Express yourself for the crowd, who votes" could be fun.
