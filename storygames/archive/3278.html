\n So I'll be starting a new Vampire: The Requiem game in about a month, at the request of my players. I want to fiddle with the system a bit by bringing in something like Aspects, Beliefs, Keys, or something similar. I'm currently leaning towards porting in a system similar to Beliefs in Burning Empires for the characters - every Vampire has three core Beliefs and by acting in accordance to those beliefs they regain WIllpower (or possibly Blood). My goal is to have the inner workings of the character's mind incorporated into the game. Aspects seem a bit more for showing off cool stuff than incorporating what's going on in a character's head, though they can do that.

Suggestions? Thoughts?

    \n Also, other elements are really ripe for WoD games (to the extent that, these days I've been really nostalgic for old Vampire/Mage gaming, and have contemplated running something this year): Like planning situation. Make up a few "major and minor players" in the town. Make an R-Map or something. Let the characters decide where they fit in that map, how they feel about the other characters. Then, just turn up the heat and add a magical vampire scroll, and the adventure will write itself.

    That's.. outside of what you were looking for, but still, might help with your pre-planning.

    -Andy

    \n They were? I don't remember any text telling me not to show them to the players. It might have been there but I don't remember it. I always showed the maps around, especially to those with established characters who would know the general political layout. I don't think we explicitly put our characters into the R-maps for at least six months after Chicago by Night 1ed. came out. So probably 1994 or so was when we started. I couldn't figure out any other way they could conceivably be useful.

    There were some secrets revealed on those R-maps, as I recall, such as "secretly controls", but it was pretty much okay with me if everyone knew it on an out-of-character level.

    Edit: I do remember keeping them secret from some new players, as I figured it was already a bit much to remember the two or three clans and coteries that I was asking them to remember, and it would be best to ease them into it once they had the hang of it...

    \n Why not go even further and allow the players to see the R-Map before chargen, and then decide where they want their characters to fall within it? The players get the lay of the land from the outset and can put their characters in line with this or that faction, positioning themselves as needed within the already-established power structure. Instant back-stabbing, cut-throat politics, if the R-Map is set up nice and tense.

    (EDIT: Of course, at this point it's probably easier to just create an R-Map in conjunction with the players, sketching out an initial web of power that is free from the baggage of a pregen R-Map)

    \n I'm in a similar position. I'm starting a NWoD game set in Victorian England with my wife and I want to bring in a few elements that will allow me to streamline gameplay without defaulting to what we used to: I use the rules whenever I feel like and they don't interfere with my story.

    I'm using a R-map for the character which I expect will grow over time, and while I am leaving her character sheet as-is (my wife is not a gamer and she is very comfortable with the Storytelling system), on my side I am extrapolating a few Aspect-like qualities from the sheet and backstory, and building the initial scenes out of that.

    I'm going to attempt to incorporate elements like Compels on the Virtue, Vice and Aspects I have listed and see if I can get her to use the mechanics to express what she's already doing via narrative. If I have a goal, system-wise, is that I want to see Willpower being used actively, something we rarely did the last time we played Vampire some years ago.
