\n I like games where the designer takes me 80% of the way there and then gives me options for that last 20%. But indie games often are really tightly wedded to their premise, and don't leave a lot of room for that.

Strangely, I think some traditional games, like Savage Worlds, do this, but they don't do it well. For example, "hey, you're in a modern setting? here's the rules for guns and vehicles."

What I wish indie games had was "Hey, you guys have a hard time visualising the scene? Have a hard time adding details? Want to reward players who take on adversarial roles? Want to emphasize some thematic point?" That kind of thing, in the indie sphere, tends to be relegated to when you choose the game, not when you've decided you like a game.

    \n Yeah, there are a few interesting issues here:

    First, as mentioned, most "indie" games have some kind of extensive "setup" phase or similar, which allows the players to do a lot of the customization before play. (For comparison, imagine a version of D&D where you had to sit down with your friends and build classes and Feat trees before playing. It would probably be almost impossible to make that easy to do, but it would certainly make the game feel more "customizable".)

    It might work the other way, as well: games that are less "coherent" (I'm using the term to mean 'focused on a specific playstyle or Creative Agenda') might need more customization to make them work for a particular group. Therefore, of course games like GURPS or Fudge or HERO or whatever are going to have lots of modular options available--many groups will need those options to make the game work for them.

    I also agree with Filip: I'd like the game to take me 100% of the way there. But I also like it if the game includes some optional tidbits that give some other ways to play the game. Maybe long-term play of your particular game is better with an additional mechanic that you don't need in one-shots, for example (or vice-versa). For games that require a lot of development before playing, how about two or three "default sets"? Etc.

    But the design of many "indie" games is often so carefully calibrated (at least in theory) that designing in options that work with what is there is impossible, incredibly difficult, or not worth the trouble.

    Now, for some rhetorical questions (woo! My favourite!):

    When you play a game like chess, tested over centuries, do you really need some "optional/modular rules"?

    Do optional/modular rules suggest imperfection in design?

    \n Customization/house ruling is the only thing that happens in gaming. It is the whole hobby. There is nothing in the hobby that is not customization. All play is house ruling. And so it is baffling to me that so little attention has been given to it. Everyone waves their hand at it. "Oh, that's a house rule, we can't discuss THAT, we can only discuss the GAME." As if any line that could be drawn between them would be useful to play at all.

    All play is house ruling. All play is customization. A game that takes you "100% of the way" is by definition unplayable - it is complete - it does not require you - it excludes what you wish to do - there is no room for you at the table - there is no food upon which you may sup.

    The first attention given to customization beyond "hey if you want to change this, feel free, hurrrrrrr", which is not helpful at all in determining what you should change, how you should change it, what you should change it to, and what effects that will have that I can recall was in Vampire 1ed, in which significant chunks of GM advice were dedicated to establishing and reinforcing a Tone of your own devising. As small as this assistance was, it was frankly made of platinum-plated fuckyeah for all that it helped me avoid many of the pitfalls that others who missed that assistance had.

    The 3.0 DMG, for all its detail, had a lot of customization points as well. "Hey, check out how you can make this a sort of India-theme fantasy game, hey, if you want to do it this way, you can do it this way..."

    nWoD has an enormous number of points like this.

    GURPS has done it a long time, probably even longer than Vampire (although I didn't know it back then.)

    But really, for the most part - even in all these examples - customization opportunities are just sort of put out there assuming that you know what you want to do and just need some brainstorming permission. That is not where the hard work of customizing is. Ideas are a dime a dozen. The hard part is implementation, getting the idea into play. Hardly any game, "Trad" or "Indie" or "story" or whatever, does that.

    \n Come on, boys. You are talking about completely different things in here...

    One thing is 100% completion rules, a very different thing is 100% completion play.

    Sure, chess is 100% complete from the rules perspective, but there is a lot of player input from the strategic and "play" perspective.

    A game that is 100% complete from the play perspective is not playable. It is not a game. A game that is 100% complete from the rules perspective can (and will) be played in any way the players want to, as long as they follow the rules as intended. No room for rules changes.

    So, the question becomes... Is customization important or cool when talking about rules? How about play possibilities (strategies, color, custom settings, characters, powers...)?

    And the thing is that with games not as "simple" and direct as Chess, there is no 100% complete rules. If only because everyone will interpret the rules as they see them, and there is no way two people can understand something that complete and complicated in a 100%. Perhaps in a 99.99%, but most likely a lot less.

    Posted By: Marshall BurnsYou guys know what rules arefor, right?
    \nThey exist to discipline the mind. They exist to teach us to cast aside what we desire and what we wish, and instead submit to the precepts of the rules. They exist to teach us to cast aside the ego. The exist to teach us that, when what we want is contrary to what actually is, we are wrong.     That's what rules are to me. They are never to be broken during play, ever, even to my own benefit or increased "fun."

    Keep in mind that the toolkit mentality is itself dependent on a certain point of view regarding the value and purpose of games, just as my own mentality is dependent on my own point of view regarding the value that games have to me.

    As to wade into the murky depth of what a game is,

    I think a game can be 100% complete in its rules and in its play, and I don't think that a game that is 100% complete in rule and play (or concept and execution) is for some reason reduced to a series of immutible steps (which is not very play like). International Federation of Chess while it still has room for ' improvisation' is clearly only within the rules, a pawn will NEVER do anything that is not expressly allowed, No piece ever will. Every possible legal move has been found, and if we wanted to we could compute every possible game (if you want to argue the point, lets consider checkers instead), and while there is still discussion over which is best, there are no new moves.

    I think your despairing over deriving infinite play from finite rules, a task which is both doable (in a mathematic sense) and pragmatically possible (threw the things like context and interaction ). This of this more in terms of language, we have a pretty finite number of words and stuff to use, but even if it was finitie of this moment, (no new words or change , ever) we could still derive infinite statements I see, I see that I see, I see that I see that I see, I see that I see that I see that I see, etc.
