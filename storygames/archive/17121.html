\n I'm interested in how the physical reality affects the games, especially tabletop games. In LARP this is constantly explored in all sort of ways. But in tabletop roleplaying games the physical reality seems to be left out of the equation. People have stressed how important all sort of things are to the social contract, yet, the physical side of things seem strangely left out of it.

Does seating matter? Sitting? Does the physical space matter?

But in my expedience it do matter. For example who is sitting next to who, and who can easily hold eye contact with who will really affect which characters in the group that will interact with each other. Love stories seems to me especially prone to be affected by how people are seated.

Something that makes it painfully obvious that seating matters is when people take turns setting scene, but the order of when they get to set scenes are decided by the seating. People set different types of scene, and the order in which different individuals is seated often creates a flow of different kinds of scenes, and if the players have a character each, it also affect who can respond to whos actions, etc.

Does the seating arrangement communicate something about status? Is there a high-status GM chair where the one in charge sit? Is there some low status position in the gaming room?

Seating is serious stuff.

So is sitting. You body posture affect you emotions, and player emotion both affect the game an bleed into the game. So whenever you sitting straight backed on uncomfortable chairs, or half lying in a sofa will affect the feel of the game. (My gaming groups have had a \u201dno sofas\u201d rules at time because people became to relaxed even sleepy, and it produced a slow an sluggish game at times.)

How you sit will also affect on how you can play you character. Can you move? To what degree? Can you stand up? Can you gesture? Can you leave you place at the table and walk around? How close are you physically to thhe other players? Can you touch them? Is touching a part of the social contract?

(And then there whole other bunch of about the physical reality of gaming that not about sitting or seating that affects the game, lighting, how the room the game take place is in socially coded, etc etc. but I'll leave it to some other discussion.)

What you experience of how seating and sitting arrangement to affect your games? Can this be more used in game design to create better games? Different games?

    \n Personally I remember one campaign I played years ago that I think became a great play experience and unique because... There was no place to sit down.

    We where playing in small one room apartment. We where four players. There was no place we could sit down all four of us. Either we could all sit/lie in a cuddly pile on the bed, and that work great for cuddly scenes, but is soon got way too warm. Or two persons could sit on the couch, and two persons could sit squeezed in on the floor on a kind of an awkward place. Then there was a computer chair at the small worktable, but you couldn't move that chair anywhere else in the room. So you could sit n the chair, at a distance to everyone else at an awkward angle to every one else.

    Since there was no sitting arrangement that worked we got creative and move around. We stood up. We loomed behind the sofa looking down on who was sitting there. We held monologues in the lone chair. We sprawled on the bed. We strangled each other on the floor. We lurked in the doorway. We stormed out into the tiny kitchen alcove. We his behind the sofa. We stood by the window, looking out at a distance.

    It was great, intense, playful and creative. In some ways it was the best tabletop playing space ever. (It was also one of the only all female gaming groups I played with.)

    \n During Games On Demand this year, I ran a game of Apocalypse World for 4 players, at a table with six seats. The way one of them sat put them directly to my right, with a gap chair between them and the rest of the players. Over the course of the game, this guy's Brainer was ostracized, threatened, bossed around, stuffed in a claw machine, and ended up taking over the settlement at the end.     \n It matters very much to me. I've gotten more and more sensitive to stuff like this the last few years, and not only in gaming but in making music (especially singing parts a capella), and most social interactions.

    Some of my typical concerns in gaming:
    \u2022 If I run a GM-less game, it's important to me not to sit in "the GM chair" (head of the table or otherwise isolated) but that the group's positioned equally.
    \u2022 When I'm a player, I try not to sit next to the GM if there is one. I always get a fair share of the spotlight where ever I sit, so I leave the places with the best possibilities for connecting/communicating with the GM to others.
    \u2022 If I know I'm going to play something where I interact intensely with another player (for instance if we've got a romantic relationship in game) I'll want to sit near that person. In most groups I've played touching is accepted/expected (usually this'll mean touching hands, arms or shoulders).

    The most recent thing about seating in my mind is from this spring when I played a few sessions with people I haven't known or played with for very long. Sometimes when we were seating ourselves it sort of drifted towards being a boys' side and a girls' side. That felt very odd to me, so I asked if we could rearrange the seating. No one seemed to mind, and my experience was that we got a better connection as a group.

    \n In a group I played in, seating was basically static, with players sitting in the same spots for however long we played that game. If we changed games, the seating chart as-it-were could change.

    The seating also changed as the circumstances of the party changed -- if characters were split up into two groups, players would shuffle to sit with their groups. If characters were tense with one another, players would move away from one another -- this was a modified version of WFRP3 that kept party tension as a stat, so if two characters were tense w/ one another, they'd split. Once party tension overfilled and things were back to usual, the normal seating chart would re-emerge.

    I've read about GMs for D&D and such standing the entirety of their game while their players sat, I believe the reasoning being that they felt more energetic that way? I've never witnessed this, though.

    \n A couple of years back, I ran a game at a convention and on the sign-up sheet I explained that, since the action was set in a night club, their characters woulldn't automatically be able to sit down anywhere. I also stated that to reflect this, there would only be half as many chairs as players and that if anyone wanted to sit down, they would have to find an in-character way of taking a chair away from one of the seated players. The pre-gens each had a small mechanical incentive for both sitting and standing, e.g. they got a bonus to their roll to achieve certain goals depending on their position.

    Sometime after running this game, I mentioned it as part of a thread on another forum, which created quite an unexpected reaction: some people very testily responded that if they'd have been in a game like that, they'd have told the GM off and then walked out! I guess some people are just very attached to the idea of sitting down while they roleplay and, I have to stress, the game I ran was extremely successful: the players got so immersed in their characters and their situation that I could have quite easily wandered off to the bar to get a drink, assured that they would have carried on without me.

    \n Random thoughts on game seating:
    * I once had an idea for a status-based seating design, where characters with high status would sit nearer the GM, and others would sit farther away so they'd be more easily ignored.
    * In "Acid moss", players sit on the floor at a distance, and each time the alarm goes (once a minute), they move closer until they're in a big heap.
    * When we play at my house, we sit in the library (fancy word for the room with the books). This is a sheltered space, a little cave of plants and subtle lighting and books and a good (but not sleep-inducing) sofa. Harder to hear the kids, and as GM I make sure not to sit at the entrance where the kids will come to interrupt us...
    * Games like Polaris and LiToS define some player/character interrelations by seating. "The player to your right is your Heart", or something like that. Choosing who sits where can have a large impact on the game; letting it be random makes for higher replay value ("yeah, we're the same characters, but now I'm controlling the Princess' gift, not the Knight's lycanthropy!")
    * There is a GM chair when we play, in that the GM usually gets to pick where they want to sit. It's not a formal thing, but sometimes it is explicit ("Hey, Steinar is GMing, maybe he should sit there...? Or where do you want to sit?")

    Other thought: At meetings I'm pretty conscious of where I sit - do I need direct access to someone to get my point across? if so, I sit right across from them. Should someone other than me do most of the talking? If so, I sit next to them, a little away from the main group.

    \n Seating matters in a weird way to me. I'm much more confident seated. I can improvise all kinds of stuff when I'm at a table. I can channel characters, talking in their voices, direct the action towards drama, all that stuff.

    But if you stand me up on a stage for an improv or theatre game -- if you make me act -- it's much harder for me. I'm not kinesthetically creative. I can't inhabit a character's body, I can only inhabit their mind.

    So if I stand up during a tabletop game, it's probably just to stretch my legs or to relieve tension. It's not about performing more effectively.

    \n One thing I notice with some games where there is lot of information on the table. Character papers, relationship diagrams, maps, markers, dice, rules... etc.

    Is that the gaze of people shift down. Instead at looking at each other and paying attention to each other people spend a lot of time looking at the table, which often have a negative affect on the interactions and story.

    In this case, standing up can a way to force people to shift the focus. If someone sits you can still "sort of" see them if you look at the table, but when someone stands up you have to stop looking at the table and pay attention to the standing person in another way.

    \n Seating style can have an enormous impact on a game using miniatures.

    When I experiment with different sorts of miniatures+high levels of player input on story element type play, I do not want people sitting at the table.

    Sitting near the table, yes. A comfy nearby area a few feet from the table, sure. Scattered chairs that can be moved around for comfort? Absolutely.

    I don't want people camping in one spot. I want them up and moving around the table and even standing during some parts of play.

    A permanent spot causes problems.

    1) I lose surface space usable for events in game especially if people have paper, books, dice, or chip bags in front of each of them.

    2) People begin to think about the space in front of them as theirs, and their perspective ranges out from the point where they're sitting,rather than thinking about the overall play surface .

    I'm glad you started this topic. It's made me think more about this aspect wrt to minis use, and how traditional wargaming minis use and seating differ from high fictional input minis use games seating.
