\n Hello folks, I've come to a design crossroads and I thought I'd turn to Story-Games for some perspective.

I'm designing a Supers-based RPG. Specifically, I'm trying to build a game that fosters the Soap Opera of comic books. Something more akin to the absurdity of 90's X-Men and to certain extent Spider-Man.

Since it's about the Soap Opera aspect of this genre I've been thinking about how to handle all those crazy inter-team politics and love-triangles etc. Looking back at the source material, here's a trend I've noticed. \n
  • A character will have a moral conundrum, find themselves in some kind of ridiculous love-quadrangle, etc.
  • The characters involved will talk at one another, no side really relenting in any measurable fashion.
  • Eventually, things will come down to a melodramatic combat. They'll blow off steam while shouting their moral position at one another.
  • Eventually, the act of engaging in the fight coupled with their dialogue will combine to resolve this situation.
\nBasically, what other rpgs would handle as a purely social encounter these comics tend to mesh with combat. In fact, they often are not resolved until that knock-down fight is engaged. Obviously, this is not always the case, but it's the kind of activity I'm trying to promote.

Now, I have a pretty hefty set of combat mechanics in play to handle your typical supers battle. What I'm wondering is, would it be truly crazy to say, "Hey, you know all those inter-team conflicts that are building up, they too can only be resolved with this detailed combat system." Furthermore, I'm thinking I could get away with doing this without building in combat maneuvers that are specifically related to Social Encounter strategies. Alright, I'm sure that last statement is weird. Let me try to break this down by way of an example using trademarked X-Men:\n
Rogue just kissed Gambit. Gambit almost died because her powers activated and siphoned his life force. She is understandably upset and feeling like a freak.

Iceman tries to talk her down, telling her that they couldn't handle Magneto without her, it's a Gift and Obligation. Wolverine tries to help by saying, 'Look bub, this is just the way we are. You gotta accept yourself flaws and all.' All of this is food for thought, but it hasn't helped Rogue overcome her problem.

Fast-forward to a Danger-Room training scene. Rogue is getting her ass handed to her by Cyclops, because she's afraid to use her powers. Cyclops however is using this opportunity to do the classic training montage/help you with your problems thing. Through the course of this fight scene, both Cyclops' speech and the actual combat itself help him turn Rogue around and get her to accept herself as she is.
\nNow, I could build in mechanics that say: Cyclops uses the move "Cutting Truth" followed by "Wise Words" and finishing up with "Empathetic Analogy." But all of that seems unnecessary. I think it could be super cool if Cyclops is instead describing his attacks with the same detail the rest of the game requires and also giving his speech at the same time. So I guess I should explain that bit of the game.

Time is measured in the game by comic book Panels. So characters can do all the actions that a comic book character could accomplish within the frame of One panel. All this to say, Cyclops really could be having a long drawn out back and forth conversation through the course of this Combat, because it's not 6 seconds or some other small finite time.

Within each panel of action (i.e. on each player's turn), players are activating super power moves to do crazy hero stuff like blasting a training droid's head off it's body into the simulated Sentinel's body. In that example, a character might be using the Moves: Accuracy (pinpoint shoot the head off the droid) and Push (use the momentum of the droid-head to topple the Sentinel). If I'm using this same system for resolving interpersonal/existential conflicts, I would prefer not to make specific Moves that are purely for social manipulation or whatever. This is because of how Combat outcomes are structured.

After some back and forth of multiple comic book panels, players roll off and count successes. Each success results in damage to the opponent's Morale. Thus, the outcome of combat rounds tend to focus less on the physical, visceral damage your character has taken and instead focus on how it's messing w/ your psyche and ability to maintain that heroic resolve. My idea was to build on this and add a different kind of result that deals w/ two opponents changing one another's mind, or helping them resolve their internal struggles, or even building new rifts between one another.

Does that method sound like it would be more fun and true to my stated gold-standard? Or would it make more sense to have Heroic Verbal moves like "Cutting Truth, Utilitarian Appeal, Compassionate Plea, etc." In which case, players would be activating them alongside whatever combat move they're making as part of the scene.

I hope what I'm going for makes sense. I'd love to hear your folks thoughts on this strange idea I have.

    \n I'm generally with David about not being told directly what my character has to think, but might be more down for mechanics that provide reasons and game situations to shape that.

    Do you have any mechanical representation of Rogues' emotional state before the danger room session with Cyclops?

    Have you checked out Smallville (also Cortex Plus Drama)? It also focuses on soap-opera aspects of supers. In it you have relationships defined by a statement and a dice value. "Aah love Remy, but we can nevah evah be... together d10"
    These relationships are used as part of your dice pool, can be challenged to be rewritten or the dice value changed during the character growth ('xp') part of the session.

    Roughly (been a while since I read it) in Cortex+ Drama, mechanically it would have looked something like this:
    Rogue rolls her relationship (and some other sources of dice) in the charged scene with Remy, lost the roll and chose to acquire some emotional stress (rated in dice) rather than concede (to whatever the opposing character wanted). Gambit may or may not have been a PC- it may have been Emma wanting to prove Rogue IS a dangerous freak who needs to leave the X-Men who has persuaded Remy to kiss Rogue.

    Later, Cyclops had a 'recovery scene' with Rogue, making a roll to attempt to help Rogue deal with her stress (remove the die), rolling his relationship with her, some Value like Duty, a distinction like Team Leader.
    In your example, he'd also be able to use his Optic Blast die in a fight or danger room scene. (Bobby tried the same earlier, but failed)

    Now, Cortex+ Drama makes recovering from Stress less of a big deal than you present (it probably wouldn't take three separate scenes) but acquiring and removing it is the 'XP' character growth mechanic. The drift would be requiring that removing stress only happens in action scenes, perhaps as a secondary goal, rather than in a downtime/transition scene. (For more on that distinction, check out Marvel Heroic Roleplaying by the same people).

    One last thought, Cortex+ even has some nifty mechanics for handling trying to do two things at once, measuring your effectiveness at both blowing up the Sentinel and helping Rogue with her issues with a single roll- chances are you won't be as successful at one as the other and will have to choose which to do better at.

    \n Depends on the level of detail you want to put into that subsystem, yeah? For examples: When writing DayTrippers I wanted a single mechanic for all action resolutions, from combat to dialogue to opposed and unopposed task resolutions. So Dialog is really just a version of Opposed Action, with the single added rule that every "loss" in an extended conversation gives you a -1 on the next phase of the conversation. I could do this because the system is intended to be loose and bear narrative interpretations. But for SpaceMaster (back in the day) I wrote a complex set of d100 rules with different Mods for different argument types, and a table with sub-tables for the critical results of different types of argument.

    Typically, different people will get off on each of these systems. But my point is: Know your system and know your audience. And when I say "know", I mean "feel".

    \n How's this for an idea (inspired by WarriorMonk):

    Assuming you already have a functional and fun system for combat, treat that as the final arbiter of social conflict.

    In other words, when two characters have an argument, what they're really doing is maneuvering towards combat; it's the fight itself which will settle the issue.

    As the PCs bicker and argue, the players can make each other offers, which affect the following fight. Maybe I offer a concession ("Ok, my character is now convinced that his mother really WAS the problem") but, in exchange, it gives me some kind of advantage in the fight ("You can't use your fire blast against me, because we'll fight near the oil tanker"). The concessions could be conditional, if that works better ("if I win the fight, you'll become convinced your mother really WAS the problem here").

    Once both players have accepted a "deal" from the other, they can move on to the fight segment. The rules here determine 1) who is the winner of the fight, 2) who is beat up, and how badly, and 3) they outcome of their social conflict.

    The argument stage is described as words being thrown back and forth, but its real purpose is to set the stakes of the fight. (These could be inverted, too, if that's more interesting: "If you hurt me really badly during the fight, I'll never trust your friend again.")

    The fight then resolves the issues which have been anted up during the argument.

    \n I agree with the general consensus, I don't like the idea of any kind of persuasion mechanic telling my character what to think. On the whole, I think that's a bad idea. And yet, it is very possible I've created exactly that kind of thing. So, let me explain the game mechanic this whole social+combat mechanism is supposed to address.

    All characters are created with a number of triggers called Limits. Limits are situations or events that would induce emotional stress because they strike at a core value of the character or prey upon a specific insecurity. When a Limit is triggered, the character suffers a mechanical penalty levied against them.

    There are a number of ways in which a Limit can be forced to trigger, including as a result of certain dice rolls. However, I don't want the triggering of a Limit to force a way of thought. Instead, when triggered, they are a narrative excuse for your Hero to get involved in some melodramatic exploration of the feels.
    For example, Wolverine's player gives him the Limit: Misunderstood. It is up to the player to define what this Limit exactly means to their character. For the GM, it acts as a guide. Tells the GM how to stress that character emotionally. Depending on how it's defined, Misunderstood could be triggered because a mob of civilians have turned against Wolverine OR it could be triggered because the other X-Men are horrified by how savagely he just took down Sabertooth.
    \nHow does one get rid of the penalties that come from an activated Limit? Well, I would love if it was through that hybrid of social&combat to ultimately "resolve" that Limit. It would be up to the player if resolution means:
    • This Limit is removed from the character sheet and can no longer cause them mental/emotional stress. Their character has finally come to terms with that particular moral/self identity quandry.
    • This Limit is merely quashed for the time-being. It remains on the character sheet, but currently levies no mechanical penalites. In which case, the character has found some solace or centered themselves, but it can still come back to hurt them.
    \nOn the one hand, this definitely forces a change in the player's character without that player's consent. Regardless of how the player's character feels, that Limit is no longer mechanically penalizing them; representing that the particular moral/emotional issue has become less of a priority/driving force. However, I'm hoping this is acceptable because the mechanical negative levied by the activated Limit is incentivizing the player to find some resolution to this emotional stress.

    The design intent could be summarized as:
    Triggering Limits gives a player fictional fodder to ham it up. When Triggered the GM presents them with a scenario that caused them emotional stress. Now the player can use that fiction to express the feels.

    Resolving Limits builds fiction that allows a player to show how their character pushed past this emotional hardship. Whether or not the crisis was "solved," ultimately, the character has come to some kind of terms with it and it's not taking a toll on their psyche.

    Ultimately, Resolution of a Limit requires some comic-book fisticuffs and monologing.
    \nNow, does that seem too much like: dice are telling me how to think? Or do you think it seems to stay just this side of acceptable?

    \n@Paul_T I like that idea. Bridging off of it, I came up with the following:
    • Players invest some kind of Emotion Points prior to the start of a fight. This represents how dedicated they are to their point of view.
    • As they deal Morale damage to one another, they also chip away at these Emotion Points.
    • When the fight is Resolved, the person with most Emotion Points has their position proven "true." Whether or not this is actually true, the fight proved that their view of the situation is the "correct" one.

    What I thought was clever about my idea was reframing the purpose of the social conflict/argument as maneuvering for the combat conflict.

    In other words, we argue so as to determine what the fight will actually be about!

    These stakes can be positive or negative, or backwards or inside-out, and no one needs to agree to stakes they don't think their character would follow, so there's no "mind control" issue.

    For instance, the heroes confront the Villain, and they want him to turn himself in. But no, he wants to escape!

    First, however, we have a big discussion, complete with snarling and gloating and appeals to the Villain's humanity. In this phase, the heroes are trying to establish the stake that "on a loss, the Villain repents for his misdeeds". However, the Villain outmaneuvers them (or rolls well, or whatever), and reduces the stakes to, "The Villain will stop gloating about his evil deeds from now on". However, in exchange, the heroes have been forced to agree to, "We will question our loyalty to our leader."

    NOW we fight, and we find out which happens: ideally, all kinds of different things could be possible. Perhaps if the Villain wins by a huge margin, he gets to escape AND they question their loyalty to their leader. But if he wins by a narrow margin, he escapes BUT he is no longer gloating. If the heroes totally kick his ass, though, they take him into custody and he no longer gloats about his accomplishments.

    \n That's an interesting idea. Using something like Dogs' Fallout could work well: you have a fight, which determines how many changes (or how big the changes) you have to make to your character afterwards.

    You might pick from a few categories or otherwise constrained choices, but you decide how the action has impacted you. This could work really nicely!

    All you need to do is to apply some changes to your character sheet to show how the interaction has impacted you. With players who buy into the concept this could be powerful and full of surprises. It also encourages reflection after the fight, which is cool.

    In my conceptual post above, my idea was that the stakes would be set during the conversation via negotiation. So there shouldn't be a danger of "rolling to find you've been wrong": instead, the rolls or maneuvers strengthen your bargaining position.

    Ultimately, though, the two sides are putting some aspects of their characters' beliefs on the table, in exchange for a chance to change the other's position on something.

    You want the villain to hand over the hostages? Ok, you make an argument about their lives' worth, and you propose that as a possibility. The Villain accepts that and counter-proposes that, fine, that's on the table, but then reveals a dark truth about one of the heroes: his stake is that the heroine breaks up with her superhero boyfriend.

    Maybe the players all agree to this, or they keep negotiating. If it goes well, perhaps one side or the other gets to upgrade their stake: the Villain does really well in the social conflict, so he gets to escalate the stakes from "she breaks up with her boyfriend" to "she falls in love with the Villain".

    Now we move into the fight, and it has much more dramatic consequences.

    \n Combat in roleplaying games are usually number crunching and nothing more. The only interaction done is shuffling numbers to the next part.

    If you want people to play out a social conflict, then you need to make rules to make them talk to each other in a certain way.

    Create rules that color how they should say or play out saying thing, and you get a step closer to something more social - both in the fiction and in real life.

    So going in with a thinking pattern that social conflict should be treated as combat is to do yourself a disfavor.

    Mike,

    Fallout works like this:

    You have a conflict with your opponent. Each time you "take the blow" in the conflict, you collect a bit of "Fallout" (measured in dice).

    When the conflict ends, you roll all those dice and look for certain things. (The total of the highest two dice, but also some other things like lowest values.)

    Then you must apply the results of the Fallout, which include things like physical wounds (or even death! - these are mandated) and changes to your character sheet, like losing Traits, gaining new ones, and so forth. There are also a few temporary results, like "repent for something you've done", or "spend a scene by yourself", and losing important items/objects is a possibility.

    It's notable that this happens *after* the conflict is over (so you can decide how much of yourself to put on the line during the conflict itself), and happens via the player choosing from a list. ("Hmmm, it seems appropriate that I discard my relationship with my father, but also increase my Willing to Kill trait.")

    This is, therefore, both damage and character growth. You generally need both - if you don't take any Fallout, you won't get any character growth.

    If all your character sheet stuff is moral stances and relationships with other superheroes, this could be interesting. After a conflict, we measure just how much impact it had on you, and then you decide how it affects your moral stances or relationships. The player retains control and decides how this happens, but the impact is shown by some kind of change in the character.

    Given players who buy into the premise and I are interested in seeing their characters transform and change their points of view after a scrap with the Villain, it could work very well.
