\nHere's the combined class description and character sheet for a class I'm working on.

The weapon and armor is deliberately left unspecified so you can pretend you have thick fur, or princess-like grace, or great armor, or a chitinous bug hide etc, and deal damage with claws, biting, a frying pan, a graceful fencing technique, kung fu mastery etc. It's a more pretend-based class.

I know even pretty new players like some mechanical choices but when you're making your very first character, you don't know what's the purpose.

DMs, take note: This class does not use dex, strength, proficiency bonus etc but does use normal inspiration, advantage, crits etc. DMs, also note that the AC is 12 + ability bonus, so 15 at level one.

The intent is to support both these scenarios: that one or some player has this class and other players have normal 5e classes at the same table and neither will be overshadowed, or that everyone uses this (hence the "scroll mastery" -- if there are no casters, put a lot of scrolls in (both dungeons and shops), works great with spell cards if you have them. If there are those who have invested in spellcasting classes, use them more sparingly but the amount and style of spells the Eldritch Knight can be a guide). In both those scenarios the DM uses the normal monster and dungeon rules.

I wanted to compete with a style of game that's popular in Sweden, games like \xc4ventyr, Fantasy OSG, Nyckelringsrollspelet etc, and with Fantasy AGE. But way simpler.

    \n Thanks for posting 2097.

    You might be interested in a different approach I took to making 5E simpler (in particular, I wanted to get rid of the many trivial and trap choices): Microlite5E.

    ---

    For the Searcher, I'd recommend one big table that has every number at every level - AC, damage, hit dice, everything.

    I also think you'll need to tweak the numbers. Losing the proficiency bonus means that their saves are going to be way too low, for example. It also limits your options with the class, since you can't (for example) let them choose a skill or two that they're proficient in.

    Some people have run the numbers on a 5e character's expected damage per round, HP, save bonuses, etc. You might be able to dig those up and adjust the Searcher in accordance with them.

    \n Hi Sanglorian and thank you so much for the feedback! I had seen three completely different takes that all used the name Microlite 5E, including yours, and yours was the first one I looked at

    Here's what my thinking was on the points you bring up (not saying I'm right or won't change my mind, just wanting to tell you my thinking so far):

    Trivial and trap choices: The Searcher doesn't have any build choices (except whether they want to use their Identity Mastery ability)

    Are the saves really that low? The Searcher saves like the equivalent of a Str 16, Dex 16, Con 16, Wis 16, Int 16, Cha 16 character at first level, 18/18/18/18/18/18 at fourth level and 20/20/20/20/20/20 at eighth level. I was rather worried that that was too high, even without the proficiency (and the indomitable feature, which your version doesn't have either, right?). That's a much higher average save than the typical character -- we usually roll for stats, so where she has an average save of +3, they'll have an average save of +1.6. (Average bonus +1, plus +2 in two).

    One big table: I tried to find a compromise between minimal help and big scary table. For example, the hitpoints are derivable from level (she has the hitpoints equivalent to a Con 12 Fighter that never rolls, so 7\xd7level+4) but I decided to put them in there, while I saw the hit dice as so minor and I wanted the teaching opportunity to show the damage bonus and the ability bonus was the same and that was it. But also. The Searcher is designed for players who have help (the DM if noone else). Things like what "1d8" means isn't explained.

    Expected damage, HP etc. I just took everything from a Champion without fighting style and the extra crit. Remember that the searcher can do ranged attacks or melee attacks and magical attacks or mundane attacks. I have to be careful, I want to approach but not overshadow Warlocks, Champions etc. I still wanted there to be some sort of reward for those who chose their weapons so that they don't resent the Searcher player. Maybe this was misguided. The Searcher already looks kind of like the best class in some ways -- it'll have to be something only you bring out when the group is OK with one, more or all players having access to it.

    Extra attacks: Is your take on Microlite also adjusted with expected damage? The fact that the Searcher gets all of these complicated extra attacks and action surges was because in two of the three Microlite 5e games I read, I couldn't see anything about extra attacks and I thought they were key to approaching damage output.

    I thought none of the Microlite 5E I had seen so far had the same simplicity that the Searcher has, and none of them looked like they could mix&match with the full classes.

    \n At our table, we don't use the skill system but you'll notice that the attack bonus is the same as ability bonus + prof bonus at every level. So a case could be made for those groups that rely on skill systems a lot that the Searcher could use the attack roll for things they are trained in. At first, I was planning to write it that way ("Use this for attacks and the saves and checks you're trained in, and this for saves and checks you are not trained in").

    It just looked very OP to me given that it spans all attributes. I've ran a lot of 5e and my thinking was "Is a Dex 16, Wis 16, Con 12 character that isn't proficient in Perception or Stealth reasonable compared to what I've seen at my table? Yeah, that's a little higher than the median but below the peaks" (I built the Searcher to match a Dex fighter).

    Also, I was thinking this: at +4 (levels 4 through 7), she succeeds on DC 12 60% of the time and DC 20 20% of the time. I often use 12-19 DC as my equivalent of Dungeon World's 7-9 result (I.e. I'll have one outcome at DC 12 and another, better, at DC 20) and now, at +4, the success percentages of saves, checks etc perfectly map to Dungeon World's Fail, Partial and Success (40/40/20 % chance) and we all know how awesome play that can lead to! And I can bring some moves over from DW and AW!

    Also, my other favorite DC when I want binary pass/fail is 15. She has 50/50 on those, with +4. A little higher than most characters but still very low. The average character has again an average rolled stat of +1, a couple skills trained with proficiency and one ability increase by level four, so average is +2 on skill rolls. So the Searcher is already better than them even without using the "trained" version. But the non-Searcher classes have the option of having higher "favorite" skills, the Searcher is just better on average, not on peak. Which I think is great.

    I think a lot of people are turned off by making mechanical choices (such as strength vs dex) if they don't understand the significance of those choices.

    \n Having differences from 5e is in itself a very big complexity overhead, and 5e is pretty simple. I've had new players start playing in minutes, what I've used is a combination of a choice between one of several background sheets where you can roll or circle or write in the margins your traits (and the background stuff was the one part for the Searcher where I didn't want to compromise, where I did want all the choices to go) and one of six pregens (old look -- new look), and that has worked well. I'm still going to do that at cons, and bring The Searcher, too, just in case. I figure that the typical con audience would want a demo of 5e as it is, rather than a very simplified class.

    So I've already gone very simple but I wanted to enable the more "pretend-based" opportunities that the Searcher brings along, and I wanted to make it easier to have people join my campaign at higher levels. My con pregens are level one, and the "new look" variant of them even have a simplified explanation for spell slots that only is true at level one, and what I've done in the past is to take a level 1 pregen and give them hit points enough to match the other, leveled players which is obviously still way too weak. Hence, the Searcher.

    But anyway: So simplifying things to this extent brings the inherent complexity overhead of being different ("What's an INT? I can't find it on my sheet?") so it the simplification needs to be singificant for it to be worth it. 1d20 + 5 for attacking, 1d8+3 for damage, and 1d20+3 for everything else. That's it, plus the hp stuff!

    Oh! It just struck me that if I change the AC to 15 or 17, the "armor save" house rule that I sometimes like to use (a save to avoid attacks instead of monsters making attack rolls) can be one of these two numbers, too! I always use AC - 12 for the armor save (and ten+the monster's tohit as the DC) [the math looks weird but it gives the same percentage chances as attack rolls]. I'll go with 15 (which increases to 16 at level 4, and 17 at level 8) because then I can just remove the whole AC part from the class! Even simpler! The DM can note that the AC is 15 and making an armor save is the same as any other save! (Edit: I've now done this change.)

    So now it's really only four numbers: 1d20+5 to attack, 1d8+3 for damage, 1d10 for recovering hit points, and 1d20+3 for everything else, especially saves.

    Simon, that's simple enough that you can play D&D!

    \n The intent with the "new look" pregens was to have every ability modifier in a trained and in an untrained version. Twelve stats sound like a lot but since they replace way more than 12 numbers on the regular character sheet, it's a big gain.

    The other idea was to precalculate as much as possible. Like disciple of life that says your healing spells restore extra according to your wis mod. I just put +3 in there since that's the wis mod that that pregen has. It's still RAW but it's just pre-figured for you.

    \n So everytime I have a new player it's aways scary choosing whether to present the searcher for to them or to give them a normal class' pregen and eventually have them make their own character.

    If they get overwhelmed by rules they might leave (hence searcher).
    If they get bored by too FEW rules while everyone else is having fun they might leave too (hence normal class).

    We had a new player now who knows NOTHING about D&D. Like the entire HP, attack, etc etc everything was new. But the player plays other heavy games (such as advanced civ) so I went ahead with the full rules for them instead of "searcher" rules. I'm thinking here is someone who will, in time, find the crunch rewarding. Just giving them searcher while the rest of us spend so much time talking about dice and numbers and builds might be a bit boring.

    I gave them Dungeonesque which is a 3rd party reprinting of Basic D&D, with some things rephrased. There's just the basic D&D classes (life cleric, evoker wizard, champion fighter and thief rogue). And I've been referring to Dungeonesque all the time while playing now for a couple of sessions. And today their pregen died so we rolled up a new character together.

    I still love the searcher and I know people who are going to get searchers if they show up. It's just some people it's a hard call, and it's a heavy consequence if I guess wrong (they might leave).
