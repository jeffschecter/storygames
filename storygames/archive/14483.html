    \nWhat is Railroading and Scene Framing?

    There are multiple answers but here are two to start:

    Railroading: Control of a player-character's decisions, or opportunities for decisions, by another person (not the player of the character) in any way which breaks the Social Contract for that group, in the eyes of the character's player.

    Scene Framing: when someone establishes when a sequence of imaginary events begins and ends, what characters are involved, and where it takes place. Analogous to a "cut" in film editing which skips fictional time and/or changes location.

    \n I personally feel like railroading means the players are going to hit the GM's mark(s) at some point, and that those marks have to do with set pieces containing established setting details, participants and lines of conflict. I want the session climax to be a showdown between the players and the villain's minion, on the castle walls, starting with the revelation that your party's pathfinder has secretly been in the villain's employ the whole time!

    No idea what the Forge-approved meaning of "scene framing" is, but how I use it is to describe the process of a player highlighting what's important in the scene. It's a tool for establishing consensus about a scene. Yes yes, there are probably guards down in the courtyard who could shoot the characters but we don't care about that right now: what's important is that the walls are narrow, you're hemmed in on both sides and goddammit, you thought that pathfinder was your friend!

    Now on that note, I think scene-framing can be used to railroad. That's probably what the kids call "illusionism." If the GM is your sole view into the world (via framing), and that view "naturally" brings you to a particular conclusion, it might feel like you chose to hit your marks. But you'd only know it was an illusion if you rejected the frame -- if you fundamentally disagreed with the framer about what's important in a scene. That's an authority vs credibility thing. The GM might have the authority to frame the scenes, but if the players aren't buying it then it won't work and the rails become apparent.

    Railroad without framing = Rollercoaster ride.

    Railroad with framing = Illusionism.

    No railroad, with framing = Improv-heavy "storygame" style play.

    No railroad, no frame = Yeah good luck with that. Anarchy at the table!

    \n Railroading means that the decisions I get to make as a player don't have an effect, or have a negligible effect, on the narrative that results from play. Usually it is the result of the GM has a story that he wants to tell with a predetermined ending, and so any act taken by a character in the fiction will be steered towards that ending. It's not *inherently* a bad thing -- I've played in some amazing games that were run very tightly on rails -- but it's definitely a different sort of fun. And it's unpleasant to think you have freedom and find out you don't.

    I think that scene framing *can be* railroading. For instance, if one scene ends with Joe's character considering murdering Sylvia, and I frame the next scene in the aftermath, where my character comes across Joe holding a bloody knife over Sylvia's corpse? If Joe doesn't get to object, and I get to decide that he murdered Sylvia, he's being railroaded, because I'm taking away a pretty significant choice from his character. If he doesn't object, or it's what he would have chosen, or he says, "oh, I have a neat idea, let's run with this," then he's agreeing to what I proposed - which is probably not railroading, unless there's a weird power dynamic going on.

    \n Railroading IS a highly subjective phenomenon, by nature.
    Illusionism is an acceptable term for when the GM has a heavy hand and we're all buying into that.

    Railroading: when players' choices about their characters and the fiction are either directly shot down by a GM in the interests of focusing on the GM's contributions; or when players are manipulated such that their choices cannot help but "feed back in" to a) a pre-determined plot or b) the GM's emergent plot.

    Scene Framing: using cues and techniques from television, film, etc. to speed up play, specifically towards the end of removing unnecessary or uninteresting details or content. Occurs at both the micro level (player: "I go downstairs." gm: "Okay, downstairs you find...") and the macro level ("several days after the battle, the king's messenger arrives with a summons...").

    Obviously, "unnecessary" and "uninteresting" are value judgments. But it boils down to the removal/skipping of content or potential content to focus on material more suitable for the game, material thought to be more interesting (by the person doing the cutting...), etc.

    Also - arguably, if the GM is controlling input for the sake of the tone of the game (silly, serious, etc.), that is a form of railroading. I'm okay with that.

    \nI thought it was pretty clear? But okay, I can go into more detail.

    "Scene framing is railroading that doesn't break what I've agreed to" is basically the same thing as I just said - it says that they are the same thing, they are composed of the same actions, and sometimes you like it and sometimes you don't.

    Railroading is, simply put, scene framing (or any exertion of game power) that someone doesn't like. This is one reason why "railroading" is a problematic term that leads people in circles, because everyone sees it differently, through the lens of their own tastes.

    It may take away a choice from your character, but everything anyone says in the game does that. The issue is when it takes away a choice that you really wanted to make, or doesn't give you a situation compelling enough to make up for the disappointment of losing a choice.

    Here are some examples:

    "You're at the doorway to the dungeon." says the GM in D&D3. The GM is able to say that in the game, by the rules. If someone wanted to go shopping before they went to the dungeon, and they are more disappointed they didn't get to go shopping than they are excited by being at the door to the dungeon, then they will dislike this scene framing, and it will be called "railroading". But if they're psyched to get started, they'll be fine with it.

    "This scene takes place in my character's trailer, her boyfriend and the zombie wrangler are there." says the player whose turn it is in PTA2. The boyfriend's player may have wanted to have a scene where he's canoodling with the cute Federal Reanimation Council liason behind the craft services trailer. If she is more disappointed that she can't have that scene now than she is excited to have the scene that has been set, then she will dislike this scene framing, and consider it "railroading". But if they're psyched to try this scene and maybe didn't expect it and it's a good surprise, they see some potential there? Then they'll be fine with it.

    "Mmm, I don't think going to gather more allies is much of a possibility." says a Runequest GM. "The bad guys are raring to attack soon. You wouldn't make it back in time to save the orphanage." The player proposing to go search for more allies presumably wanted to do that. If they are more disappointed in not getting to do that than they are satisfied with the consistency and tone of the GM's world, then they will dislike this statement and consider it "railroading". But if they like the GM's world and are encouraged and made more secure by the consistency of the world then they will be fine with it.

    This matter-of-taste makes discussions of techniques to avoid railroading pointless. What's a great decision for one group is a disaster for another, based on their taste.

    \n "Scene framing" is an element of some roleplaying games, where the GM starts each segment of play by describing a situation the characters find themselves in, and then letting them loose in it.

    "Railroading" is a disparaging term for role-playing games where there is a pre-determined course of events (the "rails") and the players' actions can only move them along those rails; the GM thwarts any attempt on the players' parts to make anything else happen, by subverting their intentions, by punishing them, by remonstrating with the players out-of-game, etc. The fact that it's a disparaging term suggests that the players experience it as a bad thing; they feel frustrated or bored by it.

    These two things seem orthogonal to me. The presence or absence of "scene framing" in the rules of a game doesn't seem to have any more or less bearing on whether or not there will be "railroading" in that game, than the presence or absence of cheetos at the gaming table.

    Railroading is making someone else's decisions for them. One way is to frame scenes such that the railroadee has no decisions to make other than the one you want him to make. The most insidious is to start thinking about it like chess, setting up complex stratagems that trick or force the railroadee into playing into your hands without him ever realizing it.

    (Oh, and the GM's not the only person who can railroad. Players do it to each other all the time. So everybody stop picking on GMs.)

It's still easy to conflate heavy adversity (esp. as applied through scene framing) with Force, but it's something you figure out eventually: there's a difference between making someone's decisions for them and requiring them to make decisions. The difference is options.

    \n I can't believe that I am the first one to point it out, but... "scene framing" as a technique DOESN'T EXIST, as it doesn't exist a technique called "playing" that is the same in every rpg.

    But, EVERY rpg, has one or more ways to frame scenes. The framing of a scene is something you can't do without, in a rpg (You open the door to the dungeon room.. someone HAS to tell you what you see inside!), But the rules about how that is done and who can do it are all over the place. THESE rules are what differ from game to game.

    Examples:

    PTA: the player say "my next scene is at the marketplace, and...", the GM MUST frame a scene in the marketplace. But he still get to choose who is there at the beginning.

    Ttollbabe: the player say "my next scene is at the marketplace, and..." but the GM can say "no, the city guard ambush you on the way to the marketplace" (the GM can frame the scene as he wants, but he can't "move" the trollbabe in another direction)

    Spione: the player can say "the next scene is at the marketplace", and frame directly the scene, for ANY character.

    Every one of these is "scene framing", but it's a DIFFERENT way to frame a scene.

    As always, it depends on the game. Not every game is the same.

    Said that, the only times I have seen a "railroading" way to frame a scene in a game is with "traditional" games (D&D, Ars Magica, etc.) where there aren't explicit rule about this.

      \n I think embedding "it's bad!" in the definition of railroad is misguided, because that then requires a different term that means "exactly like railroading in every way, except that it's possible to like it". In the wider world railroads don't have negative connotations. Plenty of people enjoy model railroads. Some people want to "win the future" by building high speed rail projects.

      Do people think the GM's turn in Mouse Guard is railroad-y? I do, and I also think it's a lot of fun (I often think of the GM's turn as railroad-y and the Players' turn as sandbox-y). The choices the players make during the GM's turn aren't completely constrained, but the sequence of events that the players experience is almost completely out of their hands. It allows the GM to craft a specific experience for the players. It wouldn't be right for every game, but it's fun in Mouse Guard. I had a few lackluster sessions of Mouse Guard because the GM was so afraid of "railroading" that he wasn't willing to push the events of the GM's turn and make it an adventure.

      \n I suspect people can argue over the definition of most words. In a recent thread I had someone ask me to define "history". I've seen people argue over the definition of the word "he" and "is".

      When I look up most words in online dictionaries, I'm often presented with multiple definitions and variations. Which is totally cool, usually context helps you pick the right definition. But the vagueness inherit in a lot of language can be used as a tactic. Usually these arguments are taken to extremes to serve some sort of agenda or political view point. It often doesn't actually have to do with defining what a word means. Context and intent are important.

      What something is and what something feels like can be completely 2 different things. That doesn't make the definition of what something is illegitimate.

      I'm a huge Robin Laws fan, but most of his attempts to show that railroading has so many meanings it is meaningless rings false to me. Many of his given definitions all have the same causes and stem from the same thing so they aren't actually, in my opinion, as varied as they seem at first. And I'm also a huge Ken Hite fan, but "railroading is a pejorative term for a game in which something is accomplished" sounds like politics to me.
