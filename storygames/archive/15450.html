    \n Good advices here. As Steve suggested, your mid-term goal should be to ensure that your neighbours will not be in a realistic position to project force against you whenever the other players wake up. It's not going to be useful for them to know that you dominate the board if they won't have the right forces in the right places for stopping you at that point. In this regard German and Italian wars in the east are both swell, and best motivated by having eastern Powers advance credibly in step with you.

    Specifically, France will benefit most from a dead England and an Italy embroiled in a continental war, so if you could arrange for an Austro-Italian war and an alliance with Russia (perhaps Italy-Russia-France triple alliance, which could be your long-term breadwinner), that would be an appropriate stepping-stone: you could depict Russia as a rising power, try to make him big and strong (and inconsequential to you), encourage everybody else into building armies to stop the Russian horde, build fleets yourself, kill England (ideally with little noise, although it's likely that the other players will realize the true nature of the game once a player falls), betray Italy and finish with Russia in second place. It is notable that Germany is a low priority threat for France as long as eastern pressure is kept up: Germany has all the reason to be invested and interested in eastern situations, so if you have a working relationship and Germany doesn't enter a western war early, then you can take your time, feed German complacence and maybe attack him in the back once you've got England and Italy sewn up.

    Of course, looking at this board position, it might not be possible to save the Russian; his southern line is so weak that only full support of the Turk will save Rumania from Austrian annexation. Then again, the Austrian should probably look to his own defenses. There should be room here for you to create your own spin that ends up with Germany and Austria fighting a war against Russia and Italy. Turkey could go either way, but you'd probably prefer it if he were playing his usual scheme of allying with Russia against Austria while waiting for a chance to "stab" the Italian. This arrangemend is a long-term goal, short-term it's probably most important to abuse the players from any early-game optimism regarding their ability to trust each other. Do whatever it takes to make Russia more powerful as a continental power, and the rest will pretty much take care of itself. Russia is your best ally here, insofar as we understand "ally" as somebody who's growing strength is also to your benefit.

    What I would do diplomatically with such a strong position would be to not deny it, but downplay its significance. Allow the other players to feel that they can talk to you and influence your direction. Let's practice: "Yeah, I'm doing pretty well now. It was lucky that I got France, it's the strongest Power on the board due to the lack of natural enemies. I wonder what direction I should turn to, now?"

    Also, have a third-party (Russia would be ideal, he has an interest in the deception) start spreading rumors about an English intent to move to the Channel and start a surprise war against France. Perhaps Russia convinced England to stop their mutual bickering, and he doesn't quite know it, but he can guess that the English turncoat move will be to attack France now that he's in peace with Russia. This will be your casus belli when you move to the Channel yourself, it'll be more convincing if the other players heard in advance that England might be moving on you; you aren't actually going to attack England, heaven forbid, but you had to make the move to defend yourself, because you heard this rumour. Once you're in the Channel and get your actual surprise convoy it won't matter what England thinks afterwards, but ideally Germany will think that you chose to attack England over attacking Germany.

(frex, on the map, the Austrian's army in Galicia is as much a diplomatic fact as it is a strategic one, yeah?) Actions speak louder than words after all, so you generally want to make sure that your moves on the table reinforce your story and your story reinforces your moves.

    So you'd generally (i) pick a target, (ii) fabricate a reason that'd be seen as justified by other players for actually attacking it, and (iii) then take what's rightfully yours.


    To answer your actual question... I'm coming up empty on foolproof diplomatic strategies you could use. Generally, I'd guess you'd want them to attack each other, but without actually asking them to do it yourself (no one likes a ringleader), which is a tall order.

    A mild way to foster a general level of mistrust is to outline a purely rational, theoretical way of why, say, England is or will be in the future be a danger to Germany, then ask if the other player has considered it. Nothing too pushy, just facts and asking of opinions. Granted, you probably already employ this fairly often already.

    Alternativelly, you could just draw attention to something that the other guy may or may not be already considering a threat, and then gracefully propose yourself as a mediator to solve "the issue."

    Example, straight from your game: "Oh hello, Italy! Listen, I don't want any of us central powers to start infighting so that the outer ones could start nibbling at us from the sides... So if that German army in Tyrolia is bothering you, I could try talking to the German to remove it." If Italy wasn't considering that army a threat before, he'll have to do it now, even if it's in the form of "Wait, what is France talking about here?"

    Basically, sowing seeds of doubt at every corner. Not all of those seeds will grow up or even bear fruit, but some of them might, and that's probably as good as you can get.

    The idea about spreading a rumour about an English attack on France is interesting! Is there a way I can pull this off without lying to anyone?
    \nWell, you'll need to lie to eg. Russia to get the rumour started, but it's a lie that's difficult to pin down, and one you can't be called on afterwards, and one you can qualify a lot. For example, you might approach the Russian to ask him about whether he's been talking with the English (quite a natural thing to ask, you should talk the most with the people you're fighting), and whether he knows if England is planning to stab you. You develop the theme by explaining that of course you wouldn't expect the Russian to reveal such a plan to you if the two have already achieved an accord, but perhaps you could convince the Russian to ally with you, instead? And then once the Russian denies one or both suppositions (the English stab and the Russo-English alliance), you'll tell him that actually, you heard this from a third party (you might name eg. Italy if you had to, as Italy has a natural motive to both know about such a plot as well as fabricating the entire thing), so maybe the Russian is holding back on you?

    The goal here is to convince the Russian that you're suspicious of England and have got it into your head that he might be plotting to move to the Channel. Once you've accomplished this, you confide in the Russian and try to get him to ally with you against the English in case of the attack; tell him that you'd rather prefer to operate on the continent, but you'll be holding back a bit of force in case the English threat realizes, and you also want to set it up with the Russian that he'll back you up against the English if your fears come true. Now, even a substandard Russia should push at you at this point to make you commit to fight against England immediately on his side; you'll be hesitant and complain about how you've been getting along with England pretty well, really. Then you break the negotiations and ask the Russian to ask the Germans (or other likely party) about it - you're interested in the alliance, but would also like to know more about the board situation, so how about Russia asks around a bit to find out whether that English threat is real.

    Ideally the Russian, now that he's been given a clear mission with a clear reward, will go and ask about the English thing from everybody. He might even ask the English. He might or might not mention you, which doesn't do any harm, as your ultimate goal here is to establish a casus belli and portray yourself as a bumbling, happy fool who just happens to end up in a war with England due to a funny misunderstanding probably concocted by the Italians or Russians. So the Russian goes around and then comes back to you at the end of the negotiations, which is the optimal time to close an alliance, and he tells you what he discovered: probably it's going to be that nobody knows anything for certain, because he wants to be honest with his new ally, but he also might color his tale to various degrees, or somebody else might even have colored it for him. No matter, you're going to take what he gives you and plot with him against the Englishman, all the while making sure that he trusts you and intends to keep everything very, very quiet. Depending on the particulars you might commit to a surprise attack on England, or you might just tell the Russian that you'll keep a vanguard while going to eg. Germany; what you tell him is not important, as your actual move to the Channel can be retroactively justified as a secret defensive move you decided to take because you thought that England might be coming at you right now.

    (What if England hears the rumour? There is a distinct danger here, for were I England, I would naturally answer this sort of rumour by actually doing the move suggested therein, or by concocting an even more dastardly counter-move. Still, even the worst-case scenario has you make a harmless bounce with England, which may be easily spun into a stab on the next turn. It'll also lay good groundwork with Russia and Italy and Germany, who all see that England actually was plotting to move against you. Self-made prophecy is the worst outcome, it seems to me.)

    The ideal outcome of this process is that your next move looks like you're going to be a happy and dumb France who just shores up his positions against Germany and Italy (without betraying either - you'll agree with them in advance about the borders, and stick to those agreements), but also moves to the Channel as a separate, accidental brainfart. Once your move comes up you declare loudly how you're doing it because the Englishman is going to bounce you there; once the bounce doesn't come, you'll be embarrassed and keen to make amends to the Englishman, so at to prep him for the convoy on the next move. The important point is that on the first move the whole board (with the possible exception of Russia) thinks that you were just making an overly paranoid defensive move; you can tell England that Italy told you that England had offered an alliance against you, and the whole board will subtly back the idea that a possible English stab had been in the air earlier. This whole thing hopefully allows you to have a surprise against England without giving the other players cause to see you as the ruthless and conniving backstabber you really are.

    The only place in the above where you absolutely need to lie is when you first concoct the story about Italy telling you that England offered to stab you. By providing this unsourced to a third party (Russia) and being skeptical about it yourself, you give yourself quite a bit of distance from the rumour. The fact that you're going to "ruin" your English relations because of that same rumour further insulates your credibility - why ever would you yourself start a rumour that causes you to make a "mistaken" move?\n

The cardinal skill in Diplomacy is to recognize the difference between strategic gains and merely material ones; the former become an organic, defensible unity that provides you with influence, while the latter are merely unnatural extensions of your sphere where you're forced to commit not only political credit, but also one or more units.     \n It occurs to me that it might not be a bad idea at a relatively green table to conquer some non-integral SCs and then outright give them out as gifts to your "best" allies (meaning, the players you least wish to stab you). Alternatively, simply supporting such allies because you can afford to help others would be a strong signal as well. This might be seen as a gracious and non-aggressive gesture from a leading player, a gesture that would seem to indicate a willingness to avoid confrontation by helping your allies get a leg up as well. In reality you would of course select any such gifts with an eye on entangling and committing them to do your bidding. When framed correctly, doing something like this can engender blind loyalty; such concrete largesse is a rare thing in secure and powerful Powers, after all.

    Examples of how to play according to this principle as a dominant France would be supporting England into Denmark, supporting Germany into Venice and supporting Italy into Munich. (Not all at once, obviously.) All of these are moves that entangle your immediate neighbours with each other while providing them little benefit; all of those SCs are poisonous to the mentioned Powers when taken before certain antecedents are in place despite their relative proximity. Sometimes you get an opportunity to develop a truly grandiose buffer state in this way; for example, you might attempt to attract Germany into helping you with some non-essential moves against England in exchange for Norway (ideally with Russia still in Sweden), which will then force Germany into essentially taking England's place as the northern buffer against Russia, which in turn means that Germany will be unable to commit against you later on, even as you may choose to attack his overly thin lines whenever you desire.     \n One interesting tactic you might consider in this situation is deploying an "ambassador unit."

    Lots of things can go wrong with this, and it requires a certain amount of organization to put into effect without pissing people off, but the basic idea is to deploy one unit far behind enemy lines. From there it becomes a lever for negotiations with players on all sides, giving you a vital, but also somewhat innoffensive stake in ongoing negotiations on the other side of the board.

    Thus, for instance, if you were to persuade the Germans and Austrians to gang up on Italy and take Venice while moving the Gascony army into Marseille, you then have an opportunity to move into Piedmont to assist Italy / Germany, and then over a number of years wander / retreat up into the no-mans land between Germany, Austria and Russia. Alternatively, if you can sell Germany on it, you might move into Munich at the beginning of the year and then out up to the front to "help" before you take the center.

    From there you can probably see the opportunies to play all sides against each other. It is devilishly hard to destroy a force when it doesn't have to defend a supply center to maintain itself. The secret is to allow this to happen naturally and to act hapless and confused about it. Rather than negotiating agreesively, you can sollict advice about what to do with the army that has just happened to end up over there. As you might imagine, everyone will have something they want to sell you with, and it gives you great leverage for your activities elsewhere.

    Finally, there is the advantage that having even a single unit over on the other side of the stalemate line makes it far easier for you to break through in the endgame. Its a nice little bit of extra insurance.

    \n Good points here, all.

    My personal experience in being a dominant player has been that at a weak table it's often most useful to rely on your tactical superiority and just bash your way quickly into victory. This relies on the concept of sociopsychological friction - players take time to come to conclusions, and if the table is on the clock (as is the case around here most of the time), the other players won't have the time to build up a Grand Alliance and succeed in stopping you even if they could have done it given sufficient time. Even without the clock it's usually useful to minimize the window between when you're declared the soloist and the moment when you win. A weak table is one where not all players come to the same conclusions on the same turns, which means that the Grand Alliance only starts up one or two turns after it becomes obvious to the second-strongest player that you're on the brink of victory. These few turns of confusion are "free" time that should be utilized to the utmost, and they will be lost if you trade speed for diplomatical deniability.

    The opposite play would be to sneak to victory, but that's more of a play for a median table, one where everybody plays somewhat rationally and you know that the Grand Alliance will have a player who's able to play a whole-board tactical game for the alliance. In that situation it could be useful to try to sneak up into a strategic position that enables you to be closer to soloing than the others realize. (As an example of such positions, England and Italy often need to sneak a fleet through the Gibraltar before their solo bid.) This isn't a high priority in my own play, though, as I don't quite believe that Diplomacy has sufficient depth for deceptive positioning on the merely strategic level. It's often more useful to sow chaos diplomatically and play a straightforward strategic game. Even when playing Italy or Russia I only play passively because the unique weaknesses and strengths of those Powers force me to.

    (The above goes for soloing, strictly - midgame attempts to turn the tables as the underdog often benefit from deceptive strategic positioning; in fact, Italy cannot be played effectively without it, I'd say.)

    For completeness, I'd say that a strong table is one where the above concept of slow vs. fast play loses its meaning because the other players have a sufficient quality of communication and analysis that they won't be deceived, nor will they hesitate to respond. These players will play a Grand Alliance almost as well as a soloist can play, and they will analyze positions exhaustively, judging your strategic strength near correctly. The key to soloing at that sort of table is diplomatic and deeply strategic, hinging on setting up eg. cat's paw Powers that draw attention and resources, enabling you to come from behind with leverage caused by the fundamental asymmetries of the game board. (I say it this way for a reason: I'm not sure if it's possible to win the game at this level of play on a symmetrical board.)

    Looking at the above, I'd say that aggressiveness is the largest differentiating element when playing a weak table: because the weak players are less coordinated, less reliable and tactically inferior, it is generally to your advantage to seize material advantage fast and bargain it into even further advantage, pushing towards the end-game directly. This is not to say that you shouldn't play the full game, using all the tools available, but the generally chaotic play makes many finer strategies unreliable, and if the game comes down to you vs. a Grand Alliance in roughly even positions, you should be able to bring in the win in the tactical crunch. It is rather annoying to be playing a finessed mid-game only to have your ally stab you randomly, ruining both of your chances of victory. These types of situations are best prevented by being quick to rise to a position where you don't need to rely on the competence of the other players as much.

    If the above convinces, then the prospective soloist should start planning for his end-game crunch relatively early. Stalemate lines (or rather, defensive corridors) play a role in this consideration, but I'd say that even more important is to plan the geographical lay of the Grand Alliance in a way that causes the alliance to be slow to compile and even slower in fielding its full strength. You should remember that you're the most important player in shaping the GA: it will consist of the Powers you don't eliminate from the board, holding the SCs you don't conquer before the GA comes into being. This foresight should aid you in ensuring that the GA will have Powers that won't be your neighbours, Powers that need to move through each other's land to reach you, and Powers that have perverse incentives against the GA (Powers whose homelands are controlled by other GA Powers, Powers that are almost as strong as you are and therefore potential soloists themselves, etc.).

    The above argument is why I'm throwing out strategic end-game goals for you so early: having a strong, land-based Russia will all but ensure that you win against the GA in the endgame, for example. Another important poisonous factor for the GA would be if you can prevent the eastern triangle from resolving; a Turkey stoppered by Austria and Italy is almost useless against any western Power. To ensure that you'll have an easy time against the GA, try to get Russia deep into the Balkans, keep Italy virile against Turkey, and cross the Mun-Ber stalemate line by dominating the Baltic Sea when the time comes.

    The above is an example of one method for masking your strength: conquer two SCs and give one to somebody else (your weakest ally, ideally), which makes it seem like you're reduced in strength when you actually just gained one SC and gave one to an ally. Player psychology is such that players are much more sensitive to the direction and speed of development than absolute numbers, so by ensuring that your most recent plays have been "setbacks", you project the idea that your empire is struggling and not nearly as dynamic as the other guy's. The best thing is that you get most of the diplomatic advantage of giving away SCs by merely promising to give them up - it's easy to invent reasons for not actually doing it afterwards, and in the meantime you're winning time and giving the rest of the board an overall impression of being a fair guy who's willing to even give up power to ensure board balance. Remember about lying: the only guy who really cares that you lied is the one you lied to, the other players really only see the board developments.

    \n The sort of ancillary cultural elements you describe (re: "peaceful nations") are pretty common in Diplomacy. The game being so simple, it encourages players to develop models of play and heuristics of interpretation that are often not much based on the game, but rather the local, accidental culture of play. My own approach regarding this sort of thing is usually to attack the local culture and supplant it with my own - I do not particularly do this because my way of play is superior (although I do think that I have a truer understanding of pure game, and it's more fun when played my way), but rather because it's to my advantage to control the terms of communication by providing the other players with new conceptual frameworks and strange new ideas. By taking up my new viewpoints and ways of thought the other players come into and play my game rather than some other, difficult to control game.

    (Why do the other players accept my framing of the game? Because I do active culture war and nobody else phrases the alternatives quite as well, and because I offer tools that work. It makes sense to treat the game the way I do, and the tools I offer to the other players are the same ones I use myself, so it's not a matter of "believing my lies" so much as buying into a system where I have the most experience and therefore an advantage. If one were to agree with my ideas on the game, then my culture war strategy might merely be seen as educational rather than propagandist - we play, I win some, you win some, and in the end you've learned the way I think about and play the game.)

    Posted By: Paul T.Is there some way that this current situation would benefit from a more reasoned, logical, long-term view of the game?
    \nWell, mostly you'd use memetic warfare to address specific tactical situations where you need to influence the game to swing to a different direction. For example, if the game goes the usual way, you'll soon be pressed to figure out a way to keep Austria alive. (A quickly dying Austria is a very bad signal for western Powers, you should do your best to prevent the eastern triangle from resolving.) A typical way to go about that would be to share your concerns about Austria with eg. Turkey, where you might attempt to convince the Sultan to help Austria survive as an ostensible client state. You would do this by discussing long-term strategic issues ranging from the benefits of keeping Austria alive to the benefits of assaulting Italy "before it's too late" to the benefits of surprising Russia with Austrian aid, etc. As long as you have a superior sense for the game, you will be able to phrase a rational argument for whatever it is you want the other players to do.

    So you don't so much apostolize about long-term views of the game, but rather inject these viewpoints into ground-level discussions of concrete tactical choices. The strategic viewpoint is your justification for why you do what you do, and your argument for why others should play the way you want them to play. This tends to work in my experience, as opposing interests do not have an equivalent machine of strategic thought backing them; just like aboroginal religions have rarely stood up to the Christian theological machine in open debate, sentimental and irrational arguments provided by other players to convince each other don't seem to be as effective as pretending to master the entire game with super-rational strategic depth.\n
    Posted By: Paul T.I am definitely trying to educate people in the game, as well, but it seems almost ignorance might be the ticket for me right now. Which particular points might you bring up at this stage in the game?
    \nWell, I usually concentrate on laying the groundwork for stabs and generally ineffective chaos early on. For example, I would definitely make sure that Austria realizes his precarious position and the interest the Juggernaut has in his quick demise. Similarly, I'd see if Turkey might be drawn into a futile Italian war, out of which I could get him to rebound into a stab without follow-up against Russia a bit later. Russia should turn against Turkey immediately after the first signs of success against Austria happen; an army in Armenia is a beautiful thing. All this stuff is accomplished by laying early groundwork, encouraging bold play from the other players and egging them on to keep up with yourself. People often say that it's not good to be seen as encouraging other people to fight amongst themselves, but I tend to abuse this notion out of my diplomatic relations early: it's not like the game can be won without fighting wars, so I should think that it's better to fight the right ones, and if we had some common interests where we could cooperate I would surely come in and join the fights I'm encouraging at the moment. Ultimately, though, everybody should play according to my strategic geopolitics, which can be easily used to explain why I have to attack your ally instead of attacking your enemy at the moment. Never worry, though, if you do well against your current enemy, we'll be allies at the end-game.

    All of this is rational argumentation that relies on substituting a given player's ignorance and existential need for explanatory models with the models I provide. Ignorance is unreliable, and what's worse, one of the simplest heuristics players can have is "attack the leader". (Only "I'm happy to just survive" and "get more SCs" are simpler.) Angry mobs are easy to get up for anybody at all when there's a smidgen of reason for it on the board, which is why I try to lock other players into dogmatic frameworks that help them resist third-party agitation like this. A properly indoctrinated player will be more amenable to betraying the mob to my benefit later on, even if a mob gets to form, as he's not attacking me merely because somebody else told him that I'm in the lead, but because he can read the board himself and see when I've been pushed back sufficiently. In this and many other ways teaching other players brings benefits to you: instead of playing a guessing game that exploits perceived trains of thought in your co-players, you move to a higher level of play where you teach the other players to make evaluations of the board positions, then predict their evaluations and evaluate better than they do.

    Oh, one other thing I like to focus on early on: by teaching the other players to appreciate the beauty and morality of correct play you ensure that those players will actually enable you to win the game later on. For example, "Ride the Tiger" was mentioned earlier: that's a line of argumentation that is simply unworkable with players who are not already deep into my particular insanity! Riding the tiger (joining the soloist against the Grand Alliance to build up your own negotiation position and prevent your own elimination later) makes no sense whatsoever for a player who has not accepted the idea that his goal in the game is survival and victory or a sharp draw (in that order). Players with unexamined and irrational values find it very easy to fall from this moral standard to lesser convictions such as "at least I can make sure you lose, too" or "I've already lost, but at least I can help out this guy who hasn't yet stabbed me once". Players like this are very troublesome for you later in the game, as they will often not see a route to victory for themselves, and will therefore play a nihilist game that will more likely than not be directed against you. Teach the correct morals early and often, and you'll find that the other players will play their own game instead of playing against yours.

    (To clarify, I truly think that "Ride the Tiger" and similar seemingly radical ideas are correct play and not merely cruel ploys - if there wasn't truth there, it would not convince others reliably. The reason for why the player who decides to ride me in the end-game ends up losing is that he doesn't have a sufficient tactical understanding of the game to turn against the soloist early enough. It is entirely fair to say that my indoctrination of the other players is directed at making them play the same way I do so as to enable me to better predict their play. My success will then emerge from the fact that I play the tactical game better than the opposition. If I don't, then I lose.)\n

    After that, I could simply attack Livonia-St. Petersburg and claim Sweden to win the game. Their next attempted attack was a move to Piedmont, but, of course, at that point the game was done. Nevertheless, I avoided that one as well, by moving to Tuscany, which added to the laughter around the table: in the last four moves of the game, I didn't have a single bounce or unsuccessful move anywhere on the board.

    Turkey used his last move to screw over his allies, destroying Austria-Hungary and Germany by invading Venice, Vienna, and Trieste.

    Because the Turkish forces were rearranging themselves to move into the Mediterranean, I even got into the Ionian.

    I suppose the takeaway lesson is: when you're "doing too well", just try to keep on going. Play good tactics and hope you can pull off a victory before the others can get into position to stop you. There's not much in the way of a different diplomatic angle, except maybe to downplay the strength of your own position.

    However, I did have quite a lot of success in using my superior position to support various allies (Scotland, Italy, Germany on one occasion), even allowing them to build new units, which garnered me some trust and made my advance overall seem less aggressive. In the end, Italy was so grateful she didn't even try to stop me from taking Tunis!

    This was an incredibly successful game for me: I never had to disband a single unit, never lost ground, never really lost a single battle (even for position), for beginning to end. I don't expect to see this kind of game ever again. (In particular, if I ever play with these people again, I have little doubt they will do everything they can to crush me immediately, as soon as the game begins. At the same time, however, I have really developed a reputation as a trustworthy and honest player: for two games in a row now, I've kept every single promise I've made and only given good advice. It will be interesting to see how these two factors work together if there will be another game next year, although I suspect fear and the desire for revenge will win out!)
