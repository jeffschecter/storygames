\n Hi folks,

For about 6 months I've been excited about running something using Pocket Danger Patrol. This game checks a lot of boxes for me: It has very clear mechanical procedures for determining events. It doesn't require the GM to arbitrarily set difficulties. It has a cool, scene-length economy that decides the result of a conflict at a macro level driven by all of the action and stunting in the individual sub-conflicts. It seems like it will play like a cool cooperative boardgame like Pandemic or Castle Panic. And it's written by John Harper, whose Agon and Lady Blackbird I've really enjoyed.

But in preparation for running a one-shot this Saturday I did a brief "lonely fun" walk-through last night and I realized that at some fundamental level I don't get this game! I need your help.

Here's the core of it: There don't seem to be any meaningful decisions for the players in Danger Patrol.

I've come to really think about game mechanics in terms of the choices they force players to make. D&D is all about risk assessment, knowing when to retreat and deciding how many resources of various kinds to expend on overcoming challenges. Sorcerer is all about deciding how badly you want things and how much you are willing to risk your Humanity in order to get them. (In fact, I suspect that it's reading Sorcerer & Soul that really got me into this way of assessing games.) Most other games I've enjoyed -- from Tunnels & Trolls to Bliss Stage -- seem to fall somewhere near one of these two poles in terms of risking either Material or Self to obtain objectives.

But in Danger Patrol the only real tactical choices seem to be:

1) How much should the PCs disperse themselves amongst the Threats? It seems like a pretty safe strategy is to just double-team the threats 2:1; this seems like it more-or-less guarantees that each marked Threat will get wrapped up in a round. Certainly in my one test run it did. The downside, presumably is setting for a perhaps-more-conservative rate of Threat reduction and thus perhaps greater increase of overall Danger.

2) When should PCs take Recovery actions in lieu of their regular turn?

But neither of these seems to embody an interesting tension -- either tactically or in terms of the narrative. The choices seem too simple and one-dimensional to offer much tactical satisfaction. And there aren't really any narrative priorities for them to be integrated into.

Why does any of this matter? Well, it matters because in my "lonely fun" situation I was taken aback to find that I didn't have any tools for making decisions about which Threats the PCs should apply themselves to. I ended up with what I consider the worst decision: If I constantly double team the Threats I believe I am more likely to win, but I'll feel bad that I am exploiting a weakness in the rules in some way. But if I don't exploit that weakness I don't feel there is any other hook on which to hang a decision and play seems slightly aimless.

It may well be that in real play, with real players, this game is going to run just fine. The players I play with are mostly pretty Sim-focused, fiction-first types and will probably engage the Threats based on purely in-fiction parameters and ask me to remind them which dice to roll round on round. But I'll know that the game doesn't "make sense" to me.

So. Am I over-thinking this? Am I just someone whose aesthetic priorities don't line up with Danger Patrol? Or am I overlooking something which would help my brain unlock this game and start to grok it?

    \n In your Lonely Fun walk-through, how many "characters" did you have in relation to total Threats? What Strengths and Weaknesses did the Threats have?

    I've run Danger Patrol several times, in all three iterations of the rules. With Pocket Danger Patrol, yeah, it's kinda gears specifically towards hard-hitting action, not so much for risk assessment. But that really kind of captures the feel of the pulp genre. Everything looks easy-peasy going in...until a bunch of Danger gets rolled.

    To date, in the ten or twelve times I've run Pocket Danger Patrol, one action scene has always lasted the entire session, and in most cases, the players got creamed at the very end. They pretty much ignored the option for Recovery, but it's always been a laugh-riot of actiony fun.

    The thing that really has kept characters from steamrolling the Threats have been the Strengths and Weaknesses, as well as the descriptions of the Threats themselves. I always try to make sure to have the players describe what they're doing before they gather up their dice from abilities. I don't want them to automatically pick their highest abilities, and so after listening to their description, I can police it and say "So, how exactly are you defusing the bomb with your Battle ability again?"

    Also, choosing to add Countdowns to certain Threats (particularly 1-point countdowns), or having Complications at the very beginning can help.

    I've also brought back staged threats from the old rules - some Threats are protected by others, and can't be touched unless the guarding Threats are taken out first.

    If, somehow, this still seems too easy, here's a couple of my house rules:
    Major Threats (big bosses, super battleships, etc.) have Strengths, but they let them IGNORE certain abilities ("Sorry, it's immune to Battle...it's just too big to be bothered by your weeny guns!").
    Complications, instead of adding Threat dice to your roll, automatically add 1 Danger to your result, no matter what you roll.

    Maude Minervois, Ghostly Daredevil
    Daring 2 Power 0 Heart 1 Weird 2
    Action 2 Cunning 2 Battle 1 Science 0

    Deeds of derring do 2d8

    Action 1 Cunning 1 Battle 2 Science 1
    Pearl-handled six-shooters 2d8

    Dagon Cultists
    Strong against Science

    Strong against Daring
    Weak against Weird, Battle

    A raging fire
    Strong against Power
    Weak against Nothing!

    A mutated mega-shark
    Strong against Action, Heart
    Weak against Heart, Power

    Deep One mariners riding things which were not bats and not porpoises, yet terribly suggestive of both
    Strong against Battle
    Weak against Power

    Additionally, the following Complications came up during play:

    The Eye of Atomato (used by the Cultists to bind Maude's ectoplasmic form)

    A Yowling Maw of Spectral Energy (opened by the Priest's ritual)

    Hungry Spectral Hounds (which emerged from said Maw)

    Blood in the Water (after Mickey got gashed by a Deep One's spearhead)

    The "anchor" (a semi-sentient carnivorous seaweed bio-weapon the Deep Ones sprayed Mickey with)

    My concern is not really a GM's concern at all -- it's a player's concern: I don't think I would enjoy playing Danger Patrol because I wouldn't feel like any of the decisions I made had any impact on the outcome of play. Or, worse, I'd be tempted to exploit what I see as "system weakness" by ganging up on Threats and making sure we shut them down, which would feel weirdly ... uh ... munchkinesque.

    Strangely enough, the folks I play with don't really think about systems this way and would/will probably just enjoy engaging the fiction, making up great color and bouncing some dice. It won't be a problem for them. But I'll worry, you know?

    \n Hi Robert! (edit cross-posted)

    I've never found the challenges in Danger Patrol to be too easy. IME, it can be tactically loose because the danger dice introduce new complications all the time that can disrupt the most well-considered plans, but only in rare cases did it feel like it didn't matter which threat I attempted next. There's almost always some light tactical play (though not super heavy).

    The use of timers and different consequences for different threats definitely helps. Like if there's a bomb that will destroy the universe and a car crashing into a baby carriage, it might be tough to decide which to save first (after all, this is pulp!) but the consequences are definitely different. And if the bomb has a (2) and the baby has a (1), then it's baby first every time, yeah?

    P.S. Players often team-up against the most dire threat of the moment, but this was never weird, IME.

    \n There's a few things in that vein:

    * fictional positioning can be written into the threats themselves ("universe-destroying time bomb")
    * the danger may be ephemeral or it may be brought up repeatedly, providing context ("the floor's still electrofied")
    * complications are often the direct consequence of character actions ("the bomb's broken, but it's leaking neon goo")
    * sometimes character personalities or traits matter ("I hate robots!" / "I'll use my shrinking ray on the giant beast!")

    All of this helps to differentiate between choices that may not mechanically be that different, in addition to countdowns making them actually mechanically different (in pacing, meaning that some are more immediate than others).

    But I was really mostly talking about the fictional consequences of not successfully dealing with specific threats. Like, if there's a universe-destroying time bomb and the characters fail to stop it... then the universe is destroyed, or may be in the process of being destroyed, or the chars may have to go back in time and stop the universe from being destroyed, etc. The consequences for that are different than what happens if the Red Apes run rampant, which naturally means players prefer some fictional outcomes to others and act accordingly, whether that's mechanically incentivized or not.

    Does that help or are we still talking about different things?

    \n Yes, yes, yes, that's much more like it. The Pocket Version is shorter in length and rules density than earlier versions, it's but not meant to short-change the fiction. It's very much not a "declare your action and roll" thing, though it does have a formal turn-taking structure that can make it feel very much like old school task resolution in some ways. But the mechanics are not enough to make the game work on their own (though danger dice help). In order to care about all that zany stuff (like a manifesting mega-shark), the fiction has to matter and have real significance and/or consequences for the characters. You can kinda play half-heartedly, I guess, but it's more fun if you enthusiastically engage the pulpy stuff happening. "Oh no, it's Doctor Coldheart and his army of robot vampires! How will we ever stop them?"
