\n There's a Short Story by Neil Gaiman i found lately, that i think would make a terrific setting for any Cthulhu game, and, mostly, a very appropriate one for the investigation-oriented Trail of Cthulhu. The title is "A Study in Emerald". As you may imagine... yup, green blood.
In the preface, Gaiman tells that he was asked by the editor to write a story merging Conan Doyle's Sherlock Holmes and Lovecraft's Cthulhu Myths; he also, intelligently, points out that there is a sort of "creative conflict" between Holmes' rational world, in which eveything has a solution, and the horrific universe inhabited by the Great Old Ones, dominated by irrationality, and that just resolving the plot in one of these two directions would have been inappropriate. The solution he found is just Stunning.

I strongly advise to just read the story, which also won a Hugo award some years ago, you can find it on Gaiman's own website:

http://www.neilgaiman.com/mediafiles/exclusive/shortstories/emerald.pdf

(the format is somewhat clumsy, but provides just the right flavour)

for those who don't want to, or can't, take the time to read it, to put it simply (SPOILER, warning!), the world has been taken over by the Great Old Ones, who now rule over earth as the royal families. Sherlock Holmes, as any gentlemen of high culture and good sense, is perfectly in favor of this, as humans clearly cannot take care of themselves, and remitting the world's fate in the Crowned Ones' hands is the most obvious solution. There are others, though, who still strive against this god-kings, to give mankind his old freedom (blasphemous fools!). These conspirators can count some interesting names among their lines, like... Dr. Watson (Holmes' assistant is actually hinted to be Moriartry, who is usually Holmes' nemesis). EDIT: re-reading, it's apparent that holmes is on the "good" side, while the flawless detective is moriartry itself.


...So, what do you think about it?
