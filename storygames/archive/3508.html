\n Forgive me for the length of the post. Half of the detail is necessary for context, the other is me just talking.

Just over a week ago I found out about Go Play SouthEast. I immediately pitched it to the wife since she has family in Rogersville (half an hour away) as a way for her to visit them with our son and I could get some gaming in and meet some of y'all. I figured she'd want to visit her grandmother more than anything, so that's how I suggested it. She flatly declined because she reasoned that an 9 hour drive for one and a half days of vacation followed by a 9 hour drive back would be too much for our little one (he's 19 months old). I could find no argument against that logic, so I quietly shelved my GPSE fervor.

Then I decided a different tact. I asked her to come with me and her parents could watch the Lil Orkster. I suggested that the venue would be a nice semi-camping experience, something she has enjoyed but hasn't done for a while, and she could play some laid back games with cool people.

She wasn't too interested at first, so I pushed for details on what didn't enthuse her. She explained that since she gave birth, her free time has been extremely limited and gaming has lost its luster. She was most upset about the length of time she would spend creating a character (she likes deep research on the character mindset, including finding just the right picture for the character and usually a healthy write-up) coupled with the time spent learning the rules of a new game just to watch the campaign fizzle out in a few sessions due to scheduling. She pointed out the d20 game I tried to run with her and another couple over Skype. It only lasted three sessions before scheduling killed it.

I tried to pitch her on the collaborative elements of Story Games and how quick chargen and rules teaching would be, but she was non-plussed. I finally cajoled her into agreeing out of sheer persistence, but I swear I think she will have a good time.

For background, my wife and I started dating and I started running D&D 2.0 for her at nearly the same time. She was a very dedicated gamer through that campaign and even moreso during the 7 year long VtM chronicle and also during several Star Wars games. She enjoys the escapism and playing characters that grow over time. She's had one Story Gamey experience, a brief Dead Inside game I ran with her right before she gave birth. She had a blast during the 8 sessions it lasted.

So I told you that to ask you this. Based on the info above, what are some selling points I could use to stoke some interest for this upcoming Go Play event?

    \n Dear Mrs. Ork*:

    I would never recommend that you attend Go Play SE strictly to make Mr. Ork happy, even though he's very nice. However, if you used to game before, I suspect you might enjoy this, particularly with the little orkling being spoiled by doting grandparents. If you feel like giving it a try, here are some games I think you might enjoy. Feel free to cajole Mr. Ork into running these for you, not only at GPSE but before and after as well.

    If you like Dead Inside, I think you might enjoy The Zorcerer of Zo, which is based on an ever more streamlined version of the same system. So far, every time my husband has run it this game has won converts. It is very light-weight and can be used to run swashbuckling, Lord of the Rings-like, or Star Wars-like adventures. Another game based on the same system which you might enjoy is Questers of the Middle Realms, from Silver Branch Games.

    I have also had good success introducing people to story games with Cat, from Wicked Dead Brewery. Other good ones include InSpectres from Memento Mori Theatricks, Wushu from Daniel Bayn, and The Shadow of Yesterday, from Clinton R. Nixon. I also really love the game Roanoke, a spooky take on the lost colony using the Wushu system. Or, if you're in the mood for westerns, you might like Ronin Arts' Vs. Outlaws. Perhaps Primetime Adventures might also be a good choice, though I confess I find its mechanics a triffle unsatisfactory.

    All of these games are very rules-light, which means that character creation takes five to ten minutes. I could suggest many others, and I'm sure other people will have great suggestions too. They also share other interesting characteristics: everything on the (very sparse) character sheet is important, and your character can be effective right from the start.

    I hope you will feel like giving this a try, and I particularly hope you will have a great deal of fun. With luck, in a few years you might want to teach some of these games to the li'l orkling. :-)

    * I rather hope Mr. Ork will have you read our responses. :-)

    \n Ork-dude,

    I'd also try the following sell points for GPSE.

    1.) There are several very nice parks nearby that have walking trails, fish ponds, swimming pools, and other fun things to do if you like the nature thing.

    2.) If she likes shopping, Pidgeon Forge and Gatlinburg are within driving distance and they have a metric shit-ton of outlet stores and such.

    3.) Cades Cove is an awesome place to spend a day (or half a day). It's an old settlement from the 1700's way up in the mountains. Beautiful stuff.

    While I don't want to discourage her from playing (far from it), it might help to know that she can do other things besides just play while she's there.

    \n ...I'm still trying to figure out where in this story Mrs. Orklord abandoned her (very sensible) objection to putting the 19-month-old in the car for 9 hours of driving, one-way. As far as I can tell, that problem's not going away even if you both agree that GPSE sounds like fun.
