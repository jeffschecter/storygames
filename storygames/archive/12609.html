\n I have long been frustrated by the standard set of flaws offered up by systems that use such. Blind, deaf, missing limb, etc. don't grab me as interesting roleplaying hooks. I'm not sure how you would tell a "greedy" character from the usual RPG character. Coward offers some possibilities, but for lots of games really playing a coward means you aren't involved in the interesting parts of the game.
I like the idea of complications that can be tagged in ICONS and other similar systems. That seems to draw out more interesting possibilities.

What are some things that would make for compelling hinderances? Are there any systems out there that have really great sets of example flaws?

    \n I'm amazed the first words on peoples' mouths aren't 'FATE Aspects'.

    To me, 'good' flaws and hinderances aren't found in the type on offer, but in the way they are implemented. I like the FATE system because it rewards players for introducing them into the story themselves, rather than having the GM / Storyteller / Uberfuhrer of the game smack them round the head with it like a rubber truncheon. Not that this approach isn't valid, it can be quite fun to be limited by the games master. I just find it more satisfying to have complete control over my character, yet still have a reason to act like a total douche when it fits my persona's personality.

    -Ash

    Dogs in the Vineyard's d4 Traits are great in this regard. "Coward" is even a favorite because you're using it in order to win a conflict.

    \n

    A lot of this comes down to systemic support. "Wears glasses" is a good d4 trait, too: a character of mine would roll those dice when the rifle came out because she was farsighted and she took them off.

    \n

    Shock: Links are stuff that a character would never want to see challenged, but we do. You can have a Link to a dependent, for instance, and the antag player will know to bring them into play and plausibly threaten them. And you want them to do that because that's how you get to reroll.

    \n I'll second aspects, as posted above. As for specific flaws that are fun (which is, I believe, what you are asking about), I always liked "animal magnetism" as a flaw. Animals are drawn to you, especially when you don't want them to be. Think drug dogs, birds trying to perch on your shoulder when you're trying to hide, stampedes turning your way, exotic animals crawling into your luggage before an international flight, etc.
