    I was thinking about this exact topic yesterday. I've been observing the various indie games and trying to isolate which bits of their strategy (planned or unplanned) help to build up to a solid set of sales and recognition. Partially as I'm hoping to do something similar with Covenant.

    From what I've seen over the last few years. From observation, I think these are the key factors:

    -Grabby premise. If it can't be explained, and sound cool, in two sentences, then it's not grabby enough. More to the point if you can't do this on demand when asked about it, then people will pick up on it. You should have one memorable and unique mechanic too.

    -Get the game played lots: a) By playtesters b) At Con demos c)by people who have gaming blogs d)by active forum posters

    - Post early and often about your game on forums, well before it's out of playtest. Particularly indie game design. Jump on anything vaguely relevant on the Forge or rpg.net, answer the posting, have your game in your sig, only tag some promo on if it's really relevant.

    - Get some enthusiastic people onboard with the playtest. They'll sell the game for you by their enthusiasm. These people will generally confirm what you say in the threads mentioned above, and advertise your work for you. Since they're perceived as more neutral, people are more receptive.

    -Get actual play mentioned as often as possible.

    -Get in with vocal people who have a similar interest. Support each-others games.

    -Reviews only help if they come from people with a rep, or they obviously played the game, or if they are the first thing that turns up on google when somebody searches for Game X Review. If you have all 3 it might do half the job of an AP thread.

    -Flamewars are good for business. But be calm, and seen as talking sense when somebody rants about your material.

    Anybody with more self-publishing experience want to confirm or deny the usefulness of these?

    -Matt

    I just posted something to the Mindshare discussion about this, but basically it can be summed up with:

    First, craft a decent product. I don't think subject matter/topic is as all-powerful as the industry and customer base has been thinking it's been for the past few years. It's a factor, but obviously not the most important one.

    Frankly, DitV is a weird setting/concept and T&J is a pretty common genre. You can't say that oddness or familiarity are the primary drivers attracting interest to these games.

    Then, push stuff out -- announcements, freebies, playtest copies, demos, review copies, mailling lists, etc. -- early on.

    At some point (say, within the first month of release of the product), ease off, make yourself available for comments, and wait for pull/customer interest to rise -- otherwise, you could alienate the customer base.

    Resist the urge to constantly keep pimping, but you can interject small, short reminders (say, one per week).

    And that's it.

    One thing that people sometimes overlook about Vincent's success:

    The Quality of His Writing.

    Like, no shit, that dude can write some prose and you'll understand every word of it THE FIRST TIME you read it. More than that, you'll actually remember the most important idea of every paragraph because he pounds it into your head, makes sure you have it, and then still makes sure to remind you of it later on. Dogs is a great game to play, yes. But that book also contains some of the best RPG writing currently available anywhere.

    And, unlike almost every game on the market (including most other indie RPGs), the book ACTUALLY TELLS YOU HOW TO PLAY THE GAME, it doesn't make you figure it out on your own through practice or by thinking about it a lot. It says, "if you want to do X, you should start by doing, Y, and then Z, and then eventually you'll get to X, see? Here, let me draw you a diagram..."

    Vincent isn't trying to sell you a product. He's a mentor trying to show you how much fun you can have playing. And that's why he succeeds, because he's not really trying to.

    Isn't "taking off" a relative concept?

    I have DitV, but I haven't gotten around to reading or playing it yet. I have no plans to purchase Truth and Justice -- isn't that the PDQ supers game?

    I bought DitV and KPfS+CS on the merits of Otherkind. In fact, I told Vincent that I was paying him for Otherkind -- he just happened to be giving me DitV and KPfS for free. :) I liked the Otherkind so much that I very subtly (and subconsciously) incorporated one of its key concepts into my own publication.

    Anyway, I don't know how actual sales of DitV have gone. What does "taking off" mean? Breaking even? Turning a profit? Some other ROI calculation?

    I had the good fortune to find someone who thought my ideas were good enough to invest in and publish, as opposed to having to do everything myself. I had external validation from the get go. And it's still tough to see the game taking off even though every armchair review has rated it very favorably.

    I've incentivized truthful Actual Play reviews with cash payments, but I've had no takers yet. When I've run demos, I've sold plenty of copies. Apparently, the game gets great response after actual play -- nearly everyone wants it. This isn't just my experience, but the experience of multiple game demonstrators, on multiple continents.

    I was never naive enough to think I was going to get rich from this. I already have a steady job that I completely enjoy. More importantly, however, I wasn't naive enough to think that a solid game design would be enough to get my game noticed. All a solid game design does is validate a buyer's decision to purchase. If the validation happens before they purchase, that makes no difference. A good game design does not provide the impetus to purchase a game.

    It's not even enough to get reviews out there. Neither is it enough to demo the game. As chadu mentioned, these are all factors that help promote (incentivize the purchase of) your game, but they're not enough.

    Because they're free. They don't cost money. They just cost time. But time isn't what reaches the common consumer. Time only reaches those people who interact directly with you, who indulge you with their direct attention, who allow themselves to become a captive audience.

    To really push, you have to hit the consumer's peripheral vision. And that takes money. The internet doesn't count. We're talking about a limited audience that's often conditioned to ignore peripheral data. I know plenty of dedicated gamers who never go to cons, who never browse rpg forums. I'm pretty sure this population is the greater fraction of the total gaming population. And this greater population is whom you really want to hit.

    That's if you want to take off.
