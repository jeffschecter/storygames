\n The thread on 2008 Releases, here, left me fumbling a little bit... I have a couple dedicated projects right now, but most are going to take a long time to get to the state where I want them. Most aren't going to be finished and released as a final product in 2008.

So, Story Gamers: What projects are you actively working on that won't be released in 2008, and what development will they see through the next year?


My answers:


Perfect, The Original and Immaculate Revision. Perfect v2 is going to undergo a lot of playtesting throughout the year. Specifically, I want to make sure that the rules for Contacts/Secret Societies work and fit the fiction, I want to make sure that the "crime cycle testing" process feels natural and unimposing, and I want to make sure that the game feels as streamlined, simple and still tactile as possible. I regret publishing Perfect v1 without enough playtesting to get it absolutely polished. It was good, it worked, but it had its clinks and clanks to it. I want to explore various production avenues beyond the one book method. What I currently envision for Perfect is a lidded box which contains the rulebook, setting ephemera and props (see Cold City's "Dossier"), character sheets, con-play handbooks ("Quick-start guide"), etc.

Argentine Wolf Boys. Argentine Wolf Boys is a game I'm currently working on about orphan werewolves who work in the President of Argentina's private secret service. Pathos + Clandestine Action. I am currently starting playtesting for it. It won't be released in 2008 because: I want to make sure the tactical elements of the game are polished and un-clunky; I want to streamline the situation creation elements so that it works better on-the-fly; I want to write the text in a way that it can be played by total noobs. I also want to explore different physical packaging options, because Argentine Wolf Boys might come packaged with "pre-crafted missions" and NPC templates and mission templates and character sheets and a bunch of other play resources.


Wash. A game of apocalyptic community. Wash is set in the last (or maybe only) village that matters, and players take on the roles of the religious elite in this sacred community at the edge of oblivion and at the bend in the river. Wash hasn't seen a first draft yet, but lots of notes currently exist. I want to find some way of handling situation (not conflict, and not scene) before that first draft gets written. Wash is a mixture of ass-kicking in an apocalyptic epic, dangerous negotiations with dangerous allies, and a game about community resource management. It's going to take a lot to get that fairly far-sweeping scope to function as a cohesive, coherent game.

    Good question, Joe!

    \n

    For me, there are two (instead of your in-fucking-sane three)

    \n

    The Gathering Storm. My 2-player "all in a tuckbox" card-driven rpg. It's set in London during the Blitz, and it's all about short, brutal and (hopefully) uplifting slice-of-life stories. Outside of the purely SIS-based roleplaying stuff, there's a gently competitive game going on between the two players -- you're trying to destroy London just so. The bulk of the work is research -- I've got to scrounge up tons of imagery for the cards.

    \n

    Imperator. This one barely makes the list, but it's there. This one's a weird cousin of Storm. It's a multi-player abstract wargame on one level (played on a chess-board). The wargame drives the SIS for a RPG about why it sucks to be caught in the middle of intergalactic war. The RPG in turn drives the wargame via altering the tactical situation (coups, uprisings, etc.) This one just needs to be worked on.

    \n

    Of course, both are backburnered when compared to Black Cadillacs. However, I've found that poking at each of them is a good way to take that every-so-often necessary break from the main project.

    \n Great thread, Joe.

    I've got several projects in the queue that I'm moving between to keep my momentum going. But with a second child due to arrive in April, I don't think anything will actually see release this year, although I may be able to get an ashcan together for something by years' end.

    The list:
    In Frankenstein's Wake - The RPG/board game hybrid wherein you follow in the footsteps of your mentor Dr. Victor Frankenstein. A couple playtests have shown me there's a lot of work to be done and some tough choices to make in how much of an RPG I want this to be.

    The Big House - A game of prison drama in the vein of Oz and The Shawshank Redemption. I've got an early first draft and a pile of notes on this one that need to be reformed into something I can playtest.

    Posted By: northerainPosted By: Levi KornelsenThe Pulse,a post-apocalypse game about day-to-day survival, need, and humanity. It's been half-ready for a while, has a massive playtest backlog, and I have a long list of ways to improve on it... but I just don't have a real head of steam to do it with.
    \nAnything to do with the movie ''Pulse'' or S.King's ''Cell''?

    *Pulls out hair*

    Not by design, since the playtest version came out more than a year before either the book or the movie, but (at least in the original notes) a little bit of yes to both?

    Our sun begins to emit a regular and complex electromagnetic pulse every few seconds - not as a one-time event; the pulse continues through the duration of the apocalypse and what comes after. This pulse frys everything from spark-plug level electrics to computers, and burns the brains of every single human being badly, driving them "feral". People come out of the feral state through socialisation with one another (at first, they're like mindless angry 28-days later zombies, then, usually a few weeks later, like pack animals, then eventually savages, and then slowly begin to recall language and their lives before).

    Which is a little bit like the Stephen King thing.

    The pulse itself causes some really fucked-up coruscations - Aurora Borealis (northern lights) type lighting is common in the sky everywhere on earth at night, and people often see briefly flaring lights of similar colors along long metallic objects - especially in the cities - something vaguely like St.Elmo's fire. There are people who swear that at such times, they have visions, or can speak to the dead, or any number of other things; many of those that claim this and seek out such experiences end up dead, in the cities, of electrical burns. Most communities encircle the old cities, looting from them carefully - there's plenty of plague-related and ghostly-crap-related superstition there.

    Which is a little bit like the movie thing.

    This annoys me.
