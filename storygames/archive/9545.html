\n This thread is inspired by comments Matthijs made and is a companion to some thinky-thinky play contest thread Joe MacDalno started.

Here's the deal - we generate a lot of smoke but not a lot of sizzle. There's a sort of good-natured contest/challenge culture that's grown over the years, and its output is a lot of cool little games. These games are often crazy departures, bold experiments, wonderful ideas. They are also often abandonware, hollow proofs of concept, or perhaps exercises in self aggrandizement and cleverness. Either way they don't get played a whole lot. Occasionally one gets followed up on and turned into something more complete. This used to be more common, seems like. The John-Harper-style mashup may be the new thing that shakes this all up.

I think know there's social capital that accrues from making these little games, which is a motivator for making them in the first place. Perhaps that's not the healthiest motivation in the world, but it is a very natural one. There are, no doubt, many others. And I know there are little pockets of geniuses who do their own thing and don't share. Be happy, geniuses, do what you need to do. The only guy I know who seeks out weird small games and consistently makes time to try them out and talk about it is Chris Bennett, who is a stand-up dude.

From my point of view there's nothing tragically wrong with any of this, but I wonder if there are good ways to get more small games played, discussed, and grown into better things instead of being relegated to dusty footnotes. It's an honest question, and maybe the answer is that the cream still rises to the top, but I don't think so*. What do you think?

*If this were true we'd all be playing Doug Ruff's The Dinner Party.

    \n I honestly think there's a hard limit to the amount of game material that the indie games scene can consume. Like, the games we make are already fighting pretty hard for limited places at each others' tables. So I guess I think it's fine that a fair chunk of our creative output doesn't always see a lot of play and the play that does happen tends to revolve around a small constellation of games that people keep coming back to. I also think that many small projects, in various stakes of being finished, inform other projects that may eventually receive wider appreciation. How many piece of music do most composers put out in their lifetimes? How many works are regularly performed? But sometimes you have to put out a bunch of smaller pieces before you can write something big or important. I feel like that's a cool space to live in, with an overabundance of games of varying quality and done-ness.

    Also, there's only so much control you can have over whether other people play your games, even if they are really good. If you don't run them at conventions and meetups, nobody may ever play them, even if they've bought copies from you. And, honestly, if creators don't like their (micro)games enough to run them for other folks, maybe there's a reason that they were cast aside, unfinished.

    Honestly, I really, really like the culture of micro-design, where everybody is capable of putting together something that's worth looking at, passing around, and in some cases playing for a session or two OR, in rare cases, playing a bunch of. That's empowerment. But I don't believe that everything worth doing is worth being preserved forever (Dev and I have differing discussions about data loss sometimes). Roleplaying itself is ephemeral and I think it makes sense that a lot of game design work is as well. It's not as if there are a finite number of ideas and that it's a grand tragedy that some are lost forever or unfinished. There will always be too many things to do and not enough time to do them, whether that's design or play (which are really the same thing, yeah?).

    I think the main tragedy of current indie design culture is that sometimes people do more designing than playing -- I know this has been the case for me at various points in my life, including the past month or so (thought that's about to change) -- which leads to some degree of detachment between the two sides of the coin of roleplaying. You sometimes forget what you are actually designing for and game design becomes a thing in and of itself, which I don't think is necessarily a great environment for design. It's like how people buy books as if they are buying time to read them. I think sometimes I design games as if I'm creating play experiences that I want to have had, as a substitute for play, instead of play experiences that I plan on having in the near future. But maybe that's just my own issues, I don't know.

    \n Yeah, this issue is actually where my desires and Ron's desires for the Forge parted ways.
    \n
    \nWhen the Forge was young and new there were few games available outside of commercial trad titles. Thus encouraging new designers to step up and design games was a fantastic thing. The more new designers we had, the more new games where being made, the more awesomeness was available, the more alternatives to commercial trad titles there were.
    \n
    \nThen IMO we hit a critical mass of designers and games. There are now already enough awesome games out there to fuel actual play for years to come. The natural periodic release of new titles by already established designers comes pretty close to fulfilling the New & Shiny fetish gamers of all stripes seem to have. So I, for one, am much less interested in new designers or large volumes of new games. I'm much more interested in seeing new titles from established designers.
    \n
    \nThis, I admit, is pure selfishness on my part. While there is a certain explosive potential in a Freshman design (the design of passion they've been waiting their whole life to release...the crazy idea no one ever told them was impossible), there's also alot to be said for the maturation and development of the craft that comes from a designer's ongoing efforts to refine their talent. So for me, at this point...I'm much more interested in seeing the next design by an author whose name is already on my shelf, designed from the ground up to be a design they really wanted to do and now have the skills to pull off...then I am interested in taking a chance on a new designer or a game force fed through contest restrictions.

    \n One thing that's not been touched here is that I for one don't really think that micro-game design and design contests are there for social capital, or that these games should really be played. At least for me this stuff is purely a part of the artistic process on the way to doing something more concrete - concrete play, concrete product. The small game is useful because it's quick to write and read, and you can get good ideas out of them. Sometimes the game is good enough to justify developing into a real product, too, but that's up to the designer.

    This is to say, I value the micro-design culture just the way it is. It serves its purpose.

    \nAh, I didn't mean that it couldn't. I was discoursing in opposition to the notion that there's something wrong with having lots of game contests and making games that do not get played. I have nothing against productizing small games - Zombie Cinema was originally specifically created as a demonstration of what sort of games come out of this micro game movement, for example. But even while games can be developed for production, I see nothing wrong with a public design process wherein we share idle design thoughts with others in preparation for serious work.

    What I meant to say, in a nutshell: when I write something like Valravnar for \xc1sagrimmr or Miss\xe4 miehet ratsastaa for a competition, it's pure design for the sake of ideas - productization does not enter into it, I write the game to participate in the contest, and that is all it is. The participation itself is the self-contained benefit of participating. Thus concluding that I do it for social credibility or because I was supposed to create a finished product masterpiece seems shallow to me; those are not why I write micro-games. They have a real and concrete place in my game design hobby, and I can discuss that directly - that place is as a development stage for ideas that are on the way to becoming something for me or for other designers; for any given game it is completely coincidental whether it becomes a product or not.

    Rereading Jason's original thesis, I have to say that I do somewhat disagree in that I do believe that the cream rises to the top. Or rather, I believe that idle micro games are such different beasts from finished product that tying the two together and making it a mission to make one into the other seems wrong-headed to me. This development from a quick micro game into a real product will only happen if a designer (not necessarily the original one) takes the game and does the real work of productizing the ideas. When we see excellent ideas seemingly abandoned and forgotten in these competitions (and each one produces these hidden gems, doubt it not), that's just an outsider viewpoint: do you not really think that the designer weighted seriously his own motivations and chose to develop or not develop the product? How do you know he didn't take the good bits of the game and design something else from the them? How do you know he isn't reserving the material for a product at a later date, when he has the time to work on it? I see no proof of any tragic waste.

    I can see how the proliferation of public design (which micro games are - they are the first stages of design made public) might worry a person if he believed that these games are being made simply because they provide one with social credit. That would mean that instead of focusing on the challenging and tiring work of finishing product people were just creating micro-games as a substitute activity. I don't personally see how creating an untested micro game is a source of respect, though; the difference between a smart micro game and a real product is such a large one that I suggest making the latter to anybody who wants to make their name or whatever. Heck, even just writing playtest reports will probably bring more "cred" than constantly writing rough, unfinished games. Could be that others have different valuations, of course - I'm hardly the go-to guy for what's hot and what's not.

    There is one, unsaid think I agree about with Jason, though: looking at the rpg scene in general, we probably should invest more in productization and marketing and proportionally less in game development. That might have something to do with micro games if people are designing them instead of doing useful work with the games they already have. Not I nor Jason can order people to stop designing and start productizing, though, so...

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarThese games are often crazy departures, bold experiments, wonderful ideas.
    \nI think this is at the centre of it. They're crazy ideas, clever, good for a laugh or some little bit of insight, but don't usually hold up as experiences worth investment of more time or money. The time limit of many contests (24 hours, 10 days) really contributes to this problem, as do the design constraints/challenges. Want an example? Take my game from Game Chef 2008, Terribly Beautiful. I loved the challenge the concept chose, but can you really see a market for a game about playing former prostitutes facing mortality due to incurable disease? Seriously? Because I can't. It was designed to fit within the contest constraints with no thought to a larger audience.

    There are a couple of other factors that work in:

    1. Burnout: both in having to take care of things in life that were neglected while devoting time to the contest, and in emotional drain, or sometimes managing to use designing the game to work through the emotional issues that inspired its creation.

    2. Remaining elements: layout, artwork, and other things people might not be good at (or don't have enough money to get someone else to do well). Me, I like a beautiful game. My budget to pay artists is....let me check the coins in my pocket. And no, I won't settle for artwork that I feel looks just "okay". As someone who works in a bookstore, I know that people buy books by their appearance, so why publish something that I wouldn't be able to proudly show off and sell there?

    I don't consider a "proof-of-concept" game to be a failure of a design contest to produce a publishable game. I consider "proof-of-concept" entries to be the ultimate achievement of a design contest, a sign that the contest pushed entrants to try something different, even if it didn't work quite right. The next step is for people (not just the designer of that game, but anyone who looks at an entry and learns from it) to take the ideas there and use them as inspiration to create something much greater. Note I said "create something," not "create something publishable."

Sturgeon's law applies doubly to incomplete work that's running around in your own head. (That makes it 99%, not 180%, in case you were despairing at the impossibility).

    \n Game design is an art. To develop your skill at an art, you must practice. Practice, in the case of game design, involves actually designing games (not just discussing theory, although that is often interesting and enlightening), and the more games you design, the more your skill as a designer will develop. There are, of course, notable exceptions as mentioned above of first-time prodigies and the like, but the vast majority of us still just have to plug away and do the work and put in our ten thousand hours.

    The most important thing to keep in mind when practicing an art is: you will produce mostly garbage. This is a good thing. You don't have to publish the garbage, or play|test it, or even show it to people, but you will have to produce it to make it to the good stuff. Failure in this respect is not only inevitable, but essential. In my mind, this is where these design contests aid greatly. They provide contexts that allow people to bust out some fast, bold sketches, and although the desire is to do the best work possible (and often to gain the respect and admiration of one's peers), the result is often that the best work possible at the time is not very good. But that's okay! That is, in fact, wonderful, because it is another step towards the things that are very good indeed, and without taking that step, you'll just sit there and dream about the things that are very good indeed without ever getting closer to producing them.

    That said, game design (like some other interactive or performative arts) are peculiar in that they require an active audience to be experienced fully, and thus is it difficult to get the full benefit of the practice without involving other people. (For example, you can rehearse your theater or practice improvisation all day, but when you face an audience with your fellow performers, that's when the real experience comes.) So, in the process of learning our craft, we will inevitably present things that are not very good to an indulgent audience who will hopefully provide honest and useful feedback, so we can go back and create more things that are not very good again and again, until we eventually strike gold. I believe that these small design contests provide an invaluable resource for this aspect of learning the art of game design, as well. I enjoy "lonely fun" as much as the next person (and I think that Brand's observation to that above is right on), but I think that there's only so far that will take you, design-wise.

    As to Jason's original question of how to get these games played more often, I have no idea. Maybe many of these games just don't need to be played more than a couple times before we say, okay, I learned something from that, let's move on. I think the ways to get them played more are either: as a designer, push push push to get your game played, work for it until you can't push any more; or work work work, until you produce something that is so irresistible that people cannot help but play it, and thus provide you with the feedback that you desperately need. And as individuals in a community, if we are interested in promoting the creation and play of these small games, it is our responsibility to take on both of those roles ourselves: push our playmates to play the quirky little games that we love, and continue to create or start more micro-contests or do whatever we can do to let more of the games inside our heads see the light of day. And really, I think we're doing a fair job of both of those already.

    In short, yay us, and yay contests.

    \n It's interesting, I find myself agreeing simultaneously with completely varied ideas on the subject of game design contests here.

    In my own part (I run the "game chef" site these days, but by no means have a monopoly on game design contests or anything), I ran that event for the same reason I developed the Indie RPG Awards, picked up 24 Hour RPGs from Philip Reed's great idea, and even the development of Story Games:

    I was supposed to be working on something else, but I got bored with that so focused on this instead.

    From a contest designer viewpoint, I loved doing it each year, even despite the rules-needling and some bad feedback: It's basically a fireworks of ideas. Every year I ran it save one I participated in it anonymously as well (in one case failing, twice kinda-succeeding). And it basically helped me do the following:
    * Write a lot, fast.
    * Be creative within constraints, and push myself to turn brain-bubbles into cohesive ideas in quick time.
    * Then throw it all together in a critical document in an organized fashion.

    Sure, it's not "real", and none of the games were really playable, but in the end that experience helped me on my other projects where I've had to do pretty much the same thing but in a longer time period.

    It's not real game design. It's training wheels for real game design.

    I'm VERY wary of stuff that gets published from these contests, even (though some people made that leap, and for the most part it's been great). But nothing's really come along in terms of reasons or feedback to make me think, "Y'know, this is a useless exercise that causes lots of bloat and no real results". Not everything need be played.
    It reminds me of the Real Iron Chef, actually: The chef takes some bizarre ingredient (natto, water, lettuce, mushrooms, etc, depending on the week) and makes 3-4 signature dishes with them. Often, they are delicious. Sometimes, they are mediocre.

    But not in any single case are these dishes meant to be "mass produced", "recreated", "perfected". The Iron Chefs go back to their restaurants and decidedly do not add the creations from Kitchen Stadium onto their menus. It was a one-time thing, basically an explosion of craft, experience and creativity. The only real difference between us and them is that it's food instead of games, and they're pros who do it for a living while we're hobbyists. So at the end of the day, I'm content with the fact that most of us don't return back to our home kitchens and turn that Kitchen Stadium food-explosion into a premier menu item; it was a one-time gig, a piece of craft produced for one event, and perhaps training to make something better, more permanent, on our own.

    \n Hi Doug!

    You, and others, have brought up the point that the extreme focus of most (all?) contests constrain creativity in unnatural ways. I totally agree with this. I think you either need to just embrace that and know your little darling isn't going anywhere, or design knowing that the day after the contest you'll tear out some element shoe-horned in just to meet requirements, or allow the contest to inspire you but ignore the rules, don't enter, and go your own way. Not enough people choose option three, I suspect.

    \n The other thing people forget is that contests are, in my opinion, for new people. For cynical old hands who've seen it all before, sure, they're repetitive. For a newcomer who's prompted to design a game for the first time, with social support, they're golden.
