\n So I know we basically play games for the express point of playing characters that have abilities, etc that we don't have in real life but what do people think of games that require you, yourself, to have a skill or be good at doing something in order for your character to succeed. Whether the two are related or not - I'm not talking about if you play a fighter you should be able to swing a longsword around though.

For example: making a thief solve a jigsaw puzzle or rubix cube in order to pick a lock, have a wizard say a tongue twister in order to cast a spell beyond his level, etc.

There are some games that do have that other element I assume, though I can only really think of Dread at the moment. Mostly because I'm making an RPG designed for language learning and is, at its core, designed to run on player skill for all resolution mechanics, I was wondering how people would respond to such a thing. Obviously playing jenga doesn't require a particularly high level of skill, but the idea of tying useful player skills (learned out of game) with resolution mechanics or benefits in-game is really interesting to me.

    \n When it comes to players needing to do things that relate abstractly to what their characters are doing, I think it's kind to provide some kind of "hint" mechanics. For instance, I recently played a D&D game where the PCs had to solve a puzzle to unlock a door before they were overwhelmed by zombies. The DM gave us a literal puzzle, bought from a toys & games store. Fortunately, the puzzle was designed to have several difficulty levels, so every time we succeeded on a relevant skill check (Arcana, Thievery, Dungeoneering), the DM took away a piece of the puzzle and let us solve it for a lower difficulty level. There are specifics I'd do differently in retrospect if I were running such a thing itself (like telling players the rules for the puzzle for their first successful skill check), but overall I thought it was an extremely clever approach.

    Now, when it comes to players needing to do things that relate directly and literally to what their characters are doing, I think that's only really fair if you're talking about (a) skills you can reasonably exercise at the table, and (b) skills players wouldn't mind learning themselves. This is why I don't feel guilty requiring players to verbally role-play the details of social skills, like bluffing and trying to persuade NPCs. Some players aren't as good at this as their PCs are, so you sometimes get the group's master diplomat stammering something awkwardly instead of using that silver tongue of his. There's a moment of narrative dissonance, sure, but it can be worth it in the long run \u2013\xa0both for the players themselves and the rest of the group \u2013\xa0to suffer through this early on. Public speaking, improvisational thinking, and creative storytelling are all excellent skills to have in general, and giving players a chance to practice these skills at the table makes our games better.

    I think the OP's game for building language skills is a good example: Anybody can safely practice a new language at the table (unlike, say, learning to swing a broadsword), and that's actually a skill plenty of players would probably find desirable to practice. I think it might also be kind to have a "hint" mechanic here, too, like allowing players to spend an extra "character point" or "mana point" (or whatever) to look up something in their dictionary every now and then.

    \n [FYI, this distinction is called "hard skill versus soft skill" or "DO versus STAT" in LARP rules discussions.]

    For LARP, I firmly believe that anything that can be actually done by the players should be done by them, not "rolled" (in quotes because I mean any stat-based resolution system). In my system the ONLY reason "rolls" exist is for GMs, not players--so that GMs can have a particular style of challenge without having to physically build one or without putting players at risk.

    So, actually, my examples would be the OPPOSITE of some of the above:
    * Have an "Int check" when the GMs don't have the time or money to make an actual puzzle.
    * Have a "Dex check" to cross a tightrope which is actually just laying on the ground, because of falling concerns.
    * Have a "Con check" to simulate disgusting or poisonous odors (the former is not desired by many hosts, the latter is obviously dangerous).

    And, from time to time:
    * Have a "Courage check" to go into a 'scary place' (when, let's be honest, it's VERY hard to make a set actually scary, due to budget and disbelief suspension limitations).

    Does this tend to mean that you can't be a "smart guy" if you aren't actually smart? Yep. You can't be a "good fighter" if you can't handle a weapon worth a damn? Nope.

    TL; DR: Everything is done with actual player skill, when not dangerous, impossible, or too expensive to simulate.

    \n Some things are essentially based off player skill in most tabletops, too. Persuasion, puzzle-solving, tactics - they're rarely rolled for (and even in games where these things can be rolled, the GM will usually let the player succeed if they make a persuasive argument, solve the puzzle or come up with a cunning plan OOC). So in a way it seems odd that exceptions are made for things like swinging a sword. Why? Basically, because your average geek can't swing a sword, I guess.

    But take a game like Apocalypse World, now the above things are mostly mechanised. Persuasion requires a roll, and cannot be done at all without one.

    But anyway, to answer the question I'm not a big fan of making players complete challenges to pick locks or whatever, outside of LRP. It's not why I roleplay, it gives an unfair advantage to smarter people and unless it is exactly mirroring what the character is doing it breaks, rather than enhances suspension if disbelief.

    LRP is different; you need to stretch a small GM team over numerous players scattered through a large site. Using mechanics for this is impractical, and breaks an otherwise immersive game down into a mini-tabletop. But I'd still prefer the task to be as close a copy to the one the character is completing as possible, for reasons of belief suspension.

    \n For larps, it depends. If you're telling me, "This is a straight boffer larp, and your skill is your character's skill", I'm fine with that. It's not my thing, but that's okay.

    However, there was one larp which had several issues. One of these had to do with the gun battle system. All of the PCs had a stat for that, ranging from 1-5. 5 was best, and these stats were visible on the badges on the grounds that this was a Wild West-related genre, and people could tell from how one moved how good one was with a gun. So far, so good.

    My character's skill was 4, really good, but not the best. But, the rule for the gun battles was: "Shoot with the nerf gun. If you hit with the nerf gun, you hit. If you miss with the nerf gun, you miss."

    So, my personal skill, not my character's? That's a 1. There was a gun battle my character was supposed to be in, and (for reasons that made no sense at the time, but did make some sense afterwards), the player running it decided to have a qualifying round. There was a large block on the ground. There was a line some distance away. Would-be competitors had to stand behind the line and hit the large block. They had three chances, and it wasn't a hard shot.

    And I missed each time.

    Now, this did not ruin my game. It shaped my game in some interesting ways, and I had a fun time despite the bumps. But, please, do not say, "Okay, here is your gun stat, and it means you're really really good" and then tell me "You use your own skill in gun battles." These are contradictory.

    If you want an element of chance, have players do good old Rock-Paper-Scissors, and have the winner add one or two to the gun score. But once you tell me it's my skill, then the stat is meaningless and shouldn't be in the game.

    \n Exactly, David. I call those kinds of systems me+. Your personal ability is at the core, plus you get some bonuses from the system to enhance it.

    In addition to damage, those bonuses can be special moves. In particular, the types of things that are generally removed for safety reasons, like disarming techniques or knocking someone backward. So you could have a Disarm attack, maybe once per fight, that would force your opponent to drop their weapon before continuing.

    In a wild west game like that, wearing a 4 might mean that you have 4 special moves per fight (which is usually pretty badass). And by extension, you could assume that someone with a 0 or 1 probably had other things that were special about them, like special abilities for "cheating" at cards or influence with the mining consortium. (Which are examples of ways that me+ design can be used outside of combat. Though, to be honest, I haven't seen "cheat at cards" rules yet that I really liked.)
