    \n You totally shouldn't play a one-shot like the "first session" advice. Two completely different things. The reason you start slow in a long-form (or medium-form) game is because it sets you up to make hard moves later. You create investment in stuff that then matters later on. In a one-shot, you don't have time for that. You better throw down hard instead.

    \n I didn't do any prep for the GameStorm games a'tall.

    I've run a ton of AW, though, so I had lots of experience and material to draw on. The Lighthouse, Oracle, the burn flats, the nomad tribes... all stuff from our ongoing games at home. So it was easy to draw up a quick map and drop the PCs down in a tough spot. The character types chosen and the results of the pre-session moves also gave me material to work with for the starting situation.

    Having said that, I wouldn't recommend that method for a first-time AW GM. If the one shot is gonna be your first time at bat, I'd grab Vincent's Hatchet City module and run that. Spend some time with it before the game and figure out what stuff interests you in the module and what you can cut and ignore. There's no need to cram all of it into one session.

    Oh! Also, you can totally steal the setup from Vincent's Sunday GS game. Zach, Joel, Harry, and Chris made a really cool crew that is perfect for a one-shot.

    - The Operator, Proust
    - The Brainer, Cybelle (could be replaced with a fighter type, battlebabe or gunlugger)
    - The Savvyhead, Jessica
    - The Angel, Gabe

    The PCs are the operator and his crew. They have a converted school bus pulling a trailer (the savvyhead's workspace). On top of the school bus, two big water tanks. The crew travels from holding to holding, ostensibly selling water, but what they really do is distract the holding while stealing anything that isn't nailed down (Operator gigs: Honest Work and Scavenging).

    The operator will roll his gigs to start, which should give you a solid idea of what situation they're in when the session opens. You'll likely have some angry hardholder chasing them across the ash desert, or the crew will be heading for their next target on the horizon (or a mix of both). For prep, some ideas about a couple of holdings and their people should suffice. (Hint: make at least one of the hardholders a genuinely good person).

    AW one-shots are uncharted territory. I'm happy to answer questions and work through this even more. It's an interesting topic.

    \n The game's a few years old now, so I thought I'd kick this thread back to life. A couple of suggestions from playing/running AW at various cons:

    - MC love letters are hot. Using them to immediately establish a PC-NPC-PC triangle is my favorite thing. It's also fun to give peeps other than the Operator an opening move. It makes them feel involved from the get-go and subtly shapes the opening narrative.
    - The Quarantine is a surprisingly effective one-shot playbook. New AW players always seem to want to know what the hell happened way back when anyway, which automagically makes The Quarantine compelling. Plus, that storage facility is just pregnant with awesome possibility.

    \n This is how I ran a AW one-shot at PAX last year that was so awesome that the same players came back later in the day to play again (including some things I would have done differently, and some things inspired by this thread and others):

    - play an Operator and their crew
    - don't offer playbooks you (as MC) aren't that excited about
    - walk them through making characters
    - try to streamline Hx assignments as much as possible; maybe by turning it into a love-letter kind of deal ("The Battlebabe backed you up in a bad deal, even though you were in the wrong; tell us what it was")
    - after they've made chars, draw a map of the general area on the fly, asking the players lots of questions
    - begin by having the Operator roll Moonlighting to see how the last gig went for them
    - if they're still in the middle of it, start there; if they succeeded, show how that changes things (and creates new complications)
    - watch everything snowball, and ask for moves as needed based on the fiction
    - create PC-NPC-PC triangles by making 1-3 dangerous/sexy/horrific badasses for them to love and hate; play them like stolen Ferraris

    \n I heard a secondhand version of this old thread during Games on Demand last GenCon, and it worked great!


    Ask: "So, who's in charge?" Somebody will chime in, or get volunteered once the players realize you mean it.
    Ask: "So, why are you in charge? Is it because you run the cult? ...toss out Hocus... Is it because you've got the biggest, baddest gang in town? ...Chopper... Because you run the joint at the center of it all? ...Maestro'D... Or just cause you're the leader?" ...Hardholder...
    A picks one of those four playbooks, take the rest out of play.

    Ask: "Who wishes they were running the place instead?" Toss out Battlebabe, Brainer, Skinner, briefly explain them.
    B picks one of those three playbooks, take the rest out of play.

    (optional)Point at the person who's "in charge". Ask: "So, who's got A's back?" Toss out Gunlugger, Savvyhead, Angel.
    C picks one of those three playbooks, take the rest out of play. I never ended up using this one.

    Spread the playbooks that haven't been shown yet for the remaining players, and let them take their pick.
    AW play proceeds as usual from there: ask leading questions, especially about the hardhold/cult/gang, why B thinks they should be in charge instead, inject something that's a threat to A's power. Don't bother with formal fronts: just point them at each other, ratchet up the intensity of what's going on, and play to see what happens.
