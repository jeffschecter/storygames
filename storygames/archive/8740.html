\n The game I have designed uses a sortition based system for resolving challenges. As such it requires the use of a number of coloured beads and a small bag from which those beads may be drawn. My fear is that people won't play the game if I don't include these items. I'm equally worried that they won't buy it if the price of including them brings the cost too high. Has anyone else here sold a game as a boxed set or contemplated doing it?

    \n The box itself isn't the problem, from what I remember when I was thinking about publishing Draug as a boxed set. Having colored print on it is what's expensive. If you want a white box with a sticker on it, that's cool; but other solutions are usually expensive (like having someone custom print your boxes) or impractical (like printing sheets and gluing them on the box yourself).

    Marcus, unless you're going for a mass market publication, I wouldn't bother with a box. Most people who are into indie games will definitely have a bag and some beads around, and won't be bothered by having to get their own stuff together. (Of course, you don't have to go all "Thy Vernal Chieftains" in your requirements...)

    \n For my upcoming boardgame/rpg hybrid, Transantiago, I'm planning on packing the board and components in a cardboard mailing envelope intended for mailing LP records, which is like 12"x12" square. And then I'll probably throw a sticker on the cover or something to make it look sharp. Those mailing envelopes that the postal service uses for Priority and Express mail (which are basically the same as what I'll be using) are pretty flexible and could potentially hold all sorts of components. You might check those out for a cheap option. Also, clear plastic tubes are underrated. Roll the booklet up and seal the tokens in the tube.

    \n Shelf wear is an issue for retailers and distributors for all products, but things in boxes add another dimension to this - they can be crushed and otherwise mangled in ways that books alone cannot. Something else to consider.
