\n The issue is the word "product". When I put My Life with Master into book form and brought it to Gen Con in 2003 it wasn't as a product. It was more like a scientist publishing his findings in a scientific journal. And Sorcerer, and Polaris, and The Mountain Witch, and WYRD, and Donjon, and InSpectres and Dogs In the Vineyard, and Bacchanal, and Capes, and PtA, and I could go on, are the same. We were all playing like crazy, exploring different social architectures for play, testing the limits of roleplaying. Putting your successes together as a game text was feeding them back into the community. Just like scientists put their findings into journal articles.

If you go into the Forge/IPR booth and you see games like Carry, and In A Wicked Age, and Spirit of the Century and you're underwhelmed, it's because you're inhabiting the role of consumer and seeing the games as products. And the product/consumer paradigm expects nothing of the consumer but the cash transaction. But you are not a consumer, you are a colleague and a scientist. A journal article engages a scientist as a participant in the scientific community. The journal article gives you something to do, something very personal to your abilities, and the work is enlarged by your efforts. Spirit of the Century is enlarged by Judd's The Revenge of Zombie Kong and the Lightning Zombies! scenario. Agon was enlarged by post Gen Con play. Games are enlarged by talking about them, and comparing notes.

To be honest, the "putting your successes into a game text and feeding them back to the community" dynamic has been eroding a bit, and so we're seeing games with less basis on the designer's exploration of the architecture of creative collaboration to the point of success. But the solution isn't superficial insider roastings couched as "open and honest" criticism; internet games build barriers that keep people out of the community. The scientific community is diminished by status games. The solution is to let the games put you to work within the community, knowing that your efforts will enlarge them.

Paul

    \n See, I agree with you in theory, but the practice, to me, seems a bit intimidating.
    \n
    \nIf I'm expected to go on somewhere and give an actual play, fine, I can do that. What I can't do so well is couch it in the jargon that appears to be part and parcel of the "fellow colleague" model. There has been a specific language that has popped up in regard to defining and talking about roleplaying games, and it looks like Etruscan to me. I've had Sorcerer in one form or another since the mid-90's, and only now do I even feel close to capable of explaining it to other people. And quite frankly, I feel that if I come on somewhere and try to explain what I'm not grasping about a game, and where it falls apart for me, I'm going to end up feeling like a buffoon.
    \n
    \nFor example, I get what you're doing with Acts of Evil, and I think it'll be awesome if you can nail down how to get players to make their own Protagonists consistently. As to how to figure out how to do that...I wouldn't know where to begin. And I feel like, if I can't help you do that, all I'll be able to contribute to the game is polishing and window-dressing, which may end up more annoying than helpful. If I can't wrap my head around the theory, and what types of mechanics are necessary to achieve those effects, how do I know what I say isn't more of a hindrance than a help?
    \n
    \nPaul, Tony, I appreciate the work you guys are doing for the hobby. But your games require, from my POV, a pretty big buy-in in regards to unlearning a lot of gaming habits that have been established in the industry. Sorcerer required, from me, a bigger buy-in in that regard than I initially expected, and it threw me for a loop for the longest time. I admit that Capes totally boggled me when I tried to read it and figure out how it was supposed to play, I had a similar reaction to Burning Wheel when I saw it in a rough draft form, and while the Equations are clear and concise for Acts of Evil, there are things that Paul is looking for and I'm looking for that I don't know how to articulate, let alone offer suggestions on how to fix.
    \n
    \nAnd quite frankly, roleplaying games are awesome as a hobby. I personally consider them superior in potential to just about every other form of entertainment out there because they directly engage you, the participant, in the experience. But there are plenty of people out there who want to be just consumers, who want to use the game as an excuse to get together with friends and entertain themselves. They won't have the time or energy or, quite frankly, enthusiasm to contribute feedback comparable to a fellow scientist giving a peer review. Plenty will try, and they should be encouraged, but I'm worried that under the "fellow scientist" model, there will be a huge buy-in of having to learn the established jargon necessary to give the targeted feedback you, the creator, crave. And I do worry about people having a legitimate criticism or suggestion, but because they don't know the right way to express it, they get ignored or shut down. I'm dubious about the possibility of raising the bar for entry into this hobby any further than it is, because I'd like to see more people gaming, and I'd like to expose more people to RPGs, and I'm worried that increased buy-in runs the risk of keeping people out of the hobby, people who'd be more likely to stay in and get hooked if they weren't worried about having an obligation to give feedback in a journal-style format.
    \n
    \nHopefully, what I just wrote was comprehensible.

    \n But if it helps, I always looked at the games on the Forge, especially in the early days, as lab experiments designed to highlight situations that many felt weren't being given proper exploration in more traditional RPGs. That's a big chunk of why the RPGPundit's criticisms of the Forge never took with me: he assumed (as you stated) that the RPG was a product, when what it really was was an experiment in design specifically made to challenge the current orthodoxies in game design. Note that it may be a great irony, but true, that a game that is given away for free will not get as much play as a game that's charged for in many cases. So the setting of price points here to create a perception of value is, in part, part of getting the game to more people.
    \n
    \nThe other theory would be that we're doing it to get rich. Which is to laugh. I'd posit that even the "mainstream professionals" who publish RPGs do so more out of love than a desire to get rich. I do it because I am compelled to do so.
    \n
    \nRon just showed me a way that I can do it so that I won't go broke, and people will actually play my game. Instead of it just remaining an idea in my head.
    \n
    \nThis makes me happy, too, don't get me wrong. Why do the science, if it's not to improve something, namely RPG play? Its not sterile research, it has a goal, too.
    \n
    \n
    \nAnd, that said, just because our ethos is not to create the same old schlock in order to sell to the greatest number of people, that does not mean that our games are actually any less approachable than others, neccessarily. Oh this may be the case in some cases, sure. But I'd posit that any difference in level of approachability is covered under Caveat Emptor.
    \n
    \nWill that mean we sell less games? Well we're not really concerned with selling a ton of games. Just enough to get the research done, and the game pay for itself and maybe research field trips to GenCon. Enough so that our spouses won't complain about the time we put into doing it. There are costs involved, you see.
    \n
    \nWe've simply made the research self-perpetuating. And if/when people have a lot of fun playing our games, then we've gone past the research and achieved the end goal, too. That of making play more fun for some people. And that way we slowly change the hobby, hopefully for the better.
    \n

And, who knows? Maybe some day soon, some university will open up a school for RPG design or such? And then we'll be off and running, student fees and research grants paying for us to become full-time design professors. At which point then, sure, the cost of the games will only be that of the distribution of the periodicals in which they come.

    \nBut it's a step.

    \n
    \nAnd frankly I'm OK with that. Using the free market to determine what's good or not is just fine with me.
    \n
    \nBut that doesn't deny the goals of the people making these games. Which is largely to advance the hobby, or just be creative in making their design, and getting to see if they've made something worthwhile to anyone. That's where it's precisely like science.
    \n
    \nAnd where the entire "industry" tends to lean that way, too. Less so for some outfits, but Steve Jackson is the original mad scientist of RPGs. Striking out on his own from Metagaming in order to make the games that he felt people would like, because they were the games he liked.
    \n
    \nWe just lean a bit more explicitly in that direction than the "industry."
    \n
    \n
    \nNote that this is far, far from a new idea with regards to how we see this community. Is it any surprise that this is what emerged on a web site moderated by a professor? People would come to the Forge and deride it for it's academic atmosphere, to which we'd respond... "that's a negative how?" Yes, it's exactly what we've always sought, and what we've gotten.
    \n
    \nLet me be really clear about this. I am no academic, in that I dropped out of college. Three times. But that's probably largely because I was studying things that didn't really turn me on. I was too busy playing RPGs to even go take my tests. If there had been a school for RPG study, I'd have my Phd now.
    \n
    \nMoreso, I hope to have my Phd in gaming someday. Again, as someone not immersed appropriately in academia, this is probably a pipe dream at this point. Or maybe not. But the point is that I think it would be great if, in fact, some university would start a program for RPG studies. Maybe not what the world needs most, but it's not a bad idea.
    \n
    \nBecause that's probably the only way that we'll ever get pure actual peer review on these "papers" that people release as games. And I think that would be a good thing.
    \n
    \nThat's not to say that all RPG production should go this way, by the way. Just like, say, computer science, there should be researchers, and there should be people working in the market to meet market demand. It's the combination of these two things that best leads to advancements in a field. And it's what we lacked entirely in RPGs before, say, RFGA, and which has started pretty seriously with The Forge, and which would have to end up at a university somewhere.

I'm sure there's more going on than I'm aware of.

Rather, it is what it is, and that's a pretty positive force. Yes I'm biased, but it's my hobby too. I'm part of a community that's improving things for ourselves, if for nobody else. I'm sure others are benefited, but if we only helped ourselves, that'd still be enough reason to do what we do.
