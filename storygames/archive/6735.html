\n After reading the crazy calamity that is the thread about the text granting the GM the "right" to change the rules let me share with you the story of my friend Travis. My friend Travis LOVED 3rd Edition D&D. I mean, this game excited him to no end. It was perfect in his mind. He offered to run the original Freeport Trilogy of adventures for me and few other friends. We said sure. We got through one and half adventures before it became clear that something was VERY VERY wrong. We were totally lost. We had no idea what we were supposed to do, where we were supposed to go, what we were looking for, nothing.

When it was clear that the group was frustrated beyond hope Travis asked me if we could trade roles. He would take over my character and I would finish GMing as he felt that something about the modules "clashed with his style." So I said sure and read through the trilogy of adventures. I IMMEDIATELY discovered what had gone wrong.

You see The Freeport Modules use both dungeon crawl and clue-chain techniques. There are certain places in the dungeons where the module explicitly instructs the GM to MAKE SURE the players discover certain pieces of information. But my friend Travis had "corrected" the module. He had assigned Spot/Search Check DCs and Listen Check DCs to all this stuff because "those are the rules." We had failed several of these rolls. The most outlandish of these was at the end of the very first module the NPC you fight is supposed to give a death bed speech basically setting up the context for the entire next adventure. My friend Travis had assigned an Intimidate Check DC to this speech and we had failed this roll.

I went back to my friend Travis and pointed out in the text where the module instructs the GM to GUARANTEE that the players receive all this information and had received the honest and sincere reply of, "Yeah, but that's against the rules. Why have rules for Spot, Search, Listen and Intimidate if you're just going to ignore them?"

Flip it around.

Some time later my friend Patrick offers to run a Forgotten Realms game. My friend Travis is super, super excited by this offer as the new 3rd Edition Forgotten Realms book had just came out and he was dying to give the setting a whirl. At one point we receive some kind of odd piece of currency I think it was. My friend Travis starts flipping through his Forgotten Realms book very excitedly trying to find out what this thing was. He starts reading aloud from various nearby nations "economy" sections and Patrick says, "Dude, you're not going to find in the book." And Travis kind of blinks at him. Patrick says, "it's something I added because it's the only way I could justify a slave nation and non-slave nation co-habitating." Travis despondently flips to the last page of the book and says, "250 pages of setting information just wasn't enough for you?"

Sometime later we run into a scout of some kind who mentions that he's from Travis's character's home nation. (Travis's character was wanted in his back story, if I recall correctly). So Travis shouts out, "I attack him before he can say any more." Patrick says, "You leap forward and cleave in his skull." Travis again blinks at Patrick. "Uh.... Initiative roll?" Patrick, "You got the drop on an unsuspecting man." Travis picks up his d20 and rolls it. He says, "I rolled a 5. You're telling me your leather clad scout has an AC of less than 5?" Patrick replies, "What about you attacking an unsuspecting target do you not understand?" Travis picks up his d8 and rolls it. He says, "I did 3 points of damage. Your scout has less than 3 hit points? Even if he does he should only bleeding out at -1!" Patrick says, "You brought a heavy iron sword down on the head of man not ready to defend himself. What did you think was going to happen?" Travis, "An initiative roll."

True story. I am not making this up.

    \n I must confess here my ignorance about D&D3.5 (after I freed myself of the grognard of the group that wanted to play only AD&D, I didn't even glance to a D&D books in the stores). The dungeon master HAS to follow the combat procedure every time, no matter the circumstances? It wasn't so in AD&D.

    Anyway, let's parse what happened. IF the GM changed a rule (or made an ad hoc interpretation of the rules for a specific case) he did it clearly and in front of everyone. No secret, no fudging. The players had the chance to contest his ruling (and Travis did), and at that time the GM decided (using his referee role, or "the buck stop here" If you prefer) to keep his ruling. No rule zero.

    I think that here the GM did an error. He should have listened to Travis and let him roll his dice. Not because "it was more realistic" (it wasn't) or "because it's in the rules", but simply because his decision hurt Travis' enjoyment of the game, for no good reason apart from the old "The GM can't be contested" habit (but I can say that because I didn't play with Travis. From your description, maybe hurting his enjoyment of the game would have been reason enough for me...). What would have been hurt by letting Travis rolls his meaningless dice?

    What would rule zero say in this case?

    Well, Travis like to roll dice and follow rules-as-written. The GM like to thing about "what realistically would happen". Let's say that they are playing the Freeport trilogy. And Travis want to do all the rolls to discover things. So the GM fudge. He make Travis "win" meaningless rolls, than give him the information in any case (but making him think that he "earned" them). He let Travis roll for initiative when when he think it's not right, but he fudge his roll to make Travis win it.
    Travis is very happy. He think he is a very good player. He find all the clues, solve all the mysteries. Patrick know the truth and his respect for Travis as a player, if possible, is lower with every fudged roll. He is playing not a referee, but Travis' ego-stroker (I described once, during a very heated discussion, the role that GM take when they do this to a player as a different kind of service to a different anatomic part. I still think that the paragon is apt, even if I try to not repeat it anymore on safe-for-work forums...). This situation can't last forever. Someday, Travis will discover the truth. Or Patrick will lose his taste for GMing. In any case, the social consequences will be very much worse than if he didn't use secrecy and fudging.
    And it will be only at this time, after years, that Patrick will discover the real "value" of that rule the GM guide told him "as necessary to play".

      \n Regarding scene framing I agree with Jeffery *if* the game as written works that way. Different games have different scene framing procedures. Consider for example the game Spione which has a phase of play called Maneuvers. Everyone takes a turn during Maneuvers. On your turn you can either a) add a detail to an existing scene or b) start a new scene around one of the Principle characters. In Spione there are only every two Principle characters.

      What this means is that if both Principles are in a scene and a player chooses option (b) above they have effectively chosen to end the current in progress scene. Now Spione is very clear that it's meant to foster a dialogue so if anyone objects they should say so but it's ALSO very clear that such objections are ADVICE and FINAL authority lies with whoever's turn it is.

      So yes, if the player was insistent that they wanted to end the current scene and start a new scene any objecting player would just have to accept that and move on with no right to complain because they agreed to *this* game and that's how *this* game's procedures work.       HOWEVER, just because 4e has two kinds of skill systems does not automatically make it twice as good as other RPGs If you ever played an extended contest in HeroQuest, that's how the 4e skill challenge plays out to some degree.

      The 4e skill challenge is meant to be a roleplaying engaged activity combined with dice rolls, but the potential for impressive RP between the GM and player on deck and the potential for players to bounce off RP with each other is excellent.

      I did this as a skill challenge: my players knew the cultists of Orcus had just done a brutal snatch and grab at the town's lunar festival and they had a hidden temple somewhere in the town. My challenge to the heroes was "find the temple"

      Now, I had no idea what the town looked like or who lived there or where the temple was hidden. The 4e skill challenge allowed each player to decide how their character would hunt down this knowledge and we went around the table with each person roleplaying their character doing something in a part of the town they were creating on the fly and I as GM would bounce off of them to fill in descriptions and away we went. Each person answered the question "how would my character accomplish this?" and each person choose different routes to success.

      Dice rolls told us if their plan brought them closer to their knowledge and dice failures put cultists on their tail or caused time to pass closer toward midnight when everyone assumed the cultists who do their sacrifice.

      I ran this skill challenge with three different groups. Each time, the group got into the scene and gave me fun bits to work with and we bounced them around the table weaving the story of the skill challenge. It was incredibly fun and FAR better structured than most indie games where the GM is relegated to a secondary role. The 4e skill challenge allows the GM to retain story control, but allows the players to get wildly creative and deeply into their character.

      Could you do this with any RPG? Fuck yeah, but they did not and 4e did so I tip my hat to this incredibly fun idea that I will be porting over to other games.

      My fav was the Dwarf Fighter whose player felt he had no skills that made sense and felt that he could not participate. I told him to forget the numbers and think how his Dwarf would seek this knowledge. His answer was to go to the worst, sleazest scumbag tavern and beat the shit out of everyone until somebody gave him a clue he could take back to group. We roleplayed out the look of the place, him kicking down the door and making his opening statement and decribed the brawl and the ugly, smashing finale.

      I had him lose a healing surge for the wounds and then make an Endurance roll to see if he could be last man standing in the brawl. And boom, he was part of the group skill challenge, added a cool bit to the game and really set in stone WHO his character was. The player was thrilled and the others told him how that scene added to the fun. It took 2-3 minutes that gave him the complete spotlight and moved the story forward.

      If that isn't roleplaying, what is?
      \nIf that isn't rule zero, what is?

      If we are going to consider Travis' problem, at least we should look at some successes.

      \n Does anybody else remember Vincent's little overlapping-circles diagram from ages ago (which I seem to be unable to locate)? What's at issue here is difference between the two kinds of "outside the text" decisions that we ALL MAKE in actually applying any game text to play: principled decisions and ad-hoc decisions. A principled decision is one that follows along an established pattern of play (be that social contract expectations, the "spirit of the rules", genre convention, or internal consistency). An ad-hoc decision is one that gets made because right now, right here, that's what the decider wants to have happen.

      The ideal game is one where it is at least possible to have the majority of play occur in the space defined by text+principled decisions (where principles are clearly communicated and shared).

      In practice, a lot of the time it's hard for someone other than the GM in a standard strong-GM game to know which one is which, because the GM is privy to a bunch of hidden information that gives context to apparently ad-hoc decisions. But I expect a lot of "bad rule zero" is ad-hoc decisionmaking, whereas "good rule zero" is principled - and it's incredibly difficult for anyone other than the decisionmaker (let alone people who weren't even there) to identify the line between those for any given decision.

      \n Oh, just caught up. It's sad that people bolted instead of talking about this part, because I think there's some wrongness and rightness in there:\n
      Posted By: Jeffrey StraszheimRegarding the "principled" thing: we can't ignore its context. Vincent is clearly suggesting the superiority of a certain "forgey" style metagamey play style that he likes. I mean .. that is obvious, right? The term "principled" is prejudicial, full stop.
      \nLooking at the definitions of principled and ad-hoc from the original post (quoted earlier in the thread), this sentiment is totally wrong. Full stop.

      Quoted from Mark W (if someone else can find where the original defs appeared, that might help):\n
      Posted By: Mark Wprincipled decisions and ad-hoc decisions. A principled decision is one that follows along an established pattern of play (be that social contract expectations, the "spirit of the rules", genre convention, or internal consistency). An ad-hoc decision is one that gets made because right now, right here, that's what the decider wants to have happen.
      \nThere's nothing judgmental in there. At all. One is talking about going with what the rules have said up to this point, and the other is about making up something new or on the fly because it's needed, even though it has no root in the rules or "social contract" (what the group has done until now). It's not hard to think of examples of both that many of us think of as "good". The decision to just say "ok you whack that NPC and he dies; no roll", and "ok let's say that we'll gloss over the ensuing fight, but you lose one healing surge, ok?"... both of those are Ad Hoc (at least according to Vincent, filtered through Mark W anyway), and most of us would agree that both were awesome, and moved play along (save maybe "Travis" for the first example :-) ).

      The part where you're right is, again, perhaps the terms were chosen poorly. If you have never heard the definitions before, native English speakers here principled, and think "moral, correct, solid, good". Ad hoc, while by the definition doesn't convey any negative connotations (edit: maybe it does? "for the particular end or case at hand without consideration of wider application", kinda conveys not thinking it through), when we hear it compared to another type of thing ad hoc seems to take on a connotation of sloppy, hasty, etc.

      It's a weird phenomenon here. I work in the tech industry, which has tons of loanwords or terms, and there's not a lot of confusion about their definition. From what I recall of sociology and philosophy as well (and this might be rose-tinted glasses) I didn't see a lot of term confusion either.

      I mean, well, what can we do, right? At least in that area the terms are defined and all. The best we can do is assume the best in people and assume that he was simply labeling a phenomenon he saw, and not declaring value judgments. And, consequently, if we then see someone coming along saying "ad hoc BAD; principled GOOD", then yeah we go all Rose and Camille all over them.
