\n Hola!

A friend and I got to talking about the difference between roleplaying and other storytelling media. One difference is editability \xe2\u20ac\u201d in most media, you can edit the story during the creative process. A director can reshoot scenes as many times as he likes. A writer can continuously go back to older chapters, rewriting them, restructuring the plot. However, in roleplaying, once something is established in the narrative, it kind of has "already happened".

Of course, this is not an absolute rule. People always go back, changing small details that happened a short time ago. Polaris, for instance, has even formalized the process in its Key Phrases, which effectively allow you to say "none of that happened". But what if one where to take it a step further, and use editing of scenes, not as a patch to take care of mistakes, but as a technique to enhance the narrative and the gaming experience?

I can imagine several varieties of this technique. The simplest one would be pure editing, wherein you play a scene several times, each time slightly different, decide upon one of the results (through a vote, perhaps), and makes that the "official" version of what happened. Such a technique might tie in to the general scene framing system of the game, and might allow players to request replayings under certain circumstances \xe2\u20ac\u201d or maybe "replay" could be one possible result of conflict resolution.

Taking the idea a step further, one could use replaying in order, not to perfect a scene, but to explore it. One could for instance play the scene from different perspectives, or focusing on different things, or slightly changing the premises each time. In this approach, there would be no need to decide upon an official version (although it would still be possible): if events unfold in a different manner in the different scenes, this might be taken as the subjective experiences of the characters involved \xe2\u20ac\u201d and might lead to an interesting exploration of the border between subjective and objective reality.

Well, these are two ideas, but it feels like I've only scratched the surface. What are your thoughts? Anyone used this technique, and what for? Anyone got other ideas for possible usages?

/Kalle

    \n We talked about replay and editing in another thread that Emily started recently (and that I can't find). I like the idea very much. I think with replaying a scene, you'd need to change the point of view or agenda to keep it interesting - replaying subtle variations strikes me as a "perfect is the enemy of good enough" situation, and also potentially tedious.

    If characters had prominent traits that reflected how they were perceived, and others that reflected how they perceived themselves, you'd be able to have amusing dichotomies by simply changing point of view. To others, I'm an overbearing fop, but to myself, I'm a concerned gentleman. Play the scene from both points of view, maybe with entirely different outcomes, and then have some mechanism for choosing which becomes accepted. For a humorous game, reinforce the future power of the trait that isn't selected.

    Funny that it's on both of our minds.

    Playing scenes out from multiple characters' points of view seems like a very interesting way to enrich the experience of a game. But the different perspectives really would have to differ in important ways in order for it to matter.

    Part of the reason I asked about it is because I've seen it done in improvisational games, where people took a given situation and replayed it, changing the game dramatically. It gave the person a chance to take back what they saw as a weaker contribution and give something else that had a much stronger impact.

    \n The Danish scenario Tjubang (http://alexandria.dk/data?scenarie=2949) explores the concept of replaying a scene: Each scene has a time constraint such as "at least 2 minutes" or "less than 1 minute". If the played out scene fails to meet the constraint, it must be redone. In my session it occurred once: When the heros best buddy got shot and dies in the arms of the hero. In this case it helped make a grotesque, prolonged death scene. The game is GM-less and uses the time contraints to pace the story.
