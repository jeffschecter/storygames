    \n The moves are meant to create a back-and-forth discussion between two players (and player and MC). One player chooses, then passes it over to another player, then back, etc. Going aggro and seduce/manipulate most especially. Also, they allow for more middle ground than simple pass/fail you get all you want or you get nothing. They'll work fine in games where scarcity isn't the issue, just look at all the hacks there are.

    Scarcity in the setting means there's no anonymous providers of stuff. In, say, the modern world, you get your water by turning on your taps. You buy stuff at a store, where you may not even have to interact with a cashier. Or you buy online, and some anonymous mail carrier drops it off. When you buy equipment in most games, it's all check a list, deduct money from character sheet.

    In AW, everything you want to get comes from somewhere, and either you find it or make it yourself, or you have to get it from someone. And if you get it from someone, they have a name, and you have to deal with them and stuff. Your water, your food, your clothes, your medicine, your electricity (if any) your home or at least the stuff you put in it, your guns, your ammo, your cars, your gasoline. None of it is ever provided by people you can ignore like the way it works in the present.

    \n Gigs are kinda like what Sage said, too, actually. Part of the point of them, along with things like Workspaces, is that there should be multiple ways to mechanically deal with the same thing. AW definitely has a series of overlapping mechanical systems. Like, if you want to kill someone, we can play that out action by action. Or you can roll for Doing Murders. Or you can just say "I shoot that dude in the back of the head" and the GM might say "Okay, his brains fly out his face." Or you can try to come up with a complex plan for killing the local hardholder and the GM might say, "Okay, you're going to need 1) planning time, 2) information about when and where he will be, 3) a good place to snipe him from, and 4) a solid getaway plan. If you have all those, you can just do it, and maybe Act Under Fire where the fire is unexpected friction." There's probably more too. Like you could write a custom move for assassinating NPCs, either generally or a specific move for a specific character. Or, to achieve similar fictional ends, you could pay some badass a few Barter to kill that fucker.

    I've seen a lot of hacks that look much more streamlined, where there's pretty much 1 or 2 ways to do most things. That's cool if it fits what you're trying to do with the game, but people should recognize that it changes the game significantly.

    \n I see what you're saying, Sage, but we're already talking about hacking AW, right? I'm not talking so much about how AW moves work, but pointing out that the underlying AW mechanics offer the MC (and potentially the players as well, if you write moves that allow them access) a variety of methods for handling the same narrative action (i.e. in this example, assassinating a dude). If you dig into the guts of AW to hack it, you should realize that you have all those mechanics, at the very least, to plug into (in addition to whatever newfangled mechanics you make up) when crafting moves and such.

    Or, maybe it's easier to say it this way: if you're writing a "when you assassinate a dude..." move for you hack, you can do it in multiple ways...
    -- have it just happen ("...say his name and he dies in the next 24 hours");
    -- tell them to use existing moves but take +1 forward or +2 to Read results or something;
    -- tell them to treat it like a Gig;
    -- have them roll a specific move and read results / pick options ("...on a 10+, XYZ, on a 7-9...");
    -- base it on Workspace, where you have to make a plan and gather materials / advantages;
    -- tell them to pay Barter;
    -- etc.

    And, furthermore, when a player addresses a specific narrative assertion to the MC (i.e. "I go assassinate him"), the MC can potentially reach into the same bag of goodies in figuring out how to engage the mechanics to make that happen, because that's just crafting a custom move on the spot, right? I guess I'm trying to say that the core system of AW is surprisingly robust and flexible, in practice. It's really cool to write a new move that really nails a particular action, but sometimes that may not be appropriate or feel right in play and AW gives you other things to reach for in those instances.

    Does that make more sense?

    \n Sure. On the AW forum, Barf Forth Apocalyptica, Vincent talks a lot about the different unbalanced parts of the game a lot. The Battlebabe, for example, breaks a lot of the principles he followed when designing the other classes (the sex move is a good example, but there are many others). Also, the stat block choices for the different classes aren't strictly balanced, plus there are some really interesting or valuable stat spreads that just aren't options for specific character classes. There's also the way that gangs, followers, holdings, and crews all are kinda similar but operate very differently in practice. There's no move for wrangling your crew or followers and getting them to do stuff, for example (unlike Pack Alpha or Leadership). The Hocus has to try to use Speak Truth To a Mob, which actually isn't follower-specific and can be used on any mob, so that's pretty different. The Operator gets nothing. Pack Alpha and Leadership are themselves pretty different. Another good example is the way different characters access Augury, with the Saavyhead getting it as an addition to their Workspace, to be used any time, while the Hocus has to roll+Fortunes at the beginning of the session and might get it or might not.

    All of these differences and unbalanced bits are a part of the overlapping systems and robustness that I was talking about earlier. They make for interesting constraints and interesting choices, both in play, since they can be used for different things and have different idiosyncratic limitations, and when choosing new moves. If everything was more or less the same, the game would seem clean and pretty and straightforward instead of messy. As things are, people often have to make do with moves that don't quite allow them to do what they want without consequences or striving hard (because what fun would that be?).

    \n Moves alter the fictional landscape.

    Especially the basic moves. They tell you what one person can accomplish in one unit of time. They set the scope of play. Their asymmetry is what makes it matter what people are actually doing in the fiction. When you're bound and gagged in the back of someone's truck, your options, mechanically and fictionally, are different than when you're standing over someone, gun drawn.

    In, for example, In a Wicked Age, "I lay waste to your kingdom with my armies" is mechanically equivalent to "I punch you in the face". In Apocalypse World it's not. That's what moves are for.

    So, if you're designing a hack, you might be tempted to make a move like this:

    When you try to get something you want, and someone tries to stop you, roll +stat.
    10+ you get what you want
    7-9 you get most of what you want but you must compromise on something
    on a miss, you don't get what you want.

    But don't do that. Instead, design the fiction you want to see in play.

    \n I made this violence move for another hack thing. I'm pretty happy with it.

    When you engage in violence, roll+force. On a 10+, you outmaneuver your opponent. You inflict harm as established or choose two below. On a 7-9, choose one:
    - You exchange blows. You and your opponent both inflict harm as established.
    - You seize an advantageous opportunity. Take +1forward.
    - You force your opponent back and disengage for a moment.
    - You detect a weakness in your opponent\u2019s defense. Do +1harm when you next inflict harm on them.
    - You improve your defensive posture. Take +1armor during the next exchange.
    - You change the fighting distance, terrain, or nature of the contest (from swordplay in the hall to close grappling on the balcony, for instance)

    On a miss, the GM may inflict harm on you as established, choose two from the list above for your opponent, or make some other hard move.

    ----------

    This is for a more nitty-gritty action game, but I think it addresses the issues with seize fairly well.
