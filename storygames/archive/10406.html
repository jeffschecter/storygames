    \n What about saying "role-playing is like The Sopranos' version of Cops & Robbers"?

    I'm kinda torn on this. Personally, I don't see a negative connotation, but I understand not wanting to associate the activity with child's play. (For example, I get frustrated that the bicycle is still often viewed as a child's toy, or at least as some sort of play thing, rather than as a legitimate & utilitarian form of transportation.)\n
    Posted By: Levi-Who-Babbles.Why say "It's like X", when you can just tell people what it is?
    \nThe reason---in the abstract, at least---is that using an analogy should make it easier to explain, as you can assume a certain amount of prior knowledge. The analogy might also engender nostalgia or interest in some people, though there's the trade-off that it might turn other people off.

    Another thing is that you want to explain why the activity is fun, or why someone would want to engage in it. If you just give a procedural explanation of play, people may not get it. Honestly, I'm not sure if saying "you get to make up stories with friends" would even do it, as I think that's still a relatively foreign idea to most people. (Personally, I haven't gotten great results when I've tried to explain role-playing as a form of storytelling.) Saying it's like Cops & Robber or playing House communicates that it's some sort of imaginative play activity, at least.

    \n Heh... you tabletop writers think YOU got it hard. Try explaining boffer/projectile LARP! I don't go NEAR cops & robbers. I start with terms like "improvisational acting" and "story telling" but then go on to say "but with sport elements--if you decide you're going to shoot me or stick a knife in my guts, you DO it with a safe prop."

    And, yes, much as Levi says: it takes so little time to come up with a two- or three-sentence explanation of the high-level activity that abridging that with an analogy or simile usually is more harm than good:
    * It's dismissive of someone showing interest when you don't take the few extra moments to be clear.
    * Metaphors are very, very easy to misinterpret; viz almost every argument from analogy, including the diaper one in this very thread!
    * The "Chinese Telephone" problem--even if your initial punter "got it" with your analogy, they are likely to drift further from clarity in the retelling to friends, if they misuse or over-emphasize the childish parallels.

    But all that said, read your audience. Some folks might totally dig the idea of being a kid again and going wild. Now if only I could think of more than three Story Games that actually CAPTURE that youthful whimsy and exhilaration....

    \n When I get asked to explain what it is I'm doing on Wednesday nights or what I spend my time writing about, I dredge through years of awkward incomprehension. Telling an amused listener that I play something like Cops & Robbers gets them thinking about some kinky s**t. To lots of people, roleplaying, let's face it, means either doing a seminar in the boardroom or getting tied up and spanked.

    The alternative, trying to sell the concept, requires some sexy thinking. Why not describe it as a 'creative workshop with guidelines'? Or even use some kind of intro, 'imagine you've won the lottery. As you get off the bus to cash your ticket, a strange looking fellow in an orange jumpsuit grabs it and runs down an alley, calling out your name... what would you do?' Or you could tell them it's like a board game, with more wheeling and dealing. Or, 'have you ever played one of those murder mystery dinners?' There should be enough alternatives now with which to sell the concept. Especially to creative people, who love creative things, and need convincing of the creative merit. They're the best gamers after all and we don't want to box them into the competitive, binary solution of child's play. There's loads more on offer.

    \nNot sure I agree with that. Normals play tennis, golf, video games, chess, backgammon, cards, dice, draughts, etc.

    Just like everyone else, I've only got one perspective, and mine tells me that people make unfair judgements about gaming unless they're given a new incentive to explore an old idea. Sometimes people absorb young prejudice in their formative (high school) years, and it never gets a chance to digest. Anxieties manifest. If I don't want to dredge up school-yard stigma, I don't enthuse about pretending and children's games.

    It's sort of like trying to convince someone that they do like broccoli. I used to live with a guy who refused to eat his greens. It was astonishing. Over time, I started to figure it out. He'd been subjected to overcooked vegetables through his childhood. Slowly I introduced him to pan roast roots, from parnsips tossed in rosemary oil, to browned kumara, to charbroiled broccoli with anchovies and lemon juice. Eventually he folded, enjoyed it, and the damn was burst. All of the food inhibitions became curiosities. 'Maybe I do like monkfish,' etc. I think the same can be applied to games, and since it causes no harm to be careful, it's my strategy with newbies.
