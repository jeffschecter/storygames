\nAll this talk about cheap games has me wondering about free games.

What are your favorite free games? What free games do you play the hell out of? Do you "collect" free games you'll never play?

Do free games get more play than $5 games, or vice-versa?

Do you place more value on a game you paid for? Are you more likely to play it? Are you more likely to love it or hate it? Are you more likely to talk about it?

Does free represent an opportunity cost for a designer? Is a free game a marketing tool? Is it a statement?

Is a free game more likely to inspire you to hack it? To approach the designer about it? Do you feel more "ownership" of a free game?

    \n I've based soo many games off the d20 SRD (both the fantasy one and the modern one, and even the anime one).

    \n I've never played or downloaded a free game.

    Well, the reality is a bit more nuanced than that. I've played a ton of Jonathan's games in various stages of playtest, and they've all ended up free eventually or never quite finished. So I don't think that counts. I've seen screenshots of Lady Blackbird and think it's lovely, but our gaming plates are really full in WMass, so I can't imagine finding the time to play it. (Part of the culture here is that everyone's got a game they want to playtest, or that's ALMOST done, or that they just finished and want to enjoy. I guess Apocalypse World is technically "out" now, so I don't know if it counts because we've been playing it on and off for a year or more; but I pretty much ONLY play games that are already in publication at cons. Otherwise it's exclusively playtests.)

    I really should download it, because I want to see the prettiness, but I just keep forgetting. It's not like the urgency of "Oh, well I have $5 free NOW, I should get it."

    Shreyas and I, as the two main (but not only!) people of 2SP, keep going back and forth about releasing free games. There are a couple of things I have going around in my brain I'd like to release for free; he's completely against free product as discounting the worth of your creation. There's a couple children's games we've been noodling about forever, and should they ever get done, all profits would go to a charity\u2014 so it's not just about money for the creator, I guess. I don't know.

    My completely-uninformed prejudices about free games include the following (note: all do not apply to every game):

    - The author/designer has sales baggage
    - The author/designer isn't confident in their design
    - The author/designer doesn't want to put the time in the product would need to make it saleable
    - The author/designer abandoned the game in the hopes someone else would finish/hack it

    None of them actually are value judgements, in and of themselves, and I can see releasing a game for free for any of those reasons. The last three don't inspire confidence in a usability sense, though. I also don't do as much hacking as most do, so picking up a game with an eye to hack it isn't a thing I do.

    \n My favorite free games are Fate 2.0 and GHOST/ECHO I've played the hell out of both of them. I don't really collect free games. I don't think price has any impact on my decision whether or not to play a game a lot. I hear that is it human nature to place more value on things we pay for, but I don't recognize that tendency in myself for rpgs at least. I talk some about games I've had the opportunity to playtest and those often don't cost me anything. I do like to be able to ultimately own a "finished" printed artifact that's got nice design and/or layout and/or artwork over a free game that may be just a printed Word document. That typically costs money.

    As to opportunity cost, it depends on what the designer does later. The big example again here is Fate. Its free availability and the culture and forums that sprung up around it as a result were a springboard for SotC's success, and now there's the Dresden Files game. I guess it was a marketing tool in that sense, though maybe not consciously. Fate was likely free in part because of the pre-existing FUDGE culture. A couple designers that offer free games have made posts about it being a form of statement, expressing a particular ideal. I've made and hacked lots of games in the last 30 years and I've never shared any of them online. They're free to the groups I play them with. No statement, just a preference.

    Playtesting and hacking games makes me feel some sense of ownership of a game. They're generally free at the playtest phase but I don't think the fact that it was free at that point is what made me want to hack it (if I do). The game's design accessibility is what will make me want to hack it or not. I suspect a lot of games that are only $5 will have such a tight focus/application, that their design won't often be one that wants hacking.

    At my income relative to my free time there really isn't much difference to me between a free game and a game that costs $15-$20. It just occurs to me that I might prefer it if more games were either free or $40 or more dollars. My shelves might be less full of games that I picked up because they were inexpensive but that I will never find the time to play either because they're basically betas that people went ahead and published anyway, or simply because there are other games I prefer to play or playtest. (this is not a slam on any of the games that happen to be at that price point)

    \n I have run the following free games (several times each) at cons.
    Beat the Clock by Fred Hicks
    Geiger Counter by Jonathan Walton
    Donjon by Clinton R. Nixon

    I have downloaded John Harper's kit games (e.g. Lady Blackbird, Ghost/Echo, Wildlings) and am eager to play those.

    Playability: This is an important question. If I were downloading free games that the designers hadn't at least tested, I could be headed for an awful experience. I'm pretty inventive and can adjudicate things easily on the fly, so I don't mind a few flaws or vague bits in a free game. Luckily, all the above games are Excellent. They work with little or no interpretation/adjudication at the table. To state a comparison, I buy a lot of John Wick's cheaper games ($5-10), and while I think they are totally worth it, I have found them to reflect a lack of testing or at least an incomplete communication of the rules. (Schauermarchen is a good example of one that I think has incomplete mechanics and may not ultimately "work." But it was worth $5 just for the seeds it sewed in my brain. Cat is an example of a better Wick game with a similar price.)

    Worth: I'm using this word to describe the financial worth of these documents to me. In other words, what would I pay for them or would I make a donation for them. In most cases I would be willing to pay the designer around $10 for a PDF of the games, depending upon how "done" they are and how clean the layout is. I did buy Donjon (totally worth it). Obviously a lot of these games could be illustrated and expanded to the point where they would be worth quite a bit more than $10, but then I would ironically be less interested in them. I like the "sketchy" quality of free or very cheap games. I would be willing to donate to a designer after playing their free game (and have).

    Ownership: I don't know that I feel more ownership over a free game. In some ways, I am LESS inclined to hack a free game than one that is more complex and for which I paid more money. I'm not sure I can explain that. Maybe it's because to pick up and play a free game it has to attract me a lot to begin with. Also, free games tend to leave more up to me anyway. The stuff I usually drift in pay-for-it games (usually the setting and mechanical subsystems) are less developed in a free game, which suits me to a T. I wanted to do that stuff with my group and/or on my own anyway.

    "Collectibility": For sure. I tend to grab free games when I see them. At the same time, I purge them frequently. After I give them a quick read through, I trash them if they aren't something I either want to play or steal an idea from. So I don't have a huge collection of free games, but the ones I do keep I love. Conversely, I have a hard time trashing a file or book that I paid for, even if it has little value for me. So my collection of paid-for games is bigger but I am less emotionally tied to most of the items in that collection.

    Statement: Hell yeah. Free games make a statement that I like. I think they are made by people that prefer the word "hobby" to "industry". They are made by people whose primary goal is fun and creativity. I love free games and the people that make them.

    \n Free games I've played in the last year: Danger Patrol, Apocalypse World (though there were sold copies of that too), Geiger Counter*, Danger Sxool*, Mare Caspium.* The starred ones may not count, since they are my own games. Free games that I've collected for being really awesome or important to learn from but never played: Scarlet Wake, Radiant, probably a couple others. I've played probably dozens of other free games over the years: Afraid, Drowning and Falling, various drafts of things, etc.

    I honestly think it's a bit funny that Elizabeth says she's never played a free game when she's been playing Apocalypse World a whole bunch and the commercial version has only been out for a week or so In my mind, playtest drafts are free games, because there's no guarantee that they will ever appear in a later version. Afraid and Scarlet Wake are great examples, where the version we have may be the only version that ever exists, so we have to make do with it.

    Honestly, for me, it doesn't matter how much I pay for a game, if it's a playtest draft or a finished product, if it's in print or PDF, etc. If it's a compelling game that's really exciting and it's in a solid enough state that I feel confident about bringing it to the table, I'll at least give it a go and have no problem playing a half dozen sessions of it. There are plenty of reasons to NOT sell a game -- like not wanting to run a business or wanting to share something with friends rather than entering into a commercial relationship or embracing a model of incremental development that may eventually lead to a commercial release (cf. Geiger, Danger Patrol, etc.) -- so I certainly don't think commercial games are better than free games, either inherently or just in common practice.

    To answer Jason's questions more directly, I'm much more likely to be upset with a commercial game that doesn't deliver the promised play experience or which I can't figure out how to play properly from reading the instructions. That's probably unfair, but it's easy to get caught up in thinking that what you're actually buying is a good play experience and not a bunch of text and play materials, even though you can't, like, blame Victoria's Secret for bad sex. But that's my impulse anyway.

    I don't think of free games as an opportunity cost, a marketing tool, or a statement. In my mind, free is a natural part of the process, basically the DEFAULT way to release game material, and commercial publication is the mode that requires more thought, planning, and careful consideration. "Should I release this for free?" is never a thought I've ever had. "Should I release this for money?" is the question I'm constantly asking myself about products. Games are like any other creative act that I share with my friends. Writing games is like cooking a meal for people; charging them for the meal just seems silly. Now, some folks do want to run a restaurant and you still charge your friends when they come eat at your restaurant, so that makes sense too, but it's a pretty different thing. And I can also understand the $5 game phenomenon as a way of saying: "Hey, can you guys chip in a few bucks to help cover costs?" or as a way of supporting the community, like they do in indie comics. However, as in indie comics, I think it's better to trade $5 games between designers (for free) on the assumption that everybody can turn out a $5 game. I've seen that kind of thing happen (and, in fact, owe Jackson a game from such an exchange), so that's awesome.

    I hack everything. There's a slightly stronger incentive to hack when the designer has said that they welcome free and commercial products based on their games, but Vincent welcomes both commercial and non-commercial hacks of Apocalypse World and Luke's welcomed non-commercial hacks of Mouse Guard (they both have set up sub-forums for them, which is awesome), so that's not really restricted to free games. Solid hacks are great things to see and many an independent game started life as a hack.

    \n I play, GM or buy for different reasons...
    \n
    \n- I play if my friends are excited about the game.
    \n- I GM if the game is easy to use.
    \n- I buy because of the above, to support someone, to learn something or if friends want but can't afford it.
    \n
    \nIf I can afford it, money doesn't enter into any of the above for me. The only time free or cheap is an advantage is if I'm curious about a game but not sure. Then the ability to try for free or cheap is a huge advantage. Which is why many higher priced games also have free quick start rules or preview materials.
    \n
    \nI don't think I personally count OGL, Fate, TSoY as free games or systems for myself because they are essentially marketing for commercial products (which isn't a bad thing) and I own the commercial products tied to those games.
    \n
    \nI don't GM or play many free games but that's probably due to a lack of support (which makes me resistant into putting any work into GMing it) or marketing (if my friends haven't heard about it they can't be excited about it) for the game. But that can be true for commercial products as well.
    \n
    \nI am more likely to play a game that I buy (or gift it) because I don't want to waste money.
    \n
    \nHow likely I am to talk about a game completely depends on if I play it and what the experience was, not how much it costs.
    \n
    \nI'm not more likely to hack a free game. I'm likely to hack if it is close to the kind of game I want to play and is easy to hack.
    \n
    \nA complete aside, I do find I'm more interested in games by game designers who I know buy and play a lot of other games. It's not necessarily logical but it does factor in for me.

    I think there's a huge difference in a free game and a playtest game. Playtest games aren't provided to you with the assumption that you will have fun. I love Apocalypse World, I think it's fantastic, but the main reason I agreed to play in both games I've been in wasn't just "to have fun." I agreed to playtest something for my friend, so that he could take feedback and make the game better and ready to publish.

    Playtesting is not free. Never, nuh-uh. Now, you love to hack, so YMMV: but when I sit down to play a published game, I'm not devoting a big chunk of my brain to critically deconstructing what we're doing and how it could be better. I don't stop the GM in the middle of a scene and go "I think this rule is trying to accomplish X and failing, what if you made it do Y?" I sit back and put all my brain into enjoying the game as presented. Being a good playtester is hard work. The game you play isn't always to a place where it's FUN yet, or may not even be completed, so the GM/designer may need or want you to help playstorm through the missing bits. That's completely different.

    And even though I don't feel this way when people playtest MY games: if I playtested something for an author, had a great time, and didn't let them know what worked and didn't and WHY I had a great time, I'd kind of feel like I was stealing from them. ESPECIALLY if I knew they had designs on publication.




    *If you'd played the first version of Apocalypse World and saw how hot it was from the very beginning, and how dedicated Vincent was to it, you'd have seen that it was headed to publication in the first 20 minutes of the first session.     \n My favourite free games are Shadows, Microcosm, and Kazekami Kyoko Kills Kublai Khan. While I wouldn't say I play the hell out of them, I wouldn't say that about any game. And, oddly enough, there are fewer unplayed free games in my collection than unplayed paid for games in my collection.

    Free games get more play than five dollar games. I don't think I've ever played a five dollar game.

    I don't find that I place any particular value on games I purchased, certainly not more than free games. I own more paid for games than free games, and it seems that the play is about fifty fifty. I don't think I like paid for games any more than free games. But when it comes to talking about it, I talk way more about free games than I do about paid for games. Way more.

    I'm not sure why people release their games for free, although when I hear people express why they want money for their games, more often than not, they make me want to pirate (not unlike the music industry). Things like, paying money makes one more likely to play makes me want to prove them wrong. Paying money shows than my work is worth something, same thing, I am though, highly anti-capitalistic, viewing capitalism as a highly dysfunctional relationship between people. So people who equate worth with money, either their own or their creations, make me specifically want to never give them any money whatsoever.

    I've never hacked a game, and were I to do it, it would probably be to a game I've paid for. But that is a, a mechanics thing, and b, just because there are some cool games out there where I love 90% of the mechanics, and absolutely despise 10% of them. And I think it's worth it for me, to alter the ten percent so that I can enjoy the ninety.

    Uh, I don't think I get the ownership question. On the flip side, I'm not sure I buy the notion that if I have a book sitting in front of me, that anyone owns it. Not me, not the creator, not the publisher, nobody.
