\n Let's say you are in a near ideal rp situation. Your play group negotiates well with each other, far beyond avoiding jerks and grief -- you click like no group ever has clicked before. Every player is highly engaged in the stories of the other players and characters. You've all mastered a GMless system for introducing challenges, conflicts, and shocks without the slightest worry of violating the Czege Principle.

Etc., etc., etc. In short, you don't really need a GM at all. There's absolutely nothing about your group or game that could use mitigating by the presence of a GM.

Why might you want one anyway?

In short, are GMs always medicine, or is it possible for them to be candy?

    \n One of my players recently brought up the idea of making our game GMful. He's totally unconnected to the SG/RPG scene, knows zero lingo, but came up with the idea organically. When he brought up the idea, I said "Sure!" but everyone else at the table shook their head. When I asked why, they basically said (I'm making it much more articulate) that having the GM provides a sense of experiencing the unknown. They don't know what's behind a given door and there is a sense of mystery and discovery associated with that. If everyone is contributing on a GM level, the sense of mystery is lost to an extent because they have the opportunity to make the decision of what's behind that door, removing the excitement of discovery. It also instills a sense of unity among the players since the GM often takes a pseudo-adversarial role.

    \n There are definitely reasons for having a GM, as others have said. There are many enough that I can't compile it all into a pithy catchphrase, though.

    One big reason that I've been rocking lately is that a singular GM is capable of holding and developing large secret developments in a way that is impossible or at least awkward (and thus requiring special systemic consideration) with shared GMing. Despite open scenarios having been a big trend in modern design over the last decade, there is still plenty of reason to go for secrets, too. It's the original purpose of having a GM, after all, historically speaking.

    For extracurricular funkiness feel free to contrast and compare a GM with online chat play. The latter has the interesting property of making secret communication trivial.

    Even positing that there could be a single superior method of handling mysteries in roleplaying is ludicrous. There are fun and valid games that use the narrative concept of mystery completely differently from each other. Some have GMs guarding those secrets, others have each player keep their own, while yet others share all secrets between players in a spirit of dramatic irony, and yet a fourth type of game has secrets created retroactively as needed. There is no profit to be found in single-mindedness when it comes to design issues like this. Dirty Secrets is a very nice game, but holding it up as an argument for No Myth being a categorically superior approach to backstory... damn, let's think that one through again.

    Aaron: the reason your stance on secrets garners so much resistance is that you're arguing from personal experiences that you wield as absolutes.     Like, just now, you told Jason there that it's impossible for a GM to possess a mystery that is genuinely solved by players. And the only way to have a real mystery would be by relying on real criminology, because apparently mysteries can only be criminal in nature, and there can't be mysteries outside the detective genre. And real mysteries are dull anyway because they can usually be solved by routine police-work.

    Going by this logic I am apparently either deluded or lying when I tell you that our D&D campaign relies heavily on asymmetrical information wielded by the GM, and this information routinely manifests in play as mysteries the players attempt to solve. The information is objective, often coming from meticulously pre-written adventure modules, and the GM's task is to present it into play realistically, according to what the players declare they are doing. Failures in investigation often leave the adventuring party in dire straits, like the time they failed to figure out the identity of the pod-person in advance and the traitorous creature caused a cave-in, trapping the party underground. Other times players succeed and gain the gratitude of the city by e.g. cathing the magical serial killer that's been haunting it.

    Insofar as it matters, in our campaign it's totally kosher for players to read the GM's or module-writer's mind and cut the mystery short with their telepathic powers. Great for you if you can do that, means that you're an excellent player ready for more difficult challenges; there's no required optimal length of time you need to sit on your ass before you're allowed to solve the mystery. Often the ability of players to solve mysteries in this way end up limited by the fiction, though; for example, in the cave-in adventure above the players totally pegged the right culprit almost immediately upon coming to the scene, before there even was any clear indication that this was a mystery adventure, but they were forced to admit that they had no feasible method to do anything about it right then. Or rather, perhaps they weren't certain enough to just attack and slay the guy on the spot; after all, suspecting that he was guilty didn't equal knowing that he wasn't human underneath, or knowing why he was guilty and in what way, exactly.

    It's close to off-topic, yes, but I'd like to have it on record that actual empirical observation of the world tells us that GMs can be utilized as keepers of secrets in a fruitful way that has nothing to do with mitigating incompetent players. Our D&D campaign, for instance, is not the way it is because the players are somehow incompetent and incapable of playing higher forms of games; these are some of the same people who've played the entire range of Forge-related games with me over the last decade, voluntarily playing some D&D for a change because it's a fresh and different approach to putting a game together.

    \n Well, I'm mostly going by this, from OP:\n
    In short, you don't really need a GM at all. There's absolutely nothing about your group or game that could use mitigating by the presence of a GM.

    Why might you want one anyway?
    \nThat is, why one might wish to have a GM. This is inherently a task of speculative mapping, figuring out different reasons for why a game designer might decide to create a game with a GM-like role even when he is not concerned with group coordination and leadership. Obviously some of these reasons will be more or less important for individual designers, but I had the impression that Burr wanted a plurality of alternatives, not a single answer.

    The opposite question of what features are shared between all valid GM-roles is a bit more obscure, it feels to me. I mean, it seems to me that to answer that we'd need to actually create an analytical definition of what a GM is, to even begin. Also, to disprove any single claim for this question it would be sufficient to cite a game that has a GM but does not utilize the role for the given function. For example, you suggest that providing antagonism could be part of the minimal universal GM role; however, how about a game like Amber where the players are supposed to provide antagonism for each other while the GM is a neutral arbiter? A group playing Amber (the default scenario, anyway) probably doesn't want a GM who provides antagonism.

    Regardless, I don't mind either phrasing of the question as long as we're clear on what we're discussing. I do, obviously, agree that there are games and gaming groups that do not want to play a game where the GM is the gate-keeper of truth. Thus having GM-kept mysteries cannot be an universal feature of the gamemaster's role.
