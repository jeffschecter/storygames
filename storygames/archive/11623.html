Posted By: AndyAgain, our "official" (scarequotes!) rules summary for these threads:

-----
1. Feel free to respond here in-thread to another link posted.
a. If you're looking to start a conversation, perhaps take your comment and create a new thread with it in the Stuff to Watch category.
b. Otherwise, to prevent too many comments in the thread, number your response (first response: [1]; second response [2]; third response [3]). If you see that you're the second person to post, "Wow, that looks cool!" to a post here, then put a "[2]" at the top of your post. Three posts ([3]) is the limit: The fourth person to post should just take their comment and create a new thread, to carry the discussion over there further.
-----
Why?

Because these threads are great: We can link to little things being talked about on the web in general, on blogs, or other forums that might be noteworthy, without having to dedicate a new thread to each and every one (although that, too, is fine if you want to discuss the item further or "open up a discussion").
Responding to these posts with a comment or two is understandable (other times there might be no comments, just cause the link was self-explanatory: nothing to say, lots of heads nodding in agreement). But we don't want to create a huge discussion in the middle of the thread: Some folks get a lot out of these threads, and don't want to look back to see the "10 latest posts" just to see that it's a heated discussion of something from a previous page. Cool? Cool.
-----

    Posted By: misubaSo yeah, a couple of us are gonna find this interesting I think:

    Sleep Is Death, by Jason Rohrer, is in preorder.

    When it says "Click here for the story," DO IT.
    \nLet me repeat that more clearly. The creator of Passage, one of the most acclaimed and controversial of the new wave of indie downloadable PC games, has created a two-player game in which one player simply makes the game world respond to the actions of the other. Here's a screenshot of what that first player will see:



    So, not everything is clear yet but it looks like we're talking Neverwinter Nights, only without rules, and you do all the level-building on the fly under a limit of 30 seconds a turn. It should even be possible to create new sprites in that amount of time, if you're good.

    The preorder is $9, which allows two people to download the game (a week before its general release) for either Windows, Mac OS, or Linux. The price goes up to $14 on the game's release.

    How staggeringly cool is this?

      \n A\xef! Celebrated F&SF writer Charles Stross just announced that Cubicle 7 is going to publish an RPG based on his "Laundry" series ("The Atrocity Archive", "The Jennifer Morgue" and the upcoming "The Fuller Memorandum"). I am hopping up and down with glee at this \u2014 I love these books, and, despite my general 'meh' attitude toward licensed games, I think they'd be a blast to play.

      The series is basically Lovecraft + spycraft + "The Office" + enormous helpings of computer geekery (Stross is a serious Unix developer, and lets you know it.) Its premise is that all those lovely alien dimensions and elder gods and soul-eating abominations exist; advanced mathematical techniques provide access to them, forming a sort of magic; the world's governments have known this since the early 1900s; said dimensional nasties have formed a secret background to wars and espionage ever since. Yes, some of this is a bit clich\xe9d, but Stross's genius is to show it from the viewpoint of a mid-level agent in the British arcane service (codenamed "The Laundry"), a brilliant techie with the typical anti-authority geek attitude who's trapped in the awful bureaucracy of a government agency.

      So in addition to busting squamous and rugose Things, there's a lot of humor about filling out forms in triplicate, and in-jokes about the proper firewall rule settings for blocking shoggoths from your intranet. It all fits together extremely well, it's both hilarious and somewhat scary in places (the dead parallel Earth in "The Atrocity Archives" is really creepy), and it combines so many things that are fun to play in RPGs. Yay!

      \n [Games Can Save The World 2]

      Same here!

      I have a few friends working towards PHDs in alternate economics. Essentially they are studying how to build ethical and humane economic systems. We had a long discussion recently about their work and I expressed that I feel no system is perfect. People will find loopholes. And people with influence who benefit from loopholes will delay fixing them to extend exploitation. The problem is we don't playtest our systems and are slow to patch them.

      I pitched them the idea of creating MMOs based on their new economic models to let gamers rip them apart. If you make it a game, gamers will break it, and then you can fix it faster.

      And save the world!
