\n So I've been running PbP games for awhile. I wasn't an early adopter, so lets say I've been doing it for a few years on various sites.

Like all things, PbP are cyclical in nature. Certain times of the day there's more chance of activity, certain days of the week (assuming relative geographical proximity), and certain times of the year (summer and winter breaks often sees posts dwindle or posters vanish). But whatever.

I'm curious about something else I'm noticing lately. It's probably always been there, but I'm just catching on (yeah, I'm slow sometimes). Especially in the realm of PbP...

So what is it with players who want crunch for crunch's sake?

What I mean is: Players who insist on crunchier games for no discernible reason. All other things being equal, especially in a PbP environment, you'd think that players would push for rules-lighter games rather than their crunchier cousins. It's really hard to compare apples to apples on things like this, I know. But there's a distinct advantage that's recently come to us that allows GMs to study this phenomenon.

Fate Core and Fate Accelerated (FAE).

These two games are basically the same foundation expressed slightly differently. They use fudge dice, use aspects, fate points, fate point economy, stress, consequences. Fate. But these two games are slightly different. If you're not familiar: Core uses two stress and consequence tracks as well as a list of 18-some skills that cover what the character can do; FAE however, uses one stress and consequence track as well as a list of 6 approaches that cover how the character does things. That's it. That's the difference between the two...

Except the page count. Core: 310-pages; FAE: 50-pages. And what's in that 260-pages of difference? Crunch. In the form of examples, subsystems, extras, more examples...

Yet, for the life of me, I can't seem to find many FAE games across my sites of choice, yet Core games are flourishing, comparatively. The count is 21 to 10, in Core's favor. Genres are across the board, which is to be expected for a "generic" system, but they tend toward fantasy. Of course.

So has anyone else noticed this odd trend? I'm sure people have. I've just never been able to compare this so directly before. It's still weird to me though.

Second question, I guess. Anyone know of any PbP sites where FAE games are common / flourishing?

    \n Oh, yeah, I'm not saying the games are bad, or those who like them are Doing It Wrong. If that's what people like, enjoy. It is a completely separate thing to enjoy a style of game than being a dick about enjoying a style of game. I wasn't trying to conflate the two.

    Different games are inherently different beasts. Which is why it's been so hard to directly compare lighter games to crunchier games. Until now.

    Mechanics make a difference, granted. (If that's a contentious remark then that's definitely a whole new thread.)

    Typically, different games are so mechanically difference that a direct comparison is impossible. With how similar Core and FAE are, I think that direct comparison is not only possible, but downright simple to do.

    Of the two mechanical differences between them, number of stress/consequence tracks and skills vs approaches, the stress/consequences one is easier to dismiss. There's a fair amount of discussion lately on G+ about abandoning the second track even in Core games as most gamers don't seem to see the need for it. In case you're unfamiliar: Core uses a physical and mental stress/consequence track, whereas FAE uses a single stress/consequence track for everything.

    Which leaves skills vs approaches. Since mechanics do make a difference, and the difference between these designs is not insignificant (the skills and related stunts section of Core is almost as many pages long as the entirety of FAE), what does that really mean in play? Or, how does that affect play and the stories that emerge?

    With Approaches you have six universal personality/how you go about things traits which cover basically everything. There's some discussion of hitting a particular approach too often, say always going with your highest, or trying to, and feeling punished if you can't. But, you're also covering the vast range of possible actions across many/most genres with a short and simple list. Approaches are doing some heavy lifting by being personality markers along with covering the same ground as skills. This works really well for games where the skill sets of the PCs would have significant overlap, bringing character distinction in the form of personality. Also, this universality permits off-the-wall games, say a premise that's unusual, say investment banking. Forceful, Clever, etc can all still be used just as well.

    With Skills you have eighteen specific areas of expertise that determine what you can do. To be fair, these are somewhat generic skills that are basically meant to cover most situations that would come up, but leeway is explicitly given to modify or add to this list when necessary. But, that's an inherent danger of this design... blind spots. Or design blindness or whatever it's called around here. And almost having to hack the system before you can use it. This works really well for games where the characters are distinct based on their individual skill sets, but is somewhat diminished in the most popular action-adventure genres as most characters would default to the same baseline capacity of kicking ass, thus eliminating a fair amount of that distinction. For our investment banking example, not only would most of the skills simply never come up, but a whole new set of skills and related stunts would need to be designed just to attempt such a game.

    I'm not saying one is right and the other wrong. Just stating my preference and wondering why the general push of gamers seems to tend toward heavier systems when all other things are equal. Core and FAE are so fundamentally similar that they can be compared apples to apples. And yeah, a bit of cursory poking around suggests that Core is something like 2x as popular as FAE.

    So maybe it's just me being confused by the whole thing, but I still don't really get it. Two games that are practically identical mechanically, yet one is a lot more popular than the other. Some of the things mentioned by others up thread are marketing, branding, and tradition.
