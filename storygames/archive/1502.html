Japanese RPG Madness

Jason_Morningstar
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Story Games
So last night Andy came over to play some Cold City and he brought along a stack of Japanese RPG rules, ephemera, and other stuff. I've been excited about Tenra Bansho for a while, and I'm extremely interested in the way Japanese designers and publishers approach the hobby. Here are some things I observed, randomly, as a guy who can't read Japanese:

Every book was lavishly illustrated and heavily designed. RPG books had color plates, usually of character archetypes. I know there's no such thing as anime style, but it's all anime style.

There are wiiiiiiiide thematic gulfs between east and west. Clinton capably demonstrated this by covering a book cover with a sheet of paper and then doing a slow reveal, top to bottom. Ok, a dragon. Awesome. And a sexy lady fighting it. Then another weird dude, then a lady in a frilly dress with a garter belt ... not so RPG but hey, still pretty cool ... and then, at the bottom of the page - SANDWICHES. The focal point of the art, a couple of delicious sandwiches. How dirty hippie can you get?

Organizing visual information - they seem to like boxes rather than lists. Every spell, every skill, every everything is in a discrete box, nine or twelve to a page. A pattern repeated across many books. It looks pretty good.

Did I mention that every book was lavishly illustrated and heavily designed?

Actual play comics - both within RPG texts and as adjuncts. You can buy transcripts of gaming sessions, and apparently this is a hobby gateway. The AP comics in RPG books look amazing.

Topical supplements. So there's this game, I don't even know what it is called, and you can buy a supplement for it that is about weddings. WEDDINGS. It comes with two sheets of perf-cut cards to punch out, 18 of them, each of which is something awesome that can happen at the wedding - like plague rats showing up, the adorable troll people showing up. The bouquet has its own card and has special powers. This is the best idea I've encountered in a long time. "You like TSOY? Here's 48 pages of amazing things that can happen at a FAMILY REUNION! The scrapbook does +3 harm!"

The games seem geared toward fast entry with character archetypes that are clear and fun-first. You make three choices and you are killing space Nazis.

One of the games is a steampunk wild west sort of deal, and it is a little incorrect - you can play native American dudes, but they have horns. This is a feature of Tenra Bansho, too, but in that game, people hunt them down and use their hearts to power machinery. Say it with me ... AWESOME!

Big thanks to Andy for being our sensitive bridge to a far-off culture, and to Japan for being completely bananas.

    Andy or Mark would be better at answering your later questions. Glossy color covers. Color plates inserted, B&W art on every page. The books look and feel like RPG books - you can see where there are rules, procedures (often illustrated with little diagrams), lists of equipment or spells or whatever. The art is often tightly integrated - so there will be a character archetype page which is half illustration and half text, flowing pretty loosely as a unified whole. The steampunk cowboy game has an entire fetish-y page of art showing you how various guns work, how to fan the hammer, how to reload a Colt dragoon. My impression having pawed through maybe a dozen books over time is that they are well designed and cleanly laid out for the Japanese consumer, who obviously has different expectations about density and flow.

    The funniest part about being able to read the squiggles and not the kanji is the ability to sound like you know what you're reading to someone when you don't.

    Quick lesson - the squiggles are phonetic, the more complicated squiggles [kanji] are symbolic (and are taken from the Chinese language) and have inherent meaning.

    However, just because you put the symbol meaning "hand" and the symbol meaning "paper" together won't mean that you'll get the same meaning in both languages. If I'm correct, you have the difference between "letter" [I wrote my sister a letter ...] and "toilet paper" [which she used as toilet paper].

    Posted By: Jason Morningstar
    Topical supplements. So there's this game, I don't even know what it is called, and you can buy a supplement for it that is about weddings. WEDDINGS.
    Yep, that's the same as the Sandwich game, the dungeon/light kingdom-making game "Meikyuu Kingdom" (Meikyuu = Labrynthe, but they also call it "Make You! Kingdom". Together you build up a kingdom not dissimilar to ARIA, but actually playable).

    As someone was asking about the cover:


    Tea and Sandwiches!

    Also, here's the wedding supplement:


    And mind you, Mark's totally right: I tend to go out there and buy the cool stuff, the new hotness, and the slightly out there stuff (like the Maid RPG, or the love/comedy game that uses Call of Cthulhu rules including Sanity checks). I pass on about half the games I see, and the ones I tend to pass on are:

    * Traditional shlock fantasy with no edge, or without some cool art or something at least.
    * Games which remind me of D&D or GURPS. "Rokumon Sekai" is a mid-tier (in terms of popularity) fantasy RPG about dark fantasy and magic, but the book reads like a brick to the face: Pages and pages of tiny-font stats and skills and magic, for 300-400 pages.
    * Games which simply have too much (boring) text and not enough pizazz, either from layout or art. GEHENNA, for example, is a fantasy arabian-themed game, with some pretty art panels in front but little that's catchy in the middle. So I'll flip through the rules, going "Hmm. Stats. Skills. Magic. OK, so combat works that way, I see. Obligatory huge background setting section. Anything unique here? Not really? OK, NEXT!". Stuff like Sword World, Ghost Hunter, GUNDAM, and a couple dozen more. They put me to sleep faster than CNN.
    * So, I've got a lot of J-RPGs. Probably at least 40-50% of all RPGs that have been officially released in Japan since 2000 (and I've flipped through pretty much every game that's been released). Even saying that, the market's not huge, so that amounts to me owning like maybe 25-35 games or so. But the ones I don't have I've flipped through, and just couldn't see any huge pull. I'm sure they're fun if you're in the right mood, but they're not worth getting into as an outsider, because there's nothing Cool to extract from the game (in terms of rules, methodology or the way the book works).
    * A lot of the games I get are from a few companies, like FEAR or the ROLE&ROLL team and the like. Other companies' works I'm more wary of until I can give it a proper thumb-through.
    The games seem geared toward fast entry with character archetypes that are clear and fun-first.
    Very true for FEAR's games (Tenra Bansho, Alshard) that Meikyuu Kingdom game, etc. But yeah, there's plenty of point-buy build-from-scratch games out there, too. Unfortunately, I'm not too interested in those overall.

    Clinton: Yeah, that's ALSHARD (or the new edition: "ALSHARD ff" (aka "fortissimo")). Basically Final Fantasy made into an easy-to-play cool as beans RPG.

    The Yuuyake-Koyake one ("Sunset Animal Shed") looks wicked-sick, and I'm aiming to pick it up. I love the map, and it seems they use the map like John Harper's Stranger Things cards, to get you involved in the town (all the characters love the town they live in, its on their sheet):
    http://www.sunsetgames.co.jp/rpg/youyake/map800.jpg
    Posted By: bankuei...That aside, I'm also intensely curious about the Shin Megami Tensai Nocturne tabletop version, if only because I loved the PS2 game so much.
    Yeah, unfortunately it's not all that exciting (for me anyway). It's as you would probably imagine (and it's been around since the late 80s early 90s in some form or another, based off of the original SNES games): About 400 solid, hearty pages of micro-fine laundry lists of monsters and monster abilities, and some setting background. But it basically focuses on the combat and monster-making to the nth degree, and doesn't really focus on anything else (like "How to emulate the games successfully", or any creative character sheet trickery, etc).
    Posted By: komradebobHow does the illustration/comic count stack up againts mainstream american products? How about color illustration vs. b/w?
    Well, simply put Japanese art is cheaper to come by (a friend's sister or cousin is bound to be good at drawing manga, so it's cheap and easy to recruit). Also, some of the games use a lot of stylized art from a few artists to keep a single motif in place, and again when pumping out manga style art, they seem to be able to do it fast and cheap.

    As for difference in length of rules and the like, I'd say it's almost comperable to most American games.

Tres interesting.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarMy impression having pawed through maybe a dozen books over time is that they are well designed and cleanly laid out for the Japanese consumer, who obviously has different expectations about density and flow.
    That's what guessed from looking at the rule booklet in the Japanese version of settlers of Catan! It felt more like a computer game manual, with photographs of play situations that were arranged like screenshots.

    What I find more interesting (and I can't believe that neither you, Andy, nor Clinton mentioned this yet) is that the "Sandwich & Wedding" game Meikyu Kingdom is obviously aimed at an audience that is used to playing CCGs.
    Posted By: Jason MorningstarOrganizing visual information - they seem to like boxes rather than lists. Every spell, every skill, every everything is in a discrete box, nine or twelve to a page. A pattern repeated across many books. It looks pretty good.
    It is right that the book is riddled with text boxes, but all of those take the distinct form of a playing card. Character classes, skills, followers, equipment, everything is a card. It's not that they chose this format for familiarity reasons. There's a page that suggests photocopying all the cards and putting them in card sleeves. (No, I don't read a word of Japanese, but there are photographs of those items in use.)
    The GM also uses cards - monsters, traps, room functions and furniture - all of which get mapped over to the 9-Room Dungeon Map Andy mentioned. (It would be interesting to know whether the GM has to pick the cards at random.)


    Bouken has also published a little (non-collectable) card game in the setting of the role playing game: "Meikyu Kingdom Card Game -Meikyu Conquest-"
    It is a game in the vein of the Groo card game, or a hilarious version of Citadels, in which you battle monsters such as the dreaded "Mayonnaise King", or explore the "Stockroom of Dungeon Geek".
    A little ad copy directs the reader towards the big brother, the RPG.
    From the English rule leaflet of Meikyu ConquestThe game uses the world of "Meikyu Kingdom", which is a fantasy role playing game by Adventure Planning Service. This world suffered from a desaster called Dungeon Hazard, that changed the sky, the ground, even the sea into dungeons. There are countless small kingdoms divided by walls, passages, traps, and monsters. Players become "Landmakers" who rule the kingdoms and explore dungeons to grow their lands.
    There are also two claims to what the RPG is about:
    Cynical Pop Dungeon Theater
    and
    theme: Dungeon Management RPG
    The closing pages of the RPG book contain campaign themes or smaller settings inside the dungeon world. Two examples are the "Metro Khan" world (that looks like "what if Ghengis Khan controlled a network of steam trains") and the "Capitalistic Holy Republic of Hagima" (CEOs ...with swords?).

    For me, this one RPG alone was a huge eye-opener. Even if I can't read what the exact procedures might be (this is a 2d6-system, right?) looking at all those graphics and diagrams got my creative juices flowing. (What is this Agon-like battle board used for - mass combat? Why has the GM a bowl of black Go pieces?)

    For instance: Just by looking at the card format I began toying with the idea of arranging them in 9 pocket sheets in lieu of a character sheet. I guess the same could be done with the 9-Room-Dungeon layout.

    I cannot fathom what presentation, layout, or marketing secrets other Japanese games might contain. (On the other hand - looking at the Gear Anique RPG gave me no inspiration whatsoever...)

    Dirk
