\n I'm toying with an idea of using setting relevant scales to define characters.
Both ends of each scale should have some mechanical advantage, even if it just interacting with characters of a similar scale ("Birds of a feather...")

I'm looking for your thoughts in general about the idea of scales for character definition.
I'm looking for your thoughts in specific about the scales I used for the example of Steam Punk. These thoughts can be about missing or alternate scales, better names, etc.

Here is a draft example using Steam Punk.
Scale ("Range")
Class (Low <-> High)
Science! (Applied/Engineering <-> Theoretical/Mad Science)
Reputation (Respectable <-> Scandalous)
Development (Physical <-> Mental)
Profession (Practical <-> Creative)
Ethics (Criminal <-> Religious)
Wild Gift (Psychic <-> Magic)

Thanks!

    \n I like the idea, but I foresee certain problems with it, that (at this point) I'm not quite sure how to handle.

    Primarily, scales like you've defined have the problem of creating false dichotomies that don't exist in real life. For example, why can't you have a highly religious criminal? Objective <-> subjective might work better for ethics. Why can't one's development have been physical and mental, and where does emotional development lie on that scale? Does everyone have some sort of wild gift? Your scale implies that no one can be without one.

    In a way, it's D&D alignment all over again. Using scales like this means it will be impossible to define certain types of characters. However, depending on the fiction of your world, that might not be a bad things: a common argument supporting D&D alignment systems is that the D&D world just works like that, with absolutes of good and evil, and very little in the way of grey areas. You could apply a similar concept to your setting here, though many players may resist that restriction.

    \n Excellent thoughts, thank you.

    I had imagined something like Ethics (Religious 3 / Criminal 2), Development (Physical 4 / Mental 1), etc. where all the scales were blurred in someway. This feels right for a number of them (Development - no you can't be both physically and mentally perfect) but for some of the false dichotomies like you called out, it may be problematic.

    As you also say, part of it is tied into setting. Or put differently, setting the scales defines the setting - if Crime and Religion are opposed, then by definition you can't have "a highly religious criminal".

    You also called out the two scales I'm struggling the most with. Wild Gift and Ethics. I like your suggestion about Objective vs. Subjective. It gets to what I was after, I think, and parrallels nicely with the Science and Professional practice vs. theory scales. "It's fine fer you in your fancy house to talk about theft being wrong, but fer me, snagging the occassional pocket book is how I feeds me family."

    Thanks again for your thoughts. Any others?

    \n Tim -

    I hadn't really settled on every character having every scale, though I had vaguely assumed they would.

    The Science! rating was the first I came up with, an attempt to set the sliding scale between the everyday Engineer - if it exists he can fix it - and the academic Scientist - he can imagine how nearly anything could work, but don't ask him to make it work. Something that runs the gamut from Engineer to Inventor to Scientist. But maybe the name of the scale doesn't work....

    The Wild Gift is another case, I was thinking about changing the name to "Potential" - to reflect the optional nature. Everyone has some (at least) mild inclination toward one or the other, but not everyone acts on it.

    \n I agree with Mike. The mad scientist is the guy who doesn't finish working out the theory on paper before he's plugging big honking cables into lightning rods and throwing switches with a mad gleam in his eye.

    I like the engineer/inventor dichotomy, but in a Victoriana setting, that's not a realistic division. We're talking about an era wherein science as we know it was barely defined (and the term not really yet used; depending on your exact moment "natural philosopher" is more likely than "scientist") and wherein almost all discovery came from practical experimentation. Watt didn't improve the Newcomben engine by working it out on paper: he just built a better engine (he claimed to have done all sorts of calculations and observations first, but there isn't a lot of evidence to support the claim).

    Using these scales, could you arrange for there to be a cross-sectional nature to them? If you have Engineer/Inventor on one end and Natural Philosopher on the other, you have a "practical/theoretical" dichotomy, which is mirrored in your Ethics and Profession scales. Can you apply that same dichotomy to Class or Wild Gift?
