    \n I took a glance through the document today. There's a lot of good stuff in here, and some things which might need ironing out. (I think your general insight concerning RPG game design which sparked this is entirely correct, by the way.)

    The text lacks orientation a little bit, which I noted in a comment within the document - that wouldn't be hard to fix. It's not easy to get a sense of gameplay by reading the document, which is likely losing you readers. There's a clear sense of enthusiasm, which is nice, but it reads like someone talking about something they're already familiar with instead of starting from the beginning.

    How many other collaborative storytelling RPGs are you familiar with? There are a few elements of your design which suggest to me that you might benefit from looking at what else exists in the field. (If I'm entirely wrong, I apologize; but that's the sense I get from the text.)

    For instance:

    Is rolling dice to determine whether "a character can do something difficult" really the most interesting prompt for storytelling? How do we decide if something is difficult? If we roll a low number, did the "difficult" thing actually become easy? (For instance, if I roll a "1" for the Opposition, then success will be automatic, won't it? If so, haven't I just totally undermined a potentially dramatic moment in the story?)

    Why is the distinction between Active and Passive opposition necessary? What does it add to the game? (Mathematically they seem almost identical.)

    I agree with DeReel's comment about "characters being dropped in a vacuum": the Frames are a great tool to describe the characters, but then we don't get any orientation about what situation they find themselves in, how they might relate to each other, and so forth. That means that we have to come up with all that stuff on the spot, when we start a scene (and, without it, how do we even decide which characters are present in the scene, in the first place?), and therefore we might fall prey to "terrible cliches" like everyone meeting in a tavern.

    There are lots of interesting ideas here, and I think it's well worth polishing this game up and taking it to the next step. My guess is that looking at some other existing games in the genre might inspire you a great deal.

    \n I don't have a great deal of free time just now to go into details, tapgiles, but here's the short version:

    1. There are a LOT of games out there which have tackled these design problems in variant ways. Do some research! There are good articles, free games, and videos, podcasts, and panel discussions which have great info. People on this forum are generally really knowledgeable and helpful, too.

    A really easy and accessible example is the Tabletop Fiasco episode, which is a YouTube video. It's a very freeform game, but you can see a good story in action with very little die-rolling (and none at all to resolve challenges to the characters in a random fashion).

    A few other really interesting games to explore might include Imagine, The Quiet Year, Archipelago, and While the World Ends.

    Two games I would *really* encourage you to check out would be Dog Eat Dog and Fall of Magic.

    The former uses a clever and simple system of tokens and "rules" to create a powerful dynamic tension between the players and characters. (Don't be thrown off by the "conflict resolution" rules in the game; they rarely come into play - it's the Rules which actually drive the story and determine the outcomes.) The latter uses a visual map and vague prompts to create a story (without die-rolling for success and failure at all) in a very powerful way, too.

    2. Traditional RPGs were all basically formed in the "adventure game" vein, where a group of skilled "adventurers" are put up against challenges they must overcome.

    For this reason (and others), the game rules were formed around characters using their abilities to overcome challenges, which usually involved some balance of player cleverness and die-rolling/application of character abilities.

    However, if you think about it, "a group of characters applying their abilities to overcome physical (generally) challenges" is a terrible fit for most kinds of storytelling.

    Your game has some great insights into storytelling, like characters being based on formative experiences and relationships, and their capabilities changing based on how things go for them at important moments in the story, but also seems to be heavily influenced by D&D in other ways. (Do those formative experiences have to translate directly into success rates at physical challenges, for instance? Maybe they do; but do consider other options, too.)

    3. In terms of your text conveying gameplay better, I'd recommend you keep reaching out to people (perhaps just with short chunks) and have them summarize what they're reading back to you. Repeat until you're sure you're getting the right message across.

    Your writing is good, but it feels a little like you're presenting the information in the wrong order a little bit (as you can see by several readers now being confused by various aspects of the larger picture).

    I'll send you a private message with something, as well.

    I think your game has serious promise! Don't overlook that despite what seems like a lot of criticism. I wouldn't be writing if I didn't see a potential gem here.

    \n On the Veil:

    I haven't played the game, and it sounds a bit mixed-up in various ways. I'm not necessarily recommending it as a model, as a result of that. However, if a game does something similar to what you're going for, you should check it out - you'll either learn what to do or you'll learn what NOT to do, and both are equally useful.

    On unpredictable vs. random:

    DeReel puts it nicely, above. Another example is blind bidding. A third example is card selection in my game Musette (which you now have) - there is no randomness there (at least potentially; depending on whether anyone draws from the deck), but no one knows what the outcome will be.

    Having the rules simply dictate which player decides, and then giving players complex or hidden motives to do so, is another useful technique.

    Personally, my "sweet spot" lies in a space where players get a lot of input into what *possible* outcomes there are, and then a bit of randomness injects a touch of unpredictability. Here's an example of a simple illustration of that idea:

    Burned at the Stake (rule add-on/mini-system)

    When it comes to a "story game", what's the purpose of one or the other?

    Unpredictability is good if you want the story to be surprising to everyone. It's bad if you want to be able to craft something carefully from start to finish (e.g. foreshadowing a future event throughout play).

    Randomness can serve this well in some contexts. For instance, if your game IS about success and failure in challenging contexts, and you want the outcomes to be unpredictable, randomness is great.

    However, note that you do NOT use randomness in the way Frames shift and change as a result of success and failure. Here, you use a predictable rule, instead - e.g. success increases your Frame (if I remember correctly). That's because you want the game's rules to take a definite stand on this issue: you want things to go a certain way reliably, because it creates a certain dynamic and underlines a certain theme.

    For comparison, in a typical "adventure game", success and failure is often a question of luck: are the dice good to you tonight? That creates a nice feeling, like gambling. In my ruleset (linked above), there is still luck involved, but less so - you get to decide, to a large extent, what your odds will be like. Instead, the game asks, "How much is this worth to you? How much are you willing to put on the line to have a good shot at success here?"

    A good example of a long-time debate over randomness and unpredictability is character death in RPGs. Do we allow it? Do we make it unpredictable? Random? Both?

    In old-school D&D, character death is highly random and unpredictable. This is reflected in both the rules (e.g. hit points) and other aspects of design (like adventure design with lots of deathtraps).

    In some "story games", we remove unpredictability, and just say, "the characters can't die". Often, that's the right call for those games.

    Other games find interesting compromises or halfway positions. For instance, in the ruleset I linked to, you cannot control when you die; but you DO control when you *might* die. This means that death will be unpredictable, to a large extent, but will never happen at an unwelcome moment in the game - we can always choose whether to put it on the table or take it off the table, as it suits our story.

    1. Roll a number
    2. Think of an event that caused you to change in such a way that you would perform well/badly in a certain frame of mind.
    3. Choose what the frame of mind is.

    1. Roll a number
    2. Think of an event from your past that had a positive/negative (based on the number) impact on your development
    3. Choose a frame of mind that reflects the event

    1. Think of an important event from your past.
    2. Roll a number.
    3. Choose a frame of mind that reflects the event and the number.

    \n Yeah, Jeff has the right idea here. It's not that randomness is a particular issue - we just got into a little side conversation about it here somewhat arbitrarily. It's that your design seems to be ABOUT a very specific kind of thing, and then suddenly we get to rolling dice and we're talking about task-resolution challenges resolved via die-rolling... to many of us it sounds a bit like the designer had all these great ideas and then suddenly gave up when he got to the next part of the design. ("Eh, let's just throw in some D&D-esque die roll here." "That's my new dish! I want to be totally different from European cuisine. Uh, but I guess I'll put cheese all over it, too.")

    But, since you asked, here's another example of something you're familiar with which can benefit from being non-random (and yet remain unpredictable):

    The GM puts together a dungeon, and lists a few hidden items in various corners. Perhaps there's a desk, and underneath it someone has taped on an envelope holding an important letter... that kind of stuff.

    Option 1: When you enter a new room in the dungeon, roll your Search skill. On a 10 or better, the GM tells you about anything hidden in the room.

    Option 2: While exploring the dungeon, you have a certain amount of time. During that time, feel free to use any methods you can think of to search the dungeon, and tell the GM exactly what you're doing and where you're looking.

    If you choose a method which would be likely to work, and search in a place with a hidden item, the GM tells you about the hidden item you find. (For instance, saying, "I bend over and look underneath the desk," would result in the GM telling you about the hidden letter.)

    You can see that the second process does not use randomness. So, how is it different from the first one? What does it say about the game? What player skills does it involve? What approaches to play does it reward and which approaches does it punish? How might the game feel different if you choose Option 1 over Option 2?

    That kind of thing is, in my opinion, what a game designer should be doing.

    Personally, I think the process (whether it's die-rolling or something else) needs to be related to the Frames. Perhaps you roll dice to overcome your own Frames, or to use them in situations where they don't fit? Something like that, something which engages with the theme of character growth.

    Otherwise your game may be just fine, but it threatens to look a bit like Option 1, above. I like designs where rolling the dice or otherwise consulting game procedures leads us to question, reinforce, or overcome the themes of the game, so we're consciously *engaging* with the source material and the desired shape of the game to come.

    The technique I'm referencing *does* benefit from sort of impartial "referee" in order to function, but that can be done in a number of ways. Just off the top of my head:

    * Players rotate through various functions as the game progresses. The game is set up so that these roles are correctly motivated; i.e. frees you up to be an impartial referee when that's your job, without "crossing wires" with the things you do in the rest of the game.

    * The secret information (in this case, the location of hidden items) is recorded earlier in some form (like an adventure module). As the players play, they can access this record to verify whether their attempts locate anything or not. Or perhaps we have a set of cards, folded in half, with the secret information on them, and our job is to place them somewhere on the table, so only one player knows WHERE a thing is and no one knows WHAT the thing is at all.

    * The secret information is generated in play, by the players, and then hidden. For instance, when we invent a new space, one player is designated to roll a die, and, on a 3 or higher, she writes the name of a secret item and places it in an envelope. Given the right conditions, later in the game, we might get to open that envelope.

    * With newer technology, who knows what we could do. Perhaps we use geolocation to key secret information, so that moving around with your phone allows you to access the information. Or perhaps we harness a whole crowd of "online players" (like in a chatroom) who are fans of the game, and we must ask THEM to reveal to us when we search in the right location.

    But, anyway, that was just for illustration; I'm not actually suggesting any of these would be good designs.

    But consider, for instance, how different your game would be if each character had pools of tokens, attached to each Frame, and during a challenge/contest, he or she had to blindly bid a number of them. (As does his or her opponent, presumably.)

    Maybe that would be a better game; maybe it would be a poorer game. I don't know - the point is that it's an element of the design to consider.

    My advice at this point:

    Keep the d6+add format of the Frames, if it works for you. That's not so much the issue here. The first issue is to decide under what circumstances you want players to be rolling that die. That's going to say some major things about your game. When do we roll dice? Who decides when those conditions are met? What do we do the rest of the time, when those conditions aren't met?

    That, I think, will be a really significant point upon which the strength of your design will rest.

      I see it as better not having the game tell you if you're smart. In Prompt, the character is as smart as you, as observant as you, as persuasive as you. This means you don't have to wait for a mechanic to let you know you can do something mentally.

      I can see how you could twist those to work as metaphors. But it would be unintuitive in play. It's much easier to pick between "Generous" "Suspicious" and "Heroic" for a given situation than between "Tractor" "Elephant" and "Crossbeam." XD

      It's a lot easier to start with a random word and make up an event and frame of mind for it. But that's now how life works, and so that's not how the game works. If someone's asked: "What pivotal event changed you forever? What belief did it instill in you? What's going on in your head when you're moved to action because of that belief? What word best encompasses that frame of mind?"... I'm not sure how they'd end up with the word "Tractor," or "Climbing." ;D

      Also, these words will be used to generate story elements and narrative. If you end up with weird Frame words, it can be very difficult trying to spin them into something that makes sense within the story. But this is specifically so that it relates back to the character, poking at their beliefs and prompting growth and change. If you interpret "Climbing" as an ambitious NPC, that has nothing to do with the PC's view of that word--about climbing a mountain and the death of his friend, and so on.

      Yes, the Frames should be conscious. You can't belief in something unless you're aware you believe it. ^^ This is also because the character will be reflecting on what's happened recently, and taking another look at their beliefs and how strong their conviction in them is. They can't reason on their beliefs if they don't know they're there.

      I guess that's one way of looking at it, with the "safe zone." Another way of looking at it is that if at any point the rules are unclear--Which Frame should I roll? What does it mean, to get +1 in this Frame?--the flow of the game is messed with, and the roleplaying is stunted as a result. So in my mind, it's simply "good game design." (Not saying I have enough experience to call my game that, by I made these design choices to strive for good game design.)
