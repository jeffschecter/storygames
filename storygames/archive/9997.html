\nRooksbridge.com is live.

So, of course, your first question is: what does that mean? What is Rooksbridge, anyway?

Rooksbridge.com is my new serial fiction project - every month, I am publishing a new short story that serves as the next chapter in the lives of people caught up in a fantastic and dangerous world. It's a world of knights and sorcerers, nobles and craftsmen, where the spirits speak and swords sing. It's a world of treacherous pitfalls, savage wonders, and desperate choices. In other words, a world full to the brim with stories.

The first chapbook, Dirty Work, is available as a free download so you can get an idea of what this project is all about.

At rooksbridge.com, you can get Dirty Work and the second chapbook, Getting By, as a pocket-sized chapbook, an mp3 audiobook, or a digital edition. Each is only a couple bucks. You can also subscribe to get updates on new chapbooks via RSS feed, an email newsletter, or through the preview podcast. And there is, of course, a forum for those who like to indulge in speculation about where things are going and who's doing what (and who).

I am incredibly excited about this project, and have promised myself and my family that I will complete the 'trial run' of publishing these chapbooks for a full year. So there will be at least twelve chapbooks, and more if this crazy idea takes off. It is my hope that Rooksbridge will generate enough interest to shoulder a chunk of our living expenses \u2014 in other words, this is my bid to become a full-time, "professional," "for real" author. Yeah, it's kind of crazy.

By now you are, no doubt, bursting with excitement and wondering how you can help me fulfill my dreams, preferably in a way that requires little on your part. The first and most obvious is to download Dirty Work, give it a read, and find out if you'd like to see more. I am aiming for micro-prices that can fly In An Economy Like This One, with next month's installment always just a couple bucks. Nice and affordable \u2014 and considering a ninety-minute movie is ten bucks, I'm pretty comfortable with my ninety-minute reads coming in under half as much \u2014 and you can share 'em when you're done.

Which brings me to the other, and perhaps more important part of this post. The absolutely no-cost-to-you help-Josh-make-this-happen option: you've got friends who like fantasy fiction? Of course you do, you're a gamer. Well, I've got a nice little half-meg file that you can email them. Send them the Dirty Work download. Tell them it's a gift from the cockles of your heart. At worst, it's a free short story they can enjoy or discard. At best, it gets me another pair of eyes on my chapbook, a pair of eyes that might wander back to rooksbridge.com to see what else I've got on offer.

Of course, you could also throw Dirty Work up onto a file sharing site. You could link to it in your blog. You could go to online fora I don't even know exist, where some of our fellow swords-and-sorcery sophisticates are hiding out, and bestow upon them the gift of Sir Sedgwin and Dame Sophia kicking ass in the Baron's name. But all I'm asking for is a couple emails, to folks you know will enjoy this story. It simply means more friend-to-friend, and it would mean a lot to me, too.

I really hope you guys enjoy Dirty Work and the rest of the Stories from Rooksbridge. I've poured a lot of time and energy into this project, and it is shaping up to be something that I'm very proud of. I'm looking forward to seeing how this project develops in later chapbooks, and I hope you'll be there to see what it grows into, as well.

I leave you with a line of links, just in case you haven't already clicked... did I mention the first one's free?!?

    I actually worked on the other side of that equation, as an editor in a publishing house, for some time. I have and will continue to submit other stuff to magazines and publishing houses and what-have-you, but at least for Rooksbridge, the pros for self-publishing a chapbook each month greatly outweigh the cons. I'd like to see what it becomes, and the 'new media format' thing is really exciting.

    \n Thanks; I'm really enjoying the project, and flexing these muscles that I haven't exercised in a long time.

    I doubt I will publish a combined volume of all the chapbooks; however, I may package up a year's worth of chapbooks in a snazzy tuck-box sort of presentation and make a boxed set. But that's way off in the future \u2014 and will serve the purpose of either bringing new readers up to speed for Year Two or clearing out my stock of print chapbooks after lack of interest kills the project. We'll see which direction things go.

    As for an RPG... there are two answers:

    1. No RPG tie-in, because nearly any Rooksbridge RPG I published would constrain and limit later Rooksbridge stories, which I don't want.

    2. The RPG tie-in is already written, and it's called Primetime Adventures. A healthy dose of how I write Rooksbridge is powerfully informed by playing and designing story games for the past couple years. I mean, I could draw out the wicked relationship map the stories use, or show how everything that happens runs on a Yes, and... / Yes, but... sort of chassis. The scene economy of Rooksbridge is very similar to the scene economy you'd find in a lot of story game titles: very lean, hard cuts, jump right to the conflict, and so on. It's one of the reasons I'm posting about my fiction here, because I have an inkling it's the kind of thing a lot of story gamers would enjoy, because it's the kind of thing they already enjoy. Which is a long-winded way of saying, "PTA rocks!"

    \n The podcast is one of my favorite bits of the Rooksbridge project, probably because I do it last. After the story's been written and laid out and sent to the printer and the audio has been recorded and edited and the sound effects laid in and the digital files are all exported, I put myself in front of my computer and get to talk as directly as possible to my audience (even if that audience is only a handful of folks so far). It's a really nice capstone. I talk about the experience of putting things together that month, what's happening with the project, play the first few minutes of the audiofile as a preview, and finally I get to say thanks for listening, which somehow means more, at least to me, than the "thanks for your support" that goes in every digital file and print copy. The podcast feels more direct than any of the other pieces; I have no idea if anybody is actually subscribed to the damn thing, but at least it feels good.
