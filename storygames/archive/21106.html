    Regarding "design takeaways" from the OSR:

    1. Minimal rules focuses gameplay. Consider "moves" from PBtA. Everything outside of those moves is handled by the fiction. The OSR is largely silent on mechanics outside of attacks and saving throws, or they are exception-based (spells, monstrous abilities, magic items).

    2. XP for treasure creates an (ingenious) feedback loop incentivizing expected gameplay. It's a brilliant departure from contemporary D&D's emphasis on quests and objective-focused gameplay.

    3. Randomizing elements and procedural generation freshens gameplay and creates the illusion of a living game world that challenges DM and players alike. Reaction rolls and morale tests are ingenious mechanics.

    \n Hello, Rafu.

    Vincent Baker himself has basically stated what I said above, iirc. The moves are a layer on top of the core mechanics. AW/pbta is essentially a conflict resolution system based on 3-dice outcomes and the (usually) 4 stats as modifiers (plus the whole thing about the GM never rolling and NPCs not using the same descriptions as characters, etc). The playbooks themselves are not a necessary core either. He said that he was surprised not more games have ditched playbooks yet (unfortunately I don't know where I read this stuff. I think it was an interview. The "core" stuff is at the Barf Forth forum somewhere).

    What the playbooks do is add "setting and feel", as you point out. The moves do the same. They codify stuff that is common to do in a particular game and makes it much easier to run than just looking at raw results and adjudicating from there. This is the reason why people come up with their own moves for stuff they find themselves doing all the time or that fleshes out their style of gameplay. I remember a nice example I found on the webs (don't remember where) where someone wanted a "piratey" move for swinging from ropes and stuff. Instead of pulling it out of their asses every time with Defy Danger, they just created a thematically appropriate move to help adjudicate results with more specific outcomes. This is basically all there is to moves in pbta.

    By the way, have you read "Simple World"? It's around the webs somewhere. Check it out. It's pbta sans playbooks or moves, iirc. Stripped to its core.

    Now obviously, if you add/change/subtract moves, then the game will play differently. That's actually what I'm thinking of doing with DW. I haven't played it yet, but although I love pbta and the combat mechanics are exactly what I want, dungeon exploration in DW seems way too facilitated by stuff like Sprout Lore, Defy Danger, and some of the classes moves. The Delver compendium class in Perilous Wilds, for example, is a fucking joke on dungeon exploration. DW also emphasises rations but doesn't care about torches/lamps and timing, which is an important element. Can you imagine running Barrowmaze without caring about illumination? Me neither. Dungeons seem to be mostly a vehicle for setting cool scenes in DW. Like Eero said in another thread, DW is a story-game with a fetish for dungeons. The exploration element is not really there, it's just scenario. Right now I'm a bit disappointed with DW. The game is called DUNGEON World, for fucks sake. I believe it's possible to combine pbta mechanics with old school dungeoneering, but I'm going to have to hack DW to my tastes. I need to keep the character centred stuff outside of dungeons but the old school element inside.

    I guess I just digressed there O_o

    Okay, XP for treasure, I get. I'm not sure how that's not "objective-focused", though (isn't "get treasure" the objective?), so if the distinction is important, could you elaborate?
    \nBest example I can think of is from Curse of Strahd. Just wrapped up GMing that campaign. The manual suggests using "milestones" for leveling up. In the module, Madam Eva (a Vistani, the setting's stand-in for gypsies) gives the party a fortune telling that hints where certain items or allies may be located. The module suggests giving out levels when the party retrieves those items or accomplishes some goal related to the fortune telling. Individual XP is not tallied.

    In retrospect, I did not like this method for several reasons. One, it pushes D&D into the mold of heroic fantasy, a place where the game is at its weakest; two, it removes the granularity from leveling and forces synchronized leveling; and three, it undermines player agency by pressing upon them a single method of advancement that discourages the players from meaningfully engaging with the setting.

    One can argue that gold-for-XP likewise discourages the players from engaging with the setting, and I would agree, but that is only because the game is focused on playing adventurers seeking their fortune. In the case of milestone leveling, the characters are theoretically given greater leeway, but the reward is externalized to the DM's whims. Complete this quest, gain a level. Follow my plot or you won't get the prize! Contrast with gold-for-XP, where the treasure pre-exists in the game world / module and the players are given the opportunity to acquire it. This shift to "milestone XP" likewise accompanies a shift in DMing style from judge / referee / impartial arbiter of the rules toward a storyteller in the vein of heroic fantasy.

    As I said, I don't feel that this style of gameplay is suited for D&D, due in part to character mortality and the strong emphasis on miniatures combat.

    This is perhaps a distinction that is mostly academic (hairsplitting is a hobby of mine) but I believe it matters.\n
    Random procedural generation, heck yeah. Well said. Reaction rolls, I haven't done it myself, but I buy it. I usually conceive of who my NPCs are from out of my own head, but letting the dice tell me certain things instead does sound like a fun change of pace. Morale tests, though? I am drawing a blank on exactly what those do, and on the best ways to use them. (It's a "run away" check, right?) Please enlighten me!
    \nMorale tests are from D&D's wargaming roots, an optional rule in B/X, and they are called for under certain circumstances. When, for instance, the NPCs suffer a casualty, or they suffer 50% casualties, they must test morale or surrender/flee. It's a great mechanic for resolving conflicts without a "fight to the death" scenario that is all too common in some games.

    Regarding minimal rules and the focus on fiction vs. mechanics: yes, you have the idea. Likewise, however, fewer mechanics emphasizes the importance of the mechanics that exist. Movement rates, lighting, bend bars, etc. in the OSR emphasize the dungeon crawl aspects of the game.

    \n Oh, Morale checks are awesome. They create more varied and interesting combat situations - suddenly someone's trying to run away, someone else is hard-headedly dogged, and someone else is trying to hide.

    It makes courage and fear a fundamental aspect of the tactical/strategic game. You have some undead monsters in your world? Or a magically enslaved warrior species? Consider how much it suddenly means just to say "You know these guys? They don't even make Morale checks."

    That's some scary &*#@!

    Also, we can now start thinking about what they mean and how we might interact with them. What can we do to make sure our allies don't have to make a Morale check before we can achieve our objective? Lead them blind-folded through the Tunnel of Scary Visions?

    If we learn that the kobolds in the caves are easily scared, but too vicious for us to take on in a fight, can we don scary masks and wave flaming swords, causing them to make a Morale check and flee instead of fighting us?

    Etc.

    \n Visibility radius is not what I meant when I inquired about emphasized lighting rules. How does that 30 foot limit (or whatever one uses) actually come up in play? What are the rules for what you can do to take advantage of that, or how you might suffer from it?

    I don't think I've ever played an RPG where lighting was a relevant rules quantity. It was fun for novice adventurers to figure this out for the first time, but worrying about it after that didn't seem rewarding.

    I did hear about one session my friends played which was all about being unable to see -- PCs trying to find each other in the woods without getting lost or accidentally attacking each other -- but I've never heard anything similar come out of a dungeon crawl.

    \n Instead of morale rolls I consistently use a less random method, that goes a bit like something Sandra mentioned on another thread. It's a simple program, like

    00-Monster turn starts
    10-Is the monster insane or irrational? If yes, fights until it dies. If no, goTo 20
    20-Is the monster below half it's HP total? If it's a group of creatures, are more than half of them unable to keep fighting?
    30-If yes, try to run away. If no, keep fighting
    40-Can the monster(s) run away? If no, maneuver, feint and fight just enough to open a path. Or surrender and try to Parley.
    50-If the monster still fighting, does it have a last resort action available? If yes, use it.

    About giving XP about other things besides killing monsters and treasure, it's a practice that makes more sense as the GM has quicker or more sensitive emphatic skills, or just that can read people better. For such GMs, the players feelings of frustration and discomfort that come from level difference between characters tend to trigger all these houserules about giving XP for quest completion, roleplaying, etc. Specially if they are used to see this ruin the fun for more than one player consistently. It makes as much sense in their minds as carrying an umbrella when it looks like it's going to rain, despite whatever the forecast said.

    David_Berg said:

    Visibility radius is not what I meant when I inquired about emphasized lighting rules. How does that 30 foot limit (or whatever one uses) actually come up in play? What are the rules for what you can do to take advantage of that, or how you might suffer from it?

    \nYou turn a corner in the dungeon. The GM tells you "the corridor continues for a distance, disappearing in the darkness." He has just utilized the illumination rules, determining that 40, 60 or however many feet is enough that the paltry light you have does not allow you to determine what lurks at the end of the corridor.

    You open a door into a room. The GM tells you "the back wall seems to be about 20 feet out, while the right-hand wall is a mere couple feet away - the door is at the front-right corner of the room. The facing fall must be somewhere in the darkness to your left, at least 50 feet away; you can see three great pillars supporting the ceiling, at regular distances from each other." When you decide to move into the room and explore further, the GM expands upon the description, telling you what you did not see from the doorway.

    You see something at the very edge of where you light carries: "There is a dark shape out there, and it is moving. Might be humanoid, but could as well be the front half of a manticore." You move curiously closer, getting to the "dim" zone: "The movement proves to be three large, silent humanoid shapes. They carry arms of some sort." As you approach, perhaps speaking diplomatically, you come to the "bright" distance: "The three are hobgoblins, muscular and savage, with Red Scorpion clan markings. They do not seem alarmed at seeing you, but they do have swords in hand, and their eyes are alert."

    Do those examples clarify anything at all? In all cases the GM references the position of the PC adventurer against the positioning of everything else before he describes what the PC sees. To gather additional information the PC needs to move about, compensating for the limited amount of light by going closer to things.

    I haven't generally found that I'd need anything more than some lightning distance abstractions/conventions, plus precise dungeon maps, to achieve the effect where a PCs subjective viewpoint and lightning condition becomes a tactical issue. You need to pay attention to distances and lightning constantly, though, or this aspect of the "simulation" soon gets laid to wayside.

    A secondary, bonus effect for illumination: the more light you have, the easier it is to notice traps and secrets. Apply condition modifiers liberally, you're unlikely to over-emphasize how very important the amount of unobstructed, even light is for noticing e.g. the blackened string trigger of a rockfall trap.

      \n In my experience the limiting factors for light in the dungeon are that high-quality lanterns are expensive (if even available), as is lamp oil in our 14th century setting, torches provide pretty limited illumination, and adventurers seem to have a great desire to have their hands free, so they mostly will not compensate by having multiple light sources. Technically speaking, if everybody had their own torch, the party would probably have just about enough light to get by; usually this is not the case, and therefore the potential for various difficulties exists.

      I do not quite know why the adventurers have not optimized the lightning issue to such a degree that it would have become rote; it may be because the circumstantial difficulties caused by lightning conditions are not formalized into a clear rules text treatment, which in turn means that the players do not quite realize how much difficulty they are causing themselves by not investing in sufficient light. Like, they don't realize that their character being uncomfortably close to the giant spider when it is spotted is not caused only by their dicing luck in the spot check, but also by the amount of light they chose to go into that room with.

      Light spells are valuable because the light is much, much stronger than what the adventurers are usually working with. Like, bonuses all around for noticing stuff, and no bullshit about having to move closer to see something clearly, or having to have your torch-bearer next to you all the time. Essentially, if a torch is "the GM is constantly making my life difficult" and a lantern is "well, this actually works pretty well", then the light spells are "yeah, this is a fantasy game, we don't sweat lightning conditions".

      As for my motivation in caring about this stuff, it's merely hygienic - the decision to simply look at the fiction and model its difficulties was already made at the starting gates of the game's process, and there has not since then been any sort of "GM considers which aspects of the fiction to ignore from now" phase in play, so I continue running the proceedings according to original specification (a robust, fluidly playing yet generally realistic tactical operations environment). The only way I would decide to stop simulating lightning conditions would be if I decided that it is out of scope for the simulation - that is, it's too unimportant to care about. This has not been the case so far, and actual practice in doing the simulation seems to make it easier, so there's less motivation for dropping it as time goes by.

      Regarding real measures for room dimensions and such, I've actually found that they make things easier over the long term. When you've got some intuitive standards for how light and sound and such behave in different spaces, you can just look at the grid map and make quick and objective snap judgements about tactical stuff. For me it's well worth it to quantify light and such in real terms instead of using relative measurements like "the room is so large you cannot see the other end from the door" as the primary geometry.

      \n David, I get your idea. Some GMs create the world first and then leave it up to rules and sim to generate the experience (like Eero) while others create the experiences and let them define the world that exists around them. I love this trick, for it saves time, it requires no prep, and allows the GM to focus on other content in the fiction.

      However starting with the effects and ignoring the causes changes the CA of the whole game. OSR CA is more about simulating real challenge, while this is more about simulating an optimal experience of the fictional world. While in play they look similar, the frame of mind makes them quite different experiences.

      Knowing that the world is "real", has precise measures that it's been there before we started playing, introduces a different set of emotions for both the GM and the players. Not knowing what's going to happen or appear until it comes to the PC's "narrative" sensorial range introduces a completely different set of emotions. Both ways are perfectly valid gameplay styles, but there's no sense nor benefit in judging one from the techniques, results, problems and advantages of the other.

      Does it make sense? If not I can try to ellaborate a bit more.

      \n I've started running my 5e game using Roll20. I have my map imported. I have "Fog of War" turned on, so I can selectively erase the mask that hides the map from the players as they explore.

      When a PC is carrying a light source, I have them adjust their icon to broadcast a round "aura" of light yellow for everyone to see. You can actually do two of them, one for bright and one for dim. Players move their own characters around the map on their turn. I reveal the map as needed for them, using the "polygon reveal" feature to expose rooms in a "line of sight" way so that sometimes there are dark areas behind walls and stuff. Occasionally, there are zombies hiding there.

      David_Berg said:

      Eero, I definitely enjoy that aspect of play in dungeons and other dangerous, unknown environments!

      Personally, though, when I'm asked, "How big is the room?" I answer, "Big enough that you need to move into it to see what's against the far wall." That's all the info we need. All these measurements in feet or meters or whatever are completely unnecessary for me. In fact, the mental effort of translating back and forth between (a) factual spatial quantity and (b) relevance to decision considerations -- I've seen and experienced that as a major drag.

      So, getting back to VSKatana's original point and my original question about it: In a game where the rules are few but important, with some of the main ones controlling advancement and death, "Let's all agree on how many feet torch light carries," doesn't make it into that class for me. So I'm wondering if they had something else in mind. "Light = 30ft" isn't really even a rule in my eyes, it's a mere description of the fiction we're already trying to model without rules. (It's not even the best tool for that purpose, at least not if you have a table full of players who understand the nuances of reflectivity and position w.r.t. light source. Bad news, adventurers: any of you who are standing behind a person with a torch, your effective forward illumination in an open space is zero feet, not thirty. In an enclosed space, different story, but still an issue.)

      Anyway, if we're playing using a system that tracks its other important quantities in feet, then maybe "light = 30ft" interacts with that in some interesting ways. But I didn't see anything about archery or flanking or backstabs or charging or whatnot in your examples, Eero.

      \nRoom sizes may or may not be important during gameplay, depending on what the group emphasises. For example, careful mapping during dungeon exploration allows you to predict where hidden rooms/locations are and if you missed some hidden door etc, so exact sizes are important for some groups. But not everyone does this. Because it has been many years since I've run anything D&D I don't know what I'd do today.

      As for lighting, in my experience everyone winged it and just went for a common sense/what-feels-right approach. But apparently Sandra and Eero do pay significant attention to it. I think that's very satisfactory, as far as someone else is GMing, not me.

      Perhaps there is some actual reason why a small band of humanoids can't bring all the light sources they need on an underground excursion, but if so, I've yet to put my finger on it.       \nFor many dungeons it makes sense. For example Barrowmaze is a megadungeon that consists in a massive series of interconnected tombs. They are for the most part totally dark and silent, meaning that not only is illumination a factor but also noise (and theres a lot of breaking down walls and excavation to do in that dungeon). There are different access points to the surface, so this plays nicely with that factor: the more access points you have the less torches you need. Here's a cool promo vid for Barrowmaze:


      The reason why you can't just "take enough torches" is that you don't know how long you'll have to stay underground. You plan it, but can you make it to the surface in time before the lights go out? What if you can't go back the way you came and have to find some other way to the surface? Plus torches may count for encumbrance. How much depends on the system. Labyrinth Lord gives weights for torches, others don't. You can also use them to attack, and you may loose them that way. So, weight and number may affect gameplay.

      \nVerbatim from AD&D: \n
      Bend bars/lift Gates states the percentage chance the character has of bending normal soft iron bars or of lifting a vertically moving gate (such as a small portcullis barring a passage). The attempt may be made but once, and if the score required is not made, the character will never succeed in the task.

      Example: A character with 16 strength is trapped in a dead end passoge by a set of iron bars which drop down from the ceiling when a stone slab is stepped on and triggers the
      release of the gate. The character first attempts to lift the gate, a roll of 01-10 indicating
      success, but the percentile dice come up 74, so failure is indicated. The character next attempts to bend the bars in order to squeeze between them, the percentage chance of
      success is the same as for lifting the gate, and this time a 07 is rolled, so the character slips out and is free.
      \nThe rules for lighting I was referencing were those given: duration and radius. Outside of that, I believe it's mostly modeled fictionally and dependent on DM judgment.
