    \n My IaWA tips
    \n
    \n1) don't make the mistake of thinking the game runs itself. You may not have any scenario prep to do...but you need to be a strong GM. IME in the absence of a strong GM the game will either flail, or one of the other players will pick up the slack and start doing the things the GM needs to do.
    \n
    \n2) Don't let players get away with selecting characters who are tangental to the conflict sources in the oracle. After you've done your brainstorming session about what characters the oracle selects, be aggressive at combining several of them together...thus tieing that one character to multiple things.
    \n
    \n3) Be ruthless when setting Best Interests for your NPCs. Target PCs for destruction and create Best Interests that will divide PCs who otherwise might become "allies by default". (i.e. its your best interest to help this guy and hurt that guy...when those guys would otherwise think they should be friends).
    \n
    \n4) Make sure you understand the Stick and what its for. Conflicts are not about resolving stuff, they're about awarding the stick...which is the power to say "accept this thing as fact or suffer damage". Make sure players understand that damage is a death spiral and in a one shot especially one that can cut your effectiveness dramatically. Think hard before you take damage before accepting the other players terms. As GM make sure the players understand that the system is about the winner making the loser squirm about the terms they're being asked to accept. I don't know if its an official GM power in the rules (I don't think it is but it should be) don't let winners of conflict be lazy with weak terms or just force injury/exhaustion.
    \n
    \n5) For a one shot...skip the We Owe List (which is pretty meaningless outside of a campaign) and just award d6s directly like hero points. Using one of those d6s out of your bonus pool is mechanically the same thing as crossing your name off the "We Owe List" but a much more tactile way of doing.
    \n
    \n
    \nI could probably think of several others. Revisiting the "Bad Habits" thread on the Lumpley forum on the Forge would be a good place to start. I'd link to it, but net nanny won't let me go to the Forge to find it.

    \n The GM has primarily 2 jobs: Frame scenes (by introducing that first scene, and after that you can get by with "Where are you right now?") and use the NPCs to put pressure on the PCs. That's the reason the GM is instructed to make NPC best interests target PCs where they are weakest.

    As a facilitator, for your own first game:
    Roll through a couple of conflicts on your own. See how it works in motion, rather than just from the text. You can do this with puppets if it helps.
    I like providing the dice, different colors for different players, and pips for advantage dice, so that you're not dragging confusion into the rolling process "which d6 is the advantage die?" "Wait, did I roll the red one or did you roll the red one?"
    Give the text another read.

    Then do what Ralph says (I haven't tried awarding tokens instead of the Owe List, but I like it in principle).

    \n The Forge has 503ed all day anyway, Ralph--you couldn't have linked (I just tried).

    Ralph forgot a couple of BIG ones (at least in terms of frequency--these would be in the FAQ):
    Do not set stakes - Folks who came from DitV and other games to IAWA spun their wheels a LOT with "stake setting." And Vincent, rightly, asked, "why? It's not in the rules to do that at all!" Say what you DO that the other guy responds "Oh, no you fucking don't, asshole!" and BAM roll for the first round, the winner stating advantage, etc. Pay particular attention tot he sample conflicts:they range over space (and time!) and might start in a struggle over one thing only to end with an entirely different statement of terms by the stick-holder.

    A small codicil to this is don't state results, state attempts. Sure, most people do a sort of unconscious, automatic retcon if their "I smash you in the mouth" is challenged and they lose (the winner might narrate," You swing, but I duck and now you're off-balance"). But it's best to initiate a conflict with attempting something, not stating it as fait accomplis.

    There are no talking conflicts - Your guy can say all he wants, and he SAYS it: done. The other guy might smash you in the mouth (and you might say ONYFDA!), but there are no "I convince him I'm right" statements of intent. At BEST, you can beat his ass down, end up with the stick, and make him SAY, "OK, uncle, uncle! You're right"... but that has not one whit of factuality, nor does it have one whit of persuasive appeal to him. You've forced him to terms which make him say something, not BELIEVE it.
    -----
    I'll add another one that might not be "canon," but it's sort of in between the lines of the RAW:

    Match the scopes of Particular Strengths - Now, sure, it shouldn't MUCH matter if someones PS scope (i.e. efficacy in a variety of situations) is broader than the other... but it can make for some "huh... that's kind of bogus" feelings at some tables if there is TOO great a disparity. For instance, "Awesome Swordsman" is no where NEAR as useful in a variety of conflicts as "Agile as a chimp" or similar. If things get TOTALLY confused, then just Say Yes--sure, use that PS if you can give me even a faint notion of how it would apply to the emerging conflict. it is better if everyone's PS is in every conflict than if one guy's is in every conflict while others struggle to shape the narrative to suit their PSes or never get to instantiate their dice.

    But, you know... play that one by ear and be receptive to the cues your players give you. I've heard "forget about PSes" before, too (but that's pretty much the "sorcery" to its "sword," if you're going all Hyborian with it!).

    HTH

    \n Good point on the Stakes David. I hinted at that with Conflict don't resolve stuff, but you hit it better. I'd expand Ryan's list of GM's tasks to include keeping an ear out for when a player says "No you didn't" even when they actually didn't SAY "no you didn't".
    \n
    \nand #6) I think this is important for a one shot. I don't know how canonical it is, but I'd tend to encourage enforceable agreements. Otherwise you run the risk of a player agreeing to terms like "ok I'll help you recover your throne" and then later skipping out on them. In a campaign this is less of an issue because a) you have a group who's playing together for a long time and b) it'll come back to haunt you later, but for a one shot it can feel really weak to win a dice off and then have the other player just reneg on whatever they'd agreed to do. I can think of situations where that wouldn't feel totally weak...but I can think of more where it would. So my advice if such should come up...allow retro active Injury or Exhaustion.
    \n
    \nFor instance. "Agree to help me assassinate Thibauline, or I'll hit you with the stick"..."Ok"...<later> "now we will go after Thibauline"..."nah, I don't really feel like it"...<Bam, then you're getting hit with the stick>
    \n
    \nIn otherwords, I recommend allowing the threat of the stick to be held over the loser's head indefinitely (until you feel like the intended purpose in the fiction has been fulfilled) rather than letting players get out of injury by agreeing to something they have no intention of making good on.
    \n
    \n
    \nAs far as Particular Strengths. You won't see (probably) how amazingly awesome Particular Strengths are in a one shot, but they are fantastic in campaigns where they basically become a deck of created setting elements. When "The Poisoner's Art" becomes a Particular Strength that is available for anyone to take when its not being used...it establishes something in your world. We had one "Devouring Hunger" which returned repeatedly and became a common trait for demonic characters to possess. We had another one "Loyalty"...which since it was given the "unique" tag really established something about the world...and the character who took it. In a campaign when they start to level up, they get really cool.

    \n I actually find the We Owe List doesn't really work (i.e. do anything exciting) for 1 shots even if you're planning on fitting 2-3 Chapters into that slot.
    \n
    \nReason:
    \n
    \nThe character who comes back next is the character at the top of the list. This will just happen to be whichever player happened to meet the criteria first...which to me has no meaning of significance.
    \n
    \nAfter you've got 3-4 chapters under your belt and the list has been seeded with many names from different chapters and different players, and names have been crossed off and players are strategicly choosing which of their characters names to cross off...and this has been happening multiple times...THEN you have a meaningful list. One that actually reflects the aggregation of the players' burgeoning understanding of which characters they REALLY care about seeing come back. The first few chapters are just seeding the list with names...there's no Wow factor there...it might as well be random for all the meaning it has. So if you're only playing the first 2-3 chapters...you're never going to see the real power of the Owe list even if you maintain it.
    \n
    \nIf this is going to be the first of several such one shots that will periodically be revisiting the same characters...then sure...but then its really not a one shot. If there is little chance of ever seeing Chapters 4-6 then there's little chance of seeing anything exciting happen on the Owe list that's worth the effort and confusion of maintaining it IMO.
    \n
    \nFurther, if you are playing with a new group of players...the degree of them grokking the point of the Owe List and how important it is to be the first person on it...probably less than playing with veterans. So the first player to make the list will likely just be random coincidence anyway.
    \n
    \n
    \nIf you are going to play multiple Chapters, I'd just go with the simpler system of whoever has the most bonus d6s in their pool. This would be the equivalent of the character that got added to the Owe list the most times without crossing their name off...rather than the character that got added to the Owe list first without crossing their name off. Which for a one shot is, IMO alot more meaningful of a measure. Because it means that players can meaningfully shoot for being that return character starting 1/2 way through play when they finally twig to what's happening...rather than being "stuck" behind whoever coincidentally was first, hoping they'll cross themselves off.
    \n
    \nIf there are a couple of players ahead of you on the list, and its clear they aren't likely to cross themselves off...then being on the list is pointless except as a bonus in a one shot (even of multiple chapters)...so for a one-shot...I recommend saving yourself the trouble and just treating it like a bonus.

    \n Well the philosophy behind the We Owe list is that you're creating an anthology of related stories and that certain characters will pass through those stories like a common thread while others are seen once and never seen again.
    \n
    \nOne of the abstract conceits behind the We Owe list is that characters who regularly win all of their conflicts are assumed to largely have their story be completed and done. That chapter becomes sort of their "spot light" chapter and there's nothing much more of interest to know about them. Similarly, characters struggling with signficant adversity still have a story to tell (unless, the mechanics suggest, they get so blown out by that adversity they aren't suited to having a story worth telling).
    \n
    \nAll of that is the abstract thought behind why the We Owe list was set up the way it was. In theory then, the list will get populated by characters facing signficant challenge (tougher opponents) who survived that challenge to fight another day (lasted past the first round).
    \n
    \nMaking the decision to take the bonus die or not is thus the choice between "this character still has future stories to tell worth telling" (not taking it) vs. "THIS is the chapter that is the climax of the character's story" (taking it, and forgoing future story presence).
    \n
    \nThis situation doesn't really start to gell, however, until you've gone through a few iterations with it. Once you, David, have several different characters on the We Owe list you start to have meaningful choices to make. "Alfredo" is nearing the top of the list and you'll have a chance to play him again. But "Burgiss" is in trouble right now. Choosing or not choosing to cross off Alfredo to give Burgiss a die is you choosing whose story you're more interested in -- Burgiss's story right now or Alfredo's story in the future.
    \n
    \nNow, muddying the waters a bit is the allowance that you can bring back a character even if they're not on the We Owe List as long as they fit one of the character concepts in the Oracle. That does IMO lessen the true power of the We Owe List but is consistant with the Vincent Baker school of design that says "give your players the ability to do what feels right to them". And in practice, it works. We used it a couple of times. However, it means your character is altered by the Oracle.
    \n
    \nWhen you're at the top of the We Owe List...you're coming back...as your own character...unchanged except as you want to change them. When you point to an oracle that reads "A wizened old man seeking bloody vengeance" and say "Hey, that's Alfredo"...then you've allowed the oracle to change your character. If he wasn't a wizened old man before, he is now...and whatever that vengeance is, its on.
    \n
    \nThere were a number of times we used this in our campaign, but always the character was radically impacted. The one I remember most is when I pointed to "The ghost of a man murdered by his own daughter" and said "that's Zahir". Up to that point Zahir had been my main reoccuring character, but due to a fairly climactic previous Chapter he was no longer on the Owe list, even though his presence in this Chapter made sense from the organically emerging story arc. In that selection we discovered, Zahir had a daughter...and had died. In the process of establishing those events we decided that Zahir had finally married the woman he'd been pursuing the entire story...one of the other characters...who WAS the We Owe character in this chapter, IIRC. So here, the single most important event in Zahir's life...the culmination of what he'd been wanting since the very first Chapter...happened...but it happened off stage. Clearly indicating Zahir was not the protaganist of this story.
    \n
    \nThat's one of IaWA's most endearing features, IMO. Once we got into it we started speculating on which of the recurring cast of characters would wind up being the true protagonist of the story. Regularly we were surprised when their stories took a turn in a different direction.
    \n
    \nSo ANY character CAN come back...but if you're playing right IMO, there will be certain characters that clearly "need to" and others that clearly don't. And as a group I would recommend ample use of peer pressure to assure that the right choices get made. And I believe (may be wrong) that RAW the GM has final authority over approving whether any potential oracle character can be claimed to be a PC or not. That's certainly the way I'd play it if its not.

    Posted By: JDCorleyI guess I don't really know why you would run IAWA as a one-chapter game.
    I've done this at least a dozen times with consistent success and great fun for all. I find most of the time, we didn't need "incentive to fail" to make the play interesting - players just got into the flow of the game and then got inside their characters' heads. Scenes happened, and the dice chosen proceeded from the details of the scene and the actions. The Oracle gives you focus and the best interests provide trajectory, so the GM can just frame 3 kinds of scenes and use NPCs to push PCs, and *bam!*, short story happens. Certainly I found it much easier and more satisfying than the oneshots I've run and played using other systems (i.e. Universalis, PTA, Red Box Hack-hack, D&D red box, D&D 3e, It's complicated, etc).

    I can think of only one reason not to have a PS: to emphasize that the character is Everyman (or to make it a mook, if it's an NPC). Any Big Player PC (or Big Threat PC/NPC) is going to have a PS, if only to have mechanical efficacy in conflicts (i.e. Sauron doesn't get bitch-slapped).

    But, as always, efficacy isn't the end all be all, here. I see the Everyman character as (a) all-but-living on the We Owe list, (b) a thematic position of accepting the whims of fate (and others), and (c) ultimately a very sympathetic character (OOC), whose player might adroitly manipulate the story though sheer patheticness (yes, that word's under debate--DON'T HIT ME, MASSAH!).
