    Posted By: Thunder_GodGood review. Let me ask a simple question, if facing only one player, why doesn't the Company just pit everything it has, likely rendering that one player out, and then each consecutive player is even more likely to just lose everything, or if the company somehow loses everything it has, wouldn't it then mean all those who oppose it will almost automatically win?

    I'm curious about the limiters. And the "First player has to reap what he sows" for the first case doesn't impress me, as after you win, you can just do it to the others, or form some alliance to King someone else.

    I think it sounds perfectly Paranoia-ish, heh. I believe Robin D. Laws's Rune functions in a very similar manner, by the by.
    The rule is, you only get back from your stake a number the other player puts forward. The company has thirty stones, the player has five. The other player bets those five (and maybe three Soap points he earned in the course of the scene by bringing in relationships and skills -- it doesn't matter). If the Company bids maybe ten points, he only gets back the five (he doesn't get the Soap -- which is a great limiter). He narrates, but he's worse off.

    On the other hand, if the Company plays it safe and bids six, the player gets back six points -- more than he started with, since the Soap counts for him as a mean of getting resources. Ne narrates, and he's better off.

    Since the winner is the one with most Resources, it's in the interests of everyone not to run the Company into the ground, since beating the Company and amassing points by means of Soap is your means of winning, and you can't get the Company's points if the Company doesn't have points to risk.

    If the Company goes out of business, you either have to stage a bailout, or everyone loses. Because you don't have a job anymore and the economy goes to hell.

    \n Right. I think I see.

    So. Yes, I suppose it could be played as a board game, to an extent, except that the rules state that 1) the introduction of relationships has to make sense; and 2) when a relationship has been introduced and defined, that definition remains. So, for example, if your cat Joey is revealed by the Company to be the President of Slugistan, in all future Situations he is the President of Slugistan and all attempts to include him (and gain points from his introduction) must make sense in that context. Which prevents you from making it just a board game.

    Having said that, if you can come up with an improbable but nonetheless plausible rationale for re-introducing Joey the Cat into a later Situation (eg in one Situation in the game he came up in, the Reps deposed him, and in the next one they wired him up as a suicide bomber. Because he had "fallen in" with terrorists -- yeah, that group of players was really into good taste) that's fun, although I suppose that makes it both morre or less like a board game.

    I don't see it being played as a board game to be a bad thing. I have loved playing it with people who have never played an RPG, and have marketed it with some people as being "like a murder party game" -- ie, not mentioned the RPG words at all. Although murder party games are pretty much RPGs, obviously.
