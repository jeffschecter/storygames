    \n I really like Small World, released earlier this year.

    The players control different fantasy civilization, created by combining a fantasy race with a special ability (creating combinations like Dragon Master Elves, Seafaring Amazons, Flying Orcs, Hill Tritons -- yes, Hill Tritons). The goal is to collect victory points by expanding your civilization over the map. However, you've got a limited amount of tokens, so eventually the only way you can keep growing is to get a new one; your current civilization "goes into decline" and you get to pick a new one to play as. It's mechanically similar to Vinci, if you've played that.

    The game mechanics don't reduce your board power as a punishment for falling behind. This is an important feature for me and some of my friends.

    2-5 players. Games last about an hour, maybe an hour and a half. I'd say it's about as casual-play friendly as Settlers, if not more so (and I am definitely a casual board-game player).

    Downsides:
    - It's not especially educational, if that matters (there is room to flex your creativity by making up new fantasy races or special abilities, though).
    - Not very resilient to losing pieces.

    \n That's really hard to answer without knowing what you like. Here are what I consider my staples, given the folks with whom I generally play games:\n
      \n
    • Settlers of Catan, which you already have.
      • \n
    • Bohnanza - a card game of bean farming (no, really), with the constraint of working with your cards in the order they were given to you, and the opportunity to trade with fellow players to help with that constraint.
      • \n
    • Pandemic - a very accessible cooperative game which has the players combatting four separate contagions across the world, treating people to buy time until all four cures are found.
      • \n
    • Carcassonne - a game that asks players to place tiles in order to build the countryside around the region of Carcassonne, in France. You place followers to score points. It's possible to place followers such that you work together with others to score points, or if you prefer, such that you steal the efforts of the other players.
      • \n
    • Rage - A trick-taking card game where each hand players declare how many tricks they'll take, and then earn (or lose) points for succeeding (or failing). It adds a fair amount of chaos by including cards which can change trump mid-trick, or eliminate trump altogether.
      • \n
    • Transamerica - a connection game where players try to connect a set of five cities with track before anyone else connects their five cities. The catch is that if you build into someone else's track, you each can then share each other's track. Eventually all track is usable by everyone. It becomes a game of getting more help than you give.
      • \n
    \nOthers I like, and play often, but wouldn't consider the best options for someone new to such games, since they're either a bit more complex, or more tightly targetted to my tastes (though, most warnings about generalizations would apply):\n
      \n
    • Tichu - Card game for four players [b]only[/b], played in partnerships. A climbing game where the object is to get rid of your cards, but with special cards, trading cards with the other players, interesting scoring rules, and options to predict your success for greater reward (but at greater risk).
      • \n
    • Mystery Rummy - Rummy-based card games themed to various mysteries or criminal situations. They all have action cards that allow greater control of the cards you get, and some can be played in partnerships. There are four numbered versions in the series (Jack the Ripper, Murders in the Rue Morgue, Jekyll and Hyde, Al Capone and the Chicago Underworld) and two others that technically aren't in the series, but easily could be (Wyatt Earp, Bonnie & Clyde).
      • \n
    • Arkham Horror - Big, sprawling dicefest of a cooperative game set in HP Lovecraft's Arkham, Massachusetts.
      • \n
    • Pow Wow - Basically Indian Poker: everyone places a feather on their head. You can see all feathers but your own. Starting player declares the sum of all feathers (including their own). Next player either challenges ("Your guess is too high!") or raises. Lose three challenges and your out. Last player standing wins. Pow Wow adds specialty feathers (negative numbers, "x2", "Max=0", etc.) to spice things up.
      • \n
    • Snow Tails - players race dog sleds by playing cards to determine speed, braking, and "drift", which is how you make your sled turn.
      • \n
    • Pitchcar - another racing game, but this one is purely physical. You set up a race course using tracks, and then flick little disks around the course.

    \n The classic French racing game, Formula D (a.k.a. Formula D\xe9) is both terrific and the best board game for more than 5-6 players that I've ever seen. You can play with 2-10, which is stunning. Also, it is a breath of fresh air in comparison to a lot of the current flock of board games, which seem to reuse a handful of the same trendy mechanics over and over. In comparison, Formula D reminds me of Clue (a.k.a. Cluedo) in that it feels like a game that should be 60 years old instead of almost 20.

    \n I'll go ahead ab be a shill. (Disclaimer: I am a Looney labs Demo Rabbit... on hiatus, but still....)

    Treehouse (AKA, the Icehouse Pyramid Game System) is pretty much a universal game kit. Get five matching sets (which would give you fifteen pyramids each of five color) and you can play something like 150 games from IcehouseGames.org. Yes, a set of five Rainbow or Xeno will run you about $50. But you'll play with them for 50 years, before you've exhausted every possible game and game style.

    On a related note, Chrononauts is basically a board game where the board is built from the cards. Easy to learn and fun, it's also got some very affordable expansions/additions.

    No one's said Illuminati yet, so I'll give it a bump. Another cards-that-make-the-board game, like Chrononauts; but it's a classic that's got some expansions. NOTE: Don't get suckered into buying the INWO TCG accidentally--it;s just not the same, to me.

    I am a recent convert to Descent junkie, but I can think of other things (like Icehouse, or three or four other board games combined) on which you could spend that much money, unless you're looking for a classic, railroady dungeon crawl feel that's all about action. You could buy expansions annually, too. :}

    Dollar for dollar, though? Get five sets of matching Treehouse and go nuts. They also make great RPG play aides in a pinch (minis, terrain, counters, state markers, etc).

    +1 to the suggestion of Le Havre over Agricola, but neither one is appropriate if you're looking for a moderately light filler of time. (And I think I'd recommend LoYang over Agricola too, with the same caveat.)

    If you aren't seeking light and quick, consider the Splotter games: Roads and Boats, Antiquity and Indonesia.

    You really couldn't go wrong with Dominion.

    Stone Age, Atilla, Web of Power, Puerto Rico, Ra, Medici, Tigris and Euphrates, Samurai, Through the Desert, Ticket to Ride, Carcassone, El Grande, Caylus, and Aladdin's Dragons are all games that should deliver fun to a wide variety of tastes. I'm not sure which of these are easy to find these days.

    \n Another vote for Samurai. You can play it online if you want to check it out.

    * It's more competitive than Settlers - a point gained for me is a point lost for you.
