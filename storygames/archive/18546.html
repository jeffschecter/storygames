\n The current discussion on GM-less games, along with guess what the GM is thinking, set off an old peeve of mine.

So the deal is this: In a traditional RPG breakdown, players control the personalities, dialogue, and actions of the main characters - and the GM controls environment and resolution. It seems to me that many people say that this inherently puts the story in the GM's hands. I find this very confusing, because from my point of view, the personalities, dialogue, and actions of the main characters are usually where the meat of the story is. In semi-Aristotilean terms, for me the core of story is generally in the views of the protagonists, and how they change over the course of the plot.

So what I'd like to hash out is sources for this disconnect.

For example, one disagreement might be over what story is. Someone might think that story is primarily about the external action, like who wins in protagonist-versus-antagonist. In this case, the GM does really have the lion's share of control, and the views and dialogue of the main characters aren't central. That would explain things, but I'm not sure who (if anyone) believes this as opposed to something else.

So what's your view on where main character personality, dialogue, and attempted action falls into for stories?

    \n I think the consequences for character action and decision are the other half of story in the normal sense. That's the aspect of the story the GM does control in the normal RPG setup, and it's really important to story. Consequences to character action are what separate a tragedy from a comedy, among many other things.

    As a memorable tweet I read said:

    Novel: A man seeks something. Commercial version: He finds it. Literary version: He doesn't find it.

    I agree it makes no sense to say "the GM controls the story" in a normal RPG setup; I attribute this usage to people who didn't think about what they were saying or didn't care about being wrong.

    In the end it's true that narrative character development is an important part of the story, but when the greater story does not change (in tone, in direction) as a result of changes in the characters then you're left with half an interesting development.

    Let's use a very, very na\xefve example. Say you have a traditional D&D campaign and over the course of the campaign your chaotic good warrior sees so much horror and so many villains get away with what they do that he becomes jaded and finally corrupted by evil. Cool! Now however the Dungeon Master prepared another innocent village to go to and save, and he's prepared let's say a dungeon under a nearby castle that they would get access to after saving the village. Now our Warrior doesn't want to save the village anymore; he wants to burn it down, take their stuff, and move on. This becomes a problem to the DM who spent his whole weekend preparing the dungeon under the castle. What do?

    (There ARE solutions here, but none of them feel very good. "Do not prepare story" is still my preferred solution.)

    \n John, back in my very early days of GMing, the player characters were not the main characters of the story. My NPCs were the main characters, and the PCs were the tools for my audience (the other players) to respond to my story. As much as the PCs got screen time and die rolls and sheets of stats, none of that had any bearing on the story. It just gave the players something else to do, as a sort of intermission. Tired of listening to Dave talk? Roll to slaughter some orcs! Describe how badass you are! Okay, the orcs are all dead, now back to the story.

    In my early years of roleplaying, this seemed quite common. Maybe it still is common in RIFTS and GURPS, I don't know. I think it makes some sense given how many roles were officially and unofficially slotted into "the GM". If I'm the evening's entertainment, then hell yeah I'm telling the story I want to tell. Your characters are just there to care and give good reactions and maybe inspire me.

    If you ask my high school friends about the "story" of those games, the first things they'll recount will be the rare moments where they did something cool that made a big difference. Maybe about once very few sessions. The second thing they'll recount will be my NPC-centric plot, which was the bulk of every session. So, y'know, call that story collaboration if you like, but the ratio is pretty extreme.

    I do like your point about views, though. What did the PCs think of all this? If you think that's an important part of the "story", then I don't know if I can really disagree. Most of my efforts at making the players care were aimed at getting the PCs to hate some NPCs and love others. It basically worked, so I still feel like the script was mine, but the players did flesh it out with some witty lines or other unique responses. When actors get to improv a lot of lines in a play, does that make it "their story"?

    \n In my first campaign I made finger puppets of the player characters to remind myself who they were. That they were actually characters that mattered.

    Lets put on my twisted lens and look back at those early days...

    Initially the group was like a single character. All of them stayed together in mothership form and the players took responsibility for one aspect of the single character's varied arsenal. "Oh, a strength check? I'm the player responsible for all the strength related aspects of this group!"

    Then we had combat and in that the character would split into several pieces, each piece controlled by a different character. They were individuals for that. But then they'd reform at the end.

    My NPCs never did that. They were always their own characters and in contrast, much stronger as concepts for it! I might have been right to run the game for those guys!

    I lasted 21 sessions as a traditional Dungeon Master before I bought Mouse Guard and discovered indie games.

    \nI think it is easy for a railroading GM to control the game in the sense of boxing the PCs in and make it so that their choices have no effect on the larger world. However, this is not the same thing as control over story. Many uses of the term "de-protagonized" often shows a horrendous misunderstanding of story, in my opinion. Consider the play Rosencrantz and Guildenstern are Dead. If played out as a game, many would say that it is railroaded and the main characters were horrendously "de-protagonized". They have no control over their lives, and they are forced into the plot of Hamlet to their deaths. However, they are most certainly the protagonists of the play, and their lack of control in no way makes them into non-protagonists.

    As a similar illustration of railroading, consider the film Escape From New York. I could create a remake where the hero is an undercover cop committed to saving the world. This might have the same action and locations, but it would be a vastly different story than the one with Snake Pliskin, in my opinion. This was generally my experience of railroading GMs. They could mostly direct the external action to where they wanted, but this wasn't the same thing as absolute control over story. So in an old Call of Cthulhu campaign, when my PC Benjamin Grimmond started taking investigation in the wrong way, my GM had him kidnapped and tortured to be rescued by the other PCs - which I certainly thought of as railroading and resented at the time. However, this was a vastly different story than if Grimmond had willingly followed the expected line of action.

    That said, it certainly is possible for the GM to have control over story - especially if the majority of play session time is the GM speaking, which I have seen in some cases. Even if they don't do this, a GM could take the lead in characters and conflict, and the players could act in supporting roles to what the GM creates, rather than taking the lead in their own material. However, the point is that players can take the lead in creating material by what their PCs do and say. It does not necessitate control of external events.

    Indeed, you don't even need external events, since two characters interacting can be a story in itself. This is something that I see as becoming more prominent as American indie designers interact more with Nordic larp, and vice-versa. Many larp stories take place with nothing outside of the PCs interacting with each other. I'm curious to see more fruits of this.

    \nPossibly, but not necessarily. A character can improv lines that create their own story, or they could improv lines that simply support the existing story.

    In my experience of tabletop play, I and other players often had strong and interesting characters - and I considered their character development to be an important element of story. I know that not all games were like this - but this part depended on the players, not the cooperation of the GM.

    \n In literary terms, a Story is a narrative piece which features characters, a setting and a plot. Putting this into an RPG, you could say the traditional GM will provide the Setting, the players will provide the Characters and in a railroaded game, the Plot as well. This makes saying "The Plot is in the GM's hands" a more accurate expression than "The Story is in the GM's hands. The story will still be in everybody's hands, since without players, even the most railroading GM can't make an story.

    \n Perhaps the game writer is the author of the story. The author of the rulebook, after all, by creating the setting and mechanics has the ability to privilege one sort of narrative over another. Story is more than just what happens in the game -- which the game master sets up -- it's also about the themes, moods and emotions explored, and game writers have a lot of control over that.
