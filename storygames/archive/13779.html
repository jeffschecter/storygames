    \n I haven't actually offered a kickstarter, but I have been pleased to participate in a dozen of them so I can try to give you a broad overview based on the best of my knowledge.

    There are two actual disadvantages with regards to Kickstarter, the platform. The first is that Kickstarter takes a modest cut (~5%?) of the total funding so you do loose out on a bit of money sent to you that way. The second disadvantage is that it does not currently allow you to directly create projects unless you have a US bank account which can interfere with the international participation to some extent.

    The challenges include choosing a minimum total amount that is low enough to be met, yet high enough to get maximum participation. This can be done effectively by offering supplemental prizes or content for ever X amount beyond the minimum. The other challenge is to have the correct number of tiers for your audiences, with each tier offering different yet appropriate prizes. This is great for pre-orders and can easily allow you to provide X numbers of copies to a customer for a certain pledge. Telling them that a $75 pledge can get them 5 copies of your $20 retail book can do wonders.

    Advantages include an easy method of tracking pre-orders, acquiring customers from different communities (such as the artist or patron of the arts communities) which may discover you via Kickstarter and a risk free method of fund-raising. I am personally intending on using a Kickstarter for my own game project,

    Hope that helps!

    \n I've helped a few people successfully use Kickstarter.

    First, I agree with everything everyone has said above.

    Kickstarter is great but it isn't a replacement for:

    - having a compelling pitch
    - creating something that actually solves a problem for which an audience exists
    - finding an audience

    In general, the people I know who have had success with Kickstarter already had some combination of the following:

    - a bunch of people excited about what they are making who want to help them
    - if they didn't have an existing audience, ties to people that do (working with creators, licenses, or ideas that already have a fan base)
    - giving lots of stuff away for free that people would normally want to pay for
    - having an amazing video that clearly states why you need this money, why you need it now, and what people who help will get out of it

    If you have some combination of the above, what really drives it home is different levels of rewards tied to different levels of financial contribution. It helps to make the smallest financial contributions as low as possible without hurting your cause so if I'm 50/50 about your project but I do find it interesting, I can donate $5 to help in a small way. But also provide lots of benefits that scale upwards and are limited in some way. Here is a great example: http://www.kickstarter.com/projects/gamingpaper/gaming-paper-adventures. I would also recommend rewarding people who help promote you and spread the word (tracking and rewarding twitter mentions for example).

    But bottom line, I don't think I've personally seen many kickstarters that have succeeded that didn't already have some sort of audience.

    \n I don't think a vid is a *requirement*, but if you don't have one you'll need lots of info about your project, pics, data, etc.

    Like, for what you're going for, link to in-depth blogs/posts on your site about your project, post pics/pdfs of some raw text, etc etc. Talk about who will be doing the layout, and show examples of the works of the previous art by the person you intend to hire, etc. At higher tiers, offer free copies of your other RPG projects if you have them (you do).

    Be specific about what you have, what your needs are, why 'You may be interested in My project', etc.

    If you do go with a video, here's a perfect example of how Not to do it:
    https://www.kickstarter.com/projects/sharkbone/threshold-tragic-superheroes?ref=category

    Notice the total lack of information about the project, just "Plz giv moneys. Here is my face".

    \n Jason, I don't think you'll have a problem. My tack with Dungeon World and Powers For Good is to release the plain text of the whole thing, free and open. Then charge some for the layout and art version, in PDF and print (caveat: I haven't hit this point yet, so I can't say for sure it works, but early signs point to yes). You might consider making the full-art version something that only people who support the project get. This isn't exactly a new approach, it's essentially what WotC did with d20: they released the plaintext for free, allowing people to create their own presentations (like the hypertext d20 srd), but charge for PDF and print versions of their nice looking books.
