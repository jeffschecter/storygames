\n So I am kicking around some ideas for a game and the more I think about the game, the more I *really* want to at least try a game that is structured a specific way. I've seen this structure work well in movies and literature, but I've not seen it done much at all in role playing. Can anyone send me some links to games structured this way or share thoughts on how to do it. I'm at the brainstorming stage and would love as much input as I can get.

Here's the basic gist of what I want to do. We'll use a movie of which I am intimately familiar: Starship Troopers to illustrate my point.

The opening scene of Starship Troopers takes place on Klendatu, the aliens' homeworld. Protagonist of the film Johnny Rico and squad encounter aliens and get basically wiped out. The last shot is Johnny staring into the camera screaming in pain as bugs surround him. Fade out.

Fade into Johnny in high school trying clumsily to flirt with his girlfriend. We're treated to about 30 minutes of Johnny going through basic and the start of the bug war until the scene returns to Klendatu and we see that he is rescued and lives on.

Then the movie goes on to show Johnny become a big hero and lead his own squad, get a promotion, etc.

So to summarize:
1. There's a bang to get everyone's attention
2. The details about the past are filled in
3. The story is finished

Now, as I understand it, The Committee for the Exploration of Mysteries is told through flashback with the outcome largely known (the expedition was a success). The goal in the game is to earn as much honor for your individual character through role playing as possible.

That's not quite what I want. Basically, I see the characters at a point where they are about to face the biggest challenge of their life and if the succeed, their dreams will come true. So, in all honesty, the big bang at the beginning doesn't need to be there. The game could basically be "Tomorrow, you're going to have a shot at your dreams and then camera dissolves to how you all got started" with scenes going around the table until the group gets to the present.

I am not sure how clear that is, but if anyone has any thoughts, that would be great!

    \n 3:16 perhaps not surprisingly has flashbacks that somewhat mimic this (also reminds me of the Infospectre's commentaries or what not)

    That said, I would chalk this up to a bait and switch (the bang and then the build up , as apposed to what actually happens in the starship troopers movie) that doesn't work well. If bangs work, they work because they require action, cutting away from that is tedious. If bangs don't work your still building excitement for something (its bugstomping time!) and then running away from it like a mad grue towards the darkness.

    Not everything that works in other forms work in games...

    \n We've been playing Fiasco this way: do a flashforward into a violent (post-Tilt in story order) confrontation, in a scene sometime before the Tilt (in game play order), setting up a Date With Destiny that has to be kept sometime in the next dozen or so scenes. Works like a charm.

    There's a questionnaire in A Penny For My Thoughts that works in reverse chronological order and might lead to this kind of play structure.

    Confessionals in InSpectres: their existence implies that the story is being told contemporaneous with the Confessionals, with the rest happening in recollection/flashback. Why not just open with a Confessional?

    Any game can have a flash-forward, really, and then you've got structure for your session, because you know where you have to wind up sometime later, and you need to plant the seeds that will grow into the scene you know has to happen. It's just an XXXtreme form of reincorporation! "You know that cowering victim from the hostage situation? It was your lawyer here - OK? - who just failed his role and screwed up your defense!"

    \n Also known as In media res. Story game wise, I can think of these games, where this is sort of incorporated into the game (in addition to those already mentioned):

    * Next week on... in PTA
    * In the Belly of the Whale from N\xf8rwegian Style. Players are telling the stories of their characters up to the point were they are sitting inside the belly of the whale and then completing the story with epilogues.

    I guess also my own game Montsegur 1244 works a bit like that with a fixed ending known from the start, though it is not actually played out until the end.

    I think there is room to explore how to use this concept as a central game mechanic.
