    \n Listing Fast and Furious, Initial D and Wangan Midnight together is a bit odd, given they're very different types of stories that coincidentally all feature cars.

    I'd argue that, at least for the first two movies, Fast and Furious has more in common with Batman than with Initial D in that the technology is a collection of powers, extensions of how powerful the characters are in "battle." Standard rpg mechanics with stats and dice wouldn't be too far off the mark.

    Initial D is about the process of learning, detail by detail. The idea of car abilities as fluff is perfect for it, because Initial D does not use realistic performance comparisons, and the mechanic of racing has no variable elements. Instead, each race is a mystery, and solving the mystery (figuring out the one spot where the performance of your car exceeds the opponent's car) almost always wins the race.

    Wangan Midnight sets up the cars as opponents to their drivers, with each upgrade to the car increasing the danger, but also increasing the potential success (much like a raise in Legend of the Five Rings). It's not skill that's the primary determiner of races, though, it's spirit. Racing is an emotional struggle with fear, how close you can take your car to the limit.

    It's just been too many decades (I can actually use the plural!) since I've seen Cannonball Run, so I'm not going to make the attempt to figure it out.

    \nFirst of all, figure out what the characters do.

    Are they all drivers? Are they competing? Does their spotlight time focus on actual driving stuff, or driving plus para-automobile stuff like mechanics and detailing? Does play extend to the street posturing, romantic relationships, drug runs, and gunfights? Is play a soap opera about people who love cars, or is it focused more on the car stuff?

    Then figure out what the players do.

    The initial description mention car-fetishism suggests a Sim game, but is that what you want? What is the payoff for playing? What are the players signing up for when they play this game? A rock'em-sock'em good time involving cars? Competitive racing among players? Detailed discussion of drift, tire selection, and air intake? Exploration of personal meaning, and cars are just the background?

    Figure out what the game needs to deliver those things.

    Think about the reward cycle from start to finish, generally starting with character generation, moving through the first and second instances of play, and considering the player's payoff between cycles. Make sure that cycle is a feedback loop that simultaneously satisfies the player and makes him hungry for more.

    Only then do you start nailing down crap like attributes, skill lists, and (god forbid) dice mechanics. You can think about these things along the way, but don't get wedded to them. You need to be ready to scrap them when they don't fit.


    Here's a precis for a somewhat Simmy driving RPG:

    Skid

    a game of street racing

    In Skid, you take on the role of a street racer. Maybe you have a day job, but at night you're all speed and gasoline, tearing down the highways and racing other gearheads for cash. Wins supply the hard cash to buy better parts for your car and repair the damage you've done.

    Play cycle:

    1. Create a character. Every player gets a driver and some friends. Distribute points between driving skills, mechanical skills, and social skills. Depend on your friends (NPCs attached to your character) for the stuff you're not good at, but expect them to have their own demands.

    2. Create a car! Cars have skills, too, and you improve them with cash. There's a huge car skill list with stuff like "custom-tuning" and "adjustable fuel lines" and "aluminum radiators."

    3. Drive. Role-play situations about your racing life. Make deals for parts. Help out your friends so they'll keep your car purring. Show off at car shows. Race! Earn money at races and shows.

    4. Improve your skills and your car skills. Experience on the road and "in the clutch" earn you XP to spend on personal skills. Spend money earned at shows and in races on better stuff for your car.

    5. Repeat. Drive and improve. Drive and improve. Intersperse scenes with friends. If you want a more action-packed game, introduce enemies, drugs, guns, and angry criminal types. Drive fast or die.

    Important questions:

    * Can you die? Since this is a cinematic driving game and dying isn't fun, maybe the worst that happens is you total your car and you get Fucked Up (hospital bills, etc.).

    * What can you improve on a car? See Gran Turismo or other console racing games for pointers. Generally interesting "skills" a car might have are acceleration, speed, handling, and braking. Also, skills for "looks" (detailing, intimidation, etc.). Also internal mechanical things like "cooling" and "diagnostics."

    * How is racing handled? I'm imagining a street "dungeon" with hidden traps, corners and intersections that require skill checks, and so on. The rules should let the player use terrain to his benefit (e.g., he knows his car is good on the straight-away, so when he hits the mile-long stretch of I-66, he makes a car Speed check. Each race has a default time (in seconds, probably) and each skill check can subtract (good) or add (bad) seconds. The fastest time wins!
