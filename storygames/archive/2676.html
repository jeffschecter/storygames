\n Clicking-around the Knight Realms LARP website, I see an award for "Best Racial Role-player, 2006."
The winner is listed as a "Gypsy", beating out a Dark Elf, High Elf, Nyad and Sylvan Elf.

I see pictures of several white guys with Japanese character names, in SE-Asian martial arts garb. And a chinese name captioning a photo of a Barak Obama-looking guy, of indiscernable actual race, who -might- be wearing yellow facepaint. An actual black guy in cornrows, holding a paper fan, in kung-fu attire, using the name "Shen".

There's green facepaint, feline facepaint, elf ears, scottish tartan and blue Braveheart facepaint, "Templars" with christian crosses on their tunics. An Egyptian mummy. A "demon" in Darth Maul facepaint. Muslim turbans and veils.

There's a group photo in pitch-black "dark elf" facepaint, mostly whites-- but one black guy, too.

Is there a difference between playing these characters "live", as opposed to tabletop?

-------

If the Knight Realms community faced criticism-- in the vein of Chris Chinn calling Drow cosplay "blackface", or Story Games members analyzing the orientalist Jabba The Hutt image as "racist"-- then the KR members would be faced with an Identity Politics quandry.

I am visualizing myself in that position.

Would my membership-by-choice in the Knight Realms community-- trump my membership by blood in an actual racial group depicted, perhaps clumsily or offensively, by KR?

Would my distaste for sanctimonious finger-pointing critics-- trump the fact that such critics' arguments may nonetheless have merit?

What about my pro-art/ pro-fantasy identity: I believe in exploration and articulation of subconscious impulses and desires... that's the only way I know to learn what each of us really wants. And yet the long journey to self-discovery make take some of us into unpleasant territory, and we may be appalingly lax about bothering to outgrow those wrong turns. How much slow-learning should I tolerate?

------

Apply this to the Story Games Community. What does it take for a member to win your support? Is it enough if they're dedicated to making or playing these games? Or must they also pass some additional litmus test: in effect prioritizing some other identity politics you have embraced ahead of membership here?

    \n I like the tattoo/t-shirt metaphor. It's evocative.

    A few years ago, my dayjob was with an bicycle activism not-for-profit.

    We were involved in getting the city to install more bike lanes.

    In one Hassidic neighborhood in Brooklyn, many jews went before the city council
    to oppose a proposed bike lane, on the basis that it would bring bare-legged women
    operating machines, on the Sabbath.

    I like women on bikes. But I also like Not Irritating My Neighbors.

    Should I have just shrugged and said "Let's ride somewhere else on Saturdays, ladies?"

    Or (ok... this is how a metaphor turns to shit...)

    Should I have stuck with the Bike Lanes Good! t-shirt, even tho it was, in this circumstance, a Scarlet Letter?



    I am trying to emphasize the 'hard choices' part where our affinities are in conflict, due to forces beyond our control.


    There's a writer's organization called PEN, where people who not only write,
    but who identify themselves as Writers, advocate in favor of literature and against censorship.

    However, their charter also states that members "pledge themselves to do their utmost to dispel race, class, and national hatreds
    and to champion the ideal of one humanity living in peace in the world."

    I see potential for conflict here. Should they oppose the burning of the Protocols Of The Elders Of Zion, which is racist propaganda;
    but which is also literature?

    Posted By: Judd

    Wanna know what I love about these people and these games?

    It is that they're fun.

    \nAt first I read that as pedantic.

    But no-- fun is a huge part of the appeal.

    However fun is often equated with frivolous.

    If somebody spends half his paycheck on wargame miniatures,
    and justifies it as the most-fun part of his life; he runs the
    risk of being judged as giving fun too much priority in his life.

    Use "I do whatever's fun" as your compass and you're likely to be
    viewed with suspicion-- as a potential fair-weather friend, or
    as decadent, amoral.

    Critic: Why do you wear blackface?
    Fun Guy: It's fun!

    To me, "because it's fun" is actually a pretty-good defense.
    But I know many others would view it more as a confession--
    of reckless disregard for other concerns which conflict with fun.

    Like Art and Free Speech, Fun needs an ACLU of its own.
    Articulate partisans willing to stand up and defend it.

    PODS on the Vine

    Using the DitV rules, The PCs are a group of Story Gaming podcasters who travel from Con to Con; interviewing and reporting.

    As in the movie Almost Famous, they must choose between Journalism: telling the listenership the truth, warts and all;
    and Advocacy: spinning the story out of a sense of loyalty toward the gamers interviewed.

    In lieu of the Progression of Sin, this game uses the Pennsylvania Crimes Code definition of "Culpability", which describes blameworthiness
    as a 4 step progression from: acting with Negligence, to acting with Recklessness, to acting Knowingly, to acting Intentionally.

    Fallout consists of repercussions from the podcast, to the reporters as well as the gamers interviewed.

    Sample towns include: Episode #1 Gencon: the nice guy who draws bikini armor, Episode #2 Camp Geronimo: the case of the wicked-fun Drow LARP...
