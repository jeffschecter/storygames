\n Okay, I am currently running an Amber game using a version of the FATE system and the game is going really well. The players are all invested in both their characters and the setting/npcs (more than just about any other game we've played). However, we often only roll for conflicts maybe 2 or 3 times each session (although a few sessions we've rolled more often). The characters are all engaged with conflicts but I'm finding that it often comes down to roleplaying and not rolling dice. We reach for the dice when we find there is something pressing that needs to be resolved. but sometimes that isn't very often. It isn't like we're just sword shopping or something for ages with nothing happening. I'm hitting them with bangs I've devised that push the narrative along in interesting ways almost every scene--yet we're not rolling the dice very often.

So. . .is this even a problem?

AND

How might we push toward the dice more often?

    Maybe we've found our happy medium though: use dice but not as often? Maybe I am making a mistake by trying to push for more rolls when we don't need to. That's why I'm looking for some advice here.
    \nYeah, that's all I meant. And my final question was not just rhetorical: What would you gain by rolling more often? Do you feel it would add more tension in the fear of failure, or perhaps more drama in dealing with the complications resulting from failure? Do you feel that having degrees of success or failure implied by dice would give the GM more inspiration in narrating the results of actions? Or would it be much like what you're doing now, but with successful dice rolls validating (more often than not) what you're planning on doing anyway through simple roleplaying?

    There are certainly some benefits to having dice color what happens in a scene, but I'd personally worry that actively trying to encourage more rolling would simply distract more from what may already be a "happy medium." If you'd like the chance of failure to inject more tension and drama, though, I wonder whether the solution might be to present the players with more challenges that beg for dice, rather than to require rolls for the stuff you're already doing.
