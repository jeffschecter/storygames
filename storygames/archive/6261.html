    \n Off the top of my head, I really like the big purple d20 that we have. I'm horrible at estimating size, but I think the die is like two inches across, so much larger than most dice. It's big enough that anyone at the table can read the result without problems. Most d20s necessarily have such small numbers that you can't see how well someone else rolled. With other dice this isn't as big of a problem, so the other similarly big purple dice we have don't get as much love. I have larger d6s that get even less love: they are as big as your hand, and so they don't roll easily.


    I don't any but nonetheless really like the non-caltrop d4 design. It fixes all the problems of d4s: hard to roll, pointy if stepped on, weird questions about where the number is located on the triangle, etc. Platonic solids are overrated anyway. Someone should make a game centered entirely on d10s and barrel shaped d4s called "Plato Can Suck It." Maybe work in some other non-Platonic solid dice, too.

    They make similar barrel shaped dice of other denominations, but the d4 one serves a clear need, where the others don't and are hard to tell apart to boot.

    \n I'm so fickle when it comes to dice, I can't truly say I stick to one particular preference for too long, or even one at a time. Partly this has to do with what games I'm playing. I need full polyhedral's for C&C and Red Box D&D for instance, so I keep a double fistful of candy-colored solids wrapped in a skull bandanna in a cigar box, solely because my teenage self would have thought that awesome. I have a ridiculous selection of varied D20's for Talislanta and Tal-derived gaming, which I tend to keep all out so I can roll the most appropriately colored for the scene at hand (Ice Giant attack? Speckled cobalt with sky-blue numbers seems apt). My current obsession are translucent d6's with pips. When starting into dice-pool-happy indie games, I bought something like a hundred cheap import clear red & blue dice and, for no practical reason, took to spilling the entire haul over the game table in an orgy of probability. For one of my groups, this meant a glass table which produced a striking light effect, reproduced below with a light table (with some frosted chessex and ivory solids thrown in for contrast). I have no idea how, but I'd love some way to make the effect relevant to play.

    \n Dice fetishism... hmmm....
    \n
    \nAnyhow, just to chime in, as I usually do, the solution to the "Caltrop d4" is not that silly square thing. It's so amazingly simple, that I don't know why we haven't seen it outside of a few applications like Mayfair Games "Road to the Whitehouse." You just number a d8 1-4 twice.
    \n
    \nOr, if you're really a geek about dice types, do a D12 three times, or a D20 five times, or whatever floats your boat. All far better than either the caltrop or the cube with points.
    \n
    \nOne of my favorite "rolled" randomizers was this game my grandmother brought me back from China back in the 1970s. It came with four sticks that were rounded, but with a flat side. Not quite semi-circular in cross-section, the cut made the flat side shorter, and made the round side go about 225 degrees around? Anyhow there were four of them, and you put them all between your hands, and you rubbed your hands back and forth spinning them against each other on their axes, very roughly. Then you dropped them, and counted how many landed on their flat sides, with the decorative rounded side up. This was, in that game, the number of spaces you moved.
    \n
    \n(The game was, otherwise, like a simplified Parcheese, with little wooden pawns on a "board" made of printed cloth with circular spaces on it. I doubt that the game was traditionally Chineese, it looked very much like a tourist item. Still. )

    For my Exalted-Wushu game, I was using Chessex red and gold Scarab d6 dice for Scarlet dice, and flat black ones for Ebon. I really liked how those added to the metagame flavor.
