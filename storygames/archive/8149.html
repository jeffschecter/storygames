\n And I will give you a level every time:

* You make in-game progress, complete adventures, etc.
* You bring some food to gaming, food you made yourself, and something you've never brought before.
* You learn and demonstrate a new, practical martial art technique.
* You lose 1 kilogram, if you're looking to lose weight.
* You make a gain on weight training, like 5kg beyond what you normally do.
* You get to the point where you can do 50 pushups in one sitting. I could go on about athletic gains ("run a marathon", "bike 50 kilometers", etc) but I'll stop there and leave the rest to GM discression.
* You demonstrate a gain made in yoga (new move, better flow, etc)
* You complete a hobby project and talk about it/show it off (yes, show and tell gains you a level). Everything from "write an RPG" to "knit the Doctor Who scarf".
* You take the reigns and GM a session instead of me.
* You invent a new cocktail, and it is delicious.
* You do some bluebooking for your character (in-character diaries, materials, etc)
* and so on.

If your character dies, you start over at level 1. Death is not common, but it's not super-rare either.

-Andy

    Posted By: JesseIt's this called life? Every time we accomplish something we grow?
    \nHeh. Ahh, looking at my post, it looks like I'm making a metaphor for everyday life.

    In truth, nope, I actually mean a real RPG, prob a trad fantasy game. See, I want to give levels for that awesome stuff above, but in a game that focuses on 10-30 levels max, giving out a level is a Serious Thing. Needs to be a game where giving out levels is Not So Serious, where a gain of 1-3 levels per session just for doing in-game stuff is totally acceptable/common. But I'd offload it to real life.

    I love social games like Ostracon and the drinking game "I never...", but ones where people get to talk about/show off the other stuff they do and gain in life. I just want to couple that with an actual (fun) RPG.

    I could do this wil D&D, but as mentioned above I'd have to ramp-up the in-life gains so as not to have people jump ten levels in a month.

    -Andy

    Edit: Back in the day, I had an idea for a "Fitness d20" supplement, where you would track and record gains made to diet and physical fitness ("I could only bench-press 100lb, now I can press 150!" "I just joined Tae Kwan Do, and now I can do a wicked Wing-Chugee!"). Turned out that, to make it uniform and consistent, with gains that would supplement character XP but not be too fast or too slow, basically was faaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaaar more work than it was worth. But I still hold an idea of coupling fitness gains with RPG "eeps". This is kind of a modified version of that desire.

    \n I don't think awarding the same reward to someone who did 50 pushops would be the same as someone rinning a marathon. The former requires nothing but a more or less fit person, while the latter demands a deeply prepared and trained one. Why would I run a marathon then?

    My first tought would be to ballance things by assigning different gains. (The pushops would give you one level, while the marathon would mean gaining 3 or 4.) But then I'm not very sure that would keep up with the Zen of the game.

    Then I tought about something. I would separate those 100 levels in different kind of neat stuff... Something like 40 "piece of cake" levels, who would give you something simple, such as some bonuses and that's it, and that you can do simple effort stuff to gain; then 40 "let's rock" levels, who would give you nice powers that make your character cooler and/or more effective, and that require some more consistent effort from you; and at last you have the remaining 20 "I am God" levels, that give you the trully breathtaking, over-the-top, signature habilities and the plot-twisting capabilities, and those require those mega good things to be achieved.

    I think the nice thing about this is that it becomes somewhat easy to clasify something: Bringing food or doing 50 pushops would give me some "piece of cake", while writting a deep and detailed backstory for my guy would actually give me some nice "let's rock", possibly while linking my levels to that same backstory, and then achieving my goal weight will at long last give me something on the "I'm God" scale.

    And then, likning those same levels to in-game stuff seems easy and coherent too. A big monster or adventure completed gives me something "piece of cake", while finishing a plot-line or reaching one of my character's middle term goals would give me some "let's rock", and those memorable campaign and/or story changing things and achieving one of my character's life dreams would translate in the coolest, "I'm God" stuff.

    With some minimal guiding, it could be left to GM's fiat (or a democratic, group decision) with ease.

    \n It seems like the idea behind giving out a level to make it happen more often and be less a serious thing, kinda destroys the level, also some of your examples are horrendously (and very funnily) mismatched.

    Consider the i gain a level in atheletics guy everytime i do something athletic

    So I run a marathon, do 50 pushups and learn a new yoga move

    Athletics Guy 3

    Then I bake a cake

    Athletics Guy 3/ Cakemaker 1

    Then I bring it to session and have a great session

    Athletics Guy 3/ Cakemaker 1/ Gaming Guy 1

    but in that session, I also completed in game stuff and a few other things on your list

    Athletics Guy 3/Cakemaker 1/Gaming Guy 1/ Sensitive Lisener 2/ Awesome Story gamer 1

    What I'm trying to get at is that by collapsing the levels down, you dont end up with levels you end up with skill-ups. If you don't want to diversify the levels (ie Athletics vs Cakebaking vs Whatever) you end up with XP.

    An idea that I've had that in order to keep the levels being levels and not (Skills or Xp) is something like the Infinite Level System, where the game never stops, their is no cap and all that math stuff works right the whole way. 3.x tried really hard to do this and failed horribly (see epic play and spell casters). Levels are still levels by virtue of being more than just collections of xp (ie levels mean something, give preapportioned benefits, etc) but you can also give em out like a monkey nutter because hey their are infinite levels.

    But if you do hand out nearly infininte levels in an infinite level game what is taht saying about the game?

    (on the other hand, I think a pretty good hack of 3.x can be made , if you take the all those levels, and let everyone be whatever they want, but reduce the level cap to 10, but allow levels to go beside each other rather competing. Ie compress the old level 20 fighter into 10 levels (Feat , Feat ,Feat , feat ,etc) and do the same for all the classes. If someone just wants to fight they can be a level 10 fighter, if someone wants to be a fighter 10/mage 10 / Awesome Incarnate 10 they can do that as well. Balance it out with a small benefit for 'unspent' levels and hack out some of the worst dnd offenders (Skill system) I think you could make a really rocking hack out of it)

    **This is upon reflection , really close to making a skill or percentile based system out of the 3.x dnd in a sense, and sufferes from the whole problem that I mentioned with collapsiing levels into skills or something of the like**

    But anyway, Thank you for posting this, and for everyone for Responding
