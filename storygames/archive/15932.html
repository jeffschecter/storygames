\n I wanted to follow up on a broader issue from the now-closed thread on [history of gaming] Confessions of a Dungeon Master - and somewhat to another branched thread on this, Player Determined Difficulty Numbers.

I'd like to talk about persuasion and seduction of player characters. There are a lot of games that include social conflict rules that can be used among and/or against PCs, but there also seems to be a common opinion that whatever the player says about the character should be absolutely true - without any question or doubt.

From my side, I'm not a big fan of the social conflict rules I've seen, but I've used them a fair bit in games. I had an earlier thread on this about how social conflict was often based on both sides being stubbornly fixed on their goals, and "win" by not listening or changing their position at all, and getting the other side to change instead. I'm not thrilled with that for a lot of social interaction, but especially for seduction. So I've never run a seduction by social conflict rules - but I have run various other persuasion attempts as social conflicts.

On the other hand, I like the idea of player-set difficulty as an alternative to conflict rules. So rather than framing it as a conflict, I might ask the player for a fair assessment of how likely they are to be convinced. I think I would like that better as a mechanic for seduction and many forms of persuasion.

So, questions:

1) Is turning to dice to persuade or seduce a PC inherently jerk-like behavior? i.e. The targeted player says they would refuse, and the persuader doesn't accept that but calls for a die roll instead. I don't think so, but I'd be interested in hearing arguments otherwise.

2) Any experiences or insight on using player-set difficulty for this?

    \n Some "social conflict" games allow you to set the difficulty indirectly:

    Any game in which conflict mechanics depend on the players spending resources on conflicts. For instance, in a game where you bid Tokens against your opponent, and the higher bid wins, you're effectively setting the difficulty for their attempt. In "stake-setting" games or various permutations (like, say, Polaris, or Otherkind dice-type mechanics), you don't set the difficulty but you do effectively get to say what it's worth to you (or your character). The Polaris "But only if..." phrase is probably the clearest example. Apocalypse World also does this, in a slightly different way.

    In Dogs in the Vineyard, you effectively get to say what it's worth to you not to be seduced. Is it worth an argument? A fight? Risking your life? This is also similar.

    The neat thing about these mechanics, though, is they are relatively social-bullying-free. In a situation where you're simply asked to provide a percentage chance for your character's seduction, with everyone staring expectantly, there is some social pressure. You're wondering, maybe, "What do they want me to say?" or perhaps, "What kind of numbers would they find believable?" and those concerns could override your own wishes. In a straight-up social conflict situation, however, everyone *expects* you to fight hard for your side of the bargain, there's no social pressure to "give in" or provide a certain range of probability.


    (I agree that seduction conflicts are not inherently bad in any sense, when exercised by a group of mature human beings with an eye towards everyone having a good time. They are certainly an area of play (like character death in some games) that should be approached carefully and thoughtfully, however. It's worthwhile considering a phrase like, "Are you cool with this conflict, or would you rather we moved on to something that's more fun for you, like the impending Battle of Doom? We don't have to play this out if this way, we can choose another way.")

    \n I'm happy with the standard Forgista narrativist game solution, as exemplified by eg. Sorcerer mentioned above. In this model of thinking player-character relations are not characterized as "total control", whatever that dysfunctional train of thought means, but rather as a relationship similar to that between the Ego and Superego: the player is responsible for exemplifying the rational intent of his character, but we accept that this rational intent is no more influential over the character than it is over normal human beings in our reality: people can and do act from base impulses, even when their higher rationality would like to desist. Many games in this tradition include a system for social conflicts along the lines John outlines in the first post; the intent of this system is often simply to streamline a game by using an universal resolution system that encompasses social situations as well as other types of conflicts, but the outcome is a game that is particularly well-suited to this type of thinking. In this kind of game players set the agenda for their characters, but momentary emotionally charged moments for the character are often partially out of the player's hands: the character can escape a dangerous situation instead of staying bravely to face death, or they can break their marital commitments with a third person, or they can lapse in other ways from the unrealistic perfect vigilance that their superego would often request of them. It's a way to bring life-like reality to the game. Although the game texts don't usually delve into this in any great detail, the intent is that social conflicts are only used to cause momentary, emotionally charged snap decisions on the part of other characters: in eg. Dust Devils or Primetime Adventures you can't mind-control somebody permanently, but you certainly can convince them to sign some contract or whatever. I usually explain this to players by saying that social conflicts can make characters do things they will regret in the morning - meaning, after the current scene.

    I call this relationship between the player/superego and the character/ego by the name of character advocation: you do not immerse in your character, it's not a one-to-one relationship between your emotions and your character's. It's perfectly possible for your character to feel attracted sexually to some other character who you as the player don't find appealing. Your task is to simply determine and express your character's thoughts, not to act as a full-body puppeteer. As long as you've made it known to the group what your character thinks (or, perhaps, would think were he not currently being seduced by the sights and sounds of the wonderful and vivid world he lives in), the procedures of the game will take care of what he actually does. In some games the rational mind is very powerful, able to brush aside most temptations, while in others the more or less natural tempter has the upper hand, resulting in a different overall feel to the fiction.

    The above suits a narrativist game well for certain reasons (imperfect characters make good drama, is probably the most prominent), but I've found that I like this approach in a wide variety of other sorts of games as well. For example, our current D&D campaign runs off this set of assumptions: players can't just declare that their characters are perfect and ever-vigilant murder machines, they need to prove this by Will checks and other systematic means. The player is simply not in absolute control of his character to begin with, and this shows constantly. Heck, the perfectly ordinary Initiative check already shows this, insofar as we're concerned: the reason for why one guy acts first and more often is that he is less hesitant, more decisive, more experienced, better prepared and better able to make split-second choices about what he's actually doing in a combat situation. The player who would try to game himself out of the initiative rules by "roleplaying" his character as particularly invulnerable to fear and hesitation would get no traction at our table.

    As for player-set difficulties for social conflicts, that's an interesting idea. I like it for certain types of games. Thinking about it, we're pretty much already doing this in our D&D game: the group prizes solid fictional positioning very highly, so much so that the players can be relied to set their own difficulties on matters where they have a firmer understanding of the fiction than the GM. For this reason, when PCs try to influence each other, I will usually ask the player concerned what his opinion is regarding the chances: could the character be compelled by something like this? Then we set the difficulty based on what the player thinks of the matter, as he's pretty much the expert regarding his character's nature. I can totally imagine how this would not work for a cynical group that'd be willing to lie about this sort of thing for momentary advantage, or one that simply played unbelievably dull characters, but for us this works fine.

Games are traditionally played with game pieces. Chess has pawns and rooks. Monopoly has wheellbarrows etc., boardgames usually just have a counter.
    \n
    \nRole playing games \u2013 even before the term itself was invented \u2013 have playing pieces too. They just happen to be called characters, and may or may not be represented by a little playing counter.
    \n
    \nOnce upon a time when it was new, the context under which role playing games were played and understood by the participants was that of miniature wargaming, chess, and boardgames like monopoly.     Posted By: jhkim1) Is turning to dice to persuade or seduce a PC inherently jerk-like behavior? i.e. The targeted player says they would refuse, and the persuader doesn't accept that but calls for a die roll instead. I don't think so, but I'd be interested in hearing arguments otherwise.

    2) Any experiences or insight on using player-set difficulty for this?
    \n1) Absolutely not. Except when it is. Traditionally, most game genres are steeped in pulp. Whether they are D&D, superheroes, or sci-fi, they - like most main stream movies - take a conceit for granted that Our Heroes may be stymied by physical obstacles of all sorts, but outside of that, no one talks them out of what they want to do.

    On top of that, it's widely believed (if not inherent to American culture) that individuals have free will, and coercion only happens at the end of a gun (or equivalent, like a waterboard).

    So, naturally, most gamers have these assumptions. And so, throwing that aside because an NPC has a high charisma is probably going to be seen as breaking a promise in most games. More so if this is the first time it's come up at all, and the player is a target.

    2) So, player set difficulty all depends on where the game falls into the spectrum of pulp-unrealism or realistic.

    Though, personally, I try to leave room in the middle and individual players can decide how much control they retain or go with the flow. Most good social conflict systems don't require a specific outcome unless it is stakes, which players usually have full control in setting. Instead of asking them how susceptible they are, specifically for the medium of the conflict, I instead might inquire about how vulnerable their character is feeling. Are they grim and focused on a mission, or tired and worn out? Did they just witness the death of a friend or the loss of their treasured magic sword? What emotions are filling them right now?

    Use that to set the difficulty, and let them decide whether they get seduced or lost their cool in some other way that feels truer to the scene.

    The intention of the conflict doesn't need to be the stakes. The NPC might be using seduction, but the goal might be to kidnap the PC, or to steal the microfilm. Even if the goal is seduction, the PC can be left with control over how exactly it goes down. They might not get seduced, but being tempted was enough to "take them out" and lose the conflict. Or maybe they get angry and lose self control, or they get drunk and pass out. Just like combat, where the condition of victory is not always death - despite deadly methods in play - social conflict victory doesn't need to be

    The consequences of losing any conflict could mean that time the PC lost control and now has to deal with the fallout. Or it could mean the time the player discovered something they didn't know about the character before. Maybe both.
