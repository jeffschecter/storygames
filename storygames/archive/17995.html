    If that's not roleplaying enough, then this might be a difficult niche to fill without custom design. "fleet tactics with crunchy system" sounds like a pretty natural fit for a tabletop wargame, but not natural at all for a roleplaying game. The closest you get are games like Star Trek, Rogue Trader or TSoY where you play the crew of ship on an adventure.

    \n To clarify: my TSoY campaigns of late have been full of comparative speculative naval warcraft with oar-manned galleys being pitted against sail-era blue water craft. However, while the game offers a compelling dramatic environment for that sort of thing, it doesn't really do that much mechanically to regulate fleet movements and overall naval realism; all that's on the heads of the players.

    Similarly D&D offers a very robust framework for this sort of thing, but again there is a total lack of detailed, regular naval war rules. You almost have to reach into the realm of the wargame to get that sort of action.

    \n In general for tactical minis play combined with solid RP support, I favor Savage Worlds.

    They have a playset called 50 Fathoms that's focused on pirate play. If they have any good naval-specific rules, they would be there. I got it on Drive Thru RPG as part of a set but haven't read it in detail (just skimmed super fast - looked interesting), so I don't know if there are good naval tactics rules.

    If it were me, I'd put together some sort of simple hack mashup of Savage Worlds and the Pirates of the Spanish Main constructable ship game. I like the way Spanish Main gives you maneuvering tactics without being slow and boring.

    Also, keep in mind that when you are constructing a roleplaying game that focuses on heavy tactical combat elements, you need to start at the beginning to figure out how loss is going to work - does losing a combat mean you all get drowned and eaten by sharks, or are you going to be more forgiving and assume that loss means, by default, that you are captured or marooned or maybe just driven off and fail in what you were trying to accomplish. I find that getting everyone on the same page up front about how failure works is the most important thing in tactics-focused games. And it's no easy task.
