\n It's a pretty rules-light system. Characters are described simply; for instance "young, fit, gung-ho Marine", "dedicated, intelligent, not-very-athletic Doctor", etc. PCs who are better at something will, under most circumstances, automatically win Conflicts against Characters (or Obstacles) that aren't as good or difficult. For example, the Marine will just about always beat the Doctor in a footrace; while the Doctor would almost always trump the Marine in an academic test.

In the case of Characters of similar abilities, the winner is determined randomly. Random checks to determine the winner are also made when conditions (environmental factors, clever Player schemes, injuries or impairments etc.) "lower" or "raise" a Character's ability beyond what it normally is.

The Degree of Success is determined by how much the winning Character beat the losing Character/Obstacle by.

Are there any holes in this basic setup so far?

    \n Looks good. You mean you use a description to convey an idea about the character, instead of an old school skill list? Like Caoimhe says, there's some games you might want to have a look at.
    Make sure you pick good words then, as they'll carry the idea. Use positive words, such as 'feeble' instead of 'not-very-athletic', or connotation-heavy words ('gung-ho' is a good one, or 'bookish' instead of 'intelligent').

    (Also, I wouldn't make those random checks random, that feels too arbitrary for this imo. I feel this undermines the idea of diversificating a cast based on their 'feel'. I don't know what the flavour of the game is, or what exactly those 'random checks' are, so I can't tell you more. What is it you are working on?)

    \n I actually came to a similar concept from another idea. It's like when you go min-maxin in D&D and you only have two high scores. Doesn't that define any character? It can be Strong and Resistant, Quick and Smart, etc. Then you add a "class" and you're set. You could have a Strong and Perceptive Accountant and have him roll a dice with the Advantage rules from D&D Next whenever anything involving these characteristics come into play, or a single one whenever it doesn't.

    However, I'd really provide a list of characteristics to the players instead of letting them come up with their own. After all, you can define the setting you're playing with only using a short list of specific words. At least I could never play Fate because of a lack of a list of proper aspects for any game, otherwise I could have loved that game.
