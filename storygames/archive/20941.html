\n I'm a big fan of Alex Robert's Backstory, a podcast that is a series of interviewers with game designers (usually the indie variety).

Back in episode #24 when talking to Mo Turkington about her anthology War Birds (a number of LARP and freeform games bout women in WWII), Alex asked the question "How does your game fight fascism?"

It's a question she intends to ask the game designers throughout her interviews going forward.

I think that's a great question. Often games are seen as merely entertainment, but I'm sure a lot of us agree that in the indie space especially there's a lot more going on underneath (or sometimes its right on top.)

It's something I've been asking myself now with the games I'm working on. I don't have to make them political, or preachy, to help oppose something like fascism through their design or messaging.

Anyone else have thoughts on the role RPGs can play, especially in times like these, in the role of art in challenging social ills?

    \n That's such a great question. I love it.

    For me RPGs are thoroughly an artistic medium, and therefore a medium for "challenging social ills", as you say. The basic function is discourse among the participant artists, which I believe to act akin to the Force: it surrounds us, permeates us and ultimately, anchors and guides us as human beings. Good art - a good roleplaying game - will bring us into discourses that have positive impact for us as human beings.

    There is also another sense to the idea: one could say that my answer above is wishy-washy in that I merely describe what art does by definition, yet I do not commit to any particular Truth about the universe in it. What about actually fighting fascism, where did that go? The propaganda function, so to speak?

    My answer in that regard is that roleplaying games are not a very good propaganda vehicle due to how much personal interpretation and thinking things out is necessarily involved; the closest you get is basically by having the GM come to the table with an agenda, but even that's pretty weak-sauce when compared to the propaganda effect of a good (well-designed) movie. A movie can draw a person towards or away from e.g. fascism much better than a roleplaying game can, thanks to the inherent nature of the medium. All passive-audience arts are strictly superior to roleplaying for propaganda, I think.

    Nevertheless, I still think that good art - good discourse - fights fascism simply because there is much in fascism that is not true. Any human activity that encourages wisdom will, therefore, necessarily be anti-fascist in the sense of enabling its practitioners to resist fascist lies. Insofar as your game discusses fascism, and insofar as fascism cannot withstand close scrutiny, the play of the game will be successfully anti-fascist.

    Of course not all rpgs do this as well; you can even make a pro-fascism rpg (D&D for instance is a good candidate) by focusing the game's necessary dialogues on issues and concerns upon which fascism does have relevant values and lessons for the players. To get a solid anti-fascist rpg the best plan, therefore, is to delve deep into the issues - not merely the talking points the fascist wants, but also the ones where his worldview is weak. Let the players consider the entire picture and make up their own minds. I believe that fascism will not, overall, stand such careful consideration.

    \nOverall, my take is similar to Eero's. I believe any sufficiently thoughtful work of art helps fight fascism by prompting people to think, as fascism and thought are antithetical.
    Furthermore, RPGs aren't just any piece of art, they're an active participation medium based on conversation as the core technology. This makes role-playing an inherently very good activity at training people into thinking for themselves and interacting peacefully as a group towards common-grounds ends. They're inherently democratic and - according to, for example, \xd8sterskov Efterskole - help people develop strong critical thinking abilities.

    Contrary to that, though, there's been a lot of fascist-leaning content in RPGs, historically: celebration of violence, weapon fetish, unquestioned venture capitalism, thinly-veiled colonialist narratives, the de-humanization of the "other", etc. Plus, a culture of play that more often than not reacted against the inherent democratic qualities of the medium, such as enshrining a cult of the almighty and infallible GM, or emphasizing all sorts of competitiveness within nominally non-competitive games.

    The net end results is that RPGs can easily be as much fascist by default as they're antifascist by default, and I'm not sure whether it evens out. The default is unreliable.

    My games strive to fight fascism by questioning or outright eschewing fascist-leaning content and techniques, by playing to the strengths of the medium and by developing a fascism-free local culture of play.

    \n I'll say a few words about my long-running D&D campaign here, because it has at times been perceived as fascist by Internet strangers, and because I think it's an interesting example of the tension between fascism and liberalism that Rafu notes, and how that can play out at the table.

    The premise of the campaign for six or seven years now has been "historical fantasy, with hardcore Gamist challenge". What this basically boils down to is a setting that is a historical as we care to make it (that is, we don't stop play for minutiae, but in principle all setting questions are resolved with historical lore), combined with all sorts of D&D insanity occurring as exceptions to the historical norm: magic does not exist except for apparently this character, this dungeon, this obscure order of monk-sorcerers. Basically like Solomon Kane and similar fantasy stories. Play has wandered over the years around the 14th to 17th centuries, mostly in Europe, with the occasional jaunt to exotic colonial lands. (Currently we're campaigning weekly in 1361 AD in central Sweden.) Low to mid-level, as we play so tight in terms of procedure, with no give from the GM side at all in terms of success and failure, that even getting to 2nd level is a major achievement.

    Now, what I describe above will in practice mean a campaign full of precisely the kind of content that tends to be considered politically retrograde: player characters are generally members of dominant ethnic and social groups (with the unavoidable unique snowflakes, of course), NPCs express historically realistic attitudes towards all sorts of things, and thus values such as colonialism, religious bigotry, racism and sexism (this last one interestingly very, very rarely towards PCs - the PC groups tend to be situated to shelter female PCs from possible consequences of flouting the gender norms of society) are regularly encountered as matter of play, from both sides of the table: a player may be inspired to roleplay their character, which is affirmed by the GM as being appropriate behavior and expected by the NPCs in the setting; the GM may likewise illustrate the social system in the scenario by having NPCs act in period-appropriate ways, which sometimes means casual cruelty and inhumanity of various sorts.

    (Anybody familiar with the tv drama The Knick probably has a pretty good sense of what all this looks like as an aesthetic gestalt. It's an American hospital drama set in New York in the year 1900, with the delightfully awful premise of showing the setting in as it "really" was, regardless of the delicate sensibilities of the audience. If anything, the show might overemphasize the horrid features of the epoch due to its hospital perspective and stylistic reasons.)

    So here we are, positively wallowing in the societal ills of yesteryear. As an added bonus, the strictly Gamist creative agenda of the exercise largely means that the actual play procedure does nothing to problematize or contextualize the events of play: there is no force in the game that would ensure that good triumphs or evil does not; often an oppressive feudal aristocracy or a thoroughly bigoted church hierarchy ends up being the closest thing to "good" in various scenarios involving cannibal monsters from underground. Equally often there is no clear moral dimension to the proceedings, it's just the way of life for conflict to erupt, providing opportunities for unscrupulous individuals such as the player characters to enrich themselves or the political causes they happen to champion.

    This next part you'll have to take on faith, because I have actually been sitting in the 200+ sessions of play that I'm describing here, and you haven't. Alternatively, you might just decide that I do not know what I'm talking about. Either way:

    My experience of how the arguably fascist content of play in a mockumentary-like D&D campaign like this plays out is that in the long term it increases the political awareness of the players, and makes them somewhat more empathetic towards the weak of society. If anything, the overall pedagogical effect seems to be somewhat socialist in that the harsh, unromantized light the game casts upon "the good old times" tends to make everybody reflect more on what, really, are the building-blocks of a primitive authoritarian society, and how the societal forces that uphold the tapestry have their origins in cruel necessity and human suffering. Players are often in the boots of the very people enacting cruel, discriminatory thinking ("The only good orc is a dead orc" and so on), which is often cause for ironic commentary from the players; in general the players will usually understand why their characters think the way they do, while also realizing how deeply entangled they are in the socio-cultural web that predetermines much about their beliefs, attitudes and the very social toolbox they have access to in figuring out how to resolve their problems. In all the entire exercise looks to us, the players, like a grand historical tragedy more than some sort of wish-fulfillment fantasy about how great it would be if you were the guy with the sword in a world of hapless peasants.

    One could argue that players who understand better why the bishop is such a thorough asshole about religious freedom will be more prone to support bigotry in the real world (because they have more of a grounding in how and why religious monoculture was upheld in medieval Europe in the first place), but as I described earlier, I don't really think that this is how humans work: understanding how things work makes you less likely to support non-applicable solutions of yesteryear, not more. If you understand why religious liberty was so feared in the medieval times, and how it was necessarily "good" in all situations, you also have a good chance to realize and appreciate how those same societal concerns do not apply in a world of nation states like the one we live in now. Learning occurs, and everybody participating in the game ends up on a more even keel as citizens in the long run.

    These kinds of experiences have convinced me over the years about my let's say liberal stance on the content of roleplaying games. In recent years it has become fashionable to elevate explicitly propagandist games (I do not intend "propaganda" as a slur here, merely a description of what's going on) while "problematizing" other games on strictly superficial basis, based on things like whether the game's setting is sufficiently multicultural. I think that this is an insufficient basis on which to attempt to understand the learning and social conditioning occurring in roleplaying games: there are ironic levels of understanding going on in games, and you can't simply read the political values of a game from its character creation rules. The fact that a game features e.g. racism in the content of play is not, alone, sufficient proof that play of the game advocates for and affirms the racist attitudes of its players.

    \n I certainly use separate rules for elves and dwarves and such - trolls as a practical example from the Scandinavian leg of the campaign.     My sense of Gygax is that he probably was a little bit fascist - or romantic of the colonialist era, if one prefers. Probably not so much politically, but emotionally; his work has a consistent sense of yearning for heroic adventure, after all, the kind that only exists in fiction, and it seems that he never questioned the basic proposition. The picaresque stylings in some of his stuff come closest (the rogue novel as a literary genre is pretty ironic, after all), but the way I read it, he enjoyed the cynical machismo of it more than any antiestablishmentarianism. For him, the vivacious, independent adventurer was the establishment he celebrated, and Alignment (an authoritative top-down morality) was the moral underpinning.

    I agree in general that D&D does a consistently good job of systematically representing a fascist world view in an epic scope. For me this is specifically why it makes for such a good anti-fascist vehicle - it shows us this world, what it looks and feels like inside The Myth of the Twentieth Century. The discourse I mentioned earlier, that RPGs engender - when it comes to D&D, it is a discourse on fascism in its widest sense. The wonderful absurdity, the points on which it is incisive, as well as the ones where it fails when confounded by real-world sensibility; it's all in there, played out in the adventures of Gygax's little fighting miniatures.

    It comes as no surprise that I find the most boring kind of D&D imaginable in the cleaned up corporate version. The biggest sin for me, speaking as an artist, is the relentless effort at historical distance: when you remove the attempts at realism from D&D and proffer it as harmless wish-fulfillment (instead of the "serious wargaming" that it might be viewed as, too), it loses its intellectual honesty, and you really are left with something resembling an unquestioning propaganda vehicle for fascism.

    \n The disagreements over this seem to mostly stem from different understanding of "fascism" on the parts of different discussioneers. Some focus on the idea of "fighting fascism" at the roots, the way art generally works to shape ideas and attitudes; others consider the message of the activity, what kind of narratives and lessons make up the superficial content; yet others consider practical politics and the negligible impact that art in general has on immediate real-world events.

    An example of how these different levels might play out in practice may help illustrate how different are the aspects of the question:

    The Name of the Rose fights fascism by illustrating how negative, fearful crisis thinking can lead man astray, and how enlightenment values help guide you to a better path. It does not directly address fascism by name; the content is at most allegorical, and mostly philosophical and thematic. The novel only fights fascism in the sense that a person taking its lessons to heart has a difficult time fitting fascism into their worldview, too.

    Schindler's List fights fascism by being an anti-nazi propaganda movie: it shows in clear, simple terms what human suffering, cowardice and bravery look like in an actual historical fascist society, and it argues against the values and cultural edifice in question.

    Thomas Mann fought fascism by creating highly politically antagonistic works of art during an era when a fascist political movement was in control of his country. He was, obviously, censored by a fascist government. Interestingly he worked in rhetoric at the time (instead of writing, for which he is more well-known), by broadcasting speeches to Germany from outside the country. This is where the line between an activity of art and an activity of political agitation begins to blur, when the art is being motivated by immediate practical needs.

    Eugen Schauman was a Finnish nationalist who murdered the Russian governor-general of Finland during the "Years of Oppression" in 1904. He fought fascism tooth and nail, with extreme, self-destructive zeal, yet opting still for a symbolic gesture instead of concrete long-term action, which sort of makes him count as an example of what "art" might look like if it actually fought fascism in the literal sense of the word. Any more concrete than that, and it's not even performance art or propaganda of the deed anymore.

    Of those four, which are actually "fighting fascism"? Are any of them? All of them? They all may be considered works of art or culture at least in some way, and they all are works that a fascist would probably prefer to not have ever been brought to light. Interesting how volatile the concept of "fighting" can be, when applied to the arts.

    I think it's likely that we do not mostly disagree about the facts (as in, what a given game does or doesn't do), it's all just quibbling about what "fascism" means or what "fighting" means. Similarly, if one thinks that only Benito Mussolini's historical Fascist party was fascist, and nothing else is, then most games probably do not fight fascism in any shape or form - even the ones that might include the historical Fascist party in the game are probably not extremely stridently opposed to a dead and buried Italian popular movement.

    Gaming as art is the idea that we engage games proactively as a form of communication and philosophy, and therefore games should be honest and incisive in a way that fosters personal growth, or at least doesn't stifle it. The big problems for this view are games that trivialize issues and bamboozle their players into false contentment with superficial truths. For this idea D&D is not as problematic as for the political perspective, but it's still a problem when gaming gets consumerist, commercial and corporate, because the corporations do not want us to grow beyond our assigned roles as consumers, and that shows in conspiciously consumerist games like modern D&D.

    Gaming as entertainment is the idea that games are a sanctuary from the troubles of the real world, and using them as vehicles for politics or philosophy is intrusive, disruptive and ultimately a breach of a social contract in the interest of exploiting other people who are here just to have a good time. Fascist content in games is much less of an issue for this perspective than it is for the two other ones, because it's just entertainment - if fascism entertains, then that's its proper place. D&D certainly is not problematic, it's the most popular rpg ever and a fine pastime.

    Perhaps that labeling helps a little in figuring out what's going on with the different perspectives people bring into this. It is pretty common in rpg discussions in general for people to misunderstand each other due to these sorts of fundamental differences in perspective. It does not help that the perspectives synergize on various ways for various people: for example, it's pretty common for people to simply consider art to be entertaining, which means that e.g. I am myself not somehow opposed to the idea that gaming is entertainment - I just happen to think that for it to be worthwhile entertainment for me, it needs to be art

      \n As I understand it, the "civil rights angle" in these discussions is usually, intentionally or not, something of a simile. This is perhaps because people sort of lack a vocabulary for saying precisely what they mean.

      I might be wrong, but this is what the exchange looks like to me:

      Civil rights activist A: "We should be hostile to bad ideas and use our liberty of association to shun bad people. That's the only way to take the strength away from fascists."

      Civil rights activist B: "I don't think that it is a good idea to compromise on free speech in our fight against fascism. If we do, we're just as bad as them. We should rather confront them in public and conduct ourselves better than they do, thus showing the fence-sitters the hollowness of their creed."

      Civil rights activist A: "But this is not about free speech, they can go speak elsewhere. I am only advocating for social shunning, not curtailing legal rights."

      Now, the bit where these two people are misunderstanding each other occurs at the moment when activist B chooses to characterize his issue as a "free speech" problem, attempting to bolster his stance with a call-back to a cherished value of free society. I do not believe that this is what B actually intends - as A rightly remarks, people do not actually have a compulsory duty to listen to other people's free speech. I believe that what B is getting at is somewhat more subtle, and by dressing it up with this particular term he ends up derailing his argument.

      What is the real problem B has, then? I think it is that he believes that patient political discourse even with your opponents is an utmost virtue, because when you stop talking and start treating other people as strictly external enemies, that's the first step towards true hostility: once you stop talking to each other you've fulfilled one of the basic requirements on the road towards the moment when you are ready to e.g. kill another human being, full of belief in their monstrosity. This is the trend that activist B in that discussion is probably worried about, he's just explaining himself badly.

      (If that scenario seems far-fetched, it's good to remember that people come into these discussions from very different backgrounds: for some a political debate like this is really important in terms of immediate social stakes; for some other people the debate might hold deep resemblance to past errors in the body politic of their own society; yet others might be participating just for entertainment. It is entirely credible that some people, when faced with anti-humanist radicalism, will react with violent shunning, while others will react by trying their hardest to maintain the bridges of communication so as to avoid further radicalization between the camps.)

      It would probably not be far off the mark to characterize these two activists, A and B, as being "radical" and "centrist" in political terms: A is approaching the problem of fascism as an external issue, an enemy to be purged by ostracizing them to death (figuratively). For B the greater threat of fascism is not necessarily fascism itself, but rather the societal consequences of the fight: if the fight against fascism is conducted in a brutal way that treats people without empathy, then this will, so B fears, lead into further aggravation of relations - a cycle of hostility that causes both sides to lay aside words and take up arms instead. From his viewpoint things could be so much worse than they already are, and at least everything's still pretty good when the fascists and anti-fascists are shouting at each other; better to continue debating than to start shunning each other. Even better if you can make friends despite political disagreements, and change each other for the better that way - B is, I think, a big believer in inherent value of human life.

      Of course that's just my understanding - maybe some people involved in these discussions genuinely believe that free speech as a value of society is in peril.
