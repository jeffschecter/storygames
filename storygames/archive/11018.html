\n
\n Hey, all! It's time to start thinking about DREAMATION 2010. The convention is February 18-21, 2010 at the Morristown Hyatt & Conference Center in Morristown, New Jersey. Last year the Indie Games Explosion scheduled 104 events. We hope for an even bigger Explosion this year!
\n
\nThere are few new things going on this year:
\n
\nPLAYTEST TRACK
\nDouble Exposure has opened a special track explicitly for games being playtested at the convention. Last year, the IGE had 10 playtest events. Hopefully we'll have even more this year. Dreamation is a great place to playtest your game-in-development. Double Exposure is even soliciting dedicated playtesters for the convention.
\n
\nEVENT TITLES
\nAll events must have a specific title. The name of the game system will not suffice. If you don't supply a title, Kat and I will do our best to get in touch and request one. Failing that, we'll assign something appropriate.
\n
\nMULTI-SLOT GAME EVENTS
\nDouble Exposure has a new policy regarding events that accommodate fewer players. Events for 3 or 4 players should be offered in at least three different timeslots, or be prepared for three different GMs running it at different tables in the same timeslot. Likewise, events for 5 players should be offered in at least two different timeslots, or be prepared for two different GMs running it at different tables in the same timeslot.
\n
\nWhat I'd love to see happen is for people to form partnerships and collaborate on designing events for the con. For instance, I'd like to take another stab at running Mouse Guard (4 players), but don't currently have a strong idea for a scenario I'd like to run. If someone else wants to run MG less than 3 times, I'd love to collaborate with them on a scenario. I'm not sure whether a dedicated page on the wiki, or a dedicated forum thread would be the best way to solicit these types of partnerships. What do you think?
\n
\nWIKI
\nThe IGE wiki for Dreamation 2010 is located here. Once you've settled on events, please add them to the Events page, using the "Comments" box. Unfortunately, the wiki got hit by a bot over the summer, so we've password-protected the edit function on all pages. You can still edit pages, you just need to use the password "ige2009"
\n
\nPARTY!
\nMindy and Mendel did a great job organizing the party last year! I hope they're willing to help out again this year, but I haven't spoken with them. If anyone wants to offer advice or assistance, please do so. The wiki page for the party is here.
\n
\nDEADLINES
\nDeadline for event submissions is January 8, 2010. This is right on the heels of the holidays, so I suggest getting started on brainstorming and event collaboration soon.
\n
\nEvents submitted after this deadline have absolutely NO assurance of making onto the final schedule. We'll do what we can, but we can make no promises. If the deadline is going to be a problem, let us know via e-mail as soon as possible. The IGE has a gmail account: indiegamesexplosion AT gmail DOT com
\n
\nWHAT NEXT?
\nIf you want to come out to the greatest gathering of indie goodness on the east coast, what should you do next? Any or all of the above:
\n
\n1. If you want to playtest a game, sign it up on the Events page.
\n
\n2. If you want to run a game, think about how many players, and how many sessions you're willing to run. Seek out potential collaborators. When you've finished, Sign it up on the Events page.
\n
\n3. If you want to help with the party, post to the party page.
\n
\n4. If you want to come to the convention and playtest at least 4 games, go to the Double Exposure Dreamation page and sign up for the playtest track.
\n
\n5. If you want to come to the convention, play cool games, and meet cool people, go to the Double Exposure Dreamation page and pre-register for the con.
\n
\n6. Show up in Morristown in February and get your geek on!

    \n All these changes originated with Vinnie. He's trying to address problems that impact his bottom line and I respect that. What we do differs in fundamental and non-optimal ways from the RPGA model from his perspective, which is the perspective of making money. The playtest thing, although weird, is his attempt to accommodate our special snowflake needs, because he also recognizes that we are a big draw and he has no desire to alienate us or kill our fun. He talked this over and got some feedback as well, so this isn't coming out of left field.

    I'm willing to try it his way. I know if it is a train wreck it'll be changed next year. It may not be a train wreck.

    For Luke and Fred Hicks, who are the two guys I can see having significant personal draw as individuals who people specifically want to play with, it is not a good model. But I'm not sure any model is a good model if a lot of people want a piece of you.

      \n
      \nA few points of clarification. First of all, I'm running very late
      \nwith my whole concept for the Playtest track because my mother went
      \ninto the hospital with kidney failure over New Year's. Were it not for
      \nthat hole in my time, I would have posted a lot more info to the Indie
      \npublishers about what is going on.
      \n
      \nNow, the Playtest track has NOTHING to do with "my bottom line"
      \n(Jason, I'm surprised at you for thinking that). In fact, I'm taking a
      \nhit on registrations ($900 worth if you count the number of free
      \nbadges I just gave out). I'm doing this because I am trying to solve
      \nseveral problems regarding playtests. I want every Indie publisher,
      \nwhether he is very popular or just starting out, to be able to run a
      \nplaytest at DREAMATION and be sure to have at least some playtesters
      \nto work with.
      \n
      \nIn prior discussions, one issue which has come up has been that some
      \nplaytests immediately fill up with very experienced players (including
      \nother Indies) and that it became impractical to "just offer them as
      \nregular events" because the goal of the designers was not really being
      \nmet. Another issue was empty tables. My idea was to give each playtest
      \na starting pool of players and then allow the publisher to add a
      \ncouple of people of his/her choosing. Most of the 17 Indie playtest
      \ngames officially running were looking for 6 players and I'm going to
      \nfill them with 4 guaranteed players - and more importantly, 4
      \nguaranteed DIFFERENT players in the case of playtests which are
      \nrunning multiple times.
      \n
      \nI see this as a real bonus for every Indie publisher to know that
      \nthere is a guaranteed audience for his/her new ideas, and although
      \nthis is starting small, I hope it will expand greatly in the future.
      \n
      \nIf it doesn't work, I'll shrug my shoulders and drop it. The
      \nsuggestions given that open playtests should also be done are good
      \nones, but I'll make sure that every one of the 17 playtest games at
      \nDREAMATION will at least go off with a mix of real players. You as the
      \npublisher can now pull a couple of friends you want in each game
      \nonsite - I'll make sure everyone knows exactly what is waiting at each
      \ntable before the convention.
      \n
      \nJason turned my attention to this thread a little later than I would
      \nhave liked, because I read something else here which is just flat out
      \nwrong. I have not mandated that any publisher run any given game the
      \nexact same way three times, nor was there any magic number. In
      \naddition, it has been explained to me last year in great detail that
      \nmany Indie games were not "modules" and were in fact just game systems
      \nthat would take a different story each time they were run.
      \n
      \nMy public objections had to do with popular GMs/publishers running 1
      \nor 2 slots of 4 players each and me having 16-20 players trying to get
      \ninto them. Therefore I asked if you could all please run popular games
      \n(or modules where appropriate) multiple times to allow the players
      \ngreater opportunity to partake. If the Indie section gets too insular,
      \nyou start losing your ability to interact with the audience that
      \nmatters most, and I believe I'm helping everyone by pushing for a more
      \nopen playing field. You don't hear the grumblings and complaints I
      \nhear from your potential customers about how they can never get to
      \nplay an Indie game so they stopped trying.
      \n
      \nIn no way, shape or form did this ever become a mandate of "run the
