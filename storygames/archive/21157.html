    \n It is true that some larps are basically set up as a bunch of people in a room, talking - thus, the difference between them being larp or tabletop RPG isn't huge. A larp you can acquire a written scenario for on the Internet is more likely to be in this category.

    I was in the opposite place sometimes, where the player base I was catering to demanded more larp scenarios and I ended up "larpifying" games that were originally designed for the tabletop, such as Montsegur 1244.

    The main insight I got is: the key design difference between a replayable larp scenario vs. a one-shot tabletop role-playing scenario lies not in stage production values, but in expectations about out-of-character talk.

    With a few notable exceptions, tabletop RPG players are expected to be casual and fluid in switching between "in character" and "OOC" talk. OOC talk is, in fact, a valuable and - probably - necessary tool for building a shared imagined space. Getting small cues as to when a player is speaking for their character, when they're stating what they're character does and when they're, you know, just talking (but that talking isn't a game "move" per se) is a key, basic player skill.

    Larp structures the conversation more formally in these regards. With several exceptions, players in a larp are expected to always be speaking in their character's voice, to always being acting out or at least miming their character's actions and body language, and to never be chatting out of character. Departures from that default situations are to be clearly signaled to other players, often only happen as prescribed by the game designer (or organizer), and are in fact what a large portion of larp design work is about - encoding OOC signals, defining areas you can't play out in-character, structuring play time into in-character moments and OOC breaks, and so on.

    \n Yes, Rafu! JUGGERNAUT in particular is intended to be always on once you start play. Of course you can break character to clarify or explain or ask a question, but by design that is an aberration. Compare with The Climb, where explicitly OOC stuff goes on, and half the players will have OOC tasks to accomplish, and this is clearly spelled out. Both games are easy for tabletop folks to understand and play, but they approach things very differently.

    @Jeff_B_Slater are you looking for freeform games and larps that translate well to a tabletop experience? You might look at some of the Fastaval games, which are usually intended to be played in a sort of hybrid mode. I recommend Vasen Road and Dulce et Decorum particularly.
