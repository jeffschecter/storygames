\n That title's a good filter! Only trufans are reading now, right?

So my Wednesday group is trying Spirit of the Century for the first time. Last night was devoted to character generation. I've read SOTC, and played a convention one-shot, but in the back of my mind I've been a little hesitant. It's everybody's darling so it can't really be great, can it? Can it?

Character creation:

... is a group affair, and a sort of game in itself
... involves the creation of explicit flags
... guides characters into fun niches and protects them there
... involves characters in each other's back stories (and encourages character-to-character flags)
... has a satisfying degree of crunchy decision-making

So as a preparatory activity with an eye toward play, it's the bee's knees. How am I going to intertwine "Digger" Watkins, the Pennsylvania Mole Man, with Princess Anastasia Romanova, on the run across Europe with a Gypsy caravan, hunted by Soviet assassins? How will I tune my stunts so that I can trick out the Electrical Mole Car? Why are we not playing yet?

Which leads to the only potential hitch in the whole process - it takes a lot of time. But it's fun, social time, and by the end of it we had a really crazy cast of pulp ne'er-do-wells and were itching to play. It's noteworthy that my brother stumbled in an hour and a half late and we got him up to speed and into the mix with no trouble.

I know all this is not news to you trufans, but I was really impressed.

    \nThe trick is to pick a managable subset. You don't need all of the PC's aspects at your fingertips -- just a small subsection (say, 2, maybe 3) for each one that you think will warrant strong/frequent compels.

    Stunts' effects should definitely be written on character sheets if you can manage it when prepping -- avoid the need to go heads-down and reference the book. Heads-up gaming is where it's at.

    NPCs should only be detailed inasmuch as you need them to be -- think 'characters on the fly', here. A few peak skills, one or two aspects, a few stunt effects if warranted, that's it.

    \n OK, we had our first "play" session last night. Steve set up a pair of broad conflicts - one was a big-ass fight in a Washington, DC speakeasy, and the other was a hostage-taking at a fancy dress ball at the British Museum. We were feeling our way with an unfamiliar system and we were just trying to showcase the system and get our bearings. These are my initial impressions.

    I was surprised at the sheer number of moving parts - my head was spinning. I offer a compel, I block, I maneuver, that gets me a +2 or some other thing, I re-roll, how does that stunt work, I create an aspect but it is only temporary, now it's gone, that's an assessment, consult book, no you can't use that skill to assess, it was a lot to take in.

    We were all shocked at how hard it is to impactanother character. Like, we worked our asses off to create a minor consequence for this KKK goon. And it was a minor consequence! Three more to go before he's down for good unless we accept his concessions. It took three of us all focusing on this one guy to bring him down, and even then I think Steve just gave up.

    I had a huge hippie disconnect with the fact that I'm not able to author anything. Or rather, I can author steel beams falling from the ceiling but I can't author the contents of a dude's pocket, or his motivation.

    We had many situations where contestants tied rolls, and nothing about the situation changed. One time I climbed across some girders and dropped on a guy like a spider monkey! Tied roll after I used all applicable aspects, nothing changed.

    Maybe we were using compels wrong, but they didn't seem particularly engaging. Under what circumstances would I not want to make things more, rather than less, interesting? Nobody refused a compel, and we all offered up a bunch ourselves. I wanted a way to compel other player characters, which mechanically makes no sense.

    My guy has "Afraid of the Dark" as an aspect. Steve compelled it in the speakeasy fight, and bang! The lights went out! Awesome! And I didn't really know what to do. I couldn't connect that to the game in any meaningful way - so was I supposed to have him quake in fear? There was no interface with the system other than me getting a reward for agreeing that the lights should go off. I decided a mechanic who was scared of the dark probably carried a light source out of habit, and he turned on a carbon arc lamp, so we were back where we started before the compel.

    So it went a little rough for me. I did get to drive the Mole Car through a wall as my impromptu sonic weapon gadget screeched "Rule Brittania", though.

    \n Fate points for declarations, yes. Very important.

    And you can use any skill to assess. The assess stuff in the book is examples. Actually, everything in the book is an example. Go ahead and use Guns to assess something, as long as it has to do with Guns.

    Another important bit (IMO) that is tucked away as an example: Tag an aspect to do a skill substitution. It's explained under Might, as a trapping, but you can do it with anything. Tag the "Darkness" aspect to substitute your Stealth skill for Fists and ninja-ambush some fool. This lets the Professor types kick serious ass once they have some Fate stored up.

    Oh! And Compel for Effect. Do this a lot. Sure, bad guys can be really hard to bring down if you're just doing harm. But once he has that minor consequence aspect, like, "All beaten up," you can compel it for effect. Like, "Your ass is all beat up. You run away." [dangle a Fate chip]. Or, if you know he has "Loves to gloat" then you can compel that for effect, too: "You're too busy gloating to keep us from grabbing the jewel and getting away." It's a great way to jump to a decisive result without having to whittle away at each other forever.

    \nAs a player, you can spend a fate point to tag another character's aspect for effect - effectively, putting a compel on that character. It could be another PC or an NPC. If it's an NPC controlled by the GM, they can work the process just like you can - the GM can give you a fate point to say, "Nope, that NPC's not going to run away," or let you spend it and make it so. If it's a named NPC, and not just a mook, that NPC would get a fate point.

    When I run it, I'm strict about the fate point totals of named NPCs - they participate in the invoke/compel economy just like PCs. When they're out, I don't give them any more. Minions never get any, unless they gain one from a tag in the middle of a conflict. Strictly speaking, then, I care about getting the fate point if that character needs fate points. Generally speaking, I care about the opportunity for structured Drama resolution that compels provide.

    Also, you can use skills to make declarations as well, as per the "Assessments and Declarations" rules. You don't always have to spend a fate point for it. I don't see why that wouldn't extend to using something like Empathy to say, "I'm guessing this person is motivated by deep self-loathing." I'd take that offer, possibly even in contradiction to my notes.

    Conflicts between named characters can take some time, and it's pretty much everybody's love/hate thing: I know folks who are totally about the hour-long setpiece, and I know folks who consider the system broken because of this. The trick is to focus on stacking free tags through maneuvers, so it's ultimately better to, say, set the room On Fire and get your opponent Cornered before you do an attack, because then you can rack up the +4 when you attack. Also, unless you're looking for a major setpiece, I'd have just about everyone take damage like a minion - maybe if it's a major lieutenant NPC, you can let them take some consequences too.

    \n There are a few things worth saying out loud here:

    - The stress track and consequences system in SOTC are a little broken from the perspective of keeping fights moving fast. I prefer the implementation we talk about on our wiki: a basis of 3 stress boxes instead of 5, and the "-2/-4/-6 consequences" rule. http://evilhat.wikidot.com/faster-conflicts for more.

    - We had a prohibition in the rules against stacking free tags. That's dumb. If you can set up a bunch of temporary aspects with free tags on 'em, hit it! Use 'em all!

    - Story-gamers take note: John's right that it's sort of a "hidden" feature in SOTC that every skill can be used to make Declarations. Look to the Skills chapter for the section on Assessments and Declarations. Here's behind the curtain: Assessments are for groups that are more comfortable with the GM coming up with stuff on the fly. Declarations are for groups that want the players to come up with stuff on the fly. So feel free to adapt the Declaration method to every skill you can figure something out for. Say, you're playing a martial artist with a strong Fists skill. Use the Fists skill to Declare that the other guy's Kung Fu has a weakness in its technique. The other guy defends with his Fists; if you succeed, your fact is true, and manifests as an aspect on him! Note that this is almost EXACTLY like a combat maneuver ("I sweep his legs out from under him, giving him an On His Back aspect!") only with the cognitive shift to creating new facts instead of taking actions. Which is the *real* secret behind it all -- Assessment, Declaration, and Maneuver are all the same basic action in the system: use your skill to assert authority over the world, which will manifest as a temporary aspect; the only difference between them is how the scope of your authority is perceived. With assessments, the GM retains authority to create new facts about the world; with declarations, the Player has authority to create new facts about the world; with maneuvers, the action takes place within the context of already-established facts. That's it.

    As to the first two items, well, that's what happens when you don't have hundreds of people playtesting your game before you release it.

    As to the last one -- we're trying to present rules in a way that doesn't threaten the "traditional gamer" too much right out of the box, so that stuff ended up a little more disguised than, perhaps, it should've been.

    Hope this helps!

      \nThe rules actually specify that fate points go to you if someone tags an aspect that's on you, so scene aspects don't count if you're doing a strict interpretation of the text. This is the intended default. Tagging any of your aspects that have been assessed counts, tagging your consequences counts, and tagging any temporary aspect from a maneuver that's been made sticky counts. He could run away, cower uselessly, go catatonic - or something more useful even, like create a quick escape route, by digging or busting out a window. What he can't do is help his friends. And later, there needs to be a scene where someone goes, "Cripes, Digger, we could have freakin' used your help in there. What's the matter with you?"

      Now, keep in mind that you totally could narrate your way out of that limitation any number of different ways. It's totally reasonable that Mole Man would carry a light source to keep this kind of thing from happening. That isn't the point. The point is, you're choosing to create a dramatic moment. The light source could totally be how you pay out to avoid the compel - "Leon starts shaking, but then he remembers his lamp and quickly flicks it on!" Or it could be grounds for the group calling that compel lame, if there's too easy of an out.

      And when you pay out to resist a compel, it's the limitation itself that you're saying no to. So, Ryan, suppose you've got Mole Man in the dark mine tunnel. You throw out the fate point and say, "It's pitch black suddenly, and you are scared shitless. Are you sure you want to be in here?" And Jason might run away, or he might go, "You know, maybe it'll be light somewhere up ahead! I totally charge forward headlong, scared out of my mind, no matter what might happen to me." That's not refusing the compel, that's the player walking right into the hands of the bad guys or monsters to get captured, half-eaten, or whatever. He's still accepting that he can't be in here and keep his wits about him.

      Which is not to say that you shouldn't put out the obvious suggestion. Just remember that's all it is, a suggestion, and that there's a negotiation process there for a reason, because there's more than one way to make a situation complicated and to make that complication matter.
