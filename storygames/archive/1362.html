Improving the 3 hours, 40 minutes

Josh Roby
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Play Advice
Posted By: Doug RuffHow about this: you know the whole "20 minutes of fun in 4 hours" problem? There's several ways of solving this problem.

Low-investment gaming is all about "let's focus on the 20 good minutes, and turn that into an hour's good gaming" Serial short gaming is similar, except you're stringing those 20+ minute sessions together. In both approaches, there is a much faster pace to the game, and because there is so much more going on, the "hit rate" of good sessions is much higher.

However, there's another approach to the problem, which is "let's improve the 3 hours and 40 minutes so that it is as fun as the 20 good minutes". The play is still as slow (in story pacing terms) as before, but it's quality slow gaming.

(Minor diversion: I think this is part of the reason that "story" gaming and "immersive" gaming don't mix. Diversion ends.)
Okay, I can see the rhetorical value of this argument, but I'm not seeing the practical value. To me, this sounds like "You know how barbeque ribs are a pain in the ass because they take a lot of work to eat off all the meat, and then you toss out the bones? Here's my idea: make the bones tastier." That 3hr40min chuck has always seemed like waste to me, properly discarded. How can it be put to good use? How can it be fun? I'm really asking; I'd really like to understand how this can work.

Can somebody flesh this idea out (har har), reference some published (by which I mean 'complete', not 'paper') designs, or point me at some actual play? What does play look like when the strategy is improving the 3 hours, 40 minutes, rather than just cutting them out?

    Here's what I considered the "other 3 hours 40 minutes" in my recent AD&D 2e group:

    - Planning (this often took three out of six play hours)
    - Calculating encumbrances, rations, etc.
    - Figuring out whether my gnome falls down the hillside on a random trip

    a) No clear direction
    b) Rules/details that are boring
    c) Planning instead of acting
    d) No aggressive scene framing
    e) Player exclusion due to character actions/limitations
    f) Playing through lengthy actions even in the absence of conflict

      I think you are describing the twenty minutes here, not the 3 hours and 40 minutes; I'd like to call this intense playing. Things are happening and moving forward, players are involved (as in, leaning in, very active, driving things forward in fast pace). Or I could be reading you wrong.

      As for the rest of the time, I don't really have any new pieces to add the one's Joshua listed. I'd like to combine some of those by saying that you'll have to make the less-intense phases of playing meaningfull.

      Let's take a look at these:

      * setting exploration
      * character development (immersion-wise)
      * in-character dialogue
      * immersion
      I see these as things which kind of relate and add to each other. In-character dialogue becomes meaningfull when characters have lot of unresolved issues and complexity, both regarding to one character (internal character issue), a group of characters (social relationships, differences, secrets, etc) and the setting (like... A lot of stuff, from racism to waking up in a strange place).

      Lenghty In-character dialogue is not very intresting if viewed from third-party perspective (the difference between making someone - the character - to talk and talking by yourself, as the character). It becomes a lot more intresting if there are plenty of unresolved issues and if you work it through with all the failings of human communication. Picturing a conversation of between three persons with an unfulfilled love-triangle (A lusts after B who lusts after C who lusts after something completely else) is intresting, but can be quickly settled if players just wish to see what happens. The same discussion bolts to an entirely different level when the same persons have much more social tapestry (person B lusts after C, but is kind of fond of A, but loathes C, etc) and if you picture yourself as person B. Perhaps you have been in a similar situation? How long did it take until the whole thing was resolved? How emotional was that time? How long did it take before you confessed your feelings to person C and how hard was that? Did you feel humiliated when he/she told you that you're not really his/her type? And what did it feel like when person A told you about his/her feelings? Picture yourself gravitating towards person C, acting all-out-stupid, feeling the shame and the rush at the same time. All the body language you didn't even know to exist. The uncertainty.

      Now, picture yourself as a totally different person - if you're an introvert, shy guy, picture yourself as mr Smooth Operator and think how you'd handle the thing. Or if you are mr Smooth Operator, picture yourself as that shy, quiet guy. Act under these constraints. Let yourself go (as much as possible). Lust after that person B or just humiliate yourself trying.

      Add plenty of other stuff, like setting issues; you're a black guy in 50's America, and person B is a liberal-minded white gal with a father who has racist issues. Or something.

      I'm guessing here, but there usually seems to be some kind of safety net, separating the player and character (the difference between thinking "Oh damn, he's in a bad position, now - how cool" and "I'm in a bad position now and boy, does it suck"). Throw away that safety net (just remember to use another safety net, a different social contract) for a while - get involved emotionally. Immerse yourself into another human being, with a rich social tapestry, along with other people who act just like, well, people. Don't abstract things like a lot of other mediums do.

      That's one way I like to fill the 3 hours and 40 minutes; even if it looks less-intense than those adrenalin-filled twenty minutes, it's engrossing and it's deeply meaningfull. Those twenty minutes of high intensity are still coming, but with ground-work like this, they are going to rock the boat like nothing else. Because they are, in a sense, happening to you and people you know, care and hate.

      Jukka,

      I hear what you're saying. For the record, I try to do both. (Or to do both for my play group. I'm not immersion-capable myself, but most of the people I play with are.)

      The key to getting at it, I think, is that you can do both what Judd is talking about and what you are talking about at the same time. You're just using different tools because you have different goals. If everyone in your group is really intensely into the immersion and IC social framework of the game, then you don't spend time doing shoping lists of weapons (unless, of course, that is important to said social framework) -- because that isn't what is going to get the fun on. You go for the scenes where person B lusts after C, but is kind of fond of A, but loathes C, etc, and skip the stuff where you're assigning your next dot to melee because you hope to be able to get the Heaven Thunder Hammer next game.

      The key issue in Judd's post is that you figure out what you want in your game, and go for that. If you want a living world with a ripe social situation that you can explore from the inside, then you need to go for that. It doesn't have to be the kind of gonzo "everything right now" play that you get in other games. That's not the point. The point is "doing what you want to be doing in game all the time, rather than doing something you don't want to do so that you can maybe someday do what you want to."

      So, if you want that immsersive world stuff (good stuff), then you need to be finding ways to get it as much as possible. And no, this doesn't mean having constant climax. Everyone with half a brain knows you need build up for climax, and if you're playing at a different pace then your length of build up will be different. That's fine and good so long as the build up itself is fun.

      Although, at this point I'm tempted to make a metaphor about sex and about how guys are always rushing for the end goal....
