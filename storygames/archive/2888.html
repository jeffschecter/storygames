\n When I'm scribbling out game designs I'm often thinking of my rules as either carrots or sticks. Mostly with the intention of keeping my designs as carrot-heavy as possible.     \n Eric: Try this description on for size (noting, of course, that it is my personal, well-nigh-heretical, take on Push & Pull): Every moment of every game, you are both imposing your own will (to some extent) upon the game and giving other people opportunities (to some extent) to impose their will. A moment is Push for you when you explicitly pay attention to the way you are imposing your will upon the game. A moment is Pull for you when you explicitly pay attention to the way you are giving other people opportunities to impose their will. It's not about what you do, it's about how you think about it (although, clearly, there are some things you will have a difficult time perceiving as Pull, and some things you will have a difficult time perceiving as Push).

    \n Sounds heretical to me. I wasn't on the original discussion here, but I think that it's interesting that these exact same terms were used by the Traveller folks way back in their GM advice rules. Here's what I find useful:
    \n
    \nCarrot: Mechanical reward for a certain sort of behavior.
    \nStick: Mechanical penalty for a certain sort of behavior, which hopefully brings about the opposite behavior.
    \nPush: Player provided impetus intended to get a player to have a character do something by creating a situation that seems to force the issue.
    \nPull: Player provided impetus intended to get a player to have a character do something by creating a situation that would seem to attract the character of the target player.
    \n
    \nExamples:
    \nCarrot: Bonus dice in Sorcerer for cool description allows the player to select for cool descriptions, and get rewarded.
    \nStick: Penalties to having a character downhill in D&D; give players incentive to have their characters seek high ground in fights.
    \nPush: The GM says that the COPs are coming to arrest the character, if they don't leave immediately, which intends to get the player to have the character leave the scene.
    \nPull: The GM has an NPC tell you that they'll pay you 2 Million Credits if you take the whatsis to Planet X, intending to get the player to have his character go on the adventure.
    \n
    \nCarrot's and Sticks hit the player. Pushes and Pulls hit the character, and maybe the player, if he feels like playing along.
    \n
    \nYou can see my bias showing in this analysis, of course. See also "Grabs" vs "Hooks." Grabs are situation that engages the player. "Hooks" are situations aimed at forcing the player to engage by making alternate behavior implausible.

    \n As I understand it, "Push" and "Pull" came from a metaphor of Indian wrestling (Stand with your right big toe at your opponent's right heel; grasp his right thumb with your right hand. he does the same. Plant both feet. First person to move a foot loses).
    \n
    \nA "Push" is an attempt to get the other person to do something by occupying a space and challenging them to enter it.
    \n
    \nA "Pull" is an attempt to get the other person to do something by vacating a space and causing them to fall into it.
    \n
    \nBoth are attempts to manipulate the other person into acting.

    \n When you add something to the shared imaginative space that everyone else must work with (or against), that follows 'Push' technique. Ignoring 'Push' is usually at cost to the 'everyone else'.

    When you add something to the shared imaginative space that someone else may layer upon, add to or ignore, that follows 'Pull' technique. Ignoring 'Pull' is usually no cost to 'everyone else' and might cost the Player who added it.

    YMMV

    I think traditional 'carrot' and 'stick' are often two kinds of 'Push'.

    \n "Carrot" and "Stick" are clearly synonyms for "Reward" and "Punishment".

    in a word: Consequences.

    "Operant Conditioning" is the psychology term for how Behavior is influenced by consequences.

    The terminology of Operant Conditioning becomes kinda confusing, in that "positive" refers to anything-- any reward OR punishment
    added in response to a behavior. And negative refers to anything-- any punishment OR reward which removes something in response to a behavior.

    So the seemingly-oxymoronic term "positive punishment" would be exemplified by spanking (adding pain).
    And "negative reward" would be something like freeing a prisoner for confessing (removing confinement).


    I offer this as an example of how the terminology of Push & Pull ought to be standardized-- in order for discussion to make any fxcking sense.

    If behaviorists didn't all agree that "Positive" and "Negative" only refer to adding and removing things/ and some behaviorists insisted on
    using those terms as a value judgement on the nature of stimuli (all spanking is negative!)-- their words would be misread by everyone else.


    In the case of Push & Pull, there's a another layer of confusing connotation-- that Push is pushy-bad-bully-male and Pull is polite-good-cooperative-girly.
    Or, from another bias direction-- that Push is straightforward and Pull is passive-aggressive.


    While I'm at it-- I'll note another operant conditioning term: "Extinction"-- which means a lack of consequence to a behavior. Like if you say hello to a wall,
    and it does nothing in response-- you'll eventually stop talking to it. Actually, I think extinction ought to refer to the part where you give up trying-- that what it
    sounds like it should mean. But according to wikipedia, it refers to the non-response.

    Does the mere absence of Push constitute Pull? Is lack-of-consequence a Pull technique?


    The only reason I know any of this psych jargon, is because of the wikipedia entry on dog training, which I found helpful with an actual puppy. That entry links to other design-relevant entries, such as "reinforcement" and "reward system".

    There's also a Learning Theory section about the 4 markers or signals which one can give a dog:
    (Release marker, Keep going signal, 'No reward' marker, and Punishment marker)
    These terms are closely related to the others I've offered, so they too may be helpful for expressing discreet-but-similar concepts which have previously been conflated in past discussion of Push & Pull.


    I would love it if someone would describe a hypothetical example of a person using Push with a dog,
    and an example of using Pull with a dog.
