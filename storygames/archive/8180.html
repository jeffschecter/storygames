    \n Egads indeed.

    There are a couple of 'standard' options that I tend to offer for French characters, focussing on different areas of the wartime and post-war experience.

    First off, the former resistant is always a popular character and gives a great basis for some really compelling hidden agendas: Seeking out collaborators at large, hiding the fact that they themselves were a collaborator, getting involved in the murky world of governmental intelligence work rather than the more 'simple' life of the wartime partisan and so on.

    Then there is the chaos of the French business world, the shady connections between the military, government and industry. While this was by no means unique to France, the ties between significant politcal and industrial figures ran very, very deep. Various re-armament programmes such as the disastrous ARL-44 tank were dogged by this kind of thing. So, there are plenty of real-life machinations to translate into Cold City terms.

    Oh, and what about France making use of German technology? Not just the weird stuff from CC, but, for example, a regiment of the Army was still using captured Panther tanks right up to 1950. Maybe there is something in stuff like that? Maybe France could be the nation that is most open about its use of the captured technology and must do things to avoid the anger of its allies?

    And as Alexandre mentioned, there is Indochina. It might be a long way away from Berlin, but the situation in Indochina was huge for France. The quest for international co-operation in order to re-assert their status as a colonial power could be really big. Interest factet: during the disaster as Dien Bien Phu in 1954, no less a figure than John Foster Dulles suggested that the US might 'lend' France an atom bomb to save the situation. In the world of Cold City, what other technologies might get used? Are there things in Berlin that could be of use in Indochina?

    Just a few thoughts. I'll try to offer something more detailed and coherent in the near future. I hope that we can get some insight from folks with a more in-depth knowledge of modern French history as well.

    Cheers
    Malc

    \n Another aspect of post-war France is the amazing success of the Communist Party who managed to seduce more than just proletariat. They grabbed a minimum of 25% of the parliament's seats between 1947 and 1956 while they were competing with a quite strong socialist party too. PCF was really in plain admiration for everything from the Soviet and remained loyal to Stalin even after his death.

    An interesting angle about the Resistance is how some of them who suffered political police oppression under the Gestapo didn't cringe at using similar methods after the Nazis went down. It baffles me how someone could inflict torture on freedom fighters (like Algerian independence fighters) when they were persecuted the very same way for the very same reasons... There's probably nice paradoxical character concepts along those lines.

    \ni my opinion, this is (unfortunately) an anachronism, althought there is something very important here.

    First, the trait\xe9 de Rome start in 1957 (EEC). In the late 40's - earlies 50's, it's time for the CECA ( CECA).

    The point is that at the end of the WWII it all begun again like at the end of WWI : french were afraid over all and they wanted to weaker germany or at least to avoid it to become one day ,once again, both a powerful and aggressive nation.

    There was no clear strategy at this time expect about the coal resources. That was in the middle of XXth the key for economy ( factories and steel). And as you know, the most important fields are in the Ruhr. So, in order to control it, the first attempt of the french government was to create a separate state from germany in the occupied Ruhr. It clearly failed after a referendum, when they asked it to the local population.

    From them, Jean Monnet imagined a new proposal : make a separation between questions of territories and resources. Than mean creating a franco-german community of coal resources, based on equal treatment. this was the definitive solution.

    I think it was a time of confusion and fears. By chance, Jean Monnet brought some new perspectives
