\n I've had an idea for a Play By Post game rolling around in my head for a while now, and I think it's time I get some constructive feedback about it. It's tagline would be something like, "Massive Collaborative Fiction and Roleplay."
\n
\nInfluences include:
\nDe Profundis -- indie correspondence game
\ndiscussions at the Forge
\nAelyria -- massive play by post
\nFaery Tale Online - little browser based rpg
\nLost the TV series
\n
\nThe forum will break down into three main areas:
\n1. The Setting - This is where players write the history, ecology, and so forth that make up the setting. Writings must be given a frame of reference (a context), which means that there is an in-game author (whether PC or NPC, living or dead, famous or anonymous) and a location for where that writing can be found (e.g., a scroll in a library or inscribed on a the walls of an ancient ruin.)
\n2. The Characters - Threads summarizing the principle and supporting characters, I.e., PCs and NPCs.
\n3. The Events - This is the forum area that most resembles a typical pbp rpg. It is where the action happens.
\n
\nThe twist that I refer to in the topic is that there is no absolute canon for the setting; events played out can occur anywhere in time and need not be continuous with other events, which result in multiple worlds/alternate timelines.
\n
\nPlaying the game would mainly consist of creating the setting (in forum #1) and playing out events with one's character (in forum #3).
\n
\nThe duties of a GM would be shared amongst the players with a minimum of official forum moderation. My working idea here is that the thread starter of any thread in forum #3 would be the default GM for that scene.
\n
\nAny questions? Please let me know what you think! My feeling is that it could benefit a lot with some rules additions I've never heard of.

    \n Hey Ron,
    \n
    \nGood question. (Stalling in the beginning is more critical than stagnation at the end.) I think we can avoid stalling in the worldbuilding if we approach the worldbuilding as playing instead of something done prior to play. I'll address Christopher's questions and you can tell me if you see something that fixes the tendency of writing projects to stall.
    \n
    \nIn the Setting section, inspired by De Profundis, writings must have a context. I foresee it as being good form to have a header at the beginning of each thread saying something about the context, e.g., "An excerpt from the Tome of Yolar, in the Library at Limpardos..." or alternatively, a date and addressee together would frame the entry as a letter. Players could follow up in the same thread with entries in a different book on the same subject or as replies to the letter. The special advantage of having an in-the-fiction context for all of history, ecology, and geography in this way is that it makes it all subjective and open to interpretation-- perfect for a game with multiple timelines. Conflict will arise about what "actually happened" when newly discovered texts reveal lies by omission, or retell a history, swapping the oppressed with the oppressors. Play might revolve around making a set of accounts consistent or more "real" than a conflicting set of accounts. Some players may write most of their words in the Setting section.
    \n
    \nThe Character section would be the smallest. In this section, players have sole authorship over their PCs, one thread for each primary character. This is a chance for players to collect together the elements of Setting and Events that define their characters as they see them. Retconning is allowed and encouraged. In situations when timelines for a character dovetail and the character has multiple realities, these can be delineated and contrasted. Supporting characters or NPCs might be grouped together by relation, with shared authorship. There could be guidelines for how an NPC might become a PC, (i.e., shared authorship to sole authorship) and vice versa. I see entries as being encyclopedic in format with lots of links/references to Setting and Events.
    \n
    \nThe Events section may be the slowest to get started (need Setting and Characters first, right?) but could become the biggest if a lot of players join up. It would probably have some guidelines typical of most PBP RPGs (e.g., no bunnying?) but have a couple novel things. Borrowed from Aelyria (and emphasized) is what they call Fluid Time, which allows a character to be played simultaneously in threads (scenes) that occur at different places and times. In Aelyria a player is limited to 3 threads and a span of time, but I don't see a need for a hard restriction like that in this game. It's up to the player of the character to keep scenes separated by time and distance logically consistent, if desired. The other novel thing besides Fluid Time is the role of the GM: whoever starts a thread is the de facto GM with the typical duties of describing the environment and controlling supporting characters. Conflict resolution would be primarily Karma/Drama, I guess.
    \n
    \nYay!

    \n The freedom to play with time has some interesting properties when it comes to character development. Since there is no "present" in the Character section, a character could, for example, be introduced on their deathbed, and scenes from their life could be played out in the Events section as flashbacks. One element of the character "sheet" could include requests for a GM to play out certain scenes.
    \n
    \nSo, say I create Hagar, an aged tribal chieftain. I could note some key moments that I would like to play: 1. His first battle and initiation to manhood, 2. The day he succeeded his father as chieftan. 3. The day he received a crippling wound, marking his retirement as a warrior. For each event, I would describe who he is, what he believes, and what his goals are. We the players know how it all turns out, but what is in question is how he will get there.
    \n
    \nIn those scenes, Hagar would be a principal character. I see the GM's role as setting the scene to challenge who he is, what he believes, and present the stakes.
    \n
    \nI mentioned before that a player has sole authorship over a character in the Character section. It's allowable for players to create written accounts in the Setting section that may include tales of Hagar and his tribe. Also, in the Events section, I think it will be allowable for players acting in a GM role to borrow Hagar as a supporting character, which may carry some limitations and guidelines.

    \n My last comment was a whisper, by the way, I didn't want to clutter up the general discussion with my "hey, can I join your game?" stuff. But I don't mind talking about this in public, either.

    My previous experience with PBP projects involved 5 - 10 players. The rules weren't specifically designed for PBP because there weren't any rules. We were approaching it as Writers doing a Writing Project rather than as Gamers playing a Game. I think that was a mistake.

    The rest of the group had written together before, a successful project that lasted (I think) a couple of years. When I joined up with them, it seemed like they were trying to recapture their past success without any kind of discussion about how they'd been successful in the past. Just about any kind of question I had about procedures, even things as basic as "If our characters get into a conversation, who gets to decide what my character says?" was brushed off with, "Oh, it'll all just work itself out somehow."

    It didn't.

    Everyone introduced their characters and then just sort of sat there spinning their wheels, waiting for someone else to make something happen, to push for some sort of conflict or introduce some sort of overall plot. I made some stuff happen to my character, just to give myself something to write about, but I was afraid to turn it into a "real" plotline because I didn't know if I was supposed to be off doing my own thing or if I was supposed to be looking for ways to involve myself with the other characters. Apparently, neither did anyone else. When I suggested on the OOC forum that maybe we should start talking about ways to get our characters together and have them do something, most of the members said that was a good idea and then sat back and waited for someone else to start. One guy freaked out and demanded we move all that discussion to a super-secret hidden forum so he wouldn't be tempted to read it and "spoil his surprise". I was the new guy, so I didn't want to push it.

    Everyone wallowed through a couple more pointless, meandering story posts and then most of the group dropped out because they were "too busy in real life."

    We tried again a year later. This time, two of us came up with a tightly focused premise guaranteed to create lots of opportunity for conflicts between the characters. When we presented it to the rest of the group, everyone seemed enthusiastic. I still didn't have the slightest clue how we were supposed to actually handle those conflicts, and no one else seemed willing to talk about it, but as it turned out, it didn't matter. The game stalled out after a few weeks of pointless world-building. Or rather pointless discussions about world-building, since very little of the world actually got built. Everyone made plans to discuss making plans for a while and then once again became "too busy in real life".

    Busy bunch, these guys.

    Another year or so later, we tried again. This time we tried more of a shared world approach: a basic setting and a single plot event that people could involve their characters in if they wanted or ignore and do something else entirely. I think that approach showed promise, but unfortunately we just didn't have enough players to get any real momentum going. Only a few of the players from the previous projects were interested, and most of them flat out fucking lied (It's possible I'm still a little bitter) about their level of enthusiasm. They claimed to be excited to participate but when the time came to actually write, their posts were short, lackluster, just plain bad writing. Nowhere near the quality of the stuff they'd contributed to previous projects. And while I certainly don't expect players in a PBP to try to impress me with their writing skills, I do expect them to write like they care. These people didn't.

    Luckily, my disappointment was short-lived; once again, most of the group became "too busy in real life."

    Our original project started with eight people. As Ron mentioned, it was definitely set-up as more of a collaborative writing project rather than an RPG and there really weren't any rules. At one time, I think we maxed with about 12 players. The whole thing lasted about 2 years (which sounds like a pretty good run compared to other projects).

    Since there wasn't a GM or a direction, it could get fairly elementary and clunky. Someone might write a post about how their character was waiting at an inn; the next post would be from another contributor and basically introduce the characters to each other. The farther into the future you set the story, the more important that question becomes: set a story ten years from now and you get to make a few conjectures about technology and politics while still writing about people who are exactly like us; set a story ten thousand years from now and you're pretty much required to make a number of Powerful Statements about the nature of humanity. Humans in your distant future might be totally extinct, they might have transcended every limit of nature and remade the entire universe into their concept of Paradise, they might be exactly like us, they might be pretty much like us only with gills and the ability to metabolize Styrofoam.
