\n There's an issue. I'll just paste from my style guide for Misspent Youth, then riff further if needed.\n
Youthful Offenders vs. Youthful Offender players and The Authority vs. The Authority player: This issue is fucking thorny. I think my goal here is to use the "player" term when it's important to distinguish between the characters and the people playing the game, and otherwise not to do so. I'm open to being persuaded otherwise.
\nThe thorniness, of course, arises out of the desire to simultaneously a) not occlude the difference between players and characters and b) not write shittily and come off like a pretentious, precious asshole.

Where do you come out on this?

    \n I think the thing to be most clear about is what is being done, not who is doing it. Everything else will work itself out. If you say "the character loses 10 hit points" or "the player loses 10 hit points" it is probably not a big deal, we all know what it means. If you say "the character says hello" or "the player says hello", then it is a big deal, because both of those are feasible, and some means of clarification even beyond the noun phrase is needed.

    I would address it like this. Of course, this prevents things like mass search-and-replace, so, easy for ME to say.

    \n My habit has for years been to write exactly: I use "character" when I mean the character and "player" when I mean the player. I think that this is largely due to my Forge theory acculturation: that sort of rpg theory simply doesn't function if you don't analyze players and character as separate, incidentally colluding elements of the game event. From that viewpoint it's perfectly natural to never mix the two up.

    While I can understand how this might be difficult for some, in practice I find that if I can't tell whether it's the player or the character who does something in my game text, then I need to think about the procedures and modelling of my game some more.

    \n You might try directing the text toward the user actively. Then it is "you do this" and "your super robot does this." Like:

    "On your friend\u2019s Go, you can give one of your dice to your friend\u2019s Raise. You have to have your character do something that would a) clearly and directly contribute to your friend\u2019s character\u2019s action, and b) be obviously something your character could do, given everything else going on. If anyone objects that your character\u2019s too busy or in the wrong part of the scene, you should graciously withdraw. Given that everybody thinks it\u2019s reasonable, though, just slide one of your dice over to go with your friend\u2019s."

    \n Yeah, I do take some shortcuts now and then. Specifically, fruitful ambivalence - in Solar System texts I write nowadays I normally say that the character makes an Ability check, for example, when I of course mean that the character initiates action in the fiction, which provides a cause for the player to make an Ability check. So it's not all cut and dried. Still, the writing begins with me identifying who is doing what and then only taking shortcuts when that improves the text.
