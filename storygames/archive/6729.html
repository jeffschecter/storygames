\n
\n
\n
\n\nJason_Morningstar \n\n\n
\n
\n\nJune 2008 \n edited June 2008 in Meetups / Conventions
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n So Steve Segedy and I are in the process of assuming administrative duties for the 24 Hour RPG event. It is its own thing, and has been loosely organized for years, and is great. But you know how it goes - we started thinking. I'm looking for some feedback on whether this would work:

What if we organized an event more akin to the 24 Hour Comic which was the initial inspiration? So rather than an isolated thing, it'd be a social thing - people would meet locally, make games, share 24 hours of creativity, frustration, and pizza. For this to work there would need to be well-organized regional volunteers to host, or find game stores that were into hosting, events. In addition to the usual hotbeds of enthusiasm like NYC, there could be virtual communities committed to participating as well. The bottom line would be a single date, a social event involving meeting others and sharing space and ideas, and a bunch of new roleplaying games.

Would you want to do this? Would it represent a significant improvement over working in isolation, just picking a date and opening fora for discussion?

Would you be willing to coordinate this locally? It wouldn't have to be elaborate, but it certainly could be. I could see it taking place in a retail store, for example, as the 24 Hour Comic events generally are.

Share your ideas, please! I'd love to invigorate the 24 Hour RPG and have some fun.

    \n Jason, the 24 Hour Comics Day event is a recent invention, started in 2004 by Nat Gertler, not by Scott McCloud or the other folks involved in the original 24 hour comics in 1990 and afterwards. 24-hour RPGs, then, started in 2003 if not before, actually pre-date 24 Hour Comics Day. So I don't think there's any particular reason we should have to follow the single-day event guidelines. Additionally, even on the 24 Hour Comics Day blog, you can see signs that many groups continue to organize 24 hour comics events on other days (like the Comics Drawpocalypse in Portland). I guess I just think it would be a shame if 24-hour RPGs become just a once-a-year thing. By all means, it'd be cool to schedule a couple dates a year when multiple 24-hour RPG events would be happening at the same time, but I strongly dislike the idea of one official day, since it institutionalizes an idea that nobody owns, something that anyone can do any time they want.

    \n Please wait until we figure out what the parameters are, unless you want to have a very vague conversation. A freindly heads-up and test of the waters would be great, though.

    Here's what I'm thinking at the moment:

    1. We pick a date for a big event. If this rends your soul, you can pick a different date, but the goal is excitement around a shared experience and collaboration. The date will not mesh with the comics event, for good reasons pointed out above. We don't want to try retailer's patience. None of this precludes writing your own 24 hour RPG whenever you feel like it.

    2. We invite retailers to host, but don't limit the contest to retail places. Any host can host for whatever slice of the 24 hours they like. If a store does not want to stay open all night, they can ask the designers to adjourn to the Pancake House at midnight. Individuals can open their homes, we can rely on individual initiative, and the "event" piece can be structured around collaboration, playtesting, and general getting-togetherness within the parameters the local host provides. People can also scatter and work alone. Maybe there is an Internet-based synchronous group as well.

    3. I'd love to add some sort of "walkathon" type angle, where people pledge to give a charity cash in exchange for our 24 hours of labor somehow. I think that would be fun and positive, and I'm open to ideas about how to work something like that. It also opens up the possibility of competition between local groups, which would be fun. We could raise a ton of cash for some worthwhile global charity doing something we love.

    4. I'm not sure how to approach this, but it'd be great if we could share and redistribute the output of the event. Again, I'm open for ideas about how to do that. If we encourage people to design analog, it'd be really neat to put together a book that was nothing but scans of the game designs as written/sketched/diagrammed. Or maybe both the original hand-made game and a playtested, readable, laid-out version side by side. This may be too ambitious or unrealistic, I don't know.

    I think this structure will be appealing to hosts, it is flexible and friendly, and beyond the games produced, it would increase the good in the world as well.
