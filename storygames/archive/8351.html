\n I've acquired a copy of Vampire: The Requiem (thank you, Matt Machell).

Now, there's many things I love about Vampire. The setting's great. Actually, OK, maybe that's the only thing, but I do love the setting. Perhaps the Disciplines, too, and the Flaws. the system is bloody awful and the text is even worse. Everything explained in the wrong order, rules upon rules upon rules.

Moreover, I've no idea what an adventure would look like. I've no desire to do a railroaded "The Prince wants you to investigate this thing" adventure. I'd love to do an open-ended thing, where everyone has goals and they fight for the city.

What can I do? I'd like to run this. I'd quite like to run it using the original system, although perhaps a minimal version of it. What I'm after, I think, is some tweaks to the original system. Any ideas?

    \n For a Vampire adventure, you need PCs that have goals and relationships, and you need some interesting NPCs.

    Either the PCs have things they want, or they just want to hole up in their HQ and ignore the other mean vampires. In the latter case, you need to threaten their HQ. Like the city starts planning to demolish their Haven. So they need to go out and Do Things. They can manipulate humans or ask other vampires for favors, and if they manipulate the wrong humans, they piss off a vampire.

    Another vampire who is asked for a favor or is pissed off will then ask the PCs to do him a favor, in return. This should be something fairly outrageous, and should lead to yet another vampire asking them for a favor. The PCs go round and round until they get pissed off and start wailing on somebody. Have that vampire cave almost immediately, and the PCs will realize they have to beat on people to get what they want, sometimes. 'Cause they're vampires, too, right? Just make sure the PCs aren't the weakest characters in the game, like if you were playing the old Chicago by Night or something. Being the middle man on the pole is much preferable to being low man.

    \n Well when we did our V:Tr a few years back I remember we did a few things. First I let them be however old they wanted. One of the players was a regent (basically a sub prince of part of the city) as well as being the Prince's Templar (legbreaker, it was a Lancea Sanctum city) , and one of her children, we just figured the points based on his age. The others were fairly new (5 to 10 years). I also asked that they all have some significant connection to a mortal. We played very strongly with the notion that Vampires embrance mostly because they fall in love with a mortal, or they really, really need that person for something. It did make the parent/child relationship very interesting. Then we basically created kickers for each person as well as establishing the general backstory.
    \n
    \nSo as I remember it we had a Vampire kung fu monk from Taipei, relatively new, who's particular order is always guided by a vampire. It was time for the master to "walk the earth" and pass the guardianship of the order to his apprentice. The character was actually the second apprentice as the first had run away, so the master was a bit desperate. The problem was the character had fallen in love with a mortal woman, and was thinking of abandoning the order. The second problem was that his father had died suddenly and unexpectedly left everything to him above his siblings or stepmother. The whole filal piety thing. This is why he was in the (American) city was to look at that end of the business. His stepmother was also connected to the triads. (The father dying and leaving him the stuff was the kicker.)
    \n
    \nWe had a young woman vampire who, after dumping her sire, returned to the city and basically wanted to keep watch over her younger sister and her family. The kicker being that her sire showed up back in town and wanted her back. She belonged to the main revolutionary faction in the city as well.
    \n
    \nThen there was the Templar, one of the princes children, who still had an on/off relationship with the prince herself (a sort of vampiric Joan of Arc, in that she heard voices.) In additon to his general responsiblities to her he had managed to raise an orphan girl basically to adulthood (I think she was 17, he was about 200 or 300) in secret. His kicker was that his 'daughter' declared she was in love with him and wanted to be made into a vampire.
    \n
    \nThe general background was unrest and potential revolution against the Lancea Sanctum who ran the city, mainly because they were extremely strict about the religious traditions of the Lancea and the prince seemed to be getting less and less mentally stable.

    But seriously, what is it you want out of the game? I mean, it's a toolkit for making Vampire games. What sort of game are you going to make?

    One nice thing about Requiem is that it scratches the itch for the "I'm a 500 year old vampire!" thing people want to do without necessarily making it unbalanced, due to the fact that all vampires are expected to be in torpor for significant periods if they don't want to go mad with satanic power.

    Here's my old thread... it might help but it's kind of rambling. Some hopefully helpful relationship map stuff later in the thread...

    But srsly, it's like saying "GURPS, I don't understand why I can't just sit down, have people make characters and run an adventure!" You can't, there's no there there.

    Here are some questions that might help. I assume many of them you won't have even thought of. Until you answer them you will never have any success with the game:

    - What is bad about the system in your view?
    - Also, do you have the main World of Darkness book? You sort of need it, most of the actual systems are described in there (and much more succinctly, I might add).
    - What is cool about the setting in your view?
    - What do you want PCs to be doing in your game?
    - Why will they be doing it (in general)?
    - Design a PC that would be dynamite for your game! Why did you make those choices?
    - Think through a potential conflict for your game that you think would be badass. What about that conflict makes it badass? Where in that conflict do the PCs interact with the system and how do they do it?

    Basically, you must design a campaign before you know what parts of the book you will use and what parts you will junk. So the setting is compelling. Too bad, there's far too much of it to learn in even a Very Serious And Long Campaign. That means working out where your spotlight will shine and that means working out what your game is about.

    You have in your hand a fucking great toolkit for making Vampire campaigns. I actually have been designing one with very specific goals in mind recently and it's different from a lot of others. I want to:

    - introduce new players who have never played before to the system gracefully.
    - introduce new players who have never played before to the setting gracefully.
    - make them feel cool when they do wacky action horror shit.
    - give them the creeping dreads as everywhere they turn someone has an agenda they're not telling (not all hostile to the player characters, but always with some secret goal)
    - give them clear opportunities to do things
    - give them clear choices and benefits to each, mostly political choices, but perhaps a few personal choices too, nothing too intense though

    I'll post up the techniques I'm using and where you can find them in the book probably tomorrow or sometime this week. But making a list like this will help a lot.

    And finally:

    One reason the "Prince gives you a mission" scenario works so well is because damn near everyone hates the Prince and someone is scheming to bring them down and failing your mission and framing some other poor schlub for your failure can often bring you badass rewards from the Prince's dire enemies just as much as succeeding in the mission will bring you from the Prince. And if you don't want to or can't delve past the surface, the scenario still works because you have a mission and a clear idea of what you're doing and if you're succeeding or not. Once you have a game designed, the scenario is remarkably freeing. "The Prince wants us to steal this occult artifact? Well, fuck him, we're going to destroy it and blame this loyal underling of the Prince, who will be murdered for it, and in return, the guy who hates the fucking Prince will back us and think we're awesome. Oh shit we're being blackmailed! Best get some dirt on him too..." And you've got something going.

    This is a great game, it has been a great game since 1991. (It is also largely the same game as it was in 1991. White Wolf never fucked it up. Think how hard that is to do.) Congratulations on owning it.

    \n My first game happened in the World of Darkness and I've had a special love for it ever since, despite knowing full well that mechanics and innovations in other games are far, far better. Hence, I present to you my freshly minted house rules for handling social conflict. All the cool kids are adding combat-like rules for social interaction to their games nowadays, why shouldn't a game like Vampire (that so desperately needs it) get the same treatment?

    Stage One: Intent
    The attacker declares what he wishes to gain from social conflict. Whether he wishes to convince the bouncer to let him through or coax information from a prisoner, wearing down the opponent\u2019s Resolve will make it happen.

    Stage Two: Attack
    Attack rolls use Presence or Manipulation, depending on the approach and an appropriate Social Skill. Intimidation, Persuasion, Socialize and Subterfuge may all change the minds or opinions of others, but other skills like Empathy, Expression and Streetwise are not used this way. The target uses Composure for Armor and the same Social Skill (the one being used to attack) for Defense.

    Example: \u201cStep away from the girl and drop the gun or I will fire!\u201d Cop rolls Presence + Intimidation, minus criminal\u2019s Composure and Intimidation (armor and Defense).


    Stage Three: Resolution
    Add bonus dice based on equipment used or effect performed, and then subtract penalties for circumstance conditions.

    Example: The cop\u2019s Intimidation gets +2 from his drawn firearm, but -3 from the hostage (the criminal suspects he won\u2019t fire while the girl is in the way).

    Roll your remaining pool. Once you have accumulated a number of successes equal to your opponent\u2019s (Resolve x 2), the attacker wins his Intent. The defender loses 1 point of Willpower and is forced to behave as the attacker intended for the duration of the scene. Should a person lose all their Willpower in this way, they are completely at the attacker\u2019s mercy. The successful Intent will remain until it contradicts Virtue or Vice, at which point the poor sap can behave freely again.

    Example: \u201cYou drop your gun pig or I\u2019ll plug the girl!\u201d The criminal rolls Presence + Persuasion (+3 for the girl), minus the cop\u2019s Composure and Persuasion. The criminal gets 4 successes. The cop, with a Resolve of 2, is forced to comply and loses 1 Willpower. Now the cop can still act freely (call for backup, tell the girl to run, persuade the criminal to take him as a hostage instead, etc) but the gun must remain dropped for the duration of the scene. The criminal earned it!


    Social Conflict often happens in turns, with each participant taking opportunity to persuade or win over his opponent. Of course, if some one is noticeably out matched, they may opt to simply walk away. An annoyed character may also cut the discourse short by resorting to violence, in which case Initiative is rolled as normal. These rules are designed to be interchangeable with rules for Combat Resolution. There\u2019s no reason characters in combat can\u2019t spend a round or two doing social attacks (asking for surrender, etc).
