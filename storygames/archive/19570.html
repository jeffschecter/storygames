\n In a recent thread the game With Great Power was recommended to me, and despite it being out of print, there are some electronic resources available, namely at least part of the conflict resolution mechanic it uses.

So I've been noodling with something similar and played a few hands with some people, but I'd like to see what others here think.

Basic premises: Scenes should be dynamic rather than static, scenes should follow the dramatic/storytelling paradigm (a protagonist with a pressing goal that's opposed by another character), and win or lose the scene the protagonist should suffer some kind of setback which informs the story going forward if not propels the story forward.

If you disagree with these premises, fine, but that discussion is probably best left for other threads.

SETUP
When you have a protagonist with a goal that's opposed by another character, that's a dramatic scene and the cards come out.

STATE YOUR GOALS
Whichever character initiated the scene with their goal, declares first. Something like "If I win...", then their opponent declares their goal, "If I win...".

DRAW
Each participant draws five cards from a standard poker deck, with Jokers in. If the GM has multiple characters in a scene, each one gets to draw 5 cards. (Not sure how this will work out in big scenes. Maybe a bigger pool of cards based on the number of PCs in a scene?)

PLAY
The initiating player declares an action and plays one card.

The opposing player declares a reaction and plays one card. For the reaction to take effect the card played must be of equal or greater value.

For example: A goon's robbing a bank. Batman's watching from the rafters. The goon declares "I shoot the teller" and plays a 6 of clubs. Batman declares "I throw a Batarang to disarm the goon" and plays a 9 of hearts. Batman's card is higher so his action becomes true in the fiction.

Play goes back and forth like this with one-upping declarations and cards till either side can't or won't continue. Can't because they've run out of cards or won't because they decide to give in.

Any player can reset the stack by playing two cards with their declaration. The lower of which is used for the new "high" card. So if your opponent plays a card you can't beat, you can play two lower cards and go back to a lower number to beat. For example: Batman plays a king of diamonds. The goon plays a 3 of spades and a 7 of hearts. The 3 of spades is now the stack's high card to beat.

DRAW MORE CARDS
Players can opt to stress an Aspect to draw 2 more cards during a scene. Only one Aspect can be stressed during a scene. This bit is a little wobbly. I'm going with the assumption that Aspects are similar to Fate Aspects, but that each can take stress, likely a simple 3-4 box stress track for each. And maybe 5-6 Aspect for a character.

MULTIPLE CHARACTERS
Every player with a character that has something at stake in the conflict must have a stated goal and must play a card every round. Play starts with the initiating player and goes around the table till everyone who wants to continue in the scene plays a card. Anyone who doesn't play a card is giving in and loses their stakes/goal.

ENDING A SCENE
When either side can't or won't play more cards, the scene is over. Whoever played the last card gains their scene goal. However, win or lose the scene, the initiating character suffers some kind of setback. Their opponent gets to declare a narratively appropriate setback for the initiating character. The GM and table are final arbiter of what a narratively appropriate setback is. If multiple players were engaged in a scene, they all get a say in the setback.

STRATEGY
Something that came up was the question of initiating player being at a disadvantage as they're going first and their opponent can simply pick the lowest card they have to beat them. That's the beauty of a card-based resolution: The player doesn't have to play a low card first. The cards are resources to spend however the player wants. They can play face cards on that first declaration, if they have them. This forces the other player to slap two cards down early to keep things going, which typically forces a stressed Aspect for more cards. In the couple of dozen hands I've played with people there doesn't seem to be any correlation between going first and ultimately winning or losing the hand. It more depends on the cards you're dealt and the strategy you use.

That's basically it. If you're willing, give it a try with someone. Doesn't need to be supers, that's just the original form from With Great Power, so it's what I went with here. Should work for most conflict types. Fairly fast and fun once you get into it.

So tell me what you think?
