\n It was supposed to be a larger gathering than it turned out being, but it was just me and a new gaming friend of mine who has a long history DM-ing D&D. He was sold on the game almost even before we got done reading the instructions.

We told a fantastic story of a community of mostly-orphans that explored the differences between religious/scientific views of events and the struggle between using resources for commerce or for combat readiness. We also weaved those things together with some supernatural elements (including a tree growing mysteriously in the middle of a river, ritualistic burying of beached fish, and a song that kept reappearing in unexpected places) that retroactively turned out to be harbingers of the Frost Shepherds' arrival (which happened on the 2nd week of Winter).

While I think I still prefer playing this with 3 or 4 people, the 2-player version had some great dynamics. We instinctively kept playing devil's advocate with each other, yet our visions for where the game should go started converging about mid-Summer and playing devil's advocate soon served only to coax the story in the direction that was most satisfying; we had bought into the caring-about-the-ant-farm philosophy the game suggests without even really realizing it. It was pretty awesome.

The only problem I had was the problem I usually have when introducing this game: enforcing the brevity that really makes the game sing. I tend to try and lead by example instead of corralling, but it doesn't always work. If anyone has suggestions they are welcome.

    \nThis is absolutely the hardest part of the game. I think that one key is to make it clear that it's not a restriction, but an invitation to experience something beautiful.

    So maybe before reading the rules, command everyone's attention and say with a smile, "There is a weird dynamic in this game. We don't talk much. We don't have rolling conversations like in most games. And as a result there is this minimalist & ethereal beauty to many sessions. The more we embrace this aesthetic, the more interesting the game gets. I'm excited about that."

    Just in some way make it clear that this is an exciting thing, rather than a difficult requirement.
