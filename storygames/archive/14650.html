    \n I just sent out an announcement email to the Game Chef mailing list and dropped these hints:

    Highlights this year:
    -- a special guest judge!
    -- a fresh and unusual theme!
    -- easier opportunities to connect with other chefs!
    -- small-scale peer review to determine finalists!
    -- faster judging!

    There's also a secret cabal of former Game Chef participants serving as informal advisors this year and they've done an amazing job, I think. The contest this year will be all the better due to their feedback, and I'm definitely going to suggest that future Master Chefs take advantage of the folks we have connected to Game Chef, roping in new people every year to get a diversity of perspectives.

      \nAnti-Stratfordianism is the claim that someone other than William Shakespeare wrote the plays attributed to William Shakespeare.

      The claims all boil down to "Some guy from a little town like Stratford, educated in a small town school, with no history of noble blood could have written these plays." Which is pure classism. It suggests that the lower classes are inferior to the upper classes. I assume Joel is upset at the Anti-Stratfordianism because it is, at base, an insult to the vast majority of humanity.

      It's pretty much bunk from a historical standpoint. Shakespeare's claims on the authorship of his plays is as good as any other author's claims on their own works. And if you look at the rest of the history of writing you see plenty of good writing by lower class, uneducated types and not much from the British noble classes (Lord Byron being one of the few notable exceptions).

      "OF HEROES.

      Each hero ranks their gifts from one to four:
      Blade and Policy, Temp\u2019rance, Divine.
      Blade embraceth all thy martial skills;
      Policy how thou bear thyself at court
      Or when thou try with words to woo a maid.
      Both skills may serve you if you choose to be
      the Duke\u2019s defender, faithful unto him,
      or if instead thou seekst Amanda\u2019s hand.
      Thy Temper all that is within congealed
      to hold, to heal, to moderate response
      (a low score might compelleth thee to act
      at times that might be better served by tact).
      Divine relates thy mortal self to God
      Whose Providential hand doth guide our lives
      (a low score here imperileth thy soul,
      though may make mortal crimes amenable).
      Exiles all, ye come into this world
      with nought but that which ye have chosen here.

      No Master of the Game exists, no Ref
      by which God\u2019s Will enforc\u2019d may come to be:
      All players are, and none above them is
      save for th\u2019 assembled will that doth emerge
      from what the Table as a group doth feel
      best represents the spirit of the game."

It wasn't that there was no Shakespeare-y-ness present, it just didn't always penetrate down to the thematic level of the games. Which I think, looking back, is pretty normal for Game Chef, but I also think the use of 4 very Shakespearean-already ingredients may have played into that somehow. And to be clear, I felt the same way about my own game to some degree -- though it has some very abstract, very strong literary-theory connections to Shakespeare[1] it also has some of that superficial-veneer-of-Shakespeare on top. Other games definitely did a better job of getting at that meaty middle-ground.

      And of course ultimately I would much rather read a cool game idea than something that's totally 'about' Shakespeare but not interesting to the person making the game.


      [1] Kind of the What I Think About When I Think About Shakespeare approach to theme, I guess.

      \n My game has soliloquys -- they're the last narrative limitation that is removed from a scene (you remove them by playing the scene repeatedly.) As far as how they are treated, they're simultaneously the only access anyone has to the inner thoughts of the characters and the only time the actors/friends can speak directly to Shakespeare, who is the total audience of the play. Since the goal of the game is to explore the nature of Shakespeare's estrangement with his daughter, and/or help Shakespeare come to terms with that estrangement, both these roles are fairly important. Soliloquys are limited to the characters of Shakespeare (who is played by the actors, not himself) and the daughter.

      The game provides very little support for the players attempting to deliver the soliloquys, unfortunately; there's just a brief description of what a soliloquy is, and a few suggestive lines about the twin roles discussed above. The original draft had some specific advice on how to deliver a soliloquy -- preparation, taking notes, relaxing, not worrying about perfect language, use of metaphor, etc. -- but all the advice had to be cut in order to make the 3000 word limit.

      I thought about including it in the supplemental material but decided to leave it out, following Jonathan's advice on seeing how the game would stand on its own. I thought about incorporating more mechanics around soliloquys -- before the ingredients were released, I had planned on designing a game entirely around building to and delivering soliloquys -- but the game really didn't need any more rules at that point.

      I see that "All's Well That Ends as You Like It" is a finalist, which was my favorite title.

      I do hope everyone reads JWalt's announcement carefully, and takes to heart the idea that winning Game Chef happens when you DO Game Chef. That little game you made in a week and a bit? The one that didn't make the lists? It's a spark of creativity that only you could have come up with, and only under these exact circumstances.

      That sounds sappy, maybe. I don't care. It's true, and wonderful. Maybe that little slice of art is sufficient as is, as a game you play a couple times, or a trial run, or an experiment you later mine for ideas in other projects. Or maybe it's something you've got the passion to nurture and grow into something bigger, something clearer, something more complete.

      Whatever's the case, celebrate that! And if it's the latter case, by all means go for it! Lots of great gaming has come from Game Chef, and not from the "winners" so much. I'm still working on my last Game Chef project, from 2008, and it's going to be something awesome real soon. And all it ever won was an honorable mention for "most ambitious concept" or something.

      And if you're a finalist, or even if you win, don't lose heart! There's no reason your little gamey seed can't grow into something splended too!

      I got on kind of a sentimental roll there, but I really want y'all to know, this is something special we all did. Take joy in that1
