I'll try to tackle this from character-immersive point of view - it should be noted that there are probably lot's of other stuff under the Simulationist umbrella, but I'm referencing to this one, since it's what I do. Also, I'm thinking this up as I go, backed up by some discussion on the subject.

    The first thing that should be understood is that the main goal of Simulationist play is to elevate beyond the fiction. Think it as a virtual reality you want to absorb yourself into, and forget that you're Jack The Player playing a game. There is no game and there are few or none abstractions - there is just you and the virtual reality, inhibited by six million other people, or something else, depending on the nature of the virtual reality.

    The second thing is that you strive to be a black box human. You simulate the reality by simulating a human (or something else), with all the human limitations, most notably, communication. Think yourself, as a real human person, in social situtation or something else; these are the tools available for you, in the virtual reality. There are no abstract mechanisms or procedures that would let you, the player, shape things or control things in the virtual reality. There are only tools available to the character - that is, pretty much the same tools available to you as a real human being. The tools probably wary from your real self tools, since character is someone else and the virtual reality is something else, but the basic approach is the same.

    There is no story, no strive to create a story, or tools to create a story. A Story is an abstraction visible only to players and since you're excluding the players from the picture, you get no story. What you do have is a potential for intresting things to happen. See Chaotic Role-Playing which explains this better than I could.

    I think those are the main how's, probably some of the why's and differences between, for example, Narrativist and Simulationist play.

    So, to answer the questions, I'm leaning on the A) it's a Creative Agenda, because there are clear goals which don't really mix with the other mentioned CA's. So, why would someone want to elevate beyond the fiction and forget the player-self in order to do that? My personal answer is that it grants access to emotional content; when you're adapting a fictional person and putting aside yourself, you get an access to that fictional persons emotions, straight and pure. They are happening to you. There are probably other goals as well, but that's the one I'm mostly sticking with.

    It could also be in the sense that it can probably be mixed with other preferences and none of the CA's have to exclude other CA's. Mine does, so I'm not the person to get into this one.

    Umm... I don't know if I really answered your questions or shed light to the subject. It's an difficult issue.

    Mmmm, re-hash.

    Guys, here's the thing about Sim as a CA (yes, I'm talking about CA in the Big Model here, and that is all). CA's have processes that the group engages in in order to fulfill their goals. Nar has the Addressing of Premise; Gam has Stepping On Up; and Sim has Creative Denial.

    Another way to put it is that both "the Right" and "the Dream" are equally important in the phrase "the right to dream." Sim is based on a process that filters (denies) everyone's input (creativity) into what the dream looks like/what the dream experience is. This is an active process; it's not sitting back and watching the pretty countryside scroll by, it's going out and changing that landscape to reflect your individual groups version of the Dream.

    There are two big reasons why people have a hard time with this. One is that the techniques that people identify with Sim play are totally used all the time across the board (as has been pointed out). The other is that one groups enacted version of their Dream can be totally and sometimes violently foreign to another groups, to the point of seeming not even like the same activity. The fictional content of play for Sim does NOT seem to follow as broadly identifiable trends as that of Nar, for example.

    But once you view it through the lens of why you're doing it, not what you're doing, it gets pretty simple. To me, at least.

    I hope that helps in some manner.

    Hi Joel,

    Without wanting to sound dismissive or patronizing, I think you should read some detailed actual play accounts of Sim games, like the Werewolf game above or my own Vampire game I linked in the Werewolf thread. That will probably be more enlightening than abstract discussion. I mean, "preset theme" is not a feature of Sim, it's just a minor technique. A player just "knowing" that his character must do one thing is not a feature of Sim, it's just an Ephemera.

    Bear in mind that the question is what the top priority is. Sim is held together by the shared appreciation of the fiction. You won't identify that by looking at single Techniques or Ephemera. It's the Instance of Play and the social reinforcement you need to look at.

    There is also not much merit in seeking some example of an obvious conflict or clash. Most of the time, it's not that obvious. It's not like, Nar requires X to happen and Sim requires Y to happen. It's more a lack of sufficient common ground for appreciation and positive reinforcement, a failure to connect and get things flowing.
