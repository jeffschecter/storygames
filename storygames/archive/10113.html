Action vs Activity.

\n
\n
\n
\n\nMJGraham \n\n\n
\n
\n\nAugust 2009 \n edited August 2009 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n In my gaming blog, I recently wrote:
\n
\nActions can fail; activities cannot. They can only end how we anticipated or in ways that we didn't anticipate. A wedding doesn't fail because the bride or groom doesn't commit to marriage, it merely ends differently from what we might have expected. The wedding happens - has happened - regardless of the couples inability to commit to one another. In this example, the wedding is the activity, commitment an action.
\n
\nAnd in Radiance, I define activity:
\n
\nAn event that forms a scene in a story. Activities contain elements and aspects that are engaged with the activity. An activity differs from an action in two ways, characters perform actions, but are involved in activities, and a scene does not take the form of an action. For example, two lovers talking in a caf\xe9 is an activity; stirring sugar into a cup of coffee is an action performed by a person within that activity. A series of related activities makes a story.
\n
\nIn my experience, many roleplaying games are action focused rather than activity focused. We tend to play a lot of games with rules that emphasize what the character is doing, rather than where their activity they are involved in is going. Pick up any roleplaying game at you FLGS and you're almost guaranteed to find rules to tell you how to deal with characters' actions in combat, but rarely will you find one with rules for where the combat might go. I blame it on our ridiculous love affair with Cartesian dualism, but that's probably an unworthy topic of discussion for story games. What I'd rather discuss is what you think would happen if games moved from being action focused to being activity focused? If you play any games that are activity focused, what kind of experiences have you had with them? Or maybe you think there's no difference between action and activity/

    \nRalph, I think that the dividing line between action and activity is that action always involves a subject with intention, while an activity does not. In the cafe example, the couple don't have to have an end goal in mind, they just have to be there. Or to use another example, if two characters are caught in an avalanche, there in an activity that requires no intent from them. In fact, the avalanche would still be happening (still be an activity) if no characters were there at all. When I think about activities, I like to ask myself where the characters at any given moment. This where isn't necessarily a physical location (although physical locations may matter for an activity, e.g. a couple can't be talking in a cafe without being at a cafe). When I think of an activity, location is more like this: "There I was talking to my friend about the new CD I brought this morning."


    Action: "My character attacks...." (There is a subject with intent)
    False Activity: "My character is in mid swing..." (This is simply a rewording of the above action and has just as much intent)
    True Activity: "Ok, all of your characters are now brawling with some local tough guys..." (This is an activity because the characters are involved in the brawl at this moment regardless of any intentions they may have).

    There's a little more to activities, and I'll explain below.\n

    \n Marcus: "some campers trying to survive an avalanche" sounds like it has intent. As does "they may want to stay together". In a brawl, there is certainly intent. This seems like an issue of scale, both time-wise and character-wise ("I'm swinging my sword" vs. "You're all in a big brawl").

    When I'm roleplaying an activity, what I'm interested in are the characters. I'm interested in how the activity will end, but only with regards to the characters' intents. I could care less about whether the avalanche knocks over that tree. It's color, and it's cool to include, but you could also leave it out. However, the characters want to survive, and if you leave it without resolving that, I'm left unfulfilled (unless you're planning to resolve it later, and keep the tension). So activities are fine and all, but what I care about is the intents. And then we're doing actions, although we might be doing it at a large scale (brawl vs. swing of sword). But mordern RPGs rarely have any problems dealing with scale.

    \nIn the example of the campers trying to survive an avalanche, you're correct there is intent. It was a bad example. What I should have said is that the avalanche is an activity. If the campers were all unconscious or somewhere else, it would be an avalanche nonetheless. The campers within the activity of an avalanche may have intentions, and from those intentions actions, but the activity exists regardless of their intent.

    Brawls don't have intentions. There are characters in the brawl who have intentions and perform actions according to those intentions, but there really is no goal or objective for the brawl itself, no way to measure its outcome in terms of success or failure.

    I think understand why you prefer actions over activities (although I may be wrong). It's a cultural thing. We're raised to think of ourselves as minds that are separate from the world, but through some mystery process that uses intention, we act upon the world. We do the same with our characters when we roleplay. We create rule after rule of to figure out what our characters can do, but spend less time pondering rules for what are characters are involved in.

    To quote the Professor Hubert Dreyfus:

    A huge amount of our lives -- working, walking, talking, eating, driving, etc. -- is spent in this non-mediated coping mode and only a small part is spent in the deliberate, purposeful, subject/object mode. Yet the reflective is, of course, the mode we tend to notice, and so it has formed our common-sense concept of action and has been studied in detail by philosophers.

    This doesn't mean your wrong to care more about what happens to characters than trees. I feel the same way. But I do think we can care about characters in ways that aren't focused on actions.

    \n I'll give it a shot...

    Activity is what happens when you're in narrative flow, you stop thinking about your characters as subjects, and whatever intentions they might have are not seen in terms of success or failure, but rather where their intentions take the narrative, where your description is going. If you've ever roleplayed and not referred to the dice (or similar) to see how well your character is doing, than you're activity focused.

    Action is a kind of disruption to narrative flow and it commonly occurs during activities such as combat. You can spend a long time narrating something, and as soon as there's a rule for performing an action in the activity, you break out of the activity focused mode of play and enter an action focused mode of play. Questions of can my character do that begin to arise. You may have spent the last ten minutes not worrying if your character can do something, but now he's in combat, suddenly you're supposed to pull him out of the narrative, treat as something distinct from the story, and roll the dice. The way in which your character becomes distinct from the story, or more accurately the setting of the story, can be illustrated with this question: when is the last time you saw someone roll dice to see if a piece of equipment could do something? We don't (or we rarely) roll the dice to see if equipment works, unless we want to see if the equipment thwarts the intentions of our characters. We just narrate the equipment doing - or not doing - what its supposed to do.

    \n Coming in kinda late, but maybe this will help:

    The avalanche, as you describe it (i.e. irrespective of the characters) is something I'd call an "event," not an activity. If there were no characters there, it would still happen (dismissing trees-falling-in-woods metaphysical issues). If one character was suicidal and rode the wavefront to his or her doom while another desperately fought to stay on top of the snow, the event would still happen. And for the sake of semantics, there's no reason to think of an event as instantaneous or momentary (like an action would be).

    You have action (task) well laid out--it's all IIEE. But I note, again, the time period distinction: very few actions can take place over extended periods of time--that strays into activity.

    So what is activity, then, but a sort of holistic view of a series of actions? In spite of the Dreyfus quote, I don't think it is very common for people to "do an activity" with absolutely no intent. The intent might be autonomic or subconscious (breathing to stay alive); orthogonal to the activity (listening to music to relax); or incidental (trying to look particularly adept at a skill when just getting it done would suffice). And I think you're absolutely right when you say that "all conflicts are activities, but not all activities are conflicts;" though, from a gaming context, an activity that didn't serve to explore, advance, or resolve a pertinent conflict could be said to be kruft (e.g. the dreaded Shopping Trip before an adventure, where the only "conflict" is "do I pay a bad price"--there's no real room to utterly fail, because that undermines the actual point of play: adventuring with sufficient tools and resources to make it a challenge but not impossible).

    Anyhow, the crux of my points are that you might want to consider events as separate from activities, because activities ultimately have intents while events "just happen" (i.e. have efficient and material causes but not final causes).

    \n Activity, for me, connotates movement toward an ending, and an event, as I see it, is more of a moment time in which something is happening. Or to put it another way, activities are becoming, events are being.
    \n
    \nAn activity is something which can be engaged in: a runner is engaged in running a marathon, his family watching from the sidelines. An activity is something that is done so as to engage with the activity: he runs with long deliberate strides. The event itself is the marathon and it happens whether or not a third party engages with it or not, but it happens in a very different way for them than it does the runner and his family. Much like how Ramadan, is an event and an activity for Muslims, but only an event for me as I don't engage in Ramadan and wouldn't really understand the actions needed to engage in Ramadan even if I were to fast during the ninth month of the Islamic calender.
