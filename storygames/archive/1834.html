    November 2006
    Joshua, I'm talking about apparent potential for psychological or cognitive effects that are prominent enough to cause concern from potential players. I trust the positive judgement (prominent effects) much more than the normative one (cause for concern).

    Broadly speaking there seems to be three classes of this:

    1) Difficult Themes - Dogs in the Vineyard, Sorcerer, and so on. Games that concentrate on themes for which some players have risky vulnerabilites.

    2) Emotional or Social Dissonance - Savagery, Breaking the Ice, not sure what else. Beyond themes, these games potentially encourage social or personal behaviors that players do not desire.

    3) Cognitive Dissonance - Transmutation (wish I had an outside example). Playing this game potentially encourages cognitive patterns or worldviews that players do not desire to have.

    #1 seems to be the easiest, and least problematic. There may be nothing pervasive about the risk, only a surface concern about the topics.

    #2 seems to occur when the fantasy is stripped, and the system is married to the themes. Thus it is not the topic of the game, but the playing itself that is 'dangerous'.

    #3 seems to be rather different. Playing the game requires you to think differently about the world, in a pervasive (and often subtle) manner.

    - Mendel S.

    Hi Judson,

    Here's my take: that what you describe is common in a minor form in many or most grown-up leisure activities - when couples are split up across teams or groups. That happens way more often, in my experience anyway, than having the couples play as teams. I like to observe, participate in, and consider such activities, and also to see them happening in my role-playing experiences.

    I think the key is the mask you mentioned. If we put aside "role-playing" in the literal sense as the primary act of the activity, and utilize it instead as a fairly minor technique that may or may not get employed by individuals, then the mask is no mask at all. And then, without it as a confounding factor, functional versions of the "risk" we're talking about here emerge pretty easily, or at least that's how it's been for me.

    I think your anecdote is awesome; it fits right in with the testimonials I used in Sex & Sorcery.
