    \n I've done a lot of work recently to "user friendly-ize" Matrix game turn resolution so that something always happens and there is no more than one roll to resolve anything.

    Players go one at a time. They roll a reality check that is usually 10 or less on 2d6 if they let the GM add details to their action)
    Other players may counter-argue. If they add to the action they make a single reality check.
    If they contradict the first action they don't make a reality check. Instead all the players pick which one they want to win and roll 2d6. The high roller wins for their pick.

    Mechanically that's it.

    The scenario around the game gives people randomly rolled information as the game is being played and structures the game into a series of chapters that guide them through a big picture story - which they must fight (or face default negative consequences).

    My solo play tests are running clean now so I'm encouraged that I might have a commercially workable system.

    \n The name d6x6 is tells you more about the range of possibilities, since there are 36 flat possible results. (By flat I mean that each result is unique, there's no bell curve or anything)

    But the name d66 serves as a better reminder of how to roll and count it, because you're using one d6 for the tens and the other for the ones.

    I think d66 is the better name to use, because d6x6 makes me think that maybe I'm supposed to roll 1 d6 and multiply the result by 6.

    That, and I've gotten used to it while playing GW skirmish games, which are my favorite things from GW.

    I like the way that GW makes it's d66 tables, because they tend to group results. So for a random encounter table, there may be 6 results that involve different groupings of skaven, 6 for undead, 6 for orcs, etc.

    It would be fun if someone made a 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 60 die, like they do for d10s.

    TAZ
    I'm not saying how you roll the dice is completely irrelevant. Of course, choosing between whether you want linear probablities or a curve is important, whether or not yu have exploding dice, how you count successes etc. I'm just saying that if you take a system, and all you do is change how the dice are rolled, it's not going to matter THAT much. (Provided you account for that change, of course).
    Case in point, when people describe me what a system is like, "you roll something based on your skill rating to determine success" is completely enough for this subject. I don't really care if it's dice pools, roll and add, roll under or whatever. And the exact type of dice is completely unimportant at that level of discussion, unless there's something really special about it (for instance, if it requires you to own lots of reltively rare dice - Dread: TFBoP, I'm looknig at you).
    The effect of the exact way dice are rolled on the overall design is similar to the effect of whether powers cost, on average, 10 mana or 20 mana, or whether every character has 5 or 10 powers available for him to choose from. Sure, it's important, but not THAT important.

    I guess what I really want to say is, it somewhat annoys me when somebody begins designing a game, and is then stuck in a long discussion of whether to use, for instance, 1d12 or 2d6, debating linear vs curve etc. My reaction at that point would be "whatever, choose one of them randomly and move on to the actual stuff that are important in your game. This is a minor tweak you'll be able to get to later, perhaps during playtesting."
