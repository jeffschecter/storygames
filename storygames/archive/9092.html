\n A few months ago, I ran a Trail of Cthulhu scenario for our group. Simon liked it and asked me to write it up, so he could publish it.

And here it is, fully published and catchily called "The Dying Of St Margaret's", because we couldn't think of a better name, except for one that gave the mystery away.

(This is the problem with naming horror scenarios. You start out with names like "The Mystery Under The Floorboards", then realise that everyone will just look under the floorboards and ruin the whole thing. Anyway, I digress.)



Basically, this scenario takes everything I love about Trail of Cthulhu and brings it to the foreground. At the beginning, you decide what drives your character to investigate; whatever it is, it will drive them mad.

It's a Purist adventure, so there's no hope, no salvation when faced with the Mythos: you get to enjoy your character going through a slow, downward spiral of impotent madness.

So there you go. I have a feeling that traditional Call of Cthulhu players will totally not get this game. You guys will, though, because it's all story-based. So if you could go and buy it, I'd be very grateful. It's available on RPG Now and the Pelgrane store.

Graham

    \n I hope people like it. Let's see. It's halfway between a traditional scenario and something more story-oriented: so it'll either be an interesting mix or a Frankensteinian failure. Do tell me what you think.

    It's now available at IPR, too, for $4.95: The Dying Of St Margaret's.

    One of my favourite things about Trail of Cthulhu is Credit Rating. Superficially, this just refers to how much money you have: if you have Credit Rating 3, you're comfortable; if you have Credit Rating 5, you're wealthy. Really, though, it's your social class: if you have Credit Rating 3, you're middle-class; any lower and you're not respectable in polite society.

    This scenario, then, makes a really big thing of Credit Rating. If your Credit Rating is two or lower, you can't be a teacher at the school, you'll be asked to carry luggage and many people won't shake your hand. However, you'll fit right in at the local pub, and you'll be able to sneak away to investigate without anyone asking questions. Mechanically-enforced snobbery. It's the way forward.

    Graham
