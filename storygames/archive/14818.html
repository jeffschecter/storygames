\n I'm realizing that I only prefer rpgs that tell me how to run them. I'm also realizing that I'm extremely tired of picking up a game that I'm excited about only to end up getting to the the GM advice section, but never finding the section that has a codified way of how the game is to be played or run.

I'm starting to drift over to board games because at least a board game needs to tell you how to play THE ENTIRE game, not just how to make characters and resolve conflicts.

These are the games that tell me how to run them enough so that I feel comfortable, well, running them:

Mouse Guard
Apocalypse World
FreeMarket (just barely)
Primetime Adventures
How We Came To Live Here
In a Wicked Age (just barely)
Dogs In The Vineyard
On Mighty Thews (just barely)

These are some games that I think will do that, but I still haven't figured them the hell out:

With Great Power
Burning Empires
Poison'd


So, I'm basically looking for games that COMMAND me how to run them. I like that constriction! I thought I was looking for no-prep games, but I'm cool with prep as long as it's structured. That's why I haven't found a super hero (one of my favorite genres/settings) game that I've stuck with.

Any recommendations?

PS: I'm not looking for GM-Less/GM-Full (whatever) games. I love SHU, Zombie Cinema, Drowning & Falling, and Fiasco; but I want a GM or at least a game with different types of player roles.

    I re-read the 3E DMG only a few weeks ago and it's very bad! Possibly one of the worst core books in D&D history ever (let's not count splats, because then it gets just atrocious). And I'm not picking on it because it's D&D - I think the AD&D DMG is great (if terribly organized) and the 4E DMG is pretty good, too! It's good only in retrospect because I was able to recognize it and pick it apart. 3.x is still the most played game at our table and the baseline system for us and frankly I'd still throw the 3E DMG on the fire and never miss it.

    "I already know how to play D&D!" Really? You sure?
    \nRon Edwards (and Rob MacDougall) argue in an ol' Forge article that there is no uniform D&D we learned how to play. I think he's right:

    "Rob MacDougall stated it best: we are talking about Cargo Cults. Everyone knew about "this new great game." Everyone had on hand a hodgepodge of several texts, which in retrospect seem to me to be almost archeological in their fragmentary, semi-compatible but not-quite, layered-in-time-of-publication nature. Also, although newly-available texts obviously modified local oral traditions, they also arose from them, generating a seething hotbed of how-to-play instructions in print in other locations. Everyone had to shape, socially and procedurally, just what the hell you did such that "role-playing" happened. How did you know it worked? What did you do it for? All of it, from Social Contract right down to Stance, had to be created in the faith that it worked "out there" somewhere, and somehow, some way, it was supposed to work here." (Edwards 2004)

    \n In the second edition of "Trollbabe" Ron Edwards added more than 60 pages (from 47 to 108 pages). Most of them are dedicated to detailed explanation of the flow of the game (with diagrams!!). how to run it, and a lot of examples. (The game is even simplified from the first edition, there are LESS rules, so the added pages are really even more than 61) .

    Seeing that Trollbabe is the grandmother of most "declare conflict, set stakes, tell what you do" games, most of that added content is useful for other games of that kind (PTA, Dogs in the Vineyard, etc).
