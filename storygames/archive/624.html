So, I've got this project in mind: to record and analyze the actual gaming practices of various people as they play. I'd want to have at the very least an audio recording of what happened, plus interviews with the participants. A video recording, and annotations from each participant on what they were thinking and doing during play (correlated with the audio and video) would be even better.

This might help us get some hard data behind some of the things we bat around as theory, and could help us see where our self-perceptions of how we play are and aren't accurate.

So here's my question: would you be willing to have your group recorded? Would you be willing to help transcribe the data? Would you want to get involved with coding and statistical analysis? Would you be okay with me publishing in academic journals based on your group's play? What questions would you really want answered by a project of this nature?

Posting here doesn't commit you to participate, I'm just testing the water to see if it's something that people would be into. (For one thing, I wouldn't want to pull all my groups from just one gaming subculture!)

Maybe it's going to grow into a not-so-little idea, but I'm curious what people think, particularly as I don't have much of a travel budget so I would need to enlist people to record their own groups. I might be able to send recording equipment though.

--Jess

    I'm really interested in both online and tabletop play. (So, Brendan: yes!!!) And LARP, for that matter, but I have no idea how I'd handle the recording of it.

    Like I said, I'm not yet ready to start this project, but it sounds like there are groups who'd be willing to get involved, so that's really exciting.

    As far as the interviews/etc.: I would probably collect the tapes, get freeform impressions of after-action reports, then hand you back transcripts of the game and ask you to make notes of what you were thinking or planning as much as you can remember, linked to specific moments in the game and if possible, to specific things that people said or did. Finally, I'd do an extended interview over the phone or chat or IM, depending on the specific questions I had in mind for the research and what needed clarification/expansion from the previous information I got from the session.

    This isn't an entirely established methodology, but I'm drawing on a couple of different research traditions (which I'm reading about right now! hence the not being ready to start yet) in designing my approach . . . .

    Jess,

    Sounds like a neat idea. How long do you picture this game session being? One reason I'm wondering is that the transcribing spoken data generally takes at least three or four times as long as the data itself, and that's if you're fairly good at it. It's somewhat dependent on the level of detail you're going for, of course, but even for a fairly broad transcription, prepare for the worst when it's time to transcribe.

    Basically, I'm saying -- neat idea! But transcription will be a lot of work, so be ready for it to take a long time, and don't get discouraged :-)

    (Of course, you may know all this already. But the first time I transcribed a ten minute spoken text, I thought it would never end, and there were only two speakers. So, forewarned, and all that.)
