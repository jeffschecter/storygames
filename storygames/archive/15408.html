\n The fact that these problems exist is a testimony to how far game design has progressed in the past decade, and a huge part of that credit goes to indie games folks, building on early ideas like Ian Millington's "Ergo" document (yes, that link is Andy K interviewing Ian in 2002).

However, in some recent game design discussions and contest games (Game Chef, Stage One, etc.), I've seen two issues that I think are worth discussing.

First is vestigial GMs, which is where there is still nominally a GM in a game, but their role has been so stripped down and limited that the GM player (1) doesn't really have all that much to do and (2) playing them doesn't seem very fun. Having a limited role can be fine if a game is really short and maybe players take turns handling the less-fun aspects, but obviously it's better if all players can be fully engaged in the game (even if their engagement takes radically different forms) and if all the players have the potential for roughly equal enjoyment (again, even if that enjoyment takes different forms). When I see a possibly vestigial GM role in a game, I often suspect that the game would be better without a GM at all, but maybe the designer doesn't feel fully comfortable in their ability to design such a game or worries that the game needs an authority figure to keep it on the rails and make sure it hangs together. Neither of those demonstrates much confidence and ambition, I think, and I would recommend that designers push past that, as their games and their design abilities will probably improve by overcoming that challenge.

The second and more complicated problem is relatively shallow GM-lessness, where a designer has not (A) come up with a game structure and premise that doesn't require a GM but instead (B) started from the standpoint of designing a game with a GM... and then excised the GM by either (1) distributing those responsibilities among the players or (2) using a "rotating GM" setup where those responsibilities move about the group during play. The reason that this is a more complicated problem is that, as far as I can tell, most notable GM-less games (Polaris, Fiasco, etc.) seem to have started from Option B rather than Option A, at least at some point in their design history. That is to say, most designers still begin designing new games using their years of expectations from GM'ed play as their foundation for design. Only rarely do I encounter games that, from the outset, were designed without the assumption that GM-like responsibilities would have to be performed by someone, either a semi-traditional GM or distributed/rotating responsibilities. However, I think this is something we can and will gradually move beyond, but it will take some work. Many Nordic-influenced games are leading the way in this regard (Montsegur 1244 does not assume there will be GM responsibilities anywhere, for example), but there are other examples more "indigenous" to the Anglo-American-Canadian indie games tradition, such as some of Jackson Tegu's games. In addition, there's still extensive room for progress on the Option B side, where designers really take GM-lessness and bake it into the core principles and mechanics of their games, rather than simply layering GM-like responsibilities on top. The more successful GM-less games (including Polaris, Fiasco, etc.) don't make you wish that there was still a GM, don't make you feel that it would all be simpler and more elegant without all this distributed/rotating business. But we can still go further, I think, working against our own design tendencies and assumptions from a lifetime of GMs.

Anyway, just wanted to bring those issues up and keep them in people's minds. GM-less play isn't something that we've figured out. What we've discovered is just the tip of the iceberg and there's a lot more to come below it, but it won't get revealed on its own and we have to keep digging to see the rest of it.

    \nJohnzo: Maybe you're right that Montsegur 1244 was not the best example. To me it still feels like we're in the early days of exploring GM-less design, so it's a bit difficult to think of games that start from a completely GM-free place. The one that immediately comes to mind is Jackon's Stage One game Differences, which is striking in how different the social setup is, but I think you can still conceivably have a more traditional RPG premise and still start from a place where you don't assume GM-like responsibilities.

    Josh: Certainly there are other alternatives! There's every other possible setup imaginable! We've just been trained to think of stories in terns of protagonists + opposition and divide things up that way in our heads. Even the kinds of stories we think are appropriate for RPGs are constrained to the ones that fit a GM-player(s) model. For example, it was interesting in reading the Stage One games to see how many designers took single-player video games and tried to make them work for 4-5 players, the traditional standard for RPGs. Why are we still stuck in the "party of characters" mindset? The GM-player thing is similar and just as deeply ingrained.

    One thing that helps me, I think, is trying to take stories that I don't think are especially well-designed for a traditional RPG setup and imagining how to make them work at the table. That helps me reconsider my expectations. So say... music videos. If I wanted to make a game that feels like the video for Michael Jackson's "Scream", having a GM just seems stupid, right? That authority setup isn't at all suggested by the arrangement of forces in the fiction. And then sometimes I can take that perspective with me when I examine fictions that more closely resemble traditional RPG setups. Like, say, The Matrix. You have a party of individuals on a ship fighting against an alien outside force, which sounds very much like a traditional RPG. But there's also no reason that the player responsible for the machines or events in the matrix has to also have authority over situations in the real world or play other humans. I mean, the machines may not even have a player at all, because they could be automated through the mechanics, since their desires are straightforward and logical.

    I don't have a great set of solutions here, obviously, because I struggle with these issues myself. Geiger Counter and Metrofinal both show signs of me struggling to deepen the GM-lessness and not being satisfied with simply distributing traditional GM responsibilities, but they're not as developed in that regard as I eventually want them to be.

    It's critical we dismantle the notion of the heroic auteur as a lone writer, director, or whatever. Even novelists need editors, agents and confidantes.     \n Just a quick question. Where does Capes fit into this model? It doesn't really fit either of the thread title categories, in my opinion. I find it to be a fantastic GM-less game. Antagonism is provided by your fellow players, authority is determined by resource allocation, and resources are gathered by creating interesting antagonism. My complaint is that it could have a little more crunch, that the characters could use a little more real differentiation (related to crunch), and the real narrative punch never materialized for me like it did in say, DitV. I have a suspicion that this is directly related to both characters and situation. If one were to take a DitV situation and characters, it seems to me that a somewhat similarly narratively punchy game could be had by using Capes to run it. Not to take away from the DitV resolution mechanics, but it is the character creation and town creation rules that make the difference between DitV and Capes for me.

    Continuing my stream of consciousness post, GM-less games have been interesting to me, but have never really had the narrative impact for me as GMed games precisely because the situation and characters have not had the same punchiness as the GMed games contain. Lady Blackbird practically runs itself sometimes, DitV can run itself sometimes, TSOY can run itself sometimes, I have thought many times that In a Wicked Age should be a GM-less game already, and each of those games has created some serious stories with amazing narrative impact. These games could, under your definitions, be almost called Vestigial GM Games. The primary job of the GM in these games is to create and then present the situation, and at times provide antagonism. When the players create the antagonism themselves, that just leaves creation and presentation of the situation as the job of the GM. In Lady Blackbird, the situation is given to you with the game, so some structure to do what GM-less games do to create antagonism would mostly eliminate the need for a GM. In some games of DitV I have played, the players actually created more antagonism than the GM, and the GMs job was just to create and present the situation, then the party blew up and shot each other in the face. In a Wicked Age helps you create a great situation, and antagonism is often provided by other players, so I have felt many times that I was closer to a player when I was a GM than I was to a traditional GM in other games.

    Here would be a fun experiment. Take Lady Blackbird. Dump the rules and play it with Capes. Likely to be a fantastic adventure game, no GM required. Situation from LB, antagonism and authority from Capes.

    As an alternative, create a situation with In a Wicked Age (situation). Play it with Remember Tomorrows turn taking rules (authority, antagonism). For added punch, use the DitV character creation and resolution rules.

    \n GM-less games that don't detail the GM functions, or rule-bind them, can sometimes work wonderfully. (Since I'm a humble person, I'll just note this is the case for Fuck Youth). I think one reason is that if person X is given one form of authority by the game - "The GM controls NPCs" - people will automatically look to person X for other things as well (when does the game begin? what's this scene like?), while if there is no central authority, people have to listen to each other and get attuned to what the other players are doing.

    So, a hypothesis that might be addressed: There's a much higher need for mutual respect, giving each other space, and listening to each other in games with no specified authority. If this need is fulfilled by the players, these games have the potential to be more personal and accepting of fragility.

    \n So, Blackrat and I were discussing similarish themes last night, and ended up agreeing[*]that in order for GMly power to be distributed amongst the players or, if you prefer, absent from any one individual, you need some pretty clear guidelines on who can do what. Our experience has been that some games (I'm thinking of Fiasco, though it isn't the only one) leave room for doubt in the mind of the players as to who can do what.

    For example
    - Who can define facts about the world outside of the current situation, if anyone? If the answer is "nobody can", then how do such facts come to be known? Purely by implication? Who is allowed to create in-game events that imply the existence of facts outside of the current situation?
    - Who can define facts about the current situation?
    - Who decides how each character in a scene acts?
    - Who decides how conflicts between characters turn out?
    - What happens if two people who are both allowed to do one of the above things disagree?

    Now, clearly it's possible to proceed without answering these questions and just kind of roll with it and hopefully everything is ok. But we have found that, in the absence of an agreed culture of who-does-what (classically, "the GM does most of those things, the players just control their characters, and the game mechanics arbitrate disputes", but there can be and are other ways of doing it), and lacking clear guidance from the game system itself, great uncertainty and lack of confidence are created. Exciting creative opportunities are missed, because players are unclear what their scope for action is.

    So anyway, back to my original point, which is that there need to be answers to all the above questions in a given game, and explicit guidance answering each of them is a good idea. I suspect, but don't know, that the phenomena referred to of vestigial/shallow GMlessness are connected to the fact that such guidance is not always present.

    [*] Blackrat isn't here, but I'm totally using my GM-authority to say that he agrees.

    \n I'm also unsatified with the design of many of the GMless games I've stumbled upon. While taking out responsabilities from one player and distributing it among all participants seems to be the way to go, most of the solutions to this either reveal the plot twists too early to allow players enjoy discovering them, or leave players to build a story without enough coherence to make any other genre but comedy, unless they all are used to collaborate in writing stories.

    Now, it's not that you can't have fun with those games, or even make them do what you want withouth hacking the system; it's just that most of them won't fully work with a group of players where at least one of them has some experience at GMing, and some crucial rules are so open to players interpretation that the temptation of crashing the game on purpose can become too strong for players that put their fun before the fun of the group.

    I've heard it before, that's the player's fault, but a system with too many holes is a magnet for players like this, so I prefer to do my best to prevent the issue.

    Well, solutions I've come up (actually hacked down) so far:

    -Hack of the Whimsy cards, poker-like version. Genre and main elements of the story are defined in 10 minutes before playing, then players propose scenes and challenges to each other in turns, however challenges are restricted to anything that uses the phrases/ concepts from the cards. To answer the challenge, players says yes or rolls the dice. On the first turn players use one card from their hand to state a challenge, the number increases each turn, so the game ends when a player has no cards left. There's a mechanic for making an epilogue after the last challenge is dealt with.

    Pros: quick to learn, story scalates pretty well in all testings done, player crativity gets a help from the cards and everybody can suggest things, so the game hardly stalls at all. Defining genre and story elements in the preparating stage helps keeping coherence of the story. Even when throwing crap in the system, the game keeps going and the story keeps escalating, though the genre inevitably turns into comedy. It makes for a good short game (2 to 3 hours long using all cards, number of cards used can be reduced to shorten the session)

    Cons: first time players have to pass through a 5 minute process of adaptation. Though it can be a bit awkward for players with no experience in RPGs, everybody gets the hang of it in a while. The game starts on collaborative mode but can turn into competitive mode quite soon, with players setting ridiculous challenges to each other. However, since there's no clear winner (the player who ends up with no cards sooner can call for the conclusion, but that just ends the game. The one with the best story can be the winner in the eyes of the group, but that's so abstract that you can't have rules for that.) the ending of the game doesn't feel competitive at all. The game can't be used for long campaings. In challenges where the life of the PCs are at stake, since the mechanics state that PCs can't die until the concusion scene, it's kind of strange to see PCs defy death until the very end of the story. The story twists can also be quite exxagerated for some players, and the way it's done, it doesn't allow any player to have narrative control for longer than one turn of the game. This makes frustrating trying to send the story in one way or another at will, but if you learn to let it go, you can actually get to enjoy the results.

    shortened hack of burning wheel for the setting, players define the objective of their characters and their relationship to each other, main threats and hopes are defined and then a competitive storytelling game begins. Players strate the next step in the sory of their characters, ther rest of the player add the obstacles, player says yes or rolls the dice. Turn of the next player and so it goes. To aid player input I'm using a sort of "apples to aples" cards, where you take three random cards to help you describe any obstacle, place, character or item the PCs find in their way. Perhaps I'll recycle again the whimsy cards for complicating the challenges and trigger the mechanism for character growth, allowing PCs to even change their initial objective according to the story they build ingame.

    \n I'm working on a more stripped down Matrix Game that is pretty darned close to GMless.

    The "game host" picks who goes first in each scene and (if the players are playing competitively) picks who wins from a set of players nominated by the players.

    The players face three scenes.

    The first player makes up a story about what happens. Other players may try to replace it or modify it with "challenges". The first player and the challenger roll dice to see who wins. The high roller wins, re-roll ties. Players get to challenge once per turn.

    Every player gets to start a turn per scene, so you go around the table once.

    At the end of the third scene the game ends.

    The host's job is to keep things moving and make certain players have a good time. That's all that remains of the GM role.

    So far the playtests have been going well. I'm hopeful that I'll have this out as a commercial game next year. The working title now is "Snapshot: 1969" cos it has a 60's theme.

    ***

    I don't think there is anything wrong with using GMs. I like to run the occasional traditional RPG but not for very long. GMing is really hard work. As a design mechanic it puts a lot of stress on one point of the machine. If that part fails (for any reason) the machine grinds to a halt and crashes. GM games also rely on a lot of expertise in one player. That is asking a lot. It's just what Kriegspiel did two hundered years ago.

    Chris Engle

      \n You can do it by making the procedures into gates you pass through as you play.

      Vincent called this "giving the mechanics teeth." Like, in Dogs, if you raise but don't say what you do, the game grinds to a halt. The other person can't answer without knowing what kind of fallout they're taking, and they can't know that unless you say what you do. So, "say what concrete action your character takes when you make a Raise" isn't just a good thing to do, it's required for play to continue.

      This is why everyone forgets the harm move in AW. As written, it doesn't have teeth. However, it's actually the "do I take less harm?" move, and if you present it like that, people tend to remember better. Still, it needs tweaking so it's not just a recommended procedure, but a gate to pass through as you play.
