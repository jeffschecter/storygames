    \n Something I just read in the live chat for today's seminar:

    Monte: the play session that I envision with the fighter and wizard fighting together is that the figher is always better than the wizard. The fighter hits someone for 12 damage and then the wizard hits someone for 4, and the wizard wishes he was a fighter. Then that happens again on the second round, and the wizard feels the same way again. But then on the third round the wizard whips out his fireball and does 16 or 20 damage total and the fighter goes ahh, I wish I was a wizard. I want each class to shine and to have reasons to want to play that class.

    I don't know, to me that sounds problematic at the least. I'm all for classes shining at different things, but having one guy who's pretty good all the time and one guy who's great twice (or however often he can fireball) sounds like a bad combination. The fighter has to take more rounds to get stuff done, the wizard has nothing to do when he's not blowing a powerful ability.

    \n I think we need to take a step back regarding the whole Fighter vs. Wizard issue.

    First of, I have learned to be extremely wary of anything Monte says. When Mearls talks about what they're doing with the game I'm always like "hell yeah!" and when Monte talks I'm like "uuuuh". I don't know if that's just bias, but mostly I think he's really bad at articulating what they're doing because he's kinda stuck in this gaming vernacular of ten years ago. I dunno. Anyway...

    So we want everyone to shine in combat all the time, yes? That's a nice thought, and 4E delivers on that very well. But it's also extremely narrow. So the Fighter should be as good as the Thief at disarming traps and picking pockets? I realize this is a bit of a strawman but you don't expect the Savvyhead to be as good at violence as the Gunlugger either, right?

    I guess this boils down to whether we regard combat as the core activity of D&D or just one possible activity. Since post 3E combat can take hours to complete, it's obvious we want everyone to be super-effective at it all the time. But if it's true that 5E's combat returns to the days of the 10 minute fight, then combat is just one of those things that you do during an adventure. And when there's a fight the Fighter gets to shine and everyone is helping and doing their thing, just not as good as that guy. And the same goes for all the other activities. It's not a big deal because it doesn't take up 80% of the session.

    A Mouse Guard character without the Fighting skill is still perfectly valid, right?

    \n Oh, definitely. 4e fighters kinda got screwed for skills, since they get the least selections of any class and their preferred ability scores mainly helped them with stuff like Endurance and Athletics so that they're behind the curve on skills for anything social or mental, even intimidation.       Posted By: Neko EwenThey're currently doing another seminar, onReimagining Skills and Ability Scores.       \nI think that's a great idea when the comparison is based on the shared fiction of the situation.

      If she can jump over it because the gap's only a few feet with solid footing on each side and a bit of run-up, great!

      If she can jump over it because the GM wants her to jump over it so he mentally set the target number so whatever STR 15 can jump over, not so great.

      Put another way, unless this is a pre-written adventure, did the GM really use this system, or did he just decide the gap was jumpable and say yes?

      For a system like that to work it has to rely on more than just flailing GM judgement calls trying to create "fun." It has to give the GM tools for really being able to decide which gaps are auto-jumpable.

      \n Latest Legend & Lore article clarifies a few points about 5E:
      \nhttp://www.wizards.com/dnd/article.aspx?x=dnd/4ll/20120206
      \n
      \n
      To be clear, we're not talking about creating a bridge so that you can play 1E and 4E at the same time. Instead, we're allowing you to play a 1E-style game or a 4E-style game with the same rules. Also, players at the table can choose the style of character they want to play.
      \n
      \n
      \n
      The way we want to accomplish handling the style of play is with a modular approach. If 3E style is about character customization and a tactical view of combat, options should allow you to customize characters with feats and skills, plus play with a grid and miniatures (and have rules that support threatened areas, attacks of opportunity, and so forth). But in a 2E-style game, some or all of these options would not be desirable. Because of this truly modular approach, it means you don't have to pick an edition style. You can have the simple, fast combat of 1E with the character customizing skills of 3E, or any other combination.
      \n
      \n
      \n
      The next big question you might have, however, is that with everything being so customizable, who makes the decisions?
      \n
      \nI think some of the answers are player-provided answers, and some are DM-provided. This is tied in very closely with my philosophy of the game overall. Players should play the characters they want to play (with DM input), and DMs should run the games they want to run (with player input).
      \n
      \nSome choices then\u2014such as whether a character has a long list of skills and feats; or skills, feats, and powers; or just ability scores, hit points, Armor Class, and an attack bonus\u2014are up to the player. Some choices are up to the DM. If miniatures and a grid are used, that's a DM choice. If the adventures are going to be about grinding through a dungeon to get enough coppers to pay for tomorrow's meal or an epic quest across the planes to save the universe(s), that's a DM choice. (That latter choice might seem like flavor only, but it can determine which rules options are taken.)
      \n
      \n
      \nA few thoughts about the latest polls & results:
      \n- Looks like only around 2,000-3,000 people are replying to these polls. There are reportedly around 60,000 DDI users.
      \n- Voters lean towards Fast & Simple rules and Tactical Combat with most wanting something in the middle.
      \n- Voters lean away from Simulationist games with most wanting something in the middle.
      \n- Voters primarily want Story-based and Heroic Action games.
      \n- In terms of importance, #1 is Story-based, #2 Heroic Action, #3 Fast & Simple, #4 Tactical Combat, and in last place, Simulationist.
      \n- I don't know if it is clear what Story-based or Simulationist means exactly.

      \nThis is a fantastic blog post.

      Also: http://critical-hits.com/2012/02/08/exploring-dd-at-ddxp/\n
      In every edition I have ever played, my favorite phrase as a DM is \u201cdon\u2019t look at your sheet, but tell me what you want your character to do.\u201d For players who only took part in later editions, that is sometimes a very difficult concept to wrap one\u2019s mind around. The more detailed and codified the rules become, the greater tunnel-vision one might get on the character sheet or on the battlemat. Clinging to the letter of the rules code is totally understandable, especially if one is punished by a DM (or yelled at by other players) for not doing so. I have had to bite my tongue (not easy for me at times) when a new player wanted to do something cool like have his fighter roll a barrel at oncoming foes, only to be told dismissively by the DM or other players, \u201cJust take a regular attack with your javelin. It\u2019s right there on your sheet.\u201d What a moment of potential exploration lost!

      Exploring the interaction with the environment, exploring how the rules cover certain situations, and exploring a fun, imaginative solution to a problem should never be dismissed so easily. Even if the solution is ridiculous or wrong-headed, there is the potential for a good DM and willing players to discuss the situation and form an imaginative and relevant consequence. What separates a good RPG from a board game is the ability to do anything, even things not written in the rules (or on character sheets).

      Somewhere between the exploration of rules and the exploration of the game world is a middle ground where, for me, the crux of the game lies. When I play, I try to keep my focus (and my mind\u2019s eye) squarely on the exploration of what my character\u2019s life and experiences are like\u2014put most simply, it is an exploration of an adventurer\u2019s life. This is what each edition seems to have moved further away from, until it is almost hand-waved. I understand that some people do not want to deal with the minutiae of tracking every copper piece and every bolt shot from a crossbow. I respect that. But I also want a game where interaction with the environment is important, whether that environment be a monster-filled dungeon or a town full of merchants. I want to avoid using the term \u201csimulationist,\u201d because I do not want rules that attempt to simulate how every single element of the game world works. But I want the game to simulate how my fantasy character lives her life.



      \nIt really sounds like D&D 5E is moving in the direction of Apocalypse World / Dungeon World. Fiction First, Moves Second.

      \nOb rant:

      Sim is a trap. It's a terrible terrible trap, because you think it does what you want, until one day you realize all fun has been leached out of your games.

      A lot of what I want from "simmy" games is "just playing the game". The agenda isn't to examine or produce anything, but to just ... experience the game. The least important bit of this is experiencing the way the rules work (which is the only thing Sim focuses on). The more important parts are experiencing the characters both from within and without (which is great, and not a trap at all), or experiencing the world that characters inhabit (which is great too, but also the sim trap.)

      Then people tell you that system matters, and you start adding all sorts of stuff to better experience the world, without realizing that those fiddly bits were never the way you experienced the world -- let alone the character.

      Sure, sometimes the old fiddly bits got in the way of experiencing stuff because it was just too egregious the way you could jump 10 meters straight up but ran only 50 meters in 10 seconds. ...the reason you experienced stuff is because the GM said that the grass is lush and wet from the rain -- not because what the rules said.

      I strongly suspect that what most "Simulationists" -- including myself -- want is fairly lightweight rules that stay out of their way and don't produce stupid results unless you ask them stupid questions. Ironically a lot of thought that has gone into conflict resolution systems speaks to just that, but those techniques are also equated and conflated with narrative play... which leads people who aren't interested in "producing a story" or whatever to understandably avoid them.
