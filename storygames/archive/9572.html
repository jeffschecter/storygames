\n Here's the guidelines I've seen before

1) Elements should have at least one, preferably more, of these: Persons in need, objects of desire, and events that add pressure.
2) Contains concrete detail
3) Can be used again without referring to the same character or situation.

I think of these as the three "judges" - if every judge gives you a 10, you should get a phenomenal element.

Of my elements, these are the ones that score the highest marks in my mind:
A hook-billed crow, feasting merrily on a royal corpse\u2026
Human eyes, presented as proof of murder\u2026
Pikemen, grousing over the mad plans of their superior officers\u2026
A poor swordsman, soaked and chilled in his tattered cloak\u2026

Are any judges missing from the panel? Could the passing entries be better?

    \n I think there needs to be a balance of static vs. dynamic in the Oracles. If they're all dynamic then there's too much going on to make for a reasonable Chapter. Sometimes what you need is an element that can be repurposed to slot into a conflict that's created by other elements' dynamism. Rather than the Oracle entry being dynamic, its position in the burgeoning relationship map of best interests make it dynamic.

    All static elements is less helpful...too much to invent to really get going strong out of the gate. But all dynamic I think is too hectic. Too much going on makes things more confusing than exciting. Also, if you have too many dynamic things its too easy for players to focus on their own little piece.

    \n In general I think the best course when making new oracles is to design with result in mind.

    That is to say, do you want an oracle that explodes out the gate because your group has problems taking implied situations and making them dynamic? Then fuck, dynamic it up. OTOH, if your group has a strong "fuck each other with best interests" vibe already going on, nut has trouble coming up with unique characters or brilliantly painted situations, then focus the oracles on ones that create those unique characters and brilliant situations.

    If you're designing not for your own group, but a con game or some such, then consider the tone of the game you want to run. Do you want to limit player input in order to get immediate, if perhaps slightly stilted results? Then tell us what the swordsman is doing so that we can all get going. Do you want to have a game where players imprint their own moral or aesthetic impressions on characters? Then most certainly do not tell us what the swordsman is doing, we need to make that up ourselves.

    If you're designing for what the average poster on Story Games wants, well, you're fucked. So I suggest not doing that.

    Personally, for me and the folks I often play with, I like to have oracles that imply imbalance without being specific about the cause or course of it. Hordes of angry barbarians (why are they angry? where are they going? who do they want to kill? Don't tell me, this is what I will make up), vindictive sorcerers (vindictive why? against whom? will carry it out in what way? Don't tell me, this is what I will make up), swords with wyrd destinies (just as the previous two, don't tell me the damn destiny, that's mine to make up) -- these are great for me, as long as I'm not told what they are or what they're doing. I like oracles that act as a spur, rather like a Rorschach blot, to get me to fill in my own blanks rather than ones that try to do it for me. I want the oracle to give me something I might not have thought of rather than some overly detailed description of something I always think of and now am rather constrained by.

    \n I think I posted this long ago in another thread on similar subject:

    The entries are good mix of (a) places; (b) individuals; (c) groups/organizations/communities; (d) events; (e) objects - sometimes alone and sometimes intermixed, generally bespeaking goals/interests/directions that can then be imagined to contrast/conflict etc.

    E.G. Unquiet Past is (very rough quick review) Place (12); Person (34); Group (12) ; Events (5); Object (3)

    Entries should often include loaded, emotional-state-reflecting terminology. Chris Chinn\u2019s ideas about good conflict. Anger, fear, adulation, desperation etc.

    for example in OP, you have merry crow (who doesn't want to play a merry crow!) and grousing pikemen. Perhaps the swordsman in the tattered cloak needs a feeling etc. replace poor (tattered and poor sort of duplicate) with "Triumphant" or "Vengeful" or "Besotted" etc.
