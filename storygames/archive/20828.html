    #1: Blades in the Dark - I think this will be "the next big thing" after Fate or PbtA
    \nWould you like to tell us why? I'm curious!
    \nYes, here is why I think Blades in the Dark is "the next big thing":

    Much like Fate, Blades in the Dark has a solid, simple, universal resolve mechanism. You have an Action Roll, a Resistance Roll and a Fortune Roll covering most basic situations. They are resolved with simple d6s using simple parameters, some options (i.e. Devil's Bargain). There is a differentiated outcome beyond success/fail.

    Much like PbtA, you have excellent playbooks tightly interlocked with the setting. Both as player as well as GM, you can start right off into the action with practically no prep. Basically, a lot of PbtA-style play is incorporated.

    Great tools like tons of vivid random tables or the project clock that help a GM easily put out enjoyable challenges out of their hat.

    Overall, Blades in the Dark brings together some of the smartest current RPG mechanics and puts them together in a concise & well-designed format. It achieves both simple rules-light play as well as rich game depth.

    Thus, I think it will be a role model, inspiration and basis for the next generation of RPGs.

    \n Some of the other things I really like about Blades are the "annoying planning removal tools" - the idea that you can jump right in and get going because if you forgot something stupid, you can retroactively do it via flashback at no cost, and if you "forgot" something that seems really clever, you can STILL flashback that at a minor cost.

    Also, equipment takes a page from Dungeon World's "adventuring gear" - you just pick how MUCH stuff you're carrying at the beginning of the "score" and as you need stuff on your list, you check it off until you are carrying as much as you said you were.

    Both of those features allow you to skip over the sorts of fiddly advance planning that often bogs down my groups.

    \n Three more things to add:

    It will be published by Evil Hat.

    Adding to Airks comment: It's even better because you can entirely skip planning & preparing a quest (i.e. heist). You just choose between 6 different plan types (Assault, Deception, Stealth, Occult, Social, Transport) and roll a dice for the starting situation of the quest. This saves several hours of time of usual RPG quests and cuts right into the meaty action. (I remember the flashback device also from Leverage RPG, btw)

    I had offered an early version of Blades in the Dark on a small gaming convention. I didn't know I had a slot, I just had the print-outs, barely new the game myself and had no prep time. It worked out great and I would like to add: it worked out great by design.
    John Harper has been vocal about favoring zero-prep GMing. The whole design of the mechanics as well as the creative and abundant random tables empower this style of play.
