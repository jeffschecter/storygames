\n With WotC bringing their character generator program to their own hosted service on the web, instead of being a separate, downloaded program, this will give them a rather unique opportunity to gather statistics on how people create their characters. What races are most common, which classes are played (or created) the most, what feats and skills are never taken, and so on.

It'll be interesting to see if they use this for marketing and product development purposes -- e.g., if they know that elves and rangers are popular, they could produce more material supporting elves and rangers, and less material supporting (say) gnomes and paladins.

If you could know how players were using your game rules, what information would you use, and how would you use it?

    \n [From a marketing perspective, such a sample is rather skewed towards the "hardcore" and the moneyed (assuming it's a pay-to-access service, like D&DI). But that's not your thread's point, so....]

    For GLASS, I intend to offer an online database (free, ideally, but TBD) tool for generating game types and, from those settings, characters attached to actual game events using those game types.

    I intend to look most closely at:
    * Choice of Priority (Player--only at risk of death when you choose--and World--higher verisimilitude)
    * Choices for Resurrect Toggle and Options (for World Priority games; illegal in Player Priority)
    * Most popular Defects, as that might show me (a) twink loopholes or (b) degree of costuming and makeup
    * How often Linked is used (it's the most complex Limitation, and I suspect needs streamlining)
    * How often Special Armor is used (could imply folks don't like the default requirement that real armor be real, with the weight and bulk that entails)

    I could go on... but mainly I'll use the data to:
    * Improve systems that are a source of twink
    * Better-target banner ads to suit frequent props (costuming) and makeup choices
    * More germane examples and Templates, for GLASS 2 ("Polished Edition").
