\n One of my best friends is tackling the old nugget, The Tomb of Horrors, and giving me weekly updates about how his troupe is doing, funny stuff that happened, etc. Every time I hear the mention of that module, it brings to mind a conversation I had while playing it years ago. I've had the conversation many, many times since, and still can't find a solution to the metagaming aspect of "puzzle" games.

The basic situation is puzzles, enigmas, traps, riddles... they're metagame by nature.

I designed several puzzle dungeon crawls, myself, back in the day; and even then would always feel bad if the *players* couldn't figure out the puzzles, or even if they were too easy, etc. Maybe I hadn't given them the clues correctly... maybe they just weren't clever enough... maybe the gimmick was flawed to begin with. Whatever the case, it just never felt right to make it so the *players* had to figure out a particular puzzle. After all, I didn't make them stand up and kill something IRL during combat, or *really* convince *me* when trying to lie to an NPC, etc. Nor did it feel right to just make them roll and tell them "The answer to the riddle is 'an egg.'" I've even tried various combinations which all turned out to be basically, "If the players can't figure it out, allow them some kind of roll." This usually lead to a lot of player dissatisfaction.

Right now, I'm kind of thinking that traps and riddles, etc. shouldn't even be designed for a role-playing scenario. Or, at least, not in a built-in way. I'm considering having a list of various clever things (puzzle traps *and* riddles) to have on hand. Then, in the design of a scenario writing something along the lines of "Deadly trap with a difficulty of N goes here," or "Gollum asks them a riddle." If they succeed, just kind of play it out with them in a more descriptive way like combat: (player gets a highly successful roll) "His riddle is easy. Your Gran used to full the neighborhood children with that one" then have the player just roll play a random answer. Or in the case of a puzzle trap, "Oh... the designer of this must have been a mad genius, you can see that through a complicated set of ropes and pulleys, one must actually brace one rope and cut another." The list of "really clever things" could be used for inspiration or when not feeling terribly improv-creative to add details to the RP.

So, what are other things people have tried before? What are things that have worked for you, or have "felt right" and have given the players a satisfying experience without insulting their intelligence?

    \n Problems with more than one solution always worked for me.

    For example, consider the Gordian Knot. It's a puzzle. You can fiddle with it for hours. Or you can chop it in half with your sword. Either way, something interesting happens, and your reputation changes as a result.

    I don't enjoy situations where there's a single answer that the players must figure out. It's more fun for me if I put them in a dungeon with a deathtrap that has a solution, and I then leave them to decide what to do:
    1. Try to solve the puzzle, which they might just be in the mood for;
    2. Push some buttons at random and then fight the monsters that appear, which is more likely;
    3. Exit the dungeon, and go find the broken-down old man who built the deathtrap, decades ago. Buy him some booze, talk it up. Get the answer from him. Turns out he used to be the Emperor's Master Clocksmith, but was exiled in disgrace after a scandal. Hired by some deranged priest, and tasked with creating a bunch of puzzle-traps to keep people out of the tomb. The players promise him ten percent of the haul, then they go back and "solve" the puzzle.

    Same thing goes for the riddle: "You meet an old witch. She gives you a cryptic riddle. What do you do?" My players will:
    1. Try to solve it (maybe);
    2. Try to bribe her (possibly);
    3. Attack (probably).

    You could probably come up with a few more options, but you get the idea. Players love finding solutions to problems (at least, mine do), but it sucks when you get the feeling that there's only one "right" answer to a problem, regardless of what kind it is. So I always create problems with multiple solutions, knowing full well that they're going to come up with something I would never have thought of.

    -- Rafael

    \n Firstly, you should be able to click the edit link on the first post of the thread and change the title to fix your typo.

    Secondly, there's a certain style of play where the players are trying to outwit the GM, are trying to solve the riddle, are trying to get through to the endgame goal in one piece. Don't look at it in terms of character immersion or roleplaying; look at it as player critical thinking challenges -- the riddle is as much a challenge for the players' brains as any combat set piece where they decide which move to take and which spell to cast. It's all about figuring out the best solution in a situation, and for that goal there must be a best solution and inoperable solutions, or else there's no thrill in figuring out the right one. Now, if your players are digging on this stuff, then presenting them with challenges, tactics-based or riddle-based, is the way to go. If your players want to immerse in character and really feel what Starbreeze Forestsong feels as she goes about looting goblin corpses, then the riddles are irrelevant, and should be dropped or handwaved ("There is a riddle about a duck.").

    \nAnd Raf has the solution for how to make these things interesting in non-competitive play. As Josh implies, simply don't tell the riddle. If you tell a riddle, immediately a player can't help but consider it a personal challenge. And he'll be sorely tempted to have his character know what he knows... even if this is totally implausible. That's bad. You've not only changed the player's creative agenda, but thrown them into Pawn Stance specifically.
    \n
    \nPresent the contest involved up front:
    \n
    \nGM: "He asks a riddle, and it's hard enough to be difficulty 4."
    \n
    \nOnce you've determined if the character has won the contest, then, if you like, present the riddle to the player. As a fun side-diversion. The player understands that his character's success or failure has already been determined, and that his own success or failure is meaningless to that. It's just some fun on the side.
    \n
    \nIf you do it in the reverse, having the roll after the player fails, then you're saying, "Well, you're not that smart, so we'll let you get bailed out by your character." And, yeah, that's just confusing in terms of creative agenda, etc. And perhaps demeaning.
    \n
    \n
    \nPut another way... let's say that a player makes a mistake in maneuvering his character in very tactical play... do we then allow the character a tactics roll to have made the better decision? No, that would totally drain the fun out of making the decisions for yourself, and having the rewards be directly related to how well you performed as a player.
    \n
    \nThe related subject here is the old "Do I let social skills resolve situations, or do I play out social encounters and see how they go?" The worry that people have is that if you allow character social skills to be used to resolve situations, that players will just ask for rolls, and not role-play out their character's dialog. If you're interested in getting that "role-play" out of players, you can still use social skills. Simply have them narrate the dialog until it becomes clear that there's an impasse in the discussion, and what's at stake becomes clear. Then move to the dice.
    \n
    \nIf the player can't narrate why his character was so convincing, then simply narrate, "Your character speaks in his usual eloquent fashion, and soon the barkeep spills what he knows about the guild." That's fine narration, and authors use it all the time. It's more important to explore the character, and how he handles things, than it is to do first person narration in most cases. Could be my own personal bias, but I feel that the latter is over-rated, and sought because most people assume it's the only way to get players to make interesting revelations about their characters. In point of fact, there are others.

    \n A few things, Ajax...
    \n
    \nFirst, my "that's bad" comment is in the context of an assumption about mode of play. An almost tautological one, actually. Basically if you're playing some mode outside of gamism (what I called "non-competitive play"), including gamism opportunities is bad.
    \n
    \nIf, in fact, player challenge is what play is all about, as I tried to indicate later, then riddles to the player are great, and you shouldn't dilute the reward cycle for the gamism by giving a simulative out. The player lost, he wants to feel that loss. Else he wouldn't be playing that way. If you ameliorate the loss, then you're basically saying that it doesn't matter how well the player does.
    \n
    \n
    \nAs for "mini-games" that's just fine. One could argue that the combat in TROS is a "mini-game" involving alot of gamism inside of the overall more narrativism oriented play. It's fine to "shift" between modes like this, so long as players aren't sent mixed messages. And riddles in otherwise simulative play, or sim amelioration of player challenge failure, is a distinctly mixed message.
    \n
    \nI'm using GNS modes as an example. Again there's also issues with stance and other creative agenda dissociations that can occur from mixing up how resolution occurs.
    \n
    \nWhen I run games these days, I make sure that it's quite clear that there is absolutely nothing that a player can do to increase his character's chances of winning contests. Yes, he can choose to have his character take on challenges that are easier or harder, but that has thematic output (especially if there's no challenge to getting the contest you want). In contests themselves, the player's role is, at most, to define how his character interacts with the challenge, which may increase his chances of winning, but only as a reward for participating in the simulation.
    \n
    \nI'm thinking of Hero Quest play, in particular. The player does create a higher chance of his character succeeding by indicating all of the abilties that pertain to the contest. But there's no challenge here, and the player is rewarded for doing this "accurately" or "entertainingly" and losing contests is not a punishment. So we have play where the reward cycles are not promoting gamism.
    \n
    \nAs soon as you put any challenge in front of a player, it's a near automatic switch in mode. Especially for competitive people like myself, yes, but I see this everywhere. Don't do it, unless competition is what you want.

    \n This has been an enlightening thread! I definitely appreciate Mike Holmes\u2019 advice to keep in mind the effect of riddles etc. on creative agenda. One other factor I\u2019ll throw into the mix is that the game is about everyone\u2019s fun\u2014including the GM. I like certain types of puzzles, therefore I like to insert puzzles into games I run. But I recognize the danger Mike and others have talked about where introduction of a puzzle could derail the game if the players can\u2019t solve it; ruin immersion; and potentially be unsatisfying.
    So, in line with the request at the start of the thread, here are some of the things I\u2019ve done to introduce puzzles in a way that both I and the players (I hope) enjoy. These ideas are not unique to me!

    A player-solved puzzle has an in-game alternative. Ex: an interlocking key puzzle needed to unlock a prisoner\u2019s cell can be obviated by a \u2018knock\u2019 spell on the cell door, lockpicking, or bending the bars. In play, a player spent about 10 seconds fiddling with the keys and then used magic.

    Use of props. Ex: a courier carries two letters, both of which are written out on paper, which are then rolled up as scrolls as props. The first copy is magically trapped to ignite if read by anyone other than the intended recipient unless the trap is dispelled. The text is in the clear. The second letter has a concealed message in what otherwise looks to be a normal correspondence, and is otherwise untrapped. The concealed message in the second letter is the \u2018in the clear\u2019 text of the first letter. In play, the players excitedly unrolled the first message without taking any precautions. It burst into flame. They circumspectly opened the second, and were at first dismayed to find it unhelpful. One player, however, quickly realized the key and translated the message, while the other characters pursued other investigations.

    Player-solved puzzles have handouts to reflect completing each step in solving the puzzle. The difficulty in solving some puzzles in game is the sheer time involved, and the possibility of making errors. Creating handouts reduces these problems. Ex: for an enciphered message, I usually have a handout with the frequency of the letters in the message. I give this to the players as soon as they start counting the letters or even as soon as someone suggests counting\u2014because obviously they are correctly guessing that the message is a simple single substitution. And I purposely build the original message so that the most common English letters are the most common. The handout lets them know they are on the right track, saves time, and limits the chance that a counting error will throw off the solution. And, of course, the solved message has a handout as well.
