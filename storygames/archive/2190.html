Front-loaded Immersion

wyrmwood
January 2007 edited January 2007 in Story Games
At a New Years Eve party I encountered a few other RPGers, whom I talked into playing Dogs in the Vineyards. Of particular note, one of the players was predominantly a larper (boffer variants), and described her play style in a way very similar to some of the ways I used to play in vampire (Mind's Eye Theater) larps, and it occured to me that this particular approach to play may be more common than I thought.

Specifically, creating a character with a deep metagame perspective - as in planning fitness with other players and their characters, ensuring specific types of situations you want versus those you don't (e.g. avoiding large combats), and otherwise pre-planning all of this into the character. And then following this by a deep immersive play - with minimal metagame thought occuring.

What intrigues me about this, is there is a self-competition aspect to this, about crafting the character that will - simply played to the hilt - produce the desired situations and satisfy the meta-game, without the need to retool or otherwise consider the meta-game while playing. The challenge itself makes things fun, in addition to the immersion.

Any thoughts?

- Mendel S.

    When we started Sorcerer, we spent an entire group meeting discussing the game and setting and tone, and another entire group meeting doing character generation (meaning that we didn't hit play till the 3rd session).

    By the time we got going, I had a very clear idea of who Jordan was. I had goals. I had snappy comebacks already resting inside my head. I knew who I was planning to hate.

    I approached each session, and each scene, with a very clear agenda for immersion. When JB (our GM) cut to the gym scene where my coach approaches to me "have a little talk", I already know that I'm going to be cold, cut him off, twist his words. I'm going to give him nothing, but at the same time I'm not going to be directly rude in any way. Just brisk.

    As far as Sorcerer goes, when the scene is mine it's immersion time. There is little to no collaboration. The scenes are quite short, because (a.) I have fairly simple goals for what I want to resolve/prove in every scene, and (b.) there is less "well, you could always" kind of talk. The scenes are very tight.

    I don't know that this is the "best" way to play a character, but I'm loving it. I have metagame goals for him. I know what I want as future demons. I am flexible on all counts, but I definitely have a vision and I have immersed myself in it.

    I'm not exactly sure on what you're asking, but I'm reading it as if there's pre-planning for what's going to happen in the game tied to the character creation. Based on my experiences and prefered playing style, yes and no. On the question if immersive play needs pre-planning of the character (regardless of what will happen in the play), then yes.

    I suspect the need to ditch the re-tooling and metagame during the play is one of the reasons, but not the only one. LARPing tends to lead you towards playing which minimizes the need for metagame and re-tooling character during the partly because it lacks tools to do these things - LARPs are distributed by nature and there's no single focal point where this kind of re-tooling could be done and communicated to other participants (which also tends to lead towards static settings); the players are mostly on their own, and without tools for metagame communication, they need a clear and front-loaded mental image of the character, other characters and setting in general.

    Another reason for front-loading is that immersive player needs it in order to, well, immerse. If the goal is to emulate a fictional person, you need a mental image of the person before you start playing. You need to have a hunch of how he thinks, how he sees other people and how he sees the world in general, in order to incorporate these things and be able to act upon them. It's not easy to suddenly incorporate, for example, the idea of character being a gay, since that affects a lot of things and feels weird if you've played the guy as straight so far. Breaks the continuity, so to speak.

    Tailoring the character with regards to the game is a tool, either for the GM (for prepared characters) or the players. It certainly can be used to achieve to avoid certain things; if you want to produce a highly emotional event in the play, it makes sense to prepare characters who are emotional and a starting situtation which is prone to create emotional events. Or if you want to avoid emotional events, you prepare a character that is somehow "safe" from them and in a starting situtation which does not deal with emotional stuff. The LARPs with prepared characters tend to start with questionnaires around here, which ask on scale of 1 to 5, how emotional/prone to intrigue/etc the character should be.

    I'm not sure about the challenge aspect; I'm prone to think that directly challenging the player is unproductive since the goal of immersive playing is remove the sense of playing a character from the player. On the other hand, a character with challenges tends to lead more intense experience.

    Posted By: Joshua BishopRobyMendel, I suspect this is in fact the mode of operation ofmostlong-time gamers.
    I'd agree with that.

    Typically I ask two questions right off the bat when creating a character: the first is "What do I want to do a lot of in this game?" and the second is "What do I really not want to do, ever?" Knowing those two things (and knowing what I personally think is fun to do in a game) guides me when I sit down and think about what kind of person my character is, what elements I want in his personality, his goals, his background, and so on. In crunchier rule systems, it also steers my choice of skills and powers: I may choose to be bad at something I want to do a lot of in order to make a big deal out of having to cope with being bad at it, or I might choose to be nigh-omnipotent at a skill when I never want to see any complications or challenges arising from it. All in all, this has made gaming more fun for me.

    Before I learned to ask those two questions, though, I made a lot of characters that were interesting but totally horrible to play. They weren't front-loaded to deliver the kinds of things that I wanted out of the game, and it wasn't possible to retcon them into something more suitable...they were just a drag. Someone else could've probably picked them up and had a grand time with them, but they weren't a good fit for me.


    ...and I'm not sure about "the challenge itself makes it fun," necessarily. I mean, I do think it's fun to optimize a character -- it's one of the things I really enjoy in d20 games and superhero systems -- but the goal, at least for me, is for it to be not a challenge at all to design a character that way. Learning to be good at designing characters that make the game fun for me is strictly the means to an end, not an end in and of itself; if I were making awesomely entertaining characters by accident and never needed to think about what I was doing when designing them, I'd be just as happy.

    By way of an analogy, in Vernor Vinge's The Peace War, one of the past-times of that setting is a game where you determine the starting trajectory of a ship and send it off in a three body problem, aiming for a target. You predict the interactions, and try to hit the target under different configurations of planets. It is clearly a competative game, a cross between darts and chess.

    The sort of front-loaded immersion I'm talking about is that same idea. You choose your character's initial trajectory, and attempt to reach your target by predicting how it will interact with everything else in play, game and meta-game. This is a definite challenge, especially for self-competition. And can be fun in its own right.

    The targets could be simple, such as the Dogs in the Vineyards character that will never escalate to fighting - designed specifically to remove the chance of intra-Dog combat. More complex targets, like achieving a long term goal, can make this very interesting. Sometimes how we overshoot our goals is even more interesting, such as the time I inadvertantly won a Vampire: the Masquerade larp. Especially as it showed how a character can exploit both player and character behaviors.

    Certainly the basic skills are common. But the learning and advancement of those skills - through immersive play - is a source of fun in itself. Prioritizing that type of fun in play is not nearly as common. Front-loaded immersion need not be simply a tool for play, but a reason to play on its own.

    - Mendel S.

    So it's a question of what constitutes "a single tool" not whether (A) Game-X has them or (B) whether they would be value-added for someone.

    On a more thread-related topic:
    From my experience here is what helps make characters 'fit' (which is really what I think we're talking about here--but you're free to disagree).

    1. Some idea of the context of the situation. I can make a character who is struggling with his family coming apart because of alcoholism, even have a kicker that uses that--but if the game focus and the focus of the other PCs is elsewhere it may not be a lot of fun. Having the issue is less valuable without meaningful 'traction.' In the game.

    2. Some connection with the other PCs (and Players on the Player level). The more relevant one character is to another (even if they are not "there" in the game) the higher I find the energy of play (IME, YMMV, I've and reasonable games where the PCs never met up).

    3. A situation that reflects *the character.* It's fine to have a character who fits with the game (the standard D&D adventuring party). Having this go another cycle and tailor the situation to the character is, IMO, the way to act on whatever 'fitness' the character has (in this case it would be not just making the adventuring party but having the dungeon specifically apply to the characters in some way).

    I think that games like AD&D-dungeoneering and DitV do two or three of these innately by having a generally constrained situation. I think most prep-work I've seen for non-module GURPS play does the first two easily (but not always the third*).

    I believe the reason these elements work is very simple: if you know what the other players are there for and there is some some attempt to align that then in many cases you will get not onlyhigher-energy play but you'll get it without requiring as much meta-game activity during play. Getting back to your suggestion, If the "tool," whatever it is, (such as the questionnaire), is focused on the single character I think it has a scoping problem with regards to what I see as the biggest factor in getting front-loaded immersionist play: it's better (IMO) to address the situation as well as the rest of the team.

    -Marco
