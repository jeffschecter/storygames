\n OK, before continuing clear your mind of all other games that have come before.



You are a blank page.




A calm blue ocean.




Now, I ask a question: I'm writing a new game
where the player characters take the role of fantasy characters
who (despite, or because of their differences)
work together to overcome adversity.

I want the game to create a sense of camaraderie - the warm fuzzy feeling of working as a team to overcome something that you couldn't overcome on your own. Also, the sense of accomplishment is important - the "I RULE!" factor is also important.

All players should be able to experience this, although it's OK if a player has to "sit out" from comeraderie in a given session - just not EVERY session.

How should my game be structured?

    \n I *promise* I'm not thinking of any other game.

    You should have a strong authority of adversity, someone who has the job to put the screws to the characters.

    You should have a character system such that it is impossible to be good at everything, and easiest to specialize in a pyramidal fashion (1 great skill, 2 good skills, 3 poor skills, or something like that.)

    You should have a resolution system that rewards group action, and makes it easy to deal with and fun.

    You should have a rewards system that rewards helping others as much as it does going it alone.

    \n sometimes for one thing to be clearly visible, the line between it and other must be clear.

    A system where the group has stats/attributes.

    Where resolution involves "personal attribute + group attribute", at least some of time.

    Where selfish actions that harm group damage group attribute (perhaps temporary).

    Where repeated selflish actions by same character deny character access to group attribute for some time.

    Where selfless actions that benefit group increase group attribute (again perhaps temporary).

    Where selfless actions also empower player to give direction to group's development (e.g., its the player whose character died for the team that chooses the group attribute's next "level up" choices or whatever).

    Where characters have personal goals but also group goals. Where the adversity level presumed for group goals is such that they can only be richly/very well satisfied when group attribute has been amped by selfless actions.

    Message is that a bunch of selfish types fail, a group succeeds, though perhaps some fun in stress between some individual 'selfish' goals to balance.

    \nI'm thinking that to do this you don't have to have all your fixes/attention be upon rules. You also have to focus on the setting and background as well.

    Frex, I totally agree with Clinton's posts above. However, if the setting is something where the players aren't encouraged to expressly work together, you could have one of those Sorcerer-style "All the PCs are stabbing each other in the FACE!!! ...in a fantasy game where they're all specialists at something or other".

    Frex: In the console game Final Fantasy X, the background of the world was such that there were Holy Summoners, who wandered the world doing good and fighting evil monsters and corruption. Each Holy Summoner has a number of "Guardians" or companions that form a tight knit group. Thus, you have that camaraderie right from the start, with no one stealing rubies from the other, and everyone doing the Victory Dance when you defeat a sabotenda-/cactaur.

    Sure, the leads in the game joined together to Save The World and all that, but the setting of the world already had this mechanism for heroes to join together, wander, and do good.

    I can't think of anything quite applicable to the RPG World, save perhaps Dread: The First Book of Pandemonium. Right from the start, the game sets up the world of Cabals and Disciples: Every PC is a disciple, and 3-6 disciples form a cabal; and these cabals throw themselves at demons. And when they defeat the demons, the survivors do the Victory Dance. In play, there is an ABSOLUTE feel of camaraderie and "we did it", with some "I RULE" (in the shit you do to defeat the demon), and that comes 70% from the game's setup and background, and maybe 30% from the fact that the rules set you up to be a member of a team, etc.

    So maybe have the hippie rule or two, but also focus on some sort of game background that supports camaraderie and teamwork, and not interpersonal conflict and stabbing each other in the face.

    \n Going with your dragon spirit idea...

    You have this group of people fighting for some cause. They are all somehow bits of a some kind of spirit dragon, that can only be brought into the world temporarily through their actions. They all have to work together for this to happen.

    Presumably, there would be climactic points in the game where the only way to win would be to make the dragon spirit appear, manifest itself, or something similar.

    The dragon gains power as the group gains power, and they are tied to each other--the dragon develops certain strengths and abilities as the characters do, and vice-versa. If one of the characters becomes capable of magic, or flight, the dragon gains that ability too.

    So, the spirit creature has all the abilities of the characters. Outside the group, they are incomplete, though perhaps powerful, and can't handle all the game's challenges on their own. But, pooling their strengths together, they can bring on this dragon spirit, and, growing in strength together, overcome adversity.

    (Also, isn't this sort of like what happens in "Power Rangers"? The individuals form together for some sort of giant battle robot? I really don't know anything about that show, as it really isn't my area of interest... except it sounded kind un-PC--the Black ranger is African American, the Yellow ranger is Asian, the Pink ranger is a girl, etc...)

    \n What I have done in my group for camaraderie (in any system or setting) is this:

    Play a session or two.

    Then stop and say "Okay, tell me what you want each other player character to think of your character."

    Wait five minutes.

    Then yell as loud as you can, "I am NOT asking how they met or what their background is, that's fictional, I want to REALLY KNOW how YOU THE PLAYER in REAL LIFE want that OTHER REAL PERSON'S character to deal with yours."

    \nYou nailed it, Andy. Part of the inspiration for Dread was a disastrous D&D 3E campaign, followed by a disastrous Rifts campaign, that I ran for some co-workers in 2000. Unlike my previous gaming groups, these guys completely lacked cohesion. There was no teamwork, no planning, no camaraderie, no banding together. They argued and fought and kept splitting up and making decisions that impeded other members of the group. Some of them claimed that the reason was that their characters had no reason to be friends -- so why act friendly?

    I knew that they were off-base -- I've been playing D&D for a long time, and this was the only group I'd ever seen act that way -- but I started thinking. Wouldn't it be cool if part of the mythology of your game was that you were part of a secret group? Your characters, like cops or soldiers, had this sort of grim, fatalistic brotherhood about them. Dread sort of emerged from that idea.
