Jargon: To NPC or not to NPC?

rossum
July 2006 edited July 2006 in Story Games
Hello, out there in indie land.

I'm reworking my manuscript of 1984 Prime (huzzah!) and got distracted by words when I realized that as a conscious style choice, I had avoided using gamer jargon â€” until I found myself thinking something like "any character portrayed by the person running the game." That's no good, no matter how you look at it, so I just dropped in "NPC" to keep me writing and on track. In the back of my head, I started thinking.

I recall Over the Edge using the term "GMC" for Game Moderator Character. I know that some games use evocative terminology as jargon: Theatrix uses "Supporting Cast," for instance, and Clinton uses "SGC" (Story Guide Characters) in TSoY. Some games, such as Dogs, simply use the term "NPC" without explanation, assuming that readers know the term and what it means. (Yes, I know I'm assuming a style of play where you actually have a GM. Cry me a river and use some soap, you hippie.) I know there's a whole host of games that make up their own lexicon, so feel free to bring those into the discussion. Treasure Tables did an article on terms for the GM a few months ago, that brings up some good questions.

Has the term "NPC" (or PC, or GM) fallen out of fashion? What other games have colorful names for player roles? What terms do you use in your games? Has someone else brought this up already? Have you ever regretted your choice of jargon (I'm looking at you, R. Sean "Hollyhock God" Borgstrom)? Does it even matter because people will say GM, PC, or NPC by default out of comfort?

    In Polaris, I used "non-player character" for a long time, because I don't like neologisms (I find them confusing.) Eventually, my patient playtesters convinced me that it was better to use a different term, because the characters were so different that they found "NPC" even more confusing. So I used "secondary characters."

    These days, I'm just classifying characters as they are in the game, without worrying about history. For instance, Bliss Stage classifies characters into four types: Authority, Pilot, Anchor, Support. Drifter's Escape classifies characters into: Drifters, those beholden to the Man, those beholden to the Devil, and Decent Folks.

    A philosophy which if put into practice is likely to lead one into risky situations.

    Which put that way sounds rather attractive come to think of it.

    Anyway, what I meant was that I think it helps encourage the GMPC thing, where the GM takes an npc and uses them as a PC surrogate, as their character in the game in the same way the pc is a player's surrogate in the game. Please note here I'm using all these terms in their trad sense for ease of convenience. That often ends in Mary Sues and all that deprotagonising stuff and with the player characters not being central to the game.

    So yes, the GM does play them, but generally in most games in a way different to the way the players play their characters.

    That said, I do agree that the notion that the GM is not a player is unfortunate, it's just the way the language has evolved. It is misleading though.

    It's why I liked supporting cast, that to me has an unequivocally good message: The players' (there's that word again...) characters are the main cast, the protagonists, the guys run by the GM are supporting cast and only exist ultimately in relation to the players' actions (even if the supporting cast are designed and played as if they had a life external to the pcs, which can be very important for creating a believable world).
