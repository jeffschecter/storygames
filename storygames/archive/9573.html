\n Why did I not think to post this to Story Games earlier?

I'm gearing up to publish Ribbon Drive, my story game of road trips, mix tapes, and letting go.
It draws its inspiration from movies like Wristcutters, Everything is Illuminated, Little Miss Sunshine, Thelma & Louise, and y tu Mama Tambien.
The tone and shape of a game are hugely impacted by the mix CDs you play during the game.
A really fun part of the game is the creation and play of mix CDs, from all the members of the play group.
As such, I'm publishing Ribbon Drive complete with a compilation/mix CD to inspire people, expose them to new music, and celebrate the art form (of mixes, that is).

I'm currently in the process of contacting artists and getting permission to include their songs.

Are you an artist?
Do you have a song (or a selection of songs) that would fit this particular mix's theme?
It's particular theme is reckoning, end times, and forsaking. The CD's title is "The Decimation of Small Things".
Mostly so far it's folksy indie rock and folk-punk.
Bands already signed onto the project: The Darkest of the Hillside Thickets, xox, Tuck, Ghost Mice, Chris Clavin, Maria in the Shower, Hard Feelings and June Madrona.

More info about the game is here. If you are a musician and want to be represented on this CD, contact me!

If you aren't a musician, then maybe you're eagerly awaiting the release of Ribbon Drive?
If so, you should just spam this thread with your enthusiasm, and I'll promptly get excited abuot the fact that pre-orders will be up soon.

    \n Graham,

    Yes!
    Except, the game includes a randomizer. At the start of play, you shuffle the mix CDs and draw one at random. That mix goes on first...

    So, while you might make up an awesome country mix (awesome + country = I am confused by my own sentence), we might draw Rafu's norwegian death metal mix titled "Apocalypse: Sweden", and have the game start in a very different tone and locale than you were expecting. The interplay between these two CDs, as the game progresses and we switch mixes, is one of the most interesting (and sometimes creatively challenging!) parts of the game.\n
    I am deeply excited about this game. Joe, I think you said it was...I can't remember...very positive...or non-conflicty...or something....anyway, it sounds good.
    \nYeah.
    The game design goal I originally had was to design a game free of conflict.
    It was partially in response to all these conflict-focused, throw-down-in-every-scene games that were bubbling up around 06/07/08.
    I wanted something that was about exploration, and being human, and rethinking your stakes.

    Some cool games came up with this idea of "giving". The Shadow of Yesterday and Dogs in the Vineyard both give you a mechanically and fictionally interesting option to back out of any given conflict.

    And I wondered... what if there was no conflict. What if there was just giving? That's the birthplace of Ribbon Drive. That, and watching Wristcutters.

    It's not entirely sans conflict in its final state. But it is completely possible to play a game of Ribbon Drive in which no obstacles are ever overcome, and no goals are ever achieved, and have it be a powerful session. So, yeah.

    It's often positive. Sometimes in a bittersweet, nostalgic, semi-tragic way. Sometimes in a beautiful, life-affirming way.

    \n Yes, Charlie, you can play this game on an actual road trip:\n
      \n
    • There are no dice.
      • \n
    • There are no tokens, counters, etc.
      • \n
    • After character creation, you don't need to do any actual writing (though you do need to check off/cross off several things on your sheet during play)
      • \n
    \nYou need to have a vehicle that has a CD player (and a mix CD for everyone) or a tape player (and a mix tape for everyone), and the character sheets, and that's it.
    Presumably the driver could have someone else check off their Traits and Futures.

    Or the driver could, you know, pay attention to the road instead of playing.
