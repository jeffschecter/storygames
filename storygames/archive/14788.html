    \n In our last AW campaign, we eventually had two of the three players with additional PCs. I don't think there was any conscious design to it, but we generally split up the spotlight by-player, not by-character -- in one case, the first character was already a bit of a hermit, which was part of what motivated the advance in the first place, and made it very easy to switch focus to stuff involving the new character. In the second case, the player's second character was specifically connected to a somewhat isolated social community that the other PCs (and particularly his first PC) were at odds with, so it was very easy again to keep the characters relatively separate. There were probably a few sessions where the player essentially only played one of their PCs, but this was never a conscious 'switch on, switch off' setup.

    I don't think we ever had a scene or situation where both characters were in the same scene/talking to each other/etc. Because of the fictional situations of the PCs this didn't require any particular effort on my part, but I doubt I would ever have purposefully framed both the players' PCs into the same scene unless the fiction (or the players) demanded it -- whereas of course we were all happy to invent reasons for other PCs to run into each other, etc.

    But of course the answer there is, as it always is: funny voices.

    \n I think I'd lean towards spotlight time by player in an ideal world, but "focus on who it seems natural to focus on in the fiction" in practice. I mean, I never go "Oh, hey, we've spent ten minutes on Alice's character, let's switch over to 10 minutes for Bob now" It's more "Okay, that's a good dramatic cutting point, let's switch over to... how about Bob's character, he's been out of the scenes for a while now since his character wandered off" or "Okay, so Bob's character sends that messenger to Eve's character, Eve: what's she up to?" Throwing more characters into the mix would give that player a slight edge on the latter but less of one on the former, I suppose.

    \n When all three of us made new characters, none of us made a new character that had anything to do with the old character. Living in different places, associating with different NPCs, all that. But it worked so that we never had that old "2 PCs in one scene, 1 PC in another" split.

    My 1st character and Andrew's 1st character were total buddies and hung out lots.

    Adam's 1st character started to shun them and went his own way. Then he met Andrew's 2nd character and they had a frenemy thing going on.

    When Adam's 2nd character showed up, he had some hijinks with my 1st and Andrew's 1st characters (the buddies). Then he teamed up with my 2nd character and they worked together on a mission of sorts.

    And voila! No more scenes with only 1 player and the MC, and no scenes with both of one player's characters (I mean, the whole point of a new character for us was to play someone completely different and interact with NPCs who wouldn't give our 1st PCs the time of day). And that's where it was when we stopped playing.
