\n I have to admit I'm a little bit of a slouch when it comes to game prep. I will come up with a rough set of events to involve the players in and then find out what they do. I think the worst/best example of this was a game where the players were looking for someone in a midevilish style city and they deduced that this person was in the palace. They were right of course and most of the game consisted of them trying to break into the palace. This took two sessions so I had plenty of time to prepare but I still winged it. They finally got into the palace and I did not have a map of the interior. What's more I didn't even have an idea of what it would look like. Now this is why I have this bad habit. I proceeded to draw all five overlapping levels of the palace as the players explored it. One of my players talks about it to this day.

So that's my sharing now to the question. I would like to hear what works for you when you're setting up a game. What do you need to feel you're ready? If you wing it like me, have there been times this has let you down?

I'm not looking for if something is right or wrong, just if it works.

    \n My favorite is adopting a set of game rules with a clear, well-defined and unambitious prep procedure and following it verbatim. Dogs and AW come to mind as obvious examples; but these days I'd be willing to consider dungeon-design for Moldvay/Mentzer D&D as "well-defined and unambitious" enough for me, though it took me 20-something years of general RPG experience to make me able to - maybe - think of it as such, so that definitely does not count as "clear".
    In principle, I'm going to allow myself to commit 30 to 60 minutes a week to this kind of prep-work, though in actuality whatever I set out to do usually takes me twice or thrice the time I planned for it to.

    \n Sometimes I feel like I've lost track of critical information -- "What was the name of that research station again?" -- but most of the time, we just handwave it away. One of the players will be like, I think we killed this dude a few months ago, and another player will say, yeah... we *thought* we did. Clearly, he had a Resurrection Stone with him. And off we go.

    I do a lot of questioning of my players. "Wait, what's this guy's deal? Is he a hooker with a heart of gold, or just a jaded hustler looking for another john to con?" And they tell me what we're up to. They're the type of players who actively seek out conflict and disdain advantage, so they're always coming up with great deathtraps/arguments/dilemmas for me. They do a lot of my work, actually.

    At con games, my approach doesn't always work, because sometimes I game with people who expect the GM to have all this stuff written and prepped beforehand. Which I get. But I just don't like the way it feels. To me, all games are sandbox, and playing any other way feels very strange. It always bewilders me when GMs say, I didn't think you were going to do that, or I figured you guys would get the message when that NPC did this or that -- to my way of thinking, it's more fun to push complicated traps (social, personal, physical, intellectual) in front of the players and then see what they do. Like I said, at con games (or games with new people), the results are mixed: some dig it, and some get annoyed when they ask what's behind the door and I reply, "It's a gruesome crime scene. What's written in blood on the wall?" They want me to tell them, not the other way around. I dig it.

    \n Well, if I'm going to be GMing a game, it's only because I'm actually, genuinely interested in GMing a game, so I'm probably daydreaming about it during free moments (commuting, waiting in line, etc.), and that makes it hard to judge how much prep I'm actually doing. A lot, but mostly easy and fun stuff: thinking about the NPCs, getting their voices nailed down in my head, thinking about what they want and what they'd do to get it, thinking about how those wants are going to bring them into conflict with the player characters, thinking about locations where stuff could happen and how to make those locations more interesting, and so on. By the time the weekend rolls around, I've logged a lot of time putting together raw material for the game. I've got a little notebook that I write good ideas down in, and I go back to look them over and see how I can improve them, and that notebook's pretty much the spine of whatever game I'm running.

    Then I sit down to do the "boring" stuff. I think about what I'm not good at making up in the moment, like names and maps (ugh, but some of our group loves 'em), and I grind 'em out and put them in my notes so that when I need a name, I can just pick one off a list. I think about stuff that players will find useful, like cheat sheets, and prep and print those. I put together stats for the NPCs and stick 'em in my notes. If it's a game with a more involved combat system (ie, D&D), I make sure that all the relevant rules for various opponents' combat powers are on the same page as their combat stats, so I'm not flipping back and forth in a book.

    If I do the "boring" stuff right, then most of the time I'm done within an hour, or two at the very most. And to be fair, it's not exactly boring -- it's just something I can't do in idle moments during the day. I have to actually block out some time, sit down, and do the work. That hour or so spent ahead of time makes the game run more smoothly, which subjectively feels like it's saving me a thousand years of agony in every game session. It's a fair trade.

    \nAs an aside, I think the thing that really bothers them there is not that you didn't prepare something, but that you're asking them to invent the clues that they will (presumably) then have to use to solve the crime (which you are also kind of asking them to invent). At least, that's what would bother me in that situation.

    If they signed up for an investigative game or you've been setting up an investigative story, it's a total bait-and-switch to turn around and make it a game where they invent a mystery rather than investigate one. In an investigative game, you're supposed to be the one telling them what's written on the wall, and it's entirely fair for them to expect that you will.

    ...of course, my understanding of how con games usually work is that most of the people who sign up for games don't really pay attention to what the game will be about, so all the advance warnings in the world about how you're going to make them invent the clues for the mystery probably won't help.

    \n I used to wing it all, and still do that with some story games and one-on-ones. When running story games I just try to learn the rules in advance. With my freeform con games I tend to prep a lot. I create PCs and NPCs and places and past events and secrets and try to imagine what the players would do next and what will follow. Of course they're going to do something unexpected at some point, so my improvisation skills still come handy. I guess it's like what you do before writing a book or a script: you've got the plot and the characters down, and you know that this cool scene is going to be in there somewhere, but you don't know exactly what's going to happen until you write/GM it.

    What's curious is that even if I plan my games in detail (check out Wikipedia for locations and dates, etc), I don't write it down. Even if I scribble down some names or notes, I end up not looking anything up. Loving names helps in this: I have understood that GMs in general have a problem inventing or remembering names. Personally I have hard time remembering exactly how rules work, especially rules I'm not that interested in.

    When I've tried to run campaigns I always plan several sessions ahead so that everything is foreshadowed well in advance.

    I'd say that if you don't want to prep anything else, decide a tone and try to stick with it, and try to get inspired before the game, by music or fiction or whatever. I don't see how that could go wrong.

    \n Onix,
    So, for me it is a process:
    1) Talk about the next game leading up to it.
    2) Once I think we will be playing this game, I come up with a basic scenario (like for Exalted, what faction is going to be the antagonists and what editions/books we will be using).
    3) I focus on what factions are involved and what those factions want.
    4) I focus on creating or finding a location that suits these factions.
    5) When making chars, I make sure the players know what the opposition will be (the NPCs have not been made yet at this point).
    6) Once the PCs are made, I figure out what NPCs would be the most interesting. for the PCs to face.
    7) Crank out NPCs (I lik to make sure I have a couple of generic ones done first, so I can set the power level, then I make one or 2 key antagonists).
    8) Look up things I am unsure about.
    9) Go!
    Hope this helps.
    Dave M

    \n My wife and I play mini-adventures with my diceless, two-player RPG while on walks or doing household chores.

    I prepare by:

    1. Considering past adventures and which established, significant NPCs and items might be fun to see again.

    2 and 3. Create a loose plot to include those. This naturally leads to deciding upon a setting.

    4. Look at my acrostic of old school adventure elements. They help me flush out the upcoming story.

    5. Make NPC names, sketch a map if appropriate, etc.

    Here is my current example.

    1. I wanted to have the next chapter in the story include a powerful but perhaps benevolent Ogre alchemist that my wife's character has briefly met before. I also want the adventure to be in the large town in which the PC just arrived.

    2 and 3. Her character is a novice alchemist. OOC she said her character would be willing to accept a job from the Ogre with a new alchemy recipe as payment. I have a Paizo Flip-Mat of a waterfront inn I have been wanting to use. Let's have the job be stopping another Ogre from framing and killing someone in the tavern. (Ogres can look like anyone they touch. A bit of mystery, a bit of combat.)

    4. My acrostic helps me brainstorm more events in an otherwise tiny adventure. A crazy Unseemly nature-worshiper attacks the inn with his pet giant crab. A hunter who has recently returned from a magical Wild Hunt shows his drinking buddies a creepy statue he found. Two drunks in the tavern harbor an old feud, which one escalates by using a recently acquired enchanted mug that creates a Beer Golem to attack his foe. The enemy Ogre is not acting alone: he has a partner hidden in the tavern's crowd, and the local noble who hired the Ogre to frame and kill a rival noble is also disguised and watching.

    5. Make up names for all those NPCs, sketch a map since it's hard to take the Flip-Mat on a walk if this ends up another on-a-walk adventure, etc.

    What happens?

    This routine results in mini-adventures that are non-linear but easy to GM because they are fairly predictable. That's what my wife likes, and it works well when we can't devote 100% of our attention to the game. Although her PC's choices have little affect on what events happen during the adventure, they hugely affect how events are resolved and therefore what material I will have to work with when creating the next chapter of the story.

    Will the PC foil the assassination? What will her relationship with the innkeeper and tavern staff be like at the end of the adventure? Will the noble who hired the assassin learn enough about the PC to become a long-term enemy? Will the hunter become a recurring NPC? Will either drunk (or his family) gain gratitude or hatred of the PC? Will the PC feel included to stay in this town or move on? Etc.

    Also, I improvise all the personalities of new NPCs. So even a "scripted" event might happen in many ways based upon what mood my wife seems to be in and what she is enjoying having her PC do.
