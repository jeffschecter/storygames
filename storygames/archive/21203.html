For my change, I said I wanted to have access to a subscene that assumed GMs will hack extensively, and set up their licences and materials to make that easy, but that also valued design in the way the post-Forge scene does.

    Eero \u2014 "Regarding the topic, Rob: What do you envision this subscene's deal to be regarding rule zero? I ask, because "valuing design" and "GMs hack extensively" could be read as contradictory, if the latter means "GM changes whatever rules they feel like for whatever reason"."
    \nIdeally? It would be very dubious about Rule Zero, in a strong contrast to the OSR. It would expect GMs to do a lot of rules changes between sessions, and assume that was inevitable, but to follow rules within sessions. That's kind of what I mean by "values design" - it sees the power of rules to coordinate people to mutual benefit.\n
    Jeph\u2014 Isn't that right here? And the whole *World-blogo-tubu-sphere as well? And also some elements of the OSR, I think.
    \n
    Problems with the OSR \u2014 often reject the idea that "good game design can lead to better games, and in particular to different games" - they favour Rule Zero and "Rulings, not Rules". They also often use the OGL out of necessity, and that's an ugly licence (if nothing else, its complicated and obscure - no-one outside tabletop rpgs knows it at all).

    The PBtA scene is something I'd overlooked - that's better, in particular that around DW where the text of the main game is CC-BY.\n
    Sandra \u2014 "But, what I\u2019m after and what I think Rob is after is a view of games as if they were software libraries / API:s rather than finished apps."
    \nYes, that's a good metaphor.\n

    Eero \u2014 "Is the legal license thing really that big a deal in this regard?"
    \nMaybe:

    1. As Sandra says, we need precise text, not just ideas. Sometimes the ideal change is just one line in a table. (Her point about github is good too \u2014 I'd like to be able to fork the repo, change a few key lines, and re-render the book for my table).
    2. Trademarks \u2014 I can see why people want them, but its not ideal if I have to tip-toe around creators in order to publish a delta of their work.
    3. Statement of social expectation is perhaps as important to me as the licence per se \u2014 I want an expectation that people will be ok with others reusing their work.\n
    Adam \u2014 At one point I thought that this is what OSR might have turned into
    \nYeah, the OSR is centered around a quite narrow set of assumptions, and expects each GM to adlib a lot of what rules can do. I like a lot of what it produces, but it's not that well aligned with the game-style I want to be playing in.


    Overall, I think I have something quite concrete in mind about the format of materials, the mode of design, and the community attitudes around reuse. I suspect that to communicate this I have to make something that exemplifies it.

    \n

    Two things:

    \n
      \n
    • Each campaign is it\u2019s own world; a wiki is centralized. Perhaps the correct rule for your game is wrong in mine; or, it\u2019s right in both but it\u2019s not at an opportune time to change it in yours. Or any of a hundred different reasons why the decentralized nature is good.
      • \n
    • The centralized nature of wikis is an ill fit for a community. We don\u2019t want a single, central needle\u2019s eye like G+ or The Forge, we want something decentralized like blogs.
      • \n
    \n

    \n
      \n
    • First of all, perfect is the enemy of good and a little is better than nothing:\n
        \n
      • No git? Just put the files up somewhere and we can use diff and other tools (including git\u2019s own tools)
        • \n
      • No markdown? Just put it in something that pandoc can convert. Or whatever, we\u2019ll find a way.
        • \n
      • \n
    • Making things so that nerds can use them is the first step in making something everyone can use. Geeky programs are the bootstraps we need to pull something together, analogous to how programs like kernels, libraries and compilers are what\u2019s under the hood for more slick user-directed apps.       • \n
    • Most alternatives to nerd tools are proprietary (Google Docs comes to mind) and/or require expensive, modern computers
      • \n
    • The problem with inventing new protocols is the protocol itself. Cf all the struggling alternatives to Twitter and Facebook, I spent some time in the identi.ca swamps trying to get it to work.       • \n
    • Fifth: we who want this, want it. Those who aren\u2019t stoked about the same paradigm are welcome to wait before jumping onboard, (if they ever even choose to do so).
      • \n
    • Also, one of the points of a markup language is that you can render it out into books, webpages etc, for reading. Getting most of the benefits of the traditional model.

      \n I totally see why somebody might not want to bother with git for their own work, because the learning cliff curve can be steep and the value added when mostly working alone often negligible.

      Here are my personal idea of how to support/be a part of this design scene without necessarily using git:

      1. Explicit permissive license.

      If you write something you want to make easy for other people to use, slap a permissive license on it and make that explicit when publishing it wherever you do.

      2. Easily importable/resusable publishing format.

      As (1), but additionally to the pdf/website/130 foot mural painted on the side of a bulding somewhere as a publishing venue, you also have a file to download linked that allows to get at your work easily and makes importing it into other work fast and as hassle-free as possible. For text, this probably means a plain text file instead of/in addition to a pdf or website. (For images some losslessly compressed high-DPI format, etc.)

      3. Added Markup.

      As (2), but additionally the text has markup added to detail layout, seperate text passages from headlines, and so on. This could be Markup (Which is really easy to use.) but it could also be some other kinds, as long as it's somewhat widely used and/or easily converted to whatever markup a potential recipient might need. (The formats pandoc understands are probably fine.)

      (Roughly ordered by work necessary and value added.)

      Work published under a permissive licence and in an easily reusable format would IMO be a great step to do, without ever needing to touch anything like git or Markdown.

      And if somebody later-on decides to try their hand at those things, these steps would make it that much easier for themselves.
