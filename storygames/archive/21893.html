The older I get, the more I think everyone should build his own fantasy heartbreaker.

ValyrianSteelKatana
December 2018 in Story Games
D&D is, and always will be, the 800 lb. gorilla in the room. (Man predicts future on Internet, is possibly correct.) It was the forerunner of the hobby, it has been revived and disseminated into pop culture, and while its popularity will dwindle, it will live on. The OSR scene alone demonstrates this.

Paradoxically, D&D out-of-the-box is fundamentally broken. It doesn't do what GMs need it to do because every GM's needs are different, and the rules are written in such a way that they presume to address every GM's needs. There's a reason that D&D has 3d6 down the line, 4d6 drop the lowest, point buy, and standard arrays in its character generation methods.

The rest of the system fails to include a wealth of options upfront, and most of them are terrible (like the flanking-to-grant-advantage rules). Instead, the majority of game revolves under prescriptivist mechanics that are often too heavy to function in a rules light game and yet too light to function in a rules heavy game. The combat engine, for instance, includes Armor Class, weapon proficiencies, variable damage, ability scores, hit points and Hit Dice, weapon ranges, feats...but at its core, it is merely to-hit vs. Armor Class. You roll the die, apply damage, and that's that. The system lacks depth while simultaneously being cumbersome enough to slow the game to a crawl. (The less said about the skill system, the better!)

This brokenness demands fixing. So the GM naturally modifies the system, tweaking it to fit the game he has imagined it to be. Subtle tweaks here and there become larger rewrites and rules collections. A half-page of house rules grows to a page, then two, then a binder full of them. Pages upon pages of rules changes and rewrites until the game that is played no longer resembles the game that was once published.

Quite frankly, I love it. I love it to bits. And I think everyone should build his own fantasy heartbreaker because D&D is the game most people start with, and reimagining it is part of the beauty of the hobby. Ten years ago, I desired nothing more than the "perfect" fantasy heartbreaker. Five years ago, I was tired of them, and I dismissed them. Today, I want them. Just to read and examine and explore.

Build the game you want to play.

    I think you're asking about the Ship of Theseus, or in this case, the Derivative of D&D. How many mechanics can I substitute before it's no longer D&D? If I substitute 3d6 for the d20 roll, is it still D&D? What if I remove hit points and substitute in Mouseguard's conditions? What if instead of getting XP for treasure, you start getting XP for solving spooky mysteries, and instead of save-vs.-death you have save-vs.-appetite (failure indicates you have to look for a refrigerator while the rest of the party looks for clues)? What if I remove XP entirely and everything is handled dicelessly but you still have to loot dungeons to "win" the game?

    It's an academic question. There exists a platonic ideal of D&D in my brain, sprung from the divine thought of Gygax, and I'm going to play in such a way to realize that ideal regardless of the entire world playing a different way.       Rangers of Shadow Deep hits a ton of those points.

      Very few miniatures games are co-operative play vs. programmed/analog AI at all. I know of a couple, and at least one where it's a bit like a GM, with other players acting as helpers playing NPCish opposition from time to time. RoSD really takes the co-op part and runs with it.


      It seems very flexible for group size. I'm not much into solo gaming, but it can be used for that. It also seems very flexible for from 1-4 players, as each player has one man character, but a "stable": of secondary characters.       Not so much kid related, but adult minis gamer related: It seems to be easily on your budget, even while mostly appealing to people who are already minis gamers. Each player's "warband" is unlikely to ever be more than 10 minis, and probably half that to start with ( and maybe only 1 main guy plus one or two pals, if you're regularly playing multiplayer cooperative). So costs are low there. OTOH, since it is possible to play solo, or just two people, there is a bit of incentive to buy/build baddies in addition to your personal dudes ( I've talked at length about this issue).

      That most scenarios are played on smaller boards (3' x3' is apparently common) it's way more likely to fit on a medium sized dining table/kitchen table, rather than needing some massive layout, nor tons of terrain.


      This all makes it easier, but it also allows participants to spend a bit more time/money/effort on the individual bits and bobs used in play ( in theory, if not in practice), meaning you may well see higher quality toys than if you're constantly trying to make big. multi- hundred dollar armies to play.

      While the game comes with missions/campaigns ( harder as your guys level up), it looks very much like it's viable to do a bit of self-scenario design (there's some very basic GMing goodness right there). Throw in some flavor text to get started and some flavor text for random events, and you're good to go.


      It isn't as murder-hobo-y as early D&D ( as played by me and my pals as kids). While you're going to be stabbing a bunch of monsters, and occasionally getting a bit of treasure, mostly the game exists in the context of a heroic struggle. From after action write ups, the first couple of scenarios have a rescue mission/clue gathering aspect, and the baddies are zombies, giant spiders and giant rats, so all critters a whole lot more justifiably killable than some poor gobbo in his grotto.

      I guess that covers most of it?

      Mechanically it's pretty simple, too, about on par with B/X but using only a single d20.

      It is missing a GM, though, which means you're very limited in an ability to try to chat u the GM and sidestep some challenges by clever thinking.

      There seems to be some ability to choose what order to take on the missions, based on your level. Not hexcrawl style, however, and you are assumed to be at least nominally Good Guys.

      Otherwise though, some solid, basic, minis using, challenge based, cooperative, campaign fantasy.
