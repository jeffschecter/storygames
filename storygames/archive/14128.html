\n There is a history of the Danish freeform scene posted at the Fastaval site. Kristoffer Apollo, who wrote the article, is a Danish larpwright. Since it's coming up, but so many of us can't go to Fastaval, seems like a good time to talk about their style of play.

The Best One Shots in the World: The Danish Scenario Tradition

Kristoffer wrote:\n
A noticeable consequence of this theorizing was that Danish con roleplaying quickly ditched systems in the traditional sense. In the mid-90s a wave seemed to sweep over the land and turn everything into \u201csystemless\u201d scenarios. Powered by the argument that if we knew how to structure the scenarios dramatically, what do we need dice and skill checks for?
\nThis is the big schism. Though just ditching dice and skill checks doesn't mean you've ditched rules and structures. Drama and the structure of stories can be as essential a system as any set of skill checks and saving throws. But this has lead them down a very different path.

Later:\n
[The Natural Born-Holmers] ideas merged with the indie influences to create a new awareness about the importance of system mechanics. And this resulted in both indie-style story games like historical drama Montsegur 1244 (Frederik J. Jensen, 2008) and scenarios drawing more firmly on the Fastaval tradition and merging it with system mechanics, like Medieval drama Joust (Anders Frost Bertelsen, Kristoffer Rudkj\xe6r, Morten Hougaard, 2009), another Absurth presentation.
\nSo it takes another turn, to come around to using more formal mechanics once again. I wish I had gotten to play Joust. I've read a translation, but I'd love to know more about how this uses influences of both traditions.

Montsegur's pedigree seems clear: a concrete, conflict-riddent premise, with pre-written characters, some elements of pre-scripted events, but all presented in a flexible format, utilizing randomly drawn elements as a springboard for player creativity.

    \n Yes, got to keep those Swedes and Danes straight.

    Jim, this article helped put me straight on some of those questions. The Danes came to their form of dramatic oriented play through table top primarily. In the other Nordic countries, larp was the form that pushed innovation forward. So at least in Denmark, theater looks to have been not a central influence. But the elements of improv and theater are there (playing off of one another and "yes, and..."; knowing a story or a role and running with it based on description and narrative, rather than breaking the fiction down into discrete quantified bits).

    The Danes (and?) have a term semi-live, which covers situations where you may shift between table top/descriptive play, to live and back again. Like if you sat down to play D&D, and then stood up to play out a particularly hairy bit of negotiation or combat.

    The biggest difference I have found in the lot of these (Danish scenario play, Jeep and various types of larp) from improv and theater (though I've minimal experience with acting) is the changes that come when you are not performing. Performance changes many things physically (in your physical blocking, your delivery) as well as what you're focusing on in play (your emphasis on being interesting/funny at a steady pace so the audience is not bored).

    Johs, are the first Danish 1000 scenarios the ones that have been archived?

    A Day In The Life and Montsegur are both amazing. They are unique works even from a Fastaval standard. The first is from 2005 and the second is from 2008. The time difference of three years is long in Danish scenario development. Every year at Fastaval is an exercise in wild leaps of creativity and innovation in scenario writing. The two scenarios are also from different (sub)traditions within the Danish tradition. As Apollo writes in the article: "A Day ..." is an ensemble scenario and "Montsegur" is a story game influence. The first story game'ish scenario at Fastaval was Per Fischer's Dark Places (http://alexandria.dk/data?scenarie=2859) in 2005 (the same year as A Day...). Dark Places was like a demo of Ron Edwards' Sorcerer in danish and we Danes were blown away!

    Apollo also mentions the "Joust" (2009) as a merge of the ensemble scenario (as A Day) and the story game'ish (as Montsegur). I think this is true and Joust is maybe the best Danish scenario ever. We'll have to get the authors Anders, Morten and Kristo to translate it in to English.

    \n A bunch of comments here, starting with Emily's ...\n
    This is the big schism. Though just ditching dice and skill checks doesn't mean you've ditched rules and structures. Drama and the structure of stories can be as essential a system as any set of skill checks and saving throws. But this has lead them down a very different path.
    \nWe Danes agree with you nowadays, of course. Even if we thought we ditched "system" back then, it's obvious that we just replaced it with a different set of rules and structures. What makes the Danish tradition baffling to outsiders, however, is the fact that these rules of "systemless" have never been written down. Instead, you have to read or play the actual scenarios to understand the unwritten premises of it all (with rule #1 being that unless you're told otherwise, the GM gets to decide). Though today, most Danish writers are pretty good at specifying what kind of structure they want for their scenario. That's one important thing we learned from The Forge and the discussions it fostered.

    Later:\n
    So it takes another turn, to come around to using more formal mechanics once again. I wish I had gotten to play Joust. I've read a translation, but I'd love to know more about how this uses influences of both traditions.
    Montsegur's pedigree seems clear: a concrete, conflict-riddent premise, with pre-written characters, some elements of pre-scripted events, but all presented in a flexible format, utilizing randomly drawn elements as a springboard for player creativity.
    \nYes, our move away from and then back to formal mechanics is quite different from the American RPG history (everybody's apparently going in circles and will probably meet at the outset again :-). You should know that Montsegur is a pretty atypical Fastaval presentation because of its rather strict reliance on formal mechanics. Joust or A Day in the Life are much more typical of our tradition - having a detailed script and then ad libbing from it. Like Jason mentioned, the GM as the "driver" or director is also very important. Things like GM cutting techniques are essential to the Danish tradition.

    Jason also observes:\n
    The performative aspect of A Day in the Life (I played at Viking Con 27) was radically different from anything Jeepform.
    \nAnd that is very true of the Danish tradition as a whole. The Jeepers are brilliant at what they do, and they have been a major influence on Danish scenario style in recent years (in fact, I would say that we Fastavallers probably consider them an assimilated part of the Fastaval tradition by now). But the Danish tradition developed differently, so it is more than Jeepform, like Jeepform is more than their Fastaval scenarios. For example, the Jeepform saying of "Everyday drama is more interesting than epic drama" does not hold true at Fastaval because the Danish tradition partly builds on a strong history of epic stories. At the moment, the Danish trend also seems to be scaling down towards everyday drama, but I expect we will see a counterreaction and a move back towards the epic in a short while.

    But as a whole, like I believe Tomas observes, there are probably more similarities between the different Nordic freeform styles (Danish/Fastaval scenarios, Swedish/Jeepform, N\xf8rwegian Style) than between those styles and many other freeform styles from the rest of the world. After all, the Nordic countries share many cultural similarities and I believe that fact is reflected in our approach to roleplaying.

    But that doesn't mean that everything Nordic is dark and brooding, as komradebob has been led to believe:\n
    I always get excited when someone starts talking about these Danish (and Swedish and general Viking) forms of RPGs.
    But then, inevitably, someone always describes it as something serious or artsy and it just kills it for me.
    \nI understand where that remark comes from, because so often it's the serious, artsy games that are referred to. I guess because they really set the Scandinavian things apart from many other traditions. But we do actually make comedies and action/adventure games as well. For example, two years ago the Best Scenario award at Fastaval went to a D&D version of Reservoir Dogs. But there is a schism in the fact that more serious stories are more likely to win awards (I mean, look at the Oscars, it's not roleplaying-specific). And when you've looked at a few years' worth of player feedback at Fastaval, you also discover that well-made games with stuff like cannibalism, paedophilia and general dysfunctionality actually seem to be very popular with the players. It's something to do with achieving an emotional high, I believe.

    \n Man, I wish Structured Freedom was more active, so we could talk about this stuff all the time.

    Not to jump on the Bob vs. Tomas back-and-forth, but one thing that's important to remember in cross-community discussions of roleplaying is that, most of the time, the people you have contact with across boundaries are a part of their own context (and, often, internal battles) within their home community. So when they speak from certain assumptions or positions, it's often out of that context, which can make cross-boundary communication more complicated. Yeah, I instinctively reject the idea that more serious or real-life games are somehow "better" than games based on genre fiction, but it's true that the genre stuff does tend to be overwhelming (and a lot of it doesn't look fun to me), so I can imagine why people might think that (even if they're still wrong).

    Still, I don't think there's yet a large number of people with enough experience in all sorts of international or diverse roleplaying circles -- though thankfully, that's increasing steadily -- for communication to be super easy yet. I mean, American indie games are still pretty divorced from American larp traditions (Mind's Eye Theatre, boffer larp) and online freeform and a bunch of other "domestic" gaming traditions. So talking with folks from even more distant backgrounds often means taking the time to talk out assumptions, even when they seem crazy or distasteful. More interesting to me is why Tomas thinks "classic" roleplaying is a "clumsy baby" and what his "beautiful and dangerous, grown up" roleplaying is supposed to look like. Maybe I'd hate it; but maybe not.

    One of the cross-cultural misunderstadings may indeed pop up in relation to Bob's question:\n
    I've noticed that this Danish tradition seems to lack the Fear of GMs we see over here. Can anyone comment on this? Does the one-shot nature of these games and short running time play into this?
    \n... because what exactly do you mean by "Fear of GMs"? But guessing, I presume it's got to do with the distribution of power between GM and players, and the fact that in Danish scenarios, we willingly hand over everything to the GM and say "you decide if nothing else is specified"? From talking with Americans and reading American games, I believe you are hitting a major difference spot-on here. It is my impression that a deeply rooted part of mainstream American roleplaying is the understanding of the GM as an opponent to the players (the guy who's controlling the dungeon, the obstacles, the monsters, whatever) - and that understanding is still present in many "indie" games. Whereas one of the unwritten rules of the Danish scenario tradition is that we are in this together, with the goal of everyone having the best roleplaying experience possible. So many scenarios give the GM a lot of power because we believe it will be used to everyone's benefit. In addition, you could say that many modern Danish scenarios moves the GM into a strict directing role, with the GM being responsible for setting up a framework, and the players acting out whatever they want within that framework.

    But writer control vs. GM control vs. player freedom is still a hot subject in Danish scenario-writing circles - it is still being widely discussed. Personally, I think that the decision on how to distribute control vs. freedom should depend on what you intend to do with a game. One size does not fit all.\n
    The other thing I noticed from just a quick skim of some of these is that they seem to be very focussed, but also scenario/setting/character specific ( with a good bit of room for interpretation/ad-libbing). Around these parts, there are a lot of very focussed story-games, but they seem to still follow the American Traditions of having lots of text taken up describing how to create characters, setting, scenario, etc. Anyone care to comment on these cultural differences?
    \nYes, these Danish scenarios are exactly that: Written for the specific purpose of being run in specific time slots by a number of GMs at a specific convention. Hence the very specific setting and characters, as they are meant to be ready to play by any GM and whatever players sign up for the scenario at the convention. And that context is then responsible for the very detailed descriptions of plot, characters etc.

    Compared to the U.S., I believe the big difference is commercial. Denmark is a small country (5.5 million inhabitants), so we have no domestic RPG market to speak of. So where American game designers aim to publish their games, the Danish scenarios are written for events run by gamers, with peer recognition being the driver. When you aim to publish a game, it follows that you need to make something that your customers can use at home, with their friends - hence you need to allow them to create their own characters and all that. The Danish scenarios are written for a specific use, typically at a convention - and because of that, that tradition has allowed the writer to take total control of every part of the game. But still, the goal is ultimately to create great roleplaying experiences for the participants.

    \n The thing is; to enact a positive change in a medium of expression, by exploring the possibilities in the known edges of it, we need to take it serious. Regarding something as art, is taking it serious. The thing we design can still be aimed at fun. And it may not.

    The Danish scenario-tradition has explored more serious themes as part of their positive change of role-playing games as a medium. So has Norwegian and Swedish designers. And some American designers are doing the same.

    Exploring various themes is as large a part of developing role-playing games, as exploring various methods of play. By embracing this fact, we enable ourselves to reach further, and leave behind the preconception that role-playing games are what they are. They are not, and never will be. This medium of expression will change, continually, as do any other medium.

    Posted By: komradebobBack to the Fastaval Danish play tradition though: How did it come about that scenarios bean to be written up and passed around to be presented by different GMs rather than just the creator of the scenario. I saw some mention of that, but I'd like to hear more about how and why that developed, and how it became a fixture of Fastaval gaming.
    \nHeh, I guess it never occurred to us that you could do it in any other way. :-) As far as I know, the centralized sign-up with games being run by multiple GMs has always been a fixture of Danish conventions - at least since the first Fastaval back in 1986. Maybe it comes from these events being run by gamers for gamers - it has always seemed logical to us that if many players wanted to play the same scenario, the convention should try to provide them with the possibility to do so. So you get the centralized sign-up for everything, the convention recruiting additional GMs as needed, and a modus operandi where you get a piece of paper when you sign in at the con, showing you which games you're gonna participate in. I suppose that piece of history also shows that there has been someone with good organizational skills involved from the beginning.
