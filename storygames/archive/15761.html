\n Recently, in the thread Strengthening Creative Muscles in Prep for 5E, I have been trying to properly articulate the reason I am looking for that specific advice. I made some fairly broad characterizations of the game without providing sufficient context. So, that is what I am going to try to do here, as well as exploring how player behavior has developed and been shaped by the evolution of the game.

Here are some excerpt to get the gist of the situation:\n
Posted By: jjafullerPosted By: JDCorleyI have no idea what 4e supposedly did that was so destructive to that ability, so I have no idea how to tell you to get it back. Did you eat a bunch of paint chips or something? I donno.
\nIt wasn't so much destructive, so much as it withered due to lack of use. 4E is a very highly structured game, combats are played on a grid. Dungeon delves are played on a grid. And the power system created a set of player actions that were nearly always better than engaging the core system. Thus, there was a strong walled garden effect, and routines were established early.
\n
Posted By: JDCorleyPosted By: jjafullerIt doesn't really say much of anything about it, which is the issue. The tactical strategy elements of the game reward risk aversion.
\nThis is exactly what I'm talking about. 4e promotes risk aversion to a drastically lesser degree than earlier editions of D&D:

- the existence of healing surges and more broad-based healing abilities means risks are less catastrophic when an idea goes bad
- the lack of save-or-die situations means risk aversion is reduced
- the quicker refresh of encounter abilities means you're encouraged to try things out with magic spells (in earlier editions this was a bad idea due to risking having to wait a whole day to get that spell back)

What I am trying to get at is not the specific elements of the game, such as visuals / grids, risk aversion and what not as they are definitely present throughout other editions of the game. I am interested in how those elements have changed in the development of 4e.

So, while it is true the 4e provides an environment that is much safer for exploring by eliminating save or die situations, and generally being more forgiving of mistakes it incentives it in different ways. My suggestion is that the power system in 4e diminishes much of the exploration nature of the game because it becomes somewhat unnecessary. Powers represent pre-negotiated character game interactions. Powers generally provide exceptions to the underlying rules that are generally better than what would be negotiated with a DM in most situations based on my experience. Additionally, they act a sort of menu for activity within the game that boxes in the players mindset.

In previous editions a player may have been risk averse because they knew interacting with the environment could be lethal. In 4e players may be risk averse because they have an explicit structure to fall back on.

Thus, my suggestion is that while player motivations may remain fundamentally the same throughout the editions, the rules in previous edition encouraged creativity to support that behavior, while as 4e presents a set of boundaries to fall back on.

And that is my ramblely, redundant two cents. I may go back and clean that up later, but for now I just want to get the ball rolling.

    \n When looking at play culture across multiple groups, a lot of factors affect behavior, beyond the rules strictly as written. Factors might include: what's not written, how much text is dedicated to certain ideas, supporting examples, emergent behavior encouraged by the way sub systems interact, how much what's asked works with or against established habits from in or even outside the game, the modules that help teach the DM the rules, D&D Encounters at retail stores, who's teaching the game, and much more.

    Past editions of Call of Cthulhu didn't say, "roll spot/hidden even when what's there is obvious or even if the investigation will hit a dead end if they fail." Yet a lot of people (not everyone) seem to be doing exactly this. The editions I have don't say to do this. But they also don't say not to. But even more importantly, when I flip through my old published Call of Cthulhu modules, they include many things you have to roll Spot/Hidden for that if you fail, the investigation hits a dead end. And I don't know why, but most people I know who run CoC (which is a lot) didn't even read the rules text. I don't know why. Maybe it seemed so simple they didn't think they had to. Maybe because it was 300 pages. But many of them learned how to play from those modules. And many of those modules have these crappy habits baked into them.

    \n I think jjafuller makes a pretty good assessment, although I'm not sure that the behaviour or accompanying design strategy originates in 4e. I'm not a DnD buff by any means, but I think powergaming through optimizing in 3.5 relies on a similar underlying structure - one that gives the appearance of rewarding them more for knowing the rules than for engaging with the GM's world. This is also the case in computer games (except Sleep is Death, which is really a computer story game) since the GM does not exist.
    \n
    \nIt's probs important to remember that the functional effect of rules on a game isn't limited to their specific and explicit statement of rules - it's also the way they are typically interpreted and used by players. So whether or not 4e states things is separate to whether it has a good but to do with a game being played in a particular way.
    \n
    \nThe first time I ever ran a game (quote recently compared to most here, I suspect,) I ran one of the 4th edition pre-mades at the suggestion of another player in my group. I spent a lot of time writing down numbers and moving things on battle grids and describing outcomes as the players crashed through the structure laid out in the book. It drove me nuts that it didn't seem like I was doing anything and eventually I cracked. I improvised a mounted combat escape-from-dungeon-location section using rules I made up on the spot. Apparently it was their favourite part of the whole game. That's how I learned to stop caring, ignore rules that don't suit me and make stuff up. You could say that it's my fault for being a terrible DM. I'm certainly not the best person in the group when it comes to running games. The pre-made and the way it describes things could also be partly to blame. In my next game, we used a different system and it was still more engaging when the players went thousands of miles in the opposite direction of the one I intended them to go than when they did things according to plan in DnD4.
    \n
    \nIt's also worth considering that different players and GMs are affected differently by each system of rules. Especially from their preconceptions, both about games and about the universe. So, in at least some cases, powers and DnD rules in general can go quite wrong. With people who know and like 4th, they aren't a problem. But that's a tautological statement.

    \n I have been a huge 4E proponent, at my table and on these forums for years. I've since reversed my position on the game, though, and I think that the people saying it gets in the way of role-playing are onto something. Just to define terms, by "role-playing," I'm talking about character development outside growing your powers, even though that's all technically part of role-playing.

    I'm not saying that you can't role-play. Of course you can. But you can play Monopoly or RISK and role-play, too. You just don't, for a variety of reasons, including table expectations. But one of the reasons is that you're being rewarded for all this other stuff that has nothing to do with role-playing.

    4E is the same way. You are rewarded for killing stuff and overcoming traps and hazards, and occasionally for beating skill challenges. Maybe your DM gives you XP for role-playing. Great! You spend that XP to level up and get new powers. These powers don't have a lot to do with role-playing as I defined it. So you start play at the new level with all these new powers, and you really want to try them out. So you do. And a lot of your focus and concentration is on the fighty stuff, because that's what the new powers make you better at. And with each level advanced, you get new distractions and new incentives to fight, fight, fight, and few incentives to role-play.

    Add to all of that the disconnection between fiction and powers, as described above, and the game can start to feel more like a wargame than a role-playing game. Or at least, when you're role-playing, it has nothing to do with the system.

    It's like saying you like driving in your car with friends because you enjoy all the talking. But your engine is really loud and the drive is necessarily fast and race-car-like and everyone is hanging onto their seats and can't hear each other. So you have to stop the car and get out to talk to each other. Is the car great for talking? Not really. Does it stop you from talking? Not when you get out of the car, no, but you want to talk while driving. That was the whole point. Not to race it really fast.

    That's my feeling, anyway.

    \n Here's a ridiculously detailed, time consuming, data driven study conducted by a DM / Psychologist to examine the following assumptions about D&D 4E:\n
    - Combat includes too many moving parts and the parts move more dramatically as the players advance in level.
    - Players gain more options in combat as they advance in level.
    - Players gain more powerful options (i.e., status effects) in combat as they advance in level.
    Study: http://theiddm.wordpress.com/2011/11/29/power-options-status-effects-mutual-assured-destruction/

    The results seemed to prove all of the above to be true.     Additionally, later posts and podcasts have indicated that the study actually didn't include ALL the power options available (just the defaults) considering you can easily gain multiple Items with even more powers, skill powers, and in rarer cases abilities from themes. So even though the above has been effectively proven true, it may even be an... understatement!
