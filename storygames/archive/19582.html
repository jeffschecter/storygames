\n Hi everyone,

I have a question regarding how Trap expert works (the thief move)

It says "When you spend a moment to survey a dangerous area, roll+DEX". I interpret this in a few ways, and can't figure out how to actually use this move 'correctly'.

Option 1:
A dangerous area would be one that might have a trap, (or even something trap-like, like falling boulders in a gorge for instance)... and would usually be a place you could survey in the immediate sense ie. search a room, a stretch of corridor, an ominous doorway or painting....
Since the move is only triggered in a dangerous area, that means that if you search for traps and there isn't any, then there is no need to roll, the GM says, 'nope you found none', whereas if there is traps, you would roll and on a 6- you triggered it (or something like that) and 7+ you spend hold.

Option 2:
A dangerous area specifically refers to the entire dungeon, swamp, evil necromancers castle etc.... and basically it allows you to roll in advance of searching for traps to generate hold that you then spend as you wander around the dungeon/place.... Basically this means that a 6- would generate ad hoc dangers, such as a trap etc, and this means that the act of searching for the trap might in fact create more traps.

Option 3
Something else completely.....

So i guess my question is what does DWorld mean when it says 'dangerous area'? Can you roll in advance and wander around with the hold to spend as you see fit, or is an area more like a stretch of tunnel, and lastly, if the area is a stretch of tunnel does that mean that a safe area (trap's wise) would automatically mean not to roll?

thanks for your help. If i'm way off, just let me know. I'm familiar with the system, but my hang up is with the wording, and also how it relates to discern realities as well, since in theory you could find a trap using that move too (although if you go with option 1, the trap expert move is better)

Thanks for any and all Dworld wisdom.

    \n I use option 3, which is sorta like option 2. But I take "dangerous area" to be a smaller location than "the entire dungeon". One particular room or cavern, for instance. Or maybe a small cluster of caverns. It depends. A Location.

    It sounds like maybe you are assuming the map exists before the game. It doesn't have to. The trap doesn't necessarily exist in the fiction until the fiction demands it. This could be due to a hard move, for instance. Think like a movie or TV show about dungeon crawlers: the writers of such scripts never bother to draw a map. So why should we? It's the dramatic arc of the narrative we're concerned with.

    -Be a fan of the characters

    \n I suppose you could run it as a Schroedinger's Trap thing, but the questions suggest otherwise. "Is there a trap here?" is literally the first question. I think it's honestly something you have to decide on the spot when the player starts looking, and you decide based on whether it makes sense in the situation. And probably sway your results towards the positive if there's doubt.

    Or you could pre-plan it to a degree. "Play to find out" is 100% compatible with a certain amount of structure. You build up things you know to be there, and then find out what happens to them. Draw those maps, and know that there's some places set up with traps. Be open for players to ask about things you didn't think of, though.

    If you search for traps in a dangerous area with no traps, then it's on you to ask the first question. The move grants you reassurance in that case: you can be guaranteed that not only do you not notice any traps, but that's because you looked well enough to rule out traps. That reassurance isn't just a whiff; it's a guarantee of safety. (Very important in trap-heavy dungeons! Imagine being able to ask "Is there a trap here? If so, what activates it?" in Tomb of Horrors!)

    And don't forget the third question: "What else is hidden here?"

    \n+1 to this, I'd run the move this way. If there's any reason to believe a certain room/area is dangerous, then let them roll. On a success, as CarpeGuitarrem says. On a failure, they have probably wasted some time, and you can make a hard move related to that.

    In response to the option 1 or option 2 question, I think others have stated before that in DW moves are quite flexible in terms of "zoom" (time and distance). You can say a particular hack & slash roll represents 10 seconds, minutes of trading blows, or perhaps even more, so similarly you could say a roll of discern realities/trap expert covers a whole castle/dungeon. I guess it depends on how much time do you/the players want to devote to that part of the story.
    But it's not a roll in advance. The roll covers the entire castle because and if the character is actively looking for traps while he goes through it. If he gets wounded on room 4 and can't continue to search for them, he shouldn't be able to spend the hold anymore (similar to defend: if you stop defending that guy/girl, you lose the hold).

    \n@cupbearer11 - I think the fact that our answers are slightly different has a lot to do with the flexibility of the game. You will see different MCs' personal styles show up in play.

    To your question: "how do you know if there is a trap there?" Again, it depends. Sometimes you might just have an area on a pre-drawn map that says "Dangerous!"     \n Ok, My View(TM) is that "dangerous" doesn't necessarily mean traps. There are lots of sorts of danger. So that sides with option 2. But sometimes you actually might have a trap you know is there or you might write "Here there be traps" on a pre-drawn map, which sides with option 1.

    I keep saying words like "necessarily" and "sometimes". That's not tactical indecision. It's situational flexibility. It depends on your prep and your style.

    1 sub 1: Because sometimes the PCs know the danger is a trap, but sometimes they only know it's dangerous, and sometimes they don't know at all. Only the MC knows, and even that is a maybe.
    1 sub 2: I define the area smaller.
    1 sub 3: How could that happen? If they aren't checking, why would they roll?

    2 sub 1: Narrative Arc.
    2 sub 2: "Show signs of an approaching threat"\ufeff. Say things like "well, in the last ten minutes you've found nothing... and nothing has found you." I.e., Build the suspense and paranoia, making it seem difficult and risky to stop every ten feet. If they do stop every ten feet to make a roll, they're probably gonna generate some fails, which means you get to stack up your moves and play em! The PCs will learn pretty quick to differentiate between discerning and searching.

    From the RAW:
    Exploit your prep: "At times you\u2019ll know something the players don\u2019t yet know. You can use that knowledge to help you make moves." - notice this doesn't assume how much prep you did. Because it varies. It just says "at times".
    Begin and end with the fiction. - this means don't just say "no." Make them see, hear, smell and feel the scary situation they're in.
    Think offscreen too. - What is that weird sound? Is it getting closer?

    \n I think that's somewhat correct:

    It's intended for you to use as you like. (Whether that's sloppy game design or brilliant game design, I'm not sure.)

    I read it as:

    We must all agree that the area being surveyed is dangerous, definitely. If you think it isn't, ask, "Why?" ("Why do you think your own bedroom is a dangerous place right now?") You'll get a good answer. If that doesn't inspire you with some good responses, I think it's OK to say, "No, you search and find it to be safe. No need to roll here." But chances are that it will.

    So: "dangerous"? Yes. Does it mean that there ARE necessarily traps? No. Could be just an area they have good reason to be worried about, and will be very relieved to hear that, no, there are no traps when they ask that particular question.

    For what "area" means, use your sense of dramatic judgement: it's probably some separate area that the character has immediate access to right now, but if the Thief is handling a McGuffin Time Bomb, the roll might just apply to the object in their hands right now. Just ask, "Which area are you searching?"

    It's self-balancing, because they'll want to focus on the area they're interested in and want to know.

    As for what happens on a 6-, yeah, I'd say it should be bad. DW characters, especially those using moves they're good at, succeed far more often than they fail. And it's better to get a 7-9 than to get a soft move in response to not rolling at all.

    \n Ok, so the key to remember is that the flow of the conversation here is all about establishing fictional details and then resolving them. On a good roll, or an excellent idea from the player (absent a roll), things will generally resolve as positively as possible. On a bad roll, or reckless/stupid action from the player, they will resolve as poorly as possible (not every single time, but as a general principle).

    So if I walk into a room, and the MC has decreed there is a danger, what do I do? I can try to do careful fictional things and hope that I don't trigger a trap. Or, if I'm a trap expert, I can roll.

    But the important part is that the game works best if the moves don't happen in a vacuum. Either way, the MC should describe your circumstances, and you should describe what you're doing, and then something will logically flow from that.

    Just because you're rolling a move doesn't mean that you aren't doing something else - it's just that the move allows you to make a roll under circumstances where your buddy doesn't get to roll at all.

    So, it really depends on the circumstances, every time (at least if you're doing it right). Walking through the trapped room blindfolded, in my view, is like voluntarily accepting a 6- on the Trap Expert roll.

    It's very hard to explain this kind of thing "in a vacuum", without an example. Generally, as soon as you know a) the fictional situation, and b) what the character is doing, it's pretty easy to resolve how things go.

    It's all about those two things, here, much more so than the move itself. What's the situation? And: what is the character actually doing?

    For instance, let's say your Thief wanders into a small room, and then the door locks behind him. The MC knows that there's a statue with a big diamond in the middle of the room, and, if the diamond is disturbed, the room will start flooding with water.

    Now, what is the character doing?

    1. Just looking around cautiously?

    Without a roll, there probably won't be any particularly dangerous consequences: maybe some time is lost, or weird noises are heard, or the character feels disoriented, or picks up a clue (the sound of water flowing in the walls).

    On a failed Trap Expert roll? The same thing.

    Either way, the MC is making a move in response to what the player said, based on the character's actions.

    2. Poking and prodding at everything, touching things and looking for moving parts?

    Without a roll, the character sets off the trap, or gets a "softer" outcome, like being *about to* set off the trap. (Kind of MC's call here, but the easiest way to decide is to keep asking more details about what the character is doing.)

    What about a failed Trap Expert roll? Well, same thing.

    Either way, the MC is making the most believable, logical move arising from this combination of her prep/ideas and the PC's actions.

    This second option suggests pretty strongly that the character WOULD touch the diamond and set off the trap, so that's (quite likely; you can ask more questions to make sure) probably what happens.

    Does that make sense?

    ---

    As for making a situation dangerous when it wasn't before - that's well in the territory of "ask provocative questions" (is that a Principle or Move in DW? I don't remember).

    So the player walks into his bedroom and says he wants to search a dangerous area for traps.

    What? How is this dangerous? The MC asks the player just that.

    The player responds with something that's on their mind and is logical to them ("Because I've been out of town for two days, and I know my rivals are keen to infiltrate my bedroom and lay a trap!"). Remember: they're only doing this because they already think that this event is likely and believable!

    If you also think it's likely, quickly come up with something, and then have them roll. All good!

    If you DON'T think the danger exists (the problematic case here), do one of the following:

    1. "That's a good thought, but a little search shows there's nothing to fear. No roll needed; it's safe."

    or

    2. Have them roll. On a good outcome, answer their questions - maybe they'll find a way to get something good out of this. On a miss, make a move, but make a gentle, soft one that is believable to you. Maybe there are no traps, but they find some scratches on the window near the lock: looks like someone was *trying* to get in recently! (And, if the character does nothing about it, now you have something to throw at them the next time they're here.)

    (I like number two whenever possible, personally.)

    So, yes, you'll be making a move in response. But it doesn't have to be a life-or-death, danger-defying move. Just make as hard a move as you think is reasonable and believable, in accordance to what you know about the situation. (Maybe if there's absolutely no danger AT ALL, their rummaging about makes noise and angers their next-door neighbour. Or they find a peephole looking into their room from the room above. Why is it here? What does it mean? Was someone spying earlier? All these moves do is set you up to make harder moves in the future, having established circumstances for them.)

    But remember to ask exactly what the character is doing (so you know whether, for instance, finding a peephole or making a bunch of noise or finding scratches on the window is more likely).

    A good friend of mine once told me that Dungeon World plays best when the players don't know what the moves are. I think he even made character sheets with the details of the moves removed.

    Because then the players just say what their characters do. (And most of the problems involved in *World games start when people stop doing that and just say they want to make a certain move, instead.)

    That only works, of course, until the players familiarize themselves with the moves. (And, for some moves, not at all.)

    So, the key here is that it has nothing to do with the move itself. It's just that, when he fails, it's your turn to make a move. And which move you choose depends on what the character was just doing.

    The Warrior who was rolling Defy Danger? He probably already set off the trap and is fighting for his life: a dangerous outcome (e.g. damage) is quite appropriate here.

      \n Exactly the same thing you would do with any other miss! Look at the fiction, pick a move which sounds interesting and believable, follow your Principles and Agenda.

      I think I gave a few examples above.

      As with any such question, it really depends on the situation. Give me an example (with sufficient detail) and I can tell you how *I* would think about it; but it won't be a "rules" answer.

      The framework of "moves" in a game like Dungeon World (anything based on AW) is less of a formal structure and more of an artistic palette. You have *these* tools at your disposal, and your goal is *that* kind of game: you use the right tools in the right spots to give you the kind of experience you want.

      Sometimes that means not making a move where another group would. Sometimes it means using a move in an unexpected place. (Once, in a Monsterhearts game, one PC dropped her glasses onto the sidewalk. Another character - someone she had a crush on, but who was himself very emotionally closed off, and often affected by uncontrollable anger - instead of helping her, stepped on the glasses, breaking them. It was just like a slap in the face. So we used the "shut someone down" move, even though that's usually for pushing someone away in a conversation.)

      \n Improv actors have a principle: "Be obvious".

      This means that, when you're making shit up, don't try too hard. Say what seems natural to you - it'll still be surprising to the other players, but also flow naturally from what's happening. It's really powerful.

      Don't try to stretch to some strange thing: don't have ninjas jump through the door, in other words. Say what comes naturally to you.

      Generally, something will come to mind. (After all, you're searching a "dangerous place"... right?)

      If nothing comes to mind, then fine, just mention a future problem. "All's good, but if you stay here much longer you'll run out of food. What do you do?" That's a totally valid MC move.

      Here, look:\n

      Reveal an unwelcome truth
      Show signs of an approaching threat
      Give an opportunity that fits a class\u2019 abilities
      Offer an opportunity, with or without cost
      Tell them the requirements or consequences and ask
      \nAll these MC moves aren't exactly horrible, death-defying stuff!\n

      Reveal an unwelcome truth - "This trap you're looking at... it has runes on it marking it as Dwarven craftsmanship." -"But that would mean that... oh, shit!"
      Show signs of an approaching threat - "While you've been looking around the tower, you can see thunderclouds forming in the distance. Did you say you left all those books uncovered back at the camp?"
      Give an opportunity that fits a class\u2019 abilities - "The runes on the trap... someone with arcane knowledge might be able to decipher them, given enough time."
      Offer an opportunity, with or without cost - "If someone was willing to climb down into the mud, you could probably pry this whole mechanism out of the chamber. Do you want to?"
      Tell them the requirements or consequences and ask - etc, etc.
      \nIf the situation is such that really, honestly, nothing bad or dangerous or scary can happen, then great! It means the players have maneuvered themselves into a position where they're "winning": they're safe, they have the upper hand, they have control of the situation. Let them enjoy it! They earned a break.

      Probably time to start setting up future trouble, though. (Or else the game will get boring.)

      When someone rolls a 6- in a *World game, that's what the rules say:

      "MC, it's your turn to say what happens next. Make a move!"

      So, given your vision of the current situation, what's a thing that could believably happen? Say that.

      You can often (but shouldn't always) think of it as: given the current situation, what's a reasonably way things could go wrong?

      In a fight with a giant, that could mean getting your head caved in. When you're eating soup at the King's manor, it might just mean you spill some on your shirt.

      It's your job to set up a bunch of threats the players have to worry about. When they miss rolls, you can bring them to bear. If the players have managed to be clever enough to neutralize all these threats ("Nope, remember, we said we draped tarps over the books!"), then, good, they should reap the rewards and face smaller problems.
