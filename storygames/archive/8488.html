\n the other day i was thinking about games. i do that a lot.
i was thinking about how i really dislike games in which, as the GM, i end up running a "traditional" Feed-Plot-to-the-Players-and-Railroad-Them kind of game, something that i think some of my friends expect. that kind of game is too much work for not enough fun. even if the players don't mind it (or even enjoy it), i don't.

in my limited experience, it seems easiest to make a game more player-driven, and get the PCs tied into the setting/situation, if the characters have Connections to NPCs. if they have Friends, Family, Lovers, Rivals, Enemies, Coworkers, etc.

some games, especially some indie games, require, as a step in character creation, making up an NPC or three.
many "traditional" games allow players to have Connections to NPCs, usually by spending points of some kind.

my friends generally have no problems coming up with some NPCs if doing so is required as part of the game. if it isn't, and if they have to pay points for it, they usually skip over it.

i think this has to do with old expectations:
the stereotypical "traditional" or D&Dish game has PCs that are nomadic and unconnected. i spend my points on making your character more badass. "why should i pay points to have an ally or a romantic interest if i could spend those points on a sweet magic sword instead? after all, having an ally just creates a vulnerability - someone that could be attacked or kidnapped or betray me. and besides, i'm just playing in the GMs story. if i need to know an NPC, I'll know that NPC whether I like it or not."

i don't really like to do games this way any more, and i haven't, for awhile. but i think this kind of thinking has become at least somewhat ingrained in my friends (and probably in me a little too).
so the question is: what is the best solution if i want my friends to give their PCs Relationships and Connections but when left to their own devices, they don't?
do i tell they they MUST do so, possibly (in games that use such systems) requiring that a certain number of their points be spent on them? do i tell them they must but give them free points to do so? the first seems that it could create resentment, while the second seems like it would result in NPCs that the player doesn't care about ("Oh, I just got him with my free points, so I don't really care what happens to him...").

possibly the best solution (or part of one of the above solutions) may be simply talking to my friends about this. if i sit down with them and say, "Hey, I want to run this game, but I don't want to create a plotline on my own and shoehorn you into it, I want to create an interesting and dynamic situation, but to do that, I need you to help me by making up NPCs that your characters have some kind of Connection to."

hmm. i'm not really sure what i'm looking for with this post. i guess i'm just wondering if anyone else has struggled with this issue and how you resolved it.
thanks!

    \n I would advise a combination of your (2) and (1b) \u2014 sit down and talk with your friends, explaining that you want their character creation to also create the game's initial situation, and then give them dedicated points for creating those NPCs. If it were GURPS, for instance, I'd give them the 'usual' 125 points and then an additional 10 for Contacts/Allies/Dependents/etc. Don't hand it down as a dictate; get them on board with the plan first, then enable them to contribute to the plan.

    To kick it up a notch, have the players create one or two NPCs each, but then require each to figure a relationship to somebody else's NPC. That way, you'll be that much closer to one big situation-relation map instead of five small ones, one for each PC.

    \n You've just hit upon my favourite style of playing and running games, for much the reasons you express. There are a few games that help people bridge the gap, games that have a bit of both. One of the best is Legend of the Five Rings (L5R).

    L5R started out as a variation of the character design structure made popular by White Wolf, with people choosing clans. The clan is a good concept because it helps define the character (through stereotypes and special abilities) and also gives the character some connections within the gameworld.

    As the background in L5R grew and was fleshed out more and more, other elements of the game gained significance until they, too, could function in similar ways. For example different spiritual outlooks could be used to make connections between the characters and factions within the game, as well as appearing as skills and other abilities on the character sheet.

    The social structure of the game lends itself to a blended style of play, too. The characters are generally subservient to some lord who will send them on missions, but the intricate social network in the setting means that the connections are both a powerful resource and a source of conflict within that.

    My one warning is that L5R is not a quick game. Because the background is so huge and intricate, and because all those connections encourage players to bring a lot of it in, it is much better for campaign games.

    I still sometimes get players who can't get beyond wanting a hook in their mouth and a tug on the line, but I find that L5R has gotten a good few of them to stretch their legs a bit.

    \n Belonging to a "group" of some kind is all well and good, but it isn't really enough for me, Graham. My moment to moment interests in games are quite fickle. Recently I watched Kung Fu Panda and I thought to myself "I should try running Weapons of the Gods again!" This lead me to think back to the first time I tried that out. I ran the official demo/intro scenario and that was fun, then we did some character creation. Both of my friends had characters that belonged to a a clan. Maybe the same, maybe two different ones, I can't remember. Neither, however, had any specific characters they were connected to beyond each other. As in many games that I tend to put into the category of more "traditional" WotG (like REIGN, nWoD, heck even Burning Wheel) having individual NPCs as an official part of a character's life, whether as a friend, relative, lover, mentor, or even enemy, costs points that could otherwise be spent on things that have a more immediately obvious and tangible benefit. and then, if you're approaching the game from a D&Dish standpoint you say to yourself "Phhh. Who needs that?"

    And yet it's both my belief and my experience that having an NPC that you find interesting and care about is one of the easiest and best ways to get a player interested and involved in the setting/situation as a whole.
    So the Green Mountain Bandits have been collecting and hoarding all the herbs gathered by the peasants living there and the mountain is the only place where the herbs used to treat a sickness that's been spreading through the low lands grows? Who cares? Not me.
    Oh, but my sister's fiancee has that sickness and is going to die without the medicine so she's disguised herself as a man to infiltrate the bandit clan? Well, now I might care... but since you, mister GM just invented that sister on the spot, eh. Whatever.

    Does it make me a bad or lazy GM that I want my friends to at least work with me on coming up with this stuff? if so, I don't care. i'm pretty sure it would make the game better for everyone.

    \n The problem I have (as both a player and a GM) is not getting people to make connections, but rather getting everyone on the same page with those connections. I can't even count the number of times that I've been totally psyched about the NPCs I've hooked in to my character and then found that they had absolutely zero traction with the GM, or vice versa. Most of the time any connection I give my PC just becomes the face for a problem that the PC was going to deal with anyway (to the point where you just dread seeing them come up in a game, because it only signals that a new problem is arriving, never anything good), or they sit idle on the character sheet and it feels like pulling teeth to try and fit them into the story.

    It isn't just me, at least, so we've had a few nights where we talked about what seemed to work and what didn't, but really only in a performance sense: we're pretty clear now on what makes an NPC fun to play or to interact with, and that's been very useful and given us much better PC-NPC scenes. Unfortunately, it hasn't really translated into how to encourage them to be more significant elements of the game for everyone instead of for just one person. Our group probably needs to have a talk about this, soon.


    Ideally, I'd like it to be possible for us to sit down and play and have at least some NPCs who everyone agrees are good and fun to play and will be able to be in the story on a routine basis AND are actually positive (that is, not just the vehicle for more grief to be heaped upon a PC, or at best a problem-solving tool akin to having 50 feet of rope in your inventory just in case you come across a cliff you need to climb down). I'd like to be psyched about how much my PC likes a particular NPC, psyched about how much I as a player like that NPC, and also have other people be just as psyched about it. I'd like to run a game and look at the connections the PCs have and think "fuck yeah, these are great NPCs and I'm going to love coming back to them over and over again in all kinds of contexts." I'm sure there must be a trick for getting our group to reach that ideal consistently, the same way that we found tricks for making NPCs that were fun to act out in a scene.

    \nI'll pass along some of the things that really clicked with our group, maybe you'll find something useful in them:

    1. Stereotyping can be your friend: if you can pitch an NPC in one short sentence and everyone gets basically the same idea, that's perfect. We love to see depth in NPCs, but we like to see it develop in play -- for the initial appearance, we really need to see a core concept that we can connect with immediately.
    2. Adding in one weird thing is good for color. The loyal but dim legbreaking thug (stereotype) who dropped out of seminary school (one weird thing) gives you a fun quirk to play with to break up the monotony of the stereotype, and is one of the avenues that we can use to extract more depth later on.
    3. Always typecast whenever possible, especially if you're having players handle NPCs in some scenes: find out what character types people enjoy playing in short bursts, find out what character types they're good at playing in short bursts, and assign NPC roles accordingly. Playing an NPC should be easy and fun whenever possible. If one person is handling all the NPCs, you may need to exclude some types of NPCs or deal with them being portrayed poorly and/or infrequently.
    4. Recurring NPCs work best for us when they've got some kind of goal or desire: something that you can fall back on in a scene and say "okay, given that I want this, I'm going to do that." It also gives the PCs reasons to talk about and think about NPCs even when they're not in a scene.
    5. Alternately (or at the same time) you can focus the NPC on a relationship: find out what a player wants out of a connection with a particular NPC (not what the PC wants, what the player wants!) and make that the baseline desire for playing that NPC. "I want the captain to think I'm a loose cannon but a good cop: I want him to call me on my bullshit in private but back me up in public."
    6. Vet NPCs the same way you would a PC. If something doesn't sound like it'll work out in play, put your heads together and fix it.

    Our group's been great with 1 through 4, and we're working on hitting 5 and 6 more consistently.

    \n The following is quite a departure from traditional playstyle, so it won't work for some trad groups.

    My solution in my (in-progress) design, the Pentasystem, is to start out with a brainstorm in which people come up with what they want to see in the game - what themes and entities. Themes can be anything from 'racism' to 'airships' - anything that's not an entity and you want to see in game. Entities are mostly groups - the Church, the Government, Organized Crime.

    Then you create NPCs (though I don't call them that, because they're played by players and there's no GM, but you can call them NPCs and have the GM play them if you want), which act as "faces" and "handles" to the themes and entities. If Organized Crime is an entity in the game, you get a hitter for the mob or a capo or whatever, depending on how many people were into that entity, and so forth.

    Now you decide how the PCs are connected to those NPCs, because this is what involves them in what is going on with the themes and entities. The hitter for the mob is Danny's brother-in-law and he has a contract on Andy. In fact, you build your PCs in part around the NPCs, and not vice-versa.

    I got this idea from an exercise I participated in here, which Josh Roby started, in which we found that if you started with the PCs they tended to be less interesting than the NPCs because they were produced with the smallest amount of information about the setting and situation.

    Having wound up these springs, you now set your story machine going.

    Oh, and [edit for crosspost], AFT's group has an excellent thing going there with giving the NPCs goals. That tells you what they're likely to do in a given situation and makes it easier to pass them over to people to play for a while.
