\n I'm starting a new campaign soon in which the PCs will be WWI infantrymen mysteriously transported to Mars for romantic adventure in a decadent society. E.R. Burroughs' John Carter of Mars and Master Mind of Mars are obvious inspirations, less obviously Jack Vance's Planet of Adventure and S.M. Stirling's In the Courts of the Crimson Kings. The tech standing in for magic will be updated for modern sensibilities (more biotech and fewer levitating rays or radium pistols), but you can think of the setting as being much like Barsoom. This is romantic, even swashbuckling adventure with sci-fi trappings, not hard SF.
\n
\nI had an eye-opening and very enjoyable encounter with Fate (in the form of Spirit of the Century) on Saturday, and found that it produces with game mechanics a lot of the results I have been striving for with play technique. It encourages character-players with incentives (and disincentives sometimes, go figure) to do what I have been urging them to do for twenty years, and has more success at it than I. This being the case, I am strongly tempted to use Fate-like mechanics (Aspects, Fate Points, tags, compels....) in my new campaign. I could simply graft those onto my favourite crunchy representation and resolution rules (ForeSight) the way that in SotC they sit on top of FUDGE. Or, considering that none of my players admits to an interest in using the crunch to good effect, I could simply adapt SotC with an appropriate skill list and perhaps Stunts. I do like the prospect of doing away with a choice of plausible values for attributes and skill levels that are realistic rather than relevant.
\n
\nEither way (with FUDGE or ForeSight as the resolutional substrate) the problem becomes choosing Aspects. Would it be best to
\n
\n\u2022 Let the players choose up to ten Aspects on the basis of their characters' lives on Earth, strike out the ones that are irrelevant to life on Mars, and let the players adopt new Aspects as they encounter new things and have "re-formative" experiences on Mars?
\n
\n\u2022 Have players choose only five Aspects /ab initio/, and then add to them organically as above to bring the total up to ten?
\n
\nor
\n
\n\u2022 Ask the players to name a full set of Aspects from the beginning, anticipating developments on Mars?
\n
\nObviously the "book" method of establishing links among the players and of guiding the choice of Aspects is not appropriate here. I was thinking of asking for one Aspect relating to childhood, one to civilian life, one to training (in Egypt!), one to Gallipoli, one to France. Or two each?
\n
\nOne of the bees in my bonnet is for establishing mutual contrasts among the PCs, so that each has foils amongst the party, each Holmes has his Watson, etc. Having four players in my group, I thought of The Three Musketeers, the four humours, assorted schemes of four temperaments, and even the four suits of minor arcana in the Tarot. But the thought occurred that with Aspects that might not be necessary. Any advice?
\n
\n
\nWhat will I have to do to produce a Fate hack for planetary romance?

    10 Aspects always seemed like overkill to me, too.

    Start them with some reasonable number (4? 5? 6?) based on their experiences on Earth.

    When they start experiencing Mars, give them a chance to earn more Aspects. When it comes up, you'll have a better sense of whether you want the new Aspect to replace an old one, or just added on top.

    Maybe set a low limit (like one or two more than the starting number), so you can get *some* new Aspects for each character, but beyond that transformation of existing Aspects will be necessary (which I find more interesting, personally).

    \n I have run several SotC convention games based on John Carter of Mars called The Spirit of the Red Planet and the system works great for the setting.
    \n
    \nWhen it comes to aspects 10 is usually too many I have found that 7 is just about right especially if on of those is a DFRPG high concept aspect that really defines the character's archetype. How many aspects they have that relate to Earth depends on how quickly you are getting their asses to Mars. If you only imagine an opening Flanders battlefield and then wham they are on Mars, or if you start them off on Mars, than any aspects only about the WWI situation are going to seem pretty worthless. However a good aspect concerning Earth and their desire to return home is a great aspect for a character who finds themselves stuck on an alien world.
    \n
    \nSo I would be up front with the players about their eventual destination during character creation then it probably won't be as big of an issue. Also as for creating foils as Storn said you already have a built in group dynamic and roles that the characters can take and niches that they can fill thanks to the squad of soldiers background. The great thing about the guest starring phases in SotC character creation is the way it establishes relationships with other characters so encourage that in character creation and have them create aspects about the other characters at least one and preferably two. The Three Musketeers thing will likely just sort of happen as players figure out what types of characters they want to play and what the other player's characters are like, so unless they all want to play John Carter clones they will likely choose roles to fill that will round things out nicely.
    \n
    \nAs for adding aspects later I would say keep two aspects blank and in reserve so they can fill them in once they get to Mars and start interacting with the Martian technology, society, and environment. This should really work for a John Carter style game where the suddenly discover that their "High Gravity Strength" allows them to make "Titanic Leaps". But it also works for things like "Custom Radium Rifle" and "In Love With The Princess". You might also consider giving them all a free stunt that defines their Earthman on Mars physiology or you can just let that be part of their high concept as Earthmen allowing them to invoke and compel it for things like high gravity strength, or barbaric Earth customs leading to interesting faux pas on Mars.
    \n
    \nAlso for my Spirit of the Red Planet games one thing I like to do is put out a sheet of setting and theme aspects that the players can tag for the first time for free to really set the mood and tone that I'm trying to achieve. Here is the list I use for the Red Planet game:
    \n
    \no\tA World of Adventure and Romance
    \no\tMars the Planet of War
    \no\tSavage Red Wastelands!
    \no\tAncient Martian Secrets and Ruins
    \no\tThe Clash of Steel!

    \nPerhaps I ought to have mentioned that I and the other players are all Australian, and that the player-characters are going to be Australian infantry. In fact, they're all going to be infantrymen in the 1st Division AIF, which was raised in New South Wales, the state we all live in. There are two reasonably good movies and a 1980 TV miniseries I would consider for military orientation if I were running a "First AIF" campaign, but my character-players aren't such research junkies as I, and I have promised not to make them learn any military history for background. The upshot is that tropes are not going so much to be my friends.
    \n
    \nBesides, one of the things that really appeals to me about FATE is that it makes a matter of game mechanics things that I have been struggling to get players (including two of these players) to do as a matter of player technique. Aspects and Fate Points promise fair to advance my agenda of decentralising authorial responsibility. I would like to give mechanics a fair go at advancing my other agenda: creating a party of player-characters designed for interaction and as mutual foils. I do have trouble with this, including one player who breaks too early from the collaborative process and starts channelling dogmatic statements about his character from the Fifth Dimension, and one who struggles badly for a character concept he feels happy with, and prefers character generation systems like that in Traveller that narrow down the choices and give him a clear lead as to where to start.
    \n
    \nI was thinking of taking four index cards and writing on each of them a clich\xe9 from the Four-Temperament Ensemble, along with maybe some starter suggestions from temperament theory. Then I would deal them out at random, and ask each player to write an appropriate Aspect on each card and pass the cards left, and so on until each player had made a suggestion on each card. Then I'd collect the cards, shuffle them, deal the out, allow players to trade, and ask each player to generate a character suggested by something written on his card. Something like that.

    \nOkay I'm going to tackle this one first cause I hope it has a fairly simple answer. I like to think of and describe invoking aspects as those moments in a book, or other story where the author specifically calls out a bit of description that helps or allows a character to do something. Howard's Conan using his "Mighty Thews" or his "Panthertine Grace" to battle hordes of enemies, or climb an unscalable wall because he was a "Cimmerian" is like the player spending a Fate Point and invoking one of their character's aspects to get a bonus. So any aspects that get invoked should have the same feel, and the Fictional Importance to the aspect getting invoked should be made clear in the game, otherwise why bother spending a Fate Point on it?

    If it is a flat bonus to Might and Athletics then it will just happen all the time and it's great to be an Earthman on Mars. And all the characters are effectively getting one or two free skill points and if you are using a skill pyramid they essentially have one or two skills that are one level higher than they would be normally.

    If it is a personal Aspect than it only comes up when it is Fictionally Important, i.e. when they invoke it and spend a Fate Point on it, or when it is an issue in the fiction, i.e. when they get compelled and earn a Fate Point on it. Either way the Fate Point economy is engaged when it gets brought up, and that means it is something that gets highlighted because they are Fictionally special and important. They still have High Gravity Strength all the time, but they only get a mechanical bonus when they highlight it with a Fate Point.

    If it is a stunt then it will mostly work out that it is just a flat bonus all the time or in specific circumstances, but it allows a little more individual control and definition. Maybe on guy finds he is really good at using the low gravity to make tremendous leaps, while another finds it easier to lift massive weights on Mars.

    If you make it a Setting aspect then it becomes important fictionally for any Earthman on Mars but mechanically the way I use Setting aspects is the first time it is tagged/invoked for the session it is Free, and after that it costs a Fate Point. So really I use Setting aspects to get the ball rolling and to reinforce the genre, color, and tone of the game I am trying to run.

    Now if you mix and match then it is really pretty simple math to be aware of how much of a bonus will be available at any given time if they are willing to spend Fate Points and if it is Fictionally Important to the scene. For instance lets say that you have combined all of the above options and the player has a stunt specializing in lifting heavy objects.

    The starting bonus of the Skill bump and the stunt give him a +2 on the roll which is pretty big in FATE as it is equal to spending a Fate Point, then he tags the Setting aspect for free since no one else has used it yet and gets another +2 on the roll. So because he is highlighting his character's strong points and the amazing strength that Earthmen on Mars are capable of he already starts with a +4 Great bonus for no Fate Points, meaning that even if he rolls a -4 on his fudge dice he is still going to generate shifts equal to his Might skill. But then he spends a Fate Point to invoke his high concept of Earthman and gets an additional +2 which is a +6 Fantastic bonus to his roll, then because if he has a Stunt based on being strong he likely has an aspect that has a similar nature and he spends another Fate Point for a +8 Legendary bonus on top of the Dice and Skill for the cost of only 2 Fate Points. An Earthman on Mars who focuses on being strong is suddenly Superman.

    So if you kept it so that the only bonus was from an aspect and the player spending a Fate Point to invoke his Earthman high concept on the Might roll, it would only mechanically be a +2 bonus for 1 Fate Point. Earthmen on Mars get a bonus to strength because of their innate physiology when it is Fictionally important, but it isn't anything that a native Martian who has worked on being strong and is also willing to spend points when it is Fictionally important couldn't match.\n
