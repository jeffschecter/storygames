    \n I actually liked that video quite a bit. I don't even like videos as audience for advertising, I don't generally bother to watch e.g. Kickstarter campaign videos.

    The video does a good job with staking the trad fantasy grounds - if that's something a person is fond of, that video will entice them, as the narrated imagery works well to evoke the genre. The bits about the structure of the campaign and the book, the timelines and social webs, is also presented in a basically appealing way - if you're somebody who's been thinking that they'd like to do this big campaign thing, except with modern relationship maps and such, then that'll attract attention.

    Specific selling points that I made note of are the image of the bound book proof (a very reassuring thing to see a good-looking book already being pawed at), the female narrator with a touch of amateur aesthetic in her rendition, the visualization of the timeline and relationship elements, and the sweeping description of the epic fantasy backstory. Those are all things that I would expect to do well in selling the product here.

    The bit that isn't working so well is the website integration argument: it comes up for real around the 6 minute mark, where the French-speaking guy is presented. That's much too late in the video to sell it as central, and an argument is never made for why it's central to the project, which all makes it look almost like an incidental sponsor advertisement. "We're making this cool, differently organized fantasy rpg campaign book, and oh here's our sponsor, they create websites." There is an annoying repeating grammar error in the text, too: "we've been developing website for ten years" or something like that, a pluralization mistake I think.

    I suppose that the way to fix the above is to either rearrange the video and bolster the website explanation so that it explains clearly what use case the website answers, and so that the website comes up early on - basically when the timeline structure stuff is explained, or even before it. Alternatively, remove most of the website stuff and just add like two sentences to the structural explanation, noting that everything mentioned here would be available in a digital online interactive format well-suited to information management, created by your team with plenty of professional experience in website design - that's pretty much everything that needs to be said about the website thing, unless you want to really try to sell the audience on it. As the video stands, this subject is left precariously midway between an incidental remark and a full sales pitch.

    I do not find the length of the video excessive, although I am also not a regular audience for such videos, so I might not realize how the audience psychology works in this regard. In practice I tuned out and did other stuff a few times, but came back when something interesting caught my ear. However, I would think that it's good for the video to be long enough for the audience to form an initial positive buying position while watching it; you want the potential customer to already be inclined to purchase when the finish the video, rather than it being so short that they're still pondering on it when they finish. The content of the video is not as important as its length in this regard, really, as it's just a psychological sales trick
