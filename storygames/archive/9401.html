\n My question for everyone is WHAT do you (as Game Master) prepare for a new campaign world?

Assume you have the rules you want to use (in my case D&D4), but you may want some specialty rules or options or adjustments to fit the style/theme of the setting.


What do you prepare ahead of time for rule adjustments?
What do you prep for and about the world setting for players (how much info on each section)?
What do you prep for character creation steps or help for players?
Do you make any materials for handouts to the players? (examples?)
Do you make a wiki for the campaign? (what does it include?)

What other general advice do you have for starting a brand new campaign setting/world to give the players a premise and ideas for how and what to do?



I'm interested in D&D4 type creation - but this could be for any campaign...

    \n I've run a lot of D&D 3E games, and here's what I like to do.

    Pitch rules adjustments and game concept, then get buy-in with contributions.

    If you can, as a best practice, pitch a few different things, all of which You'd enjoy running.

    Like so:

    "Hey guys, here's three ideas for a D&D game:

    1 A low-powered game, just Martial characters in the Heroic tier. The world is like that too. You'd play an army unit, and you'd have to choose who's the sergeant, who's the corporal, and who are the grunts.
    2 A site-based game; you guys are part of the 'gold rush' around this massive, ancient dungeon; most people don't come back, but you're the exact kind of psychos that actually manage to bring back the occasional sack of golden junk. Or not; then you roll new characters.
    3 A high-powered game; we start at level 10, come up with some hooks for how you're already in the thick of various arcane and mundane conflicts, and from there we just keep pulling out crazy stuff, rolling it back to your backstories, until level 30.

    What do you guys think?"

    Then it depends on what they say they want. But the very next thing - around when people are figuring out who's covering what roles, but before characters get made - is to have them make something.

    Like this:
    1 "So I can set up, I need an idea of a battle where one of the higher-up officers noticed you - that's why they've been added to the 'elite unit'. Think of the terrain, too, and we'll add the battle sites to the map. Also, something you guys lost to the war, so I can add that to the map too."
    2 "This will take a second; instead of a big backstory, which we can work out in the game, can you think of your character's top 3 sins? Then we'll talk around the table a bit; everybody try to think of stuff that would tempt that sin. So you have a list of sins ranked by importance in the game. Then you get each player to write 1 thing that could tempt a sin, for at least 3 different sins. And they talk about their characters ideas. I might say "OK, what are you greedy for?" and Joe says "Gold's OK, but especially having people in my back pocket." then Tim, who's not even the GM, thinks "oh, I've got an idea - the mayor is massively in debt to the casino; right now another guy is covering his debts, but that guys is actually a vampire from the dungeon", so Tim says "I've got Greed!" and writes it down for the GM to use later. Now, I can change details, but not throw it out, and people shouldn't give each other's ideas away, but they can salivate waiting for sin-gratification because they helped generate it.
    3 "Make up a small faction that you're in charge of (like a small thieves guild), or a large one that you're a rising star in (like being a small city's lord-wizard in a magiocracy)."

    Once you've had that conversation, get them to create characters. Then write up a 1 page summary of the game and the ideas you all talked about, and at most 1 page of other info for the starting PCs (short is crucial, players will want to get right to the game).

    Then get your session ready like the DMG suggests, using or referencing as much player-contributed material as possible (including their characters). Just like the game says, you don't need to have much more than a sketchy map to start; let things evolve from what the players show interest in. Like, if you're two sessions in and have them come across a cyclops rat, and they get freaked out, then use the cyclops rat, and maybe tie it in with a big beholder - genetic manipulation plot. Give the beholder a nice big swath of the dungeon, connect him to some other villains, that kind of thing. Don't think you have to write a bunch in advance to be ready; what you really need is a sense of what the players like, player buy-in, and a sack full of encounters.

    \n It varies from game to game, but I like to do a lot. An insane amount, actually.

    I like to start with a handout. If possible, I include a few cultural details that the campaign will focus on (money systems or honor explanations, or whatever). I also include the character build process, but leave out lists (skills, weapons, etc.). If there are particular archetypes (aka classes, races, etc.) that I want to limit them to, I list those with descriptions. I'm used to running games that the players don't necessarily know, so I often include the core mechanics (not all the modifiers, but how to roll the dice and how the dice relate to things on the character sheet).

    My worldbuilding gets pretty intense and detailed, particularly in the bits that a lot of other GMs might gloss over. Assuming a fantasy game of some sort I like to start with some places and people that might be found on a typical street. Each item is given enough detail that they could provide a mini-side plot:

    - a restaraunt or two, not a generic tavern, but something with personality and with staff
    - 10 items you can buy from people on the street
    - farmers (always have farmers prepared, they're everywhere in fantasy settings)
    - a smattering of other shops or people, such as a carpenter, blacksmith, shepherd, and a random passer-by chart
    - a local specialty (a food or an industry) for each place I expect to use early on
    - a random wandering animal chart (because if deadly monsters wander the places just outside of towns, just how tough are the farmers?)
    - A notable "do not do": I do not map out any villages, cities or streets. All these bits I create are dropped in as characters go searching for something of the sort.

    Does all this matter? The players seem to think so. They have openly expressed confidence that they can stop and talk to anyone, or walk into any shop in the game setting, and I'll be ready to reward their curiosity with something interesting. These bits also help establish the "norm" for the setting without having to provide a huge, detailed setting description in the handout.

    This isn't even into the adventure yet, because a lot of that will emerge from the characters, but I like to add extras here, too, such as actual foods and crafts for the players. The players get to taste what their characters are eating, or try to do what their characters are doing, or whatever.

    \n If the world information doesn't fit on one page or can be described in thirty seconds, then it's too much and it's impinging on the players' ability to contribute to the setting. The only setting information that is relevant to the game is that which is directly relevant to the player characters. If a player wants something for their character that isn't quite within the scarce setting information I've prepared, I find ways to include it. Content so included is immediately and fundamentally more important than setting bits that aren't directly relevant to the PCs.

    In play, anything that anybody says that implies or suggests setting material that hasn't been otherwise introduced into the game gets jotted down. When creating new situations for play, I marry the directly-relevant-to-PCs stuff to harvested-from-actual-play stuff to create "new" stuff.

The only setting information that is relevant to the game is that which is directly relevant to the player characters...
    \nI see this approach more and more, especially in shared/rotating GM games, but I didn't like how it made gamemastering less fun in the traditional single-GM/one-or-more-players rpg. Simply put, I think over-focus on the player characters can go too far, and I define "too far" as being when the GM feels like he/she is only there to serve and entertain the players and doesn't have stakes in the fun.

    The GM has a character, too, something that represents the GM's stakes in the game. It's called "the setting," or rather the portion of the setting that represents what the GM is interested in, much the way players make characters that focus on what interests them. The player characters are out to challenge and thereby change the setting. The GM is there to challenge and thereby change the characters. There's a balance to be struck there.

    For that reason, and others, I encourage gamemasters to continue the practice of preparing settings in as much detail as they desire, much the way I don't mind if players design their characters in great detail. The GMs won't want to hand the players all the setting details, but a GM shouldn't have to constantly worry about impinging upon what the players want when the GM gives his or her fair share of input into what the story will be about.

    \n Graham, the stuff you describe sounds pretty cool. I've played in simmy games like that and when they're well-done they're really like nothing else. And I like your focus on preparing things for the players to run across, that seems like the best of both worlds -- it gives you the lonely fun of setting prep while remaining player-focused and relevant to actual play.

    (and you cook for your players? damn.)

    Couple questions for you tho: you mention really backgroundy things like honor and coinage -- how much do you expect your players to study that stuff, preplay? Could someone who really didn't have time to study your setting play in your game? Do you feel that you get the right level of effort from your players?

    Say the players catastrophically misunderstand a setting detail in the game -- like say they don't kowtow to the nomad queen in the right way and they fatally insult her while her court looks on. How do you correct them? Do you just tell them they've done something wrong and let them rewind and try again? (and risk killing your flow and turning the game into a setting lecture.) Do you explain the error and the handwave what they've said into something more appropriate, figuring that character expertise will trump player ignorance? (and make your preparation and their responses meaningless?) Or do you let it ride and teach them the lesson the hard way?

    This was the pitfall I hit when I did detailed pre-play setting design -- I'd build these beautiful rococo cathedrals in my head and then I'd spend a ton of table-time describing the proper way to play in them instead of actually playing in them. So when I GM nowadays, my approach is that if it wasn't spoken at the table, it's not "real" yet. I still contribute tons. Probably the majority of the setting comes from me. But the players have a voice on setting too. We all come to the table with mere impressions and ideas, and only actual play can canonicalize those fragments.

    (that said, I'm glad you've got your play style figured out. I'm just relating difficulties I've had with that style, is all.)

    \nI try to keep the setting details to a page or two, and I encourage the sheet to be kept at the table for reference. One read-through is sufficient. Generally there's only one or two "issues" that I pick out of the setting to include on the sheet, generally something I'm interested in focusing the game on (I get to see the players' character sheets, they get a glimpse of mine).

    The second issue you discuss happens all the time, especially since I'm currently running Legend of the Five Rings, a pretty complex setting with some very different cultural behaviours. I use a mixture of techniques, but the main one relies on the setting prep I do. I use encounters with "normal" life in the setting like tutorials, having the behaviour of NPCs be examples of how their PCs should play. I do overlook some player ignorance and rewind only in critical situations. I actually have successful etiquette (and similar skills) rolls produce a rewind, which makes players more relaxed, knowing they don't have to make every word and action described perfect. That actually improves play flow with less confident players.

    While I may use simulationism (or rather, the illusion of it) as a setup, I also use it for narrativist reasons, too. Imagine a group of players where one player comes from a horse farm and another player has to fight white supremacists just to walk home. Their sense of "normal" is so different that it's tricky to define drama. By establishing a "normal" in-game, the dramatic action is highlighted (just as evil is easier to show in a setting that has a little good).

    \n Kev, I prep differently for sandbox vs megaplot.

    For megaplot, I come up with whatever I think is cool. My enthusiasm for what I've made up tends to enrich play and compel the players to interface with it. Them poking at what I think is cool unfailingly inspires me to come up with more cool stuff. Once I know what I want to show, I help the players make characters with reasons to care about what I want to show them.

    For sandbox, I do pretty much the opposite. I prepare almost nothing. I provide the players a bunch of broad general parameters (e.g. "low-tech, high-magic"), and lots of brainstorms of various things they might do. I then let them pick what kinds of stuff they'd like to pursue, in terms of activities ("fighting!"), payoffs ("gain cool loot!"), and color ("titanic good vs evil!"). Then I go prep a few individual scenarios that offer that.

    I can elaborate on either one of these if it sounds like a good match for what you're after...
