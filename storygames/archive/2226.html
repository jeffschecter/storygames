    Jim,

    I wrote a game called Get Out Get Away Get Wise Get Back Get Even for a competition a while ago. It's based on chase movies (The Fugitive, The Thirty-Nine Steps).

    It's not entirely successful. The biggest problem, I found, was that chase movies have a lot in them that's not actually chasing. If you watch the Fugitive, there's actually not much chasing in it. He does a lot of breaking into hospitals, shaving off his beard, investigating his wife's murder, but the chasing is an occasional action scene.

    So, the problem with Get Out Get Away Get Wise Get Back Get Even is that you don't want to play it for long. You do some chasing, and that's fun, but then you want something else to do.

    But have a look: it might spark some ideas.

    Oh wow, that's interesting. More than 15 minutes at a shot for car chases? Really? Man, I thought I was a fan of chase scenes, but I'd never expect more than 5-10 minutes max playing at a single chase. I don't actually know of any specific existing rules that provide for good chases, but lemmie spitball some stuff at you.

    Here's my first offering, and it may seem off the wall. Mexican Standoff by Tim Kleinert. But wait!, you say. Mexcan Standoff is about guns, not chases! Very true. But it is about taking a singular type of high-tension action scene and making it into a longish session of play. It's not what you're looking for, but if you're interested, it might just spark some ideas about writing something fresh.

    It's occurred to me that one could take the Dogs in the Vineyard approach to escallation and apply it to chases. Instead of talking/physical/fighting/gunfighting one could use something like fields/towns/cities/tunnels to escallate back and forth between the more and less dangerous places to be driving too fast.

    And, as it happens, I'm working on a little game design that'll include chases. I'm kind of attacking the issue from an extra-hippie one. Out of seven skills there's one called "Pursuit". I take that and attach it to a conflict system where things might be resolved in as little as one roll or in as many as a dozen rolls. So, I'll be depending on the players to narrate out good chases and letting the system be the pacing tool.

    I really like the chase rules in Spirit of the Century. A chase in that game is basically an ongoing conflict where the pursuee does something challenging (jumps a wall, for example), and the pursuers have to meet the challenge to keep up, and then the pursuee escalates and does something even more challenging. The risk is stepped up at each round of the conflict, until all the pursuers have wiped out or the pursuee loses his nerve.

    I think the central mechanic could be abstracted for lots of other interesting purposes.
