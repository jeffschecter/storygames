    Posted By: whiteknifeSo, are there any storygames that fit this manifesto's vision then?
    \nWell, I don't see anything busting out and making the splash that D&D and follow up rpgs made in the 80s, and honestly, I don't ever foresee any one game doing that again.

    OTOH, I think some of the pokings at "structured freeform" that folks have been talking about around here recently have a great potential for being very broadly accessible to lots of people.

    I was actually unaware of Paul's manifesto, although I've made much the same argument on many occasions. If I may get hippy-gamery for a moment, cutting back my own thinking on it to the core essential question of: "Yes, but what do you want?" as if in some sort of narr-y showdown, my answer would be:

    I want abroad swath of the public aware of this fun game-acting-story-thing we do, and be interested and comfortable with it, so that I can easily engage in the activity with folks I know and like, and with whom I have more in common than a hobby.

    \n This actually sort of gets at some of the ideas I was playing with for my little game chef entry, The City Burns At Midnight, which, admittedly, doesn't have much roleplaying built into it at the moment. I want to start with broad, recognizable activities (exploring, scavenger hunts, talking about how you spent your day, photography), and figure out how to layer roleplay on top of that. I'm not entirely sure I'll end up with a roleplaying game in the end, but, based on what I've done so far, I'll end up with something that is fun to play, and it might make for a gateway experience to roleplaying and story gaming among people who wouldn't naturally seek out gaming as a hobby.

    Posted By: komradebob
    Are you including the cultural environment at the time as part of that analysis? It certainly seems like a key elemnent of its success to me, much the way examining the cultural environment of VtM's success in the early 90s is relevent to understanding the success of that game.
    \nInteresting. Is the ability to capture the spirit of an age (or a subculture of an age) the most important contributing factor to success in a game?

    I wonder about another thing though: to what extent, if any, is the gaming market shrinking (figures, citations)? Does making beginners' games necessarily mean that more complex games will be played? Or does that matter?

    As an example, the Wii has expanded the market for video games. But even though I like playing videogames, this doesn't really affect me; people willing to play Wii specific games (Fit, Sports, Etc) as new users are still not interested in playing the games that I play (jrpgs, tactical games, the odd FPS). And I'm not terribly interested in playing the Wii games either.

    Perhaps I'm saying that I'm not altruistic enough on behalf of story-gaming that I am happy to help it achieve success by creating games that I would prefer not to play if this does not lead to getting players that want to play the kinds of games I would prefer to play (and I apologize for that sentence). It's not that I don't like these people, these non-gamers; perhaps we can play boardgames or knit, or watch movies or go backpacking. But I suppose the interest in making special games for beginners would make more sense to me if I thought that playing story-games was an end in itself, by definition more satisfying than other social activities and independent of whether the system or content was of interest to me.

    Too many unknowns. Just questioning the assumptions of the article to play the devil's advocate.

    \n Huh. Wow, I'd forgotten about that thing. Glad to see it even still exists.

    EDIT: And this is an artifact of a very different era. When did I write it? 1996 or so? Distribution via the web has totally transformed the field, so the audience for this sort of essay is now, well, everyone. But back when I wrote it, it was for Steve Jackson, Robin Laws, Jon Tweet, Sam Chupp and all the rest of those cats who actually had some say in what games saw the light of day. In fact this went into The Oracle, which was Lou Prosperi's effort at maintaining the terrific first steps made by Interactive Fiction (which, also, wasn't really for the masses back then).

    EDIT EDIT: I see it says 1999 but I'm sure I had drafted versions of this essay at various points prior. 1996 was at the top of my full-time writing/designing phase, so it makes sense that my first drafts happened after my Big Meltdown.
