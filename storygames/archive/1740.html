    There is no codified bluffing in Dust Devils.

    However, it can happen. Here's how:

    You deal cards, and everyone knows how many so-and-so has, etc. Each person may place chips up as Stakes. These are not narrative goals. These are just resources. If you defeat your opponents in the Deal, you double your Stakes (i.e. you win more chips for yourself). That's it. It's really pretty simple. Importantly, the chips are between you and the "bank," not you and your opponents.

    BUT, I have observed people hesitate upon seeing that their opponent is willing to place Stakes given his hand. They MAY opt to spend 1 Chip and fold at this point. This is basically bluffing.

    In my experience it's rare, but it can happen. One or two people have remarked that they like this a lot and would willingly encourage it to happen more often.

    One of my favorites is a larp system I designed using matching. The leader going into the match must reveal a number between 1 and 5, the opposition does as well. An opponent matching the leader wins, while an unmatched leader wins. The twist is, the amount the leader reveals is the base degree of success.

    So implicit in this question is: why no system mechanic for bluffing?

    Because I've seen bluffing in lots of rpg play. It seems a healthy thing.
    In Amber DRPG conventions, you are most often not playing an 'auction' character with public resources. You are instead usually playing a PC with murky to hidden resources and therefore much of play is bluffing to avoid conflict tests. This works because once conflict starts, the attribute reveal is quite clear cut and quick, like showing your cards.

    Even more significant because once two people 'reveal' in a conflict, the attribute resources of other PCs will still be hidden for some later conflict.

    Everyone' s covered bluffing in indie games, now I'll talk about bluffing in traditional rpgs. It's rare, and this may be another reason why few game designers try to make games where you can bluff.

    And by "you", I mean the ordinary player. The DM in old-fashioned D&D bluffs almost all the time, because of all that secret information. But ordinary players find it hard to bluff because they have so little they can keep secret; the best they can do is bluff other non-DM players, by not using the best talents and equipment at their disposal and thus creating a false impression of their full capabilities. This happens frequently (I've done it, and I've seen it in practically every game.) However, it's not very satisfying, which may explain why many recent games are against player character secrecy.

    One significant exception to old-school bluffing was The Fantasy Trip. It started as Melee and Wizard, tactical fantasy board games without a GM. There wasn't much bluffing in Melee as a stand-alone game, but bluffing was built into Wizard; you had to declare things like "I'm casting a Creation spell" or "I'm casting Secret Protection", but you didn't have to identify which spell, and could even opt not to cast the spell. So, you could do things like create an Image of a wolf instead of summoning a real wolf and trick a rival into attacking the Image... or create a hex of shadow, then create something in the shadow, so your opponent doesn't know whether you summoned a demon or created a hex of fire... or didn't create anything at all. When TFT evolved into In The Labyrinth, combat and magic were still handled the same way, so you could bluff the GM.

    I've done the 24 hr RPG challenge once, and the result was a game I entitled "Stakes." Basically I had been thinking about how Dust Devils and other theoretically Poker-based games didn't really allow for Poker play. So Stakes is, basically, actual Poker resolution. The chips you bet are things imporant to the character.

    "I bet my left leg."

    "Oh yeah, I'll see that leg, and raise you my Aunt May!"

    "I call with My Mother! Whatcha got?"

    Otherwise it's just Poker. So, er, bluffing is key, of course.


    A couple of notes here. A "problem" with games like Poker which make bluffing a part of the game is that the optimal strategy is to act randomly. In such a way as people can't predict from your other behavior what you're doing. Anyone who tells you otherwise doesn't understand the Game Theory behind games like Poker.

    This actually doesn't leave a lot of strategy to the game. It's an interesting addition, I think, especially as a sub-game in a more complex overall game. But alone it's not a lot to think about. In Stakes, as Joshua notes, since the currency is fictional, and representative of in-game things, the complexity comes from deciding what to wager, and if people think you'd wager those things on a bluff. These can be the "tells."

    Still not really intense, but as good or better than Poker with money in terms of complexity (where each dollar is precisely as valuable as the last).

    As for bluffing and dice, you could have dice pools that you commit. That leaves things uncertain, but, then again, since these resources are hidden, the situation is already uncertain. I think it's non-problematic.


    As for the issue of open communication, Frank, Tim is right here: If it's a sub-game of the overall activity, it'll work fine. Just as playing Poker does. Heck, if the entire game were competitive, then it would work fine as the entire game.

  • talysman
    November 2006

    But the question can then be "why don't we support it more?" Which I meant to go into a little bit:

    1. D&D is the predominate template most games copy, in some way, and D&D has very little bluffing (except DM bluffing players, as i mentioned.)
    2. Because DM bluffs occur in a context of extreme inequality that some people have had bad experiences with, designers challenging the traditional approach to RPGs tend to move towards less secrecy instead of redistributing the power of secrecy.
    3. If a player takes an action that isn't specifically covered by the rules, there's usually referee or group adjudication to verify the action beforehand. In the case of bluffing with character actions, you can't have beforehand adjudication, and gamers seem to fear after the fact adjudication -- they don't want the story to be rewritten by a "do-over".
    4. Simmy systems for magic, combat, or other things are often too clunky for player bluffs. This was a problem with the spell-point system in In The Labyrinth, for example.
