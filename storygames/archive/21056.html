    \n Well, Google+ conversations have mostly devolved into drama and politics as others have already noted. The forums are not that lively these days, but the blogs are still running.

    Against the Wicked City has a cool setting and a bunch of B/X classes.

    Dungeon of Signs has good reviews and play reports. Also a handful of cool adventure locations and player-facing rules for the author's megadungeon, the HMS Apollyon.

    Elfmaids & Octopi has all kinds of d100 tables. Like, for _everything_.

    Goblin Punch has good content in general, sometimes weird, though.

    Monsters and Manuals is sometimes more philosophical. It's run by the author of Yoon-Suin, by the way.

    Necropraxis isn't very active, but Brendan (author of Wonder & Wickedness) has posted some very cool house rules over the years.

    The Nine and Thirty Kingdoms used to provide really good ideas for OD&D specifically (I especially liked the classes).

    \nThe 'justification' was just a section of the Secrets & Answers page following the link you gave:\n

    Every Square is 5 Feet

    The idea that the [3.5e] Player\u2019s Handbook was inviolate, that it was a bedrock you could trust and swear upon, started with character creation but it ran right into game play. Specifically, combat.

    Unlike every previous version of D&D (and I mean every single previous version), 3rd Edition did not require judgment calls just to run a simple melee. You didn\u2019t have to ask the GM whether you could get past the lizard man to attack the chief this round or who your fireball would hit. You could just look at the battle map, count the squares and make your move. You could open your PHB, read a page from the combat chapter, and know exactly what you could do and what to expect.

    If you started with 3rd Edition or later, this may not seem like a big deal. Trust me, it was. Huge. It fundamentally transformed how D&D was played. As a GM, it meant I could set up the situation and then kick back and let the players decide how to tackle it. They didn\u2019t have to ask me what they were allowed to do each round or hope I ruled in their favor.

    Without this fundamental shift, West Marches would not have been possible. Or it would have been a much weaker shadow of itself. Players could never have felt that they were really in control of their own destiny if they had to play \u201cmother may I\u201d in every battle.
    \nI think this offers an interesting counterpoint to the play-style that Eero espouses.

    \n So it does, on a philosophical level (he's basically saying that objective RAW is better than dynamic rulings), although the practical combat procedures do not need to be that different. I mean, we don't play "mother may I" in combat either! The players formulate action declarations and the GM interjects corrections and observations to keep the theater of the mind tidy and clear, which amounts to the same thing on a certain level, but is entirely different as a social dynamic. And when we play with miniatures, it is no less objective than any RAW game, it's just that the rules are accessed as an oral tradition of case law instead of a big book.

    Stephen P said:



    What are some other good map/dungeon resources? Upthread, Dwimmermount (a megadungeon) and Dyson Logos were mentioned, and the One Page Dungeon Contest is an obvious huge repository. What else?

    \n
    If we're going for published products instead of just free online resources, I'd suggest:

    - Barrowmaze. Of the megadungeons, this one is my favourite, strictly based on reading (I haven't run D&D in ages).

    - Rappan Athuk. Another massive megadungeon. Looks pretty good too from some cursory reading, but it's not my thing.

    - Stonehell. Megadugeon. Also doesn't click for me on thematic terms but many people like it.

    - Anomalous Subsurface Environment is extremely good, but too much gonzo for my tastes. But if stuff like Gamma World is your thing, you may like it.

    - The author of Carcosa released 4 books last year that detail the regions bellow the LotFP version. It has more detail than the original. He will cover the same previously published area with another 4 books. I just got my copies today. It's now AD&D-based. I actually prefer this version to the LotFP version for several reasons.

    - Finally, my favourite D&D adventure author is Gabor Lux. He published most of his stuff on Fight On! and Knockspell magazines. His website has a bunch of stuff for free.

    Hope that's useful.
