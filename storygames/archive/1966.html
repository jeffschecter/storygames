I saw that tidbit about "we played X" in the niceness thread that wasn't really about niceness after all, and I figured I'd do my part.

So Wednesday night was the third play session using the latest rules tweaks for Galactic, and they're humming like a finely tuned espresso maker. It's Matt Snyder, John Harper, Chris Chinn and me, over Skype, now using Graham's fucking brilliant dice roller.

We've managed 2 to 2.5 hours at a time, due to life and time zone differences and stuff. I figure that with the short play sessions and the slowerness of online play we've probably done in three what could be done in one in person.

This tweak is drastically different from anything any of you poor volunteers has tried out, and I apologize for your sacrifice. This new stuff is way fun, and maybe it's different enough that if you play again it'll feel like a whole new fun game. Did I mention it's now fun?

As the designy guy, I'm thinking I got something right when every single conflict has one of the players, maybe even me as GM, agonizing over decisions. There's heaps of tension, and several aspects of the game have reached a critical point where lives may be lost and relationships severed.

I guess I should explain what's new: the quest building/resolving thing, for one; and the new split-purpose nature of conflicts.

To complete the game, you have to complete three quests, and as a player you decide what the quests are, sorta like kickers. Like maybe you say, "I need to find the lost shipyards of Fallon-Fe where scientists where engineers millenia ago were developing a powerful prototype starship." And you fill in a couple extra details, like name a location, a character and maybe a faction, along with a kind of cliffhanger starting scene, and the GM preps from there.

Anyone who's played PTA may well have wondered what's up with the nebulous difference between plot scenes and character scenes. In Galactic, each scene is both, and each conflict is both. Winning the plot aspect of a conflict helps you complete the quest, and winning the character aspect of the conflict helps you build loyalty with your crew. You can win or lose each aspect independently, and there's some fun agony involved in where to put the seemingly inadequate handful of dice.

The game also builds off the idea of Budget in PTA, except how the GM earns it is a little different, and there's a bit of strategy involved now in spending it.

So as for the story we maked, there were old rickety space stations, weird underground library complexes filled with traps, a deserted colony world, and a huge alien fortress built into an asteroid. Conflict involved zen-ninja diplomacy, exposing a fraud, escaping manhack-like defenses, a prison break, a perilous leap across open space in an emergency vacc suit, and plenty of trust building and losing.

Hopefully John, Chris and Snyder will chime in. If anyone has any questions about my occasionally vague comments above, fire away (early playtesters, find out how I listened! I really did!). If not, just sit back and wait impatiently, because I'm totally excited to rock with this game.

    Maybe it's only a 63% hit. I could be exaggerating without meaning to. It is definitely slower, although there's something of an adaptation curve going on, I think, where we're getting a little quicker each time. I think the lack of visual cues creates some inertia, making it harder to cut away, start new scenes, etc. Plus Galactic has some heavy switching of roles, and I suspect that adds to the online confusion. We can't all see each other's sheets readily. I keep forgetting who is whose crewmembers, etc. Less trouble with a traditional one-party group? Probably.

    Really, though, Graham's dice roller is a godsend for that game. As much as I liked Adam's mod on the Foundry, I like the interactive sense of seeing the shapes and lining the dice up for comparison. It really feels like it would in person.
