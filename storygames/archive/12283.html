\n So, I'm considering starting up a Halo TSOY game with some friends. I'm going for a gritty, violent tone where all the players are just regular humans and even your average marine is probably more skilled than most. How would you model vehicles for the Covenant like the Ghost and the Banshee.

Does anyone have any prior experience with doing this in the Solar System?

    \n Yeah, TSOY/SOlar System is all about people. It does not scale at all beyond human-centered problems and opportunities, and I wouldn't even try.

    A rare tricked out vehicle is a Secret. A common vehicle is just color, and people who think they are awesome can take a relevant vehicle-centric Key. If vehicles are essential to what everybody does, make one of the character pools the Vehicle Pool.

    \n I don't know Halo myself, so I don't know the nuances that would help in making this decision. Are vehicles something you own, or something that the organization owns? Are they personalized? Do you discard a vehicle and pick up a new one at the drop of a hat? If individual characters have some sort of claim to the vehicle (even if they're not literally the owners), then it seems to me that the equipment rules (whichever alternative you prefer) will cover the vehicle thing admirably. Just let anybody use any vehicle for color purposes, but actually only give mechanical impact to vehicles that are part of a specific character's identity by making them equipment. Perhaps allow an improvised equipment rating for using a vehicle - definitely do circumstance penalties for not having a vehicle when you could use one - of course let players make Effects out of engineering or driving Abilities or such to leverage their mastery of vehicular combat.

    \n TSoY is not the best tool (in my opinion) for any kind of setting where you're trying to simulate something, and stay true to the way things "should look" in play.

    Like, can you see a small [vehicle] defeating a [large, powerful vehicle]? Because that will happen a lot. If it messes with your sense of "how the world works", TSoY could be a poor fit. If you're more concerned with the personal drama, then it's a great fit.

    I could see having some kind of "Systems Diagnostics [R]" Ability, which you roll upon first "booting up" a vehicle, or whenever you sit down at the controls. You turn on the systems, run a diagnostics check, and a successful result becomes an Effect representing one of the vehicle's systems. Roll with penalty dice for damaged vehicles. Big, powerful vehicles have more different systems (Weapons, Shielding, Flight, whatever).

    \n But then, who's really in the business of simulating how stuff really works? I mean to say, I'm not really seeing anything Noah's plan that'd be particularly difficult for TSoY. Something like a smaller vehicle defeating a larger one is exactly what you'd expect of most military drama of adventurous bent; you just need a good spiel of how greater skill and bravery overcomes the ethnically suspect opposition. The game even has procedures for it if you want that particular detail to be ultra-realistic. (That is, don't declare conflicts over things that are not genuinely dramatic conflicts in the sense meant by the mechanics - use GM powers to declare any foregone conclusions, such as how a Banshee always beats a Ghost in combat or whatever. Only put down a conflict when you have stakes, leverage and propriety for it.)

    That said, System Diagnostics (R) is a cool Ability idea for a piloting-focused campaign; helps distinguish a bit between different pilots with different strengths, so a good call in that regard.

    \n I'm a little late to the topic.

    I think Effects partly get you there when modeling vehicles. I used a mix of effects and secrets for mecha.

    http://platonicsolid.blogspot.com/search/label/solar%20system

    I handled scale with, well, scale. They're shifts +- depending whether you're acting against craft higher or larger in the scale spectrum than yourself.

    There are a few rough spots in there that could use some polishing now that I understand the system better but all-in-all, it struck a good balance for my group.
