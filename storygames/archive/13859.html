I'm fairly pleased with the quality of TGC stuff - shiny - the cards are thicker than, say, Monopoly Deal - the paper isn't as nice as Once Upon A Time or Bicycle cards - you can feel the nubs on one side where they were punched out of the sheet, and you can feel the texture of the ink on the cards, but you have to really be searching. And a hell of a lot nicer than what I could do by myself. I'll be using them to do all my card-game-prototyping from here on out.

I expect to see more card games out of all y'all!

    \n My costs were nothing.
    It's on-demand on-demand, I didn't have any extra printed.
    Turnaround time was 9 days, total.
    Their website is very easy to use; I never had to interact with a human.

    One downside is no box.
    And Joe's right about profit. I was kind of shocked to discover they take 50% of your mark-up, if you have one. Seems like a lot for a company that doesn't edit, playtest, market, get your game into retail, or provide any liability, but I don't know much about board game publishing.
    Still, one could use TGC to cheaply & easily get beta version decks out to their playtesters and then switch companies for a final version.

    (Compare and contrast with Superior POD, which I'm having do something else, very murky interface, e-mailing back and forth, couple weeks later no cards, turns out something was wrong with my files and they didn't tell me, still waiting...)

    And Joe's right about profit. I was kind of shocked to discover they take 50% of your mark-up, if you have one. Seems like a lot for a company that doesn't edit, playtest, market, get your game into retail, or provide any liability, but I don't know much about board game publishing.
    \nIf you SOLD this to a house-organ like Studio 2 or Wizard's Attic (now defunct), you'd sell away 60% of the profits if they paid for printing and 40% if you paid for printing.

    Since this is POD, the 50% does feel high, since they offer no marketing and no storage for your product, but then again, they can charge whatever they want, with so few other options for POD customers.

    The gaming "industry" is not about pre-production costs like editing, writing, playtesting, graphics. That's all on you. The gaming "industry" is all about production costs like printing, shipping, and storage. A $50 board game will cost $8-11 per game to print, plus ship, plus store, plus market. If you print 5000 copies of that game, you're looking at not turning a profit, until you've sold 3500 of those. And all of that cost is in the production cycle. Only about 5% of your cost is overhead and other 5% is editing, art, graphics, etc. Which you never pay again, if you re-print the game. Which is why the "industry" loves evergreen products like Munchkin and Gloom and Ticket to Ride.
