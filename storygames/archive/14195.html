\n Let's say I've been thinking about men and machines, and how the introduction of machines can transform society. I've got the skeleton of a cool game that revolves on that axis.

The background is one of these two:


The rise of the British textile industry and the Luddite uprising of 1811
The rise of the nanofactory oligarchs and the Seznam rebellion on Jorca-12

They are the same game, same rules, same everything but color. Which would you be more interested in, and why?

I am a Luddite fanboy, obviously, but I've been dying to write a kickass sci-fi game for a long time. So I am happy with either choice. I'm much, much more interested in why you lean the way you do.

(Also this is just a thought experiment)

    \n As a general rule of thumb, I don't think any piece of media should be science fiction unless it has an actual reason to be. That means not just wrapping a particular historical event in science fiction clothing: science fiction has its own sense of being, it's own messages about humanity and the future and our place in it, and you shouldn't take on that unless you specifically want/have to.

    For instance, Bliss Stage is a game about teenagers in war and their relationships. It's not about, say, the Viet Minh, despite the fact that it could be because it has things to communicate about your life, about media, and about the relationship of children and their trauma to the future of society that couldn't be conveyed in a historical context.

    \n Jason,
    I vote for 1811. The biggest reason is the image of farmboys following their fathers into the city to get factory jobs, having lost their ancestral land a century ago and now forced off the landlords' land and into the mills.
    Farm foreclosures, people forced to get dangerous and low-paying jobs, rich oligarchs grabbing up all the land... there's a great many parallels, rough or otherwise, to our own times.
    Slavery is also an interesting theme, something legal in Britain for another 22 years (1833). Historically (at least in the US), white wage employees were often told they could be replaced with slaves (or free blacks, after 1865), putting them at odds with people in a situation very similar to their own.
    Capturing that sense of dispossession will be critical, I think, to conveying just how terrifying and unbalancing powerful new technologies were - small-time weavers and textile artisans were put out of business in the thousands by more powerful, productive firms that had hundreds of employees working side-by-side.This plays into the theme of overcrowding and the growth of mass society, soon former artisans (a sizeable, if poor, middle class in Britain in the 1700s) were forced into similar states of mass labor.

    \n I really appreciate all the comments. This mental exercise is really predicated on my experience with Grey Ranks, a game that is well regarded and rarely played. So as my thoughts drift to another fascinating and tragic moment in history, I wonder how to shape that into a game that will be well regarded and played a lot.

    I definitely see the "common language" all around me - almost anyone (non gamers included) can effortlessly slot into fantasy or sci-fi tropes at the table, but history panic (excellent term, thanks Rob) is sort of universal (non gamers, interestingly, excluded).

    I suspect the "common language" level of engagement with sci-fi is just a convenient gloss rather than a thoughtful use of what the form has to offer, as Ben suggests. I'm not sure that's a bad thing.

    A game that neatly sidesteps history panic is Montsegur 1244, but it is about guys with swords and a religion that might as well be fantasy.

    So a related question - let's say somebody makes a game that is about the Luddites, and is stuffed with Regency goodness, but has an appendix that kitbashes the uprising to some fantasy or sci fi alternate setting. Does this cheapen or weaken the original intent? My gut feeling is that it does, but I've seen this impulse play out anyway after publication (like a Grey Ranks/Terminator mashup, or Jedi in the Vineyard), so why not provide that critical distance for those who prefer it?

    \n Jason-

    My strong inclination is towards an SF setting, for a couple of reasons. The first is the historical anxiety that everyone's been talking about. Your invocation of Grey Ranks is insightful: I own it but have never managed to get enough people interested to play. I suspect you might encounter a similar problem here, with the mix of very specific historical context and the foregone conclusion that the Luddites utterly failed in whatever they thought their mission actually was.

    An alternate/SF setting does a couple of things: it gives players the possibility of exploring different outcomes that would feel much more organic than twisting our actual history into knots, and it also provides the players the possibility of much greater identification. I suspect it would suck to lose your factory job and be unable to try and get another because of illiteracy and the inability to move freely, but those are conditions I am not familiar with, nor, I suspect, are most of your literate, white, White Collar audience. An SF-ish setting can take those historical conditions and transpose them over an information economy that the players are probably much more familiar with from their own lives. Any game dealing with manufacturing would essentially qualify as fantasy to any of the Story Gamer types I know, they all have information economy and service economy jobs.

    I do approve of your continued fascination with lost causes either way, though.

    \nThere are a couple of reasons why, in the particular case you've given us, I favor the historical setting. First, I think the "Seznam rebellion on Jorca-12" sounds fake. Being afraid of watching your children starve is way more sensible than being afraid of abstract technology. So, while your game might explore exactly the same human issues in either setting, it seems like it could do more good by keeping it real. (But I guess the trade-off of not reaching as many consumers is a valid concern.)

    \nMaybe, maybe not. It depends on what this person's goal is: do they want people to play a historical game, or do they want people to play a game which explores these historical concepts? How important is it that the players learn about London in 1811? Is it more important than the players roleplaying what it's like to be left holding the short end of the industrialization stick?

    If it's all about Learning Y'all Some History, then yeah: an appendix that says "Okay, fuck learning some history, here's how you can make it a science fiction game with the same premise" is going to dilute the original intent. But if it's more about having the experience and less about learning the specific historical trappings of it all, then why not avoid History Panic and having to figure out a way to quickly teach newbies all about 1811 London?

    In fact, in that case it might make MORE sense to use some fantasy or science fiction setting and stick in an appendix or some sidebars to reveal that it's actually the same setup as a game about the Luddites and all this Regency stuff: that's fun stuff, and says to the reader, "these historical facts are a creative resource for you: you don't need to know them or use them when you play, but they're so cool that you'll probably want to."

    \n If my character is going to have limited ability to change events (the rebellion is going to play out in a pre-determined way, in a pre-determined place), I'd rather play historical.     If my character will have more autonomy, my interest in the game is less about my character's response to events than to the shaping of those events. So my history panic rises, my concern about emotional truth ebbs, and I start wanting the imaginary setting.

    \n Get out of my brain. Seriously. This is on topic with like, every piece of media I consumed yesterday.

    I was just reading this article yesterday on the guy who wrote Kurt Vonnegut's biography. The gist was that Kurt was working on this war tale for 20 years, and he didn't think it would be really all that relevant to the audience that far removed from the war. But...

    Then two weeks before Slaughterhouse-Five arrived in bookstores in early March 1969, the North Vietnamese attacked Saigon and more than one hundred South Vietnamese towns and military targets, killing approximately one hundred Americans in the first fifteen hours. During the first week of March, 453 Americans died, the highest losses in nearly a year. All 10,000 copies of the first edition of Slaughterhouse-Five sold out almost immediately, and the novel that Vonnegut had complained, "reads like a telegram" reached number one on the New York Times Best Seller list.

    This seems totally related to your question. You're worried about writing something historical that people might not play, and you might fall back on sci-fi to make it more accessible. I have a few thoughts.

    1 - combine the two Vonnegut style
    2 - make it impactful and relevant to current events, Battlestar galactica style, if you go with the sci-fi version (sci-fi commentary on historical tragedies seems more impactful to me if it becomes tied to a place in the present as well, since its already tied to a place in the past)
    3 - make the Regency setting equally as accessible by tying it to current events/feelings as well!

    I'm torn on which I like better. The Regency setting sounds just as awesome and alien as Jorca-12. Historical settings and sci-fi settings are so similar in my RPG mind. Part of me wants to tell you to do sci-fi though, because we need more quality sci-fi. And I watched this William Gibson interview movie (No Maps for These Territories) last night that's got me rollin on ideas with cyborgs again. But that's just my biased opinion.
