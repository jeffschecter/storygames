\n I've had a crack at a superheroes hack for Apocalypse World, which turned into a real self-anaylis about what I want from certain games and what can be left out of them. Since all my play experience of the AW engine comes from Monsterhearts, that was a heavy influence on my assumptions, but after a few false starts that were more soaperhero than superhero (too much emphasis on the interpersonal relationship of the PCs and NPCs) I went back to basics.

First of all, I asked myself what I'd want to do in a supers games and the immediate answer was, "Use my super powers!" That suggested taking an approach more like Dungeon World, which is an adventure with characters rather than character driven adventure. So, I wanted a base set of moves that were vehicles for using powers: fighting bad guys, saving victims, the whole cliche.

The very next stumbling block I hit was coming up with a set of moves and stats that zipped together neatly: lots of stats suggested themselves, like strength, speed and agility or truth, mercy and justice, but none of them really seemed to suit the moves I wanted exactly, being either too restrictive or too vague. That produced my real light bulb moment: what if I didn't have stats? All the heroes would be doing pretty much the same things, the differnces between them would come largely from their superpowers, so why not say that a basic move was just 'roll' rather than 'roll+stat'? That immediately suggested a basic model for the superpwer moves, that they enable you to roll+something, which in turn suggested a resource called 'power.' The more power you had, the higher your superpower rolls would be!

The power resource quickly became the backbone of the game: backstory choices, like those for the skins in Monsterhearts, provide ways to gain power; special moves are mostly 'roll+power' or 'spend 1 power'; power also replaces experience, so that you when you acquire 5 power, you spend it and get an advance!

There are also some theatrics about the hack that I really like: each hero is based on a 'costume' which is based on two mini-playbooks. One, the origin, gives you the first part of your hero name and a choice of motifs; the other, style, gives you the second part of the name and a choice of colours. So, Captain Frost is a made-in-a-lab type hero with icy powers, who carries a distinctive shield to match his light and dark blue costume, while Doctor Frost is a magician-type, whose costume is a hooded white & silver cloak. The origins and styles alone give 64 basic costumes, but the backstory and special move choices extend the range much further.

A fairly rough draft of the game as it stands is available on my website now: it's called Just Heroes and I'm interested in getting feedback.

    \n I'm still juggling elements of this hack: its a work-in-progress, not a finished game, so a lot of factors are undecided, or the decisions mad change on a daily basis. I started with the assumption that heroes start with zero power, but in the most recent draft, I've changed that 1 power; also, though you advance when you have 5 power, it only costs you 4, so your hero still has 1 power after gaining their advance, which means getting to benefit from it right away.

    I've also made a couple of 'complete costumes', one page versions of the playbooks that have an origin and style which are linked together: these are the 'Dark Avenger', a street-level, Punisher-type character, and the 'Gadgeteer', an inventor who can use moves from any style by building a gadget or gizmo which does that. The next draft on the site should reflecct all these changes together, plus some detail on the MCs agendas and principles.

    \n Thanks for the feedback; the question "Can you play Spider-man?" tends to be my bench test for any system, since he can be a lot more awkward to reproduce as a top-tier hero.     I actually like the idea that making the superhero of your choice is a challenge, so instead of making a clear template for any hero (well, apart from Superman, who is clearly [-]+[superlative]) I've mixed cool powers from a variety fo heroes together in most of the style-books. I want there to be discussions and critiques over what combination of playbooks best emulates Cyclops, the Green Lantern or Mr. Fantastic. :-D

    \n Thanks for the feedback JD; I've sent you the latest draft of the hack, but there's still a long way to go and plenty of room for more Origins and Styles.     \n Feedback for James! (Sent originally to him, he asked me to post it here so others could discuss.)

    Played a one-shot of Just Heroes last night. Three of us really dug it with some reservations, one of us not so much. That's better odds than I've gotten with Monsterhearts, so take heart. I like the mix-and-match playbooks, I like the way moves get colored, I like the way weaknesses are built into the origins (the Suit should be able to have problems with alcoholism, though), and our superhero tale was a good popcorn action romp with some tender moments, tough choices, and dark portents. Captain Flicker, secret government speedster on a tight leash who longed to be free, was a great character, as was Girl Winter, 15 year old ice-powered crimefighter in powder-blue and pink ... and Flicker's sidekick.

    We played it pretty much as written, traits and all. One hack I did was I cut the complete costumes in half because I thought somebody might want to play an Avenging Shadow or a Project Gun-guy but nobody ended up using them anyway.

    Big pile of negative but hopefully constructive criticism follows - don't you dare think this means you should give up on the game - I'd rather you publish it as is than think that.

    We intended to play it four-color silver age, but it didn't quite feel that way in the end. Sev thinks because it's built on Apocalypse World - Apocalypse World, with all its mixed results and failed rolls, is a great system for gritty bad shit happening and heroes who fail their way to eventual pyrrhic victory ... and that's what we ended up seeing. Part of it may be my style as a GM and reader of comic books, but I think part of it is just the AWness. I'd put bystanders in danger and then the heroes would flub their rolls so I'd hurt the bystanders as established ... (as the woman Girl Winter failed to save was carted away in an ambulance, Girl Winter cried a single ice tear that sparkled in the moonlight before it shattered on the pavement...)

    So, something to think about - there's a bunch of silver age games out there. Marvel old & new, With Great Power, Capes, etc. There really isn't a Dark Postmodern Superhero game to do that Dark Knight / Watchmen / Miracleman / Powers stuff that I dig. 'Just Heroes' could be that game - it's already most of the way there because of the mixed results and hard moves carrying over from AW / Monsterhearts. Maybe all it needs is a set of appropriate GM principles...one might be "Barf Forth Harsh Consequences"...another might be "Make Superheroes Seem Real"...

    Maybe you just haven't read enough dark postmodern superhero comics! If you haven't read Dark Knight, Watchmen, Powers, and Alan Moore's run on Swamp Thing you really gotta. Bendis's run on Daredevil - where Daredevil gets outed - is great too. Morrison's run on Doom Patrol was pretty great. Miracleman is awesome but hard to find. Invincible is solid.

    Other feedback:

    In the absence of GM guidance I came up with my own set of GM moves and some advice to myself about the first scene: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1iLEhMicTttGqPNUWwwHRqeaSRheWpu8mk-kVfpgdNNU/edit?usp=sharing

    The biggest sticking point for us was Jay Loomis and his character. A lot of things that might have been a basic move in another game we weren't sure how to handle here. Mr. Meson, not in costume, didn't want to give away his secret identity to his sister and hopeful future girlfriend, so he tried talking down a couple muggers instead of fighting. What move to use there? Seek Aid isn't quite right. We went with Stand Firm. Mr. Meson, a Pulse with physics-forces instead of lightning color, had Force Field, and wanted to use it to stop an escaping car. "But that's for saving bystanders" I said. "But I can obviously do it in the fiction." Again, went with Stand Firm. (Maybe Stand Firm is the fallback Coolness Under Fire equivalent of this game...) Then Mr. Meson wanted to rip the car door off. "But you don't have super strength. That's for Paragon." "But I'm still strong, right? Like, not Superman strong, but Cyclops strong." "Okay - sure - you're strong in the fiction, but don't get the +2 to save bystanders that a paragon does. So. Sure. You rip the door off." Mr. Meson also wanted to fly, but didn't have Flight, so we said he could make big powered jumps. In general, I think Jay felt not-as-super as he hoped to feel, and it turned him off the game. I don't think it means the game is broken, I think it just means some tweaking. I think heroes should probably start with three moves and be allowed to pick at least one of them off a different sheet. I think before play you should talk about what your power is in the fiction, with the general rule that "If the power isn't already a move you can buy, you can just have it, but it doesn't give you any mechanical bonuses."

    We didn't know what Harm & Stealing Yourself meant in the fiction. "So, I've just been shot, and I took harm. Does that mean I'm bleeding? Then I steel myself and I'm fine? So I guess I'm not bleeding, but what does it mean, then?"

    Another thing - and I think there's a thread on Story Games about this somewhere - is that letting you spend fate points on either influencing the rolls or XP is awkward. That's the sort of cool meaningful choice I like when playing a board game but not an RPG. The players hoarded their power for advances and generally seemed less super as a result - and part of why our game may have seemed gritty and high in collateral damage.

    Some of the not-quite-as-super-as-we-expected also might not have been knowing the system. I like the "Use power, you might accidentally kill someone" rule for Fight Foes, but that meant Sev backed off of beating down some villains, missed the roll, and so I said a bystander got shot and was bleeding out. In hindsight we probably would have used Save Bystanders.

    25 power for an advance? (I didn't realize that you could still get XP the old-fashioned way, by using your highlighted stat, here.) That's pretty expensive. With MH and AW people are getting advances all the time; nobody got close to an advance in our session, and they were taking power like candy every chance they could. (Another great moment - Winter Girl failed an internal monologue. So I thought about what to hit her with, and saw she had 'take 1 power when you lie to an adult' so I had the hurt woman say, "I'm not going to make it, am I? I'm dying here." Winter Girl: "No, no! You'll be fine. You'll be okay." "Are you lying?" I asked. "Probably," she said. So I tossed her a power token.) This might be fixed by separating XP from Power.

    Captain Flicker beat down a group of mercenaries and I wasn't sure how much harm they should be able to take. So guidelines on how much harm a single civilian can withstand would be cool. In hindsight, I think they should have been a small or medium gang but I didn't have my AW book handy. I'd like to see a simplified verison of the AW gang rules built into Just Heroes.

    Don't know how I feel about the Style having a specific stat for Fight Foes, which seems like one of the most important moves (though we really didn't use it that much) - seems like it would encourage munchkining. "Don't play a Kid Titan, you'll suck!" Wouldn't be a problem with most of the players I usually play with though.

    It doesn't seem like there's that much juice in the "I like X because..." bonds. I tried to bring some more into it by then turning to X and saying, "When did they see you be secretive (or whatever)?" and then turning back and asking, "And why do you (not) like that?" It's probably fine for silver age popcorn movie adventure, though. Just not enough for Watchmen.


    Anyhow, thanks so much for a fun evening and making this game so I didn't have to.

    \nAnd here's the response I sent to JD for his creative and constructive feedback:

    I'm glad you got a chance to play it and have some fun with it! The feedback is also very useful and chimes in with some of the things i have been thinking.

    You're right about the AWness bleeding through into the style of game: to a certain extent, the power points are intended to offset that, allowing players to spend points to get the additional levels of success they really need, but making it more intentionally Watchmen-like is tempting.... yeah, I'm probably going to do that, which also clears up a number of other issues. (Thinking on that now: you could actually flavour the game very easily by having different consequences for the moves in different comic book eras... a sort of easy plug and play to create the style and tone your group wants.)

    I've been planning to replace seek aid with a more simple and direct persuasion-like move: the only thing that's been holding me back from this is that I can't think what to call it!

    I've also been wondering just how broad to make the style moves: the trademark of AW is to have very focused, precise moves that do exactly what they say on the page, but as you noticed in your session, there are obvious consequences of some powers, e.g. if you have a Force Field that can save bystanders, then why can't you use it to stop a car? I guess you can just say "Your power doesn't work like that," but as noted, that makes the heroes feel less powerful than they should be. I've been really tempted to double the number of moves in each style and start PCs with more of them, but I'm not sure that really solves the problem, just reduces the number of situations where it occurs. There are two other ways I could tackle it:
    - Spend Power: players could spend power to use a move they don't have... only that's kind of like what they can already spend power to do, in most situations, e.g. the Force Field gives a +2 bonus to save bystanders but you want to use it in place of stand firm to stop the foes from escaping. Is it worth having a rule that says 'Spend 1 power to use a style move you have in an different way' or should I expand upon the idea of spending power 1-for-1 for +1 to any move? Should I make players justify how they spend their points? E.g. "I'm spending 2 power for +2 to this stand firm roll, because I'm using my Force Field."
    - Create the World: alternatively, as part of setting up the game, is it worth having a talk about the expectations of play? For example, supers could just be naturally stronger, faster, tougher, etc, than ordinary humans and so can bring that into the fiction when required, but it doesn't give them a mechanical bonus. This is sort of like the questions that come up about playbooks in MH, like what happens to vampires in sunlight and can ghosts be seen by everybody or just the select few?
    Harm is still one of the areas I need to look at: I really like the idea of the heroes trading building-demolishing blows with the villains and then shrugging it off, but yeah, it maybe needs to be called something other than 'harm' and described in such a way. One thought is that it doesn't really become actual harm until the end of the scene you're in, but that feels a bit vague for an AW based game.

    I definitely need to clarify the XP and Advancement rules: it's still 5XP to advance and you still mark experience when you roll your highlighted stat. The 'Spend 5 power to mark experience' rule is an optional extra, but I'll drop that if it's encouraging players to hoard their power rather than spend it.

    The other bits you mentioned (harm for bystanders, gangs, fight foes and the character traits with likes & dislikes) are all very much part of the 'work-in-progress': I'm still wrestling with all of these to get them into a form I like, with the whole business of fighting foes being the biggest headache. It's a knotty problem to unravel: for example, if Superman every fought Lex Luthor, Lex would be mincemeat in a split second, but the fiction stops that from happening: so how do you stop the PCs from just going out and pasting their biggest foes? Using the fiction and the rules to stop them makes it a silver age game... on the other hand, if you don't stop them, it soon degenerates into a vigilante game where the heroes are constantly on the run from the authorities, all the time. I don't mind if the heroes are fallible, but I don't want to give them the option to become villains as a more effective way of upholding justice.

    You are totally right about the munchkinism: I've been trying to ignore that problem, but it needs addressing, so fight foes will probably be reinstated as a basic move, with the style moves giving PCs special options to use with that move, like tags, etc.

    Thanks once again for playing the game: the feedback you gave is exactly the sort needed to get it into shape and focus it on what it should be about. Cheers! :-D

    \n Yes, thanks for that too: I think I've almost hit the limit for origins now and I don't intend to add a lot more for fear of diluting the significance of the ones that are already there.

    The thing I like about the two books structure is that players can debate which combinations make the best representation of particular comic book heroes, but there are no right or wrong answers. For example, one person might see Batman as the Gadgeteer, whereas to another he might best be emulated by the Adept+Shadow combo, or even the Dark Avenger. No choice is ever going to be perfect, however, and I've got no interest in trying to nail down every possibility represented by every superbeing ever to appear in any medium, so I'm happy if the process of choosing an Origin and Style leaves some questions unanswered. As I've recently been reminded, one of the principles of AW is to find out through play: a lot of the design problems I've had with this hack stem from trying to make a Silver Age-style setting fit into a Dark Age-style system. I think if I can get the basics right, then the fiction should follow honestly from the principles, without needing to be pre-loaded with instructions on how the game 'should' be played.

    \n I totally groove on the idea of Superman killing Lex Luthor, btw. Old-school superhero comics don't let anybody die or the status quo to change in any way - more modern comics are less afraid of change. Kind of like TV.

    On the harm front, maybe a move like this:
    When your character is harmed, mark harm, and tell us how and what that means for your character, knowing that the harm may be temporary if you Steel Yourself.
