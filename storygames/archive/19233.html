Literary Conflict: Man vs Nature in Roleplaying Games

\n
\n
\n

\n

\n
\n Common literary theory boasts that there are essentially a Big Four of conflict in story: Man vs Man, Man vs Nature, Man vs Society, and Man vs Self.

Man vs Man is of course, the easiest to import into a roleplaying game. Man vs Society can be slightly more obscure, but depending on how you want to handle it, it can be imported either through deliberate conflict, or by a motivation-based system, like Spiritual Attributes (The Riddle of Steel) or Beliefs (Burning Wheel). Similarly, Man vs Self can be explored as a theme through those motivation-based reward systems.

But how does one make use of Man vs Nature? Sure, you can be as direct as dropping a grizzly bear in front of the party and making them fight it, but that doesn't come close to capturing the feeling involved, or making that struggle a meaningful one.

For a long while, I've wanted to set up a campaign in such a way that exploration was an important part of the story: Charting uncharted lands, coming across the ruins of lost civilizations, the whole thing. While ruins and dungeons and whatever else are a way to make things interesting, is it possible to make man vs nature itself an interesting and vital aspect of the journey? It seems to largely be either hand-waved away, or worse: devolve into a series of rolls for fatigue or travel speed.

Has anyone had any real breakthroughs on this? Or is it simply something that doesn't translate well to a gaming environment. I suppose it is hard to be cold, starving, soaked and annoyed at the rain when the player in reality is warm, dry, comfortable, and eating potato chips.

    \n Exploration is a harder challenge I think, but games with a heavy survival theme take stabs at the overall Man vs Nature idea.

    Oftentimes The Quiet Year (by Mcdaldno) is full of Man vs Nature (well, Community-vs-Nature, but functionally the same) challenges. The most overt I can think of is the card in fall that involves a natural disaster, but many scarcities and other cards will often lead to nature-based trouble.

    Another that is very explicitly about survival is Ross Cowman's upcoming HVE Water (maybe it's still up and available for beta, I don't remember). While I haven't played it, every indication I've gathered is that it's pretty good at that cold/starving/soaked feel you mention.

    These both include Man vs Nature, and one makes it very central to its experience, but I'd say that they both use MvN as a lens to provide context and danger to more personal stories. In HVE Water, most of your physical adversity comes from Nature, but it's about the Survivor's reflections and interactions-through-memory with their Loved Ones (played by the other people at the table). In Quiet Year, Nature is often just the impetus to trigger intra-community struggles. Neither both start and end with MvN in my opinion, which I think is pretty good reasoning to use MvN: as context and drama spurs.
    Given that these are social games though. I think that as long as there's other characters around to bounce off of and interact with, those will hijack what the game is about.

    On the other hand, MvN might be good for solo or two-player play, where lone individuals against nature can play without other interactions changing what the challenge is. I don't think groups of people against nature are great for delving far into MvN because if they're played like people and heavy scarcity is involved, they'll start bouncing off each other and using MvN as context to fuel MvM conflicts.

    Of course, all this is talking about isolating a game where MvN is overwhelmingly the explored conflict form to the partial exclusion of the others, which needn't be the case. If you're content with it being a vital element of play in the form of context and drama spurs, there are options!

    (also worth a mention is Torchbearer, which does this through the very carefully limited supply capacity and ticking clock of fatigue, which isn't as tangential or superimposed-feeling as, say, D&D's fatigue. I don't think Torchbearer is as predominantly MvN as the others I mentioned, but it's worth tossing out)

    \n Dave,

    Yes, that's exactly how it works. To "traverse" the monster, you have to "defeat" it in combat. After that, you can traverse the terrain anytime you like. (I suppose this is somewhat story-like: in a novel, we might read about the Hobbits sneaking through the dark forest once, but on subsequent trips it just happens.)

    Here's a quote:\n
    Terrain: The monster\u2019s not a monster but a place. Say which stat the characters can use to traverse it. Strength to hack through vines, endurance to cross vast dunes or swim through a tunnel underwater, skill to go along a narrow slippery ledge, perception to navigate a maze, etc. The monster\u2019s damage checkboxes are called traversal checkboxes instead, and it gets two rows: individual traversal and group traversal.

    The monster gets a number of individual traversal checkboxes precisely as it would get damage checkboxes. Then, by default the monster gets twice as many group traversal checkboxes.

    Improved effect: For +2XV, it gets three times as many group traversal checkboxes as individual traversal checkboxes.

    For the players, marking traversal checkboxes means that their characters make progress through the terrain. If a player marks individual traversal checkboxes, that means that her character alone makes progress; if she marks group traversal checkboxes, that means that her character helps the entire group make progress.
    \nYou go round-by-round, trying different tactics, while the terrain makes "attacks" against you. The monster abilities in Storming... are quite varied, so there's a lot of possible ways this can look.

    For example, Vincent lists two different kinds of terrain monsters in the sample adventure. One is a creepy forest, which, if it succeeds in its attacks, it means you make noise and draw attention to yourself. Do that too much and you attract some monsters who wander the forest, and you get into a real fight instead. The other terrain type has noxious fumes and its attack drains your endurance stat if it succeeds.

    I haven't tried it, so I can't say whether it's satisfying or not!

    I wonder . . . what's the literary merit of Man vs Nature?
    \nFor me this is clearly seen in comparing The Hobbit book and movies.

    The book was a war story. Because of nature the protagonists got bored, miserably wet, terribly cold, hungry, depressed, ill, etc. Excitement and heroics did happen, but mostly the story was about continuing despite discomfort and after it is all done there are a few major (mostly distant) changes but mostly life goes back to how it was before (which you can appreciate more now).

    The movies are an adventure story. All those discomforts are overlooked. The quest is a series of exciting moments separated by only minor lulls.

    In other words, Man vs Nature teaches us that fighting the dragon is the least important part of the quest. In fact, someone else will probably do that fighting. Our role in a war is to show up, bear the tedium and discomfort, realize we contribute even when not fighting the dragon, and now and then do something very heroic with our squad--or maybe even to save our squad.

    I think that it will depend on the creative agenda of your play. I can't see how to make Man vs Nature the conflict in focus, and at the same time have as your agenda exploring the characters' motivations and beliefs - what I would term in my head as narrativist play. Mind you, I'm not saying you can't have narrativist play against a backdrop of tumultuous nature - The Climb seems to be about this, from what I understand of the above. But at that point, Man vs Nature isn't the focus - it's colour and setting, interesting to riff off to give the character developments poignancy or irony, but not the essential conflict in question.

    That may just be my myopia. Feel free to point out a way of doing that. But assuming that I'm right, I'll plow ahead and say that then Man vs Nature would seem to fit better into a challengeful play ("gamist"?) where the focus is on a characters forging against the elements. Particularly, I would think where Nature is Big - not just meeting a grizzly bear on the road to the real issue at hand, but a situation where the protagonist (person, group, settlement) is right out of civilization and in the hands of the natural environment in all its awesome power. Like the OP said, journeys (sea voyages!) would work well for this.

    If challengeful play is the thing, then just make sure there are lots of ways of grappling with the problem. Options that the players can choose, even if the situation is pretty desperate and all routes are just degrees of badness.

    If you have a situation where endurance is a focus, I would suggest either eliding the whole segment to a single conflict resolution, or figure out moments to highlight on the journey, and have endurance rolls in between impact the highlighted moments. So if the players do badly at conserving water in the desert, they are more thirsty, desperate, and hallucinatory when they come to the sandstorm. If you stay out fishing in the boat in the cold spray and wind, then when the fish appears your hands are numbed and your reactions slowed.

    drawn-out, slow-paced affairs, a question of endurance and fortitude and the inner struggle.
    \nI think this nicely summarizes the core of Man vs Nature, for me. When forces bigger than you refuse to give you a break, can you stave off despair, keep pushing forward, and still choose and act effectively? Will you ever get through to the "other side" of (relative) comfort, plenty, and rest? If you make it, you will surely have a new perspective on human life, and your own sturdiness and self-reliance. Heck, maybe that's true even if you don't make it, but still give it your all until the very end.

    Has anyone seen The Grey? If not here come ***SPOILERS!***

    There's a scene near the end where an asshole character who is injured, tired, and slowing the group down decides to abandon the struggle. He sits down on a log and stares at the natural beauty of the spot and waits to die. I dunno whether we're "supposed" to view him as a coward and quitter, but to me, he seemed more clear-headed and at peace in that moment than at any other time in the movie.

    I think that's a unique side to Man vs Nature: it's uniquely open to acceptance of defeat. If you fight someone, or society, or yourself, and lose, well, usually that's tragic. But if you fight Nature and lose? Well, maybe it's not crazy to be okay with that. Nature appeals to some of us in ways that Man and Society don't -- it's older and bigger and more majestic than us, it gives life and takes life from all creatures, it's embodied in legend as gods and spirits... it's not hard to say that, in Man vs Nature, Nature should win.

    If I were to roleplay a battle vs Nature, I'd want all these options on the table:
    - fight Nature and win
    - fight Nature till the very end and die proud
    - abandon the fight and die at peace and in awe of Nature

    An aside. We earlier compared the subtlety of bringing man v. nature to play to the subtlety involved in playing horror at the table.     In a horror game, the player getting freaked out at the imaginary scenario is, for most people, fun and exciting in and of itself.     \n (NB:- We're using vs Nature a little atypically here. Technically almost every fantasy RPG has strong vs Nature elements. Going to a dungeon and killing an Owlbear, or wandering Mirkwood killing Giant Spiders is technically vs nature conflict. I'm using it in this post and above as it's been used here. To represent a conflict with nature that's not just fighting monsters.)

    I think escalation is a big key to controlling frustration. Look at Leiningen vs the Ants or Gravity. These are stories that are full of small triumphs. The characters can exult at the achievement of small survival goals even they don't solve the grander problem. In fact I'd say that both of those stories use the triumphs to ratchet up the tension. "Ok Leiningen, the moat stops the ants for awhile, but now they're building a bridge out of their own corpses. What do you do next." Burning Wheel does a great job of this, as do most "fail forward" games.

    As always the GM should watch the mood of the table. Robinson Crusoe offers a good example of the story throwing the character a bone in the form of Friday. Don't be afraid to use the help to ratchet up the tension at the same time that you're using it to relieve frustration. Gravity does a good job of this in several places.

    Making the characters responsible for NPCs is another method that can really help ratchet up the tension. Look at Oregon Trail as an example of this. Watching the settlers you're supposed to be responsible for die one by one can be a heart wrenching - and deeply rewarding- experience. Gravity, again, shows us this.
