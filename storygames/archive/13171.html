\n I've got a setting, but no situation. It's inspired by rules-oriented fantasy like Lost, Deathnote, Highlander, and Inception. Stories that present a set of rules, then follows characters as they explore the boundaries of those rules. The setting closely resembles our modern world with some notable twists.

First, all nations are monarchies, with varying ranges of oppression. A handful really severe third-world dictatorships exist, but most nations are quite livable. All the trappings of first-world modern society exist, like suits and cars and jobs and the internet.

Second, some people have an ability to rule. That is, they can create a rule like "No one may enter this room without my permission." or "Men must bow in my presence." and the universe obeys, to an extent. It takes a significant amount of willpower to impose and maintain a rule, but it is easier when the rule is worded carefully and specifically.

Third, people who rule are, naturally, Rulers. They are members of a ruling elte. However, the ability to rule is not hereditary. Being in the monarchy does not necessarily mean you can rule, nor vice versa. That is potentially a source of tension. A rule does not need to be public for its effects to take place, which makes navigating the social dynamics of rulers precarious.

Fourth, there are Laws, which supercede most Rules. Laws of gravity, of thermodynamics, etc. You can try to break these laws with rules, but you can usually only do so on small scales. It is far easier to make Rules for human behavior than for physics, but it can be done.

The central "cool thing" of the setting is people finding loopholes or directly trying to break rules. However, that's not enough of a situation to get a story going. I need something compelling for players to do. Any ideas?

    \n Tossed this idea out in a whisper, but it might also find some fruitful discussion in public:

    At first, I was thinking that task resolution would be a generic system and there would be a mini-game for ruling. However, ruling is too big an elephant to relegate to the sidelines like that. So, there is not task resolution per se. Instead of a spectrum of success or failure, there is a spectrum of obedience.

    If you "fail" at a task, it's only because you stumbled into a rule that forbids you from doing that thing. Now you know a little bit about that rule. As a player, you can then start exploring the boundaries of that rule until it is completely known.

    I also wonder about rules you apply to yourself, perhaps called Codes. Things like "I must eat meat with every meal." or "I do not engage in violence." I wonder if those might be a useful source of points to encourage role-play or missions. Codes might be better in play if they are phrased as required actions rather than forbidden actions. It's easier to work "I must make eye contact with nobles as I speak to them" into role-play than "I must not make eye contact with a commoner."

    Posted By: jprussellthe whole notion of rules dating back all through time and across the while world strikes me as too much disbelief to swallow for allowing a credible modern(ish) world.
    \nI agree, so I actually may leave this aspect of the setting an open question. The Laws are the Laws, and we move on from there.

    As for how long a rule "lives," I'm considering something like this:

    1. A rule ends when its ruler is can no longer devote enough willpower to enforce it. That might happen in deep sleep, unconsciousness or severe stress.
    2. This restriction can be eased if the rule is written down. However, if the paper it's written on is destroyed, then only the ruler is keeping that rule enforced. That is why many rulers have their rules literally written in stone. (Or metal, or etched in diamond, etc.)
    3. Regardless, the death of a ruler ends any rules they have in place.

    I think allegiance can play a part in this, too. Say I'm a monarch and I have imposed a rule: "All men in the kingdom must keep their hair uncut." That's a tough one to impose unless most men in the kingdom have some stake in keeping that rule alive.

    \n OK, here are my thoughts:

    I could see a system like magic in Mortal Coil working for establishing rules - this way PCs can use create a limited number of rules of varying power. I think the more clauses in the rule, the more powerful but limited (focused if you will) it should be. Broad rules are easily superseded by exception rules made by other people.

    Given the limitations of rules, if something important were at stake, many rulers might work together to make it so. Say there were a doomsday device. Part of the rulers' status might come from the fact that they all hold a single rule in place to protect it - the effect being a massive and impenetrable web of redundant rules that prevent its use.

    As for mechanics, check out the board game Yspahan. It has a dice mechanic where a player rolls all the dice, places all the lowest number rolled at the bottom of a scale, all the highest at the top on the scale, and then work up from the bottom. The effect is that the next-to-highest spot on the scale only gets dice if all numbers are rolled. In Yspahan's case there are, I think, 8d6 spread across a 6-point scale each round(e.g. 111, 2, 4 4, nothing, nothing, 5 5). Players then choose all the dice from one point on the scale and the number of dice equals the number of times you can take associated actions. I could see something working like this where you, get this, roll 15d12 and place them up against a, get this, 12 inch ruler. You could then play with the number of dice in the 12 slot, the number of slots with dice in them, etc. to determine the power of a rule, the number of clauses, number of words, duration, etc.

    What if rulers were excluded from the nobility and the people relied on the nobility to mediate with the rulers (the people are afraid of the rulers and the nobles got to be nobles via a tradition of being good negotiators).

    As for the rules, read Preacher, a comic where the protagonist has the "word of god" and his words are always true or come true or have to happen. See places where his words are taken too literally - think djinn.

    What happens if two people make contradictory rules at the same time? Would this rend a hole in space-time? Is this a horrific idea or a common tactic to create wormholes?

    \n I put this in a whisper but was asked to share so here goes:

    I have been thinking about your idea for multiple colored dice in conjunction with the difficulty of ruling. What I came up with is this: (very basic) when you want to create a rule you must decide on the rules length (how long it will be active), breadth (how far reaching the rule will be), intended target (one person, many people, a certain subset of people). Each level of the rule does two things 1) it modifies difficulty, 2) it determines the color of dice that you use. How many words your rule is and your word choices will also modify the difficulty. Your skill at ruling will determine how many dice you roll and you must take at least one of each of the corrisponding colors. You can never take more than one more of any particular color more than the other colors.

    Advancing your ruling skill would require that you have rolled a number of each of the colors of dice equal to your curent skill. Doing so means that players will be looking for oppertunities to make rulings of varying difficulty and scale. This does not take into account success or failure but simply attemts. The system could be applied to all skills (exploring rules, breaking rules, remembering rules, ect) all that would need to be changed is the parameters to better fit what the character is doing.

    Let me know what you think!

    \n Ran a quick scenario with my wife last night using the following system (which I've been tinkering on for other projects, but used here as a simple experiment):\n
    You have 4d6. Two dice are red. Two dice are blue.
    On your turn, state your character's action, then roll all four dice.

    Only keep two of those dice, to create a sum of 2-12.
    A lower result means that your action is not allowed according to the rules in this place.
    A higher result means that your action is allowed, according to the rules in this place.
    Either way, you may want to make note of the rules as you discover them.
    Use caution. Trying to disobey the rules once or twice may be excused as ignorance, but frequent attempts are signs of aggressive disobedience.

    As for the colors...
    If you keep BB, that means your employer is very pleased about this rule.
    If you keep BR (where B is greater than R), your employer is somewhat pleased about this rule.
    If you keep a tied BR, your employer has no interest in this rule.
    If you keep RB (where R is greater than , your employer is somewhat displeased about this rule.
    If you keep RR, that means your employer is very displeased about this rule.
    \nMy wife Megan played a cleric, out in the DMZ that we've been discussing earlier in the thread. She is employed by a king to map the rules of this region for future conquest. The cleric had no stats because I'm not interested in stats at this point, just the system and choices.\n
    GM: You walk along a gravel road until you come upon an old stone fortress nestled in the brambles. What do you do?

    Megan: I approach the fortress openly, waving my right arm.

    Roll. B4 B5 R2 R6. She keeps BB, for a total of 9. This is allowed and this pleases her employer.

    GM: You are allowed to approach and are greeted\u2026 well, ignored\u2026 by a man who has been laying in the grass. He seems to be enjoying the afternoon sun.

    Megan: I step towards this man, again showing with right arm exposed and obviously not wielding a weapon. "Excuse me, sir. Do you know the name of this fortress?"

    Roll. B1 B5 R3 R6. She keeps BB, for a total of 6. This is allowed and this pleases her employer.

    GM: The man pays little notice to you and responds in a gruff, smelly voice: "Who's askin'?"

    Megan: I respond, "I am a traveling storyteller, singing of my travels through this region and sharing the stories."

    Roll. B1 B4 R5 R6. She keeps BB, for a total of 4. This is a pretty low result, but not too bad. Something she just said or did is a minor infraction, but she still wants to please her employer.

    GM: The man stands straight up at the mention of "storyteller." He shakes the grass off his shoulders, but never breaks eye contact. He says: "Listen, friend. I don't know where you come from but we don't do no singin' nor tellin' of no tales inside those walls. Now, my memory's bad, so lemme ask again. Who are you?"

    Megan: I respond, "Did I say storyteller? Ha! Sorry, my English isn't so good. What's the word\u2026 ah\u2026 Journalist. Yes. I am a journalist, documenting current events around this region for the good of all unaligned townsfolk. I see you have no banner on your fortress, so surely you're in need of some new news from beyond your city limits."

    Roll. B1 B1 R6 R6. She keeps RR, for a total of 12. This will greatly displease her employer, but Megan can do her employer no good if she is thrown into jail.

    GM: The man wipes the dirt from his eyes with a clean patch of his sleeve. "Ah, a journalist! Yes, we have such need of your services!" He escorts you into the fortress. "Tell me, what news comes from the war in Shiloh? Whatever happened to the Geldish baby trapped in the well? What are the latest fashions of the north\u2026"
    \nThe way this scenario turned out, it was definitely more about culture shock and navigating unknown rules of conduct. The slightest action could be gravely offensive or highly lauded. That sort of game reminds me of this Cracked article on Keqi:

    "[...]a sort of system of being polite. But it's less about being polite to people because you don't want to hurt their feelings and more about being polite so you don't look bad. Keqi overload can lead to ridiculous situations where you end up going to a restaurant nobody wanted to go to because nobody wanted to look selfish by going to the restaurant they wanted if nobody else wanted it. So you go to a restaurant everyone hates to satisfy everyone."

    That's a kind of fun, because you're not just discovering things about the region you're in, but you're also discovering things that your employer likes or dislikes. With no prep at all, you can randomly generate a town and a personality for your employer at the same time, through play.

    Of course, this scenario had no active ruling taking place. That's probably the next step.

    \n So, I think I have a handle on how to design a game based on exploring the DMZ, but that kind of seems like well-tread ground. Either it's about exploring new places and getting trouble, which is somewhat D&Dish, or about first contact and culture shock, which seems covered by Sign-In Stranger and Shock. That's not a problem in and of itself, though. The DMZ seems like the place where players can find the most room to set up new rules, stake their own claims, and have an open sandbox.

    Still, I'd like to see some way to play within the existing courts and kingdoms somehow. My gut says not to turn to randomized systems, at least not at first, but instead to create some kind of diplomatic system. Halfway between Baron Munchausen, Dogs in the Vineyard, and Polaris. A way of speaking at the table that flows naturally, but still has the feel of tense formality, with mechanical effects depending on how you the conversation flows.

    \n So over a year later, I think I'm settled on a situation for this setting. The king's sons and daughters are suddenly exiled, cut off from the line of succession, and cast out into the unruled zone between kingdoms.

    How will these pampered royals adapt without modern comforts? Which subjects came along with them? Why did the King exile them? What will they do? Will they build their own kingdom? Will they return to their father's homeland? Will they find allies in an around the kingdom? If they turn against their father, who will survive?

    In short, the whole group is a bunch of Prince Zukos. I can see this an easy avenue to introduce many aspects of the setting: The monarchy, the subjects, ruling, family politics, and exploration of the wildlands.
