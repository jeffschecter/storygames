Every resultant is either a sum or a difference of the co-operant forces; their sum, when their directions are the same -- their difference, when their directions are contrary. Further, every resultant is clearly traceable in its components, because these are homogeneous and commensurable.     \n There's no major difference, but there is definitelly a didactic one.

    The concept of fruitful void tells you not to push 'mechanical' rules into the center of your design.

    The concept of emergence tells you that, given a set of rules, player behaviour is going to tends towards semi-predictable patterns that are themselves not instructed by the rules.


    Emergence is going to be there regardless of what the actual rules are. The fruitful void is going to be there only if the stuff that emerges coincide with the goals of the design.

    \n I think these concepts are related, but pretty different!

    There are many things that are emergent in roleplaying that aren't the "fruitful void." Relationships between the characters are a great example. When play begins, we might have a few ideas about how the characters will interact, but that develops into a rich and complex thing over the course of play. But that's not the fruitful void.

    There can also be emergent things that aren't fruitful. For example, maybe we gradually find out over the course of play that certain mechanical or narrative interactions are really not fun. Say we find a certain loophole using a specific combination of rules that makes conflicts impossible or instantly over. That's emergent but not fruitful.

    The fruitful void is definitely something that is emergent, in that you can't instantly tell what it's going to be like by looking at the rules. You have to play to see how it works and explore it. But it's a pretty specific thing. It's the core of the game, but there's no specific mechanics about it. Morality in Dogs in the Vineyard is a classic example. There's no part of the rules that tells you if your character is a good person or not, of if they've made decisions that are correct. In fact, the rules specifically instruct the GM to NOT make judgments about the character's actions. Instead, the game is about the players and PCs deciding for themselves what the moral course of action is.

    \n I hazard to get into a debate of what "words really mean", but I do think of these things as distinct.

    For me, Emergence is that surprising way that purely mechanical elements can combine to produce something unexpected and rich, so much so that it's baffling that the underlying mechanics could produce such a thing. Digital computers producing interesting art; the idea of 'defensive' or 'offensive' chess play. To a lesser degree, the way that it can become more sensible to talk about a Lego construction as 'a castle'.

    Chess is interesting because there is no space "between the rules" - you can play chess online, using only chess moves to communicate with your opponent, and still 'strategy' comes into existence. The only legitimate things to do in chess are fully described by its mechanics.

    I understand the fruitful void to be about leaving an area of gameplay free-form, more or less.

    Role-playing games are an interesting bird because much of the game's state is not quantitatively encoded. Vague, qualitative ideas such as "your car is going quickly" can influence the quantitative ones, such as how many hit points damage you take when you crash.

    Entirely qualitative elements can interact - like, how is the cop's mood affected when he sees you whizzing by in your stolen vehicle? So, one way to look at the void is that it's a primary source of game state.

    I guess the cop's mood "emerges from the void", but this feels like a different meaning of emerge than I was using above.

    \n I think "fruitful" just means the stuff that emerges from the void is quality. A void from which only crap emerges isn't fruitful.

    The original point of calling the void fruitful was to justify leaving it as a void, rather than filling it in with rules.

    On that front, it seems to me that the most useful terminology here is neither "emergence" nor "fruitful void" but rather "rules vs choices":

    - As a designer, when do you want to tell players exactly what to do, when do you want to tell them to choose between pre-designed options, and when do you want to leave them free to do a great many things which you can't specifically predict?

    - As a player, when do you want specific procedures, when do you want to choose between concrete/finite options, and when do you want to invent your own way forward?

    For that much broader field of "anything that can/does occur when playing an RPG", I think "emergence" is a fine term, whereas "fruitful void" is not.

    \n Designing toward a fruitful void is not the same as designing a complex system and watching emergent behaviors come out as humans interact with the system.

    Designing toward a fruitful void is about intentionally building a gap.

    The gap in Vincent's picture is the fact that the DitV mechanic is intentionally NOT a conflict resolution mechanic - it's a conflict escalation mechanic. Things don't get resolved by invoking the mechanic. Anything that's sort of resolved invokes consequences that lead to further conflict. That's intentional. And that intentionally created gap is a fruitful void. The game gets better because of how the humans interact with that system and the intentional gap that the system leaves.

    Monsterhearts has an intentionally created fruitful void. It tells the characters that their goal is to find love and acceptance. Then it tells the GM to make love hurtful and fleeting and to make acceptance conditional at best. And it only gives the characters tools for hurting each other, not for making each other feel loved or accepted. You can't use a String to heal or aid or comfort someone, only to make them feel worse. So the mechanics and the GM instructions give you nowhere to get the love and acceptance that is your goal - they repeatedly lead you to more pain and more desire for true love and lasting acceptance. That gap is intentional and is there by design. It's not just an accident of emergent behavior. It's the fruitful void at the heart of the game.

    D&D4 has a fruitful void in it's combat system. It has complex systems of movement and attack and position management and resource management that make for engaging combat. And it gives the GM tools and guidance for how to set up a challenging combat with best practices that repeatedly deliver the intended results. The fruitful void is "how are you going to use the tools you've been given to resolve this challenge while using up the least amount of your limited resources as possible?". And it works. Thousands of people struggle for thousands of hours to come up with better and better answers to that question.

    D&D4 arguably does not have a fruitful void with regard to social interactions. The best it gives you is the Skill Challenge rules, which most groups do not seem to find satisfying, as far as I understand. These rules simplify the complex interaction of a social occurrence and resolve them in a heavily mechanical way that involves only minimal creativity or roleplay. So the emergent behavior of many player groups is to just avoid them and get to the good stuff. There's a gap there. But it's not an intentionally created one and it's not one that evokes positive, engaging emergent behaviors that are meaty good stuff for the game. It's not a fruitful void, except in the meta way that it inspires some GMs and designers to look for ways to patch fix the system.

    Yes, the fruitful void is a way of encouraging emergent behavior. In that way, the two terms are related. But no, not all emergent behavior is indicative of a fruitful void.

    \n 1.5 years after I made this thread, I've come to think of all this from another perspective so here is a play of thought. The result we're after is immersion. We can find immersion in different things, like fiction, meaning, structures, group, and setting. The fruitful void is not about creating something, like a story, but to give a certain immersion to the participants. Will, for example, the emergent behavior result in the immersion of the life-decisions of a character (meaning)? I will quote from the link:\n
    In order to attain presence (spatial immersion), you have to do a loop between the role you take and the game world. To be able to gain flow, you will do a task and get constant feedback. If you want immersion, it will be a constant loop between the player's self-awareness about the character and the response from the game world. The skill atom claims the same thing, that a loop is happening. Each time you will have to update your own thinking. Each time you get a response, you will learn something from this loop. And what is an emotional response to learning..?



    When playing a game, you will take on a role and that role will come with a WHAT. The HOW then creates the dynamic between that WHAT and the game world. The game world isn't exactly a world per se but can also be a game board, a discussion or possibly even a theme. I don't know what to call all this, where the interaction takes place, but "game world" suits the purpose at the moment. When you implement your WHAT through HOW into the game world, the response it will give is a WHY. In other words, the response can give you a sensation, give the feeling of competition, enhance your love for destruction, or a combination of those.
    \n
    • When HOW and WHAT are combined in a process, it increases the investment every time the participant goes through it: The WHATs and HOWs in Dogs in the Vineyard is: What's Wrong, Stakes, Dice, Raising and Seeing, Escalation.
      • \n
    • The WHY is the the feedback of when HOW and WHAT interacts with the game world, and should correspond to why the participant wants to participate. In Dogs, the feedback is Fallout.
      • \n
    • Understanding and adopting from the feedback is learning -> release of dopamine\u2014liquid fun.
    \nSo emergence is the result of several components interacting, but in game design the result should ideally be immersion. You create that immersion through the interaction of the components in the system. A component can be a game mechanic but also the participants.

    [edit] Operant Space as a term for "game world" is a good one.
