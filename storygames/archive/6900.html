    \n Graham, I think there are a few things happening. First, remember all that flack about people not playtesting their games enough or releasing first editions that were not really playable as written? I honestly think that has affected people's output. There are a lot of cool games currently in development that are taking a longer development route than ones have in the past, which necessarily means there will be fewer hot new games out at any given moment. Also, realize that, at least in the US, this is the calm before the GenCon storm. Everybody's knuckling up to try to finish ashcans or final version of stuff before August.

    Stuff I know is coming from folks (because I've seen them being playtested at JiffyCon and other places) includes:

    - Julia Ellingboe's The Fisherman's Wife and Murder Ballad Blackjack
    - Emily Care Boss's Sign in Stranger and Under My Skin
    - Nathan Paoletta's Annaliese
    - Shreyas Sampat's Mist-Robed Gate
    - Elizabeth Shoemaker's It's Complicated
    - Brennan Taylor's How We Came to Live Here
    - Kevin Allen Jr.'s Sweet Agatha
    - Judd Karlman's 1st Quest
    - Jeff Lower's Giants
    - Anna Kreider's Thou Art But a Warrior
    - and my game, Geiger Counter

    Almost all of these are currently (or have previously been or will soon be) available in ashcan form, which is a marked difference from development patterns of the past and a clear sign of the influence the Ashcan Front and other folks have had on the small press game development model. Honestly, I'm really excited that people aren't rushing to get these games out and are taking the time to make sure they kick as much ass as possible. I think that's a positive move for indie games overall.

    \n Guy, I think people are ultimately going to be publishing fewer games overall. Aside from folks like Luke, who've built up an entire crew of talented people that help him playtest and release one product a year, I think people are going to be releasing a single product every 2-3 years, or maybe even less frequently. That doesn't take into account the rising number of people being involved in producing games (my list above includes 4-5 first time publishers), but I think that's a significant force in slowing the speed of publishing. Also, from talking a lot with members of the post-Forge crowd and seeing all the local play conventions sprouting up (JiffyCon, GoPlayNW, etc.), I get the sense that the indie roleplaying community is much more connected, person-to-person, than it used to be and, partially as a result, a sizable percentage of folks are spending more of their time happily playing other people's games instead of always playtesting something that they're working on.

    \n Graham, here is a comprehensive list of perspective-changing games that I enjoy:
    Polaris
    My Life With Master (seriously, groundbreaking)
    Primetime Adventures
    Shock: Social Science Fiction
    Beast Hunters
    The Shab-Al-Hiri Roach and Grey Ranks.
    Breaking The Ice.

    Polaris is ground breaking. Negotiation as conflict resolution.

    My Life With Master. Fixed endgame. Very specific story structure. Epilogues. More Than Human and Less Than Human. The way the game addresses player empowerment. The slave/rebellion narrative, as opposed to the hero narrative. Group badguy creation. This is all brilliant and original.

    Primetime Adventures. Simplicity, elegance, focused-yet-flexible. Primetime Adventures is a great game.

    Shock: Social Science Fiction (alternately titled: how to facestab an alien who surrogated your child). Shock has cool pre-game stuff. Mutually inclusive stakes-setting is a brilliant and paradigm-shifting (for me) thought, and yet its such a small tweak to gaming-as-we-know-it. The interesting dynamic that appears over time, where Shock's dice seem to feel more and more like freeform, and less like die mechanics (ask me about it, if you'd like).

    Beast Hunters. The fact that it's a combat game where actually striking the opponent is rare. The salute in and out. The way collaborative competition is addressed in the game. The fact that it is designed as a two player game.

    Jason Morningstar's games in general. Lazer-focused. Often historical. Set scenes. Set characters. Constraint heavy. Focus on how you do something, instead of what you do (in my mind).

    Breaking the Ice. GMless with singularly fixed roles. A game where both "sides" want the same thing. A game about romance. A two player game. Simple brilliance.

    Those are, for my money, amazing and groundbreaking and original games. Though I might say that groundbreaking originality is often stale. There are a lot of games that feel strained to be groundbreaking, because that's the cool thing that kids to do impress Mr. Walmsley. I admit to doing that sometimes.
