  • Adam_Dray
    September 2006
    Only fake ones. Basically, for the simplest setting rules, just give everyone 2 tokens then go around the table in turns. Each player may:

    1. Spend a token to write a new node.
    2. Spend a token to connect two existing nodes with an edge (arrow). Label the edge. The authors of those nodes gain a token each from the bank.
    3. Put an ! after a node or edge he thinks is awesome. The author of that node gains a token from the bank.
    4. Put a X after a node or edge he thinks sucks. Three strikes, and the node is officially out, regardless of how many !'s it has. This has no token cost or gain.

    Do this until you feel your setting is "cooked enough to eat."

    The X's actually cancel the !'s. In fact, if there's already a ! where you want to add an X, just X out the !. If you write a ! where there's already an X, write your ! over the X to cancel it out. In practice, even one X will convince other players not to waste their turn on a "dying" node or edge.

    You have to track who wrote what. The easiest way is to use colored pens (have each player also write their name with their colored pen in one corner, as a "key"). If you don't have 4-5 colored pens, each player has to put his initials next to each new node or edge he draws. This is easy to forget.

    It depends on the group. In my playtests of rules very similar to those above, the settings were on the whacky side. I described the resulting network map as Steve Jackson's Illuminati without the cards. For Verge, that can work, but I put in a bunch of formalized structure to guide the players into creating something a little less crazy and a little more constrained and focused. Formalized structure means more rules and procedure though. What you see above is the super-simplified version.

    I think you could use something like this, in concept, for any genre or setting. Try it for fantasy or modern mystery thriller sometime!

    We're seriously hijacking Ross' thread here, but oh well.

    Mikael, the latest AP report for Verge is from GenCon Indy, and it's in my LJ here. If, by "fully implemented version," you mean a focused, ready-to-play version of the rules with conflict resolution and what-not, that's in the Verge rules link I listed above. I just finished making a lot of updates, but the reward system still needs much tweaking to be coherent with the Narrativist CA and human identity theme it explores.

    The 2d paper should be fine. Really, at any time you can restart the game and just incorporate the items from the last game that are important to you. So what if their nodes are weaker the second time around? The game isn't about kicking ass. But I have only playtested it in brief one-shots, so I don't know how it fares for real in episodic play. That's my next goal: real long-term playtesting. (You can help!)

    The X's are a powerful tool in the beginning. I've rewritten the actual Verge rules so that X's are strikes and three strikes means an element is stricken from the game entirely. Lack of endorsement isn't enough, at least in my playtests. The X is a strong flag to the other players that the thing you made is bogus, no matter how much you like it. Really, all of this is flagging on steroids.
