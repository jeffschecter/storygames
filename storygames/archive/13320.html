\n So... a quick preamble is probably in order here, since this feels like an odd kind of thread to be starting on Story Games, and I only registered here yesterday, so it's pretty fast to be acting weird.

I'm Grymbok (real name Steve, but happy to answer to Grym or Grymbok too). Been reading Story Games on and off for a while (mainly for the Stuff to Watch threads), and been gaming since 1984. These days I have a weekly game with a group of friends - the weekly game has existed in one form or another for 11 of the last 14 years.

Anyway - I'm not really an indie game guy, but I want my games to be more fun so I hang out in places like this and I like to read RPG theory threads in the hopes of finding things which will make my games better at the table. I pick up what is compatible with my style and interests and leave the other stuff alone, basically.

I registered to post here because of the thread about Right to Dream. I'd always assumed that I must have some kind of Creative Agenda prevalent in my play, and since it wasn't either of the other two it must be that one. Long story short - that thread made me realise that actually my play is primarily agendaless zilchplay, and then Peter BB suggested I post about how I have fun with that. So here we are.

What's harder is to know what to say next. I feel a bit like I've been asked to explain the appeal of Britney Spears to a Martian. It's hard to know what are common frames of reference and what aren't.

Let's start with hiking, since that was the analogy in Gregor's post which was my lightbulb moment. Here's the hiking analogy of CAs:\n
Posted By: TeataineHiking: So let's go hiking in the open country. We've never been here, we're exploring. Each step we take reveals a new patch of land, we're going into the unknown. So what's exploration for exploration's sake here? Just running around, each in his own direction, to see if we fall into any holes? Mapping the territory inch by inch, drawing intricate maps? You need to exploresomething, right? If we're doing "double exploration" we still need an object of our exploration. That's why exploration in itself cannot constitute an agenda, in my eyes. "Exploration Squared" is not an agenda.

So what are we exploring? Our own hiking skills, seeing how far we can go or if we can climb that mountain, testing limits, figuring out which shoes work best for what occasions? Step on Up.
Are we exploring what happens to people like us, stuck together out here? Where will our passions drive us, and how will we act in untenable situations? Story Now.
Are we exploring the world, seeing how it works? Or our role as hikers? Or hiking to affirm our friendship? Right to Dream.
\nThis worked for me because my wife and I go hiking sometimes. You know why? Because it's fun, and because we enjoy the sheer exploration of it. We like seeing a nice view, or photographing wildlife. But there's also just something nice about being out in the quiet of the countryside, enjoying the open air. We just plain enjoy hiking for itself.

I'm the same with role-playing. I straight up enjoy the act of role-playing itself. I enjoy getting to inhabit another character for a while (or multiple ones if I'm GMing). To use a shorthand I've used before when talking about this - it's just plain fun pretending to be an Elf.

Now there's a couple of assumptions going on here. We don't sit around once a week having (pretend) elf tea parties, because one of the things about elves (in all their many variations) is that they live exciting lives. But basically that's what we do in our group. We get together, assume our roles, and then storm the castle and save the princess. If we're on form we'll even get a knowing quip to the camera before the credits roll, and then next week we do it all again. And that, for us, is fun. We're definitely not pursuing Story Now - it's pretty classic Story After. Challenges are met by elves, not players, and so it's not Step On Up, generally. And the shared imagined space is generally all made up of cardboard cut-outs that only look convincing from the right angle - there's some degree of Right to Dream in there I'm sure, but no-one in our group has ever stopped a session to complain about lack of genre fidelity (hell, I don't think we've ever conciously tried to emulate a genre, but obviously different systems take us in different directions).

Ultimately, we're just grooving on the basic role-playing. The exploration of character and situation (with a pinch of setting).

Here's an Actual Play side of things. Our current campaign is... well it's really complex. Let's just say it's post-apocalyptic as a shorthand, so some people have tech and others don't. We're in a shanty town outside the walls of a fortress, with a second wall now having sprung up keeping the shanty town safe from the wildlife (dinosaurs, as it happens). Anyway, we want in to the fortress to meet our longer-term goals. Two weeks ago my PC made contact with the local equivalent of the mafia. They say they can help us get in to the fortress, but to prove our worth we had to steal some explosives from one of the guard towers in the shanty town.

So that was our last session. We scouted, we discussed ideas, and we eventually pursued a plan I thought had no chance of success, but sometimes you just have to go with the flow (IC and OOC that one was). So we try to sneak in, it fails (as predicted) but thankfully our point man fell back on my idea of bluffing and that worked out. We then all get in, steal some uniforms, try to avoid the people who should be there, accidentally wake the guard chief, but manage to bluff him out, and then find and rescue two "pleasure women" the guards had kidnapped as well as finding the explosives. Then we snuck out again, and I made sure the women were OK and then released them.

Was that a great session? No, not really, but it was last week's session so it's the one I remember most. Because my play is generally in campaigns I look at it like a TV show. What matters more is whether the series on average is good, rather than whether every single episode is.

Anyway, that's me and my zilchplay, and how I'm happy with it. No idea if I've hit the right beats to explain it - happy to talk more.

    \n Well, I think this is the point where it's useful to draw a distinction between PC goals and shared party goals.

    The party goal is usually going to be something along the lines of "save the village/princess/world". Yes, these are usually met. Again, the social contract for our table is that if the GM is saying "OK the adventure is for you to save the princess", then that should be something we're capable of doing. The expected outcome is success, and if we fail then we've screwed up (plot twists notwithstanding - "the princess is in another castle"). I guess a reasonable analogy would be CSI or another cop show. Most weeks they catch the bad guy, but not always.

    Player goals get less protection. Which I'm sure seems odd to many here. The GM will generally work with the player to offer them a path to get to their goals, but if the player doesn't follow it (for whatever reason) it usually just gets dropped.

    On the topic of obstacles... Well let's do an actual play example. In the first half of the current campaign we were cops on a colony ship leaving Earth. We had general orders (i.e. "stop crime"), but the party goals didn't become apparent until a few sessions in, and indeed it was practically the end of the campaign before it became apparent how the two primary goals we were offered - Stop Morden & Stop Tanya - really played together.

    Anyway, I brought this up to talk about obstacles. Basically these two people were the cause of the main crime problems on the ship. Both could be seen as terrorists with cells behind them. As time went on, their plans progressed, and so the cost of our failure became higher. So - if we'd failed at every stage (or at certain key ones) then we'd have failed the goal completely. But in most cases the obstacles just meant our final victory became more pyrric. I think that's pretty typical for us actually - failed obstacles usually mean success comes at a higher cost.

    I think this is definitely in the arena of Story Now play - what were your intentions for this character, if play had been more in a vein you were interested in?

    Story Now play could come in the form of using play events to run towards choices about your character - situations that give you a chance to push against events of play in ways that matter to you, emotionally and thematically.

    I did something like what you're describing here in a game of Exalted - my character was going to approach the plot from a reactive, emotionally engaged perspective, and I was on a tipping point - would I remain loyal to the Death Lords, or would I leave them behind?

    I would describe such a thing as "addressing Premise through character". And I should definitely add that one instance of Story Now play does not mean you're "just a Narrativist" or anything - CA does not exist to be connected to player identities or types. It describes a group interaction. What I'm saying is that it's possible to be the only person in the group interested in exploring a particular CA, and finding your at-that-time priorities clashing with those of the group.

    For me, I'd say that developing a familiarity with different Agendas means that the type of play one engages in, what kind of priorities one adopts, becomes more about the game you're playing rather than you as an individual. There's a tendency to play in a particular fashion only, when the games one has played are largely of a single type - i.e. all supporting a particular Agenda. There's a sense that one style of play is "correct", because that's what one has encountered.

    \n Yo, so, since we're going back to roots here for a sec, this would be the least derailing time for me, Noted Contrarian (or just Internet Asshole) to weigh in with a non-GNS look at your Creative Agenda.

    Creative Agenda is "all matters of imaginary importance", it's your aesthetic priorities, it's, in short, what you like. It's entirely based in your taste as a gamer. Some people have very narrow (selective?) tastes, they only like a particular kind of thing happening. Some people are promiscuous and they'll enjoy almost anything that comes along.

    So for example, take this discussion of why you didn't like spiked chains:\n
    Posted By: Grymbok
    It's the triple dip of them being a) something that wasn't in TSR D&D, b) something that never appears in the fiction that inspires D&D and c) mostly famous purely because of silly optimisation tactics that pick up purely on the rules properties of the weapon.
    \nThree great reasons. You want your D&D play to be sorta-similar to TSR D&D, to be inspired by a certain set of fictions, and not based "purely on the rules". Under absolutely no circumstances does this tell us anything about G, N, or S orientation of you or your group or your game, because all three of those boxes could contain any of those reasons, and probably do at various times.

    I don't like spiked chains either, here's my reason: "I just don't fucking like spiked chains." In any way is it useful to say that I have an "anti-spiked chainsist" Agenda? No. It's correct, in fact it's more correct than saying I have some kind of standard that the spiked chain violates, but it's not useful. This is why classifications of Creative Agendae are always worthless. When we reach that level we've already reached the level of the tastes of the players. There is no further fruitful way to subdivide it.

    So, what do I urge when there's "creative agenda clash", or, what we called for decades, "when players want different things out of the game". Well, I urge people to work out what they want out of the game, and that can include things just as simple and specific as "I like red-haired NPCs" or "I don't like psychic powers" or something more complicated or top-level-view as "I want to address ethical issues with this character's mind control powers". Then you work out how badly you want it, in other words, how much you're willing to give in and sit with some stuff that you don't like in order to get some stuff you do like. I don't mind spiked chains in a good D&D3 game so long as I don't have to see them all the fucking time. Things like that. Where can you "give", where can you not? And so on.

    Anyway, there you go. tl;dr, GNS is bad, Creative Agenda is good.

    Narrativist play has (and this is not a coincidence, but that's another thread) the best and clearest definition of all three of the supposed "CA families," and it is: "games in which players address Premise during the course of play." Premise is capitalized because it's a very specific bit of jargon from drama theorist Lagos Egri, which I'm hopefully not going to mangle too badly here but here's my take: Premise is a moral statement that's left incomplete so that events in the story can complete it, or move towards completing it. In other words, something that the players - and presumably the characters too, but definitely the players - actually care about, because something in it is at stake, and still in play.

    If play feels like dilemma-rinse-repeat, then whatever moral questions are behind the dilemma clearly either don't interest the players enough, or the dilemmas aren't being found through good drama - that is, through events connecting genuinely to character. Therefore no Premise, therefore no Narr.

If either of those is missing, you're probably looking at techniques or other small stuff, not creative agenda. I'd want to see social reinforcement (positive and negative). I'd want to understand what parts of the fiction/system were most important to the players and how they reinforced these priorities. That's how I identify another group's creative agenda.\n

      \n I think one thing that's critical in Lorrraine's example of play - and is being largely overlooked in the rush to identify a CA - is this\n
      Posted By: LorrraineGames generally work best when for me as a player when we take a goodly amount of time with character creation.       \nI dimly recall a long conversation on this topic several years ago in which the term "aerobic" narrativism was batted around to describe exactly this technical preference. I was then, and remain, unconvinced that this is practically distinguishable from agendaless play for an outside observer, and I do think it is a horribly difficult kind of play to maintain as coherent over time, but it presents an ongoing challenge to the notion of CA existing across a unitary course of play.

      This seems to me evidence that CA - where it is evident at all in a group's play - is evident only by examination of the moments of coherence within the larger context of exploration. I think most play, for most groups, most of the time, does not happen in a state of coherence. It is to one degree or another "zilchy" or what I like to call "parallel play". Coherence emerges at moments of focus when the group is entrained together in the creation of the fiction, but much fiction-creation happens in subsets or individuals and doesn't "collapse" into coherence for the group-as-a-whole.

      The Forgeian style in design, to my mind, has largely been in pursuit of a technical goal - to make coherence happen on a more-or-less ongoing pace in a shorter instance-of-play. This technique preference has overwhelmingly been confused for the Story Now CA it best facilitates; but Story Now can also occur in a more diffuse form as moments of coherence against a much larger backdrop of parallel or agendaless play. Such play often looks like RTD to observers, and also to participants who accept the equivalency between Story Now and hard-focus "anaerobic" play.

      Posted By: Dave HallettI think there's a very interesting disconnection here between the preferences of players like you (and I know there are plenty) and Story Now! as it is generally catered to in RPG designs. They work on the basis that choices reveal character, and that therefore what is need to reveal character in play is a series of relevant moral choices, and design accordingly.

      Obviously, this doesn't work for you, because you like being in Actor stance, and such games often demand a degree of Author stance from the players, etc., blah.
