    \n I suppose this is one of those arguments where one person is playing classical, another is rocking out with some alternative, and still a third is twanging away on a country love song, and each one of them is telling the others that they're playing wrong.
    \n
    \nMore specifically, this is one of those cases where a working definition of what is considered character creation would facilitate conversation.
    \n
    \nBy one definition, character creation involves putting together the mechanical components of character, and fleshing out the fictional bits, especially where they interact with the mechanical bits. By this definition, ZC is completely devoid of character creation, because there are no mechanical components of character.
    \n
    \nBy another definition, character creation is a set of guidelines which, when followed, ends up with a character having been fleshed out to a sufficient state that they can be played. ZC has this, even though those guidelines are completely optional.
    \n
    \nBy a third, character creation can be completely unguided, with the closest thing being the directive "come up with a character/concept".
    \n
    \nI lean toward the first definition, but accept the second as valid for games with no mechanical character bits. The third bit I don't accept as valid for any game in which characters are important.
    \n
    \nNathan, as this is your thread, which, if any, of these three definitions are you working from when you ask your question?

    \n Bleakworlds does this. You start with two dice for all of your attributes: Violence, Delusion and Obsession. To resolve stuff, you roll those dice: the higher number you get, the better the outcome for you.

    But, if the highest number is rolled by more than one die, the attribute increases by one.

    The effect is that the attributes you use are more likely to increase.

    \n In my two longago runs of Revelation Hero each player started with a blank Hero Games character sheet and a pool of points, and wrote down stats and skills as they revealed them in play, to reflect the sort of movie in which yes, when you need a safecracker or someone who speaks Spanish you have that person on the team! It does require players who are expert in Hero System, which is what I had.

    In the first game, the opening shot was of each character's shoes, as they arrived in the conference room to hear why they had been brought together to investigate the missing expedition to the world's deepest cave in Mexico. That was the game with the great telephone exchange. "Dust bin." "DUST BIN? Are you sure!" "Oh, ah. Ash Tray! Ash tray." "Oh! Whew."

    Note that some players are Design-at-Start and others are Design-in-Play, and some games work better for one than the other, so it can be handy to figure that out.

    Also, I've noticed some players (especially immersive) like time to develop a feel for the character before making big decisions, so would like to go through some routine before breaking the routine. Games that start in media res with a big kicker can lead to unhappy outcomes for them.

    \n One of my Game Chef entries, Liquid Crystal, starts off with a blank character sheet that is filled in as you play. Characters don't even start with a name, they have to think of one for themselves while the game is going on. The PCs play learning robots with the capacity to develop personalities of their own, on the condition that they are psychoanalysed after eight hours of operation and if they look like turning into dangerous psychopaths they get their memories and personalities erased and have to start again. As the game develops, so do their four emotions. These start out at zero, and can gain both virtuous and viceful points that increase the robot's abilities but also affect his personality. If by the end of the test period they are mostly well-balanced individuals they avoid the big wipe and reset.

    It's still one of my favourite mini-games, if only for the Cue Cards. See, robots start off with only a simple face made of liquid crystal (hence the title) which can only display the most basic of emotions using emoticon-style images. I included a set of printable flash cards, each with a useful expression on, that characters could hold in front of their own faces to show what emotion they thought their robot was currently displaying. Always loved that idea. Robots that make it through the test period get their own unique hand-crafted face capable of proper human emotions.

    If you poke around the old Game Chef sites you might still be able to find a free download of Liquid Crystal somewhere.

    -Ash
