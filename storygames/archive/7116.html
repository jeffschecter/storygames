\n Now that I decided to visit Die Odyssee the -- oldest? largest? at least, a somehow significant -- German rpg convention for games offsides the main stream -- I'm thinking on what events to prepare.

In contrast to many other German rpg conventions, Die Odyssee has an organized schedule, five slots with fixed times over the weekend, so it's ideal for trying out many different games. This leads to a nice diversity in the program. And I want to contribute to that program.

So, looking to my book shelf, all these great independent, unconventional, or seldomly played games, I find myself in a strange situation. I love these games and how they changed the way of role-playing. I love to play them as a player. I'd love to have more people playing them. However, I cannot get myself up and read them again to prepare myself for the convention.

However, I see the covers and get stories in my head for each of these wonderful settings. My lazyness is about the game mechanics - I'm just not keen to figure them out.

I'm afraid that I'm spoiled by the year-long experience of playing with inspiration cards. It started with Engel and its Arkana system. We were young and innocent and enjoyed this easy way of having fun, so we built a Arkana deck for Mage, for Werewolf, even for Shadowrun. Lately I'm just using the Everway tarot for almost everything (though it works best on fantasy influenced settings).

It's so easy: it does everything I need to run a thrilling story. I use it for campaign and adventure planning, for character creation, for group concept development, for conflict resolution, to add surprise and thrill, and to end boring situations.
Having this tool, I somehow lost the energy to dig in any other systems, at least as a GM. If I can play our story with this, why study new game mechanics?

So, what I really would like to do: play Dogs in the Vineyard, Breaking The Ice, Covenant, Sorcerer, Blue Planet, Nobilis, Blue Rose (just to name a few) -- without the system. Just the setting and the way the story goes.

And of course be honest to the players ("this is just a setting demo -- the game has wonderful game mechanics, with these nice ideas in it, and if you like the setting and want to know more about the system: buy it!")

But: isn't that lame? Cheating? Of any help to the community?

    \n Callan, thanks for your comments and the link to Ron posts (I found now the time to read it). Interesting discussion. However, I think it is more about setting than about the game mechanics, but YMMV.

    Just to clarify my terms:
    - "game mechanics / system" is the rule system
    - "setting" is the universe in which the story happens
    - "role play" is creative act that happens at the table when applying the rules (a little bit vague, since it includes a lot of different experiences like the story that is created, the experience of the characters for themselfes or the interaction between them, but for this discussion, it should work to sum them up).
    - "game experience" is what the player gets when all comes together

    And yes, game mechanics and setting have a huge influence on the role play and the overall game experience. But other factors come from the social, temporal, spatial or media context in which we play. This is why we cannot repeat game experiences. We might get pretty similar experiences by using the same system in the same setting with the same number of players of similar player types over the same medium.

    Chess has the same game mechanics all over the world. For the game experience, setting and roleplay are typically not relevant (minor exceptions: there are themed chess boards, and some players might imaging themselves to be warlords while playing it). And the number of players is fixed to two, which leads to only 1:1 interaction. For Chess lovers, the social context might also be not relevant, because the game can be reduced to moving your chessmen and still is fun (however, there might be Casual Gamers in Chess also who consider the social factor most importantt). So, the game experience of Chess is easier to reproduce.

    To answer your question: I don't think that using the same game mechanics uniformly in all groups would kill roleplay (assuming that the rule system itself does not do it). By the way, having so many different rule systems around is annoying quite annoying if you are more interested in the role play part - learn new rules at every second table.

    I also agree that things are more complicated than they look. (They typically are :-))

    OK, next attempt for clarification:

    The overall game experience at the table is composed by many factors. Some are written in the rpg books, some are not. And for the buying decision (except for factors like 'nice cover' and 'starts with a letter with which I don't have a rpg book yet'), it could be a relevant factor, how much of the written things I need to recreate an enjoyable game experience.

    So, back to the original questions: offering demo plays at conventions. The offer typically includes a combination of a game and a setting. Thinking about the "clients" of these offerings, what do players want?

    If I think of myself, I have several questions in mind:

    If I do not know the setting before:
    - Do I like the setting?
    - Would I / my group come up with interesting stories in this setting?
    - Could I use this setting in my campaign? (e.g., by time travel / planet hopping / ...)

    I do not know the game mechanics / system before:
    - Does the system work for me?
    - Would the system work for my regular group?
    - Could I convert my current campaign to that system?

    If I know both (typically not the case in demo plays, but as GM of a demo, you get those players), questions are different:
    - before play: great, I get the chance to play game X again! Can I re-use my beloved old X character?
    - in play: how does the GM handle this game? Can I learn something for my regular group?
    - If playing an existing character: do I like the way this game changes her?

    So, by offering a demo setting, players could find answers for the first three questions.
    Of course they could also judge the system I use, but by telling them that it is not the system which is bundled with the setting in this book, they should know that they don't get answers to the second three questions.

    And for the players who know the game and want to play their existing character, it depends - if the changes on the character are just on the roleplay side, it should work by converting the characters description to the new system. If they are direct influences by the system on the character description (like in Dogs), it is more tricky and maybe not possible.


    So, after the discussion so far, I'd answer my original questions like this: yes, just setting demos are lame for people who want to learn about the whole game experience. However, it still might be useful for people that are interested in the setting, but not the system, because they convert everything worth playing to their favorite general system anyway.

    Since this group might be not to large, I decided for that given convention, to offer Polaris (original rules), Engel (original rules) and Pendragon (with Everway/Arkana rules). The first two have both an innovative setting /and/ a system that influences the game experience a lot.

    And the Pendragon play is not meant to be a demo at all. It neither uses the rule system from the Chaosium game, nor a typical Arthurian story (a criminal story in Aquae Sulis (Bath), a city that tries to keep their Roman culture even if the Empire on the continent is shattered). However, I called it Pendragon, because I want to invite players with existing Pendragon characters for that play.
