    The thing I liked best about Mage, and that I've never found anywhere else, is the way the central conflict in the game's setting (between Traditions and Technocracy) mirrored much larger intellectual debates in society: between freedom and control, between nature and culture, between The Man and everyone else.

    That's what fueled all the tiresome flamewars, but it also made for a very fun setting, since you could move pretty seamlessly between philosophical debate and shooting folks in the face. That's why I want to run a Technocracy/Men in Black game using Dogs in the Vineyard at some point, because Dogs does that talking/shooting thing really well. The new version of Mage has its charms, but it has almost none of the philosophical tension I mention above.

    The things I didn't like about the rules as written would fill a long post, but I'll just mention the central that were nearly inescapable for me: the mechanics were so vague in so many important places that you had to have a rock-hard understanding of them as a group to make things really work.

    My only remaining idea for a good Mage setting is one where there is no dominant, global paradigm. Ideally this would be a modern setting where the Technocracy ended up losing in a boring, unimportant way, and now every city, state, or even neighbourhood, can have its own operant paradigm of reality, allowing for advantages and disadvantages both to its occupants and the mages who live or work there.

    Obviously, you would want to pair this with some interesting mechanics for both affecting paradigm and interacting with very different magical realities. Most areas with strongly-divergent paradigms would probably be the result of a mage or chantry's efforts, or the result of some particular cultural heritage or background (i.e. homogenous ethnic neighbourhoods). Corporations would probably be involved -- if you can run your factory in that part of Cleveland where there is no friction that's probably going to speed up production, but even better might be getting some of those Golems from the Bronx to work the line.

    For me, the idea that beliefs really matter was one of the most awesome things about Mage, and I'd make this central to the mechanics and the setting. I'd also use a lot of Unknown Armies style hooks.

    For example:

    You know when you send a parcel to someone, you give it to the mail guys, and a few days later it's delivered? Ever think about how it gets there?

    That's the point. Most people only think about sending the parcel, and the other guy getting it, and no-one cares about what happens in between. It might as well be magic...


    ---

    Once upon a time a lot of people believed that if you knew someone's True Name, the name that's written in their soul, it gave you power over that person. Nowadays, hardly anyone believes in that stuff anymore.

    Except, everyone does have a secret name. Ever give anyone your Social Security Number, your credit card number, your bank account details? Your PIN number? Some names are secret, and they hold more power than you think. It's called "identity theft" for a reason...

    Amateur Philosophy: Frame the game to specific highlight interesting philosophical questions/monologues. I actually have no idea how I'd frame/prep for such a scenario...
    You could do it like a philosophical debate-scenario in Nobilis: pick an interesting question, such as: it better to live in a free but chaotic community, or should you give up some freedom for security and reliability?

    Then pick two important NPC's or cabals to represent the two extreme sides of the argument, and something for them to fight over as the stakes. That's the situation that you throw the PC's into. By their actions and alliances, they figure out their solution to the philosophical quandry.

    So in nMage, you could have a city whose magical affairs are ruled by a unruly group of Free Council mages (representing "freedom and anarchy"), which also has a small but growing presence of the Guardians of the Veil, the self-appointed Mage police, who think the town is being mismanaged and they're freaking out the Mundanes way too much. To make the situation more fraught, put some valuable resources in town, and make the NPC's on both sides of the divide fairly ruthless in their pursuit of the valuable Maguffins.
