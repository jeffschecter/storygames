\n It occurred to me that it might be cool to have a game that was based around the use of magic in a contemporary setting, but one in which the actual spells and powers accessible to characters weren't pre-determined part of the rules. Instead, they'd be made-up by players through some sort of consensus-generating process that was an extra pre-gameplay step occurring before character generation.
\n
\nI know you could do something like this quite easily with super-abstract systems like Maelstrom or Universalis, but has anyone ever come across something like this mechanic in a more narrowly thematic or structured game? Or alternatively, has this ever seemed to anyone like a desirable feature for a game to have, apart from me?

    \n Yeah. Mortal Coil.
    \n
    \nFrom memory : in Mortal Coil, you start the game by building the rules of your world, including magic. The game assumes there's going to be magic, though it could just as easily be Harry Potter, Buffy or Supernatural. The game uses no dice or randomizers, instead, you have resources that let you take some actions. Magic coins let you state some rule about magic during the game. The example in the book is a player who's character is being pursued by a vampire. The player spends a coin to declare "vampires can't cross a stream of running water".

    \nTwo responses:
    a) So strip out that mechanic. Done and done.
    b) Player-authored changes to the underlying metaphysics of a setting can be some powerful story mojo, so don't discount this possibility too quickly. The key is to make the cost in player-resources moderate the frequency of player-authorship to what you prefer. At which point, just modify the cost up or down.

    Posted By: lordgoonMy one concern is that it allows the sorts of moves that one makes in Universalis, i.e. spending resources to charge the underlying laws of the gameworld after play begins. I was hoping to have more firmly separated stages of magic design vs. gameplay. Perhaps this is tweakable, though, and since what I really want to do is PLAY a game like this, rather than designing one from scratch, I'll get a copy of the book and check it out.
    \nSeveral respondents have offered ways for you to fix this by limiting changes to the underlying systemduring play. That makes sense, but keep in mind that much of what makes \u201cMagic\u201d cool in Hellblazer or Tolkien is the moments of discovery where we learn what it can and cannot do. Those moments of revelation are what make magic different from, say, technology in a Larry Niven SF story. Some authors treat magic as technology, narrowly defining what it\u2019s capable of. I\u2019m thinking here of Steven R. Donaldson\u2019s two trilogies, or the Harry Potter books. In those books, surprising effects come from using a known spell in an unusual way. That\u2019s very much like D&D magic. I like Hellblazer, myself.
