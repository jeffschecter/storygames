\n Everything related to skin color/race/prejudice is a sensitive subject, so think before posting in this thread. I would especially like to hear from people of color, but don't feel like you have to post (obviously). White people, don't answer yet, please - I'd love to hear from you, too, later, but don't want this thread to be overrun by "us". If this thread dies because nobody feels like answering, that's OK, too.

I'm from one of those Viking countries - Norway, in my case. I've laughed at the responses to Idris Elba playing Heimdall in "Thor" (I think it's a cool thing, and funny because it's unexpected, and I love it, even though I don't care about the movie). I've designed a Viking scenario for use in schools, and made a point out of incorporating people of color & women as main protagonist characters.

Now, let's not discuss historical settings - let's talk about fantasy vikings. If you're playing a game where (for instance) characters have fake-sounding Nordic names, horns on their helmets, keep going berserk all the time, and have a bunch of generic fantasy spells with Viking-ish names tacked on - you're not really going for historical correctness. What would you think of playing a dark-skinned character in such a game? Or meeting dark-skinned NPCs? Would it need a justification, or could it be presented with no further comment?

    \n Okay, I've been asked to explain my terse comment a bit.

    'Vikings' means Scandinavians, which means Northern Europeans, which means white folks. Some African guy is pretty unlikely to simply be there 'with no further comment'. I don't see no black people in Beowulf.

    What we're dealing with here are the insecurities of, essentially, American culture. For example, that's why we're casting these things largely in terms of black-white. That's why it's seen as desirable to throw a black actor into a quintessentially white setting, just to have a symbolic representation of part of the population. Let's turn that around and imagine a film about a tribe of Zulu who just happen to have a white guy amidst them, completely unexplained and uncommented on. WTF?

    We could instead be seeing films, or playing games, that tell the stories of black people (or Asian people, or Aboriginal people, or...), if in fact we wanted to see and hear more about them. We do not, and thus the existence of black faces in white films is in fact predicated on racism and white privilege.

    \n The way I see it is that fantasy stories aren't stories about the history of a particular ethnic group, or a particular nation, they're stories about current, contemporary issues in a fantasy milieu. They're not about historical people, they're about "us".

    Who counts as "us" is, of course, a vexed question, but to my mind, fantasies about a whites-only "us" are fantasies of white supremacy, of the erasure of non-white people.

    So I'd be cool with black vikings. The vikings are, after all, a stand-in for our own society, which is multi-ethnic. How you present that of course is more complex. "Mixed in without comment" seems like the least interesting approach. I'd like to see the black vikings as a distinct clan or moiety, but that would be difficult to do well. What "blackness" signifies on TV shows is pretty problematic, so it would be hard to make a setting in which blackness pointed to a distinct ethnic identity, without that identity being a stereotype.

    Jeff raises a good point though, about why we tend to go to a faux-historical Europe for our fantasy milieu, and not a faux-historical Africa or China or whatever. I guess it's about the way that the dominant culture's traits are "normalised" such that we don't see them. We can project what we like onto fantasy vikings, because they're "us". Fantasy chinese people or africans are not as easy to see as "us", because of the hegemony of the white viewpoint.

    \n (Danish medieval historian speaking)

    Well, in the original post's context of horny-helmets fantasy, I'd say black vikings are cool. Just one or a very few black viking would have a tiresome whiff of tokenism (as droog says), but if you put in a nice hodgepodge of physical types all running around brandishing their axes, fine.

    (Pseudo-)historically, I guess you could base something on Ibn Fadhlan to get an arab or moor, but other than that, whiff of tokenism. However if you want to play with ethnicities, there's plenty of interesting stuff in RL norse history. Lapps and Finns vs. Norse, Slavs and Balts vs. Norse, Inuit vs. Norse, Irish/British/Celts vs. Norse (& germans, french, whatever). Of course none of those are actually dark-skinned, but those have interesting stuff to play with.

    As regards whether we're playing with old stuff or it's really just us in disguise, I'd say both, ideally, if we're doing historical stuff. Hitting McDragon for a burger in your "historical" game gets old pretty quickly, and not a historical sort of old. Doing detailed and tortuously faithful historical simulations also get rather old. Historical gaming has two things going for it, as I see it:

    1) Plundering history for cool stuff. Seriously, there's a world of awesome exotic stuff, plots, gimmicks, macguffins and whatnot, ripe for the taking.

    2) Using the ways in which people and customs were different to reflect upon how they are now. For exploring themes and playing with current issues, this is much more awesome than dressing up 2011 in furry boxers and horny helmets.

    In case you haven't guessed it, I reallt like my Ars Magica.
