    \n Politics gets me good and heated. I come from a working-class background where my father managed to get a good job with a union and my mother didn't, so I tend to be very interested in class-warfare scenarios - but I'm also an escapist, so I look for elements of fantasy or sci fi in everything I play (real stuff hurts too much and no one really pays attention.)
    \n
    \nSpecifically, I tend to read stuff like "Stop Making Sense" and "Impro for Storytellers" when designing. I also pay a lot of attention to Semiotics (big fan of Umberto Eco - I use lots of crap from "Baudolino" even in trad. games) and "small spatial stories" in things like Linda Flower's "The Construction of Negotiated Meaning: A Social Cognitive Theory of Writing."

    \n In the handful of designs I've made, I've been influenced either by "fixing" an existing game type (GLASS) or working within a constraint (Icehouse Game Design Competitions, Game Chefs). Forge Theory also influenced me a lot, in the early days of GLASS design (is that "non-gaming"?)--it got me designing from the perspective of what I wanted to see in play, rather than from a "well, ya gotta have Effect X or Ability Y because you gotta!" perspective. A couple of posters at The Forge, in particular, helped me flip modes and design the possible, rather than the assumed (best example: "middle school drama clubs don't do Peter Pan" = don't include abilities that can't even kind of be simulated in LARP, like flight).

    As far as play, I'm probably most influenced by whatever I happen to be reading at the time. For instance, I was reading a lot of Iain Banks' Culture novels, and that got me jazzed to play sci fi. But I'm most influenced (again) by the games that came out mid- to late-2000s, even if I'm not playing one of them (e.g., opening with Kickers, in media res).

    \n Video games.

    Between the first edition of Dread in 2002, and the second edition in 2007, I worked on the design of a bunch of video games, and it really impacted the way that I think about tabletop gaming. I became much more focused on cutting through all the non-critical setting, the fluffy bits (like the width of a corridor) that don't really deliver emotional weight (though I can see how in a tactical game, that kind of detail would be critical; it's just not something that comes up in the games that I design or play).

    In the same way that I skip many cut scenes because they're emotionless info-dumps, I learned to slice away everything that didn't deliver an emotional experience of some kind: affection, Schadenfreude, amusement, anxiety, jealousy, anticipation, and so on.

    Video game design also changed the way that I approached scenario design: with both Dread and Spite, I zoomed in on key objectives for each 'mission,' with a focus on checking specific items off in each case. This really helped deliver the core experience, particularly during con games, pickup sessions, one-shots with first-time gamers, and so on.

    \n Factory Records.

    Factory were an independent record label based in Manchester, England. They were responsible for Joy Division/New Order and Happy Mondays, among many other less successful bands. Their entire ethos was 'fuck what's commercially sensible, this is punk art, we're going to do what we want'. Co-founder Tony Wilson once said (I paraphrase) 'When someone buys something from you they are entering into a relationship with you. So why not make that object as wonderful as possible? It's sort of a respect to the punter thing'.

    I published Other Worlds in exactly this fashion. I spent a lot of money on the production values, including hiring two editors, getting Storn Cook to do all the art, and Fred Hicks to do the layout. Not to mention spending years writing, testing, and polishing the manuscript in the first place to make sure it was as complete and user-friendly as possible. This is a terrible strategy for commercial success - an unknown designer spends several years and nearly $4k producing a 210 page magnum opus that requires no supplements - but fuck it, the result is that I have a fantastic looking game that I am very proud of and that many other people have enjoyed. But nearly six months in I still haven't quite made my money back despite being a silver best seller on DriveThruRPG. Would I do it all again the same way? Yes, absolutely! Because fuck what's profitable this is art.

    As a little homage I gave Other Worlds its own FAC number (as all Factory Records products had) and hid a few New Order song titles and lyrics in the text itself.

    Posted By: rabaliasAll the management crap I learned on training courses for work, and all the experience that I have from work of managing people, help support my play and GMing. And vice versa.
    \nOoh, that's true! I'm a manager. What I've learned is that a lot of new managers and bad managers don't really trust their staff, or they feel like they have to prove their right to be the manager by always having the right answer and so on. Whereas good managers do trust their staff and feel confident enough in their position to just ask staff what they think and admit when they've made a mistake and someone else is right. So now when I see someone post about uppity players or how 'story design by committee' doesn't work I just think: you're a bad manager, that's why.
