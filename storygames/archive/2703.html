    \n I think the most obvious trend of the 1990s was the embracing of the supplement treadmill as a marketing strategy, and connected with that the embracing of the metaplot similarly.

    I also think the idea of selling rpgs not just to gamers but also to readers is a firmly 1990s trend.

    At the most extreme, we have companies like AEG selling games which are intentionally left incomplete in order to generate future supplement sales.

    Those to me are the key 1990s trends, not every game followed them by any means but they were widespread and had a big (and IMO negative) impact on the hobby.

    Supplement treadmill, metaplot, both as marketing tools. Selling to the collector/reader instead of the gamer.

    \n Games that epitomise the 1990s approach for me:

    Anything OWoD
    L5R
    7th Seas
    Brave New World
    Tribe 8, though it did it well, the approach isn't always bad
    Heavy Gear
    Jovian Chronicles (all the DP9 ones did the supplement/metaplot thing well actually)
    Exalted, which isn't 1990s but which does follow that model

    Essentially, we're talking metaplots, supplements, splats, gamer fiction also becomes big. The use of stories in game books primarily IMO to pull in the reading market.

    In the 1980s, IMO the predominant paradigm was of the rpg book as a tool, a manual for play essentially. Being dry was not an issue, manuals generally are dry. In many ways for me Bushido is the epitome of the 1980s gamebook, everything you need is there and it is clearly laid out but no attempt is made to make it an enjoyable read.

    The noughties, the jury is still out, but I think we're seeing a bit of a combination of the two approaches, the 1980s emphasis on play and the 1990s emphasis on flavour, but that may be wishful thinking on my part.

    Much current design to me is still following a 1990s model which no longer makes any economic sense.

    \n For the physical design and marketing, you started seeing games move from box form to book form. (I recall TSR holding onto the boxed set for longer than other game companies with product lines like Planescape, although FASA did release a box set for their Shadowrun line.) This also moved games out of toy stores to other specialty stores like comic book shops and retail book stores. This book form generally was the standard size hardcover we see today for the core rule books and softcover for the inevitable wave of supplements. I'm thinking that White Wolf started marketing their game through retail bookstores at this time when very few companies were, which may be one factor in how they introduced so many new gamers into the hobby.

    Binding technology was still being perfected, so if you've got a softcover game supplement with pages falling out, it's probably from this decade.

    The interior art was usually in black and white with some color plates, although I recall a Shadowrun supplement that had a third ink color used on a fold-out city map. This didn't work out too well as they used various shades of cyan for the ten plus different neighborhoods they color-coded. The map looked like a big blue blot.

    \n A few things:

    Pretty much since D&D started, supplements have been a vital part of RPG lines. The eighties started with Call of Cthulhu and Champions in 1981, both of which had plenty of supplements. Later major eighties lines included Middle Earth Role-Playing (MERP), James Bond 007, Chill, Marvel Superheroes, and GURPS. Also, the 1980s saw the rise of the Dragonlance series which started the fiction tie-in and metaplot concepts.

    But yeah, the splatbook (i.e. player supplement) and the metaplot (i.e. story told through multiple RPG supplements) were innovations of the nineties. The splatbook was pioneered by, say, the 1985 release of Unearthed Arcana, and the metaplot by Dragonlance. But they became ubiquitous in the nineties.

    And though plenty of eighties games were released as standalone books, the nineties did see the end of boxed sets in favor of the colorful core book. Mechanically, games pretty much standardized on rules-medium (i.e. there were few Aftermaths and few Marvel Superheroes in the nineties) -- with a preponderance of dice pools.

    \n Gloss was also big in the 90s. You saw the big hardcovers with the glossy sheen on the front, you saw gimmick covers like the Werewolf: the Apocalypse claw marks, and you saw the interiors of fatsplats go glossy. In fact, Changeling's interior was full-color and glossy, which I think was a first.

    The gloss was a part of a larger change, the production of books that would be bought for their value as books rather than as game manuals, which has been touched on above. The other elements, mentioned above, are the increased game fiction and the books produced for players rather than just the GM. I think the elaborate world (rather than the expansive world) is also part of this -- contrast the former "new book, new region" model of 80s games to the World of Darkness or Heavy Gear's settings where additional books added additional layers to the the same region.

    Hell, just look at Changeling's book spines: they fit together to make a big scene when they were lined up on a shelf. They were certainly intended to be bought and kept as collections, rather than as functional documents only.

    \n Not to mention Tunnels & Trolls (two mechanics, one for combat, one for everything else, but still) and The Fantasy Trip -- both 70s games, to be sure, but the first briefly had better sales than D&D, and the second was the #2 game behind D&D for a time. I'm thinking Traveller was pretty close to unified mechanics, if not actually there, as well.

    In the 80s, there was also James Bond 007 (originating the "one big table for everything"), and TSR did several other games that had Marvel-Style universal tables -- their Conan game, one of the editions (4th?) of Gamma World, and I think there was another, but can't remember which it was. DC Heroes also did the universal table thing, albeit in a different way. Bushido was skill-based, with a d20 roll for success and degrees of success depending on how well you rolled.

    Like JD, my recollection is that only D&D and its close cousins (I'm tempted to say "ripoffs" -- Arduin and Talislanta, for example, which both started life as third-party D&D supplements) were of the many-mechanic variety. Pretty much anything that was skill-based instead of class-based had a unified system or close to it, even back into the 70s.
