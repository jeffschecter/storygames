You'd think with the billions of dollars that industry rakes in, some coin might slip between the booth babes and the coke parties into the hands of researchers who have somehow managed not to cover both their eyes and ears at the same time while screaming "LALALA" so as to block out the world around them.

But no.\n
[...]but rather because to an external observer it often becomes obvious that the courses of action open to the player are scripted into the design of the game. Michael Mateas, for instance, notices:

By watching many players interact with the system, the observer has begun to discern the devices that control the plot in the face of player interaction. This observer will conclude that the player has no true agency, that the player is not able to form any intentions within the dramatic world that actually matter. But the first-time player within the world is experiencing agency. The designer of the dramatic world could conclude - because they are designing the world for the player, not for the observer - that as long as the player experiences a true sense of interactive freedom (that is agency) transformation as variety is not an important design consideration (Mateas, 2004).

The trick of the trade of game design is indeed to make the player believe she is in control. Moreover, as Lisbeth Klastrup argues, performances by players in interaction with each other and virtual gameworlds give rise to \u201ctellable events,\u201d \u201cwhich would retrospectively make good stories\u201d (Klastrup, 2003).

    \n A friend and I were just discussing this issue as it relates to GTA4. In a nutshell, our conclusion was that the more options given us in the guise of sandbox play, the less immersed we are, and the less "real" the world becomes. Videogaming's attempt to broaden its' horizons have, I believe, effectively limited it as a storytelling mechanism.

    My last D&D experience ended abruptly with the DM/storywriter suddenly deciding that Neverwinter Nights 2 was a far superior experience than tabletop, because he could keep the players on rails and wouldn't have to be in the same room with everyone who played. I (and everyone else) told him that freeflowing play and drinking beer together were two of our main reasons for showing up; his story was a close third, but not our primary motivation. I think experiences like these are becoming more and more common.

    Videogames are linear stories, in my eyes; any sidetracking involved would be akin to a voluntary subplot, a side-story which, like those in comic books, may be skipped if it's deemed appropriate. Approaching them from a cinematic perspective, they are remarkably open-ended. Approaching from a roleplaying perspective, they are irrepairably crippled by the natural limitations of computer gaming, i.e. nonimprovisational play. Mass Effect demonstrates this pretty well, and I think which observational stance reviewers have taken has drastically affected their overall views on the game, and proceeding games like it.

    As a cinema buff, I look forward to games with driving narratives and powerful stories. As a roleplaying, I'm thinking roleplaying's going to be my primary outlet for improvisational/collaborative storytelling.

    \n So, another stray thought while I focus my mind around this.

    I recognize that different game styles value player agency with regards to narrative comparably more or less. Half-Life, for example, is a game where providing greater control of narrative to the player would be an entirely unnecessary complication - the gameplay revolves around shooting stuff and figuring out levels, with an unfolding linear story being icing on the cake. The adventure game genre (frequently excluding interactive fiction - I'm thinking games like Monkey Island) focuses on solving puzzles while experiencing a carefully crafted storyline, as if you were reading a book where you have to solve a Sudoku puzzle to get to the next chapter. Giving the player control over that story would contradict all the work which went into crafting it. Similarly with JRPGs such as Final Fantasy and Xenogears/Xenosaga, with the puzzle being a turn-based tactical combat sequence.

    None of the games I just mentioned tries to trick the player into believing they have agency. Half-Life is a shooter and makes no apologies for it. Monkey Island is a puzzle solving game. JRPGs have adopted a more and more tightly focused storyline over the years, with my latest experience - Final Fantasy Crisis Core - being exactly nothing other than a string of auto-balanced combat sequences with occasional cinematic cutscenes.

    What disturbed me about the passage I quoted was the notion of "tricking" the player into believing they had control of the game's narrative, that GTA4's missions were dynamically generated based on your relative rep with the various gangs or that TES4: Oblivion's NPCs actually were equipped with some sort of true AI rather than elaborate scripts (don't quote Turing at me - there is a difference).

    In particular, what disturbed me was that it was phrased as a general goal of all storytelling video-games, "the trick of the trade", when it's clearly just smoke and mirrors - and clumsy, awkward ones at that. It becomes painfully obvious, if not immediately then on a second play-through or when discussed with friends, that any choice will yield the same result. Compare, if you will, to a GM who runs the same con scenario year after year and it always turns out the same - how will you feel when discussing it with another player, hearing that all options yield the same result? Realizing you were tricked into believing you had agency, when in actuality you were played like a fiddle, is terrible - in whichever medium.

    Here's where I would hope that academics - you know, people who think and write about this stuff for a living - would have learned something from other types of games, like ours, where the idea of an illusionist, railroading GM (by whichever name you call them and by whichever theory you subscribe to) is nearly universally loathed.

    \nPeople have tried, and nobody has been able to come close. It's not a matter of the limitations of memory, any "scripting" is going to come off as scripting, no matter how elaborate. The true limitation there is simply the time involved. You can only script a game at a certain rate, even if you have an unlimited budget. Which, of course, nobody has.
    \n
    \nIt's a technological limitation. Nobody has yet created the sort of AI that replicates the ability of a GM to facilitate play, yet at the same time allow for the player to take play in a direction of their choosing. Storytron represents, if it's successful (and it may well not be by many definitions), one small step in the direction in question.
    \n
    \nAnd it isn't the sort of technology that you can obtain simply by throwing money at the problem. The Manhattan Project was expensive, surely, but the limitations on the work come down to the power of the minds that could be assembled and brought to bear, only certain of them being key to the whole thing. The other folks paid may have been smart, and a neccessary component of putting it all together, just as a CRPG design team is composed of many team players each doing their part. But what sets one apart from another, and what's required to make a leap forward in technology, is vision. And very few people in the world have vision. You can't pay a person to have vision.
    \n
    \nAnd then... Chris Crawford has vision, and is following it. But he may not succeed. It often takes generations of visionaries working on each other's small advancements, before we really see a change in technologies.
    \n
    \nSo don't hold your breath. Even if we had unlimited budgets, and unlimited computing power for the player, we still wouldn't know how to make the sort of game that you're asking for. We've also known for decades that Fusion power will, when it is available, revolutionize the world in terms of providing cheap energy. That knowledge hasn't hastened the day of it's arrival.
    \n
    \nMoney in that direction doesn't hurt, of course... we might find more visionaries, or at least make the existing ones have less concerns about their mortgages. But just like the money sunk into Fusion research, there's no proof anywhere that it will eventually be successful, nor anyone who can accurately predict when it will arrive. And until we can know those things, there's no way we can expect this technology to come any sooner.

      \nMike, I'm not making points on technology. I'm making points on the social contract between developer and player, of broadening the definition of "narrative" outside of "linear pre-written plotline", and recognizing that narrative can exist and be enjoyed without immersion. These are all lessons we have learned from reading, playing and talking about independently produced RPGs over the last 5-10 years. In fact, one of the milestones which addressed all of these was your own game.

      There is a social contract between the developer and the player. It is not the same social contract for every game and every player, so it is impossible to define the boundaries of the individual social contracts. It is, however, possible to consider the range of those social contracts, and what breaching them might do to the relationship between the player and the game, and the player and the developer. My point here is that "mak[ing] the player believe she is in control" when she is not can constitute a major violation of many of those social contracts, at the point of reveal (which, as stated, usually occurs during a replay or with players who engage in game state exploration). Further, the reveal is never a positive one, in my experience - the best you can get is a shrug. In my opinion, this warrants rethinking the idea that mastery of illusionism is a goal in (pragmatic) game design.

      What other techniques can you use, with today's technology? To restate what I mentioned above, what is wrong with just making the social contract explicit - tell me when I need to get back on track. Arcade style racing games will flash big red letters saying WRONG WAY when you're off course. It doesn't shatter my enjoyment of the game for it to remind me that, no, it's not an audience-murdering simulator, it's a racer. Similarly, FFCC has an elaborate story, and it doesn't allow me to try to make my own - it's like, "hey, kill those monsters so we can get on with the story, okay?".

      Narratives do not all consist of linear plotlines and they do not need to be enjoyed objectively to be valid. The Sims makes stories in a perfectly non-linear way with plenty of meaningful choices made by the player. The Sims doesn't deliver those narratives in a consistent, reproducible, or even objective way, of course. What role-playing games have taught me is that not only are those things not all that important for the enjoyment of a narrative, but as a designer it's possible to tweak the game system in such a way as to make certain thematic points come up more frequently than others. For example, The Sims is more likely to produce stories about suburban white America and its challenges - falling in love, getting a good job, paying bills, juggling kids, achieving your life's aspirations (in Sims 2) - than stories about urban black America and its challenges.

      With some careful thought, using current technology, there is no reason one could not create a video game which delivers a powerful thematic narrative with reasonable reliability, just by manipulating the system. It just wouldn't follow the movie making or novel writing paradigm in terms of what went into the design of its story and "mechanics". Perhaps it would share similarities with LARP design. Storytron seems to be aiming for something like that - introduce a bunch of Actors, give them carefully constructed motivations and occasional trigger points, then let the player loose to explore, push and prod the structure.

      Finally, you and I both know it's possible to enjoy a narrative without being immersed in it. Are you immersed in the role of mayor when you play Sim City? No? Can you still enjoy the story of your city and make interesting choices? Sure!

      So why do we have to "trick" players into losing themselves in the perceived reality of the game? Is it really worth threatening the social contract you have with the player for that tiny speck of suspension of disbelief a fake door in HL2 can provide? Yes, in role-playing games we frequently describe elements which exist only for colour too, and it can make the game more engaging. But to make the claim that developers ought to aspire to trick players into believing manipulating those colour elements amount to advancing the story by utilizing illusionist techniques, and that this represents the only functional way of conveying a narrative in a video game using today's technology... well, that's what caused me to write this post to begin with.

      \n I thought this post (root) was interesting from my perspective as a fan of IF (Interactive Fiction). I still play IF's now and then and I do find I am still (sometimes) able to invest and feel like my choices are meaningful, even though I know for a fact every choice I can make is mapped out. So I wonder if I am just good at deluding myself or if meaningful choice is measured by something other/more than affecting outcome.

      Someone above said that videogames are linear. I'm not sure if I'm reading the statement correctly, but I think I disagree. I would point out that games can have multiple endings programmed in. They don't have to be linear in the sense that all roads lead to point Z. A game can be programmed so as to end at either X, Y, or Z, all of which might be radically different, and player choice affects which path the game takes. Though I will admit that all this is still linear, just in a more complex way than I took the original statement mean.

      Sometimes we make choices in our real lives that are HIGHLY meaningful and yet we know the exact outcome is preprogrammed. My boss might present me with an unsavory ultimatum and I might "take a stand" knowing I will be fired. In other words, I am faced with a fork in the road and I know what the result will be on either side. I choose how I want to "win" that game by deciding if winning means keeping my job or winning means maintaining my ethics. That is an interesting choice and, once I make it, a meaningful one. It doesn't matter that all possibilities are programmed.

      [For some reason the quote from Tale of Two Cities is running in my head in which the gentleman facing the guillotine says, "It is a far, far better thing I do ..."]

      Also, sometimes the plot of the game just doesn't matter. It's the journey that counts. My kids play Pokemon on their Nintendo DS's and I have played enough to understand what I like and dislike about the game. It's amazing how much of their emotional investment in the game is driven by things like which Pokemon they have in their "party" (the six they carry around with them all the time). Beating Team Galactic or whatever, is secondary to other pursuits in the game. Exploring, collecting, pimping out Pokeballs, building a home fort below ground and putting cool furniture in it, etc. There's a lot of choosing going on there and if it weren't meaningful they would have quit playing the game long ago. It's meaningful to them.

      So. I guess I'm saying that programmed choices and linear plots don't completely eradicate the possibility for meaningful or interesting choices.
