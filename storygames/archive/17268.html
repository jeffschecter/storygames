    \n I think you could make great use of The Daughters of Verona in a segment on Shakespeare and theater history. While it doesn't cover a single one of his plays, it shows the general principles of classical comedy, Elizabethan five act structure and other things in a playful way.

    I firmly believe that TDoV is a good introduction to Shakespeare, and that by playing it once you will be able to appreciate and understand all of his comedies more easily.

    However, it is not suitable for the general class room, the four hour time frame makes it hard to fit into ordinary one or two hour lectures. And the 4-6 player group size introduces other problems for the traditional classroom.

    \n The Trouble with Rose (Todd Zircher) / topic: English Studies, Shakespeare, Improv / related: Ask You Like It
    --

    The Pitch:

    The Trouble with Rose was originally written to re-create Shakespearean plays. Task the students to read a work of the Bard. Homework begins with creating a play set where the students describe the settings and the pivotal characters. The also have to identify the character's strengths and weaknesses as a short sentence or two. No complex character generation, just simple descriptive phrases. During 'play' have the students trade playsets (assuming they're working in small groups) and then they play the game which exercises a variety of improv techniques.

    TTwR is free and I just posted version 2.3 (thanks to some great feedback from the Mom's basement crew.)

    The game uses dominoes and cards for those teachers that are not comfortable with dice. And, there is even a PDF sheet where the players can print and cut up some dominoes on the cheap and put them in a cup.

    The Trouble with Rose [free rules, play sets, and support material]

    \n Tabletop roleplaying is a wonderful vehicle for teaching soft skills like effective communication, teamwork and cooperation, use of language, strategic thinking, negotiation and facilitation, conflict resolution, even things as basic as turn-taking and fair play.

    For these skills the particular game doesn't matter too much.

    There's a big chunk of The Fiasco Companion in which I interview educators about how they use Fiasco for teaching and learning.

    I think the Montsegur model (highly constrained situation, premade characters with built in conflicts and questions, fixed outcome range) is highly adaptable as a pedagogic tool for any topic. Witch: The Road to Lindesfarne builds on Montsegur very capably, for example. I'd like to see one about the Selma to Montgomery marches in 1965.
