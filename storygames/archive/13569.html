\n The RPG Solitaire Challenge is now open!

We have eight challenges for folks. If you'd like to take part, you can design for one or more of the challenges. Several of them seem like they will be a natural fit to go together: like Rob's Andy Kaufman Challenge and the Living in the Future challenge.

Also, it's open season for anyone who would like to write a game for both the Solitaire Challenge and the New Ronnies. It adds more challenge, but the two sets of rules fit together, and both Ron & I encourage folks who would like to do both to go for it!

Solitaire entries are due by Jan. 11. There is more information about the rules and submission guidelines here. Feel free to ask questions here or at the Black & Green forum at RPG Crossroads. If you're working on a game entry, let us know and give a link to where you're doing it.

Here are the challenges for RPG Solitaire:

Judge Evan's Challenges:
Build a Better Choose-Your-Own-Adventure\xae
"You wake up in a room surrounded by toad people. One of them offers you a ring. If you take the ring, go to page 34. If you refuse it - hoo boy - go to page 6."

Choose-Your-Own-Adventure (CYOA)* stories are books told in the second-person with forking, linear narratives. Each page describes a situation and at least two ways for the player-reader to respond to it. By directing them to other pages through their choices, the book serves as a basic GM or computer program to moderate the decisions of an active reader. Since their heyday in the 1980s, the choose-your-own-adventure style genre has been gradually replaced with the text-based computer adventure and/or modified with dice rolls (i.e. the Narnia CYOA books) and eBook technology (i.e. with hyperlinks). But is there a way to take back the genre on its own terms?

The objective of this Challenge is quite simple: design a solitaire RPG that either incorporates or expands upon the mechanics and tropes that make CYOA novels great. The game may be a proposal for such a game, or even a short game itself. For the Category Favorite, entries will be judged on the clarity and originality of their content and mechanics.

*Choose Your Own Adventure is a registered trademark of Chooseco LLC.

The Stuff in Your Domicile
Despite austerity measures and the digital revolution, we still seem to have a lot of objects in our homes. This challenge focuses on the development of a solitaire RPG using these objects as a source of inspiration. Though the game mechanics can be drawn from sources outside your home, the game itself must significantly include the finite set of items a hypothetical player would have around. Games might involve action figures, kitchen appliances, media or something else entirely. Please keep safety in mind, of course! For the Category Favorite, entries will be evaluated on their originality, feasibility and flexibility.

Judge Emily's Challenges:
Pencil and Paper
Simple pencil & paper games such as tic tac toe and hang-man are classics that have passed from generation to generation, shared by word of mouth. Let's see if we can make some solo rpgs that can do the same. With little or no preparation, no game text needed to remember the rules, and no special objects (e.g. dice, character sheets, etc.). Just a simple piece (or pad) of paper and a pen or pencil and you can conjure up what is needed. No limitations on what kind of paper or writing implements, but the idea is to make it easy to play at any time. For the Category Favorite, I'll be looking for accessibility, elegance and easy ways to remember the rules.

Living in the Future (aka not science fiction but technology fact)
Computers transform rp. Google, wikis, flash, java, apps: the list goes on of the the information available to players and the platforms they can use to explore a world or create a story alone. Recorded audio, videos, and text of course are all on the table. The key is to create a structure or to describe the elements that exist already, that will help someone have an rp experience. Whether that is story oriented, emotional immersive, or character or world intensive is up to you. Due to the special nature of this challenge, additional files may be submitted, in addition to the plain text write up. But having an app or program completed is not necessary for the Challenge. A complete write up of how it would work, and perhaps screen shots or charts describing the use of the media may be essential.

For the Category Favorite, I'll be looking for use of the strengths of the media, extending what you can normally do in an rp, and catchiness--does this seem like something I'd do every day at lunch?

Judge Robert's Challenges:
Unlonely Your Fun
Tony Dowler's How to Host a Dungeon opened the door to designing games built around "lonely fun" \u2014 the stuff you used to do to prepare for games with friends. The Unlonely Your Fun challenge asks you to take this a step further. Design a game around the fun things you can do with RPGs by yourself, but make it so that this material touches on the experiences of others playing the game. Maybe you synch up every now and then, maybe your actions have an effect on others that's light, maybe a serial modular design that can be played by one person, then another, then another for different phases of the game. Important to this challenge is that you not violate the solitaire aspect of this contest, despite the interlocking elements. For the Category Favorite, I'll be looking for something that lets you set your own level of involvement with other players, and will be just as fun regardless of how lonely you want to keep it.

ARG! RPGS! (Or, the Andy Kaufman Challenge)
Design a game which produces a product of play in the voice of the character or characters being played. This could be blog posts, Facebook entries, journal entries, music, whatever. Ideally, the products of play will tell a story anyone can appreciate. For the Category Favorite, I'll be looking for people who successfully teach people creative skills they don't already possess.

Judge Epidiah's Challenges:
The Scheherazade Challenge
The best part of D&D when you're 12 years old in the 80s is sitting in your room, leafing through rulebook and dreaming about the impossibly long campaigns you're going to run for your players. The other players, of course, being the only thing standing between you and such glory. Now that we've once and for all eliminated those pesky nuisances, it's time to get to work on that saga.

Your mission: to design a game in which the story grows with each session and the player finds him- or herself deliciously compelled to return session after session to see where it'll go next. For the Category Favorite, I'm looking for a game that not only draws me back in repeatedly, but promises more each time it does.

The Sharing Challenge
You're an audience of one, but sometimes that's simply not enough. Games qualifying for the Sharing Award must produce something--be it fiction, poetry, cartography, what have you--that can be shared with a wider audience without comment on it's origin as product of a game. For the Category Favorite, I'm looking for a game that produces something beautiful, especially if it isn't the primary goal of the game.

      \n\nJanuary 2011 \n

      \n So I have been both working on and playtesting my game Facing The End. I submitted it for a Ronnie, and after the 20 hours that it took to get an initial draft, Ron Edwards agreed with what I felt after reading it the day after I wrote it. It was kinda a mess, but the intent and some of the elements had promise.

      So my initial intent was to explore the feelings generated during the end stage of a person's life. These times can generate significant feelings of loss and regret, but they can also be a time of heroism and legacy. It is a really compelling theme, and after attempting to write a game on it in less than 24 hours, I realize that this is a theme that deserves some time to process, refine, and eventually get right.

      My initial design took the idea of an aging wizard faced with the unraveling of the ward that he created, this ward being the only thing holding an overwhelming and uncanny malevolent force at bay. The idea is furthered with the fact that the wizard is the only person able to rebuild the ward, and the process of rebuilding this ward will cause his death. This was all wrapped up in a great chase across a continent to the place of power where the ward will be repaired, with four different opposing forces as obstacles to achieving the goal.

      For mechanics, I chose random lists of enemies mixed with random lists of locations, and honed with an escalating difficulty mechanic stolen from the boardgame Pandemic. I wanted players to really feel what their character does with regard to loss, duty, and regret. To this end, the game was solo, and the elements that were to be lost were elements from the players own life, plugged straight into the game. I stole a lot of mechanics for resolution from Dogs in the Vineyard, with my own twist.

      After a little playtesting, there are some positives, some negatives, and some interesting insights that will inform the next phase of the development for this game.

      First, the negatives. The ability of the player in playtesting (me) to become immersed in the narrative was hampered by the fact that I am not a wizard, and while I can play one, I never have actually felt I was one. This was a problem for me. I need the character to be someone who would become dificult to differentiate from myself. The wizard didn't work. Adding to this, I am not old, and the old seemed to be unneccessary for the theme in that age wasn't the thing that was bringing about the loss. That force was something else. So an old wizard was not the vehicle I needed for this narrative. Interesting.

      The opposition I created felt like it was somewhat of a stretch as far as motivations go. I needed opposition, so I just said, "These guys disagree with you to the point of violent opposition" and basically left it at that. Not subtle, and it seemed weak in play. I needed more in my opposition than a shallow caricature. I needed them to feel threatening in a believable way, and yet sympathetic. My opposition was neither.

      With further reflection, I realized that the DitV style mechanics were not adding anything to the game. Escalation is fine, but there had to be a good reason to escalate, and there had to be consequences for escalating. This was not really the game for either. It added a complexity that did not enhance my play-through, and really made me feel like I was pandering to my own fanboy nature in invoking the DitV rules. I feel a little ashamed. What can I say, 24 hours is not enough time for me to be reflective.

      For positives, I feel like there were some real successes in the initial draft. The player-donated impending losses that create the sense of regret, and set up the possibility of real heroism were fantastic. I have since refined things, and the initial draft did a really shitty job of outlining them, but in play they were fun. I am still refining their use, but I can see that they have promise. They thin the wall between player and character, which is exactly what I was hoping. The solo nature of the game allows you to really expose yourself to loss, without the worry of crossing lines or ridicule. You retain the feeling of safety, even though you are not realy in control.

      The random generation of scenes worked great as well. The list of locations I created with much help from the interwebs was very evocative. The opposition was easily generated, and it was not at all hard to go from a site, an enemy, and a difficulty to a fully realized scene. Just a little creativity and the evocative settings really took over. I think that is an important thing about smaller games. They need to be evocative, so that less text still translated into a big experience. Translating this insight into a more refined game will be a big goal going forward.

      Since my playtest I have done some revising. I ditched the all-too-DitV resolution system. No real need for conflict escalation means that another, simpler mechanic would work better. I co-opted the traits and tags style of Lady Blackbird for this. Simple, evocative, and more geared toward to unopposed roll style that better fits a solo game anyway. I have modified it in that the traits and tags will be much less explicit in Facing The End than in Lady Blackbird. This allows a freedom for the player to interpret that creates space for most any self concept, while still retaining the evocative nature of the original. I don't have to be a wizard, but someone else can be, and the narrative and the mechanics still support both equally well.

      To continue that revision, the initial situation will be changed from the aging wizard recasting a deadly ritual to someone with a magical disease. This will eliminate the problem I had with the wizard, tighten up the premise, and also allow me to reimagine the opposition as people out to contain disease. They will be believable and also sympathetic. They can oppose without being shallow, and I will be faced with even more choice. How do I feel about violence toward them? What if the cost to avoiding violence is harm to innocents? This hopefully deepens the experience.

      I also realized that some of the physical elements that I created could be combined. The Difficulty Matrix which was a physical tool used to help escalate the challenge of the opposition and set pacing could be combined with the Opposition Table, which originally was basically a random encounter table. Together, you would only have one chart to look up, and the funtionality would be retained.

      Anyway, long winded post to say that I have bitten off too much for either contest to make this a complete entry. I am jazzed about this game, and will continue to work on it, but I don't expect it to be really workable to anyone but me for a little while. That being said, I have gotten it to do at least some of what I set out to, and have high hopes for achieving my other goals. Those goals being a semi-extended game with deep immersion exploring the themes of end-of-life, loss, and heroism, packaged in a format that provides true opposition for the player/character, and plays like the start of some of the classic fantasy literature. Pretty cool so far.
