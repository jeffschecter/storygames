\n Do you REALLY want what you get in a role-playing game?

If you buy a role-playing game;
- what are you REALLY paying for?
- and do your REALLY want it?

When buying role-playing games;
- how often do you get what you want?
- how often do you feel cheated?

If you feel cheated;
- is it due to yourself being unable to make the game work?
- or is the game unable to make you work?

    Posted By: JDCorleyif I'm unsure or uncertain, I don't buy things, or I buy it on sale when I'm willing to take a gamble.
    \nThis sums up my attitude, too.

    When I was a kid, I felt like I got burned on a lot of purchases; I'd see something that "looked cool" at the store, buy it, and then never really find a use for it. Maybe it was a crappy product, maybe it was just something that didn't click with my friends and therefore never could be played. Sometimes it was an okay book, but cost too much for what use I got out of it (back then, $30 was a hell of a lot of cash for a twelve-year-old to be spending in one shot, and it was a very rare product that could deliver enough value to match up to that).

    As I got older, I started thinking more about things before buying them, doing some research to find out more, and then asking myself the tough questions ("Is this actually worth $XX to me?"). And the weird thing is, it feels good to not buy things. Every time I weigh a potential purchase and decide that no, I don't really want it that much, it makes me happy. (So basically, I'm killing the RPG industry but I'm feeling good about it.)

    \n I pay for the dream of something new and fresh. I become curious, I want to have it. Most often it's an impulse, and sometimes I get a bit disappointed when the actual game arrives, or I look at it once and put it on the shelf.

    - Get me interested enough to actually start reading a little
    - Keep me interested so I don't put the book down
    - If I've gotten this far, I always want to play.

    \nDo you REALLY want what you get in a role-playing game?

    Almost always. There are three primary things that I want in a RPG. I want to be inspired by new ideas. I usually am. I want to support the author to continue their work. I assume my purchase does that. I want to play new games. This happens least of the three, but often enough that it's all good.

    If I feel cheated it's because the game or author stinks or some weird social thing. It's really quite rare. I don't usually feel cheated even if I never play the game.

    \n When I buy a role-playing game, what I want is two-fold:\n
      \n
    • The primary thing I try to accomplish with my purchases now is to support voices that bring something exciting to the culture.
      • \n
    • Very much in second place, I'm trying to find something for my own use.
      • \n
    \nI have enough resources now (games, novels, imaginative friends) to provide entertainment for the foreseeable future. What excites me about a purchase is seeing what the creators will do with themselves next.

    As a result, my RPG purchases are just indie and story games now. My $15 bucks matters (or feels like it matters, which is just as good for me) in this market like it did for the local punk bands when I was a kid.
