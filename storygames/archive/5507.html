\n OK, listening to recordings of actual play. I cannot get my head around this and want you to help me out.

I've tried a few times, and I've listened to audio of my games being played by people far away, which has been useful for design and analysis, but that feels like work. Doing it for fun I don't understand - in the time it takes you to listen to other people play a game, you could play that same game yourself. You're listening to people you don't know engaged primarily in pointless table talk. All that aside, where do you find the time to absorb an hour, two hours, or more of audio?

Is it an effective way to learn to play a game you don't know?
Is there a participatory or vicarious thrill?
Do you feel a connection to the players/podcasters when it is over?

I'm assuming I don't know what I'm talking about (that's the way to bet, generally), so straighten me out! I know there are people here who both create and enjoy actual play podcasts, so spell it out for me. If you like, tell me about (and link to) the single most useful actual play podcast you have ever listened to.

    \nThe DreadCast is my actual play recording.

    I've truncated a three-hour game session of Dread: The First Book of Pandemonium into a few minutes. I've added music and narration to keep the pace moving.

    The goal is to show what gameplay can be like, without forcing you to listen to three hours of people talking. I think of it as a movie trailer. If it holds your interest, then maybe you'll read an actual play summary (again, a truncated version of an actual transcript, since reading said transcript would be somewhat tedious).

    So far, the DreadCast has been pretty well received. I'm working on the second installment. Any day now.

    \n I've listened to some actual play of Polaris, Spirit of the Century, Don't Rest Your Head, Shock and several other things.

    It can also feel a bit like work to me, especially if there is a lot of table talk.
    It can be helpful towards learning a game. It could just as easily not be. It could be detrimental towards that end as some people don't have the rules down as well as I myself try to.

    \n I love AP podcasts. Good ones anyway. I hate the ones with extraneous background noise, etc.,. The worst place to record one is in a busy convention gaming room. Ugh.

    AP podcasts are some of the best ways to find out about a new game before buying it. It's excellent for that.

    An excellent example of a quality AP podcast would be The Game Master Show's sessions of Don't Rest Your Head. I bought the game half way through listening to the 2nd episode. It was also very interesting listening to a group who's experience was mostly with traditional games play a story game. The people on the Game Master Show are also incredibly cognizant of the fact they are being recorded and keep table talk, stupid jokes and the like to an absolute minimum.

    I don't want to list the ones I think are the worst, but I'd say for every good AP podcast, there are 10 bad ones. Table talk, background noise, meandering conversation, etc., gets me to turn it off right away.

    I want people to keep producing AP podcasts on the off hand chance that I'll find the 1 in 10 that's not totally awful. If you make AP podcasts, keep it up, and help us listeners out by cutting out table talk, stupid jokes, background noise, and unrelated chit chat.

    \n First off in my Audio APs I tend to shave off excess space and chatter cutting them down by almost an hour.

    I don't expect people to listen to the whole thing but you help me focus in on an idea I've had recently where I play 20 minutes of a game to illustrate a point.

    My goal for listening to game play is to see how other people play. We were playing Burning Empires MUCH different from Luke's crew. I think hearing Luke's group run through a session would be VERY useful, especially in a game as dense as BE. I can see how Polaris would be fun to listen to. It would be cool to see how GMs handle certain rules and abstractions, how people make decisions and where dysfunction crops up and how it's handled.

    I don't think listening to game designers has any particular merit. Sure, they have a vision for the game but if a game works a certain way for an unattached play group I think there is a lot more to learn about the game there. Comparing the two is the most worthwhile.

    Mel and I have been talking about trading Audio APs and comparing points. Once I get the apps and techniques down, making editting easier then I hope to manage more of these. I am even thinking of doing an AP Podcast that uses these as a segment of the podcast while interviewing GMs and players about the game. Sorta like a movie commmentary.

    How do I listen. It's pretty passive. I am exercising, manual activities, on the bus - stuff like that. I don't think I would ever sit down and listen word-for-word, minute-by-minute.

    \n Jason: I'm really bad at listening. The only thing that held my attention audio-wise for more than a few minutes next year were Godzilla Gaming Podcast and the audio book of World War Z. Personally, I like reading Replays (every now and then, not like for Every Session of a campaign or anything like that), there's something about the organization and focus that I love.

    But man, the closest thing I've seen to a great audio actual play with a Purpose is Rafael's Dreadcast. I, too, tried to listen to Eruditus' Psi Run cast to try to help him out, or rate his style or whatever, but I have a low attention span and just couldn't get over the fact that I was listening to others having fun, without getting anything in return. I tried to think about it in a critique point of view and all, but I couldn't wrestle with it for more than 2 minutes at a time. Not that he's boring, it's just that it turned out to be not my thing.

    But the Dreadcast is the closest I've seen in the feel of Japanese-style Replays: That is, presenting it in a way that is appealing and perhaps enjoyable to the reader. It's part actual play, part radio drama, and very different than most actual play recordings. Give it a brief listen or skip around it a bit. I again didn't have the attention span to listen to it end-to-end (I might load it up on my workout iShuffle and see if I can go end to end on it), but I listened to it, and had far more FUN listening to it, than the normal drab actual plays. The music, narration, cuts and stuff really focus and tighten the work into something enjoyable to listen to.

    Again, Rafael shows us all up by coming out of Left Field and kicking ass in his own particular way. I look forward to, as they say on the internets, "MOAR".

    \n Other than getting permission from the GM and player, I want the fact that we are recording to be as unobtrusive as possible--and hopefully forgotten. I've seen it (the fact of recording) change behavior. So far, it's mostly been in terms of whispering or delaying talking so as not to have multiple conversations going on at the table at once. This is probably a 'good thing' whether recording or not, because it keeps everyone in the game. But I don't want the event to seem artificial--aren't there many studies that show people change their behavior when they know they are being monitored? I cringe a little when we take a break and someone asks if I want to turn off the recorder--I want it to be forgotten!
    That being said, I do try to make understanding what's going on easier for the listener. I try to avoid pronouns; I try to make clear who is acting at a specific time; I try to make clear when something we're doing is not in accord with the rules as written; and I think the dice rolls are normally announced anyway. For Ganakagok, I try to describe the image on the card or the card name--leading to a comment I'll never forget: a player describing "two walruses battle" as 'more like two lawn chairs battle!' In many cases I am pointing these things out so that it's easier for me, as the future listener, to recall what happened so that I can talk about the scene.
    Mel

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarIs it an effective way to learn to play a game you don't know?
    \nListening to Judd's Life on Mars AP recording taught me how to focus the pitch portion of Primetime Adventures' gameplay and get to the actual episode in a short amount of time while getting everyone excited about the show we're about to play. I've heard of other groups taking a whole three, four hour game session to come up with the perfect pitch. After listening to the AP recording, my pitch sessions last around thirty minutes, give or take ten minutes. These aren't just with my regular gaming group; this is with pickup groups at conventions and game days. Even though I didn't like the show they came up with, those first thirty or forty minutes of the AP recording are gold for instruction.

    I occasionally listen to AP records if there's a game that I'm interested in but don't know how to play to see how a group handles the game and possibly to see how long it takes to play certain aspects of the game. Such as in this combat-heavy game, how long does combat take? Does it sound like cool things are happening at the table or is it abstract dice rolling and number crunching? Are the players doing things because they sound cool or are they doing things to maximize chances of succeeding?\n
    Posted By: Jason MorningstarThose of you who record your sessions: To what degree are you conscious of the fact that you are recording, and how does that change your play? Do you try to be at your best? Do you "play to the crowd"? Do you try to use language that makes your actions clear (like announcing the number you roll)? Whatever your answer, unpack it a little - why do you make those choices?
    \nOnce we get into a playtest for an upcoming game, I'm giving the game developers the opportunity to get recordings of our playtest. I expect that the game session will start with us introducing ourselves and the character we'll be playing so the development team can get an idea of who is whom, but once the session starts, the iRecorderthing will just be another thing that's piled in the middle of the table. Hopefully we'll remember to turn it off when the session is over. I'd like it to work that way so the developer can hear what and how a gaming group approaches their game in a real setting.
