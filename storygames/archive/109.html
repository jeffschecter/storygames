So, trying out this whole 19 questions. Trying to get a grip on a game that's been floating around my head and computer for years, but never quite came together, and find a way to get it really focused.

If you're willing, tell me how it sounds.

1.) What is your game about?

Surviving the horrors after an apocalypse, not least of which is your own broken mind. Survival through community, especially.

2.) What do the characters do?

They barter, wander, fight, lead, follow, negotiate, starve, eat, drink, burn, rest, bleed, go mad, stave off madness through companionship, and sometimes, occasionally suddenly, they die.

3.) What do the players (including the GM if there is one) do?

Get caught up in small-group conflicts that are fueled by scarcity, hardship, and a need for companionship that is sometime slighted - and sometimes explosively so.

4.) How does your setting (or lack thereof) reinforce what your game is about?

The Apocalypse itself is the result of a solar accident - something is wrong with the sun. Something magnetic and complicated, that caused seizures, killed people, wiped out electronics, and drove everyone Feral for months. The only way to remain stable is to avoid being alone too long. Of course, there isn't enough of anything, and you can't trust anyone.

5.) How does the Character Creation of your game reinforce what your game is about?

Characters will need to select a short list of archetypes they conform to, which bolster their identity. Alone, they won't have enough. Together, they often won't get along.

6.) What types of behaviors/styles of play does your game reward (and punish if necessary)?

Having a variety of approaches available, through teamwork, will be rewarded. Internal conflict at a low level in the group will be rewarded. "Lone Wolf" behaviour will be punished.

I'm working on it.

8.) How are the responsibilities of narration and credibility divided in your game?

Traditional arrangement, with a strong empowered-players vibe.

9.) What does your game do to command the players' attention, engagement, and participation?

Players will constantly need to choose what kind of damgers they wish to face; they'll usually have a lot of choice, but every path is dangerous.

Yes. That's gonna see some changes. Advancement and Backlash are going to get tied in - a little like Fallout in Dogs, but only a little.

    Hmm. That's sort of where I was tending towards, though not quite in that way. Let me see, here, if I can hash it out a little more.

    *Traits* give the character the abiity to fulfill needs or win conflicts on an ongoing basis, whether directly or indirectly. Traits are player-generated, and descriptive. Some example traits:
    -"Good listener" - the character is a good person to talk to.
    -"Handy with tools" - the character can build things that help out in fulfilling many needs.
    -"Knows the area" - the character knows where to find resources.

    *Needs* are things that the character must have fulfilled on a daily basis, or they will suffer degrading injuries. Every character will have the same basic needs - Hunger, Thirst, Exposure, Loneliness. They will also have a few more specific needs, created and defined by the player.

    *Resources* are things that the character currently possesses that can be expended or lost. Most resources exist specifically to fulfill needs - Food, Water, Confidence, and so on. Others are used either as tools or when using tools - petroleum, bullets, and so on. Each point or 'chip' of a resource is enough to fulfill a need for one day or to be used for one scene.

    *Tools* are things a character possesses that aren't normally used up, but which can be taken away, left behind, damaged, or broken. Some require resources to use, other's don't. They include things like territory, guns, reputation, and so on.

    *Injuries* are forms of damage that can be used against you, can be used to force actions from your character, or can be used to drive your character out of play. Failure to fulfill your needs will result in injury, and conflicts of all sorts can also result in injury. Injuries can thus include things like wounds, frustration, physical weakening, and so on.
