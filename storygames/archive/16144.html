    IMO, one of the thornier issues with Dogs hacks is settings in which you don't have guns, which can complicate the Talking-Physical-Fighting-Shooting escalation stages. Magic seems like it could be a good replacement (especially in dark fantasy like Warhammer) but it's often a lateral movement as much as an escalation.     I see the competing faiths thing as less of a problem. There are competing faiths in Dogs, right? (everybody loves false worship!)

    \n Hi Moreno,

    Thanks for pushing on the question of whether this will work in Glorantha. This is exactly the part of the discussion that I want to get deeper into. That said, I'm going to push back on your stated list of problems, because right now I don't see them as showstoppers. If you want to elaborate then I'm eager to hear more details of where the Devil is in the details.\n
    Posted By: Moreno R.Mmmmm... I see some problems...

    1) The Red Goddess really exist. She is real, she talks with her worshippers, you can meet her via heroquesting. So, not only you have a "fixed" faith, that already say to you what you have to do in every case, but in doubt, you can ask the GM.


    \nI don't see that this necessarily has to be any more true for the Red Goddess than for the King of Life in canonical Dogs. There's a much quoted (and possibly apocryphal) story of a contest in a con Dogs game where a player had his character pray to God for guidance and Vincent (allegedly) replied, "Okay. You roll. I give. What does God tell you to do?" Whether this story is true or not, it seems to me that this would be a perfectly valid approach to take with the Red Goddess. She is, after all, famed as a goddess who embraces contradiction. It's not like they are Rokari faithful or something.\n

    2) Sin in Glorantha is not the same thing as in our society.

    \nWell, based on where I'm living, sin is not exactly the same as it is in the world of Dogs either. Okay, I don't mean to be a smart-ass about it. But in fact a big part (most) of why I'm looking for an alternate "skin" for Dogs is precisely that my largely atheist-humanist players are going to have a hard time looking at the game's treatment of polygamy, fidelity rules, gender inequality and so on and end up making PCs who are framed as a critique of organized religion. I've seen Dogs played this way on two occasions, and to my mind it really torques the game out of shape because it removes the players' ability to relate to their own characters' moral choices.

    To my way of thinking, this is the real reason that people are so keen to port the game to other time periods and settings. They are searching for a fantasy setting that allows their players to feel empowered to engage in the moralizing aspects of the game without rejecting the premise based on issues of aesthetic distaste. This isn't necessary for my personal enjoyment, but as I said I have seen it prove a real stumbling block for otherwise great players and I suspect it could be a real issue for my current group.

    For a long time I was keen to port the game to the world of Aldea from the Blue Rose RPG. But then it hit me that I could combine this with my longstanding wish to run a Gloranthan Lunars game and maybe do something a little more interesting.\n

    3) The structure of society is different

    \nAs with the above, I have to agree with this statement but it equally seems true between the Territories of Dogs and the society in which I live. I'm not saying there may not be structural elements in society that are problematic! I'm asking you to enumerate some of the problems you think I'll face.\n

    4) The cult of the Red Goddess teach acceptance. Even of Chaos. The enemies are non spiritual ones but foreign gods and people.

    \nHmmmmm. Now I think we're getting to the real meat of the issue. I actually think this is really interesting. Because the "enemies" really are foreign ways of living, right? Foreign worship practices, foreign magics, foreign faiths. And yet Sedenya preaches "We are all us". I actually think that this makes for a really cool moral and cultural struggle. It's not the same struggle as in vanilla Dogs (and I'm willing to hear why that's problematic) but it's an interesting struggle nonetheless.

    Compare and contrast the view of the Dogs to the Mountain People to the view of a Lunar towards the Grazers (for example). In the former case, there are a number of examples in the Dogs rulebook of various scenarios where congregations who think that the Mountain People have wisdom they could learn from, and which might be divine or valuable in its own right, are treading on the ways of Sin. Dogs players who're going by the book might feel driven to turn the Faithful away from the practices of the Mountain People. But they aren't really focussed on the Mountain People themselves but on the Faithful whom they might corrupt. Whereas in a putative Lunar Missionaries game the missionaries are interested in the Grazers themselves. And they're okay with the Grazers continuing to do their thing, spiritually, provided they also add in Lunarism as a kind of meta-layer on top of their own religion. So on the one hand they have to prove something to the Grazer community in order to get them on board with the worship of Sedenya. On the other hand, they have to prove to themselves that they are more than just the PR wing of a conquering army -- that they actually do represent a way of life and a code of living that's superior and that will lead to greater satisfaction and happiness.

    I'm willing to hear that this approach to play will take some of the slack out of the sails in terms of the inherent dramatic tension of Dogs' rules. But it at least seems to me that it could be interesting.\n

    You risk turning the game in a sort of "mission-based" fighting game.
    \nOkay. I see that if the morality underpinnings and the player-driven moral decisions get kicked out from under the game then that will be true. But as I've tried to articulate above I don't think that this is a necessary consequence of the direction I want to take this game in. It may yet be a possible consequence and I may be missing some key observations which will help me come to that conclusion. But that's why I'm talking about it here, rather than starting to stat things up.

    That took longer than I thought it would to write. I'll come back to talk about the escalation ladder in a subsequent post. (Spoiler: I agree that crossbows are not the answer.)

    \n Thank you for these responses. They're pretty thought provoking.

    @Moreno, I absolutely agree that God shouldn't be present in scenes as an NPC in any version of Dogs. But I'm not sure that means that God can't exist in the setting. For instance, one of the ways to re-skin the game that Vincent actually discusses in the rulebook was to make the PCs mob enforcers. In that setting, presumably, the role of God is taken by the Don. As GM, you could rule that the PCs are never granted an audience with the Don on-camera and that they're never able to get a direct answer out of his lieutenants as to what he thinks they should do. But the players would know at some level that the Don existed in the world and had opinions, right? Would that spoil things? How is this different from the situation with the Red Goddess?

    @Moreno, I think your second line of reasoning -- about sins in Gloranthan cultures -- is a lot more dissuasive to me. The real distinction that I see here is that in the Lunar missionary situation the PCs would not be setting out to enforce right action on members of their own culture but rather to subjugate and persuade members of other cultures. While there's a certain amount of this in vanilla Dogs (folk from back East, Territorial Authority Marshalls who don't hold with the Faith, Mountain People, etc) the emphasis is definitely on travelling amongst communities of the Faithful and policing them, which is very different from the missionary situation I'm envisioning. So I can see a real distinction here and it certainly seems possible that things won't map over. If you're journeying to oversee a single Lunarized family in a Heortling stead (even assuming they were allowed to live there) that makes the conflict much more the PCs versus the community as an extension of the larger conflict in Dragon Pass and much less about the sins and ethical dilemmas of the community. This definitely gives me pause.

    @Rafu, great point. I'd never really thought about the benefits of the tension induced by players' antipathy towards the Faith. While you've helped me see that this can actually add a real layer of piquancy to the game, I would say that I have definitely seen instances where it's served as an impediment: Players just find the tenets of the Faith too objectionable and they end up coming up with a character that's a pretty broad pastiche of negative stereotypes of religious crusader. The trouble with this type of character is that if the player thinks that the character is a moron, dupe or psychopath -- or worse, feels that he or she can't even grasp where the character is coming from at all -- then I don't think they get as much out of, or give as much to the game. It collapses all the tension out of play if you're willing to say, "Well of course it's a bloody disaster, what did you expect?" and not feel a pang of guilt along with that.

    By way of contrast, I think that the game can work perfectly well with PCs who are tasked with enforcing a morality which the players do agree with. The truth is that making good moral decisions and balancing ethics in real world situations is extremely challenging. Even if the players all agree about what is righteous (unlikely) I think that play could still be very tense and interesting as the group explored down into the firmament of space to be found by repeatedly challenging their own previous decisions. So while I do see that there's a genuine frisson to be had by playing characters who're rooted in a faith framework that's not your own, my first instinct is to say that that's not necessary in order for Dogs to deliver tense and rewarding play.

    \n This discussion about the tension between player beliefs and morality and the Faith's is extremely interesting to me. When I first started playing, I expected to feel huge antipathy towards the Faith, and was worried that the game would fail as a result*.

    In actual play, I was surprised by how much sympathy I developed for the Faithful. I could see that their Faith was a source of strength for them as much as it was also a terrible burden. I think this attitude was helped by the way the game puts the Faith in a precarious position, threatened by settlers from Back East, vulnerable to collapse from within. Our game kind of became an elegy for this beautiful, flawed, dying way of life.

    I think one of the roles of the GM is to portray the Faithful as people: flawed people, but with good intentions. They're human before they're Faithful, and I think that makes them very sympathetic. Our best games came when the town pitted something great about the Faith (strong communities, caring families, mutual support) against something awful about it (xenophobia, intolerance, hierarchy). Problematising the "great" parts, and recontextualising the "awful" parts was really fruitful material for play.

    * Interestingly, this is exactly what did happen in a later game of Montsegur 1244. The Cathars had a shitty, shitty set of beliefs, and I couldn't, no matter how hard I tried, get myself into the mindset of someone who would die for that shitty religion.
