Very interesting (I say that a lot, if you hadn't noticed).

In regards to using the Tarot with an RPG, I've seen:

* A tarot substitute used for conflict resolution, in a totally handwavey no-backbone total fiat system (Everway, though it was something new at the time so I can't blame them)

* A free online game or two where the Major Arcana is largely ignored, and the Minor Arcana is used for its number/suit only (to correspond to four abilities, each numbered, adding them together to produce a result).

* Two Japanese RPGs (Tokyo Nova and Blade of Arcana, by the same author) where the game invents a special kind of Tarot Deck of Major Arcana (archetypes), only which are used both to accomplish actions and set the theme, mood and rules of a scene (also chargen elements). Which I view as "higher" in the heirarchy of Tarot-based games, as it uses more than the numbers to sub for dice.

* Finally, an Indie (but huge and well-produced) RPG from Japan called Witch Quest, which comes with a set of about 80 tarot cards, ALL of them (and their major arcana, which seems doubled) designed specifically for the game. Haven't read enough of the game to determine how they're really used (ie if they're used for skills and the like), but did notice that there is an involved conflict resolution system where you actually lay out the tarot in one of several dozen "reading patterns", and flip them one by one to determine success/failure and the like. I think this is closest to my ideal in using the Tarot, but honestly I haven't had a chance to translate any of that system, so I can't say anything more for sure.

What I'm getting to is: I'd love to see more Tarot-inspired designs. In fact, I bought a new deck (Rider-Waite, ALWAYS Rider-Waite) the other day as my other deck is a novelty "tiny deck", in the hopes of coming up with inspiration for a Tarot-based game.

Now, having said that, and knowing that others are on the same (or similar) page, I'm wondering what are the characteristics of Tarot cards that can be pulled into games, and for this I'm speaking SPECIFICALLY of the Rider Tarot, because that's the most universal and commonly accepted (as well as awesome).

Here's a couple:

* Numbers and Four Suits. Each suit could represent an ability (ala Castle Falkenstein), and each number could be a number used to overcome an obstacle: Strength 6 + Swords 5 = 11, which overcomes the difficulty of 10! Unfortunately, EVERYONE who has used the actual tarot in their games have done exactly this, and usually little else. Uninspired. NEXT!

* Colors. I tried to think of how colors could influence resolution. Unfortunately, most R-W cards have Every Friggin Color on them.

* Positioning: Standard/Reversed, Turning them to the side ("tapping" in Magic the Gathering). These could be used.

* Layout patterns: Using a standard or unique fortune telling pattern could have different meanings, influence conflict resolution, etc.

* Major Arcana: These are different, and special, from the other numbered cards. How could this difference be accented? How could the Major arcana feel... well... Major in a system?

* Page, Queen, King, Ace... Seem "Royal" somehow, different than just another number. How could this influence the game.

* What are different ways they could be drawn or used?
-No hand, just flip a card when it's your turn to "attack"
-Have a hand of 3-5 or more cards to draw upon, and choose the card you want
-Pick X cards, Use Y of those X cards to establish a pattern or web of cards with which to frame your conflict.

What else is there? Any ideas for Tarot Tricks that might be worth putting in the Toolchest to use later in our designs?

    For my husband's birthday game (I run a LARP twice a year, usually on loved one's birthdays, or for New Years Eve and a convention) I used Tarot cards in three different ways.

    1) Characters. Everyone who was there was given a Major Arcana card for their character. That was the whole sheet: they were encouraged to take the picture and name and "go with it" as to what they were like. When I handed them out, I gave them each a particular, individual power. (I had been building a set of characters and mix-and-matching decks, but I ran out of time before the game.)

    2) Challenges. Before my husband could add characters to his party, he had to defeat a riddle/challenge based off each Major Arcana he met. ("The Magician" was based on the idea of "personal power," for example. I wrote these down ahead of time.) The trick was that he could use a character's power ONCE to help defeat another challenge. ("The Magician" had the power to ask the GM for a hint. "The Lovers" could combine two powers into additional strength. Things like that.)

    3) Crosses. He could at-any-time "cross" the challenged card with Minor Arcana (drawn randomly) and we decided then, based off the meaning of the card, how that moved the overall adventure. ("The Eight of Cups! You're on a quest!")

    If I were developing this for something more than a one-time thing, I'd probably qualify each card as having a specific meaning, and then we could work it as a boardgame...

    I can't believe that I didn't see this post before; I worked on a tarot based system just yesterday for my knight game. It's rough, but based on the "you are who you know" concept. I'm not very experienced with tarot cards, but I thought that the Celtic Cross spread looked pretty good.

    It's still half baked, but the basic idea is that in the beginning you pick a signifier card for your character. Every player has a hand. To resolve conflicts, it's like a L5R duel, players take turns putting cards face down in front of them until one says "call" and all the cards are revealed. Then you add up all the minor arcana (with a stat maybe) and the one with the highest total wins (one of my problems is that the range of numbers are pretty big, I might have to have some sort of 1-4 = 1, 5-8 = 2, 9-knight = 3 and queen and king = 4). Major Arcana act as trumps. if you play one and then your opponent calls, you win, unless your opponent played a higher numbered trump as his last card (and the Fool trumps all trumps but can't trump regular cards).

    The last card played by the winner must be used in the narration and placed into his spread (just the Cross part of the Celtic Cross until it is filled). The card now signifies an NPC influence on that character's life that reflects and reveals part of the character.

    Like the first card that you win is said to "cover" you, it is your character's Cover. It is an NPC that reveals your strength, and when you get that card, you narrate who this NPC is and what strength your interactions with them reveal (so maybe you win a sword fight thinking back to your mentor who taught you how to fight or more symbolically, you win a contest so your ship doesn't crash down onto a planet, but while doing it you butt heads with the ship's doctor who demonstrates the same "love of family" that is your character's strength).

    This continues with your Cross,a figure that shows your weakness, and so on until you have played six cards ending with your Future, a guide like character.

    And then the fun can begin. :)

    After all of your cross is filled, you can then use the cards in your cross backwards to win contests. You can reject your Future (turning your back on his or her advise) forget your Past (an ally or other character that wants things to remain with the status quo or reminds you of better days) give up on your Crown (your highest ambition like a role model or a love interest) disregard your Root (a sidekick or someone that grounds your character) surrender to your Cross (an NPC that reflects your weakness) and then finally you can betray your Cover. (It's funner writing about reversing the cards then playing them).

    TarotHammer (Using Tarot in WFRP)

    WFRP already has system of resolution, a series of dice-related mechanics intended to simulate the whim of the gods, the wiles and whims of fortune, and a whole host of more mundane factors. This article is going to consider the possibility of adding another mechanic, or replacing entirely the existing system, with a mechanic based upon Tarot cards. While it may seem controversial, or even misguided, this article seeks only to explore the possibility and make some suggestions on how this could be accomplished. The idea may not appeal to all players, but the article seeks to offer a whole range of possibilities for those willing to experiment.

    Contemplate for a moment the nature of a die roll. A die roll can only tell whether or not one succeeds or fails in a roll. It is an either/or proposition: either success or failure. What the roll doesnt explain is why exactly the result happened why the Watchman didnt believe the story or why the arrow didnt strike true. What if the task resolution suggested something other than whether or not an action is successful, but why. Perhaps the Watchman is in a foul mood over family problems and unwilling to give anyone the benefit of the doubt, or the glare of the sun turned the archers aim off a small amount. The Tarot cards can serve as a means of opening up task resolution from a yes/no proposition to an interpretive one, where chance plays a role, but the imagination and creativeness of the GM and players are just as important.

    A die roll provides an instant answer: there is little interpretation. By replacing that numeric value, with a symbol, greater interpretation is allowed, and their is greater freedom for players and GMs to decide and describe what truly occurs. The very act of interpretation and improvisation can greatly enhance role-playing, participation, and inspire the imagination of players and GM alike. Each card bears symbolic significance, and by taking the meaning of the card into account, the resolution through Tarot cards explains not only if, but also how or why.

    Lets move to the concrete, and take for an example a Noble dueling with a rival, and a burglar crossing on a tightrope to a rooftop. The card The Tower appears, featuring a Tower being split with lightning and its inhabitants spilling out an ominous card indeed. The card suggests disaster, but not one resulting from human foibles, but from an act of nature, or the failure of humanitys creations. In the duel, the card may signify the snapping of a blade or a cloak tangling up the duelists arm. For the Burglar, it may signify an unexpected gust of wind, or to the Burglars chagrin, a treacherous rope that decides then to snap. Perhaps another, more obscure card was drawn, the Seven of Swords, known as the Dark Girl. In the Duel, perhaps the blow is averted as the dark-haired daughter bursts into the fight, or perhaps the Burglar is observed from below by a dark-haired woman. In each case, the cards introduce an unexpected twist of events, that may challenge the players to think beyond just the success and failure of a die roll.

    Thus the Tarot not only ends mere yes/no task resolution, but they add an element of the unpredictable. There is a world outside of the players, and the whimsical nature of the Tarot card can help induce players and GMs to consider the world outside and the influence that it plays in the game. All too often games become myopic, focusing on the minutiae of the players and antagonists actions without taking in the context of the environment. The Tarot breaks the players out of this exclusive engagement, for who knows what may happen the next time a blade is drawn. It may not just be a series of dice rolls to determine who hits who first for how much damage, but a series of unanticipated events that may mean life or death, events that may be beyond the players control!

    Rather than just read the dice, players and GMs will read and interpret the cards. What does a Five of Wands mean during a talk with a barkeep. The Five of Wands often signifies a happy marriage, wealth, or legacy. Based upon the context, it could mean that the Barkeep is in a good mood, being happily married and eager to talk to people about his newlywed. Perhaps it means that the barkeep is attracted to one of the players. On the other hand, it could mean that the barkeep is reluctant to talk until the players spend some wealth. Regardless of the choice of GM or player, they have a greater engagement with the resolution, interpreting and improvising as the situation merits. Hopefully this will result in players becoming more engaged with the story and the context, as everyone works together to fill in the details.

    The Tarot card may also enhance the drama in what would normally be mundane dice rolling. On a roll to hit the Devil card is drawn, or the Five of Cups: a lone figure standing amidst spilled goblets. What do omens do they herald? The image on the card may instantly suggest drama in a fashion that pips on a die do not. Not all cards have such a dramatic impact, true there is Death, The Fool, and the Devil, but there are far more other cards with more subtle meaning, and thus the impact of such significant cards will not suffer. Over time, within a group, certain cards may acquire significance unique to that group.

    Last of the benefits to be touched upon is theme. The Tarot cards not only give a thematic feel, but the very interpretation of them can be used to convey a theme or feel. Since the cards are interpreted within the context of the game, the cards themselves can impart this motif. Interpreting the cards with a theme in mind can intensify a campaign about treachery and honor or sacrifice and glory. The cards are but symbols and archtypes, each card with a narrative of its own, and all are open to broad interpretation and can be adapted to fit the theme of any campaign.

    Thus Tarot cards potentially promise a number of benefits greater openness in determining the results or consequences of actions and opening up play to a broader range of actors than the protagonist and antagonist. They can help lift the game out of a dice rolling convention, whetting the imagination and encouraging more free form play. The very cards themselves offer an air of mystery and drama to a session, and the act of interpreting makes the players less passive but encourages all to really think about the cards and divine a meaning from them. This act of attributing to the cards significant meaning is also a means to further touch upon themes and ideas that the adventure or campaign may be centered on.

    The proposition of throwing away those long familiar dice may seem shocking to some. However this article provides a whole range of possibilities for using the Tarot. The options that follow begin with gradual, going to the more radical alterations. One adds the occasional Tarot card as an atmospheric accent to dice. Others simply replace the dice with tarot cards, merely changing the d6 and d100 die rolls and resolving them through cards instead of dice. Along the way, the possibilities of the Tarot are considered in various suggestions and options that the GM can do as they please with.

    Tarothammer (cont'd)

    The Tarot

    Of course, to use Tarot cards in WFRP, one must own a deck, although alternatives with playing cards are mentioned below. The Tarot deck has acquired mystical air about itself, and is the subject of considerable misinformation and myth. Its original intent seems quite mundane, for playing a Bridge-like card game, and the associated mystic tradition is an invention of the last two centuries primarily. Although the associated occult mysticism was attached later, theres no reason to let this spoil the mystery of the deck!

    There are countless decks and books out there, and GMs and players can even download from the internet cards to glue to playing cards to create their own deck. For the intent and purposes of this article, the deck primarily referenced is the Waite-Smith deck, and the primary reference is A. E. Waites The Pictorial Key to the Tarot. Those interested are recommended to seek out whatever sources and cards they find appealing.

    The Tarot consists of 78 cards that can be divided into two categories. The first category of 22 cards is what is historically known as the Trumps and Fool cards but are now known as the Major Arcana. These cards all are titled and have great symbolic significance. They are the first cards numbered 0 to 22 on the Tarot card chart accompanying this article. The second category of cards in the deck is the more mundane Suites to use the historical term; the more common modern term is Minor Arcana. These cards number 56 and they share many similarities with the modern playing card deck. They are divided into four suites with face cards symbolizing nobility and number cards from one or ace to ten. The major difference is that the Minor Arcana have four face cards, listed in order of importance: King, Queen, Knight and Page. The Suites, listed in order of dominance in relation to and with their modern playing card equivalents are:

    Cups Hearts
    Wands (or Staves) Diamonds
    Swords Clubs
    Pentacles (or Coins) Spades

    Every card has a picture, and these pictures can be used to aid the interpretation. It should be noted that Tarot decks do have an orientation. The person drawing the card places the card upright before themselves. If the card is oriented toward them with the image and title or number right-side up, it uses the normal interpretation. However if the card is upside down, it is considered reversed, and it has either a different meaning or it uses the opposite of its normal meaning. Whether to adopt a different meaning or to simply subvert the original meaning is a decision that should be made at the start and not changed. Some cards have a historical set of meanings attached to them, one not reflected by merely reversing the standard significance. However consulting a reversed card may be determined to be too complex and burdensome, and thus the group must decide which they prefer.

    Interpreting the Cards

    There is no right or wrong way to interpret a card, for there are countless interpretations and contradictory interpretations for each card. However, the basic meaning of each card should be established by the GM early on and written down. Ultimately it is up to the GM to decide, although a sample table of meanings has been included with the article to help the GM. Permission is granted to photocopy the table. It is the attributed significance of the card that is its most important feature and the thing the GM should consider first.

    That said, even with the basic meanings established, it is important to take into account a number of other factors when determining the card. One of the most obvious is the Title or Number of the Card. The card The Fool is obviously different from The High Priestess. Likewise, the number value inscribed on the card can give a GM some information. Is the number on the card for the Orcs parry higher than the number on the card drawn for the Mercenarys attack?

    Is the card drawn upside down, or reversed? If so, that changes its meaning. Many cards have an meaning attributed to its reversed aspect, while others may simply just reverse the significance or determine that since the card is upside down something bad happens for the individual for whom it is drawn.

    Some GMs may not care for the traditional meanings given to the cards and may discard them, turning instead to the illustration on the card for inspiration. Some GMs may examine each cards illustration and write up their own significance. Others may turn to the illustration when the historical significance seems of little help. For those players who may not be familiar with the meaning, they too can simply look at the illustration and make a decision based upon that alone. The illustrations are usually laden with symbols and meaning.

    Finally, a GM may take into account the cards Suite. A GM may deem that there is a hierarchy: Cups, Wands (Staves), Swords, Pentacles (Coins), or attribute meaning to each Suite. Cups may represent life and spirituality, wands may represent power or knowledge, swords signify violence, and Pentacles/Coins could represent the physical, material, and transitory. Some may trump others.

    There is plenty of opportunity for a GM to make a reasonable assessment of a cards significance and value. Should a GM feel even overwhelmed at the possibility, they can just focus on one or two of the various signifiers until they feel comfortable.

    A Note on Interpretation

    The Tarot is not carte blanche for the GM to dictate their every will and whim. They key word here is consistency in interpretation. A GM should provide all present with a copy of the significance of each card, an example is provided with this article, and GMs should feel free to use this one if they so desire. Considerable trust is being placed in the hands of a GM, and they should interpret the cards with not only an nod to consistency, but with the recognition that everybody is there to have fun and participate, not to be witnesses to some storytelling monologue performed by the GM. One way to avoid this is to open up the interpretation, and allowing players to do the interpretation. A GM may allow the player pulling the card to interpret it, or perhaps the player opposite or to the right. Ultimately, everyone should be encouraged to contribute and have fun. Thus it is imperative for the GM to be consistent and set a good example so that others have a clear idea of what is to be expected.

    Tarothammer Part 3

    Narrative Improvisation

    One of the perks of using Tarot cards is that they can signal an opportunity for a change in narrative, or even a change in narrator. Many of the Tarothammer options listed below suggest opportunities for what is termed narrative improvisation. Narrative Improvisation is simply when, for a short time, the player (or perhaps the GM) takes control of determining what occurs, rather than the dice or GM caveat. It may be as brief as a single action, where the player determines whether or not they are successful at striking their enemy and how hard, or whether or not they are able to find an inn open on a rainy night.

    How long the character assumes narrative control is up to the GM. At the minimum, it is suggested that the player gets to determine the outcome of a single act or what constitutes a single Test. They may have a sentence or two to describe what happens. The player is given great latitude, and they should not abuse it. A GM reserves the right to deny a character the opportunity should it be central to the story. On the other hand, the GM can allow a player total narrative control of all that happens for the rest of a round, or to describe what is going on around them. This gives the player an opportunity to introduce some event that they would like added to the story. Clearly stating that the character finds a bag full of gold is abuse and can be disregarded by the GM.
    There is really one rule when a player assumes narrative control: the player asserting narrative control should show respect to their fellow players, the GM, and themselves. By extension, this means that a character asserting narrative control cannot make the conscious decision to kill a fellow PC, although a whole range of lesser injuries, maladies, and tragedies may befall them if in the spirit of the game. A player may kill off their own PC during a narrative improvisation, especially fitting for a heroic end. A GM may also kill off a player if they have narrative improvisation, but this is strongly discouraged, it is best to allow the dice (or Tarot cards used as dice) to do the deed then to arbitrarily slay the character.

    There are a number of options listed below for when to use narrative improvisation a bewildering number, and it should be clear from the onset who gets to determine the narrative improvisation in each case. If a GM so desires, most of the narrative improvisation can be carried out by them, although if they GM wishes for the players to take a more active role, then the GM may decide that the players will carry out most of the Narrative Improvisation.

    Using Tarot Cards in WFRP

    The following are a few proposals for using Tarot cards as part of or all of the mechanic, relying upon a lot of options and different degrees of integration.

    Dice With a Tarot Twist

    Youve invested a lot of money in your dice. Youre attached to them. You have some dice that always roll what you want. Whatever your reason, most people are not willing to give up that old role play staple: the dice. Yet Tarot cards can still be integrated into the game. A few suggestions follow:

    Roll your dice as you normally would for all tests and damage. The Tarot cards are only there for accent, when something strange or bizarre happens. In this case, it is suggested that you only use the Major Arcana since their significance is readily apparent and there are fewer cards to have to worry about.

    The cards can be consulted for any or none of following reasons:

    -A double is rolled: 11, 55, of 00. The results are dealt with as normal, but a Tarot card is pulled to not only give the event a remarkable success, but a context in which to consider it.
    -Whenever a character wants to use their luck, they can ignore a roll and draw a Major Arcana card, and they must then interpret the card to assess what exactly has occurred.
    -When a character spends a Fate Point, a Major Arcana is pulled to reveal what the will of the gods or the whimsy of luck may offer the character.

    Tarot As Dice

    There may be something more intriguing about lifting up a card, with its title and image that is more appealing than rolling a little chunk of plastic. Using cards instead of dice does not necessarily entail a radical departure from standard role playing, instead it can be a nice but perfectly acceptable alternative to dice rolling. A d4, d6, d10, and d100 roll is relatively easy to simulate, particularly with the Minor Arcana. In this case, it is best to use two decks, the Minor Arcana for most dice results and the Major Arcana for special accents.

    Using the Tarot as Percentile

    Most of the Minor Arcana cards already have a 1 to 10 value (Ace to 10) and thus instead of rolling a die, a card is pulled first for the tens and a second card is pulled for the ones. Should one of the face or Court Cards be pulled (King, Queen, Knight or Page), there are a couple of options to choose from:

    -The card is ignored and another placed over it

    -The card calls for the play of a Major Arcana to be played over it. If the Court card appeared in the tens location, the effect is dramatic, if in the ones then the effect is more subdued. Either the GM or player pulling the card should choose and interpret the result in light of the Major Arcana card.

    -The percentile cards are ignored, and the result is improvised by one of the players at the table. If the Court card appears in the tens place, the effect can be dramatic; if in the ones place then the effect should be more subdued. If the Court card revealed was a King, then the GM determines what happens. If a Queen, the player performing the act or drawing the cards decides. If a Knight, then the player to the right decides, and if a Page, the player to the left. It should be noted that in such improvisation, no other player can kill off a player at worse they can be incapacitated. Only the GM or the card-drawing player may make such a decision.

    If used to determine WS and BS rolls, simply reverse the tens and ones place like you normally would for a roll to determine hit location. If a Court card results in narrative improvisation, those doing the improvisation should decide the hit location.

    Using the Tarot as d10

    The d10 is easy to use, even for a procedure such as character generation. It is best to use the Minor Arcana alone and simply follow the instructions for percentile, but draw only one card.

    Tarot d6 (& d3)

    Tarot d6 is a little trickier to do, but it is possible. It is possible to simply draw and discard until a card with the value from Ace (1) to 6 are found, but most of the cards are usable as d6. A card with a value higher than six is still usable: a 7 becomes a 1, 8 a 2, and so on, with the Page becoming a 5 and the Knight a 6. On the Tarot table, each card is listed with its associated d6 value for ease. The exceptions are the two last Court cards: the King and Queen. If these are drawn, players can either ignore the card and just draw another, or on a King result the GM can determine the numerical result or improvise and on a Queen result the player drawing the card gets the opportunity. A d3 result is easily attained by simply having the d6 result.

    Tarot d4

    Using the Minor Arcana, it is exceedingly easy to get a d4 result, simply ignore any number and just look at the Suite. A Cup is 1, the Wand or Staves is 2, the Sword is 3, and the Pentacles or Coins is 4. Alternately a GM can use the value of the cards (Ace, 5, 9 = 1; 2, 6, 10 = 2; 3, 7, Page =3; 4, 8, Knight =4). If a GM chooses the later scheme, they can allow the King and Queen cards to be narrative improvisation cards as in the d100, d10, and d6 above.

    Tarot Part V

    Incorporating the Major Arcana

    When using the Tarot to simulate dice, the Major Arcana can complicate things considerably. It is possible to add them to the deck as well and use them with numerical values, since on many decks the Major Arcana are numbered. On the Tarot table, all the Major Arcana are given d10 and d6 values if a GM wishes to use them as well. If using d10 or d100, two of the Major Arcana do not have values they can be ignored or used as narrative improvisation cards, the Fool can be used by the player, the World gives the privilege to the GM. Likewise, if using the d6, the Fool, Sun, Judgment/Last Judgment, and The World cards can be ignored or used as narrative improvisation cards.

    For those who wish to use the Major Arcana as accents while using the Minor Arcana as randomizers, there are a number of options they can choose from.

    -They can be used as described above under Dice With a Twist using any of the options listed there.

    -A character can choose their Suite to represent them. Should they draw percentile cards that are of the same Suite as their chosen Suite, a Major Arcana is immediately pulled to resolve the action, with the narrative improvisation done by either the player or GM (this should be determined before play)

    -Each player and the GM can choose one Major Arcana as their card to symbolize their character or their role to play. The Major Arcana cards representing the players (and possibly the GM) then mix their cards in with the Minor Arcana. Should that card be played, regardless of who draws it, the player indicated by the card carries out the action using narrative improvisation.

    -For a very narrative driven campaign, the Major Arcana can devoid of any use as a randomizer every time a Major Arcana is drawn, it triggers a narrative improvisation. For those considering this option, it should be noted that roughly every third card will result in a game that moves far from the random die roll and the majority of results will be narrative improvisation.

    -The GM adds a Plot card to the Minor Arcana deck. They may hold it aloft, a card symbolic of their adventure, although the GM is not obliged to reveal how exactly that card is significant. When the Plot card the GM has designated is pulled from the Minor Arcana deck, the GM then uses narrative improvisation to advance the plot or resolve an action in a manner that the plot is advanced. On the other hand, something may occur off scene that changes the plot dramatically, and the card is merely an obscure form of foreshadowing.

    Complete Tarot Narrative

    Easily the most radical of all, the Tarot cards are not used to determine the die values, instead when an action is carried out, a single card is pulled and based upon the situation the GM or players improvise. No rolls are made, no damage rolled; everything is determined by the characters abilities, the situation, and the cards significance and whether or not it is reversed. This style of play is only recommended for the brave and experimental, those interested in diceless mechanics, or those with considerable experience with story telling and improvisation games.

    Advice on how to run such a game is far beyond the scope of this article, but some guidance can be offered, and those looking for more information should look into the pioneers of this genre such as Theatrix, Once Upon a Time, Amber, Saga, and Noblis for further guidance.

    Some suggestions:
    -Everyone should have a reference sheet with the significance of each card listed on it.

    -Everyone should be familiar with the meaning of the cards, or the room well lit enough so that everyone can easily consult their chart.

    -When a person has narrative control should be established before the game ever begins. The player drawing the card usually has control, although some exceptions may exist, for example they pull a card that represents another player (as described in one of the options under Incorporating the Major Arcana in the Tarot as Dice section). Normally the GM gets to assert narrative control if a player spends a Fate Point.

    -Characters only have narrative control to describe their characters actions, unless GM decides otherwise or incorporates a mechanic to allow characters to gain narrative control.

    • Rich Stokes
      November 2006
      I had a game I was working on a few years back that used Tarot as the main task/conflict resolution mechanic. The game was called Urban Mythos (and yes, this was years before WotC released Urban Arcana). The setting was the modern world with magic which was all out in the open, well understood and more than a little passe. It was set in The City, which was the capital of The Kingdom. The feel I was aiming for was along the lines of "Lock, Stock and Two Smoking Wands of Fireball" or "What if Clive Barker and China Mieville wrote The Sweeny?"

      I wrote the system with Tarot Cards for 2 reasons. First, the cards lent this really neat feel that the characters were gambling. Second, Tarot cards really evoked the magic aspects of the game. One of the main themes of the setting was the way that the arcane had become the mundane. What could be more appropriate for that than mass produced Tarot cards?

      The cards worked in two ways. Simultaneously as a dice substitute and a storytelling tool.

      The short version goes like this:

      Characters have attributes and skills. There were 4 attributes (Mental, Physical, Social and Magical) and are rated from 1 to 5. They also have fairly broad skills, which again, are rated up to 5. When you attempt an action, you decide with the GM which attribute and which skill (if any) are the most appropriate. you draw that many cards from the deck. You take the highest suit card, counting Aces as 1, Knights as 11, Princes as 12, Queens as 13 and Kings as 14. Compare this to the difficulty set by the GM. If you get equal or more, you succeed. If you get less you fail. Each Trump (Major Arcana) in your hand can be used to add two to the value of the highest card as long as you narrate a Deus Ex inspired by either the name or picture on the Trump.

      For example, Blake is being chased by a pair of goons with cricket bats. There's a fence at the end of the alley and he needs to get over that before the thugs reach him to get away. He has a slightly above average 3 in Physical and a 2 in athletics. The GM sets the Difficulty to 13, since it's fairly hard as he's under pressure.

      His player draws 5 (3+2) cards. He gets:

      Ace of Wands
      Wheel of Fortune
      Prince of Swords
      The World
      7 of Coins

      The highest suit card is a Prince, worth 12: Not quite good enough. Blake decides to use the Wheel of Fortune for an extra +2. The player states that, fortunately, there is a plank of wood leaning up against the fence that he can use for a boost up to help him get over. His new total is 14 and he succeeds.

      There is also a mechanic for extra successes by the number of times you can beat the target of a test, using each card in your hand only once.

      For example, if Blake had only needed an 11 for that test, he could have beaten the difficulty twice: once with just the Prince and once with the 7 plus both trumps (7+2+2=11). That would equal 2 successes.

      The second way the tarot worked tied into the character's advantages and disadvantages. Every character had one Positive and one Negative character trait. these were basically the same as Advantages/Disadvantages, Merits/Flaws or Edges/Hindrances from other games. But each one had a trump card that went with it.

      For example, Blake has an air of respect that means people are more likely to take him seriously. To represent this, the player chooses The Emperor as Blake's Positive Trump. He also has a disadvantage which is an attraction to the "wrong kind of woman". The player chooses The Lovers as Blake's Negative Trump.

      Whenever a player draws his Positive Trump and uses it in a test, he can add 4 instead of 2 as long as he can narrate a way in which the advantage comes into effect.

      Whenever a player draws his Negative Trump, regardless of whether he plays it or not, something bad happens. This does not mean a failure, but does make the character's life more complicated in some way.

      For example, Blake is searching a talismonger's workshop for information on a client. Suddenly he hears some movement and realises that someone else is in the workshop too. Hiding, he realises that it's an enforcer from a local mobster, a nasty piece of work Tainted called Sabre-Tooth Tony. Blake decides to bluff it out. He grabs a nearby magic sword that looks really impressive and steps boldly into the room with Tony, trying to look as threatening as possible.

      Blake's player draws his Social (3) plus Bullshitting (3) for a total of six cards. The GM draws for Tony, using Tony's Mental (2) plus Balls (4) to see if he can tell that Blake's actually nowhere near as tough as he's making out. Tony gets a total of 14. Blake's highest suit card is a Prince (11) but his hand includes both The Emperor and The Lovers. So Blake succeeds, with style and panache because his "Air of Respect" kicks in with The Emperor for a +4 and also his other trump (The Lovers) adds another +2 for a total of 17. Tony decided discretion is the better part of valour and bolts from the workshop. But the Negative Trump came up for Blake. Since the card, The Lovers isn't really appropriate here the GM gets a bit more creative. The opposite of love is hate, so the GM decides that Tony is going to see Blake as a threat from now on. Blake's life is going to be a little more complicated from now on...

      Not the most original system ever created and I'll probably never do any more work on it now that I've read Mortal Coil.

      Posted By: Vaettyrthe possibility that cards could have a different impact or meaning based onwhere you place themseems totally rockin' to me. I'm still trying to figure out how to incorporate that into a game

      Does anyone have any ideas on that?

      My feeling, as someone who has done little more than look at a Tarot deck and read that Calvino book, is that an ideal pattern-based tarot scenario goes something like this:

      During a scene or unit of conflict, players take turns laying out cards in relation to one another.
      Certain patterns exist which govern some important aspect of a conflict, whether it be means or ends. There are enough patterns that it is not always immediately obvious what pattern players are developing.
      Interpretation of the individual narrated events is determined by the card played and its relation to existing cards, but not to the pattern.
      At any point where the cards are arranged in a recognized pattern, the conflict can be considered finished -- at this point the meaning of the pattern itself is applied and the cards are all reinterpreted or twisted or otherwise shifted to fit this emergent framework.

      The goals are:

      * Cards have two meanings, one in regards to an individual event and one in regards to the overall conflict. This means that playing a card to do something awesome in the moment may end up destroying your goal long-term.
      * Constantly shifting meaning on the conflict level, keeping the outcome generally in doubt.
      * Tension among players as they both attempt to achieve their individual goals and build towards an interesting pattern. A conflict that ends without a pattern would have lessened effects. Alternately, a pattern that uses a subset of the played cards could be used instead.

      One possibility is to have enough patterns so that players must always end their turn with the cards in a 'pattern', further limiting possibilities of play and guaranteeing that the conflict could be 'done' on any turn.

      I also feel like something based on dominoes could be interesting as a play constraint.
