    \n It's gonna be tough, because individual play groups vary so widely. So polling techniques like those used for elections might not be useful. Like, I could give a fair stab at finding every single gamer in Providence, Rhode Island, or say at least 50% of them. (My gaming roots there are deep but a little stale, though it is a fairly small town.) The problem is, I have no confidence that the statistics from there would say anything at all meaningful about nationwide gaming statistics, let alone worldwide.

    (Let me know if you want me to try though.)

    GenCon demographics *might* be a good indicator of female participation in gaming and related hobbies, but then there's still the problem that you're only getting "female gamers who go to Cons," and many gamers don't go to cons.

    Essentially, you need to find a way to deal with all the confounding variables.

    Matt

    \n Just some anecdotal evidence...

    I found while LARPing, that the ratios of females to males tended closer to even levels than any of the table-top groups I've been involved in. It may be something to do with the chance of dressing up in costume.

    As a further boundary push in that direction, do you consider something like the SCA to be an overblown LARP?

    I'd be interested in seeing the demographics of the other extremes of the hobby as well, from miniatures gaming, mainstream table-top, indie table-top, old school miniatures/wargaming, through to LARPing, theatreform, parlor style gaming and other specific categories of gaming. It would be interesting to see this Age-wise/gender-wise/ethnicity/socio-economic...to see if the "teenage middle-class nerdy white male" is as prevalent as the stereotypes would make out.

    From my experience, very few gamers meet all four of these stereotype categories.

    Again...just anecdotal evidence.

    \n When I started in the hobby in '81 (I was twelve), my group was me and four girls. The more athletic boys went out and played football every recess, but I usually hung out in the media center with the girls who were mostly there to trade stickers (big at the time). Flirting led to friendship and friendship led to me introducing four of them to TFT & D&D. For all I knew at the time, role-playing games were "that thing all the 'sticker girls' play with that weird kid." I don't think I played rpgs with another guy until I was fifteen.

    Most of my groups since have had exactly one woman in them, but for the last few years there hasn't been a woman in any of my regular groups. That's something I really regret and miss. I mark it down to age demographics and competing priorites.
