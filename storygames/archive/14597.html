\n Games are social. When we're at a convention, and someone stops to look at the thing we're playing, I want to invite them to join. Even if it's just for a few minutes.

I can't do this in Fiasco, because the relationship map is drawn at the start. I can't do it in Apocalypse World, because the Hx is done at the start. I can't do it in most games, because character creation takes too long. I can do it in Cthulhu Dark, because I designed it that way, but I really want it to happen in my other favourite games.

Please get on this.

    \n The challenge isn't just mechanics. It's that if you walk in late, you don't know what's already happened in the fiction. You don't know things the other players know, so it's harder for you to make something that fits whether you know it or not. You have a higher risk of creative veto because "oh, you didn't know about this other thing that already happened that makes your thing make no sense." In Microscope, a new player could walk up to the table on any turn, sit down and start playing. But I desperately recommend against it, because they would have no way to build something that worked with what had already been created.

    To back up even farther: gaming is about creating this shared fictional space, where we all have basically the same idea of what is going on in our agreed upon make believe. So games often start slow and pick up speed as we all become more confident of what is true, what is important, etc. Coming in late is much like starting again from the beginning, but even worse because everyone else knows what you don't.

    If you wanted to actively pursue this model, make something where the ignorance of late arrival is an advantage. Embrace that they are clueless, that everyone else at the table knows things they don't, don't try to gloss over it.

    The downside is that you usually can't be sure you'll have walk-ins, so it might be tough to play a game where it was a central concept.

    \n I don't think it's just about the fiction.

    In Apocalypse World, you can slot someone in, but you miss lots of the cool stuff that happened at the beginning. In Fiasco, you can only ever be an NPC. (In Lacuna, InSpectres 3:16 and so forth, it's much easier to slot someone in.)

    There's two things. Firstly, I think we've moved towards frontloading games with relationships and worldbuilding. There's something cool about that, but I don't want it to be the only way. You can come in late to a Diaspora game, but you'll never be involved to the same extent: none of the planets will be yours. Wouldn't it be more fun, in Diaspora, to point at a bystander and go "Hey, you, design a planet"? Or, in Fiasco, to say "Here's our playset. Take these dice. Work out some connections with other people. We could do with another Object."?

    Secondly, I think many games demand an exact chunk of your time. Fiasco, say, plays through in about two hours. But what if I don't have two hours? What if I want a one hour game? Or what if someone wants to drop in for five minutes? Or someone's here for the next hour, but then they have to leave? I want games where you can slot people in and out, but still make their contribution meaningful. I want them to build the world too.

    \n I think your goal is a good one, Graham, but I want to counter your argument as well -

    I'm not convinced - a roleplaying game session isn't a round of Halo, right?

    We've been playing Cthulhu Dark for an hour. We're all deeply invested, we've all made substantial creative contributions, we're all working toward something meaningful and fun together. We've bonded as a group. You can't just hand that to some jackass like giving them a turn at the console - including a new person means grinding the game to a halt and integrating them into the thing you've been building together for two hours. More selfishly, it means surrendering some authorship and spotlight time as well. Will they kill your game? They've already made it worse.

    I think this is one reason why people get insular and weird when others stumble up and want to know what you are doing and if they can join in.

    So the wonderful RPG that is as casual and free as a friendly Halo tournament is inclusive, but loses all the good things exclusivity offers.
