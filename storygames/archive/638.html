bq. The player picks up the dice, "I want to roll dice to discover that he is fucking lying!"

In a different "incredibly productive":http://www.story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=614 discussion, this came up. And it got me wondering: in Nar heavy games with the whole conflict resolution thang, this might be within the scope of the game as written. The ultimate extension of this sort of reasoning is "nuh-uh, cuz I'll roll higher than you."[1]

On the one hand, I'm awestruck by the flexibility and agency afforded to ??Donjon?? style "he's lying." That's cool. At the same time, I can see a couple of decent arguments against allowing it wholesale.

* The result can be very silly, since most narrative decisions are made in the moment. Rather than there being a shining plot thread, the story follows the thread of a herd of cats.

* Barring particularly masochistic play,[2] play becomes very much about wish-fulfillment. There's a different, equally interesting story that comes out of "he's telling the god's honest truth."

* Finally, what's the GM doing in a game where you can roll dice to make statements false? On some level I see an analogy to PC de-protagonism. If you're character is "your guy" and there's a line in the sand around him, then doesn't the GM have an equivalent line? If not, why not play sans GM? Not that there's anything wrong with that, but my humble self sees a conflict between "roll to make him be lying" and GM agency.

    - Provide adversity for protagonists
    - Establish boundaries of imaginary content (define situation)
    - Rules interpretation (referee)
    - In-game-world time management

    - Authority over internal plausibility
    - Social manager of who gets to speak when

    Bringing things back to the original example, I'd handle that by exerting MY agency to decide what the NPC is lying about. Everyone lies for all kinds of social reasons. I wouldn't regard a challenge of "Liar!" as a way of players getting to say, "The opposite of what he said it's true." I would read it more as a statement of, "Oooh, juicy, make this more complicated," or as a statement of, "Nope, not that, try something else," depending on the social context.

    I'm perfectly happy to give the players agency in the game, and some degree of authority over the setting and the NPCs, but when it comes down to it, if I'm acting as a GM then I have to be able to run a plot effectively for them. That means that I have to have some kind of mental model of what's going on in the story. If the players say, "He's lying!" then I'll choose a way for him to be lying which inspires me to make good plot for them.

    (Of course, as we discussed in the other thread, my players almost never consider that someone might be lying. But a girl can dream!)

    Since folks seem to be discussing it, I'll add that we're sharing the role of "authority over internal plausibility" with players, with multiple GMs, and also with someone who isn't actually a player or a GM. We've got a dedicated scribe for our Rome game; if you don't register your actions with her, they never happened, and she's our repository of all wisdom about, "Who did what, when, and what did it mean?" She has a huge amount of authority over the fictional world we've created - and at the same time she has very little authority in other ways, in that if she exerts that authority in ways that upset the rest of the group, she could be removed from the group without destroying the game.

    I think that games can share these things in very complicated ways, particularly when you consider that the final expression of autonomy is in, "Screw this!" The person (or people) who is most necessary to the group is ultimately the final arbiter of authority, since they can threaten to nuke the game, though they might or might not be the GM.

    Lemme bring in some teacher-fu, here. I used to teach reading to middle school (13yo) and high school (16yo) kids. Reading is definitely a skill, and I think both Judson and Ben will agree there. However, it's not a simple skill and it's arguably not a single skill. Recognizing letters, putting them together to make words, and putting the words together to make sentences (and I'm skipping steps in there) is one skill, the very basics of reading. However, if that is all you can do, you arguably cannot really read.

    The next stage of the skill is being able to string those sentences together into rhetorical arguments and stances. Without being able to do this, the reader cannot understand anything longer than a sentence. A lot of my kids were at this level. They simply could not parse a full paragraph, let alone an entire article or story. This is also a skill -- and it doesn't end there, either.

    Once you can comprehend whole blocks of text, you can move on to understanding those stories and books in the context of other stories and books -- and films and music and other things, but most importantly, you can begin to understand them in context of real life. The ability to draw comparisons between the experiences of a character encoded in text and your experience that morning is also a skill, and requires some very specific and not terribly easy mental processes. This is also the point at which the reader can actually enjoy what he's reading -- this is when you can sympathize with the character or you can enjoy a specific genre. Until you have this particular reading skill, in very real ways, you cannot enjoy reading. I don't doubt that opera is very similar in this regard.

    Now, in regards to gaming I think we're seeing some confusion between what we mean by "skills necessary to play" and "skills necessary to enjoy." In a game of something like Amber, players really need very little to play, given a competent GM. While other players may do some rather technically complicated things, such skills are not necessary for all players to play. However, there is a whole nother level of skill required for the player to actually enjoy the experience, and this includes a lot of those 'optional' skills and the very specific mental processes of buying in and investing in the contributions of others. Again, a good GM can guide a newbie player with the bare minimum of "necessary" skills to develop the "optional" skills in play, and thus a satisfied player is born. In the mean time, I suspect the novelty of the activity and the raw socialization keeps the new player coming -- not to mention the enjoyment that he sees others are having.
