    \n I helped make the tie happen - voting for YARPG and Schizonauts. I was one of the initial reviewers of Schizonauts and was very impressed with its ability to capture the atmosphere of Phillip K Dick. The fluidity of identity mechanics are very sharp.

    YARPG does a great job of capturing its genre, too, and I really like the reincorporation mechanics as a means to shape the story. The game reads well and is very approachable.

    I also really liked Scheherezade: The Sultan's Palace and What Remains for their focus less on explicit role-playing than on storytelling and construction of a story through the accretion of memories.

    \n I also made the tie happen by voting for both YARPG and Schizonauts. I admit I voted for the games on entirely different merits, and the second vote felt strangely reluctant -- but I do think Schizonauts left most other games in the dust in terms of completeness and playability. (Not to mention the premise.)

    The YARPG vote was more of a 'man, there's just something about that kid' thing. What I like most about the game is not the reincorporation, roles, etc., though they all tie into it. What I like is the basic premise about how the Normal becomes Strange through a transformation of mundane reality -- not through adding fantastic elements but by making the elements that already exist fantastic. That process has a ton of appeal for me, and is also I think a really important part of many of the best YA titles. The Pinkwater reference merely sealed the deal.

    --

    My favourite game was probably still The Novikov Principle, by John A. Such a great combination of dry wit, satire and really fascinating mechanics that evoke the central premise of the game (time travel and causality.) I wish it had made it into the top 8 so that more people would have (probably) read it.
