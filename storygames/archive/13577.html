\n I often see the description "good game, but not for everyone" in reviews of RPGs. Frex. I am curious as to what that means.

Have you ever written a review of an RPG and included this term in the review? If so, can you explain to me what you meant by it. Can you provide a counter example of an RPG "for everyone"?

Have you ever simply described an RPG as "not for everyone" -- whether that's good or bad doesn't matter. Can you expand on what you meant by that? What are your goto "for everyone" games?

Is "for everyone" the default for RPGs and thus only a handful of games need to be labeled with the warning "not for everyone"?

Thread request: If you don't use the term, save your speculation until after I get a few responses.

    \n While I often attach varied meanings to the phrase "not for everyone," I can say that I often use it to mean "you won't get it" when talking to a group of people who, well, won't get it.

    But then, I'm a condescending ass.

    My "nice" use of the phrase is almost exactly the same as the way I use the phrase "acquired taste" when referring to beer, food, or metal; I use it as a shorthand way of saying "This is very different than what you're used to, and you may not like it as a result of that. You may also just not like what this thing does, period. But I think it's cool, so I think you should try it."

    It's like the challenge on the label of Arrogant Bastard, basically. At least, that's how I wield the phrase. The game in question can seem off-putting, and you may have to want to like it.

    What I definitely do not do is use "not for everyone" when I mean "this is crap." If I think it's crap, I'll say it, and won't recommend it.

    As for the notion of an RPG that is "for everyone," I really don't think any RPG is "for everyone." Once upon a time, I think RPG's really attempted to do that, but in recent years, there's been a growing movement towards using specific games to guide specific kinds of play. D&D does this game, InSpectres does this game, Danger Patrol does the best damn game ever, and so forth. So I see the "not for everyone" as an artifact of phrasing, from an era in its twilight.

    However, I think the "for everyone" descriptor could be used to describe those games which are the most accessible and have the lowest required investment. Almost anyone can pick up a game like Danger Patrol and have a ball - even people who don't really play RPG's all that often. The game is quick and punchy, and because the basic kind of story is both simple and widely understood, almost anyone who was ever consumed entertainment-oriented media can do it.

    \n Oh, this is a really good phrase to examine.

    I don't recall using it in a written review, but I've dropped it in speech a couple of times. I guess it works as a disclaimer (if a fairly useless one), but when I've used it it's more as a gauge on enthusiasm. For example, I'm describing a game I love to friend and I'm pretty sure he'll like it, but I don't want to appear a zealot and turn him off to the game before he even plays it (some people are really sensitive and suspicious about zealotry of any kind), so I blurt out that phrase that oh so subtlety suggests, "look! I'm not an extremist! I respect that other people have valid opinions that don't agree with mine!" And hopefully it will be enough to erase his own small concern about trying it and not liking it and then having to face me. I also slightly invalidate the phrase by following it with "but," as in, "it's not for everyone, but I think you will really enjoy it."

    \n I don't use the phrase often. When I do it's usually when there are polarizing elements in the game's design that are likely to trip hot-button dislike reactions from certain people. I probably also use the phrase as a weaselly caveat when I dislike a game (or someone I know does) and I want to acknowledge that opinion without making it the central topic of conversation.     \n I used to say it a lot before I realized that aesthetic preferences are so specific in people, and so varied among people, that it really doesn't need to be said at all. Now I only really say it when a game hits an extremely narrow band of preference, or a preference that is not widely supported anymore. For example, Alpha Omega's combat system is extremely detailed and finicky to a degree not widely supported anymore, (though at one point it was fairly common, as I recall.) So when I say "it's not for everyone", I really mean "not a lot of games do this right now". The highly detailed social conflict system in Burning Empires is another example. There's not much out there even approximating it.

    \n To me, "not for everyone" means 'I am aware of significant subsets of people that I expect to be offended/turned off by some aspects of this product'. Breaking it down a bit more, I think that it really comes down to 'I expect that certain people will actively dislike this product'.

    So Britney Spears (for example) isn't something I'd apply the "not for everyone" disclaimer to. That doesn't mean that I think that "everyone" will like her music, it's just that there's nothing challenging or offensive about it, so people will mostly settle on like or not-like on it. However, something like Throbbing Gristle is rather more difficult listening, and many people will actively dislike or be offended by it. It's "not for everyone".

    In RPGs I'd usually expect to see the term used for difficult/challenging settings. Seeing it applied to mechanics is initially surprising to me, although on reflection looking at how certain mechanics have fallen out of favour over the years (e.g. table look ups) I suppose there's something to it.

    \n I have used variations of the phrase in my game reviews. What I often mean is the premise or setting of the game, usually something so tied in with the game that to remove it makes it not that game, can make people not want to play.

    A more broad example: When I wrote a review of Godlike I mentioned the characters felt real and vulnerable because the rules made them feel like that (a credit to the game). But coupled with it being set in World War II, which I knew people who had actually served in WWII including my grandfather, the setting felt too real for me and was a bit disturbing. I basically said that those who don't like the war genre or who have connections to those who've fought in war might find the game disturbing.

    In my opinion, the game isnt for everyone. Take something like D&D or Faerie Tale or DC Heroes that is divested enough from reality to be "safe" and can be scaled in terms of violence and subject matter without losing the game, and I feel it is a game for everyone. Doesn't mean everyone is going to like it, but I feel it is accessible.

    I would describe games like Maid (which I love), the Shab Al-Hiri Roach (which I really like) and Steal Away Jordan (which I will never play but admire) as "not for everyone." The Maid and SAJ for the subject matter, the Roach more because it is a tricky game and requires (in my opinion) a bit of skill to play.

    I will try and expand on this if people want.

    Edit: Hit post, then thought of this. When I say "not for everyone" in a review, I am meaning a person could have a negative reaction toward the game and roleplaying. Not a "i don't like this," but a more viceral "that is sick" or "why the hell would anyone find this fun?" kinda reaction. And since I write reviews with an eye toward non-gamers, I want to avoid that feeling.

    \n Thank you so much for taking the time to respond!

    It seems, from reading your thoughts, that we use the phrase as a shorthand or code.

    Some people use it as code for, "[specific person] doesn't like stuff like this."

    Some people use it as code for, "I'm unsure how I feel about some of the parts of this game."

    Some people use it as code for, "This game is different than the D&D/Whitewolf paradigm."

    Some people use it as a content warning, "I find these materials offensive."

    And some people use it to deflate overtones of zealotry in their pitch to play the game with friends.

    When I read the phrase online, my first reaction is, "Well who is the game for?" And my close second reaction is, "What's a game for everyone?" Those two questions are never satisfactorily answered for me in discussions about games. The game is for people who enjoy this game. No amount of generalizing can make someone like a game, but ill-considered ominous statements can certainly turn someone away from trying a game.

    And we all know the answer to my second question: No game is for everyone. Everyone knows this. It is self-evident upon even the slightest reflection by the most casual observer, and a cold hard reality for those of us deeply ensconced in gaming culture.

    \n Luke,

    Some games manifestly appeal to a greater audience than others. This is true of all entertainment. While it is trivially true that "no game is for everyone", in this specific context the phrase does have a use.

    There are games people want to play that they know a large proportion of other people won't enjoy because they have content much more liable to offend than most games. I give you The Book of Unremitting Horror. It's not for everyone. That doesn't mean I'm unsure how I feel about it, or I find it offensive. I am sure how I feel about it, do not find it offensive, and enjoy playing it. It really does mean I think (and know from the evidence) that many people do find it offensive. I don't want them to buy it. Sometime people mean what they say. Add that to your list.

    That's not to say that reviewers (and in particular playtesters) sometimes use the reactions of their perceived audience to project their own feelings. We specifically ask playtesters not to worry about what any category of person outside their game group thinks of a game. This was to stop people saying "I think novices would find this difficult" and "some people might find this offensive." I don't want to know if they think it's not for everybody; I want to know if it's for them.

    Finally, you say "ill-considered ominous statements can certainly turn someone away from trying a game." That may be true, but saying that the BOUH or Graham's Purist adventures are not for everybody, then describing what the games are about stop people playing and buying games they aren't going to like. It's managing expectations. In most cases it ends up selling more copies rather than reducing sales. People like not to be part of everybody when it comes to culture.

    \n Huh...I think positively answering "this game is for X type player" is a million times harder to answer than negatively answering "this game is not for everyone." I'm even thinking it's kind of a mine field to get into negative specifics -- "this game is not for players who are into X type play". At least in my own head, I've got too many shorthand assumptions going to really get my point across that way.

    I'm gonna think through it with an example I know well.

    Like, if I explained that "BW is not for players who don't like the idea of failing on purpose," I'm not even sure that that's the point. In my own head, when I say "some players aren't on board with the idea of failing forward," I'm also referring to a general succeed-at-all-costs sort of immersive player who advocates only on behalf of his character's interests, and probably cares only about getting wealthier and more powerful. Hell, they're probably pretty immature players. Let's just call them basement-dwelling catpiss fatbeards and be done with it. Yeah, that's a mine field. :-)

    The opposite -- "BW is for players who like the idea of failing on purpose" -- isn't the point either. I don't think any player likes it when their character fails. But a certain kind of player will appreciate that receiving mechanical inducements in return for occasional fictional complications (and then later using those mechanical inducements to wrangle temporary authority by avoiding unwanted complications) results in a more dramatic, literary-style storyline in the game.

    That's a mouthful! "This game isn't for everyone" is so simple! :-)

    \n Alternatively, when almost everyone who uses that phrase uses it to mean something other than its original use...that means the language has moved on.
    \n
    \nThe definition of a word (or phrase) is what meaning pops into a listener's head when a speaker speaks it. If "everyone" has the same or similar meaning pop into their head...then that's what the word means...because words have no intrinsic meaning outside of what people understand when they hear it.
    \n
    \nIf a handful of people hear it one way, while most everyone else hears it another...then that handful can be corrected for getting it wrong. But when 80% of the people believe a word or phrase means a thing...then that word or phrase means that thing. Regardless of what the word or phrase USED to mean. Regardless of what a small slice of the population who continue to adhere to its older definition want it to mean...the language has moved on...slang becomes the new norm...live with it. In fact, one could argue that when the adherents to the old definition continue to use the word the old way, and they represent but a handful...then they are now the ones using it wrong and need to be corrected. Languages change.
    \n
    \nSee also "Ironic".

      Posted By: ValamirCan't be. Words have no meaning outside of useage. Meaning is defined by useage.
      "Evolution isn't a fact, it's a theory!"

      This is entirely the problem with language drift and the re-purposing of words. When you come up with a new word or phrase to describe something for which we already had a word or phrase, that's fine. That's linguistic evolution.

      The problem with co-opting extant words and phrases is that it directly causes inconsistencies and ambiguities in communication. This is especially problematic now that we have vast amounts of permanently archived communications.

      Here's an example. In medieval Europe, the "bushel" referred to several different sizes of measurement depending on your location. In fact, the size of a "bushel" varied across a single country. Yet, the term "bushel" was applied to all of those measurements. As a modern audience, this makes it impossible to actually figure out what the hell was going on back then. The drift in the meaning of that particular word results in an inability to correctly interpret written records. So, when I try to concoct a beer from the 1300's, I have no idea if they're talking about a Winchester bushel, a Northern London bushel, or a Southern London bushel. Or a bushel from anywhere else in Europe.

      That may not seem vital, but the standardization of language is what permits useful, accessible records, particularly in the scientific fields.

      So, while it may not always be significant, the "drift" in the meaning of a particular phrase can be a source of great confusion in any communication, and depending on the severity of the miscommunication, this can have some pretty strong consequences. Like, say, entire swathes of people thinking that "theory" means "conjecture" when referring to evolution, even though a scientific theory is actually a logical framework constructed from a strong body of factual information.

      \n Just wanted to chime in on the whole "begs the question" thing. Saying that the most popular interpretation of a thing is the most valuable interpretation or the most valid interpretation for a person to use completely devalues whole swathes of human culture. If you had to poll the majority of people in the English speaking world, how many people would find any value in something like 'Ode to a Grecian Urn' or think of opera singers when someone says diva or the Blessed Virgin when someone says Madonna... does that mean people who cut their language finer, who get more mileage out of the words they use, should stop getting the value they do from them, or that they should stop trying to get other people to appreciate something that they do about language? To say that anyone's language is wrong is just sloppy and inaccurate. Language, like so many things, just is, and in a lot of ways is out of our control, but to martyr precise usage on the altar of populist appeal is as much of a mistake as claiming that someone else's less differentiated usage is somehow a reflection on them as a person.
