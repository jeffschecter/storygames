\n So, like many of us, I have a special place in my heart for Traveller -- specifically the Classic Traveller rules found in the three little black books. There's something about the rules and the setting implied in the rules that has always turned me on. However, the rules as they stand have never actually seemed to produce the things that I thought they should. It's like the rules teased me with the kind of game I wanted to play, but did not deliver.

For clarity, here's a post I made on RPG.net from a while back that really lays out a lot of the color and themes that I saw in the game. The thread was about whether or not there are "heroes" in Traveller. IMO, it was a bizarre, semantic non-issue. But it got me to clarify what I saw a the PC roles in the setting:\n

I think the posts on this board nail really one of the unique features of this game: that in a sprawling universe of potential adventure, your character still had to get the bills paid; in a universe so big your character could never reach its end, your PCs bones were already getting creaky as he started his life of free-lance adventure; in a stellar empire of infinite possibilities the choices your character had made in his youth limited who he was in his 40's.

This, I think, is an amazing and unique tension for RPGs. It's a completely different set of concerns than found in most RPGs -- which cater to the delightful and high spirited point of view that "All I need to do is keep going and I'll get more powerful and powerful." Traveller was written from the point of a view of adulthood, and most RPGs (and this is neither good nor bad) work from the adolecent view that lacks an awareness of mortality death, decay or limits.

Does this mean there are no heroes in Traveller, by the way? No. The tales of Beowulf, The Lord of the Rings, Malory's Le Morte D'Arthur and other fantastic legends posit the tension between fantastic settings and heroic deads against the encroaching weakening of muscles, the end of ages and life. Star Wars, on the other hand, the model for a lot of RPG sensibility from the mid-seventies onward, possesses exactly that naive-kid-in-a-big-place-stretching-his-muscles-forever sensibility. It's significant, I think, that Traveller was on the way to the printer just as Star Wars hit the screen. It's a pre-Star Wars SF setting. This makes it not better or worse than all the would be "youth" tales -- just different.

So, first think Traveller. Now think, "Clint Eastwood." See? Easy as pie.
\nAs I mentioned on another Traveller thread on Story Games I think "The Man Who Would be King" is a perfect Traveller movie. Replace the mountains that Michael Caine and Sean Connery travel into with a backwater world at the edge of the Imperium, and you have a couple of guys drummed out of the served with big dreams. Other movies that match the feel I'm going for are "Outland," "Seven Samurai," and maybe "Unforgiven," "The Searchers," and "3:10 to Yuma."

The idea of The Frontier is very important to me on this... again, the idea of expanding outward; the tension between civilization and imposing law on lawless lands; the tension between living in a romanticized landscape while needing to eek out a living; and so on...

While I'm still toying around with trying to figure out building the "perfect" Traveller system to get what I want, I doubt I'll ever really get around to it. And The Shadow of Yesterday core system does a lot of things really well in terms of strong, clear, PC driven, narrative mechanics.

One of the things Clinton mentioned was that switching out the Pools does a lot to help evoke setting and color.

Here are the Pools as written from the game:\n
Vigor represents the character's reserves of physical power, wherewithal, and mental toughness. Characters with high Vigor are often known for bulging muscles, scarred faces, calloused hands, the "thousand-yard stare," and crushing strength.

Instinct represents the character's reserves of animal-like reactions, both physical and social. This ranges from cat-like reflexes in combat to pheromone-like sexual attraction. Characters with high Instinct are often known for their graceful motion, penetrating eyes, amazing hearing, stunning appearance, or sexual prowess.

Reason represents the character's intelligence and mental power. This could range from book knowledge to an uneducated, but highly practical mind. Characters with high Reason are often known for their vocabulary, ability to identify plants and animals, skill at games of chance, or power over others.
\nSo, I'm thinking I'd replace "Reason" with "Experience." This is accumulated know-how and wisdom that comes from being around the block a few times.

I think I'd also replace "Instinct" or "Vigor" with "Ambition." The Traveller system had a Social caste built into the character creation process, and I think it's part and parcel of the Age of Empires feel that the game and setting draw on: Sir Richard Burton running around India, Afghanistan and Africa trying to finally prove himself. This would be the Pool of innate drive that helps a character move forward -- whether reflected in stamina, the ability to seduce, sell, con, endure or otherwise keep going. (Maybe it would actually be "Social Ambition" -- codified by a desire for Wealth, Social Standing, marrying well and so on...)

I've only begun toying with this. Does anyone have any other suggestions for switching out pools for the kind of game and color I'm after?

Also, any thoughts on Secrets, Abilities and Keys?

Thanks,

    \n I loved Traveller! I think it's got an incredibly coherant Simulationist design, and I'd be wary of changing it. Your TSoY hack will be quite a different game. To get all Big Model on you, Classic Traveller is about reinforcing the genre, it's about being a gritty space-mercenary who's been round the block a few times. Traveller says "Old Ex Soldiers are great at gaining wealth in a big galaxy". Narrativist Traveller would be about problematising being such a character. It would be about the tension between ambition and weariness, experience and obsolesence.

    Which is a cool game, but very different from Traveller. Some Key ideas:

    Key of Old Faithful:
    When you take this Key, select an item of technology that your character uses, which has some important history attached to it, and which is now somewhat antiquated. For example, this could be an old sidearm from the war, a dented up old spaceship, or a bow and arrow from your tribal homeworld.
    1 XP for every scene in which you use the item.
    3 XP for every time you use that item when there's a clearly superior alternative.
    Buyoff: Trade in the item for a newer model.

    Key of Bitterness (This Key needs some work)
    Your character has an old predjudice from some long-ended war. Choose a group of people (for example a species of alien, or a nation or ethnicity). The group should not be universally despised.
    1 XP every time voicing your predjudice gets you in trouble.
    3 XP for refusing to help a member of the group.
    Buyoff: Help a member of the group without the expectation of reward.

    Key of Old Wounds
    Your character has an old wound, physical or psycological, which impairs their actions. This could be a leg full of shrapnel, a missing arm, or jitters from shellshock.
    1XP Every scene in which you're inhibited by your wound.
    3 XP Every time you lose a significant opportunity because of your wound.
    Buyoff: Have the wound permanently treated.

    It occurs to me that FATE would also be a cool way to do this, and might handle the Simmy goodness of Traveller better. "War Buddies" would be a cool Aspect that could be tagged to have a buddy help you out, or compelled to meet a friend in need. "Old Wound" could be tagged for looking menacing, or for convincing people of your experience, and compelled to hinder all kinds of things.

    Well, Judson, it looks like you and I were pretty much on the same page. I hadn't thought of changing the XP:Advance ratio, and that helped a lot.

    (I will, however, keep the Gift Dice.... I just think they are too valuable to the group dynamic for play.)

    Here's what I've got:


    Character Creation

    All characters start with three Innate Abilities: Adrenaline, Need and Instinct. Set one at a value of 0, one at value of 1, and one at a value of 2.

    Characters start at the age of 18 and work are created in 4 year terms of service. The first time automatically grants three Abilities at Rookie and one Ability at Skilled, as drawn from the appropriate list for the service.

    Abilities Selection Table
    Recruit*..............1 advance
    Skilled*..............2 advances
    Expert*...............3 advances
    Veteran*..............4 advances

    After that, Players are given 4 advances per term of services [EDIT: this might be too much -- maybe 2 Advances per term? See note in a post below] to spend as they see fit, with the following rules:

    * The Ability costs are cumulative - it costs 3 Advances to go from Rookie to Skilled, for example.
    * A Character can't be advanced in the same Ability twice in a row; another Ability must be Advance first.

    If any Ability reaches Veteran or Expert level, something happened during that term of Service that's worth a story. the Player has to tell the story.

    \n Hi Mike,

    I'll be giving your posts a lot of thought, but I think there's a general framework that should be addressed:

    My goal in this is to dig out the pieces that I think worked in the game, and lessen the bits that didn't. So, while the focus is on the original Traveller, the project is about making that Traveller work -- because for whatever reason, it didn't work for me in the past.

    Now, I get that ambitionless Wanderlust is a key concept of the original game. It's called Traveller after all! That the core of the game.

    But I offer that that while it may have been the nub, it's also where the game fell down. At least for me. And lots of people I know. I'm more than willing to believe your experience was different.

    Because the common cycle of the failed Traveller game I've heard for years is: Referee generates subsetor, has a blast. Players generate characters, have a blast. Players and Referee sit down to play. Everyone looks at everyone else expectantly. Players finally say, "What do we do?" The Referee answers, "Anything you want."

    And the came crashes to a halt. Space is too big. The lack of direction overstuffs the imagination. Nothing happens.

    I'm not saying the point of CT wasn't wanderlust. I'm saying, "It wasn't always that helpful to getting a game going."

    For my version, if I need to tweak something to bring out the other parts that I did find interesting, that's what I'm going for. I don't think it's helpful to the Players. I don't think it's helpful to the GM. And, in fact, I think it's harmful.

    Now, part of this is about taste. I have never responded well to characters who just want to get money. I think it brings a raft of problems -- characters that aren't particularly interesting outside of their skill sets, characters that are in one way or another disconnected form anything we might consider "human." (I believe it was a scenario you mentioned here where a PC shot any NPC with a bag of money in the face and took the money. The NPC was offering the money for help. The PC, since he only cared about money, made the wiser and more economical choice.)

    I can say that the whole Traveller trap of having a bunch of amoral or immoral fucks who do whatever illegal or expedient thing that pops into their head really has little interest to me. I'm not saying people don't have fun with it. I'm not saying it wasn't what a lot of the GDW modules were about. I'm saying it bores me.

    I can also say, "I'm a story guy." And in stories being a professional who gets a check, and only a professional who gets a check, is boring. Look at drama, and it's never about how well a guy does his job. From the Iliad to NYPD Blue, what matters is the stuff around the job, how that job affects the stuff, and how the stuff affects the job.

    Finally, I thought the Firefly TV series was lame. It was dull, A-Team-light. I thought Serenity rocked. (No surprise, I'm sure.) I think if you take the pilot of the TV show and then cut directly to the movie, you have a really full evening of a cool SF story. All the other episodes in between (except for a few near the end of the run) are basically fluff. They meander. They wander.

      \n

      \nLike Dro, the Aslan are possessed of a dewclaw as natural weaponry. Works like the Vargr natural weaponry secret, which works like the goblin one.
      \n

      \nAslan are otherwise built well to win physical combats, as well as being instinctual combatants. They gain one die in any conflict.

      \n - Key of Land
      \nMost Aslan are persuing land in one way or another, even if it looks like they're after something else. Gain 1 EX for gaining something that is a step towards gaining land.       \n
      \n***Porozlo***
      \nPorozlo's biggest claim to fame is that it sports the one of the highest populations of sentient beings in the spinward marches: approx. 90 Billion inhabitants. It also has the highest number of polities, with some 80 nations claiming soverignty (and that number is much lower than it once was, due to wars of consolidation). As such it is hard to say any one thing about the people of Porozlo as a whole... players may wish to make up national cultures. But they do all have one thing in common:
      \n
      \n**Porozlo Keys**
      \n - Key of Intollerance
      \nPorozlo inhabitants all tend to think in very local, parochial, terms. Even the members of the other nations of their world are to be suspected... to say nothing of somebody from another planet or system entirely. Gain 1 EX when speaking intollerantly about somebody when they are in earshot. Gain 2 EX for acting against somebody who is not from your culture... simply because they are different. Gain 5 EX for the character getting into a fight due to their intollerance. Buyoff: Be accepting of somebody very different.
      \n
      \n
      \n***Darrians***
      \nAs a species, Darrians are simply humans, and get no special species secrets (pointy ears notwithstanding).
      \n
      \n***Darrian Confederation***
      \nThe Darrian Confederation is a union of worlds controlled by racial and ethinic Darrians. They are allies of the Imperium, and are known for their exceedingly high level of technology.
      \n
      \n**Darrian Confederation Secrets**
      \n - Secret of High Tech
      \nThough probably not really capable of producing a lot of high tech items beyond the ability of Imperial worlds, the Darrians have always hovered around the ability to do so, and one can frequently find examples of their handiwork at this level of technology if one looks hard enough (don't even ask about the Star Trigger). Unsurprisingly, Darrians tend not to allow this technology to get into the hands of non-Darrians. This secret allows the character to obtain tech level 16 equipment (though generally the secret of imbuement or similar rule must be purchased to obtain the specific abilities indicated).
      \n
      \n**Darrian Keys**
      \n - Key of Stoic Aloofness
      \nThe Darrians are not really snobby, but they are stoic to the point where they tend to remove themselves from the trials and tribulations of other races. Gain 1 EX if the character ignores somebody else's problem. Gain 2 EX if the character refuses to deal with somebody else's problem, even when asked nicely. Gain 5 EX if the character allows something bad to happen to something he cares for, simply to show how aloof he is. Buyoff: the character must show that he really does care about somebody else's emotional state.

      I got it. The game is versatile.

      But that's not the question. The question is, "What is the definition of the game? What is the definition of a person in in this game?"\n
      Posted By: Clinton R. NixonChristopher,

      Pools are what make up a person in the game. They are prerequisites for person-hood. In TSOY, without Vigor, Instinct, and Reason, you are not a person. This holds for adventure swords and sorcery, but what about other settings? I could see a game of TSOY set in, say, the Crusades, that used God, Man, and the Devil as pools. Or, a fairly hard sci-fi game with Vision, Craft, and Intuition as pools.

      I'm pretty sure more pools won't work. Two might work pretty well, though - but I haven't tried it.
      \n
      Posted By: Clinton R. NixonChristopher,

      One time, I thought I should write a book on how to customize TSOY for your setting. I don't have time, but here's what you do:

      - Change the pools. *Definitely* change the pools. They define your setting.
      - Ah, the abilities. Only change ones that obviously don't fit. I thought about those more than you'd think.
      - Secrets. Secrets are my sacred cow, and one I should have killed. I'm glad I didn't - I love 'em - but they're not that useful. A handful of them do everything. Use Secret of Imbuement for almost everything, and the Secrets of Blessing, Enhancement, and Specialty for everything else. The species secrets are cool for color - maybe add those in to whatever setting you have.
      - Keys are easily mixed between the core keys and ones you need for your setting. That's pretty easy to do.
      \nSo, the real question is, how does one group want to define the world and the person of their Traveller game. The issue isn't whether or not a TSOY/Traveller hack can handle Wanderlust or Ambition. Of course it can. The issue is only one of emphasis -- what is considered central to the world and to play.

      I'm not convinced this is an issue that has to be nailed down. Your group might be focused in part on Wanderlust. Mine, Ambition. Sweet!

      In the nearby Go Anywhere, Do Anything thread, Simon is working hard to make Wanderlust viable. I think it's a path toward madness. I think there are often our ideals of what we want our RPG experience to be like, and then the reality of what sitting down to play a game actually is. Simon's looking for that experience of reading "The Lord of the Rings." As Joshua Newman points out in that thread, playing an RPG is more like the process of creating the tales of Middle-Earth than bumping into story that's had all the rough edges and process of creativity smoothed out after the fact. The expectation of simply going from world to world is a dear one to all of our hearts -- but how will it work in actual play?

      And more to the point, what else is works that will produce similar effect that actually works? I offer focusing on character drives of one kind or another. These have worked well as Kickers in Sorcerer, the job in Dogs in the Vineyard, Issues in Primetime Adventures and so on. The color of the world is there to support the narrative drive of character based play that I think is almost required when the color can change as swiftly as it does in Traveller. (I'd say that a group committed to building and exploring one world -- going deeper and deeper each week into play -- is a whole different kettle of creative fish, and allowed the possibility of successful Sim play.)

      Further, I find Wanderlust as a motivation, as stated above, kind of dull when contrasted to a character who actually is trying to get somewhere.

      Finally, in the world of play I'm envisioning, Ambition is who these characters are, not Wanderlust. Wanderlust is a symptom, a behavior, yes, but it isn't one of the defining qualities of the characters I want to interact with as a GM.

      Here's some dialogue from "The Man Who Would Be King":


      PEACHY CARNEHAN: Home to what? A porters uniform outside a restaurant and six penny tips from belching civilians for closing cab doors on their blowzy women?

      DANIEL DRAVOT: Not for us thank you. Not after watching Afghans come howling down out of the hills and taking battlefield command when all the officers copped it.

      PEACHY CARNEHAN: Well said, brother Dravot.


      I want THAT. I want the rules to define those guys. I want the rules to define THAT world.

      I want Captain Sir Richard Burton wandering the Congo because he hasn't found the recognition he desires. I want Han Solo working his ass of to get free of his payments so he can be his own man. I want a marine General who wants to take back that world the Imperium considers lost. I want the tensions the the class system of Traveller implies -- not someone who can wander away from his frustrations, but someone who feels them gnawing at him all the time. I want a group of guys bound to each other through blood and sweat, aiding each other in ambitious plans that no one else would help them with or think would work.

      And, if nothing else, Imperial Ages are about Ambition. And I want the rules I want to use for Traveller to key in on that. The Imperial angle was one of the things that drew me to Traveller. But so much of the implied setting was this antiseptic, bureaucratic, paper-pushing cubicle-land. Ugh. No, no, no -- Imperial age. Movies like The Four Feathers, Elizabeth and even The Sea Hawk are the kinds of things I'm talking about. I really feel that the Imperial Age was lost in a lot of the Traveller material, and I want to do anything I can to heighten that.

      Now I understand you want something different. Or at least a different focus. Cool!

      But I don't think this has to be nailed down. I think the Pool descriptions, as Clinton notes, are variables that establish the world and what makes a person a person. In my Traveller, one of the qualities that makes a person a person is Ambition. In your Traveller it's Wanderlust.

      \n I hear ya, Chris. I probably am going a bit overboard, but I'm thinking of this material as more inspirational than actually meant to be included in actual play neccessarily.
