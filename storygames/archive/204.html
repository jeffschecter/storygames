    Once I get into Blackguard, I may mix it up a bit with Quick Draw and some poisoned short swords.

    If you're playing for raw power, don't do this. Instead, wander over to two-handed weapons, including exotics, and shop there. There's just more bang for your buck. Reach, especially, is a good buddy to have.

    Also, remember - stacking effects with damage are what you want. A strength fighter is made to kill now. So, in poisons, you want a high save, high initial damage, con damage specifically - to stack up with the high HP damage you'll do. Secondary effect is immaterial; if you can't kill a foe in ten rounds, you're in trouble anyway.

    Damage, now. That's your watchword with a strength-fighter.

    So what if you go first? Ever dropped an enemy on the first strike? Not very often, and I'm betting it wasn't a major NPC. If you're using a fast weapon, you've already gone and the chance to take advantage of that opportunity goes to someone else. If you're using a slower, higher damage weapon then your options are open longer.

    Hey, Troy.

    All in all, I agree. Do note that weapons don't have initiative in D&D3, so my greatsword is as fast as a dagger.

    Here are the reasons initiative is good in D&D:

    1) It helps enormously in surprise situations, essentially allowing you to get a "retaliatory strike" on your ambusher, as Levi noted.

    2) If you are a giant damage monster, it can matter because you can drop 2-3 weaker enemies in a round. This is doubly true of you cleave. In a party without a serious front line (which this one is), this is enormously helpful.

    3) It gives you more opportunities to ready your actions. Why is this good? Because you can use it to disrupt enemy spellcasters, which is pretty much the goal of every fighter.

    4) Fights in 3.5 rarely go past round 4. The "extra attack" that winning initiative gets you actually matters a great deal in preventing damage pile-up.

    That said, Improved Initiative remains my weakest feat, and if someone has a recommendation to replace it, I'm more than willing to hear it. I'm curious, for instance, about the merits of Bull Rushing, which is not something I've explored at all in previous games.

    Likewise, it is possible (though not likely) that someone could redeem Great Cleave in my eyes. Skill Focus: Use Magic Device might very well also be a good idea.

    Yeah, it basically did. It was kind of a circular process -- very early on there were a couple settings on the table, one a roughed out setting idea I'd had and the other Mystara from the basic D&D products. Then folks started making characters, and at some point in this process we decided not to use either of those settings and to hash one out later. So they went ahead with making characters. The longest "backstory" was a few sentences, and others were about a sentence. Concept type stuff. And then we started hashing out the setting, with these characters already sketched out. The characters were all more-or-less filled in as far as race, class, stats, etc, plus the concept stuff, but in a flexible enough stage that they could be molded as needed during the process of filling in more setting.

    Basically, the characters as they existed at that point and the D&D rules acted as points of reference for setting creation. Each character provided a point of reference, and as new elements were added to setting, e.g., "Ooh, hey, what if arcane magic is evil!" that becomes an additional point of reference, which we then followed down through how arcane magic works in D&D. During play, new points of reference are added, and various connections get canonized either during play or during post-game and between game chit-chat. The network of "canonized points of reference" is centered on the character sheets, a few events from play (especially those that ended up on the character sheets), and D&D rules. The single points of reference with a long shadow is "arcane magic is evil," which itself came from noticing something not present among the characters, so again going back to them as the primary points of reference.

    In introducing his character, Ben has added a new point of reference, with a whole bunch of implications of its own. In particular there's the whole new fact that evil and taint aren't directly connected. You can be of evil alignment and not be tainted, and vice versa, and it's taint that is tied to the dragons and arcane magic, properly speaking, in game mechanical terms. What's key here is that the relationship between evil alignment and taint/corruption was not elaborated before Ben made his Blackguard. Anything that is not elaborated isn't a point of reference yet, hasn't been canonized. We simply hadn't even considered it. Sure, maybe we had assumed evil and taint to be one and the same, and certainly the taint of the dragons and sorcery is evil in a colloquial, English language sense.

    But not necessarily in a game mechanical sense, you see. We never would have known that, though, if Ben hadn't come up with this idea.

    And this sends interesting waves across past decisions. One of the players, during setting creation, proposed that the giants were "on our side" as far as being opposed to the dragons and the corruption of sorcerers, and everyone went, "cool." And they've shown up in play in that respect.

    But until the creation of Ben's character provided an elaboration of evil, it never occurred to me that, duh, the giants can still totally be evil. They just aren't tainted.

    There's other setting stuff that came through similar chains of elaboration. But that's the basic process.
