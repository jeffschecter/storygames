\n I've been thinking a lot about optional rules recently. In the olden days when my Prime Minister was an evil hag-woman and your President was a dead movie star, optional rules could be found everywhere. Look at AD&D 2nd edition. You had five different ways to roll up your stats, two wholly separate skill systems, optional weapons vs armour tables, called shots, critical hits, the works. All of these options were considered to be within the authority of the GM to implement or not at his discretion. If you were a player you might not even have been aware that your game used the optional Plasma Disintegration rules until your GM rolled that 20 that time and got that funny look in his eye. Towards the end of this period you even had whole supplement chains devoted to list after list of optional rules - consider the 3 AD&D's Player's Option books, and all 17 Rolemaster Companions. With the advent of D&D 3, D&D 4, and the whole creator control/system matters/laser focus thing, this pattern seems to have slowed down quite significantly.

So I guess my question is this: are optional rules a good thing or a bad thing? To what extent are they supportive of diversity and customisation within the player base? To what extent do they simply represent an abrogation of responsibility on the part of the designer? Is there a particular type of game that especially benefits - or suffers - from their inclusion? How should they be presented within the text?

    \n Some good points all round.

    I think that optional rules can work, at least in the context of a simmy toolbox-style game. But I think in those cases it's incumbent on the designer to say why this rule is optional (as opposed to being core) and what effect implementing it will have on play ("Turn this dial to 11 if you want your PCs to be constantly scared of dying"). Not to mention playtesting them and making sure they work as stated. Otherwise it's tantamount to giving up. It's just throwing shit at the wall and seeing what sticks.

    See, I'm designing a game (Other Worlds). I've had a bunch of playtest feedback and I'm considering making a few changes as a result (see concurrent thread). But not all the changes I'm considering, or the problems they address, are going to be relevant to every group. Some groups might even find that my changes actively serve to reduce the amount of fun they were having before. And that's unfortunate. So in a lot of ways it's very tempting to just say the magic words 'optional rule', wash my hands of all responsibility, and let people sort this shit out for themselves.

    But as I said before, I think that would be a mistake. I think that my responsibility as a games designer is to have a specific vision of how my game is supposed to play and then make sure that every component works towards achieving that vision. My job is to make hard choices and present them as a (hopefully) coherent and functioning game engine. Putting in optional nightfighting rules, or whatever, would tend to interfere with that.

    That's what I think, anyway.

    \n I don't mind optional rules, because frankly I see them as "rules for stuff that you may or may not care about in your game".

    Let's take the more advanced combat options in REIGN as an example. I run a fairly quick-and-easy campaign, so I don't need so much detail in my fights. Heck, most of the time I use Unworthy Opponents! So labelling the advanced combat stuff as optional works great for me - it tells me that the game runs perfectly well without them, but if I ever want to dial up the detail in combats then they are there, ready for use.

    D&D 3.5 didn't have anything like this, and so all sorts of problems popped up when you tried to run quick-and-easy combats - you try to omit some fiddly little rule that didn;t seem like much fun, and then suddenly the whole thing cascades and you've got a fifteen-minute debate about attacks of opportunity and moving along the hypotenuse. Great.

    If I see an optional rule then I read it knowing that I can assess how important it will be t the game I want to run, and then include it or omit it as desired. That's a useful thing! And heck, if you don't like optional rules then just don't read any of them.

    \n OK, actual options and why?
    GLASS:
    * Player v World Priority Toggle - The former gives the choice of risking death exclusively to an individual player (Commit, and receive full Health and Natural Armor back... but now 0 Health is Dead, not Unconscious, for a limited but not short time period); the latter makes it so that any entity can kill a PC "Dead" by doing a killing blow while the PC is incapacitated (unconscious or totally bound). The former makes for more heroic and dramatic games--the death-risk only happens when it means something to the player--while the latter can make for more capricious deaths.
    ** Resurrection - Only available in World Priority games, to keep from weakening the death-risk of Committing in Player Priority. Within Resurrection, there are several Toggles and a couple of Options:
    *** No Resurrect - Want a "realistic" game where death can be ignoble or worthy? Use the No Resurrect Toggle.
    *** Resurrect Location - Hospital, temple, whatever: you Die and you'll need friends to get you to the location. Obviously, this encourages grouping or, at the least, only soloing when you're in a common traffic area.
    *** Resurrect Ability - If someone buys it, they can Res another player, anywhere, any time. This is for High Magic/Faith or Super Science games where death is merely an inconvenience and a delay.
    **** Resurrect Timer - No matter the Toggle above, this Option sets a ticking clock after which you can't be Res, period. Five to ten minutes to get to the location or to be touched with the hand of life... or make a new guy.
    **** Resurrect Risk - No matter the Toggle or Option above, there's a risk to resurrecting: anywhere from 10 to 100% chance that you'll suffer a Defect worth from 10 to 60 Exp (but you don't get the Exp bonus for receiving it!). For games where being "mostly dead" has its price, no matter how you made it back from the veil. And note that this Option has "sliders," so it's more like about 30 Options in one.

    Every choice of Toggle above, and every Option checked on, changes the relative risks for characters in-game, and that in turn suffuses the game world with a certain tone (in fact, I used to have all of them "fixed" in particular configurations, and called it "Tone," before I decided to kill Tone as a game setup Toggle and let it emerge through combinations of Toggles and Options).

    Or...

    Earning Experience
    * Variable Gain - An Option that say if you do something that advances the game (kill enemies, find information, solve puzzles, role play), you get Exp. Bog-standard.
    * Static Gain - An Option that gives X Exp to all participants, every Reset. Good for games with a lot of socializing and politicking and, thus, difficult to quantify "accomplishments." Also a cookie for wall flowers.
    --- Note that they are both Options, so they can both be on (or off--No Exp ever). Toggles are like radio buttons in computer GUIs: only one can be on in a given game.

    Spending Experience:
    * Zero Growth - This Toggle requires all Earned Exp to be spent on Single Use Abilities (ex: potions), Foci, or Tools; it reflects a game world where the PCs have all the "juice" they're going to ever effectively have, and must "tool up" to become stronger. Perfect for CoC-style play, or real-time games (where, c'mon, there's no way someone goes from pencil neck to bodybuilder in a week of game session!).
    * Balanced Growth - This Toggle means all Exp spent must have an equivalent Defect (with no Bonus Exp for the Defect). Great for games where "power corrupts" or "degenerates" those who try to wield more of it. Also GREAT for games with frequent new PCs, as no one's net Exp total changes; also simulates "aging" fairly loosely.
    * Constrained Growth - This Toggle means new Abilities and Attribute increases cost more and more, the more net total Exp a PC has. It simulates not only an inherent "maximum capacity to learn and do" (i.e. realism) it also helps keep games on a level-ish playing field, between veterans and newbies. When the field ISN'T level (Rank 3 v Rank 1), it provides a metric for GMs to use to direct advanced players to advanced content (and away from beginner content) and vice versa.
    * Unlimited Growth - This Toggle is the bog-standard: spend it when you get it, and God save the GMs when you have combat monsters and high mages wander amongst the conscripts and hedge wizards. But, hey, some folks dig that shit, so I have it there.
    -----
    I think this shows that--far from throwing a tool kit with no labels--GLASS provides a set of Toggles and Options which, based on how they are set, utterly change the game play in demonstrable ways, which in turn can impact the tone and styles of play as folks react to the relative lethality and rates of advancement.

    And I didn't even START to talk about Ability Advantages and Limitations, which let you tune a specific power or spell or feat to do EXACTLY what you imagine (in game-mechanical terms, if not precise simulation).

    Yep... HERO for LARP, specially tuned for (US) LARP expectations, styles, and preferences. Just not possible without "optional rules"... or, rather, I could release something like 40 retoolings of it, to reflect various option combos (not to mention pre-defined Ability to reinforce genre conventions). But I don't WANT to make GURPS, I want to make HERO. For boffer/Airsoft LARPs.

    \n On the topic of "games we play that actually do this thing", Burning Wheel (BW) has been mentioned in passing.

    BW is actually an excellent example of what I think Mark is talking about in the original post. It's not a sandbox-style use-what-you-like set of rules. Instead, it's presented as a set of core mechanics (character creation, universal conflict resolution, reward mechanic, plus a few other things).

    Following that, there are subsequent chapters where more detailed rules modules are presented: Advanced combat, ranged combat, social combat (the infamous "Duel of Wits") and magic.

    In the rules text, Luke Crane (the game designer) is specific about WHY and WHEN you should use the advanced/optional rules.

    My first BW campaign was a royal mess, because I didn't really take the game designer's notes at face value, and threw all the rules in. In my current BW campaign, we have been playing for a while, and have not even used most of the extra rules.

    \n Funnily enough, I think of BW as a good example of a game that doesn't use any optional rules. It's a single tightly designed engine that does all the things it needs to.

    Optional rules to me are a way of consciously making your game incomplete. Instead of presenting a single coherent mode of play, you are issuing a set of tools for people to use and expecting them to build their own.

    I think subsystems like Duel of Wits and Fight! are something different. I mean yeah, you choose whether or not you will use them, but this is no different to those times when you decide to narrate something rather than rolling for it. I think the key distinction is that the decision is made at the table and on a case by case basis. It's an extension of the core engine that you can roll out when it would be appropriate for the situation.

    You can't really do that sort of thing with encumbrance rules or optional critical hit tables or the like, because their inclusion or not has a wider impact on the nature of the system. They either apply, or they don't; there's no middle ground.

      \n So this is basically a religious war:
      Completionists - "Thou shalt not make optional rules because it forces the players to figure out what works for them and what doesn't--clearly an incomplete game!"
      Versatilists - "Thou shalt make optional rules because they allow the players to adjust rules until they figure out what works for them and what doesn't--clearly a versatile game!"

      And THAT all comes from a discrepancy in the Doctrine Of Play:
      Ritualists - "Play must and may only begin with all present and the narration in full effect." The game can't happen away from all participants; and it is not being played if there is any sort of out-of-fiction conversation; and there is an RPG-community-accepted (and -enforced) consistency to play from group to group. (see also System Matters)
      Proceduralists - "Play happens whenever the system is engaged, be it to tune rules to suit a game style or to prep for play." Part of the game is the prep, the noodling with options (or power combos or feat combos or challenge ratings or...), and the meta-level conversations which adjust (or ease into) the implementation of basic rules for a particular play group. (see also Lonely Fun)

      Therefore, there are probably only a few ways to resolve this thread in any meaningful way:
      * Do what your market and target demographic demands - some game consumers demand options for game customizing (or they'll house rule them and, if they do so poorly, will blame your game!). Exs: GLASS, HERO, GURPS, (older) D&D
      * Do what your artistic vision requires - some game designers have a VERY specific purpose of (and therefore way to) play their games, which brooks no tweaking around the systemic edges. Exs: DitV, Grey Ranks, most "focused" story games
      * Do what your business plan requires - some flexible or customizable engines are better sold as discrete products, with tunable options of the core system "baked in" for quick game starts. Exs: Burning FOO, Palladium games

      \n I have a taxonomy of optional and advanced rules. Advanced rules are rules that are more complex than I think most people will want but reflect the way that I think the game should be played (inverse square-law for calculating explosive damage). Optional rules are rules that produce some specific sometimes-wanted/sometimes-not effect in the game that is good for some games but not others (like rules for reloading for games where you are tracking ammo or rules for a blow-to-the-back-of-the-head knocking people out for pulp games).

      However: any optional rule should say clearly what it is for and how/when you should go about using it. Any advanced rule should have a suitable non-advanced rule for people who don't care that much.

      I kind of wish I could say that failure to consider both of these makes me lose respect for the game designer but, alas, it wouldn't be true
