So, what phrases have meaning to your group that the rest of us will probably boggle at? What do they mean?

We, as you know, have HARDCORE!!!. Because everyone should have a reason to be HARDCORE!!!

There's also DOOM!! (pronounced with exclamation points or without), which basically means that some kind of exciting confrontation is coming, or that someone has done something which will make a confrontation inevitable at some point. For example, getting back together with your sometimes-homicidal ex-girlfriend? DOOM!!!, because it means that you and she are definitely going to have a confrontation sooner or later, and the stakes of the conflict will be high. It's often used to warn someone that what they're doing is going to create mega-conflict later, and often used to excitedly inform the rest of the group that DOOM!! is coming.

Beatdown means something is really, really cool when used as a noun, or to administer a smackdown when used as a verb. For example, having a fist-fight in freefall is beatdown. Winning the fistfight before hitting the ground is beating down the enemy. Realizing that your enemy disabled your parachute during the fight is being beaten down.

I'm sure we have more vocabulary we've invented, but I can't think of it right now - and in any case, I'm curious to hear how you guys talk to each other about things that are hard to describe in other ways!

--Jess

    After reading a thread just like this years ago, I think at RPG.net. My group adopted this one: Requesting that another player meet you in "Palladium section" is requesting to have a private conversation away from the group.

    "Pouching the baby" is when someone comes up with a plan that's so crazy it breaks the brain just to listen to it. The plan could work, it's just mindboggling to deal with. This one's my fault. It came from an Exalted game where one character experienced a rape/seduction by an Abyssal which led the Abyssal to be pregnant. My character a Lunar, not happy about that had an opportunity to kill the mother, but knew that even despite the circumstances of the conception, the father would want the child to live, even though we'd be happy iif she died. So my devised a plan by which she could kill the mother, take the baby and, with the help of a sorcerer she knew, raise it to birthing age in the pouch of her marsupial form.

    "World is my ship" - To declare that you're going to go a little "world is my ship" means that you want to attempt to do something majorly cool but that stretches the definition of a trait, power or ability on your sheet. It's usually used to seek permission from or to warn the GM that you know that the action may not be doable. It comes from another Exalted game where a Eclipse air ship captain used one of her sail charms in an ingenious way to affect the world around her, and not just her ship.

    "Going to the crow's nest" is anything that absolutely disallowed by unreasonable GM fiat.

    "Double bagging" is a character, under some manner of protection in the story, using their action to provide a secondary layer of protection to only themselves. It's usually use derisively, as is "Biter" for when anybody is infringes or overlaps the core schtick of another character.

    "Can't sleep. Gotta go to Iraq": Is used to describe any game that is jam-packed with crazy, immediate action (note: this well predates 9/11 or the current incursion in Iraq).

    In the T&J game, Brand and I have a couple of new ones:

    "Is that inked yet?" Because T+J is structured and described (often panel by panel) as a graphic novel, this has started to be used to ask whether the thing just declared is set in stone or if it is negotiable. If it isn't inked, it can be erased, and redrawn.

    "Greenscreened" In the graphic novel, precognitive visions are annotated like a green-tinged video screen, and precog action, by action lines in the same colour. To handle the precog ability, when Brand puts something into the fiction that I want a chance to pre-empt using my power I accept his description, but declare it greenscreened - the action has not yet occured, but will, unless I can stop it.

    There's lots more, but I can't think of them.

    Since the thread was mentioned on Best Of with the suggestion that it should continue, here are some of mine:

    "Stronger than Layne... Faster than Layne?!?"
    This came from a lengthy campaign of Vampire: the Masquerade where all the players juggled characters except one. The one player who stuck to her guns played a (True) Brujah brawler named Layne. She eventually became one of the best bricks I've seen in a game that began as a personal horror game and grew/devolved into gothic superheroes. Layne had her own quote whilst they were facing a small horde of Sabbat, "OK... I can take the five on right, you guys will have to deal with the other three".
    As I found myself upping the ante to throw challenges at PCs with gobs of XP, one particular NPC was a bit studly for the party brick. As the baddie won initiative and put a whallop on her, one player in the group who often spoke excitedly called out, "Stronger than Layne?!? Faster than Layne?!?"
    This became the key phrase for anything ridiculously powerful in the game (player or non-player).

    "That's a X point shiv"
    During a two year Legend of the Five Rings game rife with bad puns, this became the witty rejoinder. If someone expressed a painfully bad pun or dumb joke, someone else (it was a random reaction) would frown, roll a few dice (10 siders, it was L5R) and reply flatly, "That's a X point shiv," (X being the result of the roll of course). This was mock damage to the player that had no effect other than to say, "Hey dude, that was a bad pun." I don't know if it discouraged puns or rewarded them, but it was fun to throw mock damage at a player.

    "I hope you have his stats"
    This was a phrase used by one of the Qualls boys, a group of three brothers who I met in college who had gamed together during their entire adolescent life. The youngest uttered this challenge whenever an NPC slightly angered him: "I hope you have his stats" (which meant he was about to throw dice to settle the score). It was as if NPCs didn't exist until they were on paper and then once they had permanence, he could beat them. It was one of the weirdest threats I've seen in a game and the Qualls boys took his threat seriously.

    "I run as fast as I can, checking for traps!"
    This phrase was born from the lips of a player called The Plez. He was playing a thief and decided that he needed to make it safely across the dungeon but didn't want to waste all that time, so he logicked that he could run and check for traps at the same time. This became the code phrase for multi-tasking for many games after.

    Back from my days in college:

    PC Glow: that je ne sais quoi that allows PCs to instantly recognize another character as a fellow PC (e.g. "there are five other people in this tavern, but I think I will talk to this angry looking dwarf sitting by himself"), and grants them partial plot immunity (in the form of Fate/Drama/Chutzpah points or GM Fiat). When an NPC does something particularly spectacular, he is said to acquire the "PC Glow".

    Pulling a Grote: when a player's character is killed because the player declared an action that common sense dictates will result in death or catastrophe; e.g. doing something contrary with a gun pointed to your head, trying to walk on lava, etc. Named after a friend who did this on several occasions. In most such cases, he expected either the game mechanics or PC Glow to save him from the GM's wrath.

    Pulling a Dean: like pulling a Grote, except that someone else's character is killed. Likewise, named after the guilty party. My favorite Fading Suns character was on the receiving end of the event that inspired this one. (Poor Reggie Hawkwood, at least his terrible aunts can no longer marry him off.)

    Once upon a time, there was a GURPS worldjumpers game in which a dear friend of mine was playing a Russian scientist.

    They were in an alternate world and suspected enemy worldjumpers of having control of a business and using it as a front to cover for their nefarious plans.

    The Brick - a plan by a player that makes no fucking sense at all.

      Here are some others:

      Fluffy Bunny of Evil--this is a term that was bestowed upon my wife by Keith, indicating that she is capable of only minor acts of evil towards another player.

      Not You!--Admittedly, this is more of a boardgaming term. Essentially, you use this when you have decided to pick on someone in particular by withholding anything that might possibly be of assistance. Like this, "Hmm....who is going to get this bonus power card. Not You!"

      Going Veteran--Lifted straight from Polaris, this means that a character is on the downward slope of a tragic story. In a pinch, I suppose that it could refer to yourself on a particular bad day.

      What dice does this use?--This arose during the stretch of time when we played Unknown Armies on Friday and Rune on Sunday. Each time we would sit down to play, my wife would have to be reminded of the rules of the game, including what dice were employed in the game and other such things, like her character's name. Once reminded, it would all come back quickly, but we needed to do it each time. So now, saying "What dice does this use?" essentially means "I'm totally clueless about how to play this game and don't remember a thing about it, even though we've played it many times." This phrase is particularly funny when the game in question has no dice. Like, say, Nobilis.

"I just know there is a shoe around the corner waiting to kick my ass."
