\n So as I think about the dialogue around system does/doesn't matter, I have this little history in my mind:
\n
\n1) White Wolf pushes the motto that "system doesn't matter" - either through the marketing for Vampire et al. or through public comments by their designers. This is ironic because they're trying to sell you a new RPG system, but what they mean is don't get bogged down in how much damage weapons do in this ruleset do vs. that ruleset: the thing we want you to get excited about is the art and the fiction and the setting and concepts that occupy more space in this rulebook than, you know, rules.
\n
\n2) The Forge adopts "system does matter" as a manifesto for game design in which the rules are tightly written to produce specific play experiences with the overt expectation that players will have a better time the more closely they follow the rules, as a reaction to contemporary predecessors like Rifts (or perhaps Vampire, I don't have the experience to say) where the covert expectation is that you'll have a better time the more you pick and choose from this big smorgasbord of loosely-written rules and mechanics and one-man's-treasure which would make you hork if you tried to swallow it all indiscriminately.
\n
\n3) Further discussion at the Forge leads to an expanded definition of system as all those things that individual groups use to decide what does and doesn't happen in the imagined space, which vary between groups even when they're both trying their best to follow the same set of rules faithfully. This is ironic because it represents kind of a return to the original White Wolf position: we agree that things should be this way regardless of rules because that's what feels right based on the pictures of vampires and the canon of fan mythos and the fact that we're in a group that's playing this game and not another one because something in the way it was presented and marketed appealed to us.
\n
\nHere are my questions:
\n
\n- Is this factually true? A little Googling of "system doesn't matter" +white wolf didn't turn up much that looked like an official endorsement, and re-reading Ron's original article I saw that he talks not about this idea having come from Vampire but rather having been around for 20 years.
\n
\n- True or not, is this analysis "dreadfully jejune" (as Chip Delany once remarked on one of my essay-question exams when he was teaching undergrads at UMass Amherst)?

    \n My take - there are two orthogonal sets of concerns that are usually muddled together when these sorts of conversations about "system" come up. One is "story-oriented play vs. PC-oriented play," while the other is "more complicated rules vs. more spontaneity." White Wolf's fans tend to favor character-oriented, spontaneous gameplay, whereas Forge types tend to favor rules-heavy, story-oriented play. What neither group favors is PC-oriented, rules-heavy gameplay, but thanks to D&D, this is the model of gaming that about 90% of the RPG community tends to take for granted.

    And just FYI, when I saw Delany give a talk in Toronto some years back, he tried to argue that Moby Dick isn't about a whale at all, but instead, is really about about "every other novel ever written." He also wore a leather belt that was covered with shiny metal studs. Hard to imagine how anything on your essay could have been that jejune.

    Marginalia/Related anecdote/PS:
    I'm was having lunch with a friend of mine just a few days ago. He has played a lot of D&D, and he has been recently running a little WHFRP. He has almost no experience of any other systems, and isn't into any kind of "theory". So... he tried running Mage a few weeks back for a couple of friends, his first time with the game. He loved the setting, the ideas behind it, the magic system. But then he started talking about dice rolls. I'm going to quote him more or less verbatim:
    "We only rolled dice four or five times during two sessions. Both times I didn't know when to ask for a roll or what it meant. It seemed like rolling dice was pointless, like it didn't matter. Most of the time I just said yes to whatever they were trying to do, I felt bad asking them to roll the dice. Once or twice, when they failed, they just rolled their thumbs a bit and looked around and then asked if they could try again."
    I had different but similar experience with running and playing WoD games for the first time ten years ago. The mechanics were apparently not satisfying or fulfilling in any significant way. Sessions often felt hollow.

I don't think WW is saying "sytem doesn't matter" it's just that its techniques and ephemera seem divorced from the elements of the exploration to me. And the techniques and ephemera prescribed by the game text is usually what ends up being a given group's system. So subsequently you have strong seeds of setting, situation, colour, probably even character but system seems all just "Eh, whatever.".

    \n "System does matter" was only kinda linked to White Wolf, like John said. In my mind, it was more about the attitude that resulted from a lot of design work in the late 1980s and early 1990s being more or less the same. Like, circa 1998, it seemed like every system you encountered was:

    -- "Splats" or character classes based on ethnicity or philosophical orientation
    -- Attributes
    -- Skills
    -- Magic or powers
    -- Character Quirks of some kind
    -- Some morality system
    -- Some form of resource points that you could spend to be awesome ("hero points" etc.)
    -- Mechanics that supposedly covered everything but mostly revolved around 50 pages of combat rules
    -- Described themselves as "make-believe for adults" or "storytelling" games (despite the huge combat focus)
    -- Hundreds of pages of setting that wasn't really set up to be directly implemented in actual play
    -- Terrible, super-railroaded adventure writing
    -- Assumed endless campaigns that would go on for years
    -- Etc.

    And, because most roleplaying systems were very similar, written for the same set of "general purpose" GMing and player techniques, often with the same assumptions about what the content of play would consist of, people started feeling like it didn't really matter what game you were playing. Everything was essentially interchangable. Why bother learning a new system? Why bother DESIGNING a new system, if it was just going to be a slight hack of the status quo?

    Really, the games that really started to raise questions about these assumptions were ones like My Life With Master, Primetime Adventures, and the like. Games that looked REALLY different from those mid-1990s assumptions about what roleplaying was about.

    To be fair, actually, White Wolf's design work, while having a lot of these traits, was really more different and original than a lot of the other stuff that was being produced at the time. Yeah, they had Rule Zero and they used more or less the same rules system for all of their games, but they recognized that their various lines weren't really mechanically compatible with each other (like, playing Mages and Vampires together never really worked). And some of their later lines (Wraith, Orpheus, Promethean, etc.) did a bunch of experimental stuff that was very cool for a "mainstream" (ha!) game company.

    Posted By: Accounting for Taste
    So, given that AD&D dominated the marketplace ...why did White Wolf somehow end up as the Evil Rule Zero Boogeyman, and not TSR?
    \n(TL;DR) From my own experience I believe White Wolf (and Vampire specifically) had it coming.

    I remember Vampire putting a lot of pressure on the Storyteller to be an auteur, to make ART, comma, Period. The Chronicle needed to be beautiful and tragic. It needed to make angels weep and devils reconsider. I remember feeling that pressure when I ran it, and so whenever players threatened my narrative, I would be scared. In fact, I liked to run Vampire one-on-one to minimize the players' disruptive influence.

    De-emphasizing rules, making them impressionistic and contradictory and broken and then relegating them to an advisory role anyway, was another useful method of player neutralization. As Robin's Laws of Good Game Mastering had it:\n
    Rules sets in which crunchy hits predominate give power to the players...Rules systems that limit the impact of crunchy bits give power to the GM.
    \nAnd of course some players who didn't want to be neutralized got neutralized1, and so we have the Forge and Fuck Rule Zero and so on.

    AD&D2E, on the other hand, brought no such pressure. None of the main rules texts really got into how a campaign is supposed to appear or feel. In fact, the word "campaign" isn't even in the index of my copy of the DMG! Is the campaign it a urban sandbox or a dungeon railroad? Is it an arena of endless monsters? Is it happy, funny, beautiful, tragic? Is it PVP? AD&D2E didn't say, and (subjectively) it didn't add a lot of value to any of these approaches anyway.

    TSR might have had the sales, but White Wolf, with their tighter focus and cocksure attitude, had the mindshare. I remember being so pumped when GURPS Vampire came out because I wanted the cool gnarliness of White Wolf to be ported to my grown-up respectable game system.

    Maybe Microsoft / Apple is a useful analogy? Seems like no one has an opinion about Microsoft anymore; Apple not so much. And I remember being so pumped when OSX was built on awesome Unix underpinnings.

    1Standard JDCorley disclaimer applies: this is not universal truth, and might not be majority truth, but it is at the very least minority truth, and is true for me as both a player and a DM, and continues to inform my approach to this day.

      \n Okay, look, guys, I know we all know what I'm about to say and I know it's gonna sound patronizing, but Imma restate it:

      Yes, ignoring some mechanical result or restriction or other sometimes results in a better story or a more fun session, and yet we do not ignore those mechanical restrictions despite Rule Zero's exhortations. Why don't we ignore them? Because they challenge our creativity. It is often more difficult to narrate dramatically fun and dynamic results within the constraints the system gives us. Sometimes we don't do as well as we might were the resolution freeform. You know what? It's actually okay to follow the rules and mess up the drama a bit. For the same reason that a poet writing a sonnet doesn't break out of meter and rhyme in the third line of the third quatrain even though the poet has a totally great idea that would be so easy to get across if that line were not in iambic pentameter and didn't rhyme with anything, for the same reason an improv comedy troupe doesn't discard the premise an audience member has given them halfway through a sketch, we, the role-players, continue to challenge our own creativity by following rules and being creative anyway. In general the rules are positive and drama-promoting; in a few cases the challenge might overcome us, but we welcome the challenge anyway.

      Now I know some people may be thinking, "why not do the same with Rule Zero? Why not let the freeform nature of the rule challenge our ability to structure mechanics or systems or fixes on the fly, and have fun with that?" It's not a good idea because Rule Zero forces us, in the middle of a game, to respond to player concerns at an emotional or social level with mechanical patches. Responding to player concerns with mechanical stuff is a great thing to do during game design, and a ticket to dysfunctional gameplay two hours into a five-hour session. Something happened that wasn't fun or interesting for another player at my table? Wait, why am I trying to fix this problem by adding or subtracting role-playing game mechanics? Role-playing game mechanics are for making up stories, not fixing someone's emotional response to entertainment. That way lies madness. Even when playtesting a game, we generally like to table feedback until after the session is over, rather than arguing over mechanics in mid-session.

      Rule Zero's no-system is, of course, a system in and of itself, which admits that some social chaos may result from the course of playing a game. Any other game would tell us, "Change the system, but do still have a system." Rule Zero tells us, "To hell with the system," and then when you throw out the system you find you still have a system, and that throwing out system is system too, and now you have even more system to deal with and weren't you supposed to be getting rid of system?

      So confusing. No good.

      And I've played in great games that were broken by crappy players, people not being in the mood, and other human conditions.

      I like Rule Zero for games where I know I trust the person who is in the GM chair. I don't like it (or at least I'm wary of it) for games where I'm approaching a table of people I've never gamed with before, like at a con; because crappy things sometimes ensue. The solution to that was easy, and that was to stop playing con games with strangers. :-)

      I like collaborative games as well. But they basically offset the problems inherent in one player having all the power, and instead give everyone a little power. You see different, cool dynamics. And I see a lot of people indicating that non-Rule-Zero is the way to go and all, or citing the power of distributed authority, but I've definitely had the sorts of problems that JD was alluding to before. Not with any frequency, mind (but at the same time, the GMs of "rule zero" games that I play with these days don't cause those "Rules-Zero-y issues" as well), but enough to basically go, "Okay, this is just the other side of this game not having the Rule Zero/One GM As God thing".

      Collaborative-style gaming corrects a problem, with a trade-off of course. It's a trade-off most of us (including JD, from his play reports) buy into when we play these games. Instead of one guy being a dick/not on the ball/etc and messing up a game for the other players, you have things happen like:
      * Everyone has differing ideas of what should happen next. Everyone agrees on something in order to keep the game going, but at least a few of the players are kinda like, "Well, this isn't really what I had in mind, but I'll roll with it to make the others happy and keep the game going".
      * Everyone has differing ideas of what should happen next. Everyone kinda pushes in a direction because for this particular conflict all the players are really invested in a set of results. It takes far longer, and more strain, to reach a consensus that everyone likes.
      * Everyone has differing ideas of what should happen next. Everyone pitches in their ideas, strain to make them all happen as they're all valid, and that scene/conflict/whatever gets really strained. Think of the story of "stone soup", where everyone throws in an ingredient, but in the end the ingredients are very different: Tuna. Ice cream. Kimchee. Frosted Flakes. And everyone has to take a big drink of this mess and deal with it.
      * Issues of people who are more (or less) outgoing stepping in too strongly, or not stepping in strongly enough. Y'know, basic people issues.

      But no rules, no matter how tightly written, will get around a player who had a bad day, people with issues of trust, boneheads, and so on. I think this was JD's somewhat exaggerated point. I definitely agree with it, because I've seen all those things I listed there. But the tradeoff I made is the better choice for me, because the problems above, while they can be confusing or painful, aren't nearly as painful as some intentional or unintentional Rule Zero stuff.

      In the end, I think that most of us posting to this thread are already beyond the whole Rule Zero thing as anything more than an abstract idea: Most of us (particularly folks - like me! - who advocate strongly against Rule Zero in general) had that shitty game experience where a shitty GM did a shitty thing, probably with full knowledge that she or he was being a shit at the time. And it sucked. So when we saw distributed models of play, we jumped on those. Not so much as the abuse victim or whatever (as some folks attribute to other folks into lesser GM powers; they are running away from some abuser situation or somesuch), more like because it was a model where things we like to happen happen more, and the problems associated with distributed play, while real, aren't as painful/disappointing as a big Rule Zero flare-up.

      But here's the thing (re Being Beyond Rule Zero for most of us): Distributed play, ownership, etc requires a lot of trust between the players. I mean, duh: Most of us play with our friends, and we trust them, so no problem, right?

      Thing is, Rule Zero is a lot about trust as well: If we were to *now* go back to those experiences we had with shitty Rule Zero encounters, we'd probably in most cases recognize much more clearly the underlying human/trust issues that were in play there, and perhaps navigate easily around them with no thought at all, and perhaps even embrace the whole Rule Zero thing again when playing certain kinds of games.

      Or use Rule Zero as a qualifier for the game's methods: Sometimes I like playing miniature games, sometimes I like Ameritrash empire-building boardgames, other times I like French abstract boardgames. Likewise, nowadays I like to play a lot in games with distributed player-GM authority. But I can totally go for some GM-as-God traddy McTradpants games (with the right GM I trust, of course!); or games built around Rule Zero but played more distributed-ly (Fading Suns, with group planet/House design, farming of major NPCs to be "owned" by players, solid input into each conflict with the GM having final say, etc); or games with distributed player-GM authority where nonetheless the organizer sets in stone some of the details (Shock: "I want the next game of Shock we play to be about Cloning in the future"; PTA: "I want to be the Producer of a zombie comedy show, if you're in come to my house on Friday night", etc).

      Anyway, just my thoughts.

      \n It almost seems like the Rule Zero debate comes down to whether one is treating an RPG like a game of Chess or Go, where the rules are immutable. Or whether it is handled more like a "let's pretend" with a general framework of mechanics with one (or more ppl) acting as mediators or referees. If one takes it to be the former, then Rule Zero cannot and should not ever be applied, but if the latter then none of the rules are absolute and immutable and it comes down to the makeup and dynamic of the individual group and their particular social contract for gaming. The RPG system is the trellis with the individual rules acting like the lattice that makes up the trellis, but one can alter the geometry of the lattice without ruining the trellis.

      \n Well, the whole "Rule Zero" debate has been around for a while, of course, and while I have many thoughts on that topic, I should maybe leave them out.

      That said, I think there is a major difference between "System Matters" games and "System Doesn't Matter" games, and it has a lot to do with presentation and the promise of the games themselves.

      * A lot of these earlier games were extremely fond of telling us how You Could Do Anything with them. Be anyone! Experience the stories of your dreams! Explore new worlds! Our game can do EVERYTHING!

      * I give a lot of respect to modern designs that are totally honest up-front about what they do well, and what they do not do well. By saying, "you will not enjoy this game if you don't like having your character die in the first five minutes", or maybe, "this game may subject you to your own childhood fears and could make you cry," you reduce the need for a large and very visible Rule Zero tremendously.
