\n I have a question that I'm hoping folks here can help me resolve. I'm working on a game idea that is set during World War II, and one aspect of the game involves researchers and civilians working as part of the German government under Hitler. This could happen off-screen as part of the back-story of the game or it could be directly tied to the players- that is, the PCs would be working for Germany during the Nazi regime. Of course, there would be no reason to play them as Nazis, and the characters could be very sympathetic- struggling to support their nation while quietly resisting fascism, etc.

So my question(s):

1) Would you find this to be a fun and interesting play challenge or would the very idea of working under Hitler be a buzz-kill?

2) Would the answer be any different if we were talking about playing hapless front-line German soldiers?

3) What lines and veils would you feel were necessary to keep play from straying too far out of your comfort zone?

Having played some of this out before, I know at least one of my friends who felt uncomfortable with this, and we shifted the scope of our play to accommodate him. Also, being an American, I'm especially interested in hearing the perspective of folks from other countries on this.

Thanks!

    \n Steve, if you want to keep the Holocaust dial high, then let me ask you a question, because often I find the sentiment (that I will soon discuss odd). You've said:\n
    1) Would you find this to be a fun and interesting play challenge or would the very idea of working under Hitler be a buzz-kill?
    \nI'm sure it can be interesting and satisfying, but what is the desire for "fun"? I think when people say "fun" they usually don't mean they derive something that can be called "enjoyment" from it, but the happy, light version, which may not be something to aim for.

    The answer to question 3 would be that a GM shouldn't do the usual "This is what I'm willing to run" thing with this game. He should tell people what the game is about, and ask them if they're willing to play. And as always about boundaries, when one person says no don't try to convince them, it's a form of pressuring, stop. About the in-game veils and boundaries? I'd definitely stay away from graphic descriptions, even if your character does do something that is... bad, I'd just describe it, even though that would take away from the emotional impact, there's an opposite risk - emotional porn.

    I think some people who talk about how their characters raped, and murdered, and tortured? They do this because they are not deeply engrossed in the character and(/or?) to show how "superior" as role-players they are, both willing and capable to play through anything. That's a load of bull. It's like teenagers who watch movies like Saw, or who watched the hot-fat shot in Watchmen and laugh.

    Bleak and stark often mean stripped down. Less is sometimes more.

    As for "Front-line soldiers", that's almost another issue. Then you'd consider why they do it, what it means to them - protecting their country, serving their leader, being one of the community? And if you just play them as soldiers without exploring the reasons, then it's pure colour, and couldn't matter less that Hitler is their leader.

    \n I love games that provide rich ground for exploring moral decisions in a compromised world. If that's something this game provides (even indirectly), I'd be excited about it.     \n So, the thing that would sell me on it is emphasizing the psychology of why good people do bad things. See Phillip Zimbardo's work (Stanford Prison Experiment, or his more recent book, the Lucifer Effect).

    It would be pretty compelling if you cranked up the sympathy factor. You are Good People. But there's a huge amount of social and even legal pressure to be otherwise. A best case goal of the game might be something like Oskar Schindler, using the system to do some good. But the mechanical pressure of the system would empower you more to feed the Nazi agenda (which shouldn't always be the worst evil of killing Jews and other marginalized groups, but helping the political and war effort in someway).
