    You mean: you've got a success target (22, say). You roll two dice. You succeed if you roll two dice and, when you put them together as a two-digit number, in either order, that number is under 22. Is that it? And you want to know how much more likely you are to succeed than if you designated one dice as tens and one as ones.

    It depends on the target number, I think. If the target number was 22, then the following rolls would succeed if you designated one die as tens and one as ones:

    11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 21, 22

    and the following would succeed if you could combine them in either order:

    11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 21, 22, 31, 41, 51, 61

    So the increased chance of success is 12 / 8. You've increased your chances by 50%.

    But it depends on the target number and I can't, offhand, think of a formula to work it out for all target numbers. You could use Excel, though.

    Not much help, I'm afraid.

    Graham

    As promised, some explanation of what I'm after. I also promised myself I would put this away and work on more pressing projects, so this is really just for reference at this point. Thanks to everyone who offered math help thusfar.

    * * *

    A God-King must have subjects - they are, quite literally, his strength. Farmers feed the warriors His Divine Majesty must have to protect the magician-priests that ensure abundant game for the farmers. Each group is obedient to a different Goddess as well as the God-King they all share. Relationships between the groups are always strained, thanks to acrimony among the Goddesses, but they are united in their love and support for the God-King they worship.

    There are three Goddesses, each an object of devotion for a segment of society. The farmers worship All-Mother, who represents the hearth, fertility, abundance, the family and peace. She hates Shield Maiden, who is worshipped by the warriors and represents power, knowledge, the clan, and judgment. Shield Maiden, in turn, hates Bear Walk, who is worshipped by the magician-priests and represents wisdom, magic, death, and the unknowable. Bear Walk, naturally, hates All-Mother.

    The God-Kings vie for the attention and favor of the three Goddesses, to whom they are eternal suitors. Among themselves, they squabble out of lust for power, and renown, and jealousy.

    CHARACTER GENERATION

    Each player character is a God-King.

    God-Kings have four statistics - farmers, warriors, magician-priests, and self.

    Rate one at 25, one at 35, one at 45, and one at 55.

    As a point of reference, your rating for farmers, times ten, is the actual number of farmers who worship your character.

    Your rating for warriors is equal to the number of warriors in your God-King's service.

    Your rating for magician-priests, divided by ten, is the number of magician-priests your character has working for him.

    Your rating for self represents your God-King's self-sufficiency and general ability to inspire awe, as well as his raw might. The God-King is singular and stands before his people, with no equal but countless inferiors.

    DIE MECHANIC

    To resolve a conflict each side will roll two six sided dice, one red and one white. The white die serves as tens. The red die serves as ones. The result is one of 36 numbers between 11 and 66.

    If you rolled a white 4 and a red 1, the result would be 41, for example. If both sides roll under the score of their central group (see below), the lower roll wins. If only one side succeeds, they win.

    CONFLICTS

    A conflict will always be opposed, and it will always have a central group. If your warriors descend upon another player's farmers, intent on slaughter, your warriors will be central, and they will be opposed by the other player's farmers. The central group is the white tens die. They will be assisted by another group, which will contribute the red ones die. In this example, perhaps your opponent's warriors defend the farmers, and your God-King rides at the van of his warriors.

    BUFFS

    You can "buy" numbers, either for your God-King himself or for his followers. If you own a number, when it comes up in play it will have an additional interesting narrative effect. For example, for warriors, a one is "An unintended alliance". If the die associated with your group in a conflict comes up on a buff number, integrate the buff into the result, win or lose. I don't have a buffs list yet. Maybe every group makes up their own.

    For example, my warriors have the one buff and are besieging the tower of another player's magician-priests with the help of an army or angry farmers. I roll a 16 to my opponents 32 - a failure. But the tens die - representing my central group, my warriors - came up one, which is a buff I have purchased. I narrate how, even though we fail, we forged a bond of respect between our warriors and their magician-priests.

    MIGHT AND AWE

    Might and Awe can be bought like bullets to enable before a really important roll. Might works against other God-Kings and Goddesses, and Awe works against mortals. If you spend a point of Might or Awe prior to a roll, you have the option to flip your red and white dice, increasing your chances of success greatly.

    Hey, Jason... I dunno if you'd like it or not, but something just jumped into my head and I thought I'd share;

    We both roll low
    Good things happen for both of us, but the low number takes the narration.

    We both roll high
    Bad things happen for both of us, but the high number takes the narration.

    Only one of us rolls low
    Good things for the low roller, bad things for the high roller, and the low roller gets the narration.

    I'm imagining that taking the narration would essentially mean that you get to decide who wins the conflict and how they win. The only constraints being good or bad things happening and to whom. Dunno if it'll work for the project or not, but it seems kinda neat at a glance.
