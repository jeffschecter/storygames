    \n Two very cool games come to mind:
    The Clay that Woke by Paul Czege in which you play nameless minotaurs struggling with their alignment.
    The Circle of Hands by Ron Edwards in which you play circle knights fighting on the edge of a magical war.

    And if those two do not rock your boat, Silent Legions by Kevin Crawford and Ben Dutters Vow of Honor were released 2015, too.
    Another (re-)release is Primetime Adventures by Matt Wilson. The game about your favourite television series that never was.

    Hope there is something to catch your interest.

    \n Fantaji looks really solid, a bit like Fate but different. I could never put my finger on what bugged me about Fate (probably the compel that I had to try to get and breaking immersion meanwhile) but Fantaji looks like it could float my boat (although it constantly asks to "play to" your traits etc. and thus isn't immersive but it looks more tactical).
    I'm planning a playtest in October.
    There is a free Quickstart called Mazaki no Fantaji.

    I have a review about Vow of Honor by Ben Dutter: http://dieheart.net/voh

    Also, perhaps the 6d6 RPG but that's not really a story game. You can read all the mechanics for free on their website and I have a review, too. So take a look, maybe it's for you, even if it's not a story game: http://dieheart.net/6d6rpg

    Nefertiti Overdrive will be due in November.

    NGS, the Narrative Gaming System, could be interesting, it's a lightweight game that centers around very competent characters. In the end, I found it too simple but its strength is that you can really build the character you want instead of trying to balance the char creation method (point buy, die rolls) with your vision of your character.

    The protocol game engine by jim pinto from postworld games looks neat, too:

    \n Basically, the original CORTEX system was "very traditional" (read incoherent and heavily GM-centric rules).
    This was used for Serenity, Supernatural and Battlestar Galactica rpgs.

    The later CORTEX PLUS was totally different, a truly "modern" game (read coherent rules).
    It was implemented in three main variations in the Smallville (drama) and Leverage (action) and Marvel Heroes (super/fantasy) games.
    Then compiled into a very interesting setting-neutral compendium.
    Then used to (re)make the Firefly game.

    \n Loads of games by me, but I shill them enough on here so instead let me suggest one by a couple of UK based gamer gamer buddies Josh Fox and Becky Annison. Lovecraftesque is a story game that aims to replicate the creeping horror of HP Lovecraft's stories but without the hackneyed CoC mythos. Players collaboratively create the narrative, and build the dawning horror themselves by contributing clues each scene, from which at the end of the game they each attempt to put together a nameless horror. I've played it only once, but I can tell you its very powerful, and if you're thinking 'Hang on, player-generated monsters, how would that work?', trust me, it works.
