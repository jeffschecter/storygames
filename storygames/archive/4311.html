\n Anybody had a chance to see Ryan Stoughton's Raising the Stakes hack for d20? It's an interesting effort to "storyify" d20 (something near and dear to my heart in light of Passages). It's interesting precisely because it's so subtle and moderate, that I think many d20 folks are giving it a spin without even realizing that they are dipping their toes in these kinds of waters. There's been a lot of mention of this effort (along with his equally interesting E6 mod) on the RPGnet d20 forum. And perhaps most interestingly, it seems like Ryan himself has no desire on associating himself or his effort with the S-G community. I might be totally wrong, and I don't know anything about Ryan, but it seems like the idea sprang up entirely organically.

    \n Wow, I have several comments which I hope Ryan will see. Hopefully these haven't been hit too much elsewhere, but if they have, the text should be cleaned up to account for the answers to these questions:
    \n
    \n - Spending Down Conviction -
    \nFollowing up on Tim's question, let's do an example. I have 3 conviction in my pool. I raise the death flag, and that gives me 6 more, so I have 9 - breaks the normal limit, but that's allowed. Then, during the combat where I raised the flag, I spend 7 conviction to ensure that I win that contest. So... Now, in order to lower the death flag, I have to come up with a total of 6 conviction... but that can't happen, because the only way I can get to 6 conviction is to raise the death flag again... and it's already up. So I can't raise it again. Right? What am I not understanding?
    \n
    \n - Spending Conviction, Option 1 -
    \nOption number 1 for spending conviction will never be used by a player who is trying to maximize his benefits (usually every player). Because why roll two dice, when I can roll 1 die, see if it's what I need, and then roll another die at need. I might roll a 20, and not need to roll again, in which case, I've burned a conviction for no reason. Why not wait to see the outcome, and then spend the point to roll again if it's insufficient? Same cost, but I only spend it if needed. If you want an option 1 that's interesting, you make it so that the character rolls 3 dice... that way it's a true gamble with a potential payout that's beyond what option 2 can give, but one done blind.
    \n
    \n - Results of Loss When Death Flag is Down -
    \nI'm not fully conversant with the D20 rules... if I'm lowered to 1 HP, do I go unconcsious (in 1st ed it was from 1-10 negative that caused unconsciousness)? What I'm getting at is that with the Death Flag not raised, if I'm reduced to 1 HP, do I get to fight on? If so... why ever raise the death flag? I can't lose if I don't. If, on the other hand, I fall unconscious, then I'm losing the contest in a way. The bad guys get away or something. This still leaves questions about what happens in certain situations. Let's say that I'm being attacked by hungry lions... if I fall unconscious, will they not then follow up and kill and eat me? Basically you have two cases for what must happen when the death flag is not up, and a character would otherwise die:
    \n
    \nA) The character can just fight on at 1HP. This is kinda lame. Basically, unless the player raises the death flag, he's never really risking anything... he doesn't even have to take the time to heal up, he can just fight the next fight at 1HP, and never take any damage.
    \n
    \nB) The character goes unconscious or otherwise loses the fight. In this case the character may well be killed anyhow, if the DM is allowed to just have the opposition kill them off after they've won the fight. So the only case in which the death flag being lowered doesn't still result in chracter death are those in which the party wins overall, saving the downed characters (Characters only die on a TPK), or where the DM decides to have mercy on the down PCs, and comes up with a rationale for why they aren't killed.
    \n
    \nThe solution to this problem in "dirty hippie" games is that the character going below zero doesn't fall unconscious or such, but that the agreement is that the character fails somehow but does not die, and the DM has to come up with a plausible explanation for how this happens. This can range from the combat being cut short by forces beyond anyone's control (the characters are separated by a chasm that opens up from an earthquake, or whatever), to the victorious bad guys coming up with a scheme as to why the capture the PCs (the lions are charmed servants of a local druid, and they bring the unconscious PCs to said druid who demands that they perform some task for them). Or any other explanation you want to come up with. Perhaps the two opponents back off from each other, and, impressed incredibly with each other's prowess, simply bow to each other, and walk away. Whatever is cool.
    \n
    \nGenerally losing with the death flag down should still be some sort of loss... there still must be stakes. The stakes just aren't death. If you can't identify what these stakes are before the combat, then you shouldn't have the combat at all, or you must require the players to raise the death flag to get into the combat. What you should ask yourself with each combat is why the combat is happening? Do the PCs need to make it to the next room? Then that's what's at stake, and losing means they run off perhaps, and have to find another way in.
    \n
    \nI'd be surprised if you don't encounter problems that people have had using your system, not understanding what to describe in the situation where loss occurs. Sure, with the extra conviction, it's probably rare. But you have to account for those rare cases where it will happen.
    \n
    \n - Stakes, Stakes, Stakes -
    \nRaising the stakes, interestingly, is simply what we'd call "setting the stakes" and what the games we play around here tend to have as a presumption from the start. That is, D&D says that the default is "fight until death." And you're giving alternatives. In the games we play, deciding that the stakes are "fight until death" is part of the same process that produces the sorts of results you imply in "raising the stakes." That is, I'm as likely to make the stakes "Run off if you fail, having to find another way in" as I am to make them "If you fail, you die." Actually I'm probably far more likely to make them the former than the latter, as the "Death flag" in these games only gets raised when there's something so worthwhile on the line that the player is fully willing to have the story of their character end here, either way. So, likely only once in a campaign.

    \n
    \n - Player Understanding of Stakes -
    \nIf players understand about stakes, an interesting thing happens. You can be "mean" to them. Rather, you can't possibly be mean to them, so you can be "unfair." That is, right now you still have to worry a lot about setting appropriate CRs, right? I note that you spend some time on the enworld site talking about how many feats equals how many levels, and how to make good encounters because of this, right?
    \n
    \nWell, what if you didn't have to bother? What if you could present the PCs with dragons at first level, and not worry? I mean yeah, the chances of winning such a combat are slim and none. But if the death flag doesn't go up, then the real question is what's at stake losing to the dragon. And you can make up all sorts of interesting stakes. And what's interesting here is that you are allowing the players a chance to kill a dragon at first level - even if that chance is minute. Something that simply would never happen in play with the normal system because players would avoid it at all costs (or call you a bad GM for throwing it at them at such a low level if/when their characters died). Using this system, you can simply throw at them whatever you think is fun to throw at them, whenever you want. That logic could be "Whatever is there on the map" or "whatever is dramatic." But what you don't have to do is to have the world modify itself to become appropriately dangerous in it's environs to the PCs power levels. Which is something that I, at least, always found to be BS. The PCs always seemed to carry a "Danger Level Bubble" around with them.
    \n
    \nThis allows the players, too, to take control of their character's destinies. Is it crazy to go looking for that dragon? Sure, but people do crazy things. The players can feel free to have their characters behave like normal humans, with all of their flaws, because they know that they won't suffer the only real player penalty that a game can deliver... elimination from ability to play a character, through that characters's death (or something else that makes it unplayable).
    \n
    \nSo, congratulations, you've cracked that nut here. But I think you have a step to go until players understand the ramifications, and you get the full benefits of what you've wrought. The players need to understand what, in fact, is at stake when they go up against something against which they're not going to raise the death flag. No, they don't need to understand the specifics neccessarily. They just have to understand the sort of thing that's likely to be the outcome. Imprisonment, disfigurement, being forced to go somewhere they don't want to go, being unable to get something that they want, etc, etc.
    \n

    \n
    \nIt's written for Hero Quest RPG, but I think you'll find that it pertains to what you're striving for as well.

    \n OK, here's the skinny on the Death Flag:
    \n
    \nI have found in many d20 campaigns (and the many others that follow the d20 formula) that death is a very, very weak point in the system.
    \n
    \nBasically, the biggest challenge for the DM from a system point of view is bringing in just enough opposition to get the players worried about the death mechanic without actually causing death.
    \n
    \nWhen death comes up in d20 campaigns, there's an unspoken "man, this sucks" around the table. The player loses the character that they've been trying for ages to roleplay and develop and generally everyone's dissatisfied. So the natural reaction is that the DM fudges death away behind his screen (I'm not a screen man, btw). A pretty common approach (which I've seen in many games and many house rule discussions online) is to make death harder and harder but still possible. This might include, having that -10 "death barrier" extended, sometimes with level, or allow a "death save" and so on. But to me the real issue is that if neither the players nor the DM want a player character to die in a scene, then why shouldn't the rules reflect that?
    \n
    \nBut that's not to say that death isn't on the table. I have had an occasions where player-character death was essential to the story, and that death was something both I and the player basically saw eye to eye on. Honestly, I think that with the character being something the player puts so much effort into, it should be the player determining when the stakes are "I won't be able to play this character anymore." Since death stakes are exciting and rewarding for me as a GM, I provided the Conviction boost as an added incentive to make setting those stakes appealing on a game level.

But the point is that I doubt that you have to explain yourself to many here. That said, again, it's good that you've done so for any non-natives that come by. And... I love that you've done so, because it's a sort of vindication for us to find that there are more like us, even amongst the ranks of D&D players.
    \n
    \n"Like us" here meaning people who would alter the system of D&D to get the sort of play which they're looking for. Many of us started from precisely the same place - I certainly did. I have to admit that I gave up on D&D in about 1981, and have been trying to design a better moustrap every since. For the first two decades of that effort, I couldn't have told you why it was that I didn't like what D&D promoted, however. It's only in the past five years that it's become clearer what the real differences in play-styles are about.
    \n
    \nSo congratulations on having, on your own apparently, come up with a relatively clear statement of the problem... to say nothing of your solution. That's quite brilliant.
    \n
    \nNow, on to brass tacks, let's talk about the benefits of the system:
    \nThe 25% figure - I made that up off the top of my head. If it matches figures thrown about by WotC or other designers or whatever... purely by coincidence. I picked that figure, because it's conservative. I mean, what I thought that the CR system was intended to achieve was, in fact, parity. Meaning that the monsters have about a 50% chance to win.
    \n
    \nBut we all know that such a figure is absurd. It would result in death far, far too often, even for relatively hardcore play... I mean how can you guage ongoing success if your character is dying every other combat.
    \n
    \nSo you're correct. The system in D&D is interesting in that it's designed to drain so much of your resources. What this means, however, is that play becomes this odd game where the main tactical decision is "do we push for another encounter today?" Which is odd, since how often is it that the answer can't be to pull back? I mean, yeah, it's possible to engineer a situation in which time pressure exists to get a job done quickly. But how often is that actually the case?
    \n
    \nWhat I've usually observed is that it's typically a case of bravado. The question usually becomes something like, "How far do we push our characters today, to prove how tough they are, and how willing we are to take risks?" Beacause... well... the real risk of character death comes at that 4th encounter of the day, right? Where the resources are running thin. And if you push for a fifth, well, the previous four were hopefully relatively easy on your resources, or you're in real trouble, right?
    \n
    \nIn practice a group, if it wants to be safe, can simply recharge after each and ever encounter. Which means, if they do, that you never have any real threat. The "loss" is where more than 25% of resources were burnt to win. Or where non-rechargable resources that the group have are burnt, and the rewards for the contest did not replenish them (you had to resort to the precious healing potions). You only tend to get characters in danger here if they do things that are tactically very unsound, like the great example given above of the halfling risking his neck.
    \n
    \nBut this is all exactly what I'm talking about. What if, instead of each combat being made into a sure thing that's designed only to drain X% of your resources... what if many (heck all of them, if you like) could result in 100% resource drain and the characters losing?
    \n
    \nWhat if you made the contests actually even odds?
    \n
    \nWhat if you made it actually likely that the characters would lose?
    \n
    \nNo, you can't do this in D&D normally. As we've said, it would result in characters dying so often. Which is why the actual percentage chance of death is so low in D&D encounters as designed.
    \n
    \nBut in your system, the characters don't die if they lose. Something else bad happens instead. So you're now free to throw whatever you like at your PCs. I'll say that again, you can throw whatever you like at them. Just ignore the CRs altogether if you like. But, in any case, jack up the level in some cases so that you get truely heroic fights... that is ones in which there's no garuntee of success, or even likelihood neccessarily. "Heroism" is all to do with characters diving in heedless of the consequences, because they know that the goal must be persued.
    \n
    \nAllow your players to have their chracters act that heroically - give them situations where diving in is truely likely to result in failure. Because:
    \n1) They might win. And then how cool is that? That they managed to defeat foes equal to themselves, or even thought to be tougher.
    \n2) Even if the characters lose, the players win. Did you read my essay? Given a situation with the death flag down, what this means is that the player is risking something other than death. And if they lose, that simply means that they now have more trouble in their lives to deal with. The character becomes more interesting to play. Now we have to chase down the bad guy who got away. Or escape from imprisonment. Or deal with the curse that we've been put under, or... well you get the idea.
    \n3) You finally get to really test your players... that is you, as DM, now can play the opposition all out trying to win. Because you know that if you do win, you don't have to feel guilty that you caused the death of a PC - beacuse you won't. You will, instead, come up with some loss condition that will make the PC more fun to play (see 2 above). So you can try to win combats with all of the resources you have at hand, and do not have to take it easy on the PCs.
    \n
    \nNote, again, how there are subtle DM tricks that people use to keep PCs alive. Yeah, you roll the dice on the table... that's great. But that doesn't mean that you don't sometimes make tactically unsound choices. What is, in D&D, the most important tactical choice? Who to attack, right? The players, being smart, will always attack the wizbang first. Why? For the same reason that my gnome mage is always getting killed in WOW... the "aggro" calculaton is simple. What's the highest damage output to Hit Points ratio? That would be the wizard, yup. Putting him down is easiest, and results in the most threat reduction.

    \n The Cost of the Death Flag
    \n
    \nThe issue that I have with the cost of lowering the Death Flag is that it sets up an arbitrary length for which the flag stays up. That is, if I raise it at the beginning of a session, it'll last all session. If I raise it at the end of the session, it might only last for that encounter. What this means is that the player will consider the positioning of the encounter in terms of when it's occuring in the session, in terms of deciding to raise the flag or not.
    \n
    \nIsn't that an oddly artificial criteria for the player to consider?
    \n
    \nI like your notions about fatalism... I agree with you that a player should be allowed to keep the flag up for a while (or even indefinitely). But the consideration of when to do this or not shouldn't have anything to do with the positioning of the event in the session. It should be all about character motives, and player desire to create theme, shouldn't it? Put another way, we wouldn't want a player to not raise the death flag solely because he didn't want to keep it up for more than the current encounter and would be forced to mechanically if he did, right? It's going to be rare enough as it is, I think.
    \n
    \nSo what I'm really for is coming up with other ways for the player to get the conviction he needs to "pay it off" or some other thing he can do to lower the flag immediately. And I think that comes down to haveing a better way to dole out conviction in the first place, other than a session-based reward.
    \n
    \nNote that this is an opportunity for you. All reward systems have two elements: you have the second part well designed, which is what the reward does. But you're first part, what the reward is given for, is, apparently, for just showing up to play. What we sometimes call "attendance rewards". That's a misnomer, however, because the players have to show up just to play (the play is it's own reward for showing up), and they won't actually miss out on accumulating points if they don't show up.
    \n
    \nIn fact, it's no reward at all, what you have. It's simply "fuel" for the system you've put in place.
    \n
    \nWhich isn't terrible, lots of systems do this. It's just that you have an opportunity to come up with something you want to reward in play, some behavior you want to incentivize, and give out the points for that. The most powerful reward systems work together on both ends. Consider EXP... you get them for killing, etc, and so that's what people do. Then you can spend them to get better at XYZ, and so that's what people do, too. The circle is complete there.
    \n
    \nConsider finding some sort of action that you can reward the players for with conviction points. This will then incentivize that behavior, and it also means that, potentially, a player can work off his raised death flag in the middle of play and not have to worry about where in the session the encounter occurs.
    \n
    \n
    \nI have to reiterate something, because I think it's gotten lost here. A key to your system, especially if you do as I say above, and make failure a relatively common occurance, is that you have to have failure stakes set. That is, sure, death is off the table here, but you have to have something on the table. Or failures will seem hollow. Failure must have some "Bite" to it, or players will feel that any contest without the death flag raised is not a real conflict. As Rob says above, this opens new vistas of play. The result of a combat loss might be, for instance, some sort of maiming of the character. He might lose an eye. Or the respect of a good woman. Or... well anything. Be creative here, and make failure painful to the character. Because failures painful to the character, make the character more interesting for the player to play.
    \n
    \nAnother note here, however... you aptly discern the real problem with character death as it being about an inability to play the character any longer. Here's an important thing to consider when setting stakes other than death... there are other conditions than character death that can make it so that a player may find the character unsuitable to continue playing. These aren't always easy to see, either. But, for instance, it could be that a player might find a character who has lost a leg to be uninteresting to play further. Some players might relish the challenge that this presents. But other players may have a vision of thier character that precludes being peg-legged, or running off to find somebody who can reattach their limb. This sort of loss may be akin to character death, in that the character will now be "ruined" for the character.
    \n
    \nEspecially avoid stakes that are degrading to the PC, unless you're certain that the player wants this sort of thing.
    \n
    \nTry to do this determination without too much dicsussion, if you can... it makes it more suspenseful if the players don't know what the outcome result of a loss in combat will be. But, that said, if you're not sure about a particular failure condition that you want to inflict on the PCs, you have one way to be absolutely sure that they'll like that failure result...
    \n
    \nAsk them. In fact, allow them to veto any particular result that really rankles with them. Ask them to come up with another equivalent, but more palatable failure. Instead of the character grovelling before the evil overlord, he resists to the end and the overlord kills his pet parrot instead, before sending the PC off to the salt mines. Oh now how sweet will the revenge be on that evil overlord when the character escapes, obtains the Sword of Justice, and uses it to run the evil overlord through!
    \n
    \nYou get the idea.
    \n
    \nNote that imprisonment is an interesting case. If you freeze Han Solo in carbonite, you do, in fact, take away the player's ability to play the character until he's thawed. So here's the key with all imprisonments... you have to still have something for the players to do. If they're in prison, then you either have to run prision dramas (which are fun), or you have to allow the escape attempt/rescue scenario. If, in fact, you've got the character frozen in carbonite, you have to then frame right to the rescue... else you're punishing the player for their loss until you do.
    \n
    \nGood scene-framing technique comes in handy here. Skip to the important stuff.

    \n Perhaps it would be interesting to have other kinds of flags? Like, the "I lose an eye" flag, or the "I run away" flag. Obviously, there are an infinite number of potential flags. This requires the GM to have a keener eye for judging stakes, but I think it preserves the balance you want. In fact, I think it rewards retiring players, because they can gamble for a simple mechanical advantage, rather than something showstopping.

      I think you may have Forge blindness, because your argument doesn't see some basic tenets of bog-standard role-playing outside the realm of reward-system-driven behavior-oriented RPGs, which, like you, I play a lot of. In "Raising the Stakes," that's not the case, on purpose: the reward system you see (gaining Conviction) isn't supposed to drive behavior. The resource system drives behavior, or more accurately, the resource system reflects and enhances behavior.

      In most role-playing games, this is how behavior and reward play out. Players have a limited number of resources, and they use them when they find them appropriate, or when they are most invested. For a simple example, imagine giving players in a D&D game one Potion of Bull Strength for their characters. One player might choose to use it when fighting a creature known to have damage resistance, and another player might choose to use it when fighting the guy who killed his brother. Both of these players choose to be more effective when it matters to them, reflecting their goals (one - to know the system and be an effective player, two - to extract revenge in story, to have an emotional experience.) This is how games like D&D, Vampire, Call of Cthulhu, and others manage to meet many Creative Agendas at once, by making the reward system secondary to the resource system, which is player-driven.

      Conviction, of course, is a resource, that you spend on moments that your character's effectiveness matters to you. It is more egalitarian than only giving it for certain behaviors, in that it allows people with different reasons for playing to have a common language for their resources, and have individual methods of spending those resources.
