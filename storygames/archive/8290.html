\n I'd call this the 'story engine' but that name has already been taken.

Picture a game where, for your character(s), all you choose is a few cookie-cutter templates. Classes, so to speak. These templates have a certain hierarchy of things, a tree of skills and feats and aspects and the like. On the other hand, everything is fixed - once you've decided on your character archetypes, you're locked in to your choice(s).

Now to control this hierarchy, we give the fiction itself, the story, a "character sheet" with its own statistics. Players, when playing their characters, earn 'xp' for the story itself. Perhaps to ameliorate the "you are locked into your choices" aspect, we could track several different story aspects with different statistics, and as said statistics rise in value, the templates have different 'trees'.

So let's say our story attributes are: "heroism, violence, romance" and they start with level 1 in each of them (I'll come up with better ones as this idea progresses, I hope). Then, say, our story has now reached 'heroism 3, violence 2, romance 5'. Thus all the players have 2 additional things from the "heroism" branch of their templates, 1 additional thing from the "violence" branch, and 4 additional things from the "romance" branch.

So I guess what I'm wondering now is - what kind of attributes should a story have?

    \n Some quick initial thoughts:

    A story should probably also have a class, based on various archetypes of stories. I can't quite remember the term I'm looking for at the moment, but there's a limited number of possible story archetypes, and the details of the particular story are irrelevant to the basic structure. Perhaps this should somehow factor into your story definition? Maybe the different archetypes can have a different persistent mechanical effect that affects the whole campaign.

    As for the attributes, going with what you have there, you might want to consider elements like Mystery, Intrigue, Exploration, Hardship, etc.

    Alternatively, you could give the story attributes based on the four types of conflict: Man vs. Man, Man vs. Society, Man vs. Nature, and Man vs. Himself.

    \n I suspect my intentions aren't all that clear, Marshall. My desire is to have a specific set of story attributes, and then have trees of skills, aspects, feats, etc. based on these attributes. So if players just choose 3 or 4, then I'm not having these fixed, pre-defined lists that are supposed to align with these attributes.

    That said I'm probably going to back away and go more old-school, where stories just have "levels" and classes just one path (a la AD&D 1st edition).

    Paul: Maybe, but I don't think so. Ideally, everyone at the table is earning this story XP, not just the person who happens to be playing banker (to use and butcher a monopoly metaphor). On the other hand, perhaps the GM/facilitator person has their own "classes" to pick from. And as these rise in level, so does the story change...

    Of course, that leads me back to story types as character classes, rather than abstract leveling devices.

    \n I think Marshall may be on to something there, Lx.
    \n
    \nTry this on for size...
    \n
    \nStory has, say, 3 attributes. The game has, say, 8 to choose from. The players decide that they want to play a story of Romance, Horror and Heroism. The other 5 are ignored for the purposes of this game.
    \n
    \nNow, Paul chooses to play the Lady's Man archetype. You go for the Femme Fatale. I decide I want to play the Lurking Horror.
    \n
    \nWe decide that the story begins with 0s in all three attributes, meaning our characters have no starting story abilities. As we play, your characters enter a romantic relationship, earning Romance XP for the story. I'm playing up Horror, because cliches are fun. The story levels up in Romance, so your Femme Fatale gains the level 1 Romance ability "enflame passion", Paul's Lady's Man gains "dashing", and I gain "forbidden allure".
    \n
    \nThis makes the game rather complicated to build, but it also makes the game infinitely extensible. People can create new archetypes and story attributes. In play leveling up is easy and fits the story, which is quite flexible, based on what the players are interested in.
    \n
    \nIt's an idea. It's a fair amount of work, but I think it'd have some potential.
