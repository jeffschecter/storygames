A player at the 'top' of the chain might come to the table with "Thwart plan to assassinate Bank Owner", and that would be the core of the mission. Another player might independently design the situation of "Prevent plane crash." Now, preventing the plane crash becomes part of thwarting the plan to assassinate the Bank Owner. We won't know how those two objectives meet until we get together & start playing.

    The prep time is for mission situation building. What's going to stand in the way of stopping that plane from crashing? What kind of choices might a team member have to make while on this part of the mission? That kind of stuff. Something analogous to both DitV's Town Building, and the stuff I used to do to prep to play Shadowrun.

    I've done quite a bit of work on Invisible Shepherds recently, and the whole GM-thing has seen some dramatic refinement. I'd like to share those changes with you.

    In a nutshell, the game is about an elite team of cyborgs tasked with preventing a very quiet alien invasion. The cyborgs are known as the Shepherds, the aliens as the Wolves, and the ignorant masses as, obviously, the Sheep.

    In character creation each player authors two members of the cyborg team, one PC Shepherd and one NPC Shepherd. For my purposes PC = Protagonist Character and NPC = Not a Protagonist Character. The Shepherd characters each fall into one of five categories.

    • The Director (NPC only)
    • The Commander (PC or NPC)
    • A Specialist (PC or NPC)
    • A Grunt (PC only)
    • A Robot (NPC only)

    Each team must contain one Director and one Commander (exactly one of each) and while it probably won't break the game for one player to play both, I'm going to encourage players to have those two played by different players.

    The Director is the character that heads up the team without usually taking part in any of the Big Action. He or she is often behind the scenes and political in nature.

    The Commander is the character that reports directly to the Director and commands the team in the field. Usually responsible for the safety and well-being of the team as a whole.

    A Specialist is a Shepherd who has a tremendous amount of relevant experience doing what the Shepherds do; Military field work, espionage, etc, etc. Generally reports directly to the Commander. Frequently responsible for leading Grunts. There has to be at least one Specialist on each team, but there are no restrictions on how many Specialists there can be.

    A Grunt is well... you probably get the picture. Possibly less experienced than a Specialist, but not necessarily so. No restrictions on the number of Grunts on a team.

    A Robot. Artificial intelligence in an artificial chassis. No restrictions on the number of Robots on a team.

    Ok, so there's lots more to be said about those five classifications and how they relate to game play, but I'll keep it at that for now. We have everything I think we need to talk about the GMing divisions.

    Pre-session, each player is responsible for authoring certain bits of The Mission.

    The player who authors and plays the Director is responsible for authoring the Objective. The Objective will generally center around The Bad Thing, which is also the Director-player's responsiblity to author. The Bad Thing is something that a Wolf has done or is trying to do. The Objective is to thwart the plans of or undo the mess that the Wolf has done.

    The Commander player authors the Wolf. Who is this 'person', what are their goals, and what are their resources? The Commander-player authors the Wolf with no knowlege of what the Director-player authors as an Objective or Bad Thing.

    Every Specialist player authors Teeth. Teeth are the obstacles that will get in the way of the Shepherds achieving the Objective. Teeth can be hazards like Guard Dogs, A Well-armed Security Team, or Lasers From Space. They can be unknown elements, like not knowing when the objective can take place, or where the Wolf is hiding. I'm kinda hip-deep in tuning up the Teeth right now, but you get the idea of what the Specialist-players need to do.

    As of right this minute, having a Grunt character or Robot character presents you with no responsibilities for pre-session prep work. I'd kind of done that on purpose so that players who wanted to avoid doing much prep could theoretically take a Grunt/Robot combination and get out of the work. But now I have a vague idea for other things that I'd like to see thrown in as prep and I no longer think that any player should be missing the responsiblity of prep.

    So, looking back on the idea that each character type brings different responsiblities to the players, and each player always has two characters, we can see that players can mix & match what their responsiblities are for pre-game prep.

    I welcome any questions, even if they don't relate to the GM-prep thing.

    -Eric

    That's a fantastic example, Jason. The minor error you made illustrates the minor issue that I've got with it.

    The Director and Robots cannot be PCs.... aaaaaaaannd... a Grunt cannot be an NPC. But with limited supplies of each it seems pretty darned likely that some player some time is going to want a specific NPC-only character over any particular PC. What I'm so obtusely saying is that I don't see any need to have the PCs chosen in the first round and the NPCs chosen in the second round. Let it get all mixed up.

    Of course, in either version, we'd have to contend with making sure that everyone gets 1 PC and 1 NPC. Because some of the classes can be either PC or NPC and some must be one or the other, there's the possiblity that the last person to pick a character might get 'stuck'.

    Twisting Jason's example;

    3 players = 1 Director, 1 Commander, 2 Specialist, 1 Grunt, 1 Robot

    J: Commander PC!
    M: Specialist PC!
    E: Specialist PC + Robot NPC!
    M: Director NPC!
    J: Um... I need an NPC, but only the Grunt is left...

    Actually, I suppose that issue could be resolved by declaring that the PC or NPC only characters have to be chosen first... but then it seems to be cutting into the funocity of this choosing-method.

The idea was to not only have a pre-set list of classes to play but to also pre-pair them and randomly distribute via playing cards.
