    \n Plenty of games include rules and situations which, if you don't want to get violenced, reward talking before attacking. As for the specifically Tarantino thing of the talking lasting a long time and subtly navigating the characters' positions w.r.t. violence throughout, I'm quite sure there aren't any systems that do that.

    You could probably do a Dogs in the Vineyard hack where instead of "talking / physical / punching / shooting" it's "posturing / baiting / threatening / shooting". But even that wouldn't mechanize the core dynamics of shift-able allegiances and triggers. You'd have to cover those things in scenario-creation and character-creation, so that's more hacking.

    Actually, if you can work with the Anasazi color, How We Came to Live Here might work for this if you crank up the Inside (societal) pressures and delay or distance the Outside (wilderness, monsters) pressures.

    The third thing I can think of is to use Burning Wheel's Duel of Wits a lot, but change the way stakes are set. Instead of being about getting NPCs to buy your side or an argument over an opponent's, you could write up some system of how each Duel establishes each participant as trustworthy or useful or dangerous or suspicious or whatever specific categories you want to break Tarantino-ism into. The standard BW win/lose/minor compromise/major compromise rules could be used to determine degree.

    \n This is the sort of thing you can model with Showdown, I think! You have a back-and-forth conflict between characters, which can take whatever flavor you want. The important part is that the entire game is about establishing the relationship between the two characters and building to one last final scene of violence.

    You could also do a neat Penny For My Thoughts hack, using the same structure and adding an endgame where each player makes a single choice and secretly prioritizes their targets of violence. I'm not entirely sure how you'd do it, but you could then have a set of rules which uses the prioritization to determine what the outcome is--anything from one dead sod to an entire roomful of mutually dead people.

    \nReally what I have found, at least for the way I like these things to work, is to have a sufficiently lethal system where everyone is just as vulnerable to a gun shot. The closer fights are to a 50-50 proposition, the more people will be cautious in the build up and hedge their bets. So I like using systems where a gunshot at least stands a chance of killing in a single hit. The rest is more about the players and the initial set up for the scenario I think.

    \n That's a good insight. Perhaps a tense negotiation scene can be modeled as various characters trying to:

    a) Get a sense for how quick their opposition is. (You don't want to draw your gun on someone who will draw faster and shoot first.)

    b) Establish an advantage for yourself when it comes to the showdown: maneuver so as to position yourself ideally while trying to throw off your opponents (psychologically, in terms of physical location - the classic "speech from the shadow", someone moving just out of sight and delivering a monologue while their target tries to draw a bead on them - or perhaps in some other fashion).

    If your rules include some advantage for declaring and taking aim, that could also really help. (You can choose to focus your attention on a particular enemy ahead of time, which gives you an advantage if you choose to attack them, but puts you at a disadvantage against other targets.)

    There is now a reason to have these tense standoffs - everyone is silently/stealthily/subtly maneuvering for advantage before violence breaks out.

    \n One thing I've found is taking the paranoia route in things like these can naturally lead to huge potential for build up to outrageous violence within the party, while still having the players feel like they are in control of their characters. In my mafia game, each character received a Family Goal assigned to them from the boss at regular intervals. These were often things like "Whack player character X". They were secret. So you never knew if the guy sitting next to you at the table had orders to kill you (and again if the system is sufficiently lethal all it takes is him getting the drop on you, so your guard is way up and you hedge). Applying something similar would probably help with a Hateful 8 session.

    \n A note on Tarantino methodology:

    The dude writes a ridiculous amount of character backstory.

    Everything we see on the screen from the actors is informed by tons and tons of things Tarantino has told them about what their characters have done and seen and been through and how they've taken it.

    Well, that and witty lines. But in an RPG, you can't script those.

    Doing everything possible to make the characters vivid and specific and alive in the players' imaginations, though, might be effort well spent.

    \n Regarding the former, the structure builds towards a climax which plays into the character backgrounds. Likewise, the very first scene, almost without exception, organically devolves into a "pissing contest" ramping up the tension and beginning the jockeying to be alpha-dog.

    Regarding the latter, and addressing the crux of a Tarantino-esque drama, are the extensive character descriptions, which players seem to have no trouble making cool. There are plenty of accessible tropes for someone less comfortable/familiar with "role playing" as well as wiggle room for more experienced/confident players.

    Another important aspect of the game is, without giving too much away, a certain time element a la Reservoir Dogs. For those invested in the experience, and prepared to stay in character, this creates lulls and awkward silences which tend to encourage the type of incidental dialog we've come to associate with Tarantino's work.

    \n I am glad to know all that! Thank you. Now I know how to parse your earlier replies, and I have a much better idea of how you tackle the issues in the opening post.

    Secret pre-gen character backgrounds, a starting situation which primes the pressure, designed interesting relationships between the characters and that situation, plus a countdown to a sort of "point of no return" (I assume at least some of the characters need to get certain stuff resolved before the pick-up) sounds great to me.

    Despite the fact that most Tarantino movie characters aren't literally looking at clocks, I think there's often a similar sense of "get stuff resolved before it's too late". Hateful Eight kind of moves between timers -- the expected storm duration, the expected appearance of other characters, etc. Even "when the meal's over" in that grueling scene where Shosanna has to sit with Landa in Inglourious Basterds, or "when we can leave without arousing suspicion" in the same film's basement scene. While often one character is content to wait it out (or even drag it out!), another usually isn't, and that contributes to the tension.

    And those character-situation relationships are not the easiest thing to guarantee in "make your own" play, so I see how the pre-gens and the heist are great ingredients for that.
