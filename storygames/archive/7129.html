    \n Plain text is an option particularly if you want to distribute just the text.

    Generally, I myself would try to find a file format that is open and widely used. Open meaning that the specifications of the format are published and anyone can program a viewer for that particular file format. This is important because closed file formats can become obsolete when the party that was responsible for producing the viewers and editors for them goes out of business.

    Based on that I would be against using MsOffice formats (not open) and a bit averse to using OpenOffice formats (are those so widely used that you could imagine just about everyone having the software needed to view them?).

    Besides maybe postscript (from LaTeX sources) and plain text, I can't really come up with really competitive alternatives to pdf and html by my requirements. And postscript viewers, while available for free (GhostScript), are not necessarily that well spread.

    EDIT: Rtf is obviously an option as it is free (and thus also open) and can be read by most of the word processing software as well as a variety of viewers.

    \n Scalability (not zooming) means that folks would need a way to edit the base font (default font size) to literally scale it up--for instance, to print Body Text at 20 points instead of the default 10 points (and automatically have all other heading fonts and so forth scale up proportionately).

    That means a desktop publishing-friendly format. Pagination will just happen, with any of the document formats mentioned above, except a custom Flash setup or HTML/XML/dHTML. And here's the rub: with those non-paginating setups, unless you're REALLY clever, you will get all sorts of widows and orphans and even lines printed half on one page and half on another (Flash, in particular, unless you get giggy with the underlying code) when the end user prints it out after setting all their scalability things the way they want them.

    You only option with such difficult requirements is to use plain text, which an end user could scale with any old document viewer or DTP program (or possibly even with a simple printer setting--old school printers had font size controls built into drivers... and even as buttons on the front of the printer, if you go back to the eighties!).

    Good luck. I'd consider just having multiple PDF formats packaged for the end user, and let them select a Large Font, or Small Monitor, or Print Perfect version (or whatever--could get to be several variations very quickly, if you want to be all things for all people) to suit their needs from moment to moment.

    Interesting problem, really. Modern PDFs are quite accessibility-friendly (i.e. screen readers, Read Aloud, zoom) but lack many controls for actually shifting between a default print size and a different page layout or a Large font version. Hmmm... might be a good RFE for Acrobat 9, come to think of it: automatic Large Font conversion of any PDF, with widow and orphan control right in Reader. it fits the "build once, use anyhow" philosophy of modern electronic document delivery.

    \n Hi Shreyas,

    Before you proceed, you might also want to consider readability. It might seem like a good idea to allow someone to expand the text to fill the width of a 21" screen, but how many people actually want to read lines of text that are 30 or 40 words long? Lots of research has found that 15 or fewer words is optimal for reading speed. There's a reason newspapers have narrow columns. Also, 15 words in 12 point text fits on almost any monitor these days.

    The drawback of allowing flexibility to screen width is that it imposes the need on the user to adjust the screen width before reading or else to slog through long lines.

    Ultimately, there is a positive payback to fixed lines of 15 or less words. This is probably why there isn't much support for flexible line length in applications intended solely for reading.

    \nCompiled HTML format is the format that Microsoft uses for its help files. It sounds like it might be a good fit for you, Shreyas:

    Advantages:
      \n
    1. Cross-platform; viewers exist for Windows, Linux, and the Mac.
    2. One document = one file.
    3. Font size is user-adjustable
    4. The page reflows to accomodate window size.
    5. Is searchable (which is damn essential for e-texts) and TOC-izable.
    6. Is copy-and-pastable (any electronic format that isn't sucks ass; I'm lookin at you, Flash.)
    7. Can be internally hyperlinked (which is awesome when done well, like with the Pragmatic Programmer books)
    \nDisadvantages:
      \n
    1. Your Mac and Linux-using customers likely don't have a viewer installed on their system.
    2. CHTML docs print about as nicely as web pages do.
    \nI have no idea about how to actually make CHTML files, though. Authoring them might be a pain in the ass. I also have no idea of the accessibility issues around them, though I guess they interact peaceably with accessibility utilities that can work with web pages.

    Posted By: AlanThere's a reason newspapers have narrow columns.
    ...which has nothing to do with number of words; rather, it has to do with how far a person can scan without moving their head. Paperback novel sizing is, in part, a factor of this common length, at a comfortable holding/viewing distance of ~2 feet (the other factor is how many signatures one can fit on a conventional web press of 48", at the time the standard was set).
    ----
    So, to recap:
    You want a standard format, common across platforms... that doesn't allow printing or text copying... and isn't HTML or PDF.
    You want viewing to be scalable... but not page-demarcated.

    Uh... there's nothing.
    PDF has (weak) copy and print security, but it uses a pages paradigm, though... hmmm... one COULD do a HUGE, single-page PDF, like 8.5" x 220", for an "eleven page" document with no page breaks, a "scroll"--that's what you want, really; a digital scroll that can't be printed or screen captured.
    HTML and its ilk is designed for all the accessibility stuff... but isn't securable (even weakly) and it's out by fiat anyway.

    Try Flash, which has the "unlimited scroll" and which can scale and which can't be copied and only is printable with a painful screen-capture and stitch-together process. You'll have to deal with some folks having issues getting a Player to run (weak PCs, odd OSes), but it fulfills most of your unwieldy requirements.

    Posted By: shreyasPretty sure, yes. I have strong feelings about separating the digital product from the physical product. I really don't want any hard copies coming from anywhere but the source, and if it's not possible to prevent those without having no digital product, then so be it.
    \nNot to throw a monkey wrench in, but you can't prevent that even if you don't have a digital product. People in the RPG "warez" scene routinely scan paper RPG books and distribute the scanned versions. I've seen many things available for download that didn't have any "official" digital version.

    The best defenses against illegal copying are easy availability and reasonable pricing. "Limited editions" or books that are very hard to find get scanned, because the people who scan them get reputation in the scene for doing it. Ridiculously expensive books get scanned for the same reason.

    On the other side of the scene you have the people doing the downloading -- and they too, are less likely to download an illegal copy of something if they can easily find it and buy it. There are some people out there who feel a need to download everything they can... but that's almost more a form of OCD than anything else. Generally, those people don't even read most of what they download... they just feel a need to HAVE IT ALL.
