    Can't really comment on the examples because I wasn't there; most snippets of roleplaying come out as boring since you're missing the framework where they happened. I'll offer a counter example which you'll probably find boring, but I found it to be awesome.

    We we're starting a mini-campaign/oneshot with a Twin Peak-ish theme. I had four players; I had prepared backgrounds for them - just scracthed five different notes about them, and picked up one thing they were really good at. All characters were FBI-agents.

    We went through initial briefing and gathering equipment quite fast; the characters hopped into car and started driving towards this little town in New England. On the way, they decided that it was time for late-breakfast, stopped by a roadside cafeteria. And spent two hours having the breakfast, going into details of who orders what, how they eat, and discussing the mission.

    Why? I think they wanted to establish the characters by exploring them and interacting with them. Coming up with details based on and expanding the very brief notes they had, both established the characters as full-fledged (and very archetype, in FBI/Twin Peaks -sense) persons both for themselves and other players. Upon leaving the cafeteria, they knew whom they were and whom the other characters were. Aside from that, they pretty much established the theme and tone of the game, which I then played along, as the GM.

    So, I think some of your examples could have something to do with simulation; players want their characters to interact with the enviroment and other characters, in order get a better image of who they are, where they are and who are these other people. Could be and probably is a lot other stuff as well. That just jumped out for me.

    Joel, in reading your examples I'm seeing CA-crash after CA-crash after CA-crash. I'm also not seeing any player-flagging techniques being used, which would obscure that problem. You, rather obviously, want some tightly-constructed, hard-hitting Story Now awesomeness. Some of your friends seem, as Jukka suggests, are looking for some more loosely-constructed character-exploring simulationism. There also seems to be some social group power dynamics (displaying mastery of bow-making techniques, GM/player "where am I standing" stuff) expressing in less than pleasant ways.

    The short form of it all is that I see a group of players, each trying to fulfill their own, divergent interests in a group environment without ever expressing what those interests are in anything but indirect means. This means that 99% of the time, the content of play is engaging for one player but "boring" for the others.

    Now, before I go on to "what to do about it," I really feel I need to say: this may not be something that can be fixed. You and your friends may have unreconcilable differences in interests such that they cannot all be satisfied with a single solution. In order to enjoy the non-boring play you want, you may need to find a new group (which may or may not include one or two members of your current group). That doesn't mean that you don't hang out with these friends, or even that you stop playing RPGs with them, but you may need to look for your preferred style of play somewhere else.

    I would suggest to you that, in order to begin to fix your problem, you first need to get a good handle on what your friends' interests are. One-shots of fringey games can be good tools for doing this. Playing the Pool or Conquer the Horizon or Lacuna, and playing it consciously to see what your friends do in the sometimes vastly different circumstances, can be eye-opening. It can also be eye-opening for everybody else: if you introduce one such game and explicitly point out things like "because there's no GM, you get to say where your guy is without asking anybody else," it can show them that a game can still function without that GM/player power structure.

    Then talk about it. Talk to people singly, talk to them in a group. Get them talking about what they like and what they don't like, what they liked about the one-shots and what they didn't like about them, and so on. That you better understand what they want is good; that they better understand what they want is fantastic.

    Then in the far-flung future, you may try alternating games, for instance, so that there's a sim-heavy get-into-details game and a heavy-hitting story now game running, just trading off times. "Roleplaying" can still be something that your group of friends does together, just with a more explicit understanding of why you do it together and how you can all collectively scratch each other's itches.

    2a - Whoso Draws This Bow From This Tree Is Rightwise Born King Of BESM. Arguments over character capability can mean a whole hell of a lot of different things. This one is going to be hard to pull up the nails on and figure out what is underneath. Once you have that nailed down, you know what kind of solution to apply, and not before. Stopping the buck is not going to actually stop the conflict. There will always be some other power to argue about.

First, you have to consider the possibility that this doesn't mean much of anything at all. Gaming has elements of improv acting in it and improv is hard to do well. Not everything you do is going to knock everyone's socks off and it has nothing to do with game selection or play style. It may just be that the conversationalists thought it was going to be cool/hilarious/insightful and it turned out not to be. Hell, if someone is bad at improvving, MOST of what they do might be lame and they're just getting off on the adrenaline rush of performance. There are other possibilities too.

    Regardless of which possibility is the correct one, the only feedback Ben and Steve are getting "in the moment" are the in-character reactions of the other players which, since they are in-character, are ALWAYS going to be seen as positive reactions, even if they are appalled/disdainful/etc. After all, they are committing to the improv. Fostering an atmosphere in which you can give negative feedback is extremely difficult for a leisure time activity. They have to be committed to improving their play, like a chessplayer. The first few times you put the question of specific feedback (as in, "what did you guys think of the scene with the three dogs and the long underwear? Funny or lame?") everyone is going to say positive things just because they don't want to jeopardize the group. You have to get people comfortable with negative feedback before they're willing to listen to it. Some are confident enough that they are immediately comfortable with it, others not so. It's a tough call.

    2b & 4) First, the reactive player thing is just a preference like anything else. My wife loves roleplaying but hates any contribution that is outside her character. "If I wanted to do that, I'd GM. I don't want to do that. Stop asking me to do that. If the game requires me to do that, I will bow out and the rest of you can play." is her firm response. So there's a chance that at bottom, Colleen may just not like to scene frame, period. I've been able to sneak some scene framing in a little bit just by really obviously and explicitly going with their expressed preference. For example, I've gotten some good response from doing this: "All right, here's the plotlines that are cooking right now, A, B, C, and D - don't forget, you all have contacts and investigation type skills. Erica, what do you want to do?" "I want to go talk to my father and see if he knows anything about the car bombing." "Do you want to visit him at home or invite him to your restaurant, or have him over to your apartment for dinner?" "Umm...at his home is good." "Do you drive yourself or take one of your family's cars?" "Oh, I use a driver." "What do you wear?" "My professional businesslike clothes, how I normally dress when managing the restaurant." "Is he surprised to see you or did you call ahead?" "It's a surprise." "So he's maybe still in his dressing gown and slippers?" "Sure." And so on. There are plenty of times when someone has asked a question and I've successfully put it back in their laps by saying "Whatever." dismissively. The downside to doing this is that it's possible, with some players even likely, that they didn't have a clear idea or preference for the answer of the questions, "Where are they sitting? Are they close enough to touch?" and if you throw it back in their lap, they'll say "Whatever" too and there's the naked result that everyone realizes nobody cares about a good chunk of the scene. That can kill inertia even when it's honest. Where was I going with this? I forget. Anyway, try breaking the scene framing down further into direct A-or-B questions that connect directly to stuff on their character sheets, then expand it out once they're comfortable with that, knowing that it's just a preference like any other.
