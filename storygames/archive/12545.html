\n
\n
\n
\n There's a school of thought that says that if your character isn't present in a scene, you shouldn't contribute to it. Rules clarifications, OK. Table talk, yes. Suggestions, no. Direct speech, no.
\n
\nI'm becoming more and more convinced that this school of thought is a load of nonsense. At least, with regard to suggestions. Letting players make them is a way of keeping everyone engaged with the game, even if their characters aren't present. Player involvement trumps wrong-headed considerations of realism.
\n
\nOne of my regular players gets extremely annoyed if players whose characters aren't present make suggestions. They even go so far as to not pay attention if their character isn't in the current scene, so they can be more 'real' when they're clued in later.
\n
\nI think I should be allowed to horsewhip them.
\n
\nWhere does everybody else stand?

    \nIt's absolutely indispensable in Paranoia, where it gives the normally merciful GM (as I tend to be) a chance to kill off a few clones without unduly disrupting the narrative.

    And I find it useful in espionage/thriller type games, where characters are often split up and asked to make very quick decisions. If there are players whose characters aren't at the scene, I'll usually let them shout out suggestions (e.g. "Cut the blue wire!"; "Swim for the shore!"; "Throw a grenade down the chute") if I think that their ideas would naturally occur to a properly trained agent (which most players are not) as a matter of course. This really helps bring the social gamers or less-than-brilliant players up to speed with the more accomplished folks.
    But I'll cut them off and prohibit the suggested action if what is being recommended is either unduly esoteric or especially clever. So sometimes I'll say "you're not there" when somebody has called out an ingenious but non-obvious suggestion that would have saved the day, and the exquisite pain on everybody's part when that happens adds enormously to the overall suspense. They usually only hate me for a few minutes afterward...

    \n [EDIT: CROSSPOSTED WITH POST 23 ONWARDS.]

    Strikes me that there are two issues tangled up with each other, that could stand to be teased apart: When you should give input (and what kind), and then when you should pay attention.

    The answer to the latter is, all the time. I'm not interested in playing games where people shut down while their character's not in the scene; I want a group who's all interested and excited in everybody's shit, and I want a game procedure that bolsters that and prevents it from getting boring. Quick transitions when characters are apart is one way to help that. Allowing at least some kinds of input from those in the dugout is another.

    Which brings us to the former issue: what input when? Here's where it gets more nuanced and individual to different games or creative agenda. Tio start with, I thik this problem can be further subdivided into: How much help and advice should you give other players? as distinct from Should you talk when your character's not involved? Working backwards again, of COURSE you should talk when you're "not there," because sitting silently while others get to speak is going to make the proceedings tedious, and we want the game to be interesting and fun at all times for everyone. That said, the talk should be the kind that's non-disruptive, by whatever the standards and tastes of the group: non-tone-breaking (no Monty Python quotes. no snarky quips in a serious scene, etc.), non-intrusive (don't talk over people, don't start an involved conversation when the scene players are deep into it, etc.) and whatever else the group values.

    So now we get down to "what help, and how much?" which in my experience is perhaps the stickiest issue. it's largely game- and group-dependent; Adam rightly points out that in a game like 4E with it's clearly delineated mechanical options, if you give too much "advice" you're basically playing all the characters. But even outside of that particular paradigm, I've found that if someone is too zealous in suggesting options, I end up feeling like there's nothing much for me to contribute--WHETHER THAT PLAYER'S CHARACTER IS THERE OR NOT. And of course, the more emotionally invested a situation is, the more I'm going to feel like that choice is mine and mine alone to make. I'll feel robbed if someone jumps in with "Oooh, what if you throw your wedding ring down at her feet, and leave her right then and there, and drive across town to shoot her lover?"

    Also, I've seen new and inexperienced players subjected to this treatment and just shut down, creatively and socially. They're too polite to object, but the wresting of control is palpable. Often they seem to think that this is just the way things are supposed to go, and it colors their impression of the whole activity. And it makes me sad.

    So that issue has nothing to do with "realism" or "metagaming" or any of that mess--I think the "you're not there!" ploy is a defense akin to nailing a Mafia Don for tax evasion. The GM in that instance probably wants to see what player A will do in this or that tense situation, and player B keeps butting in with all this overbearing "help." So when he has 'im dead to rights, the GM brings down the "not there" hammer, 'cause at least in THAT situation, there's no plausible way to justify the character advising the other, and we finally get so see what Player A will do at crunch time. The "there-ness" issue in that case is a red herring--have you ever seen a group of players in the space of a single combat round (six seconds, right?) coordinate an extremely detailed and synergistic attack plan between them, to the consternation of the GM? Same thing.

    So what we really have is an issue of social appropriateness in advice-giving, (possibly) a form of social bullying, and the issue of how to solve it--more social bullying, by a group authority figure, or some other way? Maybe we should turn to discussing what those other ways would look like? I mean, it boils down to social instincts, but how to foster that if players struggle with them or don't have them? Just saying "respect everyone" or "value everyone's input" isn't helpful at that point. Like, how? What does that look like?

    Peace,
    -Joel

    \n So, I see a couple of issues being brought up. I think it's important to realize that they're all about the player talking--they have nothing at all to do with the characters talking:

    1. Social acceptability of speaking up when we're trying to get input from another player. If you're too loud, Jane doesn't get to say her piece, that's no fun. This is probably the most common issue, right? You need to know when to shut up--but it's not really about "realism" or where your character's sitting. It's about when you, [insert your name here], are talking all over your friends.

    2. Tactical decisions--in a context where the "right" decision is important, especially a tactical game that's supposed to be a test of your (the player's) wits, someone else suggesting something to you that's better than what you might have thought of yourself is effectively "cheating". Your wits are no longer being tested.

    3. Some people's desire for a certain type of immersion: to have the information they receive as they play to be as close as possible to what their character is receiving/experiencing. That player may want to leave the room when they're not in the scene, for instance. In their case, even a reasonable and socially-polite interjection or advice from the other players can distract them from their desired sense of immersion ("my character can't hear you!").

    In any case, here's where it comes down to for me:

    If a player can ONLY speak in-character in your game--that's important to the game aesthetic somehow--then, yeah, if you're not in the scene, don't talk. But if you can offer out-of-game advice when your character is in the scene, but you won't let another player do the same just because their character "isn't there"... that's total bullshit.

    To me "don't offer advice when you're not there" only makes sense in conjunction with "don't speak out-of-character, period."

      Posted By: QueexThere's a school of thought that says that if your character isn't present in a scene, you shouldn't contribute to it. Rules clarifications, OK. Table talk, yes. Suggestions, no. Direct speech, no.

      I'm becoming more and more convinced that this school of thought is a load of nonsense. At least, with regard to suggestions.       I beg to differ. Let the other players have their moment with their characters, and they will give you yours.

      A role-playing game, in spite of being a dialogue-based activity, don't really need all of us to chatter on all the time. Be silent! Listen in!
