\n This forum seems to tackle quite specific problems for specific games. I was wondering if there are any resources you could recommend for a more general approach to designing storytelling/RPG games? I'm kinda new at this, and I'm sure experience will go a long way, but whilst I can read stuff, having not played enough, I don't always see the reasons behind design choices. On the other hand, recurring issues or problems with games would also be a good point to learn from.

I ask for pointers as I do believe storytelling games are probably the least rule-adhering games to design, but I'm generally looking for a bit of footing to work from. What I've got in mind is a storytelling game guided by some game mechanics. Think Fiasco for example, a system with a lot of liberty on how to flesh out your story, but very specific steps that ensure certain aspects of the game.

Hope you have some links, pointers, notable failures, pitfalls, articles, anything for me!

Thanks!

    \n The best way is to get direct experience with games of all stripes. Watch and experience play, and figure out how the game accomplished that effect (or if it wasn't the game but the players). There's not really an "approach" to designing a more story-based game, just a bunch of tools that are constantly being invented, discovered, and refined.

    I'd particularly recommend Vincent Baker's musings. They're especially designed as thought-provoking statements about design in games, and the correlation of fiction and gaming. Find somewhere to start, and dig in!

    Specific games I'd recommend looking at: Dogs in the Vineyard, Apocalypse World, and (as a wildcard that doesn't get pegged a lot here) John Wick's "Blood & Honor".

    \n Play as many games as you can, and read even more. Constantly be asking "why did they do this? What's the point?" Every rule has two effects: what it says on paper and what it does at the table. Sometimes it's not obvious until you see it in play, but the more you play games the easier it becomes to just read a text and go "oh, right, we reward failure in this case because it incentivizes this certain behavior, I get it..." or whatever. I've found that reading theory is really helpful but I've probably gotten more out of reading and playing the actual stuff in practice.

    \n If you're anywhere near New Jersey, consider attending Metatopia (Nov 1-3, I think). It's a game convention for designers. You'll probably bump into lots of indie game designers of all kinds, and you'll get the chance to play in the many playtest events.

    \n While I agree that playing and reading other people's games is good, I find playing and writing my own games to be better. That is, I pick up a neat mechanism or two from other people's games once in a while, but I learn so much more from just trying things out in play and seeing what they do. Hell, even mulling ideas over and thinking about how it would work and fit together is really useful. I designed about 50 (I'm not even joking) games before my first published one, and I still design and partially write down maybe 10 games for every published game (I've published three, soon four, now working on my fifth). And about three games make it to playtesting for every game I release.

    And do playtest. A lot. I've noticed that my published games have little in common with the first version I brought to playtest. Also, don't listen too much to your playtesters. They will resist changes if they have played earlier versions and they'll think your game is fine when it actually needs a big redesign.

    Other random tip: Think in terms of cycles and trends. What happens when this value goes up? What other values will tend to go up? How will this affect rolls? What will that effect on the rolls have on the value? Things like death spirals and feedback loops are great tools if you're aware of them but can ruin your game if you're not. A great way to build a game is to design it around a cycle. People will do A, which makes B happen. When B happens, C will get easier and A more difficult, so people will do C. C, in turn, will eventually trigger D, at which point A is again the easier option. Stuff like that.

    \n As others said, play as many games as possible. Read as many games as possible. Think critically about what you do in play and what you read. Identify what works and what doesn't, and think about the differences between what is on the page and what happens at the table.

    Know what your game is about. Each system encourages and discourages certain behaviors in players. (Causing your players to
    When you start to make a game, you should eventually be able to identify clearly what your game is about. Your game design will go easier if you can answer these three big questions or these three even tougher questions. You don't have to answer all the questions before you begin a design, but knowing the answer will make design decisions clearer. It may benefit you to figure out what your game is about on several different levels, as well.


    Vince Baker's best posts on game design are collected here: http://lumpley.com/index.php/anyway/thread/554

    You'd also do well to read and learn about non-RPG game design (probability is obvious, but feedback loops are way more important). You can find a ton of information online about it in various forms. (I got some good mileage out of MIT's online college courses about game design.) And reading about narrative structure and group dynamics and any number of other topics that tangentially relate to RPGs. And any reading you can find on the subject of your game, too.

    Not reading but the RPG Design Panelcast is a podcast that has some really good seminars and lectures about game design. Luke Crane and Jared Sorensen's "Game Design is Mind Control" is very eye opening and informative.

    And do playtest. A lot. I've noticed that my published games have little in common with the first version I brought to playtest. Also, don't listen too much to your playtesters.     \nI once read an article about a videogame designer whose playtesters said that reloading in combat took too long and that traveling took too long. The obvious answer would be to decrease the length of each. Instead, they added additional frames of animation to the reload animation, giving it more detail. And they lowered the player's camera angle, so that they were closer to the ground and therefore felt that they were going faster (when they still moved the same speed).

    Playtesting is a lot like that bit that Neil Gaiman says: "...when people tell you somethings wrong or doesn't work for them, they are almost always right. When they tell you exactly what they think is wrong and how to fix it, they are almost always wrong."
