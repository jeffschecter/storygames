\n We've been playing a lot of fourth edition around here, and it's showing its seams a bit, which is great. We've learned that it's difficult to prepare effective encounters for three-player groups, for example, since the game is optimized for four.

Personally, I'm quite interested in discovering things it does well and poorly. Sometimes outcomes are unsatisfying in situations that, in other games, would probably be pretty cool. The system's ability to handle off-the-wall ideas relies on the GM's largesse and inspiration to a huge degree, so trying something that isn't a known quantity can be really wonderful or a complete waste of time, depending on the GM's call. Call me a baby; I'm not really used to that.

Conversely, sometimes working within the structure of the game produces results that you wouldn't imagine otherwise. Last night the three halfling Cranwinkle brothers and their two goblin henchmen were throwing down with a white dragon, a pretty badass foe. We were churning through all our daily and encounter powers trying to make a dent in the thing's frosty hide. My halfling guy is a Rogue, and he has this power called Topple Over. It reads "Target: One Creature". So I turn up the Man-O-War soundtrack in my head, rush the dragon, make a DX vs. AC roll, succeed, and knock the thing on its reptilian ass. It was a satisfying moment for me, and it never would have happened outside the framework of the game.

    \n This came up in my bi-weekly gaming group the other day, regarding not being able to get off one's daily powers (to which I retorted that if everyoone at the table did an "Aid Another", that means that the person using the ability gets (usually) around a +10 to do the task, which is a great levelling device: Take that "need to roll 16 or higher" down to "need to roll 6 or higher".

    However, it doesn't scale to games of less than 6 players. So here's my fix:

    =====
    So, there's this rule that's really easy to overloook, that I believe
    even solves my own problems I had with my warlord: Aid Another.

    A member of your team describes how they are helping you, then makes
    an attack roll against difficulty 10 (this is pretty easy to beat). If
    you succeed, you add +2 to another's roll.

    I had times where I got bum results for dailies and encounter powers
    that were powerful, but I realized that all we needed to do was to all
    chip in and the others could have given me up to a +10 bonus (with 5
    others) to make it work. That, I believe, pretty much fixes my
    percieved problem.

    Thing is, I often roll with far fewer members. So I made a house-rule
    adjusting Aid Another depending on how many people there are, giving
    it a standard of a plus 10 to 12 (all the players, minus the one being
    aided).

    So with two players, Aid Another grants +10
    3 players, +5
    4 players, +4
    5 players, +3
    6 players and on: +2
    =====

    \n Three-player groups have proven to be difficult, but doable in D&D4: you just sort of have to write your own advice about how to prep. I imagine it'd be way harder with three strikers or three leaders. We've got a paladin, a rogue, and a cleric, and that combo's been very effective when we work together.

    I'm playing the paladin, and I love the defender role to death. It is great. I'm probably the worst hitter - the cleric's a little more optimized for punching it up - but I screw up big monsters terribly. At level 4, my AC is 22 (24 vs opportunity attacks), and we have various ways to bump that even further up. I'm almost impossible to hit - and when the DM manages to, I have the halfling second chance encounter power. I can challenge a big creature and force it to attack me - which will fail - or attack someone else with penalties and take damage. Remi - the DM - finally decided last night to just take the damage and attack someone else.

    \n The main reason I wouldn't want to allow a general aid other attack is that some classes (specifically Clerics and Artificers) already have that as one of their core abilities, and it's a big part of their cool. Also, considering that Aid other can be performed in conjunction with flanking and other move-centric ways to help each other out, I think I prefer the complexity of lots of smallw ays to help more than one big one. If it ever reaches a point where aid other is cooler than doing something, that makes for dull play.

    Just my two bits.

    Posted By: AndyTake that "need to roll 16 or higher" down to "need to roll 6 or higher".
    \nIf you're needing a 16 to hit in the first place, it's possible that something's already gone wrong. Unless you're fighting something well above your level, you should be hitting on 10-12's most of the time. Add flanking, and the myriad of bonuses you can get from team mates, and you can drop that significantly.

    For example, the young white dragon has an AC of 18 (it's level three, but it's also a brute, so it has a lower AC than normal). A level one rogue with a 16 dex (low average) and a dagger (+4 proficiency) hits on an 11. Add flanking (which the rogue should aim to get every round), and a +3 (low average) from a Warlord's "Furious Smash" - an at-will power, and you're hitting on a 6+. A really optimized party can quickly drop that to a 2+.
