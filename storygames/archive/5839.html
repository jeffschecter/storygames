\n I'm curious how all of you TSOY players out there are making use of the Weapons and Armor rules, whether you play with the default Near setting or cook up your own. It seems to me like an under-utilized mechanic in the rules, and I'm not sure why.

Our group ends up not using them much, at least partly because we often avoid going into the Bringing Down the Pain altogether. Maybe we don't use it because it seems like extra fiddly bits, but I think at least partly it's lack of imagination. I know I've used a "Medallion of Law" in one game, which gave +3 Armor in legal debates in the royal court, and in another game a briefcase full of money was a +3 Weapon in negotiations (Sway).

So how much do you use Weapons and Armor? Are they just the product of character Secrets, or do you introduce them to the story in other ways? Outside of the obvious combat model (swords, shields, etc.), what other creative and interesting ways have you put the Weapons and Armor to the test? Alternately, if you don't use them, why not?

    \n So in our TSOY 7th Sea game there is lots of weapon and armor usage -- though it doesn't always see mechanical effect as often as it sees color effect.

    Like, Olivia (PC) has the weapons and armor: Signature Weapon, Fencing Scarf (+2 vs fencers, 1 bonus die); Mayoral Signet Ring (+3 political armor vs citizens of Numa), Mayoral Seal (+3 political weapon vs citizens of Numa). Of those, the fencing scarf has actually come in the most, due to the bonus dice. As we've only brought down the pain like half a dozen times, the others have maybe been used once or twice each, and even the scarf has only been used in one BDTP. The one conflict where the mayoral stuff did come into play, however, was brutal and let Olivia crush everyone who stood before her.

    Color wise, the scarf is a constant presence in scenes, and constantly being used for stunts and stuff. One of the biggest conflicts in the game was an extended contest to steal the Mayoral Seal from Olivia (she lost, and not long there after lost her seat as mayor because without the weapon she couldn't beat the consortium that had allied against her). So even when they aren't actually changing conflicts with their bonuses, they still have an impact.

    \n Paul,

    It's unclear because TSOY doesn't work like most other games, where successful means "rolled better than my opponent" and success level means "difference between rolls." In TSOY, unless stated otherwise, any roll of 1 or better is successful (in contests, it usually comes down to who is more successful, of course) and success level is just your final roll.

    This is super-intentional: when two orators speak, both can speak eloquently and will be remembered as such, but one is more eloquent and wins the debate. The loser may have spoken Greatly; the winner spoke Amazingly.

    Yeah, I know that's different from every other game in the world, but it's spelled out in the rules. You're right about the harm stuff - having all those weapon and armor examples say "harm" and "protection" is confusing.

    \nOk, several questions:

    - Harm is dealt in BDTP, or any time it's stated as part of a conflict. That is, the Story Guide can set a conflict to be harmful, and harm will result from it. (Note that harm only shakes down after BDTP, so a harmful simple contest can result in serious pain. That's intentional, to balance it against BDTP.)

    - Both of those are right. The base harm is your Success Level, plus anything from secrets or weapons. In a perpendicular action, that harm is modified by the loser's success level.
