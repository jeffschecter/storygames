\n Amazed that this didn't pop up sooner. Interesting article from a retailer:
http://blackdiamondgames.blogspot.com/2010/08/is-indie-dead.html

Now, the title is a little flamatory, but I don't blame them for that. I'd have called it "Indie Won't Pay My Mortgage", but that's just me.

Oh, and if you're going to post here, please read the full article first.

------------------------\n
Don't get me wrong, I'm a believer that indie press titles are RPG incubators from which nearly everything good in the industry emerges, they just don't sell very well. They should go incubate over there. I've got a mortgage to pay. Being a retailer means I'll take calculated risks, not flog myself constantly after a product fails to perform.
\nMy thoughts: He's right. Indie isn't going to pay for the mortgage of a game store owner.
But then, that was never really the point.

    \n It's important to note the two stores locations. Endgame is in downtown Oakland and while a very depressed part of the world, it is actually slightly on the rebound (thanks in part to smart retailers like Endgame) and it is in the heart of a megalopolis, equidistant between the wealthy and educated Berkeley and San Francisco. Oakland itself has a massive hipster scene that is going to have a relatively high amount of gamer hipsters.

It has always been poor, but it has been particularly slammed by the recession and mortgage crisis. There is a lot of speed addiction and welfare families out there.

    I exaggerate a bit too make a point, but the point is solid. They have two very, very different communities to market to.

    \n I find it kind of funny that so much of what helped the indie scene to flourish in the past decade was thumbing its nose at distro and going direct. And now the idea that some indie games might not do well in retail might herald the death of indie? The simple truth is the same in 2010 as it was in 2001: Most indie game designers would be best served by doing direct sales only and handling their own fulfillment.

    Don't get me wrong. As a consumer, it's really nice to have a consolidator that makes many of these games available in one place. And that's true no matter who owns IPR. But many producers don't do enough volume to justify outsourcing fulfillment. And even fewer print in such a way that their per unit price is low enough to make going through distro/retail profitable.

    I think it's great that stores stock indie games, especially if they're a profitable line of business. But indie doesn't need retail or distro to keep chugging along. It just needs people with the vision and desire to produce their own games on their own terms.

I could run them, I could stock them, I could talk about them. But people just didn't want them. We had Over the Edge and its supplements; we were displaying it prominently, talking about it to customers; I was running a fricking campaign every week at my house. Not one sale of that game for six months.

    This is one reason why I'm not ever going to run a game store again, by the way. A game store is about the _store_, not about the games. If the customers want to buy Candyland and Twister, YOU SELL THOSE GAMES. And if they want to buy jewelry and novelty items instead, well, you start stocking those. Because you want to not go bankrupt. You don't buy weird fun little games that you love, because the risk is too high and your margins are soooooo small.

    \n WARNING: when I say "indie" I mean independent publisher -- same folks writing, laying out, printing, selling, marketing. Nothing more. To this I completely agree that some of us are no longer "indie" and that's a good thing, in its way. VSCA is an independent publisher and, through our deal with Evil Hat, also a literary agent of sorts. Evil Hat is most certainly a small but not independent publisher. Categorizing games is an entirely different bit to yell about. You do not need to use my definition, but be aware that I do.

    I've written about this elsewhere, but I've never felt that IPR was especially useful for an independent publisher as it encourages them to use the print-and-warehouse model of production. If you want to be small, you need to get closer to the edge than that, and really make technology do the work. Print-and-warehouse has economies of scale that make it awesome up there where you are not just some guy with a cool idea. Down here, not so much.

    So, and I don't buy most of this guy's arguments anyway, if IPR dies I predict only positive things as we are forced to address the core risks in our production, marketing, and fulfillment streams. My fear is that we will just look for another IPR.

    But I don't think IPR is going anywhere. They'll adapt just like anyone else.

    Edit: thought I'd better add (because it's implied in the rest of my text there) that it seems likely that independence, defined as I do, will tend to imply "small". As any aspect of a business grows, you will need more help, and pretty soon you'll lose your independence. Again, that's a good thing most of the time.

    \nDan: Sure!

    1. I sell a book direct online. I make good money on the sale. If the customer talks about the game or plays it, they expose more people to it. Some of them buy it, others tell their friends about it, and word-of-mouth - my only advertising - grows.

    2. I sell a book to a retailer, who sells it to a customer. I make skimpy money on the sale. If the customer talks about the game or plays it, they expose more people to it. Important: these are more likely to be people who wouldn't be exposed to the game online. Retail sales = word-of-mouth offline, which is very valuable to me. Retailers and cons are pretty much the only ways I can get offline word-of-mouth.

    3. I sell a book to a retailer, who doesn't sell it to a customer. I make skimpy money on the sale. The end.

    "Skimpy money," for Dogs in the Vineyard and Apocalypse World, is just barely enough to print a replacement copy. The book in case 2 is cash-wise break even to me, but profitable in offline word-of-mouth, which I want and crave. The book in case 3 is just break even - it pays for itself, plus just enough to print another copy to replace it in my inventory, and then it doesn't provide any benefit. To the retailer, the book in case 2 is profitable, and they order more, and we grow happy together; the book in case 3 is worse than break even, it's a cost plus a cost, and then the retailer talks bad about my games, and that helps no one.

    Every case 3 book could have been a case 1 book instead.

    \n The best retailers will act as sales agents and advertising placement for your stuff. Often, they need more support that most of us (are willing to?) give them in order to be able to achieve that. (In aggregate, retailers can also be a pretty efficient means of getting your product out to a lot of people all at once.)
    \n
    \nIMO, you either need to look at how to strengthen that partnership, and how to act in a way that treats these people as people you're willing and even eager to share some success with, or you should seriously consider restricting yourself to operating solely as a "case 1" vendor (to use Vincent's cases above).
    \n
    \nAs to Evil Hat's indieness, I feel I need to say that we have rarely made a claim to the indie label without others first applying it to us. I don't know that I, at least, have often felt truly a "part of" any sort of indie scene. For years, Chad Underkoffler and I have joked about our stuff being "too indie for the mainstream, too mainstream for indie", and I think that's continued to be the case. We aren't indie; we aren't trad; we aren't mainstream; we're Evil Hat. Period.
    \n
    \nHonestly, the cyclical nature of this topic just upholds how truly ridiculous the "indie" label is, and how little control the indie community actually has over what the label means any more. The market has taken over ownership of the term, and that means the market decides what indie means anymore, at least in a commercial, "man on the street" sense.
    \n
    \nWhich is not to say that Gary from Black Diamond got much of anything right in his post. He didn't. But he did a fine job of kicking off a new cycle of a pretty familiar conversation. :)

    Well, I returned to this thread after saying what Brad, Thor, and Vincent are saying here: boy, that's too bad. Like you guys, retail sales are useful to me fundamentally as publicity. They're the most honest way to get people excited about the game. I could buy ads, make posters, make T shirts, but all those do is increase visibility \u2014\xa0and they cost me. But among those eight, there are excited players and shop owners who like to get folks excited. Those shops are really worth my time and effort \u2014\xa0not only are they uniformly nice people with interesting tastes, but they attract other nice people with interesting tastes, some of whom want what I make.

    \n I wanted to start this by thanking y'all for the snippets of love we have gotten in this thread. This community has always been very kind to us, and I appreciate it. The relationship we have with many of you, while not something any of us could pay our mortgages off with, sure has a lot of other very non-monetary rewards.

    I am not quite sure what to say about the blog post that has kinda caused a bit of a kerfuffle. I suppose the one thing I never really like is when anyone gets on a soapbox as an "authority." I gotta say, I don't see many authorities in our industry, pretty much anywhere...especially in the retailler community. I should also say, I am not fond of a lotta the "us/vs. them" that pours out in threads like this. (Warning, generalization in that last sentence. Not pointing fingers.) As we have proven, though the money isn't the greatest, we can successfully work with just about any size publisher, with or without an intermediary in the way (be it Alliance, IPR, what have you.) It just takes a little extra time, a pay pal account, and some goodwill.

    So, is Indie dead? For fuck's sake, no. Now, i could spend a ton of time giving you my definition of "indie" cause I think more than anything, none of us sharing a definition for that term causes more arguments than it should. Anywho, I think that blog post made some very interesting points FOR THAT STORE. See, that's the problem. Retailers often get lumped into one huge ball. Issue with that is virtually none of us run our small, private businesses in the same fashion (at all). In our case, we put a lot of time and effort into this community. We _want_ these types of games to succeed, so we have worked hard to make contacts in the community (both players, and publishers). I am finally at a point, after placing my first IPR order in 2006, where publishers look for us. Man, does that help, cause I can't always keep track of what's new, and what's hot.

    Let's look at that a little further. Most good game stores have more individual SKUs than a Costco. Think about all the reaper miniatures just as one example. I stock about 500+ individual codes on that. GW another several hundred. WoTC dozens in the D&D line alone. I am super lucky to be able to get most of these items through a distributor. I dunno how good of a job I could do if I had to seek each and every individual manufacturer out, and order directly from them. I know for a fact that many of my "C List" items that I have to go direct on, I only order at best once a year. Much of that has to do with minimums and freight (cash flow too), but to be really frank: it's cause I only remember to do it once a year. Yup, my bad. I am one guy, responsable for about $200 Grand in ordering for one small shop. I also work the counter every shift. Many don't. Many Do. So, that long build up is so i can say: Man, it's a lot of work to remember to order 6 or 8 titles directly for one publisher. I think to succeed in any category for a boutique hobby related business, you have to choose where you and can where you cannot put your focus. I like small press Role-Playing. Some of the neatest innovations in years have come out of this community. Hell, D&D 4e wouldn't be the same game without the innovations right outta here. So we DO make the effort to pay pal, email, scramble, and scratch to keep as much of this communities' hotness on our shelves. So IN OUR ONE STORE, it sure as hell isn't dead.

    So, to sum up. I think the tone and some serious lack of fact checking in that blog post raised my hackles a bit. "Indie isn't dead." Don't think it will be for a long time. That said, I suppose for a single store, it could be.

    Chris H.
    EndGame

    \n What I get out of a lot of recent coversations is that the various indie-associated publishing models (whether they are currently being used by those with creator ownership or not) are ALL very much still alive and people will continue to practice and even prefer to use a diversity of them. Circa 2005-6 or so, it looked -- at least to me, and I was clearly wrong -- like IPR (including it's move towards selling to retailers) was going to eventually become THE go-to place for small press games, but it is increasingly clear that moment has passed quickly and people continue to do things like sell purely direct or sell only to local retailers they know and etc. It's also clear that small press publishers are at least as diverse as retailers and, consequently, holding them all -- or even the best of them -- under the same roof is nearly impossible. We don't have the same interests or desires or publishing goals. Also, we all seem to be perpetually dissatisfied with available publishing / distribution options and continually looking for better ones.

    So, yeah, I don't expect any of this to stop anytime soon. And, honestly, that's probably a good thing. If people are already fed up with these conversations, I'm not sure what to say, because they seem to come with the territory and be critical to the entire enterprise.
