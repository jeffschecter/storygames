    Posted By: hansel
    Any revelations on the design intention of Freedoms, Joe? What non-mechanical flags are on the original character sheet (it's been a while since I've played and I don't have the book)?
    \nHey Hans, There's a section in the book called Using Freedoms in Play (p.50-51) that unpacks what Freedoms do, why they exist, and how to use them to change the dynamics between the inspectors and the criminals. It's probably one of the best written sections of the book, and I wrote it because of certain playtest groups (yours, and another one led by Paul being unsure what to do with Freedoms. So, for the long version, see the book.

    The short version is this: Freedoms demonstrate the internal logic of Cadence. They take a great swath of the principles and philosophies of Cadence, and condense them into a symbol, a rule and a permission. Each one reveals a great deal about the society that birthed it.

    In addition to this, Freedoms disrupt the balance of authority between criminal and lawmaker. Interestingly, they don't disrupt the balance of power: the inspectors still have all of the power, and they can still circumvent your freedom-granted rights. But the very fact that they have to deal with circumventing your rights does something queer to the role of law: it turns it into a muddy, double-edged sword. Freedoms show you how to use your enemy's tools against them, a vital skill in Perfect.

    So, yeah: Freedoms shine a light on the internal logic of the world, and they provide points where control is contestable. In a small way, they change the dynamics of conflict and law.

    What would you want in Tron? You'd want something that revealed the logic of Tron, that explored how authority gets convoluted, that gave people a tangible representation of "the system."

    \n I guess Thanksgiving precludes this from qualifying as "immediate", Shreyas, but oh well.

    Basically the system is fairly trad. The player seeks to take an action and the gamemaster sets an appropriate difficulty. Let's say, for example that you're trying to steal a lightcycle to pursue an enemy program. I'll set the difficulty at... 100 (that's binary, now, so "4" to you analog folks). Now, this works just like any other thing you would do in an RPG, right? You try to beat or exceed that number. Only, instead of d20+x or percentile dice or whatever, you're using a bitstring (don't worry, it's easier than it sounds!).

    You generate your results by pulling cards out of a deck. Red cards serve as 1s, black as 0s. You get a number of cards equal to the length of your function, in this case, "commandSubroutine", which are all 1s. Let's say you have commandSubroutine: 11111. So you draw five cards. You with me so far? Good.

    In order to beat the difficulty, you need to pull a 1 in a place equal to or greater than the 1 in the difficulty string (100). This means you will win with 1xxxx, 01xxx or 001xx. The best part is, you don't even have to understand binary to do this because it's straight, visual comparison. Is your red card further over than mine? Great! You win!

    Any 1s that you pull of lesser value than the difficulty grant you carry over bits to add to your next (related) draw. You can use Power (which is also your life!) to add bits to any string. Appropriate subroutines (read: equipment) can be added to draws as well. In the example, you would be taking control of the lightcycle and could use it to add to uploadProgram draws for the ensuing chase.
