    Do you need to have General skills? I mean, when I play fish tanks, it's more about what the players do with the clues rather than the characters overcoming obstacles.
    \nThat's an excellent question, and resolves I think towards a matter of taste. Given that the central mechanic of Gumshoe is resource management, and the default mode of play is somewhat pulpy, then yes--I think there's a place to track the ability of the PCs to overcome rather conventional opposition, like the guy coming in the door with a gun.

    My main theory on how to build the diceless version was to start with the assumption that any General spend less than 3 points is suboptimal and use that to put General and Investigative skills on the same scale. That would only be the start; I think there's a need to prune the skill list a bit, and there's some question as to what the granularity of the system would look like once that's done, especially combat. But I think it's got some legs.

    The other thing I'd probably do the next time I run Gumshoe even if I use the conventional rules is dump the idea of Stability tests and just charge a flat rate. ("You were in a gunfight; everyone knock off a point of Stability.") It's my experience that players tend to get annoyed when they're asked to make the roll, and there's a long pause as people try to calculate the optimal spend. (It's an expected payoff calculation and usually straightforward but it can get a bit hairy on Mythos tests.)

    \n@Aviatrix, I'd really like to hear how your game went! I've often considered letting the Investigative Ability uses guide me in improvising the way forward, but I've never actually tried it. If I did, I probably wouldn't bother with the spine and core clues at all; I'd just have some vague idea of the monsters and forces and schemes in play, and follow the players' lead. I'm not sure how to mix the two, and if I did what I just described, I'd encounter the usual dangers of things not adding up or making sense at some point (dangers I can avoid, but not without some strain that I don't often enjoy). So I'm really curious to know what you did and how it turned out.

    Separately, I've been wondering if I've been assuming and encouraging the wrong player attitude for this genre all along. In Lovecraft stories, the protagonists don't make meaningful decisions. They follow the horrors as far as they can until they're ultimately presented with a solution or an escape or a grim demise, and then that's what happens. A GM simply putting on a show for the audience is not a bad model for this, as long as the audience chimes in with reactions from skepticism -> curiosity -> obsession -> horror ->madness. In this respect, tracking Sanity mechanically actually takes the key player decisions -- namely, those of portrayal -- out of the players' hands somewhat.

    I think it's this tension between "watch a performance that's like reading a Lovecraft story" and "play a solid game that has Lovecraft color but is really nothing at all like reading a Lovecraft story" that makes the genre such endless fodder for reinterpretation.

    @Addicted2aa: I'm not sure what you're disagreeing with.

    The authors of GUMSHOE have claimed on their podcast and elsewhere that spending before the roll and not knowing the target number create tension in themselves. I don't think that's true, and it seems you agree.

    I'm not saying that hidden target numbers and pre-roll spending diminish tension - they don't. Just that if you don't have a meaningful choice, for whatever reason, then it's not tense.* As you point out, generally speaking, you do have that information - at least a rough idea. If you literally have no idea, there's no tension.

    There are (at least) two kinds of non-tense decision: arbitrary and no-brainer.
    I was once a player in a military SF game where we were given the momentous decision of whether to take the path that went up the ridge or away into the hills. We pressed the GM for more details. There were none in this barren landscape. We asked if our military training suggested one as better than the other. He said no. We picked the ridge (I think). It was arbitrary. The game was awful.

    The no-brainer appears in a complaint often levelled at the boardgame Talisman. As fun as the game can be because of its flavour, the gameplay is boring because you always have two options and one of them is almost always obviously better than the other. (This is the critique I remember; it's been ages since I played the game so I can't say how fair it is - I'm just using it as an example.)

    So to set up an interesting General Ability roll in GUMSHOE, you need to hit somewhere in the middle ground between those two.
    If you know that you'll never have to roll Athletics again in this adventure, you might as well blow all your points on this roll. Too easy.
    If you genuinely have no idea whether you'll need Athletics again later, nor what will happen if you fail this one roll, then it's arbitrary. Pick a number at random. (This situation is much rarer in GUMSHOE - as you say, you usually have a general feel for where things are going.)
    You need to be between the two: if you suspect another Athletics roll later might be more important, or if you feel this roll is vital (to avoid a falling block of masonry, say), then it's tense.


    "You even know generally what's at stake, your ability to continue the story without obstacle. It seems to me that it is still a way of creating tension."
    This knowledge of the need for the story to go on is one reason I am broadly in favour of post-roll spending for General Abilities in GUMSHOE, at least in pulp-style games. The decision then becomes "do I accept a setback now, or will it be worse to fail later?"
    Philip Marlowe tends to get sapped early on but start winning fights later.

    *This is one reason I don't like dream sequences in Call of Cthulhu scenarios, incidentally.
