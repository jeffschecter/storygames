\n Hey all, Andy K aka "Master Chef" here.

So, there's no firm dates, but in the next few weeks you may see some Game Chef announcements here (and on the Yahoo Group, I hit up that group first with the latest announcements so make sure you're on that list if you want to be in the know).

Anyway, I wanted to organize some very light feedback based on last year's GC2007. It was the first year we used a Vanilla-style forum. It was the first year we required peer feedback and reviews. It was the first year where we split people up into smaller focus groups (rather than a free-for-all). It was the first year for 2 weeks instead of 1 to write.

This year, the only thing that I have set up as a "firm likelihood" is this:
* It /seemed/ that the feedback groups worked, so I want to look into continuing that this year.

Things that are definitely going to implement this year:
* A total step away from focusing on "winning" the event (going back to the roots of the event where the winner is chosen half-on-whim), and instead focusing strongly on peer feedback.

Things that I'm totally undecided on:
* One week for design vs two weeks. Leaning again to two weeks, assuming that out of that time people will find a good run of about 2-4 days to write their game.

I've got thoughts for how I might conduct things this year. But I'd like to hear some casual feedback from folks who have participated in the past. Things like:

1) What did you think of the feedback groups?

2) What did you think about the forum structure? How about the 2 weeks?

3) What did you think about being required to review peer games?

4) Any suggestions for future contests?

Also, a question:

It seems like every year only about 30-40% of the people who "sign up" or weigh in end up completing the assignment. I'm assuming this is a natural thing for these kinds of contests. Anyone have any thoughts about that fact?

Thanks!

Again, this is informal, so while I might weigh in with opinions and stuff, a lot of the contest is still up in the air.

-Andy

    \n My thoughts, based on random snippets of feedback above.

    on the issue of forced anonymity
    I'm in favor of anonymity for those who want it, and a general sense of anonymity for the contest, sure I can roll with that. Enforcing forced anonymity for everyone, coming up with their names for them, is going to be an administrative chore that I don't have time for this year, honestly. It won't happen.
    However, I will HIGHLY ENCOURAGE folks to take on a pseudonym of their own choosing. Perhaps pointing them to random generators to choose their name.

    Also, cute anecdote: A few years ago, right after MLWM came out and was on everyone's mind: I'm judging my assigned entries for Game Chef, when I come along this entry that's really reassuringly written, and refers to things like colored dice, endgames, etc. I thought, "LOL, this poser is trying to be Paul Czege". With that in mind, I proceeded to give it lower scores. I really reamed it, of course for objective reasons, but it was all built on a subjective foundation of judging this guy harshly for trying to recreate the forumlaic writing of Paul Czege from My Life with Master.

    on the issue of one week vs two
    Still totally torn on the issue. Looking back on last year, it seems that even though there were 2 weeks/3 weekends, only maybe 10 people actually were working those whole two weeks. It seems that everyone else did their work over one single weekend or over 3-5 days at some point, and spent the rest of the time giving feedback to others. That's just staring at the forums with a lazy eye and recalling conversations from last year. So with that, 2 weeks/3 weekends gives the Gamers With Jobs enough time to participate when they can, given random busy work loads. Yet it's also unfair, as the students/unemployed/bored can work on it for those 2 weeks, straight.

    In the end, I chose 2 weeks last year because I was hoping for people to have enough time to give each other feedback during the development process as well, rather than just at the end (the review process). With one week, people will presumably be too busy with their own project to give any development feedback. However, that might be OK: As mentioned above, not all the development feedback was good (while the review feedback seemed far more useful), so it might be best to have people sort of develop on their own in a tight crunch environment, then afterwards take the time to review/look at other entries in their group.

    on the issue of doing stuff to make sure more people participate/finish
    Yeah, I'm getting the feeling drop-outs are inevitable. No skimping on the ingredients/challenge, and no coddling. It'll continue to be rigorous(ish).

    on the issue of Bryan's item suggestions:\n
    I'd like to see these items part of the contest:

    * Easy to learn the basics or it doesn't take twenty minutes trying to explain how to play. \u201cAppealing and quickly playable.\u201d \u201cFew minutes to learn, lifetime to master.\u201d Not \u201crules-lite\u201d or \u201cdumbed-down\u201d

    \n I'm:

    Pro-feedback group. I think it worked out okay. I'd really like to have a "widows and orphans" channel, where if you're not getting enough feedback, you can post it there for general observers to comment on.

    Anti-enforced anonymity, but pro pseudonyms in general (don't like how you're treated as a "name?" Don't like how you act? Want to make a statement? Be anonymous.) Part of the deal with this contest is it is a way to create social bonds. Enforcing anonymity is handicapping social newcomers in order to make old-timers feel more comfortable about their own internet fame. I'm strongly against anything that handicaps new people to the benefit of old hands. Frankly, I'd much rather alienate old hands.

    Pro- peer feedback. If you can't take the time to read five other people's games and comment, seriously you need to fuck off.

    Anti- peer judgement, but pro- having a winner. Peer judgement is full of bitter, bitter poison. But winning is fun. We just need to get back to the spirit of the original IGC, where winning isn't as big a prize as finishing. I think that arbitrary judgement will help this.

    Anti- word count limit. Rather strongly. It takes a lot longer to write words than to read them. I'd be happy with "be aware that if your game is over 10,000 words it might be skimmed." I think that the book-length games last year were done partly because of the two week limit.

    Anti- any sort of AP requirement: dittoing Justin. Providing an IRC channel does nothing in this regard: IRC play is so alien from face to face play as to not even be worth considering for playtesting. The only way I could see this working would be under a different contest, which would be like "Day 1: design a game in 24 hours. Day 2: Playtest game. Day 3: rewrite the game in 24 hours. Day 4 Playtest again. Day 5 revise again. Day 6 layout." Which would be fun, like a game triatholon, but it's a much more limited sort of thing.

    (And I actually think that last year's contest generated a lot of play: I know that I've played a lot of 1000 kings, there's been some play of Heart of the Rose, Know Thyself, Penny for My Thoughts, etc.)

      \n I know I'm a bit late to the table here, but that won't stop me from being opinionated.

      (Also, I haven't posted here in ages. Hi everyone!)

      Feedback Groups
      I loved having a group. I felt a strong desire to give feedback within my group, and it definitely kept me motivated through my midpoint slump.

      Last year, latecomers (most of whom tended to be first-timers) got shunted into just a couple of groups. I'd strongly suggest keeping groups numerous enough that latecomers can be added in a fairly distributed fashion. I'd rather have groups that were a little bigger (or a little smaller, if there are fewer newcomers than anticipated) than see the late-arriving folks get shoehorned together.

      Forum Structure
      I liked being able to control my view of which groups I was looking at, at any given time. Each day of the contest, I'd turn on a different group and read/comment on as many games as I could. The ability to narrow it by group made this seem feasible instead of overwhelming. I also really liked the ability to bookmark threads where I was doing ongoing design feedback (most relevant outside my own group, of course).

      Contest Length
      I'm strongly in favor of a ten-day-ish contest - two weekends and a week in between. Two weeks is more for development than prototype, as other people have said, and changes the expectations for the product. I also found that I tended to procrastinate more with the two-week time limit, though that may just be me.

      Peer Review
      Awesome, but decouple it from judging. You should have to do peer reviews to stay in the running, but that's it. If the contest is ten days, maybe give ten days to write your reviews after that - so you're keeping things at a fair boil.

      I wish there were a way to give feedback about people's ability to review. I saw a lot of shitty, thoughtless, and clueless peer reviews. Doing game feedback and critique is a skill, too, and it's one that I think it's important for would-be designers to master. I don't know how to implement this other than social pressure, of course, but it's a dream!

      Anonymity
      Please don't make people be anonymous! I'm one of those people who was strongly benefited by non-anonymity in my first Game Chef - no one really knew who I was before then. I can testify personally that the benefits of non-anonymity in Game Chef are real, tangible, and important.

      I feel strongly about this, to the point that I probably would not participate if I had to be anonymous. I am myself when I design. I don't really want to pretend to be anyone else.

      Play-testing
      This is just not feasible. It also doesn't really help us know who's a good designer because it depends on logistical, not design, ability.

      Length Limits
      I don't care about this one way or the other. I'll read games of any length, and I think the super-long games will be self-limiting in how much feedback they get during the GC process.

      Ingredients
      Please give us mechanical restrictions! I found them immensely useful as constraints!

      Beyond that, I don't care about themes, or how many 'flavor' ingredients there are, or whatever. It's clear that there will be some words to take inspiration from, and that's good enough for me.

      Awards
      I don't mind the process of awards being given by individuals, but I would prefer to see the awards given by committee. Having a single individual selecting the winner(s) only tells us about that person's preferences. I think a small group would do better at recognizing games that are interesting, innovative and successful as opposed to just personally pleasing. Plus Amabile found that groups can actually come to pretty good agreement on what's creative, so I don't think we'd be losing all that much on the innovation-recognition front.

      I do like the idea of individuals having special individual awards, like "Most Profane" or whatever else sounds like fun. I agree with whoever mentioned above that it's also a good way to gently guide people's design ideas without introducing more constraints.

      I think that's about it. I'll opine more if I think of something.

      --Jess

      \n Opinions are like assholes: the more well-researched ones have a special section in the newspaper.

      \nNonetheless, here's some of mine:
      \n
      \n* The time period should be precisely as follows:
      \n
      \n from Friday (ideally not so late on Friday that Europeans will have to stay up unreasonably late to see the ingredients announced).
      \n the weekend
      \n the entire week following
      \n the weekend after that
      \n ending on Monday (ideally not so early on Monday that it will be effectively Sunday for Europeans).
      \n
      \nThis gives two weekends, a full week and a grand total of about ten days, which strikes me as about the ideal compromise between the roominess of two weeks and the laser focus of one.
      \n
      \n* Feedback groups were a good concept. There's a tricky balance to strike between too big (such that it doesn't really solve the problem of not being able to get feedback) and too small (such that, if people drop out in your group or don't participate much, you could end up stranded). I wonder if it might not be possible to start out with small groups and merge them as people drop out somehow? It might work best to do that on a group by group basis: like, say you get to Tuesday and you realise you're the only one left in your group, post to an "orphaned chefs" thread and another group can adopt you?
      \n
      \n* Precisely 23.56% of participants should be pseudonymous. That is, it should not be mandated (some people use the contest as a way to get some name recognition, and there's nothing wrong with that in my view) and nor should it be "the norm", but I am happy for it to be "encouraged" as part of the fun.
      \n
      \n* A peer review requirement strikes me as a good thing. Some people will be lazy about it and that's sad perhaps but unavoidable: meanwhile, a mandatory feedback requirement helps spread the attention that games get around a bit, and helps concentrate the mind - it's easy to get lazy about feedback, and the requirement serves as a neat reminder.
      \n
      \n* The forum software was fine, but I would like a re-skin, if only to stop me getting confused between there, here and KF all the time.
      \n
      \n* Rather than the Past Winners Awards thing, how about we just encourage a culture of additional challenges? Anyone can make a post which is like "hey, I bet you can't make a game with the ingredients which also has THIS" and if people latch onto it, cool, and if not, that's just how it is.
      \n
      \n* In general, winning is much less interesting to me than just the natural process of buzz about one thing or another, plus the highlighting of particularly neat bits and pieces that should come out of the feedback process. Hence, I am bored by numerical scores, but totally interested in judging processes which are like "hey, THIS bit is totally cool! Check this out, people!" There should be a "winner", if only for the sake of Tradition, but not because they scored the most votes or got the best score out of ten. Embrace subjectivity! If you're wrong, the people will tell you :)
