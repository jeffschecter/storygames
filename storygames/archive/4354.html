    Anyway some more info about Mob Justice and how it works. I'll put this here but it is basically a summary of the free downloadable that Graham pointed you towards.

    The game world is modern day America where prohibition never ended and society has become rotten to the core. From the politicians in the white house to the soccer mums and deadbeat dads, everyone is linked to the mobs in one way or another. The mafia stil rule on high as lords of the underworld but everywhere are gangs looking to take a piece of the action: Mexican street gangs, Triads, Yakuza and many more are moving in on the Italians. Slowly they are losing ground as they are pushed further back into their bastion of Chicago and it looks like not even their friends in Washington are willing to help.

    So there you have a brief idea of the world. I will give you a quick idea of what the game deals with roleplaying wise. The games main strength lies in the relationships that the characters form with the world around them. These relationships can be with other characters, NPCs or even particular moral codes and beliefs.

    This is by using the following parts of the character:

    Loyalties: People or things the character is loyal to i.e. the boss of his family, his father etc.

    Codes: Things that define how the character acts in society i.e. I never hit a woman

    Tells: thinsgs that make him sweat under pressure, mannerisms that make him stand out in a crowd i.e. my family, my brother etc.

    Note that under this scheme a given part of the character could be both a loyalty and a tell giving it more weight to the character.

    Their are two stats that are important to the game as well

    Reputation: the publics perception of you

    Stature: The standing you have within a given organisation

    The game uses a poker based mechanic to resolve conflicts so these two stats are rated from ace (low) to king (high). These stats show you how powerful and influential you are in the world and allow you to use story chips, the narrative currency in the game, in different ways. They also provide you with resources and networks you can turn into a criminal empire.

    However they are also subject to rapid change. If a character breaks a code or betrays a loyalty, they take \xe2\u20ac\u02dcdamage\xe2\u20ac\u2122 to their reputation or stature. If this hit is not resolved by the end of the session it can be come a permanent reduction, reducing their power, forcing them to scale back their network and providing opportunities for enemies to attack them.

    The game also encourages those with high reputation and stature to do more in the game, encouraging them to maintain theses social stats through action and involvement in the story if they want to maintain the power they crave for their characters.

    This is what Mob Justice is about, balancing the craving for power and respect with the frailties that all human beings have and ultimately asks the question: How far will you go to get what you want?

    Hope that has been helpful, any other questions I will be happy to answer them but I do encourage you to download the free preview as that has more info. than I can fit in a post!

    Cheers

    Iain

    Posted By: Caesar_XPosted By: timfireThe Mountain Witch, if played in a gangster noir setting.
    \nI like this, Timothy. Tell me more. Would you structure the Witch as some sort of crime boss? Or MacGuffin? I'm seeing the trees but perhaps not the forest yet.
    First off, let me say I haven't had a chance to play the game outside of the samurai setting. A couple of people have played the game outside of the Japanese setting, but as far as know, it's always still been about an assassination. I think switching the Witch out for some kind of McGuffin would work, hypothetically, but it's something I haven't tried yet.

    Anyway, the easiest way to handle it would be make the ronin gangster-type hitmen, hired to kill some mob boss. You could then switch out Mt Fuji for some private estate... maybe an island, that would work well, I think. An option that might also be interesting, I think, would be to set the game in some mob-controlled town in the middle of nowhere (like maybe an early Las Vegas?). That might have a bit more of an "open" feel compared with the straight climb up the mountain.

    You could set up the game such that the PCs must track down the mob boss, traveling from town to town as they pick up clues. But there's a danger, I think, of the PCs getting sidetracked and wandering around alot. If you were to do this, I would just make it clear before the game that the focus should be on finding the Mob Boss. The Massachusetts crowd (Vincent, Meg, Joshua, Julia, Emily) played a game where they were circus freaks traveling around Eastern Europe. Maybe one of them could chime in and comment on how that worked out...

    Another option here, maybe, would be to make the PCs PI's or something, but you might lose the "honor among thieves" angle. I would have to think about how to switch the Witch for a McGuffin. An assassination makes for a very clear end point, and sets up clear "sides".

    Off-hand, I think making the game about a group of hitmen hired to kill a mob boss in a early Las Vegas -esque town is cool.

    Oh! I almost forgot---I would keep the Zodiacs and and Fate cards the same. *Personally* I would keep the idea of "fate" or "destiny" or "uncanny coincidence" alive in the game. The game doesn't need supernatural elements, but I (personally) think the idea of "fate" is important.

    \n Sorry to feed into the literary tangent,but--
    \nI do strongly advise reading the original classics -- the difference between classic and neo-noir isn't as big as the difference between Robert Howard Conan and Robert Jordan's Conan, but there's a real difference. I'm also going to go out on a limb and suggest you read every damn Dashiell Hammett novel, with the possible exception of The Thin Man. At least read The Glass Key and Red Harvest, they've both been liberally drawn on in Westerns, Samurai flicks and Noir cinema ever since they were published.
    \n
    \nAs for newer authors, I like Charlie Huston's neo-noir novels. I got into him because of his "vampire noir" books, but the mainstream crime fiction is pretty good, too. And Michael Connelly's novel Void Moon is a great noir heist novel.
