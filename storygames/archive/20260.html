    \n Here are a few that come to mind:

    1. Commercial Success (or the viability of a focused game design)

    Highly CA-focused game designs are not as commercially successful as "incoherent" games (a shorthand for game design which doesn't clearly support one CA mode over others).

    For instance, some people blame D&D 4th Edition as being a failure because it was so focused on a particular design goal that it drove away players interested in other aspects of the game.

    This argument suggests that flexible, "incoherent" game systems, which don't state a clear creative focus but, instead, throw in all kinds of different techniques and potential play modes are more successful with gamers, and, furthermore, that this isn't a fluke but true for very good reasons: more "incoherent" gaming is a better way for everyone involved to enjoy themselves.

    I suppose there are two versions of this argument:

    1.a) The weak version: "Coherent" gaming is nice in principle, but rarely actually happens. Real gaming groups are better off with a game* which gives you the flexibility to adapt to each player's needs and include a bit of everything for everyone.

    1.b) The strong version: "Coherent" gaming is a myth, period. All gaming is "incoherent" by Big Model standards, and people have lots of fun playing this way. "Incoherent" gaming IS roleplaying, in other words.

    Additionally, the GM and players have fundamentally different roles, and perhaps it's not even *possible* to identify any kind of unified approach to playing which would apply to a whole group.



    * Note: By necessity (because System is defined as, essentially, "the way we play this game right now"), Forge theory conflates the text of the game with the techniques being used at this moment by this group, so it's impossible to speak of a set of game rules entirely separately from a particular gaming group's interpretation of that game text. So, when I use the term "game", above, I'm referring to the time and place where an interpretation of a game text meets a gaming group in real life and what ensues.

    \n 2. Creative Agenda Modes do not Survive Contact with the Enemy

    Essentially, Creative Agenda sounds all great and fine, but when it comes to an actual game in progress, no two people can agree on which CA mode is in effect.

    This suggests that CA modes are not a real thing, or, perhaps, are expressed too vaguely to be of any real use.

    I've seen a number of people who are very knowledgeable about the Big Model and supporters of it struggle with this issue. I'm playing a game/I'm designing a game/I'm trying to fix my game, and it's lots of fun, but I can't safely categorize it as belonging to one of the three CA modes.

    It seems like it might be this one, or maybe that one, but it's clearly not just one of these, not as far as I can see, or my judgement varies depending on which way I'm looking at it.

    \n Are Creative Agenda modes actually internally cohesive? The theory promises that they are, and there are difficulties with defining modes without this property (rather, it's questionable if CA modes matter at all unless they actually signify Creative Agendas that work well together), but the only overt reasoning in support is basically that surely two people with similar interests will be capable of understanding and appreciating each other's play, given a subject matter and technical tools they both like. Surely a Gamist will recognize that same spirit in another Gamist and respond to it, bringing coherence to the human encounter?

    Even more nefariously, empiric investigation of this matter is especially unreliable because of the tendency towards explaining intra-modal disagreement and incomprehensibility as a "non-creative", "merely technical" or somehow incidental disagreement. It is also common to come to convenient analytical conclusions where creative disagreement just happens to be explained by the presence of two different CA modes instead of just one. All this could be because intra-modal creative incoherence is genuinely rare (the orthodox position), or it could be because GNS theorists are incapable of appreciating that all Creative Agendas are unique, and that there are no real guarantees to cohesion except human empathy and willingness to work together.

    The counter-hypothesis, as indicated above, is that GNS modes do not exist, or they exist at most as guaranteed conflicts (caused by reward cycle incompatibilities, to be specific), and in fact a coherent creative agenda is caused by complex nuances and coincidences in creative inspiration. For example, two players of Primetime Adventures might cohere not primarily because they both desire and appreciate a narrativist expression, but because of a dozen different things ranging from personal chemistry to fondness for tv drama.

    Any incidental success of GNS theory in achieving cohesion could be explained under this hypothesis, too: people are finding more cohesive play not because CA modes themselves are real, but rather simply because GNS analysis encourages them to talk about creative agenda, design for it and select their co-players with a cohesive agenda in mind. All these actions may still be fully viable even if the notion of playing for "Gamism" or "Narrativism" is actually an illusionary chimera that means whatever is most convenient in each individual gaming context. These people could as well be trying to play towards a "Polpotian" agenda mode and it would work the same as long as they managed to communicate a compelling creative vision to their co-players and chose a rules system matching that vision.

    I would characterize this hypothesis as a minor assault upon the Forgite theoretical framework: although it attacks one of the oldest and most established "truths", the Big Model itself is not invalidated even if GNS theory unravels. As far as the Big Model is concerned, play either has a cohesive Creative Agenda or it doesn't, but the model doesn't tell you how to bring that about. GNS does, but is it correct in claiming that agenda mode coordination brings about coherence?

    \n In my experience cohesive play and fun are neither internally-consistent nor previously-arranged. They are dynamic, human, diverse, often unplanned, and just as often uncategorizable. Fun flexible people make things fun and flexible. That's why I consider the GNS model useful as a critical tool but useless as a method of describing anything people actually do. Sessions bounce around inside the model, systems include modes within other modes, GMs run breakout scenes and subgames with different modes, players have different moods which cause them to play in different modes on a given night, and even the model itself is fractal. IMNSHO, of course. :-)

    I'm not against the GNS model. I like it, as models go. It's useful for designers to think about. But I am against assuming that it's empirically meaningful as a description of real players, that its modes are categorically exclusive of each other, or that its modes are normative (negatively definitional or limiting) upon any class of player activity.

    I think it's both, Paul.

    Certainly there are designs that are built to actively support one mode over the others, maybe even that penalize extra-modal play. So I'm not talking about those. I'm really talking about RPG campaigns in general, and about sessions played within the context of such campaigns. I'm saying that in my experience, the modes coexist at the same table, within the same game system, and even within a single player from one hour to the next, and that that diversity is not necessarily the cause of anyone's bad experience. And although this is a side-point, I also think that for the most highly interactive - meaning least predictable - experiences, agenda can really only be pre-discussed in a negative (i.e. editorial, restrictive) way (see conversations about the X card, lines/veils, etc).

    I think the GNS model is useful in the same way that Zodiacal signs or Myers-Briggs Types are useful. I.e., not at all for communicating a truly representative portrait of a real live individual, but generally practical as a broadly-defined heuristic for talking about design intentions, or comparing in-game activity from one ruleset to another, etc.

    \n To answer a few of Paul's ponderings about what the GNS theory does or does not say:

    1) GNS theory does, indeed, imply an exclusion of Creative Agendas: you cannot be cohesive while serving several agendas at once because cohesion itself is the emergent outcome of having a single agenda. Various types of backpedaling and attenuating on this point are common, perhaps because people cannot quit believe Forge theory to be that stupid, but that's how it goes: if you think that a game can be coherently both Gam and Sim at the same time, you're being heterodox about the theory, and Jason would be entirely within his rights to call you on that

    (To clarify, I'm not saying that that it is evil or undesirable to be "heterodox", I just want to be clear on what the theory actually says. It's not GNS theory anymore if you're talking about some type of "play style" that can be freely mixed together out of a continuum of possibilities. "A little dash of Gamism to give an edge to my Narrativism" is a phrase that is utterly in conflict with what the GNS theory is attempting to communicate.)

    Most similar "put gamers into boxes" theories do not feature strict typing like this, presumably because they are technical or historical categorizations, and the phenomena they attempt to categorize really are free recombinations of core concepts that form continuous spaces. GNS is, or at least believes itself to be, different in that it would be a logical fallacy to characterize play with multiple creative agendas at once.

    2) "Incoherent play leads to unhappiness" is not a prescription of the GNS theory, but it is an important background assumption for most people using the theory - it's why we're interested in Creative Agenda, because we think that its coherence is a key explaining factor for enjoyable play. Jason is in no way reaching when he chooses to critique that assumption, but it is at most a hit on the relevance of GNS, not on its correctness - GNS could still be right about the way Creative Agenda reaches coherence or incoherence, even if it happens to be the case that incoherent play is actually more fun and enjoyable than coherent play.

    However, I will 100% underwrite that Forgite theorists generally believe that incoherent play is not fun, and that if somebody thinks that it is, it is most likely because they are misunderstanding the concept and think that their highly coherent game is incoherent e.g. because they're playing a game with an incoherent text. (For example, I'm playing Paranoia with my friends this summer, and it's a highly incoherent game text with strong Gam and Nar leanings. The actual execution of play is purely Gamist, and highly coherent.) Incoherent play is something where you get all excited about this excellent move you made, and then raise your eyes to see the blank stares of your fellow players who didn't understand, didn't care and didn't like what you just did. It is difficult to imagine that as a basis for enjoyable roleplaying.

    1) GNS theory does, indeed, imply an exclusion of Creative Agendas: you cannot be cohesive while serving several agendas at once because cohesion itself is the emergent outcome of having a single agenda.     \nI agree, but only for crazy semantic and pedantic reasons. Basically, I disagree with the gist of this.

    Definition: Creative Agenda is a group's primary priority of what's cool, reinforced and validated by the group over a given instance of actual play, right?

    How many primary priorities can a group have? Only one. If you have two, you can try to say that they're both equal and thus in first place, but you can't say that one serves the other in a /primary/ sense. That's why GNS theory /technically/ says "you got your peanut butter in my chocolate" or whatever is not fair cricket.

    However, The Big Model happily accommodates this idea. There are Techniques -- things you do at the table -- that can support one CA more than another in that given game session. You could even say -- for that one game session -- that those Techniques serve one CA over another ("The Riddle of Steel's trait system pushed a Story Now CA, while its combat system was totally pushing Right to Dream"). You can have a big soup of different Techniques, all serving one /or more/ CAs at the same time in the same play session.

    At the end of the game, though, you either pushed /primarily/ toward one (by definition, group-shared) priority, or you did not. Maybe you consciously pushed toward seven group-shared priorities. That's totally possible! I suspect Jason's group plays this way (consciously or not). The Big Model calls this incoherent play because it isn't "firing on all cylinders," to use a metaphor that got tossed around back in the day.

    If the Creative Agenda of this style of D&D were Simulationist, we would see e.g. the GM provide routine support for the players so they could play their characters better in the societal roles they represent - so playing a knight would be about acting knightly, with GM help if necessary, instead of playing a knight being a skill challenge that you can take on if you think that you've got what it takes.     \n The RPG Museum wiki page on "GNS Theory" has a surprisingly concise overview of criticisms leveled at the theory:\n
    Introduction of GNS has met with marked opposition from elements of the gaming community. Some feel that RPG's are inherently frivolous and reject out of hand that they can be subjected to artistic criticism at all, and disparage any attempt to do so as pseudo-intellectualism. Direct critics of the theory have argued that it doesn't really explain anything regarding player behavior and only serves to shoehorn game design down limited paths. Many casual players react negatively to the idea, complaining that it forces them into false dilemmas and draws artificial, wordy distinctions between elements that can, in practice, combine well with one another and mutually reinforce.
    \nThese seem like both good and recognizable critiques. I'll number them:

    3. Roleplaying games are not Art.

    This view holds the roleplaying is not a meaningful artistic endeavour, and, therefore, trying to ascribe Creative Agenda to it is misguided at best and deliberately misleading, pretentious posturing at worst.

    4. The Theory is not Applicable to Actual Play... so it shackles Design

    This view holds that the theory does not allow us to say anything meaningful about player behaviour, and, therefore, affects only game designers (with the implication that it limits their horizons for design).

    (This last point clearly can't be applied to the Big Model in general, but could be leveled as a criticism at the idea of three separate Creative Agendas. Trying to "focus" it on a specific creative goal takes the fun out of it: rather, what is FUN about roleplaying games is all the little ways the thing is silly. You're adults very seriously reacting to a story about a kidnapped princess played by a 40-something balding used car salesman. That's hilarious! Enjoy it.

    I'd say that the latter two points don't actually challenge the theory itself in any way; rather, the question the entire purpose of its existence as being misguided.

      \n The "external purpose" of Exploration (that is, "imagining shit up") in Narrativism is the artistic satisfaction of expressing a Premise in a powerful manner. Insofar as Simulationism has something similar in the way of purpose, it's "celebrating the source material" as was suggested earlier - although I would rather call it "exploring" or "experiencing" or "handling" the source material: basically the Simulationistic reason to get together and imagine some shit up is because we're curious about this particular shit, while this is not the primary determinant for Narrativism. This is what I meant by Narrativism having an external purpose in contrast to Simulationism: it suffices for Simulationistic purposes that an airship is cool and let's talk some more about how cool our airship is; for Narrativism this sort of thing is at most pacing and build-up for what the airship will be used for, story-wise.

      That being said, I'm certain that John has heard all this before in so many ways, not likely to convince him if I say it once more

That could take the form of new Creative Agenda modes or a convincing explanation for how many creative agendas are unmoded, and have been falsely assigned to what proves to be an imaginary mode.       \n I was away from this thread for a little while, and I'm very pleasantly surprised by the directions it's taking.

      My thoughts on the matter, briefly:

      1. I'm always a bit surprised to hear people saying that the forge was an anti-Sim place or ideology. I can certainly see why someone would get that impression, but it seems to me to be an accident of history.

      First of all, Sim play dominates rpg discourse (or, at least, certainly did until recent times), often being the unspoken assumed mode of role paying gaming, period. Therefore it made sense for a bunch of designers looking to break new ground to concentrate on other modes of play. In the Big model, that's Step On Up and Story Now.

      Second, the discourse at the forge went through certain periods. I'm not claiming to be an expert on any of this (I spent far less time there than Eero or John or Dave), but as far as I can see:

      * At first, the focus was on Story Now, as a new and exciting mode of play which people didn't have good tools for. Lots of innovation and excitement! It helps that Ron is particularly keen on this kind of play, and that his own game, Sorcerer, was a shot at this style of play.

      (There were lots of counterexamples, however. For instance, Vincent's first game which got him noticed at the Forge was "kill puppies for satan", which is generally agreed to be a Sim-supporting game - at least by Vincent, if not everyone else.)

      * Next, Step On Up experienced a lot of popularity, since it wasn't as unsupported as Story Now in terms of games on the market, but still a bit of a novelty.

      During this time people got really excited about rediscovering old-school gaming, which is surely connected to the whole OSR movement.

      * After that, Ron and various others started getting more excited about Sim. I remember Ron posting a lot about Dead of Night, for example, which he loved, and various other Sim-supporting games. He went through a whole phase of "colour-first" discussions, which are arguably Sim-related, even if that's not the whole picture.

      Perhaps I'm rewriting history a bit, but in my opinion it's not a total coincidence that after that Ron decided to close the theory forum and "move forward" with other interests.

      John: Those are good questions, I'll try to explain how it feels to me. I don't think that the distinction is in how detached and analytical the enjoyment of play is; instead, I'll try to show how the "external purpose" in Narrativism is, I think, about whether the players find meaning in the subject matter, or in their own unique artistic input.

      Of your examples, number 1) is probably Narrativist, 2) could be, while 3-5 are likely Simulationist, just as you say. They could also all be Sim, all things being equal; I don't think that "simulating Egri storytelling" wouldn't be a possible Simulationistic ideal. Maybe it's not the first thing that comes to mind for somebody playing Sorcerer, but who knows what springs to the minds of men in this crazy world of rpg theory.

      If we imagined that I had a more detailed view of these different play situations, including the oft-considered opportunity for reward cycle analysis (that is, I could see how, exactly, the group in question "sets em up and knocks em down"), then I would expect to be able to say with some certainty as to whether it's Sim or Nar that was going on. The difference I would be looking for would basically concern the way personal creative concerns would be put at stake by the participants: that external Narrativist concern you're asking for is basically the personal expression of the player, giving weight to the play, while in Sim contrariwise such putting yourself on the line is considered besides the point at best. Sim is more cooperative in the sense that the players are trying to pursue an unified Dream, while in Nar the individual, independent voices rise and ebb in significance.

      For example, in case 4 I would expect that the satisfaction of simulating medieval England would involve subsuming of personal values and opinions to the pretense of objectivity - biased though such simulation necessarily is in small or great ways, the players do not elevate that bias as artistic expression; rather, they ignore it, hide it, or debate it to eliminate it, to achieve that satisfaction of delving into what to them feels like the subject matter itself, unsmirched by non-canonical elements. "Constructive denial", as Forgite Sim theory calls it.

      I'm not saying that simulationist play doesn't involve personal creative satisfaction and pride, of course, it's just that in Nar the joy of play is in the meaningfulness of the expression itself, while in Sim the joy is in the quality of the material. Your case 5, for instance, could with little imagination be seen as a description of either Sim or Nar play - not because the two are very similar in whole, but because it is easy to describe either as "satisfying story creation". The Nar act of creation would be satisfying because the story feels right and novel and amazing to the participants, who are proud of themselves, while the Sim act would be satisfying because it truly captures what is the proper strength of the genre in question, leaving the participants with a surfeit of genreness in their heads, just as if they'd just finished a great genre novel.

      (Having played both story-crafting Sim and story-crafting Nar, I'd say that usually the former is much more concerned with the execution-aspects of the storytelling itself; there's mood, GM storytelling skillz and such. When doing the same thing for Nar purposes we often don't have those concerns because all the attention is in shaping up the novel events, rather than the way they are described.)

      I can totally see how the psychology of this is questionable (I mean literally uncertain, ripe for questioning - not "wrong" right out of the gate); one could see the above as quibbling about minor details of motivation where all the big parts are essentially identical. The GNS viewpoint on the practicals of it, though, is that the reason for why Nar and Sim are categorized in different boxes is not necessarily in that they "look different", but because a creative conflict exists in attempting to pursue both goals simultaneously and without distinction. So it is possible for the creative psychology to look pretty similar for both, but still be different in ways that cause practical incoherence in execution.

      An example of such incoherence from my experience, one that recurs often while playing types of dramatic games that would naturally sway towards either Nar or Sim, is the question of artistic responsibility. I mention this because it latches onto this phrasing of "external purpose" as well. It is not unknown for players in a certain sort of dramatic roleplaying game to feel pressure and critique about their creative choices, when those choices are surprising and off-beat in comparison to what the other players expected. This is not uncommon in games that are set in stylized genres, such as say superhero games, because the play group is often not uniformly literate on the genre. The Nar and Sim attitudes towards this situation, and indeed the entire significance of the situation for them, are very different: in the Nar game the genre exists to become a platform for personal expression, and therefore the only questions in criticizing another player's contribution are essentially procedural (does the player have the right to make this move now) and cooperative (does the player disrespect other people's themes, does he understand the purpose of the game, etc.). In the Sim game, in contrast, the genre exists as a reason in itself, and thus the players may well come to debate whether an individual making surprising, off-beat moves is playing "wrong" and ruining the premise of play.

      I've encountered the specific agenda distinction I describe above in superhero games, horror games and I think probably in something else as well - dungeon fantasy games, naturally enough. It would be misleading to imagine these situations as grand clashes of informed creative agenda, as in truth oddball players who make oddball moves in games are rather common, and the oddball player themselves is unlikely to be acting due to creative politics - they're just the subject of group discussion on how to handle off-beat creative inputs. Such moves mainly becomes the focus of a big debate when the nature of the game straddles that Sim/Nar line and the group becomes uncertain as to how to valuate the weirdness. (It's not always the Narrativist game that is the most egalitarian, or even most functionally able to handle weird players, I should note - a Sim game with world-building tech is often quite robust about incorporating surprising inputs.)

      \n Early in the thread someone asked about Big Model criticisms that weren't GNS-focused, so I'll toss out a few: With the stances, calling it Actor Stance has led at least some people (e.g. the hosts of The JankCast) to investigate the techniques of stage acting as a way of gaining insight. I think that's somewhat misleading to what the stance is meant to be about, since the techniques of emoting in a scripted scene or dynamically responding to an audience in improv are somewhat externally directed while Actor Stance should probably be more agentic and focused on internal motivation and perceiving the situation around them.

      I also have a draft of an essay where I critique the convoluted terminology and concepts around Positioning and Currency. The short version is that by using the slightly odd term "positioning" it can lead to thinking in RPGs-are-special-snowflakes terms when it's actually the same concept as position in other games (e.g. "the player's position on the soccer field is (x,y)" and "the player's position on the team is 'goalkeeper'" are analogous to "the character's position in the room is behind the ogre's back" and "the character's position in the kingdom is 'heir to the throne'"). This lets you recast Resources as a special case of Position (they're just very concrete, spendable positions) and makes Effectiveness into a function that takes Position as input. And then there's not really much need for a jargon term Currency (which is easily mistaken for "spendable tokens") since it's just System-in-action.

      In terms of CA, I'm not sure if I'm correct in my understanding of the Big Model, but it seems to me that there's a lot of cruft in the definitions, and some of the terms have misleading connotations. I think the idea of "reward cycles" and "instance of play" are baggage from the Behaviorist stuff about what an external observer can say, when I suspect that what it's really about is what makes things subjectively fun on a moment-to-moment basis (longer term things matter, but mostly in how they are orienting your and shaping your subjective experience moment-to-moment). I also think that the historical context of how the theory was developed lead people to assume that it's about "preference matching" and "getting what you want", but I'm skeptical. I think it's more about System configuration. People might have preferences between sailboats, paddle-boats, and propeller-driven boats but the more important differences between these different types of machines is how they work differently, not that some people prefer some over others. I've said elsewhere:\n
      This might just be me, but the "top priority" framing doesn't seem to capture the notion of incompatibility expressed in Ron's parable of the pigs. The mental model I'm working with is that things are incompatible because the "parts" are used in different ways -- as an analogy, both a tent and a trampoline are made of a big flexible sheet, some line, and some rigid frame pieces, but they're put together in a fundamentally different ways because they're trying to do different things. And some things that would be perfectly fine for one (e.g. having a frame piece directly in contact with the flat/flexible thing) could be totally inappropriate for the other.
      \nI have my own tentative model of how games work that I think accounts for GNS. The short version is that it's about making meaningful contributions, and one of the things that undercuts meaningfulness in a social situation is other people not noticing it. So priming players to be cognizant of the areas where other players' contributions will be happening helps those contributions feel meaningful. The various CAs are taxonomizing certain classes of games that happen to have some family resemblance, but that doesn't necessarily make them utterly incompatible. Here's an instance of me postulating how one might describe some elements of RtD play in this framework:\n
      I'm not 100% sure how this maps to the canonical concept of Simulationism/RtD, but in my effort to reframe things in a way that makes sense to me I've been trying to focus on how different systems are meant to be fun on a moment-to-moment basis, rather than in a top-down way. From that POV, I've been thinking that Sim/RtD might be when a game makes a contribution feel meaningful by making it about a unique contribution that's interpreted across aesthetic appropriateness (by analogy, Gamism would be when it's unique contribution gauged against whether it's relevant to "winning"). In that sense, it's helpful when games make some things more salient than others. If what's unique about your contribution is how your character portrayal is playing off a particular archetype, I can't pick up on that if I'm not on the same page about your archetype -- kind of like not being able to pick out a signal on a carrier wave I'm not tuned to. Games like Fate are regarded as Sim-facilitating because they make it easy to communicate your touchpoints and make them salient to the other players -- I can watch for how your character portrayal maps to your Aspects and appreciate the particular nuance you're bringing to it, whereas if I see you "just roleplaying" then I'm paying no special attention to the thing you're bringing to the table, and you can tell I'm not picking up on what you hoped to be meaningful, and then the fun leaks out. Doing the thing you're talking about [of established a strong shared vision], where you just discuss lots and lots of stuff about inspirational source material, can do a similar thing in a less formalized way if it causes similar concepts to crystallize in each of our heads. At least that's how I'm thinking about it.
      \nOne idea I've been pondering along these lines is that it seems to me that, while the players in properly-played Dogs in the Vineyard are making morally-relevant contributions, the GM is generally supposed to not do that and is instead trying to do a fair-minded aesthetics-based "what would happen next, given the way this world works?" or entirely aesthetics-based vivid portrayal of the situation. These seem to me to be the kinds of things you'd normally describe as Sim/RtD. So maybe we already have games that are hybridizing CA but the historical way we talk about players and GMs have masked it.

      On the issue of whether StoryNow and RightToDream are distinct phenomena or not, in a recent G+ post I noted an example of where a philosopher was noting that aesthetic issues can produce similar levels of "emotional juice" to moral issues when agreement about them is important to the parties involved. This struck me as being very similar to the "shared commitment" explanation of Sim/RtD play.

The way I remember it, Ron developed the concept originally specifically to answer the question of where Creative Agenda actuates, but then he mostly ended up using it as an analytical aid in analyzing gaming - it's an interesting observation that Creative Agenda cannot really be observed externally, so you have to observe reward cycles and understand their nature correctly, on the premise that a game will only include reward cycles that accord with the CA the play is fulfilling.

      To be specific, the "reward cycle" of a given activity of play is the arc of purposeful play starting from the moment of purpose emerging, and ending in the moment of psychological payoff - when the purpose is satisfied. Moment-to-moment fun has an interesting relationship with this phenomenon, but it's not simply that the former is a messed up way of describing the latter. (If I had to suggest something about that relationship off-hand, I'd go with "having a purpose to what you're doing instead of wandering aimlessly or waiting is often fun, moment-to-moment".)

      For example, a player can have fun (whatever that word means) moment-to-moment because they have a sustainable personal curiousity in another player at the table (perhaps they want to know the other player better or something), and thus enjoy following their antics, all without that having anything to do with anything purposeful in the game per se. Likewise we could picture a purposeful yet grim sort of gameplay where the player is momentarily not enjoying themselves by whatever measure of "enjoy" we might use, yet would be engaged by a functional reward cycle.

      Ron said at some point that reward cycles can differ in length quite a bit, and I have to say that my own play experience has amply demonstrated that; sometimes it's like 10 minutes from postulating a weighty creative idea to resolving it satisfactorily, while other times it's 10 sessions of play for a plan to come to fruition. I've written about some of our D&D sandbox gaming arcs here now and then (here's the latest) specifically because I've myself found it so interesting and peculiar to witness real-to-life reward cycles operating on a time-scale of months, over dozens and hundreds of hours of gameplay. You know it to be a reward cycle, too, when you finish it and realize how the climax of the arc was the creative payoff you'd been looking for.

      \n Yeah, you have multiple reward cycles in operation at once, generally speaking, ranging from the short-term to the long term. Less so in simpler and shorter games, more so in deeper and longer ones. The clever observer will note that this has intimate relations with the GNS claim of mode coherence: if GNS is correct, there should be some mechanism that ensures that concurrently operating reward cycles are, in fact, of a mode. In other words, one may question what exactly causes off-mode reward cycles to be incoherent.

      At least part of the answer is that while a game often has multiple reward cycles going on simultaneously, a player is only generally engaged with one at a time - their purposefulness in acting upon the game state is driven by a particular reward cycle at a given time. Furthermore, another player will only understand their action if they understand the reward cycle, and will accept the action if they consider the reward cycle legitimate (for example, most wouldn't consider it legitimate if somebody was getting their jollies by ruining everybody else's fun), which would explain much about the incohesion phenomenon - you need to generally have players at least accept each other's reward cycles, and more ideally be actually playing towards the same reward cycle, for play to be coherent. This is reflected at a more conceptual level by the idea that GNS agenda modes predict coherence and incoherence.

      That thing about reward cycles generally going off-line and on-line in turn is important for a general understanding of what happens creatively in a complex game. For example, a full panoply D&D sandbox of the sort we've been playing around here a lot lately has many reward cycles at different levels: there's the satisfaction of concluding a short-term process such as logistics calculation or combat well, there's making concrete progress in an adventure, there's achieving a mid-term goal, even one for ending a session in high spirits, and so on. There's sometimes one for simply positing, executing and achieving an exceptional combat maneuver, like the time when we used cats as missile weapons against stirges in the spring - that one didn't take long from initiation to execution to satisfaction, it was quite satisfying to see how brilliantly my plan worked despite being so ridiculous, especially in the light of how very confident my character was about making it come about

      Returning to the idea of criticizing Forgite theory, I imagine that the claim that at most a single reward cycle is relevant to a single action is ripe for deconstruction. I'll suggest that while it is possible for an action to be concordant with multiple reward cycles at different levels of play, it is not so easy to imagine how multiple cycles could be motivating a single meaningful action at the same time, unless those cycles themselves happened to be so well in harmonious alignment (that is, pushing the player to the same direction for the same reasons) as to cohere into a single, over-riding creative agenda

      \n When I was formulating my theory I made a conscious choice to focus on meaningfulness rather than "reward" for a few reasons. First, what exactly a reward is can be hard to pin down. I was thinking of games like the computer game X-Com which has a bimodal strategic/tactical structure: you have a squad-level tactical mode where the benefit of killing aliens is that you collect their artifacts which you can research in the strategic layer, and the benefit of researching things in the strategic layer is better and different weapons, etc., for the tactical mode. It seems a little weird to me to call "more gameplay" a reward (in contrast to something like the lights and bells in a pinball game, those seem more unambiguously reward-like to me). You can have a similar thing with leveling up in some RPGs: are you fighting monsters to level up to get better at fighting monsters to get better at leveling up? For me the key element I was seeing in these examples was consequentiality, which is a way of making something feel meaningful. And focusing on "consequence" rather than "reward" let me sidestep the issue of whether a reward needs to be "over and above the normal".

      And in thinking about it in an RPG sense, I asked myself "would fake rewards work?" (e.g. saying "nice one!" for your buddy's maneuver that you actually thought was mediocre in a challenge-oriented RPG) and my conclusion was they wouldn't be reliable for any length of time, 1) because we humans tend to be somewhat good at sussing out fakery and 2) faking esteem and appreciation is effortful and therefore unlikely to be sustained for very long. However, people are likely to spontaneously give these "social rewards" for things they find genuinely valuable. And coordinating and influencing what people value is an element of the System they're using. (And things like the building off of someone else's contributions and making them consequential to the game-state are a powerful non-falsifiable signal that those contributions were meaningful, which you could call a reward but then we're back to the question of whether a reward needs to be "over and above" or not). So meaningfulness struck me as the more fundamental concept, and social signals of approval, etc., are reinforcers of System but aren't necessarily load-bearing elements of the machine.

      Also possibly worth noting, Ron's Big Model wiki has this to say about Reward (something is up with the site so you have to go to "view source" to see it, though):\n
      When it's fun. Seriously, that's all. If playing this game with these people is rewarding, then you either do it more to enjoy it some more, or perhaps you say "we've done it" and stop happy. During play, when you're talking, listening, and enjoying both, then you're being rewarded. When you can look back at a session or game experience in general and feel good about it socially and creatively, then you were rewarded.

      \n Forgite GNS discussion has a sort of a hidden, or at least relatively uncontested, assumption about that question: when we say that Vampire (or other games originating in that time and place - I didn't play Vampire myself, for example, yet had similar problems with other games) "fails to do narrativism" that doesn't really speak in such stark terms about the game itself, but rather about the people reading it and relating it to what they themselves want out of the game. The assumption is that there were people who wanted it to do Narrativism, and were disappointed when it didn't.

      Contesting that assumption would be a fair thing to do - you could e.g. think that Narrativism is an artificial invention and that practically nobody wanted that thing before the Forge made it popular, at which point people shifted their preferences. This would be the exact opposite of the GNS position, which is that the agenda modes describe real human psycho-social needs that have existed independent of giving them names long before the theory, or even before roleplaying as a form.

      I don't think that it's very sensible to critique Forgite understanding of Vampire on the grounds that the game text doesn't really promise Narrativism. Of course it doesn't, as no game text of the time did - almost all trad games of the period make vaguely contradictory promises about simultaneous character empowerment, dramatic stories, exciting challenges and whatever else. Insofar as we can say Vampire to differ from other games of the early '90s, it is generally taken to be more artistically ambitious and serious about attempting to provide emotionally touching dramatic moments than something like say GURPS or AD&D. The Forgite claim is that a significant number of people were disappointed when this "emotionally touching drama" proved to be something rather different from their own creative interests - ill suited for Narrativism, that is, and a better fit for a Sim game where the GM pre-plots a dramatic story using the player characters as their main protagonists.

      So the old complaint about Vampire not being Narrativist isn't so much about Vampire being falsely advertised (even if there is a tinge of that), it's more of a claim about the existing interests and needs of the player base, and how Vampire historically disappointed them. That claim could still be wrong, of course, if you feel like doubting it, but it doesn't really have anything in particular to do with what Vampire actually does. Vampire could be a solid block of wood, and the only thing that would change in the Forgite discourse would be how stupid the Forgite ex-Vampirist feels themselves as they realize that they've been putting their creative hopes upon a game that doesn't really support their interests. As things actually are, Forgite Vampire critique tends to blame the game more than the player, which I think is somewhat fair - not in the sense of Vampire being somehow criminal, merely in that it's not very good in comparison to what Forgite people generally consider a satisfying quality of game design to be. Were Vampire merely a block of wood, intended to act as a door stop, it would be fair to say that the correct target to blame would be the stupid player who foolishly thought that Vampire was a roleplaying game of cooperative dramatic storytelling instead of a block of wood. It all depends on how much of the blame you want to put on the stupid reader or the stupid book, and in this case it's at least not outright insane to put some of the blame on the book.

      \n If My Life with Master wasn't very clear about it, and all told it was a perfectly understandable human error to get mixed up about it, then sure, it would be somewhat fair to put part of the blame on the MLwM game text. It could have accounted for the pedagogical difficulties and the likelihood of the audience reading it in the wrong creative context, and corrected for that by being more clear and specific about what it is and is not. Isn't that reasonable?

      There are plenty of game texts where I think it's pretty fair to criticize the game text for not being as pedagogically clear as it could be. Often, note, such artistic critique does not imply some manner of failure on the part of the creator so much as it tells about our changing demands for what we expect of the artifacts of art; we generally do not hold e.g. Odysseia to task for not including extensive psychological first-perspective narration like a proper modern novel would. Sometimes, on the other hand, we do think that a text is a disappointment and a failure taken in its own context and merits - the creator should have managed better.

      I think that much of the rancour people used to have for Vampire (I think much of it has dissipated with time, as time is wont to heal all wounds) derives from the specific disappointment and frustration of their own artistic goals when trying to use the game; they expected and wanted more, and the game failed to deliver. The observation that it didn't really try only tells us that the people involved were misinterpreting the text, which still leaves open the question of whether they were morons or whether the text, perhaps, wasn't as clear as it could be.

      I imagine that if Vampire was an earlier text that wasn't contrasted with other games in its marketing the way it was, people wouldn't blame it so much for not being a Narrativist game. It coming out at a time when Narrativist interest was bubbling under the cultural surface, and positioning itself as the "story game" in comparison to the dice-rolling munchkin games, certainly contributed to how people would later come to be disappointed by it. As I said, there are many perfectly good explanations for why anybody could get mixed up on this, so it is "fair" to put some of the blame in the communication failure on Vampire. (Particularly so when e.g. I would be absolutely amazed if somebody unearthed an early '90s missive from e.g. Mark Rein-Hagen where he specifically declaimed that Vampire would be a perfectly good game for a narrativist playstyle - expressed in whatever words one might have used at the time to make a distinction between genre Sim and Narrativism. I would be flabbergasted, frankly, in a gaming culture riddled with One True Way for any commercial publisher to even hint at their game not being the right tool for the job.)

      I don't have any personal relationship with Vampire in particular, but I've had similar experiences with other games. MERP, for instance, was something of a disappointment to me as a youngster, on similar grounds: it "promised to me" an adventure game of magical Tolkien-y flavour, but, well, you know what the game is really like. You could quibble about that "promise", and perhaps a close reading of the text knowing everything we now know would come to conclude that legalistically speaking MERP never promised me anything of the sort I believed it did. Nevertheless, I would hold that the young Eero wasn't the only party to blame when he failed to decipher that game's true nature and purpose in the early '90s.
