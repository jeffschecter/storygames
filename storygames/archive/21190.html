Discussion is about old version, below:\n

Here\u2019s a house rule I designed for all non-magical weapons with ammo. Javelins, throwing darts, crossbows, bows etc.

\n

If you are in Ordinary Fight (i.e.\xa0no chase or volley), you don\u2019t pay ammo until you make the killing shot.

(Ordinary Fight is a reference to another, older house rule that we're still using. Pretty much when you're in the same room, basically. Makes ranges easier.)\n

You still make attack rolls as normal, decrease their HP as normal etc. But, their HP loss represents position, threat, stress etc. You draw back your string and you keep them marked by making attack rolls on them. And then, as they go to zero, you scratch one arrow. That was when you released the string. (If someone else swords them to death while you\u2019re threatening them w/ arrows, you save the arrow altogether.)

\n

This rule is 100% optional. If you think it\u2019s cooler to blast your foe with arrows making them look like a porcupine, you are correct. But, more options, more better, right? This rule is something you can use when you wish, ignore when you wish.

\n

This still counts as a ranged attack. So, no capturing enemies (you need m\xeal\xe9e for that) + you still get disadvantage at point blank etc etc. The only difference mechanically is that you don\u2019t use up your ammo.

    \n I like it. I would not phrase it in this way (it's sort of in-your-face about the essential abstraction), but it is foundationally reasonable and in line with what HP simulate.

    The variation I like myself is to leave ammo issues undefined (D&D does this a lot) until needed, at which point a current state may be established. Similar philosophy, except I do not require a player to determine blow by blow whether they're expending arrows or not, just like I don't need to usually know precisely which sword strokes cause wounds.

    A simple mechanical implementation for a stochastic model is to equip characters with "quivers of arrows" and roll for running out on occasion (like 1/6 after every battle or whatever). As long as the supplies last it doesn't make a foundational difference how you describe your attacks. Character who aren't running out are apparently managing to scrounge arrows effectively, or perhaps they brought a few more than expected, or maybe their attacks were, indeed, mostly conceptual - we can determine the why on a case-by-case basis, just like we do with the "why did the HP drop" issue when a character "hits" something. If a character really doesn't want to run out of ammo, they'll presumably have a caddie carry extra quivers [grin].

    The player base in my experience seems split between the ones who prefer to track ammo and the ones who don't - the latter seem happy with the above sort of arrangement, while the former want to always know exactly what they have; they might prefer your model, except I've the gut feeling that this type of player is also the type that is most bothered by the abstraction in the idea of arrowless "shooting". I suspect that this split is foremost about whether a player likes having that little number on their sheet, or whether they find it ugly and cluttersome.

    When the ammo-tracking players scrounge for arrows, by the way, I use a simple assumption of 50% recovery - roll a low/high for roughly each arrow spent in the battle, and an exploration round later, that's how many arrows you managed to recover. Helps with not running out in a long expedition, and it's sort of a realistic, cool and ruthless thing for an archer to be doing.

    One more observation about your rule: the way I would do that would be to say that you scratch one arrow on a killing blow and when you roll good damage - say over half of your maximum, or whatever. If we're doing precise tracking of when arrows fly I'd prefer it if there were also some missing and scratching arrows, instead of every notch being a kill-blow - that has the potential to make the swordsmen look ineffectual, plus I like the imagery of having arrows flying here and there.

    By the way: what do you do with a player who insists on shooting into melee on the principle that merely aiming won't hurt their friends anyway? It might be consistent with the philosophy to simply split the "damage" (stress and fear) of a successful aim even between the combatants [grin].

    \n Perhaps you should rephrase the rule in a way that emphasizes consistency of the battle simulation, rather than seemingly giving archers weird psychic powers. Something like this:

    Threat Attack combat maneuver: When a character is capable of threatening an enemy in a way intelligible to that enemy, the player may opt to make a Threat Attack instead of a regular one. Threat attacks are resolved like normal attacks (attack rolls to establish the threat credibility, etc.), except damage is typed as psychic, melee fighters do not need to approach to engage (they need to be able to, though), and archers do not need to actually expend ammo (although they still need to notch the bow). The GM may assign disadvantage to a Threat Attack when the enemy is otherwise distracted (e.g. by on-going melee).

    Aiming: Instead of whatever the wussy default rule is, aiming with missile weapons is executed by selecting target and making a normal attack roll. On a success the player rolls damage, secret from the GM, and marks it down. No ammo is expended, and the enemy does not need to know that they are being aimed at. The aiming character may continue aiming for multiple rounds, but when they do release their attack, the enemy suffers the entire amassed aiming damage all at once. The aim is equivalent to a prepared action, and may be released as a reaction (causing the damage so far amassed), or the character may release it alongside an ordinary attack roll on their turn (which may miss or not, the aim damage happens nevertheless). The aim damage is lost and wasted if the archer loses sight or has to stop aiming for any reason, without actually releasing the arrow; as this is unlikely (it requires a combat event that interrupts the aim without the archer being capable of reacting in response), aiming damage may generally be considered as good as dealt, even as it is delayed.

    (You could get by on only the aiming maneuver, but I think it's good to have variety in options. If I were archering in this paradigm, I'd totally want to first aim and then go all "I've got you in my sights! You're already dead, unless you drop your weapons now!")

    Depending on the player tastes, the above sorts of maneuver options may work better for them than free-wheeling. It's not precisely equivalent to the simple "only scratch arrows on killing blows" approach, but the differences are I think generally intuitive, simple and worthwhile, and if you make threats and aiming clearly explicit maneuvers, the players should have less difficulty understanding what's going on. And of course, I think that the player of an archer will find the suggested aiming mechanic immensely interesting: the GM doesn't know it yet, but that monster's pretty much a goner any time I choose to release this shot.

    Plus, I would personally find it rather amusing if the rules generally enshrined the idea that a combat consists of people shouting vile insults at each other, plus brandishing weapons, while a bunch of archers observe each other and try to guess who's going to shoot at whom, and when. Sounds entertaining.

    Paul_T said:

    I'm not entirely sure how I feel about the sudden really really heavy importance of "distracting the achers", but otherwise it's a very workable, fun, and reasonably non-abstract take on the issue.

    \nThe way I'd play it is that it's really, essentially impossible to distract the archer sufficiently to "detract" the damage from the aim. I only wrote that bit in there to highlight the interesting possibility of something really exceptional occurring to save a character otherwise marked for death. It's important for this sort of aiming mechanic to really make sense that the damage caused is pretty much a done deal, even if the enemy doesn't know about it yet.

    Like, attacking the archer wouldn't cut it - they'd just release the shaft as a free reaction before turning to deal with the new assailant. Same for spells, an itchy trigger finger moves faster than any spell-caster (excepting perhaps the special case of an archer who has no preternatural cultural context whatsoever, causing them to misinterpret the whole situation). You would need to be able to sneak in there without the archer being aware of you at all to be able to cancel that aim. Not a normal combat condition, but perhaps just about possible in a special case, if the archer doesn't have a spotter of his own to cover his back while he's working.

    The big reason for why I'd like to break threatening and aiming off from normal attacks like this is that I find the distinctions in when and where you can use these attacks interesting. You can't threaten mindless enemies, for instance, while "aim" is uniquely both guaranteed damage as well as retractable (you can decide to not take the shot after all) and covert (the enemy doesn't even know that they're dead). However, if you delay releasing your aim for too long, you may allow the enemy to live and cause problems for unnecessarily long.

    The one big thing that I personally care about regarding archers and aiming is that D&D is really bad at assassinations. If one were to ride this sort of logic to a consistent end, it would finally be possible to pre-load mucho damage into one bow-shot, so as to actually kill a bad guy cleverly from a well-prepared firing position. It's an interesting alternative for the various "bypass the HP and cause a direct save-or-die status effect" hacks that I generally use when characters start deconstructing the combat space in this way.

    \n I love the implications of slowly whittling down an enemy's hit points with positioning - it fixes a lot of issues with hit point abstractions and allows things like assassinations. There is a lot of good synergy from this rule to all kinds of interesting things!

    I'm not yet sold on this idea of the "undistractable" archer, though. It seems to me that (especially in D&D! A game with magic and monsters and weird dungeons!) combat in this idiom so often involves strange and unexpected phenomena that justifying the damage done would either be awkward or really artificial sometimes.

    Is there ever a chance of missing, for instance? You'd have to remove it for this to work - that "final" shot has to be an automatic hit.

    In most scenarios this seems fine, but what about dragon breath, teleportation, mind control magic, shifting dungeon environments, traps... I have a feeling that this would feel really strange sometimes.

    For instance, a thought experiment:

    I'm aiming an arrow at my friend, because I've been Charmed and told to attack them. They're backing away towards a lever and saying, "Hey, I'm your friend! Don't shoot; I'm just going to pull this level here, OK?"

    I'm accumulating "damage" against them and getting ready to shoot.

    Then some instant effect happens which breaks the Charm spell - suddenly all becomes to clear to me and the illusion is dispelled!

    How do we decide whether I "shoot" or not? Is the Charmed persona in control (as would be the case if I had not been charmed - presumably the game would be biased towards always giving me a chance to shoot, regardless of interruptions, for this rule to work at all)? Or is the player in perfect control, just deciding to "take the free action" or not as suits them, regardless of the character's interests, inclinations, or instincts?

    Sandra,

    I could see a version of "PC assassination" where all you are told is "your danger sense is tingling", for each "round" of aiming, and you can try to do something to figure out what's going on, seek cover, or some other thing.

    It gives PCs a somewhat unfair advantage (for example, it is now much easier to assassinate an NPC with an identical number of HP than a PC with the same 'toughness'), but so do many other D&D rules, so I'm OK with that for the sake of playability.

    \n I left the nitty-gritty details vague, Paul, because it goes into the "plumbing" of a given campaign, so to speak. Rules texts can speak about this stuff, but we all play different versions of the game, and this is precisely the sort of thing that depends on the big-picture mechanical strategy of the given campaign. Besides, I didn't want to get dogmatic about how one should use these kinds of ideas: Sandra clearly started with the notion that "aiming to injure" needs to be a 100% equal option for the archer, no different from shooting, but obviously there's room for interesting tactical differences if one wants there to be.

    For an example of more precise rules-laying that answers every one of your questions, in something like 3rd or 4th edition D&D, with well-defined action types and such, the notion of "releasing the aimed shot" would be very simple, very clear: it's an "Immediate action" in 3rd edition, or a "immediate interrupt" in 4th. (As far as I remember, anyway.) All these questions about how different situations would be adjudicated, whether the archer could loose their shaft in time, would be answered by those rules categorically. Of course you could also decide that you don't like archers being quite so uninterruptable as those rules imply, so perhaps instead of an "immediate interrupt" you'd have loosing a readied shot be an "immediate reaction", or whatever.

    Meanwhile the above sort of terminology would be essentially meaningless to some other flavours of D&D. You'll need to figure out how things work in your own game. The concept is clear at least, I hope: the archer's got a shot readied to go whenever they need it to, and mechanically speaking we are already guaranteeing a certain amount of damage (HPs are not a realistic simulative element per se, I do not need to justify this in any way) whenever they decide to take the shot. It's a matter of reaction speed to do it in response to some interference, call it a second or two at most; you'll need a thoroughly immediate, unexpected way to interfere with them if you desire to prevent them from doing it - a true surprise. It may even be artificially difficult to interfere in that we may consider this a subjective stylistic right on the part of the archer: they're partaking of a genre convention or a simulative conceit, no different from e.g. the idea of initiative ("what do you mean we take turns to hit each other?"), so if we decide that it's difficult to get between an archer and their aimed shot, then it is so. Nobody except for myself seems to question the inexplicable capability of an adventurer to dig up a healing potion out of their backpack, uncork and apply it in six seconds flat, so I don't see why anybody would need to have problems with the idea that archers can loose readied shots quicker than you can interrupt them.\n

    \nThe way I described the putative action, this does not in fact matter. The aimed shot can be loosed reactively whenever, and whether it's a "miss" or not in fictional terms can be determined in the usual manner - just like any attack. One of the explicit benefits of this sort of aiming rule for the archer would be in the fact that they can choose when, precisely, the enemy dies during the combat round.

    If the aimed shot is released in the context of one last attack roll - which seems intuitive, of course - then perhaps that last attack check can be used to determine whether the shot hits the enemy. This is, of course, entirely irrelevant to the damage already established by the earlier rolls. If that last roll hits but the damage does not suffice to kill, then perhaps it's just a shallow wound; if that last roll misses but the damage from earlier aims suffices to kill, then perhaps it's such a narrow miss that it causes the enemy to stumble and hit their head and die that way. The D&D rules do not speak to this sort of combat choreography minutiae in a systematic way, so you get to determine the particulars for yourself.

    I trust that you understand the mathematical logic behind this: the damage has already been dealt, in formal terms, by the time the archer decides to take their shot. Ordinary attack rolls have been made, with the ordinary circumstance modifiers even. Undermine the inevitability of that damage too much, and you are basically saying that this type of attack roll is not, after all, as acceptable to take as a "I hit it with the sword" maneuver. The very idea of accepting "arrowless shooting attacks" entirely presumes a willingness to allow the players to describe mechanically roughly equivalent actions in flexible ways.\n
