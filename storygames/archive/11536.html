\n GURPS is cool because it's thorough and self-consistent. You can look up any situation, and find an arbitration process that is compatible with the processes you've used for every other situation.

Most people I've played GURPS with were drawn to that consistent quality, but they also wanted something more: they wanted consistency with the fiction, a fiction that (usually) obeyed real-world physics. We all got the impression that GURPS was trying to give us that, but our results with it were unimpressive. Sometimes, outcomes made sense. Sometimes, they didn't.

All throughout the process of turning to GURPS' system of die numbers and modifiers, we never really honed our judgment of when to employ them. Basically, we employed them whenever someone cared about an outcome of any fictional action.

I'm just using GURPS as an example to illustrate the following type of game experience:

Player: "I try to shoot the goblin. My attack roll is 3d6."

GM: "The goblin is running. That seems relevant. I'll look it up. (Flip. Flip.) Okay, he gets +1 die to his 2d6 dodge dice."

Player: "Well, it's dim, I've set up the shot, I'm standing still. That should penalize his dodge. (Flip. Flip.) Yep, -2 dice for Total Surprise. He's down to 1d6."

GM: "You're right in his field of vision! Total Surprise is for... (Flip. Flip.) ... for sneaking up behind someone!"

Player: "Okay, fine, Partial Surprise. -1 die. 2d6 total. Hey, I also have the high ground. I think that gives a bonus too."

GM: "Really? (Flip. Flip.) It gives a distance bonus for ranged shots. Not relevant here."

Player: "Does that bonus move my shot to the Point Blank increment? (Flip. Flip.) Ah, guess not."

I hate this. See how much flipping through tables there is? See how long it takes? See how the numbers and categories begin to dominate the players' attention, even if they only started out by trying to model the fiction? Maybe the way the scene's been described, the 3d6 vs 2d6 rolls don't even make much sense! Consider an alternative:

Player: "I try to shoot the goblin."

GM: "Okay. Can he see you?"

Player: "Only if he's looking up. And looking carefully enough to notice a still figure in the twilight."

GM: "Well, you're moving a bit to track him with your bow."

Player: "True. Pretty subtle, though."

GM: "Yeah. Well, if you were in the goblin's situation, you'd want a chance to notice."

Player: "He's as vigilant as us?"

GM: "Well, maybe not quite. Okay, a small chance. Let's say 1 in 4?"

Player: "Cool."

GM: (Rolls d4) "Nope, he never sees you. You can shoot him."

Player: "Should I bother rolling?"

GM: "Hmm. Well, it doesn't seem completely foolproof. Just don't roll two 1s on 2d6."

Player: "Cool."

This process is all about making sense of the fiction for the players. Anyone has the chance to lobby for what makes sense to them. Dice and numbers may still come into play, but only if fine details become significant (did I shoot the goblin in an armored or unarmored location?). And there's no delays trying to remember where the "high ground bonus" table is. I find that playing this way teaches useful habits, allows the flow of imagined events to continue with minimal interruptions, and produces more acceptable outcomes than a refined mathematical "all-purpose" system.

Obviously, there's no opportunity for boardgame-like rules mastery. Anyone who enjoys that part of GURPS would probably miss that under my system. But if the only goal is "realistically determine what happens in the fiction as painlessly as possible", I think my way is hands-down better.

Now, there's a lot of system encoded in the above example. Consensus doesn't always happen by accident. But I think it's pretty easy to learn how to get on the same page with your fellow players if that's what you're there to do. I made some cartoons to show how.

    \n Dave,

    You're probably away at Dreamation at the moment, but for when you get back, just in case you find it interesting:

    The way many of the Fudge crowd I used to be in with played this was more or less like so:

    * Figure out what the worst possible outcome of the action/moment is.
    * Figure out what the best possible outcome of the action/moment is.
    * Roll the Fudge dice (for those hip to the jargon, this is a "situational roll").

    For those not familiar, the result is one of seven adjectives (like "Fair", "Poor", etc).

    * "Superb" means you get the best possible outcome.
    * "Terrible" means you get the worst possible outcome.

    With various gradations in-between, to be interpreted (by the GM, usually).

    Not a bad way to do things. Also interesting, because the Fudge dice can rarely (once every 81 rolls, statistically) exceed that maximum and minimum, in which case you can go "Oh, shit! What's even worse than the worst possible outcome we envisioned?" And something fun inevitably comes up.

    The nice thing about this method is that, with a group that's established a strong trust and a strong image of the fiction, you can roll for whatever and whenever, you get lots of unpredictable outcomes, but never anything game-breaking. The dice won't kill your character at the "wrong time" unless the group is willing for that to be an outcome in that situation. So, no getting killed by a house cat that swipes at your hand with a claw.

    I apologize for naming the thready in a murky way. "Abstraction" is the wrong term. I could use Vincent-speak and say we're going from Cloud to Cloud without any Boxes. Or I could say that we're removing one layer of signification between asking and answering "what happens in the fiction?" Gah. None of those are quite right.

    \n A number of people here have mentioned the downsides of discussing the fiction to form consensus. The main ones are (#1) too long distraction from play, (#2) too frequent distraction from play, and (#3) vehement disagreement.

    A primary purpose of the cartoons is to show how to avoid this. As far as being able to tell how to avoid this, I am inelegant at best. But, here goes:

    #1 - too long distraction from play
    If your group enjoys discussing physics and setting features and history etc., and doesn't mind using play time for that purpose, then there's no problem. If your group enjoys discussing these things but regrets using play time for that purpose, the solution is to stay focused on what's at stake in the fiction -- can this character pull himself over this wall -- rather than getting caught up in anecdotes, theory, and calculation. Beyond that, the same rules for avoiding vehement disagreement apply and will help speed things up. Finally, if your group has no interest in discussing physics etc., you should just go for visuals and decide on the basis of who can imagine what. (Objectivity is unnecessary. If an entire group of players can somehow comfortably visualize something that a physics professor knows to be impossible, that's fine -- they shouldn't care about that professor.)

    The cartoons may take a while to read, but they represent mere seconds of real time. Also, though they represent optimal player attitudes, they do show worst-case first-try performance -- play will go much faster and require less discussion if the GM doesn't say "7 feet" to describe something too high to jump and grab.

    #2 - too frequent distraction from play
    A lot of people who are drawn to playing this way enjoy minutiae. Thus, I am lazy about picking examples that are the most compelling -- I just go with stuff that could happen, not stuff that I assume everyone must care about. Resolving the fiction in this style of play is no different than in any other style, in that you cover what you care about, and skip over what you don't care about. In Delve specifically, I encourage players to skip boring stuff by moving a pacing dial to "summary" mode.

    #3 - vehement disagreement
    The key to avoiding vehement disagreement is to strive for mutual plausibility, not perfection. Don't come up with the one thing that makes most sense to you and dig in your heels -- instead, allow for the full range of outcomes that make any sense to you. If everyone else does the same, you will find overlap. The instant you find an overlap, stop! You are done. Do not whittle and whittle in hopes of finding the one perfect answer.

    If I think torch light most likely illuminates 30 feet and you think torch light most likely illuminates 60 feet, it doesn't matter who's right as long as we both agree that torch light could illuminate 45 feet. We can both imagine it, so that's the way it is.

    Respect other people's "not plausible" calls, and don't call "not plausible" yourself unless you have to.

    \n Though I can't begin to address all of Deliverator Matt's excellent points yet, I will say that I agree with him about the need for introductory communication on these matters. In my experience, playing in the manner I'm describing does not happen by accident. I've generally seen forms of it evolve over years of going through trial and error with the same groups of people.     Can the fiction tell you yes or no, or only maybe?
    If only maybe, roll dice.

    (YES comes from player expertise.

    NO comes from player inability to envision.)

    No one is perfect at describing the fiction. Revise when you have to.*

    If something threatens your suspension of disbelief, try to fix that.
    If you can't fix it, say so.

    Look, look for, try to judge, vet, refine, act.

    Verify assumptions when they matter.

    *The fiction itself isn't being revised, just the reporting of it. Yes, this is self-brainwashing, but it's constructive.

    \n I ran Shadowrun for\u2026

    - An FBI agent
    - Scientist
    - Computer security expert
    - Salesman
    - and a 20+ year martial arts instructor

    They were a Shadowrun team in real life!!!

    And they all prioritized immersion and realism over game tactics and drama!

    My job was not easy :-(

    In fact I failed to meet their expectations. I couldn\u2019t.

    So I gave up trying and implemented a style of play similar to the one here. I got rid of all of the difficulty and modifier charts and instead turned to group consensus. I set the challenge and suggest a difficulty but then the players could adjust the difficulty up or down or declare a challenge an automatic success or failure based on what we knew or thought we knew to be realistic.

    The problem is reality isn\u2019t perception (sometimes what people think is reality is based on what they saw on a TV show!). Even an FBI agent doesn\u2019t know everything about law and the computer security expert didn\u2019t know everything about hacking. We even occasionally replaced flipping through a Gurps book with using Google on a laptop. And even experts in a relevant field were sometimes wrong. Or experts in the same field couldn\u2019t agree. Or there were way too many variables that results could swing unpredictably to extremes (a common example being one person dying from a seemingly non lethal knife wound while someone else walking away after being shot 7 times). And sometimes negotiation took longer than looking up a difficulty on a chart.

    I decided to use my experience running office meetings to find a middle ground.

    Player describes their intent. I introduce an obstacle. They describe their action and build a pool of d6s to roll. I set the initial difficulty and build a pool of d6s to oppose them.

    Then everyone else, without negotiation, can help either side by simply saying why and sliding a single d6 to the appropriate pool. It also became a way of co-authoring the fiction. When sliding forward a d6, you explain why by describing \u201cand this\u2026\u201d or \u201cbut that\u2026\u201d without contradicting what was already established or understood. And real life experts could slide forward two d6s and give two separate reasons for it.

    It actually worked really well. We weren\u2019t playing Shadowrun anymore but it was a fast compromise.

    We later changed the rules so that there was an advantage to testing against a high difficulty. The higher the difficulty, the quicker your skills advanced (ala Burning Wheel). So no one felt bad about using their real life knowledge to make tests harsh if it matched people\u2019s real life expectations.

    That said, we still had unpredictable results. 2 dice would occasionally beat 6. And just like the example where a person could die from a seemingly non fatal cut but survive being shot several times, we would use it as an opportunity to explain how that could happen. The gap between expectations became an opportunity for creativity. And since everyone helped weigh the initial odds, people rarely felt disappointed.

    This method also didn\u2019t always lead to the most dramatic results. But it fit the play style of this specific group who couldn\u2019t watch TV shows about their areas of expertise where drama overruled reality. Me, I\u2019m all about the drama! But I also like to please my players.

    \n (On hit locations, from my "fiction-first" Fudge-playing days:)

    The trick we developed went like so: the GM considers the most likely angle of the attack. It could be "I dunno, anywhere on the target", or something more like, "OK, you're slightly above him and swinging down with a sword, so it's going to be somewhere on the left torso, from above and off to the right."

    If more detail is needed, the GM rolls a single Fudge die (+, 0, or -) for vertical deviation. So, for the GM's envisioned "possible range" or struck areas, a "+" would mean "OK, the blow is on the top end of this possible range". In other words, you take the envisioned area and mentally divide it into three vertical slices. A "+" is the top slice (maybe above the shoulder, nearer the neck, in this situation), a "0" is the middle slice, and a "-" is the bottom slice (maybe the target's right arm, around the elbow?).

    Then, if more detail is needed, roll another Fudge die for horizontal position of the blow, in much the same way.

    If more detail is needed, you can keep rolling, "zooming in" each time. It's pretty quick in practice. So, a shot from a rifle at a standing man:

    Roll 1: "-". Ok, it's the bottom third of the figure, thigh or below. Left, right, or middle?
    Roll 2: "+". Okay, left side. How high along the leg?
    Roll 3: "0". Middle of the range? That's a bullet to the left knee.

    That takes just a few seconds of consideration, no longer than looking up a chart. And it's adaptable to any situation (which is difficult with a pre-designed table). It's kind of like designing a perfectly-suited hit location table for every specific situation on the fly.

      First, let me say that revision only happens when the difference matters. The GM isn't going around re-detailing the setting just to get the color right.

      Player 1: How high's the ceiling?

      GM: Bob's tall. The ceiling's a foot or so over his head. I guess 7 feet.

      Player 1: Wait, this has the standard thatch roof? The super thick stuff?

      GM: Yeah.

      Player 1: Then from outside, to get up top, it's at least 9 feet.

      GM: I guess so. Yes.

      Player 1: So that chase scene couldn't have happened.

      It isn't. It really, really isn't.

      The priority is the players' immersion (you know what "immersion" means here, so I'm not going to define it). That's only possible if they:
      a) pretend that there is an objective fictional reality, and
      b) have a means through which to view that reality

      Characters don't have stats, but are instead built out of aspect-like "facts" that detail their abilities and typical behaviors within the narrative. PC's have a character sheet with a list of facts, while NPC's can be detailed to whatever level is appropriate. The genre, style of play, setting, etc. also contribute their own facts, though often those facts are just understood rather than documented. A challenge involves a bit of negotiation, where the GM sets a difficulty, and the player accepts or makes a proposed adjustment based on factors that the GM perhaps didn't consider.

      For example, a PC has crept to within arrow-shot of an orc camp. There's a lone orc sentry walking the edge of the camp, and the PC has managed to remain undetected.

      Player: "I want to take a shot at the sentry. I'll be aiming for his head, in order to put him down without a sound."

      GM: "Okay. Your character is great with a bow, but you're at a fairly long range and it's a moonless night. It'll be tough. I'd say 50%."

      Player: "I've got plenty of time to take aim. Seems like I should have better than a 50/50 chance."

      GM: "True. Assuming you're taking your time, I think 60% is reasonable."

      Player: "I want to introduce a fact (pays a fact point). Let's say the sentry strolls over by a fire for a moment to warm his hands. I'll wait for that and use the light to help my aim."

      GM: "Okay, that's worth another shift. Let's finalize it at 70%. With a close miss, you'll hit him but it won't be fatal and he's going to make some noise."

      Player: "Sounds Good" (grabs the percentile dice)

      ----

      In play, things typically move between micro and macro level exchanges, even in the midst of a single combat. It's about zooming on what feels suspenseful, or cool, or important to the story.

      What I like about this approach is that it bundles lots of complex rules for "reality simulation" into common sense and arbitration. Using the percentage system allows you to just shift up or down by 10% to represent new facts, and everyone understands what a 70% chance means (not everyone gets the impact of trying to roll a 10 on a 2d6 versus something like 7).

      I haven't dealt with hit locations except as part of descriptions and outcomes. Wounds are a consequence of taking risks and blowing the die roll, and have an impact on relevant actions (as a negative fact) if they aren't attended to.

      \nAs far as I am aware (it's possible I could be wrong, though such things are rare) the term Rule Zero, as used at the Forge, is a pretty specific thing. It refers to any line in the rules text that says any variation on "If you don't like (this/a/some of the/all of the) rule(s), feel free to change/discard it. After all, it's your game." The vitriol heaped upon Rule Zero is rooted partially in the fact that the players don't need your permission; If they want to do this, they're going to do it whether or not you tell them too. The other major part of the hate-fest is rooted in the concept of System Matters. You as the designer presumably wrote the rules, tested the rules, tweaked the rules and tested them further. Whatever rules are in there are there for a reason, on purpose. Removal or alteration of any of the rules will have an effect on the way the game plays, which is assumed to be the way it is MEANT to be played.
      \n
      \nThis isn't necessarily a bad thing, but it could be. You're striking off into unexplored territory when you start altering a game. Here There Be Dragons.
      \n
      \n(Forgive me if you already know of the following...) A related concept that exists in some form in the Forge-based toolkits is what is called Dials. I have a similar concept that I also use that I don't recall if it actually came from the Forge community that I call Switches. Essentially, these are specific alterations to the game that have been considered and presumably tested by the game designer. Dials are basically multiple setting changes (usually with differing levels of something). These can be changed in play (exactly like your pacing dial) or can be set for a session, an arc, or even an entire campaign. Switches are essentially on/off. They're rules that are added or removed, usually for longer than a single session. The important thing about these concepts that keep them from being an elaborate cover for Rule Zero is that their effect is considered and can be relied upon to a degree. Note that the dials and switches don't necessarily have to be named thus.
      \n
      \nWhere this ties back in to where the discussion is currently going: What if the level of control that the players have over the details of the situation and setting is treated like a dial? Obviously the default setting should be where David is talking about. I think that there is something to be said for the other end of the spectrum, though... Your way of playing it, David, may simply not work for John and Paul, even though the rest of the game system works amazingly for them.       So. If I acknowledge that (a) the rule is there for a damn good reason, but (b) some players will hate it, and (c) that hatred may break the game for them worse than ignoring the rule would, then what's my best recourse?

      One option is to turn the rule into a dial, and explicitly spell out how they can do something that I'd rather they didn't do, if they really have to.

      The other option is to just state the rule, and let them fend for themselves if they choose not to follow it.

      If I was looking through a rulebook and saw a rule that I didn't get, and was told, "Or you could set it this way instead," I don't think I'd try the default. I think I'd try the dial-setting that instantly looked more appealing. If the rulebook gave good guidance for that dial-setting, then the game would help me do what I wanted. Sweet.

      Let's call that Outcome B.

      If the book doesn't communicate "reset this dial" we get different outcomes. Outcome A: the players use the rules long enough to get the hang of using them in the way I intended. Outcome C: the players abandon my rule (either from the get-go or after a brief trial) and come up with their own process that may not work so well.

      So, for me, Outcome A is better than B is better than C.

      It seems like a coin flip as to what textual approach will serve me best: dial or hard and fast rule.

      I think my preference is to put my energy into selling people on using my damn rule. Is that hopelessly unrealistic?

      I mean, I'm not above bending to practicality here. Daniel H. is coming from some "bad GM" experiences that a lot of roleplayers share. If there's no hope of reaching people's "trust mode", then I'd rather not bang my head against a wall.
