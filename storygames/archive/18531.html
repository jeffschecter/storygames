    \nWe tried this in 2006 or so with the Finnish rpg Praedor. It's a completely ordinary traditional fantasy adventure game, albeit one crafted to a higher standard than the vast majority of '90s games. The GMing tasks were basically handled by having decisions made by whoever at the table felt like it.

    Obviously the results of such a free-wheeling test depend mostly on the players, their relations, and their agenda, but in our case it worked just fine. A good example of how a rpg sometimes works with very little in the way of system (take the GM out of a trad game, you just removed 85% of the game's system, such as it is), as long as the players are on the same wave-length aesthetically. We got situations, color, some combat encounters and so on, with no discernible difference against a GM who just happened to be pretty explicit about showing the players his paperwork. I frankly preferred it to the ordinary organization of playing Praedor, and would go for it again - it's a pretty character-centered game, and allowing everybody to take care of the GMing duties while they play characters is just less boring, well-crafted as the game is.

    \n Fairy Tale Assassin League is GMless. The player who brings the game is the game host and plays the single bad guy player but their only job as a host is to help keep the game moving rather than to direct play.

    Players create the scenario by saying how the bad guy screwed the over. The bad guy brags about how evil they are and everyone knows that in the last turn of the game the good guys get medieval on the bad guy. Aside from that the players have totally equal power.

    The active player plays an action card or points to the next scene can and makes up a story. Other players play actions cards to challenge and add to the story or alter it. High roller wins. Winners get to keep the action cards (which give victory points). Almost everything that is said happens and the cards guide play towards cool stuff. All the players, including the host are equal.



    Plays in an hour. No prep. Sets up and tears down in minutes.

    As to the philosophical questions of what a Game Master is? If a game is a conversation between players (and one player runs the whole world - aside from the PCs) The there is one player who does a whole lot more than the others. All players have the power to make shit up but this one player has more scope to make stuff up in. This is true even in the world games. If the world GM is following the rules they are not making anything up to power trip on the other players but that doesn't deny that they have a hell of a lot more power which can make or break a game.

    If a game does not need one strong player to take on this bigger role then it is GMless. That is not true of many RPGs. D+D can be played using random encounter tables and with a few tweaks a room generation table or two but it would be a mere shadow of what D+D can be. Kind of like computer games that do just that. It's just not the same beast as conversations go.

    Which brings us to the rub. GMfull games often need to be seen for players to know how to play them. They can't easily be run from just reading about them. I think this also applies to Fiasco. Sorry Jason but if I'd just read the rules and not played before I'd have had a hard time figuring out all the rules (one key dice rolling rule was only in a section header and not in the following text). I remember the same confusion in reading My Life With Master. I couldn't figure it out without seeing it. This is not the case with board games, card games etc.

    Been grappling with this issue for years. Writing rules is easy. Writing coherent rules is hard. Writing rules that elegantly explain exactly how to play is extremely hard and that is what a GMless game requires IMO.

    \n Going back to D&D for a minute, I think part of what Vincent is getting at regarding using words like authority is as follows: The old model of gameplay and game design- which, as I say, is still barnacled to the boat in many places- had the 'players' as a distinct entity, sitting down at a table waiting for the 'GM', another distinct entity, to 'make things happen', and the 'GM' could be thought of as either good or bad down to the way he/she made these things happen.       \n I think "GM" and "GMless" are perfectly fine terms for folks who aren't inclined to sweat the precise details. I'd expect they'll continue to be used forever. I pretty much have to use them to communicate with certain friends.

      For deconstruction or design, on the other hand, I agree with the comments above: "What are the things that must be done in play, and who's responsible for each of them?" is more useful.

      As for @madunkieg's initial question, I've played a lot of "GMless" games which feel like taking turns GMing, but I've also played Sign in Stranger, which never feels like GMing to me. There are two reasons for this:

      1) No one ever performs the traditional GM task of inventing the environment based purely on what they think should/would be there. Instead, someone asks a question about the environment, and someone else draws a random word to use for an answer. In D&D terms, it'd be like the Wandering Monster roll, except used for, y'know, everything.

      As far as division of labor, in Sign in Stranger, the draw is made by the player whose character is most expert in the question topic. The Geographer's player draws to answer questions about terrain, the Linguist's player draws to answer questions about alien signs and symbols, etc.

      2) No one ever performs the traditional GM task of taking a mechanical outcome ("you open the vault!") and determining how that impact what the group cares about in the fiction ("does the vault contain the treasure it was supposed to contain?"). Instead, the roll result tells you how much you learn from your investigation, how useful it will be to you going forward, and whether you get hurt or stressed or mutated in the process. That covers pretty much everything of interest in the game's situations, other than what the characters choose to do.

      \n I've been reconsidering my definitions of GM in light of all that has been said in this thread. A GMless roleplaying game doesn't exist. Even D&D 4e has a GM, the game designer. If played that way, the GM can't make the sort of decisions that someone present could (e.g what happens when the adventurers throw ropes over the dragon), but the players may step in and make such decisions on the designers' behalf.

      Different games split the authority in different ways. Do doesn't split the authority very much, and players are left to negotiate what happens on their own. Shock splits the authority (amongst other ways) along the lines of issues and shocks, but leaves the players to negotiate as to who has priority over a particular intersection. Polaris splits the authority along the lines of rules, antagonists and bystanders. The Mythic GM Emulator splits authority between the programmer and the person interpreting its results into something appropriate to play.

      Because we are constructing stories (a cultural practice), someone has to control the world, the context that the story takes place in. Even monologues may be understood as an example of a GM/player combination, although I admit that pushes the metaphor to its limit. What I'm interested in figuring out now is "why do certain games take different approaches?" (see my previous posting).

I think a GM in the traditional sense encompasses one or more of these things:

      (If this seems like a new-thread thing, let me know.)

      Arbiter - decides when the narrative pauses and a rule needs to be invoked and makes calls
      Antagonist - opposes a players actions, interests, or progress for the sake of furthering the story or giving them a challenge
      Storyteller - moves the plot of the story foward
      Facilitator - explains (and enforces?) the rules of the game
      Gatekeeper - retains secret knowledge to be divulged at certain points or, perhaps, explicitly never to be divulged (e.g. the names of the moves in Apocalypse World)
      Worldbuilder - describes the world that the story takes place in

      I'm optionally considering including these:
      Delegator - assigns roles to others in the group, either temporarily or for the duration of the game (e.g. it's possibly to GM Lady Blackbird by flipping all questions back on the asker and making them answer it)
      Tonesetter - sets the mood for the story via description, names, gestures, etc.

      I've been reconsidering my definitions of GM in light of all that has been said in this thread. A GMless roleplaying game doesn't exist. Even D&D 4e has a GM, the game designer. If played that way, the GM can't make the sort of decisions that someone present could (e.g what happens when the adventurers throw ropes over the dragon), but the players may step in and make such decisions on the designers' behalf.

      Different games split the authority in different ways. Do doesn't split the authority very much, and players are left to negotiate what happens on their own. Shock splits the authority (amongst other ways) along the lines of issues and shocks, but leaves the players to negotiate as to who has priority over a particular intersection. Polaris splits the authority along the lines of rules, antagonists and bystanders. The Mythic GM Emulator splits authority between the programmer and the person interpreting its results into something appropriate to play.

      Because we are constructing stories (a cultural practice), someone has to control the world, the context that the story takes place in. Even monologues may be understood as an example of a GM/player combination, although I admit that pushes the metaphor to its limit. What I'm interested in figuring out now is "why do certain games take different approaches?" (see my previous posting).
