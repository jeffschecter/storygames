Open Ability Lists and Awesomeness

Judson_Lester
March 2006 edited March 2006 in Story Games
So, pick your favorite system with a rule like "pick three Abilities, could be anything." and look at how they handle an Ability like *Awesomeness,* defined as being applicable in any situation when my character is trying to succeed.

I can think of two typical ways this is handled: GM veto (specifically from _Over the Edge,_ and the Trait was called "Zen Mastery"), or subjective point-cost multipliers. I think the _Fuzion_ system does something like this. So each level of the Abilities costs a certain number of points that depend on how frequently it would apply, so "universal" costs too much to be feasible.

What I'd like to see is some kind of self-balancing mechanic, so that any ability works, because you feel it's cost automatically - in essence the scope is it's own burden. I think there's something like this in the _Dogs_ rules, but I can't put my finger on it; but I think that's an essential part of acheiving what I'm looking for.

If there's a hard mechanic for the cost of scope, it'd get too heavy almost immediately. But if there was some subtle emergent property, then the scope balances itself.

It's even possible that the mechanic is radically different per game. I could see a slapstick game where you want to fail at least as often as you succeed, so if your traits are overbroad, you wide up being too able.

Anyway, I'm hoping someone has ideas on this, or even that I'm overlooking something simple, or that I'm ignorant of a really awesome example of what I'm thinking of.

    Right. Note that I said "no mechanical downside". Player-defined traits can work in the same way that freeform play works -- they can be managed by the group's social expectations. That's workable, but it puts an extra burden on the players and makes the game more difficult to get started in.
    Having a trait usage cost you some resource could also work, though once again broad traits are slightly better than narrow traits: you have more options by being able to apply it or choose not too in every conflict, not just a few.

    Perhaps you could have some other drawback for excessive use?
    Both a point cost per use and drawbacks from excessive use narrow the gap considerably between broad traits and narrow traits. If a trait can only be used once in a while, then there is much less penalty to a player who chooses a narrow traits which describes some cool bit about his character. A simple mechanic for this might be to have a separate pile of points on each trait which are refreshed each session. So a rarely-invoked trait will have a pile of points to use, so when it is used it can be powerful. Whereas a frequently used trait will quickly run out of points.

    Still, as long as the trait is always invoked by player choice, then a broad trait will always be better than a narrow one. Because a broad trait gives more flexibility to the player, since the player can always choose not to invoke it. I think the only way around this is for other participants to be able to invoke a player's trait in some way when it applies.

    In an earlier version of Elsewhere, which was actually my own Heartbreaker, I had a system for skills that was (essentially) open-ended. It depended on qualifiers for the skill in order to make it cheaper to buy. In other words, if you had the skill Lore, it would cost 10 character-points to buy a single level in it. It would, however, carry across any subject. If you purchased Elven Lore, that was one qualifier, and only cost 5 points to purchase one + of. Wood Elven Lore only cost 2 points, and Wood Elven Spellcraft Lore only cost 1 point per +.

    So you could buy +10 in Wood Elven Spellcraft Lore for the same as +1 Lore. The sweet spot was two qualifiers, where applicablity and specificity balanced out (in play).

    I think I got some interesting things out of it. I don't know if a general solution exists, but three categories are coming to light:

    * Dogs-style: the name of an ability is color, and broad abilities are weak color. There's not much to keep any ability from being used in any circumstance.
    * Capes-style: some kind of cost for using an ability. IIRC, Capes Debt is per ability, so you're encouraged to spread the love around - if not that would probably be a refinement worth examining.
    * Elsewhere-style: where the breadth of an ability deterministically controls its cost - although I wonder about gaming that. ( **Superb, Utter Irrepressible Awesomeness** - 1 point per level)

    I think each has it's own place, and I wouldn't suggest that the list is exhaustive. But as a RPG Pattern, I think that there's a lot to be said in terms of Applicability, Consequences, and Considerations for No List Abilities.

    Open ability systems all seem to be in story-creation games.
