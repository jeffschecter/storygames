    \n Hi-thanks for the question! The intent is not to say trad = bad at all. It's more to say, here are all these great tools, we can let go of *all* our preconceived notions about how rules work and what a game has to look like. The reference to 25+ years is not a veiled swipe at D&D or any other system, it's simply stating that we've been doing this for a good long time now. (Though of course, compared to other narrative forms, the direct progression of our hobby is still fairly short.)

    Growing up is something we're asking us all to do. And yeah, that is a pretty confrontational way to start the document off. But we're also asking all of us to go back to our (perhaps) common roots of pretend that we did *as children* to find new ways to do what we love.

    A follow-up question: what is the strong statement and firm position? Is it that rules-based play should die out?
    \nNope. For me it is that just as once the GM was seen as a necessary component to rpgs, but now we understand different ways to do the same thing. So too do we (I would say) see a solid, unchanging set of rules as a requirement to play, and don't necessarily pay attention to the connections and communications between the players as a fundamental part of what makes it all work. The game questions that, and lays out some ideas on another approach. One that borrow liberally from acting, tabletop rp (of all types), and various live play traditions (freeform and larp).

    GMless games haven't killed GM'd games. Killing the rules in your play doesn't detract from their utility in other games. But both things question the assumption of their necessity.

    \n I've read through the document now, and one thing that strikes me when I read things like "Be in it!", "Challenge!" and "No planning!" is how similar this is to theatre sport*. Especially when I read stuff like \xbbYou\u2019ll be trying out different rules, techniques, ways of play as you go along. Maybe, at some point in play, a player says: \u201cI\u2019d like to turn out the lights and play out this scene in total darkness.\u201d\xab

    Another thing, and it was something that lacked in the Jeepform dictionary as well, is that you should describe when you should use a certain technique. The examples isn't enough - examples works best as clarification, not as explanations. Here is one example from the document of what I mean:\n
    Spew questions like bullets from a machine gun
    Rapid fire interrogation of players by other players to narrate action. Increases tension. Telescopes time.
    \nWhat I emphasised in bold is enough to make the reader understand both why and when this technique should be used.

    *Search for Whose Line Is It Anyways on Youtube, if you don't know what theatre sport is.

    \n What does it tickle?

    It makes me want to go back and revisit a LARPy one shot idea I had about a vampire vintner, his products, and his customers called Distressing the Vein. It's supposed to be creepy, and a bit sad, with the customers slowly tasting the complexity of a mortal's life, played out through vignettes as the peson moves from a state of being idealistic and artistic and is slowly worn down by time and circumstance ( all engineered by the vintner) until a fine drink is produced.

    It gives me ideas for tabletop extravaganza games with an emphasis in miniatures that aren't standard toy soldier battle games. Pretty much the bulk of my old [minis+] threads are touching on that idea one way or another, and Play with intent gives me some ideas on how to do that, and also some ideas on warm ups and tools to help with that, including ways to run them that are more scene based. Off hand?

    Welcome Back, Drac: A comedy piece set in Transylvania. Dracula returns home and everything is a mess. he just wants some peace and quiet, but the romantic hijinks of the vllagers and the schemes of new monsters and humans in the neighborhood throw all of his plans for quiet retirement into disarray.

    Race for the Goblin Throne: Played over the course of a year of scale time and over about three hours of real time. The Election Year Campaigning is on. Party politics, single issue voters, undecided zombie hordes, political dirty tricks, coalition building, poltical paybacks, surprise invasions by humans, and backroom deals all go into deciding who will occupy the Big Chair for the next century. Will it be those Hollywood Monsters and their human loving (ish) ways, or will the discredited GOOP ( Great Old Ones Party) regain the seat now that the stars finally seem to be right? And what about the down ticket races?

    In Another October: Based on Roger Zelazny's A Night in the Lonesome October, but with a changed setting. Eccentric figures from history and literature come to a small town with their animal companions. Some are Openers, some are Closers, and no one knows who is who. There are some surprise, unaligned, ringers in there too. Partnerships are made and everyone is seeking the tools and location info that will let their side win this time, either keeping the world as it is or changing it forever.

    The Occupation: The Germans have occupied your home. Do you collaborate or resist? What about those around you? Who will look out for themselves first and foremost? If you resist, how do you do it? What about the outside influences who use you as pawns, or the infighting between factions? And what about the occupiers? Could turn out a tragedy, could turn out like 'Allo, 'Allo.

    That kinda stuff.

    \n Low key in the sense that I wanted it to be played more quietly and in a small space. And a bit creepy and with a feeling of weirdness in the clients. Part of the idea is for the session to funnel in on the story of the eventually chosen victim/vintage, and people would use their own experiences with disappointments and obstructions in their own lives to inform the fiction. Hopefully done right, the vignettes develop the story of the vintage, while the personalities of the vampires blur in and out, and people switch between roles.

    Another part of the concept is that the vampires themselves choose the particular vintage that develops through play because they too have a parallel story in their background.
