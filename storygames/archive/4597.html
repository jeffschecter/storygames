\n\nOctober 2007 \n edited October 2007 in Story Games

\n
\n
\n So, Call of Cthulhu is my favorite all-time game.

I didn't even know it was broken until you bastards told me. Then when my face was rubbed in it, I couldn't help but admit it.

The game stalls if the players blow their investigative rolls and fail to get the clues. Then you have to resort to dice tricks or illusionism to get the game going again. That makes me sad.

Now, after a generous research grant and the loss of several lab assistants, here's what I've come up with: When the player blows an investigation roll, the GM still gives him the clue, but he puts a doom token in the bowl. Later when the player succeeds at a roll that the GM thinks would be more interesting/scary if he failed, the GM announces that the player failed and takes the doom token out of the bowl. Note that the character still has to go to the newspaper office to get the newspaper clipping clue.

Is that too jarring? Is it too much to accept that the results of the earlier roll can influence a later, unrelated roll, or can it work for you in some sort of karmic-balance thing where a vital success is balanced out by an unfortunate failure? I still want to make sure the player has a reason to put points into investigative skills, something he wouldn't be as inclined to do if he knew he would get all the clues in any case.

I wouldn't use this to kill a player and probably wouldn't cancel out a critical, but would instead try to use it as another tool to ramp up the tension. I imagine a bowl full of doom tokens would be a scary thing.

I'm only pretending that I thought this up--role-playing, if you will. I'm sure I'm mostly just lifting it from other places--Roanoke comes to mind. I may have even mentioned the idea before, but my memory isn't the same since Jason Morningstar showed me how to sniff glue.

So, for those of you who enjoy investigative-type games, do you think this would work?

    \n I think Daniel's idea is pretty interesting. I can sort of see a version where it's not obvious which rolls have consequences for failure (but it's all the rolls that move the plot forward if you succeed) but that each time you fail one the plot token still gets handed out, but some kind of Doom accumulates so that when things do go bad, they go worse in proportion to how much Doom you have accumulated.

    I have a couple of caveats. One is that in my experience, many horror players are there not for the fun of making a Lovecraftian story, but for the creepy thrill of experiencing it. As such, they often place a high value on their immersion, and are highly resistant to any mechanics that interfere with it. Resource management can be particularly problematic. The other is the obvious objection: if the PCs need to find the newspaper clue and are in the newspaper office searching for it, why on earth are we making them roll for it in the first place?

    \n have folks seen the "Gumshoe" rules approach to clues ~ it seems to be pretty much in line with "you find them, that's the story we're telling here, but your skills/player resources determine what value you get from them over and above finding the bare bones". That is very much like a "here are the clues, make your skill checks to learn more" approach.

    That approach can be used for any investigate style story if players buy into "this is the story where we uncover the dark truth, all that's in doubt is if we uncover the deeper layers of it, rescue those to be rescued and/or die or not".

    In this sense, clues as connectors between scenes become like "doors" in a old style dungeon. You always find them, the issue is how long to locate them (when a clock is ticking), which/what order to follow them, do you get through them "quickly" (surprising what's waiting) or "slowly" (not surprising) or only by using up resources (spells), and whether they are "trapped" (hazardous to negotiate). The secret door is a short cut, you can still get to what it conceals by a "long way around" or it is to something "optional".

    The important part of the metaphor for me is that there are, in good dungeons, choices of doors not just a linear line of them, and some means of making choices about the doors. A "clue based" adventure that is A leads to B leads to C is like a set of three rooms on a straight line in a dungeon - minimal player choice (GM wrote the plot).

    \n I don't know about the details of the Gumshoe system (it's in Esoterrorists, and they're working on a Call of C'thulhu version), though continuing the door metaphor I think you can easily present just like "fake doors that are trapped" and "doors leading to more pain than gain" and "the most efficient doors", a variety of found clues can allow skill rolls or other sytem mechanics can "winnow out" etc.

    Just as a good old style dungeon was its own milieu so that a character/player might recognize "this door was built by the Uvorak, oh they mummified their dead and liked mechanical traps, and this door has a scribble beside it that looks like the mark of the famous Rogue Rophan who retired in glory and never left anything valuable in his wake wherever he walked" and thus learn some things about what to explore. The "red herring" is the equivalent of the faked door with a trap. Players usually manufacture their own "clues" and theories fast and furious as well.

    Applying the metaphor again, I think in using the "red herring" one has to be careful not to lead to a large wasted investment. While realistic in a simulationist sense it's boring to play out. The nice thing about the fake trapped door is that "BANG" there's the pain for making bad choice, botching relevant skill checks, but it's done and now we're back to useful choices. I think the red herring needs to be resolved similarly, or else have other relevant PC goals embedded in it (e.g., if the game includes both other and inner conflicts, if there are character flags in use, the red herring doesn't get you closer to stopping the cultists summoning whatever in the outer conflict, but it does hit some character flags in the inner conflict). Otherwise just flash forward the way a serial killer drama does, we skip to finding next body and "guess that theory was wrong" (rather like the fate of Mr. Smithers above).

    \n Most botched non-combat skill rolls do nothing more than waste time.

    As for multiple approaches, frankly that should be something the players are doing, not you. Just like knocking open a possibly trapped chest with a hammer or sawing through it are valid tactics in old-skool D&D, various forms of described examination of evidence and locations and lateral thinking (looking for other witnesses, using bribery/blackmail/fasttalk/intimidation as necessary are valid tactics in CoC.

    Hell, going back and confronting a witness/suspect with new evidence or theories or re-examining evidence based on new stuff are valid approaches as well. It's sort of like the "Let it ride" concept in reverse.

    As for cheats? Illusionism?

    Personally, I don't worry about it. Seriously. My approach is that players come into a CoC scenario knowing that the main thrust of the game is to gather as much info as possible to arm themselves for a final confrontation of some sort. They are almost certainly working under some sort of time constraint, as well a some sorts of societal constraints generally. The players will need to use their brains in a very real way, and their characters' stats/skills will aid them in this.

    Generally, resources will be used up along the way, possbily including the lives of a few of the investigators and/or their grip on reality. If they really botch things, it may all turn out more horrible than usual and they should certainly feel they've "lost" the game.

    OTOH, IMO if the GM creates the scenario in such a way that the players are completely stymied or can't put the pieces together, or really get massacred too quickly, the GM should consider themselves to have "lost" the game.

    \n One use I have as a GM for failed player rolls is that I use them as a license to throw situations at the PCs. Fail the spot hidden; OK sure, you've spotted the photo on the wall, but you haven't spotted the guy hiding behind the door who now gets a free go at hitting you on the head.
