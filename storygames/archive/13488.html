\n So I think Apocalypse World is a good game. A very, very good game -- not only as its own thing, but as a model of easy hackability. However, I've played a lot of it now, amongst two groups and two different sets of players, and several items are jumping out at me as bothersome. I thought I'd share those here, in (hopefully) the same spirit as some other deep-read/deep-play analyses I've posted in the past. Please fight the urge to circle the wagons.

1) "Take a move from any playbook" is a dicey proposition. Take the Skinner, specifically "An Arresting Skinner." Personally I think the move is busted, because there's no roll involved (therefore it is a conflict-ender, not a potential conflict-extender) and is fine-tuneable by the player to hit only specific characters in a scene. If it were just the Skinner's move I wouldn't mind so much, because playing a Skinner comes with its own challenges. However, anyone can take that move. Our most recent exploit of this has been the Battlebabe, who is pure +cool -- a problem when the only counterbalance I can think of is Act Under Fire, and that girl is built to act under fire almost exclusively. So anyway, I'm not sure if this is just a problem with "An Arresting Skinner" or more broadly with the "take a move from any playbook" advance. If it were up to me, I'd *at the very least* require an Act Under Fire of the move itself, so at least when it's used by the Skinner herself there's a chance the move will result in further conflict.

2) Optional battle moves don't work as advertised. I get why they exist -- they give the players a chance to set up the fight a little bit before the GM starts hitting them with harm -- but like "lay down cover fire" doesn't really do anything but potentially hurt the players.

3) Speaking of hurting more than helping, the help/interfere rules also do more harm than good for the most part, at least 'til you're talking Hx+2 or so. Anything less than that and all you're doing is giving the MC more hard moves. It's becoming work to not just escalate a conflict to hard moves, but goddamn! It's fairly easy/common for the players to hand me 3 moves before a conflict even begins in earnest. That can't be the intent of the rule. Right now, failing at help/interfere feels like a conflict of interest.

4) Seize By Force is problematic for reasons already discussed elsewhere in this forum. It might be as simple as clarifying "take definite hold" in context with combat: If I've taken definite hold of "your life", then why is "do more harm" (or whatever the wording is) even an option? Is it just to create a "do you kill him or fuck him up really bad" choice? This again seems like a conflict of interest with the "view NPCs through crosshairs" aesthetic of the game.

5) After maybe ... 15ish sessions total now, I'm feeling kind of a grinding creative exhaustion from running AW. The Fronts were fun to put together, albeit quite a bit of homework for a nu-skool game, but even they don't really provide that much in-play guidance to me any more. I've been thinking about this phenomenon quite a lot. It might be the constrained list of MC moves. Initially it was a fun creative challenge, but now I know the moves and the challenge isn't that much fun any more. I have no idea if anyone else has experienced this, and if so what your theories on this might be. My other theory is that it feels like ... despite the Fronts and despite the pc-npc-pc triangles, the vast majority of adversity is presented by the MC. That's a lot of work. Mouse Guard, IMO an extremely similar game to GM, has a similar issue but at least there's an explicit teamwork structure to the setup, and the characters' Beliefs stand out as super-useful flags.

Well, there's my list of grievances issues. So far my recipe of updates/changes/recommendations is:

* Consider marking some moves as hardcoded to a playbook, and not take-able by other books.

* Build an Act Under Fire roll into An Arresting Skinner move, and/or otherwise introduce some element of uncertainty to the move.

* Ditch or radically rethink the optional battle moves. I'd start by thinking through how you'd want to zoom in on battle: is the point to give the players more tactical options? To give the MC more opportunities to make hard moves?

* Perhaps formally eliminate the normal consequence of failure (GM makes a move) from the help/interfere rules, and keep it intact otherwise (I do really like the 7-9 result)

* Restrict Seize by Force to literal seizings of literal, actual things. And/or clarify what "take definite hold" means in a fight. And/or rethink the "take 1/take 3" list.

* Maybe add some sort of "what do you want?" type flagging element to characters. Dunno what that'd be. Mmmaybe add a couple questions to the character creation bit, stuff other than how the character looks. I'm not at all persuaded that the moves are flags at all.

    As for Arresting Skinner...this is a tough one. I've only been on the using end of the move, and not on the receiving end, so of course I think it's amazingly awesome. What I would say in defense of the move:
    - It's temporary; unless you've got help from others, your enemies are only frozen for as long as you're stripping. If you're trying to do something like escape or pull a weapon while stripping, that could certainly be an Act Under Fire roll. Otherwise, the Skinner is seemingly as incapacitated as the people watching.
    - It's even more temporary because you need to be taking off clothing. There will come a point at which you do not have clothing, and will need to start taking it off others, or stop. You could feasibly say that at that point, it's Acting Under Fire to get over to someone else and start taking off their clothing before the angry gang members with big guns wake up.
    - You can get around it by either getting the Skinner in a place where he/she doesn't have any clothes, or in a place where he/she doesn't have any help. So, the move breeds some conflict because the Skinner needs friends to use it effectively, and in AW, in my experience, the mere act of having a friend is highly problematic.

    (2) Haven't seen these in play, can't comment.

    (3) The games I play, we haven't used Hx that much, so I can't really comment here, either. Maybe your group just has particularly low Hx? It would seem that after a bit, because people are giving each other +1 Hx at the end of every session, Hx between at least some players would get to the point of consistent efficacy. Especially if they're shooting each other.

    (4) Yeah, don't want to get much into this debate either, but I see the "take definite hold of your life" option as being similar to the Go Aggro thing of, "I want you to die, or you can suck up the hit from my gun." So, you die, or you die. Taking definite hold of someone's life, then, is letting you kill them, but it is by no means the only use of that term, the same way that Go Aggro has lots of other uses besides "I want you to die."

    (5) Haven't really been an MC, so I don't have much to say here, either, except that as a player, I can tell you that inter-character conflict can certainly erupt. In one game, we're pretty much a unified front against the evil NPC hardholder, but in another game, I've got one character on the run because some schmuck possessed him and made him commit a bunch of murders, and the hardholder (PC) believes that my guy did it. So, that PC-PC conflict is definitely in there, nicely. But then, maybe that still doesn't relieve the burden from the MC significantly.

    \n Paul, this is a really interesting post. Thanks for starting this conversation.

    1) In my experience, playbook moves aren't balanced against each other and are not supposed to be. Moves like "An Arresting Skinner" are intentionally exceptions to how players normally do things; that's part of what Vincent was intending, I think, with his "exception-based design" approach. Honestly, if players are looking for ways to chain moves together to get lethal combos, there are much better ways to do it. Brandon, I think, had a character who could instantly deal 6+ harm AP by the end of the first campaign I played in. Worrying about that, in my mind, is kinda missing the point. The rules empower the players to be (in the normal sense of the words) hard, hot, sharp, cool, and weird badasses and there's not a GM principle that says "make sure the characters face challenges" or anything like that. If you feel like the players being too badass is breaking your game, I'm not sure what to tell you, since that's partially what the game is about, yeah? When I have had trouble as a GM -- or have watched others have trouble -- it's often because we're trying to challenge the characters (we have a fictional agenda of some type) rather than "playing to find out what happens."

    2-3) In games I've been a part of, the battles moves are only triggered when you're in a battle, which means things have already gone to hell. Players never (or rarely) intentionally choose to use supplemental moves unless they've stumbled into the very specific situation when they apply, which means they hardly ever get used. Plus, typically being proactive is way better for you than Stay The Fuck Down or whatever. On the other hand, we've had great experiences with helping and interfering in our games. Frequently, we allow people to roll to help (though usually not to interfere) after the initial roll has been made, when we know that a +1 or +2 will enable it to move up to the next level of success. It's possible that this cuts down on the instances where people help "unnecessarily," but it also sometimes pulls everybody together in failure if a roll fails and then rolls to assist also fail or expose people. That works nicely, I think, so if you're having trouble with helping, maybe you should consider rolling after the initial roll instead of before.

    4) John has recently decided to treat Seize By Force as a peripheral move, because people tend to overuse it for things it's not intended for. I haven't yet played in a game that treated it this way, but I may lean towards light use of it when I run it in the future, based on other people's experiences.

    5) Our campaign exhaustion has varied a bit. Generally speaking, though, players seem to switch to new characters soon after they've opened up the advanced versions all their moves and made a couple of 12+ rolls. At that point, they've found some scrap of beauty or truth in the world, generally (often through use of the advanced moves), and it seems a good place to move on and go back to the muck. Often, AW characters seem to not really know what to do with themselves once they have found some escape from the muck of the world, so it's a good time to retire them or end the campaign. That said, it seems really up to the GM to be able to maintain interest in developing the threats that are still in play. If the players have taken down or exhausted a couple of threats and you're not really interested in the other ones anymore, maybe it's time to quit or take a break for a while, yeah? Burnout happens to everybody eventually and especially in AW, where so much is based on interesting things happening in the fiction, if you're not interested in the fiction right now, you probably should step away from the game, at least for a while.

For a while, though, this slippage may not be obvious, especially to the players and especially if the GM is mostly challenging beliefs and only sometimes slipping into challenging their abilities.       \n Christopher, I both agree and disagree with you. Let me see if I can explain why.

      I agree that as written, devoid of the baggage we as people bring into it, the Apocalypse World mechanics don't have most of the issues I've described above (like rolling +harm taken). And when I say baggage, I don't mean baggage from playing other games, just baggage from being human.

      If you were to take the basic AW system and make it into a computer simulation devoid of human input, not all of AW would work (since it relies heavily on human input) but many of these issues wouldn't be issues at all. The computer would never forget to roll+harm taken. It's codified in the rules, in the system, in the program. So when the event is triggered, the code is executed perfectly.

      But the game is made to be played by people, not by machines. So it's not enough to include rules. They have to be communicated in specific ways and then reinforced so that the average (or intended) person will remember to use them.

      For example, when you gain 5 XP, you gain an advancement. Technically, you only need 4 XP circles to track your XP. When you gain a 5th XP, you erase all your circles and gain an advancement. With a computer that would work fine. But it caused issues with people. For some people, it made more sense and was easier to use if there were 5 XP circles instead of 4. Even though when you fill in the 5th circle, you then immediately just erase it with the rest of the circles. It's technically a waste of pencil lead and eraser. The mechanic is the same, but something possibly inefficient was needed to make it easier for people to use.

      I've done professional usability testing for huge companies and probably game with at least 100+ different people (experienced gamers and new people) every year since I organize gaming events. I get the awesome opportunity to observe a lot of people playing games and to talk in detail with them afterward to better understand what was going on. One thing I've noticed is that experienced gamers, gamers with baggage, gamers who've read the Apocalypse World book twice, and people who have little to no game experience forget to roll+harm. I don't think roll+harm is broken as a mechanic. And I agree that the issue is likely with people. But the game is designed for people so certain changes may need to be made to deal with the baggage we have, as people, not just as gamers.

      I have a pretty substantial list, many psychological, of why I believe people consistently forget to roll+harm (other people have even mentioned it on the official AW forums, so there is 100% a pattern). I've even talked to people who say they do use it, and then when I've talked to their players or observed the games, they didn't. It's a tricky thing full of subtleties and in the middle of play, it easy to miss what's going on unless you have outside observers. That said, I'm sure plenty of people don't have these problems. But that doesn't mean other people don't. And that there isn't a pattern explaining why it affects so many people.

      Well, I don't have a gold standard for what makes a game "great" for me. I think every great game is great in its own way. My list of "great" games is also pretty short. But here are some off-the-hip thoughts of good vs great games (all IMO and IME, so for fuck's sake don't come along demanding I justify my Os and Es)...

      Good games:
      3:16 -- pretty darned good because it plays fast, it does the military vibe pretty well, it's easy as heck to teach. But it's not great because its endgame gets blurry due to lack of clarity on how the "I win/end this conflict" memory effects work as conflicts move from killin' bugs to scrabbling for ranks. There are also a couple poorly worded rules elsewhere but...it's been a while. Anyway, the game is quite good for a few sessions, then starts to spin apart. This happened three games in a row with three different groups.

      Agon -- again, really a good game. Clever system, hackable. However, if the players don't understand/appreciate the one-ups-manship baked into the system itself, the system will make them batshit. Fun for a few sessions, largely due to the neat range-band-and-lego-people combat system, but for us the game just kind of ran out of steam -- the premise of throwing your companions under the bus in the quest for glory is probably good for one short-ish campaign. Characters also felt a little static and flat.

      Reign -- mostly pretty good, a nice fusion of trad and useful nu-skool ideas. The Company rules don't actually do anything, and that's a disappointment given the centrality of those rules to the game's premise. There are some organizational problems as well, especially once you start using the later releases, and that made it a huge pain in the ass to run as time went on.


      Great games:
      Burning Wheel -- I feel like this one is great both despite and because of its warts. It is perhaps the most flawed game I have ever played, and yet the things it does well it does extremely well: Supports extended play (although it, too, will fall apart after a *really* long time), clear character arcs that transform the protagonists (and not only in the bildungsroman way), gears-within-gears mechanical interactions that generate ongoing situation, and constant conflict and complications that drive the game along. Mouse Guard gets an honorable mention, particularly for its various GM-facilitating tools, but really it's under the BW aegis. Ditto BE, perhaps even more deeply flawed than BW but man I've gotten a lot of good play out of it.

      InSpectres -- simple, easy to teach, tons of prompts for the GM to take and run with, and the Confessional mechanic is very clever. I feel like the Psychic Maelstrom has a lot in common with the Confessional, actually. It's not a long-play game IME but then again it doesn't pretend to be. Super-easy hook that mostly lets the game run on cliches. I can run 1 or 3 sessions of InSpectres and feel like it was time well spent. A great, little game.

      Dust Devils -- for whatever reason, I find DD very easy to run. I think it's easy to leverage a well-written Devil into interesting situations. I like the genre and think the game addresses it well. I like the flags and implied history on the character sheet. Despite some problems we've had with the "final scene" mechanic, I've run this maybe a dozen times now and it's been fun every time. An elegant little game, perfect for a 3-6 session run.

      Posted By: ChristopherI'd be interested in the list you mentioned, Jenskot. The one about why people don't roll harm. At least we might get something constructive out of this.
      \nI don't think there is one reason, but any of the following may contribute to it:

      - The Suffer Harm move works different than every other move in the game. A hit is bad for the player and a miss is good.

      - But a miss being good isn't really that good depending on the situation. Usually it means taking 1 less Harm for something else bad happening to you. It's trading 1 bad thing for another. With the main benefit being that you choose which bad thing.

      - The Suffer Harm move sometimes means being kicked while you are down. You often just failed another move, took Harm, and now even more bad things are about to happen to you. Except unlike rolling for other moves, there is no compelling upside.

      - Apocalypse World can be brutal but feels softened because the players roll everything. Being hurt is due to something you rolled and failed to accomplish. So when the MC plays a hard move against you, it doesn't feel as bad as if the MC actually rolled moves against you. But the MC reminding you to roll Suffer Harm, feels more like the MC doing something to you. I've seen many MCs feel bad when they remind players to roll Suffer Harm.

      - It's a Peripheral Move.

      - You don't gain XP for rolling Suffer Harm.

      - I've seen quite a few people purposefully "forget" to roll Suffer Harm. Psychologically it's easier to justify "cheating" when you can do so by omission instead or having to proactively take a specific action. It doesn't feel like cheating.

      - There aren't built in reminders to roll Suffer Harm. You can forget to roll it and the game moves forward as if nothing was forgotten. Nothing obviously breaks by forgetting to roll it.

      I'm sure there is way more that I'm forgetting!

      And for some people, the above might be the reason, for others it could be something completely different. But just like the 5 vs 4 XP circles, certain subtle changes may be needed to account for the human side of the equation.

      \n I just wrote these examples of turning MC questions and player answers into threats and triangles (for a conversation over email), and I figured they might be of use to someone here, too.

      ------------------

      MC: "So, who do you get your ammo from?"
      Duke: "Hmmm. This old woman named Frey. She has a huge stockpile squirreled away and doles it out to the hunters and warriors that she deems worthy."

      So, there you go. An NPC (who's automatically a threat) who's forming the first part of a triangle. For the first session, I'd make a note to wonder about what front Frey falls under (probably the Home Front, but you never know). When asking questions of the other PCs, I might ask about Frey and see if I can get the other part of the triangle in place.

      That's kind of a best-case answer. You can do it this way, too, if the player doesn't have something awesome:

      MC: "So, who do you get your ammo from?"
      Duke: "Hmmm. I don't know... someone in the holding, I think."
      MC: "What about the wise woman we talked about? Maybe she has all the valuable stuff like ammo and she doles it out to those she deems worthy?"
      Duke: "Yeah, that's cool."
      MC: "Okay. She needs a name. I'm gonna call her... Frey. What's your relationship like?"
      Duke: "Oh, she loves me. I'm the best warrior in the holding, after all."
      MC: "Cool."

      Here's another one:

      MC: "So, who do you get your ammo from?"
      Duke: "I scrounge for it myself."
      MC: "Ah, okay. Where? Is there a ruined city or something nearby? Maybe over here [pointing at the map]."
      Duke: "I was thinking there was maybe an abandoned military ship all covered over in ice and snow."
      MC: "Awesome."

      No NPC that time, but you got an awesome landscape threat out of it. And maybe start wondering about who else knows about the ship, and if anyone lives there, etc. Those can be follow-up questions or material to explore in play.

      And again:

      MC: "So, who do you get your ammo from?"
      Duke: "I scrounge for it myself."
      MC: "Ah, okay. The holding has been attacked several times by those raiders Bell (another PC) was talking about. Maybe you just pick it off the dead?"
      Duke: "Yeah, sounds good."

      Now you have a triangle, a threat that touches two PCs. Bell wants to stop the raids, and Duke maybe does, too, but if the raiders suddenly vanish, his source of ammo goes away.

      And you can do that with any question. Either you discover an NPC or other threat, or you learn what they're in control of and therefore what stuff others might want to take from them or trade for. Like, if Duke had said the ammo stockpile was his and he decided who was worthy, that's gold too. You can immediately imagine some people who don't like that situation, others who support him, etc. Triangles and threats.

      The easiest triangle method is to simply flip the relationship 180 degrees to finish the connection. Duke says Frey loves him, so you go, okay, she hates Bell. Bell says he hates the raiders, you go, okay, one of the raiders is Duke's sister who was lost in a snowstorm when she was a child. That's super simplistic, but it works great in play.
