\n I was reading the comments on a joystiq.com article, when this caught my eye:

"Somewhat offtopic from the rest of the article, but I want to respond to your opening sentence:

'I find it extremely interesting that the most poignant and memorable lines spoken in the original [Star Wars] trilogy are not said by the primary protagonist, Luke Skywalker.'

There's a damned good reason for this. Luke is what as a novel writer I'd call a 'reader proxy' (I'd guess screenwriters use a term like 'viewer proxy' or similar). The basic idea is that you make the character just interesting enough to hold the reader's attention, but simple enough that the reader can easily get behind his skull. He has to know little or nothing about the events that are happening (they'll all be revealed to him by other characters, so he learns about his world at the same time as the reader), and strong opinions on any subject are likely to put off a significant fraction of readers, so you'd usually avoid them. The end result is that when you watch SW, you can visualise *being* Luke much more easily than you can any other character, which involves you in the story and invests you in his stakes much more than riding on the back of somebody harder to sympathise with. Taken together, this means that pretty much by necessity, all the other characters end up having the memorable opinions and revelations to impart."

Has this technique ever translated into roleplaying? Where players deliberately each make a simple protagonist character to root for, but then maybe players also make other non-protagonist NPC characters that reach a bit deeper into theme and premise? Possibly the real meat of the story is told through the NPCs, while the protagonists are not much more than pretty minecarts.

Seems like this happens all the time in video games.

    \n I think that while some elements of the "proxy" character might translate well to roleplaying (for example, the character learning about the world as the players do) might translate well, generally speaking you don't need such a character seeing as your character is directly controlled and made by you rather than in a movie where you can't control the character and you didn't get to pick them either.

    In video games you (sometimes) get to make the character, and you can control them, but only to a limited extent so, I think it still applies.

    In a roleplaying game though, if you can't identify with your character, that's really your problem (or something you did on purpose) since you made the character. No need for a generic "everyman" when you can customize everything to your specifications, you know? At least that's how I see it.

    \n I was thinking more along the lines of a character's ideas and headspace being simple enough that a player doesn't have to feel like they need to analyze their character's position/philosophy/whatever when making decisions with the character. I mean, I guess some people automatically make characters like this anyway.

    I guess the part I'm more intrigued by is the part where a writer is willing to make thematic or philosophical points through other characters that the "viewer" (read: player) is relating to in a more externalized way. I'm wondering if there are things that players or even GMs can do with NPCs that can't be done well with PCs for this same reason.

    Anyway, this whole comment could have been bullshit. I'm not a writer, so I wouldn't have a very informed opinion on that
