\n I've played A Taste for Murder a few times, and really enjoyed it (once I understood the intended play style). It's easy to learn, easy to play, and can get surprisingly deep. A great game for playing after dinner with good friends, for instance!

But I haven't seen many AP reports here, I think.

So how about you? Have you tried it?

But I guess that's what Gumshoe games are for? We had a lot of fun, anyway.

    \n Yes, it's one I actually bought and played (being a murder mystery lover) and it was a blast. Typical family: proper but dotty wealthy industrialist father (Sir Arthur Geoffrey Wellington), gad-about son and his artist (! "Good God man, she's an artist? Where has your sensibility fled to?") fiancee, dour daughter, second wife for the old man (younger ex-movie starlet), family barrister, and recently returned brother (of the old man). Dad got the knife in the back in his study and the family skeletons came out of the closet (affairs, lechery, financial failure and a missing book of prized stamps) during the investigation. Great fun and it turned out that the son's fiancee was the murderess ... nasty little piece she turned out to be.
    \n
    \nRecommended and adaptable from the 30s to the 50s Britain.

    \n I played once. It was a fun game and created a nicely twisted story, but we were a little confused about how the second half was meant to go. We had been playing for a few hours, and we wanted the detective to solve the mystery so that we could bring the game to a close (since it was inevitable). We got very frustrated when people kept managing to beat the detective, dragging the game on and on, so we eventually instituted a "detective always wins rolls" rule and finished the game.

    As for actual play, the seemingly-responsible heir killed the butler who was blackmailing him in preparation for running off to America with his Jewish gay lover (I believe the phrase "my Hebrew Adonis" was used). Polite society was scandalized.
