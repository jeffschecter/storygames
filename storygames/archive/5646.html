So: I read Graham's Play Unsafe. A smart read, it is.

This morning, I recalled a sequence some like this, in some of my games:\n
GM: The barkeeper looks at you suspiciously. "We don't talk to cops like you!"
Player: I jump up on top of the bar. "Are you kidding me? I'm exactly the kind of cop you'd best listen to, dude!!"
GM: Okay, so I think we're in a conflict, let me roll out my fancy chart of narrativist conflict resolution and start declaring stakes and twiddlying widgets.
\nAnd wow, that technique isn't just clumsy (though it is). It's actually something of a blocking move by the GM there. The player has pushed forward something - an action has taken place in his fiction, and it's presumed there's some reasonable outcomes attached ("they gasp and step back" vs "they appear disinterested"), and what the GM then does it say, hold it: you've narrated something, now let me throw a boundary over it and let's questionify it and decide whether you really meant what you did there.

I'm not criticizing staking technique, but I think a clumsy technique with which it may be applied.

I think the right move here, ideally, is to take that player's input - accept some defeat, in fact - and build on top of that. If I wanted a conflict, it has to be out there without rephrasing/reversing what's already out there. So perhaps the GM should accept the contribution here - the barkeeper is stunned and stammering on the floor, but he's also looking for his secret alarm button as he start babbling, and now the GM and proactively offer a different conflict than before: does the player keep control of this situation?

(And I think this all makes sense with reversing the player/GM roles, or with players-not-GMs involved.)

    \n My gut instinct on this is that Blocking is only bad if the players think it is. Most of the time the players know exactly why the GM blocks something and they are fine with it, because an RPG is not Improv theatre, and the players expect some sort of opposition to their character's goals.

    The same goes for reaching for the mechanics. If your game has mechanics about who gets to do what (or who gets to say who is doing what) then no one feels bad about them being used, unless they think it was unjustified. I wouldn't have said that the OP's first example felt like blocking, because it seems the action has escalated to a point where I would expect mechanics to be involved. Now of course that only applies for some values of "expect". If players have been regularly shooting grunts, doing stunts, and generally getting away with murder without needing to invoke the mechanics so far, then this example *would* be blocking, because it would seem petty. It's all relative.

    Some examples from recent things I have run (all taken from the playtests of Solipsist I have posted here recently, because, well that's what I have been running ... and I get to do shameless name dropping of my product into the bargain).

    In one game the players decided to grab some horses from the abandoned farm they were in and ride to their destination. I, as the GM, said "Sorry, there are no horses" and pointed out that they had specifically established this in a previous scene. Was this blocking? No, because the players all agreed that this was true, and were happy with it.

    A scene later and they are trying to persuade a drunk Priest to lead them to a grave. They have already got loads of action and information out of him without mechanics, but we have established that he doesn't want to go. They want to persuade him, so I say, "okay, change reality to make him go". Again I didn't feel like this was blocking, because they knew they had crossed a line in what they were trying to do, and no one objected to it.

    But in the same game, a little earlier, the characters had ransacked a house for supplies, and wanted to find rope, raincoats, guns, all sorts of stuff. I could have asked them to change reality to get that too, but then I think I *would* have been blocking, because what they were asking to do, without mechanics, was totally in line with all the other things they had already done without mechanics. Asking for a test there, though perfectly within the rules, would have been petty.

    \n Here's another thing:

    The concept of "blocking", as I understand it, is that someone is making input, and trying to take things somewhere, and someone else just shuts them down. This creates a sort of "dead end" in the story--now we have to jump-start it again, bring it back to life, with a different idea.

    Some resolution mechanics can definitely have this effect--"Roll your Intelligence. [...] Sorry, that was a neat idea, but your character doesn't know anything about the Fire Swamp, so you can't do that."--but most of the mechanics I've seen in recent story game designs try very hard to avoid that.

    I think most of the mechanics we're seeing (among this crowd) these days try pretty hard to make sure that, even if the conflict "fails", it spins the story in some new direction and keeps things in motion. Vincent Baker would probably say, "unstable situation, through System, resolves into a new unstable situation". Or something. For instance, if you're playing DitV, the outcome of a conflict will almost certainly be a "No, and" or "Yes, but" or something else that keeps things moving instead of just shutting the story down right there.

    On the other hand, where I see more "blocking" is in conversations that aren't strictly about stuff in the game, so they don't get resolved through conflict resolution mechanics. Like when someone says, "Ooh! I want to play a [type of character]!" And the GM (or whoever) answers, "No. There aren't any in this part of the world," or "that's not a legal character class," or something like that. That's blocking, too, isn't it?

    \n Paul's last comment reinforces my feeling that the improv idea of 'blocking' is ill-suited for this conversation.

    Blocking is only blocking if it's an in-the-moment decision to deny your scene partner information. This can be as simple as saying 'no' to their suggestion, denying the reality of the situation ("You're crazy!") or denying the knowledge of their character ("You're stupid and know nothing!"). These have very specific, deleterious effects on the scene, often causing it to stop and have to restart itself.

    RPGs, because they're not happening in real time, usually, aren't as susceptible to blocking as an improv scene. There are many ways to block certain types of input into a game without shutting down a player completely. These are healthy, these are what create sustained play. Just saying 'yes' to everything in a roleplaying game is almost never the best path.

    \n I guess my working definition of blocking is someone puts something out there into the narrative, and someone else (essentially) nullifies it. Whether or not the block is "justified" is a separate question. (For example, I would go so far as to say that nullifying a contribution becuase of past established "facts" about the game world is still a block. It might be a justified block, but it still is a block. And it might not even be justified, if the story is described a dreamworld or set in a fairy tale or represents testimony or some such.) Not all blocks are bad. In fact, most RPG mechanics are a form of block. Even setting stakes is a kind of block, because each person takes the time to contribute a possible outcome, and one of those outcomes will get nullified. There's a sense of "wasting creative time" rather than flowing forward constantly. If you like "say yes or roll the dice" could be read as "say yes or block." That is, the rule is to not block for the sake of blocking. If blocking were always bad, the mantra would be "Say yes. Period."
    \n
    \nThat said, I'm actually on the blocking-tends-to-be-bad side of the spectrum. For example,I think a pure say yes system (sometimes called "freeform") is an OK one. I also think mechanics should be able to seamlessly default into say yes only if that is what players want to do. This is one of the reasons I prefer Polaris. For my group, about half the scenes of Polaris we do either involve no use of the conflict mechanics at all, or just "but only if..." and "that was how it happened..." Since "but only if..." only becomes a block (by my definition, a contribution put out was nullified) if someone else eventually uses "it was not meant to be" these are essentially no-block scenes.
    \n
    \n(The mechanic still matters, because of the Threat of a block still constrains and therefore alters play. But when the blocks in practice don't happen then they have little or no negative impact on Flow)

    \n "I guess my working definition of blocking is someone puts something out there into the narrative, and someone else (essentially) nullifies it. "

    I know the concept. I just don't see any nullification in the example. The PC jumped on the table and made a moving speech. Nothing about the GM's actions in the example or the design of games I know requires that that be denied. The only thing at risk is whether the player gets the response he wants -- and that hasn't even been proposed into play yet, so nothing has been nullified.

    I can see that a player might feel blocked if they expect that the resolution system will just produce a whiff -- "Sorry, you're action didn't move them to talk. What do you do now?" is a lame result.

    This reminds me of the screenplay concept of an "event." Paraphrased for RPGs it would read"

    The resolution system changes a character's circumstances in a ways that are irreversable and meaningful to the character and the players.

    Lots of game rules strive to produce this result. In actual play we don't always succeed. Any of us who've played years of traditional games (where this often happens) may still half-expect it.

    I think this is all about understanding the intent of the resolution system in the specific game and the expectations of the players.

    If the player felt he had a right to a particular response from the bartender, I can imagine disappointment, perhaps experienced as blocking. This is why games try to define when the resolution system should be engaged -- to let players know what to expect. When an in-story conflict falls outside the focus of the rules, it may be appropriate to have players describe both initation and resolution of minor conflicts -- for example when it expresses color (eg PTA or Capes). But if the in-story conflict falls within a realm defined as a focus of the game, then the resolution system engages.

    Individual rulesets give guidelines for when to engage resolution and also distribute the responsibility for arbitrating those rules. In Trollbabe for example, anyone can call for a dice roll -- and as long as no one calls, any event any player (including the GM) suggests enters the story. In other games, the responsiblity is with the GM and/or supported by guidelines of what is important. (In PTA, conflicts are about Issues or Series Premise, any other conflict can be described without the resolution system).

    Note that the conditions for engaging resolution also support or undermine the creative agenda of play. Narrativist play requires a focus on conflict that is meaningful to some theme or other and so has means of defining those themes (A definition of Humanity, Issues, character situation [see Trollbabe and DiTV], etc.)

    All this is why I suggested being more aware of player expectations. Also, you might want to reexamine what the boundaries of conflict are in the particular game and ensure everyone is playing for the same goal.

    There's one piece of advice that my creative writing teacher always told me: start your scenes 1/3 of the way into the conflict and cut out 2/3 of the way in. It's a lot of fun to read about two characters having an argument over spilled wine leading to them facing off on top of a clock tower in a thunderstorm with their families watching from below.     So in your example, I'd say the conflict was about 1/10 of the way done. It had just been introduced, and your GM wanted to skip all the way to resolution.

    One solution could be to introduce multi-step resolution mechanics into a game. The players would introduce a conflict, roleplay it a bit, then dice off to see how the conflict continues (fight or flight, conflict or compromise, threats or passive agressiveness, etc.), roleplay that, dice off to see who's feeling what, roleplay it even more, and then dice off to see who wins.

    I'm sure it's been done before, but hell if I can think of a game that's done it.
