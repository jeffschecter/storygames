\n So after reading Graham's Play Unsafe, I've been talking about the very thought-provoking concepts with other gamers. One thing that came up repeatedly in one conversation was status, specifically how status preference affects gaming. The following came out of conversation with a gaming friend, Asahira, and he's said my takes make sense to him.

A little background: Asahira, the friend I was talking to, much prefers playing to gamemastering. He also loves playing cool, badass guys and likes to figure out effective character builds and combat strategies in crunchy games like GURPS or (A)D&D. He tried gamemastering once and didn't like it very much--functional gamemastering isn't about winning, after all, and while it was okay overall, he did fall into the trap of the uber-NPC.

He's also a good sport who comes along for the ride on the dirty hippie games I run, from My Life with Master to FATE and Dogs in the Vineyard, and recently Primetime Adventures. He thinks story games have a lot of merit, though not the in the things he prefers. After trying a wide range of available games and styles he still likes crunchiness and badassery best, which I think is cool. He was and is willing to try new things, and knows exactly what he likes out of lots of options, not because it's the only thing he ever tried.

Nevertheless, I was curious why he and many others liked crunchy adventures in badassery (that sounds like it should be a game) to the point of exclusivity. And after talking to him about this I think the answer lies in status preference. In short, he and others with like taste like to win by figuring out strategic elements in a complex game, and they like to have in-game status by playing tough, no-nonsense, invulnerable characters.

Status preference explains a lot about gamemastering preference or style. For instance, I love playing both high-status and low-status characters as GM. The sidebar on reasons to play high- and low-status had a lot of "That's me!" moments (like much of Play Unsafe, really). Asahira, on the other hand, hates playing low status characters. Even as GM, as mentioned above, he irritated his players with an NPC who did everything better than the PCs and didn't ever have vulnerabilities and flaws--other than, evidently, being a cocky know-it-all.

Further, status--in the sense of winning--may also explain rules preference. While Asahira has tried and freely admits the good points of story games, he still likes crunchy, strategic rules best. While story games do have success and failure, winning and losing, they're often more about facilitating drama. These stories also tend to involve fluctuating status, more than in the sense where you can get back by the end of the session at the jerk who disarmed you and locked you up, but serious, profound, long-term changes in character and status. Good story also requires some character vulnerability, something that isn't very agreeable for gamers with a strong preference for high status.

Overall, there seems to be a lot of predictive power in status preference that I think could be a useful tool to gauge gaming expectations and compatibilities. Thinking about our differences in terms of status preference explains quite a few of the styles clashes we'd been having when we game together. (I had a hard time getting a handle on his characters, for instance, because they didn't seem to have personal vulnerabilities and never seemed to be in the wrong.) This isn't to say I won't ever gamie with Asahira again, of course. In fact, I feel like we'll both enjoy gaming together a lot more now that we understand a little more about what makes the other tick.

    So at least in my experience, the concept of status "preference" is complicated. There's a lot of reflex that goes into status positioning within a group, as well as habits that come from other games and gaming groups. Plus there's some interaction back and forth between status behavior in a friend group that also games together, status behavior as a gamer playing with those other people in a gaming group, and status behavior as a character. Changing any one of those can sometimes affect the other spheres -- e.g. the example at the very end of the thread about how changes in other players' status behavior can affect your sense of character skill / status. Historically, I've preferred stories where my character gets screwed over stories where I have a clearcut happy ending, partly because I find getting screwed more immersive, but I suspect that's related to my relative comfort with being a low status player back in my college group. (which was in turn because I wasn't on board with the group's creative agenda and it limited the ways I could contribute to the game)

    But status behavior in those three spheres can also be disjoint, sometimes really disjoint. As a minor example, one of the players in our group who historically likes his characters to "win" recently decided to shift toward playing a more vulnerable character -- someone who had something to lose -- because after watching one of my character arcs, he decided that it might be more interesting and that he might have a larger share of the story if he played someone who had a lot at stake.

    I don't suppose any of this is directly useful to your situation, since it sounds like Asahira does have a clearcut status preference. But if it's not too awkward to talk about, I'm curious how Asahira positions himself status-wise in your gaming group when you aren't actively in the process of playing a game.     \n This is a whole untapped field, and it's hugely important to everything that happens at the gaming table. Many times, as schlaf mentions in her question, it ends up tied up in out-of-game status and social arrangements because virtually all games put their fingers in their ears and yell "LA LA LA" about it. That can be okay or it can be problematic.

    I have mentioned a player in my group before. Her preferences for what to do on a Saturday afternoon go, in order:

    #1 best thing to do: game, and her character doesn't suffer any real setbacks or difficulties
    #2 best thing to do: watch TV
    #3 best thing to do: game, and her character suffers real setbacks and difficulties

    It took me forever to figure this out, and once I figured it out I moped around until I remembered that nobody else at the table really gave a crap whether her character suffered real setbacks and difficulties or succeeded at everything easily, so I started having her character succeed at everything easily and continued to give other characters real setbacks and difficulties according to their players' preferences, and everything started running a lot more smoothly and everyone was a lot more happy with her play and their own.

    Interestingly, she has a bit of an outsider status with respect to the rest of the group - doesn't always attend, came to the group later than everyone else...

    \n Hm. See, I've always been curious about the player who claims to be all into the strategy and winning and stuff like that, but then chafes at having their character's status temporarily reduced, and not seeing it as the opportunity that it actually is.

    In-game challenges have to come from somewhere, and status reduction is pretty much the only tool you have if the campaign goes long enough - if you're the biggest badass in the land, there *has* to be a bigger badass for you to prove yourself against at some point. If you're in a position of power, that power *has* to get threatened at some point. Maybe not in a big way that changes up the whole character, but in some way that provides the basic impetus to compete.

    So it strikes me as kind of a contradictory position, and perhaps more seriously, potentially suggests the player's use of gaming as some kind of... I don't know, weird coping tool. Setbacks and failures are a large part of what we deal with in life, after all - so someone who has issues dealing with them in real life, via self-esteem issues or whatever else, probably isn't going to want to bring them into the game. To be absolutely clear, I'm not saying that to make a judgment call on any of the examples given above; I'm actually reflecting on people I know and have gamed with.

    Like, I came in as a newcomer to a friend of mine's group, who wanted to run a hacked Fate game. And there was this guy there, clearly the oldest gent in the group, who seemed to occupy this strange position as a mentor figure - he pretty much responded to everything anyone talked about like he had a deep understanding, would relate stories about his multifaceted life without prompting, effortlessly received the doe-eyed, "please teach us" look from most of the group whenever he talked, etc.

    He even went so far as to tell me casually, "Yeah, I don't really advertise myself as a guru, but you know, if you're looking for one, I'm usually around." I'm serious. No lie. In retrospect, I should have walked out the door right there.

    So we did character creation, and we ended up playing the chief members of this cult in a post-apocalyptic wasteland, charlatans who were trying to rule the land by using lost technology we'd found to convince people we had power and answers. This dude naturally moves to create the cult's leader, and I don't think there was any avoiding it, and certainly this sat very well with the members of the group. I ended up making this sociopathic tech genius, kidnapped by the other cult members very young and taught the secrets of this lost technology by an AI, barely seeing any other people for all his formative years.

    So we meet a second time and play, and pretty much, shit goes wrong right from the get-go. Because this dude was the cult leader, it wasn't enough for him to defend his status at all costs - he required every other player to take the same approach, to follow him in a creepily real sense, and bickered at people to change their actions and stuff if he perceived a threat to his plans. Like, not in a cool, "let's make conflict between the characters" way, but clearly as a player demanding obedience from another player. I couldn't really do anything to make the proceedings of the first session interesting, and no one could really take advantage of any compels.

    It really creeped me out. I'm certain it'd have been a very volatile personal argument if I'd actually tried to push for any status transitions during play. I didn't return, and the game fell apart after a third session.

    I was at a bar with my friend (who, by the way, was the only one who didn't really respond to this guy in the ways I described above) some months later, and the conversation drifted back to this game, and he explained to me that Creepy Guy had been a divorce lawyer in life, but had stopped practicing and pretty much was living off of relative's/spouse's/government's money or something like that, and basically was content to bum out, leech cash, and not really do anything else with his life.

    It made me understand his actions during the game, and why he'd expend so much energy to develop that particular social position in that group, in a much clearer context.

    \n Regarding players who like winning and looking bad-ass but not challenges -- Besides how status gaming addresses real life status problems, it seems like part of what's going on may be a difference in the criteria people use to figure out whether they have status and how other groups they've been in have awarded status. Consuming a constantly reduplicated but basically static spectacle of status (looking cool) is different than approaching status more directly as being about the power you have in unfolding interactions (e.g. winning in a real competition [with your character's enemies]). Play could be about status symbols but not about power, though they blur together a lot.

    I've never roleplayed for static high status, but it sounds a little like how I used to play Sid Meier games (mostly Colonization, some Civilization) on the computer. After days where I felt completely beaten down, I'd keep restarting games on new maps and play on a given map only until something went wrong, because what I enjoyed was the sense of primal plenty and endless possibility. It was about reorienting myself through interacting symbolically with an ideal world. Meaning I was using them more like a ritual than like a game. Calling things escapist doesn't quite catch that distinction, since escapism and striving coexist more happily for some players than others, even among players who prefer sim. (of course, there are also openly competitive rituals, but, umm, ignoring those.)

    Thinking that you like strategy or whatnot when actually you don't intrinsically enjoy it can also be an identity practice. If people you know think that x is cool, you gain status by liking x, too, because it lets you identify with high status members of the group, and because now they count you as being in the group as opposed to one of those losers outside it. Talking about x that way may continue even after you've switched to a group that isn't defined by x, confusing people in the new group. (btw to people I know: this is only partly about my gaming preferences)
