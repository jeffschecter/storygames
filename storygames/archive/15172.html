\nWhat stuff should we watch, read, play?

1.Feel free to respond here in-thread to another link posted.

a. If you're looking to start a conversation, perhaps take your comment and create a new thread with it in the Stuff to Watch category.

b.Otherwise, to prevent too many comments in the thread, number your response (first response: [1]; second response [2]; third response [3]). If you see that you're the second person to post, "Wow, that looks cool!" to a post here, then put a "[2]" at the top of your post. Three posts ([3]) is the limit: The fourth person to post should just take their comment and create a new thread, to carry the discussion over there further.

Why?

Because these threads are great: We can link to little things being talked about on the web in general, on blogs, or other forums that might be noteworthy, without having to dedicate a new thread to each and every one (although that, too, is fine if you want to discuss the item further or "open up a discussion").

Responding to these posts with a comment or two is understandable (other times there might be no comments, just cause the link was self-explanatory: nothing to say, lots of heads nodding in agreement). But we don't want to create a huge discussion in the middle of the thread: Some folks get a lot out of these threads, and don't want to look back to see the "10 latest posts" just to see that it's a heated discussion of something from a previous page. Cool? Cool.

    \n The Tao of D&D blog discusses running sandbox D&D games with a classic "Illusionism" style. He even uses that metaphor:

    "Perhaps it is giving away too much of a secret, but the principles lie in the same presentation as that offered by an illusionist or other form of magician. You raise the left hand, you wave the hell out of it, you draw the player's attentions to what you going to do with the left hand, and then you hit them with the trick. You don't let them walk into the bank because something really, really interesting happens on the street that draw their attention."

      \n [Random Game Ideas, the real #2]

      I remember that thread when it started, may have even posted on it, and yet it still brings the awesome. \n
      Imagine that several different Big Bads - all with mutually exclusive goals - have decided to place a deep-cover operative within a group of heroes. They've all called in expensive favours and expended irreplaceable resources in order to ensure that their operative is indistinguishable from the real thing.

      The catch?

      By sheer coincidence, they've all selected the same party member to replace. Thanks to the trouble they went through, none of them are willing to discard their plans; as the various doubles discover each other's presence, they repeatedly waylay each other to step into the role of the guy they're trying to impersonate, all the while trying to prevent the party from figuring it out - a ruse that becomes increasingly complicated over time, as none of the doubles are completely up to date on what the others have said or done.

      The player characters would, of course, be the doubles. At any given moment, one of the players would assume the role of the current doppelganger and interact directly with the NPC party, while the others would secretly shadow the party, attempting to sabotage the current doppelganger's goals and swap themselves in at the first opportunity to pursue their own objective. Think of it as a reverse Weekend at Bernie's on an epic scale.
      \nThis is totally a Fiasco playset.

      Posted By: Adam DrayThe Tao of D&D blog discussesrunning sandbox D&D games with a classic "Illusionism" style. He even uses that metaphor:

      "Perhaps it is giving away too much of a secret, but the principles lie in the same presentation as that offered by an illusionist or other form of magician. You raise the left hand, you wave the hell out of it, you draw the player's attentions to what you going to do with the left hand, and then you hit them with the trick. You don't let them walk into the bank because something really, really interesting happens on the street that draw their attention."
      \nThis guys sets up a fine strawman in his first paragraph. He then proceeds to use the fallacy of the excluded middle to show that a "sandbox game" is one where the GM only has one toy for you to play with.

      Meh.

      \n Steam is having a Halloween sale. There's some really great titles there but my current favorite is The Binding of Isaac, a rouge-like, top-down shooter. Theme-wise it's very dark; you are a naked kid in a basement fleeing an insane mother. Your weapons are your tears and upgrades include Mom's Makeup/dress, A coathanger (that goes through your head and increases your tears), a wooden spoon (which increases your tears and leaves you with welts on your face) the Mark of the Beast and so forth.

      What's really neat about this game is that the elements (maps/upgrades/monster payout/secrets) are generated randomly each time you play, so it's a relatively unique experience every game. Plus, it's actually hard!

      I may be adapting the basic premise of the top down dungeon crawl for the Stage One design contest. It's good stuff!
