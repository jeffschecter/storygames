\n I'm listening to the audio version of Distrust That Particular Flavor by William Gibson. It's an annotated collection of his nonfiction articles.

Here's a little bit from a piece he wrote in 2000:

"Artistic collaboration is a profoundly strange business. Do it right up to the hilt, as it were, and you and your partner will generate a third party. Some thoroughly other. And often one capable of things neither you nor the very reasonable gentleman seated opposite would even begin to consider. Who...is the third who walks beside us?"

That really struck me as something. I've felt that. I studied studio art for a couple years in college and had some profound collaborative experiences in that context. I've also made art with my wife and our third isn't very expressive of a distinct personality, but we definitely have a synergy and it's great.

But what about gaming? I think there's at least a little third in any of these games we play around here. The place that I've seen it most strongly -- sometimes startlingly, is playing Universalis. Uni doesn't provide me with the most 'fun' of any game, but the emergence of this third happens so quickly and so ubiquitously that it's a thing to behold and this game occupies a special place in my mind because of it.

Does this notion of the third -- the synergy between players (artists?) expressing it's own personality; does that resonate with you? What do you think about it? When do you feel it most? Least? Is it always welcome?

    \n Yes, it's exactly the same feeling when you can't come up with a solution for something, and then a random everyday ocurrence makes it all click in your mind. It's like it isn't your own idea at first, so you can't help to be surprised by all the implications this new idea brings into the problem and how it solves many more issues you still hadn't foreseen up until now. No wonder people keep referring to this as a third person...

    Edit: First thing you need in order to experience this is to be willing to contribute your creativity to a project, ans at the same time totally let go of owning anything about the results. Whenever more than one person is in this state, unafraid of taking what others bring into the project, just saying "yes and..." by limiting themselves to push the snowball, the third appears. And it's fearsome.
