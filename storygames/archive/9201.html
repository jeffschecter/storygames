\n Just a heads up for the classical boardgame crowd.

A classic book, well THE English book about how to play Shogi is now back in print. Originally published in 1966, it got a reprint just a few months ago:

http://www.amazon.com/Japanese-Chess-Shogi-Trevor-Leggett/dp/4805310367/

Here's the best part, the part that I totally wasn't expecting: In the back of the book is a complete punch-out cardboard set of pieces, and a fold--out paper board: So you don't have to spend the extra 30-some-odd dollars for a set to play it on: The game of Shogi comes with the book. Furthermore, the pieces have English markings on them (letters underneath the kanji) so you can tell which is which.

Anyway, the book is inexpensive, teaches you how to play a kickass chess where you take the opponent's pieces then put them back on the board as your own, and I was totally surprised to see that it came with a complete copy of the game. Total steal at that price, if you're interested in strategy or classic board games.

    So yeah, Shogi is a pretty big classical game in Japan. It's regarded as slightly more cultured than Mah Jongg, it's right up there with Go for the "games that intellectual folks play that also appear on national TV on weekend afternoons".

    And yeah, the metaphors are similar, as the pieces are similar. Just last week on the latest episode of an Edo-era assassin show I've been watching, the evil magistrate describes the townspeople as "pieces/pawns" in his game. As popularized as it is in the West I think.

    Hmmm. Gameplaywise... not sure, unfortunately, which makes it kind of a non-RPG subject, though game related. Nothing more than Go, Chess or Mah Jongg would teach us. There is that uniqueness of "if you capture a piece, you can put that piece right back on the board. ANYWHERE." That might be something interesting in a tactics or dice-based game: Normally in the west, if we return a piece to the board, or add a new element to the conflict, it appears in the "back row", but in Shogi you can totally put it in their face.

    For example, my first ever game of shogi, where I was learning as I went along, I was playing my friend Shimizu who is really pretty good (and decent at explaining the moves; my only prob as that even though I can read kanji, a lot of the pieces look alike, so I got confused at times). Anyway, it was going pretty well. Taking pieces back and forth. Playing like a regular game of chess.

    Then, he pulled out a pawn that he had taken from me. He slapped it down right in front of my King, out of friggin' nowhere. WHAP.
    Shimizu: "Um... Yeah, I win."
    Me: "WHAT? Oh yeah, you can return pieces to the board. You did mention that, I remember now. Poo."

    I dunno if that can be translated into RPG mechanics, but it's pretty cool when it happens.

    Posted By: DanielSolisHey Andy, is there a chess variant that uses the Shogi put-your-piece-anywhere mechanic? 'Cause I was tinkering with something like that for a long time in my board game design days.
    There are bunches of them. Probably the most popular is Chessgi, where, like in Shogi, you can place captured pieces most anywhere on the board. Crazyhouse is very similar, but seems to have evolved from Bughouse. Hostage Chess is another game in this family.

    There are also games where you can drop your own pieces, such as the fairly traditional Pocket Knight Chess or the more elaborate Pocket Mutation Chess. And then there's my Ruddigore Chess, which, since you can capture your own pieces, is a bit of both styles.

    Andy, my 1978 printing also has the cardboard pieces and board in the back.

    It could be--some folks are already talking chess variants and trying to make RPG designs around the shogi concept. Why shuffle off?

    So to actually run with your point: Small press printers could probably put together something like that, but I doubt any POD printers could. So you've got up-front costs (possibly big ones). This is GoB's cue, and Jason Morningstar's, as he delivered (with Roach) something fairly similar (cards).

    To my imagining, it seems like having perf pages in the back of a book could make the book a bit... I dunno? ragged?... once the stuff is removed. The spine would be thicker than the actual pages, which might cause creasing of the spine or back cover, or could open the way for gradual binding adhesive breakdown. That is, basically, your back cover will end up some millimeters "wider" than the rest of the pages and front cover, because of the loss of the perfed pages.

    If it were me, I'd lean towards an insert, especially if saddle-stitch bound instead of perfect bound (i.e. small number of pages). If you KNOW the end user will remove it, go ahead and remove it for them and shrink-wrap the "set."

    -----
    Also... I'll say that I think Knightmare Chess is pretty sweet: dueling deck design meets chess, making it a far wilder board game. Teleporting kings, blocking walls, suicide-bomber pawns... what's not to love?

    -----
    For a chess RPG, look no further than the supposed inspiration for Alice in Wonderland (or was it Through the Looking Glass?). Apparently, one can actually play out a chess game using the "moves" of the characters in the novel, except for a VERY wild pawn (Alice) who has free reign to wander the board. So run with that: a board game that uses pieces with a lot of color (i.e. chess, shogi, even Carcaissone; not Go or backgammon or checkers) but require a "fitting narration" of each move. Perhaps even play one of the four-way chess variants, for multiple players (make voting on "fitness" easier). So you're not just "advancing a pawn two squares on an opening move"; rather, "Fnord the serf charges into the field, screaming 'For King and COUNTRY!' even though he knows he's bound for either summary death or a VERY long summer of warfare, staring across the trenchworks at the enemy's serfs, no one daring to try a sortie lest whole swaths of both armies be cut down in the attacks and counterattacks." Basically, just imagine the story being told.

    Or, hell, it doesn't even have to use the literal war symbolism. I could see something akin to Vampire: The Masquerade, with the king as an ancient and powerful vampire (your PC), and other pieces being various mortals, lesser vampires, or even government and social organizations under the Methuselah's sway. It's not "knight captures bishop"; it's "the FBI is called in to deal with your "David Koresh Ministry", calling them a band of terrorists and a cult because they indoctrinated those college kids" (captured a pawn or two earlier in the game).

    Guided storytelling. Heck, one could even have some bizarre non-win conditions or illegal moves, like "his wife kills him for the insurance money" (you capture your own king with your queen, making her the object to be checkmated)... "but the oldest son finds out and poisons her in her sleep" (your bishops captures your queen, to become the next target). Maybe you get additional moves, if you self-capture?

    Yep, I could go on all day with this... but I really should do some work for my pay!

    \n Oh, BTW, if you want to have your mind fucked, check out Wikipedia on Shogi variants. The book mentions some, like dai-shogi, dai-dai-shogi, tai-shogi and the like. Wikipedia actually tells you the piece names and the moves. Someone's been really busy with translation:

    Maka Dai Dai Shogi has 19 squares per side, and 96 pieces per side, including Capricorn, Wrestler, Violent Oxen, Drunken Elephant, Old Monkey, etc.
    Tai Shogi has Whales, Vermillion Sparrows, She-Devils, Southern Barbarians, Clay Generals, etc. However, it's a kind of simple game because there's no "board replacement of captured pieces", and all the pieces only move one single square.

    Taikyoku Shogi, the great mystery game. This is like the Da Vinci code of shogi; discovered just under 10 years ago upon the translation of 16th century Buddhist monk scrolls, historians are still uncovering the secrets of this game. Captive Cadets, Woodland Demons, Prancing Stags, Little Turtles, Dragon King... wow.

    Also, there are smaller fun-looking and acutal playable variants as well, like Whale Shogi which uses a 6x6 board.

    De nada, my pleasure. You really ought to give Gess a go, if you'll pardon the pun - there is an awesomely thick vein of design space around it, as you'll see if you play.

    My favorite gess variant, as I recall it: the board, instead of being 24x24, is 15x24, so basically room for just one of each normally paired piece. Each player gets 49 stones which they can place anywhere in the first six ranks, lengthwise, on their side of the board, subject to the constraint that if you don't make at least one ring/king, you obviously lose on move 0. Both players do this simultaneously, with a divider between them. You could play full-width with 75 stones instead, but the setup takes forever.
