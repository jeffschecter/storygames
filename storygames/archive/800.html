    So how come I have to have a family and stuff? I mean, sheer twinkitude would lead me to play the garden variety orphan adventurer.

    Well, it's unrelated to the skills-thing, but it's a part of the game that I'm pretty excited about, so I'm all about chatting it up here. Here's the simplified version;

    • Each PC has between 1 and 3 Issues that they need or want to deal with. Similar to, but not to be confused with PTA Issues.
    • Each session you have the opportunity to address one or more of your character's issues. The more you address them, the more Tension you create.
    • Tension yeilds dice. It's over-simplified, but that's the important part. The bigger your issue gets, the more resource you have for ass-kicking.

    So, to give some short & sweet exampling, let's say that you've got the issue of "Overprotective Mother". You would have authored your issues, there won't be any kind of list to pick from in the text. During this particular session you decide to address your issue of having an overprotective mother by calling for a conflict where your character tries to convince mom to stop calling so often. Whichever way that conflict goes, you've increased the Tension in your Overprotective Mother Isssue. Next session you'll get more dice from that issue than you did for this session.

    So, mom getting on your case lets you kick more alien ass.

    I want to give everyone who's helping me out with the skills portion of Invisible Shepherds an idea of how those skills are already connected to the rest of the system. I spent some time considering just posting up the rag-tag playtest document I have, but decide that I'd end up just confusing the issue. My early playtest documents are always written in what my wife likes to call Eric-speak. So, instead of sharing that confusion around, I'm going to give you a thumbnail of how the mechanisms hang together.

    Toys

    The skills are directly connected to Toys. Weapons, vehicles, toolkits, cyberware. The text will contain short "shopping-lists" of each type of toy, and each toy will have it's own special effects, bonus to skill roll, and a chance that using it will cause the Shepherds to fuck up their public relations. Come right down to it, each toy will have it's special effects listed in a paragraph or two and a single numeric rating between 1 and 8. That rating is the number of dice that it adds to a skill roll when you use the item.

    Each skill will be tied to a specific range of dice that are available to toys that might be tied to that skill. Right this minute that scale kinda works like this; Low 1-3 dice, Medium 3-5 dice, and High 5-8 dice. For example, the three skills of Martial Arts, Side-arms, and Heavy Weapons have scales of Low, Medium, and High, respectively. So, melee weapons will have between 1 and 3 dice, side-arms will have between 3 and 5 dice, and the heavy shit will have between 5 and 8 dice.

    Public Opinion

    When a Shepherd uses excess force in a conflict there's a chance that public opinion will sway against them. When public opinion about a shadowy force of cyborgs goes south, it causes personal problems for the Shepherds. In a nutshell, when you use more toy-dice than your opponent does, then public opinion sways against you. The bigger the difference between the toys you use and the toys your opposition uses the more sway in public opinion. The more the sway in public opinion, the less likely you are to win personal conflicts.

    It's way more complex in effect than that, but that's the important part for now.

    Scene Types

    What kind of scene it is does not directly relate to what kind of skills might be in play. There is an indirect relationship, but please don't assume that one particular skill is only for one kind of scene or vice-versa.

    The three different kinds of scenes are Personal, Mission, and Mission Climax. The three types are pretty informal and I expect that they'll naturally blend together more often than not. I imagine that it wouldn't be hard at all for a scene to blend from personal to mission, onto the Mission Climax, and back to personal before it ends. Now, the type of scene has to do with the type of conflict that's going on and that's a little less formal. There are formal mechanisms for addressing your character's personal issues, for advancing the mission towards it's conclusion, and for eventually resolving the success or failure of the mission. If you've read the other Shepherds thread, the Teeth that Specialist-players author are the meat & potatoes of both the Mission and Mission Climax scenes. Those Teeth might determine that a particular skill needs to be used for some part of the mission. But that part of the system isn't 100% built yet. I have work yet to do there.

If so, I'll amend this with another post.

The higher this rating is, the more currency you get for putting dice anywhere. Originally I was going to have this be a mission-only resource, but later decided that it'd be just fine if it was an any-scene resource. The second rating for your issues is the maximum number of sessions you can address the issue before you have to resolve it.

      Now, you don't actually get shit in the way of resources for resolving an issue, except for the opportunity to put your points from the Max-Rating into a new issue. The first rating gets flushed when you resolve that particular issue, meaning that your total resources go down. So really, you temporarily loose some resources when you resolve an issue. Particularly one that you've been addressing for several sessions. I'm hoping that this'll result in a more 'climatic' feeling for the issue, which should lead right into the build-up for the issue that replaces it.

      Originally, I did have a reward for resolving an issue. I dumped it when I realized that this would encourage players to take short-term issues over long-term and while lots of short-term issues won't nearly break the system, I don't want long-term issues to be a broken choice.

      1) How do players defend against these skills? If someone uses command on you, are you boned?

      In a word; Yes. And no. I suppose this is a good time to talk a little about the conflict resolution system. Once a conflict comes up in a scene you'll be picking out the skill that you want to resolve it with and I'll pick out the skill that I want to resove it with.

      For example; Let's say that you're playing a Shepherd who's trying to get into a secret research laboratory to liberate some research data. Along the way you encounter a guard who levels his gun at you and orders you to stop and face the wall (that's my narration). You respond by declaring that your character is gonna go all Aeon Flux on his ass with a couple jumps and flips. So, right then and there, we've framed up our conflict. You'll be rolling the Athletics & Acrobatics on your character sheet and I'll be rolling Command from the NPC sheet.

      Invisible Shepherds uses a dice-pool system. Sometimes you roll d6s, sometimes you roll d10s, sometimes you roll some of each. Each die that comes up as 5 or better is a point and just one point is needed to be 'successful.'

      So, let's say that you get more points than the guard does on the roll, but the guard gets at least one success. You've won the roll and you get to narrate the final outcome and here are the guidelines for your narration:

      • You win the stakes. In this case, the stakes include getting away with that research data.
      • Your character did some awesome jumping around. Make sure to include that.
      • Your character did succumb to the guard's command to stop & put your arms in the air. Had he rolled no 5's or better on any of his dice, then you wouldn't be bound to narrate in any reaction to his command.

      So, you could narrate something like how your character immediatley follows his command, stopping and arms in the air, and then jumping straight up to that water pipe hanging down from the ceiling to facilitate a gymnastic flip up into the open air shaft above, which leads directly into the laboratory you were looking for. Or any other variation on the three bullet points.

      There are variations on the rules that keep it from being that straightforward all the time, like being able to extend the conflict, what happens when you tie, and stuff like that.

      2) What's the deal with repair, demolitions, medical, resist torture/drugs, shadow/track, streetwise, survival and specialized knowledge like streetwise? I know that some of these we can assume that the chars are awesome at these, but I just wanted to put that out there...

      Some of those are covered under the existing skills and some don't need coverage at all. Repair falls under Technician. Demolitions falls under Heavy Weapons. Shadow & track are covered under Acrobatics & Athletics, Civvy Vehicles, and Military Vehicles. Medical, streetwise, survival, and specialized knowlege skills aren't included because I don't want to have any conflicts about them. Either your character has them or he doesn't and it won't have any mechanical effect on conflicts. Resist torture/drugs... well, I hadn't even considered a torture/drugging scene 'till just now. Lemmie talk about defensive-type skills at the bottom of this post.

      3) As far as an awareness/investigate skill, you might want to think about that. How do the characters see through the conspiracy/disguise? Now, you may a different mechanic for that, but if there isn't, then the whole quiet conspiracy of conquest becomes a crap shoot, no?

The characters are part of the one elite orginization in the world that knows what the Wolves are up to. In fact, the characters are part of the conspiracy by trying to keep the general population from knowing that there's an invasion on. No mechanisms needed.

      Defensive Skills

      One of the core reasons I decided to go with a skills list was to guide narration. Players will narrate the coolest outcome that they can think of, as guided by their skills, right? Like, when a player makes a really good Athletics & Acrobatics roll they're gonna be inclined to narrate all sorts of crazy jumping and flipping about. Or when a player makes a similarly good Civvy Vehicles roll they're likely to be inclined to narrate busting around corners and the squealing of tires. Narration like that yeilds action.

      But skills like Resist Torture and Stealth, defensive skills, really good rolls tend to get players to narrate how nothing happens. Right? Like; Ok, I'm being tortured. I roll my Resist Torture skill and get a mega-good roll. So, I narrate something like "I totally don't talk, I just spit in his eye." Cool? Yes. But not as cool as responding to torture with shit like Command, Impress, or Deceive.

      I think there are other reasons not to include defensive skills in a system like this, but what it comes right down to is that I really think we'll find better narration without them.
