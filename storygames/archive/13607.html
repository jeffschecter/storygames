    \n Thanks, Will. I'm glad you enjoyed it.

    It's an interesting discussion. You're right that the game doesn't concentrate on the mechanics of the murder: finding clues, solving the puzzle. I realised, early on in the game, that I could either make the game about relationships or evidence, so I went with the first.

    In that, it resembles P. D. James' books (which I read while growing up). She writes the whole book, then rewrites the last few pages so you can't guess who the murderer is. Thus, there's a sense that solving the murder doesn't quite matter: anyone could have done it.

    You could even say that it's not really a murder mystery game. It's a game about family relationships, squeezed into a murder mystery setting.

    You raised an issue about Side Scenes, which I probably should have explained better in the book. You don't, of course, take someone aside during dinner. Instead, you catch a few words on the stairs, or grab them during cocktails, or as you're going into dinner, or when you break after dinner. You exploit whichever social convention is appropriate: so, to talk to someone after lunch, you suggest a walk in the gardens. To talk to someone after dinner, you ask whether they would like a cigar in the gun room.

    All this becomes easier at breakfast, which isn't a sit-down meal. People arrive gradually, so you can grab a few words as people arrive or when they're the last ones eating.

    The game plays surprsingly well with six or more players. Generally, the Side Scenes become shorter. Rather than five-minute confrontations, they become quick conversations on the stairs. Indeed, whenever the players feel the urge to hurry things along, they generally just do short Side Scenes.

    Incidentally, the Black and White Dice are lifted straight from My Life With Master's Sincerity Die (and other dice) They're a blunt mechanical incentive to do interesting stuff. They also have an interesting status and comic effect.

    Thanks again. I really enjoyed the discussion.
