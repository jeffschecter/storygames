      \n
      \n Hey folks,

      Due to popular demand, we've decided to change our approach to publishing Fiasco playsets. From now on, each Playset of the Month will be a permanent addition to our free downloads once it is published, meaning you no longer need to worry about missing them.

      If you've grabbed the playset files previously, consider picking them up again- we've corrected a few errors in the text and added an introductory description that should help players decide which set to use for the perfect fiasco.

      If you see any problems with the playsets or have questions, or you want to develop your own sets and we can help, let us know!

      We'll still be publishing a collected book next year, but it will likely contain only the best playsets of the month, along with several previously-unpublished ones and some other new material. Big thanks to Chris Bennett for helping us arrive at this plan!

      Steve, it's SO easy to setup a wiki these days--FTP a file, run it on the server, and log in to begin configuring. It's even easier if your host has some kind of "Apps Market" (GoDaddy does, for instance).

      I think a wiki is better than a forum for several reasons:
      * A forum is inherently designed to be tied to dates (as is a blog); a wiki is not.
      * A wiki is easier to use with more than one writer (edit collisions are resolvable), and is marginally easier to revise a page.
      * Wikis make cross-referencing a lot easier--key to making a playset multi-page (rather than one huge single page dump).
      * Using infoboxes/templates is easier with a wiki (though, as always, someone could not use the or use them wrong).
      * Metadata, if you care; and semantic tagging, if you care (I don't see a LOT of use for that, but one could, say, find every playset with frogs in it).
      * Finally... most wikis have Talk pages, which can serve as a pseudo-forum for resolving conflicts over content between collaborators.

      Wikis, of course, require a tad more monitoring, if one wants a very consistent and professional-appearing one (no one expects forums to be consistent, and they can't really be enforced without Admin editors). A wiki with a large population of contributors must also develop a sort of social code, or a set of standards, regarding editing others work, page naming conventions, cross-referencing policies, templates, and use of user pages and sandboxes. Nothing earth-shaking, and easy to sum up in a 'How to contribute here" page linked of the main page. And MOSTLY easy to enforce (page names are a sort of bugbear: unless one is admin, if someone makes a "bad" page name, one must use a redirect to move it, making a "dead" page in the database).

      It's all REAL easy, in the end. Sounds like a lot, but there's fewer "rules and policies" to work out than in the average Game Chef. I'd be glad to help when you get it installed (or even help with that, but that means admin rights on your server, and I wouldn't exactly WANT that, to be frank--a liability).

      \n Fortunately, Steve, a playset's content (formatting-wise) is pretty straight-forward. Templating makes it even easier:
      1) Go to Templates page.
      2) Click on "Main Page Template".
      3) Click Edit.
      4) Copy all the code (Ctrl+A, Ctrl+C).
      5) Type the name of your playset in the Search field.
      It should prompt you to create a new page; if not (RARE!) search on a different name.
      6) Click Create Page [or whatever the actual text is in the wiki software].
      7) Paste the Home Template code into the edit field (Ctrl+V).
      8) Click Save.
      9) Repeat this process for each of these List Page Templates, naming their pages in the form [playset name]_[Rel | Obj | Need | Loc | Note]:
      * Relationships
      * Objects
      * Needs
      * Locations
      * Notes (if necessary)

      That would set up all the necessary format for the playset--including the dice icons, main and sub-headings, etc--with only the need to write the content and edit the cross-links on the Main page to the List pages. And maybe add in some artwork, for the Main Page.

      All that could also be done with infoboxes, but they're actually kind of a bitch to get folks to format them right.

      And trust me: there's enough OCD folks out there (like me) who will happily subscribe to an RSS feed for recent changes and click in to edit anything totally hare-brained or completely broken, formatting-wise.
      ----
      Community building could be tricky, or it could just happen. It's offering a publications space for a specialized form of creative product. it will go in waves, as folks discover the game and think of cool new playsets. Eventually, without constant promotion of the Fiasco IP, it will sunset and become VERY slow, or even dead. Then it might just be time to lock it up (i.e. remove all editing privileges) and it's just a static web site (all the "wikiness" disappears, if the wiki theme is properly coded).

      Look at Abulafia for Oracles--there was a boom, now a trickle, and probably won't be more than a couple of year henceforth.

      Or look at IcehouseGames.org for pyramid games--Looney Labs has rabid fans that run Icehouse stuff at nearly every con in the world, and they hand out cards with links to the wiki and LL site. As such, it seems like every month or so a new member joins, fired up to publish their game.

      It's all in promotion, in the end, isn't it?

      (Don't mean to tangent the thread so much, but there's only going to be one more PoM, right? Might as well discuss how to go forward with fan-created stuff here, yes?)

      The Brookmarket Zoo was a world-class institution, visited by locals and travelers alike. In the late 20th century, it was an iconic zoo, admired and beloved. Its animals were content, its visitors happy, its staff respected. Chairman of the Zoo Board, D.E. A proposed expansion project might drum up new interest in the zoo\u2026 if the city approves the new zoning plan. But rumors persist that a multiplex is offering more money to buy up a chunk of the zoo land, displacing animals and staff forever. Are these the last days of the Brookmarket Zoo? Or can someone with powerful ambition change things for the better?

      Another October playset! Well, sort of. We've seen lots of zombie playsets around, but we thought James Gabrielsen got it right with his "plug-in" approach in Living Dead, a zombie apocalypse add-on for any playset!

      Everything has changed. No more paperwork or errands or ballgames. We still fight and squabble and lie as much as ever, but not for money or drugs or power. When the world has been overrun by the living dead, survival is the only thing that matters. Banding together always seems like a good idea in theory; strength in numbers, right? Keep believing that if you want, but those people just might kill you before the zombies. Remember, only one thing matters: staying alive.

      This supplement is an add-on, designed to be used with another playset. Zombies in a nice southern town? Perfect. Zombies in the Old West? Of course! Zombies on the grassy knoll? Oswald is the least of Kennedy\u2019s problems.

      Our November Playset of the Month is Tartan Noir, by Dave Bradshaw:

      There\u2019s been a murder. A body lies dead in a run-down apartment. There are signs of a struggle. Broken furniture litters the floor. Two of the victim\u2019s fingers have been cut off. There\u2019s a human turd on the mantelpiece. Hang on, what?

      Welcome to the world of Tartan Noir: fiction with a hardboiled, cynical, violent and frequently alcoholic view of crime in modern-day Scotland. There are good guys, there are bad guys but there definitely aren\u2019t any heroes. Tartan Noir is about world-weary anti-heroes, deeply flawed characters with a variety of vices that would not normally be becoming of someone of their narrative stature. And while it is certainly hardboiled, there\u2019s frequently an edge of wackiness that separates Tartan Noir from its American cousin. So pour yourself a dram of single malt and prepare to indulge in a little Tartan Noir.

      This playset is heavily inspired by Scottish crime writers, in particular Ian Rankin and Christopher Brookmyre. Any of Rankin\u2019s Inspector Rebus series and Brookmyre\u2019s Where the Bodies are Buried and When the Devil Drives would be excellent fuel for your hardboiled, whisky-drinking anti-hero. For a touch of whimsical craziness, Brookmyre\u2019s Jack Parlabane or Angelique de Xavia novels will provide excellent inspiration.

      It\u2019s December, and for our final 2012 Playset of the Month, we put together Touring Rock Band 2, an affectionate sequel to the original Touring Rock Band, written as a collaboration by some of our favorite Playset authors!

      You were supposed to live fast and die young. At least you lived fast. Now you are not so young. Not so famous. Not quite able to rise to the sybaritic occasion the way you did back in the crazy days. Back when you were the big deal, touring the world behind an album that could have\u2014should have\u2014gone platinum. You had your moment and now it has passed. The band broke up, over sex and money and a lot of stuff that seems trivial now.

      You\u2019ve had to tone it down a little, accept a new sort of reality that involved trashing fewer hotel rooms and paying more bills on time and getting prostate exams that are actually prostate exams. But the dreams never died, and lately the various anemic side projects have been less and less satisfying. And as bad decisions and old bridges burned come back to haunt you, the idea of revisiting past glory seems better and better. God knows you need the cash. And you still have fans on the Internet who would kill to see the band get back together\u2026

      It\u2019s Been a Good Run

      This is our 36th Playset of the Month release, and in addition to being the last of 2012, it\u2019s the last one we plan on publishing on a monthly schedule. Thanks To everyone who submitted a Playset, published their own Playset, or rocked out to a Playset of the Month!

      In the future (2013 and beyond) we plan on publishing Playsets a bit less frequently, and focusing on high quality collections of playsets as well as the occasional really great ones that cross our desks and demand to be shared. Look for a further announcement about the first of those releases soon. You can expect it to be a complete trainwreck!

      It\u2019s been a while since an official Fiasco playset has been spotted in the wild. We\u2019ve been deep in the swamps and finally we\u2019ve returned to bring you this astounding proof: please enjoy this special playset release, The Beast of Sucker Creek.

      Sightings of a hairy, man-like creature in and around Sucker Creek go back hundreds of years\u2014the legends of the Rayado people tell of Ni-teshi-ih; \u201cThe Strong One\u201d who lived in the dark swamps of the Sucker Creek basin.

      Since white settlement of the region, reports of blood-chilling cries in the night and occasional sightings of the Beast have been a fixture of local gossip. Is it a madman? A crazed bear? Something strange and wholly new to science? No one knows, but those who have crossed paths with it are convinced of one thing\u2014the Beast of Sucker Creek is real, and you don\u2019t want to make it angry.

      Last summer\u2019s massacre at Camp Clearwater rocked Barrow Pines, casting a pall over our sleepy town. Only when the inconclusive police investigation and media circus died down and we tried to put our lives back together did we realize our nightmare had just begun.

      Barrow Pines is full of secrets, and the deeper we dig into the mystery, the more questions we unearth. Those of us who have dared look have come to one inescapable conclusion: whatever killed all those people last year is still out there, and it will kill again.

      To stop the horror once and for all, we must return to where it all started. We must return to Camp Death.

      The holidays are upon us! Before everyone scurries off to your warm nests to celebrate, we\u2019d like to give you a tasty new playset to enjoy! Newly escaped from Cage A-12-0022, it\u2019s called Rat Patrol, written by me and a special little friend.

      Inspired by modern fables such as Secret of NIMH, Plague Dogs, Watership Down, this playset lets you play the rats, with all that entails. Thanks to the lab, you\u2019re also super smart\u2014as smart as people, maybe smarter. You don\u2019t have thumbs and you can\u2019t do more than squeak, but you share a written language with humans, and perhaps a shared destiny.

      One day you are miserable experiments, destined to have your brains extracted to see what makes you tick, and the next you are blinking in the sunlight, smelling new smells that aren\u2019t disinfectant and terror, dumped on a lawn by kids in balaclavas. You recognize the balaclavas because you have had encyclopedias injected into your brains. First worn by British troops, whoever they were, during the Crimean War, whatever that was. You could recall the details if you thought hard enough but really, now is not the time.

      Now you are free, and you know how rats are supposed to live, and what the dangers of a rat-centric lifestyle are. Behind you is a gleaming tower full of terrible people who did unspeakable things to you and your friends. In front of you is a forest full of encyclopedic dangers you can name but whose scent is entirely unknown. On either side are others like you, rats you lived with, nearly died with, got really smart with. Rattus norvegicus, Sprague-Dawley strain, mostly the offspring of a very special pair named Linda and Milky.

      Time to get moving. Maybe to survive. Maybe to get revenge.

      This playset includes a custom Tilt, which is highly specific and recommended.

      Hey, a new playset! It's called Unaussprechlichen Klutzen...

      Fiasco is all about stupid people reaching for the stars, and in Unaussprechlichen Klutzen, the stupid people are suburban cultists worshiping some nameless abomination and the stars they are reaching for \u2014 well, they are almost right. Not quite, but almost, and this playset is all about making that final delicate alignment to bring about a new dark age. Will they succeed? Who cares! Either way it is going to be an utter Fiasco.

      All you wanted to do was impress the cute redhead from the coffee shop. Several ancient texts later, there\u2019s a mile-high squid striding across the landscape, devastating everything in its path. And you\u2019re pretty sure it just stepped on the redhead.

      This special release playset is by Graham Walmsley, creator of the classic Gangster London. For those not up on their imaginary bibliophilia, the title is an affectionate nod to Frederich Wilhelm von Junzt\u2019s Unaussprechlichen Kulten, or \u201cNameless Cults\u201d, created by Robert E. Howard and later firmly embedded in the Cthulhu Mythos.
