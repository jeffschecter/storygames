  • I need to make a new setting for my game. One option is 'fantasy's greatest hits' where I use the massive stack of material I own, but I've never actually gotten a game like this off the ground because I make it into something cool and then feel like it's not really mine. The other option is something new and original... which is of course a stratospheric amount of work for me (and work for the players too, since they can't fall back on their knowledge of D&D settings).
  • I need to find a way to let the players explore and see more of the world, because I want to show them the cool stuff I made for them.
  • Biggest hurdle: I don't want to write sessions. I'm really good at it, I can do it quickly, and I can reel players into it like I was born to it, but it's exhausting and it usually means my games get put off for ages until I have the energy to prepare them. So I need to find a way to structure my game and be ready for a session without getting inside my players' heads, figuring out their characters, and writing the session where they fall into their roles in my story.

  • Taking out alignment looks like it will be easy enough.
  • Stop being a whiner about the dice. I've been using GM rolls all the dice because I've been lazy and concentrating on taking over airspace from the big chair at the end of the table.

    \n 1. Make the setting sketchy. Don't in fact, work out a massive amount of material. Just enough that you like it, and it has the feel that you want (as opposed to having the kitchen sink feeling you mentioned).
    \n
    \n2. Allow players to have a hand in defining parts of the setting. When you do, this is a flag that indicates what they're interested in.
    \n
    \n3. Have play rotate around the characters. Quite simply, don't make up a "story" or "plot." Just make up events that happen that:
    \n A) Are presented in terms of getting the PCs reactions to them, and
    \n B) Give the player room to create responses as freely as possible.
    \n
    \nYes, whether or not we're talking narrativism, I'm talking something like bangs, where you're focusing game events on giving players choices. To be sure you're doing this, always write out the question that the event asks the player to answer. If you know the answer, then rework the event until you don't know the answer to the question.
    \n
    \n4. Base these events around the "cool stuff" that you want to show them. Don't ask them if their character wants to go there, simply have that be the basis for play. Get your players complicit in this:
    \n
    \nGM: So I've got the baron's court worked out, and I want to move the action there. Please, each of you tell me the funnest reason you can think of for why your character has moved there.
    \n
    \nActually, in practice, when you play this way, you'll find that this is pretty much unneccessary. You can force characters anywhere you like, and players won't call it railroading as long as at the end of the move they have fun decisions to make. Actually, often the fun decision will actually indicate the plausible reason why the character is now there - again, keep these decisions about the PCs goals, dreams, and personalities. As long as the "Cool Stuff" you create keeps that in mind, then the players will love you showing it to them.
    \n
    \nYou can show them the most interestingly developed fantasy terrain imaginable, and they will yawn if there's no decisions to be made about it. But say, "The corridor splits off, left and right" and suddenly you're a genius.
    \n
    \n5. If you don't like raise and teleport, etc, don't allow them. Does this unbalance some classes? I rather think not.
    \n
    \nBetter yet, stop playing an RPG that includes such abilities, and play one that allows magic to be what you want it to be. But that might be off topic, sorry.
    \n
    \n
    \n6. I don't understand your last point at all. Can you elucidate?

    \n Ryan, I'm doing a West Marches type of thing using Classic Red Box; it seems to be working pretty well so far.

    * come up with a couple ideas for dungeons.
    * come up with a couple of "campaign level" NPC's who exist outside of dungeon environments who are "sticky"
    * let the players decide what they wanna do in this context.
    * flesh out the dungeon they pick
    * maybe insert the "campaign level" NPC's into this thing, or they're affected by the delve and suggest a reaction.
    * aside from that, there is no world "out there" - between any session, players have 3-4 options on what to do next

    Incidentally, I too wanted to play around with the "cool toys," but most of the really good ones (beholders, illithids, demons, githyanki, umber hulks) are missing in Classic Red Box. But the other problem is that "cool toys" seems to keep growing: it now includes certain high level spells (prismatic sphere), prestige classes (shadowdancer), and magical items (deck of many things).

    It would probably be worthwhile to identify all the "cool toys" in D&D and figure out how they fit harmoniously into a setting.

    \nRyan... you say you don't want to do session prep, at first, because it means you won't end up playing, because you won't do it. Then you say that you don't mind session prep.
    \n
    \nEither way, it doesn't matter. Your goal, so you say, is to get out of the rut of GM-run plots. This is, in fact, precisely what the techniques I'm advocating are intended to do in play. They work incredibly well. The more prep you put in, the better they work, but they work well even with relatively little prep. So no matter how motivated you feel to prep, it's always easy to be ready with enough to make play go well.
    \n
    \nBasically items 3 and 4 above. Frame the action to your cool world locations, and immediately give the players decisions to make. Then allow play to go wherever it will go based on their decisions. Once the players see that the decisions that they make drive where play goes, then they will drive play. It is, in fact, players who see that it doesn't matter what they do, that the play will go as the GM directs, that lose any interest in trying to make decisions.
    \n
    \nAll you have to do is to give them decisions, and be sure that there's no obvious answer to the decisions. And, once they've made those decisions create new decision points from the situation as it has now changed. Make these decisions matter to the players, by making them all about the elements of their characters that interest them.
    \n
    \nThe usual technique for figuring out what decisions are interesting is to look for "flags" on the character. Does player A's character kick all ass in combat? Then he's likely looking for tough fights to participate in, and tactical decisions. Give him combats in which the player can find creative ways to use his character abilities to overcome. Don't come up with a set answer, the "right" way to win this combat. Simply have the situation be complex enough that there's no obvious answer.
    \n
    \nIf another player has it in his character background that he's a member of the wizard's guild, have the guild ask him to to partake in something that's potentially unethical, like taking money for charlatanism (casting an illusion of a dead wife, instead of really putting the grieving husband in contact with her). Tests his ethics, and his allegiance to the guild.
    \n
    \nIf the character in question refuses to perform the unethical act, then have the husband come to him directly and beg, showing that he's in deep pain. Does that change the wizard's mind (do the ends of putting the man at peace justify the means in this case)? If the wizard does the ritual, have him become famous for this, and have lots more people come to him for help. Does he come clean? Does he continue to do more fake readings? Does he declare that he's lost the power?
    \n
    \n
    \nWhat I'm saying may sound like I'm saying that you should prepare by making a "Choose Your Own Adventure" book. And it is, insamuch as prep will assume certain things about the current situation, like where it lies between sessions. But what's interesting about RPGs is that players are free to make up any response. In the case above, the player may decide to pay the husband to not tell anyone that he can't do what the guild claims he can, and then gives money to the guild, telling them that he did the ritual (this way he stays in good with the guild, and doesn't have to do anything unethical with regards to the husband).
    \n
    \nAs such, you can't prepare everything for this sort of play. Once this happens, however, you can extemporize by looking for flags again. You note that the player also included a girlfriend on the character sheet. So, knowing that he's blown all his money on silencing the husband, and paying off the guild, you have her come with a financial need. How does he explain being broke to her?
    \n
    \n
    \nThere's a simple technique checklist you go through when setting up a situation:
    \nA) What question does this ask the player?
    \nB) Do we know what he'll answer?
    \n
    \nIf the answer to B is yes, then retool the question until the answer to B is no, we don't know what the player will do.

    \n Ryan, have you read Chris Chinn's "Flag Framing" and "Conflict Web" essays? That's the sort of stuff Mike is talking about--not preparing events and scenes ahead of time.

    It's kind of like how IaWA creates a scenario without actually saying anything about what will happen... how does it create story without writing any adventure prep? All you need to do is create an unstable situation--i.e. conflict. Once you have that, *something* has to happen, and will, especially if you keep putting pressure on it. You don't have to prep any story/scene material. You don't have to write any "chapters".

    The IaWA method is a shorthand for creating conflict and unstable situations. With 4e, you'll have to develop your own method, as the GM (with as much or as little help from the players, but more makes more fun and less work), for getting tthat unstable situation.

    For example (off the top of my head):

    --Have each player write up one goal their character is pursuing, *at this very moment*. (It's NOT backstory. It's what the character is doing *right now*.)
    --For any two given PCs, create an NPC they each know. This NPC supports PC A's goal (wants it to happen--it's in the NPC's Best Interests, to use a IaWA term), but wants to stop PC B from reaching their goal. I'm sure a million ideas will come to mind as soon as you pick a pair of PCs and think about their goals for about two seconds.

    This gives you your starting "dramatis personae"--X PCs, plus one NPC for each PC pair. To start the game, just frame a scene where one of these NPCs (pick one at random, if you want) takes action towards one of their Best Interests--either provides a character with a means to move towards their goal, or tries somehow to stop one of the characters.

    After that, the game should roll by itself, if you follow Mike's advice. If it ever stalls, and none of the players are chomping at the bit to suggest something, pick a different NPC and frame another scene the same way (just like the opening scene).

    What do you care about in prep? Basically just: "What does this NPC want from this PC?" That's your "TRAPs" for your situational/relationship map. Of course, you can write "Bangs", as well, but they'll be gravy at this point, not necessary.

    However... D&D might make some of this difficult, especially if there is still such a strong reward for killing things, and no good system for non-combat conflicts. In this case, you'll have to create your setup so that there are lots of opportunities for combat. For example, each of those NPCs could be a powerful person or creature who sends troops, demons, assassins, or minions to get what they want. The PCs will find themselves faced by groups of combat-ready characters who want something from them, or want to stop them from doing something.

    The PCs might agree with some of them and do what they want, negotiate with some, and fight others. As in one of the posts towards the end of the "face the dungeon" thread, the combat system will act to resolve conflicts. In play, it will look like this: "After we defeated his Elite Guardsmen at the Bridge of Kazan, the Baron was sufficiently impressed that he decided to cede to our demands, and finally agreed to lend us the map to the Unmapped Reaches." Does that make sense?

    As for keeping the challenge level consistent, you may need to do some clever and flexible prep. For instance, consider creating statblocks for opponents that can used for more than one type of foe. For instance, you might have something like "stats for group of 20 enemies suitable to a party of level 5: customize by adding one special power for Chapter 12 and assigning an appropriate skill to their +6 skill slot." Then, when instead of the Duke's guards, the PCs decide to fight the demon assassins, you just pick up those stats and add the "Shadow Flight" power or whatever, from your list (even better, there'll probably be something like this in the 4e texts that you can use).

    That'll be the challenge--creating encounters you can throw at the PCs in flexible ways. But that should be quite doable, I would think. You can use all the old tricks, like create maps of places without defining where they actually are until the PCs encounter, etc.

    At least, I think I would do it something like the above. I haven't played D&D in ages, so I don't really know.

    \nFor isntance, if we know that a player with a torch will use it to destroy a monster that's known to be susceptible to fire, then putting that monster in front of the torchbearing character isn't at all interesting tactically. If, instead, we note that this is their only torch, and that using it to destroy the creature will use it up, leaving the character in the dark for the rest of the dungeon, now the player has a choice to make, and an interesting one. There's your "Gamism Bang."
    \n
    \nNote that it's often possible for interesting tactical choices to have actual right or wrong answers. In fact, for them to be challenges, they probably have to have these, at least in a theoretical fashion. Often we determine if the player did well by the end outcome of events. The point is not that there can't be a dominant strategy (to use the Game Theory terms), but that this strategy has to be non-obvious. In which case we laud the player if they do discern it.
    \n
    \nFor simmy play, you might have situations where the questions asked are very much "What if?" sorts of things. What if the character with the magic relic ends up at the temple that claims it? How does that interaction work out through exploration?
    \n
    \nGetting outside of GNS preferences, we can look at questions of characterization. The question could be, "How does the character act when put into a public speaking situation?"
    \n
    \nAgain, in all cases, you look for flags from play or on the character sheet or where ever you find them, and create questions around these things that you feel interest your player in working out, in whatever manner they are interested in working them out. To allow for player creativity, you make the answers to the questions non-obvious. Then you take the results of those decisions, and make the situation of play reflect those decisons, so that the player feels that his contributions are having a real effect on the direction of play (since they are).
    \n
    \nAgain, the big benefit to doing this well is that players become more and more proactive as they discern that when they make decisions that they actually have an effect on play. They start to search out more decisions, create them for each other, and even create them for themselves. And then your job becomes really easy.

    \n

Once in the dungeon, give him free rein to do as he will, this is the creativity that the player seeks, and will appreciate you for getting him to it, without having to go through the painful process of playing out the pre-prepared plot that doesn't allow them any choice, and is going to end up at the dungeon anyway.
    \n

    \nIf, on the other hand, by "plot" you mean making thematic decisions, then I'd posit that the "Face the Dungeon" method is fine. That is, let the dungeon be a fun tactical exercize that determines the success or failure of the character's non-tactical choices. Do I chase the Black Knight, or go save the Princess? Either way it leads to the Pit of Doom, but I've gotten to push the plot forward, and creatively displayed my character, and so have gotten to create plot. Plot is not "where we go" but it's "what important choices my character has made, and what that says about him" at least in this context.
    \n
    \nNow, you don't have to do this as grossly as "Face the Dungeon." You can simply "illusion" the same results (even if the illusion is an open fact). That is, instead of the player having to figure out why we're going to the Pit of Doom chasing the Black Knight, you as GM come up with a plausible reason why this is where the Black Knight is fleeing, and not the other foes carrying off the princess. If they go the other way, you rationalize the opposite. Since the player's choice was not intended to create a tactical advantage one way or another, it doesn't matter where the characters end up - only that the goal is what they chose.
    \n
    \n
    \nNow, if you're not comfortable doing that, if you want player choices to mean what they "would mean" given the hard world that you've created, then... well, yeah, you're hosed. There's no solution to this problem. But I'd argue that you've put yourself into this spot, and unneccessarily. Whatever it is that you think you're getting as a benefit to play from this arrangement doesn't nearly counterweigh the obvious problem that it's creating for you.
    \n

Only one side is making the actual choices, and only that side can be getting the satisfaction of having made those choices.

      \n Ryan,

      Could you present your setting in terms of conflicts and situations that the players can decide to sides in, without pre-writing plot? That could help.

      What I mean is, you follow this idea where the players schedule sessions with a goal/destination in mind, based on a "setting" you've prepared, but you present your setting is a dynamic one, with many "tempting bits".

      Instead of "the hills to the North, which were formerly a Dwarven capital, founded during the reign of Boobley the Fourth...", you come out and say, "the hills to the North, where a sword of great power was unearthed by a bunch of adventurers. They were cornered by a tribe of nomads, and threw it down a deep canyon. The tribesmen now hold them prisoner, but are faced by a phalanx of Dwarven soldiers, who arrived minutes after they did. Both want the sword, but neither wants to climb down the canyon first, making themselves vulnerable to the other side." (Your TRAPs method will come in handy here.)

      The PCs could hear this as a rumour or just take it as metagame information. You could present all the locations at once, "frozen in time", or just give them rumours about three or four of them after each session, in which case things will seem to be less static and more "alive".

      The interesting part is that the players can make a number of choices about *what* they want to do, as opposed to just *go there* (in which case, once they get there, you're back to writing the adventure for them). They can tell you that they want to sneak in and retrieve the sword, or that they want to help drive away the tribal warriors, or rescue the adventurers, or start a big battle between the tribe and the dwarves as a diversion, or side with the nomads and do something nasty to the dwarves, and so on.

      In each location, there is some situation, treasure, terrible problem, or what-have-you that provides an opportunity for the players if they step in. You could even set it up in a thematic way, as appropriate for D&D: for instance, each location or event contains a choice between preventing evil or of gaining great wealth/power. Maybe the tribesmen are burning down a nearby village, but if you try to stop them, the dwarves will get the sword before you can get there, or...

      If your setting is logically constructed, as you seem to plan on doing, the repercussions of their actions.will transform the implications of future sessions (the dwarves end up harbouring great anger towards the PCs, which means they will now want to go to that other place and help the minotaurs stop the dwarves from overrunning *their* ancestral home, etc).

      So, is this more *hit* or more *miss*, based on what you're looking at?

      Hi Jason,

      You haven't asked, but I'm going to outline my thoughts a bit further than before. My previous example was kind of nar like (not nessersarily nar, but narartivist themed I think). I'd like to just outline a rough example for three agendas.

      1. You put alot of prep in, and the princess declines the proposal at the end. The game is called the suck because of this.

      2. You put alot of prep in, and due to how circumstances turn out (heavy winter, deep snowfall, echo resonance...), an avalanche occurs that completely destroys the caravan and (say) all the PC's gear stored there (simply because that's where it was stored - it's circumstance/causality). It would just turn out that way, but its called the suck cause it wrecked everything.

      3. You put alot of prep in and the players lose - and they mope and act like it sucked. Bad losers. Or alternatively you put alot of prep in, they win, then treat it like it was completely ephemeral and nothing to them. Bad winners.


      In each case, the WEIGHT of all that prep pushes you to go against what OUGHT to happen.

      This sets up what I think is a writers block situation, because the writer applies force against himself. It's railroading yourself as you do prep - railroading yourself down the track you think will make your players happy. The princess accepts the proposal - railroaded. The avalanche never happens - railroaded. Difficulty is turned down (or up) - railroaded.

      We know what it's like to be railroaded in play. And here in prep, the work and the quire legitimate need for payoff is setting up the prepper to railroad himself. A direct analogy would be were movie producers, because of the vast sums of money involved, rewrite the ending of a movie because the test audience weren't happy with it.

      Or in short, I think you can apply force to yourself/railroad yourself. Just outlining the idea in case it sparks some interested discussion

      \n No, it isn't (in terms of what I refer to). The princess example is probably clearest.

      Okay, with real life glove puppets, the puppet is imaginary, right? But it's your hand inside - if you stick the puppet in a blender, your hand gets blended. That isn't imaginary at all - it's not just happening to some imaginary character, your hand is indeed being blended.

      Tying this back into prep - the massive amount of prep involved prompts this force, while the disconnection between the writer and the character makes the writer think they can apply force as they wish.
