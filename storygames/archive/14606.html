\n So I've been reading Vincent Baker's Dice & Clouds series from 2009.

I've also been reading Justin Alexander's essays on Dissociated Mechanics and Role-playing Games vs. Storytelling Games.

It seems to me that these two are talking past each other, because what Baker's analysis is missing is the association/dissociation that Alexander is talking about. For example, Baker analyzes a generic tabletop system circa 1990 as follows: \n


If you roll 10+, your character hits mine.

...And you mark 2d6 hit points off my character sheet.

...And if it's 8 or more hit points, your character knocks my character down.
\nNote that he says that marking off 2d6 hit points is purely abstract, dice-to-dice - i.e. it involves modification of stats but does not impact the fiction. Justin, however, suggests that this depends on the particular mechanic. Many formal mechanics have an inherent fiction meaning, because they are associated. Suppose this is GURPS, and the character has 16 hit points. If I say "Take 2 hit point damage" or "Take 12 hit points damage" - either of these immediately convey some fictional meaning, even if I don't add any other verbal description. The players might not picture the same details, but they know that the former is a blow that did not land solidly - whereas the latter was a very damaging blow. By comparison, if I say "Lose one Fate Point", that has no fictional meaning - i.e. it is not associated with the fiction.

A good case for this is range. For example, a mechanic might say that you have a +2 if the opponent is closer than 30 feet away. Now, if we are using a hex map and miniatures, then the range modifier is a formal interaction - i.e. dice-to-dice. However, if we are only using verbal descriptions, then the range might be a part of the cloud - in which case the range modifier is cloud-to-dice. I think a vital part of this, however, is that the range is associated. Even if it is formally noted, it has a fictional meaning.

Any thoughts on this?

    \n To add some more pieces to the puzzle (or is it mud to the water?), fictional (and not) positioning fit into this too. We had some discussion of this recently, and two more terms have cropped up that may be useful.

    To re-cap:
    Fictional Position: generating events or resources in a purely fiction manner (ie through narration, in-character descriptions or other elements of free play) that give your character effectiveness to overcome obstacles and challenges. May or may not connect to mechanical effectiveness.

    New stuff:
    Tactical Positioning: interacting with mechanics in ways that affect player or character effectiveness. For example, choosing a weapon which has a stronger attack or longer reach than another, or choosing to use a power that affects another character or element in the game world. The act engaging of or the relative state of mechanical effectiveness wielded by a player on behalf of a character or other element of a game.

    Geographic Positioning: moving pieces about on a board or other abstraction of the game world or fiction narrative. For example, knights and pawns on a chess board, or a mini on a D&D map. The real spacial relationship between the game pieces has bearing and effect on the fictional elements they are related to or represent.

    Disassociated mechanics have weak or no fictional positioning behind them. Games with no fiction often rely strongly on geographic positioning to determine the tactical positioning.

    Range crosses this: it uses fictional positioning to give you tactical advantage. But it is often very weakly held (eg you don't have to describe the battlefield to say you are medium range).

      I feel like an important thing hasn't come up, because everyone's been talking about "the fiction". I'd rather use the associated term, "Shared Imagined Space." What this gains us is that fundamental word there -- "Shared".

      "Take 12 points of damage" may have some impact on the fictions, or it may not. But, generally speaking, it doesn't have an impact on the *Shared* Imagined Space. It's that there are numerous layers of mechanics to keep all the players' fictions synchronized, all Shared. It's more about keeping everyone's personal cloud overlapping with everyone else's than it is about how big those clouds are, or what's in them.

One, I think, also has to be wary of arrows that just loop around from dice-to-dice, without touching intimately on the SIS at all.

      \n Hrm. This might be a drift, it might not; I'll take a stab at this:

      Abstract mechanic: The mechanic is not dependent on the SIS; that is, there's no arrow from the cloud into the dice.

      Concrete mechanic: The mechanic is dependent on the SIS; that is, there is an arrow from the cloud into the dice.


      So, some examples:

      "A Magic-User has d4 hit points per level." This is Abstract. Note that "Magic-User" is something that resides in the dice; it has a specific mechanical meaning and isn't informed by the Cloud -- it's not like any old being who happens to use magic is subject to this.

      "If a character mentions they're on a mission from King Osric, they gain a +2 bonus to Diplomacy rolls with the guards." This is Concrete. There isn't anything sitting there on the character sheet or in the other mechanical physical tools that tells you what the character has mentioned; that arrow comes straight out of the Cloud of the SIS.

      "When a character has the high ground, he gains a +2 to hit." This is one of the trickier ones. In effect, we don't know enough about the game to say whether this is Concrete or Abstract. If there's miniatures and maps and those are used to determine high ground, then this is Abstract; the rule in that case is equivalent to "If a character miniature is on a square with a higher elevation than the square his target occupies, he gains a +2 to hit." That makes it clearer that no cloud is involved.
      On the other hand, if the relative positioning of the characters exists in the SIS, then it is Concrete. If there's no physical prop to reference, then the information has to come out of the cloud.

      Take especial note that it makes absolutely no difference what's going on with the arrows leading out from the mechanic; there's no difference to Abstractness and Concreteness between "If a character loses half his hit points or more from a single blow, his actions are at -2 for 1d4 rounds" and "If a character loses half his hit points or more from a single blow, he is very sad." The output is irrelevant to this particular thing.

      I don't think that the entirety of dissociated mechanics can be summed up as a dissociation between player decisions and character decisions, but it's certainly the easiest to define.

      Associated: Player makes a decision to cast 3.5 fireball. The mechanics of this spell (like all mechanics) are abstracted, but the player is making a decision to cast a spell based on the number of times it can be cast in a day, the amount of damage it does (which increases based on the perspicacity of the spellcaster), the amount of space being targeted, the target's weakness against fire, etc. etc. etc. And all of these decisions (although mechanical in nature) are directly associated to the character's decision: The character knows he has a limited number of spells per day, he knows the spell does more damage based on the spellcaster's ability, he knows how much space the spell will consume, he knows that the white dragon is vulnerable to flames, etc. etc. etc.

      Dissociated: Player makes a decision to use 4E Trick Strike. The mechanics of this ability (like all mechanics) are abstracted, but the player is making a decision to use the ability based on the knowledge that he can only use it once per day (purely mechanical with no relationship to the character's decision) or he's choosing to use narrative authority to dictate that such a shot has appeared at just this moment and can be taken (a narrative control with no relationship to the character's decision). The character obviously isn't choosing to exercise narrative control. Nor is the character choosing whether or not to take the shot based on his knowledge that he will not be able to make such a shot again today (that's not knowledge he possesses).

      It's like a football receiver: It may be true that they're unlikely to make a really amazing catch more than once per game. But never once will you hear them say, "Well, I decided not to make that catch because I figured I might need an Amazing Catch in the 4th Quarter." Nor will you hear them say, "I really wish I could have caught that touchdown in the 4th Quarter, but I'd already used up my Amazing Catch for the day on that play in the first half."
