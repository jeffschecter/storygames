Joshua BishopRoby wrote...
Outside of World of Darkness drek and my most recent gaming, nearly all the gaming I've ever done has been 'doing stuff at the bidding of somebody with moral authority.' But maybe I'm just weird.

That's interesting. For the most part, I have felt that RPGs have avoided moral authority. That is, PCs are often rootless wanderers who are outside of the social system (i.e. fantasy adventurers, superheroes, shadowrunners, and so forth). Adventures tend to have the PCs being hired for money, but not recognized by the law. There are plenty of exceptions, like Star Trek and Dogs in the Vineyard, but I've felt they're uncommon.

I think this is important to play, but I'm interested in other people's experiences and views.

    When I was younger, so much younger than today, we did a lot of "working for the king" type of play. It was an easy road to adventure, with a big boss guy who sent you out on missions and quests. We didn't get a lot of moral authority until Pendragon -- but we had military authority in plenty. Good old imperialistic use of force solved all the problems, in the end.

    As I got "better" at the illusionism I moved away from that structure, however, as it gave to obvious a map for the use of force. I found ways of pushing players and characters around through more diffuse networks, and so moved into the rootless drifter and random adventurer group for a bit, then got into the mercinary and outside-the-code hero motif for the longest time.

    These days, however, I do focus a lot on moral authority (why your character is good enough to be acting as he does -- more Raymond Chandler than Mallory, though) and very little on working for the king. Which means that a lot of play ends up revolving around discovery and proof of the PC's moral and ethical character, as it is something that needs to be shown rather than just assumed.

    Interestingly, the social authority angle is often absent as well. Much as I still like cop and knight games in theory, these days most of my games have characters who are in a dubious position vis a vie the establishment. I suppose that's the dying dregs of my punk adolescence and anti-authoritarian 20s dying out slowly. Games like Dogs seem to have us moving back towards social authority characters, and we're looking more at the "now that you have power and authority, what do you do with it" type games than the endless coming of age stories of earlier years.

    Of course, Dogs is interesting in that you have both social and moral authority and an essentially endless quest -- but neither in the typical mode for RPGs -- and (in general) no "king" or boss that you're working for. Dogs work for the church, sure, and are doing what they're doing for God -- but most of the time neither of those things is all that important in the structure of the game.

    I've run Dogs-like games before I knew there was a Dogs.

    My 1986 AD&D campaign had PC clerics and paladins (and other attached folk) traveling from town to town, investigating crimes and judging the guilty. Sometimes they executed people.

    One of my diffuse and poorly-communicated points in the original thread was that, in your typical Fantasy setting, moral and martial authority go hand in hand.     I suspect -- and this may be just my biases -- that the vast majority of gaming places the PCs in positions of moral authority. Not as bishops and theologians, mind, but the people who come into a situation and right the wrongs that no one else is dealing with. That's how stories work.

    Hmmm, moral or social authority...

    Concentrating on campaigns that went more than a couple sessions, in the earliest games, the PC's were basically like forty-niners in the California gold rush, except with somewhat higher mortality rates. When the dungeon ran out off stuff to loot and the GM didn't bother to make a new one, the PC's went through a sort of "bust" where they scraped by in town and got into trouble fighting other ruffians. Eventually they were hired as caravan guards and fought off a tribe of injuns--er, goblins. No moral authority there, just "Ya gotta do what ya gotta do."

    And in fact that was the tone of much later gaming, although I suppose there were social connections and inner morals which constrained and to some extent motivated character actions. It's interesting to note, though, that a couple characters in one of my favorite campaigns had "personal missions" that might have been embryonic quests--but neither of these really had an opportunity to focus play, and in fact if they had, I think the game would have been worse since they'd have become prima donnas within party-based play.

    Other games had more overt missions in which the characters did assume authority: they actively allied with and to some extent led one side or the other in some kind of political/military conflict. The issues were more cultural than moral, though--generally a resistance or liberation struggle of some kind. In the chaotic situation, though, excercising authority meant killing for a cause, with your own two hands. Sometimes one had an opportunity to also lead a small group of followers as well. In short we were basically a "guerilla cell", sometimes with aspirations of becoming an army. Now what might have made that more interesting for the hardcore Nar types would be if we'd had to not only pick at the enemy troops and fortifications, but also fight for hearts and minds and punish collaborators--but we never made things that tough on ourselves.

    Joshua BishopRoby wrote...
    Rootless wanderers, in my experience, often have a great deal of moral authority -- I mean, the pseudo-default rootless wanderer is Kane from Kung Fu, who wandered the world making things right. Or, you know, the A-Team. In such scenarios, the primary character being outside the social structure lets them be the impartial moral force of the story.

    Actually, I was talking about "moral authority" in a sense of being in a social position such that people look to that source for their morality. In other words, "moral authority" is different than "morality". Kane can act morally without being a "moral authority". Indeed, he has to struggle uphill to get people to listen to him.

    The key question is, do NPCs look to the PCs for moral guidance? If so, then the PCs themselves are a moral authority. The other question is, do the PCs look to someone else for moral guidance? Is so, then they are acting for another moral authority.

    I find that either of these are rare. PCs rarely take to heart lessons from an NPC, and conversely NPCs rarely take to heart lessons from the PCs. This is related, I think, to my blog comment "Humble NPCs and Power Relations".

    I recently ran an Exalted campaign that fell apart over some of these issues. One of my goals was to examine the moral space that the characters were creating around themselves. I was fairly explicit that the world was in a state of moral decay and that there was no moral authority that the characters could turn to for guidance. Their patron essentially told them that they held the responsibility and that he couldn't be bothered with such things.

    The players weren't willing to accept themselves as potential moral authorities, though. They kept looking for external guidance.
