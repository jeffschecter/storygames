    \n My advice is: whichever two or so players finish their characters first, they get particular strengths, and nobody else does. Nobody NEEDS one.

    Then, if someone who didn't get one thinks of one in the first hour or so of play, they can have it then. Or hell, whenever. As long as they don't insist on a do-over because NOW they thought of one they could have used THEN, no harm done.

    This applies to you too! You never need to spend one second agonizing over a particular strength. Take them only when they pop out at you.

    Oh just to be clear: this is my advice to you, not me pronouncing on the rules. All my advice here is legit by the rules (or close enough, in the case of "or hell, whenever"). But it doesn't encompass the entirety of what's legit by the rules. For instance, it's also legit for everybody to get one and for you to agonize over them, I just don't recommend it to you.

    -Vincent

    \n Particular Strengths balance themselves mechanically. You don't have to worry about about "justifying" their use or how broad/narrow they are, or anything like that. Someone can use their PS on every roll, someone else can go chapter after chapter without ever having one. It all works fine.

    (Also: Directly and Covertly at the same time is cool. That came up in our last game as well. Not as cool as the Violence and Love combo, but close. My advice: never ever ever argue about the forms someone picks for a roll. Just nod and say, "Great. What do you do? How?" and whatever they say is fine. Ask for details; don't ask for justifications.)

    \n Don't argue. Ask for details. Ask how. It's there in the book. "When someone asks you for details, provide them."

    "I use magic."
    "How?"
    "I put a spell on their mind."
    "Cool. What does it look like when you cast it?"
    "A blue mist forms in my palm and I blow it into their face. Then we can see the blue mist behind their eyes and we know they're under the spell."

    "I'm rolling Directly and Covertly."
    "What are you doing?"
    "I'm walking up acting all innocent, then I'm sucker-punching him in the face."
    "How do you punch him? Like, all kung-fu style?"
    "Nah. Just a balled fist smashed into his nose."

    Like that.

    I'm not saying you can't have an opinion. If someone rolls For Myself and With Violence, then when they say what their character does, it should be for themselves and violent. If they say, "I bake a delicious cake for Jerry," then clearly, something is amiss. Instead of arguing, though, asking for details and asking how is a good way to dig deeper. "You bake a cake? How is that violent?" If they have an answer, it might be awesome. Playing the standards-cop ("All use of Forms must be approved by me!") is much less fun, in my experience.

    \n "What if they DON'T have an answer?"

    You pick up dice at two key moments in play, so the answer depends on which.

    Here's the first key moment in which you pick up dice: you say your character's action ("I stab you in the face!") and someone else's character can and will interfere ("like hell!")

    You've already said your character's action. Thus, you've already committed to your dice. Half the time, as GM, I just tell you which dice to pick up, if you haven't already done it ("okay! Directly and with violence, go!"). Sometimes more than one combination might fit, and then it's your choice, so I'll ask ("so that's with love, for others, and covertly, yeah? Your choice which two").

    If you're going to roll a particular strength, you need to have already included it in saying what your character does ("I stab you in the face, screaming the names of the jackal spirits!").

    If you haven't already said concretely what your character's doing, picking up dice at all is premature. Here's my current favorite example: "I get your sandwich from you! Directly and for myself!" "Like hell - wait, I mean, how?" "I offer you five bucks for it!" "Oh. I don't try to stop THAT."

    So that's the first key moment. Since the dice follow the action, you should never run into cases where the action doesn't match the dice. If you do, stop and go back, establish the action first.

    The other key moment when you pick up dice is when I say what MY character does, and YOURS can and will try to interfere. This is the more complicated case, because you haven't already said what your character's going to do. Here's how it can go:

    "I stab you in the face! Directly, with violence!"

    1. If the dice you pick up are obvious, nobody's going to ask you to justify them. "Like hell. I'm going to roll directly and with violence too." "Makes sense."

    2. If you feel like it, you can say a sentence to set up your choice of dice. "Like hell. If you get past me that means EVERYBODY's screwed. I'm going to roll for others and for myself." "Makes sense."

    3. If anybody asks you to justify your choice of dice, you have to, or choose different dice. "Like hell. I'm going to roll for others and for myself." "Really? How do you figure?" "If you get past me, that means EVERYBODY's screwed." "Oh, sure, makes sense."

    4. Even if nobody asks you to justify your dice, have a plan. The reason they didn't ask is because they're trusting you, don't screw around with them. "Like hell. I'm going to roll covertly and with love." "Yeah? This I gotta see."

    5. If you don't have a plan, be prepared to revise your action after you've rolled until it fits your dice. Fair's fair. How come you're screwing around like that?

    All 5 go for particular strengths, too, exactly the same.

    Now, here's a wicked important thing. This matters most during the first round of the action sequence, and it matters most for the person who wins initiative - for the first action of the first round. The further you get from the opening action of the opening round, the less commitment your choice of dice represents. KEEP ROLLING THE SAME DICE, unless you negotiate otherwise, no matter what you have your character do. When you answer, and when you go on to round 2 and round 3 - a mismatch between what your character does and what dice you roll doesn't matter a bit.

    You're stuck - or blessed - with the dice you opened with, no matter where the action sequence takes you (again, unless you negotiate otherwise).

    Make sense? Followup questions welcome.
