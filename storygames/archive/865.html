MikeRM
June 2006 edited June 2006 in Story Games
This: http://www.story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=44&page=1#Item_50 (graphical character sheets) triggered an idea: a game in which characters have powers which are linked to the planets (either our familiar planets or your own set), which wax and wane according to the position of the relevant planet versus the character's current position. Not in a linear way, though (strongest when overhead, weakest underfoot). No, no, too simple. In astrology, planets' influence is strongest overhead, when rising, when setting, and in imum coeli (i.e. the opposite to overhead), and they have different types of influence in these positions. See Wikipedia for details. Also, the relative positions of the different planets are relevant, and they are also complex (in opposition, in conjunction, in square - which I think means at right angles - and so forth). There is astrological software that can work this all out.
I'm just tossing out a random thought here - but has anyone heard of any game in which something like this was used? The drawbacks are obvious: Having to keep track of game time and location very accurately, and having complicated calculations (or constantly consulting a computer or charts). Maybe the good idea buried inside this is some way in which powers change over time and location so that characters don't always have the same relative powers to each other or the same absolute powers.

    The trick would be to do it so that it was interesting and added to the game instead of being a pointless and boring mechanic.

    I suggest that the solution to this is dumping this:

    Having to keep track of game time and location very accurately, and having complicated calculations (or constantly consulting a computer or charts)

    Here's a quick mechanics sketch -

    • Characters have an astrological sign
    • When you want to inflict consequences on a character, you have to move an appropriate planet into that sign's house.
    • You move planets with some kind of time resource, pushing them along their paths. At certain points along the path, the board informs you to advance other planets. (There's no attempt to match these to the relative orbit speeds of the planets; it's simply a matter of making your decisions have consequences.)
    • When you create certain conjunctions, you can inflict subsidiary effects. They don't do whatever 'winning a conflict' does, but instead mechanically manipulate resources and so on.

    OK, new thoughts on this, stimulated by this thread on the new Koplow dice catalog.

    Fred mentions Keplerian solids. One of Kepler's theories that didn't quite work out (though it was a nice try) was that the orbits of the planets would fit within imaginary nested Platonic solids. You know, like those dice you play RPG with. (This isn't actually what's meant by Keplerian solids, that's something slightly different, but I'm bouncing off the idea in a lateral kind of way here.)
    I have this odd but fascinating book right here that I got for the geometric art that's another interest of mine - Sacred Geometry: Philosophy and Practice by Robert Lawlor (Thames and Hudson, 1982) - which has an illustration of Kepler's vision of the solar system; looks like it goes (from the outside) cube (d6), tetrahedron (d4), dodecahedron (d12), octahedron (d8), icosahedron (d20). (See the Wikipedia article on Kepler for the same illo.)

    So, at character creation you throw 2d6. 1=Mercury, 2=Venus, 3=Earth, 4=Mars, 5=Jupiter, 6=Saturn (the six Keplerian planets). This gives your character two planetary affinities, a primary and a secondary. For example, someone can be mercurial and martial, meaning they first of all will resort to trickery and fast talk, but if pressed will fight. This gives you an instant "type" to play.

    So someone challenges someone else in the game (PC or NPC, doesn't matter, all are generated the same way). The challenge will have a planetary sphere associated with it: physical conflict = Mars, etc. This determines one of the dice used (the one that has the shape contained by the orbit of the planet - so a d12 for Mars). Because there are six planets and five platonic solids, Mercury gets a d10 (which isn't a platonic solid). The other die used will be the one associated with each player's primary planetary affinity.

    You roll the dice and narrate according to what comes up - there are two simultaneous conflicts going on, one in the sphere indicated by the main dice, but another in the spheres - likely to be different - indicated by the players' affinity dice, and all of this gets narrated. (For example: Sphere of Mars, physical conflict. Challenger is mercurial and veneral, challenged is earthy and jovial. At the same time that they're trying to skewer each other, the challenger is trying to convince the challenged that she should give up, using fast talk; the challenged is taking a stable stance and refusing.)
    The two dice each person rolls are evaluated separately, so you could win the physical contest (on d12s) but lose the other contest (d10 versus d8). You can then switch to your second die and have another contest on those terms. The challenged, having won one of the two contests, can either switch dice or not to deal with the new challenge. In this case, keeping with the d8 would be smarter since joviality only gives you a d4, versus the challenger's d20. Again, the outcome is narrated by both players based on their die rolls (indicating how successfully they deployed their planetary affinities).

    The stakes could also be related to the planetary affinities - and the size of the dice, which would balance things a bit (otherwise the poor old jovials will be kicked around all the time).

    Again, this is a thought-starter, not a fully worked-out system, but it makes for nice quick character generation, and the narrative emerges out of the dice rolls in an organic way.
