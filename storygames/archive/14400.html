So we act like we're going to game with the intentional, executive-function parts of the other people at the game table.

\n

But really, we have to game with the jumbled pile of person.

\n

That includes, potentially, and case-by-case:

\n
    \n
  • the hungry
    • \n
  • the angry
    • \n
  • the lonely
    • \n
  • the tired
    • \n
  • the broke / laid off
    • \n
  • the insecure
    • \n
  • the mentally ill (well-managed or not)
    • \n
  • the one without other creative outlets
    • \n
  • the one who's there to be a good friend
    • \n
  • the one who needs to be better than everyone else at this
    • \n
  • the one who doesn't even remember why snakes bother them
    • \n
\n

What do you do for other jumbled piles of persons? What do you do about your own pile when it rears its ugly head?

    \n I'm not sure how it's any different than dealing with broken/damaged/dysfunctional people in any other social situation.

    Stated another way, I guess I disagree with your opening premise: that we act like we're going to game with the intentional, executive-function parts of the other people etc etc. I, personally, think it's sort of shitty to expect/demand that people leave their broken bits at the door and bring (only) their A-game, you know? It's just friends, gaming is just another kind of social occasion, and if we can't handle each other's dysfunctions then maybe we shouldn't be friends.

    Before we go any further I want to make be clear that I really think everybody's in the same class (i.e. everyone is a "jumbled pile of person")

    \n

    Paul, your post is perfect because it shows the distinction I'm trying to make. The dysfunctions / reality of the players is treated as something that is outside of or disrupting play. There's very little in the way of advice for the upset player that doesn't amount to "try not to be upset, get a lid on it, keep playing."

    \n

    For example, if something that happens at the table makes me upset, focusing on it - or even calling time out or tryin to take a break from the table for a minute - can be very disruptive, especially if I'm not sure yet why I'm upset.

    \n

    One technique I use when I'm struggling with anxiety is to try to be predictable. Play characters with simple motivations and stick with that.

    \n

    That's the kind of thing I'm looking for in this thread.

    \n I feel the key thing is to surround yourself with people who will call you out, lovingly, on your pile when it rears its head. Those same people will also understand that when their piles rear up and you call them on it that you are doing so with love. Love or some reasonable facsimile there of, anyway. Sincere honesty at least.

    It never seems to be quite that simple in practice but it works a hell of a lot better than waiting for people to change, or spending your time with people with which there is no mutual flow of empathy. I find that sometimes a person has a pile that is so overwhelmingly intense that they don't really have much room for the whole mutual empathy thing. Their pile is all consuming.

    So, I guess I'm saying be human, be kind, but be ready to make the hard choices when you realize that a pile (yours or others) is going to destroy whatever it is that you've gathered to do together.

    Posted By: RyA related question, what do you do when someone is really bad at reading other people's offers / tells, and is driving you crazy?
    \nOkay, now *that* is an interesting question.

    I've had a few players who are just really bad at engaging at the table. They're engaging just fine with the fiction and with the GM, especially in a really traddie setup where the GM is the player's eyes and ears. But there's no need to sympathize or empathize with the GM in this setup: the player assumes the GM is impartial. Of course this is nonsense in every measurable way, which is why you see tables where some players do a better job of gaming the GM than others, and how the players who don't game the GM can feel resentful toward those other players (although, ironically and probably instructively, do not feel resentful toward the GM!).

    Some things I've done:

    * Really really emphasize scenes where the PCs are interacting with each other -- and refuse, as GM, to engage with either player while this is going on. I've used the phrase "hub or web?" with players in the past, particularly when you've got a dynamic where every interaction is GM->Player A->GM, GM->Player B->GM, GM->Player C->GM. If I find each player is talking to *me* as GM, rather than to other players, I stop, look at the other player, and gesture toward that player to indicate that that's where they should be talking.

    * Reframe or rephrase a player's offer if it's obvious that the crazy-making player can't read the offer. Not as a GM -- that is, not with anything mechanical attached -- but as a facilitator.

    * On that note, recruit your better player(s) to become facilitators. Easier if you're the GM, since you're kind of in a unique position in that you're forced to listen to the whole table all the time. I'll often go to my good player(s) and say "You know, Chris is having some trouble hooking into the situation. If you offer to help him in a scene, I'll help prompt him to at least consider it."

    * Recruiting players as facilitators also works when you recruit the crazy-making player. I'll even deliver a white lie: "You know, Jason is having some trouble hooking into the situation." (I'll say this to Chris, the problem player). "Can you help me out by facilitating situations for him to hook into? He really wants to play up the rich clueless guy angle, so if he starts acting all clueless, go with it! It'll be fun for him." Or whatever.

    * Ask the crazy-making player to GM for a while. It'll teach the player to listen to the whole table, which I think is a skill very much in short supply among players.

    \n My only advice is:

    Lead by example, and be ready to give guidance when and if they ask for it.

    I've seen some truly awesome people who visually change when they sit down at the table. Body posture, face geometry, tone, everything. They throw themselves headlong into a game, siezing the short opportunity to make something awesome, and then shift back into themselves when it's over. I didn't realize what was possible until I met them.

    And the flip side, they were able to articulate what they love, and why they do it when I asked them. It totally wouldn't have worked if they prosthelatized at the table, I don't think...

    I'm totally ready to hear some advice from everyone else. This is a good thread so far.
