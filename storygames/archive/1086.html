The appealing and well-formed first pdf

Matthijs
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Story Games
This is a rant. Hopefully, it's helpful. If not, at least I get to blow off some steam.

There's a million game designers out there now, clamoring for attention. Some of them even have rules they want people to read and comment on. I know, I'm several of them, and I feel their pain.

You want me to read your game? You have a document online, right? Here's what to do - and what not to do.

Tell me straight away what the game is about. The very first paragraph after the title. My time is a very limited resource. Don't start with a long intro, lots of setting-heavy info, tables of rules I don't understand before I've played at all, a short story.

Only tell me what I need to know. You can add fluff, color and verbiage later. Don't give me thirty pages of stuff to read that I don't even know if I'm going to use in the game. Don't tell me about yourself, your influences and your gaming history; if I want to know, I'll ask, or you can put it in the designer notes of the book you publish in a year or two.

Show me the flow. After telling me what it's about, tell me the basic concepts - the skeleton of play. Then, afterwards, you can give me the heavier stuff, if I need to know it. Don't spread the rules throughout the document so I have to read everything twice to know how the game is supposed to flow.

If you can't be bothered trying to explain me the rules in an understandable fashion, why on earth should I read them, let alone playtest them?

    Dude, I feel your pain. Been there.

    It occurred to me over the weekend that a few more guidelines might help - things I learned while tech writing. As follows:

    1. Use outlines. Some people outline first and build to the outline, some get partway through or even to the "end" and then outline and reorganize. Whatever works for you. But make sure you end up with something with a clear outline that makes sense in and of itself. There was a thread over at the Forge from a composition teacher who has a great outlining method, better than what I used; it's worth checking out. Unfortunately I can't find it (it was linked from one of the Gaming Discussions threads here, if I remember correctly). EDIT: here it is.

    2. Diagrams, flowcharts and especially tables are your friends. If you can put information into these forms, do so. You will be astonished at the gaps it reveals in what you thought were complete descriptions.

    3. Chunk your information. I used a method called Information Mapping which takes this to an extreme; everything is a paragraph-sized chunk with its own little summary/alt text off to the side. If you can't express it in a single paragraph with a summary that's no more than 7 words, it's too complicated (says IM). That's over the top for most applications, but it certainly makes things easy to look up, and to understand.

    4. Whenever you get a list, turn it into bullet points. But make sure that your stem sentence (the part before the bullets themselves, like what I'm writing now):
    * makes grammatical sense when read with the bullets, and
    * extracts as much common information out of the bullets as possible.
