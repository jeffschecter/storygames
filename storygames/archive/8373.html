    \n The definition that makes this a subset of sci-fi is the one I always ascribed to, but according to my research, it's a group that's broad enough as to be barely useful.
    \n
    \nPrior to my research, it was a specific subset of sci-fi that explored the ramifications of a particular bit of technology on society. What if we could predict murders before they happened? What if highly-intelligence self-teaching robots were common parts of our lives? That sort of thing.
    \n
    \nAfter doing research, it's essentially any sort of fiction that asks the question "what if?" from alternate history to more bizarre questions, like a friend of mine's recent "what if saying your real name created an exact duplicate of you somewhere in the world?"

    \n I've always used it to be intentionally broadening, kinda like saying "story games" instead of "rpgs". It suggests that there are many more directions that fantasy, science fiction, etc. can take us than what anyone currently thinks. While some people infer some connotation of plausibility, I think that is useful only as it is inherent to all fiction (in opposition to nonsense). What really demarcates speculative fiction from other fiction is that it steps away from where we are familiar.

    Posted By: Logos7I've been floating around here and other places and have seen the term speculative fiction come up a few times, with a few different/contradictory explanations/definitions. So you guys want to fill me in on what Speculative Fiction is to you? Is this Golden Yellow vs Brown Sugar or is it actually different/divergant usages (and no the dictionary hasn't been a help to me yet, thats among the reasons i am here.)
    \nWell, I don't know what golden vs brown sugar means, but from what I can gather, it's nothing like that at all. And there is no divirgent usage, at least not in my experience with literary circles of today.

    Speculative fiction is an umbrella term created to sound better than "sci-fi and fantasy." It covers all flavors of sci-fi, fantasy, horror, and everything else that blends them (or even with some more mainstream genre). Can't decide whether to call Alien horror or sci-fi? Speculative fiction. Debating about whether Star Wars is fantasy or sci-fi? Speculative fiction. Tired of trying to define your fiction interests as sci-fi, with some fantasy, and oh I like horror too? Forget it, just say you're into speculative fiction.

    So, as much as Heinlein protested that speculative fiction did not include fantasy or anything else that violated science, speculative fiction today has come to mean all forms of the unreal genres that normally get lumped into the same sections of the bookstore with slashes and dashes to separate the genre names.

    \n So, my friend Beth, who is more deeply involved in SF than any of you will ever be (seconded only by her husband, who is like that Times Two; and whose opinion/advice I'm currently querying on the matter), her definition is as follows:

    "My own feeling is that it's a blanket term invented so that people don't have to precisely define the boundary between 'science fiction' and 'fantasy'. There have been a lot of religious wars about that line, so 'speculative fiction' is a peacekeeper."

    That's pretty definitive in my book. It also explains why the term was created, to basically stop a lot of folks with time on their hands to shaddup about "Hyperion is Science Fiction" "No, Hyperion is clearly Fantasy!" "No, Hyperion is Science Fantasy" and so on. Looks like in that world, they too had the "...But is it Really an RPG?" debates as well, and far more intense than ours, since everyone is generally learned and Knows That They Are Right.

    So even I was wrong. What I was attributing to Greg Egan is simply "Science Fiction: Hard Science/Transhumanism". Not that that means that it's any better than other science fiction (except for his Axiomatic and Diaspora, which is in fact better than other science fiction)... some of it is downright clunky or uninteresting (Egan's Incandescence, Binford's Eater are both transhumanist, hard... and boring as hell).

    -Andy

    "My own feeling is that it's a blanket term invented so that people don't have to precisely define the boundary between 'science fiction' and 'fantasy'. There have been a lot of religious wars about that line, so 'speculative fiction' is a peacekeeper."

    \n
    \n

    For myself, it's a term I use rarely, and then only in the sense that Mike and Andy are talking about. To be that, the speculation needs to be central. Not necessarily the meat of the story, but the story pivots around it. The speculation has to be something truly speculative.

    \n
    \n

    "What if two teenagers from rival families fell in love?"
    \n "What if a shark started eating people near a small resort town?"
    \n "What if a talking cat barged in on a couple of latch-key kids and started tearing shit up, much to the consternation of their goldfish?"

    \n
    \n

    The first one doesn't qualify because it's something that just happens in the world. The second, also, even if it's ridiculously exaggerated. The third, maybe. That's something that we have to speculate about because it's not something that happens.

    \n

    I'm not sure if fiction in which normal things happen in an extraordinary environment doesn't count or I just don't like it. But, if you can give one of those stereotypical movie pitches ("... picture Friends ... in space!"), then it certainly doesn't seem very interesting.
