    \n I'd like a succinct, high-quality ludographical database, collated with bold insight and expertise. The kind where I can go after having been away for three years, or when exploring the scene for the first time, and discover the important games, the new games, whatever I need.

    The main way for something of this sort to positively distinguish itself from RPG Geek would be by being compact and consistent: whatever its defined purview, it should cover it well, and be up to date, and be compiled by an expert of the field. A quick, sharp, opinionated source of knowledge. One can always link to e.g. RPG Geek to tap that sort of big data, but RPG Geek does not understand its subject matter.

    The one thing I'm not so fond of in the above idea is that if you pulled off something that I was happy with, then I would be sad about it being entirely arbitrarily limited to GMless games [grin]. As you know, I don't hold that distinction as very meaningful myself.

    With that in mind, perhaps the even better thing for your GMless site to be, from my perspective, would be as a repository of a sharp, true and constrained theoretical overview on the constraints, advantages and design patterns of GMless games. That would constrain the contents of the site to what is actually meaningful in the concept. A designer blog would be a fit format in this regard - something that I think I've suggested before.

    Brainstorming even further: for you GMlessness is clearly an important thing. Perhaps even the matter of a school of design and theory. Maybe the site should be the anchor for that? A neat manifesto, blog to document developments in the field as they occur, some articles on the precepts of the school...

    \n Techniques lists with pros, cons, and recommendations.

    Example:\n
    Narration resolution
    When we roll (or otherwise consult a formal resolution procedure) to see what happens, the result we get tells us (among other things) who narrates.

    Pros: 1) Can elicit input from a given player at a point when they otherwise wouldn't have any. 2) Putting narration in one player's hands is faster than going by consensus. 3) Exciting uncertainty over whose voice will be heard next.

    Cons: 1) Involving randomness may not get you the player best prepared to narrate a given moment. 2) Balancing "narrate what's already been resolved" with creative authority/freedom can be tricky.

    Recommended for: 1) Games where "who gets to determine that" is part of the fun.
    2) Games which aim to regularly switch narrators and keep everyone involved in creative invention.
    \nA really well-considered, well-organized index of stuff like that would make me happy.

    \n Whatever its subject matter - assuming I'm interested in it - the main draw of a website - any website - to me is either:\n
    • A large enough community to yield a very large amount of content, of which at least some is bound to be high-quality - combined with a certain ease of access/casual interaction. Examples (on different scales) would be this forum itself, and Wikipedia.
      • \n
    • Consistently curated, to high standards. I turn to that site because I care what one specific person or small group of people thinks about a topic (examples: Ron's Doctor Xaos Comics Madness blog, Emily Short's blog). Or, I turn to it because it collects things from elsewhere I couldn't have found myself, and - crucially - it presents them to me in a way that makes it possible for me to engage with them (examples: ErosBlog, The Public Domain Review).
    \nIt's either, really - there's no way for a website to offer both sorts of experience at the same time.
    The former should naturally appeal to a larger audience (there's something in it for everyone), but starting such a website is a gamble in the first place: whether your community will reach critical mass is mostly not on you.
    The latter requires nothing but competence and hard work on your part, but the reception of it will be profoundly subjective: I might love what you do, someone else might hate it, while most people (even "in the field") won't care at all.

    (The majority of websites I actually visit, of course, belong to neither category: I use them strictly for utility or as providers of unique content. I go there to download the rules of a game, to read a webcomic, or just to look up a street address.)

    Jeff_Slater said:

    Can you give me some examples of the type of categories or specific subject matter you would most like to see covered in this database?

    \n(I've done a bit of critical analysis and databasing in my time, which is why I have some pre-existing notions about what I'd do if I was doing something like this. In case you were wondering why I hold so dinstinct ideas.)

    All of the things you mention are fine things to put into a database like this. I think that these are the most important considerations:
    * The format (what goes into each database entry) needs to be realistic in scope. By this I mean, it's much more favorable to say only the most important things about each game, and actually manage to create a comprehensive database, than to write extensively about a single game, but fail to finish the database because of the workload.
    * The database needs to be interesting to read for a wide variety of people interested in the topic. This is best achieved by knowing the topic, saying penetrating things about individual games (not shying away from the business for fear of controversy, is my point), allowing people to search for content pertinent to their current interests, and generally thinking about the audience and their needs when designing the database.

    A small foray into how to rate stuff well

    I would figure out a harsh critical rating scheme (possibly several if necessary) that strives towards truth and provides useful information about different games, coming from a place of deep expertise. (That last point means: don't review if you cannot say something non-obvious.) Basically like the star ratings that movie review scenes and such obsess over, but distilled and designed down into a sharp instrument for succinctly expressing the true nature of things.

    As a concrete example of what I mean, I created such a ratings scheme for old school D&D adventures several years back. (I intended to publish some reviews in my blog, just never got around to it before I stopped blogging.) My scheme awarded "stars" as follows:

    Solid craft: You could get a "craft" star for the adventure being well-crafted according to my own overall conception of what that entails for old school adventure modules. A binary choice, and a clear one for me, as I have rather clear and strident norms for what's acceptable and what's not in this type of product.

    Inspiring ideas: You could get a "literary" star for the adventure having literary merit or other exceptional ideas. This is a binary choice, with the threshold being pretty much whether the adventure tries to (and succeeds in) inspire with originality.

    Flawed details: You could get a "details" skull if the adventure is otherwise usable, but includes repairable flaws in important places where they cannot be ignored. This is generally caused by differing procedural standards for what "old school D&D" entails, so that the author has intentionally created something that I disagree with; for example, I consider set-piece encounters to be flawed detail, as well as conspicuously lacking stats for stuff (monsters, treasure, etc.) that is clearly potentially in play in the scenario.

    Bad Craft: You could get a "craft" skull for the scenario being overall sloppily made and not up to the standards of design that I expect of D&D adventure modules intended for other people's use.

    As you can see, the system I conceived for critical review of old school D&D adventures is sort of like a stars rating system in that if you want to, you can total up the stars and skulls (presumably deduct the latter from the former) to end up with a score from -2 to +2 (or 1-5 if you want). However, as you can hopefully see, the system is also very strict and kinda-sorta objective (I'm not really interested in engaging that philosophical issue here), and you could conceivably draw much useful fundamental information about the given adventure module by simply checking its "marks".

    Hmm, I should go ahead with this adventure review project. Good stuff, and I know I'd find it useful myself if I could just check some resource to find out which of the e.g. XRP Advanced Adventures modules feature technical flaws and which don't. That's just not something that they write in their adventure module descriptions, obviously [grin].

    Aside from the issue of how to rate reviews, you might find it interesting to know what things I chose to include in my "review format" for D&D adventures - it's sort of a similar case as yours in that I wanted a succinct format that would enable me to process a large number of entries quickly, while providing the benefit of my expertise (and work in reading all these adventures; I've read quite a few) to the readers without breaking myself with work. I wanted something where a single entry would be so short they could be displayed on a web page as a list, rather than giving each their own page. Here's what I ended up with:
    Basic metadata: Author, publisher, publication year, format (page count)
    Marks: Stars and skulls, as described above.
    Tags: A bunch of tags for indicating certain high-order concerns about D&D adventures. These include the likes of "it's a dungeon crawl / hex crawl", "it's low/mid/high level", "it's D&D / historical / asian / fairy tale / gonzo in literary genre", and so on. Things you want to know when picking what adventure modules to consider for your campaign.
    Cover art
    Verbal review: One paragraph long, strict. Not only do I find a longer one unnecessary for critical review of D&D adventures, but there are other people who like writing reviews in the sense of "long description of the contents".
    Review links: A few good in-depth descriptive reviews of the module, if available.

    Anyway, that's the kind of thing that I'd find interesting in other sorts of game databases: critical review and categorization. In the case of GMless games, perhaps recognize and categorize things like creative agendas, whether they're one-shot or multi-session, whether they require genre expertise from the players, whether the game has obvious flaws, what it is good for... all sorts of useful things to note once you start thinking in terms of expressing things that other people might find useful to know.
