    \n Sadly, these kinds of features are not part of InDesign out of the box; you need a plug-in. The further sad truth here is that plug-ins are generally expensive. DTPTools has one, and I believe it's about 100 UK pounds. Virginia Systems has tools for doing list generation (TOCs, indices, tables of figures), and I believe also in-line cross-references: I believe they have a package deal of all the plugins for about 400 US bucks.

    What I would strongly suggest is that you don't try to use InDesign to write technical documents (like RPG books). Instead, use FrameMaker.

    FrameMaker has exactly the tools needed to excellently support in-line cross references, auto-numbering, auto-updating of tables of contents, figures, etc, and indices. FrameMaker also has enough layout capability to handle most of the simple layouts seen in the RPG industry.

    What it does not do well: high-end typography and high-end layout. For that, you're right to use InDesign or Quark XPress.

    Sadly, (again), if you're on a Macintosh, FrameMaker is no longer an option unless your Mac is an old(er) PPC-based model, and running 10.4.x or earlier version of the OS (with Classic support installed).

    However, if you already own InDesign, then you've already spent some serious cash: you might be able to find a copy of Frame (used, educationally priced) for the same amount of money you'd spend on the ID extensions you need to handle long technical documents.

    I have no idea how well these ID extensions actually work, or how elegant they are to use. I have used Frame for decades and there's a reason it has been the tool-of-choice for technical writers for that long.

    Posted By: Eero TuovinenWhat's up with word processors having better tools for dynamic layout than this professional shit I pay thousands of euros for?
    \nBecause what InDesign and Quark are for is not document composition. They're layout engines. And for that particular purpose, they do very, very well indeed.

    What you're essentially looking at is a rotary saw (FrameMaker, or Word) and a jig saw (InDesign, or Quark). You can try to cut jigs with a rotary saw, or use a jig saw to swath 4-by-4s, but it's not the recommended practice. 8)
