\n
\n In many games (particularly Cthulhu games), the GM acts as the Guardian of Realism.

Player: I want to investigate the ghosts.
GM: Where would you go to do that?

Player: I go to the location on the map.
GM: OK, but the sun is setting, so you should either camp for the night or hike through it.

I think this role is important, but I'm trying to work out why. Help me break it down.

Partly, it adds challenge. In the first example, above, the GM is effectively saying: explain that for me in more detail. And this detail adds to the game.

Partly, it creates interesting situations. The second example, above, does this.

And partly it adds colour.

What it shouldn't do is act as a needless block. So here is a GM overstepping the role of the Guardian of Realism:

Player: So I force the lock open.
GM: Right. Well locks in the 1930s were quite sturdy and you're a librarian, so you can't.

But note that, if the GM had said "So someone might hear you" or "What would you use to force the lock?", it'd be OK.

What else is involved within this role? How does the role break down? What are the GM's responsibilities as the Guardian of Realism?

(If you think this role is important or that it all reduces to "Barf forth apocalyptica", this is probably not the thread for you.)

    \n Part of the Guardian of Realism (GoR) role is to differentiate between the capabilities of the characters. So not allowing the librarian to break in, is because that's the thief's job. Realism extends not just to game world but also what the characters do.

    To a certain extent this includes player expectation. Characters have abilities which they should use in appropriate situations. If there is an outdoorsman skill then hiking through the night implies that this ability should be used. Some players like to triumph through adversity, so just resolving the hiking situation is enough for them. Some players like story so the hiking situation needs to have narrative impact to be interesting, it should be about more than just a use of the outdoorsman ability. Which kind of realism are you after?

    And there we go, I've just bought GNS into it.

    \n It's a role that's very much in use when I'm GMing to children.

    "Let's break down the wall!"
    "That's an excellent idea! The wall is thick and made of stone. What kind of tools should you use? Light or heavy? Wide or narrow? Blunt or sharp? What skills or spells do you have that might come in handy?"

    "I'm going to jump on his head and bite his ears off!"
    "I'm having a little trouble imagining that. Could you mime it for me?"

    Basically what I do as GoR is to make sure everyone is on the same page, and everyone can imagine the kind of action that's going on. I also encourage the players to think on their character's resources as "real" resources such as tools rather than game resources such as chips and hit points.

    Because adults have a more established perception of reality and basic physics, and are much less likely to suggest breaking down the wall with an empty tin can, the GoR rears his head much less often in adult groups. However, when I supervise children GMing to their peers, the GoR is much more lax; if your ship sinks, of course you can swim all the way to the next island. If your army goes hungry, they can just eat grass and worms or whatever. Sometimes, as a non-GM guide, I wear the GoR hat.
    "So, guys, how long can you swim in real life? How much better are your characters at swimming. GM, how far away to the next island? What are the chances they'll make it there before passing out from exhaustion?"

    On an unrelated note, I once played in a sci-fi campaign where one of the players was a physicist. Even though he wasn't the GM, he often wore the GoR hat.

    \n Graham, to touch on Apocalypse World just a bit I think another aspect of this GoR business is to help the player tie his/her actions into the fiction. Physical realism may be a priority, yes, but what's more interesting is, say, setting stakes, or making it clear what they're attempting, etc.

    Steve, yay GNS! I would add that a) a good design and b) a group that knows what it wants will, together, use procedures/mechanics that prioritize "activating" abilities for reasons appropriate to the group's agenda (take "agenda" as broadly as you like).
    So, in the case of the hiking-by-night, it could totes be just about the physical realism (well, that's hard, and you shouldn't be able to just do it without problems!), or it could be a significant challenge, or maybe your stats are powered by Love or whatever, so you gotta see if the Establish-Relationship Scenes you've had with your PC's son are going to give you sufficient bonuses to find him in Yellowstone, at night.

    To put that idea more succinctly, the type of stumbling block the game/the group puts forth is a reflection of their priorities. For example, plenty of games don't really question it if a player announces "I'm here in this other PC's scene!" or "We go there. Now what?" without challenging that framing. If it's just not important to keep the characters apart, then don't do it. "Not important" has plenty of different meanings, of course, but here it's short-hand for "we're not interested in that aspect of the story."
    That doesn't have to be about the CA, either - that could just indicate a focus on particular elements of setting or genre. A game with a stunt mechanic SHOULD be encouraging players to do crazy moves without it being as difficult as it'd be for a real person (Exalted, I'm looking at you); at the same time, being able to pull off stunts has nothing per se to do with CA - it's just a reflection of the swashbuckling, or wuxia, or other, related genre the game espouses.

    \n The Guardian of Realism helps:

    - create situations that require real trouble shooting
    - make the setting feel real
    - me feel that my choices matter
    - enhance immersion
    - maintain the consistency of the fiction we've been building
    - encourage players to pay attention to fictional details
    - reinforce "show, don't tell"

    Unfortunately, abuse of the Guardian of Realism role has led to me to quit more games (especially Call of Cthulhu) than any other reason.

    The problem is related to blocking but it is more than that. Blocking can be a useful tool. It's more of a question\u2026 why are you blocking? The answer completely changes how I feel about blocking and the Guardian of Realism role.

    It's a tricky subject, I've seen it destroy more gaming groups than any other reason. I'm not sure it is a role I generally trust to one person. But when you do play with a person who handles the role with integrity, it creates phenomenal gaming experiences.

    \n In some of the examples that have been given, what has been "guarded" is not realism but rather the ability of the GM (or whoever) to provide adversity. If there are no explicit mechanics to create adversity ("It's a really strong lock!") then the GM or whoever has to invent the adversity-- sometimes GMs are great at this (good experience for the players) and sometimes that are bad at it (bad experiences, blocking). The role of Provider of Adversity is separate, I believe, from that of Guardian of Realism.

    As JD says, the GoR can be anyone (or multiple anyones) at the table; her conflicted role is to enforce conformity among competing visions of "reality" in the narrative (especially when one person's narration mistakes or confuses the narrative-- "No, wind drakes can't breathe fire, remember! That'd be insane!"). The Provider of Adversity often uses (should use?) recourse to the "reality" of the fiction in providing adversity ("... because you are weak and the lock is strong"), but that does not mean that the two roles are the same. Both can block, certainly, and both position themselves relative to the established fiction-- but then, that's true of any player's role.

    \n After years of GMing as the Guardian of Realism, I learned that, no matter how willing you are to look to the fiction for answers at every turn, there are gonna be cases where you just have to make shit up. "This stairway has walls around it. Why? I dunno. Why wouldn't it?" There are also gonna be cases where someone else at the table knows better than you. "Dave, you can't get leverage to do a pull-up if you've just got fingertip contact. I know, I've tried."

    In a group where realism is a shared value, I now believe that realism-guardianship should be a shared role. Like so.. That said, I do think realism goes well with strict authority on setting, so it is very clear how to go from imagining something that might be true to communicating something that is definitely true (i.e., ask the GM).

    \n Hmmm. Sounds like Guardian of the SIS to me.

    Now, sometimes people interpret that as "Guard the SIS from the players" which seems sort of wrong.

    Or "Ensure that the SIS is what I personally want it to be" which also seems sort of wrong.

    Those bibles that tv series use to keep all the writers on the same page? That's pretty much Guarding the SIS right there.

    So taking away all those bad things, I think we're left with the Steward of the SIS. His job is to make it clear to the players what's already in the SIS, and what the limits around the potential SIS are. He doesn't own the SIS, but he is invested with the stewardship of it.

    It comes up in Cthulhu games for the same reasons it comes up in all games set in established pre-defined Settings -- the integrity of the SIS is both well-defined and fundamental to the game.

    As to what he's protecting the SIS from, well, there are at least two distinct threats:

    1. Unintentional wandering. "You can't go down to the local bar because we're still in Prohibition, remember." This is fine.

    2. Intentional jackassery. "No, you can't be a 1930's ninja." This can be problematic, but it's been well-discussed elsewhere.


    In terms of GNS stuff, this sort of role is primarily associated with Sim. G and N don't care enough about Setting for it to generally be an issue; they just want to get to Situation already. I suspect that's probably where most conflicts arise -- when G and/or N players are trying to get to Situation already, but the S GM is saying, hold on, the Setting can't let you do that.

    \n What I've seen countless times:

    Something is honestly breaking their sense of realism. The problem is, in many cases, your sense of realism has little to do with actual reality.

    I've played CoC with history professors who have stopped a game because they couldn't agree about a historical fact, even though they were both experts in the field being discussed.     In my opinion, you can't have the Guardian of Realism both moderate a conflict and also have a stake in that conflict. It is a conflict of interest. It is also best not to make decisions in the heat of the moment.

    \n This is a very interesting topic, isn't it?

    Because it's so hard to disentangle the subjective from the objective. Some things are obviously not realistic (e.g. you can't fire a bullet through a solid, 3'-deep stone wall... usually), but "real life" throws all kinds of surprises at us. My father used to tell me about his time in the army, and dealing with hand grenades. They had a "firing range" where they would practice throwing live grenades at targets, to get a sense of what it's like and what the effects are, etc.

    One day, a soldier dropped a fragmentation grenade. It fell to the ground right between his legs and went off. The guy's cloak was torn to shreds, but there was no serious damage to him himself: every single fragment missed him, and he was basically just pretty badly shaken and bruised. Unbelievable.

    Not much later, an experienced training officer (who knew quite well the threat radius for a grenade of that type) threw the final grenade far into the field (well beyond a safe distance) and turned around to walk away. A single fragment from the grenade hit him in the back, killing him almost instantly.

    Now add to that the undefined nature of the gameworld space (the SIS), and there is just so much uncertainty about what a plausible outcome is that this kind of Guardian of Realism role becomes pretty subjective pretty quick.

    As a GM, when playing in this style it's often a question of maintaining your own mental image of what's happening, maintaining your ability to provide adversity, and adding detail. Ideally, all these details and corrections add interesting material or interesting choices to play, meaningful things that propel the game forward, as opposed to blocking: when you think you have a good idea about how to get through that door, but the GM just says it can't be done, that sucks. But if it just makes your choices more interesting, or challenges you to be more creative, it can be quite cool.

    You end up exploring the GM's psyche to a certain extent in this kind of play, and that can be fun, although the main feature is also the main drawback: play is only ever as good as the GM. (I find a lot of the more collaborative styles of play, or those guided by improv principles, include more surprises for everyone and tend to move along a little more dynamically. It's fun to discover stuff as you go along. But doing a strong-GM game like this can give you much more of a sense of entering an alien world or foreign reality, and perhaps some disagreements along these lines can even strengthen this feeling?)

    As a player, it's also interesting. I've often been in games where what I considered plausible or sensible ideas "didn't work". But sometimes a much less sensible idea (or one that I didn't think was possible) would be accepted by the GM. Those situations feel a little like cheating to me as a player: I knowingly suggested something I don't think would work, and now it's been accepted. Do I speak up against myself (or a party member, in a team-based game), and thereby stall the game?

    Different people have different heights they're willing to suspend their disbelief at. Like, people go to see the Matrix, and there's that part about using people to generate energy, and some people say "That's ridiculous" and others are like "Come on, it's comic book logic, it's fine." Or in Spider-Man 2, when Harry Osborn is like, "Spider-man didn't save my life! He insulted me by touching me." (Talking psychological realism here.) Some are, "Oh, come on, how contrived." Others are like, "Comic book!"

    So you kind of want your GoR to have his disbelief-suspension-height on par with everyone else's. Too high and the game's unrealistic. Too low and others feel blocked, shot down.

    \n Just had a forge convo that touched on this. I think task res is particularly prone to Reality Policing.

    task res: the question the gm is supposed to answer is - If the dice say this (task) is successful, what (result) happens?

    whereas with conflict res its - do you think its plausible that (result) could be achieved using (task)? Then let the dice decide.

    Its a lot easier to answer the second question as 'sure its possible' than it is to answer the first 'um, this is what I think is a plausible result given the circumstances'

    \nI think of that as the "Whoa, wait a minute" response: to me, it's one of the differences between a reveal that involves something happening right now, and a reveal that makes you want to ask questions about something that happened before.

    So in this case, dead brother hidden away for years and then suddenly reunited with you, that raises a lot of questions. How'd they successfully fake his death? How'd they keep him hidden? What did he do during all that time? Why is he back now? Why didn't they just kill him, wouldn't that have been easier? It changes a lot of history and tends to make you look for continuity gaffes, especially when you think they just pulled the whole idea out of their ass.

    But a clone raises fewer questions about history. The brother still died, you don't have to ask questions about what happened before, all you need to know is the crazy guy made a clone. Whatever continuity police instinct you have doesn't get triggered and you can just accept the reveal for what it is.
