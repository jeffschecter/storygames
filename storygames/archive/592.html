    There are several retailers who have made some money selling Indie games.

    That's a distinct sub-question of the question "Can you make a profit with a business model that includes selling Indie games?"

    I'm absolutely sure that the answer to that last one is "Yes" ... I mean, if your business model is to sell crack cocaine and Indie games (two great tastes that taste great together!) then you'll probably end up making enough gross income to defray your expenses (mostly, as I understand it, your peeps, bail money, and the cost of the caps you pop in the asses of Exalted players).

    But if your business model is to pay rent on a big shop, stock it entirely with Indie Games, and hope that people come in any buy so much material that your profit will, month to month, pay the expenses of rent, utilities, insurance and some sort of wage for you and anyone else who works there? That's a real dicey proposition.

    Most successful RPG shops I know of actually sell (to my mind) the intangibles like friendship and community ... and then they turn that into cash when their customers buy bigs stacks of books "on a whim." Have you got explicit plans in mind for how to create a space and a community that people will spend money to be part of?

    I'm sorry if I sound negative about this: by way of context, I have seen two pairs of friends (both married couples) do a great deal of damage to their finances because they started RPG stores (one brick and mortar, one web) and assumed that they could fill in the business plan at a later date. I don't particularly want to see more casualties racked up to that particular avoidable mistake.

    I struck out at ever game store in Duluth yesterday looking for Dogs, so I don't know of any game stores that carry indie games in my area. I've had a bit of experience in setting up retail stores and studying the competition in towns the chain I worked for was planning on moving into. Ann Arbor was one of them BTW. I'd do a couple of things before I worked up any concrete business plans:

    *Talk to the former owners of the game store in Ann Arbor to see how profitable they were.
    *Study the competition and get actual real numbers from them without them knowing that I was planing on setting up a new store to compete with them.
    *Do a intensive market study. This should be part of your business plan anyway.

    If you can find a nice hole-in-the-wall in Ann Arbor near the college with minimal rent, any game/comic store might be viable though.

    Edit: If I were going to open a game store and stock indie games, I'd set aside a 4' section that would display them. The section would carry 10 to 15 of the best selling titles, I'd stock two or three deep if reorder was good. I sign the hell out of them, and then spiff them for my sales crew for the first two months the store was open. I'd markdown the ones not selling in the first three months and order the ones that do, and then keep trying other games to see which ones sell. But this is just basic profitable retail. So, I'd do that same on every product.

    If I were to consider this, I think my concept would be "a cafe for creative people." The counter would sell coffee, smoothies, ice cream, and slices of pie and sandwiches. The main area would be at least two semi-separate rooms of cafe tables, each with a couple clutches of carefully chosen lounge furniture. The walls would be decorated with indie comic art. And there would be nightly and monthly events. Poetry night. Impro night. Indie RPG night. Author readings. Dramatic readings. An annual indie RPG con. And an annual indie comic con. November would be focused on NaNoWriMo. April would be Game Chef month. There would be free wireless (but it might be turned off during events).
