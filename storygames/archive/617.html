Serious Games

Jason_Morningstar
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games
I just got back from a great plenary session at a conference that addressed serious games, and name-checked the Serious Games Summit.

The speakers are all academics designing games to teach stuff - one guy is redesigning a microeconomics course to be taught entirely via a game interface. Another guy runs a quarter million dollar patient simulator at Duke hospitals complete with interchangeable genitals that can discharge urine on command. They are thinking about serious games in a serious way.

And here's the thing - two of the biggest challenges they talked about are the loss of intangibles / non-verbal communication and selecting a level of granularity in support of specific pedagogies in computer-mediated gaming. These are things that are easy to address at the tabletop.

They talked about structural components in games, and duh, we've spent an ass-load of time thinking about this as a community. Rules? Representation? Conflict? Story? Interaction? Outcomes?

One participant chimed in and dismissed serious games, saying that frivolous games (what we play, plus World of Warcraft I guess) have no consequences, which is the appeal. When you add a grade to your performance, this luminary argued, you add a consequence and ruin the "game" of it. I think he's dead wrong, but there you go.

Deeply interesting was the discussion of assessment, which is vital to this sort of enterprise (most of the people playing Serious Games are military, medical or - the leaders of the pack - aviation). So they are into after-action reviews, scoring, repeatability, standardization, peer assessment, and so forth - some of which tabletop games do, and some of which they don't.

It made me think about a role-playing game for medical students, complete with characters who grow and change and a narrative trajectory, with an attending physician as the GM. You could extend this analogy to put the squad leader or instructor-pilot in the GM role. It seems really interesting to me, particularly if you were to design a system to support Serious tabletop play that allowed subject matter experts to plug in the specifics of challenge, setting, and color.

Would this work, assuming you overcame the absurd cultural hurdles?

    Gaming also has some profoundly useful applications in the field of education. My mom taught eighth grade history for about ten years, and she taught about half of the class using simulations -- so each table of students was a colony in the new world, managing resources and exploring their surroundings, and then later each table of students was a family in a wagon train heading for california, and so on.     I don't know who it was that was saying this, but you really do have to think carefully about adding real-world consequences to a game design. If you're going to fail the class because you didn't do well enough in the game - and yes, I see a lot of serious game designs that basically suggest this - then are you really going to be able to explore and take risks? I think that it's not that you can't design games that have real-world consequences, but that you have to consider and account for it in your design.

    (I think the just-add-consequence-and-stir relates to the instinct that makes a lot of "serious game" designers build games that have only one correct path, and no options to play within the system. I've played quite a few games like this and they are really shockingly didactic and boring. Katie Salen, IIRC, gave a great talk about the dangers of 'propaganda' gaming at last fall's Games for Change conference, and I couldn't agree more. Games don't do well if they have just one right answer - then you get flash-based web quizzes and the like.)

    That said, I think that roleplaying games are a sadly overlooked approach to serious games that has a huge amount of potential. One of my students just designed a media literacy role-playing game, for example, which is really innovative and smart. (It uses advertisements as the resolution mechanic!)

    JBR brings up a good point when talking about standards, though - and that also gets at the issue someone brought up in another thread, about teaching people how to RP. How can we design games that create 'good enough' experiences to meet standards, when we can't control who is playing and we probably don't want to tie things down to the point of there just being one right thing to do? GM-less games and encoding meaning in the rules of the game are two good places to start, but it's a no-pun-intended serious challenge.

    JBR: if you design these games, are you going to design curriculum materials to go along with them? What content area would they center around - history, like your mom's games?

    Resolution as assessment is bad, bad news. However, the reward cycle is a good assessment in some contexts. In others, where you really want to encourage that exploration of content, real assessment is probably best done outside of the game -- play the game, have fun with it, and then assess. In the realm of education, this looks like "Play the game and learn the details of history, then take a test on the details and write a short response to the game experience." Assessment is a pedagogical tool; it's not the whole of pedagogy itself.

    And while your point is a good direction to take the conversation, I was talking about content and performance standards (Students must learn the figurative language terms alliteration, metonymy, and assonance).     If we're saying that an educational game doesn't fall within the scope of a creative agenda, then by extension, doesn't it fall outside of the scope of the Big Model? Which begs the question: "Doesn't that make the Big Model not so big?"

    Which, to be honest, strikes me as tautalogical. But, I don't think that concepts like creative agendas, and how they interact with various design elements are inapplicable to a "serious game."

    And, I'd argue that a teaching game would not be sim. In sim, players reward one another for evoking or emulating the focus, not for mastering it. A player with no good knowledge of the setting could successfully evoke it, and learn to consistantly evoke a setting he has no objective knowledge about. Contrarywise, players with book-perfect knowledge of a setting will not necessarily successfully evoke it.

    So, I'd argue that educationalism exists as a separate agenda -- one where we praise mastery of the subject -- and is probably served best by different design elements.

    I think JBR's "LLLL":http://story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=622 serves as an excellent example of this phenomenon. Of particular note: the hypothetical players encourage each other when they correctly guess the meaning of a word, and chide one another for their mistakes. Second, resolution is completely couched in correct use of the language, which give me the impression of driving learning (and given the number of "I'd play that" responses, I don't think I'm alone.)
