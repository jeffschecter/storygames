\nD&D; Manager Interviewed by Escapist on Red Box Renaissance
\n
\nI've always liked Mearls and I appreciate his honesty. Great thoughts below. He covers what didn't click about 4E with older fans and how to get back to the essence of D&D.; He also mentions the difference between narrative games and more traditional games like the original D&D; being more of a simulation. I don't think 4E is a narrative game at all (it's more of a tactics first kind of game in my opinion) but I do agree that many old school D&D; players I've gamed with want more of a simulation.
\n
\nUltimately I don't think 4E being less of a simulation was the only reason it didn't meet expectations (although it's easiest to address now). There was a series of obstacles (none having to do with Mearls) from 3E being such a large success (D&D; was competing with itself), canceling Dungeons & Dragons magazines, claiming 4E wasn't coming out soon then announcing it was coming out, mocking aspects of 3E in the 4E announcement at Gencon (while 3E was much loved), OGL being replaced by the GSL and essentially killing third party support, Paizo who ran Dungons & Dragons magazines continuing to support 3E and many third party companies sticking with Paizo, game stores excited about Paizo sticking with 3E so they can continue to sell their 3E back stock, a more "badass" art style that was neither old school nor new feeling, the Online Campaign tools advertised in all the 4E core books never coming out, yearly versions of the core books (PHB 1, 2, 3, MM 1, 2, 3, DMG 1, 2), Wizards stopped selling PDFs, the Digital Insider service made it so that you didn't have to buy all the 4E books, feeding into the old fan base's fear that D&D; was becoming a mini or card game by requiring the use of minis and selling power cards, constant rules changes & errata, and I'm sure a lot more I'm forgetting. It's been a very bumpy ride. Not to mention modern day concerns that are affecting all of publishing from competitions from digital games, piracy, retailers closing down, and much more.
\n
\nThat all said, the Essentials Red Box is what I wanted D&D; 4E to be when it first came out. There is a lot I think 4E did better than previous editions and I'm glad they aren't abandoning ship. And I'm excited they are taking risks and moving forward. The Castle Ravenloft boardgame is amazing and Gamma World sounds promising.
\n
\n
everyone from your CEO to your fanbase is clamoring for you to find a strategy to win the fight. And then you realize that the most recent edition of D&D;, in its attempt to find a winning strategy, was accused of betraying the very D&D; legacy it sought to uphold.
\n
\n
\n
"Look, no one at Wizards ever woke up one day and said 'Let's get rid of all of our fans and replace them.' That was never the intent," Mearls said. "With 4th Edition, there were good intentions. We are D&D; fans. We want D&D; to be the best roleplaying game it can be. We're always open to change, to reacting to what people say."
\n
\n
\n
The Red Box is all about saying 'you buy this box, you take it home, you unwrap it, and you're playing within two minutes.' You're not reading through 50 pages of combat rules, trying to figure out how it works. There's an immediate entry point."
\n
\n"At this point, if you're just getting into it, Essentials is the better bet, even just for the price point," Mearls said. "One of the things we're doing, since it's a smaller format, is a 6x9 soft-cover book. I just got my copy of Heroes of the Fallen Lands. It retails for $20, it has levels 1-30 of the classic four classes: Fighter, Wizard, Rogue, Cleric."
\n
\n
\n
"If you look at the Fighter and the way he works in D&D; Essentials, we removed the Daily powers to get more of a sense that 'fighters and wizards should look really different,' because that's how D&D; originally approached it," Mearls said. "I remember playing the Wizard way back in Basic D&D;, where you had one spell and you had four hit points, if you were lucky, and you needed the Fighter to protect you. That's a much different playing experience than when you are playing the Fighter, where you're in the front line, you're taking all the risks, you're charging into combat. The game you played was different. The way I like to design things - especially in RPGs - is all about that feeling, that when you approach the game, you're approaching it the way your character would. You're thinking like a Fighter; you're thinking like a Wizard."
\n
\nThese sentiments were all the more noteworthy when contrasted with the stated design philosophy of another of Mearls' predecessors, Rob Heinsoo, who had explicitly praised the sameness of all of 4th Edition's classes: "The point of bringing in powers for every character class was to make the game fun for everyone most of the time." Heinsoo had also gone out of his way to slam design efforts built around simulating a fantasy world, saying that it "might make sense if you're simulating a specific type of fantasy world ... but it doesn't make any sense for new players who want to have fun."
\n
\n
\n
Justin Alexander elegantly expresses many players' problems with 4E in an essay titled "Dissociated Mechanics." Alexander damned the powers mechanics and marking system in 4E because they were not simulating anything that happened in the game world. For example, why could a Rogue only pull off his fancy Daily power once per day? The only answer was because those were the rules of the game, not because that was how combat ought or should work in the fantasy setting. We asked Mearls if he or anyone else at Wizards had read Alexander's essay or even considered how 4E was fundamentally disassociated from the world it ostensibly simulated.
\n
\n"It's funny you brought that up because I'd read that blog entry. It's definitely something I've thought of," Mearls said. "If you're an experienced player and if you're willing to give some allowances to how the game works versus how you think reality should work, you're probably in a narrative mode. If you're more into the narrative side of gaming, you're used to taking mechanics and interpreting them to say 'this is what just happened.'"
\n
\nBut Mearls doesn't believe that most D&D; players want to play that way. "I almost think narrative games are a different hobby, where it really is group world building or literal group storytelling. In a more traditional roleplaying game like D&D;, you build it as you go and it's almost like a game of football or some sport where the action arises as you go."
\n
\n
\n
"If you are a disgruntled D&D; fan, there's nothing I can say to you that undoes whatever happened two years ago or a year ago that made you disgruntled - but what I can do, what's within my power, is that going forward, I can make products, I can design game material, I can listen to what you're saying, and I can do what I can do with design to make you happy again; to get back to that core of what makes D&D;, D&D; to what made people fall in love with it the first time, whether it was the Red Box in '83, the original three booklets back in '74 or '75 or even 3rd Edition in 2004, whenever that happened, to get back to what drew you into D&D; in the first place and give that back to you.
\n
\n"If you're unhappy with 4th Edition, I say take a look at Essentials and see where we're moving."

    \n [note: in my mind, I come across as kind of a jerk here, but I don't think I come across that way to others in this post. My tone is meant to be playful and inquisitive.]\n
    From the interviewee:
    For example, why could a Rogue only pull off his fancy Daily power once per day? The only answer was because those were the rules of the game, not because that was how combat ought or should work in the fantasy setting.
    \nOkay, this bit is a little nonsensical to me - - monsters have had powers they can use X times per Y for twenty years or more. My old AD&D Monster Manual told me that Baa'tezu could summon other demons(devils?) x times per week. And I think dryads or something had a charm power that could only be used 1/day, or whatever.

    What's the simulation-only, not-just-rules justification for that, eh? D&D's design goal has never been pure simulation; even from a perspective not informed by some kind of RPG theory, it makes intuitive sense that, as a basic design theme, one could keep insisting "Tell me why this makes sense!" As such, the number of times a dragon can use its breath weapon would either be based on some fantasy-world reasoning (and if so, why *not* as often as it likes?) OR beginning with such reasoning, and then being tweaked for the sake of fairness or correcting the amount of challenge desired.

    Tunnels and Trolls actually handles this really well: they have a slew of rather tongue-in-cheek "realism" explanations for things that seem to be clearly based on maintaining a consistent economy of resources. F'rex, spellcasters have to pay money between adventures to learn new spells. To me, this is clearly a money sink, but it's nonetheless justified in the text by mentioning a Guild of Wizards or some such, for our amusement. It also fills in the world a bit more, and I think that by playing at making a very "obvious" attempt to justify rules with in-game material, it actually comes across as charming and immersive in its silliness.

    \n Fighters also became the most equipment-dependent of the core classes. From AD&D; on, it used to be that you could make a wizard powerless by taking away the book they needed to memorize spells and the material components they needed to cast them. Now the wizard can roll out of bed naked and start operating at near-full power, while the fighter has to strap on armor, take up their particular weapon, etc.
    \n
    \nZak in Davis, I don't know T&T; as well as I should but it seems to me that AD&D; feels less dissociated because it uses different mechanics to model different kinds of game-reality. Balors and wizards have per-day limits because they're magical; we don't have real-world expectations of what they can't do, so arbitrary and "unrealistic" limits are fine. When you put those per-day limits on a horse or a fighter, it makes them not feel like the real thing they're supposed to be simulating, just as much as if you decided that a horse can summon demons or charm people.

    \n1) Why be stingy with the action points? Combos are cool...witness any video fighting game...they MAKE the game. Lets use APs as a kick ass combo engine rather than dishing them out in dribs and drabs. Extra actions make you feel cool and put down bad guys faster making the combats quicker in real time and more heroic feeling at the same time. So how would I do this? How about this. Earn 1 Action Point every time you fail a d20 roll. That way you never actually "miss" you just defer that particular hit to a later time period. Then, for simplicity, APs reset back to one after every encounter. Or, slightly more gameriffic, you can save a number of APs from one encounter to the next equal to the number of Healing Surges you've USED. I might do a triangular progression to spend more than 1 AP in a single round just to keep things a little contained, but that might be my own "oh noes...too much fun is happening" version of making them scarce to begin with.

    \n Roleplaying has to matter (for me and in my opinion, I'm not making absolute statements).

The books encourage you not to let failure in a Skill Challenge stop the game but at the same time don't let victory circumvent prepared encounters. So either way, why bother?

    Making encounters in 4E is hard. There are so many moving parts that the encounter creation rules don't give you a good sense of how difficult an encounter will actually be. An encounter equal to your level could be insanely difficult and an encounter higher than your level could be ridiculously easy. Part of the problem was your encounter XP budget doesn't take into account terrain which is a huge factor. There is so much tweaking needed to hit the right encounter balance, it's difficult to improvise. And when you create the right encounter, you don't want to skip it.

    \n Even though I prefer 4E over Pathfinder, I'm friends with a lot of people who work for Paizo, help with organized play, freelance and playtest for them. I got to witness up close everyone's reactions to 4E and the simple reasons why it didn't click for them.

    #1 reason... you have to use maps and minis.

    I like maps and minis. But a lot of people don't. In general I've found many of my pathfinder friend's tastes very conservative. That's not a bad thing. They like what they like out of game. It's fun, it's not supposed to be work and once they find something that is comfortable they don't want too much change to risk ruining it. They were already paranoid and upset over the changes from 3E to 3.5E (and many of their 3E books were made useless). And when they were told that 4E wasn't coming out in a year and it did anyway, they were pissed!

    Then the 4E announcements were coupled with mockery over aspects of 3E, their print magazines were canceled, the RPGA was forced to abandon their existing games (this led to a lot of Pathfinder converts), third party support was killed with the transition from the OGL to GSL (more people over to Paizo), 4E stylistically didn't click with the old fan base with badass Dragonborn player characters with spikes everywhere, subscription models, trying to be more like World of Warcraft (which made older gamers feel pushed out), constant errata, even more supplements to buy including multiple versions of the core books, power cards (oh no, it's turning into magic), no more PDFs, suing pirates, publishing rules advice online that used to be free but not you have to subscribe to see, threatening fan sites to close down, and on and on.

    But with all that, I think the #2 reason was the game became more prescriptive.

    They claimed to streamline grapple but instead was replaced by a gutted version where you could only hold on to and move someone. Want to choke them? That's a higher level power that only certain classes can get! But I'm holding the guy... why can't I just put him in a sleeper hold and make him pass out! Sorry. I can't tell you how many Paizo fans were freaking out about elements like this. Wait... I start out as a warlord but I can't afford a warhorse? A lot people might say, "who cares?", but easily 20,000+ people and counting cared.

    I think the #3 reason was no online campaign tools.

    When you're making a new product, you have to answer... what problem are you solving? It can't be... if we make a new edition, we'll make more money! That's not the customer's problem! Your customers don't care about that nor should they. Sometimes companies try to create problems to then solve. And that just pissed people off! What problems does a new edition fix? A lot of people didn't like the changes to 4E but so many people used to play D&D in college and then their friends moved away. Awesome... give them a tool they can use to keep playing with their friends! Many people even accepted the 4E changes they didn't like and labeled them as sacrifices needed to play online (I don't like minis but it will help online campaign play work). So people bought the core books which had big ads in the back for the online campaign tools but then they never came. For many of these people, the major problem 4E fixed for them never happened so they went back to 3E. And a lot of the old school players never even switched to 3E let alone 4, so they were happy with what they had.

    I 100% agree that there are aspects of the 4E rules that scared people away but I think there is an even bigger picture at play.

      \nFailing individual rolls have immediate Consequences like losing healing surges, taking damage, wasting time, spending money, and more. Succeeding Hard individual rolls gives you immediate Advantages like canceling a failed roll, gaining part of your goal immediately, and so on. They've changed how group skill checks work (instead of aid another, everyone makes an easy roll, if half the group succeeds, you pass the group check). And Skill Challenges are more structured with guidelines for how many hard or moderate checks to include as well as how to improvise skill use (skills themselves now have individual DCs and suggestions of unique ways to use them that may not be immediately obvious).
      \n
      \nUltimately if our goal is to find the big bad guy and confront him, we're going to do that no matter what. We won't get lost and future prepped encounters won't be wasted. The skill challenge now determines what price we pay to get what we want.

      \n Am I the only one who has used tiered stakes setting for Skill Challenges?
      \n
      \nWhat I mean is, the player(s) and I set up the stakes beforehand, but with tiers of success. Basically, you get total success with no failures, and for each failure you get, there's some kind of complication.
      \n
      \nIn one example, I ran a skill challenge for a player of mine who's character was a kobold paladin of bahamut. He had just (with the aid of other adventurers) killed a tyrant red dragon in the area who had the kobolds under its thumb (claw?). Basically, the paladin wanted to "convert" the kobolds. We ran a skill challenge to see how effective the paladin was at recruiting the kobolds for this cause.
      \n
      \nI think I used 4 successes as the win button, and 3 failures per usual.
      \n
      \n0 Failures meant that the speech was so effective that nearly all of the kobolds joined his cause and were excited to learn about Bahamut.
      \n1 Failure meant most of them were excited to join, but a portion of them (guided by a vocal leader) challenged the paladin and counter-recruited some of the kobolds to march north to join with the dragon's boss.
      \n2 Failures meant the same as 1 failure, but also that one of the kobolds who did "convert" were actually a spy and might bring problems down the road.
      \n3 Failures meant that nearly all of the kobolds joined up with the vocal opposition and marched northward.
      \n
      \nSomething like that.
      \n
      \nAfter both agreeing on this, knowing the stakes, we roleplayed it out using skill checks to determine how effective everything was. He used Religion to tell a story about Bahamut and Tiamat's beginnings and how Bahamut is kick ass. He used Diplomacy and Intimidate and other skills as well. I think he had one failure. We roleplayed that the minority marched northward under the promise not to be attacked by the paladin and his crew. Other kobolds eventually arrived after word spread about this new paladin and his conversion and they begin to rebuild the keep where the dragon lived as a new settlement for these kobolds.
