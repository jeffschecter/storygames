\n I'm wondering if any kind of story-game exists (or could be created) that would work well in a classroom setting. At this point, I'm not asking for help with the "what would this teach?" question--I can either figure that out on my own or start another discussion later on that. But I'm paid to figure out what students might learn from a particular experience.
\n
\nMy question is on the logistics of it. So here are the limitations in my most typical classes:
\n
\n1) The classes I'm thinking of have enrollment caps of 25 or 28. At any given point in the semester, the number of people showing up might be as low as 13 or 14 or as high as the cap. No real way to predict with certainty how many on any given day, and rarely will the same exact group show up two days in a row, unless it's "no absences" twice in a row.
\n
\n2) Usually my classes are either in 75-minute blocks 2 days a week or 2- or 2-and-a-half hour blocks once a week.
\n
\n3) Budget is limited. Printing copies of stuff is no problem. Buying enough d20s for five different groups to play Donjon, for example, would give me pause. But if I really needed to buy five copies of a $20 game, I could do that.
\n
\n4) No prior experience can be assumed with story-games of any sort. A class might have one or two people who've done D&D or some other conventional RPG, but maybe not even that.
\n
\nIt seems to me then like the best game for this scenario would be:
\n* super-easy to learn, requiring only a few minutes of explanation and perhaps a reference sheet
\n* built around common or cheap randomizers, like ordinary playing cards or d6s
\n* fast-playing, so that it's possible to complete some kind of story in an hour or so
\n* accessible, in the sense that someone "off the street" can immediately latch onto the concept
\n
\nThe closest thing I've been able to think of so far is Once Upon a Time, which would require having enough sets for each group, but everybody knows what a "fairy tale" is, and once you get how the cardplay works, you can pick it up and build a story pretty quickly.
\n
\nAnybody know of other games that could work?

    \n I'd suggest not going very freeform at all, at least not with an inexperienced and oversized group of teenagers.

    Instead, I'd argue that you'd be best off with a game with very strong procedures and goals.

    Daniel Solis' games tend to focus very strongly on words, usually on writing them, which makes them easy to make use of in the classroom, regardless of your subject.

    Though if you were trying to teach Algebra, I doubt you'd be asking about story games. Not that it couldn't be done. And done well. But still that seems less likely to be your focus point, given the nature of your inquiry. English or Social Studies are much more obvious ways to lead you to the "How could I use story games to help with this" thought.

    I'd like to hear a little more about the teaching circumstances, though. A 50% variance in daily attendance suggests some really extreme circumstances, like prison education or severely emotionally disturbed group living or at the very least a continuation high school (ie. high school for kids kicked out of regular schools). I taught in a pretty bad neighborhood for a year, but I'd only see about 10% absence most of the time.

    If it's a continuation school, I'd like to know if it's a credit-per-assignment type program. That might have some impact on the sorts of recommendations that would make sense.

    \n Ah. Ok. That explains the fluctuating attendance.

    So you need games that will interest non-gamer college-age students. And they need to play fast. And it sounds like if they put language in the spotlight, that would be good, yes?

    Check out Og. A game about cavemen with very few words in their vocabulary.

    Look into Breaking the Ice. About dating and relationships, which is a favorite subject for most college students. Lots of good material could come out of that for writing assignments. You could have the write the story of the gameplay in more detail, or write what happens next. Or write an analysis of some sort. And I'm pretty sure it plays fast enough to work for your time restraints, though you would want to test it out a bit first and make sure you were well prepped to get them started on it quickly.

    And, as mentioned multiple times already, Do: Pilgrims of the Flying Temple features letter writing as it's primary mechanic. Like I said, all of Solis' rpgs are very language-focused and tend to be quick playing as well.

    There's lots of designers on the forum here. They might be willing to be helpful in getting you going for much less than the cost of a full book per student. Plus, for those games I mentioned, you could probably crib together a one-pager version for the students to reference.

    \n I guess I'm wondering if you are trying to do "a bunch sitting around a table talking a story out" or more of a pure creative writing thing. Or it sounds like it doesn't matter right now to you, and you are just looking around.
    \n
    \nJust a couple idea of rough designy-ish ideas.
    \n
    \nI'm the middle of playtesting a writing game that a teacher friend has suggested that might work well in a classroom. Essentially, the game is played where a player writes a chapter as if its a journal entried gfy his character, then he provides 5 chapter titles to the next player, who must select one of those to use as inspiration for what happens next to his chareacter. This could be applied pretty easily to having the teacher just write five different chapter names on the blackboard, and the students continue their story by selecting one of them. But in this case, there probably wouldn't be much interplay amongst students.
    \n
    \nFor something more "around the table," maybe something like this: setup random groups of people around tables. Each talbe has a stack of cards with usual story/character tidbits. Everyone starts out as a "blank slate" taking turns pulling out tidbits, adding to their own story, with the other players asking questions. Sort of a much looser version of Penny for your Thoughts. Ideally, I'd imagine that it would be interesting if everyon could intermingle as characters from completely different time periods, again, keeping it loose. Thematically, it could something like an Inn at the End of the World, which is a kind of urgatory wheree souls go until someone decides they can move on. Or alternately, something playing the characters throughout time who are being dragged around in Bill and Ted's Excellent Adventure.

    \n Maybe my game The Daughters of Verona could be of interest?

    It's a game of Shakespearean comedies, it consists of three equal parts; Shakespeare, theater and RPG. It is highly structured and does a lot of hand holding for beginners, but I think some familiarity with the works of the bard is needed to actually enjoy the game. Having seen one or two movie adaptations in advance should be enough.

    I have played it with some language teachers and they liked it - from a game-perspective, no word on the pedagogy or classroom suitability and they claimed that it felt very much like Shakespeare.

    Right now the game is available for free download on the link above, but it is written in a peculiar language similar to, but definitely not identical with, English. :-) Maybe that is a problem in a language education context. If I see a demand, or at least interest in the game, I'll see if I can find an editor that can upgrade it into proper English, since it's only four pages and some card text it shouldn't be too expensive.
