\n Death is an important part of many stories: dying, rebirth, being a ghost, journeying to the underworld.

Now, games like D&D tend to cut themselves off from these sorts of stories, since death means "Roll a new character". It's sad. It misses out a wonderfully evocative story theme.

But I wonder whether indie games make the same assumption: that death means the end of the character. Sometimes it's expressed in rules like "Your character only dies if you choose to". Again, it precludes an important story element.

The only game I know that does something interesting with death is Poison'd: you face judgement, and can become a ghost. But, even then, death feels like the end of the character, when it should be the next stage of the story.

Graham

    \n Take a look at Ghostwalk for D&D 3E. It's a supplement that lets characters keep playing after they've died. I think it offers some cool "next stage of the story" options.

    Other games start with the character dead. Characters in Chad Underkoffler's Dead Inside are spirits who struggle to redeem themselves. Characters in White Wolf's Wraith: the Oblivion are ghosts who live in a world divided from the mortal universe by a thin shroud.

    Some indie games don't assume death is the end of the character. If you die in The Shab-al-Hiri Roach, for example, it's expected that you'll keep role-playing your dead, roach-zombified character. At least in every game I've played; I can't remember if the rules codify this.

    \n Storn, you're right that the "Death = character end" rule can lead to good stories. I guess I'm interested in exploring rulesets which do something interesting with the character after death.

    In A Wicked Age is good for dead characters (and other non-corporeal characters, such as gods). There's a cleverness about the wording of the rules that doesn't imply characters are alive.

    It needn't just be ghosts and underworld trips, either. I guess I just want roleplaying games to handle death more interestingly. Like, I don't think you can die in Apocalypse World. Why not? It'd be great. Ample opportunities for cyborging and haunting. Or Trail of Cthulhu. Why does that game have Health at all? Death is the least of your worries.

    I'd just like designers to think about the role that death plays in the game, rather than the default option of ending the character's story.

    Graham

    \n To try and keep Keith Senkowski's comments from becoming any more derailing and unhelpful, I will now ask a pointed question:

    Keith: you intentionally chose to keep the afterlife out of your game design. Therefore the implication is that death is final in your game (and presumably in any games you run). So, the question is: how do you make the death important from a dramatic standpoint? How do you ensure that players don't feel cheated by PC death? How does death serve your motivation and mode of play within the context of your game? How do you and your game deal with the repercussions of death on friends, family members, guildmates, coworkers, competitors, enemies, etc?

    Shreyas: in a silly and halfass way, the "live action strategy" game Long Live the King does this, with the GM doubling as a king who is degenerating in madness and sickness and the PCs being various members of the court trying to jockey to control the most influence when the king dies (at the end of turn 6). Also, it sounds like the sort of thing that might be great subject matter for a Finnish-style LARP (like that one where people take turns playing the degenerative alcoholic and factors in his life, and he will definitely die when the literal bottle that is the game's only prop is finally drained, the name of which I have forgotten if I ever knew it).

    \n Arrowflight flirts with brilliance in its treatment of character death, but ultimately delivers a great heartbreaker of thinking inside the box, all the more painful perhaps, because of what could have been.

    Characters have Mana and Spirit traits. Mana is the part of the character linked to the web of life, from which the power of magic is drawn. Spirit is the part of the character linked to gods and forces beyond the material world. The traits fluctuate in play. At the point of death, the game's cosmology has it that there is a necessary communication between Mana and Spirit, with each informing the other how to respond to dying. If a character dies with Spirit at 0, there is no communication from Spirit telling Mana to resolve its ties to the material world. The character becomes a soulless being attached bodily to the material world: a ghoul. If a character dies with Mana at 0, there is no communication from Mana telling Spirit to resolve its ties to the material world. The character becomes a bodiless spirit attached to the material world: a ghost.

    The heartbreak? The character becomes an NPC.

    Paul

    \n I like the way death is handled in many games...except when it happens in the middle of everyone else having fun, D&D -style. "Oh, fuck, I'm dead...and this looks like another five rounds of combat for the rest of you. I'm going to go meet some ladies now and re-evaluate my life choices." But it's hard to get around that issue without some other form of weirdness if you have something D&D-esque, to create the sense of risk.

    One of the moments I doubt I'll ever forget comes from a Mythender playtest involved Joe McDonald shouting, as his action against his foes, "I die and am reborn." (Purely from how he saw the fiction, not an actual death on the table.) And that action harmed the Mountain that encased him and wanted him to just die. It's the only time I've seen anyone using their own death, voluntarily, as a combat action in and of itself. (I've also had people choose to create dead/undead Mythenders, which is fun but gets gonzo fast.)

    Here's a question, though: why does "finality" have to be bad? Maybe death in a game may just mean that -- you're fucking dead. But, it also means you get to have some sort of other influence on the game that living characters can't, and I don't mean "keep playing as a ghost." Like, some sort of "closure subsystem," where you change the world somehow that impacts the other characters and your next one. To use a FATE to describe the idea: when you die, you get to place an aspect on the world relating to your accomplishments. That is the right of the dead.

    \n Death was a pretty big feature of a long-running Wraith campaign - but not in the obvious way that you might think.

    PCs in Wraith are arguably "not really dead" in the way that we as humans experience death. My father is dead, and I had to face the reality that he was gone from the world... Wraith PCs aren't really "gone", they're just rather detached. However, the supporting cast of a Wraith PC certainly have to face death, and grief-by-proxy works in a self-indulgent kind of way. I also found that the themes of death that are raised by Wraith made the players familiar with different facets of loss and finality, almost like Eskimos with regards to snow, so when the players were faced with them later in the campaign they were ready to wrestle with some good, meaningful scenes.

    Something forgotten forever. Something ending with such finality that nothing can claw it back. A person you care about more than anything on the brink of destruction. A person who refuses to deal with their grief. A person you have to say goodbye to and never see again. A person who has outlived everything that he remembers loving. And in the end, a person who faces death and says "It's okay; I've done more than enough. I'm ready to go." This is all stuff that we had happen in the campaign.

    So even though the PCs were kinda "not dead" themselves, we certainly talked about death a lot in that campaign; the dust of years, denial, courage, oblivion, release, sorrow, old age, and even transcendant death. The narrative conceit of playing as ghosts just gave us permission to explore these things without being too morbid. I think that it was worthwhile, and I don't know how I could have fit so much into another campaign.

    And then we played Victorian Age: Vampire and it was all about sex, really.

    \n In the "Death is Dumb" thread that this one spawned, I did a little summary of how one might "remove" death as a primary motivator in RPGs, and I think it might be of value here as well:\n
    Posted By: DeadlytoqueDeath is one of the great motivators of real people -- and thereby of our fictional pawns -- but in a sublimated way, usually. Look at Maslow's Heirarchy of Needs: right there at the bottom are the ones that we need to stay alive. By taking those out of the equation, the game/story/whatever is no longer about survival in a very real way, and it lets our play and design focus on more "lofty" topics, from sex to security of property to morality and creativity.

    This can be handled in games in a variety of ways. Off the top of my head:
    1) Death can be ignored -- death is not an issue in this game; characters cannot die under any circumstances (or only in very extreme circumstances); examples: Mouse Guard,and (I think) Conspiracy of Shadows, though I will admit to having not played it; possibly Dogs in the Vineyard, although there is some debate about how lethal games tend to be;
    2) Death is a semi-inevitability as a consequence of protracted play -- most campaigns of this game, when played long enough, will end in death or some other form of character transcendence/apotheosis/retirement into non-playable status; examples: The Shadow of Yesterday, Ars Magica, Hero's Banner, Dust Devils;
    3) Death is a transitory or avoidable outcome -- PCs can come back from the dead, either as ghosts, some other undead, or by simple resurrection; examples: In A Wicked Age, at least one form of traditional-play Dungeons & Dragons, most video game "RPGs".

    That said, death is something tangible and (arguably) easy-to-define. It's usually binary. A character is dead or not dead. Play is driven by the character being not-dead, so they strive to maintain that state. It's tougher in design and play terms to create situations which mirror, say, spontaneity (picking something from the peak of Maslow's pyramid at random), as clearly and as compellingly.
    \nA few clarifications and thoughts since then: As David Artman rightfully points out, Pendragon would fall into the second category.

    The second category should also be broken down into 2(a): Games where characters will eventually die because they are mortal and all mortals die (ie Pendragon); and 2(b): Games where characters will eventually die because their story will have reached its ending/it is genre-appropriate (ie Dust Devils). The basic principle of inevitability is the same, but the reasoning behind it is different.

    Also, games in the 2(a) group, rather than being an example of "removing death" could actually be seen as using death to force players into exploring other parts of the pyramid; like in real life, it is mortality that gives those other explorations their spice.

      \n So, I'm coming to this thread a little late, but here's my $0.02:
      \nDeadlans doesn't really get rolling until some or all the characters die. The manitou possession mechanic can make for some very interesting play, too.
      \n
      \nGames like D&D are specifically geared to avoiding death, or circumventing it in every way possible. I've found that in running 4E that it's nearly impossible to kill a character, and the new rituals make Raise Dead ridiculously easy. But that's because the game is aimed directly at the 'death =bad' mechanic that arose from its earliest origins- tabletop wargaming.
      \n
      \nModern RPGs arose from tabletop wargaming and in that venue a dead unit was simply removed. It's a VERY modern ethos to be looking behind the mechanics and tinkering to create story by use of mechanic. Traditionally we've had to make story in spite of the mechanics, and death is one of those.
      \n
      \nBack in the day, you'd hope a character could survive to high levels and then die in a really cool way just as the campaign wound down. Just so you'd have a cool story to tell.
      \n
      \nNow we're looking to have our games tell the stories we'd like to tell is we were just sitting down at the fire to tell stories. That's a lot more ambitious. The Orpheus tale would be VERY hard to do in straight D&D. By all rights he should have been destroyed by much higher level opponents.
      \n
      \nOdysseus visits the underworld and feeds the ghosts blood to return their memories. There's no system mechanic I'm aware of to do that.
      \n
      \nOdin gave up an eye and then sacrificed himself to himself, dying and returning, all to gain wisdom. Also hard to do in a system. But all of these are compelling stories that resonate with the human psyche. Trying to do the same with our hobbies is a lofty goal. But VERY hard to achieve, especially since you aren't doing a story focused on ONE hero. Rather you have a party, their players want different things from the play of the game, their characters have a wide variety of motivations, and they may not all be physically in the same place or in the same status (ie they may not all be dead at the same time.).
      \n
      \nHow do we make death meaningful, and yet not an impediment to fun. I don't know, but I think it's worth discussing more. I'd like to focus on how to make death meaning ful without being a barrier in games where it's NOT part of the system as designed, but I can start a thread on that seperately if you'd like me to.

      But VERY hard to achieve, especially since you aren't doing a story focused on ONE hero. Rather you have a party, their players want different things from the play of the game, their characters have a wide variety of motivations, and they may not all be physically in the same place or in the same status (ie they may not all be dead at the same time.).
      \nDirty Secrets focuses on one hero, as do many Jeepform games (for a given interpretation of "hero"). And many, story games have diverged from the "party" model of play.

      My point is not that there has been a lot done to make play after death interesting, but I can't see any mechanical difficulties in most indie games. It's a thing, but it's not a mechanical thing.

      \n I disagree, with reservations. Using a 'I have no idea how to make this work, so just roll some dice' mechanic, which is simply a method for making the GMs story objectives seem less arbitrary, is NOT the same thing as have your game designed to deal with death and dying. We can bring up exceptions to the issue, and talk about one player solutions, but that's not truly at the core of the issue.
      \n
      \nThe heart of this is you have a group at the table. Their characters have engaged in the activies expected of them as Pcs and through the course of the story one of them is dead. Other than having the player roll a new character, how do you AND the game system react to that. ("well, now that my Mage Phil died, I'll be playing his twin brother Bill, who is motivated by REVENGE!" only goes so far)
      \n
      \nFrankly a TPK leading to an Underworld game isn't the problem. It's the lone death that disrupts the tempo of play and puts a hitch in the rest of the game the GM planned until the new character can be introduced or the old one raised, or whatever mechanic is used to return the player to their status of playing. How can the player's new role (as dead guy) be satisfying to the player, the rest of the party and the GM? How can the player be ACTIVE while dead?
      \n
      \n4E reacts by making death so easily reversible that death might as well not be in the game. It's like an MMO, basically you respawn with a minor penalty.
      \n
      \nAs a storytelling GM that doesn't resonate for me. I'd much rather have a 'ghost' or 'revenant' template that could be applied to the character after death. Then the character could be played and a SECOND death would mean discorporation, or some such mechanic.

      \n What if, in , you and the other players / GM / whatever could negotiate (and renegotiate at any time) what happens instead of your character dying?

      Take D&D, for example. You're about to get into a fight with a bunch of kobolds. They have kidnapped the realm's prince and the queen has hired you to get him back alive. The DM offers, "Instead of dying, the prince dies." You counter with, "Nah, instead of dying, the kobolds send us the prince's severed hand." Everyone agrees that's awesome. During the combat, when two characters are low on hit points, the DM renegotiates, "The deal is off. If you die, you die... or the prince dies." The players can take the new deal (the prince dies) or not (if they die, it's real) or they can try to get the DM to accept another offer.
