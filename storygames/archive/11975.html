    \n Hi

    I had a similar issue a while back. Have you considered...

    * Splitting into smaller groups - two groups of 3-5 people might be easier to manage in terms of scheduling. Put folks with like schedules into the same mini-group.
    * Running games that work with smaller numbers of players - Dogs in the Vineyard works swell with just 3 players and a GM, and KKKKK works for just two players
    * Running one-shot games as opposed to trying to start a campaign or limited story-arc - one shot games obviously require less investment in terms of pitching up regularly.
    * Running games where the fact that the same players don't trun up every week is catered for by the system? I can't think of any such game off the top of my head - In A Wicked Age perhaps?

    Good luck anyhows, cheers

    \n Pick a fixed schedule for gaming (we go for Tuesday evenings) and stick with it. If it's a regular slot, people know about it and can organise themselves around it. If attendance is still erratic, you'll have to limit yourself to one-off games, or games where different players can play the same characters.
    \n
    \nSounds like you need two games to accommodate all the players.
    \n
    \nThe other thing we do at MK RPG is organise games into 8-week blocks. Most games run for only one block, then everyone gets to change. It helps to bring in new blood, as well as ensuring the people mix things up.
    \n
    \nPut your details on a website and post links to it. It's a good way to attract more gamers.
    \n
    \nNeil.

    \n There are a lot of people out there who make a great deal of noise about wanting to game but who don't actually put in the effort to get themselves into a group. Once you figure out who these people are, the best route is to ignore them completely.

    If you can get four of them to show up consistently, run a game with those four. Forget about the other 3-5. If they want a game, they can make the effort to get involved in one. If they aren't willing to make the effort to get involved, or aren't willing to clear a night for a one-shot, or aren't willing to commit to a regular schedule for ongoing gaming, well, that shows you that they're in the category of people best ignored.

    \n The way that one structures an ongoing game can also make it more accommodating of people with non-regular attendance.

    Just as an example, picture a D&D 4e campaign where all the players are from an independent fortress city, barely strong enough to stand amid the swirl of war and raiding and politics that goes on all around.

    Every session, various interests in the city post mission opportunities of various forms. Each mission has the party sally forth from the city, do some things, and then sally back.

    This structure handles an ever-shifting cast of players much better than other ongoing-game structures.

    Posted By: BullbarI started a local gaming facebook group and ended up with about 40 people in it. I was really excited and enthused and then... nothing happened. People didn't talk, no games happened as a result and the connections I wanted never formed.
    \nSadly, this tends to be the case more often than not. The nature of the internet dramatically reduces the cost of participation, which is only good if your goal can be achieved by lots of people doing only one thing, only one time. (A good example of this would be Wikipedia: it's easier, faster, and produces better results when ten thousand people each write one article than it is when ten people try to write a thousand articles apiece.)

    But if your goal is something which requires more commitment, like coming to a weekly game...well, that's not really the kind of group that the internet is good at creating. The entry cost to joining a Facebook group is just too low to select for that kind of dedication. Internet groups can be great at maintaining that sort of group after you've assembled one, though.

    \n Accounting for Taste is wise!

    For reference, I have a personal Facebook list of 170+ local RPG players, almost all people I've gamed with. With a list of that size, I can get 4-6+ people together for a 1 shot with very short notice. NerdNYC reaches over 1,000 people and can get 80-120+ people together with enough notice. I generally assume low conversion rates when it comes to Facebook friends.

    I recommend...

    > pick a consistent day & time to play
    > play no matter what every time
    > if you don't have enough people for an RPG, play a board or card game
    > no matter who makes it, let everyone know you did indeed play
    > give it 3 months before it becomes a real thing in people's minds
    > don't count on anyone showing up
    > if people require a campaign, make it episodic
    > if people miss a session, turn it into a twist, describe something cool happening that makes them absent
    > make sure everyone's power level is fairly even, so people who miss sessions don't think there is no point in coming
    > but reward players for good attendance, maybe they have greater input in what you play
    > make games first come first serve
    > if extra people come, have them play a board game or split into a separate group
    > if extra people consistently come, recruit someone to be a second GM
    > send out reminders a week before, the day before, and the day of

    Posted By: PeteRunning games where the fact that the same players don't trun up every week is catered for by the system? I can't think of any such game off the top of my head - In A Wicked Age perhaps?
    Storming the Wizard's Tower is like that, too. EDIT TO ADD: I think maybe Lacuna is also like that.

    Another idea: I ran 52 straight weeks of Call of Cthulhu as a "floating campaign", where each session was a one-shot, but they all took place in the same continuity. I ran the game for whichever 4-8 people wanted to play on a given Saturday. This works best if the GM holds on to the sheets, I think - in fact, I just re-located my thick folder of everyone's character sheets, sorted by player name, last week, so I have a kickass treasure trove of fully-statted NPC sheets again (maybe a hundred of them!). Character "advancement" in CoC is a wash, so a new character could be thrown into a party of five veteran investigators, and not be at any real disadvantage.

    That aside, my other suggestion is to make yourself a brand - "I promise that if you show up, we'll play something fun." Always have three games scouted out and ready for a given session, one for 2-3 players, one for 4-6 players, and some idea of what you'd do with more. Stay enthusiastic no matter what your turnout is, and you'll start having players come back.

    Posted By: cwilburThere are a lot of people out there who make a great deal of noise about wanting to game but who don't actually put in the effort to get themselves into a group. Once you figure out who these people are, the best route is to ignore them completely.
    \nThis, a million times this.

    I've been running a very solid and steady 4E group in NYC for nearly a year now. I think my success is partly due to luck, to be sure, but I also think I made some smart moves early on. We did a whole session just to meet-and-greet and create characters together. I hammered home the idea that we will play, every Thursday, at this location and time. I worked my butt off to make sure the first couple of sessions were rockin' hard. (I even had a friend come to help me run NPCs at the first session, which worked great.) I'm good about emailing, communicating with the group in general, making announcements at the start of each session, asking for feedback, that sort of thing. I've also shown time and again that I respect when it's getting late, when we need to wrap, and that I'm good at pacing that sort of thing (in general, combat in 4E can be a little time-bloaty, but that's another thread).

    One PC and a few NPCs have come and gone but five players have been in the game since the beginning, and I only allow six at a time, so that's pretty damn good. (And the guy who stopped PCing came back and NPC'd for awhile when his schedule allowed, even helping me plan out a major area for sandbox play, which was amazing.)

    I haven't socialized with everyone from the group outside of the game quite as much as I'd like, but I have seen everyone outside the normal game at least once for a one-shot, and two of the players I see fairly often at other events (most NerdNYC stuff, also Dreamation in one case).

    Mind you, if I simply wanted to start a gaming club, where we meet every week to try out a different one-shot game, I'd've taken a very different approach. Make sure *you* know what kind of club you want to start. I think the two are very different. (There's a thread around here somewhere where Jenskot talks about how people who want to play trad games actually seem much more likely to commit, and commit hard, than indie gamers.)

    I have a whole rant about scheduling games. The short version basically boils down to: it's not, generally speaking, more of a commitment to game than it is to sing in a community choir (something I've also done in a few different contexts). And of course there are always things that will be more important than either gaming or choir, causing you to miss a session / rehearsal: sudden loss of job, medical emergency, hell, even more normal sickness. The real issue is when people prioritize gaming lower than all other social activities, so that it always winds up losing out. (Related to this is that people would rather do something like gaming with their friends, which is why it's important to reach out to your players and befriend them.)

    Matt

    \n A few years ago a local guy in my town started a meetup group for Gamers in the Toronto area even though the Toronto Role-Players Association already existed. I joined the little club of about six members. We would get together once a month or so for a social at a pub and organize games online by posting events.
    \n
    \nIt's about six years later. Through perseverance, thorough organizing and being dogged about posting events, making new events, having a steady stream of campaigns and one-shots, our club is at 350 members or so, beating out the much older TRPA. If you want to run a gaming club, the key is to post one-shots or social events people will come out. Then you'll find your active members and just boot out any non-active members to keep your club clear of 'clutter'. That being said, it is a level of dedication that you have to be prepared to make.
    \n
    \nAs a club, we have about ten - twelve organizers on the board that are constantly working to make the club more efficient, keep it clear of unwanted clutter (bad members, unwanted behaviour, inactive members, etc), keep events going, and keep members happy by listening to their needs. It is basically a job I have that I don't get paid for. The amount of flak you get from members is sometimes alarming. But we keep a high number of organizers to keep the club running smoothly (ish) and to keep new ideas flowing. The main thing we found in drawing people out was a monthly social that had board games, card games, and the odd one-shot. We also have a board game monthly, an rpg monthly, and several rpg one-shots a month.
    \n
    \nI would not recommend starting a club unless you're prepared for the responsibility if it happens to grow beyond any expectations you have.
    \n
    \nIf you start one, post events on that facebook group in a more public setting. If people come out, cool. If not, well, you have your answer as to how many on the facebook group are going to be gaming. or do a pub night where people can rsvp yes/no and there's no big risk because it's at a pub. Call it a "Meet and Greet" if you want.
