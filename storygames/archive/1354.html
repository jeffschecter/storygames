Bad lessons from mainstream games

Judson_Lester
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Story Games
Okay, I recognize that I've been trained into two really bad habits by mainstream play.

One: anything presented in the game text as a mystery of the setting is off limits. No group I'm in will ever follow up on that mystery. Why? Because I've been whipped like a dog by metaplot running roughshod over my plans for a setting. ("Well, don't use that suppliment" "But I paid for it" "Well, you didn't have to buy it" "Why did they print it then?") Tell me decisive facts about the setting and I will play to or against them, but leave anything up in the air and I steer well clear.

The other reason behind this, and it's more justifiable I think, is that if there's a vague point of any kind, then by playing into that void, I might be stepping on someone's toes. If the setting says that the King has one daughter, and she's missing, and I make the True Princess, what if someone else wanted to be the True Princess?

Which leads to two: I hedge my character concepts. I'm loath to make decisive statements about my characters for fear that they won't fit into the campaign the GM put together. No point in being a born in New York, never left New York, never will leave New York cop, if the GM start off with "we're playing in Sydney." And I've gotten whipped on this one, too. Had several characters I'd built up and loved cored by a dictum from behind the screen. So now I build little half ideas into characters, with the hope that the other half will come out of somewhere.

=============================

Now, I've seen games that address my first problem. Clinton is really clear in TSoY: do whatcha like because I will never contradict you. He also provides lots of clear facts, and really awesome strong statements about Near, and not much in the way of vague mystery.

My second issue I think is just bad habits left over from weaker GMs. Some of it's in the "you must write the whole campaign before chargen" mentality - that I think is spawned by "so that we can sell you campaign books" supplement mill model.

What I'd love to hear is how various processes and theory models address all this. Shared character creation, flags, single book games all come immediately to mind. Other schools of thought also supplicated.

And, hell, what bad habits do you have?

    On Heroquest, Max is correct about said section. But a couple of caveats. First, Robin Laws wrote Hero Wars, and material from that was lifted from it for Hero Quest. He may have rewritten some for it, too, I'm not sure. But look at the authors list for HQ, and you'll see several names there. So any incongruity between Mr. Laws work in other games and in HQ should probably be put down to somebody else having put those words in.

    If that stuff is in HW as well (it might be), I think that this edition, too, had multiple authors.

    Somebody who can correct me on this should, if it turns out that Laws wrote that section. For the record, as well, I'm not even sure that Laws wrote that part of OTE...though that I could believe. He wasn't the primary author in that case. But he did give great advice in the sections credited to him.

    HQ goes back and forth between a set of coherent advice that sometimes approaches DitV in terms of effectiveness, and then lots of other material that directly contradicts the good stuff.

    What's interesting about the bad advice in this specific case (how to avoid apparent character sociopathy brought on by players who aren't really playing HQ), is that it at times gets close to being good advice. That is, rather than black that section out, I read it a tad differently than most. What I see is "inform the player that you as another participant in the game is a significant act, by calling for a contest for the character to accomplish it." That way you get to see what abilities exist (or do not exist), on the character sheet that make him the killer that he is.

    Generally speaking, I completely agree that using in-game hammers to smack down problems with players not playing by the rules are bound to fail. But in this case, I think what we're talking about is an agenda difference, and, in that case, I think that mechanics are precisely the thing to bring everybody onto the same sheet of music, if it's going to happen at all.

    From that POV, maybe Laws did write it. Yeah, the text in question smacks of, "In playtesting I had a player who saw HQ's lack of limitations on character behavior as an excuse to be an asshat, so I've included suggestions on how to fix that." If that's what it really is, then, yeah, it's wrong, wrong, wrong. But if it's really more about traditional players not getting the paradigm of play, and how to get them to understand it, then I can forgive it.

    HQ's challenge was that it supported a new paradigm, but still wanted to be able to cater to the RQ players. Not an easy task. So, if it's confused, this is why.


    But, generally I think we all agree that any advice that has to do with punishing a player in-game to get them to "play right" is seriously misguided. As a general principle, I think this covers half of the lessons listed above.

    I'm going to go out on a limb to figure out why such text is common. I think that a game designer looking at his design will think, "Gee, remember that asshat Ted? What if the GM running this game has an asshat like that in his game? What advice can I give the GM to deal with this? Ah, I know, the GM can punish the player by not giving EXP." And the designer does this even though it's not what he did with Ted. Ted was expelled from the game. Or the game collapsed because of Ted. Or the GM worked it out with Ted on a social level, taking him aside and talking it out.

    Because what did not happen is that the Designer/GM here punished Ted in-game, and it actually made Ted play right. But the Designer not knowing how to say how to deal with this outside of in-game consequences, resorts to what he feels he can write into the game.

    Oh, actually many games have suggestions about "taking the player aside and talking to him" as well. The problem is that the designer in this case is incorrectly identifying the problem behavior as protected. That is, the player is considered the sole authority on the character, so you're not allowed to give them feedback on what they should have the character do - that would be considered playing their character for them. As such, the player isn't considered to be out of line, so you can't correct the behavior on a social level. So you have to correct it on a in-game level is the assumption. So the "Taking the player aside" text refers only to "OOC" behavior.

    To say nothing of the fact that "taking the player aside" is indicative of punitive measures (else why do you have to keep it private), instead of simply working out play together. But that's a whole 'nother discussion.

It's mainly about dealing with asshattery, particularly of the "my guy's invincible" type that plagues GMs. And the solution of letting them feel the consequences of their own actions is a good one, at its core. But the way it's laid out, it's really a thinly veiled excuse for the Mighty GM Hammer. It's not "Here's some consequences, now you have to deal with that." it's more like "Here's some consequences, and now you are SCREWED."

    Mind you, I ate it up at the time. I lovingly read it to my more trusted players, chortling about how this system would put those "powergamers" in their place. (Incidentally, it didn't, they walked all over me, but then, I never really pulled out the Hammer.) But now, I look back on it and. . .well, dammit, it's just so passive-aggressive. As I write this, I'm not so sure the problem is in the screwed-ness; the severity of the consequences are variable and don't have to be an all-out pooch-screw. No, it's more the tone; it's not putting the problem out in the open, it's hiding behind a GM's screen and acting like it's not you making that pooch-screw happen. It'd be much better to just throw out the clusterfuck the PCs have earned, smile and say "hey, you asked for it. . ." Hell, it doesn't even have to be a bad thing; for all it's "Oh shit!" factor, it can be a springboard for cool situation, rather than a punishment.

    Damn, I sure went on, eh? I gues we've found one of MY bad lessons learned: THe GM-Player relationship is adversarial, because they outnumber you and will walk all over you.

    So,uh, there,I'm even on topic. :)
