    \nI based those figures on a scale of $20/hour, for a 5 hour game with an hour discussion afterward. Running a game is generally worth about 2 hours prep, I figure. Something pretty complex, like Burning Wheel or Smallville, might bumpe that up an hour or two. I decided the extra handling time of setting up a Skype conference was worth an extra hour, and the extra drudgery of listening thoughtfully to a 4-5 hour audio is equivalent to a couple more hours.

    Now, that's all assuming I decided to offer this service for money in the first place. It's tricky to evaluate what you're worth charging money for, when you're talking about something you love doing. Because all three of those things are something I've done for free, at least in part. I've watched games being played, though usually not the whole session. I definitely play games with people for free (duh!), and I'm more than willing to discuss it afterward--what went well, what fell flat, what could be improved, what new techniques could be brought in, etc. I've done that for free and at some length with a close friend or two, and online i've been on both the giving and receiving end. And while Kevin Wieser has mysteriously not called me up for any Skype critiques, I've certainly listened to many hours of Walking Eye AP recording, simply because I wanted to. (Though it still is a drudgery, attending to that kind of audio for any length of time. I had a long commute that enabled me to do it.)

    So, the thing is, people who do what they love find it in themselves all the time to charge money for it. Musicians take gigs and sell CDs. Consultants in all kinds of fields, professional AND hobby, get paid simply to talk to people about stuff they do, and how they might do it better. Professional mediators help people sort out their shit for money, whereas a thoughtful and trusted relative might do that without a thought of charging.

    I'd have a hard time convincing myself it was OK to accept money for roleplaying and roleplaying-related services. But that's something I could overcome by reminding myself that my time and skill is valuable, that I do have insight and expertise to offer, and that getting paid for doing something you love is a helluva lot better than getting paid for something you hate. I've had to go through a similar process to convince myself it's OK to charge money for a story game I've written.

    There are some issues and landmines, for sure. Like, if playing, watching and critiquing roleplaying games is a job, then where do I draw the line? Am I always on duty, and thus have to turn down casual coaching requests because "sorry, I charge for that?" Or do friends get a pass? Or do they only get a pass case-by-case, if you feel like it? It's like having a friend who's a masseuse; everyone is hoping for free backrubs from her, just this once, because I'm so tense and we're pals, right, but if she gave out free massages to everyone she knows she'd wear herself out and still not make rent that month. If I entered into this kind of venture I'd be sure to seek the advice and perspective of people I know who have done similar kinds of coaching/tutoring gigs.

    I did run a story game for money, once. A primitive skills organization I'd worked for as a camp counselor asked me to come up to their adult camp and run a fireside storyjam to help teach the trackers storytelling methods that they could use to process their woodland experiences. I ran a stripped-down In a Wicked Age with playing cards, using an Oracle that we made together based on wildlife sightings from their day's tracking.

    It was nice. It was fun. it was a learning experience for me, as well as for them. And I made bank. It wasn't a game with friends, though. I maintained a politely professional relationship with the event organizer, and if he contacts me again, I'll do the same.

    Would I be willing to charge my friends for a service like this? I think so. I know someone who charges friends for music lessons, and I wouldn't dream trying to wheedle them out of him for free. On the other hand, he HAS given songwriting workshops for free, when he chooses to. So it's just something that requires more careful navigating than a serveice offered to strangerss.

    Peace,
    -Joel

    \n So, first, let me stress that I am also happy to give supportive critique and help unpack gaming experiences with you, for free. Absolutely. My answers above were taking seriously the "hypothetically, I'm hiring you as a tutor" premise. But hell yeah, I'll talk to you about this shit, as a friend, on or offline, anytime we have the time and space for it.

    Second, I've found that the most useful critique isn't grounded so much in telling you everything you did wrong, or what you sucked at, but in looking at what worked well, what felt awkward, what could be improved, and so forth. Sounds similar to the "three improves, three sustains" you were mentioning. Was that Ross's technique?

    I've noticed in the midst of your wonderful enthusiasm to learn and improve as a new storygamer that you're tending to focus on how meager your skills are and how little you know and how much you screw up, at least by your own self-judgment. I've played with you just once, but it was one of your first games ever if I'm not mistaken, and I found you pleasant and skilful to play with. Not that you couldn't improve, but I didn't in the least walk away thinking "man, cool game I guess but Morgan really brought it down," or 'Jeez, that Morgan guy messed up the whole session!" I didn't think a single critical thought about your play, in fact. I really dug what you were doing. yet in your excellent actual play report you seemed to think you had done a poor job at playing the game. The issues you raised were good ones to reflect on, but i'm much more comfortable discussing them as learning experiences than as screwups.

    The key here is that the debrief process is focused on how you felt when you played, rather than someone's arbitrary judgment of how it sucked or was awesome or whatevs. Even if that arbitrary judger is yourself. Maybe especially. It's more helpful for you, in the long run, if you can learn to contextualize your own experience outside of value judgments. Not "well, I really dropped the ball there," but rather "When I did X, I felt like the momentum of the game stopped dead." And then I (or Joe, or Ross, or whoever) could say "oh, what do you think made it do that?" And you could reflect, and say "well, by presenting X in terms of Y, I think I inadvertently gave the impression to the other players that their input wasn't needed there." Or whatever.

    This all teaches you (for whatever value of "you"--the "you" in the above paragraph is actually "me") to evaluate yourself honestly without guilt-tripping or running yourself down, and to focus on improving what works for YOU, not measuring up to some monolithic outside idea of what constitutes "good gaming." The tutor/debriefer/helpful friend can provide input on best practices, their own impressions from observing and playing, etc, but it's a process that's aimed at aiding your own drive for self-improvement, not imposing our own standards on you.

    My friend Willem Larsen, founder of Language Hunters (formerly Where are Your Keys? for those in the know) has used these techniques for years, and I've found them very helpful for debriefing facilitating experiences of all kinds, from leading languagehunting tables to running story games. It's helpful without doing verbal violence to each other.

    Look forward to much playing AND much talking at Cosycon!

    I do tend to have a self-deprecating way of writing, but that is just to level out my near asshole-level of self-love. Don\u2019t worry, there\u2019s no \u201cbad self talk\u201d going on in my head. I think I kick ass. That\u2019s why I\u2019m certain that I will soon be the BEST at all story games. Evah.

    Also, I always \u201cfeel\u201d like everything went great during every session. I have huge fun every time I roleplay, just like I have since ran a poorly prepared session of Pirates and Plunder in middle school. You suggest letting my own feelings guide me, but I already do that automatically. I already have access to how I feel. I want more.

    After a lot of reflection and other stimuli I can figure out some things to improve.     I\u2019ve been having a fascinating discussion with Wilhelm since he whispered to me in this thread, where he makes similar points about not letting others\u2019 opinions influence me unduly. What I keep trying to tell him is that I\u2019m not doormat. I\u2019m going to think critically about any advice given and test it against the world. And with that defense up, the best strategy for me is to solicit as much harsh advice from as many sources as possible. The person dispensing advice will also have to deal with potentially being ignored.

    My play-style has an audience just like if I wrote a paper or you designed a game. I wouldn\u2019t submit a paper that hadn\u2019t been edited by at least one other person any more than you would publish a game that hadn\u2019t been playtested. Also, I would never tell my editor to only give me feedback on the parts of the paper that I worried were poor, just like you probably don\u2019t pre-censor your playtesters. Can those who offer critiques give you advice that you don\u2019t think is helpful or correct? Of course. But you evaluate it as best as you can and then either accept or reject it.
