    \n I've used Fronts for my "All Wizards" D&D3.5/PF one-shot / introductory session.

    Here they are:

    Front A: Curious Goblins
    1) Justina reports seeing some small shapes nosing around in the bushes near the north side of town. She shot arrows at them but didn\u2019t hit anything; they scampered off.
    2) Five goblins and a lizard-hound (use Riding-Dog stats) are spotted on the fields of the Oakes farm.
    3) A large goblin raiding party\u20146 goblins mounted on 6 lizard-hounds!\u2014strikes into the center of town
    4) A pair of Hill Giants stomps around, bashes some heads, and leaves
    5) \u201cThey\u2019ve brought a cave troll!\u201d

    Front B: Panic on the homefront
    1) An appropriate community leader (see NPCs list) asks the PCs what they intend to do
    2) A large crowd of villagers gathers at the base of the tower (or wherever the PCs are) demanding aid/protection
    3) The Leaping Lamb is burglarized, finger-pointing and brawling ensue
    4) The villagers hurriedly gather what they can carry/load into carts
    5) The fleeing villagers are slaughtered on the road by boars; a few escape into the underbrush with no equipment or food

    Front C: Finishing the job
    1) The ghost of an appropriate Master appears to cryptically warn the PCs
    2) An appropriate NPC (actually a succubus!) claims s/he knows how to rekindle the magical defenses, but it requires some ingredients from the swamp\u2026
    3) Tiny portals open in several places at once; a horde of Quasits pops out and begins causing havoc
    4) Rumbling and bright lights from the top of the Tower.
    5) A portal opens at the top of the Tower, a demon lord of Hell steps through

    \n I applied a variant of AW's front rules to a pre-written In Nomine adventure. The adventure is about a series of events that, if left unchecked, could lead to the Biblical Armageddon. Since the events could happen in any order rather than along a linear "countdown," I used checklists rather than countdown clocks. (Like, in the front relating to whether an Archangel might Fall, they get a "minus" in a checkbox if he discovers the PCs are spying on him for the other Archangels, but they get a "plus" in a checkbox if they publicly testify on his behalf to the other Archangels.) Perhaps allowing players to explicitly address a front with both "positive moves" and "negative moves" works against the sense of creeping doom that comes with a countdown clock, but it felt appropriate for a setting where hope means a lot.

    The checkboxes themselves were a pain to deal with as an "interface," though. It would've made it easier to see how things were going along the way if I'd used some kind of visual aid like a countdown clock. If I do this in the future, I'm going to use something like the round table in Shadows Over Camelot: You put a black sword on when you fail a quest, and a white sword on when you succeed at one, and you lose if the table fills with more black swords with white swords, but you may do the quests themselves in any order.

    \n I'm currently working (where working=thinking about how I need to write something down) on making some Fronts for my Trail of Cthulhu adaptation of Masks of Nyarlathotep.

    Masks lends itself pretty readily to this (maybe more than other Cthulhu scenarios) because of the various conspiracies in each location--each one can be considered (to borrow from DW) an Adventure Front, and all tie into the Campaign Front: the Big N wants to bring cosmic weirdness down to good ol' Planet Earth.

    I've found in my preliminary work that it really focuses me on what's going on, what each Threat wants to achieve, and how they work together (or, because I like my wheels to be within wheels, against each other.)

    \n Thanks, Deliverator! And I just got a chance to read Johnstone's posts linked above \u2013 great examples in there that can definitely be applied to a variety of other settings.

    I've been thinking about this thread a bit, and I remembered one other thing that I found useful in thinking about fronts in other kinds of games: going in with an idea about what triggers their advancement. In Apocalypse World, "advance a countdown clock" is always an option on a hard move, presumably informed by what's going on in the fiction. In a game where you don't make hard moves, though, it's not always necessarily obvious when is the right time to advance a countdown.

    In the In Nomine game I mentioned above, I had it kind of easy: The fronts had conscious adversaries behind them operating on their own schedule. All I had to do was arbitrarily decide some deadlines for when things needed to be addressed by the PCs before they just happened. (Probably the most depressing was when the PCs spent a night spinning their wheels and wasting their time on a red herring, only to hear one of the Seven Trumpets of the Apocalypse blaring out in the morning while they were waiting in an airport to get to where they actually needed to go. But what's an Apocalypse without a few moments of futility and despair, right?) I imagine it might be a little trickier to determine the pacing of fronts that aren't so purposefully directed, though, like the spread of a disease. Maybe you just advance things when you feel like things are getting boring, but it could be nice to have in mind a more deliberate mechanical trigger (more so than a fictional trigger, as there's no telling what the players will do).

    \n The reason I like ordered countdowns -- ie having the grim portents proceed in a specific order -- is so that pacing isn't an issue. You just look at the top entries of all of them and pick the one you like best, when it's your turn to say something, frame a scene, whatever. Or when you don't know what to do next, look down, pick one, keep going. You don't have to worry about linking them to hard moves and whatnot.

    The point being that you do most of your "thinking about the future" ahead of time, so when you're playing, you can concentrate on thinking about what is happening right now. Plus, it's more organized and deliberate than using random tables.
