    \n Here's a description I wrote for someone who asked in an unrelated forum.

    ========

    In a nutshell? It's a narration-trading game, it's not *exactly* an RPG,
    though it's close. It's designed for two players, though there are rules for
    expanding it to more. The constants of the game are, that it's about a badass
    hero of one kind or another (and there are a number of kinds, and you can make
    your own), in a strange location. In the location are a Monster, who will kill
    you, and a Lover, who is willing. The game is written using the first person
    for the quasi-GM, and the second for the quasi-player.

    The "player" chooses a description for the hero, and a location, from
    a list, and adds to the description. The "player" also makes up a
    Goal for the hero that he is trying to achieve in that location (this
    is not chosen from a list.)

    The "GM" elaborates the description of the location, and creates from
    whole cloth a Monster and a Lover who will be there. For the Monster
    he chooses from four pairs of descriptions, i.e. does the monster
    attack Singly or in a Group, in a Civilized or Savage way, and the
    same deal for the Lover, does she desire the hero Innocently or
    Wantonly, Helplessly or Knowledgeably, etc. Other than choosing from
    those pairs, the GM is making things up out of whole cloth, not
    choosing from a list.

    The "GM" describes a place within the location, which includes the
    Lover. The "player" describes his entering the place.

    After that play proceeds in a series of "Goes." On your Go, you
    describe what happens next. The other person can suggest and
    contribute but you have final say on what happens. You can control
    the other person's "stuff" during your Go -- the GM can describe what
    the hero does during his Go, and the hero can describe what the other
    things in the world do during his Go. So it's unlike a traditional
    game in that way. But the player still invests himself primarily in
    the hero, and the gm still primarily focuses on external forces and
    persons.

    You can tweak how "tight" you want to play it, making it a little more
    or less like a traditional RPG in that way.

    Each "Go" contains exactly one significant forward-moving event, one
    thing that happens that moves the story forward, changes how things
    are happening. Once that happens, your Go will be over soon. You
    can't hog the story -- ony one major even happens before you give
    control back to the other character.

    At a certain point the Match will start. It starts when the player
    takes decisive action towards the goal, or the GM has the monster
    make a significant move.

    The Match is something that happnes during Goes -- the GM and player
    not only move the story forward, but also collect a stack of dice
    which will determine the outcome of the story. They get dice for
    totally different things. the GM gets dice for having the monster
    smack down the hero. the player gets dice for taking action towards
    the goal, and either accepting or rejecting the Lover, physically and
    otherwise.

    There are certain limits during all these Goes -- you can't
    definitively kill off the Hero or the Monster (though you can appear
    to) -- nor definitively obtain or lose the Goal -- all the things that
    are going to be determined by the Match have to remain on the table.
    Other than that, anything goes during Goes.

    Eventually some conditions obtain which trigger a final determination
    of the outcome of the match. This will mean that the player either
    gets the goal or fails to get it, and also gets a certain number (zero
    or more) of Good Dice, each of which is used to purchase a positive
    outcome. If you don't explicitly purchase a positive outcome, you
    don't get it.

    Positive outcomes include:

    * overriding a failure to get the Goal, and getting it anyway
    * Killing the monster
    * not getting killed or horribly wounded by the monster
    * saving another character who is in danger, including the Lover

    etc.

    You also choose as a player (unconstrained by the dice) whether you
    accept or reject the lover, and if you accept them, whether you stay
    with them in the location or take them with you.

    After the determination of the outcome, you keep playing in Goes until
    you've made all those things happen one way or the other.

    Then you switch roles, and the "gm" is the new "player" and vice
    versa.

    If your hero dies, you make a new hero next time it's your turn to be
    the player.

    If you stay with the Lover, you are done being the Player, and don't
    get to make a new hero. Your story is over.

    Otherwise there are rules for how the outcome of this story affects
    subsequent stories with your hero.

    And that's about it.

    OK, that's a big-ass nutshell.
