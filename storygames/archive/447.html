When do things get too... meta?

JasonP
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games
[ For the purpose of discussion below, meta-metagame refers to mechanics that are very (if not totally) disconnected from the action inside the game, a step removed ]

I've been working on a system for a while now. I've played it four sessions, and each was good. One thing bugs me though, and this is what I will be thrashing on about here.

The game is pretty removed from the conventional rpg, using story/drama power as the basis more than stats. The core is very solid and works well. Outside the core is 'advantage'. This is represented by cards which the players hold in hand. Through various rules, the cards can be played to: Override rolls of the dice, Protect their character from fallout, Establish new character situation (like a stat), and so on. These rules all seem well and good, connecting the advantage cards to the roleplay.

There are other rules to establish motion of cards. Just holding onto a bunch of cards and using them seems... bland. These rules aren't connected to the roleplay but pure metagame. This is where my feelings become mixed.

Sometimes the card motion and play comes to the forefront, and then the roleplay falls to the back, and I'm not sure how I feel about that. So I find myself asking: When do things get too meta? How much metagame is too much? For the kind of drama-based play I want the game to provide I obviously need metagame, so the question becomes how much?

I guess I'm wondering if other designers/players have an experience in the realm, and their thoughts on the matter. :)

    I am not sure that the bracket that started off this whole conversation might not be itself part of the difficulty you're facing. There are all sorts of mechanics in all sorts of games that cheerfully straddle this line: action dice in Spycraft, Drama Points in Buffy...anything related to genre, anything at all, makes idenfitying "meta" mechanics incredibly hard. When someone spends a Drama Point in Buffy to say "I think I'm okay!" and restore a bunch of Life Points after a fight, surely this is deeply connected to the action inside the game. In fact, it's a key part of the game's simulation of TV action-comedy style combat. Yet of course none of the characters know anything about Drama Points.

    I would suggest thinking of a different way of identifying what troubles you about what you've got and what you're aiming at.
