    \n I played this game a lot. It was a point of pride at the time that it was "realistic", in stark opposition to games like D&D, which means the core system was ridiculous and very difficult to play but did allow you to bleed to death point by point.

    The core idea is so good - you are a modern dude expecting to be activated in the chaos and terror that would immediately follow a global nuclear exchange and take charge of reconstruction. Instead of emerging five years post-war along with thousands of other dudes, you wake up 250 years too late, more or less alone, to a world of barbarism and gigantic porcupines. Your core mission is unchanged but it just got 250 years harder. Still, you are well equipped to the point of absurdity, making you a god among disease-ridden screwheads. What are you going to do?

    In practice what you do is get in a lot of gunfights, because there are no rules for anything else.

    \n We played this game back in the 80's in college. As I recall the first thing you did was get a map of the US and a compass and nuke us. Draw circles around all the cities the Soviets targeted. Depending on the megatons the circles were really big. The blast zone uncoated areas were the less radioactive. This exercise alone was interesting and showed why nuclear war = bad.

    The game itself billed itself as being very realistic in a modern war way. The rich guy with psychic powers who foresaw the war and stowed away all the soldiers and weapons bought a lot of (then) modern equipment. I'm certain they need to up date that because armored cars from the late 60's are no longer "new".

    The scenario itself was kind of cool but I suspect at this point my experience and what the game said diverge. I know our GM made up their own stuff. We were in the Upper Peninsular of Michigan (which had no nukes targeted at it - surprise!). We encountered Laplanders because they are the main group who live there now. I think the GM did not use the combat rules but instead used a set of skirmish game rules from miniatures.

    It was interesting a fun but not really innovative. It wasn't what I'd call a story game. The rules did not help create story but didn't get in the way of it anymore than D+D did.

    It was fun. If I was a new gamer I might buy it.

    Chris Engle

    \n Deep nerd cred where it is due: I own one of the first 100 copies of the first edition, which wasn't even bound (!), signed. Apparently when they went looking for Soviet targeting data they were rebuffed by various US channels but got the same information from the UK MOD. Nuking America was a fun part of pre-play for the GM, that's for sure.

    I actually liked the notion that Morrow bolt-holes were from different eras, and guys from the early seventies might wake up to find all their comfortable tech had been replaced by super cool stuff from thirty years later.

    \n I played a fair bit in the 80s and have revisited it several times since (always using simpler mechanics).

    However a key bit of colour for us (that contrasts strongly with Jason's experience) was the later "Role-playing Supplement" that used a licensed version of BRP to add a skills system to the game. This was interesting because "Recon" teams (that the game seemed to pitch towards for most PCs) were mainly NOT ex-military types but a bunch of "useless" liberal arts graduates with basic firearms training! Thus you'd have a team consisting of a geography major, a dentist (oh for a real doctor!), an english lit grad and an electrician with a f-ton of ordnance and an armored car in the admittedly gonzo 250 years after the bomb. (Most of the military guys went to "Mars" teams, the scientists to "Science" teams).

    This was, for us, an interesting setup. We were the (heavily armed) good guys, out of contact with our main base, inexplicably delayed waking up, trying to convince the suspicious natives that they needed our help but our skills were near useless (for fighting or re-building) and we had all this glorious kit that the local strong-man wanted his hands on...

    \n I ran Morrow Project back in the 80s, and had (still have) all the supplement adventures. I've wanted to run it using Apocalypse World (possibly mixed with Reign of Fire). If I ran it now, what would really interest me is exploring WHY the PCs thought waking up in a nuclear wasteland was a better life than what they had before volunteering. What's your motivation? Was it the mother of all bad breakups? Also, the fun of discovering the new world, figuring out what's going on, winning the hearts and minds of the natives, and being big cheeses until your ammo runs low.

    A full spasm nuclear war seems like a pretty unlikely future now, but if the players were willing to sign a blank check, they could wake up 150 years after some other cataclysm and have fun figuring it out. 7th Sigma would be fun, but really mean.

    We used Recon teams, whose expendability made for good adventure. I still have fond memories of my military veteran players setting up camp each night, complete with fire zones, emplaced claymores, rally points, and such. Not just pulling the V-150 over and catching some Zzzz's.

    No PDF for the Kickstarter means I'm probably not interested in v4, though. It's not the 80s anymore.

    \n Yeah, I'll admit it. I'm old enough. I played this a couple times back when it came out. And I remember loving it at the time. In fact, it made such a mark on my RPG-psyche, I've been quixotically trying to find it ever since. (I have to admit, I located a scanned PDF -- just for nostalgia, don't ya know -- and that dampened my enthusiasm. I didn't really remember the thing looking so much like a list of military supplies...)

    For the longest while, the Timeline website was totally dead. All the hints and rumors had come to naught. But lately, they've been pretty active on FB. Editing is proceeding apace and I understand the kickstarter is moving forward.

    I'm curious how it's going to turn out. This game is *such* a product of Reagan-era nuke paranoia that it's hard to see the game setting working as well as it did back then. I agree with other posters -- getting out the compass and the atlas was an eye-opener for certain. But given how the RPG world, and maybe more importantly my own personal tastes, have changed, the actual system will be a big question mark. It's hard to see why I wouldn't just use some form of AW to remake Morrow if I really wanted to.

    Also, and I don't want to take too strong a position here, the FB postings I've been following make me, well, hesitate a bit. The stuff they've been putting out there seem really "gun-ish" and while I understand that was a big part of the original, it's not where I am nowadays.

    Fingers crossed!
