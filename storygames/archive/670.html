The Lion in Winter

Brand_Robins
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Story Games
So Mo and I were talking the other day over brunch about things we'd like to do in games. She said it would be fascinating to her to make a game where the point of the reward system is to get you to lose. You don't, for example, get Xp for beating conflicts you get Xp for getting your ass kicked. I said I wanted to play games where you didn't "advance" at all Ã¢â‚¬â€œ where you started off as good as you were going to get and went downhill. We then started banging those two ideas together, and came up with about a dozen different permutations that we thought would be neat. This one is the one that I've chosen to write about today.

The Lion in Winter

"Do not give up hope! Even if you lie at death's door
For the lamp gives light before it is empty
And the wounded lion still knows how to roar."

The concept of the game is simple: reverse the whole "you are a farmboy that grows into his power" motif that has dominated so much of gaming. In this game you start off as a Lion In Winter Ã¢â‚¬â€œ one of the greatest men and women of the day, just ever so slightly past the apogee of your power and ability. You are the greatest swordsman the land has ever seen, you are the queen, you are the elder statesman who has put presidents into office, you are the grandmaster smith of the guild. But you can feel it starting to slip, ever so slowly. There are young sharks who want to take you down to prove themselves, old enemies whom you've bested in the past who wait patiently to weave your doom, and most hauntingly of all your slowly dawning realization that this isn't who you wanted to be.

The game's intent is to bring you face to face with some of the choices of the mid and post-mid life protagonist. Do you continue to fight for the things you have built up with so much pain and effort, even though you know you've seen the moment of your greatest skill pass and can wait for nothing but the slow decline, or do you face giving up everything you've worked so hard for to start over as a newb at something ruled over by punks less than half your age?

Your characters start off with a core mastery in which you are the best of the best. Even if you aren't what you yourself were a year (or five) ago, you're still better than any single other individual in the world. You can also have other skills, at lesser levels. The basic "advancement" system is then based off if you win or lose the challenges you get into. If you win a challenge then you get to keep your core mastery at its current level. If you lose a challenge, you have to drop your core competency Ã¢â‚¬â€œ but you gain quick advancements in lesser skills. So you always face the choice of staying good at what you are the best at already (but never getting better) or branching out and trying new things at the expense of your mastery.

Anyway, that's as far as I've thought it through. Seems like a nifty concept that'd take a lot of work to turn into a game. But I do so love the idea of itÃ¢â‚¬Â¦.

    Reminds me of a game design I have languishing in my folder: Magic's End. Basically, players are archmages in a world were the gods have just left, and magic trickles away. Its a difficulty number based system, where mundane skills are rolled as dice, and magic is just a lump sum (e.g. a mundane Dodge = 4d8 (totaled), while a magical Shield = 20). But each time a magic skill is used it drops by one. So it is both more reliable and more visceral.

    Part of the idea behind the game was that the loss of the gods meant that destiny (basically different races had built in destinies, as heroes, villains, sidekicks, etc.) and the general social structures were no longer in place (namely archmages don't run everything any more). So the characters must choose to either learn to live mundanely, or use sacrifice rituals to permanetly destroy some of the remaining magic of the world, for a small boost to a magic skill.

    Additionally, I was considering almost exactly that concept in an old RPGnet article on opposition mechanics. Although at the moment I'm working on the Coming of Age version.

    This is such a cool idea--this whole idea of increasing inability in a game has been on my backburner for a little bit now in a vague, indistinct way. I'm so happy to see someone thinking about it in a concrete way.

    I agree with Shreyas, though, that diversification seems to run counter to the mood of the game.

    Mechanically, I would be really interested in seeing something where you paid for victories with your expertise. Just a few weeks ago, I saw a special on PBS ('Holy Warriors') that developed the Richard the Lionheart v. Saladin conflict during the crusades. In that case, it is clearly Saladin who is the 'Lion in Winter' with the young punk Richard having the sort of wild success Saladin had in his own youth.

    The cycle in that conflict does not have Saladin diversfying--quite the opposite, he finds his former strengths waning, and the more he tries to use them, the more rapidly they fail him. That seems the pinnacle of the tragedy involved in the Lion in Winter storyline--not just the loss of one's expertise, but that the very use of that expertise hastens the end, no matter one's success or failure.

    To do that, though, you need to really be able to dramatize the choice to engage or not engage, so that when the player engages, they do so because they are willing to wager their most valuable and unique resource in a desperate attempt to change the world just one last time. You want them to hear the slow build towards an epic crescendo where they either vindicate their genius one last time or are broken...

    Oooh, Mo, I like the axis of happiness vs. glory. Very tragic!

    A related idea that I've been thinking about is the question of, "Can the work I've done go on without me?" Give the character something that they care a hell of a lot about - the kingdom they've built, the discoveries they've made, their family, their reputation, or any of the other things you mentioned in your example. Then force them to defend it with lessening and lessening resources. Only by sacrificing of themselves can they preserve what they've created - so the player is constantly forced to choose between Pain Now (my legacy gets fucked somehow) or Pain Later (I take a hit to my abilities now to preserve my legacy, but hurt my ability to protect it later against a potentially worse threat). There's more than just a death spiral to this game, though - the player can turn to other characters to help protect and preserve the legacy as they fail. By the end of the game, they've gone from a solitary, fantabulous hero to a dependent member of a tight-knit community which is their only meaningful resource.

    I love Isbo's notion, too (and I"m sure you can see a lot of it in my idea, above).

  • Old Scratch
    May 2006
    I think it's a great concept, but I'm a little less keen on the whole skill aspect.

    When I read this, I made a couple of cognitive leaps: Lion in Winter -> Whose Afraid of Virginia Woolf? -> "The Bad Lieutenant". All about people who are in sharp decline.

    And it made me realize that what defines them is not their skills, but their relationships.

    I'd drop the entire skill concept and make it all about relationships. Not the best swordsman, but a reputation as a great swordsman, and I think there's an important distinction there.

    The whole game would be guided within the framework of these relationships that define the character. Frame a scene and the conflict would be resolved through the transformation of the relationship, with a slight leaning towards "burning bridges" rather than "building bridges". I immediately thought of it as relationships that defined by a descriptive quality ranking and one that goes up or down as one consequence (never stays the same) or rewritten.

    Example: A master Swordsman has an encounter with their young protege. The scene will focus on their relationship and their different needs. Their relationship could improve (unlikely) or decline (more likely) qualitatively, or it could be completely altered (from "Mentor to a Prodigy" to "Traitor to my Apprentice" or "The Moon Outshines the Sun".

    Initially, I thought of a built in endgame when one or more of these relationships reaches a critical level or all the relationships have been transformed, and the story ends there with a post-game analysis of that character's legacy as in the epilogue in My Life with Master.

    Those are some of my initial impressions... Love the idea, and I'm totally inspired by the decline of a Grey Eminence.
