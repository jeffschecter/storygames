\n Okay, so one day I was on wikipedia looking up Atomism and I came across this:
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomism#Geometry_and_atoms

I immediately went, "Whoa! Those are all dice shapes!"
I started thinking about how one could possibly create a game system or mechanic that used the four classic Greek elements and the shapes associated with their atoms.
I haven't thought about it all that hard, yet, but I'm aware that there are few new ideas under the sun, so I wondered if anyone else had ever considered this or done any tinkering with it.

The one thing that does strike me is that any system which used those dice in different ways to represent different elements would probably have to one in which larger or smaller dice were not inherently "better." Especially because they jump from an 8 sider for Air to a 20 sider for Water. A system based around these would have to somehow allow a 4 sided die to be just as effective, in its own way, as a 20 sided die.

    \n I think the issue of different elements being 'better' or not could be avoided in a couple of ways. One which would allow for numerical balance but be a pain to create would be to use custom dice with something other than the standard numbers on their faces. Alternatively, and my preference, would be to build the system so that the different elements do different things and are not directly in competition. That article talks about the shapes reflecting the nature of the elements and the 'd4' fire being more 'mobile' than the d6 earth.

    All d6s could be earth and are equal to one another, the same as all d20s are equal water dice. If you did want to put the elements in competition against one another, you could use the dice to 'build' the competitive force; a fire 'spell' requires 35 fire points (on average 10d6) while the equivalent water spell requires 105 water points (on average 10d20); in this example you need 10 dice either way.

    Other mitigating factors could be that the smaller the die, the more predictable it is. You're more likely to get a certain value on a d6 roll than a d20, so you could go ahead and allow water to be potentially more powerful but the actual power you harness depends on how close your roll is to a target number; using earth you could never be more than 5 out, while with water you could be up to 19 out.

    \n You could count matches on the dice. A mechanic I've always wanted to do something with exploits the fact that d4s roll matching numbers all the time, but they're low, while d20s don't match very often, but when they do, it's potentially very powerful.

    Rough idea:

    Roll your pool, which is a whole mess of dice. d4s are your "fire" power, d6s earth, and so on. Set aside any dice that don't have a match. Now look at your matching sets. The type of die rolled determines the type of effect, and the number on the die determines the power of it. If this is a magic system (which would be cool) any set with a d4 in it could be assigned to damage, d6s could be assigned to healing, d8s to something else, d20s could do some wacked out shit. Maybe they act as an amplifier for any set they're in? Oh! and maybe if you roll a matched set that contains opposing elements, you remove both dice from the set? That would reward players with complimentary pools. I'm just making things up here. What about special effects for combinations of elements? Like, a fire and eath set can damage scenery? (that's if this is still a magic system).

    I don't know as much about Greek elements, but Japanese elements have emotions and personalities associated with them. If that's the case for Greek elements too, you could get a neat thing where you've got to use a matched set of dice to narrate something in the game, and it has to relate to the emotions related to that set. Kind of a Dogs-like mechanic?

    \n I love the addition of the D12 void / spirit based magic and its discovery, and that helps define the mechanic more as well, because clearly it the mystery is so thought after then there must be something extra special about the D12's as well, which makes them better than the other dice.

    That can't be high numbers (D20's are better), or pairs (D4s, D6s and D8s are better). You could just say that you need 12's to succeed ... no, that 12s are wildcards and count as a match for a set? So any time your roll either produces a 12, or a set of dice that adds to 12, then you get an extra success on top of any pairs.

    So 3D4 rolling 3 on each dice gives 3 successes, but 3D4 rolling 4 on each dice gives 4 (a bonus for a total of 12)

    Each 12 on a D12 is another bonus in that case, regardless of the other values, but that assumes mixed pools. Maybe you can roll your D12s on every other pool as a bonus, since it complements the other elements? D20's can roll 12s too, but less often than D12s

    To incorporate the sizes you can say that when comparing successes the magnitude of the value from the pair breaks ties. Failing that the highest single value rolled breaks ties.

    So you roll 4D4 and a bonus D12 against my 3D20

    You roll 1,2,2,3 and 12, I roll 10,10,9. We both get a pair, but my pair beats yours because it is a pair of 10s not 2s. On the other hand your bonus 12 gets you three successes, so you win. Even if it had rolled an 11 instead of a 12 you still win, because your 12 beats my 10, but if my third dice was a 15 then *I* win because 15 beats 11.

    I still think that makes D4s better than D20s, but if there is some extra use put to the magnitude later then it could work.

    \n Using my pretty shaky math skill, it looks to me like, at least for rolling two dice, d20s are marginally better than d4s (i.e. the average roll is .3 better), and that in any case rolling matched dice is better than rolling a combination. This is especially true for d20s, because they have so many results that can't match the other dice. This leads me to feel that the dice balance themselves fairly well without any additional restrictions. I'm not sure what effect rolling more than three dice has though. Whether that rewards the frequently matching d4s more than the higher rolling d20s is an important point, but it's beyond my math ability.

    I think what we really need are ways to reward dice combinations, so that rolling matching numbers on different types of dice is more powerful than rolling matches on identical dice. I think using opposed elements would be cool as well.
