Games that deal with time in a real way

Caesar_X
February 2007 edited February 2007 in Game Design Help
I've been searching for RPGs that deal with time in a way that affects the game mechanics. Not necessarily what happened an hour or even a week ago. But changing game dynamics based on the past, present or future. This could be flashbacks, flashforwards, and the like that involve the same characters. Which would preclude the interesting "ongoing" sense of time in a game like Hero's Banner since it switches to new characters.

Have you seen any games that do what I am describing?

    Aaaaaaah!

    Hating to pimp my own still-in-development work, but Grey Ranks does exactly this. Each scene takes place on a specific day, moving forward in time across a span of two busy, awful months. The scene number is used to measure adversity, so it ramps up from 1 to 10 across as many scenes, which closely corresponds with the real-world situation reflected by the dates I picked.

    You play out missions taking place on those dates that are player-authored, and missions are interleaved with flashbacks and other personal scenes.

    We occasionally play a game of d20 CoC (yeah) where the team goes back in time for various weird-ass missions. they have a clock with them, a special clock that shows them when it's time to get back to their original location in order to be picked up by the time-portal thingie.     It seems to me that this thread is about two totally different things. Maybe it's just me, but in my mind "dealing with time" using a physical clock has little to do with characters having flashbacks...

    As for the former, we use a timer, a very old mechanical one which "beep" sounds more like a fire alarm. If the characters are short on time and have to do something really fast, I set the alarm for 10 or 20 minutes, and when it goes off, the expected bad stuff happens. For example, last week the characters were on a treasure hunt, and had to search in a place which was possible to enter only at low tide. We never play dungeon crawl, so obviously the players would've liked to be as careful as possible and would've discussed all the little details for hours if it wasn't for the alarm.

    As for the latter, I am actually designing a game now which does have some mechanism for dealing with flashbacks (or flashforwards). It's called Great Naruto Storytelling, but I'm afraid I don't have any meaningful reading to offer you just because currently it's all in Russian. However, I'll try to explain right here in a few words, feel free to ask for more info if you need it.

    A flashback always has a goal, a trigger and a plot. You can't just say "well, let's stop the game and I'll tell you a funny story about myself". A goal is usually "explain something" or "show something" (in a wide sense). A trigger is an action that initiates the flashback: a question, an attack or anything else that can give you the right to say "oh yes, this reminds me of...". A funny variant is when the Master introduces a new character, and a player says "hey, I know the guy, we went to school together!" or "I once fought him, it was long time ago" (the system allows you to do that) - and then the story of how exactly they met follows. A plot can involve several characters, so you begin with listing what do you need ("in order to tell this, I need a 'wise one-armed teacher' and a 'little sick brother'"). Any player at the table (or on the forum, if you play online) can say "I'll take that", and play that role inside the flashback. Everything about such a character besides the short description is up to the player who took it (e.g., the reason for losing an arm can be child's foolishness or a knightly exploit - if the flashback master didn't mention it, (s)he doesn't mind). Then the flashback is played with all involved players, and the rest just have to sit back and enjoy the show. The main lines of the flashback are determined by its initiator - if the 'little brother' has to die, he dies, no question asked. At the end of the flashback its goal is achieved. If it was to explain why the character had an ugly scar on his face, it's explained; if it was to make a decision, it's made and the reasons're explained; if it was to get back to your senses and find a reason to fight for, the character gets up and receives an appropriate combat bonus.

    Flashforwards have two significant differences: first, they can involve existing characters ("ok, tomorrow evening I'll drop in to see you"); second, they can be played simultaneously with the main storyline in the case of a forum-based game (the goal does not have to be reached here and now). I could show you a nice example, but yet again, guess what language it's in?

    I hope I answered the question (and hope I didn't type too much ;) ).
