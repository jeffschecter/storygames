\n I'm getting closer to finishing the first full version of Directions Storyplaying System that I've been developing for the last years. It's a GM-less story game system that encourages a certain style of "storyplaying". At the same time, it is highly modular and can be adapted for different styles of play.

Now, I'd like to introduce the basic game structure and see if you find the terminology & basic structure accessible:

A collection of handouts for Directions Storyplaying System is available here for further reference.

Essentially, Directions is mildly pushing you towards playing with a less structured game flow; while there is a structured framework as fall back and to get you started.
So, you basically oscillate between:
- Group Reflection out-of-character, possibly including host rotation or new plots.
- Structured Storyplaying using more formalized scene framing.
- Free Storyplaying with free game flow and rudimentary structures (not necessarily freeform play).

Of course, depending on your group's playing style, the game may have a completely different grativational center for you, i.e.
- Free Storyplaying for the group that likes free flowing roleplaying,
- Group Reflection for the those more inspired by "storyboarding",
- Structured Storyplaying for those who prefer roleplaying structured scenes.

So, my question is less about your preferred style of playing. It's more if you can basically understand the terminology & structure presented here and if these make sense to you.

    \n Thanks @Paul_T.

    On "Briefly Reflect Story":
    Directions is a GM-less game system that highly relies on player common consent (even when characters can be in dramatic PvP conflicts).
    So this step is usually used when you have a new (rotating) Host who get's to frame next scenes. While the current Host basically can frame scenes and pull the story in certain directions, it is important to involve all players and find a common ground.
    Reflection in the group intends to
    1. generate new ideas/inspiration for the shared story
    2. ensure everybody is on the same page where the story is headed

    While this is possible in conversation, there are additionally tools like the Compass and the Surprise Meter that help you with this (both I've previously presented on story-games).


    The Compass provides an opportunity for each player to give feedback, like i.e. they'd like to see more action scenes or intimate dialogues (or they have needs beyond the story, like needing a break).

    While players can agree on where the story should be heading, it is also good to stay open for unplanned surprises.
    The Surprise Meter indicates how much in game play things work out as planned or go in unexpected directions.

    \n I think this is one of the most interesting posts in the while, but I don't know really what I can say to contribute about anything. I think "Group reflection" is more important than people think and especially where people should reflect, together, what has happened to the story. Reinforcing the theme, is probably something I'm missing, and it doesn't have to be in the group but the game mechanics can do that or just the structure of how scenes are set up. Sometimes, people tend to forget about what kind of atmosphere - or even playstyle - they should endorse.

    I can't say much without ... not an examples, but troubles in shared narrative that you normally would find in a group that your structure of play can breathe through.

    Another thing that I think is really backwards in roleplaying games is that you create characters first and then play out a story. It doesn't make any sense, and when I asked (read: forced :P ) my girlfriend - who were playing amateur theater at the time - to create a character to my game, she didn't understand the purpose of skills and abilities. This because I hadn't set the theme or told anything about what the game was about. I was just so used to create a character and then start to play.

    You solve this in your first setup.
