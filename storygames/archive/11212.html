\n The most extreme example I\u2019ve seen, a player didn\u2019t want un-consensual sex in a game and then proceeded to have their character rape another character.
\n
\nBecause I say I want it, doesn\u2019t mean I do.
\n
\nExtreme behavior like this is uncommon but the underlining premise isn\u2019t. I\u2019m no longer amazed by the number of \u201cviolence is never the answer\u201d goals that lead to exactly that. I write down, \u201cmy goal is to build bridges\u201d and then proceed to burn them down. Once play starts, it doesn\u2019t matter what I said I wanted, anything goes.
\n
\n
\n
\nPlenty of Fish is one of the biggest dating websites in North America, four times larger than Match.com. It\u2019s ugly with almost non existent customer service. But it\u2019s effective. They create 80,000 successful relationships a year. How? They don\u2019t listen to their users.
\n
\nThat\u2019s not true. They do listen. They don\u2019t listen to what they say but what they do. If you tell Plenty of Fish you like blond nonsmokers but spend all your time clicking and gawking nicotine-addled brunettes, the system adopts.
\n
\n
\n
\nBurning Wheel does this. People complain that Beliefs are a way to control player characters. They\u2019re not. You\u2019re rewarded when you play your Beliefs AND when you play against them. And you can change them!
\n
\nYou\u2019re not rewarded for how your character behaves. You\u2019re rewarded for how you communicate. It\u2019s a feedback system.
\n
\nI\u2019m not holding up Burning Wheel as the end all be all. It\u2019s not. Nothing is. It might just be scratching the surface. If anything, these feedback systems aren\u2019t \u201creal time\u201d enough for me.
\n
\nThat\u2019s why nothing beats a traditional Game Master who\u2019s a phenomenal listener.
\n
\nThe best system can\u2019t beat the best GM in terms of real time content customization. Non-computer systems aren\u2019t intelligent or responsive enough. Hell, computer ones aren\u2019t. People rule here. But not everyone is a phenomenal listener. And that doesn\u2019t mean we can\u2019t make better tools. That\u2019s why I\u2019m writing this. I want better tools.
\n
\n
\n
\nI don\u2019t have the answer. But I suspect some of you do. Or one day will.
\n
\nRock,
\nJohn

    \n This makes me think about swapping out unused traits or skills for ones that are more immediately useful, and that whole segment of game design. That sort of thing has a lot of horse-and-cart about it though - there needs to be a way to define traits as meaningful, to the greatest extent possible, such that they'll actually get used, but then again it's not so bad to have some "turnover" and allow for a character to change over time. Polaris handles this in part by making individual Aspects distinct, in the scene economy, from Themes - you exhaust an entire Theme at once, but you come up with in-the-moment content based on specific Aspects.

    A lot of games are very bad at being responsive in this way - how many Dungeons and Dragons/World of Darkness games have we played where character creation feels like packing for a trip, but you don't know where you're flying or where you might be eating and recreating when you get there! I've tried to take the opposite approach: choose a skill, and I'll try to make it important in play, but there's a certain sluggishness to this. What if a player's priorities change over time, like you said?

    I think that having a relatively small number of character abilities is better than having too many, and would certainly push matchmaking of different traits to the forefront: in Firefly, there's an episode called 'Shindig' in which Inara's (secret) love for Mal helps him win a sword-fight. Mal knows absolutely nothing about fencing or disciplined use of hand-weapons, so he's going to be killed by his opponent, Atherton, unless something else happens. It does: (I think...) Inara tells Atherton she'll marry him if he lets Mal live, and Mal uses that as a diversion and beats the guy's face in. Some kind of... "Secretly, I Love Mal" trait would be used there, such that Mal could switch from Fancy Fightin' to just plain Fightin', a skill that he actually has. I think it's debatable that you'd have to even differentiate Fancy Fightin' permanently, seeing as there are a few occasions in which the crew's more rough-and-tumble physical and social abilities fall short in the face of Alliance technology, fancy fencing technique, or so-phisticated rich-folk etiquette.
    It's a bit like having Guest Star skills, maybe. I dunno. I'll give it some thought!

    \n Another way to handle problems with initial character creation is to make that creation In Media Res. Traditional roleplaying games don't act like stories. They try to act like some approximation of life. With In Media Res, a characters' abilities aren't so much determined ahead of time as they are "discovered" during play. Did Rodenberry determine when he created the Vulcans that they would have an inner eyelid that would protect Spock from being permanently blinded in one episode? I seriously doubt that these things would be worked into the characters until the writer of that episode needed them to have that skill or ability.

    \n Here's a mechanical idea.

    Your skill list or trait list or whatever has little key icons next to each blank line.

    By default, these skills and traits are unlocked. You can replace one unlocked item with any other trait or skill during play. This costs a skeleton key. You start play with one of these skeleton keys.

    You can lock an item at any time. Doing so gains you a reroll, or more dice, or something for that one roll. Color in the keyhole to lock it. Whenever you lock an item, you earn another skeleton key.

    You can unlock a locked item, but it's difficult. Maybe it costs two or three skeleton keys.

    \n Zac,

    In a sense Keys are a very stripped down version of Arcana, but they lose out so much of the core dynamics of Arcana because they don't constrain the advancement. The soft limitations of Arcana spending means that players tend to put their actions where their words are. If you wanted to be more magically and scholarly you had to act more magically and scholarly. We actually had a spy character who started seducing people in one scene and ended up embracing an unexpected dual arcana path for her character (lovers and empress) half because of the fiction and half because of the positive feedback. Artesia is a game where you can actually "fake it till you make it", which I must say is pretty impressive.

    Speaking from a year or so game of Artesia I ran, I would cut the magic and combat systems down brutally and balance the lifepaths (at least a little), all before taking much of a knife to the Arcana system. If you must though, I suggest scaling down the Arcanas by a factor of 2 - removing or reworking world to match. Arcana is basically an onus on the GM, and that means having a decent sense for what each one means. But you need enough variety to represent different avenues of stories, with overlap of both abilities and themes. That means that a subtle turn can make your seducer into a Lover or an Empress based on whether she is getting to close or is cruelly manipulating. Another such turn can make your warrior into Death as she slaughters the enemy or Strength as she overcomes a stalwart foe.

    Paul,

    I may have partially answered your question with John and Zac, but I'll try a little more. My perspective is that the best way to find what someone wants in play is to observe what they interact during play. System-wise, that means a game which ensure a player's actions during a game are recorded in such a way as to express the most important elements of the fiction to that player (or to the players as a whole) lets the player actually communicate what they want. It removes many of the social and introspective limits of declaring in advancing what you hope you will enjoy doing. In this case, you do what you enjoy, and if you don't enjoy it, you typically can change tact. In essence this is the power of empiricism over rhetoric.

    In Artesia, the Arcana that a character accumulates will tend to be the most important elements of the game for that character and that player (although there will be a level of group and GM preference related as well). In one of my playtests, Homeworld Project, the rise and fall of the major elements of the game work in much the same way - individual character actions drive those elements either up or down, and those then feed back into the adversity.

    \n Mendel,

    I agree with you about observation in play, but don't many games limit themselves due to their structure? For instance, in a D&D game where I'm most interested in political intrigue and/or romance, I may find the need to increase my fighting abilities (i.e. kill monsters) as much possible so as to make sure my character doesn't die before the romantic arc is resolved. If, say, character death is a constant threat, it's hard for me not to act towards that--but that's not necessarily representative of my goals or desires for the direction of the game. It takes careful game design to get around that problem, or at least to alleviate it.

    What I've seen as very effective is mechanics that allow the players to choose and label bits of the game, displaying their interests that way. In this sense, open-ended mechanical concerns like Dogs fallout work very well: by encouraging players to modify elements of [in this case, their characters] we have a very easy and clear way to communicate which way we want the game to go. "Actions speak louder than words," as it were--and there we agree totally.

    \nAbsolutely a concern, but I'd rather say it takes forethought not to design such an over-powering thematic and situation requirement into play, or care to design the impact of that requirement so that there is still room for flexibility in player/character goals and desires to arise dynamically. But in truth, this is as much a problem with Dogs in the Vineyards* as it is for D&D - some traits and belongings will simply be more generally useful in conflicts than others, and in Dogs you want to be able to win conflicts almost regardless of your particular goals and desires.

    I've been noticing that it may look like I'm knocking Dogs in the Vineyards here (and above). I'm really not trying to. It's just far from a game that I consider to encourage character identity to arise from character actions. Perhaps from the editorialization of those actions, but that isn't the same thing at all.

    To my mind, the idea that actions speak louder than words should mean that if you want to communicate something it isn't enough to attach a metagame tag to your actions, you need to act toward a state in the game to achieve a new character state. In an odd way typical advancement systems have a very narrow version of this - if you want to become a more competent Dog be bad at talking and good at killing. Likewise, if you want to gain levels faster in D&D learn to be good at earning XP, which for many, but not all, editions means learn to defeat the baddies efficiently and safely. Advancement systems tend to tell you how to clearly communicate competence through your actions - but not much else.

    More general character change however has a wealth of uses by making something limited the player packages decisions to let part of her character go during the course of play. The provides a wide amount of nuance - because at once there is a sacrifice, a gain, and the current situation, and any one of those could be avowed reason. And perhaps the avowed reason is the only desire, but if you keep sacrificing something you "want" because the situations you get into "demand" it, then perhaps you should re-evaluate what it is you want or why you get into those situations. Likewise, if you really "want" something, the system tells you flat out how to get it, but you don't just get to spend a open slot on it, you need to seek out the moment in the fiction that gives it to you. Of course, part of the trick (in play, game design, and social contract design) of this is making sure that there aren't gatekeepers blocking your way, in the same way as a GM who has cast you into the lonely wilderness in Artesia is effectively blocking all sorts of social-themed Arcana.

    - Mendel

    Posted By: David Berg* How do you classify what is played into categories that accurately describe "importance to player"?
    \nThere seem to be two strategies for this. Either have them emerge (with annotations by players) over the course of play or have them pre-defined. Of course, the truth is that no matter how "pre-defined" you want things to be there will be some notional drift in the categories you use. That's good. As a designer you should exploit that to help make your game robust, rather than assume that your pre-defined categories will be inviolate.

    Some quick examples:

    Artesia's Arcana is a great example of pre-defined categories, but they are notably a bit cumbersome.

    One of the sub-mechanics I use in Homeworld Project is Panache, your styles of being. You get two of these for your character out of a list of seven: Mystic, Noble, Pilot, Rake, Rogue, Tech, Warrior. You basically get a benefit doing something related to one (or both) of your Panache. You also can, once per chapter, use a Panache other than one of your two selected Panache, writing it down as a Panache vote, so called because once five votes are tallied, if there is a clear majority, that new Panache replaces one of your old ones. From one side this is a mechanic that offers you a way to change your Panache, and from the other it presents a risk to tapping into alternate Panache too regularly.

    In my game Drift, players always have five total Focii (Cultural Truths and Technological Insights) while there is a list of these suggested, these can easily be invented on the fly. What helps make them more real is the addition of Babbles, which are short phrases or jargon that come up during their use, (yes like Technobabble). The Focii come and go frequently, limited by the local planet and the culture and technology of the character's ship. Over time, the characters have to choose between stagnation or giving up what remaining connection they have with their homes. \n
    Posted By: David Berg* Who does the classifying? If it's the game system (player = unreliable; GM = too much work), what's its formula for "X action = Y preference"?
    \nIn Artesia's Arcana, this is typically done by the GM, which takes some ramp up time, but is by no means as difficult as it first appears.

    In the case of Panache votes, usually the other player involved in the bout will need to agree that the Panache vote is relevant to the situation. In practice that isn't terribly onerous.

    In the case of Drift, I haven't explained how the actions turn into changes in the Focii. The method is essentially by characters expending additional effort during the mission. Every extra die of effort is a point of Drive that is recorded on that Truth or Insight. It makes that Focii more persistent and important, and increases the likelihood that one of the other Focii will be eliminated in its place. The clock for when a Focii is replaced comes from the actions without Truths or Insights, and from trying to heroically reduce the consequences of failing a mission conflict. Every part of that decision comes from the player, but the accounting is direct. \n
    Posted By: David Berg* How is such data recorded, when every instant of play is a data point?
    \nThis is essentially a false premise. For your design's purposes, not every instant of play can or need be recorded. So you minimize the error, and the effort as best you can and as well suited as you can make to match the needs of your design. And you make that record serve multiple purposes.

    I suspect you are confusing evidence and hypothesis in this context. If I design my game correctly, the very fact that some players use "this skill more than that skill" is strong evidence that they prefer "playing scenes with deep interpersonal stakes" over "liking solving tough puzzles". But what a game record is going to extract is evidence, not perfect validation of a hypothesis. And don't forget that there is a massive domain of possible hypotheses - no amount of game recording, after the fact play re-writing (aka actual play reporting), or player interrogation is going to give you a definite answer. But it can narrow the scope, which can be enough for some purposes.

    The key distinction I make is between what is relevant to the dynamics of the game being designed, and what isn't. Certainly I can study player dynamics and try to reveal interests from a game agnostic angle (I've built an entire theory of RPGs to do just that). But if I'm building this into the game, I want to use my feedback as much as possible, rather than waste the effort of recording this particular trace of play. This really comes back to Rule Number One for me: "have a reason for everything in your design, including the things that aren't."
