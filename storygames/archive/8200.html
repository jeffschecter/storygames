\n So now I know why I've been avoiding forays into "real" game design - I suck at math.

Okay. So I have a problem that I'm not sure how to solve. Say you have a "success" system like Poison'd - roll a d6 with 50% odds of getting a success, accumulate more success than the opposition to "beat" them. How do I calculate the probabilities of beating people with varying numbers of dice? Ie, what are the odds of beating somebody with a die pool of 6 if you only have 4 dice?

I asked my mathie husband, but unfortunately he's not so good at statistics. I poked around for die-rolling programs that I could use to figure this out and struck out.

    \n How detailed do you want your answer?

    Basically, you are looking at two independent binomial distributions. The independent thing is good for you.
    You want to look at each possible case (1 success, 2 successes, etc.) and compute the probability of the other side rolling fewer successes. Then you add up those probabilities weighted by the probability of each case.

    Based on your example, I am rolling 4 dice, you are rolling 6 dice and each die is 50% likely to come up a "success".

    Case 0: 0 Successes. No chance of me rolling 'more' successes than you.

    Case 1: I roll 1 Success (on four dice). Probability of you rolling 0 successes: 0.015625. Prob of this case: .25
    Case 2: 2 Successes. Probability of you rolling 0 or 1 successes: 0.109375. Prob of this case: .375
    Case 3: 3 Successes. Probability of you rolling 0, 1, or 2 successes: 0.34375. Prob of this case: .25
    Case 4: 4 Successes. Probability of you rolling 3 or fewere successes: 0.65625. Prob of this case: .0625

    If I've run the numbers right, this says I (with 4 dice) have about a 17.2% chance of "beating" you (with 6 dice). (0.015625*.25) + (0.109375*.375) + (.34375*.25) + (0.65625*.0625).

    As a note: Excel has the BINOMDIST worksheet function that can compute all the numbers for you.

    Alternatively,

    \n

    You're using numbers as a vehicle to arrive at the kind of play you want, right? Looking at cold statistics isn't going to tell you what works and doesn't work in play. So, don't. Grab some numbers and prod at them until, when you play the game, they feel right. That way, you don't need to be good at math to design the game you want.
