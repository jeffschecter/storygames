    vgunn said:

    For instance, in the game anybody can hide. Hiding is an all or nothing thing. Either you're hidden or you're in view.

    Someone with a thief background, however, has a chance to hide in shadows. A thief generally prefers to hide rather than to hide in shadows. It's nice to have a chance to hide in shadows when you need it, though.

    \nI think that might be a bad idea, for the reason underlined.

    One of the more common buzzterms in stealth gameplay design is partial failure margin. Or, in the words of a particularly spoony internet personality:\n
    If you get caught, you're fucked. I repeat: if you're the rogue, and you get caught, you are fucked. You're fucked. You're. Fucked. You ever play Hitman and you get caught? Right? You ever see those videos of that guy playing Hitman and he gets caught and he sucks? You're fucked.
    \nI'm butchering a complicated design theory, but the short version: tension comes from almost failing. The closer you are to the edge, the more interesting the game becomes. Think of the time when you're on your last hit point, but you're already deep into a dungeon and there's no turning back. That's the partial failure margin.

    You know your game better than me, and I might be overstepping my bounds by bringing this up, so I'm just gonna leave a couple tricks to extend the margin:

    1. Reaction time. That is; if you get spotted, you have a chance to react. A guard sees the player, the player shoots the guard in the head before the alarm's raised. We don't want to lock this behind classes, so maybe every player is guaranteed a chance to react, but the thief gains a bonus.

    2. States of alert. Or rather, when you get spotted, no-quarter combat doesn't immediately start. Keeping with the Hitman theme: in Hitman (2016), there's a boatload of non-combat states clearly given on screen. You can be Suspicious, you can be Found Trespassing, you can be Hunted; tons of situations that aren't 100% hidden, but not 100% revealed. Your disguise might be compromised, the guards might know someone's in the building, but they don't know where. You've been set back, but it you can still salvage the situation if you put in the effort.

    For the tabletop, you could judge a "near miss" as tipping off someone to investigate. Or your improvise skill can be used to partially mitigate an alarm; ask the guard where the bathroom is, offer the monster some food, that kind of stuff. Maybe the guard thinks you're just lost. Maybe the monster just wants a friend. Who knows!

    3. Extra chances. Normally, this would come from spare lives, but you could achieve something similar with a luck system. You have a set amount of luck points, depending on your skills. Each time you're seen, you can use a luck point to reroll. When your luck runs out, you risk getting caught. Very game-y, but it's definitely dramatic.

    Just stuff off the top of my head. All these are significant bonuses to the player, so keep that in mind when determining difficulty. Again, you know your system better than me. If you're going rules-lite, then this added complexity might slow the game down. Or the game-ness might somehow compromise your narrative.

    Just something to keep in mind. I just spent way too much time on this, so I'll come back later before tackling those skill descriptions. Looking forward to your game!

    \n Here's the plan. I'm gonna try and restate the skill descriptions, and we'll see how close I get. If I get close, good. If I miss the mark, then something's being lost in translation.

    Plan: Prep work. Covers intelligence gathering, logistics, contract negotiation, bartering, and the like. The better your Plan, the better your situation when you depart.

    Ruck: Carrying gear and loot. Covers running, swimming, climbing, hauling, and the like. The better your Ruck, the more you can reasonably carry.

    Explore: Field cartography skills. Covers navigating, distances and slope estimation, hidden traps and passage discovery, valuable plants and minerals identification, and the like. The better your Explore, the better your odds of recognizing dangers and natural treasures.

    Camp: Survival skills. Covers shelter building, foraging, kindling, first aid, mindset, and the like. The better your Camp, the better your ability to cope with nature.

    Improvise: Quick wit. Covers reflexes, processing speed, snap decision making, jury rigging, field crafting, and the like. The better your Improvise, the better you can cope when everything goes wrong.

    Snipe: Sneaking and spy skills. Covers hiding, moving quietly, physical dexterity, tailing marks, eavesdropping, and the like. The better your Snipe, the better your odds at remaining undetected.

    Exert: Feats of raw strength. Covers heavy lifting, bashing through doors and barricades, throwing, breaking things, and the like. The better your Exert, the more force you can put on objects.

    In writing these, I tried to keep the list as formulaic as possible. Single sentence summary, then a list of examples in progressive tense, then the skill's overall benefit. The idea is the similar structure makes it easier to parse; the brain doesn't need to decipher grammar, so it can focus on the content. However, formulaic writing could bore the reader, which would cause them to start skimming. There's probably a middle ground somewhere.

    The only thing that confuses me is Snipe. From the name, I assumed it means "sniper skills", but that more accurately describes Explore. Snipe seems more like "guerilla skills"; ambushes, sabotage, that kind of thing. Explore includes gauging distances and finding hidden objects, which would help with ranging weapons and spotting targets, respectively.

    This skill set genuinely interests me. I like how everything's focused on wilderness expeditions, and how each seems important. They're pretty well distinguished, too; if I pick two skills at random, I can see a personality framework. The Ruck and Exert specialist is the big guy that relies solely on strength, the Plan and Explore specialist is the Lewis & Clark style explorer, the Camp and Improvise specialist is the highly adaptable survivalist, the Snipe and Improvise specialist is the highly trained scout, and so on. It triggers the part in me that likes creating characters. Keep it up!

    \nWould like to second this. While I do think the descriptions could use a bit of tidying up for crystal clarity, I think the names need a rethink. While I was able to get past most of them, they were a pretty large stumbling block while parsing the descriptions. For instance, without reading the description, does charting a route fall under Plan or Explore? If you need a chart a new route halfway through an expedition, would that be Improvise? I'm still not sure what Snipe is; the name and descriptions seem to indicate two different things.

    Assuming the descriptions are accurate, here's my quick recommendations:

    Plan -> Prep
    Ruck -> Fitness/Carry     (depending on how its used in the rules. If it's primarily used to increase encumbrance limit, reduce encumbrance penalties, etc., then Carry. Otherwise, Endurance)
    Explore -> Survey
    Camp -> Survive
    Improvise -> Improvise
    Snipe -> Spy/Scout     (depending on expected usage/game theming)
    Exert -> Strength

    These are all off the top of my head; they could probably be polished by looking at a thesaurus. Instead of switching between verbs and adjectives like I did, you could make them all verbs of consistent tense, or make them all adjectives. Don't want to write your game for you.

    Also, I realize I'm being super-duper grammah nazi, but it really matters. If there is any ambiguity to your mechanics, especially the core mechanics, then someone will miss the bell. At best, different groups will play with ever-so-slightly different rules, which could potentially lead to confusion when players change groups. At worst, players will deliberately exploit your wording to give their characters power. Because people are mean No one wants to be friends with an alien locust
