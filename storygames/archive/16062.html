    \n Hi Klaus!

    I'm not sure if I understand your question, but I think what you're asking is this:

    "Does a game with only a few mechanical procedures have the same ability to support long-term play as one that has a lot of mechanical procedures?"

    The simple answer is yes. All other things being equal, there's no reason why you couldn't get satisfying play from a very simple game for the same length of time you'd get satisfying play from a very mechanically complex game.

    But!

    Here's the twist, and I think maybe the source of your concern. Satisfying play usually requires some kind of sense of "progress". That progress can take many different forms. In games like D&D that progress is about going up levels, getting access to new mechanics and new fictional content. In a game like Sorcerer, the sense of progress is provided by getting closer to answering the question "how far will you go to get what you want".

    Often, one of the things that the mechanics of a game provide is a way of formally tracking that "progress". That's a useful thing. Depending on what "progress" means in your game, it might be useful to have a mechanical way of marking that progress, or it might not.

    \n Prime Time Adventures has this issue, sort of, because it has such a light set of rules and no real mechanism (or need) for character progression, but it does have a specific plot structure and individual character arcs to give some definition to the game. There's nothing wrong with an iterative game that involves characters and situations that change without improving -- In A Wicked Age and, potentially, Spirit of the Century work this way.

    If you have a strong premise that can be re-skinned to fit different situations, that's golden IMO.

    \n Why do you want to add rules to your game?

    I think the ideal game has the least number of rules possible. Easy to learn, easy to understand, easy to play. (And for a designer, easy to playtest and/or modify.)

    Adding rules should always be considered skeptically: "Will this *really* make the game more fun, and enough so to offset the extra hassle of adding another rule?"

    Is your game easy and fun to play? Then you're done.

    Is your game suffering from some problems with long-term play? If yes, do you want to be able to use this game for long-term play?

    If yes again, then consider changing something about the game. That's not necessarily adding a rule: it might be changing a rule or maybe even taking away a rule.

    \n I think slow progress in story and characters is the key to long-term play. If the progress is fast, the changes gradualy lose their meaning. What does it matter where we are now, when that's going to change in a few moments? Also, the game becomes harder to follow. Fast progress can work for short games, where people are eager to see how it ends and thus pay attention. But few people can be attentive all the time, week after week. Fast progress is exhausting in the long run.

    If there is no progress, the game's just the same, week after week. That becomes boring.

    Posted By: Hans c-oThat's just the setting, though! What about thegame?
    \nThe game itself is based on a pretty simple engine. You only roll when you are uncertain of the outcome or under duress. There are more quirks than this, but step by step here's what you do...

    1. Outline what your goal is
    2. think of all the bad things that might happen
    3. roll a handful of d6s
    4. take the highest roll and compare it to the degree of success.

    Highest Roll Chart (Degree of success)
    1-3 = No such luck...goal fails, bad things happen.
    4-5 = Closer...a step closer to the goal (you can try again and take +1d6 to your new attempt; if you try again, the bad things are the same as before)
    6 = Victory!...goal succeeds, bad things disappear.

    It's clearly influenced by a lot of stuff I've seen around here, especially stuff I've talked about in other threads. That said, we are having a really good time whenever we test it. It seems to just "make sense" to everyone I game with.

    \n Klaus,
    \nSo, the trick with long term Story Games is that the character has to be able to change, without necessarily gaining mechanical power.
    \nDogs in the Vineyard does this well.
    \nShadow of Yesterday can do this once people understand the system.
    \nInSpectres did not work for me, at some point the players get so many franchise dice that you can't do anything to shut them down.
    \nAny game that lets you change a trait, is a great way to have character development without having to contend with Power Escalation.
