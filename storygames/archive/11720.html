    \n My mom thinks it's good that I'm making money as a writer, and appreciates my stick-to-it-iveness after I disappointed her horribly in college by telling her I wanted to stop doing politics and be a writer.

    There are actually projects I haven't strongly pursued because of my mother. If I ever finish Addict, the recovery game where phases of play are based on the twelve step program, it will be dedicated to her. I, uh, don't know how she'll feel about it.

    She's also very tolerant of my playing habits. She is only familiar with the original D&D, which she hated because she rolled for race and got "an ugly orc, and I wanted to be something sexy like an elf." I thought and considered telling her that indie games take more from improv, but she was a professional actress for many years doing experimental theatre and Serious Russian Plays, and considers improv the lowest form. She's probably better off thinking roleplaying is where you have to pretend to play ugly people.

    \n Great topic, Graham.

    My mom is responsible for the skills I have that I'm most proud of. She taught me that if I read, I would never be lonely or bored. From there, my mind expanded thanks to all the reading I was doing. I wound love ancient mythology, especially the Greek, and in 1980 or so, I asked my mom for the D&D books. She got me Red Box, followed by Blue, Magenta, and whatever else there were in that series.

    My mom never gave me shit about gaming, unlike my dad, who'd come into the room DRESSED IN HIS FUCKING CIVIL WAR REENACTING UNIFORM and make fun of us. Fucker.

    Anyway, I eventually gave her a copy of my game. I don't think she read it but she likes the way it looks and I know she's proud.

    She told me if I become a millionaire from this, I have to buy her a house. When I was done laughing, I said I would.

    \n I've tried to explain roleplaying to my mother, but it's like trying to explain calculus to a rock. She just doesn't get it. I think I confused matters by getting into LARPing before tabletop, and the closest she's been able to get to understanding that hobby is "musical theater". Strangely, despite that my mother is Catholic and comes from a family of conservative whack-jobs who don't believe in evolution or global warming, I never got the 'RPGs/fantasy is a work of Satan' thing from either of my parents. So at least if she totally doesn't understand it, she doesn't condemn it.

    My mother was proud when I published Thou Art But A Warrior and when I had modest success with sales, but I think that was more because she looked at it as something concrete that I was doing with my Fine Arts degree that she paid for. She was impressed with the physical artifact of the book, especially since I did it all myself. She was not impressed with the political statement I was making with the game once I explained that aspect of it to her, but she had the grace not to say so.

    \n When I was much younger my mother banned D&D from the house, and thought my interest in roleplaying games was unhealthy.     \n My mom supported my entry into role-playing games thirty or so years ago. All she knew of them at first was that I was a "dungeon master" and that my four players were all "cute" girls. Though they were divorced from one another and pretty poor, my parents agreed communally to buy me the 1st edition Monster Manual I talked about incessantly. Over time, my mother understood that my imaginative games were considered "nerdy" by the mainstream. And then she supported it all the more.

    These days I think she understands my continued participation in the hobby through the lens of my participation in improvisational theater. Sometimes when I'm teaching a new boardgame she advises everyone to be quiet and pay attention because I'm in my "dungeon master" role. She has occasionally hinted at wishing I was less "escapist" because she imagines I'd otherwise have have provided her with a grandchild, even though childlessness was primarily an environmental decision unconnected from my main hobby.

      \n Great to read so many stories of supportive parents.

      My parents staged an intervention when I was 14. I had been playing D&D with faculty brats from the local liberal arts college for a couple years. Mom finally heard the "Satan" stories, and talked Dad into putting a stop to my favorite hobby. We spent about an hour talking it over.       They relented, but I had to invite my friends over to our house for our next session. Dad watched us play. He saw the miniatures and the dice and the decision-making, and was like, oh, it's a strategy game. And he's been cool with it since.

Now, when I say I'd like to publish my own games, my parents smile politely, but they both have this nervous, "yeah, right" look in their eyes.

      So I guess that puts me at the "proving them all wrong" stage.
