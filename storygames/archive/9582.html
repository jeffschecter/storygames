    \n From a strictly legal point of view:

    1 - I'm not sure what the "we" you're using is, but if you give a copy to someone else, that's no longer your "personal use", since someone else is using it.

    2 - Everything is copyrighted by default. Under the Berne Conventions on Copyright (which the US and most of Europe are signatories to, along with a lot of other countries), anything someone puts in a "fixed form" (i.e., records in some manner, rather than performing it "live" or just imagining it) is automatically copyrighted, and no copyright notice is required. Only things that are explicitly placed in the public domain, or whose term of copyright has expired, are not copyrighted.

    3 - Whether or not you're selling something makes no difference for whether it's copyright infringement. The most it may affect is what sort of damages they'd be able to collect from you.


    Now, from the practical point of view:

    If your circulation is going to be that small, chances are that none of the people whose work you took is ever going to hear about it. They're not going to sue you or prosecute you for what they don't know about. If you want to minimize the chance of problems, I would suggest giving your contributors and players paper copies, instead of the PDF -- that makes it considerably less likely that one of them will decide to post it up on the 'net, where it could be easily copied thousands of times and become a much more major deal.

    \nDisclaimer: I'm not a lawyer, so don't take my words as legal advice.

    Practically speaking, if all you're doing is downloading and printing the file, you're probably fine. Just don't host or otherwise share the PDF, and if you're paranoid, delete the file when you're done.

    The standard strategy these days is to go after hosts and uploaders, not downloaders. But don't think that makes downloading legal, things may change in the future and companies may (once again) decide that pursuing downloaders is in their interest.

    But that all said, given the context in which the PDF was created, there's probably some sort of implied permission for downloading and sharing. You're probably safe as far as any original content is concerned, but the question is whether any of the art/fonts/etc was used without permission.

    \n Scott, another way of looking at this: this website grew, to an extent, out of The Forge, which prizes creator-owned games.

    With that in mind, tracking down copyright-owners and asking for their permission is considered a cool thing to do. Using stuff without permission, less cool. If you genuinely can't track down the owners, perhaps put a note in the PDF saying that, if they contact you, you'll gladly credit them.

    Nobody expects you to do the impossible, but it'd be nice if you attempted to do right by the people whose work you used. Doing right usually means getting permission but, if that's difficult, find another way to show your willlingness to credit people.
