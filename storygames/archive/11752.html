\n Stop me if this idea has already been done...

But imagine that the Starter Set for chess comes with 8 Pawns, a Feeble King and a Queen.
That force costs 20 points to field: 1 point per Pawn, 3 points per Feeble King, 9 points per Queen.

You can buy sealed packs with additional playing pieces: the knight, the bishop, the honor guard, the jester, the paladin, the rook, stronger varieties of King...
Booster Packs come with 3 additional playing pieces.
Big Packs come with 8 additional playing pieces and 2 additional field blocks (which are overlays and add-ons to the chess board).

So, like D&D Miniatures or Dreamblade, etc, only Chess. But not really.

CREATE PIECES! Tell me what they look like, how the fit thematically, their names, their moves, and their cost-to-field.
Awesome.

    \n Here, let me help you out with this with some chess variant pieces that actually exist:

    The Anti-King. The Anti-King is in check when NOT attacked. It cannot capture opponents pieces, but can capture friendly pieces. If it is in check (not attacked) and can't move out of it (to a place where it is attacked), then you are checkmated. (I believe our very own Peter Aronson invented this, unless it's a different Peter Aronson.)

    Pincers: Pincers move like rooks (horizontally, and vertically, never diagonally), and capture pieces when they are adjacent to a piece that is flanked on the other side by a friendly piece.

    Retreater: Retreaters move like Queens, but capture by moving directly away from an adjacent piece.

    Leaper: Leapers capture by jumping over a piece in a straight line. They must be able to land on the next space.

    Long-Leaper: Leapers can capture multiple enemy pieces if they can jump over them all in a straight line, jump over no friendly pieces, and land on a space next to the last piece he jumped.

    Immobilizer: Moves like a Queen, cannot capture (or check a King), but immobilizes all surrounding enemy pieces.

    Imitator: Moves like a Rook except when capturing - in that situation, they must move like the piece they captured would have moved to capture.

    Berolina Pawn: Normally moves one square diagonally forward in either direction. Captures by moving straight forward. (The opposite of a normal pawn). May move two squares diagonally forward on its first move.

    Reflecting Bishop: Moves and captures as a bishop, but "bounces" off the edges of the board.

    The following can all be found in detail at the Wikipedia article on Betza Chess.

    Fers: Moves and captures like a bishop, but is limited to adjacent diagonal squares.
    Alfil: Moves and captures two squares in any diagonal direction, jumping the adjacent square.
    Wazir: Moves and captures like a rook, but is limited to adjacent horizontal and vertical squares.
    Dabbabba: Moves and captures two squares in any diagonal direction, jumping the adjacent square.
    Cardinal: Moves and captures as a bishop or knight.
    Bede: Moves and captures as a bishop or Dabbabba
    Charging Rook: Moves as a rook forward or sideways, like a king backwards.
    Charging Knight: Moves as a knight forward, like a king backwards.
    Chancellor: Moves as rook or knight

    Edit: PS Betza chess is really what you're looking for. Back when I was into chess bigtime, I would go to the bigger tournaments mainly so, when I was obliterated in the main tournament, I could get into variant games and mini-brackets.

    \n At one point, Bandai produced a collectible piece Chess variant called Navia Dratp, but it wasn't particularly successful commercially.

    You can find huge number of piece types at www.chessvariants.com. Many (but by no means all) are collected in the Piececlopedia.

    Assigning point values to pieces can be problematic. If you restrict capture to replacement capture (normal chess capture except for en passant), and avoid some of the more exotic piece characteristics (changes type when it moves, needs to be captured more than once, teleportation, etc.), you can use Ralph Betza's work here as a starting point for assigning piece values. Of course, the power of an array is not just the sum of the values of the pieces, but also depends on how well the pieces work together. (Navia Dratp didn't have a problem with this issue, since collectible games just assume some decks/sets/whatever will be better than others.)

    (JD, I didn't invent the Anti-King, just Anti-King Chess which makes use of it.)

    \n Why is there a cost to field the king? Surely if you don't field a king, you can't lose, so you must be forced to play a king, in which case why give it a cost? Just reduce the starting funds by 3.
    (Also, if the king costs points, why not field 2, or 3? Would the opponent have to mate just one or all 3 to win?)

    Here's my take on a slightly different way to handle the king (achieves the same effect you were after, I think):
    The basic king is a mandatory piece; you get one in the basic set, and it must be fielded. It's move can be one space in any direction, standard rules for check and mate apply.
    There are no other king pieces, but boosters (and maybe the starter) contain crowns, which cost points and can be added to the king piece (no more than one crown).

    Crown Example:
    The Rookslide Crown - 1 point
    The Kings of Rookslide are of a long royal line, lengthened by their willingness to put their subjects in danger to save themselves.
    At the start of the game indicate a piece on the back row. As long as the King and that piece have not moved since the start of the game, the King may swap places with that piece instead of a standard movement. This counts as both pieces having moved.

    \nThe Smugglers:

    Turn a pair of rooks on their heads. They are now smugglers, able to shepherd your pieces from one point on the board to another through a tunnel beneath the board. Smugglers may not move. Smugglers cannot be turned back into rooks.

    If your piece lands on a smuggler, it may immediately move to the space occupied by its partner. On the next turn, that smuggled piece may move as normal from its new location.

    If an opponent lands on a smuggler, it may use the tunnel as normal or it may simply capture that smuggler.

    \n The Beholder

    Moves slowly, like a King--one square in any direction.

    However, instead of moving the Beholder, the player may use it to "compel/geas/mind control" any enemy piece that is two squares away from the Beholder. Enemy pieces *next* to the Beholder are safe; only enemy pieces two squares away can be controlled.

    When controlling any enemy piece with the Beholder, it behaves in every way as though it was an identical piece of the opposite color, for that turn only. It may move, capture, or use any other options normally available to that type of piece.

    \n Similar idea:

    The Hypnotist

    The hypnotist moves like a king. When it lands on an enemy piece, you do not capture it. Instead, the hypnotist and that piece will share the same space.

    On your next turn, if they still share the same space, you may move the enemy piece in any legal manner as if it were yours. Your control of that piece ends after that turn.

    \n Fire. It occupies 4 spaces in an L shape, like a Knights move. It moves by "spinning" on one or the other end (i.e. turning the piece 90 degrees, as long as one of the ends remains in the same space). Any piece it moves over is captured. There is only one Fire piece, and either player can choose to move it on their turn in place of making a standard move. If Fire moves so that it would go off the board it is taken out of play. Fire cannot be captured, except by moving a Rook to the space on the opposite long side of Fire than where the Rook started (i.e. the Rook needs to pass over the three spaces of the long side, not the two of the short side). Knights can jump over Fire, but if they would end their move on Fire they are taken out of play.

    \n The Worm

    The worm begins as a single token that moves like a king. When it captures another piece, that piece is removed from the board. A new token is added to an adjacent unoccupied square. The pair of tokens continue moving in unison, like a worm slithering around the board. Only the "head" of the worm may capture pieces. Any new captures add tokens to the worm's "tail." Any token may be captured at any time by regular pieces. If this results in the worm being bisected, then any tokens not connected to the head are removed from the board. Only capturing the worm's head will completely remove it from the game.

    \n The Juggernaut

    This piece is a single six-sided die, turned to the number 1. This piece moves like a queen, but only a number of spaces equal to the number showing. It may not move less than than number of spaces. If the number "5" is showing, then the Juggernaut must move five spaces. After each movement, the die is turned to be one number higher. 1 becomes 2 becomes 3, and so on to a maximum of 6.

    The juggernaut does not capture. Instead, it pushes any pieces in its way in the same direction in which it moves. If a pushed piece hits another piece, then both of them move together until the juggernaut has ceased its movement. There is no limit to the number of pieces it can move at once.

    If any pieces are removed from the board in this manner, they are considered captured by the opposing player. In other words, if you push your own pieces off the board, they go to your opponent. If you push your opponent's pieces, they go to you.
