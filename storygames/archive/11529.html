\n John and I were just chatting about the "I wonder" statements that are supposed to guide some portions of GM planning in Apocalypse World. The GM is supposed to use these statements -- like "I wonder what the deal is with the Train People" or maybe slightly more specific -- to place holes or questions in the setting for the players to help fill, so that it's not entirely the GM's personal project but a collaborative effort.

I mentioned that this reminded me a bit of the dice questions in 1001 Nights, where you place a die and raise a very concrete wonder, such as: "I wonder if the king will find his lost love." These are, I think, supposed to indicate your interests and mark out possible future directions the narrative could proceed in.

Then, I was thinking about how these kinds of small voids might be used in GM-less/GM-ful games to mark out territory that the group wouldn't explore in pre-game planning but would reserve to be addressed in play, including larger questions such as "What dark purpose are these monsters intended for?" and more specific ones such as "I wonder who will try to steal the secret files." You could also have character-specific questions, like: "I wonder who she really loves" that raise issues without answering them.

Honestly, I feel like these smaller voids serve somewhat similar purposes to the much-discussed "Fruitful Void" that provides a central question for the game to revolve around -- "What are the consequences of violence?" "I wonder what friendship is worth" -- but instead make the smaller details of a game interesting by making them complex and open ended. Even if you have a scene where a character declares her love for someone, the "I wonder who she really loves" question may be smoldering in the background, ready to rear its ugly head in the future.

I think Vincent is smart to phrase these as "I wonder" statements, not questions, because that suggests there are multiple potential ways to answer the question and that you can keep trying to answer it even after someone has put forth an answer. What you want to do ideally, I think, is demarcate areas of interest that you want to explore, not really puzzles that you want the answer to. Answering the puzzle definitively may not be important at all, but addressing the issue definitely is.

    \n Gregor, yeah, that connects them nicely to the Hopes you set forth during campaign planning in Bliss Stage. Like:

    1. I hope we discover what the Bliss is.
    2. I hope we create something that will last.
    3. I hope we find where the adults have gone.

    In Bliss Stage, you definitively address (or forever leave unresolved) one of these hopes when a character "Blisses Out." So that's a model where questions/wonders serve an iterative, countdown purpose as well as framing the overall situation.

    In some ways, the Keys in The Shadow of Yesterday are character-based wonders ("I wonder where my character's Greed will lead them") that also serve an iterative, countdown purpose across the entire campaign, since you can only take each Key once and the Key of the Bodhisattva eventually removes characters from the game.

    Interesting. I wonder what other similar implementations exist that I haven't connected together before.

    Posted By: jaywalt
    Interesting. I wonder what other similar implementations exist that I haven't connected together before.
    \nIn Ribbon Drive, every character has two Futures.

    They're the only two potential-future-events that your character is allowed to bring up. So if you don't have a Future like "I hope we sleep in a hotel tonight", then you can't start that conversation. If no one has that Future, then you sort of just end up sleeping in a hotel, or not, that evening.

    Futures can come true. More likely, you'll think about them and explore them and dialogue about them... and ultimately you might come to a point where they no longer matter to you. If that happens to both of your Futures, then you become the protagonist.

    Futures are the driving mechanic of Ribbon Drive.
    They're things that you start out being certain about, but over time you challenge and... wonder about.

    It feels like we're in similar territory here. Yes?

    \n A different idea: Jason Godesky's making a post-apocalyptic game called The Fifth World. In the version I playtested a few months ago, each player started the game by identifying a local landmark, and then every other player would suggest how that landmark had changed after/during the apocalypse. So you'd describe one place, then get three to five ideas on how that place had changed, and pick the one you liked the best to be the true one.

    What if you had a game where that was the main method of exploring the setting? Each player poses a mystery at character creation ("I wonder what the big explosion seen over the mountains last year was" or "why does no one recognize me any more".) Then each scene of the game, the player who starts the scene is trying to provide an answer to one of the various mysteries the other players posed. They frame scenes where character uncover clues that point towards one explanation. Or they introduce mysterious NPC types who claim to know the truth, or whatever. Once everyone has given an explanation for a given mystery, the player who originated that mystery can frame a scene that finally solves the initial mystery. Then they can pose new ones, perhaps mysteries relating to the conflicting clues found when previously investigating.

    \n Joe, Steve, Bret: Yes, definitely.

    Nick, you're on the same wavelength with me here. My thinking about this is partially influenced by earlier conversations about key chains (2006) and GM keys (2009). Basically, I'm trying to connect together different memes involving structuring play through setting up open ended questions or points of exploration. You could possibly even argue that thinking about game design in this community has gradually shifted from strictly structuralist (fixed traits, countdown timers, strict scene framing) to a somewhat more interpretivist approach (regularly changing traits, ending things when they feel resolved, looser framing). It may mostly be a shift of degree at this point, though.

    On the whole, I find these kinds of soft-but-substantial guidelines to be really powerful as a tool. They provide a sense of purpose and direction without feeling restrictive, which feels really right to me in play.

    \n Here's a thing about "I wonder" in AW.

    It isn't just a technique that helps steer a narrative or something, or even a question to be answered. It's not about going out after the answer (or multiple answers) and driving towards a resolution.

    It's about honest-to-god, hands (fucking) off, wondering. As in, "Over here, in this part of the game, I will remain open and curious, not all planning-out-a-cool-thing because I am the cool GM." It's about recognizing a space for your authority not to extend into. A place where you're not the one who is responsible.

    So... nothing like Keys, or Bliss Stage hopes, or character-arc questions. It's a reminder not to be a tight-fisted dick who overplans every little thing when you GM. A reminder to let the chips fall where they may.

    See how "I wonder what they'll do when they run out of food?" is the opposite of "Oh man... when they run out of food, they're gonna have to scavenge in the Rag Waste and man do I have some shit out there for them." Both of those techniques ("I wonder" and also "I figure") are good for the game. The "I figure" part is playing your fronts and their countdowns, generally. So wondering (and later, stakes for your fronts) balances out the other part.
