\n I created the first version of this game for an Italian game design contest. The game was originally thought to be focused on themes such as racism and the like, but in the end just went for simple love stories, which wasn't something I really wanted for my game.
\nThat's why I re-wrote it, this time using another game as the backbone for this. Although it retains the general setting (the social outcomes of the coming of a new species on Earth), this time I feel the system draws more towards the choices I wanted players to take in this game: choosing between losing what you are to be appreciated by others and start loving yourself at all costs.
\nI'd like to hear from you on my idea. You can find the game here: http://www.box.net/shared/mqic753tm8yoo1piltuj

    \n Mike, here goes:

    Last things first, the endgame determination is entirely up to the GM (Venus), obfuscated by tallying up her decisions for that character. The only conceivable influence is the player describing the set dressing at the very beginning of the scene.

    Your conflict resolution mechanic makes it impossible to "lose" once you pick a side on each of the three pairings (d6+non-negative will always be positive). Since the modifier for a side is improved by winning with it, the mechanics just disincline a player from attempting the other side. Note, it would be nice if the outcome was more in doubt and if the outcome had something to do with the endgame. As it is, you can literally spend 10 scenes focusing on how you learn to love yourself and then have the GM throw it all away in the endgame.

    Ultimately, I think this game needs to be GM-less, the Venus role seems to be absorbing the interesting decisions of the player (what is conflicted in a scene, how your character grows until the endgame) but otherwise she has little interesting to do (this GM role does not, for example, provide adversity in any confrontable way).

    Lastly the themes of racism and being a new species, don't seem to get mapped to the conflicts of this game. I figure you have a solid idea how that works in practice, but it needs to be communicated to prospective players.

    I hope that helps,

    -Mendel

    \n Thanks! Yes, it helped a lot.
    \nI'm happy to know that the game isn't functional (I'm not kidding, mind you). I was quite undecided about the role of Venus, to be honest. I can try to solve it in two ways:
    \na) re-defining Venus' duties to make the role more interesting and less decisive;
    \nb) eliminating it and simply making other players take up that role during the next turns.
    \nI also could change the conflict resolution rules to eliminate the all-positive outcomes, maybe by making Venus roll something against the character. This could leave results more up to chance. I'd also like to add the option for the players to put any proposed hook at stake: by doing this, you decide how and when the conflict will start, instead of Venus; you also gain the chance to add options that follow up the conflict outcomes, should you win, while Venus could erase what you put at stake should you lose, or something along these lines.
    \nAbout the last part of your comment, I think I could start by adding more examples to each Area and by defining better the game setting in game terms.

    \n Ok, so I thought about it and figured out a couple of ways to fix the issues.
    \nTo start, there is no fixed Venus. The player sitting right in front of you takes up the role.
    \nEach character has other four statistics: "You're too young," "You don't belong to this place," "I'm afraid of you" and "I'm better than you." Each stat has 3 points and Venus has to distribute 2 more points as she sees fit during the very first scene. During conflict, before rolling, Venus puts a die in front of the player and chooses one of the stats: the choice will determine what will happen during and after the conflict. The player can accept or propose another stat (this can be done just once, so the player has the final word over the conflict theme), then they both roll. Venus rolls 1d6 + the stat value, then they compare the results: the player wins if rolls over Venus; else, he loses.
    \nBut losing this roll doesn't end the conflict: if he wants, the player can go ahead and call for another roll. The conflict is not over, then: consequences are not yet added to the fiction; instead, whoever won the previous roll just gains the upper hand, both in fiction and in gaming terms, gaining a +1 over the next rolls. The chosen stat is still active.
    \nShould the player not be yet satisfied by the results of this second roll, he can choose to roll for a third (and last) time. Again, the conflict is not over and consequences are not yet final, but it ends anyway after the roll.
    \nIf the player loses the first roll and gives up, everything follows the manual as I've already written; should he give up after the second roll, he has to add a point to the value of the stat used in the conflict; should he lose the third and last roll, Venus has also to add something to the opposite Area or can erase something from the related one.
    \nHow about these changes?

    \n Hi all! I've managed to delve a little bit more into the game, developing it further.
    \n
    \n"Venus in Furs" is a roleplaying game about sadness, rejection and love. Players take the role of Morphs, weird hybrids between humans and animals that start coming out in the world all of a sudden. Caught between different kingdoms, Morphs suffer variour degrees of social rejection.
    \nThe game structure is quite simple: each player creates and owns a single character, which is contrasted by another player who creates for him conflict scenes that have to lead the character to a choice: be what other want you to be or live according to yourself. It's quick, 6 scenes per player, and everybody can participate to the scene development in play.
    \nYou can see and download freely the game from this link: http://www.box.com/s/152pb8jpjvxfarjegx9l
    \n
    \nI'd like to see critiques and suggestions about the game, since I haven't yet had the chance to test it.
