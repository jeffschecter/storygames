\n How common is this?

I've been playing 1 shots with different groups (mostly strangers) of Pathfinder, Vampire, and Mutants & Masterminds as part of a project I'm working on.

In these unrelated groups, the following was common:

- player states action (I try to disarm)
- player rolls
- GM determines success or failure
- GM describes what happens in the fiction, even the PC's actions

Now I'm not talking about when a GM summarizes actions to create a feeling of cohesion. I've commonly seen GMs (and I do it as well) re-describe what happened in a round so everyone is on the same page before continuing.

What I'm saying is the players don't describe what's happening, even their own success or failure. They only describe the initiation of an action, "I swing my sword", never the results.

I've played with groups where this is standard but I was still surprised (it may have just been something I haven't experienced in a while). And I know people who really enjoy this style. They said they play to socialize and experience the GM's story. They enjoy seeing if their actions succeed or fail but describing them isn't important. One person described playing as rolling dice to activate the GM. It's almost like pointing to the GM and saying... "go GM, go!"

In these games, some people like it so it's great but others didn't. And even though different people wanted different things, and it would be trivial (I think, maybe I'm selling what's involved short) to give everyone what they wanted (GM describes Bob's success but Sarah describes her own), in these groups there was no flexibility. I talked to everyone about it. GMs just said this was their style. And the players who didn't like it said they like enough of the rest of the game that they just deal with it. Some players who also GM say they deal with it because they will get to describe what happens when it is their time to GM.

For me the issue wasn't that I couldn't describe the results of my actions. I play plenty of games where there is no description. But in those games you have plenty of other things to do. In these games, I felt like I spent 90% of the time passively listening. Even in games where there is a railroaded plot, I felt like I was doing something. Maybe that's the key, in those games I felt like I was doing something. But in the games I recently played, there was no illusion. In fact, it was very honest about what the game was and wasn't. And a lot of people loved it. The other thing that struck me was the games had 7-10+ players. Maybe there just isn't time for the players to describe anything with that many people!

Have you experienced games where players never describe the results of their rolls? How common is it?

Best,

    - I don't want to be bored
    - I want my character to be important to the game
    - I want to make choices and describe what my character does
    - I want what I choose and what I describe to matter
    - I want to socialize with people

    \n Hi, folks-

    As someone who runs a LOT of D&D, with my main long-running game a slightly more free-form version of this style, I think that some of you are sort of missing what I have come to realize is the point here, which is that those gamers are enjoying and interacting with these games in a manner much more similar to video games, or console RPGs than what the Story crowd parses as tabletop.

    If you take this play style and compare it to a Final Fantasy game or whatever, it's significantly MORE interactive and autonomous than that... If you have a halfway accommodating DM and the group wants to go fight Giants in the jungle, he can whip up that adventure for you, and then you can bring your dude there and run the level. But you also don't have to know all the rules, just those that your PC needs, and if you're not inclined to do much more than come up with some basic background and then enjoy the tactical part of the game, you're set.

    I suspect that you'll find that most of the people who play this way will swear they a having piles of fun. They want just enough interactivity out of their entertainment to feel some agency without the pressure of having to perform all the time. As someone with a theater background, it's very natural and doesn't feel like WORK for me to come up with all that description and narration; I choose to be extremely flattered that a group of smart, fun people find it compelling enough that they ask me to keep doing it, rather than getting upset they don't want the same things out of gaming I do. When I want an open world with full agency, I go hang with the JiffyCon crowd, and have a great time both places. And frankly, sometimes I just want to chill out, have a beer, and play a fancy complicated board game where someone else handles most of the math and the rules, but I get to spend 5 minutes coming up with a background, and that's totally cool and I am still GAMING and anyone who says otherwise can go Burn a Wheel.

    If you can get the right group, then having a narrativist story game nerd run for a bunch of more casual gamer interested in the kind of play you're talking about is actually the best compromise there is.

      Posted By: David BergSeems like an ideal sytem for this kind of play would be one in which char-gen was itself a fun game...
      \nThat's what D&D 3E is! The game is Feats and multi-classing.

      Feats are arranged in trees with a series of minimum requirements, stacked bonuses, and Feat effects that combo into other Feats. But it's not always clear how to maximize all the combos and finding the right Feat can mean going on a treasure hunt through dozens if not hundreds of sources.

      Which meant D&D 3E's biggest fans had bookshelves of 30+ feat books! It wasn't uncommon for these players, not the GM, to spend over $1,000 on character creation. And by focusing these books on the players, you had a larger pool of customers since for every game there is only 1 GM but multiple players. And given how much money players invested, they were disincentivised to upgrade to 4E. When a new Magic the Gathering card set comes out, your old cards don't become useless (although really old cards can but there is enough of a buffer that it's relatively painless).

      Feat combos in 3E took heavy inspiration from Diablo 2 (the video game) and Magic the Gathering but Wizards did a great job obfuscating the direct inspiration. Where in 4E, the sources of inspiration were more naked (powers actually became cards and the game's language became more video gamey using terms like builds, defenders: tanks, strikers: dps, controllers, leaders:buffs, marks:aggro). People complain about 4E video game-ness but it existed in 3E. But how something feels is as important as what something is.

      Multi-classing in 3E lets you be anyone. Now you didn't have to depend on the group to be a great spell caster, combat machine, or stealth master. You could be a one person army and do a bit of everything! It wasn't uncommon for people to frequently switch classes. And because the 1st level of every class were filled with amazing bonus powers (more so than 2nd level) people would take 1 level of a bunch of different classes to dip into all these benefits. And you could even multi-class into levels of monsters! I could take 6 levels of demon (technically a monster template was worth a pre-determined number of levels).

      And even though D&D is known as a game of teamwork, multi-classing encouraged players to think solo. It was all about my guy, not the team, and it was thrilling. People would spend hours making new characters that they never played. I would see people come to table and bring a folder of characters, trying to decide which to play this game. They were like decks of magic cards. But these weren't just sheets of mechanics. Every choice had fairly vibrant color. Granted the fiction behind a multi-classed librarian, ninja, barbarian, psionic, theif, ranger was strange but people seemed to have a blast trying to figure out it all fit together. Levels became more like lifepaths rather than traditional class roles and niche protection. Niche protecting was thrown out the window. But 4E threw multi-classing out of the window. Now everyone had a specific role to play. You went from being a multi-classed librarian, ninja, barbarian, psionic, theif, ranger to just a Defender. 4E killed the lonely fun and insane customization options. Now you have your role and you must work as a team. You know how in competitive card games, it's all about finding loop holes before the game company releases errata disallowing your potentially game breaking combo. 4E was like a huge errata update to 3E disallowing everyone's characters that they spent incredible amounts of money to build. But they did it all at once, rather than slowly over time to make the pill easier to swallow.

      In 3E, the real game was character creation. The real tactics were building your character, not playing them. Although playing them was super fun because you got to test your character, see them in action, and find out if you made the right choices before the official game even started. Pre-play was a HUGE part of 3E. Even the Feat books were written in ways that were fun to read, not just use for the game. Hours of fun! Where in 4E, one of the explicit design goals was to make the game less about pre-play and more about playing with a group together at the gaming table. Tactics were focused on the game itself, not character creation. Even the books became less fun to read. It was all about play the actual game, in a very specific way, and forget the pre-play lonely fun. But that meant alienating a huge percentage of their customers.

      It's amazing. On paper, 3E and 4E don't seem THAT different. But when you see how people play, or pre-play, the subtle differences are huge. In so many ways, 3E was brilliant but in subtle ways that don't make immediate sense. Where 4E's strengths were simultaneously much more obvious and less appealing to a huge subset of D&D fans.

      Posted By: Jason MorningstarDoes anybody know of a similar anthropological expedition in the other direction?
      \nI'm a biased party, but I have run several indie games for these groups, especially die hard pathfinder fans, had debriefings afterwards, and took notes. I don't have my notes with me but a few quick examples I remember...

      - spending resources to affect dice after they were rolled was considered cheap and felt like cheating (although before a roll was fine)

      - using traits against themselves broke their immersion and brought the game in directions they are not interested in

      - they said they didn't like failing rolls and then succeeding anyway but with a cost but I observed that many of them (not all) did enjoy it

      - miss effects (like in D&D 4E powers) felt cheap

      - mechanics with no fictional causes broke their immersion (although this varied quite a bit and was full of contradictions, it was more like new mechanics with no fictional causes broke immersion

      - they hate, hate, hate scene resolution

      - they don't like it when mechanics tell them how to feel, like the angry condition in Mouse Guard

      - resource stats where it isn't clear how much money they actually have was confusing

      - they didn't like combat mini games

      Those are all negative examples. There were plenty of positive ones as well. Players liked beliefs, instincts, limited traits, limited skill lists, life paths, relationships, stake setting, escalation rules, being rewarded for giving up in conflicts, mouse guard style helping mechanics, world burning, freeform character creation, and a lot more.

      I should stress that my sample size, even across 20+ people, is still too small to make generalizations.

Socially, it's a good solution, so long as enough players are reasonably engaged at any given time.

      Posted By: hans chung-ottersonPosted By: Bret GillanPosted By: jenskotHave you experienced games where players never describe the results of their rolls? How common is it?
      Pretty common. I'm running Apocalypse World right now and that's how that works. Oh and the Burning Wheel game I'm playing in works like that too.

      I'd just like to point out (however your group plays is fine) that according to the book in BW, the player describes successful outcomes and the GM describes failures.

      Technically, Burning Wheel has nothing to say about who narrates the result of tests. What it does say is that when a test is successful, the character completes the action in the manner described by the player when stating his Intent and Task before the roll. The GM is explicitly given the power to embellish.

      So really, either the player or the GM (or both!) could jump in after the roll is made to narrate what happens, so long as they remain true to the Intent and Task behind the test in the first place. It's not about designating narration rights, it's about ensuring that the result of the roll is true to the player's stated intent and task.

      In my experience, since the player always states the intent behind the test and how he plans to accomplish it up front, it seems very natural after the roll for the GM to embellish a bit to fit the action into the larger context of what's happening in the game and then move on. I have no problem with any or all of the players jumping in with additional embellishments. It often adds a lot of flavor.

      I often find that games that are more methodical in assigning narration rights are somewhat awkward in play. For instance, while I'm a big fan of Nine Worlds, one of the things that often felt forced during play was the way that each player who won points in a conflict was required to add a bit to the narration. Groups that in games with no specified narration rights would supply easy and free flowing narration of the results of a conflict would suddenly struggle with it in Nine Worlds, where the narration was more systematic. Something about being put on the spot, maybe?

      \n Back in high school, I GMed for a fair number of players who were there largely because their friends were doing it, and it was hard to hold their attention.       I spoke to a few of the players yesterday about front loading and they made an interesting observation!

      First they agreed that it is very common when they play. It allowed them to guide the GM without breaking from character centered play. The problem they frequently found was it made whiffing even more frustrating.

      Games like D&D are designed for you to fail 40-60% of the time. Failure typically means nothing effectively happens. You miss and you don't move backward or forward towards your goal and neither does your opposition. You don't have to make misses = nothing, but most people do. Add D20s have a flat 5% chance of rolling any result and your fixed bonuses are usually negated by your oppositions steadily increasing difficulty. So it's not uncommon, even if you expect to fail or succeed 50% of the time, for you to have a game session where you fail almost every roll. Which is balanced by game sessions where you seemingly succeed every roll and score multiple natural 20 critical successes! It can be quite exciting!

      But for those whiff filled sessions, front loading narration can grow tiresome. You can only say "I jump off the table, spin around, gnaw my teeth and swing down... chopping it's head off" so many times with nothing happening before you retreat back to "I swing my sword" ::rolls::.

Action / Hero / Drama / Fate points that allow you to affect an action result after you rolled are considered Cheating Points.       \n Daniel, I think it's possible to channel that enthusiasm in a couple of different ways, so as to not totally squander it.

      1 - make the full description a demand you're making of play. In Polaris (I know, I know, I always go there), you deliberately make the most far-flung claim possible, to see how much you can get away with during the negotiation process. You push hard, forcing your opponent to get in gear and find a good way to make you compromise, lest he have to give you everything you asked for.

      2 - have rolls that address the conflict at the heart of the situation, or at least at the heart of this moment. Instead of giving us a detailed idea of how your ax tears through his shield and into his chest, why not say, "I strike him with my ax so that..., and roll to see whether or not striking him, with your ax, does that thing. You can still with the ax, even if you fail the roll; the point of the roll, in this sort of mechanics, is to settle whether we get the prize that's lurking behind our actions. Or, in Forge parlance, use Conflict Resolution.

      A secondary effect of using Conflict Rez for combat is that players can evoke the right amount of fighting skill when describing their characters, but without always winning everything. If my character is a master fencer, it'd be weird and annoying to have him totally flub his sword-fighting skill checks. Not that he'd never make a mistake, but if your character is supposed to be talented at something, then the dramatic tension comes from "But does he defeat the guards in time to stop the high priest's ritual?" rather than "Does he defeat the guards?", leaving the question of "Does he do it in time to stop the ritual?" up to the GM or something.

      \n Time's past, feelings are nicely settled, and I have a few conclusions.

      Nothing surprising.

      Gaming is friendship (for many).

      Just like friend who've known each other for years, there are assumptions, expectations, scars, fears, habits, and rituals that go unsaid and in many cases are so ingrained they are never consciously considered. They are tightly woven into the bonds that hold people together and by extension their play (especially playing RPGs that can become a form of moderated discussion and is heavily social in nature).

      How quickly new people, strangers, adapt to these friend groups often determines acceptance or rejection. It's like having a party where the host wants you take your shoes off but doesn't want to have to say it (or it's so standard they never think people would ever enter your home without taking off their shoes). It's up to you to notice that everyone else has take off their shoes. And often the people who don't, aren't confronted, or reminded, and even if you ask if it's ok and they say it's up to you, they sometimes don't mean it and have a very specific preference. Instead of direct communication, you will simply not be invited back after a few instances of these unsaid rules being broken. And even if it may be helpful to have a formal list of what not to do, I would imagine being handed a huge checklist upon entering someone's home might be off putting (even if it's honest).

      Playing games is like any other social interaction. You may need to verify, by asking or observation, that it's ok to narrate your die roll just like you might have to confirm if it's ok to grab a glass from the cabinet or ice from the freezer. It's best not to assume, even if asking what the lines are may feel weird. And even if in your house, and your family's house, and your coworker's house, and your 10 other friend's houses it's all done 1 way, it may not be that way somewhere else (even if the other people assume everyone else does it the same way they do it). Hell, even in the same house, even when everyone agrees to act a certain way, doesn't mean they want to, it's often all about compromise to keep the large whole intact.

      As a stranger among friends with predefined but unsaid codes of conduct reinforced by years of habit formed in isolation from what other groups may accept as normal, I can't take for granted that I'm the outsider. And although I personally am a strong believer in compromise, adaption, one size doesn't fit all even if the majority wears one size, that doesn't mean my beliefs are shared or even wanted. I'm the guest in someone else's home.

      That's a lot of text just to say... these groups weren't the ones acting weird. I was.

      And it may seem small, but it's the social glue that holds friends together and reinforces their group identity. That's powerful stuff to mess with... and my preference is to proceed with caution, respect, and understanding.
