Are there really no RPGs for the Mainstream?

Eric Provost
December 2006 edited December 2006 in Story Games

I'm in the process of re-reading a lot of the stuff about reaching the mainstream with RPGs. While I've been doing so, I see an awful lot of folks saying that they still don't feel comfortable introducing RPGs to people outside the hobby. The most common reasons seem to be either the complexity of the rules or some discomfort with the 'geekyness' of it. At the same time I see plenty of folks banging their drum about making games for mainstream folks, with the implication that such games don't yet exist.

But don't they?

I see a whole potential holiday shopping list of non-geeky & non-complex RPGs out there. The Shab-al-Hiri Roach. Totally not complex. I'd play it with my mom. Right after taking "Murub" out of the deck. How about PTA and The Mountain Witch? They take up lots of time, but they're not complex. The arcane knowlege they require from their players is television and samaurai films, respectively. Those aren't mainstream? There are a dozen other games out there that I've heard of, have this gut feeling that they're just as close to mainstream, but just haven't played or read them yet.

If these games really aren't mainstream potential, then what is it that you see in them that isn't mainstream?

    Here's my thoughts on this. It largely depends on what we mean by "mainstream." I think the largest untapped potential for bringing people into the RPG hobby is people who already love to play games but don't play RPGs. I would happly play Shooting The Moon, PtA or even My Life with Master with anyone who would already jump on board for a game of Clue or Monopoly.

    What I don't think we have is game TEXTS that make RPGs accessable to the same people without an "in the know" leader. I don't think someone who is familiar with family night boardgaming can pick up My Life with Master or even PtA and really know what to DO. This is because we still have not developed a language for the procedures of play. Texts often start with elaborate descriptions of setting or characters with out saying what function these things serve on a game play level. Texts often describe how to resolve conflict without specifing the scope or purpose of conflicts or how to set one up or recognize its ending.

    Now as far as roping the rest of the world I find that the situation first model works best. When talking to absotutely non-game savy people about RPGs I usually pick Dogs in the Vineyard or My Life with Master to discuss because they have strong upfront easily relatable situations. Interestingly enough I usually get a, "I'd like to try that sometime...." response in kind a of an understanded musing tone. Back in the day when I would simply try to describe D&D, I'd usually get a "Hmmmm... interesting" response in a kind of hesitant tone.

Some narrativism supporting games have very little in the way of narration, perhaps even less than D&D.

      I should first point out that I see a gaping void between "describe your sword", "open the door", and things like Waiting/Tea, where your input is actually important because it constrains and affects what comes after.

      Colin, in my experience people are generally rather smart and creative. People like creative authority. They even tend to assume authorities they don't actually have - I've had countless experiences with OUAT where new players misunderstand the rules in ways that give them excessive ability to interrupt, or abilities to edit what's come before, and so on. No one ever misunderstands the game in ways that give them less authority.

      My hypothesis is that this is because authority in OUAT matters. You might need to plant a subplot way in the beginning of the game to get your endgame to come off right - it takes a bit of planning to finish a story off with "When father saw her babies he knew he hd to allow the marriage." Whereas, with a game like D&D, your narration doesn't matter; it's not authoritative or even informational to the other players. Everything that matters is embedded in the miniatures game.

      Consequently, these games where narration is weak and insignificant actually cause narration atrophy, because one can be much more effective in the minis game if one's attention is not divided by this other thing that matters not at all.

      So, like, there need to be constraints, but they have to encompass some power, or the kids will just not play in that sandbox.
