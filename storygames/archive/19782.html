\n In this thread I said I was planning to run a campaign inspired by SGU but ported to 5E. My girlfriend, who is supposed to co-GM the game with me, has convinced me we should set it on an actual spaceship rather than a sailboat, to make it feel more like the show. We're still using 5E, but now with some Spelljammer stuff added in. This change in direction has also helped me settle on a title!

So I am going to need the "island" generator to be slightly tweaked to be more of a "planet/planetoid" generator. But otherwise all the same stuff.

The game will focus on the tension engendered by resource scarcity + largely untrained, civilian crew. The point of the randomly generated islands is for the PCs to go on them and get what they need, if they can, in terms of food and other supplies. Meanwhile, the ship is not under their control. It seems to have a will or even a mind of its own. They can work to unlock its secrets by doing Parsely-style investigating. That's probably going to be the toughest design work we have ahead of us.

Also: we're planning on recruiting 12 or so players, and allowing up to 8 to come to each session. 4 can go planet-side, the other 4 can stay on the ship and deal with the crew and/or work on solving the puzzles.

So, what do you think, S-G community? Does this sound fun? What potential pitfalls do you see?

    \n Well, with my partner and I both GMing, we can have two separate tables, each of 4 players. And yes, the internal problems of the ship will be one "front" of the game.

    The large pool of players thing is a way of dealing with the realities of adult schedules. It also models the way many ensemble TV shows work fairly well, with some people only appearing in the background in certain weeks. Advanced Wizards and Wizards works that way too\u2014I have about 10 players, but only 4 or at most 5 come to any given session. It seems to work just fine. Obviously it creates pressure to complete a concrete objective/plot each session, but that's a good thing IMO. Also, following the model of the SGU TV show, there's an at least reasonably good in-fiction reason why the away team has a very finite amount of time to complete its mission: the ship is leaving, with or without them, in a set amount of time (i.e. at the end of session).

    \n Jared Sorensen's Parseley games are a thing we're both fond of. Action Castle, Spooky Manor, and the like. I actually run Action Castle from memory as a "party trick" with some regularity.

    The idea with integrating it into an RPG is that characters' abilities won't matter for purposes of this part of the game, that it's pure player intelligence that's being tested. There isn't any die-rolling, combat, etc.

    \n Jared's Parseley games are minigames that mimic the old Zork/Infocom-style games where you enter "VERB OBJECT [PREPOSITIONAL PHRASE]" sentences and get results back (e.g., "GO NORTH," "GET GUM," "PUT GUM IN BACKPACK"). Each game, such as Action Castle, comes on a glossy, folded card with each room described for the GM and a list of exits, objects, and a few notes for adjudicating actions in the room. It's a MUSH in simple, printed form.
