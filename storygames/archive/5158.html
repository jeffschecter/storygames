I know this idea has been around since the first caveman crept out of Blackmoor - but I would love to see a normalized rating of games. \n
    \n
  • I'd love to know that when a game is called "awesome", that means it's actually in the top 5% of released games.
    I'd love to see 50% of games being called "below average".
    I'd love to know that when a game is called "crap", that means it's worse than 95% of what's out there.
    • \n
\nNever going to happen, of course. It takes a lot of resources to actually do this properly - someone would have to read and play a representative sample of games and set up some standards. However, perhaps this would be workable for purposes of game reviews etc:

- We decide on some categories we think are important for a good game. Ease of play; play satisfaction; innovation; appearance; readability - stuff like that.
- Within each category, we find 7 benchmark games. Games that represent ratings of Terrible, Poor, Mediocre, Fair, Good, Great, and Superb (for example - I just stole this from FUDGE, obviously).

Assuming setting up such a standard is desirable and feasible, what should the categories be?

    \nI feel the same, but in a different way:

    There was really no way that I was going to play Shadowrun again. Then Eric Provost stepped up with a set of house-rules and a setting mod. We're still FUNDAMENTALLY playing Shadowrun, and it's a fucking blast (definitely 80% or higher). If I was playing Shadowrun at a convention or other "use the rules as is, the play style as-is, and the default setting", I would be clawing my eyes out.

    When Mark Causey runs The Shadow Of Yesterday, I. Fucking. Play. He throws down hardcore, and in a really fun way. Another GM, another setting, I might not be quite as motivated.

    Rifts? Fuck that noise. And yet, I'm planning on running a Rifts Africa game, with most of the original rules, this year: turning down the rules, turning up the setting, and using a bunch of "story games tricks" to make it bearable and fun.

    A lot of the game experience comes down to:
    The GM (in games with a GM)
    The Players
    The setting
    The rules/rules mods
    The level of creativity
    The level of enthusiasm

    To look at games themselves through this lens, without any of the game experience stuff above, well I don't mean to be harsh, but it's a lot like the old Ghostbuster RPG description of "Cold Science". As if the game experience is wholly the book it comes from.

    I mean, the idea of starting up a series of RPG reviews in the classic "game book review" sense, and keep it on a "No, a rating of 50% IS AVERAGE, not 'crap' " scale would perhaps be worthwhile. We don't have as much creep in RPG reviews as there are with console game reviews (where "average" is 80%). However, unlike console reviews there's a ton of subjective stuff going on. I was completely unsold on Unhallowed Metropolis because of a boring-ass con experience (which yet showed off the completeness, and unfortunate mediocrity, of the system. The rest sucked, though: My buddy Travis *fucked the game to life* so that we wouldn't waste any time). And yet, I do not deny that for others they have the game in their top 10 percentile because of the exciting setting and the creativity and character-play of the people at the table.

    With console games, the GM, players, rules, creativity, all that is not applicable, so the range you're left with is still subjective, but far more narrow. I can say "Clive Barker's Jericho was very pretty, and yet it sucked", and will find my opinion reflected by a majority of gamers. Try that with GURPS, or Best Friends.

    In the end, looking at your scale, if I tried to contribute, I can honestly not think of a game other than Space Master that I would put below Fair, and I can not think of a single game that I would classify as "Superb". But maybe I'm just being deceived by your rating ranks, which themselves again seem to heap on a big spoonful of subjectivity. I could rather see a game divided up into 14 categories, where each category gets a subjective rating ("Ready to play with minimal bookkeeping"; "The game setting makes the reader excited to play"; "the rules are creative and thought was given to the style of play they were built for"; "Playing a Role is the most important aspect of this game"; "Swing Vote", etc). Even the choosing of those categories will be somewhat subjective. But it's a lot easier to stomach rating a game like GURPS (which again for me is "won't fucking touch it as-is, unless someone runs it for me with a creative setting, rules mods, and some enthusiasm; then I will totally happily play it, and LOVE the experience! Which these days, happens A Lot") Superb in some categories, Fair in others, Good in many, and Terrible in a few, than to just give it a blanket "Mediocre" or something.

    Does that make sense? Not to harsh the buzz, I actually want to keep a dialog going about this issue. Maybe we'll all learn something.

    Mattijs,

    \n

    Well, pushing "book review" culture seems like a step backward from pushing "actual play" culture, so it's not really suprising the subject has provoked some knee-jerk hostility. Brand Robins had started writing some stuff about the validity of game reviews, which I was interested to hear about. (I'm sure he'll publish that DitV review any day now.)

    \n

    Anyway, to get back to what you actually wanted to talk about...

    \n

    I think some categories that you could rate on might be:

    \n
      \n
    • Presentation: How attractive/professional the book appears, including artwork and layout. Does it draw a person into the text or does it turn one off? If somebody glances through this briefly, will they get a good idea what this game might be about and be able to decide if they want to play?
      • \n
    • Construction: The physical quality of the book itself. Not so much eye-catching bells-and-whistles (that's Presentation) as just being quality materials and assembly. Does the binding fall apart after a couple uses, or can it take a realistic amount of wear-and-tear due to handling? Does the non value-added price of the product seem to correspond to the object you get?
      • \n
    • Intelligibility: Does the text concisely and opaquely present the game the author is trying to describe, or is it poorly-organized or does it make unreasonable assumptions about the reader's a priori knowledge? Does this stuff make sense to anybody except the author and the people he plays this game with?
      • \n
    • Digestablilty: Is the stuff which potential players are required to understand small enough that they can reasonably be expected to learn the rules, or will one person end up "carrying" the rules and end up as the "rules lawyer" for everyone else?
      • \n
    • Completeness: Does the text actually present everything you need to know to run the game, or will someone end up putting a lot of work into the game before they can actually play it? Not so much "GM-prep," as the practical matter of filling in significant swaths of system that are not clearly or workably addressed by the text.
      • \n
    \n

    These are areas I think you can find wide agreement on standards for good or bad, and not as mere personal tastes. I mean, nobody's really going to argue that they like books that fall apart, right?
