    \n Callan,

    This is the same issue as certain in-game actions and narrations earning the "gonzo" tag. What is gonzo to some players will be perfectly in-keeping-with-genre-expectations for someone else.

    If you narrate swinging from a chandelier, and we all nod in approval that your character would indeed do that in our swashbuckling game... then awesome. I step it up by doing a backflip off a wall, landing on my enemy combatant's head. Everyone gives me this rolling-eyes stare and no one congratulates my creative narration.

    Damn it.

    I thought... swinging on chandelier was a little zany, so is the backflip move. I blew the border, as you put it.

    There are two issues going on here:

    1.) We have different limits
    2.) We are operating on different convention expectations.

    Either I went "too far", or I went in the "wrong direction". Possibly both. The border isn't necessarily in one direction. It is in all directions surrounding the SIS. It extends further in one (or more) direction, and that's because we're stretching it to accomodate genre expectations.

    How do you know where the borders are? Partially by talking about conventions before play. Partially by reading people.

    Largely by incrementally testing them, against different directions, to find comfort zones. Did you save up 100 points and blow them all at once? Did people get behind that, or shy away? Did you save up 150 points and blow them all at once? Did people like that magic, or did they avert eyes?

    Likely, borders expand with time, because trust is gained. When we start play, the border is really 0. Until I believe that your intent is to play a fun game, I won't be ready to accept ANY point expenditure. Then as we start to play, I'll be comfortable with being hit by a 25 point fireball. And as I grow to trust you more, and we explore our borders mutually and with ongoing expansion, it's possible that I'll be comfortable with 200 point fireballs hitting my mage.

    There's a reason that the tried-n-try D&D campaign is rags to riches. People feel awkward with their peers narrating glorious kings and lord-emperors slaying dragons in a single sword stroke. They aren't willing to lend that credibility at the start of the game. So we have to start earlier than that, and reach and push those borders gradually. In many games, chargen is about initializing reasonable starting borders.

    \n Callan, this is actually why I really like games with mechanical "border setting rules" that can be accessed in play.
    \n
    \nI understand well the importance of borders and staying within them. I just have zero desire to read a 5000 manuscript detailing every possible permutation of where that border is, nor do I enjoy extensive "line and veil" discussions pre play.
    \n
    \nI prefer to establish those borders dynamically during play: its easier, it allows for greater creative potential, its more convenient, faster to start playing, and because sometimes pushing borders is really interesting.
    \n
    \nMost of the time Joe's nodding vs. eyerolling is sufficient to establish precedent...say that covers 80% of all border setting needs.
    \n
    \nSOMETIMES, it doesn't. That can be because someone disagrees that an action was eye-roll worthy, or because sometimes something tactically important is at stake and depends on where that border is. On those occassions (say the other 20%) its quite useful (and functional) to have a formal "border check" mechanic.
    \n
    \nDogs has the rule which instructs players to abide by the aesthetic of the most critical person at the table.
    \nUniversalis has the Challenge mechanic which allows players to bid resources for the right to set the border where they want it.
    \nSome games give specific veto power over specific aspects to specific players and make those players the "border patrol" for their area of responsibility.
    \n
    \nLots of ways to do it. But doing it in some fashion (preferably one that fits the game) is IMO essential.
