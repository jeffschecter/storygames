    \n Just off the top of my head, doing my very best to express artistic instinct via inadequate words:

    Use the dot at the end of the exclamation point. Change it into something else that conveys danger: a biohazard symbol, a lightning bolt, a skull, a spiky blob, etc.

    Maybe superimpose "Achtung" in white within the long piece of the exclamation point and "spiel" in small text within the dot. "Spiel" may end up being too small.

    Possibly share the dot at the end of the exclamation point with the dot of the "i" in "spiel". (Again, putting "achtung" within the long bit.)

    Consider a second color. IMO difficult to convey "danger" via black and white. Even a percentage of black, maybe.

    New font. Something with harsh edges. Rounded-anything conveys softness, not edginess. Avoid square angles as well.

    This is where I'd start if this were my logo project.

    \n A major consideration is how large you'll be reproducing it. The text is legible now, but I have a feeling you'll be printing it MUCH smaller and the text will not only lose its graphic impact, but also its structure. Also, consider its use on websites. In my experience, vertical logos take up more valuable real estate than horizontal ones or logos that are contained in a(n implied) shape.

    Have you considered reversing the text out of the exclamation mark? Are you married to the concept? If I were your art director and you were a designer working under me, I'd have you sit down with a sketchbook (not the computer) and draw 20 variations. Pick the best three, then refine.

    \n This is a big improvement, Darcy. I really like the break-out look.

    One thing to consider: The ratio of type size to the rest of the logo means, at smaller sizes, your type won't be readable. The outline style of the type hurts readability, too. Maybe try reworking things so your type is still readable at a variety of imprint sizes.

    I also think it's worth thinking about the roundness and sharpness of parts of the logo (the triangle corners vs. the sharp-edged top of the bang) and how they work together or don't.

    This one is definitely worth reworking a bit. Later, we can get down to the nitty-gritty of logo work.
