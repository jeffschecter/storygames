\n So I think most of us are aware that Stars Without Number is the current new crypto-old-school hotness. I confess, I've gotten sucked in by the promise of Traveller-on-meth sandbox scifi play. We're starting a game in a couple weeks.

One of the most charming things I found about SWN is that it actively promotes hacking and houseruling. The game engine is so simple that it's also pretty easy to hack, especially if you're at all hip to how this style of game typically plays out.

I'm thinking about some houserules myself, mostly along the lines of ripping out the (thin to the point of transparent) XP/advancement system for something else. I'm not prepared to post the details yet, not 'til I've had a chance to see it in action. John Harper's been playing a lot of SWN, though, and he posted his house rules in another thread. Surely others are running and hacking this game?

What are your house rules? Why did you add them? Not really interested in picking apart the suitability or function of any given hack, just curious to see what people are doing with the game.

    \n At times when I playtest, I find myself doing it with a single participant, or with someone who really isn't keen on the significant chance of sudden PC death inherent in the rules as written. In these cases, I make sure to get a short list of things precious to the PC, a list I add to as play progresses. I cycle things around so that I always have a handle on the three or four people/things/goals that have the most emotional purchase on the PC.
    \n
    \nIf the PC then gets into a situation where they would have died by the rules as written, I simply engineer matters so that they survive by the skin of their teeth. Then I look at the list, randomly pick an item off it, and break it. It doesn't necessarily have to be a direct outcome of their failure- it might be related to the actions of some other antagonist in the game, or a delayed consequence of some questionable choice the PC made some time ago, or sheer bad luck. They may not even realize what was broken until they get a message about it or return to the neighborhood. Whatever way it's broken should be a way that allows an interesting PC reaction- you can't argue with a micrometeorite strike through your daughter's vacc suit, but you can respond to the hypernationalist locals who burnt down the refugee camp you set up for some ex-slaves three sessions ago.
    \n
    \nHowever it's sliced, every time they "die", their world gets significantly worse in a manner to which they can respond. This leaves players with the assurance that they'll be able to keep playing their character as long as they desire while simultaneously leaving them with a distinct dread of combat and other physically dangerous situations. They can never be sure what element of their world is going to be screwed over if they go down, and so naturally, they imbue the unknown with the worst possible outcomes. Simply listening to their fears often clues me in on angles and aspects that I'd never have recognized otherwise.

    \nXP for loot is pretty fun. To keep things sane, I don't do 1 credit = 1 xp directly, though (storing and transporting loads of wealth isn't an issue in SWN the way it is in D&D, so PCs can more easily snag gobs of credits). I usually look at all the loot and give an XP award scaled to rewards for their level -- p. 132 (or a bit higher if the haul was exceptional).

    For example, the PCs just scored a massive haul from an adventure that I tagged as level 7, so they'll probably get something like 5,000 xp each from the loot alone.

    We also do character goals (p. 65), usually 1-3 per PC. Depending on the complexity and challenge of a goal, it's usually worth between 250xp and 1500xp. A character just recently added a really tough goal, "Discover the cause of the Scream," so we initially set that at 5,000 xp. But after some reflection we thought it would be better to break that up into several smaller goals that can be achieved in sequence.

    \n We found the weight limits on Teleportation to be waaaay too low. We increased the weight limit at each step by a factor of 10, roughly.

    In standard SWN, after you teleport you can only perform free actions for the rest of the round. We dropped this restriction, allowing teleporters to jump and then act normally. This is really powerful! We like it this way.

    In exchange for this, we have an additional restriction:
    When you teleport, make a ME save. If you pass, you may teleport again immediately. If you fail, your power must recharge for 1d8 rounds.

    \n I use the scale on p. 132 to judge what "level" the goal is in and of itself, regardless of their current character level.

    Simple, recurring goals (like our Pilot's goal to chart new courses) get rated near the bottom, a fraction of level 1 reward (250 - 500 xp). Charting several brand-new courses would be a big deal for a level 1 character, and get them close to leveling up. For a level 5 character, it's become old hat, so it doesn't scale up.

    A "change the world" type goal is a level 9+ goal, but we haven't seen any of those quite yet. So far, their goals have been evolving roughly in step with the level advancement, getting a little more grandiose as we go.

    Oh, I also do the same thing for XP rewards from Missions, which are essentially GM-authored goals that the players take on.

    \n A few new house rules cropped up tonight.

    Helping is capped at +2. Helping rolls get variable difficulty, based on the situation, instead of defaulting to 7.

    Psychic torching! You can torch to use a power you don't know, as long as you have the discipline. But it's really risky. You roll a number of torch dice equal to the power level of the ability you're using. So if you use a level 9 power that you don't know, you roll 9d10, and read each die as a torch die for the results. (1-4: lose a point of Wisdom. 5-8: lose a point of Constitution. 9, 10: Nothing.)

    Quick looting! Need to strip a derelict hull for loot? Here ya go.
    The salvage team makes a roll, using Tech/Astronautics or Tech/Postech. See which difficulty level they hit:

    7: 1% salvage value of the vessel
    9: 2% salvage value
    11: 4% salvage value
    13: 10% salvage value

    Base salvage time is 48 hours. If they take 4 days, they get +1 to the roll. Helpers can use the main skills or Tactics. Failure (6 or less) means a complication, like a salvager getting trapped in the wreckage, vacc-suit damaged, unstable ship system explodes, etc.

    \n In the interest of full disclosure, here's the looting ruling John actually used in play, as opposed to the nifty one he thought up on the bus on the way home

    Looting roll was Postech/Int assisted by another Postech/Int, and Exosuit/Dex (representing some labor frames we pulled out of the ships locker). My character, Ishtar Jane, the Captain, lead the roll, and got a 12. John had a bunch of d6's in front of him, and I think he started with 2d6 for 7+, added a d6 for hitting 9+ and another for hitting 11+. There was another d6 that we would have gotten at 13+. So 4d6 actual. Then he rolled those, got an 18, and that became 18K credits worth of salvage in our hold.

    Now we were answering the question of not only "how much salvage is there" but "what's the value of what we can cram into the 10K of hold space we have."

    A generalized looting house rule might want to consider not only the value, but the bulk of the salvage. Cargo space is precious.

    \n Regarding advancement: I'm working on a two-part solution.

    The first part I'm not yet ready to reveal in its entirety, not 'til I've had a chance to play it. It's a modification of Burning Wheel's artha/reward cycle drawn in very broad terms: get a low-level bonus point for striving and complicating, get a high-level bonus point for achieving and per table vote (MVP, Workhorse). As you spend points, you earn your XP: 100xp per low-level (Fate), 250xp per high-level (Persona). How those points get earned is what I'm working out right now. It'll be some combination of Traits (already provided by the World Creation rules!), player-set Goals, and ... something else. Instinct maybe, or "Attitude" (Belief-ish but directed at some discrete sandbox element: faction, alien race, corporation, religion, government, planet, etc.).

    Anyway, at least half the XPs needed to level up will need to be generated by spending your points.

    The second half is a list of Achievements I'm working on:

    Traveller: Visit five new planets: 1000xp
    Far Traveller: Visit five sectors: 2500xp
    Contact Specialist: Meet five alien races: 1000xp
    Friends In High Places: Formally ally with a faction: 500xp
    Enemies in High Places: Make an enemy of a faction: 500xp
    Visit ancient ruins: 250xp
    Ooh shiny: 250xp when you acquire your first artifact
    I am become Death: 500xp when you acquire your first piece of maltech
    Buy a Starship: 5000xp
    Savings Account: 500xp for saving up $5000
    1%er:2000xp for saving up $50,000
    Back from the dead: 500xp the first time you are resurrected
    Improving Your Lot: Spending 30 days at each lifestyle level is an achievement!
    Improving Your Lot (Slum): 100xp
    Improving Your Lot (Poor): 200xp
    Improving Your Lot (Common): 300xp
    Improving Your Lot (Good): 500xp
    Improving Your Lot (Elite): 1000xp
    Improving Your Lot (Peerless): 2500xp

    My thinking here is that I want to prompt the players to go engage in the sandbox for its own sake, and this is one way of doing it. Also thinking about a "when you level up, write a new custom Achievement for someone at the table. It will be worth 100xp x the level you just achieved" type rule.

    Anyway, no more than half your level-up can come from Achievements.

    The points are probably off but I think this is the structure I'm gonna try out.

    \n I'm thinking about adding a flavor mechanic to basic skill resolution. I always like it when resolution has the potential of adding more to the situation than just pass/fail.

    Throw 2d6 as normal. But if the dice come up double odds ("1,1" "3,3" "5,5") then a bitter flavor gets added to the situation. If the dice come up double evens ("2,2" "4,4" "6,6") a sweet flavor is added. "Flavor" here meaning a twist or complication. Note that flavor has nothing to do with the skill and effort of the character making the roll, its possible to pull some sweet on a failure and sour an a success. Its just a parallel process to introduce fun complications and opportunities.

    \n Something that caught my attention and want to leverage: Lifestyle costs!

    So there's a lifestyle cost table in the book and I've tied it into my achievements thing earlier already (xps for escalating your lifestyle) but there are a couple things I want to work out:

    * What does each lifestyle level buy you? All that comes to mind is that "good" and up probably includes investment in a private residence somewhere. But what else might lifestyle buy you? Anything? Conditional penalties to...I don't know, some applications of Persuasion, based on proximity to the target's own lifestyle? Just spitballing here.

    * Consequences of failing to pay for your lifestyle. How many days/weeks can go by if you literally put 0 Credits into your lifestyle? What happens? Thinking about a temporary -1 Con each week you don't pay.

    * If you're living on a ship, how does that change your Lifestyle cost? And how does that fit, if at all, into the 100 credits/day/crewmember math (and the 20 credits/day/crewmember stores cost, which I assume is inside that 100/c/d payroll) that a ship owner has to deal with? One way: steerage conditions reduce your lifestyle cost (whatever it may be) by 15 credits (so you can life "common" at net zero or "poor" at earn 5/day), crew cabin conditions reduce by 20, luxury cabins by 50. But that feels janky and doesn't actually address the inflow-outflow math of ship ownership. Mmmaybe we take the 20/day stores cost as absolute and 100/day payroll costs as flexible -- typical crew pay is 80/day, and they can live however they want within the confines of the ship (10/20/50 per day). Anything left is gravy as long as the captain is paying. If the captain stops paying, obviously lifestyle will take a hit. And too if the captain doesn't pay for fuel the ship simply doesn't go anywhere and if he doesn't pay for stores the ship is uninhabitable.

    This probably all sounds fussy and ridiculous, and it is, but it's also something you could set up once and forget about, until someone wants a raise or to increase their lifestyle (tied into bullet #1).

    Seems to me like Lifestyle costs are *this* close to being a useful thing that pushes the characters deep into the sandbox. So I'm trying to hammer out the lumps to make that happen.

    \n Here's what we're doing with Lifestyle:

    Slum: 90 (-1 to all rolls due to poor nutrition)
    Poor: 300
    Common: 450 (+1 to Culture rolls to interact with working-class people)
    Good: 750 (+1 to Culture rolls to contact or interact with rich/powerful people)
    Elite: 3,000 (+2 to Culture rolls to contact or interact with rich/powerful people. Access to mid-range NPCs without bribes or prior contacts.)
    Peerless: 30,000 (+3 to Culture rolls to contact or interact with rich/powerful people. Access to high-level NPCs without bribes or prior contacts.)

    Standard shipboard lifestyle is Poor (delicious nutrient paste!). If you want better, you pay out of your own pocket.

    Those Culture bonuses and access to NPCs have made a big impact on the game so far.

      \n I\u2019ve consistently struggled with how XP and advancement are presented in SWN as per the RaW, and have been trying to design my own solution. I think I\u2019ve finally found it.

      To review, the XP allocation method given in the SWN text is vague. Page 65 states:\n
      Characters are awarded experience points by the GM upon accomplishing certain goals, defeating meaningful enemies, or plundering insufficiently guarded wealth. It\u2019s often wise to have a word with the GM at the start of a campaign to find out just what sort of activities earn experience in his game; some GMs only award experience for accomplishing story goals, while others might only hand out rewards for acquiring great wealth. Experience point awards for defeating enemies tend to be nominal unless related to some story goal...
      \nPage 132 gives general guidelines for how much of a Reward characters should get per session, both wealth and XP simultaneously:
      \n

      The table provided gives examples of rewards appropriate to a character of a given level. To determine the total award for an adventure, simply take the highest PC level in the group and multiply by the number of PCs. The third column shows the average number of adventures it will take for an Expert to gain a level using the award given. Warriors and Psychics will rise slightly more slowly. If you prefer a faster or slower rate of advance, just alter the numbers accordingly.
      \nAnd that\u2019s pretty much all the RaW gives you for managing advancement. There\u2019s no defined formula for attaching numbers to the accomplished goals, defeated enemies and plundered wealth alluded to. As I recall, the writer Kevin Crawford says he deliberately kept this system indistinct because he wanted to leave plenty of room for GM\u2019s to decide what earns XP in their own games. A Space Marine campaign that\u2019s mainly an ongoing bughunt would presumably stress rewards for combat, a Star Trader campaign would reward cash earned, et cetra. The problem, as I see it, is that there really should be more explicit connections between the text\u2019s clear focus on sandbox exploration and the PC-motivator that is advancement. And if you\u2019re going to present a scale of potential XP rewards of 1-to-Thousands, as implied by the Class Level progressions, there really has to be a methodology that justifies both those extremes (and not just render it meaningless by rounding it off, like AD&D2 with its lazy \u201dOne-Tenth of whatever the PC needs to level up\u201d guidelines).

      Speaking of rounding off, its tempting to go with \u201cthere are no XP, just Levels\u201d as can be done with D20, but preserving the staggered advancement rates of the different classes and a general OSR aesthetic dissuades me from that.

      I thought about expanding on the menu of Achievements presented earlier in this discussion by PaulB, but I found it doesn\u2019t scale well (500 XP is a big boon to a 1st level character, but a mere pittance after they\u2019ve reached 9th) and too fiddly for my tastes, too subjective.

      What I really wanted was something as elegant as the reward cycle in Old School D&D, where wide varieties of adventurous player activity are encouraged by just two reward buttons: \u201csome XP for encountering opponents, more XP for recovering treasure,\u201d both of which are calculated by easy formulas. I could just implement the same buttons in SWN, but space adventure is not the same thing as dungeon delving. Long-range travel is much more common, non-violent social interaction is expected and spaceships are a distinct feature.

      So first, I decided on a basic award formula, something that was easy to use and remember, and widely applicable:

      (Base Stat)squared x 10
      [Annoyingly, this forum doesn't recognize formatting for suprerscript. In situations where the Base State is less than 1, the final XP value is 5]

      Then I looked for ways to apply it to the core elements of space adventure I identified above:

      Personal Encounters: Any time the party has a perilous encounter with a being or creature, the XP reward is calculated based on their class level or HD (level is added up separately for each opponent per encounter, not totalled. 12 1HD pirates are worth 120xp, not 1440xp). Creatures with notable extra abilities can be bumped up a level or two for calculating XP. I was pleased to note the formula produces XP awards not far off from the table in BXD&D. Note I intend to implement this for a variety of encounters, not just combat. Sneaking by, parlaying with and even bargaining with opponents will be rewarded, as will fast-talking the twerp in the landing tower denying you landing clearance, as will bartering for the price of silk in the bazaar (where the peril is financial).

      Ships: Base Stat is the ship\u2019s Armor rating, which happens to match up well to its overall menace. Note, I intend to give out the same reward for any ship the PC\u2019s use themselves during a session. Same as with personal encounters, this isn\u2019t solely about combat.

      Planets: For every session the party interacts with the perilous aspects of a particular planetary environment (as opposed to just cooling heels in the spaceport) they get a reward based on 10-(the Tech Level of the planet). Admittedly, this is a bit broad and subjective. The justification is that generally having less tech networks to fall back on makes things harder, and its a gentle push for players to look for adventure on more rugged worlds.

      Factions: Meaningfully encountering the Factions of a sector earns a reward based on the Faction\u2019s (Force + Cunning + Wealth). This only accounts if some significant part of the Faction is aware of the PC\u2019s interaction and may have cause to react to it specifically. Vandalizing propaganda posters of the Theocratic regime doesn\u2019t earn XP, taking up a significant mission against them (or for them) does.

      Profit: Not using the formula here, just straight classic 1 credit earned equals 1 XP gained. I really don\u2019t care how they profit, it can come from working part-time at a Jovian-Burger stand as much as chasing bounties; I trust players would rather do the interesting thing.

      At first, I wanted to also calculate awards for distance travelled (both on a planet and through space) but couldn\u2019t think of any approach that wasn\u2019t too subjective, and also realized that, like number of bullets fired or hours strived, travel is just a means to achieve objectives and doesn\u2019t have to be tracked.

      In practice, it should play out like so: The party encounters an expatriate naval officer, who hires them to contact someone on the next planet and recover a dossier. Price is haggled, and agreed at 5,000 plus passage. They travel coach to the planet, sneak around a bit, fight some guards, find the contact, get the dossier, then trek over desert and steal a shuttle to get past the Spaceport lockdown. Back at the starting point, they ditch the stolen shuttle and collect their payment.

      160xp for the expat officer encounter (fourth level).
      0xp for the trip out (didn\u2019t fly their own ship or encounter one on the way)
      490xp for time on planet (Tech 3)
      80xp for sneaking past soldiers and peons (four 1hd, one 2hd)
      150xp for fighting a guard squad (six 1hd, 1 3hd)
      90xp for finding the contact (third level)
      5xp for stealing/flying the shuttle
      640xp for working for the Freedom Fighters faction (total faction stats of 8).
      5,000xp for fee.
      Total of 6,615xp to be divided among the characters who survived the adventure.

      \n Some time ago, I switched to using cards for determining iniative in the SWN demos I was running at conventions. This eliminated the possibility of ties, made it easier to track and (as a minor aside) made it harder to cheat initiative. Over several plays the system developed so that it also provided tactical choices without a map and a bit of battlefield chaos. Enough players commented positively on this approach that it seems worth sharing publicly.

      Use a single shuffled pack of regular playing cards, with the jokers.

      [Aside: I prefer a deck of Copag EPOC plastic cards, for ease of shuffling and (somewhat) in-genre presentation. This Starlight deck is also aesthetically tempting.]

      When a combat begins, deal every character a single card, including NPC's. Groups of cannon-fodder opponents can be treated as mobs of up to six or so figures per card.

      Characters receive an additional card per point of Dexterity bonus, and another one per +4 Attack Bonus.
      [Experts: Lvls 9, 17. Psychics: Lvl 11. Warriors: Lvls 7, 13, 19]

      At the start of each round of combat, each player chooses one of their cards to be their initiative. At the end of the round, used cards are collected and every character is dealt a single new card.
      [I don't bother to reshuffle used cards back into the deck until its half-used. Except for jokers, which I shuffle back in at the end of each round.]

      Initiative is counted down from Aces to Dueces (A,K,Q,J,10-2). Ties are resolved in alphabetical order of the suits (Clubs, Diamonds, Hearts, Spades).

      However, Face cards and Aces are considered to represent Opportunites the character has the option of seizing, at the cost of having the value "reversed," putting them at the end of the initiative order (so thhe full initiative progression is A,K,Q,J,10-2,rJ,rQ,rK; for reasons stated below, reversed-Ace isn't used).

      The possible opportunites are:
      Jack = Double movement rate for the round, or take a +2 bonus to saving throws and Armor Class
      Queen = take a +1 bonus to a skill roll or a +2 bonus to an attack roll
      King = Take a bonus action
      Ace = take any of the J,Q,K oppurtunities, or skip the round entirely to catch their breath and recover HP equal to 1HD+level.

      [at least those're the Oppurtunities I generally use; they can be swapped for others more appropriate to the current battlefield/situation]

      Jokers cause chaos. When one is played, the player immediately draws another card to use as their initiative (so playing a joker can be a gamble to get a high card). However, the GM gets to mandate some unexpected complication for the character. A weapon jams, they trip over debris, their compad fritzs out, something like that. If their second card is also a joker, then the complication affects the entire battlefield and everyone on it. A stray bullet punctures the hull, sparks flash from a severed power main, a dust devil kicks up sand, etc.

      As said, I like this approach because it creates the feeling of nuanced battlefield, with potential openings and changing conditions, without requiring any pre-scripting or mapping.

      \n I'd like to highlight something that I may have breezed through about that Initiative system: characters who get multiple cards only get those multiple cards on the first round, but they keep the cards they haven't used yet. Each subsequent round, they and everyone else gets just one. So extra cards, if you warrant any at all, are a resource to be shrewdly used for the entire combat. Some players have asked why they'd bother to use a low card in lieu of a high one, to which I reply that banking the high card gives you the chance to unleash it on the most useful round, rather than taking it when it comes up.

      An interesting side-effect is that characters who get extra cards become a sort of joker-filter, since they have higher odds of drawing them but can choose to use a different card. Thus having a quick combat-savvy fighter in the mix ends up making the fight go smoother for everyone.

      \n Basically I'm just using this thread to keep track of SWN hacks as I think them up. And I'll be running SWN at Boston's Arisia again in January, so I've been mulling more ideas lately.

      First, a minor tweak, codifying something John Harper touched on elsewhere about Backgrounds and Training Packages, that they should count for more than just the specific skills they grant. I'll rule that for any task or challenge which seems within the umbra of the character's Background or Package, they're always considered to have at least Trainee level (0); a soldier may be no doctor, but sure knows damn well how to apply a field dressing. Also, Backgrounds and Trainings can be invoked for contacts when in the right places (the "Circles" mechanic mentioned upthread).

      Next, I'm still not quite there with XP. Most of the system I lay out above I'm still pleased with, but I keep chewing over the rewards-for-cash part. I very much want to encourage players to shwredly consider risk-versus-reward, but cash is a very volatile pay-off. Its easy for characters to salvage thousands of credits from the aftermath of even a modest mission, and as they grow in levels they will, not unreasonably, expect bigger fees for their services / rewards for their heroics (and things like "50,000 unmarked credits in a jump satchel" are very in genre). Looking back in this thread, again, mention of the Lifestyle threshholds has me thinking something might be derived from them. Perhaps evaluate a reward by what level of lifestyle it can support for 30 days, number the levels (Slum = 0, Peerless = 5) and use that as the Base State for the "(Base Stat)squared x 10" to derive XP.
