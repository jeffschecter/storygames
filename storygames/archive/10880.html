\n Heya,

I'm going back through a lot of my old games and editing them. As I do, I'm noticing a trend in my writing. I switch back and forth using "player" and "you" when describing the rules. Let me give you a sample, this is from Cutthroat:

The Rat player also has a special responsibility. After each Raid, the Bikers must brag about what they did. The Rat chooses the best bragger or braggers (explained later in Advancement) and those characters receive a reward. It is a very important job and one you should take seriously. Even though you\u2019ve been kicked around a lot by the other Gang members, the Rat is a very important role to play.

I switch from third person to second person in that paragraph. I don't like that, so I want to make it consistent throughout the book. But there are times when third person seems too formal or distant and other times when second person just isn't specific enough.

So my question is, when reading a game text, what do you prefer? What are some games that managed the benefits of third and second person well? Or, at the very least, explained the rules well.

For people who design games, what are the guidelines you use when writing? Do you use player, you or something else?

Peace,

-Troy

    \n "You" could be the player (in a character creation section) or the GM (in play advice sections). "The player" would only be used in GM-facing sections, and only after a particular one has already been introduced. Beyond that and a few other basics, I don't have "guidelines," per se; but I do try to enforce a consistency of voice. The key is to establish direct audience (who is likely to read this to USE it, right then and there) and then be careful when shifting between imperative, procedural statements to general, explanatory statements.

    You could rewrite it as "You, as the Rat player, also have a special..." and solve that particular paragraph--establish the direct audience ("You, the Rat player,") but then safely go on to third person, in describing what any and all Rat players do (choosing and rewarding). I suspect that is why you shifted person: you were elbow deep in second-person, direct instruction (which is FINE) but then you wanted to be able to make a generalized statement, which drew you to use third person.

    \n If one is uncomfortable using a proper, neuter, third-person-singular pronoun, one should avoid it. Also don't forget "it," which works quite well in reference to a character of indefinite gender, but not so well with players who presumably at least present a gender. In such instances, I am not afraid to say "he or she" and move on. Another useful convention is to just use "he" or "she" throughout and apologize to the radical feminists or affronted misogynists in the front matter. But no apology for "they" as singular will sooth exacting editors. And, hey, it takes maybe ten minutes to do a selective search and replace on "they" with "he or she," so what's the problem with being correct? Author using whatever crap-ass Engrish gets your ideas down; edit with an eye to being seen as a professional with a command of the language.

    I keep a strong divide between the character and the player, in writing rules; but that's in part because I am writing LARP rules, where YOU actually do stuff, AS your character. I swing a padded sword: YOU get hit, your character gets damaged. You throw a packet when your character uses a Ranged Ability like a spell. Your character doesn't throw a packet; and you are not casting a spell. It's not very hard to maintain, and I think it makes me stay more focused on separating procedural instructions from flavor text or explanatory stuff. YMMV.

    Yes, I'm a grammar Nazi. I'm paid quite well to be one, in fact, amongst other useful skills.

    \n I looked this shit up.

    English has never had a gender-ambiguous singular pronoun, and there have always been occasions where one would be useful. English speakers and -writers have been improvising solutions for as long as, y'know, English. "They" has always been a common solution. Probably the most common overall. It's partly because we also use "they" painlessly as a number-ambiguous pronoun: "at the beginning of the fight, one or more of the players should roll their dice."

    In the 19th Century, a bunch of guys decided to establish "he" as the standard acceptable gender-neutral singular pronoun. They thought that it was about time that English had one. Only one, no more improvising, and they thought that "he" should be it. "He," they figured, would be correct far more often than "they" or any other pronoun would be. They were wrong, but they were partly successful anyway, as we see.

    The Oxford Guide to English Usage, where I looked this up, doesn't hold strictly with those 19th Century guys, and good on it. It considers "they" perfectly acceptable, with as venerable a history and more robust a tradition. It doesn't stop there, though. It goes on to say that correctness schmorrectness, what you need to do as a writer is write good sentences. All of the available solutions are ugly and wrong, because English doesn't have a gender-ambiguous singular pronoun, nothing to be done about that. All you can do is choose the one, case by case, that least fucks up this particular sentence, so suggests the Oxford. "When a pirate is unarmed, reduce his profile to 1," but "every parent should be proud of their child."

    I almost never use "they," because I think it's ugly. Maybe twice in the last 5 years. I'll rewrite a paragraph to avoid it. But I'm like the protestant in The Meaning of Life. I could if I wanted to!

    -Vincent

    \nThe problem is not whether it is correct or not. Some people are against the use of a singular 'they' (because they were taught it was wrong), although due to its prevalence in spoken language most people will not notice a singular they until it's pointed out. Similarly, some people think that 'he' cannot be gender neutral (because 'he' can also be gender specific), and may become offended when they read it.

    Tactically, it seems better, between these two choices, to use the singular 'they'. Those who are against 'they', if they notice it, will only say that you have bad grammar. Those who are against 'he', when they notice it, will say you are a bad person.

    You choose the words to get your point across. If your reader is distracted by the whole he/she/they debacle, then it will limit their understanding. See you probably didn't even notice.

    \n If I want to offend people, I'd like to do it with the text, not grammar. This means our house grammar style has to be seamless to both the old-fashioned and new-fangled. We avoid the use of "he or she" by the use of the plural they, and by naming specific people with specific genders, and making sure we have a balance of each gender, not in gender-stereotyped roles. We have also used the "GM is female, players are male" approach. We do distinguish carefully between players and characters, even though I'm well aware gamers don't do this at the table.
    \n
    \nSo, when considering grammar, I think "Will this irritate a proportion of our audience. Can I reword it so that it is not clumsy, but does not irritate them?" Jane Austen and Shakespeare used the singular they, but I can't, because, as we've seen, it drives people to paroxysms of fury.
    \n
    \nWhen we were producing one ProFantasy product, I had to talk down a group of three writers and a female editor who were very insistent on using "he" with the usual weasely disclaimer. It was hard work, but worth it.
