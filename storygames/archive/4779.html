    \n It's kind of a false dichotomy. I'm a full time lawyer, so it's definitely not my primary business. Moreover, I operate with the goal of breaking even on any product (thought that's hard to do in its own right, if it's your goal). I have no (immediate) designs on publishing products for the primary purpose of making money. I do not publish for the purpose of seeing my name on a product (though I admit that's a nice feeling on some level--silly, though--all it takes is Adobe Acrobat and a dream). I am trying to build a brand though. Mostly I publish because I enjoy the design process and the, when I've finished something, I understand I can bring joy and entertainment to others by publishing it rather than letting it sit on my hard drive.

    So, am I hobby or business? Little bit of both I would say.

    \n I always wanted to do it full time. I just had to do it part time from about 1996 to 2004 because it took us that long to generate a stable and large enough income stream to make that possible for us.
    \n
    \nIt makes for different considerations when you have to generate full time income levels from your designs, but its not like you have to sell out on your designs to make it possible. A large and steady back catalog with a means to sell them and healthy profit margins makes it doable.
    \n
    \nPlus you get to consider all the various synergies you might never have considered if you stuck purely to design for design's sake. For instance, leveraging board game designs for use both board game production and computer game production. Leveraging an game world IP for publication in print, PDF, online subscriptions, for free online content that is ad driven, and for use in fiction. Being able to cross certain boundaries with your ideas can really give them the potential to fly further than otherwise.
    \n
    \nNow, myabe, back when I first started publishing, I would have nver thought that some of things we do when running the business would also have turned out fun. Things besides just game design. For instance, I tend to like the creative process of applying our print and bindery machines to make our own goods. I like marketing. And I enjoy strategic business management. When I started the Guild I only knew I loved game design and knew not a lick about those other things.
    \n
    \nNext on our plate is to continue evolving our company to give us more opportunities to spread our game ideas further. Selling games into dollar stores, continuing to develop our online game adaptations and the business around those, devoping a multi platform RPG, and this winter it looks like we'll be opening our second retail store. The first one we ran back in 1999-2000 as a sort of test store experiment but did not continue with it. The new one will be a hybred store between print/copy/binding services and a game store. Its what makes sense for us at this time, to move the company out of our collective basements and garages. But it is also another test store format to see if this model would be viable to duplicate in other markets. I think it seems very viable because it generates revenues from four strems instead of just games and it lets us focus on brand building (ours and select other small publishers) rather than just stocking whatever the hot collectible of the day is (in fact, we intend to stock and sell very little top tier merchandise). With the continued growth of PDFs as a game format and means of distribution, there is an obvious an growing synergy there as well.
    \n
    \nMoves like this take a lot of pre planning and thought, but boy, they are exciting to implement as well.

    Posted By: guildofblades>>He feels that these amateurs burning through their time and money just end up saturating the market with crappy products,<<

    That's Crap. I personally believe "industry experience" has very little to do with the ability to design a god game. It may have a great deal to do weather its packaged all pretty like and if the person can turn a good dime or two selling it, get it out there and known to enough people to matter, etc, but some of the absolute best games have been designed by people never published before. In some cases that can even be best because they aren't tied to existing industry "standards" with regards to what is and is not commonly accepted design ideas. No one has beat them down yet on new ideas telling them they aren't </p>
    \nYou missed the point. I wasn't talking about industry experience when I met Ryan and said what he is paraphrasing. I was talking about expected income from profit against time spent. It doesn't matter if you've been designing for a long time or not. A game designed with no particular expectation of compensation (or an expectation that is excessively low) depresses the standard of practice for the sake of slaking an ego and burdens retail and distribution with indexing or choosing between too many products. For instance, a few back of the matchbook calculations tell me that when you divide the electronic marketshare by the number of publishers you get, well, shit -- and that's before ceding at least half of it to leaders like WotC/WW/etc.

    This affects the value people place on creative work, promoting relationships and payment levels I consider to be exploitation. People want to keep costs down to such a degree that the community expects creative workers to screw themselves over. I don't think it's any coincididence that indie publishing dogma celebrates the person ponying up the dough as the creator, no matter the work s/he actually does. It's a convenient ideology when you want to pay artists, editors and even writers/designers crap because you can claim you're the *true* creator, and all of those other guys are mere instruments.

    I think it's also has a bad effect on the community, as the rush to publish for compensation has helped poleaxe the previously thriving free games community. This isn't the only factor though; the shift from the static web also had an effect. For instance, much of what I've released for money myself is actually available for free in alpha versions, but it's hard to find, since it's attached to blog entries. Nevertheless, free original RPGs have kind of withered. When you go to indices you find a lot of dead links and stuff that hasn't been updated in a long time. People who republish free versions of their stuff are helping to a certain extent, but I've found in many cases they attach licenses that effectively discourage informal participation or they put them up in a non-portable format, like multiple wiki pages. Again, part of this is that the nature of the web has changed, but part of it comes from the fallacy of looking at *everything* as a potential commercial endeavour.

    I prefer to do work that's socially responsible when it comes to the labour and consumer sides of things. It means that I produce things that I expect will make enough money to justify my working day and help support my family. It means that I pay partners so that *they* will have the same expectations and they're not just doing it for ephemeral, bullshit reasons like increasing their reputation in some subculture. Finally, it means that when I don't have those expectations it turns into a blog post or a PDF.

    It's not when it's paid, it's how much.

    One of the things I am absolutely adamant about with MUM is that the endeavor either be worthwhile in terms of absolute dollars and cents and not merely from buddies feeling good helping buddies, or involve proportional risk and reward for all participants. With Leo Lingas, I negotiated a cheaper rate with him, but I thought better of it and paid him his standard. With Chris Huth, I reassessed his likely labour after the initial estimate and paid more than contracted, because it was the right thing to do. Oh, I felt a bit of a *sting* as it bit into my bottom line, but I like being able to say that when you buy Aeternal Legends, you're getting the fruits of labour that's been well-valued.

    By contrast, I've encountered a couple of occasions where I was told that I could get everything taken care of gratis or for next to nothing, "because the indie scene is so happy to help people out." I don't think there's anything wrong with this enthusiasm and mutual aid. I merely think it's incompatible with an end result that pays the publisher above a reasonable threshold and the artist (or whatever) less.

    If you want a specific example, look no further than Adept Press. The passive-aggressive suggestion of 20 bucks or less per illustration sucks. The "do as much as you feel like" rhetorical dodge, combined with Edwards' influence on the scene . . . not good.

    This is not a problem with indie games alone. There are companies that make plenty of money on the backs of freelance writers that make a penny a word or less. With larger companies, there's also an unequal balance of power, but in many cases the creative workers make a decent amount of money. I don't like that imbalance, but it's the way business works. But I absolutely draw the line at the point where the worker can't even claim a decent amount of money from the experience.

    With specific reference to the current of ideas in indie gaming, I think the emphasis on a single, auteur-like creator is specifically damaging. I see lost of people boasting about the money they've made, how going small and part time allowed them to realize their visions. I don't see anybody boasting about the money they've made for *other people*, how they're running things so solidly they can do well by artists and editors.

    It's not easy. I know that some people promise the world and then *don't* pay, and that sucks, so it's better to promise what you can afford instead, but if you can't afford something that equates to paying a reasonable amount for someone's time, that still sucks.

    \n Malcolm, I don't think Ron is necessarily an example of the typical artist-designer relationship in indie games. Generally speaking, I contact artists that generally work outside the roleplaying and fantasy illustration market, ask them what a given project will cost, and pay them whatever they ask. I have never negotiated prices with an artist. I totally agree that people volunteering work for free is a nice gesture, but bad for business and properly valuing their contributions, so I really hope nobody takes anyone up on that.

    Many small press d20 publishers operate in the way you describe, paying small amounts for writing and art and having most of the money channeled to whoever is organizing the projects. I know of very few non-d20 small press publishers on Story Games or in the wider post-Forge community that operate that way. Among almost all of IPR's clients, for example, books are the result of a single writer with no additional writing freelancers. The Burning Wheel folks are a bit different in this regard, but I don't know enough about their operation to speculate. I find it hard to imagine that Luke is anything less than a complete professional, however. Often times, writers will also do their own editing and layout (see Matt Snyder, Luke Crane, Joshua Newman, etc.). Sometimes, they will work with other indie game designers who are hired to provide editing (Rich Forest has done a bunch) or layout services (Matt, Joshua, and Luke have done a fair number of books, as has Jennifer Rodgers, a go-to illustrator and designer for at least a dozen indie games). When supporting other indie designers, it's true that Matt may or may not choose to charge less than market price for his services (Matt?), but maybe he can speak more to that. Jennifer is in the process, I think, of building a great career as a professional designer and illustrator, so I hope she's charging fair amounts for her magnificent work, since she deserves to do really well. Artwork policies vary from book to book, but generally artists are paid adequately and up-front, I think. Sometimes non-traditional illustration methods are used, of course, from public domain images (Steal Away Jordan) to strange artwork (Polaris) to using stock photography (cheap!) to having no art at all.

    That's my experience of the "Forge influenced" (note, not just "Ron-influenced," though he's clearly a part of that) small press publishing model, which is why I find it hard to reconcile that with the picture that you're painting.
