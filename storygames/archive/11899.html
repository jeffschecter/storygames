\n 'What's your Tenacity?' I asked a player in Cthulhutech in the process of becoming a tager.

'7,' he said, just as another player said: 'You can't answer him'.

(You need an 8 to successfully complete the tager ritual).

Even as a twelve-year-old, I knew you didn't share mechanical details with other players. Your character wouldn't know, so why should you?

But in most story games, I'd happily share that information player-to-player. If a character is heading towards horrific failure in the tager ritual, I'd expect the player and the group to know it and welcome it.

My story gaming instinct arose in other circumstances too. I was reluctant to spend drama points, because failing was more interesting than succeeding. But because of Cthulhutech's play style, that hesitation let down my team.

So my question to you is, do you play like a story gamer in traditional games? Do you have to consciously resist the urge to do so? Or do you embrace it?

    \nSay what now?

    Even as a twelve-year-old, I knew that talking about how low your THAC0 was or how many plusses your sword had was part of gaming. You're playing the wizard, you talk about what your spell choices are and the new cheesy technique you thought up for using Levitate as an offensive spell. You're playing Cyberpunk, you talk about how much damage your SMG does and how your cybereyes let you see a fly half a mile away in pitch darkness. You talk about how many dots of Celerity you have. You compare your PC's skill bonuses with the other players so that you all know who's going to be making the roll to investigate the crime scene or talk the jumper off the ledge or whatever.

    In the "traditional games" of my youth, you shared mechanical details all the damn time...because mechanical details are fun, because sharing them is part of the experience, because it's a point of comparison, because it's a convenient conversational hook to hang your discussion of the game on, because it helps you learn how the system works, because you're sharing best practices. Plus, you've got a shitload more mechanical details to talk about than you would in, say, Primetime Adventures or IAWA or whatever.

    I'm honestly baffled that your upbringing was so utterly alien. Just what the heck was going on in your 6th grade class, anyway?

    \n Games for me are systematic, enclosed and coherent universes. I like it when I know how a given game works, and I approach each game from a clean table without bringing in any preconceptions. This is methodology learned from having my trad experience overturned by the Forge a few years (damn close to a decade, actually) ago, basically, after which I learned to look at games from ground up instead of just a superficial view. I don't like games that resist this sort of comprehensive analysis by being incomplete or incoherent; I need to craft my own theory as to which of the possible complete games implied by the text I'm going to execute before I can play a game like that. For complex games this is often a process that might take actual play to complete, but usually I know after a session or two at the latest what I want to do with a given game, if anything. After that it's all just execution.

    The above means that just like I don't really have a problem with bringing trad assumptions to indie games, I don't have indie assumptions in trad games, or in OSR games or in anything in general. I just try to read each game as its own thing and do what it tells me to do, interpreting as necessary. It's notable that this doesn't mean that I can't benefit from using a wide variety of techniques; many games involve wide leeway in player or GM roles and the tools used in executing them, for which reason you can also import tools and technique from one game to another. A classical example is importing explicit conflict resolution via task resolution; it works in almost all skill based task resolution games. Open OOC, relationship mapping, bangs, scene framing... there's a long list of techniques that are actively beneficial for many games that never mention such things themselves (often because the game writing paradigm of the time didn't involve real and useful GMing advice, note).

    One thing about importing technique between traditions that I find annoying is when people treat games interchangeably as identical with each other. If this technique was a good idea in one game, it's going to be a good idea in every game, amirite? I actually bitched about this at my blog a while back, writing about misapplied narration sharing that seems to come up now and then. Being used to playing in a specific way is a poor excuse for drifting games in my experience, not the least because that sort of inflexibility when taken to extremes can make you pretty poor company at a mixed table of play when you end up playing a game that doesn't work like your only number. I'm reminded of how one in ten roleplayers seem constitutionally unable to play Zombie Cinema, a game that otherwise has a pretty good track record; they just don't have the tools for shifting out of their usual perspective, so they play every game like it was GURPS (seems to correlate ).

    \n In many editions of D&D;, the Dungeon Master Guide is totally a Story Game*.
    \n
    \nBut the various Player Handbooks and modules described different styles of play that sometimes contradicted or didn't support the advice in the DMG. And often people who play Story Games are people who GM Trad games.
    \n
    \nOf course there are many reasons for this, people who are attracted to the GM role may be attracted to Story Games. They are likely more exposed to gaming in general, doing more research online and thus have more of an opportunity to experience different styles. Many of the GMs I know tend to go to more conventions, and play with more people. And when they aren't GMing, they may want to play games that give them part Trad GM authority even in a player role.
    \n
    \nAs someone who GMs a lot (easily 1-3 times a week, for 50+ different people a year) GMing has made it difficult for me to just be a player. When I play, I much prefer Story Games. As a player, I want to be able to describe my success or failure, I want to scene frame where appropriate, I want to talk out of character when clear communication is necessary, I want failure to push the game forward, I don't want to have to find the fun or read the GM's mind to figure out what to do next, I want my character details to be important to the plot, and I want equal spotlight.
    \n
    \nThe Dungeon Master Guide has advice to incorporate these styles of play into Trad games like D&D.; But procedurally they aren't hard coded into the main system or supported by examples in many popular modules. There is nothing stopping you from incorporating these elements. And there is advice on how to do so. But their inclusion is unreliable and group specific. Which is why this becomes a big issue at conventions playing with strangers. I won't play D&D; at a con. It's too random what play style you will get. But it's less an issue when I play with people I know. If our play styles mesh or if I know people are flexible, I'll play any game with them.
    \n
    \n*Many people define all RPGs as being Story Games. But in this thread, I'm using the definition of "Story Game" implied in the first post.

    Posted By: Bret GillanYou've mentioned your approach of playing each game as true to itself as possible before and I've been interested by it. But it seems to me that some of your techniques are actually house rules. When you say 'explicit conflict resolution via task resolution', can you explain what you mean? If I understand you correctly, that sounds like you're deviating substantially from the rules of most games (what I'm imagining is negotiating the stakes between player and GM).
    \nJoshua touched on this in passing: many story game techniques are not incompatible with traditional games. Conflict resolution is one such example: the majority of traditional game design does not prevent you from using the task resolution system to resolve conflicts; in fact, I expect that even if the texts are woefully deficient in enforcing it, many of these designers would have expected you to do exactly that. I basically base this supposition on the fact that there are just two common and intuitive ways of using task resolution systems:\n
      \n
    • The GM calls for a task resolution check with the full intent to have this matter and a firm understanding of what are the consequences of victory or loss. This is conflict resolution, it just happens to resolve the conflict by consulting a specific task resolution, or perhaps a string of such. Many traditional games basically assume that you're going to play like this; I can't name any specific game that would've expounded on the importance of fairness in task resolution in length, but that's because it's sort of obvious for anybody immersed in playing like that to begin with.
      • \n
    • The GM calls for task resolutions as a matter of pacing, illusionism and giving the players something to do. The results of the check are used according to GM whim and the situation: the task resolution is called according to objective criteria ("whenever the character does something"), but the outcome only influences the events insofar as the GM can still narrate the game to remain within his design spec for the scenario. This isn't explicit conflict resolution (conflicts still get resolved, that dice rolling just has little to do with it), but it certainly is common advice in mainstream games, especially after 1990. Basically any game that characterizes the GM as a storyteller and mandates him to entertain the whole group of players advices you to play like this.
      • \n
    \nIt is notable that the two attitudes above are just methodical advice for the GM in mainstream games; there is no game system element to it, both of those attitudes can be accomplished without deviating from the procedures and mechanics of the GM role. Specifically, you can easily do conflict resolution in traditional games: some of them actively assume it (I think - as I mention above, I suspect that any game that doesn't specifically advocate GM cheating is really just assuming effective task/conflict conflation), while others don't, but they can still be turned around simply by disregarding the GMing advice and reinventing the means you use to fulfill the duty. I don't think that the latter is changing the game; the GM has a degree of freedom in his choices just like the players do, and if a given GM decides that he's not going to cheat on the dice rolls, it'd be pretty far-fetched to decide that he's no longer playing the same game.

    As a simple example, I noticed while reading the newest edition of the Warhammer RPG (the boardgamey one) that the GMing advice in the GM's book is firmly illusionist: according to the book your job is to provide a Warhammer experience, and you do it by cheating on the dice and doing whatever else it takes to create a specific play experience for the players. Considering that the game also includes a seemingly complete and functional game, should I consider that verbiage in the GM's book to be systematic, or merely advice? It seems to me that many ignore that text and still "play Warhammer", so perhaps it's more along the lines of advice: this is the way the designer recommends for you to fulfill your GM role, but if you can hack it without cheating, that's an option, too. Interpreting that text as mandatory makes the game's design incoherent (for one, it requires you to break the rules it presents), so even if I consider the GM's book as authoritative as any procedural rules in the game, then I still have to choose which rules I'm going to follow in playing the game. If I decide to accord more importance to the rules everybody uses while only paying lip service to the rules that only concern the GM, I don't think that that's something you could call a house rule - I'm just doing my best to understand the game text and implement a coherent game out of it; it's not the only coherent alternative reading, but it's an option.

    The above explains my justification for seeing conflict resolution in a game like MERP or D&D or such, I think. If you're wondering about my use of the word "explicit", then consider this: just as the rules of most games do not mandate you to hide your character sheet from the other players, likewise they do not say that the GM should hide the reasons for dice rolling. In the vast majority of traditional games it is entirely reasonable to go into a dice roll by saying that "Bob, it seems to me that your captive might get away here. Do by all means make a knot-tying skill check to see if he might've escaped into the night while you were asleep." That's explicit conflict resolution, no doubt about it. Going explicit is usually a good idea because the game is more fun when the other players can enjoy what is going on as well when you demand for dicing, and it also allows the other players to interrupt and give input into your conflict pacing choices. Finally, being explicit about why you're rolling the dice is useful because it keeps the GM honest and prevents him from accidentally shifting the goal posts once he's seen the outcome. I have this habit in playing D&D that I forbid the players from rolling the dice before I've spoken the target number out loud; I don't necessarily tell them in D&D why they're rolling, but I insist that the dice roll doesn't count if I see the result before I've spoken a string of rules-legalese against which it'll be compared. That's explicitness in action, but it's still not a game-system issue - I'm just describing GMing technique here, not mandating procedures for how the game is played. Traditional games just have such wide leeway in how you can do the GMing that you can import many, many techniques without changing the system of the game one bit. That's even a reasonable description of traditional game design: you need to have a big, fat lacuna in the place where the game might be expected to tell you specifically what to do to achieve the adventure, just so every GM can slot in their own approach.

    \n Yeah, while I agree with JD and walkerp, it looks like the OP was setting up a discussion of this sort of (for every word in this sentence, put an "asterisk" and "for lack of a better word") "story games playing in a classic/traditional environment, where your style clashed with the other players".

    Kind of an interesting topic. My regular gaming group spends about half our time playing the New Hippie Shit, and about half our time playing Shelf Classics with All Those Story Games Tools to Get to the Point (Castle Falkenstein, L5R, with Blue Planet and Vampire somewhere on the horizon). So for us, we do That with This and have awesome success.

    But failure stories are fun, too. I don't have any that I can easily recall. As a player, I've never been in that sort of culture clash situation.

    As a GM, I DEFINITELY have brought this hippie fire storm down on unsuspecting players. In each case, the results have been positive.

    Quick example:
    Me, GMing D&D 3e a few years back. Alan is playing a rogue. We are in an abandoned, ancient fortress.
    ALAN: I search around the broken, ruined bed frames again.
    (I notice this is the third time he's doing this in about 10 minutes)
    ANDY: OK, noted. You don't find anything.
    ALAN: OK.
    ANDY: BTW, What are you looking for, anyway?
    ALAN: Anything unusual, out of the ordinary.
    ANDY: I mean, you yourself: What are you hoping your character finds?
    ALAN: Well, he's just looking for something unusual.
    ANDY: I get that, but what are YOU having him look for? It sounds like you have something in mind?
    ALAN: (not wanting to step out of the character's pants, after some hemming and hawing) Well, I was thinking that maybe somewhere around here there might be some kind of historic tome or something that explained what happened to the people here.
    (And yes, he would have been totally happy just doing Search rolls, not finding anything, describing searching to no avail, and moving on to do it again 5 minutes later; to him it was simply a question of finding what the GM prepared for him, and not about suggesting things to happen in the game world)
    ANDY: Huh. Interesting idea.

    At this point, I know Alan from experience is big into verisimilitude in the setting, so I don't say, "Oh, great idea! Yeah I'll just make that shit up now!", even though I literally had no such ideas to put a history/past into this quick dungeon. So instead, I come up with some elements in my head, and about two rooms later I have some partially hidden tapestries that show pictures of some sort of giant exodus: Alan feels vindicated in his searching, he gets to see what he really wanted to see, the veil of verisimilitude remains, and I get to use his feedback to make the adventure that much more interesting, and appeal to the players.
    Note that this was something like... 7 years ago? Within one year we had all dropped that veil to get straight to the meat:

    ALAN: I strike up a conversation with that guard. *I'm wondering if he has any opinion on the princess.*
    ANDY: (Aha! He certainly does now!)
    (roleplaying in character between character and NPC commences)

    -Andy

    \n JD's assertion that there is no difference between how his 12 year old self ran D&D and how I run, say, Sorcerer is absurd. So I'm going to answer your question more honestly.

    My answer: Yes. My biggest success was with running 7th Sea. I did have to do a few things.

    1) I hardly ever rolled anything against a static TN. If it was an action that normally required a static TN it was usually something just worth saying yes to. This had a tendency to devalue certain skills. Basically, simply having the skills became narrative justification for taking certain actions and little else so there was no point to advancing them.

    3) By consequence 99% of all rolls were opposed and thus were moments of conflict resolution between characters in motion.

    2) I dumped all the Heroes out of the setting. I told the players that any character in the setting material classified as a Hero exists if you want to take advantage of them as a resource but I as a GM will, never introduce or use them on my own. Interestingly enough, the PCs never once called on them.

    3) I had to completely change the Healing rules. The Healing rules in 7th Sea assume you know what the "Act Structure" of your game looks like. Since it's impossible to discern the act structure of this style of play except in hindsight, I often had to just "eye ball it" which I hated doing but there really was no other way.

    4) The Reputation rules move too slowly relative to the action in the game. When one scene can completely change agenda/allegiance of an NPC to the degree that it became absurd to apply the Villain rules to them or when the Hero because acting on so many complex shades of grey naively referring to them as a Hero was silly. Similarly a minor character would suddenly take on VAST importance such that mechanically treating them as a Brute or Henchman was absurd despite them not accumulating enough XP to promote themselves. In short the whole Brute, Henchman, Hero, Villain distinction simply broke down relative to their emotional value in the fiction.

    5) Even with all that I found myself backed into a lot of mechanical corners. At one point, one of the players took on the Schattenman from Eisen... who has no stats because he's clearly meant to be a GM Fiat machine. I literally had no way to properly adjudicate what should have been a rather tense and complex conflict. It was very frustrating.

    Outside of those kinks and hiccups though the game worked relatively well about 80% of the time. It's just sad that the 20% that failed were often the most emotionally engaging or intense moments.

    Jesse

    Posted By: JDCorleyPretty much my whole history with "traditional gaming" (I still hate this phrase - are we really going to put Toon, Vampire, Champions, D&D, Rifts and GURPS in the same category and pretend we can talk about them fruitfully as if they were one thing?) is story gaming.
    \nIt's an interesting topic, and one I'd like to see have its own thread. For my part, I totally do exactly this, grouping those games together: my understanding of the textual history of rpgs is that there is a discrete tradition beginning with the first games and spanning almost everything written until late '90s, at which point postmodernism hits and people start going back to basics to really critically repurpose the whole exercise. The distinction between traditional and progressive design is important, because the traditional texts rely on a common understanding of prior art - sometimes a mistaken assumption, but often a powerful short-hand when both sides of the communication really do conform to the same assumptions. This is just a historical analysis, not an absolute deductive property of game texts, note: there have been out-of-tradition design in all decades and there still is a lot of traditional design, but I think that it's quite feasible to claim that what once was absolutely dominant (the one tradition) is today much less so. I try to avoid using a term like "story game" in ways that encompass games from both sides of this divide because for most topics it'd just be confusing; the games have so little in common as culture that the similarities are accidental rather than systematic transferrence, as is the case within the tradition.

    In case it interests anybody, my understanding of history is that we took up progressive design largely because '90s traditional game design amply demonstrated to us the limits of the tradition: D&D-derived assumptions about character ownership, character optimization, challenge-gaming, party-based adventuring and other things, while powerful for their own culture, proved a crucial limitation for getting into the pure-bred story game that many of us looked for and had futilely tried to accomplish within the tradition. The tradition had to be broken with to get into design space that couldn't be reached within it.

    (I should note that "the tradition" I discuss above is not the only subculture that could be viewed as a tradition in a historiographical sense. Forge is totally a tradition at this point, transmitting its own verbiage and viewpoints that are useful in understanding Forge-based game texts. Nordics have their own tradition, too, and wargaming has a tradition that sometimes steps into rpg land. D&D is a tradition that can well be interpreted as separate from the generic big tradition, as there are plenty of people who only play D&D and preserve D&D-specific culture; the OSR is a powerful example of a D&D-based tradition, for instance.)

    My own experience in light of the above viewpoints is that it's eminently reasonable to not get stuck on the word "story" and insist that all games with story are "story games" when other people are obviously (obvious to me, anyway) using the term as a vague shorthand for a certain type of progressive gaming. This thread doesn' t even make any sense if you refuse to accept for the sake of discussion this usage - if a traditional game also is a story game as the OP intends, then the question of whether you can use story gaming techniques in trad games is trivial and no discussion is necessary. I do accept that the historical root of the term "story game" is in Andy specifically wanting to focus on story as a goal instead of historical or analytic traits of games - he could have called this the "progressive games forum" or whatever if he wanted to limit it to Forge-style games, that's clear enough. However, others use the term differently, and especially in combination with the term "trad gaming" it only makes sense to understand "story game" as pointing towards "Forge-style progressive school gaming" instead of just vaguely any story-related gaming at all. I don't mean that everybody everywhere forever needs to use the term in this sense (I think that "story game" is very useful as a goal-related term that transcends historical traditions), but in this case the OP wants to speak of tradition-crossing, so insisting that his choice of words doesn't depict any traditions seems obfuscatory.

    \n I agree with both of you, Olman, Jesse: something like "traditional gaming" is necessarily a very high-level perspective that will only make sense among people who have shared some similarities in experience. I might even say that people who choose to use this sort of terminology self-select in a manifold of ways. In this context it's not surprising that the people who are most impressed by the achievements of the Forge culture are often the ones who experienced typical '90s game design as a disappointment. Among this subset of hobbyist it's easy to speak of "traditional gaming" and expect a similar understanding of its strengths and virtues, as they not only share goals for their play, but also share experiences that led them to their current goals. Take that same terminology, however, and try to apply it to somebody who skipped the whole '90s (playing older games all through it, say) or actually prospered as a hobbyist in that culture, then they'll have a quite different understanding of what "traditional" means.     \n To return to Chris' original question (and I will document my assumptions about trad and story games as they come up so we can be clear.)\n
    Posted By: SanglorianSo my question to you is, do you play like a story gamer in traditional games? Do you have to consciously resist the urge to do so? Or do you embrace it?
    \nI DM a 4E game for a bunch of longtime gamers. It is a traditional, strong-DM game in that I'm the guy directing the story, and I have the primary responsibility for antagonization and for introducing complications and bother. And I get to draw all the encounter maps, and no one gets to say what's in them but me.

    I call our style of play 'subway play' (and it might be presumptuous to name it, this might be something everyone does, so I hope I don't sound too high-n-mighty with this.) Our play alternates between encounters ( the subway lines ) and decision points ( the stations ).

    The encounters are railroaded. I'm driving the train at that point, and if the party veers too far away from the material I've prepared, then all bets are off -- we might even have to pause or abort the session to let me regroup. Luckily, the buy-in level at the table seems pretty high, so as far as I can remember this has never happened. Railroading the encounters gives me the chance to really ruminate on them and try to make them as gnarly as I can, without the risk that my prep will be wasted.

    However, when the party leaves the subway line and hits the station, they have decisions to make. I openly announce and solicit the next story-direction from them. At this stage I don't provide any of my own input aside from maybe a menu of choices--which the players are totally free to ignore.

    Where I believe this may contradict the assumptions of 'trad' play: The players' decisions can be for in-character reasons ("my guy would really want to go to the City of Brass and look up his girlfriend there") or for player preference reasons ("Spelljammer killed my father. Don't ever as me to play D&D in space") or for authorial reasons ("It's time for us to finish that story thread and move on, it's getting a bit old, let's tie it up or abandon it.") I don't believe that any one agenda is prioritized above any other agenda at our table. I do believe that some groups would balk at using any but in-character reasons to guide play this way -- I definitely would if I were still a GURPS-playing teenager.

    This mode of play has helped our table, whose members have several different play agendas, to have a successful campaign together. Those who are into challenging encounters get them; those who want to author a shared story get that, those who want to live the dream can do that.

    \n This is a case of unintended consequences and a case of the writers not fully understanding the real-world social impact of that rule. From that textual instruction there is a very natural social progression among the real people. It looks something like this.

    Player A and Player B do something that Player C wants in on or otherwise leverage. However, Player C's character has no knowledge of what Player A and Player B are up to. So Player C being creative and clever person has his character do something that he can provide ample in character justification for that will put him in the fictional position he wants relative to the actions of Player A and Player B. However, the astute and socially observant GM knows full well that Player C is declaring that move specifically because of his knowledge of Player A and Player B's actions.

    So who wins? Player C's reasonable and creative in character justification for the action or the GM's social observation of the OOC reasons Player C has even jumped through those creative hoops in the first place. An unresolvable argument ensues that is no fun and extremely exhausting for the GM especially if Player A and Player B are calling foul on Player C's move.

    Being the social creative police of the group is NO FUN. So the simple most obvious solution is to clamp down and really keep the information secret Make sure that what the CHARACTER knows is EXACTLY the same as what the PLAYER knows such that it becomes IMPOSSIBLE for the player to announce actions for his character that does not stem from direct character knowledge because those things are literally identical.
