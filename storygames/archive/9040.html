    \n Knolls and hillocks are small hills
    Dale is a valley and dell is a small wooded one
    A grove is a smallish stand of trees, usually lacking undergrowth. A copse is usually a small dense stand of trees.
    A hollow is a sunken area.

    Canyons are usually called gorges outside of America, and are sharp valleys usually formed by water, sometimes with a river at the bottom. Confusingly there is also a Canyon Lake Gorge.

    \n I love me a good karst - uneven, rocky terrain created by the differential weathering of soluble rocks. They have adventure-friendly features like underground rivers, cenotes, sinkholes, and caves... sky's the limit! (Cenotes, very deep, rocky, steep-sided holes, usually with water at the bottom, are also a cool thing to have on your map. Bloodthirsty gods might live at the bottom of the deepest ones, accepting sacrifices flung into the darkness.)

    Mountains, just plain mountains, are boring - and rare! Usually, they occur in groups. \n
    • A lone mountain is often a stratovolcano, which offers lots of fun possiblities from a constant run of lava to sudden eruption.
    • Ridges are high connections between two peaks, which can be dramatically exposed if they're above the treeline.
    • A mesa is a mountain with a notably flat top. Maybe you could build a fortress, or even a city, perched on a mesa.
    • Mountains often have foothills, smaller features that rise above the surrounding lowlands. If you're farming in a Mediterranean-climate valley, you might plant grapevines or olives on the foothills that flank it.
    Often, a valley is defined in terms of the mountains around it. The difference between Silicon Valley and the Himalayan Valley of Flowers is pretty significant.

    There are a lot of things going on in "the woods". You can differentiate wooded lands by the kinds of trees and animals and smaller plants that grow in them - any decent nature guide for a local area will give you ideas. \n
    • An old-growth redwood forest in California, besides the obvious unthinkably gigantic trees, has ferns, Douglas iris, Scoliopus lilies, and bush-like big-leaf maples growing sparsely in the damp and foggy needle litter on the forest floor. Very few large animals can find enough food there, since most of the biomass is a hundred feet up in the air, so these forests are surprisingly quiet, with the silence broken by the rustle and twitter of mouse-sized winter wrens.
    • Your forest could be a second-growth (post-logging) redwood forest, with a variety of competing types of trees, all much smaller and closer together, where deer browse on tender shoots and flee from mountain lions, and dozens of species of birds flash through the air.
    • Or it could be an old-growth pine woods, like you'd find in New Jersey. There, a few species of pine grow low and dense, their roots covered by slow-moving acidic water stained red by iron-bearing minerals. The soil is saturated with water and poor in nutrients, and carnivorous pitcher plants rise from the ooze. Bats and small birds feed on the bevy of insects that buzz through the air a few inches above the surface, and silent owls swoop down from time to time to take one of those as a prize.
    • Or it could be an alpine forest of aspen. A hundred thousand identical thin white trunks, crowned with leaves of dusty green on top and silver on the bottom, rise from a carpet of tiny succulent flowers (I think they're called kalanchoe). A ground squirrel chitters at a bear who's climbed the mountain to have at the last blackberries of summer, growing along the cold and fast-flowing stream that carries the snowmelt off the peak. The leaves turn over at noon, silver side up - it's going to rain.
    Never tell your players that their guys are in "a forest." Tell them what kind!

    Clearings can be more than just clearings, too. You can spice up your clearings by talking about why there are no trees in this spot: \n
    • Maybe a giant tree that grew there for centuries has fallen? If the trees are big enough - think giant redwoods - this can be a dramatic clearing in a dense forest.
    • Maybe there is a large outcropping of rock here with little or no soil on top of it, which characters would perceive as anything from a rock formation rising dramatically from the forest floor to (cue your thief or ranger) a change in the soil from soft loam to noisy gravel.
    • Maybe you're right next to a stream, and the soil is too wet and yielding to support trees right at that spot.
    • A really large clearing is probably due to a fire, and there will be (depending on how long ago that was) some burned stumps, mushrooms, and thousands of tiny opportunist plants that need a temporary clearing to get access to the sun, grow, seed, and die.
      • \n
    In any case, clearnings are good places to put altars to sky-gods who must be able to see the services performed to them.

    Use vanilla words like "clearings" when you think it makes things clear to your players. Color can come from the details.

    Saddle - an area of low ground between two hilltops

    \n

    Depression - an area of low ground in the midst of otherwise flat or high ground; may be synonymous with hollow

    \n

    Spur - an area of higher ground, usually leading down from a hilltop between two areas of lower ground

    \n

    Draw - The lower ground between spurs.

    \n

    If you have a ridgeline made up of a series of hilltops, you're very likely to have a series of spurs and draws on either side of the ridgeline.

    \n

    I was taught a method in Basic Training for remembering these features. Make a fist. Each knuckle on the fist is a hilltop. Tracing a line along the knuckles makes a ridgeline. each finger is a spur, and the area between each finger is a draw. The place between two knuckles is a saddle.

    \n

    Cut - Man made area where earth has been cut away, usually for a road or similar feature

    \n

    Fill - Man made area where earth has been built up. Again, often for roads and other such.

    \n

    These aren't the most evocative of terms, but they describe things pretty succinctly.

    \n Hollow has a specific meaning in the Eastern U.S. I don't know if its the same meaning used elsewhere.
    \n
    \nA hollow is a feature distinct to the Appalachian mountains in America, although I suspect you could also find them anywhere you have a very old mountain range eroded down to nubs in a temperate climate with plenty of rain. You'll also find them around the great lakes where glaciers from the last ice age scoured out broader shallower valleys, so I suspect the feature is common in Northern Europe, though almost certainly called something else.
    \n
    \nA hollow is a valley carved by a stream or very small river, which rain wind and a lot of time has eroded into a depression far wider and more gently sloped then a ravine or gorge or other types of river carved channels. Since rivers and streams were the roads of early settlement in Appalachia country, "hollows" were important and distinctive terrain features...saying someone "lived back up the hollow" meant "keep going upstream". In central PA, the term "valley" was used for the big wide spaces in between ridges. "hollow" was often used for the smaller valleys branching off from the the main one, or the ones way way back branching off of those.
    \n
    \nSince, the ground would often be boggy or prone to spring flooding, many such places weren't farmed and left to grow wild so "back in the hollow" also came to mean "the really wild wooded area way back there". Great places for things like poaching, or setting up illegal stills, hiding from the law, throwing underage drinking parties, parking, or storing large quantities of drugs.
    \n
    \nDown south you'll hear it pronounced "holler", at least by older southerners.

    Posted By: David BergDavid,
    Any thoughts on how to define a swale or glen as distinct from some of the other stuff mentioned here?
    \nA swale is a low tract of land, often moist and marshy. Or, if man-made, something like a burm to control runoff. Or, on a beach, a lower area of sand between sand ridges (dune lines?), parallel to the coastline.

    A glen is a valley, typically one that is long, deep, and often glacially U-shaped; or one with a watercourse running through such a valley.
