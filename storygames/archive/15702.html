\n Are you ready to be a Heroine? To travel through dangers untold and hardships unnumbered?

Heroine is a storytelling game for three or more players. One player plays an adolescent girl who is pulled into a strange Other Land filled with magic and danger. The other players take turns Narrating and playing the Heroine's quirky new Companions from the Other Land.

I've made it through the first round with my editor, and I have a playtest packet ready to go. If you are interested, please let me know. I would be particularly glad to get some more women playtesters, but everyone is welcome!

    \n Lisa, the game is one to three sessions long. Every game has an introduction, three acts, and an epilogue, but the group decides ahead of time how long the acts are.

    ivan, yes, the game is gender specific insofar as the stories it emulates are about a heroic young girl. The player of the heroine can be any gender, and as can the players of the other characters.

    Cam,

    \n

    Yes, Labyrinth was definitely an inspiration. The original title was Dangers Untold, Hardships Unnumbered.

    \n

    Jackson,

    \n

    If A is the heroine and B is narrating, then C plays one of the heroine's companions from the fantasy world, (e.g. the Tin Man or the Mad Hatter). C also can spend points to become the narrator. B would then play a different companion.
    \nThe heroine role and and companion role are different sorts of PCs. The narrator is a sort of GM. Part of playtesting is figuring out whether we have narrative control divided up right between the different roles.

    \n

    In terms of your other questions, yes, narrative sequence works roughly the way you describe, though other players have influence. In the playtest version, character creation is pretty free-form, though I am hoping to include Vincent Baker-style lists in the character guides. I would say that the game is inspired my love of literature with adolescent girl protagonists. I have never been an adolescent girl, but I am interested in hearing input on the game from those who are (or were).

    \n

    Finally, gender inequality is not hardwired into the fiction. I considered addressing it in a future supplement. The core themes of the game are bravery, kindness, and cleverness. I believe these are the heroic qualities I admire most in Dorothy Gale, Alice, and Sara from Labyrinth. If you want to think of it this way, the only gender issue directly addressed in the book is that the main character is a girl. As a consequence, the game is agnostic about whether any conflict is combat. Violence is not privileged or excluded by the mechanics.

    I'm not sure my point about gender was clear. I'm trying to say that I hope to emulate certain stories, and in those stories, the heroine doesn't solve problems like Conan or James Bond, or like a D&D "murder hobo," as I've heard PCs colorfully described. These heroic young girls struggle between being childish (afraid, confused, or selfish), and being heroic (brave, kind, and clever). Certainly, there are stories about young boys that address these same issues, but I've decided to focus Heroine on protagonists that are young ladies aged 8 to 18.

    \n

    I'm happy that there are some other games focused on female characters, but it's safe to say that there is no surplus.

    \n We played Heroine today.

    I played with two women in their early 20'es. I played the Heroine, and they took turns playing the Narrator and the Companions.

    The story was about a Bavarian orphaned princess in the early 19th century. About to be wed to some prince against her will. She discovered the Other World behind one of the curtains in her room. There she met a kind witch, a cunning priestess cat, a giant spider, some cleanerbugs, palace guards and finally, the wicked queen. Two chapters per act, prologue and epilogue, made for a two hour game.

    I took some notes during the post game talk:\n
