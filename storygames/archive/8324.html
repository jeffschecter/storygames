\n In the process of writing Diaspora I find that a lot of talk about the reasons behind the rules creeps into the text -- game design notes, as it were, intermingled with other material. This seems valuable to me, because I think understanding why a rule exists (or at least what the intent is) might be a powerful way to clarify the rule itself and to pull disparate tables closer to the intended play. I don't see that a lot in games, though, and I wonder if there is a deliberate effort to excise that? Do we not want to see the man behind the curtain? Or is it just boring?

    \n When writing the rules to a game:

    State the rule in the simplest way possible.

    Give an example of said rule.

    Restate the rule.

    Save commentary for the back of the book, supplementary material or your website. The book is a piece of instructional reference. Commentary hinders reference.

    \n I don't know if it falls under the definition of dicta, but I find that certain types of in-rules commentary are very harmful to the process of local interpretation that's necessary to properly enjoy an RPG. For example, if a game says "If you think the declared action of another player is especially creative, you can spend a stone to increase their chance of success." Is that a rule which I must follow? Or is it advice by the designer suggesting a way that the rules can be applied? What if I think the declared action is creative but I don't want them to succeed?

    Count me as preferring rules which state what must be done; leave the designer's notes and advice for the back of the book.

    \n I find that writing rules for a roleplaying game, a cooperative endeavour, is bullshit. What you are writing is instructions, not rules like one might find in a boardgame.

    Consequently, your priority should not be to make your "rules" clear. Your priority should be to make your intent clear. Players who don't understand why the rules are as they are will misinterpret anyway, as it is pretty difficult to follow instructions you don't understand. People routinely leave rules out of rpgs, and I can't blame them - when even after reading the whole book you don't know what the designer was trying to do, it's pretty easy to just choose your own intent and pick and choose the rules you yourself think will support your goals. Combine this with the fact that historically rpg writers routinely lie to their audience by writing untested rules or rules they themselves wouldn't use or rules that are for different purposes than the other rules in the same book, and it's no wonder if rpgs are difficult to utilize.

    For an example of this phenomenon, there are something like half a dozen roleplaying game designers in Finland. Of these a full third have said in public that they don't actually use the rules of the games they write - they just write the sort of rules they believe people expect and want, but who uses rules in actual play, anyway? When this is the basic level of trust between the author and his audience, how can you expect me to follow any rule without understanding its purpose? The culture of rpg writing has ruined any chance for taking texts at face value.

    Therefore, when writing full-blown rpg prose, I strive to make my intentions clear. If that means commenting on my own rules, so be it. Elliot's example is actually pretty good - how the heck should I know when I should spend a stone to increase another's chance of success, if I don't understand the purpose of the game itself and of this rule? Why should I engage in boardgamey nitpicking of "can" vs. "may" when I don't for a second believe that the author operated on that level? On the other hand, if I understand the purpose, then the rule itself is also obvious. In some games the intent is so simple and obvious that it doesn't have to harped upon through the book, but often rpgs are complex beasts and it doesn't hurt to reinforce and contextualize the material several times through the text.

    \n 1. Why should I play?

    2. What are the stages of play?

    3. What are the core rules in each stage?

    4. What other tools come with the system, and when are they appropriate to use?

    5. How can the system be used to make play fun?

    6. How can the system be abused to make play unfun?

    7. How can the system be used to get unfun play back on track?

    I think these are all must-answer questions. You can start play knowing just the answers to the first three questions, and you won't fully appreciate the other answers till you get a handle on the basics anyway. But still, that's like driving a car knowing only what it's for, how to turn it on, and that the wheel turns, the accelerator goes, and the brake stops. It's going to be one wreck after another until the answers to the other questions are understood.

    (P.S. There should be an RPG about car and sports analogies.)

    My all-time most-hated gnash-my-teeth-every-time-it-happens rules writing thing: Mixing procedural text and color text. The latest offender I read was 3:16, but it happens in pretty much every indie game that I've read. Blame White Wolf, probably; they were/are awful at that. It gets really, really confusing when you use terminology that has specific mechanical meaning, but then turn around and use that word as just color.

    The inevitable result of this is someone posts a rules question and someone says "No man, that paragraph is just color text." And then post another question and get, "RTFM! The text clearly states..." when you thought the referred-to paragraph was just color text.

    Anything anyone can do to clarify that something is instructional and not merely evocative would receive high marks in my book.

    \nThis is generally my position on the matter.

    I'll stake a claim to having a fairly extensive knowledge of Forge theory, which I continue to augment and supplement by reading a variety of game design sources (e.g. the Rules and Play textbook, Second Person, and my recent discovery Gameplaywright.) And it is my ongoing deep desire to let none of these discussions into the text of my games.

    For example, Dirty Secrets play makes extensive use of Lines and Veils. Lines about issues with children are actually hardcoded into the game system, while general Lines and Veils can be addressed through judicious use of Jurisdiction and Appeal. But I never use the term "Lines and Veils" in the text. Nor should I. Those are terms of art that we use to discuss game play and design. Players of my games shouldn't have to learn terms of art or jargon; they should simply be able to play.

    Now, I will say that I distinguish between answering "why?" and answering "how?". I include a sizeable section in Dirty Secrets explaining how to best use the rules to create an enjoyable story. The entire manuscript of A Flower for Mara is one long procedural document, explaining the "how?" step by step. But that's about teaching play, not discussing design. In general, my design rationale never appears in the text of my games.

    I will note that this was commented upon favorably in one review of Dirty Secrets on RPG.net:\n

    One thing I\u2019d like to stress out about the prose is how much it stands on its own. I haven\u2019t read that many \u201cindie\u201d games, but something I noticed in some of the ones I did read is that they seemed to require prior knowledge of Forge theory in order to get the most out of them. Not being a Forge luminary in the least, I was somewhat bummed by this. When I buy a game, I expect it to stand on its own, not require me to read essays and basically buy into a very specific view of roleplaying games in order to understand how they were designed or how to play them. Thankfully, this is not the case here. You can buy Dirty Secrets and understand exactly how it works and what it does without having ever heard of Forge theory.
    \nEmphasis mine.

    So apparently I'm not alone in my opinions on this matter.

    Plus, of course, Luke. ;-)

    Posted By: TonyLBI think that if the rules are clearly enough presented that they can be used as a tool then what I need is not so much explanation as inspiration.
    \nAh, just the words I'm looking for.

    Technical writing and fiction work at cross purposes, which is why they mix so poorly.

    Good technical writing aims to explain, which means it should answer a reader's questions before they're even asked, raise few new questions, and have only one correct interpretation.

    Good fiction aims to inspire, which means it should take its time in answering the reader's questions (the whole issue of 'pacing' doesn't even exist in technical writing), raise many new questions along the way (often the story ends once all the interesting questions are resolved), and have multiple valid interpretations.

    A technical work typically only gets discussed when it's broken, which is ironic because it means only the broken works get any amount of press (his is especially true in software). And unlike a technical work, there is no wrong way to interpret a fictional work. It means what it means to you.

    There is also a third kind of writing that the Devil and the legal profession use, which is neither technical nor fictional. It seems technical at first glance, yet it's often more open to interpretation than even works of fiction. As a rule, an RPG should NEVER be written like a legal document.

    \n It strikes me (and this will bear on RPG design theory, so I anticipate conflict and have my dice ready) that RPGs do not sit firmly in technical space. That is, though they are certainly rules, frequently they are rules that, even when their authors don't intend it, benefit from flexibility in application and even vagueness. User interpretation may make them better games by providing space in which the players dabble in game design -- an admission that game design might be a part of the hobby rather than external to it. If that's the case then perfectly executed technical manuals may fail to deliver game precisely because they admit no confusion, as their perfection is a result of misapprehending the nature of their topic.

    Now, I tinker with everything, so obviously this is me imagining everyone is exactly like me. But most RPG players I interact with do this and do it at a semi-conscious level where rigid uninterpretable rules would be a barrier to tinker (by denying permission, which a vague ruleset grants or even demands). If I want to get that "permission to tinker" explicitly in the rules (and I do), isn't calibrating my document as a technical manual a miss-step?
