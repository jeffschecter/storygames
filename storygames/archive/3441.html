Second order bribery

\n
\n
\n
\n\nTonyLB \n\n\n
\n
\n\nJune 2007 \n edited June 2007 in Play Advice
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n We're all down with Keys, right? If you're not down with Keys go study TSoY. Then come back. We'll be waiting for you.
\n
\nWell, actually, that's a lie. We aren't going to wait. We're going to proceed without you. You're screwed.
\n
\nOkay, so a Key like "Support authority" is a bribe to the player, right? They do this behavior (which is presumably something they chose to be bribed to do) and they get specific tangible benefits: in this case game-mechanical points.
\n
\nBut it's also sorta-kinda a bribe to everyone around that player. If the GM provides an authority figure who could (conceivably) be supported then he is rewarded with attention from that player. He's rewarded by getting the burst of spotlight that comes with being the guy who can hold out the first-order bribe. That bribe to the GM that is encoded in the presence of a bribe he can offer the player? That's a second-order bribe.
\n
\nWith me so far? Okay, let's get funky!
\n
\nI was making the "Obedient" persona for Misery Bubblegum, and I was thinking of something very like the above: Support authority. But, really ... that's a weak-ass second-order bribe. It bribes the GM to introduce authority figures, but there's nothing more specific than that. As long as they exist, the player will support them (whether they need it or not) and the GM gets the attention. It's sort of like giving someone a Key that says "Breathing in and out: 1 xp." It's not that it's too powerful ... it's that it's just not giving any interesting guidance.
\n
\nCheck out how the second-order bribe changes when I replace "Support authority" with "Make excuses for authority figures."
\n
\nIsn't that neat? Suddenly the GM is incentivized to make questionable authority figures, and all the other players are incentivized to question them ... all in order to set up the Obedient player for the chance to cash in on their Key-whatsis by making excuses for those authority figures in response to the questions and complaints levied against them.
\n
\nAnd all from an understanding of second- (and third-) order bribery. Fun, huh?

    \n Bill: The second order effect is, in fact, the other players: They are motivated to question any authority figure, because it gets you to defend that authority figure. They're motivated to question authority figures even if the GM presents them as totally unquestionable. They're motivated to question GOD.
    \n
    \nThey get two rewards: One, you get XPs, which puts the group as a whole one step above the GM (as is often the competitive model). Believe you me, I can say from personal experience that a group as a whole garners a lot more XPs off of Keys when they treat it as a group activity. Second (and more important to my mind) they get your undivided attention the moment they offer you a chance to fulfill your Key.
    \n
    \nNow, yes, they are also motivated to take Keys (like Rebel) that interlock with yours. It both helps them seek the second-order bribe more easily and means that they've got a Key that you're probably feeding them lots of opportunities for. Synergy there.
    \n
    \nThe third order effect is the GM. The GM cannot directly make you defend an authority figure just by introducing a sketchy one. If nobody's attacking, it doesn't matter how vulnerable the authority figure is to attack. Unless, of course, you proactively start defending them even before they're attacked, which would be hilarious (and good strategic play!) But, on the whole, I think that the GM is motivated to introduce questionable authority figures because all the players (you and everyone else) are motivated to interact around them in this particular sequence. He knows that someone will attack the authority figure, and then you'll defend them, and it'll rock. Or if he doesn't realize that, he soon learns.
    \n
    \nIt's like setting up a giant pile of fireworks, handing the GM a match and saying "Well, y'know, you don't have to throw that match in the pile ... I'm just saying, here's a big pile of pretty bangs, here's a match. Do whatever you feel like."

    \n Hmmm. Interesting. I'd always thought of Keys as a means to an end -- specifically, the particular end of 'get XP.'

    Now I'm starting to consider them as a means to a consequence.

    'Support authority' isn't really pregnant with interesting consequences in comparison to 'Make excuses for authority figures', which is just groaning with them.

    Consider one of the old standby keys like Key of Bloodlust ("Your character enjoys overpowering others in combat. Gain 1 XP every time your character defeats someone in battle. Gain 3 XP for defeating someone equal to or more powerful than your character (equal or higher combat skill.) Buyoff: Be defeated in battle.") It implies that the character is going to be dealing with the consequences of beating people up, which can be interesting.

    But I'll take my new-found appreciation for consequences and see if I can't tweak it out a bit.

    Key of the Bully: Your character enjoys overpowering weaklings in combat. Gain 1 XP every time your character defeats someone less powerful (lower combat skill) than your character in battle. Gain 3 XP for defeating someone much less powerful (combat skill half or lower) than your character. Buyoff: Be defeated in battle by someone much less powerful.

    Now we get a character who is running around pummeling little old ladies and mewling orphans. Excellent...

    \n Oh, anyway, back to the conversation.

    One thing that's needed to make this work is for everyone at the table to have full knowledge of the characters and their keys, and likely to have them in front of them at all times. Because, you know, people forget. I know I've forgotten things about my own RPG characters, and of the players in games I'm GMing; I don't even TRY with other folks' characters.

    I've been in a really annoying D&D game, and one thing that annoyed me was that the players hid their character sheets from everyone else. Beyond that, however, D&D games are just so hard to keep in your head because there's so many crunchy bits. Both of these things (player-to-player secrecy, and too much information) suck.

    In Bone White, Blood Red, it worked out well that we could all look around and see each others' character cords (colored beads strung on leather cords) as well as the story cord (the GM's equivalent of a story cord).

    It occurs to me that I've almost never seen a character sheet for a roleplaying game which includes spaces for other people's characters on yours. But it seems to me that if you're using a sheet (and not, say, a spread of cards in front of each players, or beads, etc.), it actually makes a LOT of sense to have a section which lists the keys/aspects/skills/whatever for your companions, because in mature groups that will definitely aid in the process of group cooperation and player-to-player "second order bribery" as Tony describes it here.
