    \n I'm not sure I'm *100%* clear on your question - are you basically asking how to deal with player declarations that seem to be outside of the guidelines for creating NPCs and threats?

    If so, here's how I would handle the kinds of scenarios you suggest. You may want to handle them differently, it's more of an art than a science. I'm assuming you're in a level 1 chapter.

    For the two orcs, yeah, I'd just stat up two level 1 orcs. If that's too easy, well, the PC was pretty much asking for an easy challenge.

    For 50 orcs, I would avoid making 50 super-weak orcs. I might say, "OK, so the main group of orcs seems to be upping stakes and moving somewhere! They send a squad of six orcs to slow you down while they continue on their way!" So then they fight six orcs and we've added something weird and new - the orc pilgrimage - to the fiction.

    For a dragon... that's a bit tough. With the group I ran Donjon for, I might just ask, "Really? A dragon? Like, not a dragonling or something?" Remember that this doesn't have to be a guessing game - see the example on p. 47 (at least of my copy) of the GM and a player negotiating over whether a trapdoor in a swamp makes sense. You can ask them if they really want there to be a dragon, and talk about the implications.

    If they said, yeah, I might decide they asked for it and give them a dragon of some serious power level, regardless of the rules. If I didn't feel that was appropriate, then I might try something like:
    - "The dragon would torch you if you tried to fight him. But he wants to play a game of riddles!" Stat up a riddle contest as a level 2 thing.
    - "The dragon is so huge only his left claw fits through the doorway!" Stat up "the dragon's claw" as a level 2 monster - if you defeat it, the dragon flies away holding its bleeding hand and hates you for later.
    - The numbers are all relative. Who says level 2 is weak? Maybe level 2 is enough to level a whole village, and level 10 monsters are, like, dragon gods. Think of the difference between the power of a level 2 D&D4E PC vs. a level 2 Old-School D&D PC.

    But in general, it might help to explain that, in a game like Donjon, you and the players are *collaborating* to create appropriate challenges. If the players want to break the game, they can - at least certainly to the point of making the game disjointed or un-fun. If they're throwing "50 orcs!" at you as a sort of stump-the-GM move, it's not likely to work very well. They should be thinking, do we want dragons to be level 2 threats? Does that kind of fiction make sense to us? Is it fun? Because they have as much say over whether or not a level 2 dragon is ridiculous as you do.

    \n Monster Encounters should be at the appropriate level for the DonJon. If a player wants to "see" a Dragon, that's perfectly fine. They "saw" it.

    Now the GM has the power, the potential, and the responsibility to take what the players declare and twist it. Maybe it's a Dragon that has minions, and the PCs fight the minions now. Maybe it's an illusion of a Dragon cast by that sensible little Level 2 Kobold. Maybe it's a Hex meant to scare people away from some treasure. Maybe it's a beautiful young woman dressed in a Dragon suit for some naughty cos-play. Who knows? The GM.

    It's hard to play DonJon if you take things too literally.
