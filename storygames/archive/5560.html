    \n Things I Liked: Fast-paced play, lots of cool narration, a good way to get a Con started. Manages to create a semblance of a narrative. I really like how your fears become positive resources when you confront them, and how choosing NOT to confront them flavors the narration of the game. I love that the character sheet is a clock, and really guides play one you grok its design.
    \n
    \nThings I Kinda Liked: Gamey-game mechanics make planning your moves important. Taking risks is fun and carries real danger (you might get knocked out of the game).
    \n
    \nThings That Would Keep Me From Playing Again (this is long, but overall I have a positive view of the game):
    \n1. Creative disconnect lead to some hard shut-downs of player input (the Squid argument "The Squid is actually a cultist in a squid suit, right?" felt much more snippy at the table than it does in the Podcast). This was viewed as a problem by everyone at the table, and was discussed a lot in the post-game. So no, there are no tone reinforcement rules right now, but I would be surprised if there weren't any by the time the game was complete.
    \n
    \n2. I literally had no interaction with about 60% of people at the table. Even in a large game of the Roach I'd have a scene or two with pretty much everyone, getting into their gaming brains. In this I pretty much only interacted with Rob and the guy playing Bob the Hobo. That meant I was disconnected and vaguely uninterested with around half the people at the table. Toward the end, as people got knocked out, this changed a bit for the people still playing, but still, I would like to see ways to force interaction across the table, as I felt like there were a couple different 'zones' of play that weren't interacting all that much.
    \n
    \n3. Getting knocked out sucks. You lose half your game, and if you keep narrating for the person you were narrating for you TAKE half of the game from the person to your right (because they're no longer narrating for you). Narrative power is a super-important, super-limited resource in the game (for engagement, if nothing else), and it felt weird that Rob wasn't really narrating for Bob the Hobo even though we'd agreed to share those priveleges. I think this was because I was still in Bob the Hobo's physical proximity and I had been sharing his playspace in the game up to that point. This plays again into the isolation of players from each other, as well as the idea of tonal compatability. Until you're forced to, what the other players are doing simply doesn't effect you that much, due to the varied Fears and ideas people have about what's appropriate to the game as set.

    Posted By: renatoramMaybe instead of sequentially moving there could be some kind of effects that trigger a "jump X player seats, that player is the one framing your next scene". Even a simple d4 could do the trick, I guess.
    \nThere are a few problems with any sort of "skip a player/reverse the flow" elements:

    - As a time-pressured game, there's value to only being obligated to keep tabs on what's going on for you and one other consistent character (the dude to your left). I'd be worried about introducing too many moving parts; the game tries to ride on a knifes' edge of making you feel overwhelmed enough that your adrenal system kicks in, but not so overwhelmed that it becomes paralytic to taking action.

    - The person who gets skipped, or the half of the table that gets ignored by a reversal of flow, could get really screwed -- not getting a turn for much longer, in which case the problems folks are trying to solve here are exacerbated for them: mutely sitting there accumulating doom tokens.

    I'd also observe:

    - The time period of "sitting out" is meant to give you a chance to relax and just be the audience for a bit. (I should note I've seen folks come along and simply watch an EoD! game from end to end and not actually sit down & play.)

    - Folks who escape do have an (optional rule) that lets them come back in and basically sacrifice themselves to give someone else a better chance of making it out alive. (They come back in at 10 doom, but give one person +2 escape, or everyone +1 escape, something like that; done at the "right time", this could also add a minute to the clock, which might help someone. This also might work better by putting them at 10 pain & panic instead of 10 doom, which would simply make most kinds of failure deadly.)

    - The above suggests that some sort of interesting "reincorporation" of folks who've died would be an interesting way to go. I haven't figured out what to do there, though.

    - The game doesn't run much longer than 2 hours in most configurations. So the idea that dead means you're out has never struck me as *that* much of a problem.

    That said!

    Variants I'd be interested in trying would be to allow folks to increase their doom by one in order to change the flow of the game. You could increase your doom by one to say:

    "Skip!" Instead of framing the scene for the guy to your left, you frame the scene for the guy to *his* left.

    "Reverse!" If the flow of play was clockwise, it's now counterclockwise; if counterclockwise, it's now clockwise.

    The one problem with the above is that it's fairly abusable; two colluding players could really screw everyone else by doing a "low push, so I bleed off doom rapidly, but I spend doom to do a ping-pong Reverse! move with the other guy". But assuming I can put workable "don't be a dick" guidelines into the game, and flag these as optional rules, great. Alternately, I could also put in a "Denied!" clause that lets someone increase their doom to deny a Skip or Reversal move, which might be all the safety-valve this notion needs.

    Posted By: renatoramFor Fred: at which development stage is the game? (note that I did not ask for a publication date :-) ).
    \nPlaytesting-at-cons. Right now Escape or Die! is the game I'll bring to conventions -- zero prep, plays super-fast and gives you time to spend hanging out with folks & talking. (For this, I've come to realize that it's an excellent morning game -- since it wakes you up -- rather than an evening game.) I haven't started writing the rules out in "long form" yet -- it's an amalgam of what's on the character sheet and what's in my head. Most of the optional stuff's in my head, since the character sheet is too-crowded with text as it is. I think the "production" version is going to have to move the rules summary elements off of the sheet and onto a third reference page.

    I'm also working with Russ "Contract Work" Collins to replace the game's timer with a set of MP3's of appropriate length to the number of active players, so you can have the full "action movie soundtrack" experience and a genuine doom-chime noise. Five folks at the table means play the 5-minute track on continuous loop, etc. \n
    Posted By: renatoramAlso, can you show us the character sheet,
    \nUm... maybe? *rummages* I don't have the files online right now. It'll have to be a later thing. Besides, revision is afoot.\n
    Posted By: renatoramwhat setup you use when playing? (egg timer? poker chips?)
    \nI give everyone a black (doom), red (pain), white (panic), and blue (escape) token, as well as 12 chits to use (6 for fears, 6 for edges), and two post-it notes (for tweaks). And, yeah, a cooking timer.\n
    Posted By: renatoramI was also wondering if there are (or are planned) any "tone reinforcement" mechanics, or if it's left at the decision and agreement of the group (kinda like the veto system of Dogs and Wushu).
    \nYep. I tested them out with the second (unrecorded due to location) EOD! game. We had a 14-year-old at the table, so it was a good chance to set some Ground Rules when we created the situation: "The game will stay in the supernatural genre." and "Descriptions will stay at the PG-13 level."

    \n As the game currently stands, I keep running into problems. One thing is that during the game, I'm uninvolved with most of the people at the table. It will be difficult for them to reincorporate my stuff if they aren't invested in it at all.

    Ideas
    1. Each turn the remaining players MUST reincorporate at least one character or event related to each of the deceased characters. If not, the player whose stuff wasn't reincorporated has the option of spending X Escape to HALVE the time of the doom clock (so 30 seconds per player) for that round.

    2. The deceased player may spend X Escape to pause the Doom Clock and request a 'Quiet Scene' for a number of players equal to X. This scene is a break in the action (it can be a flashback or happen in actual time, as long as it is plausible) and allows for some bonding between players. General Rule: Players sitting farthest from each other should be in these scenes.

    3. If an element from a deceased player's play hasn't been reincorporated in the previous round, that Deceased player may spend 1 Escape to trigger a flashback scene between the deceased player and one other player. This should set up a new complication for that player, and occurs on the clock, after which play continues as normal.

    Do any of these sound plausible, or do they break your conception of the game too much?
