\n I want a game that explicitly changes the social dynamics in a group. I want something that turns everything around, or upside-down.

I'm not talking about MLWM's teachings about dysfunctional relationships, and how they could be transferred to real life. I'm not talking about the age-old "listen to the guy holding the rulebook, because he's important".

Nor do I want something that exists only in the ritualized space of the game, to be dismissed afterwards as "just a game". I want something that sticks.

    \n To make the game bleed into the real-world social dynamics, you have to have real-world consequences. Like, for example, instead of losing hit points, you lose fingers. Just imagine how the real-world social dynamics change when the fucking cleric heals Sarah instead of you, and that means the loss of your pinky at the end of the encounter.

    Another way to blur the line between RL and game is something that people have been doing and talking about for quite a while on the Forge: techniques that involve getting at issues people at the table care about or have some RL stake in -- i.e., Narrativism plus certain amplifying techniques. Imagine a game where, during character creation, the player to the left of you got to graft on a personality trait or issue that was based on their perception of you-the-player. Now you're explicitly playing a version of your real self at the table. This has the potential to change real-world social dynamics.

    You like cruelty? Breaking people down into pieces? Then Scapegoat is your kind of fun.

    Secretly choose one player to be the scapegoat. The scapegoat could be a man or a woman. If the scapegoat is a woman and the other players are all men, Scapegoat can be even more enjoyable. We will assume he is a man. If the scapegoat knows that he is going to be the scapegoat, he will not play, so all the other players must pretend that you are playing another game. It can be another roleplaying game, a board game or even, for advanced players, a group therapy session.

    Each of the other players chooses a quality to be triggered by the scapegoat. This can be for example, passive-aggressive, easily offended, snitch, sneering or belittling.

    During game play, any player can make the secret signal \u2013 a right thumb on the table will suffice, and, in passing mention one of the other players. That player should, within five minutes, use their quality against the scapegoat. Once they have, each other player secretly votes with a left thumb on the effectiveness and realism of the acting player. Votes are tallied, and play continues clockwise around the table. The player who causes the scapegoat to stop playing the game loses all their points, and the game ends. Points are tallied and the victor is declared.

    If you have played correctly, the scapegoat will hate you and your friends, and may post about you on the internet. You will have to judge whether telling the scapegoat they have been playing is more or less fun than keeping it a secret.

    Variants

    Experienced players can start the game at any time simply by making the secret signal; other players can improvise their qualities as they are required.

    Single Player Scapegoat. Choose a game where you are the GM. Use all the listed qualities and techniques to spoil the game for all the other players. It's important that they stay playing for as long as possible. If you spoil their enjoyment of roleplaying games altogether, you win.

    In Reverse Scapegoat, everyone sets out to make the other player feel good. The appearance of sincerity is a must. The game ends when the scapegoat makes a general statement about how kind everybody is being. You must not reveal that you were playing reverse scapegoat. Playing a game of Scapegoat with the same scapegoat immediately afterwards is particularly enjoyable.

    In Scapegoat Freak-Out, players make creepy comments or perform actions that make no sense. The aim is to freak the scapegoat out. When they are freaked out, the game ends. If this is taking too long, any player may stand up and declare Freak Out loudly. Everyone marches around the scapegoat, pointing and laughing, then leave the room.

    In ScapeWord each player picks a word. Whenever the scapegoat says your word, you get a point. There are no turns. You can encourage the scapegoat to say your word, or not say others' words, but not directly.

    In Scapegoat Extreme, you can only win if the scapegoat hits you. This is a dangerous game. Do not play in bars - it is too easy. You get extra points if you are hit by a nun, police officer or marshal artist.
