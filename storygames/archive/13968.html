    \n Here in the UK, Conception & Indiecon both kick ass: they are held in the same location, a holiday village on the South Coast of England, but the former is in late January while the latter is in November. Both of them take the form of extended weekends with 300-500 gamers descending for days of table-top & LARP; Indiecon in particular is very supportive of story-games. I playtested no fewer than 7 games the last time I was there, including 3 sessions of my Game Chef 2010 entry, which went on to win the contest. There is talk of this years Indiecon playing host to an indie design contest, with prizes of support (in the form of getting art for your project or publishing vouchers) for the winners.

    \n For a USian, if you just want to play, and you like the sort of games that get a lot of love at this forum, the smaller gatherings cannot be beat. Go Play Northwest is astonishingly good, Camp Nerdly is unbeatable unless you are afraid of giant hornets, Forge Midwest, Nerdly Beach Party, Jiffycon.

    Other than GPNW I'm unfamiliar with west coast conventions. Dreamation is the only con I know that views an "indie" presence as a confirmed money-maker, and they go well out of their way to be welcoming and hospitable to this crowd. This is reflected in the diversity and quality of games offered, and the diversity and quality of people who make strong efforts to attend.

    For smaller regional gatherings that are not focused obsessively on Story Games darlings, I've heard a lot of good things about Fear the Con in St. Louis.

    I think it is Fred Hicks who calls Gen Con his selling con, Origins his networking con, and Dreamation his playing con. That seems apt for a publisher.

    \n It depends on your taste, to a certain extent, and where you live in North America. Personally, I find that I can't commit to running more than 1 game per day, can probably play in 1 or 2 more, and then need something else to do (sit around and talk with folks, wander the distributors booths, go outside for some air and food, etc.) to refresh my brains. Also, I really have trouble with multi-day cons, since my brains turn to slush after about two straight days of the above.

    Consequently, traveling across the country for a multi-day brain-slushing event makes less and less sense. Living in Seattle, with PAX Prime and GoPlay NW and Gamestorm and several other smaller cons within a few hours of me, those are the ones I'm most excited about. This past year, I did Friday evening and most of Sunday at PAX, trading my badge to a friend for Saturday. That day in the middle was great for a brain refresh. I did something similar at GoPlay, just showing up for the afternoon and evening activities, but spending the mornings at home. Obviously, for out-of-towners, that may make less sense, but Seattle is a great place to visit and spending the mornings wandering downtown (where both PAX and GoPlay now are) sounds like a great idea to me, a nice mental break from the insanity.

    If I was still in Boston, I imagine I'd do about the same thing with PAX Prime and JiffyCon, maybe riding the short train down to Dreamation as well. If I was more centrally located I would think more about GenCon and Origins and Forge MW. And in the Southeast I would probably do Dreamation and Camp Nerdly. Less sure about the Southwest and California.

    Lately too, some folks have been breaking into non-gaming-specific cons like comics, anime, and steampunk affairs, but that depends on you feeling a connection with those crowds and interests. Me, I'd probably fit in better at Botcon, Staple!, and Stumptown.

    \n Here in Finland I recommend Tracon and Ropecon - the former for the superior logistical arrangements and the fresh crowd, the latter for tradition and unsurpassed game culture showings. Both sell several thousand tickets, apparently. Tracon is much better as a sales venue for indies, for largely the same reasons you have in the USA between old and established vs. young and hungry conventions: free tables, more respect, less groupthink. Ropecon is more worthwhile for the sheer number of veteran roleplayers in attendance, which will also translate to better sales for anybody willing to court the mainstream.

    For small in-crowd conventions I recommend my own house cons (the best gaming in Eastern Finland!) and the bi/triannual Maracon in Oulu, which at this point has a history rivalling all other Finnish conventions excepting Ropecon - very strong tradition, solid culture, international level of gamesmanship drawn from a small pool and cultivated with patience. Southern Finland doesn't boast the equal of these northern and eastern opportunities for some reason, perhaps because everybody is so busy setting up larger cons.

    The only pan-European convention I've sampled is Spiel Essen, which I judge in many ways to be the equivalent of Gencon for Europeans; the greatest difference is that Spiel is primarily boardgames and only secondarily roleplaying, but the 400% higher attendance numbers basically still give you a plentiful roleplayer audience. The terms for participation are slightly better for indies, too, I understand. While the free gaming is undeniably worse than at Gencon, it's still basically what you make of it (via precon Internet scheduling and willingness to think on your feet at the fair, say), and the fair floor is in its own class altogether - you can spend the convention just talking, buying and participating in demoes, easily enough. Definitely my recommendation for any Europeans considering an international convention experience this year, and not only because I'll probably be there with bells on myself.

    Posted By: Jason Morningstar
    For smaller regional gatherings that are not focused obsessively on Story Games darlings, I've heard a lot of good things about Fear the Con in St. Louis.
    .

    \nI just got back Sunday from Fear the Con in St. Louis. It is the friendliest, best run small con I've ever attended. The attendees truly do embrace the idea of community.
    The con officially started with a Wing Night socializer on Thursday. Friday was 3 slots of gaming, plus a comedy show and a trivia contest. Saturday was 3
    more slots plus a live podcast recording.

    Throughout the entire con people would pleasantly introduce themselves and talk gaming with you if you looked bored,alone, or sat still too long

    By then end I felt like I had been gaming with many new friends instead of just seeing people at a convention. Conversation about all aspects of the con started on their forums
    before I had finished driving home, including discussions of people collaborating on games for next year.

    As a player and GM, it was wonderful. For a publisher/vendor, it would probably not be financially viable. The attendance was about 200 folks. The dealer room was essentially
    1 RPG/board game dealer, one soap vendor, one jewelry vendor, and a puzzle auction for charity.

    The games were all over the spectrum. For story games, there were at least 2 sessions of Fiasco, one DRYH, several Dresden, one PDQ, several Dread/Jenga hacks and I personally ran 2 Lady Blackbird games and 2 Smallville games. I've most assuredly missed some. There were also many of old school games - The Tomb of Horrors was run, BattleTech, and a ton of Savage Worlds.
