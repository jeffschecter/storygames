Nobilis--one of my favorite but unplayed games--rewards players this way:
Every time there is a successful and enjoyable game session--that is, everyone had fun--the characters receive a Dynasty Point [which can be spent to buy resources for the group as a whole]. At the completion of each successful, enjoyable story--one where every player had fun in most of the sessions--the players choose whether to receive one new character point [which can be spent to buy things for that character] or a chancel point [used on the team's HQ].

What I do not like about this--and keep in mind, I haven't played--is that it encourages players to say they had fun, regardless of whether they did or not. You don't want to be the one hold-out, saying, "Naw, this sucked. No XP for all of us, then. Try again next time." It's bad because it incentivizes a reaction, and also does it at the end of the game, when it's too late to fix it.

A potentially better approach: use something like Fan Mail in PTA. Each player has a stack of individualized tokens. Every time another player does something to make you say "Whoa, cool!", award that player a token. You can only receive one token per scene, but can award as many as there are players. Once you get a token from all the players, you can redeem them for points. (Nobilis has like 3 different kinds of reward points, I'm not sure how this would break down just yet.) There's still the Oscar the Grouch problem, but it's obvious during play, and can be addressed in real-time.

Has anyone tried the rules-as-written? How does it work out? Am I making too much out of a non-problem? What other variant rules have people used?

Plus, I finally figure out some applications of the Never Say "No" principle which had not been obvious to me.

    Yeah, the problem isn't the players thinking, "God, this sucks." First, that's usually quite obvious. Second, it's a self-correcting problem: one way or another, it's going to get resolved at the player-level.

    The problem is players thinking, "Hey, that was all right... I guess..." which is harder to diagnose, and doesn't always get resolved quickly. The person is telling a little white lie for the sake of politeness, and it's tricky to call them on it tactfully. The Nobilis rule-as-written gives them an extra incentive to do this. (The rule, of course, is a pretty small factor, since the social incentives are very strong, but still it's not optimal.)

    The root phenomenon has nothing to do with gaming, nor with (specifically-) geek social fallacies. Sometimes people stay in relationships (jobs, friendships, marriages, etc) where they're bored, or dissatisfied, but not unhappy--or at least, not unhappy enough to break things off. So you just coast for a while, and procrastinate on the hard work until there's a crisis or a better opportunity.

    I'm not sure that the Monarda Law offers such ironclad protection of player narrative input as all that. I believe that the way it explicitly works is that you can't say "no" but you can say "well no not quite". I believe that the options you get are explicitly layed out as:

    "You can do that, but it'll cost you"
    "You can't do that, but you can do this!"
    "You can try!"

    So (to take the example from the initial, linked-to thread) if the players say "We capture his sister" the GM is, within the spirit of the Monarda Law, completely entitled to say "he doesn't have a sister" so long as he qualifies it with something else with broadly the same connotations ("...but he has a dog which he cares about very much").

    Similarly, like the "encased in amber" thing, you can also say "you can try to kidnap his sister, but she's an /unbelievable badass/" or otherwise hose the idea while remaining completely within the scope of the Monarda Law.

    Finally, the issue with the Monarda Law is that it's presented very much as GM advice, not as a game rule, so the GM *can* always ignore it (compare with, for example, the Let it Ride rule in Burning Wheel which states, very explicitly, that the GM cannot ask a player for a second check against the same ability, and if they do they are cheating).

    Now don't get me wrong, I do *like* the Monarda Law, I just think that it's less of a big thing than some people make out. I think it's a step in the right direction, but it's a smaller step than it looks.

    I haven't looked at the text in a little while, but you're right: the Never Say "No" Principle doesn't mean the GM can't hose the players. But it does mean that the GM can't say, "Nah, that's conceptually wrong from the get-go." What the players want to try is at least possible, which means the GM has to make some adjustments to the imaginary world.

    Even if the duke doesn't have a sister, or a dog, or a favorite servant, he used to have a sock-monkey that he loved more than anything in the world--but he threw it in the furnace at age 12, when he realized the unhealthy power it had over his thoughts, and scattered the ashes across the land. Here, the GM is trying pretty hard to hose the players a little, but the GM has to work for it. In fact, instead of a hose, it's concrete mixer: the GM is building onto the imaginary world to keep the players far away, instead of closing doors in their faces to keep them out. The players, if they're persistent, can explore these new additions, and figure out something else to do. More importantly, the scene which the GM intended to be about how hard-assed the Duke can be, ends up being all about funky miracles and player scheming. As it should be.

    There are two important thing about what she says:

    Firstly, unlike the rule in Trollbabe (apparently, although I haven't got the game so I don't know for sure) the Monarda Law *isn't* a rule, it's a guideline, a piece of GM advice. It's given in-character by Idaly St-Germain and what it says is "If you wish to make sure that you do not slip and accidentally place your established story over the fun of the players in the game, a strict adherence to the Monarda Law shall save your soul from peril."

    This is not, in any sense, system protection.

    Secondly, the options you are given instead of saying "No" two are actually just "No" by the back door. Specifically you are told (by "Idaly") that you should say (and I quote):

    "Yes" if their course of action seems innocuous and interesting.
    "How?" if you don't see any way they can do it.
    "You can try!" if it seems possible but unlikely.
    "Yes, but there's a catch" if you can think of a good catch.

    Of these three answers "How" and "You can try" are almost identical to saying "No". In fact they're arguably worse, because now instead of the GM being the Arbiter of Reality, he has become the Arbiter of Cool. Suddenly instead of the GM saying "no, you can't kidnap the guy's sister, because he hasn't got one" the GM is instead saying "you can only kidnap the guy's sister if you can *convince* me that kidnapping his sister is *cooler* than what I have in mind."

    Nothing in the actual, textual description of the Monarda Law makes it into anything even resembling system protection of player creative input.

    I don't think that any of the Monarda responses are identical to "No".

    You know the improv thing about how you can accept or block someone's input, and blocking is generally bad? No is clearly blocking, but these aren't as clearly blocking: they're not stopping the player, they're modulating the player's input. Far from preventing the player input, they are saying "Give me more input" and zooming the game's detail in on that attempted action. "With a catch" and "You can try" both adjust the input somewhat, but don't prevent it altogether. Instead of saying "That input is nopt awesome enough" it says "that input is almost good enough, and if we work together it can be really awesome". Which is totally different. And all of the four Monarda responses give a player something to build on, whereas "No, that doesn't work" doesn't.

    "The following is very mystical but local in nature and therefore a level 8 miracle of Aspect at most..."

    Sorry, but no. You cannot breathe life into an inanimate object through any miracle of Aspect: you could sculpt a statue so lifelike that everyone swears it's about to open its eyes, but you couldn't actually bring it to life. You cannot even heal other living creatures with Aspect, not directly, although you could learn surgery in a matter of moments and then proceed to operate flawlessly on someone's heart. Aspect measures the distance between all the physical, mental and social abilities of a common mortal and those of a miraculous being. It specifically does not cover mystical or magical powers.

    But conceivably you might discover the existence of an obscure Rite which allows someone to create such a golem, and there are always other alternatives... Does your creation have to leave the confines of the Chancel? If not, use Realm. Does it absolutely have to be made of clay? If not, use Domain.
