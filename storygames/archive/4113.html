Why indie games are cheap and expensive

\n
\n
\n
\n\nGraham \n\n\n
\n
\n\nAugust 2007 \n edited August 2007 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n So, I really don't mean to be a pain in the arse, here. But I get really exhausted with discussions over whether indie games are cheap or expensive. They never go anywhere. Nobody listens to each other and no resolution is ever reached: people who think they're cheap say they're cheap, people who think they're expensive say they're expensive.

And, of course, these discussions don't change anything. When game designers set prices, they don't think: oh, I must find that discussion on Story Games about whether indie games are too expensive.

Here's points you can make, on either side, to prove that indie games are either cheap or expensive. If you are a game designer who wants to charge high prices or have lots of disposable income you want to give to game designers, see section 1. If you are a consumer who wants to keep prices low or have little disposable income, see section 2.

1. Why indie games are cheap\n
    \n
  • Compare them with other ways to spend the evening. An indie game costs \xc2\xa320. Taking five people to the cinema costs \xc2\xa340: and good indie games are more reliably good than films, theatre and TV.
    • \n
  • Compare them with other hobbies. Buying a computer game costs \xc2\xa350.
    • \n
  • Compare them with mainstream roleplaying games. They cost more: and you need to buy lots of supplements for them. An indie roleplaying game has everything you need in one pack.
    • \n
  • You can play indie games again and again.
    • \n
  • A good indie game is better quality, in terms of art and writing, than a normal roleplaying game.
    • \n
  • We buy indie games to support designers: it's a community thing, not a commercial thing.
    • \n
\n2. Why indie games are expensive\n
    \n
  • Compare them with other ways to spend the evening. An indie game costs \xc2\xa320. Going to the cinema would cost you \xc2\xa38: and films, theatre and TV are more reliably good than an average indie game.
    • \n
  • Compare them with other hobbies. Doing a crossword costs only 65p.
    • \n
  • Compare them with mainstream roleplaying games. They're more reliably good and you'll play them more times than you would an indie game.
    • \n
  • You never play most indie games you buy or, at least, only play them once.
    • \n
  • A average indie game is worse quality, in terms of art and writing, than a normal roleplaying game.
    • \n
  • We buy indie games to support designers: it's a community thing, not a commercial thing.
    • \n
\nNote, how, if you're arguing they're too cheap, it helps if you talk about good indie games. If you're arguing they're too expensive, it helps if you talk about an average indie game or some indie games. And, of course, the general idea that indie games are awesome pushes the argument that they're too cheap above the one they're too expensive.

Surely, in practice, it's much more market-driven? Game designers will charge higher prices if they can; consumers can either buy them or not.

Graham

    \n There is another aspect to cheap/expensive apart from the relative monetary values (and to be fair, at GenCon, the exchange rate was so favourable, nothing was expensive and restraint was having to be my watchword!). What I'm talking about is the opportunity costs with regard to time in preperation for a game and the mental activities around gaming.
    \n
    \nThis might just be me but I find the more concentrated focus of a good indie game on one evocative premise, combined with what is usually a shorter book without some of the traditional bells and whistles means that I can read and comprehend a game *and* conjure up a workable game on the in/out journey to work on one day. I cannot do that with a traditional game. I know they are two different beasts, but as soon as I had read half of the Dictionary of Mu I was all over the concepts and uing them in a gaming (Burning Mu, coming soon...) whereas I am still struggling as to the point of Scion. I readily admit this could just be my experience and my personal foibles.
    \n
    \nTime is money though, and unless you value the game-building aspect of gaming more than the game-playing aspect, a game that gets you to the point quicker should be more valuable. And *I* find that quality in 'indie' games.

The consumer knows he may face the follow on cost of the paper book later if he likes the game - I know I feel like that anyway. And from the consumers point of view there is a zero per unit cost to the supplier. But the publisher is taking a risk by providing a small market of consumers with a copy of his game that can be copied and distributed effortlessly.

    Lemme give a little publisher's view:

    \n

    It costs me about $5.50/book to print and ship a run of100 books.

    \n

    I have to pay to get to cons. That's between $10 (piling into a car for Dreamation, which I go to free as a publisher) and $1000 (Gen Con, which cost a shitload this year, but still came out OK). The average is probably around $200 and I probably go to 6 a year.

    \n

    I love to eat. Love it! Designing games isn't a hobby for me. I don't have hobbies. Some things make me money, some things make me other things I need. Games make me money and are deeply satisfying to create.

    \n

    Now! That means that, if I sell 300 copies a year (If! When you sit down to make a game, you have no idea how successful it's gonna be, and if it's going to have to float you through your next idea. "Science Fiction like Philip K. Dick" is probably going to sell better than "Cavemen inventing culture", so Shock: is going to have to support its sequel at the beginning of history), that means that I need to invest $1500 into printing and $1200 into con-going, never mind my own efforts. So that's $2700 there. So I look at this and think, "If I sell 200 of these, and I'm charging $15 each, that's $3000. I will have made $300, which I would accept as a price to make a business card for someone, but certainly not design a game and a whole book."

    \n

    So I think, "What if I charge $20?" That nets me $1200 \xe2\u20ac\u201d still pretty shitty for the amount of work it takes just to write the damn thing, never mind design it from scratch. But if I charge $25, I bet I won't be able to hit my 200 copy minimum because people will compare it to other things you can do for $25, and I don't feel comfortable selling it for the same amount as The Mountain Witch. $24 sounds just like $25, so $23 it is!"

    \n

    You will note, however, that financial realities take hold over this principled guessing, though: Shock:1.1 now costs $24 because my printing costs went up substantially.

    \n

    The fundamental question, though, is the last one: "If I raise my prices by 1%, will I lose 1% of my customers? What about 2%? 3%?

    \n

    When those numbers cross, when I lose more customers than I gain income by increasing the price, I'm charging too much.

    \n

    If you think the game costs too much, don't buy it. If you think you'd like it, but it costs too much, you're either as broke as I am or you suspect that the game doesn't do what it promises.

    \n

    It does.

    \nI agree with you that it is a silly discussion.

    The reason we have things which are 'cheap' or 'expensive' is that we do not have perfectly efficient markets. Consumers don't get to know exactly what value they can extract from a game before they buy it, while manufacturers aren't aware of exactly how much money the consumer is willing to pay for the game and generally has to charge the same to all consumers, even though some are willing to pay more than others (though obviously there are strategies for evading this restriction.) Lacking perfect information, producers have to position themselves relative to the rest of the market and consumers have to make a judgement on the value of the product to them.

    Something is 'cheap' if, by accident or design, the producer sets a price lower than the consumer would have been willing to pay, or if the consumer get less value than he anticipated. It is expensive if things go the other way.

    Generally, these 'cheap' vs. 'expensive' games discussions have focussed on the value to consumers, which is pointlessly subjective. The element due to producers making decisions about market positioning might be interesting, though.
