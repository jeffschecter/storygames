    \n I'm in love with how simple yet powerful Fantasy Flight's narrative dice system is in Edge of the Empire. I've run a couple campaigns of it for a bunch of friends, most of whom had never played a tabletop RPG before, and it really facilitates great roleplay, and makes the GM's job easier at the same time with the possibilities of success and failure, but with added conditions.

    If you're not a Star Wars fan, there are hacks into standard fantasy such as Sky Wars: Edge of the Kingdom. In all I just love the dice and what they can do for encouraging roleplaying by giving the players the agency to describe just how they succeeded with negative consequences, or failed with positive ones.

    \nFall of Magic may be an option, but you would need a few sessions to get the most from it. It is a simple game and I have gotten very good results with newbies when playing it. It is amoung my three current favorite Collaborative Story Games\u2014the others being Fiasco and Follow. Out of these Follow probably requires the most story gaming experience to pull off, but with experienced players you can generally get good results with a very simple and elegant system. The most important things to do to have a successful game of Follow are to keep a consistent tone and to resist the urge to go gonzo; the system will create plenty of creativity, unexpectedness and novelty without the players pushing it, and if they do the story can get out of hand.

    \n I'd never use D&D. Ever. Too many rules, and the players often feel they lack "skill" at playing.
    I'd go for MonsterHearts. It's a theme that everyone can recognise, it's very much drama and very little rules.
    Or Monster of the Week, if anyone has ever watched a supernatural show of any sort. It should be nicely familiar.
    Or learn Danish, and download a scenario from alexandria.dk ;-) You can find some with English translations as well, all free and legal and with the authors permission.

    \n Dungeon World is like D&D, but has a more narrative focus, and is considerably easier to learn. I run a one-shot every two weeks (almost always with strangers); they seem to enjoy it. Of course, whatever system you choose, it should be one you know really well.

    If I wasn't... maybe Follow, or Microscope? The Quiet Year?

    You could always go super simple and use The Free Universal RPG, or The Tearable RPG.

    \n I think it all depends on what type of game you want to play, right? Simulationist? Gamist? Narrative? Character driven? ...

    But in any case I think it needs to be kept simple to start it, which is why one shots might be awesome, or games with almost no rules, like Roll For Shoes or RISUS. These two are my goto systems for quick play with new people. If they like it I'd probably suggest other games.

    Other pretty simple games are Lady Blackbird and Lasers and Feelings and, if you have it, Fall of Magic.

    Anyway, my two cents.
