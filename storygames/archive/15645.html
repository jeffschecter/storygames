\n i was part of a way too large G+ topic and wanted to bring a little of that here.

here is my current draft, any of you word smiths want to take a crack at it?

to define rpg i will have to start with what a game is.

Game
A form of play directed by a set of rules.

Narration game
A form of game in witch players use narration as the primary method of interacting with the rules and game state.

Role playing game
A form of Narration game where players use the first person narration and dialog of a particular fictional character to interact with the game rules. Often including a player who narrates the fictional world and many other characters.

Story game
A form of narration game with rules that focus on the telling of a story.

So I would define the games we play primarily as narration games, narration is their defining feature. We narrate and that narration has some effect on the game state. Note that Narration game is a very broad category including things that are not story games or rpgs, games like dixit also fall into this category.

    And Jason's got the right of it, the "Oh man this again?" thing, but hey, like the topic "What game is the best for supers?", I don't mind as much seeing it. It does not yet fill me with crippling ennui.

    But to take it a step deeper:\n
    Posted By: GrahamStory Games is just the name of a website.
    \nIndeed! That's the primary definition of Story Games these-a-days. But let's go further!\n
    Posted By: GrahamThe term was, as I remember, coined by Clinton R Nixon toavoida debate on definitions: that is, to avoid a debate on whether particular games were roleplaying games. (Fine, he said, let's call them story games instead.)
    \nMostly! It was indeed coined by Clinton. It was after yet another discussion on RPGNet back in the day where this discussion was popularized nearly monthly, "X (game from some Forge developer) is NOT an RPG!"

    The very topic at the time that set the ball rolling was this: "My Life With Master is NOT an RPG because it has an endgame condition. Role-playing games by necessity (and apparently definition) open-ended." There were more games from that year disparaged as not RPGs because they had these endgame conditions or rules on "what you feel/think" (nuh-uh, no game is gonna dictate to me what my character thinks!), a stable of characters and not just one, and on and on. But mostly it was the endgame thing that kind of set us off a bit, particularly Clinton. Because any way you slice it, MLWM is a fucking role-playing game. Might not be as contentious a stance now, but a few years ago it was.

    Two things happened from that discussion:

    1) My definition of Role-Playing Game solidified in my mind, and became the standard for what I do and do not think of as an RPG. "Do you PLAY a ROLE? Then it's a ROLE PLAYING GAME." (usually using the word "fucking" in each of those sentences)

    Interestingly enough: By this definition, unless you drift Universalis, Universalis isn't a role-playing game (unless you squint, because you really don't play a role all that much), which is a stance I later discovered that Ralph and Mike (the designers) had been yelling in people's faces since day one! :-)

    2) Clinton posted some eye-rolling post on the blog he was using at the time, basically throwing up his hands at this idiocy, saying basically (in intent, not literally), "Alright. Fuck it, let's just call them "story games" instead."

    (I registered the domain that afternoon! Was actually considering using it as a gaming imprint/ "company" until the idea to make a forum came along, and here we are)

    ---

    My gamey definition of Story Games as a thing, I think I put it on the wiki, "Games where we focus more on the story and less on 'my guy'." You can of course aim to do that with traditional RPGs. It's simple: Create a stable of characters instead of Everyone Gets One (whom they play exclusively forever). Boom, that's instant story game.

    But that definition covered under an umbrella games with multiple characters, scene-framing, endgame conditions, etc etc etc. The thought there was that you're still "playing a role", so I haven't gone at it like a Neoplatanist philosopher, grouping scrolls into bins and carefully drawing lines in the sand of what does and doesn't make it in.

    Pragmatically, though, it's a great term: If you say "Anyone want to meet up for some story games?" You will get folks turning up expecting some game like Fiasco, Microscope, Primetime Adventures, etc. If you say "Anyone want to meet up for some RPGs?" You'll have to give a long list of what you do and do not mean by that, so that the guy turning up expecting D&D or Shadowrun (and whose interests lie no where near what we talk about here) won't be disappointed.

    Beyond that... \n

    \n Tyler, if I were going to come up with RPG categories completely out of the air, they would cut across "traditional" and "indie" divisions, like:

    Mission-based games, in which you are here to do a thing, including some veins of D&D, The Mountain Witch, Mouse Guard, etc. GM-driven. I might also include things like My Life with Master here, because even though the characters can choose to do all sorts of things, the goal is to play out a specific kind of experience.

    Sandbox games, including other veins of D&D, Shadowrun, White Wolf stuff, Apocalypse World, Freemarket, etc. Also GM-driven.

    GM-less/ful games, since this tradition is still in the early stages, so we can combine things like Polaris and Fiasco together, even though they're way different. There's probably also some veins of D&D that fit in this category too, like online freeform GM-ful D&D or, like, when kids attempt to "play D&D" during a roadtrip in the car, just by themselves with no materials.

    Are there others? Maybe. The thing with "story games" is that it's often used as a stand-in for "indie games and games that indie games people like," which isn't descriptive of the type of games, really, just the community of people and the games they happen to play. Practically at the table, Apocalypse World has a lot more in common with Vampire than it does with Fiasco, even though AW+Fiasco have supposedly come out of the same community of design and play. They perhaps share a common perspective on game design, but that doesn't necessarily translate into games that are at all similar in practice.

    "Indie games" is not a type of games, necessarily, and the shared similarities in design -- due to mutual influence -- have waned in the past few years as the community has grown larger, more diverse, and more confident in expressing a diverse range of design and play interests. This is only going to continue, even with big impact games like AW and Fiasco spreading certain ideas across the design landscape.

    Honestly, the thing that people who play "story games" or "indie games" have most in common, in my experience, is their desire to play a whole bunch of different kinds of games and their drive to try new things. I feel like you draw the same crowd to meetups by organizing them as opportunities to try out new games.

    \n This is such an old topic. I'll lay out the party line (meaning, how Forge theory defines some things):

    Numero uno, to even discuss things like terminology you basically need to accept certain postmodern tools to even be comprehensible: there is a need to understand that words by themselves do not have objective definitions, but rather language is a social construct - words mean what we agree upon for them to mean. Often this social coordination of communication becomes an authority struggle: you can show your loyalty to a given tribe by the way you use words, and you can display your authority by influencing others to use words your way.

    Furthermore, even if we're all being egalitarian and objective, with no tribalist bullshit getting in the way, there's still the issue of useful frameworks of thought: people use words for different purposes, and for this reason they have different needs for what those words mean. This is easy to see everywhere around us: words are constantly being repurposed to make them more useful for different situations. The important lesson here is that it's wrong to ask what a word "means". The useful question is, which framework of thought is most useful for my purposes in this situation, and therefore, what do I want words to mean?

    Basically all discussions about rpg terminology in the Internet become such clusterfucks because of those two issues: on the one hand we have people fighting to brand "roleplaying" as a term according to their own wishes on what to include or exclude, and then on the other hand we have people who actually need to use words to think, and they're obviously trying to find words for concepts of utility.

    Now that we understand the inherent arbitrariness of terminology, the substantial issue: when we ask "What is roleplaying?", what is usually really being asked is "Where are the boundaries of this interesting thing of ours?" I actually gave a lecture on this in 2004-ish at Ropecon, where I outlined some of the basic interests people have in the concept of roleplaying. The point of my lecture was that different subcultures use the term in different ways simply because their interests are different, and therefore they do not need or want to use the word in the sense other, contrary subcultures use it. For examples, consider:
    • What I like to call "traditional" roleplaying from the late '80s and '90s involves a strong core of gamers who know what they like in gaming. They have a vested interest in defining "roleplaying game" in ways that emphasize their interests. I've heard such traditional gamers claim that a roleplaying game is a game that has an all-powerful GM, for example. Another common requirement is that each player has to take the role of a fictional character and play the game from in-character viewpoint. Furthermore, the task of the GM is to provide content for the other players to interact with, while the players form a party that resolves challenges posited by the GM. From outside the subculture it's obvious that this is a historically organic, essentially conservative and non-analytic definition: "roleplaying is the shared properties of the games this gaming culture of ours has spawned", one might say. It's an entirely valid way of defining roleplaying, I have only sympathy for anybody who feels that games that lack these traditional features have lost what made them interesting for the traditionalist: he has his hobby with its received wisdom, and the label on that hobby is "roleplaying", so of course he's going to be upset if the same word is being painted on stuff that he's not interested in.
    • Nordic freeform rpg culture shares an almost universal model for what roleplaying is: roleplaying is a practice where participants pretend to experience fictional subject matter (events, feelings, etc.). This is (or was) a radical off-shoot of the mainstream of roleplaying culture, one where people pushed the boundaries and abandoned many of the traditional elements of the roleplaying game. For example, game-like mechanics were largely abandoned. The motivation in this subculture is experiental, as I understand it: people like to play because they enjoy the flow experience that humans can induce by using their imagination in a concerted manner. "Roleplaying" here is often literally "playing a role", meaning "immersing in a fictional character", or a bit more widely simply "immersing in a fiction". I've met Nordic theorists who will quite clearly argue that games like computer roleplaying games or even just particularly compelling novels may encourage an activity that is indistinguishable from roleplaying.
    \nThe wise observer will probably notice that there is nothing inherently wrong with either of the above subcultures and their ways of thinking. They're just using the same or similar terminology in different ways because their interests and concerns are fundamentally different. Both types of roleplayers can point to examples of their practice that are clearly roleplaying games in their opinion while falling solidly outside the frame for the other subculture. It would not be useful for either to abandon terminology that they have genuinely developed through a historical process, starting from the same or similar starting points. Of course it would make an armchair theorist happy if eg. traditionalists continued using the term (they have a pretty strong historical affinity to it, after all) while Nordic freeformers and other such off-shoot subcultures started calling their hobby something else. It's just interesting to ask - why should we care about some armchair theorist in the real world?

    Finally, I'd like to share the definition of roleplaying that I've found the most useful myself. It's the one utilized in the Big Model of Forgean rpg theory, and I prefer it because it focuses on roleplaying as a social, purposeful activity, which I think is the core of roleplaying in comparison to some other arts. Because the model reflects my understanding of roleplaying well, I like thinking of roleplaying in its terms.

    So, the Big Model claim is simple: roleplaying is the practice of Exploring Shared Imagined Space, and a roleplaying game is a game where exploring the SIS is a necessary practice. When you see a game and wonder whether it's a roleplaying game, the question is simple: is there a Shared Imagined Space, and is it being Explored? Insofar as such exploration is incidental we may say that a game involves roleplaying; where exploring the SIS is central and necessary we may very properly say that the game in question is a roleplaying game. What then, is the Shared Imagined Space? This is also simple: the SIS is the conceptual virtual space we discuss and describe in the process of roleplaying. I often use the Star Trek holodeck as a comparison: the players have the ability to conjure up imaginary spaces, objects, events and even people in their imaginary holodeck, which we call the Shared Imagined Space. Where such is present, the chance of roleplaying (exploration) occurring is rather high.

    It's notable that I couldn't care a flying fig about whether the above thing is called "roleplaying"; the important thing from my viewpoint is that the conceptual space limited by the above definition is the space where the interesting, "roleplaying-like" stuff happens insofar as I'm concerned. It's also interesting to compare the Big Model theoretical structure with other definitions of roleplaying, such as the two I outlined above, as it again allows us to see how there's a large bit of overlap between the notions, but also clear swathes of areas where the definitions differ. A traditionalist or Nordic immersionist, for example, might consider a Fighting Fantasy book to be essentially a roleplaying game, while the Big Model would not, as there is no Shared Imagined Space present. Similarly, neither the traditionalist nor immersionist would count things like Universalis or Microscope roleplaying games, while from the viewpoint of the Big Model its differences from traditional roleplaying games are merely incidental technical details that do not impact its powerful core SIS.

    --

    As for "Story Game", it's a marketing term with shallow history and little in the way of cultural backing. I use it on the cover of Zombie Cinema because I think that it captures the feel of the game well while seeming appealing and exact to people who don't know what it is. Just like "roleplaying", though, it's ultimately just an arbitrary word, and anybody who actually cares about what it means beyond the necessity of understanding what another person is saying should take a deep breath.
