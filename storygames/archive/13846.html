\n I'm working on a system for a game set at an ancient martial arts dojo. I have what I think is a great system for sparring matches, but sparring and meditating are not all one does. What about when the beautiful noblewoman comes to visit and everyone tries to impress her? What about trying to convince the corrupt taxman to lower the dojo's taxes without getting violent?

I need a system for currying favor, one that works one-on-one, in competition with an opponent for a third party's favor, and in cooperation as a group.

Are there any suggestions for good ones, or systems that could be adapted for this purpose?

    \n Similar to Graham and Rich's suggestions, using those systems as guidance, take your mechanic for resolving martial conflict and map it to social conflict.
    \n

    \nInstead of grappling / pinning / incapacitating someone, you are shutting down their argument with hyperbole and misdirection.

    \n So I just wrote a post about how adapting the sparring system wouldn't work, and then deleted it. Short version: in sparring, your opponent is constantly trying to thwart you. In social situations, this is not always the case.

    And then I realized that this was a "if there's no contest, don't roll" situation and I needed to rethink things.

    To give a little more background on the sparring system: it's basically a fast-paced game of Battleship. You have certain techniques you can throw, and your opponent puts blocking tokens (poker chips) on her character sheet to prevent you from hitting certain places. You throw techniques until you lose the offensive. Your opponent moves chips around to cover where you're trying to hit, and throws defensive techniques to attempt to regain the offensive. As you force your opponent's hand more, you build up points of advantage, which lets you eventually score.

    To adapt this to a social situation, one would need to...
    * Slow things down a little.
    * Create a chart of heroic deeds, social actions and fantastic feats that would take the place of the sparring board.
    * Have an opponent.

    That last one is important to being able to adapt the sparring system, but it also makes sense that without someone working against you, there isn't much of a challenge. If you're trying to woo the princess, either a) someone else will get there first and he's your opponent, b) she is not yet impressed by you and she's your opponent, c) her father/brother/etc doesn't like you and they're your opponents, or d) she secretly wants you to succeed and anything you do will work. (Goddamn princesses always pushing you around, making you do extra work when they already had their minds made up.)

    I'm not sure how to make it work for larger groups. The sparring rules for two-on-one are simple and emphasize the fact that you're going to lose. I might just have to make it a genre convention within the game: you face others one-on-one regardless of the arena.
