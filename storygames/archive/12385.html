\n If you're like me, you've been following Joe's Gun Thief with interest as a publishing/creation thing, even if you're not terribly interested in that particular game (sorry Joe).

$5 seems to me to be a good threshold for considering something a "micro" game. What's out there that costs between $5 and $1? I think I'm primarily interested in stand-alone games, as opposed to supplement-type stuff. (with apologies to Brennan's Campaign Frames for Mortal Coil).

So, uh...Gun Thief. $3 pdf, $5 print+pdf
The pdf of The Dance and the Dawn is only $5, so I want to mention it, even though its $12 in print. Also, Remember Tomorrow is $5 for the PDF.

What else? Are there any interesting or productive lessons we can learn from looking at extant micro-games?

    \n Cheerfully ignoring your instructions about supplements, my Trail of Cthulhu scenarios The Dying of St Margaret's and The Watchers In The Sky are $5.

    I think that price point is a major reason they've sold so many copies. Those two scenarios are often Pelgrane's second- and third- best selling products of the month (after whichever big thing has just been released).

    The lesson there, I think, is that dropping the price needn't reduce your profit, because your sales increase.

    You also needn't write something as long for that price, so the word count pressure is off. However, the quality must still be there. You've still got to playtest, revise, edit properly and so on.

    \n I really like this movement as a way of saying Short Games != Ashcans, which was part of that whole anti-ashcan fracas a couple years back. Honestly, I can think of a lot of indie games that I would like more if they were 50-90% shorter, so this is pretty exciting. I really want to see the "RPGs as tiny booklets" meme keep spreading, partially because it makes publishing even more accessible, as long as we simultaneously keep raising the bar for quality and accessibility of game texts. Sure, you can have relatively quick turnaround with micropublishing (as with Gun Thief or XXXtream Street Luge) and spin out a cool idea into a clever little game, but there's also room for some breakout successes in this category, which we could see if Lady Blackbird ever comes out in this format, for example. Booklet publishing drips with potential.

    \n Brainstorming...

    A publisher might do well selling 2 versions of a game.

    One version for a general audience. A full book with explanation on how to roleplay, inspirational art, sample scenarios, sample characters, and examples at full price.

    Then a second version at a fraction of the size for experienced roleplayers. A very low production version that assumes you've played many games and probably won't play by the book anyway so it focuses on what's different about this game.

    Both still need to be extensively playtested.

    There are many problems with the above. Is there such thing as a general audience? Is this games similar enough to other games that roleplaying experience can fill in gaps properly to make play functional? But food for thought! I suspect this approach would garner a lot of sales at Gencon, especially with both versions for sale next to each other.

    You could also include an option to upgrade versions! Or make the 2nd version PDF only. You can have a printed copy available for browsing and when people want to buy, just email it them right then and there! With the small size and minimal production, the file size should be minimal.

    \n Yeh, I decided to define micro with a price point cuz it's hard to argue about. I mean, can you imagine if we tried to talk about whether a games content makes it micro?* Jeez.

    *Please please please don't go down that road.

    I think it's been pretty well established at this point that there is a strong PDF market, and that PDF's can be a solid part of a games release (whether as a preview, a parallel product or an always-included bonus). The cool thing about PDF's from the publishing standpoint is that they're essentially free money. It's actually pretty hard to make a print product that's only $5 with general print-on-demand technology, unless the page count is super low - especially once you include shipping. So how is a print micro-published product achieved?

    - Be like Joe and have a low page count + a printer that specializes in the kind of printing he needs.
    - Be like Ben and DIY
    - Do an offset run to get unit costs super low (which is what I think Kevin did with Sweet Agatha, tho it's not micro)
    - Use the print product as a loss leader and compensate with PDF sales. Is anyone doing this? it's counterintuitive, but here's some numbers out of my butt:

    Say I have a 20p game, and I want to print it through, oh, MagCloud. As far as I can tell from not actually having signed up with MagCloud yet, it would be $4.00 to print and $1.40 to ship. Let's assume that for whatever reason I'm fulfilling them myself, and it's important to me to keep to the $5 price point including shipping.

    There's also a PDF version, with additional or exclusive content. It is also $5, and the print version has a clear "buy the PDF and get these extras" sell line. Hypothetical customer goes "Hey, that's cool", clicks to it and spends another $5 for the PDF.

    So:
    $4 (print) + 1.40 (shipping to me) + 1.40 (shipping to customer) = $6.80 - $5 (cover) = -1.80
    $5 (PDF) - ~.50 (paypal fees or whatever - some fraction will go somewhere) = +4.50
    Total profit: $2.70

    So to break even you would need roughly 2 PDF sales for every three print sales.

    Is this worth it? Who knows, but it's an idea.

    \n Also, depends on your sweat equity. With a modern photo printer you could print and saddle-stitch (staple) a decent quality color booklet in your home with a pretty large page count for less than $5 in materials.     \n Note: if you're looking to price based on the same rubrics "larger" (read Luke Crane or Fred Hicks) publishers use, they price their final product at around four to five times the production costs. If memory serves. But that 75-80% covers your other costs -- writing, editing, art, layout, marketing, whatever -- so it's not 75-80% of pure magic profit.
