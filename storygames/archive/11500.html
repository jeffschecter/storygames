\n In the thread on detailed settings there was a nice tangent on using movies to get across the feel of a setting to a group of prospective players in a relatively concise way. This idea would seem to hit my situation well. I don't have a regular gaming group, but I do have a fair number of friends whom I think I could coerce into scheduling a single evening together to do some gaming. This could turn into a handful of followup sessions, but I can't rely on that -- we're all busy, we all have families, and gaming is at best a fourth or fifth priority to most of us.

So the idea is to schedule "movie and a game" nights. Get people together in the afternoon and watch a movie. Break for dinner, over which the game is discussed -- possibly even characters created. Then run a quick session of the game afterward. What I'm looking for are suggestions for good Movie & Game pairings. Ideally I'd like games that can deliver the goods in a single 3-4 hour session. Using pregen characters is an option. The movie doesn't have to match the setting of the game exactly, it's more to get everyone in the same mood quickly. It doesn't even have to be a great movie -- cheese would be completely acceptable.

Bonus points if you can suggest a dinner that fits in well with the rest.

Some ideas that have occurred to me, to get the ball rolling:

Starship Troopers & 3:16
Raging Bull & Contenders
Sky Captain and the World of Tomorrow & Spirit of the Century
The Call of Cthulhu (2005) & Trail of Cthulhu

    \n The Big Lebowski and Fiasco. Jason M would say "Blood Simple" or "A Simple Plan", but for my game I say "The Big Lebowski".

    I've actually done this with my gaming group (well, we did movies one session, and the game the next session) with
    Zatoichi the Outlaw aka Zat\xf4ichi r\xf4yaburi (Netflix has it in their "Zatoichi" link, disk number 16) and Tenra Bansho Zero
    I also showed (after Zatoichi) an episode of the TV series "Hissatsu Shigotonin".

    The reason for Hissatsu Shigotonin specifically was that HS is an ensemble piece with a set pace/plot, a satisfying revenge-drama with cool lines and varied characters, but the costumes, set pieces and design help people imagine what the world looks like (I would have also picked Baian the Assassin for this aspect, too, a great series). I chose Zatoichi, particularly Zatoichi the Outlaw (my fave of the series) because it shows in very basic, easy to understand terms how the "Karma" mechanic of the TBZ game works: Zatoichi making relationships, acts cutting to "one year later", relationships changing, bonus dice, attachment, etc.

    For food, I suggest noodles.

    -Andy
