\n I'm looking for examples of games that are easy to play either via video or chat.
I'm particularly interested in games that have either no randomisers or only use very simple randomisers to resolve conflicts. Examples of short games would be great.

(I feel like there was a thread about this recently, but I haven't been able to find it.) Here's what I've got so far:

\u2022Archipelago / Itras By: uses descriptive resolution cards
\u2022Dream Askew: diceless. Players have areas where they are strong and weak
\u2022Code of Unaris: optimised for text and chat. You edit over previous contributions
\u2022Viewscream: I'm not sure how this works
\u2022A Doomed Pilgrim In The Ruins of the Future: only one person needs rules and they only make simple dice rolls
\u2022Firebrands: uses sheets with limited amounts of moves
\u2022S/Lay w/Me: uses narration 'Goes' and limited dice rolling at the end
\u2022The Parsley games text adventure games

What else is out there that you think is cool or interesting?

    \n I saw that you already has the Parsley games on there, which was my obvious suggestion. I tried to run it on a forum, but I ran out of steam. As the GM you need to keep track of the current game state, and can of course keep notes outside of the game for this, but I just got bogged down in keeping track of everything. The dynamic is very much weighted towards the GM doing a lot of work, since the players mostly write two-word commands, but as long as you're prepared for that it should work. It was also hard to gauge the players' interest in the game, since they only write "GO WEST" and the like, which can of course be solved by a separate out-of-character thread. I just felt that the players' creative input was limited compared to the amount of work I was expected to do as GM. I've considered restarting the forum game, and not mention that they've waited years for the game to respond to the latest input, as I still like the idea of it.

    I was introduced to chat-based B/X D&D by people here on Story Games and I think it worked great. We had a shared Google sheet with PCs, and you need a way of rolling dice preferably in chat so that anyone can see the results. If you do low-level play, it mostly consists of the GM describing things and the players stating their actions or dialogue. Combat is usually short and deadly enough that you don't have long drawn out segments of dice rolling and rules lookup. I found the fact that you had to wait for the full description by the GM very evocative, as I had time to picture what was going on and formulate my response.

    \n I think Ben Robbins' Follow is very easy to play online; basically, you can do it on Google Hangouts and use a shared Google Doc for the little bit of info players refer to in-game and you're good to go.

    There is a very little randomization in the game. Basically, players choose and then draw either red or white tokens 3 times per game (when playing in person); but, you can easily have players simultaneously type 'r' to choose a red token and 'w' to choose a white token, into the chat box. Then just have one of the players draw the tokens everyone chose and report the results to the other players.

    It probably sounds more complicated than it actually is because I've stated the entire process, but it's super easy.

    \n It occurs to me that you could also do something like this:

    Each one contributes a die of the appropriate colour to a pool. Make them really big dice, like d100 or d1000.

    Then roll all the dice. The highest result is the one that is "drawn" from the bag.

    Now you can do "draw from a bag" with an online dice roller.

    I'm looking for examples of games that are easy to play either via video or chat.
    \nIt made me laugh yesterday that this discussions is for me a bit linked to this one, because we had this conversation on our Czech roleplaying discussion server before Christmas. It was more focused on principles that good play-by-post games and via-video played games should have, so I can't unfortunately share a list of suitable games.

    In the debate we identified conflicts as worst time-consuming and obstructing element of play-by-post game. Even in Fate, which is mechanically pretty simple, you need to confront the obstacle, wait until Storyteller sets difficulty (or rolls against you), then you can roll yourself\u2026 and then you can say what happened. Time-consuming. As a solution we agreed on reducing the need of exchange between player and Storyteller in conflict. It is a place where there is potential to speed thing up and make the play more fluent. Example for us was Apocalypse World where you always roll against set number \u2013 you don\xb4t need the Storyteller to set you difficulty. Second thing was interpretation of result. In a lot of games, Storyteller does that\u2026 but waiting for him to show up (when you already know if you succeeded or not) just doesn't make sense. Solution could be to let players interpret their own successes and let them continue their story not to slow thing down. Storyteller steps in only when players fail to succeed, makes life more complicated for them a bit and then pass point again to them.

    Based on this discussion, we (my brother and me) made a play-by-post game in epistolary format inspired by Dracula, whose mechanics are something in between Apocalypse World basic mechanic and Lady Blackbird. It was based on aforementioned principles (\u201cset the difficulty yourselves\u201d and \u201cplay until you miss or you have no more good ideas\u201d). So far seems to be doing the job well enough.
