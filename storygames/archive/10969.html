    \n If you are angling for an "old school" version of D&D, you've got three major choices, in order of rules engagement and fiddliness.

    a) Original D&D (aka "0e" aka "The 1974 rules"). The three little brown books. The rules tell you how to hit things & die, cast spells, search for secret doors and bash them open, and make reaction roles. The rest you make up as you go. Very open ended, highest requirement on the GM & players to "fly by the seat of your pants." A difficult text at best.

    Your white label alternative: Swords & Wizardry

    b) Basic/Expert D&D (aka "Red Box" aka "Moldvay" aka "Mentzer"). Red/Blue soft covered books that came in 9x12" boxes. Middle weight rules engagement, most procedural things nailed down, but still a lot of wiggle room. Well written & approachable.

    Your white label alternative: Labyrinth Lord

    c) AD&D (aka "1e" aka "The original hardbacks"). Lots of fiddly detail and tons of mechanical engagement. Lots of stuff nailed down. Tons of strange and baroque subsystems. Written in High Gygaxian.

    Your white label alternative: OSRIC

    My own suggestion would be either:

    a) OD&D via Swords & Wizardry if you want rules light, fast teaching time, lots of fiat, and wild & woolly. You'll want to study Philotomy's OD&D Musings in advance to understand what the issues are. The Quick Start is actually pretty awesome, it gets you generating characters and in the dungeon in 15 minutes, and lots of handholding for the GM.

    b) Basic, either by laying your hands on some original Red Box, or downloading Labyrinth Lord. Slightly higher teaching time, but less obligation on the GM to make it all up.

    Oh yeah, and in any case, you'll want to check out Matt Finch's Old School Primer to get into the mindset. And yeah, start with the Rules As Written, and house rule as necessary once you actually understand what is working for you, and what isn't.

    \n I've been running a labyrinth lord game. The players enjoy it, but as we reach the higher levels (the 8-12 range), things start to break down a bit, and a lot of the unfairness inherent in the system starts to grind upon us. Halflings are stuck with no abilities ever, wizards dominate everything, you've heard it all before.

    Don't get me wrong, it's still fun but don't be afraid to throw some stuff in to fix un-fun-ness, because some will arise.

    \n I'm playing Red Box DnD now, and my GM has expressed his frustration that there isn't any sort of conflict resolution system that he could use for, say, chases.

    If you just use turn by turn movement rates, you can never catch somebody, and the normal fudge-it system for tasks is roll 1d6, get a 1 or 2 to succeed.

    So be on the lookout for that.

    (I suggested the roll d20, get under a stat solution, as well as Chaosium's Resistance table, where you have a 50% chance if stats are equal, and plus/minus 5% for each point of difference. Dunno what he'll choose, especially since monsters don't have stats.)

    \nThis is an area I had some confusion with in the past. Original Dungeons & Dragons was the first stuff Gygax and Arneson came out with, and it's also called 0E D&D. The three booklets, the white box, that sort of thing. Then Advanced D&D (1E) was released, but it was too complicated for some people so Mentzer (I think) simplified the rules (for one thing, races became classes) and released Basic, Expert, Companion, Master and Immortal sets (Basic and Expert were then compiled into the Rules Cyclopedia). This is Basic D&D or B/E D&D (Basic/Expert), because it was a simplified version of Advanced D&D.

    Advanced D&D was, of course, a more complex version of Original D&D which was a role-playing game that half-used the rules of Chainmail, a miniatures game.

    Then there was Advanced D&D 2E, then Advanced was dropped for D&D 3E and 4E.
