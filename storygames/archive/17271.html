\n Hey, so Cthulhu Dark is a great little game. I especially like how tightly it focuses on Insanity and the emergent play (and attendant complications) that comes out of the investigators' need to try to keep a lid on Insanity.

Let's think up some cool ways to hack Insanity for other games. You have to have three things: first, what will replace Insanity and what triggers a check; second, what characters may do to try to reduce the thematic stat (note: reducing the thematic stat should force the players to complicate things-- you get a chance to reduce Insanity, but only if you hinder the investigation by destroying evidence, burying leads, actively misdirecting or even attacking the other investigators, etc.); and third, what it means fictionally when you risk the thematic stat (sometimes this will be obvious, sometimes not).

I'll post a couple to start things off. But first, one thread rule: let's leave hacks for combat (like, Harm, or something) out of this and instead focus on the thematic stat.

    \nRage
    Use Rage (or the Beast, or whatever) for a game which highlights monstrous fury (e.g. Werewolf).
    * Roll to increase Rage when you are insulted, harmed, or suffer a setback or loss (literally or metaphorically).
    * Reduce Rage by acting out violently, especially when it is out of proportion to the circumstances (e.g. by beating someone for \u201clooking the wrong way\u201d at your girlfriend, by killing a useful NPC for a minor failure, or by unnecessarily making enemies with rash words and threats).
    * Risk Rage when you are willing to accept violent collateral damage to get your way.

    Infection
    Use Infection for a zombie survival kind of game.
    * Roll to increase Infection when you are harmed by a zombie or are otherwise exposed to the source of the disease.
    * Reduce Infection by selfishly (and perhaps irrationally and unnecessarily) abandoning others to their fate in order to try to keep yourself safe and healthy.
    * Risk Infection when your own health and safety is less important than accomplishing your goal (e.g. to rescue a loved one).

    Treason
    Use Treason for a game of Paranoia.
    * Roll to increase Treason when you engage in Treasonous activities (e.g. interacting with cultists, plotting to overthrow the Friend Computer) or when you are accused of Treason.
    * Reduce Treason by implicating, framing, and accusing others of treasonous and banned activities.
    * Risk Treason when you are willing to do anything, even if illegal, to succeed.


    How about some others? Wild West? Victorian romance and upper class social maneuvering? Spies and espionage? Heist-gone-wrong? Dungeon delving?

    \nHeat
    Use Heat for a heist-gone-wrong game inspired by Michael Mann's Heat (1995).
    * Roll to increase Heat when you engage in criminal activities (e.g. robbing banks, killing cops) or when you are accused of crimes.
    * Reduce Heat by going to ground and laying low, or shacking up with someone you're close to.
    * Risk Heat when you take the money and run, come face-to-face with the police, or come out guns blazing.

    "Don't let yourself get attached to anything you are not willing to walk out on in 30 seconds flat if you feel the heat around the corner."

    \nLove

    Use a separate Love die for each lover whom you really shouldn't be in love with.

    * Roll to increase Love when your lover interacts with you, either positively or negatively.
    * Reduce Love by actively avoiding your lover. (The GM should take this as a cue to pursue you with entreaties or place an even less suitable lover in your path).
    * Risk Love when you call on the lover's aid.
    * When Love reaches 6, you are hopelessly in love. Run to your lover and throw yourself on their mercy.

    - You choose a name, class and alignment for your character

    - You always win fighting monsters below your level.
    - You always loose fighting monsters above your level.

    - Risk to increase level when you act according to your class or alignment

    - At 6 you are the most badass an probably can go retire

    \nCover-Use for games where the characters are spies or policemen inserted into a hostile situation under an assumed identity.

    Inspirations: Wish Me Luck, Donnie Brasco, Reilly: Ace of Spies

    * Roll to increase whenever you directly interact with any NPC with a direct interest in sniffing out people just like you
    * Risk whenever you want to succeed and you have to show off skills, knowledge, or background inconsistent with your cover identity
    * Reduce by one pip by narratively describing how someone close to your or an innocent suffers some great harm because of your activities
    * Reduce to currently available minimum by describing how your superiors pull you back to safety. Each time you do this, your minimum goes up one. For example, the first time you do this, Cover is reduced to 1. The second time you do this, Cover is reduced to 2, and so on.
    * When your Cover reaches 6, narratively describe at first opportunity the bad end your character comes to off screen. Going out in a blaze of glory is not allowed.

    \nAsshole

    For when you play an inquisitor at any kind that are bound to protect the people by following your belief. (I've only played Dogs once, but this are my thoughts from that great session.)

    * Roll to increase Asshole when you can't help damaging a person physically, mentally, economically or socially. For example, using force to reach your goal, destroying something for another person or play people against each other.
    * Roll to reduce Asshole by putting your own belief aside to help other people.
    * Risk Asshole when you are willing to overpower the people that you are trying to protect to solve a problem.

    \xd7 Your belief is about cleansing the world from blasphemy, sodomy, prostitution, drinking, abuse of children and women and to convert any non-believers. (I have probably forgot a lot of stuff, so fill in the gaps.)
    \xd7 Any person you interact with will reveal a secret of some sort about the village that you're trying to cleanse.
    \xd7 When you are about to reach 6 in Asshole, you start to question your own faith. When you reach it, you renounce your faith and your profession.

    \nComprehension
    You don't really get it, but you are starting to. Things are not what you assumed. You assumed you'd come home a hero, but signs are pointing to something else. They are sending what's left of you to a hospital far away from everything and everyone.

    * Roll to increase Comprehension when you are confronted with evidence that the conventional narrative is bullshit.
    * Reduce Comprehension when you are medicated or manufacture evidence supporting your assumptions.
    * Risk Comprehension when you accept, in principle, what you are being told.

    When you reach 6, you completely understand what's going on and your place in it. That can't be good.

    \n What if we inverted it sort of...

    Stamina starts at 6.
    * When you do some kind of prolonged physically strenuous activity (running, parkour, fighting), roll 1d6. If the result is equal to or above your Stamina, you lose 1 point of Stamina.
    * Increase Stamina by 1 when you take a few minutes to catch your breath. Set it back to 6 when you rest for a day.
    * Risk Stamina when you strain yourself and push yourself beyond your limits.

    EDIT: It would be kinda interesting to tag this on to an AW-hack (like Dead Weight comes to mind since I just rediscovered it). As an example, instead of rolling Stamina as it's own thing, maybe it's a way to push yourself when you're making a Move.

    When you need to haul ass, roll+quick. On a 10+ you gain distance or catch up. On a 7-9, choose 1:
    - You maintain your pace.
    - You gain distance/catch up, but you need to keep your shit together or leave something behind.
    On a miss, you lose distance or fall behind and the GM will make a hard move as usual

    You can Push Yourself by gambling a Stamina point. When you Push Yourself, add another d6 to your roll and take the best two dice of the three. If your Stamina die rolls equal to or above your current Stamina, you lose 1 point of Stamina.

    That way, if you have high stamina, you only lose the stamina point if your stamina die rolls high. But as your stamina dwindles, even a low roll can cause you to get winded and lose a stamina point.

    \nThe Hunger
    You're an addict. You need your fix. You need it bad. But the more you use, the more the hunger in you grows. And so you need it even more.

    Your Hunger starts at 1. Whenever you are tempted, offered drugs, or experience times of great stress, you roll your Hunger die. If you get higher than your current Hunger, your temptation overwhelms you and you gotta get a fix right now. Increase your Hunger by 1. Otherwise, you're keeping it under control, for now.

    If your need for a fix spurs you to action, then you can roll your Hunger die as part of an action. If your Hunger die is higher than any other die, make a Hunger roll.

    When you reach 5 Hunger, you can try to Kick the Habit. But you gotta go cold turkey: you can't even be around other people shooting up without rolling your Hunger. When exposed to stress, drugs or temptation, roll your Hunger die. If you get less than your current Hunger, you can lower your Hunger by one (and still keep rolling to lower your Hunger).

    If you ever reach Hunger six, then your habit has destroyed your life. You are taken out of the game, in a manner that is up to you. Do you overdose? Inject some drugs cut with enough drain cleaner to kill you? Get caught by the cops and put away for life?


    (PS: To make it into a game about vampires, simply add "When you use your vampiric powers, make a Hunger roll.")

    \nDespair
    (NB: this was originally intended for a zombie apocalypse like setting)
    Start with 1. Name 4 things you hope for (finding a loved one, protecting someone important to you, finding a new, safe place to live etc.) 5th and final hope is your own survival.

    Roll to increase Despair when you encounter atrocities of the zombie apocalypse around you.
    Increase Despair, when one of your active hopes can no longer be fulfilled.
    Risk Despair, when you actively pursue your hopes.
    Reduce Despair by neglecting the threats of the zombie apocalypse by acting "normally".

    When you increase Despair due to a roll, you lose one of your hopes.
    When you reduce Despair you get one of your hopes back or get a new one.
    (NB: the number of hopes should always be 6 minus Despair)

    When you hit 6 Despair your character loses his 5th and final hope: to survive. You get to describe how they end it all.

    Or you could say this: At Hunger 1, you are a casual user. You don't require regular fixes. At Hunger 2, you need to get at least once a month, or you suffer withdrawal (insert withdrawal effects component here). At Hunger 3, you need a dose once a week. At 4, you need it every day. This also pushes the action toward the interesting part of the game, as your addicts slide quickly into depravity, then teeter on the edge of self-destruction.

    I like what you have so far with Dark Ronin. I think that does capture a lot of the feel of ronin-themed samurai films, particularly like The Samurai trilogy or Lone Wolf and Cub.
    My only suggestions at this point are just some areas needing clarification:
    -Make sure to mention that you roll your Face die (specifically) if you are willing to risk Face in your action, not just "a die"
    -Under social situations, you mention that if the Face die is lowest then you must roll your Face die to see if you lost a point, whereas originally you said it was if the Face die was the highest result. Considering how you regain Face, you may consider keeping it as the lowest die.
    -Under Failing, you mention that the opposing roll must be "lower" than your roll to make it fail. Shouldn't it be "higher"?
    -I like the way you do duels. I wouldn't change a thing there.

    And one possible change:
    -For improving Face, instead of it be from winning a Duel or dramatic social challenge, maybe making it a significant duel or social challenge. Or perhaps (tying it in with rolling the Face die), when, if you include your Face die in your original roll, if it is the highest result, choose to use the next highest result instead to gain a point of Face (assuming this doesn't make you fail).

    With those minor modifications, I'd play/run Dark Ronin at the drop of a hat.

      \n I was immediately struck by the brilliance of Cthulhu Dark, and this thread (especially Deep) convinced me that it would be the perfect system for classic Noir storytelling, my favourite genre. Now I can't imagine using anything else for my games - once you go Dark, you can't go back.

      Noir

      * Roll to increase Noir when you disrupt the dark order of things by asking questions that should not be asked, or simply by being in the wrong place at the wrong time.
      * Risk Noir when you are willing to make a new enemy, leave fingerprints on a crime scene, reveal your secret weakness.
      * Reduce Noir by giving one of your enemies what he/she wants, letting goons knock you out, destroying evidence, betraying a friend or an employer, chasing love away.

      When Noir hits 6, your fate is sealed. Spend what little time you have left to reflect on what could have been. You can also try to take down the bad guy or save your friends in a last burst of heroism - you will probably fail, but who knows ?

      \n I'm mulling how to make Revolution Dark, for playing out scenarios of massively out-gunned and out-numbered average citizens taking on monolithic bastions of oppressive power. France in 1943, England under the Tripods (or Daleks), New York under the secret Vampire Clans.

      I'm thinking of using three factors, Harm, Heat and Hope. I'd like to have them interact with each other.
      Harm is much like Insanity in base CD, except for representing both mental and physical strain. Harm can be lowered by lying low in a safehouse or with friends or family, but doing so puts the safehouse at risk, greater risk the greater the current Heat.
      Heat was explained earlier in this Discussion. I'm thinking there should only be a single Heat die for the entire table, and it should be volatile. If Heat reaches 6, a character is lost (PC or NPC).
      Hope is another single-die, and the goal is to get it up to 6, representing a solid blow against the oppressors.
      If I can mix it right, players should end up accumulating Harm and raising Heat trying to trigger Hope.

      \n More ideas for Heat, for an Insurrection-themed game:
      Any and all characters can risk Heat in a scene and action, but its cumulative to the single tracking die, so Heat can jump up fast if the characters get sloppy or desperate.
      At the start of play, each lead character gets to name two support characters, contacts who can help or shelter them (which lets them reduce Harm). Each of these support characters' names is written on a slip, then all the slips are mixed in a bag or box. Also, an equal number of blank slips are mixed in. Each time Heat reaches 6, the players narrate the resulting crackdown and assign it a number from 1 to 5. First, Heat is lowered by the chosen number. Second, a number of slips equal to the chosen number are drawn randomly. If a support character's name is drawn, they're narrated as having been caught and disappeared by the authorities. Also, because the support character is interrogated or the authorities can draw connections through them, a slip with the name of the lead character the support character was attached to is added to the box. If, on subsequent crackdowns, a lead character's name gets drawn then they narrate how the oppressors eliminate them. I suppose then the player promotes a support character to lead status. Also, leads can gain more support character's during play, whose names will be added to the box.
      So ... that process should create a situation where early on players are more willing risking Heat, but then the buffer erodes, the authorities start getting closer and things get more desperate.

      NickWedig mentioned trying to use other types of dice, and that got me wondering about changing up the dangerous die types like Heat to make them more tempting for the players. Problem is, fewer sides (d4) makes the die weaker, while more sides (d8+) makes the condition they represent more stable ... unless you flip the way results are read, so 1 is the best result, 6 is the worst. Then a d4, more likely to roll a 1, becomes a powerful boost, while also being more volatile than a d6.

      \n I tend to be pretty improvisational when GMing, so my scenarios would go like this: you travel to the near future on a minor errand of some sort, only to see a newspaper report of your own deaths during a bank robbery gone wrong. You can't change that this happens, so what do you do?

      Very quickly, the PCs realized that they only had to fake the own deaths, not actually die. So they set up the bank robbery and were present as both the robbers (in ski masks) and as victims, but that was all their plans at that point.
