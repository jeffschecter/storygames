\nI launched a Kickstarter for my new high-tech, hard-boiled roleplaying game today. It's called Technoir. So cyberpunk, but with more of an emphasis on provoking your enemies and tangled conspiracies. It certainly counts Lady Blackbird and Fiasco among its influences. And you'll see some mechanics stolen and evolving out of my previous game, Chronica Feudalis, as well.

And definitely check out the Kickstarter video, it was a lot of fun to put together.

If you're interested about the game itself, it's currently in open beta playtesting mode. All the documents needed are available for download at TechnoirRPG.com.

    \n Thanks, Judd.

    The game has three basic stats verbs (abilities or skills), adjectives (positive adjectives are traits, negative adjectives are wounds) and objects (your gear). Verbs are rated 1-5 for a simple d6 dice pool, keep highest mechanic. You add in extra dice when you use your adjectives and your object's tags. Each verb has an icon for easy identification.

    When a roll is successful, you inflict an adjective on your target. And that's the key to the game. It's all about how you change the narrative by the adjectives you inflict.

    Plot nodes are where the Fiasco influence comes in. So far I have Transmission booklets (like playsets) for the Twin Cities and Los Angeles, but more are on their way. Each Transmission includes 36 plot nodes: contacts, events, factions, locations, objects, and threats. You randomly roll them and use them to create an evolving node map as you play. Each time you hit up one of your connections (contacts) for info or favors, they randomly generate new nodes they tell you about or somehow connect themselves deeper into the existing map. The nodes by themselves don't do a whole lot, but as you connect them and relate them to other nodes, story starts to emerge. They seed content for the GM and quickly create these twisted conspiracies.

    \n Not to pick on you, Jeremy, but could you discuss your decision to go with a softcover book?

    As a consumer I find that if I'm interested enough in a game to make a purchase that I'm interested in only pdf and hardback. Softcover books age poorly and really start to look abused even if they've been treated with care. I don't buy many rpgs, but when I do I'm willing to spend a few more dollars to get a product thats printed as a sturdy hardback. If a game is short enough that I don't think I'll mind reading it as a pdf then I may only buy the pdf. I'll grudgingly buy a softcover book if I'm super stoked about the game since I do prefer a physical copy, and it seems like it has become more common for games to only be printed as softcover.

    I own and have played Chronica Feudalis, and I dig your design aesthetic. Technoir is one of those games that has me grudgingly putting in for a softcover. But I'm willing, like with Diaspora, to go a little farther for a hardcover. Any chance of that becoming an option?

    \n Lots of things have been clarified. If there was a point of confusion you had before, definitely check over that section again and see if it's cleared up.

    Here's some important things. Mind you I've been working on this revision over the last couple of weeks, so I'm sure I'll forget stuff.

    EXPOSITION

    \u2022 The three exposition paragraphs are completely rewritten.

    INITIATION

    \u2022 I worked on player buy-in and GM responsibilities section a lot. Trying to manage expectations well with this.

    GENERATION

    \u2022 There are actual names to pick from in the Generation chapter.

    \u2022\xa0I actually say that you start with three Push dice in the last step of Generation.

    \u2022 I removed the flincy adjective. I have no idea what flincy means.

    \u2022 You can now declare the benefit of being high from the deal favor after you roll.

    \u2022 I got rid of Interface duds. Instead I just added its gesture input tag to the specks. Specks are now a one-stop shop for using the Interface.

    CONTENTION

    \u2022 I clarified some stuff about recharging Push at the beginning of an action. I clarified that the Push dice you spend to increase adjective severity are any Push dice in the roll that weren't eliminated by Hurt. It's okay if they were part of your result.

    \u2022 I added a "set-up" move that lest you inflict positive adjectives on the environment you can use later.

    \u2022\xa0I talk about putting contentions on hold.

    \u2022 I added a section on the outcomes of contentions and how the consequences should be determined by the adjectives you inflict and not by pre-determined stakes.

    \u2022 I clarified some in the Push Dice Economy section. This is a pretty important section to read.

    \u2022 I added running examples for the Contention chapter.

    RESTORATION

    \u2022 I added an option for individuals to reset their push dice buy playing out a scene that exemplifies one of their relationship adjectives.

    \u2022 I wrote an overview of all the advancement options as they otherwise get burried inside of other subsystems.

    \u2022 I added extended the running example through the Restoration chapter.
