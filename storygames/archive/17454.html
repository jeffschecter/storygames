\n So BurningCon this weekend was a lot of fun. One exciting aspect was what I believe was the public debut of Thor Olavsrud and BWHQ's Dungeoneers and Dragonslayers. I played in Michael Prescott's session of the game on Saturday.

What the game is, from what I could tell, is Mouse Guard hacked into supporting the sort of highly principled OD&D dungeon crawling that Luke and Eero have been posting about to everyone's delight this year: dwindling resources, desperate choices, and deadly combats.

A few things:
- The game started with a little bit of "shopping", but Michael limited us to buying food and light sources, having already equipped us with weapons and armor. Characters in Ds&Ds have limited carrying capacity. You have two hands, each of which can hold a thing like a weapon or a torch or a small sack. Small sacks can hold two things. You have three torso slots; armor takes up at least one. You can use one torso slot on a satchel that has three slots or two torso slots on a backpack with six slots. Et cetera.
- Your light and food will be drained. Things didn't get severe in our con session, but you get hungry every four turns (I believe the turn structure is similar to Mouse Guard, where a "turn" is a test for the group) and your light sources last 2-4 turns each and provide light for 1-3 characters.
- I made perhaps the single most fun dice roll of my life on the first turn when my halfling thief used his bow on a scripted Maneuver, suppressing the enemy orcs with the help of my comrades. I rolled like six dice and tons of stuff happened, it was fantastic. I've not played Mouse Guard, but the conflict-scripting when applied to D&D combat makes for a spirited and whiff-free experience.

So that's some of what's going on with this thing. It's still cooking, and I wouldn't expect Thor or Luke to comment much about it on the internet yet, but maybe some other folks who've played can chime in about this thing and how cool it is. Especially someone who played in a game where there was some mapping, because ours didn't require it.

    \n I was the GM in Michael Prescott's game. Glad you two had fun!

    The game is very gamey - there's literally no reprieve from struggling through the various optimization problems it presents you with. At a story level, this is one of the things I love about it, the way that the challenges of dungeoneering are so tangible that the players themselves find themselves making hard choices without any prompting from me.

    Me: "Finally, you come to the cavemouth, this could be it! The pit of sorrows! Do you enter, brave adventurers?"

    Player: "Just a second. What are we going to do about Tomas' leg? If we camp here, I could try to mend it while you guys forage. How many torches do you have, Randall? I have some oil, but after losing the lantern in the stream we're now reliant on you and Ernst, and he needs a hand free for his bow. Can anyone carry this large sack?"

    In preparing for the con, I was faced with a choice: do I set up the characters as a newly formed set of players might, or do I bake in all the cheaty expert-player tactics (those I know of) in as instincts?

    There are tons of things that players can do to improve their odds that aren't necessarily obvious at the outset. These are really cool, but because many invoke neat little economies of various sorts sprinkled through the time, camping and skills rules, I felt it would drive up explanation time.

    John's right, the last part was a slog. Normally there's a lovely tension of "How far can we push it?" - bearing in mind you still have to get home, even if you make it out of the dungeon.

    My crew, however, wound up trapped inside the dungeon owing to a "Wait.. where are you standing?" oversight. As the boulder crashed into place, it dawned on me that is the single most dangerous event that can happen in the game, as far as I know. My guys were barely alive as it was!

    Otoh I am having some trouble piecing together the game logic through which players might naturally discover the mechanism. It's sort of there -- intents can encompass the whole party (I lead with Dungeoneer with the intent of getting the party safely down this hole) but it is IMO much less clear that you can have an intent with such an explicit mechanical benefit (I make merry with the intent to alleviate this mechanical condition that several in our party exhibit).

    What might be missing is the AW-style permission/expectation of fictional and mechanical back-and-forth. Things like "if the party is acting scared and overcareful, they get the Afraid condition." That totally wouldn't work in Ds & Ds for various reasons, but IMO that is the logical construct necessary for the secret Nature rules to be awesome or reasonable or consistent. But the game is very tightly and mechanically tactical, condition management is vital to player/character success, and withholding tools from the players doesn't seem to fit.

    Thor says his three playtest groups have figured out how to make merry, and I believe him. My two groups did not, and I don't know if Michael's group did either. In the end it probably won't matter much. I mean the rule is still a rule, and I think in this day and age it won't be hard to run into this online.

    What might be fun to see is an invitation to GMs to allow or create even more "secret rules" for Nature descriptor use. Once the players know it's possible, you might see a lot more experimentation. My dwarf adventurer, for example, might... I don't know, deliver a stirring speech about avenging our party against a nasty foe and alleviate Anger conditions in the party via his Nature's avenge a grudge descriptor.

      \n It's kinda perverse that this game looks slated to go down in history as "Isn't that the game with the secret rules?" since basically all of the rules are out in the open. All the neat little skill synergies are out in the open, most of the recovery rules are on the actual character sheet, and there's nothing secret about fight strategies. It takes a few times out before the party starts gaming lighting, camping, and instincts. Other than, I suppose, the monsters, everything is totally in the players' view.

      The one exception is a few paragraphs on page 107. They're labelled "Secret Nature Rules", which is only partly correct since they're not even complete rules: unlike all the other skill rules, there's no list of factors, so the GM just has to invent an obstacle. It's much as if the section had said, "Carpentry can be used to make siege engines," and left it at that.

      This is not a game about secret rules.

      Having said that, this is obviously a bit of a PR pothole. In a con scenario, none of the players know the any rules - they're all being spoon-fed whatever the GM thinks is important (in my game, we skipped over a lot of useful things the abilities can do, in the hopes of having an adventure with a representative subset). So when the game designer pulls out a tactic that's somehow 'doubly invisible', which super-effectively addresses the central threat of the game (accumulating conditions), it comes across as really alienating.

      If Thor hadn't used the word 'secret', this conversation wouldn't be happening. Nobody would be any the wiser that the suggestion for merrymaking is on page 107 and not page 38.       \n Tracking resources is remarkably easy, and the timing is tracked by the GM. All the PCs need to do is tick off the appropriate number of torches/candles/oil flasks/rations/wineskins, and figure out who's holding the light. It's always a kick in the gut when the torches burn out, and you need to light another set, and see the numbers dwindle in your pack. That's what really got me when I played. Light sources, as I recall, vary in duration between 2 and 4 tests, and illuminate 1-4 people. Those are tests for the whole group. Whenever anyone makes a test, everyone's stomach growls, and the light dims a little. Rations and light can also be lost as failure consequences. The pressure is incredible, and its really easy to go a bit too far, though there are mechanisms to protect you from getting stuck... if you spend the resources to activate them. Its especially brutal when you only have two PCs, because your carrying capacity is reduced, there's less of a chance that any of the PCs will have resource substitution abilities (like light spells, the ability to cook rations to stretch them further, or abilities that help remove conditions... like Merrymaking) and its entirely possible you won't be able to carry out enough treasure (assuming you find something halfway valuable) to pay for the resources you've expended. A friend and I joked we ought to set up a business selling torches =P

      But I bet we'd have to join a guild. Back to the dungeon!


      When the pressure's turned on, it's a really intense, fun game. First time I played, the two of us made it back with one torch remaining, a couple of rations, and some shitty silver plate. We'd lost some other gear too... I think we dropped my theif's tools to get some treasure, lost a helmet in a fight, and Tim's wineskin tore while we were clambering over a rotten wooden barricade. But we had scouted it all out, learned a lot, and when two new players joined, with their packs full of gear... we lost no time in exploiting them!

      \n In our run with the system we played two adventures over five sessions. At the start, I only had two players, a Halfling and a Wizard (sporting Eldritch Darts, an offensive spell). Given that I've been playing a pile of BW over the last few years, planning the dungeon was a nostalgic throwback to the pre-planned (and seldom used) dungeons of my role-playing infancy, tons of fun.

      From the GM's chair, the game is really fascinating - unlike BW where I'm constantly wracking my brain to ride the ebb and flow of the action, knitting together my sketchily planned challenges with whatever actually happens in the session, this was like a wind-up toy: give the players a little push, and suddenly they're in a detailed debate about who's got a free hand for the lantern; do they have enough food to continue further; should they stop here or press on; is it too risky given that Tomas is Exhausted? Even if they turn back now, will they survive the weather between here and Kjornig? After a few sessions, it was apparent that the players were masters of elements of the game that I only had an observer's view to, which is an eerie feeling.

      The game is heavily MG-based, as I think most know at this point - and it makes some really good additions to Conflict. Team members drop out of combat as the disposition drops, so combats end up with a slightly more desperate feel than they did when all hands could fight until the last. Monsters have pre-calculated dispositions for different conflict intents, which is super simple but has some cool effects.

      Perhaps taking a page from *World games, monsters never set conflict goals, the type of conflict is always determined by the players (as long as it's an appropriate option given the narrative). Most monsters are substantially easier to drive off than to kill outright, so the net upshot of this is that while players are regularly in deeply over their heads, it's unlikely for them to actually die unless they've decided deliberately (or desperately) that killing is on the table. I had some fun with this, as I made a zombie monster that was much easier to kill than to drive off.

      Much has been said about the inventory system. It's very cool, though it does lead to quite a bit of erasing. Unlike BW (which just isn't about encumbrance), it really matters whether you have a spare hand for that lantern. This leads to a fair bit of erasing - I was seriously considering dry-erase or magnetic/label character sheets at one point.

      My favorite scene from the whole playtest centered around inventory - the players had fought their way into the crypt master's flooded sanctuary. He seemed dormant for the time being, but the players could hear the swish of endless crypt servants moving to trap them, giving them mere moments to loot the place. This led to a desperate scene, hastily discussing whether they could survive without .. tinderbox? Can we do without that? Can you carry this urn? I can't.. or.. well, if I drop these torches. How many do you have? Is that enough to make it back to the surface?

      Ds&Ds has a very different 'turn sequence' than MG. My main complaint about MG was that I occasionally had to start a GM's turn even when the pace seemed totally wrong for me to start beating up the players. I recall one moment in a MG game where my players (who had escaped a losing battle by leaping into a river of icy meltwater) had every condition checked off, were lost, missing important gear. They tried to patch themselves up, failed, and then it was my turn again. Ugh.

      Ds&Ds' turn sequence, however, is much more player driven. Players earn checks not to pursue their own agenda, but to Camp - a reprieve from the turn count that's ever gnawing at their bellies and using up their light sources. Checks are still a wholly metagame concept, but it works, and the pace feels more natural.

      The turn cycle, incidentally, is utterly relentless. Returning to town isn't any reprieve, either - and while it's the place to go to recover, it's hideously expensive. A simple but clever way of working out your lifestyle costs forces you to consider every piece of town business carefully. Is it worth it?

      In the final tally, my players had very mixed feelings about it. One player had grown very attached to his character and was reluctant to stop, others were sick of the inventory system, which recedes a bit once you get used to it, but still involves lots of erasing.

      The game yearns for a clearer avenue for players to direct their own fates. The overall relentlessness and the tightly interlocking systems (the way that food, foraging and cooking work, for instance, are extremely clever) made it hard to see how the game was going to transcend the 'take whatever adventuring hook you can' paradigm of the indiscriminate adventurer. There's a labor-of-love 'town events' table that I (in the end) abandoned completely, as I found it hard to incorporate random encounters and have the game feel coherent. (Personally, I find it a lot easier to ad lib based on an evolving pressure, such as the successively worse symptoms of growing unemployment or pressure from a nearby pack of marauding orcs.)

      Anyways, when it comes out, definitely play it!

      \n I have thought a lot about the idea of "secret rules" since I read this post. I haven't played any demos of Ds&Ds so this is really just theoretical.

      I think the upside of having secret rules is that they may help a player experience the same sense of wonder from a fortunate or hard-fought discovery that a PC would feel. What they allow for is a sense of learning about the way things might work, which (in my experience) is often present in the fiction and rarely in the mechanics. It doesn't always have to be player ingenuity that unlocks these secret rules, either. For example, if an elf PC has an instinct to "sing when times are darkest" the character's in-game actions may help the player discover things about the way game works.

      This is common in video games. You essentially know what each button does but you don't always know how certain weapons will affect certain foes, or how different ingredients might interact; that is why game guides and walk-throughs are 300+ pages.

      There is a risk of player frustration but there is also an opportunity for something special to happen; the player and their character learning about the game world at the same time and seeing that reflected in the fiction.

      \n I've been thinking about this more and more, and I think I've come to the conclusion that what I like the most about DsnDs is that the conflict system's stakes are symmetrical, and that different monsters are better at dealing with different stakes. I've been looking at the FATE Core PDF from the kickstarter, and it's really neat, but one thing that's always driven me crazy about FATE, and for that matter Green Ronin's A Song of Ice and Fire, is how you take lasting damage in conflicts, and that's intended to convince you to concede out of the conflict. In the BWHQ games, once you start a conflict, you're locked in, and you have to play balls to the walls to win. Once it's over, you work out the damage that's been done (Fight and Range and Cover are glaring exceptions to this rule, but both are only really broken out when the stakes are DEATH). And I find that way more satisfying. In DsnDs, it's streamlined by requiring you to stake what you want to get - if you want to kill the other guys, well, you need to risk death yourself. If you're only trying to drive them off, then worst comes to worst, you have to retreat instead. It's really elegant, and reminds me of a system Fuseboy worked up for mass combat, where the scope of your intent modified your disposition: If you're trying to rout the enemy army, while they're just trying to delay you, they get more narrative hitpoints because they aren't trying to do as much. I wonder if that could be ported over, for the times when it can't really be symmetrical. I mean, the fallout of being captured by zombies is death, so a Capture conflict isn't quite symmetrical.
