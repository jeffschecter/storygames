\n Judd's use of the term "turtling" in this thread reminded me how uncomfortable it makes me anytime somebody uses it. Along with jargon like "rules lawyer" and "munchkin" I feel like it slaps a overly-simplistic and pejorative label on a complex set of behaviors. I've been around conversations that went along the lines of "and when this happened, that player fucking turtled" and they bother me.

I don't think anyone shows up to a game, especially a convention game, planning on holding back and refusing to engage with the rules, the game content, or the other players at the table. I think people show up wanting to engage with the game and their fellow players. When people "turtle" then, it's because there are obstacles or issues that are preventing them from being able to comfortably engage with what's going on. But I often get the sense that "turtling" is supposedly the fault of the unengaged player. Like, here is everybody else, bringing all the awesome, and what the fuck is wrong with the guy who looks uncomfortable and isn't in here getting muddy up to his elbows with the rest of us?

Especially when a lot of indie games expect a level of player pro-activity and involvement in decision making that may seem rather alien to folks from other gaming backgrounds, I think the expectation that everyone will be able to jump in and start playing "our way" is blatantly unfair. Is it not worth our time to help people who don't instantly connect with a game to find a way in? Even at convention games, I feel like players have a responsibility to make sure everyone else at the table is having a good time and feels involved. And sometimes that means going above and beyond the call of duty, making the game measurably less fun for everyone else, in order to get a reluctant or overwhelmed player to engage. I just feel like that should be part of the social contract when a group of people sit down at a table to play together.

For example, at Story Games Boston, we play a large number of games that require heavy player involvement. One of the biggest obstacles for newcomers and even some regulars is rotating scene framing. Many people, even after playing indie games for a while, would much rather have someone else frame a scene than have to do it themselves, either because they have trouble coming up with ideas on the fly, they are not confident in their own ability to frame scenes, or the just feel like other people would do a better job. Should we play games that have one or two people framing all the scenes, to make them more comfortable? That could be one approach, but generally we just have the other players step in and help out. Honestly, I'm not sure if that allows the uncomfortable players to get more confident in their scene framing, but it's part of a process that will hopefully lead there. But I don't blame them for "turtling" when the baton gets passed their way. I try to find ways for them to stay engaged.

Another example: I was playtesting Escape or Die with the Evil Hat crowd at GenCon last year. It was 11pm, I was really tired, and the theme we'd decided on was Tron-style old-school computer stuff. For the life of me, try as hard as I could, I could not get that vibe to flow through me for some reason; the witty ideas just wouldn't come, despite Rob sitting across from me doing his hilarious "I'm Peter Packet; I make the internet go!" routine. And Escape or Die is all about doing things quickly, since the game itself is on a timer. But everyone else would stare at me while I sat there for 3 minutes trying to come up with a creative idea... and ultimately I'd just say something really, really lame. Bless them, they went with it anyway, but it was like pulling teeth. I felt really shitty afterwards, like I'd let everyone else down.

Basically, my point is that there are all sorts of legitimate reasons players might not be able to engage:

- Lack of confidence / Feeling intimidated
- Lack of familiarity
- Confusion / Don't understand rules
- Tired / Out of it
- Not excited about / uncomfortable with the direction of the game

And none of these are their fault or, necessarily, things that they can do something about on their own. So I feel like labeling all of them indistinguishably as "turtling" both masks what's really going on and implies that there's nothing the other players can do about it, that it's somehow due to a choice on the part of the unengaged player, the player who's most likely sitting there feeling like complete shit for not being able to connect.

    \n Jonathan:

    I see turtling as something distinct from not being in the zone, or from having problems with a particular game. I think turtling is a consistent pattern of behavior that carries from game to game. A person who turtles is going to be very resistant to group attempts to help them get into the game, and changing games is going to have little to no effect.

    I speak from personal experience. My husband invited a close personal friend of his into our gaming group - we'll call him M. M flatly refused to engage with emotional content in any of the games we played. When we played With Great Power... he turtled like a demon and tried to frame Suffering in terms of bad but emotionally neutral things happening to his character. We switched to Universalis, hoping that might be a more comfortable fit. Again the same pattern of behavior - he would only frame emotionally neutral scenes.

    The thing is, whenever we were playing a game where the default mode was silliness, he was totally on and engaged. We played a silly game of Donjon and he was a total hoot. It wasn't discomfort with the rules, it wasn't fatigue - not when it happened every session - and it definitely wasn't lack of familiarity with the group. Hell, everyone in the group had known each other for *years*.

    So I'll agree with you that not everyone can bring the awesome 100% of the time, and that we shouldn't demonize people when they have an off day. But I also want to put it out there that turtling isn't an imaginary phenomenon.

    \n Wow, I have a totally different meaning for turtling. Every use of it that I've seen/heard has referred to a specific tactic of heavy defense in a strategic game involving multiple, competing players. Turtling provides a means to wait out while more proactive competitors weaken each other; at the right time, the turtle becomes a tiger, pouncing on the hindmost to snag victory points or whatever's at stake.

    As you seem to object as much to the concatenation of causes into one vague term, as you do to the presumption of individual blame rather than group blame, I figured I'd mention that (original?) definition, as it is more concrete and specific. Stick with "trepidation, ignorance, confusion, exhaustion, and disinterest"--the very five divisions you've unpacked so far above--and address each one individually, as each requires a particular set of techniques or approaches to counter.

    In short, forget about tilting against the (misuse?) of the term "turtling" and focus on the solutions to those five sources of disengagement.

    My 2\xa2;

    \n My definition of turtling is the same as Anna's. You see it a lot if you run a lot of games at conventions. These are players who seek to defend their characters by not engaging in any conflicts. They almost never assume the spotlight themselves. When asked what their character is doing, they invariably seek to hide or place their character as far from a current conflict as possible.

    In my experience, most of these players tend to see the goal of a convention scenario as surviving the session unchanged. This idea is incredibly common.

    Generally, when I encounter one of these players during a game, I'll take him or her aside and say, "Look, this is it. You're never going to play this character again. You've got four hours. Take some risks and have some fun with it."

    I have about 50% success with that. The others just refuse to engage.

    The people who fall under Jonathan's five reasons are very different than those who are turtling, IMO.

That may have its roots in defensive/unengaged play -- what you want to do is a threat to me (the player or my character) and I have nothing further to add to that. Or it may have its roots in control: It's not my idea, so ... no. Or it's because what's happening is at odds with my visualization of events, even if it's cooler/better (actually, this sounds a lot like the previous idea). The end result is, it's a creative cul-de-sac.

      I don't have a lot of AP experience with turtling or blocking, but I know I've done both and they've felt different (as I use those words).

      AP turtling: My last D&D (3.5) game, I'm playing a magic user and I've accumulated some high-level spells the DM doesn't know what to do with, since they're giving me the power to derail his game. So it becomes a power struggle: I've arranged my character's development to solve his plot, and his plot can't handle it. But he's a viking-hat DM, so he fiats away spell effects, whatever. My reaction is to lock down. Don't engage with the game. Don't give the GM any ammo or ideas as to what our plans are. Classic dysfunctional behavior.

      AP blocking: This one's tough, because I'm probably more guilty of blocking as a GM than I am as a player, and the motives are way different. Oh, here's an unfortunate one from a PTA game I was in: Some scene comes up where another player is describing his thing: does the setup, sets up the NPCs, lays it out pretty good. And, like, 10 minutes into his exposition I'm all "Nah...I don't buy it. That's not how we've been describing (NPC)." And that's it. I'm not turtling because I have nothing at stake. But I've just delivered a fairly crushing social blow to a fellow player, with no real malice intended, but the air goes out of the scene and we're left with blah. Not a proud moment for me.

      Does that make it any clearer? Does it still seem similar from the outside? Because I can certainly see a case where there may be a difference without a distinction.

      p.

      ANOTHER EDIT: So perhaps I should be thinking of blocking as a turtling technique. That actually helps clarify the issue in my own head quite a bit.
