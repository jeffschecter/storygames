\n I see no one's mentioned Echo Bazaar here yet. As a 'casual' web-based game with only minimal social components it's admittedly a bit off-topic; but I think its theme and RPG-like mechanics will appeal, and its ideas on narrative structure have bits of tasty meat to chew on.

So: Echo Bazaar is a game set in a fantastical Victorian dream-London that has through some calamity been transported to a cavern deep underground and populated with Rubbery Men, Tomb-Colonists, visiting devils, Sorrow-Spiders, the Starveling Cat, and all manner of fungi, in addition to the expected pickpockets and laudanum addicts and faded music-hall singers. Your first task is to escape from the prison of New Newgate (carved into a giant stalactite) by dropping to a passing dirigible, after which you have to find a squat and some unpleasant odd jobs to build up your stats.

The stats are fairly RPG-like: primary ones are Dangerous, Watchful, Persuasive and Shadowy; and through tasks and lucky card draws you acquire other ones like Ruthless, Magnanimous and Steadfast that can alter the story line. There are also conditions like Wounds and Nightmares, which cause Bad Things to happen if they get too high, but whose cures are in some cases nearly as bad. (Telling friends your nightmares relieves you, but gives the friends nightmares. Laudanum ensures good sleep but raises your wounds...)



The overall structure is a bit like a choose-your-own adventure novel crossed with MMORPG-like stat-grinding. But the game encourages you to play in little chunks throughout the day, by doling out your action allowance at a slow pace, so tasks that would get repetitive after an hour are fun as little morsels.



What really stands out is the world-building and the prose, which have a cheerful, wordy black humor that reminds me a lot of Neil Gaiman's "Neverwhere" and China Mi\xe9ville's New Crobuzon novels. Admittedly, fantastical Victorian horror/humor is hardly a novelty, but there's enough originality to the setting to keep Echo Bazaar entertaining.

I just ran across a series of three posts on the developers' blog on how the storytelling mechanisms work. I found these really interesting, and I think relevant to traditional RPG design \u2014 especially for RPG authors considering how to create modular bits of plot for GMs to deploy.



I've been having great fun with it for nearly a week now, which is a rarity for me with "casual" games. It's free to play; you do need a Twitter account for login, and the game does make you tweet a semi-ad when you first register. Thereafter you can optionally sometimes tweet other semi-ads to gain extra actions, but you can customize the text, and the game leaves you completely in control of whether or not to do it. The devs seem like good upstanding people, quite the opposite of shady Farmville cloners. I really recommend it.

    \n Mmm. I've hit the content cap for Watchful and the limits of how far my Ambition has been written. I'm still drawn on to keep playing, admittedly just barely, by the lure of a guest room at the brass embassy, much more so than seeing what happens when I max out my other stats.

    There's a number of interesting lessons to be learned in the game as far as RPGs go. I've been thinking a lot about setting design, which is admittedly something I normally give a pass compared to system design, but the idea of locations largely defined by the sorts of opportunities a PC might find there is an intriguing one to me. And I'm impressed at how powerful a motivator certain "brass rings" can be: a setting with high-end goals, very out of reach of starting characters, but very visible to them.

    \n I had a bad first experience with Echo Bazaar where I spent all my initial moves and didn't get out of prison, so I quit right away and have never gone back. It was very opaque at the beginning what you were supposed to do and experimentation wasn't rewarded at all. I randomly selected one action and it failed, creating negative feedback. So I selected all the other actions in turn, and they all failed. So then I started back at the beginning again and, because I didn't select to fail at the same thing multiple times in a row (and who does that? that's simple Pavlovian conditioning, right?), my stats didn't end up improving enough in my first set of moves for me to be able to escape. And thus, nothing interesting happened and I had to wait a full 24 hours before attempting it again. So I found better things to do that actually rewarded my time.

    So, take that as a warning for folks just jumping into the game now. It is very grindy, there's a lot of opaqueness about what you're actually supposed to do to make progress, and the first few turns can lead to nothing of substance occurring. Though, if you can get past that, obviously a lot of people find it fun. Though I bet that, even if I was to continue on at this point, my lack of patience with grinding would lead me to having the same response as Nathan.
