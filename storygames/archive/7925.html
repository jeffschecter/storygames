\n My question is threefold:

1 - How did you start writing role-playing games?

2 - Why did you continue writing role-playing games?

3 - Do you have an agenda to your game design?

To answer my own questions:

1 - I was in a dry-spell with my literary exploits, and needed some other outlet for my imagination and creative motor. I stumbled upon role-playing games, found them dumb at first, but then I played them some more and got frustrated with some narrow elements (as in: these games don't do peaceful social interaction very well), so I had to make my own. My first serious design was Muu, a poetic little game on peaceful small creatures living in a forest-glen.

2 - I discovered that role-playing games was fun and furiosly effective in engaging both myself and other people. When I saw how deep and dark the games could become, and how light they could make us feel, there were no turning back. I had to explore this media to the core and the fringes.

3 - My artistic agenda is to make games that will change us; you and me and all our friends out there. I will work with any theme and method that allows me to create effective games within this context. I will even make fun games for that purpose. Games for change is my slogan. I do not expect other game-smiths to have similar agendas at all. Some may, but your agenda may differ from mine in any way thinkable, and still be a powerful agenda.

    \n 1. Well, actually I used to design board games and card games all the time, because I found it to be something enjoyable. I also played a lot of D&D 3.5, and eventually, the two met, and I started designing some RPGs.
    2. Well, I find that I enjoy RPGs a lot, and I love designing my own content, so I often will create my own system to fit my needs and the needs of my players. Originally, I strayed from card game design because RPGs require less graphic deign, something I'm pretty terrible at, but after a while in the field, I've found that there's something about RPGs I enjoy more than any other form of game. They're just more creative, I guess.
    3. An agenda? That would be "Game inspired design" by which I mean non RPG games. I think that adding elements from board, card, and video games into RPGs can make for a very fun experience if done right. My goal is to do it right, and bring some new innovation to RPGs.

    \n1 - How did you start writing role-playing games?
    Most everything I've ever enjoyed, I tried creating on my own at some point. When I started playing Palladium's Robotech RPG with my friends, writing material for it seemed only natural.

    2 - Why did you continue writing role-playing games?
    Of the creative endeavors I've gotten into, it's one of the most enjoyable.

    3 - Do you have an agenda to your game design?
    I try to make my primary goal creating games that are fun to play with my friends.

    More recently, I've been wanting to do whatever I can to encourage people to draw inspiration on anime naturally rather than either acting like it's somehow a precious Japanese diamond, or doing nothing but bland system-doesn't-matter universal systems.

    Luckily my gaming group is all anime fans, so there's a lot of overlap between the two goals.

    Posted By: TomasHVM1 - How did you start writing role-playing games?
    \nFirst game was Three Stooges Role Playing using a d30. I made it... because the d30 came out, and it's wild range of results suited something madcap. And there was no "competitive" game of which I was aware at the time, so I made that a big element, too (two or more teams of Stooges vying for the same/similar goals).
    Most recently, I started designing GLASS because I was sick to death of bloated, kludgy, genre-bound LARP live combat systems in my area (and elsewhere). A bit of HERO, a bit of NERO, and much cleaner and less chaotic combat elements... and GLASS was born.
    Since then, I have had Game Chef motivate me to write a very "anti" RPG, where I tried to break every paradigm of play--you are your character, in a fictive mode; no dice or sheets or stats, all done with plush dolls and markers and knitting equipment; I Will Not Abandon You play, designed to push the real person to face (and hopefully resolve) real life issues. It's For Mature Audiences.
    Finally, I was playing some Roguelike games, and it occurred to me that something like Universalis could run one... but with stats like DitV... and single player versus a team of GMs who eventually are themselves in competition. ASCII @HACK was born (and still awaits proper testing).\n
    2 - Why did you continue writing role-playing games?
    \nSee above. Ideas come, I think they represent an untapped niche or market, I write them. TESTing, though... that's my bugbear.\n
    3 - Do you have an agenda to your game design?
    \nGLASS - Provide a universal system for con play and for portable characters between LARPs. WAY too many LARPs exist in a vacuum, booming and busting because of their insistence on home-brewed (i.e. fantasy heartbreaker) rules. Meanwhile, folks who'd be interested in play are (often) driven off by complexity, disparity in efficacy or plot attention, and the Good Ol Level Treadmill. I want to change that--make the d20 (HERO, really) of LARPs, basically.
    For Mature Audiences - See above.
    Stacktors! - The Icehouse Game System (pyramids) has over 200 games developed for it... and only one (nar-style) RPG that's sort of half-baked. Until Stacktors! RPG, which is the first trad RPG using only pyramids to play and resolve (i.e. no dice, no character sheet--though cheat-sheets would be common).
    ASCII @HACK - Become rich and woo women. Actually, because I see a niche for it--most "roguelike" RPGs take the approach of simulating the random dungeons and loot and such--I take the approach of GMs v. player, each with narrative authority over events in play ("random-like").

    \n1 - How did you start writing role-playing games?
    I always liked designing stuff. When I was a kid and found out Lego I asked for it every birthday and Christmas after that. A couple of months after discovering super-hero comic books I started creating heroes and new stories. On the year I started playing RPG I wrote two heart-breakers.

    2 - Why did you continue writing role-playing games?
    There is no way I can learn a game without thinking "Wouldn't be cool if..." or "This idea is great, how would it work that mechanic...".

    3 - Do you have an agenda to your game design?
    Each game has it's own agenda, but the general idea is: try to create a model about how the medium works, decompose and reconstruct the model leaving a few parts out, build a good game that fits in the new model. This means that every game is consistent according to a model and breaks the expectations of the model.

    \n1 - How did you start writing role-playing games?
    I started to create RPG's almost as soon as I started playing them, in the mid-80's.
    I'm a French guy, and was living in a small town, so there was only a very limited choice of RPG's around there.
    That's why I started to create RPGs, to play stuff not covered by available RPGs.
    For example, in 1987, I've created a RPG called No FuTuR because I loved Blade Runner and wanted a dark-cyberpunk RPG (which were not available back then).

    2 - Why did you continue writing role-playing games?
    I fact, I wrote most of my games when I was in high school (1986-1990), including Wuthering Heights. I've calmed down since then.
    I think RPG-design is an interesting hobby, and I still like to create stuff not covered in other games.
    Since 2006, I started to create 1-page-RPG's, just for the challenge...

    3 - Do you have an agenda to your game design?
    I've got no real agenda, besides participating into challenges (such as my 53-RPG-in-2008 page, or the D\xe9miurges en herbe contest in november).
    I've got lots of other long-term projects such as Le r\xe8gne de Mu (Churchwardian fantasy), but also lots of aborted stuff.
    I'm not really planning anything.

    Posted By: TomasHVMI do recognize that each game has it's own agenda, kind of. My question is what YOUR agenda is.
    I, for one, can't disassociate those concepts. Each game comes due to a different desire of my own (agenda) and so each game "has" its own agenda in equal proportion to the degree to which my desires can be reflected in it. Some games have major agendas (me and it; ex: GLASS), some have minor agendas (FMA: participate in a community contest; throw out all conventions; make friends), and some are just "cool ideas" that I realize, in developing, have a niche (and thus comes my emergent agenda; ex: A@H was a lark that, now, will probably be my first for-profit game).

    Make sense?

    Now, if I had to have a Meta Agenda For All Game Design Efforts, then it would be simple, and much like my Meta Agenda in life, in general: win friends, influence people, woo women, educate, retire in comfort (not necessarily in that order). After all--that's what's Good in Life (I'm no Conan).
