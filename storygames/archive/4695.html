    Posted By: John HarperI've only heard the term applied to videogames -- Grand Theft Auto, Crackdown, Dead Rising, etc.
    \nWith those games in mind, that's how I played (ignoring the limitations they have to have because they're video games).

    The method my friends and I used were basically like relationship maps. You have some number of NPCs, I try to start with around four big ones, but it depends on how much exploration you want and how big of a setting, larger means you should have more NPCs ready. These four each have different goals that conflict with each other in some way, some slight some major, which will create natural allies and enemies. Something about them can't be ignored. Either because the NPCs are important figures in the characters' lives, or because their goals effect the characters (at least half should be in some negative way and the rest just "different" but never overly positive) or some other reason. You can break this down further and have more NPCs, or just more minor ones with further fractal goals of their own. This way, something is always happening, but the players are free to do whatever they want. If they try to ignore them, since the presence is effecting them anyway, they can't truly ignore it and so there's still events happening. Chances are they won't ignore it, but you still give them the freedom to choose sides, decide how they'll interact with the NPCs, maybe even decide to work for an NPC you expect to be a villain. But since there's no plot, there's no problem. You just have fictional people with goals, who adjust in response to unexpected changes like people do. The structure is in place for the players to explore the setting.

    Spirit of the Centry has a distilled version of this in one of the three ways to create an adventure. All the current World of Darkness stuff is made to be sandboxed. They don't even give you a completely explicit setting, just the societies and their rules that you use to set up your own setting.

    I've played and run a lot of games like this, and they were fantastic. There was always an ongoing plot, but only because character creates plot. Exploring the PCs and NPCs eventually creates it. NPCs with big epic goals can lead to big epic plots without diminishing the ability to explore.

    \n I see "sandbox" as likely meaning one end of a scale.

    You can have a "heavily prepped, mapped out D&D dungeon focused" campaign in which the GM is constantly asking and open to Player input and PC interests, they want to take on the drow, a drow pops up in this dungeon with a plot hook to a dungeon full of drow. They want to explore stealth and trickery as tactics instead of combat, the dungeon full of drow is one where there are lots of opportunities for that. The GM is still throwing out the plot hooks. Why are you in this dungeon full of drow that calls for lots of sneaking? The GM knows and tells you etc.

    The other end, what you're probably looking for, is described by Chris Chinn in his articles about setting up a few Conflicts with the attendant NPC's, these being conflicts of interest to the PC's, and then just asking them, "what do you do"? This requires a system/DM ready to handle less prep-timed play. I suspect it works better for narrativist/story type play with resolutions heavy on narration etc. (e.g., DiTV type rules) rather than gamist "Challenge" type play.

    Implicitly, it's the way Mortal Coil advises to set up story.

    Chinn's articles on conflict webs - protagonist play which is what I think is the wide open sandbox.

    \n Ryan's method is one of memetic things that I sense people in college, at the mercy of borrowed D&D books, have come up with.

    You wake up with a knock at your apartment door. It's 4PM so obviously you're still in bed. You throw on pants and pop the deadbolt, and here comes everybody. You've got two friends and this girl you've been dating for two weeks; they bring over five people you don't know. Everyone has supplements you've never heard of, and asks you what level they should make characters at. You're about to protest when you see the pizza and beer.

    So what do you do? You throw on a shirt and grab your sandbox. The little black box for all those notecards you never used in Poly Sci. Instead, you doodled a simple encounter: The Hook (what happens) The Bait (why they might care) The Hint (who/what helps them, how, and why) and What They Get if they choose to, along with possibly What Happens if they don't. Pull out half a dozen cards and spin a few yarns, and you've got a memorable game. It might not suck people into a stark personal space, but it'll be fun. Everyone'll get a laugh, and you get free pizza and beer.

    That's sandboxing to me, in a nutshell.

    \n Situation exploration:

    - We're God's Watchdogs! We judge the faithful.
    - We're dungeon delvers! We go into dungeons to fight monsters and get treasure.
    - We're Shadowrunners! We pull off heists against mega corps.

    Minimized situation exploration (the sandbox):

    - We're pistol-packin' true believers in the wild, wild west. We ramble around and fall into trouble.
    - We're tough adventurers in a fantasy world! We travel to strange places and have escapades.
    - We're Shadowrunners! We do whatever the fuck we want, and kill those that cross us.

    Minimized. But of course it's still there. There's always a situation.

    \n Most of my early GMing was sandbox play, meaning I would create an environment, or world, in which the players would exist. There would be major cities, important NPCs both good and bad, areas for adventure set-pieces (you know, dungeons, ruined temples of evil, dwarf holds, haunted forests, graveyards, whatever). Then the players would roll up characters who would be placed into the world and they could roam around, explore, interact. As GM, I had to make sure that adventure found them at some point, but they could interact at will.

In sandbox mode, if I find a troubled town, I have the option of (for example) just hanging out and chatting with the locals and maybe buying a deed to the local mine and trying to make some quick cash on a gold strike. The priority of play isn't situation focused, in other words, even if there is corrupt worship and such going on.     \n Please note: I'm not looking for a particular Creative Agenda to be supported. I prefer Story Games, and even though that implies a CA preference, it isn't.

That is, a sandbox Dogs game (which I can't imagine, really), would have towns, and Dogs, and when the Dogs decided that their obligations to the town they were in weren't strong enough to force them to act, you'd still have something to do?     Completely different question: Say you were playing some game like D&D or Shadowrun. At the end of a tightly focused story arc, the group decided to keep playing in the same world they'd developed, but not the same characters or not the same time/location. Continual repetition of this reminds me of sandboxing. Does communal exploration of a defined setting while addressing the same Premise, vis a vis 'jumping around' or quantum leaping, constitute sandboxing? What about different Premises (e.g. fantasy world, first game dungeon crawl, second game political intrigue, third game mystery solvers)? What if it were a highly documented series of AP with different groups of people?

    By the way, I'm not going anywhere in particular with this, so don't think I have an agenda. I'm just asking questions.

    \n To me this style of play is very simple:
    1. Create an environment with stuff going on (Situation).
    2. Require that none of it either demands action on the PC's part nor effects them in a truly significant manner at the start of the game.
    3. Make sure they have a proper handle on / briefing on the environment (could be in the form of a map, a good description of a cyber-punk world, etc.) in such a way that:
    .- They can get involved with the stuff going on if they so choose.
    .- They understand how to pursue their own agendas if they so choose.
    4. Unleash!

    NOTE: it helps a lot if the PCs have a solid relationship to each other and if they have decently strong, but not overwhelming goals that are, at least, reasonably aligned (or else the PCs are liable to help each other out).

    \n My experience is that many people think they're doing the sandbox thing, but there actually is a significant level of pressure inherent in the situation which is merely rendered "deniable" by framing it as a logical consequence of the initial circumstances. I suspect that this is in fact inevitable - every situation, when coupled with a set of ideas about what "reasonable consequences" look like and near-total power over deciding NPC responses to PC actions (said ideas always embedding a particular aesthetic judgment and ideological position at some level), contains the seeds of only a limited set of viable PC actions.

    I suppose one could posit players who genuinely don't care whether the outcome of their actions are fun or interesting, and are totally willing to abandon "dead-end" games where their options have been rendered irrelevant by circumstance, but in practice I would say more than half the so-called sandbox games I have played in have either degenerated into "dead-end" states or developed a clear organizing premise as the players begin to learn which kinds of actions will be successful and which will lead to failure and stagnation.

    In other words, I think the "sandbox" is an ideal, an El Dorado, which is aspired to but never (or very rarely) exists in practice. Eventually it either collapses into futility or incoherence or is revealed as Ouija-boarding.

      \n I thought I called this out in my original post, but I want to stress it, because as far as I can see Marco is basically agreeing with me. The "sandbox" definition that Hargrove (jdrakeh) is working from seems eminently achievable and I've seen it work fine. Narrow franchise "you're adventurers in search of gold and glory", open details "pick your gold & glory adventure from among many opportunities, each with its own flavors" = great. It's the more overarching "open franchise, open details, you can do anything" kind that I've very rarely seen give good results. Either it fragments into N individual games, often with significant friction, or it is steered back into line periodically with illusion-y or Ouija-boardy techniques, or the "open" is only apparently open.

      \n One thing I've noticed with sandbox style play, which we can all point at whether or not we agree on a definition, is that it works better when the players are very proactive. Passive players will not enjoy the freedom to do anything. My hypothesis is that this phenomenon (both sides of it) has to do with goal formation - active players form goals that they will pursue and thus bring themselves into contact with more and more of the sandbox. Passive players tend to want the goal defined by the GM so they can follow along. This leads me to wonder if there is a way to obtain a similar effect as sandbox without opening the game up to the possibility of goalless stalling. I'm tempted to frontload as much as possible.

      Would you still consider it a sandbox if we agreed on a setting, the players made characters, and I asked where they wanted to go and what they wanted to do before the game started? I suspect that more active players in the group would dominate this discussion - which is OK, since the passive players don't want to be involved anyway.
