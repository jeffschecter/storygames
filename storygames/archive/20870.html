\nNote: this is just the base, more will be added. I've kept it generic, but would add flavor to suit the genre. Looking for some feedback on how it reads and the overall design.

Every character comes with seven attributes. There are three scores, three saves, and one lucky number. Roll 3d6 seven times and then decide on where to put each result. Higher numbers are better for your scores, while lower numbers are better for your saves. For your lucky number, use personal preference.

Your rolls will help you to choose a class. In this game, your class is a combination of background and skills. You'll begin play at level one. Your level is how experienced and talented you are in your class. If using your class, an attempt will always succeed--unless difficulty or opponent is above your level.

Whenever you attempt to overcome an opponent, obstacle, or dilemma, you'll need to use one of your scores and roll a d20. If the result is equal or below your score, you have succeeded. Rolling your exact score is a critical success. If you attempt an action outside of your class or the difficulty is greater than your level, this number becomes the chance of critical failure.

Use CONFRONT when facing another opponent in a non-lethal situation. Use AVOID when trying to get past a physical or non-physical obstacle. Use SOLVE when confronted by a dilemma.

Sometimes in the game you will find yourself in danger. When this occurs you'll need to make a save. Roll a d20 and if the result is higher than your save number, you have succeeded. If the difficulty or opponent's level is higher than yours, then the difference is added to the save, making it harder to succeed.

Save with COOL when responding under pressure. Save with GRIT when faced with adversity. Save with QUICK when reacting on impulse.

Sample Character

Name: Felix "the Cat"
Class (level): Cutpurse (2)
Scores: Confront (12) Avoid (15) Solve (14)
Saves: Cool (8) Grit (11) Quick (10)
Lucky Number: (7)

Let's say Felix is attempting to break into a safe. The difficulty for the safe is a 7. If the difficulty is 2 or less, the attempt automatically succeeds. Since this is not the case, the difference is 5 and becomes the risk factor for the cracking the safe.

He will use his AVOID score of 15 to try to crack the safe (since it is considered an obstacle). Felix needs to roll 6-15 to be successful. A roll of 15 is a critical success. A roll of 16-20 the attempt fails, but nothing bad happens. A roll of 1-5 is a critical failure.

Felix rolls a 2 and his attempt at cracking the safe fails badly. Not only does the safe not open, but the three guards in the next room overhear him and give chase. Felix manages to get outside and looks for a way to escape.If Felix's level is higher than the guards, then automatically escapes (at least for a while).

If the level of any of the guards is equal or higher than the thief, then Felix has to make a save roll and the difference in level is added to the score. One of the guards is level 1 and soon gives up the chase. However, the second guard is level 3, and the third is level 4. Take the highest difference, guard (4) versus cutpurse (2) and add that number (2) to the save.

Felix makes COOL save as he is attempts to escape. He has an 8, along with difference in level makes his save number a 10. Felix will need to roll an 11 or higher, or else be caught.

Keep in mind that you can, once per session, switch out a die roll and replace with your lucky number. It can be switched for either a score or save. In this example, Felix's lucky number is 7. If he had decided to use his lucky number when attempting to break into the safe, the roll of 2 would have been switched to 7 and the safe would have been cracked instead.

    \n To elaborate - since I've realized my words sounded harsh - I believe your rules as written would be good for a game where you have some other kind of limited resources to manage, so that "just failing" a roll still means something to the general flow of the game.

    I can see, for example, a "heist" game (but really, almost any of the "quest" templates provided in Ben Robbins's Follow would work here) where you have a limited, shared pool of d20s, representing time and resources: whenever you roll one, you "use up" a d20. All PCs draw dice from the same big pool. When you fail, the problem is you've wasted time and resources - you've used up a d20 - without accomplishing anything! Class and level become crucial: the core winning strategy is divvying up tasks between PCs so that as much as possible can be accomplished without rolling. Critical failures typically breed situations where you have to make saving throws, potentially using up more dice from the pool! Maybe, those rare lucky "critical successes" add dice to the pool (these represent doing things super-quickly, conserving resources or finding new useful resources on the field) adding a little extra incentive to the gamble of a roll.\n
    Keep in mind that you can, once per session, switch out a die roll and replace with your lucky number. It can be switched for either a score or save. In this example, Felix's lucky number is 7. If he had decided to use his lucky number when attempting to break into the safe, the roll of 2 would have been switched to 7 and the safe would have been cracked instead.
    \nThe switch is permanent, isn't it? After doing this, Felix's lucky number changes to "2"... Better use that up on a save next session!
    I like it that, this way, you can end up with some hard-to-use lucky number, which adds extra weight to the choice of whether to switch a failure for a success, depending on your exact roll.
    If this was a game about criminals and heists, I'd use their current, final lucky number as a measure of their future luck (how happy of a happy ending they get) when a PC finally chooses to retire - maybe a Fiasco-like table (but happier!) or a roll under (above?) your current lucky number mechanic or such.

    \n I am really coming around to the d20 resource pool that Rafu mentioned. Not sure. I will probably test it a few ways to see what works best.If going this route, I'd probably have a number of ways to refresh the pool. Of course a critical success on the die roll should have that reward.

    For combat, right now I am thinking of a Morale/Effort/Adrenaline system. A lot depends on the complexity of combat.

    Let's say you go into combat these scores. These change over the course of combat depending on how successful you are and how long the fight lasts. How much armor you are carrying and the weight of your weapon, while offering more protection and dealing more damage, burns through your effort faster.

    You can attempt a number of different maneuvers, more complex maneuvers require more energy pull off and with some of the more difficult maneuvers, a failed attempt could result in leaving you tired, demoralized, or exposed.

    The goal is to gain an advantage on your enemy through a series of maneuvers and deliver killing blows.

    I could keep it as one score HP, which takes into account morale, effort, and adrenaline. Though I like the idea that each maneuver requires effort. Hitting or missing effects morale, and burning adrenaline can help you in the short term, but hurt in the long run.

    Perhaps your effort points can be used first before having to dip into the d20 pool.

    Now a couple things can come into play if you go with the pool. One, you'll want to get the fight over ASAP. The longer it goes the more dangerous it gets not just for you, but the whole party. Two, if you drain the pool and you still need to check a box. You could take it from fatigue, stress, or even pain.

    Things to think about for sure!

    The original idea came from fantasy wilderness survival, where resources are scarce and valuable.

    If I went with the d20 pool, how do you determine the starting number of dice? What happens went it goes empty?I've got a set of conditions, things like: Thirst, Hunger, Fatigue, Exposure, Filth, Pain--each with three boxes. If any of them get to the third box, you're out. So perhaps to refill the pool, you've got to check one of the boxes (depending on the situation).
    \nWith the d20 pool idea, I was thinking of a heist/crime game, where nonlethal failure is an option: exhaust your collective pool and you have to give up - you don't get the money or whatever it was.
    Unless giving up on your exploration and going back to the starting point is an option, I would handle wilderness survival differently.

    When you say "fantasy wilderness survival", I imagine an adventure is essentially a string of trials you have to endure - a sequence of saves. Pass all of those saves and you've made it through the journey, but for each failed save there are dire consequences: you lose an essential part of your PC (a heirloom item, a limb, a piece of your sanity, dignity) or maybe you lose your PC outright, depending on how you want to pace the game.
    In these context, confront/avoid/solve rolls represent optional, voluntary actions a PC undertakes between prescribed saves, when presented with an opportunity, in order to gather additional resources in the wild. "Just failing" here is a viable outcome: it represents a wasted opportunity (no retries).
    PCs begin their journey with very limited resources, representing their gear and maybe some knowledge of what lies ahead. Each piece of resources is a concretely defined item or fact, not just an abstraction like in my dice pool idea. Resources are spent on saves, to improve your chance of success or grant you automatic success, instead of or in addition to your class level - did I mention you really don't want to fail any saves?
    The only way to gather more resources in the wild is to succeed at the optional confront/avoid/solve rolls, but in case of a critical failure you end up in some dangerous situation and have to pass an extra save - above and beyond what's required to complete the journey - potentially using up resources instead of gaining any.

    \n The core mechanic is similar to what you described above. Each character has a number of trait cards. You can key some of those traits as part of your character's action, and roll that number of dice.

    Each die that's a failure results in that trait being exhausted and unavailable for use. Once a challenge scene ends (with obstacles and what not), you can have some social scenes. If your character is involved in the social scene they get one of the exhausted cards restored.

    So in your game it might not be worth such a strict scene cadence, and maybe all the dice are combined as a group rather than individually, etc.
