\nI just posted a version of this over on Jonathan's blog...
\n
\nJust got back from Gencon.
\n
\nThere's seemed to be a strong divide between fiction matters and fiction doesn\u2019t matter among the players I interacted with.
\n
\nMy main focus was to run games where fiction matters for non indie gamers. I ended up running multiple events a day, from 12pm to sometimes 5am. Most of my players were fans, writers, and developers of Paizo and Wizard\u2019s. I'm not going to name names. But I would say over 50% of the games, the players could care less about the fiction. I witnessed pacifist characters repeatedly murder and mutilate children because it made them feel powerful or they were in the way.
\n
\nIt was bizarre.
\n
\nPart of the fault is my GMing style. I try to never railroad action or behavior. The story goes where the players take it. I feel like I'm on their ride and I go where they take me.
\n
\nIt felt like playing grand theft auto where the fun for many people was running bystanders over with your car. Except I don\u2019t want that in my roleplaying games.
\n
\nSome dude carved up the body of a dead nun he knew that was just killed by a monster to make weapons out her bones.
\n
\nI talked to many people about it afterward and the consensus seemed to be that many wanted to use RPGs as a way to vent and do things they wouldn\u2019t be allowed to do in real life. Immersing and creating didn\u2019t matter as much as exerting power in taboo ways.
\n
\nTerry played quite a bit of Call of Cthulhu. We both really, really dislike the system. But at Gencon, CoC seems to attract players who care about the fiction we are creating together. And none of them seem that interested in the CoC system either. But the culture around the game is that it attracts certain players. So it\u2019s a safe bet if you want that specific type of play. Regardless of the fact that the rules better support hack and slashing than immersive horror gaming. The priority is finding people who share your interest. The system supporting your play seemed secondary.
\n
\nGencon was a very interesting experience this year for me. I felt sad I didn\u2019t see most of my friends but I wanted to really play with as many different people as possible outside our circles.
\n
\nI can see the appeal behind White Wolf calling their games storytelling even if their system wasn't that different than D&D.;
\n
\nI'm exhausted and I've lost my voice!

    Posted By: jenskotI talked to many people about it afterward and the consensus seemed to be that many wanted to use RPGs as a way to vent and do things they wouldn\u2019t be allowed to do in real life. Immersing and creating didn\u2019t matter as much as exerting power in taboo ways.
    \nThis is interesting. I facilitate a lot of games with the purpose of introducing new players to the kind of games i like (indie games, story games, story engines, alternative games or whatever). Mostly these people fall into two groups, people who either don't play role playing games games at all and people who play primarily mainstream roleplaying games. My experiences have been mostly very, very positive, but what I've found is that the first group is very interested in fiction. they want to create and tell a story. their characters have motives, drives and emotions. They are aware of the fiction they are creating, and will make choices that facilitate a better story over a better outcome for their characters. the second group often behaves in an opposite manner. they're interested in mechanical advantages, resolutions that favor their characters and winning. none of these are bad things, but this is also often the group that gets upset when things don't go their way, that isn't interested in outcomes that involve bad things happening to their characters, forgoes creating or using fiction in favor of getting what they want and looses interest when faced with adversity that can't be resolved through violence.

    I always come away from these games feeling like traditional gaming is a very poor starting point for the kind of games I enjoy.

    \nContrary to popular belief, system won't make people play differently much of the time. It'll only support people who play the way that the system supports. If you do see a difference in how people play one system and then another, it's mostly likely because they're "ambidexterous" in terms of play-styles, and like playing both ways. Not that the system is taking a x-ist, and forcing them to play y-ist.
    \n
    \nBut the culture that plays Cthulhu aren't the way they are because of the material, nor are the vampire people the all moody because of calling Vampire:TM a moody game. The Goths existed as a culture prior to the game coming out, and migrated into playing it. Note that those traditional gamers who also migrated into playing VTM play it like D&D. The Cthulhu folks, I believe, having been there through their formation, are a culture that results from certain political actions in the RPGA (scoring the CoC events on "role-playing" early on, as opposed to scoring on tactical proficiency).
    \n
    \nWhen my friends and I first came to CoC from D&D and the like, we played it like we played D&D. Because that was our culture.

    \n It's a good point Scott makes. You can, in fact, enforce your culture at the game table, if you want.
    \n
    \nJohn, the way you put it,
    \n
    \n"I try to never railroad action or behavior."
    \n
    \n... is interesting. Call this a snap diagnosis, but you sound like you have something related to "abused player" syndrome. That is, instead of being a player who clutches onto their character in terror, worried that the GM will take away your prerogatives, you're a GM who is fearful that your input will be seen as abusive. As though, to use a sensitive analogy, you're a father who was abused as a child, who decides to let their children do anything so that you can avoid any chance that your actions will harm them.
    \n
    \nThat would be terrible parenting. And probably isn't really good GMing. As a GM, in most cultures, you're going to have thrust upon you a mandate to control the social aspects of play. Not fair, shouldn't be the case, but it's how things often go. If this is the case, and you don't control these things, then the social aspect with a bunch of strangers (especially ones who do not know each other) will fall apart.
    \n
    \nScott, what with his occupation, understands this implicitly, I think. Basically this is the age-old con problem. How do you make a social group out of a set of players who really don't know each other?
    \n
    \nKnow what my solution to the problem is? I largely do not play with anyone that I do not know. Playing with the myriad people from this forum and the Forge, gives me more than enough players (I lamented to somebody that I hate the fact that I can't play with all of them each con).
    \n
    \nNow... I'm not suggesting that you should abandon the masses. As Scott points out, you can in fact make this work. It's just always going to take a bit more work. And I personally just don't see the point. But if you want to play with as many new people as possible, then you may well have to take the leadership role.
    \n
    \nNote that this is the real reason why the CoC games are better. Do you note how they all know each other? To the extent that they even annoyingly refer to their ongoing games, or previous games played together as in-jokes? While their games are relatively functional, what's going on is that they're guarding against potentially disruptive outsiders who occasionally stumble into their games. If you turn out to be non-disruptive, if you adopt their culture, then, in a year or two they might remember you, and you might be in.
    \n
    \nOr you can just go have a drink with them after the game (or however you socialize personally), and get to know them in a way they'll remember.
    \n
    \nThe point is, overall, to remember that a functional social layer is key to all play. If you don't have that, what you have is several people all playing different games at the same table. And what good is that?

    Perhaps I see the split differently. Tactical/puzzle, power/risk, drama/story, these are all different groups to me (think of it as my alternative to GNS). The taboo vs. story play you describe I see occurring along a second axis, one of selfishness to group responsibility. Taboo play like you describe is extreme selfishness combined with drama (not power, as it's more about acting out than winning).

    The role of the system is definitely secondary to finding the right group in the short run, but over the long term it is very important. Part of the reason that rpgs had a bad reputation is because the games attracte, amongst other groups, subculture of taboo-fantasy players, but didn't try to appeal to many other audiences. After a while, the medium of rpgs became associated with certain types of play. There are many ways rpgs are now pushing out of those stereotypes, including through the indie design market, parents seeking games to play with their kids, etc.
