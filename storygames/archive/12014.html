Posted By: hans ottersonPosted By: ccreitzthe Mythos-SAN-knowledge and spells to keep you alive-MP-POW economy
\nas Yunus basically said, I don't get this. It works like this.

The elements of the central economy are:
- SAN, a score from 0-100 representing your sanity level. At 0, you're out of the game; losing more than 5 at once also inflicts an insanity condition on your character. You lose it by learning Things Man Was Not Meant To Know (TMWNMTK, see below), casting spells, doing crazy-person things like shooting your friends, or undergoing terrible stresses. You get it back, a little at a time, by successfully holding back the darkness for one more day, or by advancing a skill to 90%, up to a max of (the lower of 100-Cthulhu Mythos or 5x your POW stat (see below for both)). Odds are good that you lose it faster than you get it back, though. SAN loss is a death-spiral mechanic - the lower your SAN, the easier it is to lose more.

- the Cthulhu Mythos skill, representing your knowledge of TMWNMTK. Gaining Mythos inevitably comes from losing SAN, because you read a book or saw a thing that revealed a terrible truth incompatible with the consensus worldview (and your tiny monkey brain). But you NEED to know this stuff to stay alive, because it really is the truth, so veteran investigators are always balancing their precarious SAN against enough Mythos savvy to survive one more day. In the fiction this knowledge takes the form of things that, if you tell them to a psychiatrist, will get you treated or committed.

- spells, a list of intermittently useful magical things you can do. Bad, boring Cthulhu means replacing guns with Withering; good, fun Cthulhu means flying through space on the back of a summoned and bound Byakhee, or reducing a resurrected wizard to his vital salts, or using Withering instead of a gun, knowing the costs, to make it easier to get away with a murder. If a PC does that last, you need to make sure to follow up with the Unspeakable Oath - you win! Maybe, on that character's way down, it would be fun to deconstruct the evil sorcerer archetype...

- POW, your innate magical power, a score usually from 3-18. This is your starting and usual max MP (see below), and many magical effects key on it. Some very cool spells call for a permanent expenditure of POW.

- MP, your magic points, which you spend to cast spells. If this goes to 0, you're unconscious, and it goes without saying that there are spells and effects that drain MP, so between keeping the PCs casting and including drain effects, MP is a front that the GM can certainly use to exert pressure.

- HP, hit points, which for most PCs will be in the teens. Two gunshots, or one critical hit with a gun, will kill many PCs - they're pretty fragile. HPs usually come back between adventures. It goes without saying that at 0HP, you're out of the game.

CoC is built around driving characters toward 0 SAN. If you do that, you'll get your Lovecraftian vibe. But it's only really fun if the PCs effectively spend their SAN, because of choices they make. Punishing them with SAN loss is stupid and not-fun; the worst CoC GM move, which I've been guily of a few times, is to have a PC open a closet and WHOOPS ELDER GOD IN THERE LOSE d100 SAN! Remember that SAN loss is a reward for making a Cthulhu story, a sign from the GM that you're all on the same page about the genre, and not a punishment.

So, how do you, the GM, give the PCs the opportunity to spend their SAN? Obviously, you RAILROAD THEM! Seriously, most CoC published material is awful illusionist crap. OK, one more try.

How do you give the PCs the opportunity to spend their SAN? The usual way is to put pressure on the characters' HP via supernatural danger. It doesn't take much, since (as previously noted) most PCs can't survive two gunshots. To get out of danger, they need knowledge, raising their Mythos skill, and probably spells. Once they warm to magic, make sure that you push its associated dangers too. Then, to preserve their HP, they'll spend SAN to increase Mythos. They'll expend MP and probably POW to leverage their Mythos knowledge via spells, but that too will cost them SAN. In other words, as they learn TMWNMTK, truths they need to survive, their minds will crumble at the revelation. The central economy in the mechanics underwrites the cosmic horror fiction.

Doing it without railroading is not hard. Try following Vincent's town-creation rules, basically. What is the immediate threat? (Special genre question: Why might you need magic or Mythos secrets to defeat it?) Who wants what to happen as part of that threat? What will happen (and when) if the PCs don't get involved? When I recommend Pagan's More Tales of Terror, it's because it supports this style of prep so nicely. You don't have to commit to an explanation at the beginning, only to the events. See what the group is driving toward and play with their expectation, whether that means fulfilling it or subverting it.

The best published adventures (e.g. Chaosium's "A Cult of One", Pagan's "Night Floors") support that style of play with a bunch of well-fleshed out prompts to help when play gets stuck, but open structures. But only a few can be the best, right?

Alternately, you could start with nothing at all, or close to. When a PC does something expecting to find a clue, fish instead. Once you've fished a few times, build a threat in play. With a highly genre-savvy group, this works well, and if (like me) you're a fan of lazy GMing, it's the most fun way to play.

In the other thread, I said that the best spell in the game as far as I'm concerned is Call Power of Nyambe, which (IIRC) costs 1 POW, 0MP, and some token SAN, and gives you d10MP, which you can use to cast other spells. I trust everyone can now see why I love it so.

    Seriously though, I don't find it convincing when people insist to me that a game needs to be interpreted in a highly special way not supported by the game text and relying on special GM technique that looks surprisingly similar to codified GMing advice in other games. Of course CoC is going to look like a good game if you play it like Sorcerer, to put it simply.

    The above is based on familiarity with Chaosium texts, mostly - for all I know Pagan puts the game on its head and effectively rewrites the dynamics of how and why you use the various rules subsystems (which I think Colin describes accurately) in some sourcebook that I've never read. I sort of doubt that, though - to me it seems that CoC is successful in catering to its fans exactly because it is railroady, well-researched (by rpg standards) storytelling with the GM as the head honcho. Any sort of discussion of the range of solutions the players might exercise to the scenarios is carefully limited to whether the monster got away.

    I do admit that the issue of game text critique is tricky with traditional games that are, I believe, intentionally written to embrace variety and GM autonomy. Should I judge CoC based on a) its own play advice, which is very incoherent; b) the best practices of the best GMs, which might not resemble the game text at all; c) solely the mechanics, assuming that I would be implementing them as a part of a total game system where I supply the non-mechanical parts of system myself to accord with my own tastes. If we go by b) or c) in game text critique, then the critique comes to be about much more than what is written or intended by the game's creator, and most games will be entirely valid and commendable - there's always somebody out there who got a highly functional and fun game out of any game text with selective reading and the right local conditions. Most significantly, because almost any game dynamics can be implemented without mechanics as the American textual tradition recognizes them, we might argue that the ultimate roleplaying game is the one that does not have rules at all; this is a known practical stance among freeformers.

    I'm not very serious about undermining Colin - I think it's great that he's managed to brew a fun game out of the glorious mess that is Call of Cthulhu, and his game seems pretty fun to me. I do also think that it's good to be critical of even a game you've played successfully. I've had fun with Cthulhu myself as well when I played it regularly in the '90s, but I wouldn't ascribe that to the game text as a game. At most the fun I've garnered from playing CoC has been due to the game's evocative fiction, and even more likely it's been because the group in toto has been willing and unified in our wish to play a horror rpg, with CoC as an incidental freeloader in our play.

    Getting a functional CoC-equivalent that'd make justice to the visions of the game's many scenarios is something that I'd love to do at some point. I'm somewhat sceptical of any claims that the game is already excellent and I should just learn to play it correctly, as it seems that any such claims rely on effectively rewriting the game's non-mechanical procedures, player roles and play goals extensively. At least write it down and put it out as a supplement so I can review the correct method of play at leisure as what effectively amounts to a new game text. Bonus points if the new method involves a conversion guide for how to make something like At the Mountains of Madness more than a year-long railroad with one tricky choice at the end.

Still, a modicum of critique is warranted in that if we are to improve in our understanding and design (for those of us interested in game design), we have to be able to confess to the short-comings in the games we have.     I have an interest in the question of game texts vs. game culture, as I myself spent my first ten years in the roleplaying hobby largely disconnected from the larger world. In that sort of condition, which to my mind should be assumed as typical for new roleplayers, the game text is your only friend and the fact that somebody somewhere has wretched a functional game out of the incontradictory mess is a rather dim comfort for the rest of us. The viewpoint must be different when you've paid your dues and learned to utilize the unruly beast that is the game to your satisfaction. The quirks and incoherencies of the actual game text become ignorable background noise when you actually know which parts to take seriously and what you're doing with the game.

    Insofar as the game mechanics of CoC go, objectively speaking, there is much good game design in there. The game has plenty of dross to my tastes, but the bits Colin highlights are all there and interesting. My own experience of practice is that the dross tends to scatter a group's attention and increase the risk of inefficient play when you get stuck comparing the damage potentials of rifles vs. shotguns or distributing points into Spot Hidden vs. Climb, but I'm sure that the reduced game that ignores all the "sim-by-default" as it's often called is actually pretty strongly to the point. It's a shame that I can't bring myself to even try to create characters for a game like CoC at this late date, it's almost a physical illness I get from the idea of distributing however many hundreds of points over a list of fifty skills with contradictory instructions as to the methodology I should be using to do it. No more two-hour chargen sessions for me, thanks.

    What all this indicates to me is that Call of Cthulhu desperately needs new game texts that go beyond scenario writing and actually explicitly branch out the multitudes of potential games lurking in the hidden potential of the GMing culture of the game. I'm sure that the dedicated players of the game would have wisdom for the rest of us, if only they wrote it all down into rules and procedures. Trail of Cthulhu is in the right direction in this regard, as is some of the Pagan stuff (I'm thinking of the Hastur Cycle stuff in Delta Green: Countdown which I've been reading lately), although I have to say that none of what I've seen so far really satisfies me in terms of productization: nowadays I have a very wide range of supremely honed, honest game texts to play with, so that tends to be the standard against which I measure game texts. Most CoC material is supremely beautiful prose but primitive as gaming material in comparison, I find.

I'll clearly need to get off my rump at some point and make a game or two to satisfy my personal limitations in engaging CoC. Ideally something that allows me to leverage the ginormous existing literature of tragically railroady, supremely detailed, imaginative CoC scenario material.

    \nWell, in four color superhero comics, the heroes only save the world until the next maniacal Nazi superscientist tries to blow it up. It's about discovering dire secrets that reveal something dark about man's own nature or history, then surviving to tell the tale, probably from your sanitorium cell.     Lovecraft's stories just aren't the relentlessly dreary and nihilistic things some people seem to be trying to categorize them as. Sure, in the big picture, to stand up against the unnameable horrors from beyond space and time is an act of sheer futility...but the big picture is not where the story is. The story is about the people who stand up, and Lovecraft was a canny enough pulp writer to know that the people who stand up in the face of ridiculous odds must win something, even if it costs them their lives or their souls to do it. Even if he winked at the readers and told them that it won't really matter a thousand years from now, he didn't seem to have a problem with letting some of his protagonists scratch out a win against the eldritch horrors here and there. Really, while the overall Cthulhu Mythos is ostensibly about a war that humans will lose in the end, many of the stories he wrote are about the battles fought in that war, and the good guys are allowed to (in fact, required to) keep winning those as long as it can be made into an entertaining and exciting tale for the audience. And on that level, it doesn't matter if you think it's entertaining and exciting in its own right, or if you think it's entertaining and exciting because Lovecraft is stage-whispering "spoiler alert: everyone dies anyway when the Stars Are Right" in the last paragraph: a win is a win, whether it's futile or not.

    \n One thing I am going to try and emulate is the refusal displayed by quite a number of Lovecraftean mythos protagonists to face the facts and realize the truth. The reader often knows quite early in the story what\u2019s going to come and how it\u2019s going to end while the protagonist and the narrator (sometimes combined in one voice) play dumb and pretend everything is basically ok, no need to get upset, dumdedum, let\u2019s whistle in the dark, don\u2019t think about it, don\u2019t go there, oh, I\u2019ll just take a look into that old journal while I\u2019m at it.*

    Because, you know, when the protagonist looks truth in the face, the plot has run its course and the story is over. That\u2019s one of the author\u2019s favourite tools; much of the tension comes from the clash between the readers knowledge of horrible things going on/ going to happen, and the protagonists denial of this knowledge, culminating in the devastating scene where the facts can no longer be denied and oh god it\u2019s all true madness swoon horrible shrieking and a quick letter telling the story of how it all came about.

    Of course, if you hint to your players that they\u2019re just walking straight into the pit of monsters, they\u2019re not going do to the \u201cI\u2019m sure there\u2019s another, entirely reasonable explanation for all these wet, slurping noises and the toothmarks on these bones here\u201c routine, no, they\u2019re going back to their car and break out the shotguns and the dynamite.*

    Unless, of course, they know the rules and traditions of the genre and you have made it clear to them that you are sticking to them. If the players know rule 1, as formulated by Colin above, and you play your part, too, you can have a hell of a time. If you don\u2019t whack your players on the head as soon as their characters look firmly the other way (with small, anxious peeks into the dark), I have found they will gleefully ride their guys deep into the mud and blow their sanity in the big climax.

    I haven\u2019t encouraged this kind of behaviour with rules, yet. But I just realized that, in game terms, the protagonist\u2019s denial of the horrible truth is a means of preserving his SAN score. Of course, players cannot actively engage the Horrible Thing while looking the other way. But if you use SAN points as a currency, as per Colin\u2019s and others\u2019 take, you and the players can balance things.

    If they look away and play along, their SAN stays intact. Maybe the gain even gain something, SAN points, XP, Emergency Dice, Drama Points, whatever your system has.

    If they want to engage the menace, they have to pay something in order to face it. Switch off the disbelieve, pay the SAN cost, get the action going. When confronted as a group with a supernatural menace, the first character forced to realize the truth is paying the heaviest cost.

    I imagine a group of Investigators in a deserted building, confronted with a security guard coming towards them. They have been expected, obviously the guard is happy to see them, judging from his hurry. Oh my, look, he has been hurt. He\u2019s coming straight at them, in a kind of shambling way. So, what do the players do? Do they pay the SAN cost right now and blow the zombie away? Or do they wait to the last, reaping some benefits and racking up the tension with willing suspension of belief, until finally one character gets attacked or is forced to face the facts, paying the cost while the others get off lightly? (Yeah, well, not the best example. Try a trusted NPC getting posessed and displaying obvious signs of derangement, perhaps.)

    This way, spending SAN points drives the resolution of the plot forward, while hogging SAN allows the GM to have his evil guys do evil things right under the PCs\u2019 noses.


    Footnote:
    * Usually, willful stupidity of this kind is swiftly punished by many traditional/gamist GMs: ignore the hints, run into danger, die. One significant way in which Coc and many of its published supplements fail the source material they claim to take so much pain to emulate.
