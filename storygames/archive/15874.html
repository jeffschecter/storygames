\n Those folks who remember me from when I was posting a lot a couple of years back know that I have an abiding interest in war as a roleplaying setting. Not only do war games provide the requisite action many of us demand of our gaming, but they also open the door to interesting historical and moral/ethical questions.

When I was a young lad, the war to roleplay was Vietnam. I grew up in a city filled with military bases and most of the kids I gamed with were the sons of veterans of that war, just as I'm sure many of our dads were the sons of WWII vets. Vietnam was "our" war and, thanks to movies like Rambo: First Blood, Part II, we didn't have to stop fighting it in our imaginations just because it didn't go well for our side.

I suspect that the teens of the near future will fight and re-fight Afghanistan and Iraq in their games. It's only natural, really. You play what you know. We already see it happening with the explosive popularity of stuff like Call of Duty.

Anyway, specificity seemed very important to us back in the '80s when I was playing those games of Recon or what have you. There was [insert war] and we were telling stories about it. That mindset seems to have kept with us \u2014 witness all those GURPS supplements involving WWII, for example \u2014 but I've noticed a few cases where the war under discussion doesn't have to be any particular war at all. These games center around War as a concept, seeming to proceed from the assumption that all soldiers everywhere have a commonality of experience.

Right now I'm thinking about two games: Shell Shock and Black Cadillacs. Shell Shock just comes right out and says it: "Shell Shock is a set of rules which will allow you to tell the stories of these soldiers. There are no predetermined setting. You can tell the stories of US soldiers confronted to the horrors of the Korean war or the lives of guerrilla fighters on the Polar front of the Mars independance [sic] war of 2135." I have not seen a physical copy of Black Cadillacs, but I've read enough about it at this point that I'm sure it says something similar. Feel free to correct me if I'm wrong.

Until just today, I had not thought carry. a game about war. was of a piece with the previous two games. I know carry only through the (fairly rudimentary) version posted to 1KM1KT and it's very much about the Vietnam War. But Adam Dray said something in another thread about it: "carry doesn't really present a portrait of the Vietnam War at all. It's just a game about war in general. Any color or theme comes largely from us. I chose to set this particular game in the Battle of Hu\u1ebf just to try something a little different. I've never run a game of carry set in any particular place or on any specific date."

I find this very interesting. There's certainly no shortage of games about specific wars \u2014 we even have The Bloody Forks of the Ohio, which involves the French and Indian War, which is about as "niche" as you can get when talking about American wars \u2014 but is there something more appealing about disconnecting our war stories from history and focusing on fictional conflicts? I get that it may assuage the social and political flare-ups that might occur with an historical scenario, but are there other benefits? Are we better off using Shell Shock to tell the story of "guerrilla fighters on the Polar front of the Mars independance [sic] war of 2135" than that of German soldiers in the siege on Stalingrad? I honestly don't know, which is why I put it to you here.

    \n The experience I've had with Carry is that it provides enough to present a "theme" or "backdrop" of Vietnam, but the mechanics aren't explicitly tied to that engagement.
    Carry is about the interconnect of the squads (or fire teams or what have you) and about the issues inside the characters. I agree with Adam, it's essentially portable to any engagement, so long as you want to focus on the tension in the group, and build towards that eventual blowout.

    The bonus is that it's been a hard blow to people that want to find glory in war, as they watch the game system focus on a different side of things, a more personal and often tragic side.

    That's how my experience has been, but your mileage may certainly vary.

    \n [edited heavily at the realization that I was repeating what Sam! said.]

    Depends on your table. Cop-out answer, I know, but I think the possibilities of reactions to any particular choice of conflict and how it is presented obviate almost everything else.

    Coming from a miniatures wargaming background, I've seen an interesting phenomena: 'I Won't Play...' It comes in many flavors: Nazis, Communists, The Confederate South, The Union North, Vietnam at all, any current conflict, Sci-Fi, Real History, The Man, Aliens, Specific Nazis (Wehrmacht OK, SS not so much), any conflict in living memory. Different things set off people's 'squick' factor.

    A lot comes from the 'authors intent'. Is the game organizer/GM/table trying to 'make a statement about (war)'? Or are they trying to explore in play the various aspects of people in war? 3:16's futility, carry's Burdens & the consequences of failing to bear them, Grey Rank's children of a doomed revolt, Shell Shock's psychological grind, etc. I don't think we're ever better off with the former. Well, I'm not. If I want to know your position on (war), I'll ask you. But even if we disagree on (war), a fun game can still be had in the second case.

    That said, for some people setting the game in a fictional war removes the emotional link. I can understand that, even if I don't share it to the same degree. Does Grey Ranks work better in Warsaw, Poland, Calumet, Colorado, or Nilosyrtis, Martian Free State?

    \n Practically speaking, with some gamers, setting a game in a real historical period sets off a lot of disputes about what the infantry weapon really was for the average soldier of that period, the loadout on T-72 tanks, the stopping power of a panzerfaust used against Jeeps... everything grinds to a halt as people argue about historical facts. I used to game a lot in the Army and this was a constant annoyance (paradoxically, Twilight 2000 was the perfect game for our group since it was basically about us, where we lived, only after the war).

    I think the basic type of combat is more important than specific locations, when it comes to re-skinning a game. Carry can be used for any sort of counterinsurgency, I think; you could use it for Israel's invasion of Lebanon pretty easily. I'd like to see good indie RPG's covering insurgency from the other side, I've never seen one.

    \n Here's the tricks I'm using for the 3:16 reskin to model low-level fights in Vietnam:

    1) Use the lowered 'damage rates' Gregor Hutton made for some of the later playtest versions of the system - AD 316 & Carnage Amongst The Tribes (still love to see those in print). Instead of rolling bigger dice, it starts with 1 kill, and moves up to maybe a d6 or d8.

    2) The end result, 'kills', isn't confirmed kills at all. It's 'body count', the number your team estimates you killed. There may be actual bodies in there, there may not. Depends.

    (Body count math joke: 2 confirmed dead + 1 probable + 2 pigs = 37 enemy KIA.)

    Also, Ambush Alley and FNG are spectacular tabletop minis games.
