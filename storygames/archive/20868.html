\n I was wondering if there are any Story Games out there that are GMless but give a GM feel. Is it possible to make a GMless game were the plot and NPCs are directed (kind of like a game with a GM) rather than improvized or made in a collaborative way (maybe it would need players to take turns GMing? or maybe there is a mechanical answer?) and the game could have somewhat longish campaign, kind like a PbtA game without the MC. Basically, the players would be going into encounters and experiencing the plot 'blind' most of the time, without framing a scene or collaborating, etc. I'm just wondering how successful collaborative GMless Story Games have been at offering modes of play typical of GM focused RPGs. I hope this makes sense Anyway, if anyone knows of any games that do this successfully, I'd love to know about them; also, if anyone has any ideas on the matter, I would love to here them Thanks

    \n Tentatively, there are games where collaborating toward achieving a pre-determined story arc trumps improvising, and all the improvising you do is in service of a known endpoint. Matthijs Holter's Archipelago is one such game: at the beginning of each session, a "destiny point" (a session endpoint, achieved goal or big twist) is written down for each protagonist character (through a semi-collaborative process) - play then consists of collaboratively maneuvering to achieve those discrete end states.

    There are also "GMless" games where a number of players act in concert, creating and depicting a situation, a cast of characters, etc., all functional to seeing one individual main character through it. Some involve planning. One such game is Ben Lehman's The Drifter's Escape, where the player playing the drifter (the main character) is sent out of the room for a few minutes, while the other players lay out a charged situation for her to encounter (based on a set of oracles). A similar setup, but without the planning, is found in Steve Hickey's Left Coast: one player plays the main character, while the other players manage a cast of supporting characters and an overarching mystery. The supporting cast players don't get to make a plan together, but each has their own individual secrets for the main character player to uncover, plus they can communicate by showing each other their secret notes during play.

    On the other hand, not all games with a GM role involve pre-planning of a plot, coordinated directing of NPCs, or anything. Consider games such as Vincent Baker's In a Wicked Age or Ben Lehman's Bliss Stage: both have a "GM" but are effectively no-prep games, where the GM's job is to frame scenes according to a few general rules or principles, than play her own characters ("NPCs") not unlike PCs, except more disposable.

    \n Great post by Rafu!

    This depends a lot on what, in particular, you're looking for. For example, In a Wicked Age... has a GM... kind of. It says you can take turns GMing chapters after the second chapter.

    But the oracles and Owe List work together to give the game a direction, even though no one is strictly in control of it. It can create that "building a campaign/world together" feel, despite being a no-prep sorta-GMless game. Great for long-term play.

    My game Land of Nodd has a similar thing going on, where there is no single GM, but, nevertheless, the game's rules create an illusion over time of an "underlying plot" that no one really has access to.

    There's so much "middle ground" there!

    Finally, there are games with existing storylines/shapes, like Fall of Magic, maybe Follow, Downfall, and Final Hour of a Storied Age. I haven't played these, but I believe they all start with a sort of "storyline" that you play through together and head in a particular direction despite being GMless. I don't know if that fulfills your criteria at all!

    \n A note on Land of Nodd:

    This game has a particular strong "GM feel", not just because of the "metaplot", but also because of the Narrator-Protagonist relationship. When played right, the "GM" (Narrator) is a really strong, almost overpowering figure, with the Protagonist a plaything in their hands, desperate to escape their fate.

    My NYC designer friends describe the game as "Railroading made fun". I can totally see why they say that.

    \nKagematsu
    One person is taking the role as the samurai Kagematsu, and the rest of the participants are village women trying to convince Kagematsu to save their village. It's not said out loud, but the one playing the samurai is basically a game master. It's a brilliant game too, and the printed book has tighter rules.

    https://kagematsu.wordpress.com/kagematsu-rules/
