\n Hey, I've spent the last few years working on the 2nd edition to Perfect.

Essential background information for the uninitiated: Perfect is a game about criminals, and why they commit crimes. The game has a rotating spotlight, and when it's your spotlight you go through a few phases of play: Committing a Crime (which is a narrative in which you succeed at your crime, whatever it is), Discovery Test (where we see whether the inspectors catch you), Conditioning Test (if they caught you, then we see whether they successfully brainwash you), Reflection Scene (a brief vignette where you return to your normal life - a "downbeat" where we see who you are when you're not in the heat of a crime).

Recently, I've started killing darlings pretty aggressively, in an attempt to bring the game closer to my new play preferences. The biggest darling at the moment is my game's setting, Cadence. It's a Victorian-Dystopia. When the saintly Queen Abigail died, parliament completely reworked society to better serve her memory - now everything functions automatically, in a static balance. Inspectors roam the streets, looking to enforce all the rigid social laws that guide people's lives. Love is all-but-illegal. You wear government issued grey. You are controlled. Well, they thought they had you controlled...

And this setting is fun to play in, for me. But there's this issue where I like creating settings through play, and I dislike relying on a central figure (a GM) to disseminate canon.

So I've got this idea. I can provide some central tenets for writing your own setting, a simple "style sheet" that you fill in before and through play to create your own world of Perfect. You can simply use Cadence (my completed style sheet), or you can make your own.

There are a few mandatory elements for a Perfect society: the law enforcers must be distant from criminals (external to society, faceless enforcers, other-than-human), the society must be big enough that there are strangers in a community, the enforcers must rely on psychological warfare (specifically, Conditioning sessions of some type), conditioning must leave criminals capable of committing future crimes. Other than that, it's fair game, so far.

So, I'm trying to figure out what's going to be on that style sheet.
Right now, I'm thinking:

1.) In three words, this society is ___________, ___________, ___________.
(blanks could be: steampunk, Victorian, futuristic, industrialized, modern, mostly rural, mostly urban, European, religious, capitalist...)

2.) It's like ______________ meets _______________.
(blanks could be: Blade Runner, Clockwork Orange, 1984, Dexter, Handmaid's Tale, Fahrenheit 451, Equilibrium, A Scanner Darkly, Survivor, any genre fic...)

3.) The three control axioms are ______________, ______________, ________________.
(blanks could be: Love is Vulgar, Colour is Banned, Women Are Weak, Violence Is Always Wrong, Marriage Is Assigned, Children Can't Choose, Immigrants Are Dangerous, Men Must Be Subjugated, Respect Your Elders...)

4a.) What kinds of social organizations exist? __________________________________
(blank could include: professional, arts-related, political, religious cults, mandatory volunteer assemblies, celebrity fan clubs...)
4b.) How are organizations controlled and regulated? _____________________________
(blank could include: by government, by managerial body, by members, membership cards, initiation trials, mandatory membership for certain classes, self-declared membership...)

5a.) Who enforces law? _______________________________
(blank could include: inspectors clad in black, heavily armed riot police)
5b.) What technologies do they use to condition? _________________________
(blank could include: dehumanization tactics, psychic projections, neural uploading, electroshock therapy, waterboarding, memory flushes, hypnosis, starvation, physical torture, media barrages, implanting microchips...)

The idea is that after this style sheet was created... on a given player's turn, they would choose an axiom that their crime conflicted with, create a law that their actions will break, and then narrate the vignette of breaking this law. Thus, through play a system of laws and customs would be established, with these laws always embodying some of the culture's guiding axioms of control and structure. You could break laws that'd been established in previous crimes, as well!

You'd also write specific organizations for your characters to be involved in, which reflected 4a and 4b. So if we were playing in Cadence, I might create the Lamplighter's Guild to be a part of, or create the Art Appreciator's Society (every character has 2 group memberships).

So, if you see issues (stuff missing, redundant elements, boring aspects, improvements), raise them.
If you think this looks sound and compelling, fill in a style sheet for me!

    \n The Confederacy of Sanguine

    1. In Three Words, this society is: mostly rural, religious, and capitalist.

    2. It's like Atlas Shrugged meets Handmaiden's Tale.

    3. The three control axioms are: respect your betters (be they whiter, richer, or older \u2014 in that order), do your job, don't rock the boat.

    4a. What kind of social organizations exist? Churches, business conglomerates, historical re-enactment societies, sports clubs
    4b. How are social organizations controlled and regulated? By committees, often by individuals with social clout, by fashion and dress, by geography.

    5a. Who enforces law? Lynch mobs of masked men who meet at night
    5b. What technologies do they use to condition? Humiliation and dehumanization, threats, and physical torture

    Playing in Sanguine is more about breaking rules than breaking laws. Sure, there are laws, but they mostly cover "don't kill your neighbor" and suchlike. Lynch mobs routinely break laws in order to enforce their rules \u2014 and are rarely prosecuted for it.

    Thinking in terms of "product" for a second, Perfect will be presented as a build-your-own-setting game, but attached will be 4-6 completed style sheets, as well as a fully fleshed-out setting (Cadence).

    So, imagine that you've got this book... Pages 1-X are how-to-play, with running examples drawn from both Cadence and other settings.
    Pages X-Y are style sheets (a page for The Confederacy of Sanguine, a page for Skinnyghost's setting, plus 3 more, FOR EXAMPLE).
    Pages Y-Z would present Cadence - a style sheet plus 5-10 pages of images and additional background.

    This way, you can create your own setting for an ongoing game (or a one-shot), or you can just grab a style sheet from the ones presented, or you can immerse yourself into this favourite setting of mine, Cadence.

    For those of you who have played Perfect before or read it, can you stat out a Cadence style sheet for me?

    \nI the spirit of my $.02. I would prefer:
    \n
    \nPages 1-X are how to play, with running examples drawn from Cadence (full stop, no mention of other settings).
    \nPages X-Y introduce the concept of style sheets and show how Cadence was drawn from / out of a style sheet.
    \nPages Y-Z are alternate style sheets with the instructions on how to create your own.
    \n
    \nIn otherwords lead completely, utterly and fully with Cadence (which IMO is too awesome a setting to relegate to just "one example among many" status). THEN show people how to build their own "Cadence" by breaking Cadence down (reverse engineering a style sheet out of Cadence, if you will).
    \n
    \nThat worked really well in Penny for My Thoughts which holds to the central Amnesiacs in an Institution Setting throughout the book and then building out the options for alternate settings only at the end.
    \n
    \nSo I guess to phrase this succinctly: Style Sheets, Yes. But don't dilute the power of Cadence in the process.
