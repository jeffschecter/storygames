\n I'm moving forward with bringing out Ganakagok -- where a quasi-Inuit people in a dark world of ice grapple with radical, world-altering change -- in playable form. My plan is to have the game book and Ganakagok tarot printed and ready by the time Dreamation rolls around next year. But check out the cover art I just got!



The artist is Jeremy Mohler, who has done some other game-related work as well as art for a Canadian media company that's creating graphic versions of Inuit myths like the story of the sea-goddess Sedna (whose dismemberment populates the oceans).

    \n I appreciate the feedback, particularly the criticisms. As certain members of this community have brought home to me over the years, I have no flair for layout. Luckily, the label in the image above is just a mock-up. My buddy Dave, who designed the Ganakagok cards and teaches graphic design, will do the final version assuming that all proceeds according to plan. I will make sure that people's comments are taken as advice.

    I guess "mythopoetic roleplaying" doesn't really belong on the cover. Even the spelling (admittedly a variant albeit an accepted one of the more common "mythopoietic") is an attempt to balance the obscurity of the term with the fact that it absolutely describes what happens in the game: it feels like you've just played through an origin myth or just-so story of an arctic hunter-gatherer people. That is, I thought spelling it "mythopoetic" would make the term less strange than "mythopoietic," since even if you'd never encountered the word before, you could break it apart into "myth" and "poetry" -- and if that appealed to you, then you'd be exactly the target audience for the game.

    So the interior art will be a mix of old woodcuts that Jason Morningstar found in a book in the university library where he works--funnily enough, a friend of mine ran across the same book in an antiques store not 15 miles from my house, so I have the bibliographic info somewhere--and public domain photographs from the Library of Congress photoshopped to make them look more night-like.

    \n One suggestion to the cards layout, instead of placing the Attribute/Gift/Burden/Opposed things in the bottom, you could place them on the sides (increasing the font size by a couple of points). This would reduce a little the readability (because the player would have to read in another orientation) but increasing the font and placing them in easily recognizable and more distant places would increase long term usability (e.g. players would associate Gift with top left and would scan the relevant info faster).
    \n
    \nAnother thought that crossed my mind, if the suit/numeric meaning is "reusable" (i.e. same number different suit would have the same numeric meaning and same suit different number would have the same suit meaning) you could put it in a booklet that describes the cards in more detail (like tarot books) to regain space and avoid putting too much information on the cards. This increases the cost to interpret the card which is bad in usability, but increases the appeal to learn the cards and their interpretation (some people like to be challenged).
    \n
    \nA final thought: as I look at the cards I keep missing more iconic/layout references to focus my attention. All the information up to the bottom box (with the meanings and traits) is easy to find and scan, but the bottom box makes me switch from scan to read mode, which make the cards a bit harder to understand if I'm not already familiar with them. I have no idea of which kinds of icons/layout changes would help though, as I'm just a wannabe visual designer.
    \n
    \nP.S. Indeed it's a great cover and "mythopoetic roleplaying" sounds really appealing to me.
