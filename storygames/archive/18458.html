\n As discussed over in this thread, I'm working on hacking Apocalypse World to run at least a few sessions of a Mass Effect game for my friends. Some folks in that thread seemed keen on offering feedback, so that's what this thread is for.

I wish I had time to build something airtight from the ground up, but for now, I'm mostly cobbling this together from existing rules, moves, and stats. I just want to make sure I'm not breaking anything terribly when I do decide to tweak something major. The main sources I'm borrowing from include a couple other Mass Effect hacks, Apocalypse Effect and Mass Effect: Apocalypse, as well as The Regiment: Colonial Marines. Really, I could run any one of those, but I felt like tweaking things a bit and got bit by the design bug.

Anyway, thanks in advance for feedback. I'll comment again to ask some specific questions as they come up, but please also feel free to jump in with any random suggestion that you think ought to be in a Mass Effect game.

    \n I figure a good place to start is with just a general summary of the framework I've got in terms of stats, basic moves, and playbooks.

    STATS

    I'm using hard, cool, hot, and sharp basically unaltered. Maybe they could be renamed or rethought as something more Mass-Effect-sounding, and I'd certainly entertain suggestions, but I've kept them as-is so far because they seem to work fine and are familiar to my players. In general, hard is for fighting with weapons, cool is for biotic powers (given the emphasis on biotics' mental discipline in the lore), and sharp is for tech powers (given the emphasis on engineers' intelligence/cleverness in the lore). This means that biotic characters are also good at acting under fire, and tech characters are also good at finding and taking cover (originally "stay the fuck down" in AW, but I added some stuff here to make it something you're more likely to do with some frequency). Hot still does what you expect hot to do, with some moves cherry-picked from other games and renamed things that would be familiar to Mass Effect players (e.g., "Hypnotic" is now "Paramour").

    I replaced weird with alien (see this thread). It's used for moves when you do something that makes you seem horribly alien to others, or when you do something that reminds you just how alien others are to you. Maybe that's not the best way to do double-duty, but I am happy with it so far. It has no basic move associated with it, but each species has a roll+alien move, and there are a few other moves scattered around. The most important is the healing move, Medic, which has you roll+cool (on your own species) or roll+alien (on another species).

    I also replaced the term history (Hx) with loyalty (to slip in another Mass Effect reference), but otherwise it's mechanically unchanged.

    PLAYBOOKS

    Playbooks have a side A/side B approach. Side A is your class, showing stats, gear, and most of your moves, along with the other stuff you mark up during play (like harm). Side B is your species, with basic move text, species move, and a picture. All the species-specific stuff is on one page, and everything else is on another page, so you can just pick two pages, slap them together, and be good to go.

    I couldn't decide whether I wanted the playbooks to just be the six standard classes from the video game, or whether I wanted to have some more flavor, so I decided to have my cake and eat it too. I'm making "core playbooks" for the standard six (Soldier, Adept, Engineer, Infiltrator, Vanguard, Sentinel) and "optional playbooks" for players who want a little more flavor and a little less combat built in (e.g., Broker, Pilot, Thief, Doctor, etc.).

    I am cutting a LOT of powers from the Mass Effect games, mostly to preserve niche protection for the core playbooks. When I was throwing in just about every power in the game, the playbooks all felt like they were doing the same thing, and most of the moves basically boiled down to "kill dudes in marginally different ways," which looked really dull. Now that I've stripped things down, the classes are reminiscent of Mass Effect 1 in their diversity: The Soldier has reliable damage and durability at any range, at any moment; The Sentinel is durable and great at protecting others, but has little offensive power; The Adept is good at moving people around; and so on.

    \nBASIC MOVES

    Act Under Fire, Read a Person, and Help or Interfere are unchanged. I might have gotten a bit overzealous with changes elsewhere, though, so let me know if you think I broke anything.

    I overhauled Go Aggro and Seize by Force to remove some ambiguity that confuses some of my newer players. Now, if you're firing on somebody who's not ready for it, it's Open Fire (but you can still use this move to force them to do something without bloodshed, as with Go Aggro; it basically turns it into a 10+ roll on Seduce or Manipulate). If it's open combat, it's Trade Fire (almost identical to Seize by Force, but you choose fewer options so things don't get too confusing with all the bonus effects from powers and ammo mods).

    As a side note: Looking at this, it might seem like you're equally effective in a fight with three different stats. I won't get into the details of this right now, but basically, you can't use biotic/tech powers every single move without messing yourself up pretty badly, and they don't do as much harm as guns. So, most of the time, these moves will be rolled+hard.\n
    Open Fire: When you get the drop on someone, if you're fighting with\u2026
    \t\u2022\tweapons, roll+hard
    \t\u2022\tbiotics, roll+cool
    \t\u2022\ttech, roll+sharp
    On a 10+, you choose 1. On a 7\u20139, the GM chooses 1.
    \t\u2022\tYou hit. Apply harm and effects from your weapon/mod/power.
    \t\u2022\tYou graze them, but they know you mean business. Apply less than full harm.
    \t\u2022\tYou miss them. Call it a warning shot.
    If your target's still alive, now they choose 1:
    \t\u2022\tThey prepare for open combat.
    \t\u2022\tThey freeze. Treat as if you'd rolled a 10+ to intimidate them.
    \t\u2022\tThey hit the deck, flee, or barricade themselves in.
    On a miss, you blow your advantage; use trade fire instead if they shoot back.

    Trade Fire: When you engage in open combat, if you're fighting with\u2026
    \t\u2022\tweapons, roll+hard
    \t\u2022\tbiotics, roll+cool
    \t\u2022\ttech, roll+sharp
    On a hit, trade harm-for-harm as established by weapons or powers, and apply effects from powers or ammo mods. On a 10+, also choose 2; on a 7\u20139, choose 1:
    \t\u2022\tDamage (cause +1harm)
    \t\u2022\tDefense (suffer \u20131harm)
    \t\u2022\tControl (seize a person, place, or thing).
    \t\u2022\tImpress (inspire fear, outrage, respect, etc.)
    If you attack with a weapon or power that does no harm or only s-harm, don't pick damage.
    \nRead a Sitch is mostly unchanged, but I took out the +1 bonus for acting on answers (that's now its own move, like the Sergeant's Veteran's Instinct move from the Regiment Colonial Marines), and I added one option so the move can be used when scavenging, investigating a murder scene, or other kinds of stories:\n
    Read a Situation When you read a situation or examine your environment, roll+sharp. (...)
    \t\u2022\tDo I see any evidence of ____?
    \nSeduce or Manipulate has been renamed Charm or Intimidate (a reference back to the games). I reworded it a little to get around some confusion among my players. It's functionally identical, though ... I think.\n
    Charm or Intimidate: When you try to charm or intimidate someone, tell them what you want and roll+hot. On a hit, NPCs will do what you want if you offer something to help persuade them (e.g., a sincere argument, an even trade, a believable lie, a credible threat). On a 10+, you can make good on your offer later (or not, if you prefer). On a 7\u20139, they need some proof right now that your offer is genuine. When you use this move on PCs, on a 10+, both; on a 7\u20139, choose 1:
    \t\u2022\tif they do it, they mark experience;
    \t\u2022\tif they refuse, it's acting under fire.
    What they do then is up to them.
    \nThis next one is actually an optional move, but I figure I should throw it in because Session End doesn't make sense without it...\n
    Interrupt: Once per session, you may do one of the following when you roll:
    \t\u2022\tWhen you show great heroism, honor, or compassion, roll+paragon instead of another stat. Gain +1paragon after the roll.
    \t\u2022\tWhen you're a total badass, doing whatever damage you must to get the job done, roll+renegade instead of another stat. Gain +1renegade after the roll.
    If paragon or renegade reaches +4, reset it to +1 and mark xp.
    \nAnd finally...\n
    Session End: At the end of every session, choose at least 1, up to all 3:
    \t\u2022\tSomebody knows you better than before. Tell them to take +1loyalty with you.
    \t\u2022\tSomebody did something really heroic. Tell them to take +1paragon.
    \t\u2022\tSomebody did something really badass. Tell them to take +1renegade.
    If anyone's paragon, renegade, or loyalty reaches +4, they reset it to +1 and mark xp.
    \nI'll post about other moves and stuff sometime later on. That seems like more than enough of me being wordy as hell for now.

    +ALIEN MOVES

    Medic: When you attend to an injury, roll+cool (if it's someone of your own species) or +alien (if it's someone of another species). On 10+, choose 2; on 7\u20139, choose 1:
    \t\u2022\tStabilize them without needing to spend medi-gel.
    \t\u2022\tHeal them (or yourself) 1 health per medi-gel spent.
    \t\u2022\tEveryone in your squad spends 1 medi-gel and heals 1 health, triggered remotely.
    \t\u2022\tThey regain consciousness and get back in the fight (provided they have some health).

    Unique Ability: When you draw on your strange talent, roll+alien. On a 10+, it works safely and effectively. On a 7\u20139, it works, but you strain yourself or freak people out; take \u20131 forward. On a miss, it doesn't work as intended, or it does work but you're stunned, harmed, or out of control. When you choose this move, define your ability, where it comes from, and its downside. Examples from Mass Effect include:
    \t\u2022\tTouching a Prothean beacon led to visions, but they're vague and disorienting.
    \t\u2022\tSuffering mind control led to a psychic link with others like you, but you're never really alone in your own mind anymore.
    \t\u2022\tCybernetic implants augmented you physically, but you're not sure if you're even in control of your own mind anymore.

    Xenoscience: When you dedicate yourself to a project involving alien artifacts or specimens, roll+alien. On a 10+, you can do it, but it has a cost or risk determined by the GM. On a 7\u20139, it has multiple costs or risks. Costs/risks might include resources, time, materials (on hand or otherwise), and/or danger. On a miss, by the time you figure out what the risks were, it's too late.

    (Asari) Embrace Eternity: When you meld your mind with someone else's, roll+alien. On a 10+, hold 3. On a 7\u20139, hold 1. While you\u2019re joined, spend hold 1-for-1 to ask questions:
    \t\u2022\tWhat was your lowest or highest moment?
    \t\u2022\tFor what do you crave forgiveness, and of whom?
    \t\u2022\tWhat are your secret pains or joys?
    \t\u2022\tWhat do you hold closest to your heart?
    \t\u2022\tWhat truth is your mind hiding from you? (GM may also answer for a player.)
    If this was an intimate exchange between PCs, both gain +1loyalty with the other.

    (Batarian) Raider: When you give a non-batarian an order or a warning, roll+alien. On a hit, they choose:
    \t\u2022\tThey accept your order/warning.
    \t\u2022\tThey freeze.
    \t\u2022\tThey back away, hands where you can see them.
    \t\u2022\tThey attack you.
    On a 10+, take +1 forward against them as well. On a miss, they do what they like and you take \u20131 forward against them.

    (Drell) Relived Memories: When you relive a memory, roll+alien. On a hit, describe what you remember as if reading hastily scrawled notes from your subconscious. On a 10+, the GM will add details that would be helpful to your current situation. On a 7\u20139, the GM may add extraneous details; it's up to you to make them useful. On a miss, you get lost in your memories.

    (Human) First Contact: When you're touched by something truly alien for the first time, roll+alien. On a 10+, you get all 4 below. On a 7\u20139, choose 3.
    \t\u2022\tIt leaves you with an ability you didn't have before.
    \t\u2022\tIt causes you no immediate harm.
    \t\u2022\tIt leaves no permanent visible mark upon you.
    \t\u2022\tIt leaves you in full control of yourself.
    On a miss, the GM chooses 2 for you.

    (Krogan) Rage: When harm reduces your health, roll+alien\u2013harm instead of making the harm move. On a 10+, the pain just makes you angry; take +1 now to trade fire. On a 7\u20139, you lose your footing, lose your grip, lose track of something, or miss noticing something (GM chooses 1). On a miss, the GM can choose one of the aforementioned options for \u20131harm.

    (Quarian) Scavenger: When you need some small object, roll+alien. On 10+, you have just the thing. On 7\u20139, you have something pretty close, so long as it isn't too high-tech. On a miss, you used to have it, but it's sold, stolen, or busted.

    (Salarian) Hyperactive Metabolism: When you need to do something quickly, roll+alien. On a hit, you can do it in half the expected time, and non-salarians can't roll to interfere with you. On a 7\u20139, you still act fast, but non-salarians can't follow what you're doing well enough to roll to aid or follow through for you.

    (Turian) Duty & Honor: When you help a teammate who's making a roll, you may roll+alien instead of making the help or interfere move. On a hit, they take +2 now. On a 7\u20139, you also expose yourself to fire, danger, retribution, or cost. On a miss, your teammate marks xp instead of you.

    (Volus) Client Race: When you need a service performed, put out the word and roll+alien. On a hit, you find someone who can do it. On a 10+, choose 2. On a 7\u20139, choose 1.
    \t\u2022\tYou can get it done quickly.
    \t\u2022\tYou can get it done by the best.
    \t\u2022\tYou can get it done cheaply, or get more for your money.

    (Vorcha) Regeneration: When you take cover while injured, roll+alien. On a 10+, regain 1-health; on a 7\u20139, regain 1-health or keep fighting, but not both. Don't use this move if you take harm from burning.

    \n Especially 3 had much less focus on dialogue. But here's what I have to say about that: What's memorable about the Mass Effect games? What made 'em special? The combat was fun, and dialogue in games is hard to make fun (often interesting, but not often fun - as a side note, I credit Deus Ex Human Revolution for being one of the only games I've ever played that pulled off a legitimately fun gameplay experience from talking). That said, I think that for a lot of people, aside from a few particular major setpieces, the plot, setting, and character interaction is significantly more memorable than the combat. In a hack, it's those elements of Mass Effect I think are best to see. It's also HARD. Remember that not every element of the games needs to appear in a hack, just the things you think are really important.
    Basically the issue was the games had a thematic message of talking as much as you shoot, but the gameplay experience didn't really support that message all the time.

    Looking at other dialogue-based games certainly might help, but I think that I wouldn't really want too much mechanization of conversation. Monsterhearts (my go-to dialogue-based hack) does a pretty good deal of mechanizing conversation because it sees a lot more talking than direct action, but Mass Effect has its share of both. Tough to think about.
    Here's what I've been thinking about through the day: are Charm and Intimidate already kinda like Manipulate and Go Aggro, respectively? They kinda are from where I'm standing. They're how you get things done in a conversation - one by bribing and persuasion, the other by threats. The difference I see most apparently is that Shepard never was forced to follow through on Intimidate threats because the games usually only provided you the option to do it if it would be successful. Cuz Intimidate doesn't really work with the existing structure of Seduce/Manipulate.

    Other thoughts:
    The Alien stat. I'm tripping over your use of it a bit. In a lot of ways, your use of it is very much tied to the idea of things actually being literally aliens. However, in *-World games, the Stats are almost always adjectives that describe the personality of the character, not the physical truth of their being. This is what I was getting at in the last thread that sometimes humans are more alien than the aliens themselves, and aliens being more human than some humans. Making the moves with Alien trigger almost exclusively over things literally about species, with Unique Ability basically being the only one where being a different species doesn't actually matter to the move. This is why I think that Biotics are a great thing to go under Alien. Alien may not mean psychic, but psychic stuff definitely separates you from baseline humanity. (plus at this point I don't really buy Biotics as Cool )
    For example, I totally get why you have it so Alien matters with Medic. However, I disagree. Our stats are about OUR personalities. Why would me being all weird and different from humanity make me good at healing other species - not everyone is different from humanity in the same ways. Maybe it makes sense that a pretty out-there tech-head Quarian could do good healing for a human with cyber-implants, but how does a Krogan being all crazy and nothing like us help us heal other humans, since he would be triggered into using that stat for the roll. (also about Medic, Cool is already pretty commonly used cuz Act Under Fire is the catch-all move and is only somewhat appropriate for the move, Sharp might make more sense)
    Do you kinda get where I'm going with this? Taking advantage of the stuff that makes me different is Alien, but working on stuff different than me isn't. If being different made me good at everything different, that kinda lumps everything together to me.
    Of course, this can make it a bit harder to use Alien as a stat sometimes. But I think the thematic power is worth it.

    So, the class/species thing. That ain't bad, not at all. I do think, however, that you lose some interesting design space. Two things.
    1) Options. If you're splitting Species off into its own page, give each species choices to make. There ain't just one form for each species, gives lists of options. Maybe most of the Look and Name choices go on the species chart, with maybe clothing stuff going more to the Job chart. Also, give multiple move choices. Maybe everyone gets the same move for sure, or maybe only one to start with at all, but give some additional ones if someone wants to mechanically expand on the specialties of their species instead of their job. I mean, not all Krogan are alike after all - Wrex and Grunt would have different move selections, even though job-wise they would be very similar. Even Samara and Morinth would play very differently species, while job-wise they would be almost literally identical.
    2) Here's a different path: look at Dungeon World's handling of races. You see, as it is you have one thing that all members of a species start with, and that might not be helpful to their job selection. DW did it so that you picked a race as just another option on the playbook, giving you a move. Thing is, if you picked a Halfing Thief or Fighter or whatever it gave you different Halfling-related moves to keep them relevant. This path would immediately make it possible for their species move to be relevant. Yeah, you wouldn't have every species available for every job, but if you're gonna be an Adept, you're probably not gonna be a Krogan, and if you're gonna be a Vanguard, you're probably not gonna be a Volus.
    I prefer that first approach now though, having written this.
    Also, I'm not sure it's really a great idea to split the classes into more-or-less "combat" and "non-combat" and then call the non-combat ones optional. Sorta sends a weird message. Remember that playbooks are the archetypes of the genre, the types of characters whose adventures we care about. The ones you came up for the "optional" ones are probably Playbooks. The Pilot, The Doctor, The Scientist, The Big-Guns Guy, The Ex-Cop, The Disciplined Soldier, The Assassin. What archetypes are really important to the universe? Cuz I gotta say, when I see Wrex I don't say "Vanguard" and when I look at Liara I don't say "Adept", even if that's true.

    So where's the Hanar move? I can't play Blasto if I can't be a Hanar! (I'm, um, mostly kidding)

    Paragon/Renegade! Tough thing. The big thing I notice is that, by wording, Paragon is for heroic stuff and Renegade is for badass stuff. Pretty true to the games, but, trouble is, those things aren't really mutually exclusive. Can you be BOTH? I'd kinda recommend looking at a sliding-scale stat similar to The Angel in Monsterhearts, that could have cool ideas in it.

    Oh, and one other thing you just mentioned: "getting xp for adding stuff for your codex when you ask volus diplomats about their culture, I guess." That's probably a dumb idea to go much further with, for a few reasons. a) It's easily abusable for xp, b) it doesn't bring the fiction anywhere, it's just a standstill for an infodump that, face it, the players are probably just doing to get xp, and c) it puts the MC (GM?) on the spot, which sucks, especially if they feel like they don't want to contradict established lore but aren't knowledgable themselves.
    Honestly I think the "Shepard asks stuff or finds open infodumps on computers about the societies of the galaxy" thing was kind of a blunt tool Bioware was using to get the exposition out in the open while still optional. Asking about this stuff was never the memorable stuff of the games, even if the information itself was, so see if you can't find a more organic or fun behavior to reward.

    I'm done for now I think. This is all just musing, take what you want from it, a real ME hack is just something I'd care to see.

    \n Re: zoomed out fighting, biotics, and other chaotic thoughts.

    It's weird for me when people replicate Mass Effect's combat mechanics in RPGs, down to specific power names and effects, shields and armour, weapon types etc. They were important in the original game because it was a shooter; fighting was the only thing it dealt with and the only way it could differentiate between characters. However, your moves and playbooks should be based on the central concept of *your* game. So ok, if you're making a game about a soldier squad in ME, fine, zoom in on the combat, have specific combat powers, combat roles and so on.

    But personally, I'm bored with that - I want to play in ME world without necessarily replicating the ME shooter emphasis. (As a rule of thumb, I don't like games where combat mechanics are more complicated than diplomacy mechanics). Your description sounds like you want to allow such an experience as well. With that in mind, I'd be perfectly happy having a "soldier", a "biotic" and a "techie" playbook. Such a techie can be a combat squad member or they can be a junkyard techie. I'd also be quite happy with a generic "attack" move, a "when you use your biotics"/biotic move (usable for attack purposes AND stuff like lifting crates to open an entrance, or lifting and stealing a data pad out of someone's table), and "when you mess with someone else's equipment"/tech move.

    What I would like to avoid with biotics is the effect you can see in fantasy systems, where most of the magic spells in spellbooks are only useful in combat. It makes sense if the system is focused on combat, but if you want to allow non-combat adventures, you need a wider choice of spells. Similarly, ME games described only those biotic power usages that would be useful in combat, but lift for example is begging for out-of-combat usages.

    So from my point of view, barriers, fortification, lift, throw - these are all just colour, and they were quantified only for the shooter video game purposes. Just think of biotics as an equivalent of WoD Mage Forces.

    Perhaps there's no need to add a new clock/countdown. Just have people take one segment of physical damage when they overextend themselves - since biotic skills rely on tiny bits of foreign substance embedded in flesh, so it makes sense you hurt after using them too much.

    Also, I like Biotics being tied to Cool, although it may interfere with people's idea of a berserker-Vanguard.

      \n Neonchameleon is 100% correct. The ApW system isn't a setting emulator, it's a genre emulator - it consistently allows for types of stories. ApW is about scarcity and communities surviving that scarcity. DW is about small fries facing great odds for glory and riches. MH is perhaps the most targeted of them all: it's a teen sex horror story. This is where stats, basic moves, and playbook selection become very obviously the key factors, as yo get to choose what's most important for the stories you're trying to tell. When you emulate setting, you want to encompass all sorts of stories, and thus you need all-around basic moves that cover most situations. When you emulate genre, you can choose to exclude certain things that don't help those stories (such as MH dropping the Sharp stat and Reading moves) and add those that would only make sense for those kinda of stories (Turn Someone On).

      Here's a personal example with this hack: If I was the one in charge, I'd stop debating the nature of how being a paragon/renegade impacts the characters. For me, I don't want to hear about those morality stories, they're not the things I'm interested in in an ME game, and thus I'd leave them out. Comparatively, I'd focus on the exploration of an alien galaxy and give moves like "When you first step onto an untouched planet..."
      That's a different sort of story. Maybe not the one you want to tell, but it could be. What sort of game you want to run should be always in your mind, because it's locked tightly into the ApW system.
      Just remember: this system isn't meant to be a toolkit for every story in the Mass Effect universe, just like how ApW isn't a toolkit for every single post-apocalyptic story (hence why we have other games like The Quiet Year).
      With intention to use it for a small campaign, you can be even more focused. If you know you want the rachni in there, what role should they be? You could make it about reconciling with a misunderstood species, you could make it about destroying a dangerous threat, you could make it about conflict between the Krogan and the Rachni, or other things, and build from there.
      EDIT: Those things there are premises, seeds of where things are starting, not quite the same as specific stories as you'll still be playing to find out what happens. They're themes that can be expanded into the types of stories you intend to play, and then you can model the rules to play those sorts of stories.

      \n In re-reading my post, I think I've come up against a more fundamental problem with Give Orders. It's use on PCs doesn't feel like a Basic Move. On NPCs? Sure. On PCs? If feels like a playbook move. Why should every player as one of their most basic ways of interacting with the game systems be able to assert authority over other players by giving them orders? That's ... odd. Really odd. I don't grok that at all, especially not as a Mass Effect thing.

      I feel like there are some gaps in the Basic Moves. It's possible some of these things aren't needed, but to be convinced of that I'd want more depth (through more moves or tighter synthesis or through interlocking playbook moves) to the ways of doing already on display. Here's what I'm not finding:

      There's no established way to build non-hierarchical but nonetheless mechanically relevant relationships with NPCs--of particular relevance is that there's no way to build authority and rapport separately from using it to give orders. There's no established way to assess the environment, NPCs or "clues" or what-have-you. There's no established way to get stuff (doesn't have to be a barter move). There's no established way to interact with missions, jobs, or chain-of-command--your authority plays into a key basic move; surely someone's authority is key to why you're here and what you're doing whether it's authority you're following or authority you're running from (this sounds like exactly the sort of thing the playbooks can clear up). Lastly, there's nothing that really evokes an exploration of alien thought--Enact a Crazy Plan says it does this, but while I wouldn't argue with the stat choice, I don't think it creates a sense of delving into thought, orthodoxy, alien-ness or similar which you say you're interested in doing.

      Zooming in on one of those that seems most "basic move-y" for a moment, I'm coming around to being ok with there being no way to assess the environment, but again I'm missing some establishment, maneuvering, and similar moves to buy that completely--or some principles that make it clear the MC's side of the field is a mostly-open book such that knowing isn't anywhere near half the battle. Even then, observational moves are an important boon to the MC in these games--they give the MC a chance to establish fiction in a tight context while also giving the MC a sense of how much wasn't picked up on. Miss a move? Well, sure you can develop some nasty surprise as part of hard moves and front moves or off-screen badness or what-have-you in response to any failed roll, but having a missed Read Person roll sets your gears turning and helps you think about the situation in terms of what the PC might doesn't know about this person. Taking away those moves takes away those nice, cozy trigger moments. This holds even if the player doesn't fail and even if there are no moves at all--sometimes there's just no secret until the players start nosing for them and you realize it would be cool if there was something there after all. But the moves make asking questions about the world and the characters part of the player's basic mechanical vocabulary when they aren't sure what they want to do and when we're playing to find out what happens ... players are going to lean on those moves a lot.

      \n Yeah, I think my advice in the other thread applies pretty well to this crazy plan move too.

      Here's an example, I hope this is a legit use of the crazy plan move: Me and my team are fighting some enemy. I'm gonna bust open the airlock doors for a second so the enemy gets sucked out into space (this is a space setting isn't it?). I figure my team will all know to hold onto something, right? They better.

      Just the 10+ results, to keep it simpler. Other GMs might do these all differently, but here's my take.

      1.
      Okay, with the positive move, let's say I choose a rep to be ruined, because I really want the plan to work and fuck everything else. The move tells the GM that the plan works but a rep is ruined. As the GM I probably say the team thinks you are reckless now, you almost got them all killed. Or just one teammate (the important one) thinks that.

      If I make essentially the same choice with the negative move, it tells the GM that I'm opening the air lock to send the enemy out into space but the consequences are not immediate and my teammates are spared. As the GM, I'm kind of baffled as to how that happens, but I might say that the enemy gets some shots off (while you and your teammates get a hold of something) before being dumped into space, maybe. The upside maybe is that your rep isn't protected, so I will evaluate what all the NPCs now think of you separately.

      2.
      What if the player chooses the consequences are immediate? As the GM, I figure, yeah, the consequences for you. Roll to see if you go flying out into space along with the enemy. You can't hold on and punch a keyboard at the same time. No rep is ruined, but no rep is saved either, so I think I would evaluate for the NPCs based on the results.

      With the negative move, the same choice says that your rep is protected and your teammates are spared. Okay, so actually maybe this version is a bit better for you? I'd say your teammates are fine, you're not. But they still think you're cool if you do survive. So it takes out the role-playing of each of the NPCs maybe.

      3.
      And if the player chooses to throw their teammates under the bus? Cool, they all have to roll or get sucked out. They will definitely hate you later. How could your rep not be ruined? Oops, you just picked two choices by mistake! Or maybe not, I suppose, and that's just in this instance. Other times they might not know it was your fault. This example is just not a good time to pick this option.

      With the negative move, it tells the GM that the consequences are not immediate and that your rep is protected. Which actually sounds completely different than what the positive move dictates. As the GM, do I then maintain a slight pause before the airlock opens and decide how each NPC reacts? This has the full possibility of allowing the subsequent events to totally derail the crazy plan. Or do I say it totally works right now, but you get court-marshalled for it later, because it's totally illegal and/or reckless? But of you go up before a trial board for endangering your crew, they will stick up for you because your rep is intact. I can't tell if this is better or worse, but it seems a lot more convoluted and like it could turn into a scene resolution thing.

      Final score:
      Seems to me like the positive version gives me clear results, but the negative version makes me think about what's going on more. If I'm not doing that thinking already, the negative version might actually be better. But if I'm already considering things closely, the positive version gets me to the results quicker and then I can ponder the ramifications after we know what happens right away, not in the middle of that. Which is pretty much what I was trying to say in the other thread: with one we get to the results and then I think "hmm, what happens because of that?" and in the other, I'm doing some of that thinking before the outcome is announced. So perhaps the positive version applies itself better to a wider range of GM styles.

      On the other hand...
      Going back to Ghost/Echo for a second, you might be able to get a fun kind of guessing game going on with a negative choices move like this if the GM picked the consequences before the roll but didn't reveal them. Then you pick based on what you think is going to happen. Guess right and you make all the consequences impossible by saying they can't happen. Pick wrong and you make stuff that isn't going to happen impossible. It could be like a variation on the workspace rules, but with the added step of the player getting to cancel some requirements (I did this in Black Seas of Infinity), only the two sides make their picks independently. Dunno how well that would work, but this made me think of it.

      \n Honestly, the reputation system is still very much experimental. We have tried it maybe 3 or 4 different ways so far, and this one won't get tested until later this month. Based on feedback from my players and what I feel like I can keep track of, I wanted to come up with something that fulfills the following:

      1. We get to pass around little red and blue stones.
      2. Interrupts get to majorly influence success.
      3. The rules for Interrupts are clear and consistent.
      4. You have Paragon and Renegade scores, not just tokens to spend.

      So, no, you don't actually lower your scores when you spend tokens. You start at +0 for both (unless you decide you want to do something like Mass Effect 1 and give small starting bonuses based on origin story, but I leave that up to you). Once the scores add up to 10, getting +1 in one score means getting \u20131 to another score, possibly shifting back and forth or maxing one side out at +10 with the other at +0. I estimate that +10 is about as big a bonus as you can give in this system to make it game-changing (effectively like selecting a Paragon/Renegade option in the video game) without being totally game-breaking. We'll see if I'm right.

      \n How do you mean, about paragon and renegade points?

      Mostly I used them either as an afterthought ("Wow, you were REAL jerks on that one, here, have a renegade point") or to just get them to snap out of analysis paralysis ("Look, you can either keep your word and get a paragon point, or you can kill this criminal and get a renegade point, but if you have another idea and would rather get an XP, now's the time to say so").

      My first group thought they really wanted paragon and renegade scores that meant something numerically, but every way we tested doing that suggested to me they liked the idea in principle more than in execution. "I spend this point and I'm AWESOME" always felt better than "I spend this point and get +2 to my roll/damage/resistance, which may or may not be enough to make a real difference." In a more fleshed out version of the game, I could see taking that further \u2013\xa0like, once Paragon or Renegade reaches a certain score, it unlocks special moves, like how you advance moves in Apocalypse World or get corruption moves in Urban Shadows. We stumbled over everything more detailed than "spend a point, be awesome!" for our wee little games, but I'm very interested to hear ideas on how else it might be approached, if it's an idea worth pursuing at all!

      \n Oh, I think I get you. We actually tried out a few different versions, including implementations that were more specifically constrained in terms of what you could use the points on. Like, if you were going to spend a renegade point, it HAD to be because you were being a badass/meanie \u2013 you couldn't use it to jump in front of your friend to take a bullet for them. The result of that, though, was that players ended up not spending their points or feeling like they understood how to use them well. Partly they felt like they had to plan ahead to use them, when what they really wanted was a way to improve rolls that went wrong. They like rolling! Replacing a roll entirely with passing a token just wasn't something that occurred to them often.

      The way it's implemented now, there's still a narrative component to how they spend their tokens, but admittedly, it's more at our discretion than baked into the rules, and the consequences of interest are more fictional than mechanical. Like, if someone wants to spend a paragon token to improve them shooting somebody, I might prompt them with, "Okay, paragon interrupt! How do you save the day?" And then maybe they'll say, "Hm, actually, I just want to kill this guy, so I'll hold onto this to help somebody later." Or perhaps, "Well, I'm not trying to kill him, just get him to surrender. Can we say the shot doesn't hit him but gets him to freeze so we can talk?" And then we have a new roleplaying situation that leads things more into what they want to actually be doing, which is nice. But yeah, not really baked into the rules.

      The tricky thing for me in using any paragon/renegade mechanic is that while I'd like the mechanics to support the story, I want to make sure the "big choice moments" are driven more by what the characters (and their players) think is The Right Thing to Do than by what points they'll get for it. Like, my first group discovered a cybernetic species of robo-bugs that posed a threat to all life, and they needed to figure out how to deal with them. They opted to go to Cerberus for help (renegade) instead of the Council (paragon) because they figured Cerberus would get the job done no matter the cost. And they did, sort of! They figured out how to control the robo-bug menace so it would only attack the targets they chose. Players got their renegade points, but the real consequences were in what happened in the story.

      There's probably a good way to make the points themselves matter more, but I have been more concerned with just getting the rules to tell stories that feel "Mass Effecty" without getting too much in the way. I'd be very interested to hear other mechanical approaches, or examples from games that do anything similar very well. I gotta imagine some games have done cool things with special "fate point/plot point/bennies" that tie in more directly with fiction...
