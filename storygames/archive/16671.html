\n Andy K told me to post this here, so here we go.

I'm hacking Matthijs Holter's Archipelago 2. Running a session on Saturday night using dice (everything from d4 to d20), and instead of Resolution Cards, I'm making a table that the players have to roll on. You know, like damage. "You rolled a 4? That's a Yes, But. Let's see what the table says..."

I'll be playing a Wolven Ranger with a magic axe.

The players will roll to see who wins a fight: so if there's a verbal altercation, or a brawl, each player rolls. High roll wins, and then the winner chooses the Yes table or the No table, then rolls randomly (depending on what the player wants the outcome to be).

Since some of the Yes results will be Yes, But or Yes, And, there's no way of knowing how things will shake out. Should be fun.

I'm thinking that I'll print a map up before hand, or maybe paint one, on a large piece of posterboard. Then I'll use minis to keep track of where characters are, and each 'turn', a character can move up to two positions. That will restrict which characters can participate in a given scene, forcing players to use different characters in each round. Theoretically. We shall see how it goes!\ufeff

As for the dice, you've got a pool of six dice in front of you, and each time you want to do something, you roll one of those dice. Each time you do, it's used up. After they're all used up, you keep rolling d4 as the default whenever there's a conflict. You roll against another player when there's a conflict, so a d20 is probably going to win against a d4 (though there's always a chance!). The idea is, you've got two powerful moves, two medium, and two weak (one of which, the d4, can be used over and over again). So you have to decide how to approach each conflict, since you don't know what the other player is going to be rolling.

    \n I think this table is less like Rolemaster, and it's more like, there's a piece of paper with six sentences on it, and each one has a number written next to it. And you roll a d6 and read that particular sentence out loud.

    Although I think I need more outcomes. Here's what I got thus far:

    1. Yes, but...
    The character succeeds, but something unrelated goes wrong, for the character or someone he cares about.

    2. Yes, and...
    The character succeeds, and achieves more than he expected. Unfortunately, it's too much.

    3. Yes, but...
    The character succeeds, but the consequences are completely different than what he expected.

    4. Yes, and...
    The character achieves a complete victory in this particular situation.

    5. Yes, but...
    The character gets what he wants, but only if he chooses to make a certain sacrifice.

    6. Yes, and...
    The character gets what he wants, and misfortune befalls his opponent.

    1. No, but...
    The character fails, but another positive thing happens instead, unrelated to what he was aiming for.

    2. No, and...
    The character fails, and something else goes completely wrong.

    3. No, but...
    The character realizes that he needs the help of someone not in this scene in order to achieve the goal.

    4. No, and...
    The character's failure impacts someone he cares about in a horrific way.

    5. No, but...
    The character fails, but realizes that this may have been for the best, because of an unforeseen circumstance.

    6. No, and...
    The character fails, and a new threat manifests itself, promising even greater calamity.

    \n Even with tables as simple and well organized like this, the game flow is somewhat interrupted. Believe me, I've playtested designs that use tables for conflict resolution and just the act of checking a table after rolling the dice is enough to slow down the narrative momentum that the fiction has gained so far. It's the equivalent to stuttering when telling the punchline of a joke. That's why I'm using cards right now, you keep a wide range of variety in the results and yet you get a quick procedure for conflict resolution.

    Posted By: RafaelPosted By: David ArtmanAdd the rule "give your winning die to the losing player" and you've got a VERY interesting metagame underneath this basic structure.
    Hmm. So we joust, you throw a 20, I throw a 6, you win, then I keep your 20?
    What kind of metagame emerges?Basically, the metagame (maybe wrong word?) is that one has to decide if something is important enough to risk their best die or a very good die, to roll it. Further, if you do it via blind bidding (i.e., we each hide our die in our hand, then reveal simultaneously) I might wonder if you will lowball (especially if you know I haven't lost my 20 yet) and so maybe I roll my d12 instead.

    And failure (say I roll my 20 and lose) gets somewhat mitigated because I don't lose my die AND I get an extra 6 from you. And, notice that victory carries a real, mechanical price now--not as harsh as your original "it's gone forever" method, but also not as limited in terms of how many rolls a given person can make (that aren't suck-ass d4s). Plus, you get the classic "hero's arc" built right in: I lose tons of rolls, then--WHAM--I can come out swinging with d20 and d12 stacks.

    (aside)-----
    You could make this die passing mechanic work just fine with your dice-to-trait assignment mechanic by merely stating that one can't use a die size higher than the "original" or "base" die size when one evokes a trait. SO you win my 20... but you can only use it if you evoke your d20 trait. Perhaps I will maneuver in the fiction to make it a very tricky (or a "that's bullshit, man") evocation.
    (/aside)-----

    The metagame, then, is more about literal player versus player (not PCvPC!), tracking dice used and dice passed, bluffing: nothing to do with character positioning in the fiction (necessarily).

Otherwise, you can get to a point where ever problem looks like a nail (or has to be described as one, with much shoehorning!) and all you've got is a broken hammer.
