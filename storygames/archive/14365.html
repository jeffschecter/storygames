    \n Some drifts:

    * Lots of little tweaks and changes to BW's procedures, stuff like waiting until the end of the session to hand out artha (semi-institutionalized in BE and fully endorsed in Adventure Burner IIRC) and letting players use DOW to resolve conflicts between their characters (RAW specifies it's only for resolving conflicts between players, but "conflict" is never really well described). OTOH I'm pretty allergic to drifts some other players/GMs have tried that directly touch BW's resolution mechanisms -- like I have a GM who likes to make up MOS benefits on the fly, and it seriously cheeses me off because it feels like it's interfering with my stated intent.

    * Sometimes we had someone besides Gwendolyn hand out missions in MG. Just an appropriate-at-the-moment call. Didn't seem like a big deal.

    * Played around with the static-giving procedure in Lacuna, stuff like making the players think they'd logged out but then giving them static while they were "awake" as a cue that they were, in fact, still in the dreamscape. That whole game is about having fun dicking around with the players so this just seemed like one more way to mess with them.

    * We hacked the hell out of the company rules in Reign because fuck if we could make it work as advertised. Never worked, total failure, interest just sort of petered out after that.

    Most of our drifts I think arise from the current GM being intuitively dissatisfied about some aspect of resolution (range of possible outcomes feels goofy or boring), or on occasion because something makes total sense inside the narrative but a bit of guidance feels arbitrarily restrictive (Gwendolyn hands out missions) and not like an interesting creative constraint (ie most of the MC moves in AW).

    \n I drift when I don't know a rule off hand and no one else at the table does either. Sometimes we look it up (if we've got the time, it isn't a tense moment, or we think it's a really anchoring rule) otherwise i'll just make it up. Sometimes i try to put myself in the designer's shoes (i feel sort of comfortable fiating based on "what would Luke Crane do"). Usually that rule will stick for the rest of session. Between games we'll double check if we think of it. Sometimes we've drifted, sometimes we got it right.

    The other time is when we're playing in a sub-optimal situation, like at a con with a limited time frame. I've many times drifted MY OWN GAMES in these situations, to expedite or simplify a system so we can get on with the good stuff and not get bogged down in something less important.

    \nWhich games?
    Hero Wars

    How?
    There's are two tables a player uses to decide how much of an Augment he gets when he rolls for an Augment, and how much of a modification he's going to get if he tries to modify his Edge.

    I changed the odds on the table, making them more in line with Augmentation results table in HeroQuest 2 and I ripped the Edge column out of the whole process.

    Why?
    As the table stands, the odds to try to get an Augment are just not worth rolling in the Hero Wars rules -- the potential upside of a bonus is matched by to much risk of a penalty if you roll badly on your attempt for an Augment. The new table has a risk of downside, a soft middle of "no bonus, but no penalty," and a chance to get really nice bonuses even bigger than the original table if you roll well. Thus, more incentive to roll for the Augment, even if there is a risk of a penalty instead of a bonus.

    I ripped the Edge column out because I have yet to find any mention of how or when you roll for the Edges to become more effective during a contest. I like the Edges, I see a lot of potential with them.... I just think that column in the table is some sort of vestigial mechanic that got dropped somewhere along the line on the way to the printer.

    \n Oh, I just realized this thread tittle was about "drift" -- which seems bigger than just changing some tables around. Hmmm...

    Well, I'll just keep going anyway. I think this one is more drifty, anyway.

    In the Hero Wars rules, you can roll a character's abilities one of two ways: You can roll a d20 against your ability's value to see if you succeed in opposition to another character rolling a d20 against his ability. The relative results of success or failure are then compared on a table to see how the conflict turned out. (Complete Victory, Marginal Victory, Total Defeat and so on.)

    Or you can just roll the d20 against the ability as unopposed as a skill check.

    In my cheat sheet write-up of the rules, I'm ripping that unopposed skill check right out. We'll only have opposed, conflict resolution in play.


    So, basically in Hero Wars, I'm looking at this really efficient conflict resolution system with an in credibly flexible Ability character description mechanic ready to soak up any situation-rich setting ("Hates Lunar Empire" is as valuable an Ability as "Loves Wife"), where the mechanics detailing how a character plugs into his or her community are far more detailed than anything to do with how many gold pieces the character has, that encourages the use of adding fictional detail of character's behavior in conflict through the use of a bonus mechanic....

    And there are these passages in the rules that have this strange feeling of being present because of vestigial memory of game design that are there because, well, "That's how you make an RPG!"

    For example, there are rules for handling different qualities of armor and weapons. It's implied they are optional, sort of. They're there because, I think, someone along in development said, "But what about the difference between a dagger and a short sword!!!" But they're quite fiddly, with little pay off. The game I want to play is focused on the passion and nature and colorful qualities of the characters, not the gear. So I'm ripping them out as well.

    In short, I'm culling all the things that get in the way of making play focused on the compelling characters and their behaviors.

    \n I've run Dogs in the Vineyard around a 100 times for easily 200 different people. Maybe more, I've lost track! Unfortunately, 80% of these games are 1 shots in 4 hours or less. And it's not uncommon for me to run for people who have no or minimal RPG experience. So over the years I've drifted Dogs quite a bit\u2026

    1. NPCs. Instead of statting up NPCs, I use the NPC array dice option from Afriad (Vincent's never published horror hack of Dogs). It's faster and allows me to improv more making the game feel more player driven.

    2. Escalate. Another rule I took from Afraid, the first person to Escalate to a new conflict arena ignores the current attack (you don't have to block or take the blow) and makes an attack of their own. I never ever considered changing Escalate on my own till I played Afraid and then I found I could never go back. The few times I tried the old way, it felt anticlimactic that when one person Escalate to get more dice, the other person could then Escalate to also get more dice (assuming they are comfortable going there in the fiction). It felt like a zero sum game. With the Afraid rule\u2026 Escalation was a moment everyone at the table jumped out of their seats for! The tension it brought was high\u2026 you want to be the first to Escalate but you also want to wait for an attack that's worth Escalating for\u2026 just fantastic!

    3. Giving. When a PC gives up in a conflict, they keep their 2 highest dice (like NPCs do) and don't have to immediately use them but can save them for a future conflict where the fiction feels relevant. I found when PCs just kept 1 die, they rarely gave in. But 2 dice was just enough of a tipping point that it made PC lose willingly, which was major fun in the fiction. We changed it so that you can save these dice for later because often it felt right in the fiction and also made giving up more appealing.

    4. Traits. You can save any or all your Trait dice to be assigned in play (liek Relationships). Made character creation faster and the Traits players chose in play felt more true to their characters.

    5. Relationships. Instead of making Relationships that may never be incorporated in a 1 shot, you choose Relationships from a list of NPCs in the town you are about to play ensuring your Relationships are immediately relevant with minimal effort. Additionally, 2 of your Relationships must be shared with other players. My enemy may be your best friend. When you define the Relationship\u2026 I give you full authorship. You can define it in any way\u2026 make the NPC who I thought was a good guy into a bad guy\u2026 anything!

    6. Help. Players often feel frustrated that when a conflict starts, they can't interfere even when there is no reason in the fiction they couldn't. So I allow outside PCs to help by rolling a relevant Trait, Relationship, or Belonging and giving the highest rolled die to anyone in the conflict. But you can't use those dice in your next conflict.

    7. First Aid Conflicts. I used to use them but in a 4 hour game where we only really have 3 hours to play, it took too much time. So I removed dying from Fallout and replaced it with higher Fallout consequences. But although this makes play faster, it also means that it is almost impossible to die from a non-gun wound (say from a weapon wound). I need to refine this to bring back the original lethality of the game without the slowness.

    8. Short Term Fallout. I've added options like, "you\u2019re alone and in trouble" to encourage PCs to break apart since in Dogs, the Dogs united mainly means they will win every conflict but more importantly I've found solo scenes to be narratively exciting, revealing, and encouraging of independent thought rather than defaulting to group think. I've also experimented with, "if you're wrong, admit it and apologize", "if you've sinned, confess and repent", and "make a promise you don't want to make."

    \n Zac and CK sidebar:

    Interestingly, I'm excited about using Edges for weapons, equipment and magic items. They don't seem like extraneous froo-froo to me. But I was planning on using them in a specific way, and you've named for me why I'm intrigued with using them.

    Such items will be unique and named: named swords with history, gifts from the gods, forged by community and so on. Each would have a place within the community or the history of he character's journey. The would be bequeathed, or hard won, or forged specifically for a character for a specific quest or action in play.

    This means one might not end up being worthy of the gift by the community's standards -- and the standards might be higher because the character possess this amazing thing. The character, wearing or wielding this sword or ring, might be expected to be an exemplar member of the community. If he fails to carry that responsibility well, some might try to take it from him. There might be tension because one family is getting glory from the use of the item over another, and so on. Societal and communal tradition and tension will be a vital part of any items of this type.

    Thus, although the item gives a mechanical bonus in combat, it also is tied always into the societal and social conflicts.

    With that in mind, I'm thinking there will be a combat Edge, but also a social Edge: "I am Hrathgar, wielder of Bayrol," and yet get a bump because of the prestige of carrying that sword.

    \n When I was running Primetime Adventures three times a con compulsively, I got into some hacking of it (or at least, I think these were hacks):

    * Get each person to give me their own Premise for a show, then use that material to build a show out of.

    * Create a group, brainstormed cast list at the start of play before any characters have been created.

    * Allow only one conflict per scene, with everyone either coming down on one side or the other.

    #1 is unnecessary in PTA 3 (though possibly still useful), #2 is part of the game, and #3 is stupid and wrong and bad, and the new way of coming into a scene with a question is fucking genius.

    Edit: Why!

    #1: To make sure that quiet people are forced to be creative right away (see Microscope's Hot Seat), so that one or two people's creative visions aren't the sole arbiter of what we're doing. Plus I can usually synthesize something together that people will like out of this.

    #2: Thinking "What does the show need?" before "What do I want to play?" is a good way to orient yourself to the approach required by the game, especially for people familiar with mainstream rpgs.

    #3: This was a response to a complaint people had about how it was hard and confusing to resolve multiple conflicts at once. I have since fallen in love with the notion of multiple simultaneous conflicts, thanks to Mouse Guard. Also, as I said, PTA 3's handling this so much better. I really need to write a blog post about it.

    There are a *lot* of places in the Shock: rules where rules like your second one are allowed. It says things like, "player x decides" but doesn't tell x *how* to decide. That's deliberate for exactly the reasons that Jamie is giving: sometimes you want a particular effect, other times you want a different effect. If I'm playing hardcore 80s cyberpunk, I have to think very carefully if I want to put people as Links at all. I'm alienated and misanthropic by nature. It's the opposite if I'm playing an Ursula K. LeGuin story, where everything is about complex interpersonal sociosexual relationships.

    If I recall, the first rule is listed at the back of the book, too.

    I'm starting a new project soon that will show all of the "hard points" in Shock: where you can attach new gadgets and how to make those gadgets interface with the rest of the system and each other. On the other hand, there are some rules that, if you change them, it will really fuxx0r the system, and I'd like to know if anyone messes with them and gets effects outside my experience:\n
      \n
    • Antagonist credits \u2014 I've found that adding as little as one Credit makes Protagonists helpless and leaves Antagonists with no good strategic choices to make. Removing one makes things way too easy for Protags.
      • \n
    • Increasing the number of Features a Protag has by one means that they very likely will win their initial Intent, which means they won't get another Feature, which means they don't get to use their Links as much. Decreasing it would mean they're practically guaranteed to fail in their first scene \u2014\xa0it would be better to just guarantee a failure.
      • \n
    • A larger number of Praxes is absurdly confusing and eliminates choice. A smaller number (2) gives you *very* flat story, leaving players to either shoehorn their actions into inappropriate Praxes or keep doing the same things over and over.
