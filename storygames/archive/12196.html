    What about the in-my-mind-very-closely-related:

    Can't figure out the puzzle? Neither can your player!
    Can't figure out the optimal battle strategy here? Neither can your player!

    \n The game world is always murkier for the players than it is for their characters. The characters inhabit the game world, and have nothing else to memorize except what fictional things they see in their fictional world. My players have their real lives to keep track of, too, and I choose not to slow the game down with things like, "Wait, what was the Sea-king's nephew's name again?" For the player, that dude is a person with seconds of "screen time," but for the character he was a real person that's been seen and maybe interacted with a dozen times.

    And there's the added wrinkle that, sometimes, when a player doesn't remember something... it's the GM's fault. Maybe I gave it a stupid name. Maybe I didn't describe the thing, or the person, well enough to make them memorable.

    Finally, and maybe most importantly, sometimes weeks of real life have passed between sessions, whereas only minutes of game-time have elapsed. For the player, the clue about the trapped corridor was a month ago; for the character, it was ten feet and five minutes ago.

    If roleplaying a routine is fun for you\u2014as in Graham's starship-intrusion example\u2014then go for it. Sometimes that kind of memory game is an important part of the play. That's cool.

    But I don't believe that RPG characters and their players are joined at the brain. Sometimes you play characters who are smarter or sharper or dumber or duller than you. And you are always operating in a fictional world that is a degree (or several) removed from you, unlike your character, for whom that world is real and tangible and memorable. I don't look for places to punish the player, and that's what some sorts of "gotcha" shenanigans sound like to me.

    I could be wrong.

    Cheers,
    Will

    I've done this, as GM. It's frustration, based on what Thor's saying.

    My mouth went, "Well, then, I guess your character doesn't remember."

    But my brain went, "Fucking hell, I'm already tracking ten thousand setting facts, and you can't be bothered to track three or four? Try harder!"

    I eventually stopped doing this, and now use the following system:

    "If you think your character clearly would remember something, say so, and whoever in the group remembers should give the info. No one seems to have trouble buying into this, and once everyone's bought in, there's no dissatisfaction in play. (Of course, my solution fits with the aims of my particular game & group and might be stupid elsewhere.)

    \n To all,

    RPGs are so diverse and flexible, and played under so many different conditions and with so many disparate groups that it is problematic to point to any component and say "Oh, yes, always good" or "Oh, always bad."

    Analogy time.

    If I'm cooking for my family, I know all the conditions present, such as what they like and what they are allergic to and what they are in the mood for right now.

    If I'm cooking for strangers, no. So if I make a really spicy pad-kee-mao for them. Well, probably not a good call. But that doesn't mean no dishes should be spicy.

    If I put crushed up peanuts in my pizza sauce. Hey, lots of people are allergic to those. I should probably check that out or abstain. It doesn't mean peanuts are a bad ingredient.

    If I make a dish that doesn't keep well in the fridge, someone might rightly say "Hey, that dish doesn't work if you put it in the fridge and then reheat it 2 days later." And they are right. That doesn't mean all such dishes are bad.

    \n Saying that a character forgets something if the player forgets seems to me like an unwelcome artifact of characters-equal-players play. You know, from all the old advice in game manuals which give advice like "the GM should try to surprise the players if the characters are surprised," or "players shouldn't have knowledge that their characters don't," or all those "DON'T READ THIS SECTION IF YOU AREN'T THE GM!!!" disclaimers and all that.

    At least for my group, that approach is extremely limiting. Frankly, it treats us like we're stupid-- dramatic irony is used all the time to good effect in drama, literature, and so forth, and there's no reason that it can't also be used well in roleplaying. If a player forgets something, then the rest of the group (GM or other players) tell him/her. Why not? The interesting question isn't "Do I, the player, remember that fact?" Instead, it's "Should my character remember that fact?" A GM is not the privileged source of creation in roleplaying, the whole group is. I've always found that our games are much more successful (poignant, meaningful-in-a-literary-sense, intellectually and morally stimulating) when we trust players to make informed decisions about the setting and their characters. Our method is simple: tell the player the information, and then she can decide whether or not it is better (by whatever qualification she chooses) if that character knows/remembers.

    (Yes, some people will argue that this breaks a feeling of immersion or some-such, and that's fine-- do whatever suits your play style the best! But I personally think it is false to assume that the only way a person can only enjoy a game is if she wholly identifies with her character, including states of knowledge, etc. YMMV.).

    \n I am often terrible at remembering the names of NPCs when I'm playing, even when I actually take notes. (Which is at least part of the reason why I don't really take notes anymore, but more on that in a bit.)

    So, names. I've tried being better with them and didn't get very far with it, and I think I finally figured out why: the hardest names for me to keep straight are:

    * names which are very similar to other NPC names ("Bob" vs. "Rob" or "Elidryn" vs. "Gwendolyn", for example; the more the names sound alike, the more I'm likely to confuse them)
    * names of NPCs I interact with very rarely (3 times a session and I'll remember their name; twice, I probably will; once, I'll start to remember it the third or fourth session in a row; less frequently than that, good fucking luck)
    * names of NPCs I don't expect to interact with ever again (I will *never* remember these, sometimes not even *while we are still talking to them*)
    * names of bland NPCs (they come back, but they have no memorable qualities, and so I don't have anything to attach a name to nor any reason to want to)
    * names of NPCs who I assign "clever" nicknames to (the nickname will always supplant the actual name in my head)

    ...which, with the exception of the last one, basically suggests to me that I need both uniqueness and repetition (and the expectation of repetition) to put a detail into my memory. If a detail is flung out and doesn't get flagged in my attention as something that'll come back again and again, it won't stick. I expect a lot of details to go into various scenes, but as a player, I don't consider it my job to remember every single one of them any longer than an hour or so at the very most; when we come back to the game a week later, I really only want the "important" stuff in my head. (As a GM, I feel like I need to keep track of details in a more systematic and persistent way, but only expect the players to remember the stuff I emphasize via uniqueness and repetition. The rest of it they can ask me for if they've forgotten and think it's important.)


    As far as note-taking goes, I force myself to stay organized as a GM, because it makes it easier on me later on...but as a player, I tend to just jot down the broad strokes of things that directly impact my character rather than try to document everything that happens in the game; doing more than that just seems like too much busywork for too little reward, so I stopped doing it. I also got sick of spending more time organizing/reading my notes than interacting with the other people at the table.

    So nowadays when I need a detail two weeks later that I don't recall, I just ask; and if I find myself having to ask more than once, I'll either remember it on my own or I'll write it down when the GM shoots me the "for fuck's sake, this is the sixth time you've asked me that" look.

    \n I think what isn't being talked about yet, is how much information we get in our day-to-day lives that is not verbal. If you meet that cute girl with the red hair and square glasses and have a good conversation with her and all those visual cues, body language cues and context cues add up... you might remember her very well, even if you forget her name the next time you see her.

    Since Players only have that verbal cue to bring up (unless like jason above and often, myself as a GM, we use pictures to help give another visual cue) a name that is shorthand for an entire, often incomplete persona. On top of that, names like Bob and Smith have modern day context.... Ellowin and Tarn and Albannadar don't. We all know that Bob is a pretty common name, and often shorthand for William. We all know that Carlos often mean hispanic naming convention and that Aiko is probably japanese girl's name. In a fantasy game, all that built up, common world experience is out the freakin window. So, Ellowin just sounds like "placeholder X".

    Next, we've got to talk about context and time. What is happening to the characters can be pretty immediate stuff. The characters met Gish three episodes, which happens to only be about a day past in character time. In the player's time, its been 3 weeks or 3 months. Lots of other stuff has been overwritten in our brains from that point. How does anyone expect to remember?

    I take pretty copious notes. I ask for NPCs names twice and since I often have odd spellings for their names, I ask the GM how he/she spells the name.

    Another odd thing about names in superhero games. Codenames are often easier for me to remember than the characters's secret ID name. Why? Because codenames are supposed to indicative of the super. Dr. Deathkill is probably not a shining paragon of restraint and justified violence. And because codenames get repeated a lot at the table, while the secret IDs are not.. I sometimes forget my own PC's secret Id and have to look the damn thing up.

    Now. Mapping and figuring where one is. There are so many cues in our lives, that old house with the odd red shutter that is broken, the gnarled tree that looks like a rhino smoking a pipe. But these cues are not found on a typical dungeon map. There is no description of the shift from ruddy brown rock to a deeper blue shale that might say to everyone, "hey, we are going down lower". That minute detail is left to the imagination, because it would slow the story down to a crawl if it was included. While figuring out that room X is above room Y in an underground complex would require a die roll from this GM, figuring out how to backtrack from a simple 4 room tomb would not.

    One of the things I've stressed as a GM is not just describing an NPC but their demeanor. I will blatantly lie in an NPC voice, but if that NPC is obviously lying, I don't leave it just to the dialog the NPC is spouting. I will say, this NPC is being shifty, his body language is off... you KNOW he is lying. Not every NPC is that obvious, but I have used that more than once.

    I want to give the players as much information as I can, in as succinct format as I can. And it is an art form and sometimes I fail miserably... thinking I've given them the pertinent motivation or clue to the next idea... and when I do fail, I go meta and say , I made a mistake, here is what I was trying to convey... now you have the information, you guys can make a decision and move on from there.

    \n I'm on the opposite extreme as a player. I've found that in many situations, if I don't consciously pay attention, my mind wanders. The two solutions I've found for this are to do something repetitive and mindless with my hands (making chain armor and knitting both work well), and to take notes.

    That said, I'm of two minds on the "I don't remember" thing. For one, the player is not the character, and the game session is not the in-game experience; it may be a week or a month since the last play session, while only an hour might have elapsed for the character. So I'm not irritated by occasional "I don't remember" moments. On the other hand, if it's chronic, it's a sign that the other players are not as invested in the characters and the story as I am, and that gets really old really fast.

    I've also found that it's really difficult to try to change player behavior without being explicit about what's going on. "Look, guys, I'm really getting frustrated at having to remind you all the time about what you did in the last session, and every time you meet an NPC it's like meeting him for the first time. What can we do to get you more involved in the game?" This works so much better, I've found, than punitive approaches like "You don't remember? Neither does your character. Guess you'll have to start taking notes or something."

    \n Player forgetfulness can be put to good use. Let's say your into your fourth session of a campaign and neither the GM or the players remember anything about X from the first session. Chances are that X probably wasn't that interesting, and if it isn't interesting, it probably shouldn't be important. Which is why I'm a big fan of ascending minor NPCs to major roles and vice versa depending on how players react to them.

    Having said that, I'm not sure that player forgetfulness should be synonymous with character forgetfulness. I once played a superstitious thug that spent about half a session forgetting that he was superstitious simply because I forgot. The other players just let me get on with it without trying to correct me and it was only towards the end that I realised my mistake. On the one hand, I think it made my character more believable in the way that people are seldom one thing all the time. But on the other hand, I can see how if that kind of thing happened too many times, it would become impossible to get a grip on who the character is.

    \n On not playing one of your character's aspects, just like an NPC (or any other fictional element) that seems to be fading into the background, if you can't manage to hold onto some aspect of your character, it probably wasn't interesting in the current situation. It may be that the GM (and the other players) didn't present you with opportunities to be superstitious, or it may be that when opportunities arose, they weren't interesting to you, so you subconsciously chose to "forget".

    Burning Wheel has an interesting way to deal with these sorts of changes. The trait vote can remove traits as well as add new ones. If no one is playing one of your traits, let's get it off the character sheet and get something more grabby for the current situation on there.

    If the GM (or other players) aren't playing into one of your traits, they (especially the GM) need to step up, or communicate why they aren't hitting that trait in some meaningful way.

      \n Sometimes you can learn about what your players want by what they remember and what they don't. I have one player, for example, who constantly remembers certain visual details of things from months, even years previous, that I made up on the spot at the time and have long since forgotten. When I mis-state one of these details, it totally breaks her immersion. Since I usually try to use intentionally sparse descriptions (in order for the players to fill in the blanks in their heads, leading to stuff I could have never have thought of), I've had to retrain myself to pay way more attention to certain kind of details than I would normally, as this player obviously cares about them.

      Also...\n
      Posted By: marknauCan't figure out the puzzle? Neither can your player!
      Can't figure out the optimal battle strategy here? Neither can your player!
      Can't figure out what to say to seduce the guard? Neither can your player!
      Didn't map the dungeon and therefore don't notice that the room you are in is right above that other room? Neither does your player!
      \nI think the first of these is the real conundrum. The others (including the initial topic of this post) can usually be dealt with with some combination of common sense, taking the situation into account and, occasionally, stunting mechanics (especially useful for things like seduction, depending on the game). But the puzzle problem is harder (unless the puzzle really isn't intended as puzzle, but just as a really sophisticated locked door). But the point of a puzzle is in the figuring out, so most approaches that work for the others tend to suck the air out of puzzles. Seems to me a technique that worked decently for puzzles would work well for the others.
