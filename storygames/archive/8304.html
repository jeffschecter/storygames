\n First off, I welcome any and all conversation however tangentially related to my post. I'm not a big fan of OP ownership, so take this where you like.

I game with a table of forty-somethings. We've been around the block a few times. Most of us have been gaming since the late 70s. When 4e came out we decided to give it a shot -- it's slick and sexy and we have fond memories of D&D despite spending the last several years pretty far afield.

We read it. We were impressed with the quality -- even precision -- of the design. We set some stuff up. We played it. And then we dropped it, probably forever. I am interested now in why we dropped it and am trying to take apart the experience because I'm sure there's some game designery ideas in the wreckage somewhere.

Our experience with the game was VERY combat heavy. Long chains of fights -- hugely fun, complex, tactical fights -- with a few short colour scenes here and there. We had good motivation, good purpose, and some fun characters. But it never really flew.

So the past few years we've been playing games that really emphasize characterisation and story -- Dogs in the Vineyard, Spirit of the Century, Reign, Diaspora (our sf fate hack), and the like. What I think happened is that these games, by helping build a strong and even complex sense of motivation, forced us to start building opposition that had more dimensions than just Evil. We were bumping into areas where the player characters were distinctly amoral compared to their opposition, even, and players struggled with that and characters grew and developed and sometimes became unsalvagable while others saw one proverbial Light or another.

So when we started enjoying 4e, I actually found myself feeling a little sick. I had grown used to humanising the opposition in games, and here we were murdering them unquestioned on the basis of the labels Good and Evil. I couldn't really find a way to dehumanise some of the opponent -- I mean any otyugh, sure, no problem, but the de rigeur first level opposition is frankly sympathetic. I like kobolds in 4e. Murdering them for their loot just doesn't work for me at all any more.

I'm not saying that D&D has a moral defect in it or that people that play it have one. In fact I think what our table has acquired is a defect or blind spot -- having come to love a certain kind of story with certain kinds of opposition, we just can't get our heads around the idea that a Good or Evil label is sufficient for motivation. That whole genre is (perhaps temporarily) lost to us now. It wasn't that 4e failed to engender role-playing, it's that we found ourselves uncomfortable role-playing in it. Our characters were not people we liked. They were exactly the kind of people I normally have no truck with -- people with reflexive moral assignments they are prepared to follow up with violence. But more than that, there was no hope for them to change. I could stomach a character like that who could change, I think.

I'm pretty sure there's something about the advancement system that drives this, because I know I felt a pressure to provide combat scenes in order to keep up the advancement pace. But there was also the fact that it's just fun to kill stuff with 4e. If we weren't pretending to be the killers, I think it would have been fine.

    \n Some follow up thoughts.

    I can easily see a bunch of guys in their 40s getting together to "kick it old skool" like they did in the 70s & 80s having (falling into?) this experience, especially if they were using a module to run the game. Once you've decided to play a module, WotC offered or otherwise, you're probably pretty set in the paradigm that module offers.

    There is some schizophrenia between the DMG and the other D&D materials (also, I'll allow, some within the DMG itself). The modules I've seen do have a fair amount of kick in the door, kill the "monsters," and take their stuff with not much rationale outside you are a PC and they are "Eeeevil."

    The DMG offers a lot of great advice towards providing engaging scenarios based on player preferences. The PHB gives some service to talking about character motivations, etc. But there aren't a lot of supporting mechanics and what there are are overwhelmed by an avalanche of combat mechanics. That's fine. The combat is really fun. But you have to find your own reasons for why you fight. There's no +2 bonus for having reasons and there's no penalty for having none.

    This isn't necessarily addressed to the OP, but I wonder whether those of us who consider ourselves "System Matters" warriors or who approach a game design wanting to follow carefully the designer's intents, not going beyond what's discretely offered by the text sometimes fail to bring and apply what we've learned from other games or from our years of role-playing experience.

    I bring Sorcerer's kickers and bangs, PTA's scene-framing, and FATE's Aspects to every table I sit down at. To my benefit and hopefully others'.

    I know I've seen some discussion on this board about incomplete rules texts that assume a kind of knowledge that's been gleaned from seminal indie texts, neglecting to put those play protocols into their own game...

    \n This attitude that the D&D rules don't drive people to this situation just doesn't jive with my experience, which is very similar to Brad's. The rules do very much support an attitude wherein the GM has a determined adventure, which has unthinking combat included, which can't be worked around and shouldn't be worked around. It also actively dehumanizes the opposition and certainly does not encourage John's otherwise solid advice of laying background motivation for the murderizing. The fact that the game is completely stuck in a normative D&D paradigm that always chooses D&D convention over reasonable realism doesn't exactly help as the fiction is used as the bitch of the combat railroad in every way imaginable. The end-result is that you get a lot of situations where questioning the moral basis of the tactical situations is actively disruptive of play. I'm reminded of the Keep on the Shadowfell, which exemplifies this thing: it starts by having apparently fanatic murderous kobolds throw themselves upon the PCs in waves to be killed with no maneuver, negotiation or quarter given on either side, then continues with the PCs going and murdering humans who are obviously mind-controlled or at least working for motives the PCs know nothing about. After that it's off to the dungeon to "defeat the evil cult", which in practice seems to consist of slaying every single goblin that's making their home there, preferably by ambush. Of course, the only way I could deal with this as a player when it was dropped on me without preparation, context or justification was to first go all pacifistic (which ruined the game as it ground to halt and off the rails with my character doing stuff like withdrawing from unjust combat encounters) and then stop caring about the game as anything but a tactical experience, distancing myself from the horrible, horrible roleplaying aspect.

    Answering that challenge to D&D with "you don't have to play it that way" is just another way of telling people that they shouldn't do things they don't want and that they can house-rule whatever they want to play the way they want. Sure, but that's trivially true of any game.

    The practical solutions like fielding human or demi-human opposition only rarely and with appropriate heroic context, on the other hand, are pretty fine, although I'm not myself that enthusiastic about the extremes of turning the game into some sort of angsty story game. Give me either inhuman opponents or a real war situation generated via real strategic-level interactions at the table, and I'll be happy with the combat focus. (The first part, "real strategic-level interactions" is the reason 4th edition fails by my lights, by the by - it's also a direct inspiration for my actual play this fall, as I played a very interesting campaign of primitive D&D which was everything this new edition is not.) Pure murder, which is what the D&D rules and GMing instructions simulate and advocate with level-balanced encounters, lack of morale and retreat rules and flimsy pretexts for aggression, turns my stomach. Just be honest enough to confess that that's what the game does and label your fixes as what they are, house-rules - blaming Brad about somehow misreading the game text in relation to how it supposes you should deal with the fiction is dishonest. Insofar as I see it, Brad was playing exactly the sort of fiction that the game advocates; moving from that to deep characterizations, complex motivations, player-negotiated combats, avoiding murder and other stuff like that is clearly drifting the game.

    It should be obvious to anybody reading the rules of the game that the way the designers make this palatable to themselves is mostly by tuning out the fiction and focusing on the skirmish battle game with traditional D&D conventions. So it's sort of never delved on how and what and why the player characters are doing; the name of the game is to win the GM-created encounters, and that's all there is to it. At least that's what I get from the rules and the official adventures, which are very clear in dealing with the fiction only as a necessary story context for the string of combat encounters. It's like a Warhammer miniatures campaign which only turns to roleplaying in the sense of having fictional choices and player improvisation matter when you bring a drastically different motivation into the rules. A perfect example of how a game can be played very differently from how it's written simply because of expectations - people expect a roleplaying game with D&D 4th edition, and so they play it like one. (Which I don't want to refute or reproach - obviously the new D&D makes for a bitching combat engine for a heavily house-ruled fantasy adventure game.)

To NOT fight the things fails to honor the immense effort the DM has taken in crafting the situation where those games allow the players to engage with the beefiest part of the game mechanics.

    With 4E you can run a game where characters meet all kinds of people with all kinds of motivations and they can decide how, when, why and indeed whether to oppose them. If they want to move to the fighty mechanics, you can say, "five minutes smoke break," create the encounter and fight it out.

    Brad, the notion raised in this thread of campaign framing really resonates with me. I'm not sure I'm going to dip a toe into 4E because the thrust of the gameplay isn't my cup of tea--I don't tend to care for wargame-like tactical gaming. That said, I'm pretty sure a number of narrative frames would contextualize a 4E game in a way that doesn't drop it into a binary judgment style of cosmology.

    Here are some ideas--not saying they're good ones, but maybe they'll offer food for thought.

    - Rearchitect alignment itself. To some extent, the Moorcock Law-Chaos axis (dropped out of 4E) fudges the morality into something more relativistic. A Nobilis-style breakdown could work too, albeit with tinkering to fit (Light, Dark, Heaven, Hell, Wild).

    - Put together a more gray-area frame that keeps the DnD alignment but decouples it from actual player behavior. Make alignment a player choice but in the shared fiction, characters are just "born" that way and don't necessarily have to follow their natures. In this case, acting within alignment might grant a bonus, or acting against it might incur a small penalty or hesitation. Only keep the really extreme shit tied to absolutes. This might be the Perdido Street Station flavor, lets you have a very mixed set of alignments in a party.

    - If you don't want to hack the system (or the alignment bit, at any rate), find a tight campaign frame that puts characters into a meat grinder that justifies gratuitous violence. Midnight might offer a good example here. Another might be to play a DnD Reservoir Dogs game. Go all Tarantino with it. Defend the shit out of a village or something. I dunno. Might be too far from the default assumption of dungeon-delving independent adventurers.

    Edited to add my reading of the rules jives with what Eero has said--4E is essentially geared to provide a series of high-octane tactical combat set-pieces, which it apparently does very well. Options for resolving a combat-prepped scenario via non-combat means seem fairly limited to nil within the rules, though I supppose you could double down an encounter as a non-combat challenge alongside the combat stats.

    \n D&D; has always been like this -- and I've been subverting it for years. There's nothing in 4e that makes it harder to subvert than the older editions; they've all been centered around killing monsters and taking their stuff, unless you twist things to change it.
    \n
    \nPersonally, I think Keep on the Shadowfell did a better job than most D&D; modules. (Warning: spoilers ahead.) The kobolds attack first and are fanatics that fight to the last man. We don't get a clear idea of why they're fanatics, but I get a feeling that that's supposed to just be built into them in 4e -- by default, they're fanatical dragon-worshippers. In this case, they've just shifted their fanaticism to something else.
    \n
    \nOnce the players see that the kobolds are that kind of fanatics, and hear that they've been attacking and killing travelers, and may be preparing to try to take the town -- well, at that point, it makes sense to do something about them. Since they're fanatics, they won't listen to reason, so that narrows the options considerably.
    \n
    \nFollowing from there, the players find that the kobolds have been answering to a goblin, who also seems to fight fanatically -- and they find that the goblin has a symbol of the cult of Orcus on him, and a letter linking him to someone who seems to be a priest in the cult of Orcus. That leads them to discover the secret of the keep, and keeping the cult from creating an opening into the Shadowfell is definitely a priority.
    \n
    \nSo, the killing in the adventure comes from first self-defense, then defense of the town, then defense of the region. Taking the stuff of the monsters is incidental.
    \n
    \nThe battle locations and opposition are set -- it's hard to do otherwise in an adventure module -- but the ways that the PCs deal with them are not. The ambushes force things, but both the kobold lair and the dig site are set up with GM advice about how PCs might sneak around and scout, then position themselves. The advice there is still combat-oriented, but a smart party may well come up with other ways to deal with things -- e.g., having a rogue sneak in and try to get things, trying to disguise one or more party members, etc. Like 3rd edition, 4th actually has rules that can be used to adjudicate that sort of thing... but people still tried them back in 1st and 2nd, when it was up to GM fiat.
    \n
    \nThe free RPG day module -- the only other 4th edition module I've seen so far -- does a much poorer job of setting things up, but I think that's because it was designed to be very short, and to "drop in" to any campaign easily.
    \n
    \nPersonally, I like subverting things to make players question what's going on. Some of my favorite past bits I've done were:
    \n
    \n* An orc tribe that was suspected of ambushing and killing travelers -- but were actually innocent, and were being framed by a group of bandits who had an illusionist leading them.
    \n
    \n* Hobgoblins who were moving westward, and attacking and stealing along their path -- but who turned out to be fleeing from a group of gnolls who were trying to kill them, and didn't want to be fighting the humans or taking their stuff.
    \n
    \n* Finding a group of apparent cultists in black robes, gathered around an altar with a young girl lying on it, unconscious -- only to discover that the girl was already dead, and everyone was in black because it was a funeral.
    \n
    \n* Coming across an apparent fight between gnomes and goblins, and going in to try to help the gnomes -- only to discover that the gnomes and goblins were friendly, and playing a sporting match, not battling.
    \n
    \nAll of this (and several more besides), I did in first and second edition AD&D.; My players quickly learned to look below the surface, and not jump to conclusions based on D&D; stereotypes. There's nothing in 4th edition to prevent doing this sort of thing there, if one wishes.
