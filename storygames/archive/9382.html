    \n Since the probabilities of each draw are not independent you can't get an answer just from usual probability formulas. You'll have to draw your little binomial trees. I can't do ascii but for the Red Black question with two draws it would look like this.
    \n
    \nFirst draw: Branch One: 50% chance of Red. Branch Two: 50% chance of Black
    \n
    \nSecond Draw is either:
    \nBranch One-a: 49% chance of Red, with Branch One-b 51% chance of Black (first draw was Red) or
    \nBranch Two-a: 51% chance of Red, with Branch Two-b 49% chance of Black (first draw was Black)
    \n
    \nThus there is only 1 path through the tree that gives you 2 Red: Branch One (50%) followed by Branch One-a (49%) the probability of both draws being Red is thus 50% *49% = 24.5%
    \n
    \nTo do 3 draws you'd need to go down another level in the tree:
    \nBranch One-a-1: 48% chance of Red, with One-a-2 being 52% Black (Where first two draws were red)
    \nBranch One-b-1: 50% chance of Red, with One-b-2 being 50% Black (where first draw was Red and second Black)
    \nBranch Two-a-1: 50% chance of Red, with Two-b-2 being 50% Black (where first draw was Black and second Red)
    \nBranch Two-b-1: 51% chance of Red, with Two-b-2 being 48% Black (where first two draws were black)
    \n
    \nYou can see there are 4 paths through this tree that yield two Red:
    \nOne-a-1, One-a-2, One-b-1, and Two-a-1
    \n
    \nThe probability of One-b-1 is 50% * 49% * 48% or 11.76%
    \nThe probability of One-b-2 is 50% * 49% * 52% or 12.74%
    \nThe probability of One-b-1 is 50% * 51% * 50% or 12.75%
    \nThe probability of Two-a-1 is 50% * 51% * 50% or 12.75%
    \n
    \nThis part I'm a bit fuzzy on, but I think you can just do the usual probability addition here:
    \n
    \n(11.76% + 12.74%) - (11.76% * 12.75%) = (24.5% - 1.5%) = 23%
    \n(23% + 12.75%) - (23% * 12.75%) = (35.75% - 2.9%) = 32.85%
    \n(32.85% + 12.75%) - (32.85% * 12.75%) = (45.6% - 4.2%) = 41.4%
    \n
    \nSo the chance of 2 Red on 3 Draws is 41.4%
    \n
    \nYou'd do the same thing where X is even larger numbers but you can see your binomial tree begins to get exponentially bigger with each additional draw.
    \n
    \nYou'd also do exactly the same thing for your other questions, but for suits each level would spawn 4 branches and for individual cards each level would spawn 13 branches making the calculations even more arduous.
    \n
    \nThat's the blunt force trauma way of doing it. Some code monkey has probably already automated it somewhere, however.

    \n Once you draw 3 cards then you're guaranteed to have same color. Once you draw 5 cards you're guaranteed to have the same suit. If you only draw 1 card, you obviously can't get anything. For other values of X:

    P(same color | X=2) = 25/51 ~= 49%

    P(2 of the same suit | X=2) = 1 - P(all different suit | X=2) = 1 - (52-13)/51 = 24%
    P(3 of the same suit | X=3) = 1 - P(all different suit | X=3) = 1 - (52-13)/51*(52-2*13)/50 = 60%
    P(4 of the same suit | X=4) = 1 - P(all different suit | X=4) = 1 - (52-13)/51*(52-2*13)/50*(52-3*13)/49 = 89%

    P(pair | X=2) = 1 - P(all different values | X=2) = 1 - (52-4)/51 = 6%
    P(pair | X=3) = 1 - P(all different values | X=3) = 1 - (52-4)/51*(52-2*4)/50 = 17%
    P(pair | X=4) = 1 - P(all different values | X=4) = 1 - (52-4)/51*(52-2*4)/50*(52-3*4)/49 = 32%
    P(pair | X=5) = 1 - P(all different values | X=5) = 1 - (52-4)/51*(52-2*4)/50*(52-3*4)/49*(52-4*4)/48 = 49%
    P(pair | X=6) = 1 - P(all different values | X=6) = 1 - (52-4)/51*(52-2*4)/50*(52-3*4)/49*(52-4*4)/48*(52-5*4)/47 = 65%
    P(pair | X=7) = 1 - P(all different values | X=7) = 1 - (52-4)/51*(52-2*4)/50*(52-3*4)/49*(52-4*4)/48*(52-5*4)/47*(52-6*4)/46 = 79%

    I don't know exactly what you mean by the trickier part with the A, B and C. If there are other cards drawn that you don't know the value of then none of the above changes. If you do know what the other cards are, then it becomes tricky mostly based on the fact that it depends on the value of those cards, of which there are many possibilities, especially for high values of A,B or C.

    Oh, are you talking about up to three other players each drawing 2 to 7 cards each?

    EDIT after reading Valamir's post: I think he only cares about the same color, not what color, so the initial branch of red vs black is irrelevant, making the math a bit easier.

    \n I think we all crossposted. Whee. Here's mine anyway. The good news is that my numbers match Tim's.

    Same color? 100% minus the chance that all the cards are different colors. With 2 cards, it's 1 - 26/51or 49% With 3+ cards, you'll match two by color 100% of the time.

    Same suit? 100% minus the chance that all the cards are different suits. With 2 cards, there's a 39/51 chance of drawing two different suits. With 3 cards, there's a 39/51 * 26/50 chance of drawing three different suits. With 4 cards, there's a 39/51 * 26/50 * 13/49 chance of drawing four different suits. With 5+ cards, you'll always find at least one match. So you'll match by suit 23.5% with two cards, 60.0% with three cards, 89.5% with four cards, and 100% with five or more cards.

    Same value? 100% minus the chance that all the cards have different values. With 2 cards, there's a (12*4)/(52-1) chance of drawing two different face values. With 3 cards, there's a (12*4)/(52-1) * (11*4)/(52-2) chance. With 4 cards, there's a (12*4)/(52-1) * (11*4)/(52-2) * (10*4)/(52-3) chance. The formula generalizes to

    1 - ( 4^(n-1) * [ 12! / (13 - n)! ] * [ (52 - n)! / 51! ] ),

    where n is the number of cards drawn, ^ is raising to an exponent, and ! is the factorial operator. The "1 - expression" is a reminder to subtract the whole thing from 1 to get the chance of it NOT happening.

    Here's an Excel formula (A2 is the 'n' value):

    =1 - ( 4^(A2-1) * ( FACT(12) / FACT(13 - A2) ) * ( FACT(52 - A2) / FACT(51) ) )

    Plugging in values of n {2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7}...

    n=2, p=5.8%
    n=3, p=17.2%
    n=4, p=32.4%
    n=5, p=49.3%
    n=6, p=65.5%
    n=7, p=79.0%

    Enjoy!

    \n OK, I'll explain the mechanic I'm chasing after with these probability queries.

    I have a tendency to over-design mechanics. I love to make them intricate things that have neat options and all kinds of information from one stroke. And, playing cards are usually better than dice because each card has so many variables like color, rank, and suit.

    I recently went back to my game chef 2008 design, The Lady Killers. I forgot how much I liked the design, which I had to abandon due to the time constraints of the contest. There were some sketchy notes about a mechanic where players tried to match ranks by playing 2 cards from their hands.

    Fast forward to a week ago or so. I rediscovered the design, and started blowing the dust off. Right away, I knew the mechanic was ignorant of the possibilities. But, there was something elegant in there.

    The Mechanic

    Players draw a number of cards based on their character scores. Numbers likely will range from 2 to 7. So, players are drawing anywhere from 2 to 7 cards. LIkely, most draws will be 2-4 cards.

    To resolve stuff, a player looks at his or her hand and picks any 2 cards to play. Playing any 2 Red cards is a success. Playing any black cards is a failure. Easy. Turns out a draw of 2 cards will create success 50 percent of the time. PERFECT. Just what I wanted.

    Of course, I can't leave well enough alone. I've got to tinker, damn it! That's where more of the probabilities weigh in.

    First off, I'll use the difference between the cards as a consequence. Think fallout. You play an Ace and a 9, then you've got 8 points of "pain". Matched pairs are great because the difference is 0. That's a "best" result.

    Second, here's a way to break down results:

    Any two black cards: Failure! Risk pain to others.
    Any two Clubs OR Any two Spades: Failure, but with player's input. Risk pain to others.
    Any two red cards: Success, but with GM input (small complication, I guess). Risk pain to yourself.
    Any two Hearts OR Any two Diamonds: Success. Risk pain to yourself.

    Third, now I want to tinker with a way to make face cards do special stuff. Here, I'm less interested in the probabilities, although I may have to chase after that later. Something like this:

    Any (red?) jack in your 2 cards = gain a nickel
    Any (red?) king in your 2 cards = gain a dime
    Any (red?) queen in your 2 cards = gain a quarter

    (Which leads me to the alternative of using black face cards to make players "pay" more, i.e. black king means you have to pay a dime. See? Told ya' I like to fiddle too much.)
