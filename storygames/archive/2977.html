Special terms bar entry to RPG discussion and, ultimately, harm it.

\n
\n
\n
\n\nC.W.Richeson \n\n\n
\n
\n\nMay 2007 \n edited May 2007 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n Perhaps its natural that folk who love to write and think critically about RPGs would create special terms to express their ideas. The classics, of course, are the often misused GNS terms that so many folk have different ideas about as to render their very use almost meaningless. When posed to a relative newcomer, however, they and the many other special terms we define and use can be intimidating. We, as people who want to discuss the hobby generally, need to use fewer of these in order to lower the entry into RPG discussion and to encourage new people to join in on these conversations.

My current background is in the U.S. legal system, and one of the raging issues of the past 20 years has been the way lawyers draft documents. Lawyers tend to use specialized terms that have specific, clear legal meanings but are almost incomprehensible to lay readers. We all understand what we mean when we use these terms and writing styles to draft contracts, licenses, and other legal documents - we're writing for each other. Imagine, however, if we wrote this way when dealing with potential clients or spoke this way while trying to carry on a conversation with a lay person about their experiences with the law - it would be intimidating, it would make the conversation difficult, and it would be dumb. A smart writer knows to write to the audience.

In much the same way the lexicon that has evolved around RPG discussion is arcane and only easily understood by other people who invest similarly large amounts of time. Unfortunately, this leads to a barrier to entry into these discussions - few people want to dig through the terms of a contract any more than they want to look up what a "bang" is. That many of these terms are poorly understood or carry different meanings makes it even more difficult and even presents a barrier to communication for those otherwise inclined to learn this special language.

We must get control of our growing lexicon if we want to continue to attract new people and continue to build useful terms within our hobby. To that end we need to accomplish two tasks.

First, we must stop inventing new words for easy to understand concepts. I know it's tempting, but it continues to increase the barrier for other people to talk with us. Those who spend the least time immersed in theory may, in fact, have some of the most useful actual play observations to bring into these discussions. We need that.

Second, we need a place to maintain a list of terms and their definitions. A Wiki isn't going to cut it because it's too vulnerable to argument and disagreement over terms. Rather, one of the major roleplaying sites has to step up and maintain a list. Let us argue over the terms, but have a neutral arbiter ultimately take a working definition. Once we have this we can promote it through use and, before we know it, we'll have a potent new tool available. By rejecting terms that only reflect ideas easily mentioned or referred to the controller of the lexicon can prevent an excessive build up of terms.

Thoughts?

    \n There are a lot of mis-perceptions and revisionist-thinking going on here, probably because most of you weren't present when the "jargon" were created.

    1) It's important to understand that much of the terminology, at the time of their creation, were never meant to be theory terms. Many terms started off as off-hand comments. No thought was given to the term, the poster just needed a word to describe their idea. Later, someone would reference that discussion, and would pull out the word. People would keep doing this, and before you knew it, the word would become "standard" terminology.

    Really, how much thought do you think went into a term like, "The Impossible Thing Before Breakfast"? What kind of academic would make up a term like "TITBB"? Or "El Dorado" or "Typhoid Mary"?

    2) Related to the above, most of the terminology DID start off as simple descriptions, words that the "lay person" could understand. But the ideas behind the words grew as people's understanding of play grew.

    GNS-Sim is a perfect example of this. At one time, it really did have the connotation of "simulation-play". If you read the original essay "System Does Matter", Sim is defined as the "player is satisfied if the system 'creates' a little pocket universe without fudging" and "include[s] the well-known subtype of the Realist." But as people's understanding of Sim grew, it morphed into the idea of "the Right to Dream".

    3) Early theory discussions used many more "general" and commonly-used terms. But it became apparent through time that different people had different ideas about what the terms meant. In other words, these "commonly understood terms" actually weren't commonly understood.

    Railroading is a good example. Railroading means different things to different people. So the ideas of GM-Force, Illusionism, and Participationism all grew out of a need to differentiate what people meant when they said "railroading".

    This last reason is a big one. Back in the heyday of the Forge Theory/GNS boards, people would sometimes talk for like 4-5 pages before they realized they were talking about different things using the same words. It's simply impossible to have on-going, in-depth discussion about any topic without specialized terminology. It's a very practical issue.

    It's as simple as that. It feels like there's an assertion running around that theory doesn't have to be accessible to non-designers because it's meant for designers. As a designer, I can tell you the jargonism in theory is exactly why I don't engage in it. At least in my specific case, that seems to fly right in the face of the assertion I'm talking about here.

    It feels like jargon is the dividing barrier between theory and what I call practical design principles. Some of you probably see theory and pragmatic design as one and the same; I'm betting you have less of a problem with jargon comprehension than I do. But for me, they're night and day. When someone says "your game should support such-and-such a principle from GNS theory", they have not taken me any closer to understanding what they say. When my buddy Rob says to me, "i want you to take the narration parts of your game out of the game for a moment and look at the dice game you've got, and make sure that that's interesting independent of the roleplaying part", he has given me a practical design principle, couched in natural, clear language, and I understand what he's getting at right away. The latter helped me make Don't Rest Your Head a better game. The former is pretty much crap, and always will be, where I'm concerned.

      \n*If* I were partof the intended audience for a discussion of theory (and I'm not assuming that I am) I'd like it if people who used jargon in that discussion didn't:

      b) Assume that by using the jargon they have no obligation to explain, at the time of its use and in the place where it's used, what it means
      c) Assume that pointing someone at a longer-than-a-paragraph explanation of what a piece of jargon means is an acceptable way of explaining what it means

      I offer this as a way of bringing someone like me into the fold because (to elaborate on the above, point by point):

      a) ... because using the jargon by itself is, for me, incomprehensible.

      b) ... because if you're really interested in jargon not being a barrier against folks who would otherwise pass the standard to be a member of your audience, you can show me evidence of such an attitude by making sure that your message does not make a bad assumption about the reader. By explaining inline at at-the-time what you mean when you choose to use jargon, you're showing that you have a genuine willingness to explain and are cleverly heading off misunderstandings at the pass, striking an up=front blow for clarity rather than obfuscation. The term no longer means what it meant when it was coined, but everyone's intentions when the term was coined were honorable. They were trying to be clear and jargon-free.
        4. \n

      Simulationism isn't the only term that started out with a clear meaning borrowed from popular usage and evolved into more opaque jargon. For example, Push and Pull seemed very intuitive at the beginning, but as the discussion continued, the terms evolved. I avoid using them, myself, because I think they have become highly specialized terms. The new meanings are not as obvious to me as the old ones. Even the term "GNS" has become more opaque, in the sense that everyone uses it incorrectly to talk about "the three Creative Agendas"... except that there are now four Creative Agendas.

      \n

      It's good to be as clear as possible when creating jargon, but language evolves, and words can become less clear over time.

      Posted By: Anemone...the words used in science are overall very well agreed on and cover very specific, real concepts. I'll grant you that there is the occasional acronym that really only means "we dont' know" (e.g., SIDS), but even so it's eventually replaced by better terms.
      \nDo you know how those "well agreed upon" terms come into being? Other than cases where there is a nomenclature body that certifies them (and even then, you'll still see usage that defies the nomenclature: try industrial chemistry sometime), terms happen just like they happen in this community. Somebody makes up a label for "this thing I'm looking at", and it either gets adopted, gets modified, or gets lost in the shuffle. Why "quark"? Somebody made it up. It stuck. Sciences with low barriers to entry (Computer science since the 80s, engineering practically always, the natural sciences up until around 1950 or so) are particularly prone to opaque, in-jokey terms (see "bug" in computing, "Sexy Son hypothesis" in biology, "fullerene" in chemistry...)

      The process of publication, refereeing, and collaboration refines terms over time. We don't really have much that's equivalent to that, but the closest we DO have is discussions on message boards and mailing lists and blogs. The thing is - those have close to zero barriers to entry - everybody feels like they're an expert. So the refinement process gets drowned in noise. "Agreed upon terms" just don't happen without some kind of institutionalized process of review - which is inevitably exclusionary.
