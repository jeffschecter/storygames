    \n In Archipelago the initial situation is built collaboratively by the players, who each then claim ownership of and authority over one aspect of the setting. PCs have Fates that are usually resolved each session, and those are authored by other players and used as benchmarks to point toward in play.

    Perhaps off topic, but there are a lot of killer games that are predicated on fixed initial situations - the creative constraints of the "adventure" are not in the setup but in the interaction and exploration of that setup, and in the resolution. Montsegur 1244 and Witch: The Road to Lindesfarne are good examples of this.

    \n In A Wicked Age starts by randomly choosing four sentences from an "oracle" out of a set of 52 (physically, you draw playing cards and look them up in a chart.) Each sentence describes something evocative, like "the burglary of a magical library" or "a hermit priestess, practicing obscure deprivations". Half the fun of the game is collaboratively piecing these together of these into a situation, a set of PCs and NPCs, and the bonds between the characters. Then you get to set it in motion.

    \n If you don't mind a Google translated text, I've written the scenario writing methods for fifteen games on another forum.

    Here I've divided the games into the following categories:
    - Railroads: There exist a scene in the beginning, an ending scene and the scenes in between are either something that the players must pass through or have the choice to pass through. The GM's role is to present a story and keep the players on the rail.
    - Sandbox: the GM has written a number of places and the players may choose freely which ones to explore. The GM's role is pretty much unknown for me.
    - Fishtank: the GM creates factions with relations to each other nor individual agendas for each faction. The GM's role is present the relations between the different factions and to create consequences on the PCs' actions, based on the factions' agendas.
    - Bang driven (translated as "bomb run" ): the players flags what they wants the scenario to be about through their PCs. The GM's role is to use these flags to create dilemmas, and depending on how the PC will react, also show the consequences of the players' choice.
    - Collaborate storytelling / samber\xe4ttande: all participants make up a story together. The games with this kind of method can have a structure to prepare the game, but also a structure on how to play the game. The GM's role is either non-existent, rotating between the participants, or to organize the story and created complications for the PCs.

    Here are the list of the games that I've been listing. If you want to know how you should write scenarios to the different games, click on the link above.

    Railroads
    Feng Shui
    Call of Cthulhu
    Drakar och Demoner 2 (Swedish RPG)
    Dying Earth

    Sandbox
    Deadlands

    Fishtank
    Unknown Armies
    Mutin\xe9 (Swedish RPG)

    Bang driven
    Noir (Swedish RPG)

    Collaborate storytelling
    Bl\xe4ck (Swedish RPG)
    Oskrivna Blad (Swedish RPG)
    Lady Blackbird
    InSpectres

    Mixed
    Spirit of the Century (three suggested methods which are either a rail, bang driven or a fishtank)
    Mouse Guard (is both railroaded [GM's turn] and collaborate storytelling {players' turn])
    Hammaren och Trollsp\xf6t (Swedish RPG. The text is so loose that the reader will use the suggested method in different ways, depending on the reader's previous experiences in how to write scenarios.)

    NOTE: I'm not interesting in discussing my definitions of the terminology I've used when I categorized the scenario writing methods in the different games. Not in this thread anyway.
