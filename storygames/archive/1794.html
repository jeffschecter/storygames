I found this snippet from an interview with BSG staff writer Anne Cofell Saunders in a recent Creative Screenwriting Weekly email amusing.
What is your favorite part of your job?

In many ways I'm going to work on the Battlestar Galactica every day. I know that's kind of silly, but in truth, I spend a lot of time with these characters and what they know and what they believe and what they would do in that universe, and you grow to love them.

It's sort of like getting to play Dungeons and Dragons all day. I used to play D&D in high school, and you get to put together these worlds. Ron has created this great world, and you get to play with the characters in the world. You have hour-long conversations in the writers' room over what Starbuck [Katee Sackhoff] would or wouldn't do. It's really fun! I also don't have to deal with any actual death. The stakes are high because it's Hollywood, but nobody going to die on my operating table.
--Paul

    You know David Cronenberg's eXistenZ,
    where the actors pretend to be virtual reality video game avatars,
    complete with bugs in the program?

    I watched The Spanish Prisoner last night, and it's practically
    a parody of a Primetime Adventures play transcript.


    (SPOILER)

    There's a flat quality to the acting, as if the actors understand
    'this is the scene where we show _________", but they're
    hesitantly improvising the details on the fly.

    It's easy to visualize the character sheets of all the characters,
    with their signature quirks and their pithy secrets.

    The 'thriller' genre conventions hang over the picture like
    a cloud of "well, we said we're playing a thriller tonight,
    what does that mean, exactly?"/ punctuated by spurts
    of THIS IS A THRILLER moments where some player
    gets a hot idea and really lays it on a bit too thick.

    Then, at the end, the thing picks up momentum and
    it's like the players got carried away/ got drunk
    on showing off their nutty indie creativity/ and
    the layers of intrigue get waaaaaaay too tricksy.

    Then, exhausted by the effort of the session, the
    players get groggy and slaphappy and childish
    and somebody ends the session by geeking-out
    and having a freaking JAPANESE SCHOOLGIRL
    WITH A GIANT RIFLE appears out of nowhere
    and save the day.

    Literally. That's literally how it ends.

    I do recommend The Spanish Prisoner.
    But mostly I'd only recommend it to indie gamers.
