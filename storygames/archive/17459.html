\n Which kinds of GM control do players consistently find acceptable in traditional RPGs?

- GM control over what?
We're talking about over the players', and by extension, their characters' choices

- What's a unit?
Something the rules text that affects a dice roll (or other fortune rule)
Example: Add +2 or +5 to a DC, set a DC to a familiar level (15, 20 or 25)
Example*: Modify a monster by +/- 2 from the 'standard difficulty' compared to players' levels

- What else can the GM do?
The GM ad-hoc controls areas of the fiction that don't affect the players NOW. However, this sets precedents presumed to be accepted by players later as the basis for invoking "units of control" later

- What's lame #1?
Any time a confusing situation arises that seems to be in some kind of grey area between "units of control" and the ad-hoc control.

Scenario A, GM ad-hoc is OK and sets precedent
GM: "The blackrock goblin clan controls the routes between the northwestern towns. You'll find lots of danger if you travel there, and supplies may be sparse."
[The players explore the area between the towns and randomly encounter a monster that is too dangerous for them to slay. They run away after losing one of their horses]

Scenario B, GM ad-hoc is spontaneous and could be lame
Player: "I want to go to the store and buy a crossbow."
GM: "You won't find a crossbow there, the blackrock goblin clan controls the routes between this and the next town, so shipments don't always make it."
Player: "That sucks, I can't even try?"

(Now, let's fix it in the spirit of the OP:)

GM: "Okay wait, yeah that's lame. Let's roll for it, but I'm going to add a -5 penalty due to the blackrock clan raids"

What sticks out to me is that when a game text ONLY defines dice rolls for players using text that's within the context of the fiction (i.e. "Make a skill/ability roll whenever something your character tries to do is in question") no obvious solutions to this grey area are in the text. One may be led to think "Well it's not about the character's skill, so I guess we don't roll anything"

What are some other lame scenarios that arise concerning GM control, and ways to fix them in this same spirit?

-- John M.

* - Starring the non-obvious one. I considered this an example since in the past, GMs have used their ability to design encounters as a way to railroad when other methods were more clearly unfair. Not to say there can't be monsters that are way outside the player level range when a precedent has been fairly set ahead of time. I guess if anyone comes in to discuss, the concept of setting fictional precedent for future rulings would be a big part of this discussion.

    I think what this thread is about is this: "Gimme a roll for that".     \n Walterman, you beat me to it. I realized this morning the nature of the player's motive is more important than I gave credit for and it bears mention so the thread doesn't get confusing. Maybe it should have been included in the examples.

    Example: The player wants a crossbow. He wants to remove the heads, melt down silver to make new silver heads, and then replace them on the bolts to fight the werewolf menacing town.

    In that case, yes, it's still important for a GM to dig to find intent. You still do the roll with bad odds (read on), but then upon failure leave a path that leads to an alternative.

    This isn't what the thread is about, but it's so related that I want to thank people for calling it out. It's a fantastic match for the style of GMing I'm assuming with this thread. I'd like to point out that you can look for a player's intent regardless of whether you rolled dice or simply said "No, but...". This is why I say it's not what the thread is about.

    -------

    Back to the purpose. There are games designed around the idea of characters being challenged by a world that's worked out logistically through nothing more than percentages of receptivity vs blockage to their attempts to shape it. I play with players who want that; they want the sharp edges of the world to bump up against them and poke every once and a while to remind them that it "feels real".

    I don't believe rule-0 accomplishes this play goal*, but when a player agrees that a dice modifier matches the given fiction, they've bought into the plausibility of the result (again, talking about myself and players I've played with).

    The reason these are called units of control is because it's control over the specificity of this fictional result now, as interpreted by things that have come before. It doesn't care about player motivations (but the GM of course does, which is why he has the creative flexibility mentioned above). In a way, it's not really GM control, it's "game text control". I think I posed this thread as GM control because that's how rule-0 is often used, and that's what this is supposed to replace.

    I have one more way of framing it. What matters here is the dice roll with a potentially small chance of success. The GM could say "No, but", but instead he laughs an evil laugh and says "Remember about the goblin raids? You're searching the the shopkeeper's inventory and coming up very bare on supplies. [rolls dice to see if a crossbow is there]" These extra rolls make the world seem like its pressing against the player. It makes an issue out of something that you might otherwise not make an issue about. For a moment, the player is a little tense because there might be the crossbow they need. If the roll comes up a failure, then they might reluctantly decide to rough the shopkeeper up a bit to find out who in town would actually have one. The goblin raids are making everyone act like beasts.

    In short: the dice roll is the world reminding the GM how mean (or merciful) it can be. Sure, the GM can ad-hoc something that follows intent and keep things fun, but this extra step is for those who dig it (I was thinking also a good way to open the door for people who aren't used to things like player intent? Maybe not, though). It would be neat if I find a way to formalize this into an actual rule where players call for the GM to make rolls.

    I understand there are players who don't care about the specificity of the fiction or being challenged by the simulation of a harsh environment, so this thread might be fruitless to some, I guess.

    * -- At best, rule-0 can force the game along when a table is split down the middle about interpreting the fiction through a ruling, and someone has to break a tie. But then, I wouldn't implement a rule-0, I would simply say the GM is responsible for breaking ties :-/

    \n I'm confused, and I want to understand what you guys are talking about. I'll admit I am able to follow examples WAY MORE BETTER then wordy theory jargon, so bare with me. Again, I like examples a lot, so here's an example:

    The players receive a quest in Big City to travel to Little Town a week's travel away and investigate the goblin raids that have been attacking Rich Merchant's supply caravans. Rich Merchant explains that his company is the only one that supplies the general store in Little Town, but his caravans have been attacked and destroyed for the past few months. The GM then further clarifies the implicit challenge by stating that the players should expect supplies to be seriously limited when they arrive in Little Town, given the raids. (Fair warning to stock up on gear before leaving Big City, without it be stated blatantly.)

    The players leave Big City, travel a week to Little Town, but right before they arrive, they are attacked by the goblin raiders. They defeat the goblins and Level Up. One of the players buys a new Crossbow Skill. Afterwards, they get to Little Town and go to the general store to buy a crossbow, but alas, as the GM suggested, the shelves are bare!

    Now - in this example, I don't think it's unfair for the player to find a lack of crossbows, given the fact that all the established fiction stated the general store hasn't been stocked in months. Would you, as a player, be upset? I mean, I'd be disappointed, but I wouldn't cry foul and say "fuck the cheatin' ass GM."

    Now, as the GM in this example, I wouldn't say "Oh okay, fine you find one." since that kind of negates the established fiction that I purposely made clear beforehand as a meaningful challenge element to this mission. To me, that's the same thing as saying, "Oh okay, the goblin raiders don't attack you since you guys aren't prepared for an ambush." I mean, at some point, the challenges presented by the GM need to have some significance, whether the challenge is an ambush or a lack of supplies, right?

    I would however offer alternatives in the crossbow situation, such as finding a local owner who is willing to sell, traveling back to Big City to buy one, or even hiring a runner to go to Big City and bring one back for you. In all of these alternatives, buying a crossbow would be more expensive - the owner would charge more given supply and demand, the travel back to Big City would cost money, or the runner would charge a fee for the trip, maybe even a big fee given the established danger on the roads. In other words, as a GM, I don't want to block the players arbitrarily, but I don't want to be a push-over either. I'm a fan of my players, but I'm not there to make it a walk in the park. Is that dysfunctional on my part?

    I also really like the idea of allowing the player to spend an Awesome Point or something to bring some good fortune - maybe the shop keeper knows of someone who has a crossbow that owes him money, suggesting that they speak with that person and offer to pay their debt in exchange for the crossbow. The spending of that Awesome Point saves them the time of finding the owner of the crossbow on their own, and also gives them leverage so as not to get gouged on the price (they might even be able to get it cheaper given said leverage!). I personally feel, as both a GM and a Player, that spending that Awesome Point should give the player a favorable fictional route to the goal, as opposed to forcing a roll with a chance to retcon the fiction in my favor. To state it differently, I'd rather my Awesome Point force the GM to provide a new favorable opportunity to get what I want, rather then just force the GM to let me roll dice to determine if the shop has a crossbow after all, thereby negating the fictional challenge pre-established by the GM.

    \n There are at least three things at work here that I see:

    Conflict of Interest\n
    Scenario B, GM ad-hoc is spontaneous and could be lame
    Player: "I want to go to the store and buy a crossbow."
    GM: "You won't find a crossbow there, the blackrock goblin clan controls the routes between this and the next town, so shipments don't always make it."
    Player: "That sucks, I can't even try?"
    \nThe players could be right to be suspect. The GM has resources they don't have access to, and is in charge of meting out their access to effectiveness. That crossbow might be any of the things that David and Rafu mention, but one major thing it is, is a unit of effectiveness. There is a potential conflict of interest in having the GM decide when you get it. Or determining what level of threat you face as in the other example. There are different ways to deal with this. Using chance is one. Also, establishing guidelines and even a culture of how to negotiate differences is another way. Freeform and larp do this much more than tabletop.

    Weighted Rolls to Gain Concensus\n
    Separately, I'm still not following you on why a player who wants a crossbow should be less annoyed at "you have to make a roll with poor odds; oh look, you failed" than at a simple "no". My gaming history has seen just as many "I shouldn't have had to roll" or "that target number was too high" arguments as "why'd you say no?" arguments.
    \nOne of the things that chance does is that it eases tensions people can have about unequal distribution of resources. An example from a class I took on social conflict over natural resources was hunting rights. Say there are a limited number of them, less than the number of people who want to get a license to hunt. Giving them out one at a time can cause problems: people may think there is a political reason why someone gets instead of them. First come first serve may leave some people angry at not being able to go during business hours etc. So some state hold a drawing. Everyone has until such and such a day to put their name in, then the allotted number are chosen. Charter schools often use the same process. The reason is that psychologically, it feels more fair. It may not be particularly more fair than the first-come-first serve option, though it may be much more fair than an option that is open to political influence. But people will accept that everyone had the same chance, and it eases tensions. Usually.

    Dice and chance in games can serve the same function. That's what the initial example says to me:

    Establishing/Fictional Positioning\n

    - What's a unit?
    Something the rules text that affects a dice roll (or other fortune rule)
    Example: Add +2 or +5 to a DC, set a DC to a familiar level (15, 20 or 25)
    Example*: Modify a monster by +/- 2 from the 'standard difficulty' compared to players' levels

    - What else can the GM do?
    The GM ad-hoc controls areas of the fiction that don't affect the players NOW. However, this sets precedents presumed to be accepted by players later as the basis for invoking "units of control" later

    - What's lame #1?
    Any time a confusing situation arises that seems to be in some kind of grey area between "units of control" and the ad-hoc control.
    \n David addresses the establishing of precedent in his rules of thumb to be consistent and predictable. The use of dice or weighting rolls to do so, is one tool to establish consistency and predictability. These ease the tensions with perceived conflict of interest for the GM. It's tempting to say that the more autonomy and transparency there is in the system the less danger of this, but 3.5 vs. 4e could arguably have that difference, and there is a lot of disgruntlement about 4e. But that's a total side issue.\n
