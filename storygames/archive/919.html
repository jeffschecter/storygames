Jason_Morningstar
July 2006 edited July 2006 in Play Advice
Children fighting a war. That's what my game is about and it is more than a little unsettling to think about the implications. I'm very serious about respecting the ground truth, and that means that the teenager you are playing may fall in love, but she may also die and/or go crazy and have everything she holds dear smashed before her eyes. That last bit is, in fact, nearly certain. So that's a little creepy, and it gets creepier when I work on the game and push it in directions that will make it more fun to play. I'm addressing what amounts to recent history, and Grey Ranks could conceivably be seen and read by people who dodged mortar rounds in Warsaw. It will certainly be seen by their grandchildren. I find myself pondering the ethics of my endeavor, and I wonder where hard lines need to be drawn regarding content and presentation.

As a designer, how much should I worry about my game causing harm? How do I balance respect for the intrinsic horror of the subject matter and the fact that it is a game, to be enjoyed on some level? What do you think? Have you seen games that address (or fail to address) real tragedy, or real brutality, in a responsible way?

    Jason -- if I read you right, your ethical concerns stem from a desire to adequately respect the real people whose lives your writing represents. I think there are two questions that pop out from that concern (that is, I see you asking two different questions at the same time): Â First, is it respectful to make a game about this at all? And second, is my game sufficiently respectful of their lived experience?

    The answer to the first question you will have to grapple with in order to be comfortable with the answer; one could argue that by representing that experience as "art" (i.e., in a fictionalized form), you are in fact doing something admirable, raising our awareness and knowledge of an experience that no one playing the game would otherwise encounter. In that vein, Grey Ranks is more respectful and let' s say humane than, say, another infantile fantasy game.

    The answer to the second question will depend on the criteria that you adopt: what does it mean to adequately or respectfully represent the experience of human beings under any conditions, when you are not a member of the group you're trying to represent? Humanistic and qualititative social science researchers deal with this question when they do "ethnographic" studies of other cultures. One technique that's been used has been to present the writing to a member of the culture (or their representative) and solicit their comments and feedback, incorporating them to the extent possible in the final write-up. That may not be practicable in this case, but it may be enough for you to imagine a survivor of the war, now 90 years old, watching you play the game with your friends. What do you expect his or her reaction would be? It might be gratitude; you never know.

    Jason --

    You're talking about how much reverence you should give your source material. The answer is simple: As much reverence as you, the artist, want to give it. No material -- none! -- is worth any reverence intrinsically. An irreverent take is as valid as a reverent one.

    I don't think that's the real issue. I think that the real issue is twofold.

    1) You are asking for permission. We can't give it to you.

    2) You are concerned about your game in other peoples' hands. Will they be properly reverent? In addition to the basic point, above, you as a game designer are not morally responsible for the content of the games played by others. They take a responsibility to that content as well.

    People *will* do their own thing with your game and they will ineveitably use it for art that you would find abhorrent. If this is unacceptable to you I suggest you not design the game.

    Ethically off-limits...hmmm. Okay, so starting with the presupposition that we are not talking about free speech (which outright lets you do things that may not be ethically sound), I would say it is less a matter of *what* gets presented than *how* it gets presented.

    so why not unethical? It retains the humanity and inhumanity, the gravity and the grace (to borrow a phrase from Simone Weil) that make up life.

    I think the most dangerous, most potentially unethical, style of 'real life' rpg's is the effort to erase those two dimensions, to make it all about this heroic agency, to elide the way in which terrible circumstances 'take possession' of people, make them as much objects as subjects. We don't like to see that, but I think there is something salutory in any endeavor that fosters reflection. It's one thing to play nice escapist rpg, tell heroic stories and all that, quite another to try and tell a 'true' story that 'teaches' its players that those heroic escapist tropes are how the real world works.

    Actually, Jason, one of the things that I value most in roleplaying is that ability to be "selective about interpretation." Whereas most other media place a large, large portion of the interpretation-selection in the hands of the creator and only a little (comparatively) in the hands of the audience, in roleplaying where the participants are both creators and audience, they have a vast, almost unprecidented amount of interpretive power. I'll point at PTA as a great example of something that allows a group of people to "make a television show" that would never actually see real airtime because it's about what the players care about and interprets what they care about in the ways that they see fit. We can throw extreme examples out there (a group of furries on a space station!), but the potential is better served by more moderate examples such as a family drama exploring gender issues or a high school serial about class. PTA can be a powerful tool to allow players to create powerful and deeply personal interpretations on just about anything.

    So in the case of Grey Ranks, I see your project as something that will put a lot of power, authority, and permission in players' hands to explore a troubling piece of history. Will some players play the game irreverently and perhaps "irresponsibly"? Yes, invariably so. But I suspect they're the minority, and what they do is their business, not yours. You're producing a game that allows all your other players to do something incredible, and that's a good thing.

    Off-limits to game designers in general? No. Off-limits to specific game-designers? Probably. I should not make a roleplaying game about American partisan politics, for instance, because it would be ideologically-biased partisan drek. So while every individual game designer has probably got a number of topics that he or she really isn't qualified to cover in a responsible or quality fashion, I don't think there's any topic out there that nobody could do. I certainly don't think that the art form itself is incapable of handling any "tough" topics.

    Jason --

    Do I think that there are issues that are handled poorly? Yes. Look at, say, Little Fears.

    Are there issues that I don't think that a role-playing game, as a medium, can handle appropriately? Damned straight. But these are concerns about limitations of the medium. If they can be handled appropriately, they should be, and as we advance our game design technology, we're going to be able to handle a wider and wider range of topics well. If I draw a line in the sand and say "this, gaming can never handle this" then, well, I'm setting myself up for being wrong.

    It could be that you don't have the ability to handle the material of Grey Ranks as well as you'd like to. In that case, either develop your skills to the point where you can or drop the game. You can do it or you can't, and you're in the best position to say which.

    You will publish this game or not, and face the moral consequences thereof (good or bad -- I think probably mostly good.) No amount of fretting and hand-wringing beforehand will absolve a single bit of them.

    yrs--
    --Ben

    P.S. I think it may be a mistake as a designer to prevent your players from making statements you consider abhorrent -- simply because that means that you are also crippling their ability to make statements that are powerful and meaningful. See Card, via Tony, above.
