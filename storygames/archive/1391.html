What do you ignore?

TonyLB
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Play Advice
Over in Frame my Scene, Eric raised a good point that aggressive scene framing is (almost by definition) going to blow by some scenes that could be played, but aren't. The particular issue was that some people responded to "I want to get the Hand of Flame back from the Count. Let's sneak into his castle!" by skipping the "sneaking in" and framing directly into the castle, post-sneaking.

So, yes, aggressive scene framing is always going to be as much about what you skip as it is about what you frame to. It is saying "This is important," in a way that inherently implies "That other stuff? Not so much." It's good for people to be on roughly the same page about this.

So in the worst-case scenario where, actually, the player values the sneaking and doesn't care about the activity inside the castle, you've miscued and screwed up.

And, likewise, in the best-case scenario where the player values the confrontation inside the castle and doesn't care about the sneaking except inasmuch as it gets her there, you've correctly cued and done a great job.

I would argue (and I know that this may, in fact, become an argument and I'm fine with that) that in the wierd-case scenario where the players values both in equal proportion, it's impossible to go wrong.

If you give them the sneaking, and then give them the confrontation, they're happy campers. You've given them two scenes that were exciting!

If you give them just the confrontation ... well, what's your next scene? If we assume that it's going to be lame then you've blown it, but why assume that your next scene will be lame? If you give them the awesome confrontation scene, and then frame directly to the awesome dungeon-escape scene then you have (again) given them two scenes that were exciting. My experience is that players are, generally speaking, totally okay with that.

The arguments against skipping a valuable scene in order to present a different valuable scene seem (to me) to be as follows (1) an argument from scarce resources ... we haven't got that many valuable scenes and (2) an argument from structure ... that sneaking scene establishes something specific that we can build on, which is missed in the later scenes.

I think #1 is extremely dicey. #2 is more plausible, but I refuse to believe that every scene that the players would enjoy is a structural scene. Sometimes they just want to do something trivial and fun, and while it's totally cool to do it, it's totally cool to skip it too.

Thoughts?

    Couple thoughts.

    First of all, nobody is going to want just Sneaking and a Gunfight. Any given player is going to have a whole lot more things they're interested in. Which is what Tony's trying to get at. If the player wants Sneaking, a Gunfight, a Court Scene, Wilderness Survival, and Naval Combat, is there any difference between a set of scenes including Sneaking, Gunfight, and Wilderness Survival and another set that includes Gunfight, Wilderness Survival, Naval Combat? As long as you are consistently hitting flags, does it matter that you could have hit a flag but you passed it up in favor of hitting some other flag? To complicate matters, what happens if the other characters aren't too interested in a Sneaky scene, but this one guy is? Is it okay then to skip over the group-sneaky scene and plan to have a solo-sneaky scene to scratch his itch?

    Secondly, this is why I'm increasingly disinterested in flagless games. I look at a character sheet of Random Game X and the first thing I look for is "where do I indicate what I care about?" Can you get flags outside the character sheet? Sure, but why should the GM or other players maintain some secondary sheet listing off what everyone is interested in? Especially since, when you get down to it, that second flag-sheet is actually more important than the character sheet.

    Thirdly, as for what I ignore in a game? Seriously, depends on the game. If we're playing ninjas, I want the Sneaky scene. If we're playing cowboys, I want the Gunfight scene. And so on. What is and is not important is hugely contextual, depending not only on the players but which gamebook they're playing out of and the individual game that they are playing.

Which can be done by just having an amazing group, or can be helped along by having a system that is unambigious about what its flags are and how they work. I find that both, together, work out the best.

    Also, if you put resources into something to be game effective and get the game effect, but don't get the flag effect then it isn't a waste of resources because that isn't what you were using it for. It's only a waste if you don't get what you wanted out of it, and saying "you wanted game effect and didn't get a flag so it's a failed flag" is a logically flawed argument. Watch this:

    Max wants to not be mind controlled
    Max buys an ability to not be mind controlled
    Max is not mind controlled
    Therefore Max's desire to confront mind control has failed.

    See how at that last point it all goes haywire?

    You make it work only if you add right after the second line: Any ability you take is a challange to the GM.

    That works perfectly correctly if you are playing FLFS. Last playtest document I saw Josh had a bit in there about how your abilities are things you want to be challanged on, to push and be pushed around. It does not, however, work if you are playing D&D -- where you often end up with adventures created without any reference to the character sheet.

      The constellation of things I'm simply not interested in (as opposed to actively disinterested) is stupidly large.

      "I am not interested in sea turtles killed by red tide."
      "I am not interested in the scullery maids' gossip."
      "I am not interested in weather patterns."
      "I am not interested in kobold culture."
      "I am not interested in asian-themed fantasy."

      And in the end, what's the real use in identifying stuff that one player isn't interested in? It's not like it turns that into forbidden territory; it's just uninteresting territory. It might be interesting for another player, in which I certainly hope that the uninterested player can let his friend have fun for ten or twenty minutes. And if the GM is facing down a list of fifteen things that players are interested in, why is he ever going to stray into uninteresting territory? The players want to go to New York, Boston, Chicago, Providence, and Washington D.C. Why would the GM take them to Los Angeles?

      Now, stuff that players are actively disinterested in to the point that they will have a negative reaction, that's worth flagging. You can use lines and veils, you can just not go there, you can do that stuff while they're not in the room, whatever. But that's important information for the GM and the other players to have so that they know that territory is not just uninteresting, it would be a detriment to fun at the table.
