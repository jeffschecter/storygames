\n Hey friends,

I know some of you are following Grey Ranks so I wanted to give you an update. The text itself is in good shape. It's been reviewed by two Poles for errors of fact and grammar - BWHQ's Radek Drozdalski has been extra-helpful and corrected all my swear words. The final book will be perfect bound, 6x9, and I'm guessing about 125 pages.

Eight of the 10 interior art pieces are done and I couldn't be happier with Jeff Bent's work. Here's a particularly gonzo piece - that's Janek; he's 15. One thing I'm very excited about is that the interior art is sequential, following the extended examples of play in the book and telling the story of a Grey Ranks crew visually. The "gunfight" piece takes place on 1 August, 1944, as the example crew gets in a firefight in Szucha Alley, outside Gestapo headquarters.

The mechanics are solid now after loads of playtesting, and I'm doing a final hardcore test before giving it to the Mad Irishman to lay out at the end of the month. So I'm guessing that I'll have books for sale by Camp Nerdly, first of May. I have to say I've never been more excited or proud of a project.

    \n Sure Rich. First of all my first draft was a Game-Chef-esque local contest entry in the fall of 2005, and that version was a train wreck. It's been iterative and I've returned to the drawing board a couple of times. Recently I went back and looked at early drafts and realized I've retained almost nothing. I talk about the process a bit in this thread, too, which I'll repeat for convenience:

    Here are my playtesting approaches, in descending order of quantity:

    I have a little brain trust I bounce ideas off, and much "testing" goes on by proposing and challenging design goals and assumptions via email and face to face.

    I've had a number of mechanical playtests, that looks like this:

    1. Me sitting at a desk running the game's numbers, start to finish or maybe just to test a particular portion over and over and over again.
    2. Me and a couple of friends doing the same thing, until things utterly break.

    I've run the game as a one-shot (using various points of attack) for friends and fellow travelers:

    1. In my living room or at an apartment con with pals.
    2. At conventions for interested strangers.

    I've solicited semi-blind tests (I'm available to clarify and answer questions before the actual session), with widely distributed play groups - thanks, guys!

    I'm running the game absolutely straight, as intended, for my weekly game group as well, now that everything is as tight as it's realistically going to be. We're playing our second session tomorrow night, and half my local crew has playtested various earlier iterations as well. Grey Ranks has been in development for so long (relatively speaking) that I've gotten some pressure to just get it done, and I'm sensitive to the "better is the enemy of done" argument. Perfection isn't a design goal, but well-tested, functional play definitely is. Happily, I'm seeing the game deliver. Unless the final playtest implodes or something, I think it's in good shape.

    \n Blindtesting helps with the "comprehensibility" problem - anything you get a question about is almost certainly poorly written. I also run my manuscript past two people - one an extremely detail-oriented math nerd and the other a loosey-goosey English major, neither of whom are gamers. That's a development step I forgot to include. If they can both make sense of it, that's reassuring. I don't agree with your dichotomy - at least my work process has design and explanation tied together. I can't really test something until I can also explain it on paper, but that's just me.

    Thinking back I think the breakdown is:

    4 semi-blind tests of varying length, from one to three sessions
    10 single-session and convention games

    Accounting for short sessions, call it 70 hours of face-to-face testing? That's a rough estimate, anyway. Plus mechanical tests and brainstorming sessions I cannot count - many additional hours.

    \n Hey Christan, the game is explicitly about playing Polish child soldiers. Who those child soldiers interact with, and how that occurs, is up to the players, with systemic input. You generate situation elements for each chapter (a technique yanked from In A Wicked Age), and many of the choices involve either groups of Germans or named individuals. For example:

    Leutnant Karl Thiel, thoughtful officer ashamed and proud of his duty, which is a choice from grid position B-4 that a player could choose to offer. Who is Lt. Thiel? How will he appear in the game? Entirely up to the group. He could be sympathetic or a vicious beast.

    In the most recent playtest, one player was on A-4 during chapter three and chose Amalia Fissler, an ambitious physician's wife and ardent Nazi. The players framed a mission that involved killing her husband, and there was a fantastic scene where this gung-ho Polish teenager vaulted their garden wall and came face-to-face with Frau Fissler, who was this charming, intelligent woman. Frau Fissler was played by another player, when asked by the player of the guy framing the scene. All we knew going into it was that she was an ardent Nazi, and it was enough to spur a very dramatic and cool scene.

    There are some situation elements that invoke Germans and leave no room for ambiguity; some are plucked from history and involve atrocities. But players always have the choice of picking other elements if that's not where they want to go with the game. I should also point out that there is no mechanical reward for incorporating situation elements. But they are evocative and fun to play with, so people generally wrap them into the ongoing narrative in interesting ways.

    \n Naw, except in the epilogue, where players describe the fate of their character. I certainly address the Soviet "liberation" of Warsaw, which took place in January 1945 (the game ends on 3 October, with the Polish Home Army capitulation to the Germans). There are some situation elements related to making contact with the Red army, and their progress in eastern Poland gets mentioned throughout the game ("They're in Lwow! They're in Radom! They're ... sitting on their asses a kilometer away.") The Soviets basically sat around on the east bank of the Vistula and let the Germans crush the best and brightest in Poland, then rode in and arrested any Home Army soldiers (of any age) who weren't dead or in a German POW cage. Actually, they arrested the ones in German POW cages, too.

    Also! You don't need to care about any of this name and date stuff to have fun playing the game.

    \n Thanks, John, I really appreciate that.

    A horrible anecdote from last night's playtest: The crew infiltrates the Wola district on 4 August, which has been re-taken by the Germans. Their mission: locate and free their old scoutmaster. They find out he's being held in the field house of the Wola football stadium and sneak over. The field is covered in corpses. One of the players frames a mission scene for another guy - the field house is packed with Polish prisoners, and German guards are forcing them out, one at a time, and making them run for the exit on the opposite side - 100 meters. On the roof, there are Germans having a party with deck chairs and sniper rifles. And the crew is watching through field glasses, and the next Pole is shoved out the door - and of course it is the scoutmaster.

    So the guy in the mission scene (Segedy here at S-G) has a dilemma. He can toss a big die at the mission to help it succeed, or he can toss a little die and basically make it fail while looking awesome himself. But if he uses that big die, he has to narrate a personal failure. He's the crew sniper - he's got his dad's old rifle, the symbol of everything he cares about. There are too many Germans on the roof for him to take them all out. So he draws down on the scoutmaster, the guy who taught him how to shoot, and puts a round through his knee. No running for him, he lives to be rescued, and the Germans are all over the crew - success and disaster all rolled into one. It was a cool moment. Awful but cool.

    \n Actually, you're not wrong- the game is designed for three sessions of play, covering a very specific period of the Warsaw Uprising. Although it's not a campaign-based system designed for many sessions (and if that's what you're looking for, there are many great games for that), it does support repeated play. Even though you're playing through the same basic time-frame, the story can be radically different depending on the players and the characters, elements, and missions they develop.

    I'm really excited to see what will happen with the game once it's in the wild- I think people will come up with entirely unexpected twists on the game, and I can't wait to hear about them.
