Screw Design Duels, how about a PLAY Duel?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nAndy \n\n\n
\n
\n\nDecember 2007 \n edited December 2007 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n No, I'm not peeing in Cheerios or anything. But I was looking at the design duels, and in line with my whole "design is awesome on the side, where the main talk is about play" vibe, and thinking what we can do about play...

Anyway, I was thinking, "What would a Play Contest even look like? What would it judge? What would it entail?"

I mean, with a design contest, the idea is simple: Make the better/more fun game (or, in the spirit of the contest, "have fun attempting to make the better game, in a personal high-feedback environment").

What about a PLAY contest, though? Here were some thoughts I had of what one might look like, I'd love to hear others' ideas:

1) Play as many RPGs as possible within two weeks. Score given to number of sessions played.
-- The same, but weighing instead the variety of games you play.
-- The same, but instead focusing on running games (as GM) or facilitating non-GM games.
-- Or giving points to having an equal number of games ran to games played.

2) Run a game, someone else runs a game, and the players "score" the session on various criteria.
-- Maybe the same players in both games. Or maybe different players attempting to score on the same plane.
-- Perhaps a setup where the GM describes the session she will run.

3) Run or play in an entire campaign in one week. The campaign must consist of at least 20 hours of play. If you do so, you win!

4) Two players play three games, where they are given three very different character types, and are asked to play them "more in character" than the other. They are rated on their performance.

5) A third party selects four different games, like RIFTS, COLD CITY, DOGS IN THE VINEYARD and TEENAGERS FROM OUTER SPACE. The participants have one month to play in or run a session of each. All participants that do win the challenge. No consideration is given to whether the participants already own the games or not, but they must be games in print.

6) Play in or run 6 RPGs that are available for free. Go! The first three to meet these goals, giving a brief AP of each, win the contest.

Other ideas? I'm not saying "let's do one!" (at least not now, anyway). But imagine that me and Graham were to throw down and say, "Oh yeah? Who is MORE AWESOME at PLAY, huh?", we could have a range of ideas to use to run such a showdown.

-Andy
