\n I've been toying with some fonts lately, and it struck me that we had a font porn thread around here a while ago.

But instead of asking what sorts of fonts are good, or how they should be used typographically, I'm asking something a little different.

I've modified a font depicting the full standard set of 28 dominoes in a horizontal configuration, I had to add in quite a few to complete the set, and a further refinement will have capitalised letters depicting each corresponding domino in a vertical configuration.

My next project is a new dingbat style font made up of generic rpg related numerous symbols, I know there's a few like this around already, but I'll be offering the result free on my website.

For starters I'm looking at creating a bunch of symbols representing dice and facings: 6 cubic symbols depicting the numbers 1-6 for a regular cubic die, another 6 depicting the dice values with pips rather than digits, 4 triangular symbols with numbers at the bottom representing the faces of a d4, 8 octohedral symbols, etc.

So the dice values and facings are easy (60 different symbols), and I'll probably throw in the four standard card suits (hearts,spades,diamonds,clubs). But what else would people be interested in seeing?

Once I've generated the font once, I might create some variants of it (sci-fi dice, Japanese numeral dice, roman numerals, etc.).

    \n Don't forget it's vitally important that your font is set up to be embedded into PDF files! If you can't embed it, you can't sell it to others or send it to the printer's. I get so sick of finding nice 'free use' fonts, only to discover they can't be embedded. If your font program is too old and won't let you set the 'embedable' flag internally, let me know. I have a small utility that can force the embed flag onto any font. It's illegal to use on commercial fonts because it circumvents the usual failsafe, but if (like me) your font creation package is too old, it's a lot cheaper and easier than learning / buying a new package.

    -Ash

    \n Alright, so I'm looking probably looking at 2 fonts.

    The first of which will consist of the various dice, and showing the various die results.

    The second of which will consist of other interesting glyphs and symbols such as the elements (though this sort of thing can be highly subjective), map symbolism, card suits, action symbols and other general assorted bits and pieces.

    I can probably cut down the complexity of the first font with a simple fix. An alphabetical keystroke sets a die outline (a=d4, b=d6, c=d8, d=d10, etc.), a numeric keystroke sets a negative space then writes a numeral (effectively placing the number on top of the previous die shape)...in this way, all of the combinations possible for a d4,d6,d8,d,10 and d12 can be applied through a combination of two keystrokes (Alpha followed by Numeric), and these possible combinations take up less than 20 characters within the font.

    d20s blow it out to 26 total characters if my theory works...still leaving the possibility for upper and lower cases to be inverses of one another. That's less than the domino font which currently has over 50 glyphs in it's horizontal format.

    I'll have to check on the ability to embed these fonts into PDFs, thanks for pointing that out Ash. A quick check through the user documentation indicates that this should be possible, but I won't know for certain until I try to open one of my PDFs on a computer not containing the font.

    \n I wouldn't bother with the elemental symbols. Alchemical fonts are already better at that.

    I would include a handful of simple dingbats that would be useful on character sheets (e.g. empty circle, filled circle, empty box, box with a slash in it, box with an x in it, filled box. I have produced (but not released) a font that contained a very small numbers of such symbols, but in characters that you might use for the ASCII representation of such things. For example, The * character was a filled circle, the o character was an empty circle, the _ character was an empty box, x was the box with an x in it, etc. The idea was that you could build web pages using that font, so it would look good for people that had the font installed, but if they didn't, the page would still be basically legible.

    Also, most font systems can hold way more than 128 characters. Unicode fonts can hold tens of thousands. Something like TypeTool can handle these easily.

    As far as PDF embedding, this is where a real font editor will save you a lot of grief (vs. a typical freeware Win32 crapplication). Where most budget editors fall down is screwing up embedding, particularly when PDFs are moved to other platforms. A typical problem is that, for example, using linux to open a PDF that looks great on windows reveals one letter in the font that displays as a box, or not all. Better editors don't have such problems, for the most part. One litmus test for this used to be if they supported OpenType, they were usually OK. Not sure if that still holds, though, as OpenType is more common than it used to be.

    \n Jason, I see what you want, and appreciate that at small sizes the font will be tricky to read. There are two simple reasons why I haven't done the font in the icon style that you've depicted.

    Firstly, the matter of negative writing on a shape. Most people who'll be using the font will probably be using a word processor or page layout software, rather than a piece of graphical software. I wanted the font to be fairly intuitive to use, type a letter for a die, then type a number to place a value on it (causing a backspace, a typing of the numeral, then a forward space to return to the proper pace in the text).

    If I read it correctly, you were one of the people who indicated that the idea of combining die and numeral in this way seemed to be a good idea.\n
    Posted By: Jason Morningstar

    I like the idea of atomizing die type and value.
    \nIf I use positive solid blocks for the die shapes, and positive solid shapes for the numerals, they will simply overlap...eg. black number on black die. You just won't see the numeral.

    The problem is that most word processors can't handle the idea of cutting negative shapes from positive.

    So the typing process for a die becomes...type text in black, change font for dice, type die shape, change text to white, type die value, change text back to black, change font back to text style, resume typing.

    This becomes complicated further when certain text editors/word processors have trouble determining which of the glyphs is on top when rendering the text for a printer or PDF output.

    I'm trying to cut off potential problems before they occur...but I guess a little troubleshooting before a formal release will remedy that issue.

    Secondly, using hyper simplified glyphs can be even more confusing than using graphical representations of the dice.

    Is the horizontal diamond the d8 or the d10? All three of these dice (d4, d8, d20) have triangular sides...which is the triangle again?

    You'd need a decent symbol key (near the front of the text, or in the index) to explain what the different glyphs represent.

    On second thoughts, to clarify things for the widest possible audience, my existing glyphs would be enhanced through a similar key.

      \n Jeph, indeed your reading is correct based on the information I have provided thus far.

      My fix for this is simple...the dice font will generate a die glyph when an alphabetical keystroke is pressed.

      lower case a-f = opaque polyhedral image glyphs (a = d4, b = d6, c = d8, d = d10, e = d12, f = d20)
      UPPER CASE A-F = transparent polyhedral image glyphs (A = d4, B = d6, C = d8, D = d10, E = d12, F = d20)

      lower case g-l = the Morningstar series filled (g = triangle [d4], h = square [d6], i = vertical diamond [d8], j = horizontal diamond [d10], k = pentagon [d12], l = circle [d20])
      UPPER CASE G-L = the Morningstar series outlines (G = triangle [d4], H = square [d6], I = vertical diamond [d8], J = horizontal diamond [d10], K = pentagon [d12], L = circle [d20])

      lower case m-p = card suits filled (m = clubs, n = hearts, o = spades, p = diamonds)
      UPPER CASE M-P = card suits outlined (M = clubs, N = hearts, O = spades, P = diamonds)
      lower case q-t = alphabetical card ranks (q = Ace, r = Jack, s = Queen, t = King)
      Upper case q-t = other card related symbols (Q = Outlined Joker [the red one], R = Filled Joker [the black one], S = blank card glyph [card front], T = filled card glyph [card back])

      I'll probably expand out the card suits as well to account for pentacles, coins, and those other suits found in eastern European card decks.

      numerals 1-0 = applies a backspace and types the numeral over the previous glyph.
      HOLDING SHIFT and the numerals 1-0 = applies a backspace and types a double digit of 10 plus the numeral over the glyph. (eg. Shift and 4, does a backspace then types a small "14" on the glyph instead of typing a "$".)

      The only issues I see here is that certain European countries have different keyboard layouts than the standard one found in the English speaking world. But hell, you can't be everything to everyone...

      The " - " and " + " keystrokes will apply a negative or positive to the previous glyph (a la Fudge Dice).

      U through to Z will be for reserved for specifically game related icons: "a target symbol" indicating target number "a hand holding dice" indicating "roll the dice", "a hand holding a card" indicating "pick a card", "simple head and shoulder symbol" indicating "player" (filled and outlined versions to differentiate between players, or player and GM)....etc.
