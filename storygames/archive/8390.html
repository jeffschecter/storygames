\n
\n
\n
\n
\n This is just a preliminary test of interest.

I'm currently in the stages of finishing the N\xf8rwegian Style book, an anthology of Norwegian games translated into English. When that's done, I want to make a book called Foreign Style (F\xf4re\xefg\xf1 Styl\xe9?) - an anthology of games/scenarios in other languages, translated into English. The goal is to make cool games from different gaming cultures available to everyone. At this stage, though, this is only an idea - I'm not committing myself fully to this project before the N\xf8rwegian Style & Knutepunkt books are out (sometime in April).

There's a lot happening that I only barely know of. I know about the cool Fastaval scenarios from Denmark, I know Philippe Tromeur is making French mini-games by the dozens, I know the Italians have their Flying Circus, I suspect something is stirring in Poland... Some of these things are clones of the U. S. mainstream, some are based on completely different cultural assumptions.

So what I'm envisioning right now is something like this:
- One or two games from each participating country
- A 1-page intro for each game about that country's play/design tradition
- Each game must have been playtested before submission
- All games in English, edited by an editorial staff of 4-5 volunteers, proofread by a professional
- Published via Lulu
- Marketed by participants and other enthusiasts at cons around the world

So. Sound fun? Any ideas?

    \n I really didn't want to get into a discussion about the title at this early stage, but it seems it can't be helped. So, to show where I'm coming from:
    - In my view, irony has to be subtle, otherwise it doesn't work. "Foreign Style" might have connotations I'm unaware of, which means I might drop it; but I really don't want a "We're Being Really Ironic Here, People" style title.
    - I don't want a nice, harmless, catch-all title like "International Games", "World Games" or similar. I wouldn't buy that book. I want something slightly edgy, slightly provocative, a bit arrogant, that makes people think and wonder.
    - I don't want the "lesser"/"primitive" thing either. If anything, the implied prejudice should go the other way, towards arrogance: "How To Play Games In A Sophisticated Fashion, Even If You're American". (Which would be too blatant, but still it's more in the vein of what I'm looking for here).

    So! With that in mind, feel free to contribute. But honestly, I'd much rather let the discussion about the title lie, and focus on practical issues. Especially this: What possible problems and challenges should we be prepared for? I'd love to hear from people with experience from similar projects - Jonathan's experiences with Push, for example.

    \nThis book will not be able to show the entire gaming culture of any country. It will only be a snapshot, showing more-or-less-random contributions from different countries. (Sort of like the "World's Best SF" short story collections - not all countries actually have a culture for writing SF short stories, but the books are fun anyway). The one-page scene descriptions will be able to provide interested people with some pointers for further reading/research.

    The value will be in that one game that makes you go "Wow, can they do that? Can I do that?" I hope every reader will find at least one such game in the book - something that inspires them to expand their play and design.

    \n I think it's a cool project. It will certainly not be representative of any country's mainstream RPG culture, because, well, it's the same everywhere, as others have pointed out. It's the same glossy, 300-page fantasy games. Everywhere.

    However, it will be interesting as an anthology of fringe RPG culture all over the world. In Sweden, there's a big mainstream glossy book-culture, and there's a large and thriving forgian indie game culture in Swedish, with games like Ber\xe4ttelser fr\xe5n Staden, Superg\xe4nget (published as "Supercrew" in English), H\xf6stdimma and my own Oskrivna blad. There's also the Jeepform stuff, and a lot of weird, experimental freeform shit. I don't really know much about that stuff, but that would probably be the most interesting for this project. There's also the wonderful blog "Den raljerande nanoapan" ("The Rambling Nanomonkey") which posts reviews of games that don't exist. That's pretty edgy, and has some really awesome games (which, sadly, as I said, don't exist). Maybe a couple of reviews from there would be suitable? I know Dimfrost, who writes them, pops in at SG from time to time.

    But the page describing the gaming culture? It would be mostly about which copied American games have been most influential (Rolemaster and Gamma World, by the way).

    \n I prefer to be paid for my work, at least when it is published outside my own share-ware style forum-game posts.

    I want other people to make money on their games too. A designer gives a lot more credit to his own project by asking a prize that gives him something back for all the work. As a player it is nice to give the designer some real benefits for giving me a game.

    This is true for anthologies too, especially if the anthology may be sold worldwide ...

    Proposal:
    - Give every designer 300 $ for a game in the book.
    - Give the man organizing the book 300 $ too (and if he has a game in it as well, he will get a share for that one too).
    - Let any designer promoting and selling the book by himself keep the profits of his sales.
    - Split any profits from sales on the web in equal shares for all designers, plus the organizer.

    A small pot of money, but something to start with ... and the profits may be a nice supply of change ...
