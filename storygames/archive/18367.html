\n This is something that I've thought about these last week, and it's thoughts that I can't go on with by myself. Last time we played, all my character's goals (and therefore mine in this case) were killed off one by one, which made me loose all my investments with my character in the game. Is this the same thing as having your character killed? In both cases, the player is loosing the main investment in the game but I'm not talking about the character itself but about what it strives for.

Could one simple solution to this be to separate the character's goal and the player's goal? As an example, a player could strive to show the ripple effect of the death of it's character. Another example could be to overthrow the king. The character's goal could be something different, but it's something the player strives for while playing. I would appreciate a game where the goals between the characters and the players were more (not implicit) separated. Where the game itself actually talked more about this to make the participants aware of it.

I'm not sure where I'm going with this, so I'm mostly throwing out a thought.

    \n I'd say that an important feature of game design is how player and character goals resolve. In a good game, the resolution itself leads to a new situation. As a simple example, designs which a zero-sum tend to do this really well: if you achieve something, it's always someone else's loss. So, quite naturally, other people will now have new goals related to the fallout of your success.

    I'm playing in a Diplomacy game right now, and it does this very well: it's set up so that you never have enough resources to effectively hold on to what you have, and never enough to achieve your next goal. So you must lean on other players for help, even while your successes are creating problems for other players. This sort of thing can work well in more story-oriented games.

    Another good example is Otherkind Dice, or any game based on the idea that success usually comes at a price: "Ok, you've captured the princess's heart, but... she's being shipped off to America! ...her family is facing an uprising and about to lose their grasp on the throne! ...she's just been engaged to someone else!

    No matter what you've achieved, there are important repercussions which create new situations for you or others to deal with.

    And the same should go for when you fail or your goals slip out of your grasp. (This works quite easily if that happens because someone else achieves their aims, as opposed to one actor simply failing.)

    \n An example of killing a goal:

    So, I was in a game with a gm and one other player. The other player's character decided to do a very complicated ritual that would alter the magical flow in his home town. I asked the gm, "So, is this a plot specifically for his character? Because I'd like to get involved, but I don't want to get invested in this plotline if it's just a plot for him."

    The gm assured me it was fine for both of us to be involved. And there was a cool adventure about gathering information that involved time travel, New Orleans, and Marie Laveau. It really rocked.

    And then, the gm ran a solo adventure for the other player (which was not out of line, as this was deliberately created to be a game any combination of us could pick up) during which the whole magic flow alteration was done by gm fiat because the PC happened to mention he wanted this in front of an NPC whom the gm decided had the power to make it so and would logically do it just for the asking, with strings attached.

    This was years ago, and I didn't believe even at the time that the gm deliberately tried to shaft me. The gm tended to forget things and gm on the fly. It happens. It was a mistake, it happened, we moved on.

    But, my character's goal of helping out with this ritual and making sure everything went right was completely killed, dead as a door nail. It's possible that the other PC's goal was killed by this as well, as the PC was no longer doing a ritual and keeping control of events, but this PC had been handed a juicy situation in return: he owed a favor to the NPC who'd made things as he wished them to be. My PC had a goal made irrelevant, with nothing in return.

    \nI actually think it's the same difficulty (give or take) to create a new character as making up a new goal with an old character. See the last paragraph in this post if you want to know why. I otherwise really like what you're saying. I've been realizing these last years that roleplaying characters, my own at least, are very one-dimensional. They strive for one goal and one goal only, or they tend to follow the same personality no matter what happens. A multi-layered character changes depending on the situation. You're not always a greedy dwarf, because you can sometimes give coins to children. That's depth when it comes to personality.

    In the movie Transporter, the hero always follows his code as a driver. Always ... up until he's going to deliver a body bag and discovers that there's a (of course) beautiful woman in the bag. He then breaks his code. A code that the audience have been force fed with is unbreakable. I think it would be better if it would be established beforehand that he would never hurt a woman and then had to choose between his two codes. That's layers. That's why a greedy dwarf can give coins to children.

    It's the same thing with goals. You can try to reach it, but you should try to reach it in different ways, depending on the situation. You should also have other kinds of goals, and change your priorities on what's more important to achieve. I think it should also be clear what the next path will be as soon as the main goal is reachable. A dwarf could have as a goal to become rich, but as soon as the dwarf is getting close to the goal, the next goal could appear so everyone could help the character advance towards that. It's somewhat what Paul_T is talking about. I think it would be good to have several goals to reach, perhaps one primary and then 1-2 secondary goals. Perhaps you can suffice with being the town sheriff instead of the mayor?

    One thing I've been thinking of lately is having end conditions. When one or two end conditions are reached, the story ends. The traditional roleplaying games both have it and don't have it. Finishing a dungeon is, as an example, an end condition but the character will advance to the next story. Goals can be typical end conditions, and if people starts to notice that one person is close to it's goal, perhaps they will go for their secondary goals instead? Let's say it's a GM-less game about an election for the new mayor in a nameless western town. When the mayor is selected, the game ends. Other posts are sheriff, ranch owner or whatever fits the genre. All participants strives to both put their characters on the best posts but also have a better post than the player to the right. Perhaps you get 5 points for defeating your left-hand neighbor, 3 points for being a mayor, 2 points for sheriff and so on. This may sound like a boardgame in the same genre as Diplomacy, but I think it's possible to pull this off as a roleplaying game if you got the right structures (including the rules).

When that happens, it's about the same difficulty to create a new character with a goal than creating a new one with an old character.

    2. Look up the relations to that character on the map. (takes around 10 seconds)
    3. Frame a scene with the relations in mind by creating a situation and throw in some characters in it.
    4. Play out the scene with the characters.

So you don't really need to know ALL the relations - just the goals. Who wants what. Play to find out how. The character's with a clear goal is also usually the ones that you remember. Those had a story to be told.

    Back to goals: these are designed to give players an idea for what their characters are doing when the story begins, to guide their interaction with the fiction. Situations created by the GM or other players however can make them non-relevant, and as in the case Lisa Padol posted above. In such cases, when the player has invested in the goal is equivalent to a character which has seen the same anount of investment from the player. Thus, the effect is the same: kill an character or it's goals, you're denying the player's Investment. That's the core of the issue here.

    So I think you should probably be as careful with the character's goals as with the character itself. As a GM, both should be given the same chances of remaining and making an impact in the fiction. Yes, both can be easily replaced, but the Investment made in them won't come back that easily.
