\n Hi everyone! I'm kinda new to these boards and I generally just design RPGs for me and my online friends to mess around with. But I thought I'd try my hand at releasing something to the public. I would love to start my own company one day and I was hoping I could get some input.

The game https://drive.google.com/file/d/0B0rQNwd22ivsSE1GTERMcEZIUHc/view?usp=sharing is called Ghosts Like Us. where you take on the role of a haunting entity at some sort of property trying to scare away (among other motivations) the new inhabitants using your ghostly powers. Not a lot of stats, super rules light. More a comedy story-telling game than what someone thinks of when they talk table-top. It's completely unplayed, untested, but I liked the idea of not having dice or a GM.

I'd really like input. Whats good, whats bad, if you'd like to play and let me know how it plays that would be cool too. So far I havent seen anything out there like this. But to be honest it was a single days work so my feelings wont be hurt if it crumbles to dust. haha. Sorry about the design work im still an amateur!

    \n I really love the setting, the random tables and the general idea of playing haunted-house-ghosts!

    I also like the 5 Scenes (or maybe they are more "Phases", played out with multiple scenes?). It would be nice to flash them out more with a prominent structure.

    For design, I find some of the line spacing too compressed. It's probably a design choice, yet in my opinion diminishes readability (and there seems to be enough space on the page).

    As for game mechanics, I find the coin economics a bit too deterministic and I think I wouldn't enjoy "having to play fail" (sort of like self-compels) all the time just to have enough successes.
    Generally, for the humans, I'd prefer simpler rules/stats.
    I could imagine simple mechanics for the ghosts and just a number of checkboxes (hit point style) for each human or just one for the family. When all boxes are checked off, they "crack" and move out.

    Actually, this would work great as a hack of John Harper's Lasers & Feelings
    http://www.onesevendesign.com/laserfeelings/
    Instead of "Laser" and "Feelings", you could have just the two powers:
    - Raw (physical, kinetic, shaking up things)
    - Subtle (psychic, spooky, cause shivers)

    I could really imagine playing it like this.

    playing the fails might be an opportunity to make something funny happen. But even if you don't like playing the fails, there's an element of strategy there. You could always push the scene more towards something you don't care about just to fail on purpose.
    \nI get that is meant to be used strategically. I think this is nice if you can use it as an additional mechanic, i.e. like in Fate, where you can use self-compels to collect Fate points (as bonus points).
    However, if the core mechanics constantly forces you to play fails, this may get tiresome and eventually wear down the comedic element.

    If you want to stick to the coin mechanics, here is another idea:
    - Conflicts are resolved via coin flipping.
    - Your ghost has a "coin pool" that it can additionally use to turn around fails (2 coins for certain succcess; 1 coin for a 're-roll')
    - Your ghost can add a coin to its pool when it 'intentionally fails' a situation

    You will eventually find out through playtesting how it works best.
    Keep us posted how it goes!

    \nI'm a little bit opposed any kind of tactical thinking because you want people to collaborate and get a feeling of togetherness, which wont happen if there are any signs of competition. If this is how you want the game to be, perhaps you can have two different tables of suggestions where one is about helping the occupants while one is terrorizing them. Perhaps the humans doesn't need any stats, but goals or other kinds of desires?

    I like the original mechanic with coins, and I would playtest it first before I change it, if I were you. What I think you need to think through is HOW the game is going to be played and then form mechanics around it. Right now, it seems like you created a mechanic and formed play around it. I guess it works too. It depends on what you think is more important: the theme of the game or the gameplay. (I do either one in different projects, truth be told, because it depends on what I want with the game.) If you want to focus on the gameplay, then come up with a reason why someone would not like to flip all the coins in one scene, or what it means for the others - or the story - to flip the coins.
