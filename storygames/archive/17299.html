    \n I just realized why I like @Benhimself's communal roll system. If anything, it's a very "pure" way of doing it.

    A roll in Otherkind doesn't necessarily belong to any one of the characters, it just belongs to the fiction. Normally, only one character has a stake in the outcome, and thus that player is responsible for making that roll and assigning dice. In situations where more than one character has a stake in the outcome, however, they all get to decide how the dice are assigned. It's the same general rule ("define situation, stake-holders roll and assign dice") applied to either an individual stake-holder or multiple stake-holders. The trick is deciding who gets to choose which dice go where.

    To further generalize, both goals and dangers can simply be classified as outcomes. Normally, the single stake-holder will choose one outcome (the goal) and the GM will choose the other (the danger). With multiple stake-holders, we can each player choose one outcome, and the GM adds in one more. Interpreting each outcome as a goal or a danger is subjective.
