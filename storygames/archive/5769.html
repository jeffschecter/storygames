\n I am donning asbestos long-johns for this, but here goes:

Many of the current "problem" threads up now on topics like "unreadable games" and "games as poor books qua books" and "lack of criticism/playtesting" and even a dash of the "cult of personality" issues all seem to me to have a common underlying issue.

To wit: many indie/small press game designers (to my eyes) seem not to have done much work for established companies in the extant traditional adventure gaming industry before crafting their own products.

Let me unpack that a bit. This is not a value-judgement of good vs. bad on either side of the aisle here. I am not holding up the extant industry as a shining beacon of much of anything, but there's one major thing it has that the indie/small-press movement doesn't: age.

Elsethread, someone (please shout out who this was!) mentioned that the indie gaming movement's in its adolescence. I'd say that statement is spot-on.

The extant industry is in late adulthood, possibly even middle age. Many of the problems cited in these threads came up for the extant industry companies back in the late 70s, throughout the 80s, and even 90s. (One can debate how successfully they've met these challenges, of course.)

There are lessons to be learned from dealing with a more mature/established publishing structure. There are also lessons to be ignored. Engaging with the extant industry is a valuable experience.

At least, I've found it so in my game writing/design career, covering everything from how to work with editors and developers (as a freelancer and as a publisher), how to administer playtests via the internet, how to find art and artists, and so forth.

(I'd also say that my interactions in the indie/small press field are continuing to teach me a bunch of things about sharpening creative vision, personally running small playtests, methods of publishing, and methods of promotion.)

I'd frankly recommend everyone try to do some freelance work for an established company whose products you like. Heck, there are a number of indie/small press companies today that give a very trad/extant working experience.

It can be eye-opening.

    Posted By: Jonathan WaltonChad, is there a way that indie designers could gain experience in this way and yet make sure they won't be royally screwed?
    \nRoyally screwed is a relative term. I'll talk about my experience and leave the judgment to you.

    I've contributed to three Ars Magica books. I'll be clear up front: you don't do this for the money. All of the Ars books are written by people who are fans first, writers second. The pay right now for these books is 2.5 cents a word (up from 2 cents when I started).

    The first book I contributed to involved an open call. David Chart, the Ars Magica Line Editor, was looking to bring some new authors into his pool, so he put out a call for vis sources. These were extremely short (100-200 words) descriptions of sources of magical power. I thought it would be fun, so I wrote one a day for each of the 15 vis types. I submitted them, and David printed nine of them (I think) in the Covenants book. I got paid, but not much.

    After that, David asked me (and several other authors) to join his solicitation list. Four times a year, he sends out a request for proposals for the next book in the line. Unlike previous editors for the game, David has a specific book in mind, often with an outline (with wordcounts) already attached. The next book that I contributed to was an anthology book, where each chapter was written by a different author and riffed on a particular theme. (Ancient Magic, if anyone cares. I wrote the Grigori chapter.) David called for chapter proposals, I submitted one, and he accepted it. I think I wrote 3000 words for it.

    The last book was a more usual situation for the line, which is to say that David sent out a request for proposals, and a group of authors teamed up to write a proposal. I think we had eight authors on the Normandy book. I wrote about 9000 words for that one, including half of one covenant, all of another, and a chapter on Arthuriana in the 13th century.

    All told, I made a few hundred dollars. I wrote some material that was very specific to that particular game, so the fact that I don't own it doesn't particularly bother me, as I couldn't very well republish it anyway. I learned a lot about myself as a writer (externally imposed deadlines to that to you), I learned how to collaborate with other writers, I learned how to deal with playtest feedback (Ars books go through two rounds of external playtest), and I learned how to work with an editor (though David has a lighter hand than most). I wouldn't say I got royally screwed at all.

    Posted By: JDCorleyEdit: I know, I know. All right thinking people hate White Wolf with the blazing fury of 172 brain damaged suns. But still.
    \nJesus Christ, this made me laugh way harder than it deserved.

    As for what Paul said and Jonathan...

    Freelancing for a career isn't really the greatest idea, but then neither is publishing indie games. Go into it with your eyes open and knowing what it is you want, and you stand a good chance of getting it.

    For example, if what you want is to learn something of the internal processes of a company, then you'll want to go with a company that has such things. Like, White Wolf or Wizards or another company that isn't just one guy. If you're doing it to learn about things like editing and customer focus, then you'll want to make sure you go with a company where developers talk about that with freelancers. (Again, White Wolf devs have been good to me about this. Most other companies not so much.)

    If you want to get some publication credits, or make a little money and have some fun, then you're still good. Just make sure the company you're going for has a decent reputation and doesn't seem set to collapse into the ocean. If you want to make lots of money... well, you won't. If you want to make more money than you'd make doing your own game, that's going to depend on a lot of factors. For example, I made more money freelancing one year than Vincent and Matt Wilson made on Dogs or PTA, but the next year I made nothing while they kept making money. It isn't worth it to Sorenson to write for White Wolf, but it might be worth it for someone whose 25,000 word book only sells 50 copies.

    Mostly, though, I'd give the same advice that folks like Vincent give to folks looking to publish their own book -- don't put anything into it you can't walk away from, know why you want to do it and what your payoff is, and keep those in mind at all times.

    As to the whole idea of the OP... I'm kinda so so. There are things that I learned via freelancing for mainstream companies. Good lessons. I'm not sure all of them would directly apply to indie publishing. I'm not sure I couldn't have learned them another way. I think, for example, that the Indie community has done a pretty poor job of asking mainstream publishers for information without the whole thing turning into a mud slinging shit storm. Many of these folks are more than willing to talk, right up until the point where we start telling them they don't know what they're doing and are dinosaurs. I can't imagine why they'd get defensive at that, but they do.

    Here comes some crazytalk: the lack of ownership of your creative work is actually a pretty damned important experience to have. SRSLY.

    It teaches you a bunch of stuff:

    * Watching the "actual owner" protects/controls/expands it help you learn IP husbandry.

    * When it's going to end up someone else's material at the end of the day, they are more interested in you getting it right (for them) and give you more comments.

    * If the editor/line developer tells you to change something (and you can't immediately change their mind), bollocks to your vision -- you either change the text, have it changed for you, walk from the project, or get booted. This is how you learn to take criticism, editorial advice, and how to put it into action, IMAO. (I've changed, had changed, and walked from projects. I don't recall ever being booted from one, though.)

    * You learn to develop some detachment from your creative work.

    * You develop networks of associates doing writing, editing, art, layout, development, and publishing.

    These are all FANTASTIC things to take with you when you strike out on your own as an publisher.

    Because, look at Yours Truly: for the past several years, I've done almost no freelancing, but focused on my stuff, which I own lock-stock-barrel (Hm, I wanted to say lock-stick-barbell.) The lessons learned working for bigger publishers were extremely useful to my self-publishing efforts.

    And then look again: I'm freelancing for Evil Hat on Dresden Files right now. There's a certain back-and-forth you get freelancing that you don't get on self-generated stuff, or even collaborations.

    It's neat.

    Posted By: Jonathan WaltonAwesome. Next question: how? If I'm Chad or Jared or Brand or Paul, it's pretty easy to build on my industry rep and connections to get some sort of freelancing gig, whereas folks like me have to compete with everybody else who wants to write for Rifts or what have you.     \nWell, nobody's born with industry rep or connections. (Actually, I guess some people could be born with industry-- sorry games-lawyering).

    My first freelance sale was an article to Pyramid Online. They still take article submissions. They are NOT an SJG house organ; they'll publish an article on just about any game you can think of, and pay you for it.

    (Before that, I was making bigass posts on the GURPS mailling list and throwing up campaign info on my webpage. This was back in the days before blogging-qua-blogging.)

    Today, I'd participate in mailling lists for games you like, post thoughtful/new stuff on your blog, participate in playtests, write and submit a couple articles for paying venues, and keep a running CV of your work. Answer open calls. Make column or sourcebook pitches.

    If you're coming from the indie games end of the pool, it doesn't matter: all your playtesting and writing and publishing info (mainstream industry, indie industry, real world industry, etc.) should be on that CV. You can provide that (upon request) when answering open calls or making pitches.

    \n As I see it, what many rpgs need (not just indie rpgs BTW) is improved writing, explanation of procedure, and strong editorial input to point out what's being assumed and not included. Given the low budgets of self-publishing, we can't afford to hire editors or writers, so it's do-it-yourself. You can learn these skills from many places -- writing school, books, friends. Or you can make a barter trade with another small publisher and do each others editing for free.

    To preserve creativity one wants to maintain some distance from the convential approachs -- so I would encourage any indie designer to gain their presentation skills outside the RPG field! We already have plenty of exposure to convention.
