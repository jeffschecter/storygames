De-Geeking Gaming

James_Nostack
December 2006 edited December 2006 in Story Games
Based on Matt Snyder's "You are Not Your Games" thread, and my recent thread about "Outing."

If I'm understanding Matt correctly, he's expressing annoyance that there's a perceived link between gamers, and broader geek sub-culture. Obviously these two groups aren't necessarily connected, but in practice they often are. And this can complicate the lives of folks who like gaming, but who don't strongly identify with the geek sub-culture. (Matt, I trust you'll correct me if I've misread you; I'll edit this post accordingly, or if necessary delete the discussion.)

So: assuming that a hypothetical person might want to get the geek out of gaming, how would that work?

(To save everyone some time, there have been about six kajillion threads on RPGNet about this, and the frequent recommendations are:
* Don't feel like a geek, and you won't be one
* Create games that are all about casual gaming
* Create games with much simpler rules
* Create games that are less laser-sharky
* Things are fine as they are, we don't want the Norms stinking it up
* Etc. )

    • Easy to learn the basics or it doesn't take twenty minutes trying to explain how to play.

    • Include more items to spark creative imaginings or you don't have to make stuff up and play pretend.

    Posted By: BryanHere's a few design ideas that I've thought about:

    No prep.
    Easy to learn the basics or it doesn't take twenty minutes trying to explain how to play.
    Add a board or some physical representation of the imagined space. "What kind of game is it?" "Oh, it's a little like a board game?"
    Lose the term "role-playing." It's a story game.
    Somehow reduce or remove a central power figure or no GM.
    Include more items to spark creative imaginings or you don't have to make stuff up and play pretend.
    I generally agree with these for a pick-up and play game -- certainly the first three and the last one.

    Oh, and a big one: Shorter playing time, and no expectation of multiple session play.

    This goes with the last point -- one of the things which most RPGs (including indie ones) are weak on is introductory scenarios. These are vital for a pick-up and play game. Even if there isn't a GM and the players can create story on the fly, asking the players to invent characters and a plot out of whole cloth is offputting to most people.

    Also, whatever the physical components are, they need to be easy to roll out and take back. D&D has some nice components, for example, but a battlemat is a real pain to roll out and draw on. There's been some products to lay out as tiles instead, I think, which would be a definite improvement.

    I think the why question is important, since it changes a lot of what you'll look for. For example, if I want a game to play with my non-geeky friends, removing the GM isn't necessarily going to help. At least, there's a place to have a knowledgeable moderator or central figure, since I'll be playing. If I want a game I can give to my non-geeky teenage cousin to play, then removing the GM may help.

    As a small plug: Last week, some gamer friends showed up at my place, but the GM for the game we had been planning to play cancelled at the last minute. I literally took Dead of Night out of my jacket pocket, to use. I printed out and handed them sheets, and read them the intro to one of the scenarios. We played it out in around 2 hours.

  • Josh Roby
    December 2006
    Posted By: BryanAre character sheets a "fetish" to avoid?
    Bad, stupid, non-functional character sheets are a fetish to avoid. Character sheets that needlessly list irrelevant stuff (eye color) and leave off important stuff (motivations) and character sheets that present information in poor ways (Nobilis has you to check off which code of beliefs you ascribe to) need to be jettisoned out the window. The assumption that players will know what a character sheet is for needs to go.

    However, well-designed and functional character sheets are good things. I like to think of these as character playmats to reinforce the interactive, tactile use that I'm getting at. In fact I suspect that character playmats could handily replace the board in new-to-roleplaying folks, and lend them an air of familiarity and comfort.

      This will come off as pedantic, but it may spark something for some.

      Bryon wrote: "Lose the term "role-playing." It's a story game."

      I'll say this: all this Interminable debate of late about what is and isn't an "RPG" is having my head spin for no good reason.

      In part because it's tied to the geek factor. It's all a matter of "who's in and who's out". It's all, in my view, part and parcel of that "I'm a wounded outsider, so I'm going to make other people outsiders..." which, of course, only keeps the wounded fellow an outsider.

      It's part of the culture of arcana, special words and knowledge and so on that is part of the geek culture. Those "mundanes" over there don't understand us -- but that's okay, because we're calling them "mundandes" and we know what we mean.

      I bring all this up to say -- calling them "story-games" is just another way of confounding the issue. It's a new noun that someone will confront and won't know what to make of it.

      Follow this: Someone says,"I've got this new game, Trivial Pursuit."

      "Oh, really," says the second person, "What's it like?" Or "What do you do?" Or "What happens?"

      See, no one is going to describe it by what it is. It's going to be defined by what you do.

      Now for the pedantic part...

      I suggest not calling them Story Games.

      I would put on the cover. "A Game for Making Stories"

      Or...

      A Game
      For Making Stories

      See, simple. What is it? It's a Game. What do you do/what happens/how do you play?"

      "Well, you make stories."

      To my ear it's a step away from the arcana of geekdom. It says, it's just a game. (Which it is.)

      It says, "It's about making stories" (which people love to do).

      Christopher

      I think this thead and my fizzled one are titled in a way that comes off antagonistic and causes strife. De-geeking has connotations of cleansing - the similarity to denazification made me nauseous when it struck me.

      Can I suggest a much healthier way to look at this side of the argument? Decoupling. I am interested in the extent to which it is possible to decouple the activity of gaming (the imagining, the stories/adventures, the social creative thing) from the other elements typically hitched to it. This doesn't mean that these elements should be taken away from gaming, just that they are free to accompany it or to not do so.

      Now, "decoupling gaming" still is a big issue - really a whole bunch of big issues - but I hope by framing the broad conceit better we can discuss it a bit more amicably.
