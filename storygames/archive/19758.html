\n So since the original Analysis of Adventure Writing thread asked to have discussion moved elsewhere, I guess I'll go ahead and start one.

While some games are clearly designed with a specific "adventure format" in mind, I'm not at all convinced that the categories are as strongly tied to game as the original post seems to posit. Yes, Fiasco is designed to produce a collaborative story-telling experience. On the other hand, Deadlands can easily be run as sequential play, fish tank, or sandbox. Many traditional games are surprisingly silent on how to structure a game.

I am also curious if anyone has an example of a bang driven game, as that isn't a term I've heard before.

    \n The term "Bangs" comes from Ron Edwards's Sorcerer. It's a very effective technique that's used by a lot of "story gamers" or games. Something like Monsterhearts comes to mind, although it isn't particularly explicit in this regard (and Apocalypse World is similar).

    I'm not sure I agree with Rickard's strict definitions, however - like you say, they are all techniques which might be applied in different ways.

    If I was running a "bang-driven game", for instance, I would almost certainly also set up a relationship map (essentially what Rickard calls a "fish tank"), and it's hard to say which "type of game" is going on.

    There's a definitely a difference between "sequential play" and a GM using Bangs, relationship maps, and similar tools, however.

    But that can shift at any time during play, from scene to scene. My understanding of Rickard's analysis so far is that he's looking strictly at the texts (what does this book tell you to do in play?), not trying to explain every possible type of game which takes place using that system.

    \n That's true. I discovered how I myself sometimes used sequential play while playing a fish tank. I think we apply different techniques during the same session without thinking of it, and that's something I think is important to realize. (We know so little of our hobby.)

    So we got:
    - models (ex. sandbox)
    - techniques (ex. sandbox)
    - a mixture of models and techniques (ex. running sequential play with collaborative storytelling)
    - a mixture of different models (ex. sequential play within a sandbox)
    - a shift of different techniques (ex. shifting between bang driven and fish tank)

    No wonder it's hard to talk about this. It's bound for misunderstandings. One person talks about the technique ("Deadlands can easily be run as sequential play, fish tank, or sandbox.") and another about the model (me categorizing it as a sandbox based on the text in the book).

    \n How do you make a good movie?

    That what the original thread seems to boil down to except the focus is on roleplaying games.

    Movies need a lot of technical expertise in order to be made, yet you can have a perfect directory, perfect cast, perfect staff that does everything perfect technically but the movie still sucks because the story is uninteresting to the audience. And all the time there are good movies that are made that break the "rules" of good movies.

    Now mastering the technical details is important to making a good movie. Bad cinematography can be very distracting in a otherwise good movie with good acting. I am sure people can think of lot of other technical mistake that hurt a otherwise good movie.

    But in the end it is a matter of luck whether good technique, story, and acting combine into something that sells to an audience. Especially when it comes to great movies. This makes for a situation where generalization about good movies is not really possible or useful. The best you can to list out what could use in making a movie, why you used it, and how it worked out for you.

    Then when it comes to a new movies when you chose how to make it, you go in knowing why you are using what you are using. Rather than doing things just because that how things are done.

    So what this has to do with roleplaying games?

    Because RPGs have a similar problem. Ultimately what they are about is presenting an interesting experience to the players. The game is just a tool to this end. An important tool, but just a tool. Specific tools work well for specific people and not for others. Like movies the situation is such that generalization is not possible or useful. The best you can to is talk about specific techniques and what happened when you used them.

    Then with that information a group can decide how to approach a campaign.
