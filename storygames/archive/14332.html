    \n The roots for this are deep!

    A dungeon is a series of branching and escalating dangers, after all. Escalating dangers are the core of all adventure fiction. We're drawing on deep deep wells here.

    In terms of immediate predecessors, Danger Patrol owes a lot to Talislanta (the first version of DP was made in 1998, which was also when we started seriously working on Tal 4). I know Apocalypse World has a strong link to Tal, too. It was the partial-success mechanic of Tal that provided the "yes, but" outcome that we loved so much. Yes, you do get the idol, but you set off the giant rolling rock trap!

    Burning Wheel was also an influence on games in this style, with the way it handles failure -- as an escalation and trouble to respond to instead of "that didn't work." That concept is made even more explicit in Mouse Guard, with its option to turn a failure into success + a condition.

    Oh, and I should also mention InSpectres. The way it handles partial success, failure, and mounting Stress was a big influence on lots of designers (I think Vincent lists it as an influence on Poison'd).

    There are many more! Vampire, Ars Magica, Heroquest, Unknown Armies, D&D, T&T -- once you start unraveling sources, it gets complicated fast.

    \n I suspect you're being too modest, but am having trouble articulating why I find it just so awesome.

    At the very least it's a way of visualizing information that made me say, "I get it" and by "I get it" I don't just mean the current situation in a given game, but a visual language of story! (Danger: Holden's dad will be very angry. Danger: Holden needs a place to stay.)

    But maybe it's more! A lot more! Maybe it's also a way of formalizing bang-oriented GMitude to the point where ordinary people can do it. Maybe ... it could be made GM-less. Maybe even a computer could do it. (When Elizabeth talked about making Echo Bazaar more story now I had no idea what she meant, but if you talk about it in terms of dangers that reproduce, suddenly it seems possible.)

    You might have noticed I'm giddy.
