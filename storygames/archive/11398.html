    \n Determine behaviors that you want to occur and reward them XP in proportion to how badly you'd like to see that thing happen.

    For example, you'd like to see someone betray their friends, or you'd like to see someone make peace with their most hated enemy to accomplish a greater good. Offer your players XP to do that.

    Also, check out sweet20: http://files.crngames.com/cc/sweet20/experience.html

    \n I've wanted to run a Werewolf game for a long time, and I finally got some people who have an interest in it. But I, too, dislike the WoD XP system. So just last night, I wrote down a system we'll use that mixes some points from Primetime Adventures and D&D 4E.

    You generally don't have a hard time with "chronicle" meaning "campaign," or "chapter" meaning "session," but WoD's got this third term, "story," for a short number of chapters (maybe 1, maybe 10) with a single major plotline. I formalized this a bit: our chronicle might have as many stories as the group wants to play, but each story will last for six chapters. At the start of each story, you assign a 3, two 2's, and three 1's to those six chapters. The chapter you assign a 3 to centers on your character. In a 2, you play a secondary character. In a 1, you play a supporting role. At the end of the chapter, you get that much XP.

    Kinda skimpy, though, right? Well, in addition, we've got a pile of index cards. Anybody can add an index card to the pile. Each one has a mission on it, like, "Steal the fetish." You can label it with the name of a PC, or with "Pack." When they fulfill that mission, you get 1 XP. If it has a PC's name, that PC gets 1 XP. If it says "Pack," everyone gets 1 XP.

    So, in your spotlight chapter, if you accomplish two missions, you'll have 5 XP. If you play a bit role and your pack accomplishes one thing, you'll get 2 XP.

    I'll find out if it actually works starting next week, assuming we can find one more player.

    \n(long story was in the offering, but it gets convoluted and not to the point).

    Anyway. The hardest thing to make happen has been what feels to me like immersion. Having the players try to get in the heads of their characters, and make the characters act on their motivations. Like the character who is "fearful" and then ignores dangers or jumps into fights. Or the player who says their character is curious and investigative, then sits back and just listens the whole time and doesn't make the character _do_ anything.

    \n Sounds like well chosen Keys are exactly what you want, although I'm unsure about the "story key". It sounds slightly counter to your stated intent.

    Keys will reward the characters for doing what they said their character will do ("playing in character") in a concrete way, but the story Key will reward them for being heroic even if they are playing a cowardly (or otherwise unheroic) character.

    If you think that rewarding heroism is equally (or more) important to rewarding "playing in character" then your plan looks good to me.

    Edit: Oh yeah. You might want to keep an eye on how quickly your players are advancing. My own experience and intuition tells me that a clever and driven player will probably be able to hit a key quicker than the Storyteller system would usually hand out XP. Personally I wouldn't worry, since I think Storyteller is a little stingy as written, but you might want to keep it in mind.

    \n My favourite method of handing out XP is quest completion, and it was almost implicitly the method intended for AD&D 2e based on all the adventures written for it. Players have a story goal that becomes fairly clear early on into the session or arc, and once they complete it they get a sizeable reward. What they do in between is up to them. It's a fairly traditional method of handing out XP, but it's not the explicit Y XP for killing Z monster system.

    I guess what that does is changes XP from a task resolution system (1XP for good roleplaying) to conflict resolution (10XP for completing the story). I think it works quite seamlessly with almost any system that makes use of XP.
