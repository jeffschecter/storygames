\n I'm working on a system which handles the outcomes of conflicts by allocating penalty or bonus dice based on successes. From what I've heard about Sorcerer, it sounds like that's exactly how it works in that game. I'm kind of stuck at this point in my design, so I decided to come here and ask a few questions:

Can you explain to me a few things about Sorcerer?

1. How do you decide how much of the "damage" is temporary and how much is lasting?
2. How long does "lasting" damage last?
3. Is there any lasting damage from social conflicts, or other non-physical conflicts? Or do you just get bonus dice on one followup roll?

I'll be extra happy if you have some insight into _why_ the answers to those questions are what they are, and how it affects play, but that's, like, extra credit or something.

    \n Hi Paul,

    1. There's a damage chart for that, where for example damage from fists or bludgeoning weapons do X successes temporary plus 1 lasting damage.
    2. All lasting damage lasts until the end of the entire conflict (whereas temporary only goes into the following 'round'). After the conflict ends, lasting damage is halved, and that rest stays with you until you do something about it (medical help, whatever).
    3. No, but bonus dice yes. Unless of course you have some weird kind of demonic power that does "social damage", that would be possible. But that's colour I suppose, it still would be either non-lethal or lethal special damage according to the rules.

    Remember that a point of damage (lasting or not) is part of Sorcerer's currency and subtracts from any roll.

    \n Just to refine what Per said.

    1) For physical damage there's a chart that converts Victories on the die roll into Temporary and Lasting penalties depending on the type of weapon used. These penalties affect ALL your scores and thus ALL interactions.

    2) Temporary penalties only affect your next action. Lasting affect all actions and are halved at the end of a scene.

    3) There are no such penalties for social conflict. In 99% of cases just rolling the victories as a bonus into another roll is sufficient. However, it would not be outside the scope of the rules to have "lasting" penalties that affect a *specific* type of interaction. For example, let's say there's a guy who rough ups his girlfriend. So, you come and intimidate him about staying away from her and score two victories. It wouldn't be unreasonable to apply a two-die penalty to the boyfriend for EVERY interaction he has with the girlfriend. But again, that is pretty rare. This is, in fact, how Binding rituals work.

    \n Actually, the rules don't quite say. Or rather, they say once you have penalties "greater than 2xStamina" your PC has "bones exposed, hemorrhaging, guts hanging out, needs intensive care."

    The implication is that your guy is dying -- but not dead -- on the cusp of that point within fiction where a character could live or die.

    What determines death or not? Two broad things:
    1) the fiction of the circumstances around the scene (is intensive care available; could a demon get someone to intensive care; and so on....)
    2) where the character is in relation to resolving the Kicker, cause that's what the game is all about.

    The group must draw the line on a case by case basis, judged within the parameters of those two conditions.

    For a big-ass explication on this, Ron recently made a really cool post on the matter.

    Posted By: Paul T.OK, my interest in Sorcerer is climbing very rapidly.
    \nClimbed enough to get a copy of the rules for yourself...?

    1) Okay. Let's be clear. It's not "undefined." At 2 X Stamina a character is DYING and will die unless tended to with intensive care. So, does the fiction allow this to happen? Does any character take action to make this happen? The Players at the table make decisions about this. But it's not willy-nilly handwaving. When that threshold is reached, decisions must be made NOW.

    2) You can play Sorcerer with any-sided die you want. The rules discuss this, actually. Basically, the larger the die, the larger the odds are to have more decisive victories. d20s will produce more victories in a roll, while d4s will produce a closer range of results, and thus, less emphatic victories. d10s are the default, because those the dice Ron uses, and uses as examples in the book.

    3) Sorcerer & Sword allows PCs who do not have bound Demons, so yes, it is possible by the rules. However, significantly, Sorcerer & Sword expands the rules one can use for sorcery, in introducing Pacts with Demons. Pacts are "deals" with Demons, while a Binding is like a "relationship" with a Demon. The significance lies in the fact that if a PC decides he needs to get access to more power it is available -- through Binding and Pacts -- and Pacts make getting power less of a long term commitment. Remember that dealing with Demons is a stick and carrot a PC can grab for because it makes getting things done easier. But a Sorcerer might end up banishing his own demon, or having it banished by another PC, in which case he'd have to contact, summon and bind a new demon. Or not. The Player, of course, makes the choice of how far the PC will go, and by what means.

    I'm not sure exactly what you mean by defining Demons as "mundane/real." I've come up with 6 things you might mean, so could you clarify?

    4) There's no problem as far as I know. But it's one of those things that is experienced best in play.

    The key is that the Player "receives" the information from the die roll ("He did beat my Will!" or "He didn't beat my Will!") and then makes a decision on how to proceed on behalf of the PC.

    Last weekend I was playing Sorcerer & Sword. My guy was trying to kill another PC. The PC I'm trying to kill shouts, "I'm trying to help you save your fiance!" He made Will roll against my Will. He won. If I continued, I'd be attack with his successes tacked onto his next rool.

    I have a choice. I either press on. Or don't.

    I chose not to press on, and the other Player's PC and my PC joined forces.

    Ron and I were talking about this a couple of weeks ago and he pointed out this happens a lot. Players are given, through the color added which is attached the die roll, a bit of information that gives them pause. Sometimes they take the "hit" and change their course of action (as in the example above), and sometimes they press on with the odds not worse against them.

    It seems like it would lead to problems. But it doesn't. It just dumps a choice on the Player, informed by the details of the fiction. It works fine.

    \n I'm not sure I understand what you mean by "mundane demons and sorcerers", Mikael, but there's certainly scope in Sorcerer to colour what demons and sorcerers are just the way you want and still jam on the game's big metaphor of dysfunctional relationships.     \n I think the whole mundane vs. supernatural thing is muddying the waters. So let's put that aside for a minute. To be perfectly clear, everything I'm about to say applies in full to the 100% supernatural context of Sorcerer. Slavering fanged monsters chained in my basement, rune covered rings that let me turn invisible, a misty serpent thing that whispers the secrets of the universe in my ear.

    Okay given ALL THAT. The words "hidden world" do not apply to Sorcerer. There is no "hidden world." Sorcerer's do not "see reality for what it is" or anything like that. Sorcerer takes place in the here and now world as you and I understand it.

    What that means in practice:

    There are no Sorcerer/Demon only agendas. Anything a Sorcerer would be "up to" is anything you and I would be up to. I once played a Sorcerer game set in post-Katrina New Orleans. What the PCs cared about were things like family safety, preservation of property, establishing legacy. The content was supernatural as all get out but what was "at stake" in story terms was everyday ordinary family politics in the wake of a disaster.

    Another important aspect is moral accountability. Demons, even when acting supposedly on their own, are an extension of the Sorcerer. A Sorcerer can not escape moral accountability because his "demon did it." The demon is only here, because he WILLED it to be here. In real world terms he can not escape the consequences of the real world by shunting responsibility on to the demon. If the police can find even a shred of evidence linking the Sorcerer to a crime (even one that the forensics team can't quite explain) they are coming after the Sorcerer.

    This is why playing a "mundane" makes almost no sense. As soon as a character had any kind of *meaningful* interaction with a demon that was not simply shunted off by denial their Lore score would become one. Congratulations they are a Sorcerer in game terms. No special training, indoctrination or being "let in on the hidden world." There is no "hidden world" Sorcerers are not in on a special party no one else is invited too. They're just arrogant strong willed people, who wanted something so badly, they found a way (quite likely of their own devising) to bring this *thing* that does not belong here to help them out.

    Does that help?

    Jesse

    \n Cross posted with Jesse (and I agree with what he wrote -- especially about mundane vs. supernatural muddying things....

    Here's a thread where Ron lays out a couple of posts on this matter.

    It thought these quotes from the thread do a good job of laying it out:

    More profoundly, not only are there not several ways sorcery works, there is no actual way that sorcery "works in the setting." In fact, it's fundamental to the concept of the game that there is no magic, and that demons do not exist. That's why Sorcerer is not about hidden secrets about the universe that only a few people know; it's about the few people who have defied the universe they live in. That's also why types and styles of sorcery, in a given setting, do not reflect setting but rather characters.

    and this...

    In Sorcerer, belief doesn't mean shit. Reality, whatever it may be, cares nothing for human belief. Such belief is limited to within the human experience and can only "talk to itself."

    So where do demons come from? The answer is, they don't. There aren't demons. They don't exist. Reality doesn't permit demons to exist. Demons are not in the setting, not in another dimension, not in Hell, and not in any sort of alternate or even psychological space.

    Demons don't exist.

    So that means that not only do demons not exist, they aren't created either. You can't explain them as psi-manifestations or psychological effects on reality either, because all of that is just more "this is why they exist" explanations. You have to throw out any and all justification of demons' presence, at the level of understanding the setting.

    That's what I mean by defying the universe to be a sorcerer. You don't just whip into existence some sort of "thing" which you happen to believe in. That's boring, just more magical thinking. Magical thinking is expressly stupid and meaningless in Sorcerer.

    No, to get a demon and Bind it, in Sorcerer, means the character stepped fully outside any and all explanations of any kind. The character had to break the universe to do it. No one can explain it, most especially anything resembling "order" or "purpose" in the universe, if indeed there is such a thing, and equally, anything like "the power lies within you" as well.

    Sorcerer settings are full of rationalizations of sorcery, but that's all Lore is. I want to emphasize that this sentence applies fully to faith-based and emotion-based explanations too.

    I wrote:

    You can play Sorcerer with any-sided die you want. The rules discuss this, actually. Basically, the larger the die, the larger the odds are to have more decisive victories. d20s will produce more victories in a roll, while d4s will produce a closer range of results, and thus, less emphatic victories. d10s are the default, because those the dice Ron uses, and uses as examples in the book.

    So, d6's will not provide as many decisive victories as d10s. (In other words, there will be more ties among the dice, and fewer Victory dice gained and rolled into the next roll.) When someone succeeds in a conflict using d6's, they succeed with fewer Victories and so have fewer bonus to carry over into the next roll than if they were rolling d10's.

    Does that work for you?

    \n Steven,

    I'm on a Dresden Files love/hate kick so all is said with much apologies to Jim Butcher. The problem when discussing this "demons don't exist" issue or "what does lore mean" issue is that it causes people to do an unproductive two-step. One shifts between a discussion about narrative devices to be used and discarded as a matter of artistic choice and trying to construct a mental "model" of the game world.

    One of the things I don't like about the Dresden Files is that Dresden is WAY too chatty in his head. In particular he's very chatty about how magic "works." So I could easily see a fan crawling through all the novels to date and pulling out the rules for magic that Dresden muses about and proclaiming that "In the Dresdenverse this is how magic works." That fan then goes out and gives some other fan's work of fanfic a hard time because he "doesn't understand how magic works in the Dresdenverse." Or he role-plays with his friends in the "Dresdenverse" and one of his players pro-offers up an idea but he shoots it down on the basis that, "No, that's not how magic works in the Dresdenverse."

    And all of that kind of thinking doesn't apply to Sorcerer. Sorcerer doesn't model anything. It lays down a set of narrative concepts for addressing a certain range of issues. So, yes, in your created fiction you might very well have a master imparting the principles of sorcery onto a student as the basis for your Lore. Hell, we might even as a group go ahead and spell those principles out. But all we're really saying is, "This is how it 'works' right up until it doesn't" No one is allowed to deny someone else's creative input on that basis.

    For example, we might agree that all Sorcery has to involve a family heirloom. But then three or four sessions in someone starts describing a ritual and another player says, "What's your family heirloom?" And the player performing the ritual says, "That's just it, I don't have one." The only appropriate response from the group is a shocked gasp exactly as would happen from an audience watching such a story. No one can go, "But we agreed that Sorcery requires a family heirloom. That's how magic in this setting *works*"

    Make sense?

    Jesse
