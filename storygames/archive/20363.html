    \n I agree with WarriorMonk's assessment, and I *have* played lots of 2E and 4E as well as 3.X.

    Now, 5E gets *less* OSR-ish in the sense of deadliness as you level up. PCs are pretty fragile for the first few levels but it gets harder for them to outright die after that. There is still a sense of fragility, though: I inflicted serious pain on a party of 3 14th-level PCs with 3 CR 8 monsters simply through luck and because (due to fictional positioning) one of them was separated from the other two at the start of the fight. This general sense that PCs aren't immortal even at high levels has several sources:

    1) Bounded Accuracy: low-level monsters can still hit high-level PCs more than 5% of the time\u2014even if it's only 20% of the time, that still changes things radically from the situation in high-level 3E or even 2E, where Orcs become a joke. In 5E sufficiently large numbers of low-level foes can indeed cause problems even to a big group of PCs, though it's rare that you'd actually want players to fight, say, 100 Orcs. What's nicer, though, as I alluded to above, is that *mid-level* monsters can still be a challenge for *high-level* characters.

    2) The way saving throws work. A lot of people on Big Purple complain about this, but I think it's brilliant: by default, without spending Feats or ability score increases or getting a magic item or whatever, 4/6 of a character's saves will not improve throughout the entire course of the game. This means that it's always worthwhile to try to avoid even letting the enemy cast their spell or use their ability in the first place: in other words, 5E supports "combat as war" where 3E and 4E aimed for "combat as sport."

    This is, incidentally, why everyone complains about the CR system being so wonky. I don't necessarily disagree that it could have been a little more smoothly-implemented, but fundamentally, most of the complaints about it stem from misunderstanding the function of the system.

    3) The relative caster-nerf. I say relative because full casters are still awesome (and, unlike in 3rd edition, the semi-casters [Paladins and Rangers] and even 1/3 casters [Eldritch Knights, Way of the Four Elements Monks, and Arcane Tricksters] actually get serious utility out of their spells*). But, even full casters get significantly fewer high-level slots per day than they did in the past, which is notable\u2014again, I didn't find it all that hard to challenge a 14th-level party as long as I kept the pressure up: they could get out of a jam or two by blowing their big spells, but after that they had to be pretty conservative. Also, the concentration rules mean that casters cannot just spend 3 rounds outside the door of the big bad's cave turning the PCs into demigods via buff spells. There are good buffs, to be sure, but again, the PCs have to actually be strategic about their use.

    Now, I do have some serious criticism of the para-text, the advice for using the rules, particularly in the DMG. 5E really is trying to be all things to all people, and thus shies away from outright telling readers a lot of the stuff I'm saying here\u2014things that, I think, are important for gamers trying to get the most out of it to understand.

    I'd also like to note that, although it is OSR-supporting in many ways, it does also have story-game elements, in the BIFTs and Inspiration. How much or how little a particular group plays those up actually does little to determine how gritty the game will feel\u2014the bonus from Inspiration is small enough that it's rarely pivotal. Rather, to me it's simply a question of whether the players bother to portray their characters as interesting people or not.

    Matt

    *In 3rd edition, many of the "semi" caster classes felt a little weak because their spells felt like "too little, too late" to really compete with the godlike power of the full casters.

    \n Different people see 'OSR' as different things so I may not be your target demographic. However, since I am the self-proclaimed King of Grognards (80-90% of what I play regularly came out before 1990) I personally think I should be in there somewhere.

    From the instant 5th was announced I was excited beyond containment and hanging on every developer word. This is mostly because as central as BECMI/1st/2nd was for us, we felt abandoned by 3rd and 4th.

    The initial promises were what I considered the best possible compromises we could expect from a major commercial release...IF they managed to follow through. About the time development on it changed I started getting scared and on the WotC forums even predicted it was going to switch to edition 3.75 by launch. By the time development ended I was almost totally negative about its chances.

    After giving it some time I've begun to play it a bit to give it a full chance. I will say that we don't play it very much, so I'm far from expertly versed in it. That being said I think it's FAR better than I feared, but it's also very close to what I thought it would be. It's just that it's also a fairly good game despite that.

    I am disappointed that it's not closer to Basic/1st/2nd in my opinion. I tell people now that it's reminiscent of the older games...it alludes to them, gives a nod to them, but does so within an almost entirely modern framework. I would say 3.75 is the closest edition numbering for it. It's 3rd edition, with a modern independent hack overlay, as it would be played by people who preferred the old school 'roleplaying, not rollplaying' style.

    I am disappointed that most of its professed 'modularity' is missing, or broken. We've tried to swap various things in and out almost entirely without success. It's really meant to play more or less as is. If you're going to try and hack it closer to what you prefer you have limited options and it gives the game a chaotic, unfinished feeling...at least without going beyond the included options and making it a full homebrew.

    With that said I think there's a lot of really good things, or least good attempts. I love the new save system, approve of bounded accuracy, think some of the indie-inspired options have merit (especially for newer players), love the streamlining and simplification, etc. I think it plays well as the system it is, just like 3rd and 4th did. Good games in their own rights...just not what I generally mean by D&D.

    I'm in no way concerned by it, but neither do I feel much like playing it (other than as a forced endeavor to ensure giving it a fair try). We still play 3.5 regularly (mostly for our kids that grew up on it), but otherwise would ALWAYS choose BECMI or 1st/2nd. Well, actually we play our own version, but barring that it'd be those.

    It's a pretty cool game, but I see it as it's own game, and not a continuation of the D&D lineage (which for us stopped with 2nd). However, i'm ENORMOUSLY biased as D&D was probably the single most impacting thing in my life (outside of like, marriage, child, and death...and it even gives those a serious run).

    When 2nd first came out we were momentarily skeptical, then fell into their mindset and decided it was a rational evolution for the most part.     Anyway, because we were so used to AD&D we essentially treated 2nd like a cleaned up, more organized evolution of it, instead of the very different game that it really was.     It was also 1992-1996 that adulthood caught up with us and changed our gaming paradigms. The groups fragmented somewhat, other things became important, and we found a LOT of entirely different games we all wanted to try out. I think that, more than the editions themselves, are what separates 'old school' from new for me. So many of the old greats were either abandoned or drastically changed around that time that it seemed to represent a fundamental shift in gaming ideology. Without question 3rd is a fully 'modern' game to us (I suppose shifting the current indie craze into the 'post-modern' category).     \n The differences between 1e & 2e fall into two categories: obvious but minor, and subtle but serious.

    Obviously it's presented more as a finished work, and less as the compiled notes of a rambling mad genius. Classes are missing/changed, or would appear in later works in different forms (like kits). Also the classes are now shoehorned into groups around the 'core 4'. Minor tweaks to races (and 1/2 orc missing). Many things are rates or options instead of a chart/table (THAC0, NWP, thief skills, priest spheres/wizard schools, etc). Psionics finally playable (if just barely). Experience awards altered. Level limits/race restrictions eased. Surprise & inititative changed. Weapon vs armor tables replaced with type vs armor. No segments. Some pretty significant monster changes (demons/devils, dragons, etc). Attribute ranges broadened (and gender differences abolished). Loss of unique spell lists. I could go on, but these are some of the biggies.

    Then there are a host of other things that may not be picked up on, or subtle effects from the obvious changes, that actually have major implications.

    By changing from tables to rates on things (like thief skills) you're leaving 'simulation' and moving more towards 'gaming'...specifically towards build options, min/maxing, power gaming, etc.

    Even though classes are grouped by 'core 4', abandoning the subclass concept shifts us towards the idea of all classes (and levels) being unique and equal. This is a gamists specificity mindset, as opposed to simulationists general representation one.

    By changing from rules to options on so many things (weapon type vs armor, unlimited advancement, etc) you're reinforcing 'building', and thereby the gamist preference. The same is sometimes true of the inverse, changing from option to rule (NWP for instance). It also continues the shift from simulationist, but this time towards narrativism.

    Kits open the door to prestige classes and class spam, while devaluing 'class'. This is further reinforced with the loss of level titles.

    Loss of unique casters/spell lists drastically alters the feel of magic, as well as setting the stage (but confusing) power sources.

    Many of the changes create much stronger characters. This both decreases lethality and begins the power inflation.

    Experience award changes shift away from treasure, and towards combat. However, they also include stronger support for other awards (like roleplaying), which kicks off much of the current indie concepts tying roleplaying to mechanical aspects. Either way, the core idea of 'smart play' is largely abandoned.

    Easing of reality rules (and dark themes) moves us towards political correctness, and also power inflation and precious snowflakeism. For instance, penalties for low ability scores are relaxed, and no longer limit choices as much.

    Considerable loss of wargaming, kingdom level focus, and anything concerning the full mythic arc of a hero (like with the failure to include immortality rules from BECMI). Interestingly this is somewhat resurrected with the Birthright setting, and Greyhawk Wars.

    Changes in things like NWP, and increased rules specificity lead us out of roleplaying and into rollplaying.

    Again, I could go on and on, but these are the types of things I'm talking about.

    So for those of us coming from 1st and basic we didn't notice most of those changes, and continued trying to play the way we had, just with things cleaned up. In doing so we missed the actual game...which may not be a bad thing, depending on preferences.

    Looking back now I see 2nd as a gateway game...the transition from old-school to modern. It's literally the 2 between 1 and 3. If I had to rule one way or the other now, I'd say it's modern. However, at the time we neither saw that, nor played it that way, allowing it to remain old-school in practice (if not theory). Besides that, there's more than enough elements from AD&D/basic for it to be worthy of the 'old-school' nod.

    If it isn't logical (at least to some degree) I can't suspend disbelief to enjoy it. That's why I'm a...realism gamer (trying to avoid the laden term simulationist here). Think of it this way:

    In our world let's say someone dreamed of becoming the greatest woodworker of all time. First they'd need natural inclination, sure, but then it's all about training, experience and time. You have to spend quite a while (8-24 years) as a neophyte/novice before you even know what you want, and you're even ready to apprentice in your field. Then you have to spend years (historically 3-7) picking up the basics as an apprentice. After that you're a journeyman working for someone else, maybe forever, or if you have the drive and talent you might create a masterwork and become a master craftsperson. This could take any amount of time, and was dependent on finance and raw talent more than a set period. Some rare few might eventually rise to the status of Grandmaster after years/decades...the acknowledged best of their field (often only awarded posthumously).

    So the breakdown of time spent is: long, medium, short-long (depending on talent/money), very long, forever - if you're the one.

    If you're less tradesy you can apply that to academics:

    Long time in primary/secondary school, medium time for undergrad, short-long for grad, very long for doctorate, and then world reknown if you're the one.

    It's only reasonable to treat character leveling somewhat the same way. So in our version of D&D there are 36 levels, broken into 6 tiers (novice, apprentice, journeyman*, expert*, master, grandmaster[i.e. immortal]) of 6 levels each. Experience and requirements to level vary by tier**.

    * We split Journeyman and Expert both to get 6 tiers (because everything is 6 in our game) and to differentiate between someone on their own for the first time, and someone who is a master of their craft (an important, but missed distinction in previous games and our own reality actually).

    **In BECMI it's split by number of levels: B-3 E-11, C-11, M-11, I-0 (in reality there's a huge time investment far exceeding a mere 11 levels, but it varies by class). However level gain is also on an increasing experience requirement, though experience awards likewise increase. The result is a short, long, longer, much longer, very long breakdown (or short, long, shorter, much shorter, very long where experience awarded exceeds the rate required to level) that defies the previously established logic. We could have merely adapted the level spread (to say, 5-2-3-5-8-13) but because the game changes mechanically by level, not by tier, it caused problems...not to mention being hard on ocd.

    Now some people don't have any interest in the first tier. They want to start at the commonly established apprenticeship, not while still a 'classless' novice. No worries, go ahead. For us though, we think the story (and the mythic form) is better served when we experience what leads us to our class apprenticeships and provides us our basic background skills.

    Conan the Barbarian (original movie) doesn't start with him as a competent swordsman...it starts with him as a boy, becoming huge through hard labor, then apprenticing, then proving his worth, then striking out on his own, etc. It follows the form. Granted, it condenses the crap out of the first few tiers, but then that's Hollywood for ya.

    This lets us be in an epic story, in a fantastical and epic setting, but spend considerably longer at the low-end of the power/ability spectrum...as a common man. It makes us more completely experience and earn what we may eventually attain, thereby appreciating it more and being a full story.

      If we were at the same table (which doesn't work between editions, I know), obviously your character would do better at those aspects as a result of raw probability, but knowing I had limited probability I would be forced to 'play smarter', which would likely end up giving at least an equal result. Obviously if we were both playing that same style you would end up mechanically superior, at which point it comes down to intent (game or story).

      So you have a 'build' (at least more of one), and I have a 'character in a story'. \n
