    I'm with Daniel - my question is, what's the point of the authors photo, anyway? Is there any beyond the effort to celebratize a name? Honest question.

    \n
    \n

    It's because people want to make a connection with a creator. In its most perverse form, you want to own that person's creativity. It's done in the art world all the time and it's kind of ugly.

    \n

    But less perversely, we're built as humans to see faces. Having someone recognize your face is an important part of publicity in a very direct way. It will help them remember which books were by you, which means they'll want future ones. It's branding at its most basic. Just like when you see someone you like and you want to see them again.

    \n

    So the reasons to do it are good.

    \n

    The reasons against it are that we're shy creators who don't want to self aggrandize and we fuck it up a lot. It's stupid.

    \n

    Elizabeth, would you take some pictures of me?

    \n Shreyas sort of has it. All the professional (which for the purposes of this post I'm equating with financially successful/making a living by their art) artists I've known, regardless of medium, do the face time. They actively promote not only their work but themselves as well. They go to events, host events, never pass up a chance to be featured in a magazine article or get their face in the paper. They rub elbows, with other artists and with people who are likely to have wallets thick enough to purchase their work.
    \n
    \nI don't see it as "selling out" or any other derogatory label you want to put on it. If that sort of self-promotion isn't something you (an artist) finds appealing, then it's simply the price to be paid for doing something you love for a living. It's the flip-side of the coin, the business of being an artist.

    Posted By: Thunder_God"Why be shy?"

    What an odd question.
    \nTangent time: Only if you accept being shy.

    Some folks might not guess it, but I used to be shy as all fuck. Seriously. People freaked me the fuck out, and unless I was feeling particularly bold, I didn't tend to introduce myself. A few years ago, I chose to stop acting shy. I got out there and mingled with people, even when my gut tells me to just chill and hang silently. At some point, it started to become easier to do all that. And now, I'm significantly less shy -- sometimes not shy at all.

    Another case of "fake it 'til you make it" -- you can choose to not be shy, if you're willing to work at it.

    \n Art is communication.
    The more you understand about a person, the better you communicate with them.

    This is why author's photos and bios are on books. This is why people read interviews and watch DVDs with the commentary on.\n
    "There is nothing special about me. I am a painter who paints day after day from morning to night [...] Who ever wants to know something about me [...] ought to look carefully at my pictures."
    \nKlimt was a dork. A good painter, but a dork. Without context, nobody is going to understand the pictures. To some extent, everyone will insert, create, or discover context, but that's not always enough.

    Question: Is Dark Magus by Miles Davis cool because it's just plain cool?
    Yes.

    Is it better because we know Miles Davis was such a fuckin' nutcase?
    You betcha.

      \n You know, it's possible to really fuck up a piece of art by associating things with it. By watering it down, i should say.

      Like, to humanize an artist, yeah, a photo will go a long way,
      but what if you wanted the piece to be more arms-length-feeling, or to allow the reader / perceiver of the art
      a freedom to imagine what they will of the creator, or to not conceptualize of them at all: allow the piece
      to stand on its own, like an artifact of uncertain origin.

      example: Kevin's art for Ribbon Drive has people, but faces are never shown. The people are removed from
      their real identities; that could be anyone's hand trailing out the window. Your lover, your daughter, your sister
      the summer before she died. If Kevin had put joe's face at the end it would have been a shock back into reality,
      a premature return from where the daydreaming should be beginning, not ending.

      related: ok, use your face if you are creative and want to publish, to sell it. I am totally planning on doing this,
      getting people to identify you with your art is a great way to humanize, softly manipulate, etc. BUT! if you are creative and want to
      SHARE, then a) selling isn't the main deal, and marketing sure as hell isn't the main deal. b) publishing might be a good way to
      share what you got with the people you want to share with and c) selling the thing is most likely a good way to recoup your costs.

      Elizabeth said: "The best way to share whatever you've created with the people you wish to enjoy it is to MARKET, MARKET, MARKET"
      i would say this would take a great deal of your time, to market so much. Perhaps if you want to find a lot of new people to share your
      thing with... sure. Marketing seems like an activity very different than sharing; i would hazard that the best way to share things is to
      just share them. I don't mean "give away", i mean "co-experience".

      & Chris said: "If you want to increase whatever level of success you have you market yourself and your work. Period."
      and i say that this is true only during those times when success is confused with monetary wealth. i say this as someone
      interested in making money from my work, and also as someone interested in success.
