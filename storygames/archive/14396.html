\n I searched for previous discussions on this topic but couldn't find any to my surprise.

Is it ok to "steal" a mechanic from another game? When is it not?

What's the difference between publishing a system hack and publishing a system hacked together from several other systems (is the former tribute and the latter plagiarism)?

If you look around, many systems have direct mechanics taken or influenced from another, I'm trying to determine how far is too far. I'm also assuming you're giving the original game credit for these mechanics at the end of your text.

Context for my question - I'm working on a Fantasy game and pretty much straight up stole Graham's Occupation and Insanity Die rules from Cthulhu Dark (Insanity Die is a Power Die in my rules for Mages).

I also took some other stuff but one example is enough.

    \n Get to a point where you have a serious, playtestable draft, and include your list of references at the back. If you feel you've got something strong, play it. Improve it. Play it some more. Then e-mail your references. Everybody here is aware of the stealing/building on thing, so you can expect them to be cool. But on your way to that, you also might find serious changes you want to make to have it fit better with your game.

    A garage band that hasn't had a gig yet shouldn't worry about whether their Monkey Wrench cover can be their hidden track on their studio album. There is so much play that needs to happen - as in, playing, making it better, and seeing how it works and if you love it - before you worry about commercial consequences.

    Have some playable thing, and play it, and then worry about your references.

    \n I'm not a lawyer. This is not legal advice. That said, I know IP lawyers who are gamers and unofficially have mentioned:

    - Games mechanics, including those in RPGs, can indeed be patented under US Patent Law.

    - Generally, when it comes to patents, the lines between RPGs, Boardgames, and even video / mobile games that use similar mechanics are becoming increasingly blurred. It is very common to patent game mechanics in video and mobile games. This will become an increasing issue as RPGs and Boardgames migrate to digital devices such as tablets and eReaders. Patents for game mechanics also exist in Boardgames. Wizards of the Coast has at least 1 patent for game mechanics in Magic the Gathering.

    - To be awarded a patent, the idea must technically be new, innovative, and non-obvious. It would be difficult to patent the vast majority of RPG mechanics because very few are new or proving they are new would be difficult. But ideas that shouldn't be patented sometimes get through the patent system.

    - Just because you have a patent, doesn't mean you have a strong patent that will be enforceable in court. Some say that the prequalification system for obtaining patents is broken. Often you don't know how valuable your patent is till it is challenged in court.

    - Please note, patents protect ideas. Copyrights protect the expression of ideas. Trademarks protect names, words, phrases, logos, symbols, designs, images, or a combination of these elements.

    - The common statement that "RPG mechanics can't be patented" is false.

    - But are those patents worth it? And how much do those patents protect you? Are you going to make enough money to make spending money to protect your patent worth it? The best protection is lots of money and hiring professionals to investigate all the angles to see what leverage you have. But is the money involved worth it?

    - People are increasingly going to court over disputes regarding video game mechanic patents. What will happen when there is no perceived difference between a video and board game? Even if you are too small to be worth suing for financial gains, larger companies may seek legal action or send you warnings as a matter of setting precedent for future cases.

    But these are all issues you probably don't have to deal with.

    I would argue most RPGs are hacks of RPGs that came before. RPG design culture seems to overall with hacking culture.

    So is it cool to use mechanics from other games without permission?

    I think it depends how unique the mechanic is. If it is such a commonly used mechanic that it isn't even clear whose permission you would even ask\u2026 then I would say not to worry about it. But if the mechanic is unique to a specific game or designer, to the extent people would respond, "that's like Circles from Burning Wheel" then I personally would recommend asking permission but at the least give credit. You could also take inspiration from the designers you are building from. Do they give credit in their books?

    My IP lawyer friends would say don't give credit because it may be admitting fault in a future conflict. And they would say, if you want permission, make sure to get it in writing. But if it is a game mechanic that is commonly used, document it\u2019s common use and don't worry too much about it.

    When you ask, "what's the difference between publishing a system hack and publishing a system hacked together from several other systems", can you give us examples of such systems?

    Regarding Graham's Occupation and Insanity Die rules from Cthulhu Dark, my recommendation is to ask him for permission and give him credit. He's a very approachable person. His contact information is at the bottom of the 4th page in this document.

    Is this for a game you are publishing? If it is for personal use, I wouldn't worry about the above. If it is for publishing, how close are you to publishing? If it is in the early stages, don't worry too much about this. By the time you are finished, your mechanics may look nothing like your initial inspiration.

    \n To expand on the patentability... game mechanics can be submitted for a patent in the US, Japan and maybe in some other country that was so stupid(*) to permit patents on procedures, methods, algorithms and concepts. Other countries would not be bound in any way.

    Bear in mind, though: the fact that you can file a patent means almost nothing. The patent office does not check the content of the filing, it just registers it regardless of its sense, validity, contents or innovation. Nobody checks if there is prior art. Only if and when you get sued over it AND WIN you get to ask for compensations.


    (*) Yes, stupid. Yes, harsh. No, I won't expand much, there's a ton of literature online about this. Short version: billions of dollars of costs, stifled innovation, no point.
    Thought experiment: a Turing Machine can implement any P-complete (computable) algorithm that exists, or will ever exist. End of patents: there is prior art.

    \n It's polite and appropriate to thank the person whose ideas you're using. It's not necessary to ask. I always feel awkward when people ask me if they can use my ideas - it's not my place to say yes or no, so it's hard for me to say yes. I usually wind up explaining this instead. That'd fulfill law, ethics and manners all three. Better yet, when the thing you're making is done, say it in the text somewhere! It's always fun to read my name in somebody else's designer's notes.

    Posted By: TulpaCan we please not use the word "steal" when talking about ideas? It gets confusing and suggests something morally reprehensible might be happening.

    Can we also never group copyrights, patents, trademarks and trade secrets together?
    \nTo borrow a term from the music industry, perhaps "sampling" would be be better term.

    There is definitely some confusion in this discussion as to what is "acceptable" and what isn't when it comes to sampling ideas from other games.

    Copyrights, patents, trademarks and trade secrets are all very specific terms. They protect different things in different ways. You don't want to mix them up.

    Sampling a mechanic idea is perfectly acceptable and very common practice. The only time it comes into legal conflict is when the originator has managed to get a patent for it. This occurance is very rare simply because the trouble and cost usually isn't worth it for RPGs. In the case of WotC, they felt it was necessary to protect M:tG because of the number of copycat games that sprung up overnight.

    Of course, much of this community would simply consider it rude to use a mechanic idea without crediting it. However, its very possible that the "originator" may have lifted the idea from someplace else.

    A mechanic idea cannot be protected by copyright. Copyright protects the text used to describe the game, but not the idea. Lifting entire sections of text for your own use is legally unacceptable under copyright.

    Trademarks are used for protecting specific terms and images, but cannot protect an idea.

    Trade Secrets is just keeping things hidden, like the formula for Coca-Cola. With RPGs, you pretty much put everything out in the open.
