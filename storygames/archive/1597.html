    Okay, so my take on it is this: there's serial fiction and then there's serial fiction. Matt calls it episodic and serial fiction: that is, in episodic fiction, there's a new thing happening each episode but the basic structure of things doesn't change. Classic Star Trek is a great example, as is Mission:Impossible. Batman, Spiderman, and "classic" comic book heroes are good examples. The characters don't change, their relationships don't change, their drives don't change. They just apply all of those things to new stuff each episode. In serial fiction, there's stuff every installment, but the stuff impacts the characters as much as the characters impact the stuff. Here we're looking at Battlestar Galactica, Lost (from what I understand, at least), and Alias. In comic book land, we're talking X-Men -- not the movie X-Men, but the comic book X-Men who have seen some pretty significant changes over time.

    Actually, remember when Spiderman had the little "Cosmic Spiderman" bit? Did you get annoyed? Did you enjoy that? That was Spiderman crossing the line from episodic to serial, and fan reaction beat that down quickly, so Spiderman returned to his comfortable old self. See also: Mystique becoming a ninja hottie, and Wolverine when he had the bone claws. Comics are nice because the conflicts are blatantly external. Mystique gets a who-am-I conflict, Wolverine gets a what-is-happening-to-me conflict.

    But to move this out of television and comics and onto the gaming table, I think all of this is perfectly possible to implement in a game. This is, in fact, stuff that the years-long D&D games have been doing for decades, now. The two characters who were in love eventually get together and get married. Instead of the budding romance conflicts, now they have married couple conflicts. The rough-and-tumble outcasts fight the man until they win, and then they're in charge of the city/kingdom/whatever. Their down-with-the-Man conflicts are replaced with I-am-the-Man-now conflicts.

    One thing that I think is important to note is that the new conflicts usually come about as developments of the old conflicts. This means that the new conflicts are looking at the same themes as the old conflicts, just from a different angle (marriage vs courting, still about love; administration vs rebellion, still about power).

    The other bit that I think is important is that there's almost always more than one conflict on the table. The two outcast lovers are struggling against the Man. They'll eventually consummate their love and take over the city, but those things don't usually happen at the same time. Say they marry before they beat down the Man. So with this overly simplistic example, you've got three stages of conflicts: courting and rebellion, marriage and rebellion, marriage and administration. Which would be a very different progression and a very different story than if they beat down the Man and then got married (courting and rebellion, courting and administration, marriage and administration).

    Briefly, in Full Light, Full Steam, this is accomplished by swapping out thematic batteries when you're done with them. You've always got three, and you can only swap out one at a time between situations. So there's always some familiar thread available to you from last time, and in play you're going to swap out stuff that's no longer interesting in favor of stuff that is -- and the stuff that has become interesting is most likely stuff that's been developed in play.

    But I'd like to hear anybody else's thoughts on what's possible, or techniques they've used, or mechanics that support this sort of thing. How do you switch out conflicts to keep the developing game interesting?

Going up a level in D&D is pretty cool, but is it what makes a character memorable? Burning Wheel lets character's beleifs grow and change as a result of things that happen in gameplay.

    Josh,

    Are you familiar with The Riddle of Steel's Spiritual Attributes? You have five of them. They are rated from 0 to 5. They're things like Drive, Faith, Passions, etc. They go up and down and can be replaced. In play they act sort of like a conflict mixing board. As they get swapped in and out, ramped up, spent down, etc the play switches focuses around those Spiritual Attributes for any given character.

    As a very personal point I find this kind of play (and this kind of storytelling in other media) problematic because it is very difficult to get it to end. And I value endings. In the rare occasions that I write fiction I usually start with the ending. Now this is not to say that nothing ever ends in the stories you describe, they do, but because there are other sorts of conflicts that are currently getting ramped up or ramped down and thus resolutions lack a certain "killing blow" feeling I like in my story resolutions.

    As a case study my Riddle of Steel game didn't end, it stopped. It stopped because the situation just kept get getting more complex and more baroque and although little things here and there came to an end people were constantly ramping up new things and it was nearly impossible for all the players to get things to line up and come to one momentus resolution for everything and everyone.

    I also suspect PtA's Screen Presence values are alos meant to achieve the effect you describe. Spotlight Episodes are more or less meant to signal the end of a character's issue or current conflict. Because the Spotlight Episodes are stagared and placed relative to other character's Screen Presence it produces the "climax" in the middle of other stuff being ramped up or down I mentioned.

    For reference, the only game I've ever really gotten the sense of "killing blow" resolution out of is Sorcerer. My Life with Master comes in a close second but I've only ever played it to completion in a con environment. Funny, considering that MLwM has a literal killing blow built into it. I suspect this is why I recieve applause whenever I run it. I'd still like to play a multi-session game of MLwM to completion and see if it has more of a heavy hitting resolution.

    Just my thoughts on the subject.

    The game I'm currently designing and hopefully will release under the banner of Evil Hat Productions, tentatively called Threads, is pretty much exactly about this.

    Unlike TSOY's Keys or PTA's Issues, which primarily mechanize *suggestions* of situations, threads mechanize the situations or goals themselves. So, you'd actually have "Prove myself as a soldier: +1" on your sheet, and that represents a *thing* you want as a story, a known point of investment. In moment-to-moment conflicts, you have the choice to "weave" a thread in, which give you bonus dice.

    However, using these bonus dice gives the GM a currency that they spend to stack opposition up against you and delay the achievement or resolution of your thread. Simple pacing, basic fiction structure. Story 101.

    However, when the GM uses that currency, the value of your thread also increases. So you can get more dice as the conflict progresses. But using those greater dice gives the GM more currency for his opposition. Et cetera. So there's a continual process of escalation.

    What keeps it from sucking? A definite endpoint built in that you're working toward - reach the max, and the next conflict that the thread is woven into is the final one. This is a configurable dial - maybe it's +5, maybe it's +10. Maybe the GM gets lots of opposition currency when you use your bonus dice, maybe he only gets one point. Depends on your own sense of pacing, what type of game you're trying to do.

    Also... new threads can be started at any time. You bascially just note your desire to invest in a situation, and write the new thing down as "New thing: +1". Then you start weaving it in.

    I'm probably about a year away from releasing it. I'm hoping it'll be good, and address some of what's been discussed lately.

    You know, the "conflict levels up" concept has got me really fascinated, actually. Here's why:

    In D&D for instance, characters level up, increasing in effectiveness. However, the monsters all level up too, not on their own, but tied directly to you--IE, challenge rating. They level parasitically, in that the challenges you face are scaled up to your ability to meet them. Essentially you gain power for the privilege of fighting more powerful enemies. I've occasionally thought that you could just call a goblin a troll, and a wolf a dragon, and save a lot of time.

    But a "conflict" leveling up , if handled just right, could be a great alternative. "Conflict" could be a a great grand goal, like "liberate the kingdom," or if you wanted more bog-standard D&Dish play, it could be something like "emnity with Orcs," so that all Orc encounters would be scaled up as it levels. This has two advantages over Challenge rating: 1) if it truly levels independently (not "OK, it gets the same EXP you get"), then it would be possible to outstrip that particular conflict as you struggle against it, by doing what's necessary to gain EXP faster, and 2) it would be possible to have several different conflicts running at once and leveling independently, so that the one you really focus on will shoot up to Epic Struggle status, while others that aren't important will be left behind. This severs to create (to borrow a term being knocked around in another thread) a nice contrast between the different arenas of conflict, saying essentially, that Luke rocks over Stormtroopers but really struggles with Vader. Or insert Spidey and bank robbers and Doc Ock. Or whatever.

    Perhaps this is getting off topic from the OP, or perhaps it's one method of "breeding" conflicts to use for "revolving door" play. Heck, if one conflict becomes your Big Thing, chances are once it's resolved you've got one or several candidates groomed to step in as the new Thing. Whaddya think?
