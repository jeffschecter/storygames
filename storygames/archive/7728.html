\n
\n
\n
\n\nMatthijs \n\n\n
\n
\n\nSeptember 2008 \n edited September 2008 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n On this forum and in this scene, full of indie design people, people are focused on the publishers' and creators' rights and their situation. I want to take a look at things from the other side for a while.

IMPORTANT NOTE: This thread is, specifically, for talking about what someone A) buying a game and/or playing a game can expect from the product, the designer, and the publisher. It is emphatically not for discussion of what people shouldn't expect - that will just lead us back to creators' rights.

When I buy a game, I feel it's reasonable to expect these things, among others:
- That the game works
- That if it doesn't, from my point of view as a customer, I have someone to contact who will at least hear what I have to say and respond to it
- That the game has been playtested thoroughly, including external blind-testing
- That the game text has been edited by a professional
- That the components are durable (the book doesn't fall apart, the cards don't lose their color after a few uses, etc)

What else?

[EDIT] I've modified this to talk about "reasonable expectations" instead of "rights". [/EDIT]

    \n I'm with E on a lot of this. However, in the spirit of talking about what I will get pissed off about if a publisher pulls, I'd say the few things I really expect to the degree that I could maybe be pressed into calling them consumer rights when I was pissed and talking loudly would be:

    1) That I not be fucking lied to in order to get me to purchase the damn thing. I may forgive you for this if you get me the replacement parts, files, or information for free or really, really cheap (like, say, a free upgrade .pdf or the 7th Sea book that came out free at FLGS to make up for the suck of the main books).

If you give me "cards" printed on plain printer paper and you're a $2.50 Cheap Ass Game, well... okay. But give me the same cards and charge me $75 and I will find you one night in a dark ally and exercise my consumer rights upside your head. Of course, I also kinda think maybe I should have done some research on that (caveat emptor, right?), but fuck, we're talking about expectations of consumers, not their responsibilities.

    I myself tend to have very low expectations of games - I certainly don't expect them to playtested in a specific manner or come in a specific quality package.     \n The only difference between rights and expectations is that "rights" implies a generally agreed-upon (and sometimes explicitly defined in order to be enforceable) standard of reference. If a large enough - or significant enough - group of people promotes these expectations, they will become recognized as rights.

    I would certainly be wary of dealing with someone who believes the only rights a customer has are those explicitly spelled out by whatever consumer laws are currently in effect in his home jurisdiction.

    I would suggest updating your original list to add Brand-Robins points - though all could be covered by one case concerning misrepresentation.

    Also, number 1 is really part of number 3 (since "works" is a subjective term). And number 2 is vague and ultimately pointless unless you change it into some kind of satisfaction guarantee - and, while important, those are almost never considered a consumer "right" because they are equally subjective (and open to abuse).

    \n Does you expectation change if you're getting something for free?

    I guess I could go either way on that. Like, I don't see that there's a fundamental difference between something that's published for free, and something that's published for $50. I guess my expectation is that the author has created something they're proud of, and that what they're expecting from me in return is what they believe is reasonable. So I guess I don't have a hard set of criteria for what I expect of a product, I just expect the producer of that product, the artist, to have some integrity about the product.

    Part of that's tied to what Brand was saying. It sucks to feel like a chump, especially in this hobby where no one's really making buckets of money, and we're all in it for the love. Any money I spend on some asshole is money that won't be going to someone more deserving.

    So if your product has shoddy bindings and there's typos everywhere, but you're proud of it, and you don't hide its flaws, then I'm happy to buy it for what it is. If you can't get anyone to playtest your game because no-one's heard of you before, but you need $5 to cover the cost of printing a playtest rulebook, I'm cool with that. So I guess it comes down to this: I expect the artist to love what they've produced. If they don't love it, they shouldn't be publishing it.

    \n I think there is definitely a barrier between those things that the consumer can expect that the designer can effect, and those that consumers expect that the designer has no means of impacting.
