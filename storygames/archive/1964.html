What Isn't a Role-Playing Game?

James_Nostack
December 2006 edited December 2006 in Play Advice
(Note: I'm not trying to be flippant here, but have a semi-serious discussion.)

So, I'm one of those guys who likes the Forge, and those guys come up with funky games like "Capes" and "Universalis" and "Polaris," and lots of other stuff. And there's this understanding on the Forge that these are all role-playing games. (In contrast, there's a loud section--I don't know if it's a majority--of RPG.Net that wouldn't consider these RPG's at all.)

So here's my question for people who define RPG's pretty broadly: what's an RPG? More importantly, what's NOT an RPG?

Like, one of the definitions of an RPG I've seen is, "An RPG is a process whereby people hang out, and imagine some stuff happening, together."

So, if anyone's familiar with Vincent Baker's LEGO wargame, Mechaton, Vincent calls it a "wargame." Yet Vincent, Emily, and Joshua are workin' up a war in an imaginary city, with imaginary politics, and silly little government ministers, and things. How is this not an RPG, albeit one where authority is distributed through unspoken social norms rather than a game text?

If it is not an RPG, what is the imaginative or formal difference between this mode of playing Mechaton, and Dungeons & Dragons?

At the other end of the spectrum: why is Primetime Adventures a role-playing game, but collaborating with four other people to write a TV series not?

Hypothesis: a "role-playing game" is a process for creative collaboration, and its "mechanics" settle creative differences between the participants. Traditionally the outcome of the creative collaboration is a story of some kind (Event A, then Event B, then Event C), and the mechanics are geared to reflect this assumption. But why couldn't one create such a process for, say, writing a piece of music, or collaborative architecture? (I.e., why are we hung up on stories?)

    I think a key part of roleplaying game as it's usually used is that the players (either via the characters or not) make substantive judgements of value with regard to the fiction.

    Let's unpack that a tiny bit: What I mean is that in a role-playing game, you are free to decide that, for instance, rescuing the princess is a bad idea, and that such a decision concretely affects you gameplay. In Monopoly, you are free to decide that overcharging for property is a bad idea, but you are not mechanically allowed to stop, so that's a null decision.

    Here is an edited excerpt from the first draft of my (unpublished) article on The Big Model Without Jargon:

    In roleplaying games [ ... ] the â€œboardâ€ and â€œtokensâ€ are negotiable, unlike in chess or Monopoly. I write â€œboardâ€ and â€œtokensâ€ in quotes, because although some gamers may use a physical map and some kind of counters or miniatures, these are not the real board and the real tokens. The real action occurs in a fictional world that the players are creating together; they describe what various fictional people and creatures are doing, the locales they are passing through, and the events that occur in those locales.

    Roleplaying games have rules that govern who can introduce which kinds of elements to the fictional world, as well as when and how they may be introduced. And here's the important part: the rules also govern how they may be used. The board and tokens in roleplaying games are fictional and completely negotiable, but this is true of other games where the object is to create some kind of story, even in a game as simple as â€œexquisite corpseâ€. The difference between roleplaying games and other story-creation games is that the contents of the fiction can affect what can be added or what can change. The players aren't just playing with words, they're creating a world together that is described in words.

    Thus, although Vincent, Emily, and Joshua are creating additional fictional elements like government ministers, these fictional elements have no effect in turn on the fiction. They're just window dressing for a traditional wargame. Compare that to Dungeons & Dragons, where a player could imaginatively describe a way to permanently alter the board (dungeon) or tokens on the board (monsters, objects) in an unforseen way, and that alteration has an effect on what other actions may then be attempted. I may say to the DM "Wait! You said these goblins were mining, right? That means they have mining tools. And since we can't seem to get this magic door open or bust it down or anything, let's just dig a tunnel through the wall!" The description of the goblins as miners, originally just color, indirectly creates fictional picks and shovels, which the player characters can then use for digging a tunnel, which alters the geography of the dungeon.

    To me, that's the most important feature of a roleplaying game: recursive alteration of the fiction created by the game. I've seen some games labeled as RPGs that have very little of this recursiveness. I've made the mistake myself of designing games where the fiction created has very little effect. The less recursion there is, the more the game resembles a traditional board or card game. The more recursion, the more roleplaying.

I think Capes is "just barely" a RPG because it has these tools for making fictional stuff "stick."

    Here's something that I think is definitive of the tabletop form (that is, distingushes it from CRPGs, for instance). RPGs vary from wargames in that there are places where participant judgment is used to make some determination instead of always having a rule to reference. This enables RPGs to be infinitely more versatile, because you can have an infinite number of inputs. Whereas in any game game, there are limits on the inputs, and anything else done on top of that is solely color.

    For instance in a RPG, you can say, "I command my unit to move just ten feet out of the trees. In a hex-based game, you can't do that, because you have to move either into this hex, or that. Even with tapemeasure wargames you can't have the units move off of the board without losing them or something. In an RPG you can. And the GM or somebody will be there to look at the rules and figure out how to apply them to this new situation. Computer can't do that.

The "game" for the wrestler's isn't about winning the match -- it's about the way they do it.

      There are two issues here:

      "RPG" as label: It's pretty clear that the term "RPG" is simply a label. So when trying to define the activity, you have to decide whether it's worth maintaining that label.

      Defining the activity: Now, when attempting to define the activity, there are basically two approaches one can take. First, you can take a historical approach. That is, you can look at how the term has been used in the past. With this approach, pretty much anything that is a descendant of DnD is an RPG. This is includes computer games, and possibly some boardgames.

      The second approach is, err... qualitative. You attempt to define the core of the activity as an activity. Some people like to say that it has something to do with "playing a role". Some people here says that it has to do with the recursiveness of the imaginative content. Whatever.

      So, before a definition can be made, you have to decide what kind of criteria you're going to use. Both historical and qualitative approaches are valid, and they each make more sense for certain purposes. But obviously, each approach is going to include or exclude certain things that the other would leave out or embrace, respectively.
