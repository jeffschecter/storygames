\n What are some fun, small story games that could be played with non-roleplayers?

For example: some friends come round for dinner. We eat, laugh, talk etc. After dinner someone says: "Hey, let's play this game!"

Things that could work well in this situation:
* Rules that can be quickly explained up front, or rules that can be explained as the game progresses.
* Minimal setup.
* Playtime of 2 hours max. Less is better.
* Bonus points for GMless.

I have one friend in particular that will often bring up politics or random contentious subject of the day. On occasion it would be good to have a surprise in the bag to pull out and inject some lightheartedness.

    \n *self promotion alert*

    My game The Box fits this niche nicely. I've had games as short as 1 hour including rules explanation. All you need is the supplied cards and a D4, and it is GMless. The concept is to create a story around discovering a strange container, opening it, finding out what's inside, and divining the true nature of the object you discover. It has pre-set scenarios that give good story seeds for players to run with if they choose, and the card prompts limit creativity enough so the game is not intimidating, but they still allow for great freedom of responses. I've played with many first-time story gamers and players catch on immediately, and there is usually lots of laughing

    Here's a link to my latest prototype version. It's definitely a late-stage prototype and fully playable as-is, just working on small-scale improvements. If any Bay Area folks want to play I'll be running a few sessions at Big Bad Con in October. Hit me up!

    \n I routinely run 2-hour-or-less Fiasco sessions, sometimes to folks who've never played an RPG (or played only very simulationist, crunchy RPGs). Explain as you go is great for these folks, especially when you 'surprise' them with The Turn (and at that point, explain the 'metagame' of angling for dice colors to favor good or bad outcomes for PCs).

    The Quiet Year is pretty great for beginners, too; though I'd forget trying to enforce player silence when not narrating. Though that's a big part of the game's 'feel', newer players will have more fun collaborating and making suggestions for each other's additions. In a similar manner, the Contempt tokens could either be dropped or be a way to 'veto' an addition, if a majority of players drop tokens during an addition--that's a slightly less aggressive way to object than speaking up directly (for some folks, at least).

    Finally, at the risk of showing my Peak Story Game Era, Contenders is super easy to teach, and everyone's seen Rocky so explaining the gist of the contrasting stats is straightforward. That said, don't expect to get through the 'full game' but rather scale back to half the number of... err, I forget what stat... to 'win'. A fast-moving 'full game' barely fits into a 4-hour con slot.

    What all those have in common is that they really aren't intended for 'campaign play' but rather build a single story arc (or not even, for The Quiet Years). Much like most of the game suggested above.
