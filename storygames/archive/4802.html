\n Does anyone know of a game or, better yet, designed a game in which there is one "player" and many "GMs"? Essentially, where there's one protagonist and the other participants take the role of opposition. Obviously, some of this is just semantics, but I can see a subtle distinction there that might prove interesting to explore. I'm thinking of, say, a game where the player is a dragon and the GMs take the role of orc hordes and dragonslayers and enemy dragons, etc.

    \n I was originally just going to type Dirty Secrets three times and leave it at that. Like, Dirty Secrets. Dirty Secrets. Dirty Secrets. But then I thought maybe I should expand a bit.

    The game has one protagonist and many GMs. During each scene a GM is either dominant (in charge of the narrative) or acting as an advisor. Advisors have some level of power, mostly in the form of veto, but are encouraged to offer suggestions, and, of course, play NPCs. The game runs smoothly, and the mechanics ensure a cohesive story without any pre-play prep or any discussion during the game, it's all based on what happens in play.

    The designer is on this board as Great Wolf.

    \n I keep thinking this is fruitful ground that hasn't been fully explored. Focusing on one character would better emulate other modes of fiction, where a primary protagonist is standard. Even more interesting is that I think it would aid in externalizing internal conflicts of that protagonist, making a game more about drama than melodrama.

    The Drifter's Escape fits this mold, pretty well, as I recall. I also wrote an outline for an rpg with multiple GMs and one player, but have never playtested it or done any more work on it. I really should, though, as I like it more than many of the game ideas I come up with.

    \n I've played Polaris scenes where there were effectively multiple adversarial GMs versus one "player"

    One form this takes is when the Moon characters are leaning-toward-hostile to the Protagonist. An extreme case of this is when there are no moon characters and the Mistaken loans a couple demons to the moons.

    Another case is when a moon character is the center of attention/what's at stake in a conflict between the Mistaken and Heart. In this case one of the "GMs" is probally "benevolent," but from the Moon's perspective that might not be so clear.

    Mistaken: "But only if Deneb (Moon character) is stabbed."
    Heart: "But only if the wound is not fatal"
    M: "But only if Deneb is scarred horribly for life."
    Heart: "Hmm... ok. That's cool. And that was how it happened."
    Moon: "Do'oh. You bastards"

    \nAbsoultely. The ashcan PDF is at: http://mastermines.wordpress.com/projects/know-thyself/

    Unfortunately, I have found a few pitfalls I haven't been able to completely solve with this model of one PC-many GM. It's a very antagonistic role, where the GMs (called the Chorus) are elements of the Player's (call the Amnesiac) hostile subconscious, and play is done through a series of fever dream-esque scenes. There are moments when memories are regained where the authority shifts, but that don't happen as often in the game.

    I haven't been able to utterly tackle these issues, which primarily stem from every Gm having a stake against the PC but also having equal authority to step on each other's toes. Talking about that would be a lengthy post or three.

    I will also say that Polaris doesn't really fit in this case. What it has (for any given heart) is more akin to two influential players (the Moons), one GM (the Mistaken) and one PC/GM (the Heart). The Heart has equal and profound authority in Polaris, enough to say that there are two dueling GMs with people shouting from the wings.

    \n Maybe I should flesh out the "Moon acting like mistaken case" with an AP example. The demon in the scene was a spirit-voice in the head of the protagonist; the moon's character was the protagonists teenage daughter who was sleeping with a demon. I was mistaken.

    Me and the Moon were tag teaming the Heart, trying to put him in a very uncomfortable spot. The moon would say something like "You don't really love me! All you care about is your stupid knight-business! Waah! (I run away)" Then I'd come in as the demon voice "Are you really just going to turn away from your daughter? She needs you right now. Go to her" If he abandons her, I grin evilly, say "And so it was..." and call for an xp check for callousness. If he runs after her, I say "but only if by abandoning your post demons are able to kill an innocent." The moon sets up the protagonist, the mistaken knocks him down.

    We have regularly had incidents like this, where the Heart after the scene will comment "damn, it was like dealing with multiple mistakens. You bastards."

    \n Sure, I don't think that's an example of a solid multiple GM setup -- that's a GM choosing to share power. Now, that might have produced powerful play because it worked (and I've had similar moments in my own games of Polaris), but if it hadn't worked then the Mistaken could have blocked it, and the Moon would have just had to accept the block and move on. That's why I say the Moons are influential, but lack true authority -- they only get authority if one side lets them. Now, if Justin sees that as a multi-GM setup, then that's fine, but the rules don't actually treat both sides has having equal high authority.
