    \n Tying this back to the Southern Gothic thread, one of the biggest modern musical voices of Southern Gothic is Nick Cave. Dude's an Aussie. But he assimilated the mythology of SG and has made it work in performance, album after album: murders, revenge, ghosts, convicts and executions, religious conversions, oppression by religion and salvation through it, in both psychological and life-after-death senses.

    Write what you know, but write what excites you too. Some of my very favorite books are the Aubrey/Maturin novels of Patrick O'Brian. They're historical fiction, and while the main characters are an Englishman and an Irish-Catalan Francophile (the nationalities of O'Brian's upbringing and of his somewhat wandering adult identity formation), the intense living quality of the series comes from insane research and reading of novels, letters, and archives of the Napoleonic period. That, and a living knowledge of sailing ships.

    The problems of the historical novel and of the speculative novel are just the problems of any fictional writing amplified. The world is multifarious and detailed; whole populations with different historical memories, values, and marks of identity live right on top of each other, in London as well as Atlanta, Jerusalem, Kashmir, and any number of highly ordinary places. Portraying a world that seems real and interesting is hard no matter what milieu you select, and I am pretty sure that a portrayal of any place is going to ring false to some people familiar with it, no matter what.

    \n To me, the feel of veracity is the important thing. If you can make your players really feel like they're there, that covers a multitude of sins (even deceit). This is one of the very impressive things about (dare I say it?) Blood Red Sands. The setting is gripping and visceral.     \n So, when I was first writing Blowback, it had a rule that you had to put the game in wherever you the players happened to be playing it (which lead to a first playtest of spies in Greenfield, Massachusetts; if the game works there, it works anywhere). It was actually Graham suggesting that placing the game in the town you're playing in was boring which got me to change the rule. Now the rule is that you either play somewhere that everyone knows extremely well, or someplace no one has ever been to. I feel that works pretty well.

    My favorite scenario to run for Blowback is in post-Katrina New Orleans. Now, I've been to New Orleans three or four times, but not since Katrina. The scenario I run is about the warring factions of people who want to preserve New Orleans' history, and the people who want to get rid of all the poor people and dilapidated housing, and turn it into a Disneyfied version of itself. I have zero firsthand knowledge of that; I only know what I've read and seen on television. But the scenario works well and no one has told me it seemed unrealistic or unfair to the survivors of Katrina. And for a while, I actually considered making New Orleans the canonical setting of Blowback; I changed my mind because the book will be long enough as it is.

    I think the crux is in what you're trying to emulate. If your goal is to emulate the exploration of a location, then you need to know that location well; if you're trying to emulate a type of fiction, you have to know that fiction well; if you're not trying to emulate anything, who cares? It's like saying you can't write science fiction unless you're an honest-to-God scientist.

      \n I think that the 'problem' of writing about a foreign locale, or period in history, when you are neither native nor expert is one of the reasons people shift to fantasy and sf. By using an alternate reality you can take the elements of that setting that intrigue you but put them somewhere where you can happily fill gaps and make mistakes. So if you want to do something set in the Roman Empire, because you want gladiators, legionnaires, and chariot racing, but don't feel confident with your scholarship, far easier to create something that has those elements, without having to be accurate.       So if something about the American South captures your imagination, I would see if you cannot port that a 'fictional' setting based off whatever cliches or myths you want to exploit. The same is true of someone outside London writing about London. And it might be better for it.

      Posted By: Timothythe local geomythology is indigenous, and I'm not.
      \nIndigineity is a tricky little issue, and I'm probably doing something foolish even trying to say anything about it here, but, that's never stopped me before.

      Tim Ingold is one of my favorite anthropologists. The Perception of the Environment costs a lot, but in return, you get a book that's pretty hard to read. But if you pay all that money for it and spend the time to try to read such dense prose, you'll be rewarded with some amazing ideas. If you want to know where I got the cockamamie ideas which follow, you'll need to read chapter 8, "Ancestry, generation, substance, memory, land,"

      Europeans recognize individuals, which we conceptualize in an essentially Cartesian dualism: body and soul if you're religious, or nature and nurture if you're not. So, we're very big into genealogy. After all, genealogy tells you where you came from, which tells you your nature. So, to Europeans, indigeneity is a matter of descent. If you are descended from indigenous people, you are indigenous. Then we get into a big, complicated dance of, "Well, how much indigenous ancestry do you need to count as indigenous? Is someone who is 1/64th Cherokee still Cherokee, or not?"

      This has become very important to indigenous groups in the modern world. In an effort to find some kind of redress for past grievances, we now have laws which respect indigenous peoples, which makes it an important legal point to understand who is indigenous and who is not. You get all kinds of dishonest types who try to claim indigeneity for the legal rights. And from that perspective, I have no problem with the modern discussion about indigeneity. I am fiercely in favor of the rights of indigenous people.

      However, as Ingold illustrates, this is a matter of these people working within a European construct to find some way for Europeans to recognize them. It has very little to do with any indigenous group's traditional view of itself.

      And notice I said "traditional view." Most indigenous people have had traffic of one sort or another with European ideas for a very long time, and many of them have come to new views that incorporate European views. That doesn't have much bearing on the traditional views of their society, however.

      Where Europeans focus on genealogy, indigenous traditions more often focus on experience. It has nothing to do with genealogy, and everything to do with your own dwelling in the landscape. Indigenous traditions, by and large, have little interest in genealogy. Yes, you'll often hear talk of "the ancestors," but they rarely go into the kind of detail that a genealogy provides, charting precisely who descended from who. In fact, if you really follow closely what they have to say about "the ancestors," you quickly realize that it has nothing to do with genealogy at all. They typically don't honor their ancestors because of their genealogical relationship, but because their ancestors' participation in the landscape created the landscape that they participate in today in so many real, concrete ways.

      I've heard some indigenous people speak of "becoming native" as a continuing process, one that they never complete, but continue throughout their lives. I've heard others contrast the Biblical creation story as something that happened long ago, to their own understanding of creation as an ongoing process, and the work of humanity lies precisely in that ongoing work of creating the world. Both of these seem to reflect Ingold's point. From most native perspectives, you don't become native because of your birth; what you do makes you native or not.

From that perspective, your statement becomes very difficult to understand. Yes, the local geomythology has native roots, that would seem like the very reason why you'd engage it. If you don't have very strong native roots yourself, then that seems like an even stronger reason to engage it.\n

      To use anything fictional example that I think most here will know, The Wire focused completely on Baltimore. Baltimore played a bigger role in that series than any other character. They didn't shrink from changing Baltimore's history, though. They gave Baltimore a different mayoral history and different key politicians, and showed no qualms about changing even that. They had experiments with drug legalization, political shifts, and shake-ups in the police force that, in real life, would have had an enormous impact on the city's history. In the case of the incident that they based on a similar, real-life event, it did have an enormous impact on Baltimore. But most importantly, through five seasons, they remained true to the spirit of Baltimore. Nobody cared that much that the history diverged, or that they fictionalized some details. It still felt like Baltimore, because they kept true to that spirit.

      So, bringing this back around to Graham's original question\u2014more importantly than how long you've lived in a place (or haven't), or how many details you know about it, how well do you understand the spirit of a place, and how well can you capture it?
