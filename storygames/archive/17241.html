\n I just finished my first publicly-released play test version of Undying, my Vampire the Masquerade - Apocalypse World hack. Check out my blog post about it here and download a copy!

Undying is an exploration of vampirism through the Apocalypse World medium (modified extensively). It\u2019s about the struggle of vampire vs. self \u2013 a vampire\u2019s two halves: the human and the beast \u2013 and vampire vs. vampire society \u2013 the fickle workings of status.

I think it addresses a lot of the comments and interest expressed in the recent VtM threads here. I used those discussions as feed-forward into this revision.

    \n@TildeSee \u2013 Great question! Note that the 5-7 and 8+ architecture only applies to moves where you roll +Humanity vs. people (which includes your connection).

    I started the game design with the Humanity/Will sum to zero stat concept. I wanted Resisting Temptation, the most important Humanity-driven move, to have no better than a +1 stat to for it \u2013 so you can\u2019t be too good at resisting the Downward Spiral. On the other side of the coin, Will drives your vampire moves, so going to +3 is the limit (mathematically, going higher on 2d6 pushes you into auto-succeed territory).

    The problem that sum to zero causes, is that your character is likely to fail more often than not if I make you roll straight +Humanity for dealing with people when you have a non-negative Will score. From pretty much all vampire myth, you should be able to succeed against most people, at most things, most of the time. My solution is to effectively add +2 to all your dice rolls on moves against people. Now, when you have a +1 Humanity, it\u2019s like rolling +3 against them and, even if you have +3 Will, you\u2019re only effectively rolling -1 against them.

    Right, so why not just have Humanity and Will add up to +2 so that Humanity mechanically works right with people in the first place? I probably should have. Why I didn\u2019t: first, because Resist Temptation works with the normal move architecture and is biased against resisting \u2013 the way it should be for an emo vampire game; second, Observe is biased against understanding people \u2013 that sounded good to me; and third, the Devil\u2019s move Monster makes them roll all +Humanity moves on the normal 7-9 and 10+ scale, which biases them toward violence as the solution for Prey-related problems \u2013 which (I think) gives the playbook a really cool synergy. Also, aesthetically, it says that people are a lesser challenge than vampires, so you succeed on a lower result.

    I like the approach in theory; but, having written this, I\u2019m going to reconsider that design choice. Particularly, if it trips people up in practice. So, to those of you giving this a shot, please let me know how it went.

    Thanks for asking!

    \n Fair question. In contrast is the other gaming project I\u2019m working on, the Regiment, which is quite the opposite. Let me say that the intent of the language is not to offend, though some of the language is probably offensive. That\u2019s not what you said or asked; but, since we\u2019re on the topic\u2026

    I think the language sets the tone I want for the game, which is dark, personal, and gritty. So I wrote it largely in the second person (I wasn\u2019t super-consistent) with a lot of fragments, run-ons, and foul language. It\u2019s modern, for sure; but, this is supposed to be a VtM hack, not dark ages or Victorian, nor is it my intent to make a setting-neutral game. Incidentally, I\u2019m going to run a Victorian era Parisian game next week. We\u2019ll see how that goes.

    Back on topic - I thought that talking to the reader this way would convey the game\u2019s message of struggle and personal horror clearly, in a way that proper, clinical English might lack. Even if the technical aspects of the rules are defined precisely, if they don\u2019t have the right emotional punch, particularly in the context of Vampire, they miss the mark. The language choice is intended to hit that mark.

    I may be wrong; but, that\u2019s the choice I made. I think the world is too uptight and serious these days and could use some R-rated, 80\u2019s-style action movie throwback, so yippee ki-yay motherfuckers! Game on.
