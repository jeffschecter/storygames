    \n If you're JUST talking about managing the setup phase (and, to an extent, dice passing) I'd suggest FlockDraw:
    http://flockdraw.com/

    If you want to actually roll online, go to Graham's site:
    http://www.catchyourhare.com/diceroller/

    It's easy to label "who has what dice to give or roll at Tilt & Aftermath" and to roll and pass dice (put in the Password "artmanfiasco" to see how I did it for a defunct game).

    Between Skype, FlockDraw, and Dice Roller, I think just about any game using dice could be run.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarThe notecards are just a reference, so you could also have one person write them up on a virtual whiteboard or paint app.
    When my group plays IRL, we actually use a piece of easel-board paper rather than the index cards, and we seriously prefer it that way. The cards don't go flying off when there's a breeze, there's lots of room for writing additional details down as they come up in the game, and we can use the Mr Sketch markers that smell like artifical flavors and write REALLY LARGE so everyone can see everything.

    What I'm getting at is that David's suggestion of FlockDraw strikes me as especially suitable, given my meatspace Fiasco experiences. You don't get purple text that smells vaguely grape-y, but that seems like a minor loss.

    \n Jason, at some point I need to take a picture of one of our giant post-it sheets with a decent camera, so you can see how we keep track of our game state. We actually give each scene a title, and write down a record of those titles along the outside of the sheet... and we write down the names of new people, locations, and stuffs that we introduce... and we write additional details that we discover about our Details and Relationships... the result is what makes it possible for me to write APs like the really verbose one that I wrote for our second game, without even listening to our recordings. Then I fold the sheet up and stick it into a magazine box for future reference. It is a really slick bit of tech. The best part is the ability to use really big fat-tipped markers, a different color for each player, and to write really large, so everyone can read everything and reincorporation is natural and fluent.

    But that's largely beside the point; I know that everyone but me plays Fiasco with index cards, and it works perfectly well. I actually showed up at Endgame with both giant post-its and a deck of index cards in case a pickup game seemed necessary (we wound up using an easel sheet to list our current thematic elements for Grey Ranks). The germane bit for this thread is that my experience, with the physical-world tech that FlockDraw metaphrases, indicates that FD will be highly appropriate in supporting internet-mediated play. Good luck, Rich!
