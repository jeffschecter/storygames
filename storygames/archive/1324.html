Let me talk about the card game "Peanut Butter and Jelly."

There are two types of cards in the game ... some which are the components of your sandwiches (bread, peanut butter, jelly) and some which are meta-cards (flies to ruin other people's sandwiches, ants to steal ingredients).

You play an ingredient card, it starts a sandwich. Immediately, people are thinking "I'd rather play a meta-card than an ingredient, because I can get stuff plus slow down my opposition." But they're pretty much screwed, because there are way more ingredients than metas in the deck. So occasionally they get a meta, but mostly people are just playing ingredients.

When you complete a sandwich you remove the cards from the deck, and keep them as a stacked sandwich. Sorta like tricks in Bridge.

But ... but ... when you play a meta card, it goes back in the deck.

So there are three phases of the game:

First, the ingredients are prolific, and metas are rare. Sandwiches are built. Conflict is sporadic. As more sandwiches are built we shift inevitably into ...

Second, the ingredients are getting scarcer, and metas are therefore more common. Sandwiches are sabotaged left and right. Conflict is rampant. As sandwiches manage to get completed (often by the most rapid, brutal sequences) we shift inevitably into ...

Third, the ingredients are all but depleted. The metas are now virtually useless, because there are not enough ingredients to give them power. The game slides gently to a halt.

Now ain't that slick? Doesn't that make you want to design a two-resource game?

    I was thinking about a game wherein you draw a hand of cards, then are trading to ceate sets (kinda similar to the PB&J game). Initially, though, you can trad with some non-player entity (entirely rules governed, like the Bank in Monopoly), but as you go on in the game, trading with the Bank increases in cost: you get fewer and fewer cards each time you give the Bank some cards. Whereas trading with other players stays a constant cost, slightly above the initial Bank trading cost. so as the game goes on, you see a shift from no player interaction to player cooperation.

    For an RPG, you declare that trading with the bank requires your character to work against the other ones, or piss them off somehow. Then trading with another player requires your characters cooperating. This means the different cycles of the game equate to an initial stage where the PCs bicker among each other, then start cooperating as other threats take priority. Like how many family dramas and ensemble television shows work. See how in The Incredibles, each character starts out annoying the other characters in various ways, but then they come together in the end? Like that.

    If you want, add a final competitive end state to the game, wherein only one player wins (or one loses), meaning player cooperation naturally breaks down as you approach the end.

    I've always liked the tension mechanic used in the Weakest Link. Each player takes turns answering one question at a time. Each correct answer is worth more money than the last, but is also more difficult. You can choose to "bank" when it's your turn, but that reverts the worth of the subsequent questions back to their lowest levels so you have to climb all the way back up again.

    Translating this to a storygame:
    Let's say you have set up stakes for a scene.

    There is a singular, valuable resource. As you play out a scene, this resource builds up. This is good, you want that sweet, sweet resource. Even better, the rate of resource growth doubles every time. One, two, four, eight, sixteen, thirty-two, and so on.

    The snag is that the more resource a scene is worth, the more likely it is that the scene won't be resolved on your terms.

    You can choose to "bank" if you want. This represents a climax to the scene, appropriate for the amount of resource that the scene was worth. Congrats, the scene then resolves on your terms. The group as a whole collects that delicious resource and it's another player's turn to have a time in the spotlight.

    If you "break," by failing a roll, wager, etc., then the scene is not resolved on your terms. The accumulated resources of the scene evaporate into the ether.
    This seems to model an "accelerating buildup with sudden drop" form of storytelling, which might be suitable for cinematic games with very quick scenes.

    This has been used to create tensions in duels in Legend of the Five rings.

    He who strikes first, strikes first, but the longer you wait the higher the damage. But who breaks first.

    If you imagine that certain actions by the players gives the GM a pool of points. When this pool reaches certain levels (Points of no Return) the GM can "buy" either upgrades to the faceless badguys, complicate the situation or change the environment. In other words each Point of no Return lets the GM escalate the situation and this taxes the players resources harder. Forcing them to make tougher choices to get more resources in turn until they eventually choose to resolve their keys/kickers/Beliefs whatever and then you have your climax.
    You'd need a resource systems that rewards dealing with character issues based on the level of trouble it causes them. Keys seem perfect for this.
