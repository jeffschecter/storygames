    Well, you asked, so:

    \n
    \n

    Considering it'll have a thumbprint size in the GenCon book, and we're not staking marketing careers on pushing it on Coke cans or something, I think it totally fits the purpose.

    \n
    \n

    This is saying "Oh, well, it doesn't have to be good anyway."

    \n

    Here's how logos work:

    \n

    1: They catch the eye. They do this with shape, mostly, with color secondarily. That's because a) the color will appear to be a different color depending on what's near it, b) it will often appear in black and white because that's how it's been printed, and c) only the center of your eye sees color, so if you want it to grab eyes, you have to shoot for the non-fovea parts.

    \n

    2: They become recognizable. The best logos activate the structures in your brain that recognize faces. AT&T, Pepsi, the Hammer and Sickle, Go Play, they're all roughly face-shaped and use asymmetry to become more than just a shape.

    \n

    3: They should evoke, but needn't state directly, what the product is. Logos generate a feeling; they aren't ads in and of themselves. They're to be associated with whatever ad you use. It's like a comic; here's a guy's face (the logo). Here's what the guy's saying (the content).

    \n

    And here's an important thing about graphic design: "I like it" is not a reason to do something. Liking a design is tangential to the power it has. If it's powerful, people will recognize it and like it because of what they associate it with. If they like the logo but it's not powerful, it won't do anything. Just like a guy with a weak chin is less likely to be taken seriously than a guy with a strong chin, the qualities of the product's face has a lot to say about how the content will be accepted.

    \n

    And, finally, here's one of the most important lessons I've learned as a graphic designer: never get everyone's opinion. That will lead to the least offensive logo, and that doesn't matter. If we made the Games on Demand logo an exploding baby, everyone would object and no one would ever forget it. Since you want to alienate fewer people than that would, I don't recommend it, but asking everyone for their opinion will lead you away from the ideas where you're stretching, and those are the ones that will work. "I don't like it" is often code for "I noticed it", and "I like it" is often code for "It doesn't offend me."

    \n With the black bar above DEMAND nearly but not quite touching the word, you're creating tension in the logo, which you really don't want because you're trying to promote GoD as fun, not as something that causes anxiety. Plus, when printing this on a variety of items -- possible t-shirts, banners, fliers, and the like -- the very thin green line between DEMAND and the black bar may be lost on some of the signage and present on othere.

    I'd either let the black of the DEMAND overlap or bleed into the black bar or increase the spacing between DEMAND and the back bar. I'm assuming that the main usage of this logo will be on a banner in a crowded room to get people to come to your location. In that case, you'll want readability to be a factor. That post where you used three logos? Shrink your text down to the DEMAND on that one, leaving plenty of green space around top and bottom, making certain to leave enough space on the top to make the word stand out. However, don't use the font in the third logo. It will be illegible across the room.

    The GAMES floating in white has that stand out -- I'd divorce that from the black bar as well. The main thing you're selling is GAMES. ON DEMAND is a qualifier, so visually have them separated. In the post with three logos, the first logo has GAMES equal to DEMAND. When catching the eye of your customer, you want to promote GAMES primarily. In the third one, the word that will be most visible across the room is ON. This doesn't inform anyone. The second one needs some minor tweaks. Even though the font may be seen as boring, the purpose of your signage is for people to find your location, not to attract people to a brand. Keep the boring, legible font. I'll work up a revision based on your original in a sec.

    What I've done here is a fast modification of your initial design, as you can tell by the rough drop shadows for GAMES. Unfortunately, I don't have the font used for that word, so when I moved the word up from the black line, the lower part of the text was cut off.

    The thing that really bothered me is your logo for GAMES would be unreadable from across the room. Manually coloring in the drop shadow to solid black from the green you've chosen helps that stand out and calls shifts attention to GAMES.

    If you go with a logo like this, remember that if you want to shift to a darker green color, you'll start to make the GAMES into a blur. If you do that, remove the drop shadow from GAMES. You'll also want to but a white stroke around DEMAND to let it pop out a bit. Black on dark colors is too hard to read, almost as bad as red text on black.

    \n The other thing you'll want to consider are colors. Are you choosing green because it's the color of Go Play? If you are, you've got the wrong green. The Go Play green that was created by the SG folks is right around CMYK 83/19/100/5, yours is 55/0/100/0. Go Play green is a darker green and you'd want to trap around the DEMAND if you go with that green, black, and white for your logo.

    This banner will be for a game room in GenCon, correct? Do you have any images of the room from last year? If there were other logos and banners and whatnot around the room, look at their color combinations and try to pick something that will stand out from the others. If this will go on a wall, look at the wall coloration. On a booth, look for the booth drapery colors.

    Here's a quick look at color psychology: http://psychology.about.com/od/sensationandperception/a/colorpsych.htm Check out the color links in the table on that page. Green is a calming color, but it's also a color associated with madness. Go figure.

    Posted By: Andy...Considering it'll have a thumbprint size in the GenCon book...
    \nThat's another consideration. You'll be providing a one-color logo (that's a black and white logo) to be printed in the GenCon book, so you'll want a logo reads well with and without color. Back to Kat's three logo post, that first one in the program book will have GAMES rendered in outline and shrunk down to an unreadable size. All that you'll probably see in the book is ON DEMAND, and that informs no one. Drop shadows like the one on my revision will help a bit, but you're still looking at a lot of white space and a thin line when the image is shrunk to about one inch across and printed in one color on newsprint. Because you have a printing solution that is that small, I would strongly recommend that you not use drop shadows on GAMES.
