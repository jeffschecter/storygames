\n I won't get into a long essay on character death, TPK pros/cons/history. I'll just jump straight into the thought I had, based on playing too much Resident Evil 5, Persona and Pixeljunk Monsters lately.

These games are very tactical, especially on higher difficulties. When you die in the game and get that game over screen, it sucks... and yet, it's awesome, because you know exactly what you're going to see: If you replay that encounter, the enemies appear and act in pretty much the same way, so you can prepare differently, try different equipment or tactics, make better use of the playing field, etc. Get better at the encounter, rather than the encounter being packed up and shut away if you fail.

Some thoughts, based on console RPG conventions:
*If a character totally dies but the others survive, the "dying" character is left with 1 HP and no XP for the encounter, even if he defeated like half the enemies before he went down.
(note, this wouldn't work in EoD, where character death works differently, but would for 4e I think).

*If there's a TPK: Game Over. The players can either decide to leave it at that (and roll up new characters), or to replay the encounter. If they replay it, *the GM changes nothing*. No weaker monsters, "stupid" tactics, etc. He plays hard again, but the players, knowing what to expect, can plan differently. In 4e of EoD, this would represent manuvering in new ways, using the landscape, knowing where likely stronger NPCs are so they can be addressed tactically, etc. In doing so, maybe a penalty is imposed: The players get less XP (half?) if they make it through, or fewer items/treasure.

*If there's something in the middle (some characters fall, others survive) the players can take a unanimous vote to replay the encounter from the start. But at some kind of penalty.

I wouldn't want to use this in a game without a lot of tactical depth to it (like L5R, Shadowrun, CoC, etc: Stand toe to toe and trade blows until one falls; or roll attacks and dodges with high dice, little terrain/tactical effects, again until one side falls). This idea is more for games with some sort of tactics and strategy, where redoing an encounter means more planning/strategy than "hoping for higher die rolls/luck this time around".

-Andy

    \n Cool ideas!

    Speaking for Empire of Dust, it's pretty much designed with this in mind. As written, it's pretty much TPK-proof. FPS video games were a big inspiration and one of the things that I noticed is that you never really *die* in those games, the game just smacks you on the wrist and says "you suck and did not stand up to my challenge!" Of course this pisses you off and makes you more determined to take another shot at whatever the challenge was. The permanent injuries in EoD are meant to be the slap on the wrist (that pisses you off without taking your character from you) and the defeat table is specifically set-up to help you work a "re-do" into the fiction (without having the feeling that the GM is just remorsefully holding your hand after his NPCs stomped you). In practice, many of the "you get captured" results tend to play out exactly like a "re-do" because you skip past the boring shit and pick up when the PCs have a chance to escape (which usually involves a running fight with the same or similar opponents). I guess the difference is that it's worked into the fiction rather than being totally "meta."

    On a related side-note. Years ago I played in a friend's Rifts game where he used "save points" that we could go back to if we didn't like the way things turned out. He ended up abandoning the idea after he realized that the game lost a lot of its mystery and tension (we would have missed out on so much cool stuff that came from "negative" events). Of course, that game was more heavily centered on exploration. I think the idea is much more viable in a tactically focused game.

    \n I just played a game of Red Box D&D last week, and was surprised by how this quality was already present in the game, albeit in a different form:

    The DM had written up a pretty major dungeon, complete with monsters, treasure, and so on. A group of brave (well, actually not-so-brave) adventurers would go into the dungeon, and sooner or later they would either a) all die, or b) retreat to the town with whatever was left of the party. In either case, then new characters were rolled for anyone who had died, those new characters were hired as mercenaries by the remaining "adventurers" and the group would enter the dungeon again.

    I don't recall anyone ever making it to second level, so there weren't any issues of "character balance" (there's very little "balance" in a 'roll 3d6 for each stat' game anyway, except that everyone's pretty unlikely to survive for too long).

    What made it interesting, however, is that stuff would *change* while the group retreated to the town. Some other group of people was quickly removing treasure from the dungeon in some way (I didn't get to find out, but the other players knew about it), meaning that each such "reset" meant fewer potential gains. Monsters that had been routed from parts of the dungeon might reclaim territory, or even arm themselves with weapons left behind by dead adventurers.

    This dynamic was fun--the new group would go in with a sketchy map of what they remembered from "last time", if there had been any survivors--but, of course, Red Box D&D has a very different type of tactical environment, and part of the appeal is that encounters can be played out VERY quickly.

    All that said, I feel like replaying an encounter over again in any game *exactly the same way* sounds like a bit of bore, at least for the GM. I feel it would be far more interesting to play things out again with a twist. After all, the players now know what to expect, have an idea of the opposition's strengths and weaknesses, etc. They are going to adjust their tactics and try to take advantage of the situation. I think it would be far more interesting if *the opposition did the same*. They, too, would adjust their tactics for the "second match". That way, there is some interest and some surprise in it for everybody.

    I think that in most games, the successful side (in this case, the DM/GM) has less incentive to modify their tactics anyway. On top of that, it's more work for the GM/DM to do so, especially in the middle of a session. So, I think the overall dynamic of the players being the drivers of change in tactics would still be there. But I like the option for the GM/DM to throw in a twist.

    Note, also, that the Red Box D&D-style "roll up new characters, face the dungeon again" is different in that the players may choose to face the same encounter once more, but don't have to. They can try another branch of the tunnel and try to get at the treasure another way.

    Could the only choice being to replay the exact same encounter be a bit of bore? It seems that there should be other options, at the very least.

    \n In a third edition game I ran we had the "Trial and Error Experience system". It was a solo game, so the small party (one PC plus one NPC sidekick) threw off the difficulty estimates of the system. It was also a gestalt game (basically each character had two classes that advanced together), which threw off the difficulty estimates int he other direction. Consequently, we never had any idea if a given encounter would be really, really easy or really, really hard.


    So the system went like this: every time the main PC died, we reset to just before the encounter and the PC went up a level. Then we tried it again. Dying by levelling was in fact the only way to go up in level, so I as GM tried to make encounters extra difficult, but not impossible by any means. so the PC dying and levelling happened about 5 (?) times over the course of the game. The new abilities and new tactics meant the second time through the encounter went a lot better for the PC.

    After some iterations of this, we built in story reasons for how it happened. One explanation used a little bit was stolen from Lone wolf and Cub: the PC would imagine the fight in her head, and see how it could go wrong. So she'd do something different (the second version of the fight, which became true in game). Later on, the deaths turned out to be alternate timelines, and the PC's survival was tied to having an Epic Destiny. She couldn't die before fulfilling her big purpose, you see.
