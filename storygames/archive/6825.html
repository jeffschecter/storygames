\n Having moved to the San Francisco bay area just a couple of months ago for work, I'm currently looking for a role-playing group. I've been using http://nearbygamers.com to find people.

I'm thinking of it like dating, right? Step 1: Meet someone on internet, talk, figure out if you want to meet them in real life. Step 2: Meet in real life, get to know each other, get something to eat, talk about what you want out of gaming / relationships. Step 3: Plan a campaign or a one-shot, Step 4: Go Play! (I leave it to your imagination what step 4 might be in the dating analogy...)

But just like dating, sometimes you get into a really bad scene. You're clearly not compatible at all, but the other person doesn't realize it. You don't want to see them again; you don't even want to finish dinner (or character creation), you just want to Get Out Now! But you don't want to be a jerk about it.

Last weekend I had a pretty bad experience like this. Partly I want to vent about it a little, but more constructively, I want to talk about two questions:

1. What are the warning signs that help you recognize that the group or game that you're about to join will not be fun for you?

2. What's the most diplomatic way to decline participation? (How about if you've already created a character?)

    \nCall me nuts, but what's wrong with, "Folks, I was kinda lookin' for something __________, and it looks like y'all [dig | don't dig] _____. Gimme a call if you're ever wanting to try out some __________; but I've pretty much gotten tired of _____ over the years and want to branch out."

    Kinder than my last girlfriend's "we're just too different" vagueness. Which left a bit of a "Wow, rather be alone than with me?" feeling that is unsavory, at best--give a reason why and it's usually all good. If the reason why requires pointing out an odious flaw, then console yourself with the fact that negative social feedback generally results in positive change... or killing sprees.

    So... to plug in one deal-breaker from the above, at random:
    "Folks, I was kinda lookin' for something less stank of piss, and it looks like y'all don't dig bathing. Gimme a call if you're ever wanting to try out some personal hygiene; but I've pretty much gotten tired of cat piss over the years and want to bug out." Yes, some verbs might require tweaking in your final MadLib Exit Line.

    You know, be diplomatic and shit. Honesty rarely hurts feelings for long... and besides, who gives a toss what these CroMagnons think, once you're out the door?

    \n To answer my own question 1, now...

    First warning sign for me is when nobody is talking to each other about anything outside the game. Last weekend: the GM has just invited several complete strangers over to his house, and after "hello my name is" and shaking hands he wants to jump straight into generating characters for a long-term campaign. I mean, socially, this is pretty weird, right? Even if some role-players don't mind it (when I was a little younger, and less aware of social interactions, I wouldn't have noticed the weirdness), how am I supposed to know whether I even want to role-play with these people or not when I have no idea who they are yet?

    In this case, the GM was using his status as GM and host to sort of totally dominate the pace of the conversation, so there was no chance for me to even try to start up a conversation with the other players about who they were or anything outside the game. It made me pretty uncomfortable. It was also pretty clear he expected significant buy-in to a long-term campaign (committing to a certain time each week, making characters suitable for long-term play) without even asking whether that's what the rest of us wanted or not.

    If this had been for a one-shot at a convention I might have felt differently, but this was at the GM's house and he was clearly expecting us to commit to a long game without getting to know each other. I felt a little like I had met someone on a first date and was immediately asked to pick out a wedding dress.

    \n If you can find a gaming club or other regular gathering where you can get to know folks casually, that's a plus. Whether or not you can do that, once you're to the point of auditioning (or auditioning for) a group, ask lots of specific questions about playstyle and tone. Discuss what kind of character options would work for the game. Do not allow yourself to project your own prejudices onto what they say. If the detailed and specific information you get from these discussions doesn't feel good to you, don't go for it. Alternately, if you haven't been able to get detailed and specific information out of them, don't go for it; while it's possible to find a great group that's not comfortable talking explicitly about goals and expectations, it's not very likely.

    Also, be very honest and detailed about what your expectations are, and what kind of game you would like to participate in. Give them plenty of opportunity to tell you that your kind of roleplaying sounds stupid and lame.

    As to question 2, just keep it simple, and keep it about yourself. "I don't think I'm a good fit for your group, so I'm going to bow out." You are not some holy prophet come out of the desert, who must bring truth to the people. You don't need to tell people what's wrong with their game.

    \n Hey, guys: Thanks for all the advice; I should clarify that I've already bowed out of this particular game.

    The game was D&D 3.5, with numerous house rules, in the Planescape setting.
    The house rules were another warning sign: the GM had an entire 3-ring binder full of pages and pages of his house rules, neatly printed out, and he presented them as non-negotiables: not "What do you think of using these house rules?" but rather "In my campaign, we do it like this..."

    The only reason I stuck around as long as I did is because I really, really love the Planescape setting, and I've run it several times but have never gotten to be a player in it, and here was a group of people all apparently enthused about the setting. So after the first session (which was all character-creation and campaign-prep), even though I was thinking that I would probably not enjoy this GM's way of running things, I decided I was willing to give it a chance, so I came back for the second session, thinking I'd make a decision whether to continue or not after I had played with these people a little bit.

    Went back for the second session. There was still no talking about anything outside the game, and the GM made it clear that we still weren't going to be playing because there was still more prep to be done. "Maybe your characters are finished in your minds, but they're not done to my satisfaction yet", he said. That attitude was another sign that I was not going to get along with the GM on either a how-the-game-is-run level or on a pure personality level. It seemed like he was very committed to "big design up front" -- as in, deciding everything in preparation rather than in play, on both sides of the screen. My suggestion that we leave some parts of our characters' backgrounds sketchy, start the game in medias res, and fill in missing stuff later with flashbacks was met with blank stares from all around. My suggestion that I'd rather not track encumbrance was met with an "I'll track it for you, then" from the GM.

    I don't want this thread to turn into "OMG they were having BadWrongFun", but this game was starting to sound about as fun to me as doing homework. The rest of the players were in full blown "Hey, can I have this particular ability from an obscure supplement?" mode.

    So anyway, the GM asked me what options I was gonna take from his House Rules list of extra combat options (cuz D&D3.5 isn't already complicated enough int that regard?), and that's when I said "You know what... I don't mean to be rude, but... I don't think I'm going to be joining this campaign after all. I don't think I'm a good match for this group's play style."

    Awkward silence. One player: "I didn't know we had a play style yet."

    What I wanted to say, but did not say: "Look guys, I can already feel a personality clash between me and the GM here, so if I stay in, I guarantee there's eventually going to be a power struggle between us that will destroy the game for everyone else."

    What I actually said: "Um, so... yeah... it's not that I don't like y'all, but I don't think I'm very compatible with this group, and besides, six PCs is an awful lot so it might be better if I bow out... there's another campaign I'm thinking of joining instead... Good luck, I hope you all have fun, bye."

    I felt like it was a pretty lame series of excuses, but I made the right decision.

    1. What are the warning signs that help you recognize that the group or game that you're about to join will not be fun for you?
    \nOnce you are at the table/scene, you got to give it a chance and accept whatever surprises the other part comes up with. Trust that this will be a good experience. If you look for errors or excuses to back out from the beginning, it is very difficult to build trust.

    If we are talking about how to screen a game group before you have commited to play: Ask them about recent gaming experiences that they enjoyed. And why it was fun.\n
    2. What's the most diplomatic way to decline participation? (How about if you've already created a character?)
    \nAfter giving the fun a fair chance to happen - say 1-2 hours - then it is ok to go meta. Step out of the fiction, check with everyone: So how do you think about this? Try to be very specific and ask about something that has happened at the table. If you then feel that the mood at the table is not moving in a satisfying direction for you, then you can say: Ok, I have tried this game. It was fun to xxx. However, I don't want to play any more of this. Is it ok for you, if I quit?

    I have once been too polite to skip a game that was not happening for me. I sat for 10 hours and followed the GM railroad the plot. I will never do this again.

    Almost all of my gaming experiences with new players have had moments during the startup of the game, where I have considered to back out. By staying put I have had some of my best gaming experiences.

    Posted By: JonoAnybody know any good ways of explaining "I want to avoid creative agenda incoherence" in a comprehensible and non-holy-desert-prophet-like way?
    \nIf you're trying to convey that you're specifically in to big model narrativism, no - that's a pretty complicated idea to convey accurately in a sentence. You can say that you prefer to play with a focus on shared authorship and collaborative storytelling, but the difficulty, in my experience, is that most railroading GMs think that their games are built on shared authorship.

    Some of what you said was about what you didn't like, which was basically pre-plotted, fudge-heavy games with no player autonomy. If you want to convey that positively, i.e. as what you do like, you could say that you like sandbox-style play, where events and encounters are not planned in advance.

    Ultimately, a lot of this comes down to what I said earlier - describe what you want out of gaming in detail and at length, and give the other players plenty of opportunity to tell you that it sounds dumb. That will tell you what you want to know. Trying to come up with a shorthand is largely counterproductive, because in most cases, players who are not familiar with the language will just find ways to interpret it as referring to the way they play.

    \n"Your kind of fun is not my kind of fun."

    If they want to understand what your kind of fun is, throw a one shot of e.g. PTA. After that, everyone will be much more able to discuss player vs. GM balance, story now vs. story before, scene framing, conflict resolution etc. Then they may very well end up deciding that your kind of fun is not their kind of fun.

    This approach has worked with my long time gaming buddies. We are now able to talk about what particular features of a game that we like and don't like.

    1. What are the warning signs that help you recognize that the group or game that you're about to join will not be fun for you?
    \nEasiest, is if you get through 1-2 sessions and aren't having fun.

    In my group, frex, about I dunno 5-6 years ago, Rafael Chandler (Dread:TFBOP) came to my weekly game session. Unfortunately, it was when one of the other players, not me, was DMing D&D 3e, and unfortunately, he wasn't a very good DM. That is to say, he's the kind who, if you do something unexpected, he'll do whatever it takes to try to get you back on the path of the adventure. If you Must Do X to get to the next area/scene/end, then by god you HAVE to find and do X. He's creative, which is why we let him DM from time to time, but too long and things start to break down.

    Anyway, Rafael ended up rolling up a rogue. The DM didn't think to include him until like 2 hours into the game, instead looking for a "good place" for him to fit into the story. Also, the DM didn't know that Raf was "trying out" our group, not coming in this one session and thus committed to a long-haul campaign (differences in expectations). Raf's rogue jumped into action and tried to stab an NPC (basically a mob boss) because the NPC was holding information, threatening us, etc. In D&D you might not do that, but in any action movie? You'd totally do it. But unfortunately, rolls were made, weight put on the player, and the rogue got punked quickly, and the story resumed course. In the end, the style that he wanted wasn't there in our group under that DM, and it didn't work out (see below).\n
    2. What's the most diplomatic way to decline participation? (How about if you've already created a character?)
    \n"Crap, at work I've been assigned to a new project which is taking up more of my off-work hours than I can control. I'm really very sorry, but it looks like I'm going to have to back out for now. This totally caught me off guard."

    A white lie, everyone saves face. Some folks disapprove of white lies, and would suggest that you instead "help them improve themselves" by, as you walk out of their session, you break it down for the GM bluntly what it is you don't like about him, the group, the play, etc". Personally, I don't like to be confrontational, it's too delicate a situation to balance without looking or acting like a dick. For me, giving them criticism is something you'd do if you plan on sticking it out, not if you plan on bailing.

    In the above example, Raf bowed out, saying he had an early work day tomorrow. Later, he wrote us saying that work picked up and he won't be able to come. Then, on the side, to me, he said "Holy fuck that was boring. I seriously can't commit to that, man, it was driving me fucking nuts." I was thinking that he might have more fun under me or another GM, but unfortuantely that campaign (which got more interesting as it went on) was to go on for a while, so we didn't press the issue. Rafael's excuse didn't offend me one whit, and I didn't reveal that white lie to the DM in order to "straighten him out" or whatever. Later on I gave some pointers, after adventures were over, but never a lecture.

In this case, I think the onus of leaving courteously falls upon the person who has the issue with the play, not with the group who's fine with the player's contributions.

    Also Niel's comments and the follow-on comments about honesty? Spot on. In both of my most prominent cases, a little up-front honesty would have eased my own sense of insult.

    \n Comments about how to drop 'constructive' hints while leaving a game group remind me of this story:

    \n
