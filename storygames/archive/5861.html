\n I may be way off the mark here, but I was just looking through some older games the other day and got to reading my boxed set of D&D Diablo II edition, which I believe came out in the 90s, and I thought "This is Fourth Edition." Defined character classes with point-cost special abilities, monsters with limited stats and one or two special attack abilities, magic weapons that can be generated based upon key words, the use of miniatures and maps for tactical fights... isn't this what I've been reading on the net and hearing on the official podcast?

And I think the D&D Diablo II game is rather cool how it runs you through a tabletop version of the video game, so I am already pretty partial to it and think it holds up well over time. So, again, am I off the mark here?

    Posted By: merb101And the D&D guys are just taking two popular character types from different settings and making them core classes. Can't fault them for that.
    \nI can fault them for moving the game closer and closer to a "Race = Character Archetype" sort of gross over-simplification, the racial monocultures become racial monopersonalities, with every member of a race essentially the same character. The Tiefling, for example, is being prompted as the race fro players who want to play anti-heroic good-guys-in-black-hats. That annoys me, because it makes the Tiefling even less realistic. It also limits creativity. New players will be lead to believe that if they want to play a dour, no nonsense, stubborn character they have to play a Dwarf, and if they want to play a wise-cracking antiauthoritarian libertine they have to play a Tiefling, etc.

    Pretty much everything about 4E doesn't appeal to me. If I wanted to play MMORPG, I'd play World of Warcraft. The more and more D&D tries to emulate computer games, the less and less it embraces the very things that make it different than video games, and the more and more it approaches the horizon over which lays Epic Fail. If D&D becomes a slow, clunky MMORPG that takes ten times as long to set up, costs ten times as much to get started, and doesn't even offer cool shiny graphics, then what's the point of D&D? Why not just play a real MMORPG?

    Oh well, one good thing has come out of 4E. I finally got off my ass and checked out what else was on the market, and discovered the Indie Revolution.

    \n I'm thrilled with the direction 4E is going...I don't care so much about the races / "setting" stuff because that's pretty easy to change and will probably be among the first wave of supplements that 3rd parties come up with.
    \n
    \nBut its clear to me (at first blush anyway) that they've finally gotten a firm handle on "what is D&D; about" and have ruthlessly purged all of the tangential noise.
    \n
    \nOf COURSE its going to look more like Diablo and MMORPGs...for the simple reason that there are more people playing MMORPGs...right now at this instant...than who've ever played D&D; in their lives (and if you count Korean's...that's not even much of a hyperbole).
    \n
    \nBut I think 4E looks very much like the sort of game us Indie gamers can get excited to see. I don't know whether the 4E team would eagerly embrace...reluctantly concede...or vehemently deny being influenced by Indie-RPGs...but from what I've seen 4E could easily be a gamist indie design.
