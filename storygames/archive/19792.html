    \n The short answer is: no technique can do that.

    The X-card is an insufficient, but worthwhile, attempt. It's a resource to make it less dangerous to play with strangers, but not a panacea. I'm playing a certain game with someone I'm intimate with, we already know each other's boundaries quite well, and though the rules of the game mandate we have X-cards in front of us, we never get to use them - we're never going.

    The X-card is analogous to safe words in Nordic larping (I can look up links if you're interested): an emergency brake for when shit has already happened, but it's not yet so bad that we can't manage to pull a brake anymore. This accounts for such a narrow range of uses that the conventional wisdom, since years, has been that safe words don't work. Still, no larpwright in their right mind would do away with them: rather, those are considered to be a baseline and starting point from which to improve, experiment with additional techniques for "player safety" and "de-fucking". Most such techniques that I know of involve "buddies" and are specific to the reality of larping, where the main issue is: you can't usually stop the whole game to deal with one player's problems. Stopping and resuming the game is a non-issue in tabletop rpgs, fortunately.

    I wouldn't think of any of the above as techniques for setting boundaries, because establishing a road map through repeated use of an emergency brake doesn't sound like the best idea. The role of the X-card, to me, is more about affirming that we care, as a group, and this goes a long way about establishing an emotionally safer environment.

    Slightly more useful for setting boundaries are the now classic concepts of "lines and veils", as long as they're known and used proactively. When the fiction is going someway that you expect something you're uncomfortable with to be a plausible outcome of, declare you're drawing a line. Do it before you're actually there. Call for veils liberally when the fictional facts concern something you don't want to hear the minute details of, and do it before some other players starts reveling into it.
    Of course, this is attention consuming, and therefore best reserved for things you really have some issue with, 'cause you're on edge about those no matter what.

    For fine-tuning preferences, I like a technique such as the "Try a different way" ritual phrase in Archipelago.

    In the end, though, I believe you can only reliably "set boundaries" the long, hard way: by getting to know your co-players over time, both through playing together and other social occasions, personal conversations, developing relationships and an actual friendship. If you're playing with real, intimate friends you can (as a side-benefit of you relationship) play with your boundaries and push them, knowing exactly where to stop. When you're playing with strangers, well, that's also fun and an opportunity to get to know each other, but basically you have to behave yourself and play safe, to estimate your and others' boundaries very conservatively.

    \n A fundamental precept of the X-card and similar tools -- as I understand them -- is that justification is not required. You don't have to explain why -- you don't have to say 'no dogs: I have a phobia of dogs because of this time I was a kid and...' you just say 'no dogs: please.' And that's that.

    And even in groups without a specific tool like that, I think 'no dogs, please' should be a worthy/adequate request. Yes, it's possible someone might deduce that you have a phobia of dogs, but they might also guess you had a dog who died recently, or any number of other things. Some people might push for an explanation, at which point I think 'I don't really want to get into it, I just don't want any dogs, if that's okay' is the way to go. And if that's not enough, then this isn't the group for you.

    I mean if you are actually unable to say the word or describe the thing even in general terms, then it gets harder, for sure. If I was in that kind of situation, I would consider printing out a statement in advance, either to hand to someone or to read directly from (if that will make it easier), OR trying to have the discussion by email beforehand (possibly using a pre-written statement, again.)

    Or I mean the truly ideal situation is probably having a friend you play games with, who can come along and can advocate on your behalf -- or I mean, even if they just like, drop you off at the game and stay to have a chat about it with the group, or whatever.     \n This is a pretty tricky design problem - how to actually and concretely avoid taking the game to unacceptable territories when it is not possible to chart those territories in advance. You'll know when you've erred, but then it's too late. This has nothing to do with whose fault it is or who "should" be or act differently, but it is possible that what is being asked here is simply impossible for strangers to accomplish together. I could credit the idea that the only reason we manage to not offend and hurt each other in regular freely expressive play of rpgs is that people are usually not very easy to hurt by the random give and take; take away that default state (however "default" it may be in reality), and it is questionable whether play is possible at all.

    (Design thought experiment time: let's say that we're playing a roleplaying game together, but in this game there's a secret topic ban, and you lose if you mention or reference the banned thing. It could be "superheroes", "Cthulhu mythos" or "Monty Python", you don't know because it was chosen randomly in advance and is only known to the referee observing the game. What does the rest of the game look like, and does it have any rules or processes surrounding the topic ban, perhaps to help players avoid stumbling upon it accidentally?)

    It's not a solution to the general case of how to accomplish play with any group, any time, but were I regularly experiencing anxiety with new groups, I would maybe explore a gradual, consciously shy strategy of learning to play with a certain set of people, with the goal of building towards roleplaying together. In other words, first learn to know the people so much that you can talk with them about your personal foibles, and only then play self-expressive games like roleplaying games together. Perhaps start by watching movies and playing boardgames together, then move on to talking about personal rpg history, and then if it seems like this particular group of people might enjoy playing together, try to play something simple and predictable. Treat roleplaying more like an act of intimacy than an open social event?

    Playing with close friends should help in two ways: they're less likely to offend each other's sensibilities, and when they do they're easier to forgive.

    Moving on to a different approach, I think that Ricardo mentioned an interesting and historically viable strategy: play a conservative game with a strong GM. D&D, particularly, has an impressive history as the go-to game for culturally disparate groups and socially dubious situations, and it has generally braved these challenges by being limited enough that it rarely offends anybody. This heritage can easily be seen today in how D&D is often contrasted with new story games: people who dislike and perhaps fear the idea of potentially hurtful and offensive game topics discuss D&D as a safe, harmless alternative that preemptively promises meaningless entertainment. "I don't need pretentious politics in my elf-games" is a typical example of the sort of rhetoric that comes up, with the implication that D&D is "just a game of pretending to be dwarves and elves", entirely harmless. And that is the truth, if you play it that way!

    So what I'm thinking is, if there is an unknown mine field of prohibited content that could ruin the game out there, one strategy I might opt for would be to play a game with a simple, convention-based fiction, one that is unlikely to stray. Nobody needs to chart the minefield or warn anybody away from it if the game is not likely to stray there anyways. The right game might not be D&D, depending on where the mine field lays, but the idea is the same as the one we see in conservative corporate D&D: highly convention-based, limited to official modes of expression, and so on.

    A related idea would be to play with a group that is unlikely to press yours or anybody else's buttons. Playing with children works wonders in this regard, although experience tells me that a certain percentage of roleplayers are socially oblivious enough that they'll still do their off-color joking when you've got a couple of nieces or whatever at the table. That's only the more basement-grown fringe, most gamers will surely clean up their act in the presence of minors.

    \n Regarding the use of the GM in this context, I largely agree with Rafu's points about the weaknesses of the method. Don't sell it short, though - the strength of the GM filter is not in short term tactical work, but rather in instituting a longer-term campaign ethos that better accords with the standards the GM perceives as befitting a hobby worthy of their attention. (I express it that way because I'd like to say "hobby befitting of freemen", or as a better English translation a "hobby befitting gentlepeople", but I recognize that different people self-identify in different social groups, so ultimately it's just a matter of asking what manner of behaviour you deem acceptable for yourself and your own peer group. Are e.g. sexist jokes really up to your standards as a group, that's the question, no matter why your standards might be whatever they are.)

    As a concrete example of how the GM may well be a powerful tool in building up the community standards, an anecdote from a couple years back when I did a lot of GMing for mixed-gender teenager groups for various reasons:

    Finland has a rich linguistic history of swearing, and it is perhaps somewhat more acceptable for a wider range of social situations than it is in English - more of a rhetorical emphasis thing than "I'm a crude redneck" thing. (I personally suspect that this is an outcome of the republican/egalitarian political culture here; it's more acceptable for people to own up to earthly cultural ancestry and there's less pressure to try to pass for higher class. But I digress.) There is a wide range of more or less crude and unsavoury swear words for your delectation, and naturally their acceptability and stylistic range change with politics and demographics - different age groups and social groups use different terminology.

    Now, one particularly annoying feature of traditional youth language in Finland is the use of homophobic terminology as swear words. I'm particularly keen about this myself, as I grew up with this vocabulary, and still use it by accident, despite not liking it myself anymore. It's a strange feeling to have an aesthetic tool-set that I like in terms of poetics and good phraseology of the language, but that I detest in terms of its subtext. It's just not cool to my mind to call something "homo" when you mean that it's flawed or built wrong, even if it rings well rhetorically.

    (Out of general interest in swearing culture, it might be interesting to note that in my particular sociocultural box religious swears have maintained their ironical 20th century position as old rhetorical standbys, while the homosexual and racist terminology has seen the sort of downturn in acceptability that I describe above on my own part. My sense is that most of us in my generation strive to replace those politically unacceptable ranges of expression with scatological and sexual swears - I particularly favour "vittu" - "cunt" - myself as a homely, harmless and life-affirming alternative.)

    OK, so this homophobic swearing culture came up a couple years back when I was GMing for a bunch of teenagers, and I had an opportunity to squash it from a position of authority because I was the GM, the gatekeeper of the game, the elder statesman of the proceedings, and so forth. It was a teenager boy using a particularly strong homophobic swear-word in a purely rhetorical context (not directed at anyone real or imagined, but the particular term is an old rhetorical standby in the local lexicon), in passing, without any particular thought put to it. I even used the "not at my table" authority talk that I usually never draw on in gaming. Didn't make a big deal of it or get angry or anything, I just explained what I said above, that this terminology had grown to bother me over the years, and let's pay attention to whether we really want to use it - remember that you never know if any of your friends might be homosexual themselves, so there's clear and present risk in gratifying our poetic senses in this regard, even if we mean "nothing" by it.

    So my point here with this rambling anecdote is this: during the next four years of playing with these particular youths, seeing them go through their high school, I never once heard bigoted swearing from them. (This is somewhat exceptional for Finnish youth culture, although hopefully not so much in the future.) I don't really consider this any major feat in defense of liberal values, as the people in question are quite liberal themselves as far as I know, so it was just a minor matter of terminology adjustment. The point is that it was easy, it stuck long-term, and it proved that a GM can quite effortlessly do fine-tuning on the ethos of their game table. I didn't even need to hesitate about lecturing the other players, being the authority figure at the table just made it natural for me to wield the power to set standards to my liking.

    That being the context for thinking about the GM as a possible tool in setting limits and standards, I can't help but think about whether a sensitive GM who strives for a generally safe and welcoming environment, and pays particular attention to whether all players are enjoying themselves, might be an effective tool for the kind of socio-psychological situation Sandra is facing here. The GM has notable advantages against the X-card, note: he's powerful and doesn't need to hesitate to use power and draw attention to himself, for instance.

    From a player perspective the "use a strong GM to set the standards" might work best by playing with a trusted GM who you are comfortable sharing your difficulties with. This isn't a complete solution as to what to do at a convention game or such, but I feel like it is entirely viable at a home game. I've myself in the past been that strong GM who looks after shy, more uncertain and vulnerable players, and to me it's seemed like it's worked pretty well at times. Specifically: when a player says something accidentally offensive, it's much easier socially when the GM can be the one to ask them to drop that line of talk. There's no need to even explain, you don't need to tell anybody at the table that this other player here is a basket case who can't handle that sort of joking. A strong GM can take the onus themselves if an explanation is truly warranted and the actual beneficiary of the policy doesn't (yet) feel comfortable airing their foibles openly with the group.

    \n There are activist techniques that are used to assuage social situations and maintain productive group cohesion. A couple of them may be applicable to gaming groups. Among those is the use of Temp Checks, which are periodic pauses during the meeting in order to get a general feel for how everyone is doing at the moment. (Some meetings use assigned "Vibeswatchers" or "Caretakers" whose job is to watch the mood of the room and stop negative processes from continuing, although this is admittedly rather far for the typical gaming group to go.)

    Another valuable technique is Taking Short Breaks from (during) the process (game) itself. Just five minutes is fine. Such shifts in attention and focus can not only give people a chance to relax, but also provide a little distance from which they can discuss what has been happening in-game, or pull each other aside and discuss things privately without interrupting gameplay.

    I know this isn't "solving" the "problem" but I thought I'd throw it out there.

    \n Summarizing what's been suggested so far (because I want to keep track of it all). Here are some ways we've come up with to make safer play environments:

    -Lines/Veils
    -Pre-game "movie rating" or "movie-title-as-indicator-of-content" consensus
    -Trigger warnings
    -X-Card
    -Safe words
    -"Try a different way"
    -"Strong" GM and adventures that have a tight focus on "safe" material
    -Attentive and supportive GM
    -GM's and groups that don't introduce problematic material with strangers
    -Have a buddy who knows your triggers and can advocate for you and/or support you
    -Temp Checks
    -Short breaks from play
    -Group that is sympathetic, like-minded
    -Group of players you know and trust
    -Group that is balanced in terms of gender, race, etc.
    -Group that includes children
    -Games with mechanisms that can provide a dark/despairing mood without needing to rely on sexual violence.
    -Games that are asexual and/or comedic and light and/or focused on other material in such a way that sexual violence would be difficult to introduce during play

    (let me know if I missed something as I was re-reading)


    General consensus of the thread as a whole:

    All of these have value. All of these have limitations. There is no sure method for creating perfectly safe spaces. There may be situations where the best you can do is simply leave the game and/or group.

    I think we've said, basically: No the X-card is not enough. No mechanism is enough. There are lots of things to try, but none -- not even a group of folks you know and trust -- is going to be safe all the time.


    My two cents:

    1. Approaching a definition: A "safe" group is one that attempts to provide as safe an environment as possible using whatever methods are appropriate to the type and style of play the group wants to engage in. Safe groups are clear about their intended subject matter and the methods they use for ensuring player safety. Safe groups are not necessarily safe for all players. (Maybe "safer groups" would be a better term)

    2. I guess one way to get a sense of how safe a group is before playing with them is to ask what methods they use to create a safe environment. If they say "None. It's your own responsibility if you get hurt" or "I don't know, we never thought about it" then you know something right away about the relative safety of the group. If they at least have some answer, then this is an indication that the group cares on some level about player safety.

      \n I think that the theme Sandra and Lisa touched upon there is a good one: despite the discussion tendency to address "safer" games as an unconditional good, it is in fact a creative preference/need/priority. It's the old "nobody gets hurt" vs. "I will not abandon you" dichotomy, basically: for many people the attempt to bring safety measures into the game is basically pissing on their fun, because they want to play with a different social contract. Sort of similar to how biking helmets can be perceived as an unconditional good by some at the same time as others perceive them, and the public insistence on them, to be ruining their biking fun.

      I can say this because I'm usually, left to my own devices, very much a proponent and practitioner of unsafe gaming in nearly all senses of the word. (I say "nearly all" because I've never really seen the point in self-destructive and stupid behavior, so you won't really see me e.g. falling off a roof dead drunk after a D&D session any time soon...) Whether it's a choice about an experimental game that could prove unentertaining, or about spontaneous creative risk that might provoke the other players, or about playing a game that might shake me up emotionally, I find myself gravitating towards what I perceive as the more ambitious, more novel and more exhilarating option. In other words, the problem of how to make a game safer is actually quite novel to me, simply because I usually spend all my time figuring out how to make it more powerful, which is in many ways the opposite of safe.

      (It should not need to be said that while this temperament has made me many like-minded friends over the years, it does mean that I have offended frailer hobbyists on occasion with hard play. I'm trying to be better about it nowadays with new acquaintances, of course; age brings wisdom and so on. Most of the time, with most people, it is possible to discover a creative compromise that satisfies everybody's needs for particular sorts of safety and particular sorts of creativity.)

      It is good to be conscious of this phenomenon, as it explains much of the obscure resistance that safety measures encounter in dialogue and practice. For example, when a thread about this topic gets a reply questioning the necessity and desirability of safety, it's probably not really intended to question the validity of the premise; rather, it's an expression of the opposite creative drive, from a person who enjoys a surfeit of trust and feels annoyed by the mere idea that somebody might want to interrupt their good flow at the game table with an X-card or other safety measure. I feel like this has historically been a bit of an obstacle to dealing with the subject of safety in gaming; the topic has not been taken as seriously as it could be due to the way it is perceived as a creative threat.

      I won't need to look far for a practical example of what creative resistance to safety looks like, as over the last couple of years our local old school D&D has revealed some pretty serious creative differences between players who want D&D to be about risk and challenge, and players who want D&D to be about adventure and (fictional) accomplishment. Basically the question is about safety, as some players truly hate the nihilistic, stark failures of old school D&D, while others revel in the feeling of genuine challenge and emotional stake it brings. Things have progressed to a point where the more extreme wings of the safety-vs-challenge split basically won't play together anymore: the hardcore challenge-addicts grow vaguely bored and contemptuous of the more balanced, toy-like modern D&D, while the softcore players grow terrified of the increasingly sharp, merciless and procedurally clean games run with systems like Mentzer, LotFP and various homebrews.

      (I should clarify that the creative discord I describe regarding our local D&D circles is precisely that, creative - there's no social animosity, just an increasing recognition that when it comes to this highly specific form of gaming, we practically speaking enjoy nearly opposite kinds of play. Causes a lot of debate about the theory of the game, of course, but it's pretty clear that the divide is here to stay.)

      In fact, getting back to the topic of techniques - insofar as I am correct in recognizing that some players around here prefer e.g. Pathfinder or D&D 4th or 5th over less mainstream options because they're safer, then we should be asking what is it that makes them so! It's not an X-card, certainly, and I don't think it's the GM maintaining a more even-keeled game, either. So what is it that makes one D&D table a distressing stress machine, while another is a wish-fulfillment pastime?

      Phrased like that, I think it's pretty obvious that the primary safety strategy of D&D lies in making the content and outcomes of play more predictable while reducing the personal stakes the players have in the outcomes. Specifically, the kind of D&D that is recognized as preferable by my safety-oriented D&D gamer friends features a heavy focus on balanced combat encounters, character builds and essentially linear plots, all of which work to reduce the risks of failure and the consequences of such failure. In other words: players rarely lose in this type of D&D, and if they do, it's not really considered their fault - it was the fault of the GM for misbalancing the encounter, the fault of the game system for being unbalanced, etc. All this means that failure is more rare, and when it occurs it doesn't hit as hard as it would in a game that emphasizes personal responsibility for success.

      It is notable that these structural safety/unsafety strategies do go hand in hand with commensurate content strategies: safe D&D tends to feature literarily, culturally "safe" content - established genre tropes - while unsafe D&D tends to feature unexpected, novel phantasmagorias that might prove surprisingly unsettling as literary ideas in addition to being game mechanically unbalanced.

      In summation, it seems that at least in some cases local gaming culture successfully establishes safe gaming spaces without explicitly asking what any particular individual would consider safe: rather, players gravitate towards games that offer consistent, predictable experiences that are unlikely to be downers. It's a sort of consumer market approach to safety - make games that are safe and fun to experience by the customer.

      I do realize that I somehow managed to ramble back to the point I started with on the last page of the thread - that the choice of an appropriate game goes a long way towards avoiding psychologically difficult game situations. I guess that's my go-to answer on this

      I think that the more high-level you get, the harder it is to make rules without sounding authoritarian
      Precisely. At a certain level the creation of rules for higher-order interactions stops being "a game" and becomes more like the pursuit of a prescriptive or mandatory social praxis. This can push into areas of ideology and even political doctrine, and cannot help but include one's personal, theoretical, habitual, or unprovable ideas about how the world works, or how groups work. The problem is that those ideas - each "correct" in its own right and from a given perspective - can span the entire breadth of human experience and social understanding. They not only differ from culture to culture and group to group, but maybe even from session to session, person to person, or within the same person at different phases in their life. That's a complication.

      One reason established conventions like "Robert's Rules of Order" or "X Cards" are so commonly relied on despite their imperfections is simply the fact that they have a precedence which makes it easier to consider them as "valid" and "given". They're useful because they're already there, instantly available without debate, like the idea of "forming a queue" or "majority rule". Exactly because they are not discussed or brainstormed, but adopted whole-cloth. "Good, now that we know what procedures we're using, we can get on with doing the actual thing we want to do."

      Because only wonks really enjoy spending time talking about the theoretical mechanics of socio-political processes.

      ---

      To complicate matters further, people take totally different stances on the whole idea of playing an RPG at all. One says "This is a relaxing way of having fun" while another says "This is a serious artistic medium and I'm here to perform", plus umpteen other positions, all equally valid from their own perspectives.

      Here, the Auteur GM has an advantage. In the world of Art, we at least have the expectation - the tacit certainty - that the artist is in control of all epistemological stuff (the "rules" of how to understand, operate within, or interact with this particular piece of art). This gives the artist a degree of certainty exceeding even that of Robert's Rules; the artist IS the source of the world depicted by their art, and if anyone should know how you are to experience that art, they would be the one who could tell you. (Although of course, if they don't tell you, you're on your own.)

      ---

      Further complication still: Who exactly is the "artist"? The game designer? The GM?
      I think it's both, plus players as well, but in different ways.

      With whom does the responsibility for creating higher-order social rules lay? I think it trickles down a binary tree: The designer has the first option of advocating or establishing higher-order social rules, and may even implement them within the game mechanics themselves. But if the designer abstains from this, the onus will fall on the GM to do so, and if the GM doesn't either establish such rules or personify them literally (as "rulings"), the onus will fall to the group as a whole - who will also have to come up with procedures for adopting their own new rules!

      This is much easier when you already have established social relations within the group itself. In such a case it can go pretty much unspoken, until a moment of uncertainty arises, and then it can be handled on an ad-hoc basis. Many game systems deliberately eschew meta-rules for this exact reason. More interested in writing narrative toolsets than social rulesets, their authors expect or assume that the individual GM or group will make up their own mind about that stuff. They expect or assume that people who gather in a regular group already have some preferred or habitual methodologies for talking, arguing, and resolving higher-order debates. Even if that methodology is "the GM is always right". At least it's certain. Mussolini made the trains run on time.

      For relative strangers, it is more difficult. Thankfully for such groups, it turns out that meta-rules (like the X card) can indeed be application-agnostic, since they are systems in their own right. You just gotta choose one and make sure everyone sticks to it.

      Final complication, linked to the first: This shit is basically BORING and the opposite of play.

      Invite a newbie to what you promise will be a fun and exciting game, and start off by spending an hour talking about social contracts and shared creative agendas. Explain all the jargon terms such as "lines", "veils", "triggers", "CA", "TA", and "x cards". Explain their histories and justifications. Explain that the group must determine its own procedures and agendas before play can begin. Take votes. Attain consensus. See how many people consider that fun.

      \n I haven't used an X-card, but anonymity would reduce much of my personal skepticism about it. Specifically, my everyday play here in Helsinki mostly happens in such steel-hard macho environs that even proposing the X-card would be somewhat ludicrous. When I consider how I myself would hesitate to actually use the X-card here, despite being a clique leader and social insider, I can only imagine how the sort of introvert, frail or uncertain person who actually needs it would be able to use the X-card in this type of gaming culture. Take the somewhat touchy gamer I discussed earlier in relation to MLwM; the only way he has ever been able to suggest that he's hurt or distressed or angry about something at the game table is by spiraling sullenness (politely ignored by the other players), followed by an emotional breakdown of whatever degree, bowing out and perhaps apologizing later for unsportsmanlike conduct. I can't really see this person using the X-card except as an utterly self-loathing gesture of martyrdom: "See, I'm such a weak pussy that I'll even call a time-out against your entirely legit play." What's worse, unless explicitly restrained, the almost inevitable reaction of a crowd like this to the use of the X-card - I would bet money that the first thing out of anybody's mouth would be questioning the legitimacy of the X-card call, followed by blaming the wielder for their feelings.

      (I know I make us sound horrible there regarding each others' emotional well-being. In the defense of this social microclimate of gaming, I'll say that we value creative camaraderie, sharing social esteem, empathetic dialogue and rational compromises highly; while using an explicit prop like the X-card would feel ridiculous, we often have entirely serious conversations that lead to concrete actions regarding creative interests and social dynamics of play. "You have a problem, you talk about it" and so on, you know how that goes. It's just a cerebrally fronted way of dealing with these issues, one where the greatest sin is unsportsmanlike table conduct, and the greatest virtue is a patient and analytical ability to explain to others what is bothering you, so creative concord can be modified. It's a functional, stereotypically masculine creative environment with the strengths and weaknesses one might expect. Sadly, it's an environment that tends to be rough on certain types of people, even as others prosper.)

      Anyway, my point is that thinking about the utility of the X-card around here, I would say that making it anonymous moves it from an extremely marginal, situational option into something that I would not particularly hesitate to use in any game I've played over the last year. Once you turn the X-card anonymous, you're basically talking of a real-time feedback system regarding the enjoyment of play; in fact, there's no reason why such a system couldn't have other buttons aside from "I'm feeling ill" - it'd be just as easy to give anonymous positive feedback about what's happening in the game. Most importantly, this change moves attention away from the "I have a problem with what you are doing" model of X-card intervention, and more towards "we have a problem that needs to be considered". People familiar with the sort of creative environment I describe above can see how this removes a massive amount of social pressure from the user of the X-card: instead of having to now stand up and defend their objection in a debate, they can just let somebody else take up advocating the complaint (if the group's truly committed to rational decision-making, there will be an advocate), and let the problem get fixed by the group without having to themselves become the guarantor of the change.

      This shouldn't be taken as a critique of the X-card, I should say; it's just me speculating about how, although these tough dice-jockeys well might have an use for it, the X-card as it is might not fit well in their existing social paradigm. (Specifically, I know that I describe the use of the X-card incorrectly above; that's because a tool and its intended use are different from each other, and barring a controlling interest ensuring the X-card is used correctly, that's about how it would go with these people.) A phone app you can use discreetly or even between sessions to provide anonymous feedback, instant or not, might react in a much less volatile and more constructive way with this sort of intellectual totem pole arrangement of a gaming group

      Of course it would be entirely valid to speculate on whether a group that does not use the X-card naturally has a weakness to their social toolbox; not only different, but flawed. Goes pretty deep into the politics of the matter, but one might argue that a group that cannot deal with emotional reactions empathetically - unless they are couched into compelling rhetoric by a socially creditable individual - is cruel, perhaps consisting of individuals who are detached from their feelings, or possessing of a group culture that prizes individual strength over realism about the human condition. One might well say that if a group is bad at using the X-card, that's all the more reason to challenge them to learn it, and thus learn to listen to each other's feelings without constant testing of the mettle behind them.

      \n Speaking as an "auteur GM" (and well aware that I represent a minority here), here's my take on consensus. I am not convinced that attaining pre-play consensus on content is necessarily vital, always desirable, or even fully possible. Among friends it is superseded by normal ad-hoc dialogue and softened by familiarity, and among strangers it is rendered moot by the fact that the artist whose work I'm viewing isn't me, and therefore doesn't reflect on me. I am free to leave in the middle if I wish. I'm free to righteously say "Fuck you, you said you wouldn't do that." I am free to walk away thinking whatever I want about the artist and the work.

      That said, however, I would say that any game that trafficks in dark or psychologically-charged content but doesn't tell you to expect that right on the box (flyer, ad, handout, pitch, etc)... is either a badly-conceived plan or a deliberate attempt to be shockingly cruel. Which is rather adolescent, no?

      This is the reason we put ratings on movies and videogames. And the more specific the rating system is, the more useful it is. For instance, the letter "R" (restricted audiences) doesn't tell me much. But if you say "No violence, moderate cursing and sexual situations" I can make a more informed decision about what you're offering. And if you tell me what kind of sexual situations you're talking about, I can make a better decision still. This goes for GMs as well as designers. I see it not as a question of consensus, but more like truth in advertising: Put the ingredients on the label, let the buyer decide before buying.
