\n Here's what he has to say:

"The main thing that doesn't appeal to me about RPGs is the concept of the Gamemaster who has ultimate control over what happens during a session. I want the bad guy to have to compete within a defined set of rules with the goal of trying to beat the good guys. HeroQuest, Middle Earth Quest, and I think Star Wars: Imperial Assault accomplish this. If there is a Gamemaster deciding whether everyone lives or dies it sounds more like an experience than a game to me, and I prefer games. That said, the dungeon crawl concept, especially with campaign mode and character leveling, very much appeals to me."

Are there any RPGs that you'd recommend for him?

    \n So it's not so much that they don't want a GM, but that they want a strictly-limited GM?

    Primetime Adventures puts a massive amount of power in the players' hands; the GM only gets to frame one scene from the get-go, and the rest of their power is limited to responding to the players and providing resolution for scenes, which is guided by card draw and explicitly-established scene stakes. But it doesn't sound as competitive as your player might want.

    Marvel Heroic Roleplaying is a very head-to-head RPG where the GM is under some very strict parameters and has tactical decisions to make. It's probably the most restriction I've seen on a GM, with the exception of...

    Mythender, where the combat fights are very tightly limited. It's a very boardgamey RPG in some respects, and it's also quite competitive. The rules are tight.

    \nD&D 4e. is really good at dungeon-crawling game-ness. If the GM follows the encounter-building guidelines--or better, uses a commercial adventure module--there's no GM "deciding whether everyone lives or dies." It's up to player skill. Character leveling is the main thing, of course.

    None of these games have dungeons or levels, but they have strictly limited GM roles:

    Annalise is a cooperative story-telling RPG (generally built for slowly creeping horror) where players share the GM role scene-by-scene, but that role does not include deciding what happens. That's left to ALL the players and the dice.

    Misspent Youth is a post-cyberpunk RPG where the GM never rolls any dice and never determines what happens, except to interpret the outcomes that the players roll.

    In a Wicked Age is a swords and sorcery RPG with no GM. [Edit: Totally has a GM!] Conflicts are always between PCs, and the resolution involves "negotiation with teeth" (e.g., "how about your wizard gets the crap kicked out of him and loses his magic staff? or you can take this game-prescribed penalty to your stats... your choice.")

But they also highlight features typically associated with role-playing games and which make them completely unlike a boardgame such as Descent or MB's HeroQuest.

    Seems to me there are two major forks in this path:

    \n

    One, the relatively mechanics-driven non-GM\u2019d games like Warrior Heroes and other THW \u201cimmersive games.\u201d The GM as a role isn\u2019t assumed to exist, the mechanics drive the experience, and the dice make the \u201cgame,\u201d pushed solely by decisions of the players and the outcome of the random generation sources. D&D 4th verged over into that space, which pushed a lot of outrage-buttons on that one, justly or unjustly.

    \n

    This is a cool place, but it has more in common with a lot of the modern story-directed board games and wargames than what\u2019s considered most of the RPG industry.

    \n

    The other branch heads far more over toward Microscope and Kingdom, pushing entirely out of the realm of GM\u2019d games all-together and over into shared-narrative-power storytelling.

    \n

    Notably, it\u2019s worth noting this bit from your opening:

    \n
    \n

    I want the bad guy to have to compete within a defined set of rules with the goal of trying to beat the good guys. HeroQuest, Middle Earth Quest, and I think Star Wars: Imperial Assault accomplish this.

    \n
    \n

    What you imply here is that you really want something more along the first line, or maybe not even an RPG at all, but a modern board game or even tabletop wargame. There\u2019s no problem with that; classic wargames create a narrative by building a series of experientially-directed situations.

    \n

    That seems more of what you need.

    Now I'm wondering about separating the role of playing the opposition from refereeing the result.
    \nWhat do you mean? In more modern RPGs, each person plays a specific role and everyone referees the game. The "opposition" in the group emerges from following the rules of the game and playing your role as best as you can.

    \nI meant having a formal separation of the referee and opposition/NPCs. It'd basically be the inverse of the modern GM-less game. What stuck me is that the role of the GM wraps up several disparate functions, and while there are historical reasons for them to be put together there's nothing that says that the person who referees the result of a conflict and the person who poses the conflict need to be the same person.

    Modern GM-Less games give everyone the simultaneous roles of playing a character, generating opposition, and refereeing the result. But I'm wondering if traditionally GM-roles can be broken down and given to entirely separate people.

    As Paul_T says, the wargaming origins of D&D et al. are pretty close to this, with something like Braunstein being a good example of this. The only modern example of anything close to this that I'm personally familiar with is Callisto, and possibly Chris Engle's Matrix Games, but I'm curious if anyone else has explored this design space.
