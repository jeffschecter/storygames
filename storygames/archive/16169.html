\n
\n I played Leverage for the first time over the weekend, and I've been reading over Marvel Heroic Role-playing that uses a related system. Playing that brought up a stumbling block for me in some systems: GM-level (rather than NPC-level) resources that seem pitched as though they are tactical.

I am fine with NPC-level tactical resources. That is, a given NPC has resources, and when they are in a conflict with a PC, I can play out the NPC and use their resources tactically according to how I see that character. For example, in HERO System or Dogs in the Vineyard, I can make my moves based on what I think the NPC is trying for, and use my best tactics towards these ends. (As a side note, I can't play more than one tactical side at once, so if the NPCs are on multiple sides, all but one of the NPC sides are pretty flat and/or dumb.)

However, if there is a resource that I can't approach in-character as an NPC, then it presents an issue for me - namely, what am I playing for? As a player, I will generally use out-of-character resources to play to win. As a GM, I will use out-of-character resources to push the PCs so that the opposition is dangerous, but to generally let them win rather than beat them. As a player, though, if I think the GM is just letting me win, the whole process of conflict can feel like jumping through hoops.

So examples...

In Polaris, I find I can play to win as the Mistaken. The game still works if I try my best to achieve my ends as Mistaken. However, in FATE, I have trouble with enemy Fate Points. Partly this is expectations. In Polaris, players generally accept that horrible things will happen to the PCs - and indeed sometimes players enjoy this so much that there is a different problem. However, in Spirit of the Century or Dresden Files, with a solitary GM, I have trouble playing to win.

One key part of this is this: As an opponent to the PCs, in many rule systems my best move is to take out the easiest target. Whoever has the highest offense-over-defense ratio should be targeted and dropped first. However, as game play this sucks for that player, so I try to avoid doing that.

    \n So what I'm looking for: ideas on other ways to play tactically as a GM or GM-like role. Right now I have:

    1) Play my NPCs tactically according to their in-character personalities and resources. This works for me.

    2) Play out-of-character to win. This works for me in some games, like Polaris, but doesn't work for me in other games.

    3) Play out-of-character to make things tough for the players but pull punches rather than take out characters or such. This doesn't work for me at present (though I'm open to other ways to view this).

    \n Never go against a Sicilian when death is on the line. Or a GM.

    Take death out of the equation. Make failure about other, interesting consequences.

    Then, play to win all you want. Cause winning is awesome, cause you get to those other, interesting consequences. So when the GM wins, that doesn't really mean that the players lose. Their characters might lose, but the players win cause they get to see the awesome consequence stuff.

    But if the GM winning means that the game grinds to a halt and sucks, then nobody wants that.

    When people get roundly defeated in physical conflict, they get captured. Then you get to do that scene where the badguy tells them all about his dastardly plans before putting them into some crazy deathtrap contraption that they escape from when he leaves.

    Happened to the 60s Batman ALL THE TIME. That was how he did about half of his detective work. He'd pull some bizarro Sherlock-Holmes-inspired wackiness out of his ass to find the initial caper location, then he'd go and get beat up, then the Joker would tell him his other big plans plans and put him in a deathtrap, then he'd escape and go foil the plans. He practically HAD to fail in order to succeed in the end.

    Of course, that works better with a swing mechanic, the way JD describes in Smallville. Not sure if MHRPG works quite that way. But as long as you approach failure as leading to interesting, awesome consequences rather than boring campaign halting blah, then that should help a lot.

    \nYeah, that's a bummer.

    It's not something that I really have to deal with when I'm GMing these days, because the fun of GMing for me has very little to do with whether I'm playing the NPC opponents in a tactically sound manner; I just want to play a bunch of NPCs in conversations, listen to the PCs talking to each other, figure out how the world and the NPCs react to what they're doing, and come up with good setpieces and situations that the players can grab onto and run with. Whether a given combat encounter or other problem is challenging, meh, I couldn't care less. I'm willing to adjust the difficulty to the player's preferences, because whatever they want (easy, difficult, impossible, etc.) makes absolutely no difference to me.

    I did misjudge it pretty bad once a few years back, though; I thought that it made sense to make the various goons and thugs the PCs were fighting very defensively-oriented, so that they could take more of a pounding and stick around longer (since the PCs were so focused on offense that I was worried fights would be over before they even started). The end result was the PCs hitting the bad guys and invariably doing very little damage, and of course, the entirely-defensive posture of the bad guys meant that they rarely hit the PCs and also rarely did any damage, and the players hated that. It was a disaster; they were as frustrated as they would have been if they had been missing constantly, plus they didn't feel even remotely threatened by the bad guys.

    I kept wondering what I could do to even things out again, and then finally, I realized that the players were happier with bad guys who could hurt them but who also fell down quickly: that way, the party might get beat up a little bit, but they would still win if they focused on taking down the most dangerous targets first. Suddenly, fight scenes flowed better, people were smiling rather than wincing when the dice came out, and I re-learned that I really needed to pay close attention to what the players actually wanted, especially when it was something that I didn't care about one way or the other.

Those are things that I would absolutely do as part of my #2 option of "playing to win". Playing to win absolutely does not mean being boring or bean-counting. Within this mode, my basis for decisions is that I don't want things to seem too easy, quick, or anticlimactic - but I also don't want them to be dull slogging or quick failure. I'm trying to make the going be difficult leading to a heroic success. Thus, I will spend resources to make things tougher on the PCs if they are breezing through too easily. However, if they are getting bogged down, I may change my spending choices to lighten up so the players can get through more quickly.

    I realize that's not the only way of deciding pacing, though. The question is, on what basis do I decide? Saying that the Doom Pool expresses how bad things have gotten is fine, but how do I decide how bad things have gotten?     \n If you don't think failure is fun, then turn it into a matter of escalating consequences.

    In other words, the more the players are losing, mechanics-wise, the more their victory is costing, fiction-wise. They're still winning, if they want to, but it's costing them more and more to do it.

    So have heroes get Taken Out. Then give them an option to incur a cost of some sort to be refreshed. Or give them the option before they get taken out.

    If getting Taken Out seems boring, sucky, disheartening, blah, whatever, then figure out a way to make it more awesomer.

    Or just choose not to play games with mechanics that don't work for you. It's not like there's a shortage of good options.

    \nWell, if we're talking about ideal mechanics for this - my tastes at the moment would lean towards the following:

    1) There is a pool of communal resources among all the players.

    2) A player pulls from the pool when they would be taken out of the conflict. This should represent the group giving up something. I'm not committed yet to when it should be decided what was given up. (Having to make something creative up on the spot can be cool, but can also slow things down.)

    3) The real challenge for the players is to get through the conflict while losing as few of these as possible.

    4) Once a player is hit, they are at an immediate disadvantage, but it is harder to go from "hit" and "taken out". The result is that it is more effective to hit all of the opponents at least once.

    The last is important, because without something like this, the preferred strategy will be to hit the most vulnerable character - then keep hitting them and taking them out repeatedly until the communal points are fully drained.

    In general, there should be as quick a progression as possible from the first PC going down to all the PCs going down.

    \n I've been grappling with this a bit in my Fate campaign - it's my first time GMing Fate. Fate has both a pacing mechanic - a pool of fate points for PCs and NPCs - and shifting narrative control - fate points can be used to declare a bit of fiction. It has been an adjustment.
    \n
    \nI've been TRYING to not pull any punches - which is hard for me, as I am something of a soft touch. Here's what helped:
    \n
    \n1. The fate pool for NPCs turns out to be self-moderating. If I use them all up in one scene, my NPCs get creamed in the next. So, I have to decide as a GM where I want to put the next oomph.
    \n
    \n2. I've been giving each NPC very clear and specific goals, usually NOT simple PC death. They want to get information, or escape, or kill an NPC close to the PCs - whatever. They can win without leaving a PC corpse.
    \n
    \n3. The NPC goals also determine how invested they are in a fight - ie, when they concede a conflict. So I can play an NPC to win - right up to the point he starts losing, at which point he folds like a wet noodle.
    \n
    \n4. Finally, Fate has a nice system of Consequences that have teeth (both in the fiction and the mechanics). The most likely outcome of the PCs getting hammered is a series of Consequences, which is good bloody fun without the hard work of writing a new PC in to the story. Consequences leave marks (you could easily go from "Handsome Devil" to "Scarred Survivor," or from "Favored Heir" to "On The Run.")
    \n
    \nI guess I'd sum it up as, "set limits, then play to win within those limits." I am still having a hard time juggling compels, but it seems to be working. My players are more nervous than they used to be, anyway.
