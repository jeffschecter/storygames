    \n Ryan, you're missing a key point about DB design, in particular queries and report generation. I'll try to break it down without transcribing the MS Access Help files.

    Your current process for POD cards would take a single PDF from a designer, store it on your file server, and send it to print when you receive an order, ya? Update to the PDF is just a matter of replacing the one on the file server.

    OK, the DB -> PDF process is functionally IDENTICAL to that, with only a difference in interface. Instead of grabbing a PDF and (metaphorically) passing it through to your printer; you open the DB and press "Make A Booster" or "Make a Starter". Then, the entire query engine would randomize--both within the whole set AND for commonality AND for current total count, at the same time--and pass that to a report. The report, in turn, would format the data (stats, text, art, attributions, whatever) onto *whatever* layout you require for your printing and dye cutting process. Out spits a PDF, which you pass to the printer; and even that could be automated, if the PC running the DB has the printer as its default Network or Local printer. BAM! Randomized cards, from the whole set, fitted to your mechanical requirement, and counted out for the purposes of making sure (say) each rare card is equally represented (or not).

    It would be truly a waste of everyone's time to have the game maker hand-manage that process from home, e-mailing individual booster or starter sales to you, to pick-up and run through your printer and packager(s).

    SO, is that easy? Well, hell, no. It's a fairly significant bit of query work, for instance (tracking output, in particular; randomizing numbers is easy) and getting the report(s) to fit right would take trial and error. Then, of course, there's the designing and balancing of 200-300 cards; working out pricing for your 18-card boosters (say) and for your 54-card starters (say); and sleeving or boxing.

    But you wouldn't ever be hand-sorting (*shudder*) or somehow "caught out" by a report not working with your process (well, no more than someone's static PDF sheets would, at least). Want to change a card? E-mail a new DB table and overwrite the old one (or e-mail new card art, to replace old art in the folder structure from which it is called--wouldn't want to embed the art in the DB, just to avoid bloating).

    Finally, you seem to think "installing" a database is somehow hard. Got Office? "Installing" means copying a MDB file and some art files; open the MDB and, if it's well-written, all you see are the two buttons above (and maybe an admin login option, to do on-the-fly changes outside of Access itself).

    In fact... you might consider adding this as a future service, if you can find a good DB programmer (I am a "mere" architect). You could provide a "design" interface to game makers, with which they design card formats and stats and layouts; and the resulting tables could be sent to you, to be run from within a "production" interface, that pulls, randomizes, and PDFs them into your 3x3 sheets. Again, an even bigger job--the ability to layout the cards freely is a LOT tricker to code than a known layout for a single game--but what a killer feather in GoB's hat: the first TRUE POD CCG provider!

    *stares wistfully into space* it's not... impossible....

    Posted By: guildofbladesThe bigger challenge, as you say, would be coding the layout. To get accurate front and back side printing and especially to get accurate die cutting, layout positions have to be precise.
    \nWell, Access reports can be laid out with a precision of, what, 1/100th of an inch? If that's not good enough, you're fucked anyway, I figure.\n
    Also, I can't see how it would be a good idea to make all the text editable and alterable, because that means not only would we have to position the cards at specific positions on a page, but we would have to position every text element.
    \nBut, no, you wouldn't. For every card, the report *IS* the layout of the card: art, text, numbers, icons--EVERYTHING has a location "on" the report layout. Don't think of a report as some banal list of records in rows: it can be laid-out in any format, pulling cards from the query (a card is a record of references to other tables, of strings, and or external calls for art).

    For example, this might be a Magic card record:
    KeyID: 111999555 (or whatever)
    Name: Counterspell
    Card type: 5 (looks-up from "CardType" table to see that means "Instant")
    Casting cost: UU (parsed into "two blues" by query + lookup from "CastingCostTypes" table)
    Card text: "Counter target spell." (string)
    Artist: "L.A. Williams" (string, or could be look-up, for better structure)
    Rarity: 1 (look-up from "RarityType" table to see that means "Common")

    Now, the record (PDF output) from that would be populating fields on the record layout... and those could be anywhere. They could even change in reaction to some of the data (ex: blue background JPG, for Counterspell, because it's two blue to cast).\n
    Further, that means companies would all be stuck with the same card design, essentially. Because to have different designs would be a reprogramming of the positions for all the card elements onto the sheet.
    \nYou're not taking it far enough. Yes, one could twiddle the data tables and the record layout (and impacted queries) and make a new database.

    But what if, as part of the overall database interface, those very setups and designs are part of the DB, the part that a customer of yours would get, to "setup" their card game for this system. THEY do the "programming"--which would really be more like typing and drawing in a WYSIWYG layout program, ideally--and you just take the tables, queries, and reports that this "design" tool generates. Plug. Press GO.\n
    And while yes, that would be possible, I'm not really sure its practical for a POD card printer to be charging a $1,000 to $2,000 programming set up charge for a new game to be set up in the system.
    \nNo, see above.

    Plug. Press Go.\n
    I think it would be far more practical simply to design a randomizer that could pull high res tiff card images and plop them into the proper place on the sheet for printing. Then clients would simply upload all their card images into a profile through the internet.
    \nOh, sure, that would always work fine.

    And a hybrid would work fine, too: have those card TIFFs be singles only, but sorted into folders by rarity. Then, the DB could still do the randomizing-by-rarity, individual card count insurance (query) and layout to the page (report), but the "data" backend would be a mere table of card folder and file names from which the query plucks files references, to build up a random sheet.\n
    And done this way, a publisher could establish any rarity level they wanted for each and every card. There wouldn't just have to be commons, uncommons and rares. Each and every card could have its own frequency set.
    \nUm... not really.

    If you randomize, you randomize. The only way to ensure "frequency" of a given card using the above "list it multiple times to make it more common" method is to keep a count per card. Basically, you are quasi-random: you have to randomize ONLY from within the set of cards which have not yet been "hit" by a call to print them, until every card has been called once; then repeat. And the *real* bitch is even harder: even that "randomize once per cycle" process could--by very low probability--make a booster full of rares.

    No, the algorythm for pulling cards by rarity and count is a bit more complex that "pick a random file name." It would almost certainly require a rather sophisticated query.\n
    And yeah, maybe this is something we could tackle in the future.
    \nFrankly, the hybrid solution should be all-but-trivial to roll out. No data backend to speak of; card appearances can vary wildly, even within a particular game (not to mention game to game); you don't have to program anything (after the initial DB setup) other than a card list table (i.e. cut & paste from a directory listing in a command prompt). Talk the idea through with your SQL person--she (he? pronoun confusion above) can perhaps explain better than I how minor such a "pick and poke" DB query and report layout will be.\n

    \nNot necessarily - you simply 'weight' cards so that when they are printed, their weight increases and they 'sink' relative to other cards that haven't been printed in the same rarity.

    For example, we have 5 rare cards, A - E, all starting with a weight of 0. Each card therefore has a 20% chance of being selected. In a booster print, A gets selected, and its weight increases from 0 to, say, 0.1. When the next print for the booster comes around, cards B - E have a 20.025% chance of being selected (the 0.1 from A divided 4 ways), and A has a 19.9% chance. This evens the odds, so to speak, even on small runs.
