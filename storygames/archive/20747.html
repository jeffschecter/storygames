\n The future of mass market gaming will be aggressively apolitical. The most popular games will avoid promoting any specific set of values, and only account for political trends when not doing so would send a stronger message. It's the case for games like D&D and Pathfinder, and the case for monster licenses like Pokemon. Any game which does not follow this principle will never reach mass market.

But things have become more political than ever before in this interconnected society. You can no longer control context like before. Games designed for one region are leaking out to others they weren't and causing a lot of misunderstanding. You can't market your game to a specific audience because you have no control over who it reaches.

Which means Teletubbies all the way down (and even that didn't escape the ire of Christian radicals). Which means the most popular games will be the ones which only target people's base instincts to gamble, compete, collect, and socialize. Which means effort will be put into removing meaningful interpretations before release.

Of all the reasons 'Indie' games have not reached the mass market, this is the one that makes the most sense. I'm hard pressed to think of one Indie title which does not deal with challenging moral dilemmas, and often such is the core of play. There's nothing wrong with 'dumb' fun, and 'smart' fun isn't inherently better, but the market appeal for the latter will always be smaller.

Counterpoint?

    \n I think the question of whether gaming and RPGs are facing any sort of pressure toward apolitical, ethical blandness is pretty interesting.     My personal takes on the latest subtopics:

    Videogames vs tabletop RPGs

    (A) Choosing between options scripted by someone else in a videogame and (B) authoring your own options in an RPG, are entirely different types of choices. Ethically dubious behavior is possible in either case, but I'd level different sorts of critique. I'd say the moral risk in "I did KKK stuff cuz the game made me" is more cowardice and the moral risk in "I responded to an open situation with KKK behavior" is more hate. Note, though, that I say "risk" not "actuality". Just because a player could be a thoughtless collaborator or a hateful bigot doesn't mean that they are. To know one way or another in any given case, you'd have to be there (at minimum).

    Some RPG play is more like videogame play in this respect (effectively choosing between options scripted by the GM or scenario) and some is more authorial. So I see the span from (A) to (B) above as a spectrum. It's hard to even know what sort of moral hazard is present just from the statement "in a tabletop RPG". Might be just like a videogame, or it might be nothing like a videogame.

    Opportunities for abuse

    Let's say I make a game which features the KKK, and that the most obvious way to play it is for someone, GM or player(s), to act out the behaviors of KKK members. Is this good, bad, or neutral? Is whatever emerges in play my responsibility or not? Does it depend on who I share the game with? Does it depend on some blurb in the book about my intent? Does it depend on whether the mechanics punish or reward KKK-like behavior?

    I don't have a clear answer. My position that this sort of game-making doesn't deserve censorship has more to do with my understanding of contemporary American culture than with any ethical absolutes. If our cultural landscape were flooded with "Let's play Nazis and the Klan!" pastimes, then I would be all in favor of killing that trend, and censorship might be the way to do it.

    If someone were to present to me a novel and compelling argument that America is flooded with pastimes that establish positive orientations toward bigotry, then I might change my tune. I think this is in essence the argument being put forth against various entertainment products -- not that one game over here which might include rape is single-handedly deforming the world, but that it's another drop in an already dangerously-overfull bucket.

    I'm going to feel a lot better about my KKK game if I hear that only my diehard liberal friends are playing it than I will if I find it's made it into a bunch of small towns in Missouri which are known for KKK presence.

    Anyway, in lieu of a clear answer, three anecdotes:

    1) I think this article on stand-up comedy nails the risks quite well. Writing and performing a rape joke doesn't make you a rapist, but it doesn't leave you 100% in the clear either.

    2) Eric Cartman. Sometimes we're reminded that he acts the way he does because of insecurities and demons and messed-up parenting. Sometimes being an unrepentant asshole comes back to bite him. And other times, he's just a blast to watch. He's the show's most compelling character, conceiving and executing all sorts of entertaining shenanigans, unfettered by morality. On a show that is definitely not for kids but looks like it would be from the animation style, is Cartman just as corrosive as pro-social children's programming is positive? Or is he some sort of lesson?

Does giggling as we play the "dwarf tossing" card stunt our empathy for little people? Now that I'm asking that question, and am thinking about the issue, I feel that I can answer "no" on my own behalf. However, I could just as easily have played the card and never thought about it, and in that case, "yes" is a risk.

    I still enjoy CAH in the right setting, but I do intend to try to insulate myself against callousness-normalization when I play by maintaining a little critical awareness, and I do intend to acknowledge the wrongness when I contribute something wrong to the group. This is more or less how I view roleplaying slave-owners and KKK members and vampires and the like too.

    My friend who's quit CAH and I are more or less on the same page about the dynamics here, and the decision about "not worth it" vs "worth it, if" is pretty fine.
