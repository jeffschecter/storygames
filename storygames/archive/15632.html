\n Dear game writers if you design games or write scenarios where players have to read long sections of text loud, please consider people with reading or speech problems.

While it a nice idea that everyone gets participate and take turns reading aloud, if you make it one of the rules of a game, please consider adding: "But do check if anyone have trouble reading aloud, and if they do, pass the task of reading aloud to the next person."

Why? Shouldn't groups be able to figure it out on their own?

Well, no.

Often when you play an indie game, you wanna play it as written the first time because the game maker have designed it the way they did to archive some effect. When a game insist that is essential that everyone should take turns reading aloud you assume the gamemaker think this is really important to the game.

In general it is not that essential to the game, and pushing everyone to try reading aloud, in the case when someone has real reading or speech trouble is just painful and makes the game run less smoothly.

Thank you.

/Dyslectic Elin

    \n "Think about whether any of these procedures create difficulty for anyone playing ... and adapt the rules accordingly?" seems like part of the subtext of the rules to any story game.

    Elin, I respect the point you're making, but I think you may be trying to have it both ways. If the game rules make a big deal about everyone reading out loud, then the game designer probably felt it's important to the game, and you can't just say "it's not that essential" without knowing the game. But if the game text just mentions in passing that people take turns reading, then it's probably not crucial that everyone read, and it doesn't seem like that needs to be made explicit.

    I don't know any story games where everyone reading out loud is essential, but I can imagine that there might be some. Not every game is going to be accessible to every player, whether because of disabilities or just lack of talent. "Dread" isn't going to work well for a player with cerebral palsy; even a high-functioning autistic or Aspie would probably be stymied by "Breaking The Ice"; and I tend to avoid games with really involved combat systems because I have just enough ADD that my attention wanders during lengthy "encounters".

    I don't know any story games where everyone reading out loud is essential, but I can imagine that there might be some. Not every game is going to be accessible to every player, whether because of disabilities or just lack of talent.

    \n
    \n

    The key point in including accessibility text isn't always because an adaptation exists that will make the game accessible; off the cuff, for instance, I can't think of a replacement for Jenga in Dread that doesn't equally require fine motor control, because that class of games (the ones where every action creates an escalating risk that a system will collapse disastrously) is all about fine motor control.

    \n

    Similarly, anyone playing BtI with Helen Keller is going to have a very different experience than playing BtI with some-person-who-can-see-and-hear. Guaranteeing accessibility to everyone might not be possible, but it's not the whole point.

    \n

    At least part of the point is that you, as an author, communicate that you are aware of accessibility issues, and respect them. I think it's our responsibility as designers to think about how accessible our games are as we design, and try to maximize at that stage, and then acknowledge that we have limits to our expertise, and hope that audiences who do not feel that the out-of-the-box design is accessible enough feel empowered to take that final step with their group.

    \n Thanks for bringing this up, Elin.

    I really respect the desire to play something as written, to embrace the (potentially unfamiliar) experience being offered.
    Being open to that magic is wonderful, trusting the author is wonderful.

    I think Shreyas has pretty much nailed it down. I could add that whatever accessability issues could be forseen can be addressed independantly -
    for example, with Silver and white (which demands that each player read a section of the text aloud), the issue of one player reading aloud with more difficulty than the others - totally foreseeable.

    Interestingly, I included the everybody-reads-aloud procedure in Silver and white to combat a different accessability issue - shyness. I theorized that if a player has already begun talking before
    play "starts", and if they are involved in the process of teaching the game to the the other players, then they have an in-road to involve themselves in the fiction.
    This aspect was mainly included for the benefit of players who are entirely new to the medium.

    For my part, i don't mind listening as someone reads aloud more slowly than is "expected"; but if that situation makes the reader so uncomfortable that they stop having fun, well... that's not worth it. So i'll address this in the next version.
