    \n Is it just the "powerful" nature of the skills rules that you're objecting to, or something else?

    My gut intuition here is that the +1 bonus, due to how it makes full success more common, while actually be a more game-changing modifier (in terms of making the characters "powerful") than the rules as written.

    Don't forget that a 7-9 is often a difficult choice or a tight spot - that's very different in feel from "never failing". A character who rolls partial success after partial success tends to get deeper into trouble, rather than feeling like a "winner".

    However, if you're still not convinced, I'll suggest my favourite solution for this kind of thing:

    Roll a d4 as a bonus die along with your 2d6. For your result, use the two highest dice.

    This hits the "best of both worlds" in this case: it doesn't change your odds of a full success (not all that much, anyway), DOES mean you're rolling a lot more 7-9 results than before (like the existing rule), but still preserves a chance of full failure, if that's important to you.

    \n I also thought like you; but in the end, once I got to play, I loved the original skill method (just don't use it in combat unless very specific situations appear, like ATHLETICS when jumping into an enemy from a roof or AWARENESS when taking time to aim for a target). What I did, though, is to limit the number of skills a character can have. In my games, they start with 1 and can get a max number of 3 (instead of the five from the original). This prevents characters from being Immune to fail in every aspect!

    Also, if you want to stick with a mechanic bonus, I suggest this: If a roll falls under the domain of a skill, the player may choose to re-roll a bad result, but must stick with the second result.

    This approach adds tension, choices and the odds are beneficial but not crazy high.

    \n I definitely think that the "skill" rule in WoDu is one of those things that "looks scary" on paper, but, once you see it in play, does exactly what it should.

    Don't forget that, as the GM/MC, you can be as harsh as you want on a 7-9 result, so long as you remember to give the player what they were going for. That gives you free space for lots of fun outcomes. (And you can be more harsh on a miss than you would be on a 7-9.)

    For instance, someone with Leadership could try to sway a crowd to support their claim against the local ruler.

    10+: They have the crowd's support.
    7-9: They have the crowd's support, but there is a downside: perhaps they want you to make an unpleasant promise first.
    miss: You win the crowd's support for the throne, but they start a riot, lynching people and starting a fire.

    The only thing you can't have with a skilled character is that they try to do this thing they are skilled at... and they have no effect at all.

    That's a pretty slick rule, in effect.
