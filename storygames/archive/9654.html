    \n After many years of these conversations, I think I've finally come to side with the people who say that it's really hard (and often straight-up wrong) to try to categorize games or people according to Creative Agendas. It really is about categorizing general trends exhibited across many, many instances of play, since individuals and games can easily move between different Creative Agendas all the time. It's really about tendencies and modes more than anything else.

    So the real answer to your question is: depends on who's playing it, what they're doing right now, and what they've been doing previously during the rest of the session and the rest of the campaign.

    \nFrankly, I don't think Premise need be articulated directly at all. I've played half a dozen games of Dirty Secrets which is full bore Story Now. I couldn't tell you what the Premise of a single of those games was. With some careful reflection I'm sure I could identify the central crisis the Investigator was facing and with some more careful reflection I could probably refine that into an articulated Premise.... but why? What mattered is that the narrative moment-from-moment was compelling in terms of real-world human passions and culminated in a thematic resolution that was built and arrived at through play itself.

    That's why Burning Wheel is deceptive. Assuming people write Belief's that they care about in real world human terms and assuming the GM hits those Beliefs the way the book tells you to, you get Story Now. It doesn't have to be articulated as an official Premise. People who are invested in the developing story AS a story (and not as an intellectual construct) interact with the Premise quite naturally. Most of the people who can't grok Burning Wheel are confused by what appears to be a tactics focused system when in reality those tactics are a support beam for what the game is really about: standing up (or not) for your Beliefs... FOR REAL.

    \n Paul, I think you are confusing the old threefold model (aka GDS) that indeed talked about the moment-by-moment decision of the players, with GNS (than don't do any such thing). It's a very common confusion, that really don't help discussing these things outside of The Forge... :-(

    Jesse is 100% right, by the way. BW doesn't need any formulated premise to be a story-now facilitating game.

    A creative agenda is observed during the full course of a game (from 1 to 20th level in a D&D for example), not "moment by moment". In every game session there will be moments where you'll try to "win" a fight, but it's not at that scale that you observe the creative agenda

    If a group want (consciously, or by habit) to play with a different creative agenda, all they have to do is drift the game. This drift can be really subtle and transparent, or very minor, or need a strong house-ruling, or can't be done without destroying anything in the original game: often people confuse the difficulty of this drift with game coherence, but they are really two different things: if a game facilitate (or provoke) "story now", it's coherent, even if a minor drift is enough to change it to a Right to Dream facilitationg game.

    \n To clarify what Moreno said a bit, the exact time-span we use in analyzing CA appearance nowadays is a single "reward cycle". This is the span of interactions that provides the payoff a player is looking for in a game (which happens to also define the play's CA: your Creative Agenda is the reward value your play is seeking to fulfill). I'm mentioning this because I've never analyzed D&D in terms of really long-term play - it does have some reward cycles that go on that long, I suppose, but surely nobody would play it if those were the most important ones.

    For Burning Wheel (or any game, really), the first step in GNS analysis needs to be forming a theory of interactions for it: what are the actually interesting and significant parts of the process of play for the players? If BITs factor highly in your analysis of what actually is interesting in BW, then you're probably leaning towards playing it in a Narrativistic manner, because that particular mechanic happens to be a linchpin for the dramatic matter of character identity in this game. If, on the other hand, you're really interested in how characters are shaped by their experiences and how the character burning process creates organic characters, then it might be that you're seeing the simulationistic potential in the game. The tactical parts in the combat system could conseivably inspire some sort of gamism as well, although the chargen system and that stuff would probably get fragile if the players oriented towards that from the get-go.

    I don't know about the hypothetical simulationistic or gamist ways of playing the game, but I do understand the narrativistic reward cycle in BW somewhat: the reward payoff comes in the form of situations that force characters to rely on or deny their BITs, which in turn reflects dramatically as either affirming or transforming the character's identity. The payoff often comes after the action, when the players are considering whether and how the BITs should change. The other xp systems support this identity development from the margins, too. It's really quite similar to The Shadow of Yesterday, BW just has more complex complementary mechanics for what happens as consequence of choices made. HeroQuest, TSoY and BW are from this viewpoint on one line from lightweight to heavy mechanics, even while the Creative Agendae they support are near identical.

    But the important part is, when we're discussing whether "BW is narrativist", what we mean is to ask: how do people derive satisfaction from playing this game? If just one way is dominant because it's so obvious and the rules do such a good job at that, then we might say that the game is coherent and indeed "is" narrativistic in that sense. But all that comes second, after we've first figured out the reward cycle and what the people are actually enjoying when playing the game. The road from that practical play situation to the game text as an abstract entity is long and convoluted, and the many layers of interpretation in between may well change how people see the game.

    \n Separate reward cycles should be able to deliver rewards for different CAs, agreed. I don't know if it is feasible to have conflicting cycles like that exist within each other during play, though; in parallel I can see it, it's the same old effort at fulfilling two priorities at the same time. In shorter term and longer... perhaps - the closest I've experienced in actual play is different CAs being satisfied in turns, as waves of 1-3 sessions at a time, swapping to the next CA as the composition of the play group changes or priorities shift for some other reason. I wouldn't say that those games have a significant long-term reward cycle that were formed out of smaller events, though; my characterization would be that they're just a string of separate cycles tied together by the ephemera and contents of play. When the CA gets shifted, incompatible content seems to get strongly pushed to the backstage until it gets a chance to re-emerge later in more favourable conditions.

    I'm not dogmatically opposed to the idea of hybrid Creative Agenda, it's just that I haven't witnessed it myself. I have a suspicion that there is some social dynamic reason for why a single CA very efficiently conquers a play environment when the game gets really going (something like the players getting focused on reinforcing the reward cycle they're actually interested in the most), but I haven't the words and vision to verbalize this clearly at this point, and I don't think anybody else has really proffered a model for how the social CA develops out of individual interests during play. Perhaps somebody writes me a game at some point which includes a set of instructions that allow me to reliably provide a hybrid experience; if they do, that'll be a pretty clear nail on the coffin of the GNS theory.

    Still, the point about BW stands - it's not the game itself that has a CA so much as it has one or more practical readings about how it's played - those playings have a CA in a much more concrete sense. The interpretation of BW as a narrativist engine gets the most attention with the local majority here in this corner of the Internet, which is why we say that BW "is a narrativist game". Heroquest is a lot like this, too - I've met many people for whom the game is setting/drama simulation, simply because they play it differently from how the Forge crowd does. It is not particularly contradictory to the theory for somebody to post about a radically different interpretation of the game, full of different technique and different combinations of the rules; they might overall strive for a radically different game experience by doing that, and possibly end up playing BW (drifting it, if one wants to presume that there is some "canon" reading of the game text) within a different GNS mode.

    Posted By: Zac in DavisBW has no Premise, in the Forge sense; no embedded, specific theme or moral quandary. As such, it can't support Story Now unless the group devises a Premise, plays with it, and hey, the mechanics don't get in the way.
    \nGames that support Story Now don't need an "embedded premise." Nor do most of the most influential games from designers who were members of the Forge have an "embedded premise."

    Premise at the Forge is defined as, "A generalizable, problematic aspect of human interactions. Early in the process of creating or experiencing a story, a Premise is best understood as a proposition or perhaps an ideological challenge to the world represented by the protagonist's passions. Later in the process, resolving the conflicts of the story transforms Premise into a theme - a judgmental statement about how to act, behave, or believe. In role-playing, "protagonist" typically indicates a character mainly controlled by one person. A defining feature of Story Now." (emphasis mine).

    In other words, premise is a thing the players bring to the table as issues. They belong to the protagonists and game play will resolve the issues in some way, creating theme.

    If we play a super hero game -- any super hero game -- and we all create characters that are focused on either vengeance against criminals or making sure criminals are brought to justice, the game's premise could become, "Is Justice superior to Vengeance?"

    I suspect that what you meant to say is that Burning Wheel isn't about anything the way Sorcerer is about dysfunctional relationships or Dogs in the Vineyard is about forming a moral framework and the lengths to which one will go to defend it. All games are about something, and it has nothing to do with whether the game supports a Story Now agenda or not. D&D 4e is about the journey from zero to hero. Someone else could come along and write a fun, functional, and engaging game that supports Story Now, the Right to Dream, or Step on Up using that same idea.

    Burning Wheel is very much about something. Burning Wheel is a game about choices and consequences. It is about fighting for what you believe.

    Posted By: Ryan Macklin4e is, in fact, neither inherently Gamist, Simulationist, or Narrativist -- it's what the people at the table make it.
    \nIf I may throw something out there to ponder. One thing mechanics do is that, if you follow the procedures of play, they have a great influence on what you're going to spend a lot of your playing time with and what kind of opportunities for certain interactions among the players you get at what frequency.

    Let's take 4e. Sure, you can try and focus on the story and premise in that game. But once you start a combat, you're going to spend a considerable amount of time with it, right? And during that time, it seems to me that you'll have a considerable number of opportunities to address challenges and probably not nearly as many opportunities to address your premise, test your character's passions, etc. Sure, you can have thematic decision points in 4e combat, but I would argue that the rules set ensures that your challenge-addressing actions vastly outnumber those. You could avoid combat, but then, you're avoiding the vast majority of the rules.

    Burning Wheel's BIT system, on the other hand, can come into play several times even in combat. There are more built-in opportunities, given the same amount of playing time, to address premise when using that system. And in fact, the game gives you a way of resolving combat without the complex systems if you like (with a simple comparative roll), which allows players to spend their playing time on other things if they so choose. All of that puts it much further in the story now-promoting camp than 4e. Like we say, it "supports" a certain agenda more than another because, when you use the rules, you get more playing time with and more opportunities to address story now.

    The reason that Beast Hunters can support story now play to a certain degree is that goals allow you, in any given combat, to let the character make a meaningful thematic decision using the mechanics. Any combat can become a question of what could be worth risking your win for (because you spend precious resources achieving story goals, and those resources are the same you'd use to defeat your enemies). But most of the rules are more focused on giving opportunities for setting up and addressing challenges. You're going to spend a lot of your time doing maneuvers, and every maneuver is a chance to step up plus a chance to make a risky move (picking dice over guaranteed rewards).

    So: while I agree that a game itself isn't determinative of an agenda, it seems to me that a lower-case system surely has a big impact on what we're going to spend a lot of our playing time doing and what kinds of decision points we are presented with using the system during that time.

    My friend John, who has been playing 4e quite a bit lately, has been trying to talk me out of my serious doubts about it as a game, and in particular my contention that the rules box out Story Now play. I am beginning to see where he might be coming from; let me toss out a few things I've been mulling over.

    Consider this premise: "'We must hang together, because we will surely hang apart.'" 4e is all about teamwork in combat: a Controller slides an aggressively-committed Striker out of the dangerous position he took up to make a devastating attack; a beleaguered Defender steps up and risks serious harm to ensure that a flanking Striker maintains his favorable situation. Suppose that, in the fiction, the characters don't like each other at all, but have a grudging respect for each others' expertise. Then, when I activate my encounter power to drop a cloud of Minions, opening a path for you to charge in and land a telling blow on the Elite bad guy, we're not just playing Battletech; we might be having a conversation about our interdependence; when your guy, instead, screams at mine to drop those minions but instead I'm busy establishing a safe position from that Elite, we're talking about our mistrust.

    Consider, instead, that maybe we can act like WotC designed what didn't matter. We're presented with a choice of extirpating a colony of kobolds or turning back an ambitious (maybe even greedy) group of human miner colonists who want their land. What matters is our judgment about who we'll be fighting. That the fight will itself be a fun game is a lagniappe.

    Just my $.02.
