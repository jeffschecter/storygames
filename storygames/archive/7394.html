\n Do you feel as I do that Dice are important to a true game? If you have a computer rolling the dice then you may as well play a video game. Feeling the dice and holding the dice and rolling the dice creates some of the best moments of play.
\n
\nWith an RPG, the RP plays an important part but once it comes down to a dice roll you don't want to click a button. You want to hold and throw real dice.
\n
\nHow many of you have a favorite die you use on special occasions?

    \n I have favorite dice for sure, I think most gamers do, and I like different dice for different games, and depending on whether I am the GM and so on. So for example when I run GURPS I want a white D6, a green D6 and a red D6 (though I'll use black sometimes), why, no idea.

    But I've played in a number of play by email / forum / IRC games, where there are no tangible dice, and I don't mind them at all. Sure I'm not playing with dice, but I don't really miss that, just like playing those computer games of Magic was fun even though I couldn't use my favourite sparkly card sleeves. The dice fetish thing is fun, but its not the essential part of playing

    \n As the producers of boardgames already know, there is a component of pleasure in touching and using dice, cards, token and other physical object. Even the LARPers that frown on dice and cards, then go on using props, costumes, etc.

    With the CRPG taking more and more players away, I think that these props will become more and more important in a game success, not less. The pleasure of rolling a die or showing a card or moving a token is still one of the few things you cant have on a PC.

    \n The dice is essential in classical roleplaying games. As a randomizer it is well suited: being fast and easily readable. The ritual of rolling the dice is important too, bringing the conflict resolution down to a physical level. To do so influence play and player in ways beyond what we may express in words..

    In a lot of modern roleplaying games it has been replaced by other means of randomizers, or made redundant due to the game method. So it is not essential to roleplaying games per se.

    \n Dice are great fun, and can be a wonderful dramatic focus (to steal one of Tomas' terms). Like all cool things, though, they can steal attention that the designer wanted to focus on other things - if it's a lot of fun rolling dice to fight in a game, for example, players will seek opportunities to do so.

    \n I design & play on a principle that dice & other stuff make tangible the tension elements in the fiction. Or, to put it another way: I want to craft stories with my mouth and my hands.

    Zombie Cinema does a trick I'm fond of, and have used in some of my early (in the you-haven't-seen-it style) designs: putting the die out as a signifier of conflict. They can be used in your game for so much more than just rolling.

    Mythender uses lots of dice, initially designed thinking "dice = fun! more dice = more fun!" And it works, but the game started to sizzle when I added a larger, different colored temptation-style die in the game (technically called the "Mythic Die" but also called the "Fuck You, Macklin! Die"). That die, due to how it's set up, is meant to have more narrative badassery attached to it, so that it as an object transforms into something more than "I'm grabbing that die, too." We'll see if that actually happens in a long-term game, though.

    But, that's how I like to play with dice. I like to do more than roll them. I like them being physical objects of import.

    \n I posted this because I knew it would be of value here and create a discussion. Yes, I sell dice but who can blame me. They are always fun and you can never have too many.
    \n
    \nI also feel that cards are important to some games. We often play Twilight Imperium. For those of you strictly on the RPG side, this is an industrial board game which uses so many parts, pieces and rules that keeping them organized can be a pain in the arse. The one game piece that never gives us problems is dice.
    \n
    \nCards are great but if you know all the cards in a deck or don't get new ones often enough they can bore play. Everyone knows what a dice can do but what it actually does when tossed can still be a nail biting experience. Much better than say flicking a spinner.
    \n
    \nAnyway, thanks for the comments guys/gals. It seems that I am not alone in my belief that dice rolling still adds to a game in this modern era. Now if we can just get a dice that can change the number of sides it has according to what you need at the time. Say, a morphing dice (far future I fear) that would be an ok modern change to the classic dice. Again for me clicking a button doesn't feel like it has the mojo factor some dice rolls require.
