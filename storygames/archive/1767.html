    My off-the-cuff summary of Greyhawk

    Since Greyhawk is the world that co-evolved with Advanced Dungeons & Dragons, most of its basic tropes have been lifted and dropped right into other generic D&D fantasy settings. Many of the details exist solely to support the odd conventions of the D&D rules. For instance, every leader is some sort of high-level PC class. Still, there are a few things that are relatively unique to Greyhawk.

    It is a world in decline. Long ago the big magical empires wiped each other off the face of the map, the survivors fled east into the eastern end of the continent called the Flaeness, which is the only developed part of the campaign world. Since then, smaller "big" empires have already come and gone in the Flaeness. This of course, means there are a bunch of powerful treasures and magics, the likes of which cannot be created anymore, stashed away in ancient dungeons waiting to be discovered.

    More than other, sanitized D&D settings, it features the issue of race. There are four major human races which are (refreshingly) not 100% analogous to real-world racial groups (as well as three lesser races that are pretty much based on earth peoples). By now, they've all pretty much intermingled throughout the Flaeness and race isn't a big deal, but having the history of migrations and the cultural intermingling in place gives surprisingly plausible reasons for current political setups. It also gives us...

    The Scarlet Brotherhood are a mysterious organization of Suel (the blond-haired, blue-eyed race) martial artist race supremacists who seem to have their fingers in much of the bad evilness going on in the southern Flaeness, particularly the slave trade. I dunno, the combination of Western fascists with Eastern fascists makes for fun villains.

    There's Iuz the Old and Overking Ivid too, but they're relatively generic big bads.

    Yeah, the fact that the Circle of Eight, led by Mordenkainen -- Gary Gygax's own character -- are not necessarily good is always interesting.

    Carl Sargent probably gets the credit for making Greyhawk darker and less silly than its original incarnation. Under his editorship, the Greyhawk Wars (a big meta-plot thing) occurred and the bad guys nearly won.

    Really though, the whole thing is constructed for the sole purpose of justifying, "Why do we adventure?" It's best left sitting in the background while various bizarre dungeon crawls and evil humanoid exterminations play out.

    I'd dump the crap elements like the Grand Duchy of Geoff if we're going to awesome-o-fy it. Assuming this is a remake along the lines of Battlestar Galactica, let's grab the core ideas and build from there.

    1 - Balkanization. Everything used to be part of a great Empire, not that long ago (a few generations)--and every place has its own special Imperial heritage, myth, and rationale for being the center of a new Empire. This is deeply ingrained. Everyone wants the throne. Intergenerational hatreds, pacts, oaths, and feuds are rampant. No one group is strong enough to win, so alliances and leagues are necessary. But these are ever-shifting.

    2 - Oaths and Pacts. Some of the ancient oaths are magically binding on various parties, nations, or races if invoked by the proper beneficiaries or heirs. If you can dredge up the right lore on the oaths, that is. You might rip the Dark Ages: Fae for ideas on Stone, Iron, and Gold Oaths.

    3 - The Imperial capital was Greyhawk, the city of faded glory. The prize for the various contenders. Held, looted, sacked, and fought over like an old bone, over and over.

    4 - Hatred. Since hatred is fed and sealed with generations of border wars, insurgency, assassination, torture, massacre, and mass sacrifice, the evil demigods and spirits are currently ascendant.

    5 - Adventuring. Aside from the need for mercenaries and magistrates, there's a demand for ancient secrets both to validate claims on the throne and to dig up artifacts and lore that might give one side the necessary edge.

    6 - Pre-load Feud: Players all start with a bloodfeud against another nation and must take a named NPC or small group of NPCs as an enemy. Bonus is double experience, maybe, and favored enemy bonuses applied against these foes.

    7 - Pre-load Allegiance: Players each favor one of the factions or nations striving for the throne. Neutrality is possible, but if it becomes known, that position becomes a social stigma. Give experience for helping one's chosen faction in some way.

    8 - Magic is Degraded. Compared to the arcane might of the good old days, magic is a weak sister. Except for blood magic and magic that draws off the power of feuds and division.

    9 - The Land is Wounded. Individual nations take on something of the nature of their people, their oaths, their hatreds, and their dreams. So each nation may have subtly different laws of magic, for instance, or may twist with the moral fabric of its people.

    10 - A Ray of Hope. Old Imperial banners, magics, and gear, if used selflessly to heal the land. Maybe.

    Okay, so getting one of the Vecna artifacts is like the culmination of years' worth of slaying stuff to be awesome enough to deserve it. But, being good Narr monkeys, we know the good stuff startswith just giving the players what they want and seeing where the story goes from there.

    The Hand And The Eye
    A Sorcerer & Sword one-sheet

    Long time ago, at the cusp of world domination, the archlich Venca's body was sundered, and in a perversion of the Osiris myth, the pieces seek to reunite so that Vecna may be reborn. The PC sorcerers have voluntarily grafted one of the relics of Vecna to themselves to gain personal power.

    The relics are obviously Parasite-type demons, and have to be the character's starting demon. Whether the character amputated his own flesh just for the graft, or used it to replace an already-missing part is at the player's discretion. It's recommended that the infamous Hand and Eye of Vecna be the first two relics selected, with other parts invented as necessary to accommodate additional players. Use the D&D texts' description of powers for inspiration, but certainly don't slavishly adhere to these either. (For reference, the canonical lesser relics of Vecna, as described in the adventure Die Vecna Die!, are the scalp of Vecna, the first digit [thumb] of Vecna, the second digit [index finger] of Vecna, the third digit [middle finger] of Vecna, the right eye of Vecna, the molar of Vecna, the incisors of Vecna, the foot of Vecna, the heart of Vecna, the skin of Vecna, and the last digit [pinkie] of Vecna. But seriously, don't use all of these in a given game.)

    Reimagining Greyhawk

    The game is assumed to take place on Oerth, but you may want to avoid setting it in the Flaeness to avoid getting tripped up with creative baggage. You could set it someplace else on the continent of Oerik, perhaps west of the Twin Cataclysms, or set it in a different era than the standard Greyhawk campaign, perhaps long before the Twin Cataclysms occurred.

    As described in Sorcerer & Sword, the tidy dichotomy of quaint non-human "races" is right out. If desired, demi-humans must be drifted into either genuine human beings bearing a shared, ununusal culture and phenotype (recommended for halflings) or alien, terrifying anthropomorphic nature spirits, (recommended for elves) unsuited for PCs and handled mechanically as demons. (But see the first three Lore descriptors in S&S for exceptions!) Golblinoid races and their ilk are replaced by various bands of mongrel near-men, similar to those in proper sword & sorcery. Other "monsters" are handled as types of Immanent demons.

    (As a fun aside, in this setting an NPC like Iuz the Old, technically a demon in D&D terms, is actually a powerful sorcerer with the Half-Breed descriptor, who gets his power from his Demon Lord consort Zuggtmoy in a clear example of the classic "demon girlfriend" of core Sorcerer.)

    Humanity is defined as the ability to freely, bodily carry out one's own desires. At zero humanity, the graft (or whatever) usurps control of the host, horrifyingly trapping him as a slave to his own flesh. Humanity is gained by successfully using the demons as mere tools towards achieving one's own (original) goals. Humanity is lost when the sorcerer compromises his goals for new goals (presumably made available by the demon's power) and generally allowing the line between the demon's personality and his own to blur.

    The relic demons have a shared Desire to reunite by means of all being grafted to the same host. Vecna is reborn and basically destroys the world if a) all grafts are bound to the same host, and b) the host hits 0 Humanity.

    Unlike the D&D rules, hosts are not necessarily killed if a relic is violent severed from their body, since they're sorcerers and can do all that death-defying willpower stuff. This also keeps players from being eliminated if a sorcerer is making progress on the path of collecting all the relics.

    It's important to emphasize the morally "Grey" aspect of Greyhawk in this game. In particular, the institutions of Law should be portrayed in an unpleasant light, with members of civilization's establishments being pompous, self-righteous fuckwits, only scarely preferable to the inhuman horrors of chaos. (Clerics of Pholtus are good candidates here.) The concept of "good" is only possessed by individuals, those exceedingly rare individuals fortunate enough to remain innocent (and frankly naive) of the ancient evils that permeate this world. Protecting these individuals from harm probably counts for Humanity gain, too.

    Most importantly, this game is "no myth," with elements from the existing Greyhawk setting not being a part of the world unless explicitly brought in by players.

    Recommended reading for tone: Vecna: Hand of the Revenant, Iron Hammer Graphics, 2002.
