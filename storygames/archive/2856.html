    \n There's two kinds of feedback most creative endeavors need: encouragement and criticism. That's the same if you're writing a novel, touring as a band, or publishing your own game. Without encouragement, many people will not be able to muster up the energy to charge ahead. But they also need criticism to refine their creative endeavors.

    I think of SG as more of an encouragement place. I think of the Forge as 100% criticism. When I want to have something pounded to bits, I post it at the Forge. When I want to share my excitement about something without the need yet to have it be torn to pieces, I post it elsewhere, like on my LJ or here. That's not to say there's not good critical feedback here; there is, but it's nowhere near the same thing.

    The issue is related to something Matt W. just posted about: lots of us aren't at the Forge much anymore. I poke my head in there almost every day, but I don't post as often as I should.

    I think it shouldn't be expected that we always criticize everything everybody does at all places without also sharing your enthusiasm. Mutual praise is not toxic per se; it's toxic ONLY if it's the only feedback you get. But harsh criticism without support is ALSO toxic, because it demolishes the efforts and makes it look like no one gives a shit about what you're doing anyway.

    So: middle ground. I say keep the Forge for the harsh, direct criticisms, keep other places for more supportive environments, and make a consistent effort to participate at both.

    \n Sure, actual play is great. Unfortunately, by that time, you've already sold an unbaked, only somewhat playtested game to a bunch of people who had the expectation it was done.

    And that is going to color those people's perception of other indie games. And that includes their perception of indie games not associated with the Forge. To be really blunt, those people would have some justification if they felt they were ripped off.

    You don't have to care. That's your right. But if you do care, if you don't want to give game players and game buyers a negative perception of the quality of the games produced by your colleagues, then you should listen to what Matt is saying.

    The first step is not to think about putting out a revised edition. Instead, it's to recognize that many games that were released at Gen Con at The Forge last year weren't finished games. They were ashcans. They were ashcans with pretty, perfect bound covers and polished looking layout, but ashcans still.

    And don't get me wrong. This is not the first time the problem has reared its head. In 2005 we released a slew of revised editions of games, many of which had been first released in 2004.

    Calling them ashcans is not meant to be derogatory. Most of the games from the early days of The Forge spent some time as ashcans. Some spent years that way. The ashcan step is a healthy part of the game design process, when you get outside criticism and playtesting.

    And just to be clear, the problem is not that these games were sold. It's perfectly acceptable to sell an ashcan. Michael Miller showed us that. A number of early Forge games allowed people to buy ashcan versions of PDFs with a discount for the eventual finished game. Ashcans can be sold, given away, or just demoed, depending on your preferences.

    The only caveat is that people need to know they're paying for an unfinished, unseasoned version of the game and that their feedback will help lead to a finished, polished version of the game. Once that happens all my issues vanish. I think Matt and Ron and Paul and others feel the same as well.

    My suggestions:
    1. The rush to Gen Con is proving hugely destructive, in my opinion. It's ok to slow down. It's not really ok to skip outside playtesting, outside criticism or editing in order to get it ready by Gen Con. Bring it as an aschan instead if it's not done.

    2. Playtest, playtest and playtest some more. You need both in-house and blind outside playtests.

    3. If you don't want to post your rules text online (perfectly valid), send it to picked colleagues who can provide some honest and constructive feedback.

    4. Listen to your playtesters and critics, consider what they have to say, and make changes if you feel their comments are valid.

    5. Hire an editor. And I don't mean a copy editor (though it would be good to have one too). Let them help you organize your text. Let them help you find text that is missing or rules that are poorly explained.

    6. Bring your finished text to Gen Con (or not) and kick ass!

      \n There's lots to respond to, so bear with:

      The Forge Brand
      I'm going to stand with the folks saying the Forge Brand is dead. It's functionally useless, it does not describe anything about the products, it has zero quality control or barrier to entry. Now, to be fair, Forge Brand was never anything formal or intended; it sort of just happened, and it was useful and reliable for a few years and now it's not. I don't think we should expect anything more from an accident of game design history. We can, however, learn from it and try to intentionally build brands to do what Forge Brand used to do. This might mean your own personal brand; it may mean something like the Collective Endeavor brand; it may mean something informal like the Those Guys In Masachusetts brand. This is where we can take a cue from Luke, who used Forge Brand as a springboard for launching his own brand, complete with little three-quarter wheel on fire.

      Half-Baked Games
      This is a problem, yes. It is not, however, a new problem. As with many things indie, Sorcerer is a prime example. The forum threads explaining how to play the game that you already bought the book for are well-known. There are multiple supplements that continually refine what Sorcerer "is." Ron has repeatedly said he has no plans on compiling those volumes together or releasing a revised edition. Now, from what I understand Ron is following a slightly different publishing model akin to academic publishing, where each book is an artifact of his thinking on gaming at the time. (Correct me if I'm wrong, Ron!) That's all well and good, and has some pretty potent advantages for the gaming community as a whole, as it lays out the practice of roleplaying and the developing thought on it and encourages readers to participate in that development. But it also directly conflicts with this aesthetic of a game book that you can buy, read, and play without any other participation.

      In this thread, Tony has espoused a similar publishing aesthetic: the old games are there as signposts and the new games will be better than the old games. Or on the other hand, there have always been unfinished games at the Forge. Shatterzone, for instance, or 1984 Prime. I believe the former is dead but the latter is being revised, which is awesome. But the point is: this is not new. We should not expect indie games to be miraculously exempt from Sturgeon's Law -- especially since indie games are defined by their lack of gatekeepers and their standards.

      Which is not to say that there's nothing to be done, of course.

      Playtesting, Ashcans, and Editors
      There are in fact many things to be done, and none of them are a magic bullet. "We need more playtesting" is like saying "The world needs more good people." While there is such a thing as too much playtesting, it's really really hard to get there. To my mind, there are two sides to the playtesting need: quantity and quality. It is very hard to get both. From my own experience, FLFS had a lot of playtesting but I received little actionable feedback. (This was not, as I told myself on occasion, because I was just that awesome and didn't need revisions.) With my later games, playtests have point-by-point guides on what needs thorough playtesting and what I'd like to hear back about. SoL may have a freaking form to fill out. This is not because I know everything that needs to be addressed, but because multiple, specific questions provoke people to think and develop their own understanding of their experiences -- and it's that understanding that I need, not "X was neat, but Y was kind of boring." I also have an absolutely awesome weekly playtest circle, more on which later.

      I am intrigued by the ashcan thing. I am avidly watching this experiment, and have high hopes that it will develop neat new strategies to address the playtesting issue. My biggest concern right now, though, is one of volume: we already have more games than playgroups. If every game being developed goes through this ashcan stage, it seems to me we will see the same glut in ashcans that we saw in the "finished" books at the last GenCon booth. So: I await the Ashcan Front's results.

      Beyond playtesting and revising the game design, jehosaphat do some of these games need some significant editorial work. To clarify: not "you have a dangling participle" but "the order in which you present these topics does not aid comprehension" and "the structure of the book is counter-intuitive." There is this really big step between "functioning game design described in a word document" to "physical book to be sold as a product" that a lot of games gloss over. This is a completely different step than playtesting, and I've seen many many designers conflate the two. The obvious solution is to hire editors, but to be perfectly honest I don't see this happening in indie games. That directly conflicts with the low-overhead DIY ethic of this community. I do not know what can "fix" this issue.

      Playtesting Network
      Somebody mentioned setting up a playtesting network or conglomerate. I am incredibly lucky to participate in a weekly playtest circle in Los Angeles. We have four designers and two just-players-(so-far), and about ten projects between the lot of us. That means that, just on our own, we usually have something to play/test/beat with a stick every week. However, we can and have traded playtests with other designers outside of our circle. The arrangement is simple: we'll playtest yours if you playtest ours. While we have the significant advantage of living in a major urban center, it really was not very difficult setting our group up, and I totally believe that it can be replicated elsewhere. Enough of these groups trading playtests back and forth would be a pretty potent playtesting network.

      I'll reign myself in, here, but I think the jist of my point is thus: there is not, has never been, and never will be quality control for indie games. There can be, however, quality control of individual games and individual brands, and my money is on creating that. Doing it "for the community" is not going to work as long as "the community" has an open door. That's just a pyramid scheme. Quality control is about sustainability, and the first step for sustainability is in limiting what needs to be maintained. Once you do that, quality control is easily within reach.

      I've said before, I believe beta playtesting is extremely important. The lesson I learned form developing/publishing The Mountain Witch was that the way you make a game "work" is through explaining the game text... More specifically, games don't work on rules alone, they require very specific "soft" play techniques. If you don't (or can't) communicate these techniques through explanation/advice text, people won't "get" your game. I actually consider the writing/explaining to be, oh, 60% or so of design. Yep, I consider actual rules development to really only comprise, maybe 30% of a "finished" game. Beta testing, in my mind, is more about developing your advice and explanations than it is about the rules.

      [edit] And you can only develop your explanations/advice by being forced to explain yourself 6-12 times to people who just don't get it. You really need a significant number of beta-type playtesters. [/edit] Finding beta/independent playtesters is difficult, though, and that's where the ashcan thing comes in. Forcing people to spend money encourages buy-in.

      But I think there's another issue at work here. People design much more privately than they use to. This encourages the illusion that a design or text is "finished". I believe the more open design you use to see at the Forge would fill in alot of the holes of a design/text similar to beta-testing. Because of the more open attitude of the past, beta-playtesting as a discrete stage was probably less crucial than it is today.

      \nAlexander's brilliant idea is to create a private google community where we will work with the designer in small groups exchanging feedback. Then after the process is over, and with the designers permission, we will make the process public.

      This is what I did to develop My Life with Master. The development forum, sans the rules text itself, was made publicly available at The Forge once the game was complete. And it is the same process I've been using for Acts of Evil. The population of that forum has grown over the months I've been working on the game, and now supports probably somewhere near thirty members.

      But the game isn't done, there are still some thorny design problems, and my local posse and the population of the Acts of Evil forum are pretty much tapped out for insights.

      I don't think the commonly cited half-baked games of Gen Con 2006 are the product of designers having struggled to create small communities. I think they're the result of designers struggling over what to do when their small community is tapped out for insights before the game's design issues are solved. Do they publish it anyway? Or do they put it on the shelf, and accept that their future designs will benefit from what they learned working on it? I never hit this point with My Life with Master. And I'd hazard that most of the stand-out indie games never hit this point. But I hit it with Soul of Man, after much playtesting, and I put the game on the shelf. And now I'm at that point with Acts of Evil.

      I could just make the playtest forum public. But then I lose the "small community" benefit we're talking about.

      Yes, some designers are struggling to create small communities. And forced to work among the creative entropy of a large community, mostly what those designers produce are incomplete games. But I can't see any evidence that the half-baked games we're discussing are the output of designers struggling to create small communities. They're the product of a designer who's tapped out his small community choosing not to confront the threat of creative entropy from taking the design to larger community. What this designer wants is a way to import just enough insight to get over the hump without losing the heat of the small community.

      I think doing an ashcan is one solution. When I ran an Acts of Evil playtest at Forge Midwest last year I realized that I'd inadvertently identified two members of the game's eventual population of adherents, and that exactly what I needed was adherents from the future to tell me how to solve my design problems. I think doing an ashcan is an endeavor of identifying a small community of your future adherents. Trading playtests and trading feedback are often a grind of mutualism. Your future adherents are a true heat proposition. They want you to realize your game as much as you do.

      And if anyone has achieved design success using a different solution for your tapped out small community, I'd love to hear how you did it.

      \n I've been following the ashcan and QC threads from a distance, both here and at The Forge. I'm not a designer and I have no intention to be, so I usually stay out of the discussion when it feels like a club discussion. But I feel I ought to chime in a few points to give perspective.

      First of all, let me give my background: I have been an avid gamer for nearly 25 years, and I am married to another avid gamer. I like both playing and GMing, I have played about 100 systems (not counting separate editions of the same game), and probably read three times as many. I am very involved in the local gamer community, including helping organize clubs, weekly games, special game day events, and annual conventions. I love tinkering with systems and adapting them to various settings. In the last few years I've been buying small press games like candy. I'm an active member on many online fora. I think I can say that "I'm a player, not a hater!" :-) So now I'm going to yell at "you designers" a bit but I want you to remember that I am your most devoted fan.

      Quality. Yes, it's true: the quality of small press and indie games is very variable, in terms of both content and presentation. Overall I would say that to my eye, it appears to be improving. However, there are more games being written each year so more of the half-finished games are bound to make it to publication. I support and applaud everyone's efforts to improve their own products, and I promise that in the future I will try to give truthful feedback instead of staying silent on flaws. However...

      Critique. I can tell you that whether here, on The Forge, or on other boards, it's really hard to give that kind of feedback in light of the "convert" mentality of some fans. The attitude that makes it really hard is the well-wishers shouting down criticism with claims of "You just don't understand how it's supposed to be played," or drowning it out in a chorus of "Awesome!" Very often also, it feels as if critiquing is an activity in which only game designers are allowed to engage. Here's my thing, though: while another designer may be able to give you pointers, overall \u2014 who the fuck cares about what other designers think? What matters is what people who might buy and play your game \u2014 GMs, players \u2014 think.

      Playtesting. How can it possibly be that hard to find playtesters? There are thousands of gamers out there that would be delighted to playtest games for the simple pleasure of having their names in the cover of the final version, getting bragging rights and insider info, and getting a free PDF. I love to run playtests, and we frequently run playtests at our local club's weekly drop-in games. But if you're looking for playtesters by checking The Forge, Story Games, and other designers' blogs... Buddy, you're at the demand side of the chain. Go check the supply end! Advertise! Participate in your local gaming lists! And I approve of the idea of having a feedback form for your playtesters if there are specific questions you want answered. I always pay close attention to the playtest instructions so I know what the author is looking for.

      I see a big discussion of what technology to use. That reminds me of myself, running my first campaign lo these decades ago \u2014 lovingly handcrafting maps that people would probably never glance at, detailing scenes and NPCs that would be bypassed by the players' quirky strategies. If you spend your effort in playing with technologies, you're probably drawing away from the effort of actually trying to recruit playtesters. A colleague of mine calls it "Procrastination through interest": fiddling with a task you find more interesting in order to put off a chore.

      The Forge label. I'm here to tell you that the "Forge label" is not a big selling point except for other people on the same thread at The Forge. The rest of gamers either have never heard of it or have a neutral to unfavourable impression. In case you're wondering, I'm personally neutral, meaning I have as many bad impressions of The Forge label as I have good ones. There are some neat games that came from there, but also a terrible lot of insularity. So don't feel like a majority of the people who might be interested in your game will be attracted by a Forge blessing.

      Insularity and inbreeding. Holy crap, but it's an inward-looking community. Here are some quotes from the current discussion that made me laugh my head off.\n
      Posted By: JuddThe most important thing I've taken from the Forge is that the people who play games are just as important (if not, moreso) than the people who design them. Or to put it another way, the act of playing a game is as important as the act of designing them.
      \nWow, thank you. As a friend said, "next we'll be saying that drivers and passengers are as important as auto engineers."\n
      Posted By: TonyLBDo you get the alternate perspective, from which I can say "Releasing the piece-of-crap game that is your best first effort at the honest-to-God, this-is-not-a-drill act of self-publishing is absolutely the best thing that you can do for yourself and for the community, especially if you make lots of mistakes and therefore learn a lot in the process"?
      \nUm, no. Frankly, from the minute I pay money for a product, I really don't want to see your first effort. If it's published as a finished game, I want a reasonably finished game. Otherwise, yes, ashcans or free downloads or what have you. When I buy a book, it's not about the game designer's growth process and self-fulfillment. Even with the authors I really love, if I think this particular product sucks ass, I don't want to buy it. No matter how fulfilled the author would be.

      BDFD? When I read some of the games, and some of the game discussions, I feel like the real label should read "BDFD \u2014 By Designers, For Designers." Seriously, there is no reason in the world that a well thought-out game should read like the unholy love child of Ayn Rand and L. Ron Hubbard. Like Jules said, "English, motherfucker, do you speak it?" Open an indie game at random. Some are a pleasure to read, yes; but how many are full of jargon or redefined words, sure to confuse or annoy the average gamer?

      I sometimes think a few designers (mostly the very new ones) harbour the secret belief that if they make their game a bit difficult, if they play hard to get, they'll only get the smart gamers. Well, no. You'll probably get the dumb ones \u2014 the ones that haven't figured out that there's a shelf full of legible games they can play. Don't assume that the average gamer can't "get" your game because s/he's not smart enough. Most games, indie or not, can be understood and played by most players. Whether the player and the game are a good fit is a different matter, but if your book is written in jargon, even (or especially) Forge jargon, chances are most players won't stay long enough to find out.

      "Whate'er is well conceived is clearly said,
      And the words to say it flow with ease."

      Now go out there, write games that a regular person can read without pulling out their hair, and post some calls for playtesters on your local mailing list, at the FLGS, on RPGNet, whatever. I'll try to read, playtest, review, and recommend as many as I can.

      I am interested in playing. Full stop.

      Which is something that doesn't always get the respect you'd think it would.
      \nThis is something I've been wanting to post about, but I figured maybe Brand would post about it first.

      In one of these recent threads, someone said something like 'we're producing more games than we have players', and I was surprised nobody picked up on it. Because frankly I think it's true. We've got threads and threads full of people talking about how many indie games they're just dying to get a chance to play. Brand, who appears to actually roleplay about twenty times more often than mere mortals, still talks about all these games on his shelf he hasn't gotten to yet.

      So yeah, we have this culture of design. We have endless discussions about its health, about where it should go next. But I see no corresponding culture of play.

      And then, people wonder why it's hard to find playtesters, why getting feedback on new games is harder, whatever. Maybe it's because we have too many goddamn games, already, and maybe it's time to start encouraging each other to play them.

      We have a 'designer challenge' thread 100 posts long. There is no corresponding 'play challenge', even though, in theory, every game produced should lead to hundreds if not thousands of play experiences. I mean, games are supposed to be reusable tools -- you shouldn't need to design a game for every new campaign you want to play. That just seems screwy, to me.

      --

      I'm not saying people should stop designing games, or stop talking about game design, or that the designer challenge thread isn't awesome. I just wonder what happened to the other side of the coin.

      So much of the insight and the focus of the whole 'indie' scene is the understanding that good design encourages fun play. But sometimes it looks like that's the only thing that anyone thinks encourages good play anymore, and that's so obviously wrong. Yes, we all want awesome game designs, but there's a lot of other things that encourage fun play. I think the current indie community is having a lot of trouble providing that other stuff.

      \n I think I may not have made my point very clearly on the issue of designer culture, and on designing games for other designers.

      The O.P. was discussing the problem of games that are rushed to publication before they are ready. I think it's perfectly fine for a game designer to write a game for other game designers, a game for people who will have the additional context to understand them, as Shreyas put it earlier. But that kind of game is not the one discussed in the O.P.: it does not need to get published on the mass-market. It can be distributed to fellow designers as, yes, ashcans and PDFs, traded or sold for printing cost. You write Mutant Mitonchondriae, you send me a copy and in exchange I send you a copy of my game, My Dinner with Entree.

      But if the authour goes to mass-market publications \u2014 yes, that includes Lulu, IPR, Your Games Now, RPG Now, Drivethru RPG, etc. \u2014 then the implication is that unless clearly marked otherwise, this is a finished product for general use. A majority of gamers should be be able to open it, read it, understand it and play it without having to e-mail the designer, scan the forum threads, or otherwise do extensive research on "How does this play."

      Therefore, for any game that is mass-marketed, people who play games are not just as important as the people who design them \u2014 they are more important. They're the ones shelling the money and buying something that should work (note I'm not saying "something they should like.")

      Say for example than the bit I am proudest of in My Dinner with Entree is Mechanic X; I think it's great and my designer friends like it too. Before going to production I do blind playtest, where the playtesters have to play the game as provided without getting additional information from me, and a majority of them then say they hated Mechanic X or couldn't figure out how to use it. I got my work cut out for me, don't I?

      If I want to publish this game for general use, then I have to go back to the drawing board and figure out how to make it work and explain it better, then playtest again. If I can't get a majority of playtesters to use Mechanic X, then I can get rid of it, I can market the game to fellow designers and clearly describe it as such, I can continue playtest with revised versions, I can abandon the project for a while, I can seek help from other designers, but I sure as Hell shouldn't just slap a nice cover on it, insert a note that it has been playtested by 354 people, and publish it on Lulu. It would be unethical because it would mean I am willing to sell an unfinished product as if it was ready.

      Note that there is nothing particularly indie about this. Note also that for the first 20 years of the hobby, it was almost a tradition for publishers (FASA, Chaosium, White Wolf, GURPS, etc.) to do just that \u2014 say they playtested but not address any of the feedback received, or rush to publish without playtesting, especially for supplements. The differences are (A) that as creators-owners, you guys can decide when you are ready to publish without being rushed by the company; and (B) that instead of one bad supplement reflecting on the company, one bad indie game can reflect on the entire industry.

      I'm glad Shreyas came out against detailed Actual Play posts. I agree with him completely. I almost said something myself, but I thought that might be too controversial, even for a list of controversial ideas.

      \n

      But now that he's opened that bag, I want to say why I think Actual Play posts, as we have come to think of them, are not helpful for quality control. They're trying to do too many things at once. For example, there are instructions on the Forge about how to include the names of all the people involved, which characters they chose to play, how they interacted with each other, moments when they seemed to turtle or to struggle for control with the GM, and so on. These instructions are really intended for GNS analysis, back when people were trying to investigate GNS more and analyze where their play group was having problems. Most of it is useless for quality control, unless someone is reporting that their group, which normally has no problems, had problems when playing your game. Even then, it's only useful if you can eliminate non-game reasons for the sudden problems.

      \n

      Another problem is the focus of most AP reports on the story that play session produced, or the "insular drama" and "arcane events" that Shreyas mentions. I believe the focus on that comes from the idea of AP as a selling point for a game. Maybe that's useful after a game has reached the post-ashcan phase, but when the game is still being developed, all the designer wants to know are moments that stood out during play: high points and low points. "We spent 45 minutes following the prep instructions and got so bored, we almost gave up." "We totally weren't expecting the lightning-fast exchanges and general excitement of the repartee phase!"
