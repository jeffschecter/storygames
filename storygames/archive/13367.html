\n So I'm starting to brainstorm a little noir game that builds suspense by giving the players massive power to shape the events of the game in their characters' favor, but with a very limited number of times they can use that power. For example, imagine a detective who knows he can overcome six enemies in direct confrontation because he has exactly six bullets. If he runs out and gets into another scrape, he's dead and his player knows it.

The idea's still very fuzzy, and I need inspiration, so here's the question: How do your favorite games maintain (and ratchet up) tension throughout a session? I'm asking specifically about rules and techniques that mechanically reenforce an increasing sense of dread, chaos, or weight to a session as it progresses. (E.g., Static in Lacuna, or the Jenga tower in Dread.)

    An important part, to me, about the tension in Dread is the physicality of the tower. You are constantly reminded by the presence of this tower that one of your characters, at least, will die. Further, it's also random\u2014you don't know whether this pull will be your last, but you do have a good sense of when it's risky.

    \n

    So: physicality, and randomness. These, I think, are good.

    \n

    In the idiom, something like Russian Roulette might work?

    OK, to play with a real roulette-wheel mechanic for a moment, ignoring feasibility: have a wheel, and each time they do X, put a skull on one of the squares in it. Each time they do Y, spin the wheel. Skulls are bad.

    \n

    Of course, the other side of any limited resource mechanic is a refresh mechanic. Typically, such as in Dread or Mouse Guard, this is something genre-required, but distasteful to the characters. The idea, thus, is to make this action appealing to the players, by rewarding them, even if the characters might not like it. (Though I guess, in Dread, the resource is shared, so it's still distasteful to the player whose character dies. But also satisfying in a more meta way.)

    \n Just having 6 bullets and no more will make for cautious play, I think. I think you might look to gambling for models -- most popular gambling games create an inevitable downward trend of one's money, but with enough rallies and opportunities to win some back to fool you into thinking you might win big. Perhaps a mechanic by which you can risk some of your resources to be more effective, and very good rolls will actually replenish that resource -- so people will be tempted to take risks in the hopes of staying in the game longer.
