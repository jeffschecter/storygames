\n Hi All,
\n
\nI'm trying to work out what sorts of problems might arise from creating a game that uses, for lack of a better term, a round-robin style of game mastering -- a different player assumes the mantle of GM with each session. IAWA has provisions for this, but how would it work with a more traditional game?
\n
\nHere's what I have in mind:
\n
\nDuring the initial session everyone rolls a character, including the person who plans to GM that first game. Their character is either run as an NPC or -- and this I like better -- actually serves as the "unreliable narrator" of the scenario; she/he is presumed to be there, but they take no active part in the goings-on. If, by some chance, that session ends with the party being killed, the current GM's character is the only survivor. For the next session, another player takes up the GM's mantle; his character vanishes from the spotlight, and the story continues to unfold -- perhaps off on a new tangent every session as different storytelling styles are explored.
\n
\nAre there any games that already use such a system? What difficulties should I expect?
\n
\nThanks!

    \n I played a game of Runequest that worked this way. We changed GMs every session, and it worked fine. The overarching plot did get pretty bizarre, but it was intended to be a light-hearted game from the beginning. I'm not sure how well this would work for a more serious story. Part of the fun in that game was in ending your session in such a way as to make continuing the story as challenging as possible, or beginning your turn by twisting the events that went before into a new perspective.

    For a more serious game, you might need to put some ground rules in place about how everyone builds on the previously determined story. Or change GMs after a story, not a session, as already suggested.

    We played that the GM's character took no active part in the story, which worked well enough.

    On a totally unrelated note, thinking about that game reminded me of a silly but rather fun element that got added. Some evil shaman or god cursed us, so that anyone who could see auras could see a word somehow in there. If any one of us said that word, we got transported to an alternate plane of... whatever the word was. I seem to remember that we spent time in the planes of beer, wankel rotary engines, ants, and seeds (amongst others).

    \n Our group did this for a WWII game, but we structured it so that each session was an independent episode that involved characters selected for a mission in some far-flung part of the globe. We rotated GMs each session, with each doing one session per game year, often coordinated with events of the war. So one session might be a raid with the USAF in February 1944 and the next a paratrooper insertion in Normandy in July. We used a troupe-style cast of characters, and the players for each session would choose PCs to play for the session. There was some overall continuity and character development, but it was definitely secondary to the overall story.

    \n Hey Scott, I played/GMed in the game Steve is describing. We had a box of characters on index cards, and the GM of the moment would give out some general information, and the players would root through the box to find their favorite guy for that sort of mission. There was very little consistency from mission to mission - some were bizarre, some were comic, some were gritty, some (well, mine) were Lovecraftian. Characters developed over time and had some shared experiences. NPCs emerged, both allies and villains. The most satisfying thing to me was the reincorporation of elements from GM to GM. We had some memorable villains and NPCs who seemed to transcend personal preference and GMing style to reappear in fun ways.

    \n The beauty of our system was that it didn't matter who showed up to play each week, since it was all self-contained from game to game. Something to watch out for then is when your story runs over into two sessions. We had a few GMs (including me) that had trouble closing in one session, meaning that we spent the next session finishing the story. It wasn't a big problem, but it threw us off our game a bit.

    Howard's stories seem perfect for this sort of thing- very episodic in nature. If you can tie things up in a neat bundle by the end of a regular session, the next GM doesn't have to worry about untangling leftover threads and remembering where you left off.

    One problem I see with using Conan or something similar is the focus on a single hero character. Our system worked because we had a big cast, and it didn't matter if the PCs for one session were completely different than the last. It also didn't matter much if the next GM set his story on the opposite side of the world- there were characters to pick from in the Pacific theater as well, so we didn't miss a beat.

    \n I designed an Adventure Creation Procedure for Red Box Hack, back when it was mega popular. I do not know if it would work for something more severe in tone.

    The idea was that you would create a party of characters together. Then you discuss, in-character, and decide which character to send away on a side quest. In the WWII game, I suppose, one member of the team might be sent to call for reinforcements, or to gather important intelligence, while the rest attempt to storm a fortress or something. Whoever had their character sent off (or volunteered to take the mission) becomes the GM for the game. To end the session, the GM just narrates her character returning to join the group. The GM also collects XPs based on how the game went, and we find out whether her character succeeded in her mission.

    I never got to playtest it, though.
