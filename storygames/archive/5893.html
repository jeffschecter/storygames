\n I'm writing a free game to be our local meetup's icebreaker. Well, actually this is the same game I'm always talking about but that's what got me off my ass.

The game uses a sort-of-stakes thing with a strong GM. I want to provide a cheat sheet for what happens with the dice, and currently I have 2 such sheets, depending on whether the GM initiates or the Player initiates the task. I highlighted the difference on the second sheet in red.



Since some players will have never gamed before and others will be GMing it straight from my notes, I want them to be able to go through it visually if they have to.

I know it's ugly - does anyone have a suggestion on how it could be better?

    \n Second one's much better, but I'd scale up your fonts to use all space in a box (minus a bit of padding, of course). Or, rather, I'd tweak the Style of the in-box text until the box with the most text is nearly completely full, less padding.

    Bold and italics is overkill, for your callouts--not what I'd call them, BTW: I'd say sidebars, FWIW--a callout is actually a technical term for a specific drawing element, and these ain't callouts (link). Anyway, I'd just use bold, unless you can find a proper--and therefore legible--bold-oblique font (i.e. not a roman font being forced italics and, thus, looking like it does above).

    Notions for tweaks:
    Move On The Clock sidebar above the No! arrow next to it. Move The Stick sidebar to underneath the box that it's current next to. Tuck the Snake Eyes under the Using... and Base or Ranked boxes. Integrate the blue "GM Chooses" junction into the box from which it flow, and move that box down into the top-middle. All of these shifts will allow it to be generally larger on the sheet and, thus, more legible. If not...
    Does your flowcharting progam have a "realign and pack" function? Give it a shot, if so. Without actually playing with the boxes manually, I can't tell... but I feel like there's a lot of wasted space in the most recent iteration, even as it significantly improves on the former.
    Or... GM doesn't state and takes the default and Are we on the clock could be on the same row, which would eliminate the need for the extra-long connector from the other Gm doesn't state box (this presumes that you merge GM Chooses into the Start box).

    Don't mix diagonal connectors (Yes, No) with right-angle connectors (the rest).

    Vertical align center for all, or vertical align top for all--don't mix and match.

    Consider an landscape layout (again, I'd have to play around more than I have time for, but I intuitively feel this flow 'wants' to be more horizontal). I think the sidebars are sort of "crowding you" out of space, and you're having to stretch things out too much to accommodate them.
    For that matter, is there some reason you couldn't pack the box-specific sidebars into the boxes to which they refer (i.e. The Stick, The Clock). You'd probably have to give up on the "all boxes same size" advise... but, hey, that's not a hard-and-fast rule anyway (else why are there sizing handles on those objects)? Better if the rule in use is RIGHT THERE as they are told to do something; even those sidebar boxes are sort of "disconnected" from the associated flow (they'd actually be callouts, if you used an indicator to connect them to their associated flow boxes). But I wouldn't do that: put the rules as succinctly as possible into the boxes. This will also have the ebefit of making those "important" boxes--the ones with the most associated rules--the largest and most prominent; a good thing, in my eyes.

    OK, enough for now... have fun with it (I actually, nerdly, embarrassingly LOVE to make process flowcharts).

    \n Sure, going under the box might work, but it of course uses up more vertical space. Making the arrow visible through the box would be bad, as it would lay behind the text and probably ruin legibility.

    The method itself works fine, though--used a lot, in Troubleshooting and Testing charts (ex: going through a checklist where each question could have one or more possible responses and actions, prior to moving to the next thing to check). It might not work for you, but it's perfectly comprehensible to me--and that's TWO whole data points for Ryan.

    As a reference aid, I favor something comprehensible and efficient over something rambling but complete. In this chart, the key info is all captured; and the only real goal left is to fit it onto a page (or two per page) and clean it up for fast visual scanning--few, if any, players will actually trace out the chart every time, all the way through... even new players. Most folks will grok it after a pass or two, and then will only want to be able to quickly find the spot they're at when a choice or roll must be made (hence my suggestion to bold key text in each box).

    ------------------
    That just gave me an idea, actually: flow chart + character sheet. It wouldn't work for a "laundry list" style of game--tons of spells or traits or skills to list, each with slight or major variations in rules--but it would work GREAT for tightly integrated, rules-lite, strict-process games. Imagine a flow chart sheet which has squares for every bit character data, exactly where they're needed at each point in the procedural flow. Then, laminate and get some wet-erase pens or grease pencils and you've got a pretty powerful reference tool!
