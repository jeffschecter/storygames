What *makes* fantasy?

timfire
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Play Advice
I've been thinking about fantasy alot lately, as it pertains to my design work for "In a Land Called". Last night I had a conversation about it with my wife on the subject, prompted by a blog post by Victor.

We came up with a few things that "make" fantasy, or at least, elements that make up what fantasy is.

Simplified Reality: My wife said the reason she likes fantasy is that everything is so simple, so clear. There's a clear order to things, and when something goes wrong, it's clear why it did and what needs to be done to fix it. The vague-ness of life is removed.

Exaggerated Reality: I added that reality has a certain exaggerated quality to it. I think this goes along with the above. Life isn't just black and white, it's also good-very-good and evil-bad-evil. Men are strong, monsters tough, and castles big.

Externalized Reality: My insight into fantasy is that internal emotions, struggles, and conflicts are externalized and given literal form. This is why I believe fantasy is most often about physical violence. The physical nature of violence is an externalized form of the internal struggle we feel everyday. I believe this element is combined with the two points above to create the kind of grand imagery we associate with fantasy, such as monsters and knights.

Otherness? Victor argues in his blog that fantasy has a certian "otherness" about it, that the reality of fantasy must be different from the reality we live with. I'm not sure if this is a distinct trait or if it's just a side-effect of the above points.

Wonderment? This might just be another word to describe the "otherness" Victor mentions, but fantasy tends to have a certain "wonderment" factor. There's something about good fantasy that connects with our childhood non-rational mind and emotions. I'm curious if people think this is a distinct quality unto itself or if it's a side-effect of other factors.

What do you think? Have I missed anything? I know there's the medieval color thing, but I think that's a given.

    I once upon a time, long long ago read an essay about the differences between Sci Fi and fantasy which added another element to the mix (sort of a cross of your externalized and simplified realities): emotional realism. Which is to say that in fantasy the thing that is most emotionally cathartic is often the correct thing to do, as opposed to SF where the most logical thing is often the correct thing to do.

    Thus in fantasy if you suspect your wife may be possessed and her possession could destroy the whole town, and all the authorities tell you there is no way to save her, you fight tooth and nail to save her because love is the most important thing! In SF, otoh, you may well take the opposite course of action. (It's worth noting that this essay said that most SF looking movies are really fantasy with SF costumes on, as its a rare movie that places the values of logic, science, and intellect above those of the emotional heart and tear jerking cathartic response.)

    Anyway, I was never sure how much I agreed with the essay, but it did make an interesting argument. Fantasy worlds are ruled by the heart, SF world are ruled by the head.

    The other thing that the combination of Simplified, Exaggerated, Emotionalized, and Externalized gives us is a lot of disguised political rhetoric in fantasy. A lot of fantasy fans will like or not like a fantasy story based on how closely its unacknowledged and unexamined ideals of issues of power, race, violence, sexism and similar issues match with their unacknowledged and unexamined ideals of the same. Because fantasy is made of such big bold strokes it fits into much the same "meaning making" niche that legend and myth used to, right alongside comic books and propaganda pamphlets.

    Because of this many fantasy worlds have dual issues of "how I wish the world really was" and "how I fear the world really is" running about under a thin layer of elf-eared frosting. In good fantasy this is theme making, in mediocre fantasy its transparently allegorical, and in poor fantasy it is masturbatory and full of smirking self-congratulations.

    October 2006 edited October 2006
    I have a somewhat different take on this idea. It's not quite, IMO, that fantasy is from the heart, and sci-fi from the head. Indeed, I'm sure we can find all sorts of examples of fantasy characters acting logically, and sci-fi characters acting from the heart. Assimov's characters tend to be heart-oriented, and it would be hard to see the work of the man who coined the term Speculative Fiction as other than Speculative Fiction.

    What's really going on is that fantasy assumes that the pre-rational mindset is true in the world of the fiction. Put more simply, magic works, or at least seems to from the character's POVs. Sure, it's possible to see the magic in Conan as smoke and mirrors - I recall one story where Conan actually deduces that this is true with one magician. But Thoth-amon's magic sure seems "real." To say nothing of the supernatural beings that litter the stories. Demons, gods, giant slugs.

    Sure we can postulate these things as having some sort of rational existence in the world in question. We can make it a sci-fi world if we want. Jorune and Tekumel both do this to an extent, actually. Anne McCaffrey does it in Pern. The difference between Pern and the world of Greyhawk is simply that we assume a priori that magic is real and does work in the fantasy world.

    In fact, from that POV, fantasy is just a large subset of Speculative Fiction that focuses on the question, "What if magic and the byproducts of magic were real?"

    By byproducts I mean dragons and such. Beings that only make sense if magic works. Oh, sure, again we can talk about telekinesis, and propose it having some basis in physics, and explain away the dragon as just a very interesting animal. But, again, in fantasy we don't do that. We instead assume that the dragon can fly because that's it's nature, and that in this world, that's enough to make it fly.

    In fact, fantasy literature makes the assumption that pre-rational societies do, that physics doesn't exist. Rather that the "natural" explanation for how things work is based on the mythic underpinnings of the thing in question. Spiders don't spin webs because they've evolved to do that, they spin webs because they all descend from Arachne under whose curse they suffer. That, in fact, everything can be explained by this sort of account of it's origin, if you only know it. That all things come from this sort of mythic origin.

    What's fascinating is how in RPGs we're constantly trying to make magic something that exists on top of a rational universe. John Kim has a good essay on this subject: http://www.darkshire.net/~jhkim/rpg/magic/antiscience.html

    I'm not sure why this is.

    But does Elrond use some psionic powers to be magical? Nope, in fact he's never seen even doing a "spell" or anything. What's magical about him is that he is unaging, and that everything he does has an aura of grace about it. Things he creates have qualities that mortals cannot embue to their creations. Elrond just is magical. Middle-Earth isn't just a place where magic exists inside an environ governed by physics...Middle-Earth is magical. And so is everything in it. Some of it is just more obviously magical than other parts. It was all created by Illuvatar, and it all functions according to the way he created it. Not to some impersonal physics that pre-exist.

    It's in this way that fantasy is like Myth. The sense of wonder comes from the awe of understanding that the universe in question has meaning embedded in it. That it's not the impersonal universe that we live in today. I think that all the other trappings are unimportant. Simplified reality? Well, no, just one which makes sense, because it was not created impersonally. Exaggerated reality? Well, not really, just a non-rational reality. Otherness, wonder? Well, that's the attraction of magic, something we've discarded largely in our world.

    At least that's how I've understood fantasy for a long time now.

    Mike

    Mike Holmes has aced it, I think. Fantasy at its root is premodern and mythic in its worldview - things work because they make mythic or metaphorical sense, not rational or scientific sense. This is why, I think, most fantasy novels that have an elaborate "This is how magic works, you have your four elements and your seven whozits and they interact to produce..." fall rather flat for me.

    Tolkien and Lewis have been tremendously influential on modern fantasy (though they certainly didn't invent it), and they were both in love with the premodern and specifically not in love with many aspects of modernism. So were their predecessors like Dunsany and George Macdonald. Saruman, to take one example, shows his evil nature by chopping down trees, building machinery and polluting the environment.

    To me, one of the key dividing lines between fantasy and science fiction is this: Fantasy works in premodern terms, and science fiction in modern terms (which may look like emotional vs rational, but it's more than that). Of course, there are genre-breakers like Le Guin who go far beyond those categories - which is wonderful. But one mark, to me, of poorly written fantasy is that it approaches fantasy tropes with a modernist mindset.

    Fantasy covers a lot of ground, but my sense of it is that viewing it as "just" speculative fiction whose premise is "what if magic were real" misses the point. I can't help but inject my personal judgment though when I admit that a lot of so-called fantasy is just that--science fiction with magic.

    I came to this realization during a conversation with John Morrow on my LJ, which drifted into the old controversy over what to do with the orc children, now that we've killed their parents? I draw the line at the idea of a fantasy world where the "absolute evil" of orcs is represented as a biologically inborn trait, rather than the product of a fantastic moral cosmology. The former is a kind of science fiction--Aliens, basically--the latter, fantasy. Elsewhere in the mix are swords & sorcery (an amoral but anthropocentric universe with magical trappings) and actual myth, which is similar in spite of the portrayals of actual gods & goddesses (at least in Greek myth).

    I'm sure there are plenty of ways to break things down. Let me suggest, though, a reason for distinguishing the category I've identified as "science fiction with magic" from "swords & sorcery". The difference is that "science fiction" is about the themes that arise from scientific speculation. Thus, "if economic inequality grows and organ transplants are perfected, will society begin finding more and more ways to take people's organs for the benefit of the rich?" (subject of a couple Larry Niven stories). "Swords & sorcery" on the other hand is about more basic stuff. The "technology" is essential to SF (even if it's magical technology); in S&S or "planetary romance", the technology is secondary.

    To my mind fantasy is just a matter of "let's decide exactly what is true to enable X", where X is a story, or a theme, or even an aesthetic. That's what's useful about it; you're unconstrained by mere facts. What purpose you put this freedom to is another matter. There's some amount of this in any fiction - you always have to create characters and events that didn't happen - but the label "fantasy" indicates a sweeping "fuck you" to reality for whatever reason. It's a "yes, yes I know that that's not how things really work. You are very clever. Now shut up and work with me here, ok?"

    And the problem there, as with all sufficiently broad ideas, is that it's bloody difficult to say much useful about it. Or at least I do. I mean, we've (as in writers and other people who aren't me) have gotten into certain habits about what's "safe" and accepted under the fantasy header, and what things people don't like reading about in a real world setting and may require a radically different context to enjoy. But I'm not sure if that says anything about fantasy other than to serve as examples of how it can be used. It seems better to work backwards from the elements/themes/feelings you want and try to work out the conventions for signposting them or techniques to evoke them

    As to the dichotomy of sci-fi vs fantasy - I'm not sure it's a useful idea. There's obviously a blurry border between them at the best of times, but I think that there being a border at all is mostly out of habbit. I suspect that all the easy things to point to re: sci-fi/fantasyness (rationalist worldview vs absolute morality, explained vs unexplained, robots vs dragons, whatever) are more force of habit than anything else. Hell, may as well lump in superhero comics while we're at it. The tropes have enough traction that they're useful telegraphing stuff to the reader (Oh, I get it, it's magic. Now I know where I stand) but I don't see anything fundamental about any of them.

    Fantasy is just so open an idea that unless you constrictively redefining it there will always a counterexample to any specific answer to "what makes fantasy fantasy"?

    Tim,

    To try and drag myself kicking and screaming back towards being helpful, I do have a couple of thoughts about some of those questions.

    For how we make pre-rational/scientific/modern assumptions, I think Heroquest offers one solid model here. In HQ all magic is tied to religion, and belief and magic go hand in hand in a powerful way. (A similar, though less complete system can be seen in 4th Edition Pendragon.) Combine that with Glorantha's semi-consensual reality (where the consent of the gods mediates direct human consent issues like those from Mage) and you get a world that is mythic in a powerful sense, as what everyone believes is both true and false, and magic is always subjective and prone to change.

    I'd also note that this is one of the areas where lots and lots of folks have real, serious problems with HeroQuest. Many of us are so very used to thinking about our modern and post-modern rationalistic thought as "the way people think" that breaking out of that paradigm can be difficult. Heroquest, I feel, has often stumbled in giving guidance in this area because of the scattered nature of the Gloranthan setting material where Artesia did a better job of portraying it in the setting material, but then lacked a system that backed it up as powerfully. (Artesia's system is good, but tends to be a bit too directly explanatory where Heroquest allows for relativism to some degree.)

    As for the role of the society vis a vie the individual... that's a big one, and not easy to sum up. The Lord of the Rings shows a different slant on it than does a Wizard of Earth Sea and both are radically different than Conan. The one constant that I do see is that the individual vs the group in fantasy is almost always a sharply drawn contrast, where in the author puts forward their views on the nature of the individuals relationship to the collective from the perspective of the individual. So Conan is against civilization in a real way, as Howard saw the collective as being something that makes men weak and corrupt. In Lord of the Rings the good individuals must step forward to lead the good, but undirected, people -- often at great cost to the individual. In Wizard of Earthsea, OTOH, the individual has to learn to subsume his interests to those of the group and to rely on their help and friendship to overcome his own issues.

    I don't know that fantasy has to be about magic, but the mythic tones of fantasy seem to ensure that it will have some element, at least, of what Mike called "the embedded meaning of the universe." Of course, it is worth noting that in some fantasy this embedded meaning is "madness and violence will overtake everything" -- as seen in Howard's work. The embeded meaning will be there, and will often resonate at an emotional or atavistic level, but that doesn't mean it has to be a good meaning, and it doesn't always have to be directly displayed in "magical" terms.

    Of course, I suppose this all depends on the definition of magic we're using, doesn't it? Are the Arthurian Romances about magic?

    And finally, in so much as fantasy worlds are something that have an embedded meaning, there is going to be some kind of moral stance involved. Myth and legend are powerful tools of meaning and morality generation, and even their bastard degenerate child of fantasy is going to share in that to some degree.

    Yeah, I think that those thematic elements, individial vs. society, internal vs external, all are susceptible to counter-examples.     OK, it's sci-fi, but very mystical sci-fi. :-)

    Mystical is not Mythical, by the way. For purposes of these discussions.

    I think that Brand's on target. The issue of magic, actually, is confused by the fact that in a pre-rational society viewpoint there is no magic at all. Magic, as a concept, always means "something that doesn't behave by the normal laws of the universe" or something like that. But in a pre-rational mindset, the fact that things work by what we'd call "supernatural" agents is how everything works. "Magic" is not an exception, but how everything works.

    Or, rather, again, there's no impersonal description of the universe existing outside of laws that bound it outside of magic. No physics. Things fall because that's the way somebody made the world. Not because of gravity.

    In fact, epistemologically there's really no difference between our POV and that of the pre-rational mindset. Meaning that gravity is a good description of how the universe works, but it still doesn't at all tell us why gravity exists. We still either have to discover it's meaning, or assume it has none.

    So when Brand says that Hero Quest links magic to religion, I always bristle a bit (this isn't the first time we've been through this). I'd say that it's more accurate to say that magic is the belief of the characters in HQ, and that these beliefs have mechanical weight. As I always point out, there are lots of atheistic beliefs in Glorantha, and that some of them specifically lead to magic abilities. For instance, "Sorcerers" wield the same sort of magic as other sorts of Adepts and Wizards." The difference between the two is one of what they believe - Sorcerers believe that the essence that they manipulate is an impersonal force, and the others believe that it's given by a monotheistic God (or even by other beings), and that when they die God will send the Sorcerers to hell for abusing his essence.

    Brand knows this objection, BTW, and doesn't really disagree if I'm not mistaken. So this isn't aimed at him, but at most everyone else.

    What's important is not the relativity which Brand notes (though that's interesting). Instead it's that the effects that we call magic are produced by the character's beliefs in how the world works, and to the characters this is just a normal manipulation of their world. Well, normal is an overstatement. It's just not an exception to some set of laws. It's abnormal in that in looking at their "magic" we focus on what we'd see as magical, and what the characters see as extraordinary results of their beliefs.

    That is, the character believes that his breathing is "magical," too. But he also knows that the ritual he knows to steal your breath is "special" in that it can only be done by appealing to that "intelligent design" of the universe. You breathe "naturally" in that nobody teaches you. But you learn to steal breath by, say, finding a spirit that'll do it for you. Or by emulating a god that did it.

    Yes, in both of these cases you know that you can steal the breath this way because of some story that somebody told - a myth - that explains how it can be done. But, then again, there's a similar story that explains why you can breathe "naturally."

    HQ is remarkable in that it attempts to do this, as Brand says, without indicating that any one belief system is correct. They all are to the extent that they all work, and the characters are left to puzzle this out. That's not the subject of this thread, so I don't want to debate it here (if you want to talk about this phenomenon, then we can discuss it in a different thread). But the key is that the system enables character beliefs to have actual physical manifestations.

    That's fantasy - where we assume, unlike the modern (or post-modern) mindsets, that these beliefs are what make everthing happen. Meaning that what we'd call magic can happen.

    I don't think that there's anything else common to fantasy, truth be told. Like most genres you can create most any theme. It's just about the color, really. Looking at the above essays, Moorcock would say that there's nothing similar between his works and Tolkien's thematically...but he'd still say that they're both fantasy.


    As was said above, I think that it's less important to think about what fantasy as a genre means, than to think about what you want your game to be about. If all you have is that it's "fantasy," I think that you'd have to agree that this is going to leave it pretty darn broad. Which is fine, if that's what you want. But trying to say it's about fantasy, and then hoping that this narrows down what the game is about is both impractical, and doing design in reverse.

    Mike
