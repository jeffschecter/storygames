Back in the 90s, before Baron Munchhausen even, Charles Ryan developed a game called Psychosis and John Fletcher wrote the first episode called Ship of Fools. Lisa Smedman would write the second installment called Solitary Confinement. If further books were made, I never saw them.

For me, this was the moment my head snapped off my neck and gaming stopped being about barbarians and fireballs.

Each book of Psychosis used the same set of rules and a tarot deck of cards for resolution (and still required a GM), but each story was different. Each game was about untrustworthy memories, but from a slightly different POV. The method of player and GM interaction, aligned with a story of someone trapped inside their own mind was ground-breaking at the time. Here's the marketing blurb.

I blink, and when I open my eyes I'm in an unfamiliar room-an unfamiliar body-an unfamiliar mind. Yesterday's memories are as clear as a photograph-but one by one they all prove lies, and even my own name eludes me. Dark shapes linger at the edge of my vision. Strangers glare with menace and loathing. The whole world has turned upside down, but nobody else seems to notice.

What the hell is happening to me?

Psychosis is a unique roleplaying game in which nothing\u2014not your name, your memories, or even reality itself\u2014can be trusted. Ship of Fools contains the complete game system and an entire six to eight session campaign for four to seven players.

You'll need a deck of Tarot cards to play Psychosis.

For me, there is no other seminal moment in gaming when I said, "Holy Shit!" Except maybe playing DITV for the first time. But even then, that was a lower case "holy shit." This game was not for everyone in the 90s, and it's been lost to the annals of time.... I think it's an undeniably important in the spectrum of designs that died after/during the advent of magic the gathering.

[My new enterprise Dying Memories is similar, but without a fixed story. Sort of an homage to one of my favorite games.]
