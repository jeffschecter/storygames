    Alrighty, check this. Let's say we have a voted contest between four games. One's obviously incomplete or clearly mechanically broken. One's seemingly complete, but utterly traditional. One's mostly complete and pretty traditional, but with a couple of truly clever or innovative flourishes. And one's mechanically solid, but utterly non-traditional in mechanics and/or content. If the two dozen voters are all designers pretty keenly familiar with traditional games, which game do you think wins the vote?

    My bet would be the mostly complete traditional game with the clever flourishes. I think it would edge out both the more complete, but more traditional game, and the one that's the truly non-traditional experience. Because I think the voters will identify the broken/incomplete game, respect the innovative flourishes more than the solid completeness of the traditional game, and divide over the unconventional game.

    So, if you're trying to select for innovation from among the RevEng games, what can you do to identify the games that maybe deliver it?

    Well, my thinking is that the unconventionalities of the innovative games are more likely to provoke voting inconsistencies than what you'll see in the votes for the broken, the traditional-with-flourishes, and the solid-but-traditional games, so maybe you look for range/inconsistency in the voted scores for the various games? Well I'm no statistician, so this ain't sophisticated, but at the same time I'm not not investing money on these numbers either. All I want is for potentially innovative games to bubble up to my attention. So these stats, for games with more than one public review only, represent a ranking by the difference between the high total score and the low total score. I think of that calculated number as "noise" in the voting results. These are the games where the assessment is most in dispute.
