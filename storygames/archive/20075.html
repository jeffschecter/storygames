    \n To clarify, this is the definition of relativity that I mean.

    3. something dependent upon external conditions for its specific nature, size, etc. (opposed to absolute ).

    Role-playing games are so about this; the non-mechanical making(understanding) of relationships between things. For example; that perilous cliff and I, but when it comes to the nuts and bolts, they usually fail horribly. When I write "non-mechanical", I'm talking about the people part of role playing(the fruitful void, i.e. you/us/them). Most RPGs are an absolutist's dream.

    \n Hah, that's great. I'm running a Fate Core space opera campaign right now called "Andromeda," where the ships all move at relativistic velocities. My favorite part of the game is the time dilation table for figuring out how much time passes in the relativistic frame while the rest-frame universe plods along at pedestrian speeds.

    But Nathan is talking about some kind of relativism; which kind is absolutely opaque in the OP but the clarification helps; it looks like maybe you're interested in what could be called "narrative relativism," in the sense that players bring different things to the table as they interact with each other and interpret in-game (and out-of-game) events, but games by design assume that players all work a particular way. I don't know. That seems to be true insofar as it goes, but smoothing over individual differences so that play can proceed non-solipsistically seems to be what game design is supposed to do (feature not bug territory). But I'd love to hear the case for narrative relativism as a design goal!
