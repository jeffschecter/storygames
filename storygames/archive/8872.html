\n So I'm thinking of the next game I'm going to run for a college gaming club. I'm batting around a few game ideas, but the one thats currently sticking with me is some kind of Twin Peaks + Silent Hill The Movie + Stephen King Miniseries + Carnival fiasco. Character-focused drama that involves some kind of dark supernatural element & rural sensibilities, set in either modern times or possibly a recent period that would be fun to act out. Main characters may or may not have superpowers.

Here are my requirements:

* Complete game with considerable playtesting done.
* Works with a campaign that goes for 8-16 sessions.
* Works well with 3-6 people present in a session rotated from a pool of around 7 people.
* Doesn't require rules-hacking (Unless its super simple rules-hacking that you can put in your recommendation).
* Is based around a reward cycle palapable to a soapy, character-centered kind of game.
* No Vampires/Werewolves/Angels/Capital D Demons

Here are some benefits:

* Rules work particularly well for horror.
* A substantial amount of "The GM as Game Facilitator" techniques are laid out in rules.
* Provides support for providing equal participation among players of broadly varying degrees of pro-activeness.
* No 'Tinkering with my little dude until he's extremely proficient in combat according to my particular interpretation of the rules'.
* Rulebook is less than 250 largish pages to 350 smallish pages.
* If a person goes into it trying to play D&D, the result has a good chance of being enjoyable.
* The Reward system has little room for 'The GM chooses to reward people based on extremely subjective criteria'

    \n You might want to check out With Great Power... It's designed for four-color superhero stories, but I think to do what you want would mostly be a setting hack, and not really require any rules changes. It would just be a matter of requiring genre-appropriate choices during character creation (e.g., no cosmic level powers, emphasis on relationship attributes, etc.) The central mechanic relies on inflicting pain, risk, and harm on characters' aspects and relationships, which seems ideal for generating melodramatic, soap opera-y play.

    Where it might fall down:

    - The structure of the game is designed around there being a "big bad" that the characters band together to defeat. If you really want this to be about the PC-PC conflicts, it might not work that well. But, I think it could do really well for a Buffy-style game where there's a main enemy and a general goal, but plenty of soap opera along the way.
    - I don't know how D&D players would approach it. "D&D style play" probably won't break the game, since I'm not sure you could even do it. But D&D players may be confused and frustrated if they're locked into that way of thinking.

    The reward cycle centers on using mood (in the form of Claims) to influence conflicts and keeping your character strong enough to resist the Vampire's influence at the end. The game works best for longer play, not one-shots. It can produce creepy, moody play.

    \n The two systems that immediately come to mind are:

    World of Darkness (the core nWoD rules book), or the free Nemesis RPG.

    JD messed one thing up in discussing how people are rewarded in Wod he was talking specifically about XPs. Luckily, I have a GamingReaver-to-HumanSpeak translation disk, and am able to speak on it furher. XPs are not a big part of the reward cycle, but WILLPOWER is, in the sense that you are looking for.Look at WoD, in the section of Willpower, Vice and Virtue (p 95/96) and there you have it.

    "3 - Being able to articulate something your character learned during the session"

    I think that's what you get XP for, but for Willpower (what you use to overcome severe obstacles, giving you a bonus to do things), you regain it in the following methods, tied directly to your character:
    If you do something that hits off your VICE, you get a point of willpower back. This can happen once per scene max (well, you can do your Vice more than once, but you only get rewarded once).

    If you do something that hits off your VIRTUE, you get ALL of your willpower back. This can happen once per session max.

    So yeah, think about the above, and you can see that at least New WoD is really "based around a reward cycle palapable to a soapy, character-centered kind of game".

    I mean, it's not as much as "spend a Willpower, get an automatic success!", but if you want you can minor-tinker the rules to have Willpower grant you a +4 instead of a +3 to actions, something simple but potent.

    In any case, the "Reward Cycle" comes in two parts: XPs and Willpower. XPs are not the reward cycle that gives you the Story Goosebumps, it's Willpower you should be looking into.

    Oh, and the One Roll Engine (ORE) system and I had a messy fallout. Some furniture was broken, and she still has all my good CDs. I thought I loved her, but now I wonder if it was anything but hate. Fucking bitch. Overall, it's slick, just requires a lot more numbers in abilities in order to do anything, more than the author thinks.

The core rules take up a total of like 15 pages, and you can ignore all the magic stuff and just create normal characters (in a campaign I ran for almost a year, there were 2 magic-users and 3 normals). There's not as much of a reward cycle as WoD, but the Madness Meters are fucking cool as shit, and great for any Dark Supernatural game.
