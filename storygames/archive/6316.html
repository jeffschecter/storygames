    \n I found play to be very interesting. In fact it lead later to a discussion with Ron, and then in the car back to Milwaukee with John above, and Thor Olavsrud as well.
    \n
    \nI don't want to get too much into the specific issue in question, but I had noted in a recent thread how Ralph had stated how it was that BRS required narration so that you could use the mechanics. And I'll simply say that I'm not sure that it does require narration. I think he may be being hopeful. Anyhow, I may try to tackle this issue elsewhere.
    \n
    \nNow, that said, my problem here is highly theoretical. That is the play was a ton of fun, and, what's more, we narrated like demons. But what's interesting is that the game is a pretty fascinating tactical excercize. One, in fact, that was intense enough that one got the feeling that one was doing it badly. It was definitely a "learning game." And, it seeming to be intentionally competitive as a design, to me this seems like it was a really good thing. I like a learning curve on such games. Especially as all players will be on it at the same time, so you're not getting hosed by not knowing it at some particular level. We all made big mistakes.
    \n
    \nBut the point is that given that it's so strongly gamism based, and the rules are somewhat general, narration may be sorta ancillary.
    \n
    \n
    \nMy biggest compliment here is that I couldn't find anything troubling in the rules in terms of obvious optimal strategies or the like. It seems like a good game on that level. That said, I'm sorta suspicious of the rules as they stand... I have an intuition that there might be an optimal strategy. Ralph was aware of one, for instance, regarding the yellow dice, that he was working on correcting potentially. What I'd be worried about is that there might be as yet unseen strategies that win out every time, and that's trouble.
    \n
    \nI'm not a good enough game theory mathmatician to predict this (I don't even know how hard it would be), but I wonder if that's a rout that can be used in this case to see if it's OK. If not, then all one can do is playtest, playtest, playtest.
    \n
    \nWith a game where the player stakes are not really well spelled out, and it's not distinctly competitive, I think you can get away with some "slop" as it were in terms of whether or not the game is free from degenerate strategies. But BRS is not such a game. One player wins, and, maybe more telling, one player is the loser. Each has repercussions on the next game you play. So it's clearly designed to promote an attitude to play to win.
    \n
    \nAnd, another thing that Ralph has working for him is that the game is set up so that one player will have problems dealing with any other player... the main strategy is to gang up on other players. So, that said, a lot of balance issues will sorta work themselves out due to this.
    \n
    \nAgain, I think he's got it by the throat here, and that the tactics are very fun. I loved stomping on people with my expensive purple D10s (indicating sorcery).
    \n
    \n
    \nFrom the POV of other mechanics, there were times where it seemed that the order of things was not clear. Most important to everybody was "when do I get to buy new dice?" Timing on this is critical, and there didn't seem to be any clear rule on it, other than I'm pretty sure you couldn't do it during a round.
    \n
    \n
    \nRelated to the question of narration above, too, it seemed like there were only two actions that a player could take:
    \n1) Go get your goal from whomever is in the way.
    \n2) Separate your opponent's dice pool.
    \n
    \nThe problem with each is that (and this seems similar to the problem I had with IAWA), that if you accomplish these things, they can be undone trivially easily. The "Chronicler" role, when played, can do most anything, and can undo any result that separated your dice pool. Except.. well the rule is that a player can stop the Chronicler from doing anything by going to dice, saying, "That was not how it happened!" Meaning that any attempt to go to get your dice back as a third option is going to likely get blocked by the player who took them away (after all, you're now weaker, the reason he took them away, and he was able to win when you were stronger, so you're going to lose big now, right?)
    \n
    \nAnd, in any case, if you go to dice, then your turn as Chronicler is over. So even if you get aid from another player, at best you're getting back to square one by getting your dice back. When you and any theoretical partner could simply make move 1 above, and go get your goal.

    \nBasically the rewards for the actions you can take are not well enumerated or varied enough. That is, there may be more to it than this, but I couldn't see what the other options were.
    \n
    \nExcept that you can do something that is neither getting dice or your goal, I suppose. But the problem is that in such a competitive game, I'm not sure what the use of these would be. Ralph calls it "positioning" and I think that it refers to the idea that a win means you can use some abilities you might not have been able to use before. But it seems that if you're creative you probably can get away with using any ability in any situation.
    \n
    \nThere is a "Challenge Rule" in the game (simplified version of that Universalis game, sheesh), which could be used to prevent "abuse"... but that seems like a potential conflict of interest. And, the way the rules work, all you have to do is have one confederate, and you will always win challenges. So, again, strategy number one, get a confederate.
    \n
    \nSans "appropriateness" being enforcable, practically, "positioning" comes down only to creating a situation where if the opponent loses, he loses dice. So, while your tactical decisions in pushing dice around are intense, your strategic decisions seem extremely limited.
    \n
    \n
    \nAgain, this is all how it seems from one play, and I may well not be seeing something.
    \n
    \nOne rules question, and a couple of suggestions:
    \n
    \nWhen do you spend RP that you won in conflicts on buying new dice? Can you do it immediately? If so, why is there a separate box on the sheet? Why not keep them all in the same box? If they can't be spent immediately, and this is a holding pool waiting for the next chapter, that makes more sense. It would also mean that winning wouldn't have the double reward of not only making your opponent weaker, but making you stronger (it's important that attacking is likely to harm the attacker, too; it's all one big Mexican Standoff to start). At least in the short term.
    \n
    \nIs that how it works?
    \n
    \nI think that you need "character sheets" for each thing. That is, it would be cool to have a sheet for each component that the player has that contains all of the dice assigned to it, and the RP left to it, etc. This way you get a real feel for what dice are coming from what, and what'll happen if positioning results in a die pool being separated off. More importantly, it indicates what dice are lost if the associated white "hit points" dice are lost.
    \n
    \nThen you leave the sheet you had to be each player's "battle mat."
    \n
    \nThis leads me to think that you might want to allow players to protect assets by not committing them to the battle mat. Rarely is that going to be good strategy, but it would be interesting if you could engineer things so that this was the case. The idea being that dice can only be lost from assets committed. You could "Screen" in some cases this way (leading to creation of small screening components meant to be sacrificed, which might balance the propensity that we noted for players to build the largest components possible).
    \n
    \nThe "Battle Mat" should have a flow-chart on it that describes things like the precedence order of dice colors in resolution, and when you can purchase new abilities. That's key to understand if you're to have any chance of playing well.
    \n
    \nAlso, given that the colors have so much meaning, and in the name of getting conflicts to go faster... ever considered pushing three dice instead of two?
    \n
    \n
    \nOh, and one more thing. Yeah, we love lying, sure. But one of my favorite rules is how you can tag a player with a black die once during being Chronicler. Largely the function of this serves to give players an incentive to work with each other, or risk seeing the dice in question used against them. Yes? The problem is that even a D10 didn't seem to be any impediment to players betraying. At every chance my thought was, "That player should defect from his alliance, it's the best move, despite the black dice."
    \n
    \nThis is in part because black dice have no special power. They are too cool for that..l think you should come up with some nifty power for them. Like they can be added as a third die for free or something. Or, alternatively, you might want to allow the players to negotiate for more dice than just one black one. I mean, why not? If a player is willing to accept more dice, why not let them make that committment?
    \n
    \nYou could do both.
    \n
    \n
    \nThis is all just what occurs to me off the top of my head. The other compliment I can give Ralph is that his game made me think about it and whether or not it's working and how, far more than anything else I played at ForgeCon.

    Whether the "game" is tic-tac-toe, chess, baseball, language, etiquette, education, science, religion, law, business, politics or war, the entire set of rules governing the system cannot be spelled out. No matter how hard we try to indicate what is required, allowed, and proscribed, some of the most fundamental principles of playing the game will always elude us. And yet, paradoxically, we can still play the game\u2014some deeper rules are always operating (i.e., affecting the players' behavior) without the players' being aware of them.

    What do we mean by a game?

    A game is a play activity that consists of an object (a goal or goals that the players are trying to accomplish) and constraints on the players' behavior (what they must do and/or what they may not do in attempting to achieve the game's object). To play a game is to pursue that game's object while adhering (more or less) to its constraints. Some of these constraints (the "recorded rules") are explicitly spelled out and are what we generally understand to be "the rules of the game," but every game is also governed by constraints that are rarely if ever made explicit. Some of these "unrecorded rules" are literally unstatable.

    An example with Tic-Tac-Toe

    Suppose I challenge you to a game of tic-tac-toe. Could anything be more straightforward? But just to be sure, we review the rules. We'll play on a 3x3 grid, we'll alternate turns, we'll play only in empty squares, I'll play X's, you play O's, I'll play first, and the first player to get three of his/her symbol in a row, column, or diagonal wins the game. Aren't these all the rules of tic-tac-toe?

    Well, for one thing, nothing has been said about time. Is there a time limit between moves? Normally, we both "understand" that there is, and we both "know" that our moves should be made within a "reasonable" time, say 20 seconds. If one of us takes longer, the other starts to fidget or act bored, may even make not-so-subtle comments, and eventually threaten to quit. Without having stated it, we have accepted a tacit time limit. And because we haven't stated it, it is fairly flexible and very functional.

    Is it a rule, or isn't it?

    Suppose it is my turn and, no matter what I do, you will win on your next move. Couldn't I prevent that from happening, within the rules stated, by simply refusing to play? Nothing in the rules forces me to move within a particular amount of time, so I simply do not make my next move. Haven't I followed the rules and avoided losing? And yet, if you've ever played a game, you know that this strategy is almost never employed and would be completely unacceptable. Anybody who seriously resorted to such a tactic would be considered childish or unsportsmanlike or socially undesirable and would probably not be asked to play in the future. This behavior seems to violate some fundamental but rarely stated principle of the game without any of us ever having to discuss it.

    Self-defeating rules

    But can't we state the principle it violates? Can't we just make that principle one of the rules of tic-tac-toe and other games? The answer is\u2014 yes, of course we can, but we will not eliminate the problem. Suppose we add the following rule: Players will make their moves within a reasonable amount of time. Have we solved anything? What is a "reasonable" amount of time? One minute? Five? 30? A million? And who determines what is reasonable\u2014the player whose turn it is or the other player?

    Such a rule is actually self-defeating because it calls attention to the fact that we cannot spell out what "reasonable" means.

    So why not just specify a time limit for each move? Because we would just create even more perplexing problems for ourselves. For one thing, we would have to indicate when a player's time is running and when it is not. If one player had to answer the phone, for example, would we count that time or wouldn't we? To state the rule fully, we would have to list every life situation that could possibly interrupt a player's turn and state whether it should count against that player's time limit. Obviously, we could never complete such a list.

    Practical solutions

    A far more practical "solution," the one most of us have used all our lives in "friendly" games, is to say nothing about time limits and rely on our opponent's intuitive understanding of a "reasonable" time for a move and his/her desire to keep the game moving and therefore enjoyable. In other words, we depend on unstated\u2014and probably unstatable\u2014"rules" (really just expectations) when we play a game for fun.

    In tournament or professional games, of course, we cannot leave things so loose, and various methods have been employed to solve the time dilemma. Generally, specific time limits are spelled out, as are specific penalties, including forfeit, for exceeding them. Official devices are employed for timing moves\u2014chess clocks, the shot-clock in college and pro basketball, stopwatches in baseball games and tennis tournaments, and so on. But once we move beyond "reasonable" to "official" time, we create a whole new set of problems, problems that can no longer be solved with a simple agreement between or among players.

    \n A conflict of interest is internal to one individual. It means he's got more than one interest that he's supposed to be looking out for, and each interest drives him in a different direction. Such that it becomes difficult to impossible to tell which interest is being served given a particular decision.
    \n
    \nIn your game, the player has the interest to win, but he also has the interest to keep the game "aesthetically sound" by challenging things that are somehow out of bounds by his standards. This is a conflict of interest because any decision made to veto another player's offering could be, in fact, a simple attempt to thwart that player, to advance the original player's goal.
    \n
    \nGiven an objective way of determining if a particular decision is actually being made on aesthetic grounds, we could know if a player had violated his mandate, and we wouldn't have to worry about any conflict of interest... we could tell if they were "cheating." But given that aesthetics are personal, and vary wildly between individuals, it's very hard to tell if a challenge being made is being made in the name of aesthetics.
    \n
    \nWe trust the other players, but if a decision comes up that differs from our own aesthetics, we're prone to wonder about it. Worse, not wanting to put the other player under suspicion, it becomes best not to proffer anything that might get challenged, so as not to place that player in the position of having the conflict of interest in the first place.
    \n
    \nNow, if in fact the challenge mechanic is actually just another part of the whole competition, if it's just a mechanic which players use to win, then we don't have a conflict of interest.     \nHere's the thing. I want this game to work for me. If there were a way to get it so that I wasn't uncomfortable with it, I'm pretty sure that it wouldn't make the game bad for you Lance. I could be wrong about that, but is there some problem trying to figure out a way to solve this problem so that the group I represent, no matter how small, might also be satisfied? Indeed, I've been honest about this, I'm actually looking at this for my own greedy purposes because I'd like to make a game like this, but am running into precisely this sort of problem in my design efforts. If Ralph has solved the problem, then I want to know how he's done it.
    \n
    \nAnd, call me pompous, but I think that the issue is central to solving certain issues that are at the very center of all of RPGs. There is no more important subject than this one right now. Leave it to Ralph to attempt something that would address this, to try to solve this problem.
    \n
    \nBut I'm afraid that he has not. While I'd agree that having what amounts to third parties intervening in challenges will more likely support a group aesthetic than just involving a couple of players, I think that it's a case of better, but not solved. Let's say we had an audience that did all of the voting, an audience interested only in a good story, and not in winning or losing... That would solve the problem, of eliminating the conflict of interest. Probably not at all what Ralph has in mind for the game, probably impractical, and not a solution that I like, either, but it should serve to show you where my problem is, at least. When a player is involved in trying to win the game, no matter how "removed" his pawns are from the action at hand, there's always a bias. Ralph admits that this is expected to happen, in fact, that it's part of the game.
    \n
    \nKnowing myself, and the people that I play with all the time, if we played with the understanding that it is OK to vote based on tactics, I'm seeing the aesthetics of it falling away entirely in short order. If they ever get established at all. And then, once again, you'll have the challenge being used constantly as a degenerate strategy.
    \n
    \nWith a mixed group you'll have me not doing that for social reasons, but feeling like a dolt for not pressing every mechanical advantage given to me. If I understand the rule to be aesthetic in nature, then I'll always feel uncomfortable with the conflcit of interest.
    \n
    \nAnyhow, I think we've reached an impasse here. I'm not seeing either side saying anything really new. Though I'll be reading in case I'm wrong.
