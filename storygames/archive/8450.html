    . . .

    Ok, seriously, I have no ideas, but it might help to identify what would make a game good for Star Trek. For example, given how soap-opera-ish Star Trek often was, might a system with mechanics for relationships work? Assuming you wanted to reproduce the television show, a system friendly to splats (species, department) might be in order. If you simply wanted to run around the setting, something more freeform might be better suited. Ars Magica-esque troupe-style play might be a good idea, so that you could play each other's supporting cast (ie. redshirts, side characters) and thus reproduce the feel of the series, where not every main character had a big part in every episode, without forcing players to sit out.

    That's all my thoughts so far, but I'll stay tuned.

    \nPrimetime Adventures is best for long form series, with characters dealing with issues. As it stands, it is a game that could only have been created to model TV in the last 10 years, since TV has changed a great deal in the last ten years. New distribution models of content (cable and DVD in particular) tied to new business models (cable audiences that can be smaller, more niche and more devoted) have created new kinds of content.

    I would suggest -- and I'm pretty sure I'm dancing with a lit match around a powder keg here -- that the original series really wouldn't benefit much from having PTA as the system. It's a problem of the week show -- and the problems are out there -- not in the hearts of the crew. This isn't a harsh on the original series. I think it's great. It's just different. And not what PtA is about.

    \n Finding the right system for a Star Trek TNG game has always been my personal holy grail.

    While I love PTA I don't think it lends itself to the more formulaic aspects of TNG episodes and its focus on character relationships and character development doesn't really reflect the series treatment of its characters as archetypes.

    Whereas Dogs in the Vineyard doesn't seem to work well with the very soft, easy-going conflict solution of the series. (And even though I know it's the reason why many consider that particular incarnation of Trek very PC or milquetoast, I firmly believe it's one of the reasons why the show managed to attract a mainstream audience.)

    And most traditional games do not really mesh with the rather careless attitude towards setting logic (the most obvious example being Trek's technobabble) in favour of dramatic license.

    I've had great fun playing TOS-style games while playtesting Matt Wilson's Galactic. Although I'm not sure this is of any help to anyone. For DS9-style games, PTA is the way to go, I think. And I completely agree with the InSpectres-variant suggestion for Voyager.

    \n I've been working on a Star Trek campaign actually (nothin' wrong with the LUG system from what I can tell from my rocking chair on my busted down trailer porch, damn hippie kids), so I have some random thoughts on the property

    The Prime Directive, for example, is lame when seen as a rule that can't be broken without consequences and is cool when seen as a prophylactic against imperialism. Quite sneakily, in TNG, most of the time when the Prime Directive has to be violated by the protagonists, someone else has previously violated it worse, and on Enterprise, they gleefully get themselves embroiled in situations and they always make it worse. On Voyager there was no reason to even bother with it - the danger of imperialism when it's one ship far, far from home, is not appreciable. If you don't want your characters to be high-tech gunboat diplomats dealing with indigenous peoples, there's a pretty strong institutional bias against it - that said, virtually every example in which a gamer says the Prime Directive is lame ("Are we really going to let that planet killing asteroid wipe out that civilization because of some stupid regulation?") is portrayed somewhere in the various series as being not a big deal if it's violated.

    John Kim's advice to take the TV serieses as TV adaptations of "what really happened" is the game-creation engine for me. TOS was a high-concept space opera. TNG was a bit of committee porn in response to that and had a considerably greater concern for politics. Deep Space Nine was done by a noir director who was looking for a compelling ongoing story for the first time. Voyager was an exploitation melodrama. And so on. That means you can use the ridiculously detailed canon, but you don't have to adopt the same cavalier attitude towards science/continuity/women/aliens/economics/etc. just because it's in there. You can say "well, the reason nobody mentions buying anything in TOS is because that just wasn't part of what the writers of those shows were getting at, and in the other serieses, traders like the Ferengi are played for laughs because economic issues just aren't what they were focusing on, but nevertheless those little dudes dealt themselves into the situation, which should tell you something about what REALLY happened..."

    My wife and her best friend often chuckle that there were never any "good Vulcans" on the Star Trek shows. I disagree. I think the main problem was that the writers never let being Vulcan be right. There was never a show in which going with your true feelings was completely the wrong thing to do and a dispassionate analysis shows the way to the best outcome. That's a sucky thing to do to player characters. There should always be a situation somewhere that they are the best match for. Basic spotlight sharing concept.

      \n I'm only really familiar with ST:TNG. The thing I'd have to say is you can't have a system that has consistent or balanced mechanics. In the series, technology only existed if it furthered the plot. There are tons of nit-picks about what they could have done if they'd used some technology that had been introduced previously. The closest to any mechanical consistency was the episode where it was determined that going faster than warp 6 had negative consequences on subspace, so a law was passed that you could only exceed warp 6 in emergencies. I'm not sure even how much that was followed in later seasons, as it was entirely inconsistent whether they had the Enterprise-D going off at the end of an episode at warp 2 or warp 5 or any other speed. Space battles were similar, sometimes they'd fire one shot and disarm the enemy, other times no effect. Sometimes they'd be hit to no effect, other times there would be major damage.

      What I'd suggest is that a task resolution system really doesn't handle it. I'm actually not familiar with any conflict resolution systems, but I'm familiar enough with task resolution systems to know that they really don't do Star Trek well. Particularly I'd say that randomness in combat goes against star trek, and that they should be handled free-form. For instance when Worf wants to avenge K'Elhyr and kill Duras, it's very emotional. Worf should win or lose the fight based on how emphatically his player narrates the actions. If it really moves everyone, then he kills Duras. If it lands kind of flat, then the crew from the enterprise interfere. Similarly, when characters died it was because their actors wanted out. Their deaths were abrupt and sudden. It's almost as if character death in a ST:TNG game should only occur when the player decides their character has had it and they want them out. They give the GM/Narrator/etc permission to kill the character, and at some point in that session, something happens and the character is just dead. I think if a game really emulated the feel of the series, any mechanics and randomisation would only be present to drive the plot until someone has an idea of where to take things.
