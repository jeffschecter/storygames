\n As this excellent thread says, I'm planning on starting up a podcast. I'm calling it The Score.

My intended audience is independent role-playing enthusiasts and story gamers.

I'll do interviews with game designers and other active community members (convention organizers or forum hosts, for example). I may do simpler commentary solo.

But, what I really hope to do is cover issues and trends. That may take a while to figure out how to do. Basically, audio features that combine my commentary with recordings from folks.
Think NPR for indie RPGs.

That's my concept, but getting there won't be so easy.

What I want from you
I want to hear some ideas! Who do you want to hear about? What trend? A particular game or event? Let me know, right here in this thread. You can volunteer yourself.

Obviously, I can't promise to interview everyone. At this point, I'm simply trying to get my sources to step forward for me!

I will try to log onto Skype tonight: matt_snyder. If anyone's around, drop me a chat. Might get some recording going, too.

EDIT: I may be remembering my Skype ID incorrectly. I'll update tonight once I confirm it.

    Posted By: iagoJust keep it short. Too many podcasts are not bite-sized (which should not be taken as me telling EVERYONE to shorten up; but the longer format ones I have the time for, have eaten up all my listening time for long format ones; so any new podcasts will need to be more interesting than, say, Sons of Kryos and/or Have Games Will Travel, in order for me to listen to them -- unless they're 20-30 minutes max, in which case it'll seem a lot easier to keep up). Not to mention enforced brevity means improved focus.
    \nYea, show length is a tough one. We shoot for the 35 - 60 minute format on 2d6 Feet in a Random Direction (yea, what a range there, eh?). It's really something that - ultimately - the podcasters themselves need to be comfortable with. No padding; no filling up empty air (which is why Chris and I will wrap a short show and leave it as such), but don't prematurely end a good topic or segment either. And listen to your listeners whenever you can get feedback - we've gotten from folks more that the show is too short than too long (which still kinda freaks me out), and try to hold to about 45 minutes, and exceed when we feel like we've got solid content to do so. We split our first interview / discussion with Chris Pramas into two shows, for instance, to help keep it digestible for our audience.

    Also, Matt - consider the frequency of your show and the sustainability thereof. It's easy to get aggressive, put out several shows, and then start to burn out. We set our goal at 1-2 shows a month, with 1 being our minimum to sustain regular listening, and set something of a predictable schedule for our listeners. We've hit 2 shows a month about 60% or so, and July was a 3 show month because we had great, time-sensitive content (and because it was our 1 year anniversary for the show).

    And above all - have fun with it.

      Posted By: Matt_SnyderWhaddya think, Seth?
      \nWell, as a bonus follow-up, I'll fill that out right now.

      The one thing that Legends of Alyria hasn't yet achieved is a number of good AP reports. The only AP so far that isn't from my group was the game played at Go Play Southeast. I'd like to see more. I'd like to say that it's not an ego thing, but that would be a lie. Part of it is definitely an ego thing. I put a lot of work, sweat, and heartache into that book, and it would be nice if there were other gamers out there who appreciated me for it.

      But, more than that, I would just like to know that there are other gamers out there that appreciate the game, regardless of whether or not they appreciate me. Back on the old Gaming Outpost, I once said that I'd be happy if I knew that, somewhere, someone was rolling a Moon Die. It's changed to a Clock Die, but my attitude is still the same. I'm hoping that GenCon will help get the word out.

      But, beyond that, I found myself nodding when I read Ron explaining why he doesn't want to revise Sorcerer:\n
      That clarifier, though, raises a key point which I haven't articulated in any of the discussions yet, or barely. It is that a given work, to me, exists in a given place and time. Sorcerer is my mid-late 1990s game, written by me shortly before and during my thirtieth birthday. It was born from my deliberate decision to return to role-playing after I'd done what I could with Champions and Cyberpunk, after I'd read Prince Valiant and Over the Edge, and after I'd been astonished at playing Zero....The aging prof in his early 40s, contemplating things like fatherhood and job-threatening political activism, is not the Ph.D. candidate who needed to write, play, and publish Sorcerer over ten years ago. I'm that man now, and the work I want and need to produce isn't Sorcerer.

      I mean, I love the game, and I still play it, and playing it still surprises and transforms me. I haven't posted about our game last summer because I don't think it's suitable for internet discussion, politically and emotionally. I think as a game it's stood up to time's usage far better, far more amazingly, then anyone could have expected. (Ten years of successful commerce with no revision? an RPG? wow! That's like Amber.) I also respect and even quite like the (b) audience, and I'm here for them.

      But I don't need - and quite possibly am incapable of - writing a text for it again.
      \nObviously the specifics are different, but the idea is the same. I remember talking with Crystal (my wife) at one point, expressing the concern that perhaps Legends of Alyria had missed its time. After all, it has inspired two major indie games (Universalis and Polaris), both of which seem like they have overshadowed and surpassed its ancestor. However, I played Legends of Alyria after finally getting it into book form, and I was delighted to find that the system still holds up and supports quality gameplay. Maybe I'll end up playing with someone at GenCon, and I will enjoy the experience.

      Still, I find that I've moved on. Graham mentioned that I sounded enthusiastic about Dirty Secrets. It's because I am totally excited about it, because I mean every word of it. The game claims to be a noir detective game, but it's really a lot more.

      Last year, I moved my family to Orange Street in Peoria, Illinois. This is part of a neighborhood that is on the knife's edge. On the one hand, this is part of a larger area dubbed Renaissance Park, which is being targeted for a lot of economic development. On the other hand, it can be a rough area. Last August, I drove right past a gun fight within four blocks of my house. We hear gunfire from time to time. I can see a house of prostitution from my kitchen window. I can identify some of the crack houses in the area, and I'm quickly learning to see the telltale signs to identify others. I've not been in a fight yet, and God willing I won't have to be, but I carry a fighting knife, just in case.

      And I've discovered that many of the "losers" who live in places like this are still human beings. I've heard "good" people in the area dismiss the lower class people around here. They don't keep their property up to code, they're loud, they're annoying, they might be criminals, and they interfere with the goal of keeping property values high. Therefore, they are dismissed as "roaches" who need to have the hammer of the law dropped on them.

      But one of these "roaches" wrote this poem:\n

      I'm to start a brand new life
      A life without drugs
      I'm really kinda scared
      I'll want lots of hugs

      I know its sounds silly
      But let it be
      I'm going to do it
      Cuz Jesus lives in me

      I'll get my strength from Him above
      Cuz He loves me so
      Hanging on tight
      Never letting go

      Thank you, Jesus
      For you are my vine
      Without You
      My life is intertwined

      When temptation comes
      And I know that it will
      You take the place
      Of marajuana, cocaine or a pill

      Thank you, Jesus
      For all that You've done for me
      I know I'm gonna do it
      Cuz Jesus lives in me
      \n
      These people need to meet Jesus, and the only way that it will happen is if His followers are willing to live with them. If we just visit, or set up homeless shelters, or the like, that's just cultural imperialism by another name. This isn't a matter of "them". It's a matter of "us". I've put my family on the line. We're all in this together.

      Through Dirty Secrets, I hope to provoke players to reflect on their own attitudes towards other people. How do they feel about "criminals"? What about "the law"? What about the blacks? The whites? The poor? The rich? I want people to open their eyes and see the horror around them. Then I want them to act. Not with the swift retribution of the law, but with compassion.

      Yes, I'm excited about the game mechanics, and yes, I'm excited because I think that my design skilz have improved a lot, and yes, I'm excited because this is a game that I still love playing.

      But most of all, I'm excited about Dirty Secrets because I'm in deadly earnest about it. I meant every word, and I'm very serious.

      Seth Ben-Ezra
      Great Wolf
