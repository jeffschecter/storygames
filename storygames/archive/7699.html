\n I found a copy of H\u014dL and BUTTery H\u014dLsomeness (2002 editions) at the gaming store that I visit in Jacksonville, FL, when I visit my mom (same store that sold me a complete Zendo box for $10!). As I read through it, two things struck me pretty hard:
1) While it is very GMful (ha, no shit!), it seems to provide a LOT of player agency in character creation... so much so that there's not even rules for character creation (until BUTT H\u014dL). The authors seem to think whatever the players think is "right" for power-level and special abilities and such is... well, up to them (i.e. no canon).
2) Over and over again, the authors emphasize that their system is really simple so that you can focus on roleplaying (they mean "acting") and story telling, not chart referencing or "crunch" in general.

So I ask: could H\u014dL be one of the first "story games" (as in shared-authority, high-narrative-focus)? Or is it just so rules light that they THINK it enables such play (but lacks real systemic hooks to GUARANTEE such play)?

    \n Not to derail, and if you ask I can start another discussion, but The Storyteller Games are traditional games though, through and through. They don't really do much of anything mechanically that drive story forward. The text suggests "yeah, we're all about actual roleplaying" but the rules suggest otherwise. It's really not mechanically any different than D&D in most respects - heavy combat system, task based resolution, locked in power lists, completely GM driven complete with metaplot, suggested GM fiat, Rule Zero, and heavily secrets oriented. It's doesn't get much more trad than this. The morality stats try to hint at mechanically move story forward but they uaually and easily get ignored with no consequence to the integrity of the mechanics. I am pretty certain Ron Edwards talked about this back in the day and I think Clyde or Rob brought it up again in a recent interview.

    \n Jon Venbakken and me wrote five games in a series back in 1990, called the Impro-series. They deviate from the classical rpg-format in different ways, some of them quite strongly. I believe 3 of them can qualify as story-games (marked with a * ).

    Impro En: the most traditional: with a GM and players.
    * Impro Ven: the GM decides absolutely everything, the players may have to play the scene again if it is "incorrect"
    * Impro No: the players have all the power, the GM reduced to deciding which player will be the storyteller at any time \xa4
    * Impro M'tu: no GM or system, there is players and setting and story
    Impro To: the number of players and GMs are decided at game-start, very classical in set-up otherwise

    \xa4 Impro No makes use of Metode Noblesse: a set of formal rules on how to talk about the characters, how to keep in-character, and how to decide on conflicts.

    I believe we were rather early in our experiments.
