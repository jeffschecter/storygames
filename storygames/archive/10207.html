So the discussion of wild talents in Dark Sun got me thinking - what are some instances you've encountered of good randomness in chargen? Please talk about your experiences playing with these randomized chargens; any of us can pull something cool-looking out of a text, but the proof of the pudding is in the eating.

\n

I don't really want to get into a fight about how cool it was or wasn't to roll all 6s and 8s in your mental stats in AD&D; if that was cool for you, by all means share how it worked that way!

\n

One neat thing that I found with the DS thing is that you get this mechanical doohickey at no opportunity cost, that might reinforce your core character concept or it may encourage you to grow in an unexpected direction - once I played a preserver with Graft Weapon, which was mostly just a thing I didn't care about, but proved to be a lifesaver in a pinch, when showing off wizardcraft was a socially foolish thing to do.

    \n Randomness is cool when it's for stuff with little or no differential in mechanical effectiveness. Rolling to see whether I'm from Brandywine or Gondor, whether I'm a warrior or a wizard and whether I can bakes cakes or make chairs can all be cool and interesting; rolling to whether I suck or rock is as lame as a lame thing on a lame day with its lamest hat on.

    Dragon Warriors background tables were cool; it's random stats and hit points weren't. Legendary Lives kinda-balanced stats and funky lifepaths were interesting.

    \n I don't enjoy randomness in character design in games like D&D, where there's no safety net if it turns out you have rolled up a dud character.
    I do enjoy randomness in character design in games like Pendragon, where things like personality traits and passions and family background do affect play mechanically and story-wise, and stats make a huge difference to combat - because even if you roll a 'weak' character, you can still shine, and the game thrives with characters of different power levels in the same group. And that uber-kight you travel with - one day, he'll be dead or retired due to aging, and you might take his place as the combat monster (if that's what you want) even if you started out weak.

    Posted By: Jack Aidleyrolling to whether I suck or rock is as lame as a lame thing on a lame day with its lamest hat on.
    \nI can deal with it ("it" being, "randomly determine how skilled/excellent you are"), but only if it's for like a one-shot or mini-campaign.

    As for randomness in character generation to help define a character, I've been a big fan of it for a while.

    A lot of Japanese RPGs with lifepaths or emotion charts or "see which major background NPC you know" charts use something called "RoC": "Roll or Choice". Basically, you roll first, and a lot of time that will put a cool spark of *something* in your character. But, if the results are totally contradictory to what you're looking for, or if there's a particular result that you really, *really* want, you can pick something else.

    I still think it counts as Random, even though it doesn't totally force you to keep the results: More than often, that thing you land on will immediately set off sparks; and even if you don't keep what you land on, most likely the player is already thinking of her character in a new way.

    For example, in the game Double Cross, I was just making a character last week (to try to understand the game). I decided that I kinda wanted a juvenile delinquent student (that is, he rolls with bad crowds, but he's more like an honorable leader than a punk or thug). I picked his job and role, his power set, his individual powers, name, etc. Then, it told me to use RoC to determine things that happened in his life (one major event; I ended up sticking with what I got, which was "Misfortune: I screwed up in some major way, and people remember that event").

    Anyway, the *sparky* bit was when I came to the "RoC to see which major background NPC you know, and also your relationship with them": There's a list of about 20 major NPCs in the setting (and I mean "major" as in "they're in the book", not as in "they're all powerful/have metaplot/etc": One is an elementary school girl, another is a crazy assassin, another is the leader of the "Justice League", yet another is a black marketeer, etc). Anyway, I had already determined that I wanted to pick the relationship with either the badass assassin (where the set relationship is "you hate and fear this person"), or perhaps the leader of the secretive Justice League (where the set relationship is "you look up to this person like a father or brother"). Anyway, I landed on "average but spunky high school girl", where the set relationship was "you're not related by blood, but she's as important to you as a blood sister". My brain went BOOOOOooooooooom, and I mentally rewrote my character that instant (without wanting to, even; it just happened): He's all gruff on the outside, but deeply loves this girl (in a sister-way, not a girlfriend way) his next-door neighboor girl that he grew up with. He goes out of his way to keep her safe, and she's the only one who sees him as a nice, kind guy.

    So that's why I like randomness.

    \n Does unexpectedness count as randomness? If so, one could easily turn Emily Care Boss' world-creation system in Sign-In Stranger into a character creation system. From what I understand, she derived it from the Exquisite Corpse surrealist writing exercise.\n
    The exquisite corpse exercise requires at least three or four people. Write an article and an adjective on the top of a piece of paper, such as "the blue" or "a lovely", and fold the paper so they can't be seen. Pass the paper to the left. Now write a noun on your paper, fold it again so it isn't visible, and pass it again. Continue this with a verb, then another article/adverb pairing, then a noun. Unfold the papers and read them aloud. These sentences can be mined for story ideas or phrases. You can make the sentences more complicated if desired. The game gets its name from the first sentence the Surrealists generated in this way: "The exquisite corpse shall drink the new wine."

    \n I like randomness in character generation when it's optional. I don't want to be tied to what the dice say for everything, but sometimes it's fun to play a random person. Either way, I strongly prefer a game to offer non-random options alongside the random so I can make my choice, and the random version should always produce a result that is reasonably equal. I hate the whole 'Roll 3D6 six times' thing because two characters using the same method could have wildly different levels of ability. A better option would be 'Roll 3D6, assign it to one attribute, then subract that number from 21 and assign the result to the next attribute.' Though more complex, it at least means that everyone has the same total number of attribute points.

    \n I think while D&D's handling of it was pretty bad, 3d6 in order works pretty well with HackMaster, since you get bonus build points to keep the stats you got, which you can devote to increasing the stats a bit. It balances out quite nicely in that aspect. D&D is a bit weird in that it comes from random generation roots but really works best when done in a point buy system, because of how influential high stats are.

    Another thing I liked is with Millenium's End, which has 10 stats, and you distribute 25 dice across them, giving 1 to 4 to roll for the stat. You control which stats will be at the higher end, which will be at the middle end, and which ones will be at the lower end, but there's still some randomness. You also don't get the wide difference you do in D&D, you get maybe a 5%-10% variance in ability, so you can still roughly work with your character concept. This method, of distributing 24d6, or doing a 9d6, 8d6, 7d6, 6d6, 5d6, 4d6 arranged as you like across the stats turns out to be pretty fair and actually results in characters with stat bonuses in most places they want them, while still having the potential of an interesting lower stat.

    Overall though, if you have to go to ridiculous ends to produce consistent and averaged results, it's best to just not use any sort of random generation. If the randomness produces interesting, useful, and fun results across the board, then it's a valuable system to work with.

    \n Diaspora does not use randomness during character generation at all, but in that same first session we do generate a cluster of worlds, and that process has at its roots a random element. A final cluster looks like:


    A lot of that information is derived and invented, but the three stats per world (Technology, Environment [still Habitat in this old playtest image], and Resources) establish a skeleton on which the players will lay down story per world. They then draw from this during character generation (by establishing their backgrounds within this cluster) which in turn feeds back into world description. These random bits are the seed from which the setting, the characters, and usually the adventure itself all grow. The linkages between systems are also randomly determined, and these linkages drive the development of political relationships between systems.

    Witness, for example, Gravity's Rainbow from that cluster, wholly derived from three random numbers:

    Technology: +4
    Habitat: -1
    Resources: +0

    The stories of Gravity's success are many, and is the subject of histories and fictions popular throughout the cluster. Gravity was a technological success, then later it housed the Tyrants; it spawned trade conglomerates, empires, and of course, fulfilled all the dreams that technology could whisper into the imaginations of its inhabitants. Gravity was ascending, and at some point, thousands of years ago, it decided to remake itself. A ringworld would take resources, and it would take time, but that, above all, would show the universe the magnificence of which it was capable. The project was never finished, however, and Gravity's Wheel, as it was to have been called, was only partially built. As it is a thirty-degree arc, Gravity's rainbow, stretches between LaGrange points, providing an amazing amount of territory. And with this space, the people were happy. there was no longer a drive to improve because competition for land, and for space, reduced to almost nothing. Pastoral idealism fills much of the land, along with castles, ruins, remnants of attempted technology, and the evidence of failed experiments. But the arcworld is bleak, and lacks the biodiversity that is told about in stories. And the wonderful technological empires, no longer capable of traveling freely between the stars, are no longer to be seen. The technology exists, and it is still maintained, but in the otherwise empty system (all other planets had been incorporated into the arcword's mass long ago), most visitors are greeted with the rolling countryside, stretching up into the sky. And from the jump point, looking down on the system, one sees only the star and the glistening arc, which is Gravity's Rainbow.

    * Arcworld
    * We should have kept the trees
    * Fatally satisfied

    And so I think this is where randomness really shines in creation of anything: providing a kernel around which to accrete interesting story.

    \n I like systems. I'm kind of weird about it. Remember in the World of Darkness books how there would be splats and then lists of sample concepts? Whenever I played a World of Darkness game I would start with the first splat, first concept. If I played again I would do the second splat, first concept. And so on until I ran out of splats and then onto the second round of concepts. As a result the splat I used was always "outside of my control" and would force me to work with concepts that I wouldn't necessarily play if I was just choosing. My friends eventually figured this out, too, since it's similar to some of my other weird patterns and so when we'd get a new White Wolf game they'd look to see what my first character would be.

    One character I made using this method was Adad the Bloody Boar, a Dawn Caste mercenary prince. He was old and tired and exalted in a battle where his mercenary company was betrayed.

    I find having that random input, outside of my control, really helped give me a seed to create an interesting character around.

    \nYou know, the initiation scenes in Dogs are themselves a good example of randomness in character creation. They're a good example because A) you get a trait either way, but it may not be the trait you expected and the randomness involved is used as a teaching aid for the game at large.
