    I feel that "why text" is important, but because such texts can't overcome ambiguity and subjectivity--they'll impact different persons differently because of their respective experiences, etc.--"why text" is an unreliable medium for transmitting the experience which the designer is trying to provide.

    Matt, for the sake of the conversation, I think you need to pretend that 3e really does tell you how to get the characters to the conflicts. (Personally, I don't know the game aside from brief glances at the rules, secondhand chatter, and extrapolation from AD&D1e.)

    A better example might be my experience of playing Illuminati, a sort of board/card game, with a couple of friends in college. (This was the original pocket game published by Steve Jackson Games, which I don't think has changed much, but shouldn't be confused with INWO or GURPS Illuminati or whatever.) The game is clearly functional in the sense that the rules are unambiguous, you can get a winner in a reasonable amount of time, there's good interplay between players, and so forth. However, at the end of the game, my friends expressed weak enthusiasm at best because it didn't really give the feel of skullduggery, scheming, wheels-within-wheels, and puzzle palace conspiracies. The expectations formed by the advertising and color text weren't met. The "fiction" that they were hoping to interact with through the mechanics of the game simply wasn't there.

    What's funny is that one of these friends was later persuaded to play an abstract card game called "Dahimi" (among other names), in which your relative position in each round of play is determined by your performance in the previous round. The high-ranking players get to take good cards from the low-ranking players before the round starts, and the highest-ranking player opens the round, which is itself an advantage. The game is played ad infinitum, with no money or points changing hands, until the participants get tired; there's no particular "win-lose" or other significance attached to the final positions. The rules of the game are brief, but the strategy within each round is reasonably deep, rewarding calculation, luck, and skill. As part of the lingo of the game, the top players are called by terms of greater and lesser royalty, middling players are "bourgeois", and the bottom players are peasants. Over years of playing the game, informal house rules have been added: the top player gets to decide where to sit after each round and has control over the music; the peasants have to share the job of shuffling and dealing; peasants might also be responsible for fetching popcorn from the bar or whatever.

    The friend in question loves Dahimi. Why? Because the mechanics are engaging and the overall structure that's developed around it strongly supports a comical fiction of social climbing in a world where the nobility is always scheming for the top spot, the bourgeois is making the best of its lot, and the peasant gets it in the neck--unless he's very fortunate and very clever.

    The issue here is that some people approach their games the way my friend did Illuminati. They're attracted to certain elements of the notional subject matter, and if those elements aren't represented by the structures found in the rules, they're disappointed. Other people approach games the way my friends and I did Dahimi. They take the structure of the game and, perhaps prompted by certain cues, they relate it to an appropriate fiction. (Of course some people don't bother much with a fiction at all.) But with RPGs, the subject matter or point of attraction may be so strong for some people that the rules structure plays a relatively small role in shaping their overall experience, and in fact, if too much of the experience is encoded in formal, mechanical rules, it may weaken their experience by depriving them of their right to subjective interpretation and individual contribution to the fiction.

    Yep, that's the one I'm talking about, Josh. This is something that's never going to be resolved until everyone sees things my way and agrees that it's a big "both".

    What's more controversial about my position, though, is that I see some people who believe in strongly aligning the mechanics and the introductory text. But what they don't realize is that their belief in the superiority of this method is founded on:

    a) A belief that the introductory text can be pinned down precisely, so that mechanics can express it perfectly (well, adequately). Result: highly structured games. Risk: missing the point for a portion of your audience. They know how to play the game, they just don't see why they should care.

    b) Or, a belief that the introductory text can be pinned down precisely, so that it can adequately fill in the blanks regarding motivation and procedure. Result: vaguely structured games. Risk: thrashing and confusion by a portion of your audience. What they get from the introductory text isn't enough, after all, to tell them how to play the game.

    I think I had a (c) and maybe a (d) going into this post, but I don't remember what they were. Maybe a mirror image showing the risks & rewards of seeing introductory text as fluff. But (b) is a really meaty issue because it's where I see comments about certain games only working procedurally if you approach them with a particular mindset. It's easy to say that a given game is boring because its mechanics don't really depict the fiction in a way that interests you. It's quite a different thing to say that you can't even play a given game because you can't grasp what it is you're supposed to be doing.

    if you aren't concerned with the impact of the game text on how other people are going to use the game, then you're desiging the game for your own use, and that's it.


    This is why I don't entirely buy into System Does Matter, and exclusive DWM projects. Anyone can write and publish a game. Read Hybrid lately?

    But if you're publishing for others, you must necessarily take into account player variability and provide multiple options and methods for your game. Elsewise, you're pretty much relegating yourself to obscurity.

    DWM creates the essence of your game. It's the bare-bones how.
    DWDM creates "teh kewl shiny." It's the how else.

    You need both.

    For my own game (the Dead of Night RPG) DWM created Survival Points (basic rules) and the Tension mechanic (advanced option). Everything else is DWDM -- even the system for Attributes and Specialisations. In a versatile horror game, you absolutely need a mechanism for writing characters out of the story -- Survival Points. Horror often involves varying degrees of fear and grim description, with events occasionally out of the characters' control -- Tension. Tension is important to Dead of Night in the same way that Sanity is important to Cthulhu. It's necessary to play the game I wrote, but it's not necessary to play a horror game. Players can change everything else in the game without changing the game itself.

    I think it's helpful, but not necessary, to include the why in game text. If you explain the how well enough, the why will be obvious to most players, or the players will create their own why after experiencing the how. As I think about it, why seems completely geared towards marketing text. It might attract people to the game, but only an effective how will keep them playing.

    only an effective how will keep them playing.
    You'd think so, but the funny thing about RPGs is that if you make it too clear how and why to play them, they start to look like board games.

    While why is geared towards marketing, for some games and some groups, it can supply the motivation that encourages the construction of an effective how out of the social interactions of the players, so that gaps in the mechanics can be bridged.

    Take Diplomacy for example: there are no rules that tell you how to negotiate or make agreements. But there are clear mechanical procedures for what happens on the map, a clear criterion for determining a single-player winner, and a socially-ingrained conception of "winning" which guides play. "Winning" is the fictional "why" that, along with the state of the game board, informs the actual-play "how" of negotiation.

    RPGs usually can't rely on such a widely-understood fiction as winning/losing. Yet they manage to be functional when the motivations of the players are sufficiently in-synch--even to the point of supporting freeform play with no overt rules. So if you're writing the game, you can depend on "why" text to some extent, and you probably have to if you aren't writing a game that falls back on "win/lose" to motivate and interest the players.

    Hmm.

    I make a distinction between writing different sorts of how.

    How you must:
    Usually a no-no for a flexible game. These are playing-dictating. Tic-tac-toe is strictly a how-you-must game.

    How you ought:
    These are player-limiting. Change or go away. Nearly all games have some bits of this.

    How you can:
    These are player enabling. When the player decides they are interested and ready to try something, the options are available.

    For RPGs, I prefer to eliminate the must, minimize the ought, and include the can.

    Excellent breakdown. So here's the thing: you're playing an old-fashioned dungeoncrawl, when suddenly the guy playing the fighter decides his character freaks out and wants to gut the wizard, who's out of combat spells.

    Must: "PCs cannot directly damage other PCs," hard-coded into the mechanics of the rules. You can't hit other PC's, can't shoot them, push them off cliffs, etc.

    Can: "Any actions are allowed which are not explicitly forbidden by the rules." The player says, "My guy is paranoid and thinks the other guys are going to cheat him out of his share of treasure. Hasn't anyone seen Treasure of the Sierra Madre?"

    Ought: "Characters are friends and allies who should work together to overcome the challenges of the dungeon. Combat should be discouraged as a way to solve interparty conflicts." Fuzzy, but reasonably functional. Still, just how far are you obliged to help others? If you can only increase the chance of everyone making it out alive by decreasing your chance, what do you do?
