    \n Sometimes when I GM Fiasco at a convention, I list it as 2-5 players. This means that, if the game fills up (and it does, because Fiasco is pretty popular) then I don't get to play. Instead, I explain the rules and help orient everybody else and provide general advice. Tell them what makes for a good PC relationship and what might be a problem. During the game, I throw out suggestions and ask for clarifications and try to keep everybody on the same page. A few times, the scene framer has even recruited me to play NPCs. It's not a full-on GM, but it is similar in a lo of ways to being a GM with a light touch. I don't frame scenes or resolve issues (as those are part of the game), but I help indirectly guide the experience of the people at the table.

    (I've done that for a couple other GMless games, as well, though for other games the cap on number of players is less of an issue, usually.)

    \n Interesting idea Nick, over on rpg.net where I also asked this question Komradebob talked about how the facilitator role could often be a sort of GM-light role (I'm paraphrasing here, not directly quoting) which struck me as probably true. I hadn't thought about a facilitator who wasn't simultaneously a player.

    Of course, in trad rpgs a GM who is simultaneously a player tends to work (famously) badly. In these sorts of games though the issues which arise merging those roles in trad games don't really arise.

    \n Fiasco came to my mind as well. I've facilitated twice, playing each time, and it was clear to me that what I did would shape what everyone else saw as What We Are Supposed To Do or What We Are Able To Do. So, I'll often be the first person to frame a scene -- but, both times, the other players took the ball and ran with it from there, coming up with way cooler stuff than I would have.

    \n A GM in a GMless game is more like a host than the one in control. By guiding and directing players to take the next step they are exerting more power than the other players. So yes, I think this can be done. My Fairy Tale Assassin game does this as part of the rules even though the host player is equal to the other players in power.

    \n Not intentionally, but I've seen people start GMing in games that have no GM, just because of old habits dying hard (Polaris antagonists, for example).

    re GMing to teach GMless, I find it's far more effective to be a player in the game, leading by example, than sitting on the sides and correcting/prompting. Less abrasive for the new players too.

    And facilitator definitely does not equal GM.

    \n The one time I played Ocean (not sure I'd play it again, I'm not a fan of the mechanism where it costs you life points to do cool stuff) it was GM'd. It didn't feel 'right' possibly because there was a feeling that sometimes the players had agency to describe things, and sometimes the GM took over in a very old school role and described 'stuff that happened'. When this was because someone had lost all their dice it led to a pretty silly death that had as all scratching our heads saying 'eh?'

    I've heard of someone GMing A Penny for My Thoughts and I can't imagine that adding to the game particularly either.
