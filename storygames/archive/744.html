Games, Kids, Smarts, School and SES

kleenestar
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Story Games
So, I've been teaching a game design class all week - which was originally supposed to be for college students and faculty, but got switched around to be for a wide cross-section of high-schoolers here in Pensacola. Originally I was disappointed with the change, but it's actually been an absolutely fantastic experience which I've been ranting about on my blog.

Back when I was planning the course, I asked for suggestions for games that I could run with kids of this age, figuring that hey, why not hook them on indie stuff while I'm talking about the function of game mechanics and the connection between story and system. People were extremely helpful - but in retrospect, I'm really glad that I prepared both for the best and for the worst, because I don't think I could have successfully played a single one of the suggested games with this group. (Some of them, yes, but only maybe 1/3 of them at the max.) Instead I ran a game of Dread (the Jenga version) and a super-simplified game of FUDGE pirates, which was about the maximum complexity they could handle.

Based on this week's experience, it seems that when we think about teaching games, we often think about teaching kids who are "just like us" when we were kids - but the thing is, we're the role-playing success stories. It's hard, at least for me, to understand just how different the experience of other kinds of kids might be.

Here are some of the problems I ran into, which I hope we can take as design challenges for figuring out how to make role-playing more approachable. After all, these kids are our next generation of role-players! The kids who were the most troubled (held back in school, learning-disabled, low self-esteem/family problems) are the ones who opened up like flowers in the role-playing activities - so it's not just good for us, it's good for them. One girl's mother told me that she hadn't opened her mouth in the classroom for a year or more, but in the game I ran she was throwing herself in, having fun, and asking if we could run more of it the next day. This is Important Stuff, at least for the group I've been working with.

- About half the kids cannot read. I gave them a half-page rules summary, and this group of kids looked at it and said, "I really want to play, but it's going to take me half an hour to read that and understand what it means." (Not in so many words, but one did ....) Can we create games that are easier to read, or that aren't text-dense with the starter rules, or that have an easier vocabulary without being dumbed-down?

- Many of them don't want to spend a lot of time learning the system. If they have to struggle with it, they will give up. It's really important for them to feel like they are in charge and capable, because they will give up if they can't solve the problem or if they feel like they are "doing it wrong." Many of them fear taking risks, even in play - and, I imagine, rightfully so given the experiences they've had in school so far. Can we decrease the risk of "playing wrong" while still making things dramatic?

- They are not happy about sharing the spotlight, particularly the ADHD crowd. I was trying to run a dramatic combat scene, with half of them holding off the attacking mob and half fighting the pirate king. While I was switching my attention back and forth literally every three minutes, the kids who weren't currently acting were constantly complaining and really, really upset about not having my attention. This may be an artifact of them liking me as a teacher, but I think it's also a design challenge that we might have to consider. Attention is a scarce, powerful resource.

- The more real-life experience of failure the kid had, the more they seemed to want excitement and success in the game. While we can design cool narrative indie we-love-adversity games all we want, if we want to try to reach these kids, we have to give them what they want. They didn't seem to mind dying horribly in the horror story - as long as they were cool and successful doing it - so I don't think it's as simple as character power = success = happy players. But this bears analysis and thought, because they didn't seem to want either immersion or drama in the senses that we think about them.

I know we've already got some discussions open about class, gender, etc., but I really wanted to share my experiences, particularly with the academically troubled kids in the group. I'd like to try to figure out some other concrete differences, and maybe to design some role-playing exercises for the next time I run something like this. In an ideal world, I'll be working on a brief curriculum that any game designer can take into a classroom (and one for educators, that they can use to teach about game design), and I'd appreciate the help of the collective genius of this group.

--Jess

    First, thank you, Jess, for posting this. It confirms some suspicions I've had for the last couple of years - especially after some experience as a temp teacher: Many games are designed by grown-up smart kids, for other smart kids. What we think of as an easy-to-learn game or easy-to-read game text can be so hard for many kids as to be completely useless.

    I'm going to ramble a bit about designing games for players who are young, inexperienced and/or not very interested in - or good at - reading.

    An easy-to-learn board game is a game that has a total time for set-up and rules instruction of less than five minutes. Ideally, you should be able to understand most of the rules just from looking at the components. This is what most games played by most people are like. Even chess and go can be explained in about this time.

    In role-playing games, where the "set-up" usually consists of character generation, it's okay to spend some more time on set-up - it's fun in itself. However, it's not okay, if you want to reach a broad audience, to assume that someone's going to sit down and read more than five or ten pages of rules.

    I've tried a few different role-playing games with different age groups at different schools. A quick summing up of those games, to show what I'm talking about:

    1. Three boys, aged about 15. One autist, two with learning disabilities. I let them choose from three pre-generated characters (magic-user, fighter, thief), each of which had three stats. Each stat had a picture of a D4, a D6 or a D10. High rolls were good. We used the blackboard for tactical maps during combat. The kids were very into it; the autist, who'd played D&D before, helped teach the others how to play. The scenario was just a few scenes of shopkeeper interaction, theft & combat. We only played once, for practical reasons.

    Lessons learned:
    - Keep rules extremely simple for a first game. Even with pictures of the dice, one of the kids sometimes had trouble picking the right die to roll.
    - Let players try anything. Let them succeed at almost everything. Roll dice for suspension; keep the failure rate very low.
    - Don't expect any heavy immersion, dramatic interest etc. Players with characters called "Fighter Dude" can still get very enthusiastic about the game.

    2. A classroom role-playing game for 11-year-olds. Everyone drew a labyrinth, put three treasures and three monsters in it, and GMed in turns, like so: "You see a corridor to your left, and one to your right. Where do you go? Right? Okay, you meet a skeleton!" Meeting a monster meant you lost one life. You had to get all three treasures to win. They had fun for the 45 minutes we had available.

    Lessons learned:
    - Even games that seem inanely boring to adult, experienced RPG'ers can be great for beginners or young gamers. Compare playing professional poker or Magic: the Gathering to War, which kids can play for hours (I did).
    - Once again, don't get too caught up in drama, characterization, plot etc for a starting game. Keep it as simple as you can, and then make it even more simple. (In the "advanced" version of this game, I let them make traps instead of monsters. They had the exact same effect - lose one life - but different color).
    - It's not hard to be a GM, as long as the rules for scenario design, conflict resolution etc are clear and simple. These children were rudimentary GMs, designing their own scenarios, after five minutes of explanation.

    3. An after-school game for two consecutive groups of six 8-year-olds; boys and girls in each group. They were little forest creatures, and could choose to be moon fairies (magical), troll fairies (strong) or sneaky fairies (stealthy). Hardly anybody went for strong - the girls mostly liked magical, the boys sneaky. I think we rolled some dice, but don't remember. They had special abilities, which I winged along the way.

    They had lots of fun. They laughed at the poor fairy who slipped on a rock and fell into a river; they were very scared at the big troll they met at the end, but were - of course - able to beat the troll (one group used mind control magic to lure him out of his cave into broad daylight, which turns trolls to stone).

    Lessons learned:
    - Some of the kids didn't get into it at all. I'm not sure why; perhaps the setting was too fluffy, perhaps they were expecting a board and some rules, perhaps it was all a bit too vague and they weren't sure what to do.
    - Make scenarios really, really, really simple to start with. This scenario was a simple investigative one (follow the tracks of the kidnapped head fairy), but there were some moments of: "What on earth do we do now?" Investigation should probably be used mostly as flavour, not as an intellectual exercise for the players.
    - Kids this age love playing with adults. Getting lots of attention from a grown-up GM telling stories and pretending to be a troll is wonderful. It was easy for me to do, but the exact same game with a child GM might not have worked at all. There's a big difference in making games for an experienced GM with inexperienced players, and a group where nobody has any experience.

    So. I'm not sure how useful all this is, but these are all things I have in the back of my mind when trying to design games. It's important, to me, to remember that even if you don't like reading, and you don't like complex systems, you can still love role-playing. I'd love to see more simple, fun and easy-to-start role-playing games.

The startling concept that we can make games for people besides ourselves is also long overdue in our thinking. Designing for others, and designing for their needs and abilities, will only strengthen all of our designs in the long run.

    Ok, I don't like the semiliterate talk because it obscures some important stuff. We should be careful how we use terms like 'literacy.' Most often, we treat it as a knowing-that sort of knowledge--I know that this word 'dog' refers to this animal over there, that 2+2 equals 4 and so on. However, once we start thinking of it as a know-how things change. In other words, being literate means that I know how to move from 2+2 to 4+4, or 8+12 without having to rotely memorize each step. Its functional--both in the sense that it serves a function and also that it employs flexible 'functions' capable of dealing with multiple situations.

    That confusion is bad because it makes it easier for us to treat people with shortcomings in the know-that deparment as if they have shortcomings in the know-how department, that they are no better at applying ideas than, say, an 11-year-old with the same know-that knowledge.

    These kids have tons of the best sort of literacy (I know, Jess, you already mentioned this, I'm just talking about it a little louder)--they know how to manipulate the 'language' of popular culture in which they are immersed. It's why they know how to tell a horror story. They aren't passively observing it, they are absorbing the principles of the matter and developing the most functional elements of it. What they haven't been given a chance to do is develop them into greater sophisitication through the application--which is what an rpg can give them!

    Which is just a longer way of saying: we have no reason to talk down to these people. They aren't less smart, they just haven't applied their smarts in the same places we have. If we start doing 'Blues Clues' or 'Teletubbies' versions of rpg's, they'll roll their eyes and ignore it. That should never be the model. Give them something that challenges their strengths and they'll move towards it, even if it involves 'buffing' up on areas they are not strong.

    Identify strengths and desires first and foremost, then use those as a way to address their shortcomings, use them to fuel that work, keep them invested in it when other difficulties have them down (and, yeah, I know, a heck of a lot harder in practice than on paper). I don't think that is pussyfooting around to say--"you are literate in this way, I want to show you how to be literate in other ways." It's pussyfooting (and demeaning) when we retreat and say 'well, they've got some literacy, let's pat'em on the back and give them a cookie.'

    They'll be more challenging to engage because they want *story* not math and geometry-style logic. Most of us here are math-y and/or rules-y enough that a game can hold our attention based on its internal complexity--based on the system underpinning it. For these kids, we need games that let them tell cool stories, without a 'cool system' being there to amuse us when it doesn't quite work. They need good systems that let people tell good stories--exactly the sort of thing we are already working on.

    The challenge is to shift where the complexity occurs, not to strip it away.

    Can we just assume that nobody's talking down to anybody, that we know that people can think well even if they can't read well, and that we want to make games that are cool, fun and interesting for their intended audience?

    I'm not (just) being nitpicky--Jess opened this thread with the aim of creating a curriculum--not just making games. The use of terminology is pretty important in that situation. And, as is my mantra, clearer terminology leads to fewer errors. We think with words, the better the words, the better the thinking. I'll also add that the literacies (plural) talk plugs directly into actual discussions going on in pedagogy discussions--something else that is good for a curriculum.

    What I have been talking about leads toward a broader question of how we would go about generating recommendations for this curriculum. Namely, what are the strengths these students bring to the discussion? What are the weaknesses? In what ways can those be used?

    Here are the concrete pedagogical-curriculum ideas that follow from what I was talking about. (Progressively less concrete as I go):

    General points: Especially in the early phases of the class, discussion should be the primary means of communication. Any handout provided should be the subject of a discussion, with not only explanation occurring but 'framing' questions as to why you would have a handout, the sort of information it provides and so on. Encourage them to approach the handout as something they can make suggestions about ("You know, I really need to know x, not y" or "this is too hard, I can't make sense of all of this at once"). This will help build the sort of critical skills they need to appreciate *design* concerns.

    1. Begin the curriculum with simple story games--maybe even something as 'easy' as cooperative circle storytelling. Teacher begins a story (verbally) and prompts students about where the story *could* go. Discuss how they might go about developing the story together. Let the students lead the discussion with a mind towards touching upon the following points:

    --Taking turns (including time limits, sharing, etc.)
    --Developing the story coherently (including how a single player can change the direction and mood of the story without breaking its coherence).

    Very important at this stage: Let the students develop their own explanations for what is going on here, don't make them parrot back words used in the curriculum (like coherence).

    After some discussion, initiate a discussion about starting a story and how everyone could participate in it. This will likely occur organically, from the students, but can be prompted. Allow participants to formulate time limits and negotiate some ground rules for the story. Establish whether or not there will be commentary allowed on the story as it is being told, but make clear that commentary will be necessary. A good idea: video or audio recording of the storytelling session, esp. if commentary not allowed during.

    (so, so far, two sessions)

    2. Move up to a discussion of why some games have more structure than the improv storytelling. Allow them to freely bring in examples from boardgames, childhood games, what have you. Important just to establish why you might want more guidance in the game. Illustrate examples from the storytelling session that might illustrate why rules would be useful. For example, conflicts between storytellers that might have been avoided with other rules. Introduce more complicated storytelling games (perhaps "how to host a murder mystery" style--lots of good pseudo-rpg stuff here!). Possible discussion points (again, ideal if they produce ideas on their own in response to conversation):

    --Taking on the role of a single character in a broader story as a means of making it easier to share a story?
    --Question of a central person whose job it is to keep things 'on track'?

    3. Probably good to touch on wargames. Can be pretty general, brief mention of origin in actual strategic training of officers--importance of 'near' accuracy to reality, and so on. Showcase some simple boardgames of this genre--allow players to discuss whether or not they seem to meet the 'goals' of a wargame. (is Risk really a 'good' wargame in this sense?). Mathematics should probably begin to show up in very limited form (simple tasks, dice rolling, looking at charts)--perhaps small, small excerpt from broader framework.

    4. Question of video games and realism--highlight how they can be hyper realistic, how they have realism 'coded' whereas tabletop required players to 'know' the code.

    5. Start playing some basic rpg--gosh, the old Marvel Superheroes rpg seems ideal--it's amazingly key word driven with a very simple, color coded chart. The genre has become very popular, so it is likely something they will at least recognize. I don't know how easy it would be to get hold of...

    Something else that might be important for a 'intro to game design' sort of curriculum:

    Peer review (and more) should be hardwired into the course. The goal is to foster a critical eye towards the production of games and the best way to do that is to make the students play the key role in the evaluation of the games. Don't call it peer review if you don't have to--just establish the cooperative ethic that peer review aims to instill. I think this sort of curriculum is well-positioned to do this. Unlike so many other places in school, I suspect kids feel entitled to evaluating games. Just channel that evaluation, encourage productive criticism. the prompt method might work very well. Like:

    Kid 1: "Man, I hate this game."
    Kid 2: "Me too, it totally sucks."
    Kid 3: "I dunno, I kinda liked it."
    Instructor: "Kid 1, Kid 2, what didn't you like about it?" "Kid 3, what did you like about it?"
    Kid 1: "dunno, it just made no sense."
    Kid 2: "I don't want to read all that stuff..."
    Kid 3: "Well, I like the idea that you could play..."
    Instructor: "Kid 1, was it too word-y? Or was there something else that didn't make sense?"
    Kid 1: "I guess the words were alright, but it had all those charts and I just couldn't figure out what they were for..."
    Kid 3: "Yeah, I just ignored those, but I thought this part was great..."
    Instructor: "Ok, so maybe this game has some good ideas but could be explained better. Let's talk about those cool parts a little before we take a look at the charts..."
