\n So over in TonyLB's Formula, pacing, landmarks and conflicts thread I said:\n
Posted By: MikeRM

Firstly, what we're talking about is kind of a personal plot arc or character arc, like "hero refuses call, hero accepts call, hero rises to challenge, hero gets overconfident and fails, hero comes through in the clinch" or "boy meets girl, boy gets girl, boy loses girl, boy gets girl back", or "bitter character holds back from commitment, bitter character is confronted with the power of love, bitter character risks accepting love", or... well, there are lots of them, and we recognize them when we see them.

I might have a little barn-raising where people suggest some at some point. I have a use for them.

Secondly, not all of these steps are conflicts, but all the ones I can think of are in some way related (or relatable) to conflicts. Some are changes of personal perspective which influence what someone will get into conflicts about; some are avoidance of conflict (which tells us something about the character); some serve to prefigure the conflict; some to make the conflict, when it comes, more poignant; some give resources (or, I suppose, take away resources) which could be used in later conflicts. I'd welcome suggestions of examples that have nothing to do with conflicts whatsoever, but I can't myself come up with any offhand.

Third, many of them (as you've alluded to) don't have unknown outcomes, at least not at the meta level - the character may be unaware of the outcome, but as a story-savvy audience, we know that the first two sons of the king are going to fail because they were rude to the old lady/fox, and the third son will succeed because he was helpful.

There's no need for a resolution system for these ones. We know what's going to happen and we narrate it. Maybe the way in which we narrate it becomes significant in some way in the long term, but what we're looking at here is not a conflict because it's not a matter of "it could go either way". We might even say going in, "Oh, I know how this one goes." That's what makes it a formula.

I mean, shy girl meets attractive boy, roll dice to see if she talks to him? I don't think so. Of course she doesn't talk to him. She's Shy Girl. But maybe something about the way she doesn't talk to him, or the way her player describes the scene, becomes significant and can be reincorporated later on when she does talk to him (which, of course, she eventually does).

Yeah?
\nSo, this is that barnraising. It's also a split from Tony's thread, because Tony's interested in a wider scope than this.

Here's one possible formula for formulas. It's based, unsurprisingly, on my gateways idea.

Title
Conditions:
- Series of steps - numbered when sequence matters, bulleted when it doesn't.
- Probably about five give or take two.
- A set of alternatives can be given for one or more of the steps.
- Some of the steps can be conflicts if you like. Mark these: *.
Attainment:
A new static situation that arises as a result of completing the arc. This can be getting a new resource, being able to take a particular action you couldn't before, a change to the character...
Challenge:
A new dynamic situation that arises out of completing the arc.

EXAMPLE (adapted from my example in Tony's thread):

Shy Girl Overcomes Shyness
Conditions:
1. Be with attractive boy in the same room and wish you could talk to him.
2. Talk to a confidant about the boy.
3. Try to follow confidant's advice next time you're with him, but you're unable to speak up.
4. Succeed at some non-boy-related challenge.*
5. Be with boy again at a tense moment when your previous success is relevant, and succeed again.*
Attainment:
You're able to confess your feelings to the boy.
Challenge:
Deal with whatever boy's reaction is.

Clear enough what I'm looking for?

More examples, please. Generic as possible.

IMPORTANT EDIT: I plan to use these as cards that people can have in front of them during the game, as part of the Pentasystem. My plan is to issue the Pentasystem core rules as Creative Commons-Attribution, so anything I put in it will be free-for-all and can be used in anyone's game, with acknowledgement. I'll credit you in my text.

    \nLonely Boy Enters the World
    Conditions:    1. Exclude yourself from a group
    2. Ponder the reasons you're cut off from normal people
    3. Embrace your loneliness
    4. Fail utterly at something, because of lack of support *
    5. Overcome your shyness and join in *
    Attainment:
    You're able to pretend to be one of the crowd
    Challenge:
    Now you're living a lie ... you'll never really belong. You knew that, right?

    \n So, when I was making, the Avatar game, I noted a bunch of these that appeared in the Avatar: The Last Airbender cartoon, most of which are pretty generic and applicable to other things. The interesting thing about Avatar is that some characters follow the same arcs over and over again, repeating the same mistakes or at least exhibiting the same behaviors. I think that might be important to consider. For example, Jayne in Firefly is partially enjoyable because he's always dealing with the same predictable issues.

    Here are my Avatar arcs, in your format:

    Learn a Lesson
    Conditions
    1. Do Something Recklessly or Ignorantly
    2. Fail Miserably
    3. Pout
    4. Be Coached By Others Or Reconsider Your Approach
    5. Do the Same Thing More Intelligently
    6. Succeed
    Attainment
    New ability or process or bit of information.
    Challenge
    Stop making the old mistake.

    With Great Power
    Conditions
    1. Responsibility Sucks
    2. Angst About / Shirk Your Responsibilities
    3. Your Responsibilities Provide Some Important Advantage / Catastrophe
    4. Embrace Your Responsibilities
    Attainment
    You are resolute, more responsible, more mature.
    Challenge
    Responsibilities still suck sometimes.

    Romantic Love Interest of the Week
    Conditions
    1. Meets Random Thing
    2. Random Hot Thing Acts Really Hot
    3. Swoon
    4. Flirt Shamelessly / Pursue Crush
    5. Random Hot Thing Turns Out to Be NOT WHAT THEY SEEM (??!)
    6. Get Over Random Hot Thing
    Attainment
    Caution, Heartbreak.
    Challenge
    Forget them and move on. And maybe you'll encounter them later and it'll be weird.

    More Than One Way to Skin A Cat
    Conditions
    1. Problem Encountered
    2. Attempt to Solve the Problem with Obvious Solution
    3. Solution Fails or Makes Things Worse
    4. Disbelief, Angst
    5. Lateral Thinking Used to Find New Solution
    6. Solution Works
    Attainment
    Lateral thinking skills! New solution! Let Stuck-in-the-Mud!
    Challenge
    The new solution doesn't always work either. And maybe it has additional consequences.

    The Power of Play
    Conditions
    1. Engage in Immature / Reckless Behavior
    2. Be Criticized for It
    3. Immature / Reckless Behavior Leads to Easy Solution of Otherwise Difficult Problem
    Attainment
    Embrace the importance of immaturity / otherwise non-significant characters! Zen wisdom!
    Challenge
    Play, that fickle beast, also gets you into trouble.

    Don't Judge a Book By Its Cover
    Conditions
    1. Judge Some Person/Situation
    2. Be Completely Wrong
    3. Mistaken Views Lead to Badness
    4. Your Mistake is Discovered
    5. Express Regret
    6. Use New Knowledge to Triumph
    Attainment
    New knowledge! No longer an ass!
    Challenge
    Stereotypes persist!

    \nWomanizer falls in Love
    1. Do womanizing
    2. Meet "love of your life" in some unusual circumstance
    3. Feel sense of curiousity and attraction toward this unique person, but disregard these feelings and continue your bachelor ways.
    4. Suffer a negative consequence of bachelorhood, such as lack of meaningful relationship.
    5. Meet "love of your life" again and pursue
    6. Be rebuffed by new love interest but continue pursuit.
    7. Win through resistance and achieve relationship.
    8. Encounter personal resistance to committment and change which destroys the relationship.
    9 Realize that you have lost the thing you desire most.
    10. Rekindle relationship with true love.
    Attainment
    Romantic relationship. New outlook
    Challenge
    Pull of free wheeling lifestyle continues to threaten relationship.

    A couple more thoughts to throw into the mix.

    1. These don't have to be just single-character deals - they can be story-shapes that are about the whole "big story" that everyone's in.
    2. (I don't know much about Burning Wheel, but) one way to look at these would be "life-paths" that take place in the game's present rather than the past.

    Oh, and Jonathan, looping round to the same one again is excellent - totally realistic as well as fictionalistic. "You now have - exactly the same challenge you had before, only bigger, but you have more resources!" would be one way to play this (kind of the D&D "levelling up" way). Also what Tony and I were discussing elsethread about struggling against your true nature and never actually breaking free of it, but the struggle being an interesting thing nonetheless.
