  • Rob Donoghue
    January 2006
    Common reasons:

    * Death is the one surrender of character control that many players are comfortable with - Social and intellectual combat generally requires a surrender of character control ("I don't _want_ to back down") that violates the core principal of play for many players, that it it _their_ characters. Even explicit forces (like charm spells) are not as intrusive as determing what a charcter _wants_ to do. There are soem holes in this reaosning, and it can be addressed both through trust or through comfortable mechanics, but it is a genuine, visceral knee-jerk response that makes up in sincerity and emotional strength what it loses in intellectual rigor. In the end, even if the character dies, ti still is MY character when it dies.

    * Poor metrics. Take every argument about what a terrible model Hit Points are and multiply it times 1000. Physical combat rules may have a lot of options and depth in play, but the underlying model of watching the health bar tick down remains a very simple foundation. Net result: Most systems for doign it suck, and those that don't require buying into the model, which is its own sales pitch.

    * Relative youth - As an extension of this, wargaming had a lot of history when it started rolling into roleplaying, and much of the model is build upon a strong foundation of that history, either directly, or in response to it. Social combat is much younger, so it's going to suck for a while.

    * Isn't that the point of roleplaying? - A lot of players just wouldn't see the need for a system, since this is the stuff they "play out". Whether this is because they are comforable with free form interaction or if it means they view this as a competition won through acting and argument can vary from place to place. And heck, some people just like to talk.

    * Why the hell take the time? - There is a persuasive (and self perpetuating) logic that says the rules density should correlate with interest level. Fighting and dungeoneering is interesting, debates aren't. Stick with what works! The corrolary of this is of course "It's expected combat is detailed, so it will be".

    * Fruitful Void - Probably misusing the term, but I look at the games that have th emost satisfying non-combat conflicts (Amber, Ars Magica) and they tend to do so because of a rich setting and a strong framework that allows for the contests to be meaningful. With that, they don't _need_ full combat systems. Of course, these systems also work with light combat systems too, so they mostly exist to make it clear this isn't an either-or sort of situation.


    Honestly, if someone came to me with one of these reasons, I'd probably argue with them, but for most of them (except why take the time) I'd be content with their reasoning.

    -Rob D.

    Yah, Matt's point deserves some magnification. Assume that player A gets his enjoyment from the crunchy combat, and player B gets his enjoyment form playing stuff out. In a normal game, they basically end up alternating their payouts, and it works out ok. If play switches to a mechanical resolution rather than, shall we say, acting, then suddenly player B isn't getting his payout. Now, Player A is now possibly gettign a double playout, so while it may be zero sum, it's probably a bad decision for the game.

    Put another way, I know many player who frame it as this: they want rules and resolution for the things they _can't_ do. They can't have fights, pick locks, or climb walls (though thank NERO, some try) so they project that duty onto the game. They _can_ however talk, think, intrigue charm and scheme so the game woudl merely get in their way.

    At it's most extreme, you may encounter players who will actively disdain social rules as a crutch for people who "can't roleplay". While this position has more holes than a cheese grater, its proponents are often quite passionate about it.

    I see a player being able to say, "My character won't do that," in a social context far more than a physical one. "My character won't take a bullet wound," is acceptable in very few games whereas, "My character won't betray his mistress!" is, perhaps, far more playable.
    This is a good example of a difference in scale, which is something that comes up a lot when this issue is discussed.

    What I mean is, "You betray your mistress," is an outcome, not a discrete event. "You take a bullet wound," is a discrete event, not an outcome.

    To really compare these, we need to choose two similar items. I think these are equivalent:
    (roll, you lose) "You betray your mistress."
    (roll, you lose) "You are defeated in combat."

    I think it's equally odd for a player to say "I won't betray my mistress," or "I won't be defeated in combat." Both of these assume the unspoken addition of "never, under any circumstances," which is pretty silly. Of course it's possible for your character to be defeated in combat. How? Why? Well, we can play it out many different ways and find out. Likewise, it's possibe for your character to betray his mistress. How? Why? We'll find out (either before or after the roll -- or a mix -- depending on the system in use).

    A lot of games mix scales like this, though, and it distresses players. They're used to rolling for the flight of a single bullet when they're fighting (discrete events) and then suddenly they get tossed into large-scale outcomes when "social rolls" come into play. It can feel like they're losing control over their character's actions.

    A system that handles scale well can help avoid this problem.

    What John and Chris have said.

    This all comes down to how people are accustomed to framing forms of conflict. Physical is no easier than mental, it's very simply just what most people are used to in RPGs. Once you get in the habit of being able to create reasonable stakes for conflicts, then all distinction of type of contest goes away, and it all becomes a single organic whole.

    I got to this point playing HQ quite a while ago. People draw swords, or fling words, and there is absolutely no difference in feel mechanically in play between the two things. That is, some physical acts we do not use the resolution system for (or, rather, we use the "automatic success" or "automatic failure" systems included, to be technical). Some social acts we do not either. The key is in being able to identify when a conflict becomes interesting to have. Which, once you start doing it, is as easy as knowing that it's time to start a combat in D&D.

    Easier, actually. Here's the mystical process. When you think it might be fun to have a contest, one where everyone will like the possible positive or negative outcomes of the contest, you speak up and say, "Hey, contest about X?" The other players will agree or disagree. When everyone agrees (tacitly, potentially, in addition to actively), you have a contest around the issue indicated.

    I postulate that this works for all play. Even in the most gamism driven game, if none of the participants is really interested in a particular contest, then it's not worth doing - what'll happen, however, is that in the name of challenge, players will accept most conflicts. But even in some games you'll see the GM say, "Ah, it's just a bucha kobolds, you guys just plow them under - let's move on."

    The problem, really, is the idea that RPG resolution systems exist to turn "let's pretend" into a game where you can't say, "You missed!" when shot at. So many game texts say this, and so many people actually sorta adhere to this in their agenda for play. But it's really wrong. We don't do conflicts in RPGs to prevent the other players from "cheating." Like all "games" we expect that they won't cheat as part of the criteria for allowing them to play in the first place.

    We do conflicts in RPGs because they add something interesting to the play. For gamism it's where you get to show off your tactical ability. For simulationism, it's the engine that makes the world function "as it should" or "like it's real." For narrativism, resolution systems are springboards providing information to spur more interesting situation.

    Roughly speaking.

    Once people get past the idea that there are some sort of perfunctory places that resolution has to be used, in order to keep things "fair" or something, and that resolution is a tool used to tell the tale in a RPG, to be used when it's interesting to everyone to do so....once you get to this point, suddenly the whole notion of one sort of conflict being neccessarily any different than another just vanishes into thin air.

    Like Chris says, it's fear that the game will break down if you don't have these hard and fast rules on what resolution must be used for. That the game will devolve into "Let's Pretend." When players and GMs trust each other, this fear goes away, and play can be solely about conflicts that are fun. Whatever those happen to be for the group in question.
