    Random ideas:

    Opposing Stats
    Source: Stolen from a Japanes RPG (Chivalric Warriors of Fusou)
    So, say you have like 6, 8 or 10 stats: They come in pairs (perhaps drawn out on opposite sides of a hexagon, circle, etc) and together have to add up to "a number" (15 in the example game). So if you have "Virtue" at 9, "Passion" will be 6. Strength 10 means Speed 5. Yin of 3 means Yang of 12, etc.
    So stats just aren't these ever-increasing units.

    Random Story Elements
    Source: AG&G
    The idea of rolling on a chart geared towards the setting, and then being FORCED to put those elements into play is something that is really exciting to me. As Remi says "It scratches a deep itch I have". I'm trying to find a game to push this idea into.

    Set Characters
    Source: Game Chef (last year), and the thread on design goals for gateway RPGs hit that bell again.
    I'm very interested in making a simple, quick game where there are something like 6-10 set characters, with a choice of three personality types or something: So the sexy enchantress can be the lusty power-monger archetype, the quiet and ernest type, or the "paladin from hell" type, and from that point on the game looks like half RPG, half "How to Host a Murder Mystery".

    The Group Clan/Org/Party Character Sheet
    Source: Originally my own, but refined and driven home after seeing it in use in a Japanese RPG called "Infinite Fantasy", a simple game based on a huge web-based for-pay play-by-post fantasy game.
    So, a leader is chosen among the characters in some way (their "leadership" score is higher or whatever). That person controls the "group sheet", which has maybe a few, or maybe many, resources to manage or control (in the example game, the leader gets to choose one of his special abilities, and Everyone in the whole party can use that ability, only fewer tmes per session. Also, parties have an alignment that kind of function like TSOY's keys: So the Party, rather than players, have keys). This is cool because it brings back the old school D&D party back into action, and gives it some glue without being arbitrary and lame.

    Totally Different Mechanics for Different Players
    Source: IIRC, I heard this first from Jonathan based on one of his games. Depending on the role/type of character the player chooses, the mechanics will be VERY different for that player vs another player. It will look like they're playing two seperate games, but they influence each other.
    Example that I'm thinking of: 5 players, one is the GM: Two players pick "protagonists", and their abilities and play styles look very much like traditional games: stats, skills and whatnot. Then the other two players pick "support", so they control a pool of a few characters, run with many of the GM-made NPCs, and use completely different dice and make very different rolls (almost like PTA-style rolls). Together they work with and against each other to push the story forward.

    Tactical Game using a Battle-mat or Chessboard
    Kinda been rolling this one around for like a year, it's my take on Final Fantasy Tactics or Disgaea the RPG: Basically there are RPG scenes with a fanmail mechanic, and the more fanmail you have the more influence you get during the tactical parts. When the board and pieces come out in between RP scenes, you move and fight tactically, and tactical gains there (like falling enemies and the like) give you points which you can use to influence your RP-side character. The character sheet has two parts, the RP side and the Tactics side (like two halves of one letter-sized paper).

    The Mancube
    Source: Lame idea I came up with on my own, but am currently hammering out for playtest.
    You draw out your character on a specialized piece of paper: Filing out blanks as appropriate with skills, passions, fates, and a few numbers. When everyone is done, you cut out and fold this paper up into a cube. When you do things (drifting between conflict and task resolution), you roll the Mancube and go with the results. One of the sides has your character's Fates, and an Arrow: That means that the person who the arrow is pointing towards gets to take your cube and move it to the face that they think is the most interesting.
    Currently stuck with how to roll the cube: I'm thinking that you would also roll it before scene framing to determine what kind of scene you Should frame next.

    Hero Quest/Descent... But an RPG (Hero Quest and Descent being those huge board-game family-game dungeon crawl style games with the little dungeons and pieces and stuff: But the above are all about loot and killing monsters, this is all about acting and telling a story based on set elements)
    Same as above post's PreGen Characters, but in this case the pregens are printed out all pretty-like, the game is more "gamer-y", and you have resource dice and stones to play with. The game itself has a board and resources and the like (maybe a GM, maybe not), and the story is pretty much set as per the board (drifting between politics, combat/dungeon and emotions/romance), and the players have to do their best with their resources to get through the story. The story ends up being different each time you go through because of all the bizarre variables and acting. Heavy RP, and LOTS of Fanmail.

    So those are some of the ideas I'm sitting on, I can't think of any others for now. Feel free to throw yours into the pile, even if you have just one or two. Maybe someone will find something worth sifting through, and can polish it up and make it work.

    Oh, the only one I hold kinda sacred above is the Mancube one, only because it's both very gimmicky and very lame, but I'm revelling in the lameness. If you are, like, truly inspired to pick up this totally geeky idea and run with it, go ahead but I'd appreciate a heads up first (if I don't do anything with it for a month or two, feel free to just grab it and go). Other than that, though, take as you will.

    Saga
    Character-driven story system. Start with a conflict web with a main conflict in the middle. Link characters to conflict and each other. Each link allows you to have one trait for that character. Traits are either general or specific; selecting which specific categories of traits are available (e.g., violence, sex, intrigue, war) also serves as flagging and establishes rewards (for currency that buys traits and other stuff) that relate to that area.
    Resolution mechanic is twofold: you draw cards according to traits. You discard a certain amount. The remaining ones are separated into numbers and faces. Number cards are added, higher number wins the stakes. Face cards are compared highest to lowest. Each "trick" you win there gains you one influence point. These are immediately spent on gaining advantages (more cards against this character -> gain momentum ->pacing toward showdown), changing the other character's traits (change "master swordsman" to "in love with my character", lowering efficiency but also allowing for complications and rewards), or taking out the character completely (cost depends on number of traits). So you can win stakes but have your character altered or killed/exiled/driven insane in the process.

    That's all I've got time for now.

    Figures instead of Classes
    Instead of a class which gives you currency to buy specialized resources (Class X gives you two feats and one spell, Class Y gives you one feat and three spells), have a list of Figures (as in Historical Figures) that players pick. There can only be one of each Figure at the table, because Figures are named. Each Figure gives you a set of currency to buy specialized resources, just like classes, so you've got the same flexibility. You buy stats and whatnot to highlight whatever different aspects of that Figure you want to feature in the game.
    Destination: this is going into my way-backburner project Sons of Liberty.

    Roll High, Roll Low
    A quickie resolution system. Declare stakes, and everybody rolls two dice. For every applicable stat you have, roll one more die. The highest die wins stakes; the lowest die gets some other thing (maybe narration, maybe resources, maybe something else).

    Also, on a related note, my Portability Goals:
    1. Plays in one two-to-four hour session
    2. Character Generation uses Chinese Menu (one from Column A, one from Column and takes five to ten minutes
    3. Character Generation employs significant flags
    4. Character Generation becomes Situation Creation through play
    5. GMless/GMful
    6. Uses handy tools: no polyhedral dice; perhaps playing cards
    7. Hand of playing cards = resources = diminishing resources = pacing
    8. Play can continue "next session" with different players

    These are the things rumbling around in my brain that aren't Spirit of the Century, Dresden Files RPG, or Fate.

    Faith & Credit

    Honestly, this is Rob's idea, so I can't get into it in too much detail. But I encourage him to write about it if he feels up to it!

    Gilgamesh

    I'm looking to take some of the ideas I used in Don't Rest Your Head and turn them into a mythic heroes kind of game, possibly with a Babylonian focus -- weird gods, bronze-age hipness. I keep finding myself using greek terms for certain concepts, but the mapping from DRYH would go a little something like Discipline -> Judgment; Exhaustion -> Hubris; Madness -> Pathos. (And Pain -> Destiny, perhaps). The mechanics would function a bit different where those various pools dominate, and instead of Fight or Flight there'd be a number of passions, like Fury, Lust, Zeal, Sorrow, which would consume you when Pathos dominates, that sort of gig. Probably a little finer detail on the things you carry and what matters to you, rather than the full-on oomph of the Exhaustion and Madness talents of DRYH, etc. It's an early concept, but I think it'll have legs.

    Sun God

    In the spring, He is in love, and the land prospers. In the summer, He is betrayed, and His wrath scorches the land. In winter, He regrets, and the world passes into ice and darkness. How long each of the three seasons might last is never knowable; it depends on the growing or fading passions of the Sun God.

    This might be a LARP-style scenario ala Amber Diceless, or a background mythology for the fantasy game of your choice, or a "gods walk among us" foreground mythology where you play mortals tangled up in the Sun God's court.

    The setup focuses on one of the two cusp events of the cycle. Either it is winter, and "we must find a new love!" Or it is spring, and "we must conceal her infidelity!"

    Touchstones here are Polaris's lyrical take on things, Amber's "everyday gods", the fallable gods of ancient myth, and a 24-hour game concept I wrote up called "Divinity" that I'm noodling around finding a more developed home. Speaking of which...

    Divinity / Matching Mechanic

    When I wrote up Divinity, I was taken by the idea of a mechanic that let you express character sheets on a business card, carry around a single 6-sided die, and yet still get some depth out of the resolution mechanic. Divinity came reasonably close to that mark. It uses the idea that your die, numerically, might mean something on its own (Lowest, Midrange, or Highest), but if you had traits that what you rolled could "match", then matches would promote the quality of the result to a higher set of tiers (yielding a progression of Lowest roll, midrange roll w/no matches, Highest roll, midrange roll with a "stat" match, midrange roll with a "skill" match, midrange roll with a double match). This is all a very dense way of expressing something that the linked PDF above expresses much better. Regardless, this idea continues to bang around in my brain and look for other homes.

    Well, Fred tagged me elsewhere, so what the hell. Faith and Credit is a historical game set in Post-Colonial America under the Articles of Confederation. America under the Articles is sort of a black hole in most people's knowledge that boils down to "they sucked, and then we had the Constitution" but it's almost custom made for gaming.

    You have a wildly diverse, but loosely affiliated, populace and geography with intense political factionalization over both the big issues (Central government versus States rights) and small ones (individual state squabbles). You have a compelling conspiracy to change the government and you have pressures and influences coming in from Europe, all to say nothing of Native Americans, Pirates and Freemasons. For a handful of patriots working to keep the nation together, there's an infinite array of possibilities.

    Even beyond the potential for adventure, you have the advantage of a huge cast of familiar characters to throw in who, honestly, are easy to work with. One of the joys of modern biography is that so long as you can find a few core points out about someone, you can get crazily interpretive with it.

    Really, this is a challenge to myself to do two things: First, I want to make a fun historical game without needing to add in any weird elements. Honestly, there's so much to work with that adding magic or the like would end up diluting it. The other thing I want to do is make an _American_ swashbuckling game.

    [b]The Girls From Yesterday[/b] - a roleplaying game involving a group of jilted women in suburbia who are dealing with the 5 stages of grief, might never be made, but I've got a rather interesting mechanism for moving through the 5 stages of grief. Each stage is represented with a die (Denial is d4, Anger d6, Negotiation d8 and Depression d12) and also ranked with a value. Whichever stage has the highest rank is the one you're dealing with for any given conflict, which in turn determines the die size you're rolling.

    If you roll at, or under, the rank, you move that many 'points' from the stage to the next stage. If you roll over the rank, you add another point to Denial. You're through grieving when you clear out everything, meaning you've made it to Acceptance.
