\n So I was listening to the latest episode of the Ogre Cave Audio Report, and they mentioned Free RPG day. The thing that specifically caught my ear was that not a lot of small press publishers are involved.

My question: have those of you who have self-published games been approached to participate in this activity (my suspicion: no)? Or did you simply decline?

I can't say I'm too excited about this. I mean, color me pessimistic, but I don't think White Wolf's Geist or whatever else will show up is going to be that amazing, at least as far as my play preferences are concerned. But if, say, the demos that are run at Gencon for these indie games showed up at Free RPG day, I'd be totally stoked, and I'd imagine that it would be great publicity for you gamesmiths and the community in general. Is this possible for the future?

    \n I've got mixed feelings.

    On one hand, I think giving stuff away is good. It's a nice thing. I'd happily pick up a copy of Geist.

I mean, I know there's a commercial impetus behind it, but at least let me feel there's an element of fun and excitement.

    \n To answer your question, Hans, free RPG day costs a lot of money to participate in and seems to basically be a gamble, as to whether it actually reaches an audience for whatever game you might like to promote. You have to print either 600 or 1800 copies of a product in order to participate in the lowest levels of the promotion and these are distributed to participating retail stores, which then give them away however they see fit (I heard rumors that some places even sell them for $5). Printing even 600 copies of anything is expensive for small press publishers, even if it's a little saddle-stitch booklet that costs a couple dollars.

    \n There was also a cool little quickstart rules booklet for Dragon Warriors (which was an early 80s UK answer to D&D) which I didn't pick up because I already have the new Mongoose edition, and wanted to leave it for others. For those who haven't seen DW, and who are interested in things retrogamey, it's worth checking out. A somewhat Basic D&Dish feel, with a few cool little twists of its own (like how armor works, or a somewhat standardized ability check), but with a very different world feel, somewhat more authentically medieval with fairy tale touches, compared to old skool D&Ds pulp swords & sorcery ethic.

    \n All in all, I thought the free stuff was worlds better than in past years:

    - Dragon Warriors was pretty and awesome, looking exactly like a short indie game or playtest ashcan
    - Geist was gorgeous and kinda interesting; I only didn't pick it up because the first PC had "Tiger Style Kung Fu" which made me want to throw the book
    - Rogue Trader was gorgeous and 40K-tastic, didn't get it because I still haven't played Dark Heresy
    - Paranoia looked cheap and crappy in comparison, but it was mostly the old school Palladium-level layout, I think
    - the Eberron Adventure was pretty solid, but not playable on its own, which was an odd choice. They should have included the little quickstart from Shadowfell or Basic Set. Also, I continue to find it really, truly baffling that D&D adventures don't include all the maps you need to play the game at a size you can actually use. Even a link to a downloadable PDF with extra maps would have been cool, or an indication of which Dungeon Tiles you need to buy. Instead, you get an included piece of cardboard with maps for 10% of the included adventure and no guidelines for how you map the remaining encounters. WTF, WOTC?
    - the Pathfinder monster book was pretty but completely disposable, mostly an add for Pathfinder, seems like

    That was all that was left by the time I got there.
