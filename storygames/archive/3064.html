    \n You know, I've also not been able to put my finger on it too much, but have always felt this hint of ageism, or at least an aversion to older characters, in most games I've played in. I think there might be a few reasons for it, I dunno though:

    * could it be because popular media, like TV, movies and comic books, always portray the hero(es) as young, in their 20s and early 30s?
    * could it be because people don't know what it feels like to play an older person, so it subconsciously gets stricken from the palate when designing a character in an "all age levels are treated mechanically equally" game?
    * could it be simple ageism? We can't see older people as kung fu hacker badasses, and can't think of anything comperably awesome that they Could do as an older character?
    * older people tend to have families (which restrict an "adventurer character") and tend not to have wild romances and the like?

    Also, that last rhetorical point brings a further question:

    What are "awesome things" that older characters could do that younger characters can't?
    A few thoughts:
    -- Have far more people connections, and mechanical support to make them hugely influential (grew up with the mayor, know the corp exec's sister from college, etc)
    -- Far more general knowledge "skill points".

One was JMstar's viking old crone from Art Grace Guts, and my best friend's bard character, who was in his 60s or so at the beginning of play (no mechanical effects). That's out of like 20 years of gaming. You definitely have a point.

    \n Andy,

    For me, the answer lies in the Hero's Story. Old Folks are not who Hero's Stories are about. They have come through their story. They are the sages, the oracles, the Mad Blind Men. At the end of Oedipus Rex we find him slashing his eyes out, he has completed his journey. In the future episodes we find him not in the role of the protagonist, but instead in the more traditional role of foreteller. (my recollection of Oedipus at Colous is less complete, but this is his role in Antigone)

    There is perhaps nothing that says this transformation must occur while we are young. It could perhaps be claimed that the lack of transformation is what makes us \xe2\u20ac\u0153young.\xe2\u20ac\x9d The mechanical benefits of being \xe2\u20ac\u0153old\xe2\u20ac\x9d are some of the things that are the by product of this transformation. The contacts, the experience, the knowledge. For me, playing through the accumulation of these rewards is what makes up the Hero\xe2\u20ac\u2122s Story.

    It could be argued that Old Folks don\xe2\u20ac\u2122t accumulate experience. Not in a real world sense, but in a Narrative sense. They have transformed into who they are. Their journey is complete. Of course this conceit is untrue, but it is perhaps the basis for an overwhelming amount of Literature.

    All that being said, a game could be made of the experience of the Old Folk. This is not a game where your hero transforms, but is one that guides the transformation of the next generation of heroes. How far will you be willing to go to teach your charge the life lessons he must learn. Will you put the needs of the community first? How will you affect the life transforming events upon your charge? With compassion, dignity, pettiness, harshness? Have you chosen the right charge? Was this decision made for you? What are the tests you will conceive to prove your Hero? Are there others vying to prove their Hero\xe2\u20ac\u2122s Destiny. What manipulations are you willing to commit to give your Hero his place in Destiny?

    \n Judson,

    I hope I came across as trying to describe my impression of why there is a predisposition in gaming systems, gamers, literature, etc... toward chronicling the "young folks" journey. It seems historical, as in, "That's the way it has always been." I wasn't trying to say that's the way it should be, or that it makes a good playing experience.

    Maybe the discussion should be framed in context of games being predisposed to the big transformation vs incremental transformation (if in fact they support transformation at all.) Big transformation being defined as a journey contained within one campaign, at the end of which the character has become who they will be. Incremental transformation being defined as the journey never ends, the hero in constant change.

    As a youth perhaps we are predisposed to the big transformation. We await the day we will become who we are to be. As we age and find the big transformation to be a false path, we open ourselves up to the idea of playing the incremental transformation. Maybe later we discover the path of incremental transformation to be too similar to our own lives, and we seek to play through the big transformation because it provides resolution, an end point, finality.

    It may also be that the big transformation is better equip to handle theme. By providing finality, the effects of transformation can be clearly felt. The moral of the story can be clearly demonstrated. Incremental transformation leaves things messy, unresolved, life like.

    \n Interestingly Traveller doesn't have a lock on age = skill = good, any lifepath system has that (as mentioned above). After Traveller, GDW used the general lifepath method for all of their RPGs as a house system. That said Traveller, and most like it, also make a point of countering the impetus to making old characters by having rules about how they characters become decripit with time. Starting with physical stats, but even reductions in mental ones as well in some cases. This is the "balance" to incentivize characters not always being pushed to be as old as possible.
    \n
    \nThat said, you still always pushed in Traveller. Why? Because of anagathics (age mitigation drugs). That is, in play the usual course of action was for every character (who wasn't pushed out earlier due to the system) to save up enough money to get anagathics and pay for their monthly maintenence. Every character.
    \n
    \nI don't know if it was "ageism" in the system... it was just a balance mechanic. But I think that Traveller wasn't really trying to promote age as a positive factor. It was just the "realistic" way to create characters by their thinking. A way to have background associated with the end character's abilities.
    \n
    \nAnyhow, what the system never addressed was development post career. Yes there was a development system in the game, but it was about taking classes, and, as such, making NPCs, you never gave them abilities beyond what the chargen system would give. Mostly you used pregen NPCs from JTAS and such, which pretty obviously came right from the chargen methods - in fact it would appear that they'd roll them up at random, and then give them interesting hooks. So, with anagathics, you'd have these 150 year old characters no better off than a 30 year old character.
    \n
    \nI've always struggled with elves and what to do with them. If you've got a character who's 6000 years old... just how much does he know? 100 times as much as a 60 year old human?
    \n
    \nI like the idea, Paul (age as something worthy of respect). I think it's very subtly complex, however.
