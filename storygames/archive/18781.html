\n I've been working on a game for about a year. The premise (in brief): It is three generations after the Deluge, a great flood that washed away a life-threatening poison. Our technology is essentially dead (with some limited exceptions) and monsters and magic have stepped out of the rain. Ever since the Deluge, there has been the rain; non-stop, sometimes thundering hard, sometimes barely a mist. Not terribly original, perhaps, but I'm going for a game in which the cities of today are the ruins explored by expeditions in a fantasy game.

RAIN uses tarot cards for both its mechanic and the character ... "interface." By that I mean that players use cards laid out in front of them for their character sheet, the environment and the NPCs are also represented by the tarot.

I've written a book for the players and a book for the GM. Both are about 30 pages long (font 12, no columns).

I would love to get some feedback from my fellow Story-gamers. I've put a lot of effort into making the mechanic engaging and driven by the narrative. There is a lot still in my head, so questions about setting and clarity are EXACTLY what I'm looking for.

I'm also looking for artists. Ahh, better clarify: I'm looking for VOLUNTEER artists. This is a labor of love for me and I am hoping to start promoting the game (once it is complete) as much as I can - for free. I don't want to make money off it, I just want to share what I think is a really good idea. I am not an artist, though, and I'm looking for some talented folks to help me out.

For comments on the game, please reply to this thread so everyone can see your thoughts and not repeat them. If you are interested in helping with art (and bless you), please send me a whisper or a message.

Thanks!

    \n I can see your confusion regarding how spells are cast. There are two kinds of spells: the specific ones granted by Strengths and the free-form magic accessed by the Skill Sets.

    The spells from Strengths are cast simply by flipping that Strength; there is no card draw. The free-form magic works just like any other skill (mechanically). You narrate the magic and can either flip the Skill Set to reduce the Threat or challenge a card and draw.

    I have been test-playing the rules quite a bit, but that has resulted in a lot of rule changes because I have a tendency to tinker when given the slightest excuse. I'm not allowing myself to do that as quickly now, but I'm not going to start test-playing these rules until the end of this month. I wonder if requiring a card-flip for Set-Ups is too much, especially given that there is already the chance of consequences.

    And, yes, the fact that every use of the mechanic requires narration or description means that a lot of mechanic equals a lot of story. And since the mechanic is either a simple flip of the card or a single draw from the deck, I think it's a good balance.

    Thanks for the feed-back. I'll put in an example of the two kinds of magic.

    \n We've started test playing the system and so far it's going really well! You can read about it here if you like.

    One element of the game mechanic that is working very well is the consequence system. The game uses Tarot Cards for just about everything. When a player wants to do something dramatic, they draw a card from their constructed deck. If that card is inverted, or if they fail to resist an action from someone acting against them, they get 1 to 4 consequence markers (the one thing for which the cards AREN'T used).

    In these first few sessions, my players have really latched on to those markers and what they represent. So, one player had his backpack destroyed in a scuffle with goblins (consequence), then another player took it with the promise to replace it. He still doesn't have it back yet, so he's kept that consequence marker. Since he lost his backpack, he has gotten into an argument with a merchant because she was making fun of his name (two consequences), and got into a fight with dead dogs (two or three consequences). Most of those other consequences he has recovered from - but he still doesn't have his backpack so he's hanging on to that marker!

    And that has kind of been a thing: when they play through a recovery scene (an idea I got from John Harper's Lady Blackbird), they are very choosey about which consequences they feel they have recovered from and which are still too fresh or heavy, whether a physical wound or a point of embarrassment. I don't know why I didn't see this coming - it's pretty much written into the game without being explicitly so - but it's great to see.

    \n Another player has joined the game and I wanted to put the character creation to the test - instead of sitting down with him before we played, we made his character as we played the game - he made decisions as things came up and without much help from me. You can read about it here.

    It worked pretty well. He did need to decide upon his Nature before he was able to play. As part of that, he also ended up choosing his Identity, which seemed to flow pretty naturally - looked like he had no problem choosing how people saw his character or what social class he belonged to.

    I asked him one question about his strength - it seemed like "Strong" should be a strength for a blacksmith. It looked like he was very easily able to put the rest of the character (skills, realm, and a second strength) without much issue while playing the character.

    The group role-played really well together. We have two players who have never role-played before and it seems they are having a lot fun and getting into the characters nicely.
