    \n A setting (or even a story) is not what an RPG is 'about', any more than Trivial Pursuit is about trivia.

    As with any kind of art, you can't really tell the audience what it's about. You have to somehow give them just enough information for them to be able to decide what it's about for themselves. It's a tricky proposition to say the least, and it can be somewhat uncomfortable for an artist when an audience decides that the work means something other than they intended (which is almost all the time ).

    But that's the trouble with art: an audience has to make it theirs to appreciate it. But I digress.

    One practical technique I've found is to list a bunch of random conceptual fragments that you find have a strong thematic resonance with the work, and then try to figure out the kind of resonance those elements have with everyone else. For example, when I hear 'fastlane', I think of 'cars', and 'risk', and 'taking control in one's life'. I have no idea what the title means to anyone else however. I'm not very good at this.

    But neither are most companies, which is why they tend to stick to using concepts with well established resonances.

    ...

    Sorry, my art and marketing studies got a little smushed together here and created a kind of proto-peanutbuttercup of pure thought you see before you. Not sure if it tastes great. May depend on how you eat it.

    \n Lxndr, I was in the "fantasy caper" Fastlane game at GenCon last year, and although I haven't read the rules, I think I have a good sense of what it's like to play the game in a non-modern setting. That said, I think what you're fighting against are the connotations of the basic elements of your game- the name, the roulette wheel mechanic, and (if what I've read above is true) the casino-based play examples in the text. Even the marketing text on your website. These represent the loudest, most obvious aspects of your game, and they all suggest a modern crime/casino action story.

    I realize that you've worked out mechanics for using dice instead of an actual wheel, but as your games central mechanical gimmick, it's hard for some folks to get around. Unlike common dice- which most everyone generally associates with "games", rather than specifically with gambling- the roulette wheel is unusual, and has clear associations with casinos. I've actually made this same argument about using playing cards as a game mechanic- I think it creates an initial clash of expectations for players, unless they play the game enough to get used to it. Games that take advantage of these associations (Dust Devils, Bang!, etc.) do better at overcoming this.

    So I guess what I'm saying is that I think the basic, powerful elements of your game represent (an apparently unintended) expression of setting- it's a game about high-stakes action, betting everything you have to get what you want, etc. I say embrace that, and make the game about that, instead of fighting it. Then move on and make another game with much of the same underlying structure that is finely tuned to another genre or setting that you're interested in, and encourage others to do the same.

    I think there are a number of ways to approach the thing, and I don't place a value judgment on any of it. But I do think it's important to figure out which one you want to be, and set your standards of happiness on what goes along for the ride:

    - I write my own game! But it's for me, no publication necessary.

    - I publish my game! But it's as a work of art, not a product oriented on sales.

    - I publish my game! It's for sale to a specific niche of RPG players.

    - I publish my game! It's for as wide an audience as possible.

    - etc

    To help me be interested in your game, I need you to give me a hook to hang my interest on. For myself, I'm not interested in pure generic systems. There are people who are. But I get the feeling that FL isn't just a generic system. The title, inescapably, says "modern" to me. That's a consequence of a still-current basis to the metaphor. All the buzz I hear about the game, which amounts to your posts here, is that "no, it's not modern, it's not anything." Which strikes me as doing a disservice to your game.

    Constructive advice:

    Speak positively about your game. Do not negate statements about it without providing an alternative positive assertion.

    "It's not modern" is a losing battle. You didn't mean to, but by naming it "Fastlane," you give the solid impression that it's a modern game. Embrace it! Give it a single rooted setting. You don't have to go so far as giving it a canon, but you could do worse than making it "the game in the casino." Fuck, I'd play that casino game right now. Because that's really different.

    Figure out what your game is about. My preference is that "life on the edge" doesn't cut it. That's either so broad as to be little better than "everything!" or the filler copy of every action movie trailer ever. But like I said: just my preference. Again, find a concrete setting that supports the theme really strongly - ideally one I haven't seen before. Themes play a lot harder when they sneak up on you wearing a story.

    Ask yourself (or even better, explain to me, because I just don't get it) why you feel like a concrete setting would be detrimental. What virtue does FL have that would be injured by a concrete setting?

    Granted that there are such virtues, you have a tougher road. You need to be able to figure out what FL is about and push that three times as hard, because without the trappings of something concrete, you have a lot farther to go for you potential audience can answer the question "What's that Cherry guy getting at?"

      Alexander Cherry's promo text for Fastlane
      The future is always uncertain - it is full of unfulfilled promises, and terrible predictions that never come to pass. I'm not one of those that's totally against PC-PC conflict, but in general, I prefer a little more cooperative play. By doing so, I sharply curtailed his contributions to the game, until later on when his lucky number came up. The final scene, where he tried to blow up the ship my character was on, was... Pretty intense. If I'd not hit my own lucky number twice during play, I'd have had my character die, and fail in everything he was trying to do. And I think I'd have been okay with that, because burnout and loss is part of the game.
      \n
      \nAs for addressing thematic issues... I'm a little more iffy. I mean, sure, it's almost certain that you're going to be put in a situation where you've got two different favors or factions pulling you in different directions. Loyalty, sacrifice, addiction and obsession are the common themes of Fastlane. But this depends on the GM and players wanting to address those themes. I think that, if the group didn't want to address any such issues, they wouldn't face any real pressure from the mechanics to do so. But the attitude of favors, debts, shifting loyalties and alliances is pretty much mandatory, given how the rules work.
      \n
      \nAll of this and no setting: Yes, definitely. The hard part is in expressing this to people without specific setting. That is to say, it can be expressed in any setting; The idea of "live fast, die young and leave a good looking corpse" is equally familiar to swashbuckling lady's men in the Caribbean, scrappy street-rat computer whizzes, and dapper, dashing gamblers. Likewise, the themes mentioned above can be addressed by a Lancelot, Guinevere and Arthur, or by people planning an 1001 Arabian Nights style heist.
      \n
      \nAs for whether or not the game would be hurt by a specific "default" setting.. I'm on the fence. Dogs in the Vineyard certainly is, even though you couldn't tell by it's wild success. I was heavily resistant to playing it for quite some time because of it's setting. Once I finally got frustrated with his persistence and gave in, and let Lx tell me about it, the mechanics captured my interest, and Actual Play reports sold me on the setting... But the setting was originally very offputting, and is the reason why I've never played it with any group that wasn't based around Lx and myself. Basically, Lx doesn't want people to look at Fastlane and go "Oh, it's just a game about X" and dismiss it, even when those people might very much enjoy it, as I enjoyed Dogs in the Vineyard.
      \n
      \nOn the other hand, including settings, plural, as he currently has, and through situation-exposing fictional vignettes, doesn't seem like a bad idea to me. It doesn't tie the game to one setting, and it can help illustrate what the game is about better than someone just telling you what it's supposed to be about. This is a game where Actual Play will sell it better than any elevator pitch. I've yet to hear of or read an Actual Play report of Fastlane that didn't sound awesome, but if Lx were trying to sell me on the game just by talking about it, I'd probably pass, too.
      \n
      \nOne final note about the roulette wheel: I love the wheel. I am resistant to any efforts to remove or de-emphasize the wheel. That isn't to say that the dice mechanic shouldn't be emphasized, but I think the game plays much better, and is a great deal more fun with the wheel. You can enjoy the game with the dice, I'm utterly certain of that. But if you dismiss the wheel, then you're missing out on something that really helps make the game what it is. Maybe the dice should be moved up to being the "default" way to play, with the wheel being a strongly encouraged alternate, but I definitely support that it be STRONGLY encouraged.

      \n Okay, on to the thought experiment part of the show.

      FL distinguishes itself by:

      * Supporting a style of play that is, presumably, not well-supported through other means. <<-- this to me is the big marketing question of FL. Is it true or mere assertion that the FL engine (and its various procedures, decisions, etc.) produces a measurably different experience from other conflict-focused engines? I'd like to dig in a bit about <i>how the FL experience is different than the DitV experience, the Burning whatever experience, the ORE experience, etc.

      * Generating a play experience (ie "the story") that results from that play style.

      * A novel mechanic that "requires" the use of a difficult-to-acquire piece of hardware.

      Okay, so let's assume the game shines when it's addressing what it's designed to address: Conflicts based on high-stakes gambles made by characters who can't afford to lose.

      So we need a way to a) give those conflicts context and b) reach out to players to whom that idea is appealing.

      First bit. To my mind, the heavy reliance on setting materials in most games is to give the conflicts context. FL by itself is a contextless engine, not what one would think of as a fully realized "game." It's good that the game already includes starter settings. THIS NEEDS TO BE MADE ABUNDANTLY OBVIOUS. Otherwise, the message I get from the game is, "Here's a cool way to frame and resolve conflicts, but you still have a lot of work to do to before you get to start playing."

      Conflict without context has no emotional or imaginative touchpoint for the participants. There's no reason to opt into the conflict because there's nothing at stake for the player (other than "winning").

      Okay, so how do you frame and resolve conflicts without using setting details for context? Genre comes to mind: take a long, hard deconstructive look at a genre or two in which "live fast, die young" is a guiding principle. Example: Swashbuckling High Seas Piracy. Rather that enumerating which fake nations use which fake boats, we tear into the ways in which Swashbuckling High Seas Piracy addresses "live fast, die young." The net result is a critical analysis of the genre written in such a way that situations (conflict + context) literally leap off the page at the reader. This is the way Shock: deals with its subject matter. The genre is social science fiction; the setting is irrelevant.

      Another way to frame and resolve conflicts: Mechanically build situation into the game setup. This might be as simple as a checklist: genre, location, name three things that are important to you, what would you kill for, how many turns or sessions will this game feature, each player provides two details about the location, etc. etc. Or it could be a more involved point-buy type of thing a la Burning Empires. There is shockingly little setting material in that game, yet the game's conflicts are packed solid with context. You lay out the situations in the beginning and go from there.

      Now...the second question. How do you reach out to players to whom the idea of play driven by high-stakes gambles is appealing?

      I'm not convinced you can really sell a game to someone who isn't already interested in the theme, subject matter, what-have-you. Most published games get around this by offering a little something to everyone: You might not like the setting but the engine is cool; I'm not a mechanic-oriented player but I like the resulting game play. Whatever.

      Actual Play isn't bad but I really think y'all have a higher opinion of it than you should. If you're gonna post AP, it MUST be snappier than 99% of the gamer writing that's out there. IT. MUST. BE. BETTER.

      A set of demos -- sample situations and characters -- would do wonders in showing off that FL is a highly flexible settingless engine.

      Possibly a quickstart rules giveaway. Don't describe all the various bets you can make, just sketch out the extreme basics of the game so you can play through the demos without buying the game. Since FL is a contextless engine, you might as well let people look under the hood. My intuition is that the value-add, the thing people will spend money on, is the work the author puts into making the engine immediately useable. The genre analyses, for example.

      I've gone on way too long but I absolutely agree that Lx can and probably should sell the game without a default setting.

      Last item: Lx, make an arrangement with a toy manufacturer to resell roulette wheels through your website. I'm sure there are manufacturers with whom you could arrange an affiliate relationship. At a bare minimum, provide a link to Amazon: $20 for a basic set.

      And another last item: If you don't feel up to the task of digging into genre, consider licensing the FL engine to someone who does.
