    \n Always, always, always bring pre-gens (unless it's a game where character generation itself is something you think integral to the play experience, of course). Always assume that someone will show up with nothing: no dice, no pencil, no sheets, no character, no knowledge of the rules. If you can whip up convenient little hand-outs that summarize major points of the game for people with no or little or bad knowledge, all the better.

    Really seriously think about your time constraints. 4-hour slots are usually pretty standard, and once you factor in players showing up late, having to introduce and explain everything, bathroom breaks, and giving people time to get their things together and make it to their next scheduled event on time, that's practically 3 hours you have left to play.

    \n I don't know what games you plan to run, but if they require prepared scenarios, locations, or events, please bring a lot of stuff, but only expect to use material for half you allotted time. If it's a four-hour session, expect to use two-hours worth of material, instead of rushing the group through stuff. If you prepare a Dogs in the Vineyard town or an OSR dungeon, make them small. It always easy to introduce more wrinkles and more stuff than it is if you realize that you've only done the setup of the problems in the Dogs town and then time is up.

    Decide if it's important to you to present the games exactly as written or if you're prepared to fudge if needed to keep everyone happy. If a roll kills a PC after half the session, decide if you want to fudge to keep the player in the game or bring more pregens to introduce, or how to handle this.

    I supervised a My Life with Master convention game with multiple GMs that I didn't know many years ago. One GM came back after the session and was exhilarated and said "The group loved it, and we didn't use the rules at all!" like it was a good thing! It turned out they hadn't tracked changing attributes and had just decided when to start the endgame, but the group thought it was great. Not so great if they wanted to learn the actual game, but as long as you're open with the group I think it's fine to skip parts of the game that will take too long ("Normally, this would require a laboratory roll, modified by blah-blah-blah, but since it's cool if you get the potion you can create it overnight in camp!"). Be prepared to be ruthless to your prepared stuff and to the rules to ensure that the group has fun then and there, it's all that matters at a con.

    In both Dogs in the Vineyard and InSpectres, I've created characters as part of the four-hour session, because I thought it was an integral part of the game and that it gives the group a shared understanding of the characters. But in a Whitehack game I brought pregenerated characters (lots of them) because the focus was on the dungeon, and I wanted to explain the rules as they came up in the game.

    Also, be respectful of different players. At a convention, sometimes the group has played together before, but not at all in the way you usually do, sometimes some of them know each other, and sometimes no one knows anyone before the game. This all gives a different group dynamic, so try to pick up on who talks most and who's silent and include ideas from everyone.

    \n Treat kids like little adults. They know when you're talking down to them. Especially the smart, gaming kids.

    If parents are there, sometimes they'll answer for their kids. I've found that children can handle themselves just fine if you can break them free of their parental yokes. I generally wait for the parent to jump in and answer, then get quiet and look at the child and ask them, "What do you think? What do you want to do?"

    Sometimes kids (adults, too) get stage fright though. If they totally seize up and can't continue, offer them A, B, C-style choices, or even either-or choices. And maybe add, "Or do you just want a little more time to think about it?" and make it clear that it's okay to take a bit of time. Pausing puts a lot of pressure on people, though, so if they want more time, change gears or go to another player, then come right back.

    In general, set table expectations around breaks and stuff. I tell my players, "You're grown-ups. If you need to take a break, just go." Otherwise, people will literally wait for you to schedule a break.

    \n Generally, if you have played games with strangers on Roll20, you can handle a convention game.

    The main difference is venue. Expect to be stuck in a loud room full of other gamers. Be prepared to speak loudly and clearly. Bring a bottle of water to stay hydrated, or you'll lose your voice. (Sometimes you luck out and get a private room at Dreamation, though.)

    I plan to be there, too. I'd love to meet!

    Folks tend to hang out in the lobby and bar area in the late evenings (8pm and on) and swarm the place at midnight after the last gaming slot (well, the last reasonable slot -- Dreamation is 24/7). If you're brave enough, aggressively introduce yourself. People will not mind; in fact, they'll generally welcome it, because they're also introverts. Find me and say hi (and remind me where we know each other from).

    \n Have everyone make name stands out of folded index cards for themselves before you get started (names on front and back so the players to their sides can read them).

    Do your best to make the chairs angled to form a circle, so everyone can see everyone else.

    With GMless stuff, I make sure I'm not seated at the head of the table. It de-emphasizes my dominance as a facilitator and encourages players to talk/play amongst themselves more. Depends on what you're running and where you want players' focus to be.

    If you're expected to wrangle players, I suggest just casually talking with people who stop by your table. "How's your con going" etc. Starting with a hard pitch often scares people away.

    Have fun!

    \n See you there! Say hey if you see me.

    Take care of yourself - stay hydrated, take breaks, check in with your table and generally look out for your own fun. Resist the temptation to not sleep the night before. If your games end up in the open room they will be very loud so plan on that in both physical demands (throat lozenges) and content (nothing too subtle).

    For some reason that utterly baffles me, people show up completely unprepared to play. So bring pencils and extra dice or whatever you need to play, just in case.

    It's easy, out of sheer excitement, to jump right into the game - resist this urge. Like Ben says introduce yourself, make sure everyone introduces themselves to one another, have a brief safety talk (I use the X-card when playing with strangers, so I explain that) and setting of expectations. I like to tell people where the nearest bathrooms are. This is five minutes very well spent, because it sets the tone for the session.

    Respect everyone's time, including your own. Start on time, end on time. If you aren't used to working in a 2/4 hour framework, practice.

    If you are likely to get caught up in things like I am, delegate. I usually ask a player to remind me when we are two hours in so we can take a break, for example.

    You can absolutely rely on Dreamation staff to help out if you have a problem, do not hesitate. You probably won't! But if you do they have your back.

    \n Here's my X-Card talk:

    "OK, here at table six we all trust and love each other, and part of that is playing with respect and care. I brought a tool to help with this - it's called the X-Card, and the idea is that if we drift into themes or events that you'd rather not play around with, you can tap the card, or pick it up, and that'll be a signal for us to fade to black, or fast forward, or retcon, or take a break, or whatever needs to happen. It isn't a big deal, and usually the problematic stuff will be instantly obvious and we'll just move on. Sound good?"

    Then I make sure to actually use it, maybe X-carding myself, fairly early on to model its use in play.

    Sometimes people who are uncomfortable will joke about it, or use it jokingly, and you must shut that shit down immediately, gently referencing the expectation of trust and love.

    \n Yeah, just stop mid-thought and go "oh wait, no" [tap, tap] "let's not go there, sorry." It doesn't have to appear affected, since it's totally permissible behavior generally.     \n I've only run a couple of con games so this thread is more informative than contributive on my part The advice has been very good. I would advise running anything you plan to run before you run them at the con, as others have said; making sure you have enough material, and that you have a sense of what the shape of the session should be given the time constraints, will spare you a lot of trouble.

    I'm running at Dreamation for the first time myself this year (I ran some Trail of Cthulhu and a couple of sessions of Night Witches at Dexcon) so I will just reinforce one thing: the rooms are freaking cold, especially in the winter, and especially if you're not used to the weather in the Northeastern US. (I vividly remember nearly freezing to death one time when I wore a dress without tights.) I always bring a long scarf and a sweater with me. Be prepared

    You may well get a small conference room all to yourself. Basically, there are three types of rooms: single party conference rooms, slightly larger conference rooms with two tables set up in them, and a large ballroom with several tables in it. Most of my experience has been in the single use conference rooms; I suspect the indie games tend to get that treatment. But do be prepared to need to shout if you get a ballroom table

    (The single use conference rooms are also the coldest; most don't have a window, so you get the full effects of the AC.)

    Dreamation is a ton of fun; I'm going to be facilitating Fall of Magic and Nemesis 382, and running a purist (not a usual mode for me!) Trail of Cthulhu adventure. One of the best parts of Dreamation is a chance to meet folks from here, and designers you respect. Why, you may even get to play the Shield Maiden daughter of a disgruntled Gothi who is played by Jason Morningstar (And I did the best rp'ing of my life that Dreamation in a game of Witch: the Road to Lindisfarne.) Look me up if you want (ask for Cat, not Kat Miller) or sign up for one of my games. And most of all enjoy! You get to share something you love with people who want to love it!

    \n Philosophical aside: as an activity provider at an event that people paid money for, I've always felt like I should look to the players' good time above my own. The thing is, though, one of the ways I'm best at helping players have a good time is by spewing energy and enthusiasm, and I'm best at doing that when I'm running stuff that excites me. If what excites you is completely compatible with general convention best practices, then great, but if not, I don't think there's a clear correct path, just safer and riskier options.

    Personally, I've tended to go risky, cooking up brand new scenarios and being eager to see what players do with them. I've had two spectacular successes, one serious failure which seems obvious in hindsight (amnesia & hidden information? with strangers? some of whom may have had shitty GMs? BAD IDEA), and a bunch of okay to very good sessions with that approach.

    \n As well as the X card, make sure you're familiar with the con's conduct policy so you know what to do and who to call on in the very very very rare case of something going askew.

    Also, as you're running a kids game, double check the rules on that. I'd expect their parents to be present or - at the least - on hand. But there still seem to be the odd parents around happy to leave their kids with a stranger for four hours.

    On timings, I do set my mobile to go off after 2hrs 3hrs and 3.5hrs. It's not for everyone but I find it helps me. If you aren't at the climax by 3.5 hours you need to hit it immediately.

    IMHO if you finish 15mins early that's fine. Even 5 minutes late is unforgivable.

    Also make sure you're early to the convention and to every slot.

    Pretens - beware making them all white males unless you've got a damn good reason. Two tricks here. Firstly, just put the data on the sheet and leave off name, gender etc. Allow players to choose these. Alternatively, produce two copies of each sheet, one for each gender. I laminate these back to back and reuse them at other conventions.

    And enjoy yourself! It's fun and it's addictive.

    Also make sure you're early to the convention and to every slot.

    Pregens - beware making them all white males unless you've got a damn good reason. Two tricks here.     \n Because it's kids...

    Be firm and follow a turn order, otherwise one or two kids will take over. Always address the least active ones.

    Have as a goal to praise each kid at least once per session.

    Let them talk but if they steer away from what they were talking about, help them by summarizing.

    Press extra hard on that this is something you do together. Train on beforehand on how to build on each others contributions, and ask questions to the other kids what they want to to based on the previous kid's action.

    Focus more on the spectacular than anything else.

    It's a playful game. Let them set the lowest bar.

    Play for two hours tops.

    Have a few of these points in a checklist that you go back to and continously remind yourself to implements.
