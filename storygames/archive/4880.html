\n I find that social combat systems are one of the most diverse and problematic aspects of RPG design. My co-author and I are currently in the fairly early stages of putting together an RPG (I won't bore you with pages of details) and right now we're putting some thought into how we'd like social combat to work.
\n
\nThe following problems crop up a lot with social combat systems in RPGs:
\n
\nMother May I?
\nPlayer: I've got the diplomacy skill coming out of my ass, I can convince the jailer to give me the key, pleeeeeeeeeeeeeese?
\nGM: No, he ignores you so you don't get to make a roll.
\nPlayer: Fuck.
\n
\nDiplomancer
\nGM: And what do you say to the guard to convince him to give you the key?
\nPlayer: 31 on my diplomacy check! Booo yeah!
\nGM: And what do you say?
\nPlayer: I got a 31, he damn well better give me the key.
\n
\nFreestyle:
\nGM: Nobody gets any social skills, just roleplay it out and I'll make the NPCs do whatever I think is realistic.
\nPlayer: But I want to play a character who's much more witty and charming than I am.
\nGM: Too bad.
\n
\nDeterministic:
\nGM: You failed your greed check and reach for the coins.
\nPlayers: Its obviously a trap, I don't touch any!
\nGM: Too bad, you failed your greed check, now roll to dodge the trap.
\nPlayer: Grrrrr.
\n
\nDualistic:
\nPlayer 1: Thanks my my high social skills soon he will be putty in my heads. Mwaaa ha ha.
\nPlayer 2: This is boring, I'm going to smack him upside the head with my club.
\nGM: You miss and now the NPC attacks Player 1.
\nPlayer 1: Fuck, will all of my successful social rolls against him help me at all in combat against him?
\nGM: Nope.
\n
\nPC Aura:
\nGM: And the beautiful maiden impores you to rescue her brother with honeyed words and promises you a monogramed handkerchief as a token of her affection.
\nPlayer: *yawn* Diplomacy doesn't work on PCs, if she wants us to get off our asses she damn well give us something that's worth more money thank a hanky.
\n
\nAsshole:
\nPlayer: You mean I can get Shiny Points that make me more powerful by acting like an asshole in ways that annoy the rest of the party.
\nGM: Yup.
\nPlayer: Cool, now do I get more Shiny Points if my character also has Tourette syndrome?
\n
\nWhat we're trying to do is make a social combat system that avoids all of those pitfalls, which is easier said than done. Any suggestions/vague brainstorms about how to do about this would be greatly appreciated, as would recommendations of RPGs that would be good to mine for social combat ideas.
\n
\nOur basic outline of stuff that we're looking to implement is:
\n1. The same rules apply to PCs and NPCs and these rules are solid rules not just GM fiat.
\n2. Good social skills are not mind control, they can't force PCs or NPCs to ever do what they really don't want to do. PCs retain free will at all times unless there is some kind of magical mind control element at work.
\n3. Social Combat has meaningful mechanical effects.
\n4. There's plenty of synergy between social combat and brute force skills.

    Here's some advice:

    1) In your writing, no matter what system you go with, friggin outline all that stuff up there in your first post, in that book. Put it in the readers' faces. Tell them that This Is Wrong, and give them suggestions as to ways to stop it, get around it, deal with it, and just not fall back on that behavior. Give little play examples, like how the Mother May I GM might have not gone the cockblocking route, etc.

    2) Why make the system equal for PCs as NPCs? PCs, the players' characters, are probably the only ones that matter. Perhaps make two different systems. Hold their standards apart. Perhaps make rules that NPCs can never "make PCs believe stuff", but they can give them a Happy Point or something.

    Otherwise, perhaps do something like this:
    (incidentally, this is a side rule for Tenra Bansho English edition): PCs can "be convinced" (by losing a Diplomacy roll-off or something) like they convince NPCs. However, if the player wants to resist, maybe there's a box they can check off, called "Belief". It's like the "Death Box" in games that have that box. Basically, if you check that box off you get to wave the damage you just received, and get some extra Social Hit Points or something. But if you then go to zero, not only are you convinced to do whatever, but you believe it is the right thing to do too (even grudgingly), and will seek to do it unless overridden by another later followup roll or something. Maybe.

    Or

    Again, make Social Combat dynamic. I had a dream of a game (this was before BW's Duel of Wits) where social combat took place on a battle mat, where you had three social resources to balance, and all sorts of points, roll abilities, and tricks like flanking, etc. And physical combat? Oh, that was just hand-waved based on the description the players gave. In other words, the exact polar opposite of how we normally see it go down in a normal RPG. Just a fantasy, but might be worth pursuing. Attacks of Social Opportunity, Point/Argument Flanking, etc.

    \n Let me step back into big picture mode for a second. At the heart of it, this is about the distribution of authority in your game. Who gets to decide what, and when and how do they do that. You say you don't want mind control. So what exactly do you want? What part of the fiction are players determining, and how is this resolved?

    First, you want player characters, with resources of authority tied to them. That alone is a big step, but because it's the traditional way most people don't recognize it as such. Most of the players (except the GM) affect the fiction through one character, and their ability to affect it is tied to the character. It doesn't have to be this way--the players' ability to affect the fiction could be independent of their characters' fictional abilities.

    Now, the second big step you're making is to hand over authority over the character's "inner" workings, their psychology, to the owning player, while placing the "outer" workings (such as taking damage) within the determination of the resolution system. That, too, is a step with vast consequences. I've played in many games where both inner and outer workings were up to me, i.e, if you shoot a gun at my character, I get to say whether you hit and what kind of damage you do. Every time, no exceptions.

    There are games that don't completely do this; see Call of Cthulu's sanity, KULT's shock system, or Das Schwarze Auge's negative attributes. Some games give the option of giving some of that authority away through drawbacks/flaws. So if you want social skills to matter, you need to figure out what exactly the exceptions to the total control of inner workings are that you're handing to the resolution system. I can't tell you how to implement social combat if I don't know what in the fiction can be affected by it. But this doesn't have to be mind control, if the fictional facts are simply "you're afraid" or "you have doubts." They can leave the final decision of character actions still up to the player.

    With the things that are completely within the players' control, you might want to use incentives instead of direct authority. Make it a temptation, like FATE's aspects. If you choose to let your character believe the NPC's lies, gain a resource point you can use to your advantage in some other way. This can be powerful if the entire system is set up around it (like I suggested here a little while ago).

    Alternatively, you can implement penalties for not following the impetus of the social skill used. If your thief lies to the guards successfully and they still try to catch him, they get a -5 penalty because they're confused, or have doubts, etc. They can also work like Oaths in Agon, in that you can call on them at certain points.

    Finally, social skills can place conditions on other characters that have predetermined effects. These can be penalties, but they can also be other effects. Having a predetermined "Scared" effect (D&D has a few of those) that you can inflict on others with your Intimidate skill is a good example. But "In Love" could be another effect. Also see aspect tagging in Spirit of the Century for a very broad, undifferentiated type of condition. I've got a game called Pentripolis in the works that was based on using conditions for all types of conflicts, including combat and social attacks.

    In summary, with your basic setup of player characters with related resources and fundamental player control of the inner workings, you can:

    1) Give clearly defined exceptions to the authority to the resolution system;
    2) Use incentives to give weight to social skills;
    3) Use penalties for going against the outcome of a social conflict; or
    4) Allow social skills to inflict specific conditions on other characters.

    This is probably a non-exhaustive list

    Posted By: Daztur
    Our basic outline of stuff that we're looking to implement is:
    1. The same rules apply to PCs and NPCs and these rules are solid rules not just GM fiat.
    2. Good social skills are not mind control, they can't force PCs or NPCs to ever do what they really don't want to do. PCs retain free will at all times unless there is some kind of magical mind control element at work.
    \nHere's a question about #2: how important is it that PCs be unable to force NPCs to do things they don't really want to do? Is it a really strong consideration for you, i.e., "no one should be able to force anyone else to do something with these social rules" or is it more "I don't want NPCs to be able to force PCs to do stuff and since I want PCs and NPCs to play by the same rules that means PCs can't force NPCs either"?

    Because the problem I'm seeing is that if you don't want anyone, PC or NPC, able to be forced into something, then it's still all going to come down to GM fiat. All the GM has to do is arbitrarily decide "OK, no, this NPC really doesn't want to do that, so you can't make him" and no matter how good your social mechanics are at that point, they become essentially meaningless.

    If you decide it's cool for PCs to be able to force NPCs to do things but you want players to always remain in control of their character's actions, an easy solution would be to just say that NPCs aren't allowed to initiate social conflicts. Like, the king can say "I demand you end your quest for the Mystical MacGuffin right now and marry my ugly daughter instead", but he can't actually start the process of dice rolling to make that happen. Only the player can say, "Oh, hell no! My guy's totally going to talk his way out of this!" and pick up the dice. If he wins, he gets what he wants: the king changes his mind. If he loses, he doesn't get what he wants, and he has to decide whether his character will give in and marry the princess or refuse and accept the consequences. But either way, he remains in control of his character's decisions.

    \n The problem I'm personally seeing here -- the sense of "it's all or nothing" -- is handled rather well by things like Burning Wheel's Duel of Wits system. There is a winning and losing side, but how far down the winning side is brought determines the sort of compromise that much be done. Real life isn't a series of absolute victories/defeats, like the phrase "social combat" would suggest.

    I've seen the "you lost, submit or be penalized" sort of technique used, and have even fiddled with it myself, but in truth it's as uninteresting as "you list, submit." The Willpower or whatever just becomes ablative armor for your character's freedom. You still get to say "no," just like if you didn't resort to mechanics, until you're forced to say "yes." Sure, there's the fallback of "save your willpower for the big arguments" or other such comments, but look at how players tend to react when control of their character is removed from them.

    (Aside: we're talking about roleplaying games with traditional player-GM roles. The player-character is the player's only anchor to the world, so when that is wrenched outside of his control, then you disconnect the player from the game. Look at folks who call bullshit on that sort of play and stonewall the game -- they admit they're out of control, and regain control by acting against the game. Look at folks who accept their character's predicament and play it to the hilt -- they admit that their character didn't get his way, but they're regaining control by playing it out. The heart of this issue is only of an outside element controlling the only force the player typically gets to control -- his character.)

    Okay, maybe that's not much of an aside. Look at BW's Duel of Wits again -- if you as a PC lose an argument, you can still propose a compromise (a disastrous victory not withstanding, but those don't seem common). That's how you as the player get to retain some control, and thus keep the player-game connection going.

    So, taking this idea back to the "you lost, submit or spend a willpower to say 'no'" idea, this isn't a reasonable player choice because there's only one good answer. If you make the idea keeping willpower interesting, by perhaps making willpower (or whatever currency you deem suitable) useful for another mechanic -- and not just as a sort of armor, but to actually do something -- then the choice is between "do I use this currency to defy this loss" or "do I submit, but keep this currency for later because it's also handy for these two other things that I like doing"?

    Of course, there are many, many different ways to present reasonable player choice in a game system, and getting players to accept choosing the bad thing is a difficult, but I think rewarding, quest. That needs to be kept in mind when trying to build a social conflict system that doesn't suck.

    \n Another thing you have to keep in mind is how much the consequences of a social conflict "stick". Often, I find that there's a big distinction to draw between a change in a character's actions and a change in their convictions.

    Like, take an example that could happen in any Dogs game: Brother Artax finds out that one of the townsfolk has acquired the habit of filling himself up with drink and coming over to Artax's sister's house and paying her unwanted "visits", but because he's so high-profile and well-connected in the town, she hasn't said anything about it. He storms out into the night with his gun at his side to go put some bullets in the bastard.

    Another player's Dog is on watch, and stops Artax in the street, saying, "Don't do this. We serve the King's justice, not your own. You have to take your feelings out of this. Don't make me force you." It's been well-established that the Dog on watch is the physical badass of the group, and has a lot of traits like "I'm a big scary mofo 1d8" and whatnot.

    Assume that Artax's player loses or Gives in that conflict. What does it mean? Practically speaking, only that Artax won't follow through with his plans in that fashion, in that moment. It doesn't mean that the other Dog convinced Artax that he's in error, and it doesn't mean that his attitudes or beliefs have suddenly shifted. For all we know, the follow-up conflict to that is Artax trying to sneak past the Dog on watch to go do the same deed. Or, Artax might stage an "accident" later, or whatever.

    A lot of uses of social skills work the same way. Losing to a seduction attempt doesn't magically make you emotionally attached to someone. You might wake up the next day and be horrified by what you've done. Being convinced to help someone in the middle of duress doesn't make you loyal to them - in fact, you could grow to resent them later.

    Take your PC Aura example, for instance: Suppose the maiden does handily charm the living crap out of a PC. All that means is that right there, right now, the PC will agree. That has nothing to do with whether or not that PC will end up actually doing it, once all the details come out.

    Making this kind of thing clear is a big key to making a social combat system work well, IMO.

    I still run afoul of this pitfall. There's no integration between physical combat and social combat.

    Avoided. Social combat works on PCs but doesn't force them to do anything.

    Avoided. Being an asshole doesn't help you in social combat.
