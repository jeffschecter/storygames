\n Hello Internet friends!

I want to take this for a spin, so if you are in the mood for helping out, I welcome it. I have this idea that involves generating a play map as a group, but that map needs certain structured constraints. I think this accomplishes it, but I need a bunch of maps made to see if there are any weak spots. I'm also interested in the total number of settlements that appear on your maps. I need to tweak the hazards to keep them within a certain fun range. Anyway! Run through this yourself if you like, knowing that in practice a group of players would do it together. \n
MAKING THE MAP

Start with a ten by ten grid containing 100 squares.

ADD HAZARDS

A hazardous area has a value between two and six. Take turns adding hazards to blank squares on the map wherever you like \u2013 you have:

Sixteen 2 -point hazards
Eight 3-point hazards
Four 4-point hazards
One 5-point hazard
One 6-point hazard (The most dangerous place in the world)

These can be spread out or clustered as you prefer. Each square can potentially hold a single hazard and no more.

ADD WATER

Taking turns, fill in fifty spaces of lake and ocean. It is perfectly acceptable for water spaces to contain hazards of any value.

OUTLINE THE LAND

Spaces that intersect at corners can have penisular connections or be islands. Be creative!

ADD RIVERS

Any space that is

1. Land and
2. Does not contain a hazard and
2. Is adjacent *diagonally* to any land hazard

\u2026contains a river. Connect it to the nearest body of water, running through other squares if necessary but not running through hazards. Also connect rivers in adjacent spaces to form a single stream sharing the same drainage. It\u2019s perfectly acceptable to employ a little artistic license when adding and connecting rivers.

ADD SETTLEMENTS

Any space that is:

1. Land and
2. Does not contain a hazard and
3. Is not adjacent *horizontally or vertically* to any hazard (land or sea) and
4a. Is adjacent *horizontally or vertically* to a body of water or
2b. Contains a river

\u2026contains a settlement of some sort. Mark these on the map.
\nThanks again if this looks fun to you and you try it out. The lame move of dividing the grid perfectly in half and putting all the hazards out at sea works fine, by the way - you get ten settlements, which is the upper design limit in my head.

    \n In retrospect, the math suggests the issue. Every threat removes 5 spaces from being valid places to put settlements. Since there are only 50 potential spots to begin with, and there are 30 threats, that makes the problem clear.

    So that said, what if its wasn't horizontal or vertical adjacency, but distance? A settlement can be on any land space with water, but it must be at a distance from a threat equal to the threats value (including both the square it's in, and the square the threat's in). Under that model, settlements can be adjacent to 2's, but must be removed from the 3's 4s and 5s, which makes for decent danger-is-far-away map.

    Alternately, you could place some number of settlements _first_ then have their horizontal and vertical spaces be safe zones, but I admit that's less organic. Perhaps if it was some minimum - one or two at the outset - then add any more that the map allows by the end?

    \n Rob, people are weak. We are small in the face of a large, ferocious, uncaring world. Alone we're pretty much screwed - we can probably do a creditable job in our age- and gender-assigned tasks, but nothing more. And outside those tasks we're helpless. Now there are spirits, bound to objects, and these can help make us more than just weaklings. I carry an amulet that makes me a better hunter. My brother has a soapstone idol that allows him to paddle in the dangerous straits I wouldn't dare approach. Everyone from our village enjoys the protection of a monolith that makes us better warriors. When I meet a guy from another village without any spirit objects helping him fight, I win. I kill him. If I try to paddle where my brother goes, I will fail and die. If I lose my amulet, I can't hunt worth a damn. We have places to go and things to do, and success hinges on these spirit objects, because without them, we're nothing. But they don't get along, not at all.
