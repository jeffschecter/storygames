    \n As for the Quiet Year game, I don't know.

    In general though, I think there are three common ways for Play by Forum games to die.

    Most commonly, life happens to a player. The others stand around waiting for him, but then eventualy start back up, however with much less frequency then before. The habit of play has been broken.

    Then, games often seem to get stuck in an unexciting place, and neither the players nor the GM seem willing to get it going again. I once joined into the middle of a Twilight 2000 PbP game, and in six months of real time the players never did anything but talk listlessly about where they might go next.

    Lastly, from time to time players will leave a game. If you don't have a matching stream of people joining, then the game eventualy will be dead.

    However, none of these are things the GM can't handle.

    Most importantly a GM should keep to a posting schedule, and if everyone has not posted yet, do the GM's turn anyway. This keeps the game exciting for the players that are there and keeps the flow going.

    A GM should break the game into explict chapters and scenes. This lets small and large stories constantly be achieving closure. By explictly ending individual scenes when they have crested, the GM can keep the game from dragging. It is also easier for new players to come into the game since they don't have to fit everything that's ever been written before into their heads - they just have to worry about this scene. And finally the end of chapters give players a graceful, not disruptive place to leave when they need to.

    \n Synchronous gaming - face to face or online - forces you to pay an up front cost. On Monday night, I'm going to spend three hours with these guys. You can't half-ass it, at least from an attendance perspective. Either you're there and so you might as well participate, or you have to explicitly decide, "I'd rather just stay home and watch Dexter." Synchronous games don't happen until the play group is at least willing to make that initial investment, so your players are at least vetted for that.

    Play by post, on the other hand, has a very low barrier to entry. You can sign up with as little as a few minutes to spare on an irregular basis. As a result, participants with extremely low motivation/determination levels can participate, because they're not forced to give anything up.

    It's easy to procrastinate - you can always do your turn "later". This is impossible with a synchronous session - either you're there at the appointed time, or you miss it entirely.

    The other thing is that participation has a positive feedback loop. If we're all posting like clockwork, it's great. If the GM is late a day or two, well, it sends the message that this is okay; next thing you know, a few other are a day or two late as well. Since nobody actually wasted a three-hour face-to-face session with nobody showing up, everyone can pretend the game 'might come back' for along time, because nobody actually has to pronounce the time of death.

    \n I've thought about this a lot too. I think there is certainly a "life happens" element, but I also think it's often used as the excuse, rather than being the actual reason. Call me cynical, but I see a number of reasons why PbF games fail.

    - Gamers often use PbF games as a stand-in for real life games. They have the itch to get a role-playing session going, but for whatever reason, they can't make it happen, so they use PbF games to fill the void...but they're slow paced so you never get to scratch that itch while it's still itchy. After two or three weeks, having barely hit the surface of the adventure, they just get bored of it and lose interest. Many gamers have a little bit of ADHD when it comes to what they're excited about.

    - One of the things that used to be a turn-off to me was the long, drawn out posts from certain players. Some people really like the writing aspect of PbF, and often see it as a built-in group of people to read their short fiction or an opportunity to flex their creative writing muscles. Personally, I enjoy the interactivity, so long flashbacks and dream sequences and drawn out in-character thoughts quickly tire me out. I much prefer frequent, shorter posts with more back and forth.

    - Most of the time when you start PbF, it's with a bunch of random pick up gamers. If you've played a few online MMO's, you know what a PUG is. They're hit or miss, and a lot of the time you'll get people fighting for the spotlight. They'll butt heads in character, but you can tell by the tone of their writing that they're really frustrated as players. Sometimes that can be dealt with on the social level, but it's one thing to deal with your friends butting heads, but mediating for random internet people can be difficult to impossible.

    - They're tiring on the GM. It's like managing a department. You've got to provide a lot content over long periods of time. It just gets old over a few months.

    - Internet anonymity makes it much easier to just walk away and disappear. It's not like they ever have to speak to the other players again in most cases.

    \n There's a cost vs. reward thing happening here. PbF is certainly convenient for a lot of people; it's accessible, asynchronous, and so on. There's a cost to it though. Some people find it harder to express themselves that way via text vs. face-to-face, verbal communication. (Sure, some people prefer to write than speak.)

    The rewards are slower than f2f games. Your reward is only partly the writing. Mostly, it's the reading other people's responses to you. Creative Agenda theory, right? Other people have to validate your contribution for your group to really gel. Even if everyone is posting regularly, the pace can be slower than you need it to be, in order to get a reward that pays for the high cost of all that writing work. When it's time to log into the forum and check your posts, and you log in and no one has responded yet ("but it's been three hours!"), you get discouraged. You check in less often. You take longer to post, because your expectation of immediate reward just isn't there.

    The compromise is synchronous text role-playing (i.e., mushes). It means sitting there for five minutes while Bob writes his monster paragraph pose, but you're probably surfing the web till your screen flashes. Then you pose (immediately, a short two-sentence response, taking a minute or two to type, because you like to keep things moving). Mentally, you start adjusting to that 2-5-minute back-and-forth and not really noticing it. There's a weird time-dilation effect that happens with mush-gaming.

    \n Okay, let's turn this bus around. What tricks and tips do you use to keep interest in a PbF game going and well running?

    - Avatar support (like RPoL) or embedding custom avatars goes a long way towards making it clear when people speak in character.
    - It's good to have a GM that's willing to check the forum thread several times a day so that they're responsive to players on various schedules.
    - Posting maps helps to reduce confusion on where the GM thinks the players are.
    - Use maps with grid coordinates so that players have a common reference system.
    - Maintain an OOC (out of character) and and IC (in character thread.) This will keep the dice rolling, rules checks, and other housekeeping out of the story.
    - Allow the players greater narrative control over the minor stuff even in traditional games. After all the GM still retains the veto if they get carried away.
    - Make up for the lack of feedback at the table with more descriptive writing, leverage those other senses.

    Any others?

    Okay, let's turn this bus around. What tricks and tips do you use to keep interest in a PbF game going and well running?
    \nI don't PbF, but I understand the format well enough to suggest things. Adding to what Todd already said:

    - Set expectations carefully. Start a PbF story with guidelines that include how often you expect people to post, how to take a vacation for a day or two, and how
    to politely bow out. That way, games don't get left in the lurch.

    - Give up the idea of ownership of character. If anyone can play any character, people can drop in and out. Other people can keep the ball rolling when one person slacks off. Add a 'if so-and-so is on a roll, please let them keep doing it' guideline to avoid three people vying over one character. Or 'a character becomes publicly available to the first claimant after being idle 48 hours' or something.

    - As GM, don't just ask questions. Ask questions, suggest possible answers, and describe possible outcomes for those answers. That is, lay the story-map out in front of the players so they can see where they're going. Don't hide information from players and hand bits of information out, piecemeal. That just slows everything down.

    - If you see dice-rolling is going to happen soon, get all the players' intents, and then manage the dice all at once, quickly. Keep it moving.

    \n I agree with asynchronous play having benefits of it's own. I'd even go so far as to say that the fact alone that you can take your time, as much time as you need, before doing anything in the game, enables a truly different kind of play. For instance, a common and sage piece of advice in tabletop is to do the "obvious", perhaps because doing something is always better than doing nothing. In asynchronous play, you don't have to. You can mull it over and come up with something clever or dramatic, without being limited by your speed of thought.

    It's also possible to invest more in setting, character and color in general than in tabletop. Personally, I dislike any setting that I can succinctly explain in 15 minutes for the purpose of starting the game, or requiring players to read the book before the first session (oppressive social footprint and all). They won't be able to actually remember all of that fully in play anyway and all that detail is wasted. While PbF players will gobble up 20-pages in shaky grammar and be excited to play in that world. And you can take a breath and refer back to that information at any time. I don't think we often notice how oppressive the need for spontaneity and fast thinking in tabletop really can be.

    That's some pros. Cons, I think, are already apparent.
