Meshing external conflict to multiple internal conflicts

TonyLB
January 2007 edited January 2007 in Play Advice
So, suppose you have one player, who has one internal conflict for their character. You, the GM, present an external conflict. There are multiple techniques for making sure that this external conflict helps and highlights the internal conflict, and that the conclusion of the two of them happens in synchrony (and often due to each other).

NOW ... suppose you have two players, each of whom has one internal conflict for their character.

Do you mesh a single external conflict with both internals? If so, how? How do you make it connect to both? How do you get all three things synchronized to each other? What sort of connection does that imply between the two internal conflicts, and how does that play out?

Do you expand to two external conflicts? How do you keep those working together well with each other?

    I was thinking about something similar to this yesterday.

    I'm working on a '40s-'50s-style, rocket space patrol game, using deas I worked out in Depths of Kanthe, plus a few more things, like tying personal values to external conflicts. The basis of this (not completely worked out yet) are:

    1. roll up the starting situation and flesh it out (conflict between miners and space pirates, for example;)
    2. while playing out the conflict, plot twists will occasionally occur, making the situation more complicated;
    3. some of those plot twists will be external, but some will link to internal values.

    For a space patrol game, the internal values would be pure and simple, maybe with two per character, each a single word from a list (Love, Honor, Duty, Justice, Compassion, Courage) interpreted in either a positive or negative manner. The two words are combined into a single one-sentence description of the moment of decision when the character chose to join the patrol.

    I'm aiming at random creation of situation in potentially GMless play: random results (probably a coin-toss on a playsheet listing external and internal conflicts) occasionally indicate a plot twist, and sometimes this twist invokes one or more of those words and indicates it manifests either positively or negatively. For example, the results "positive Love" might indicate an NPC in the current scene suddenly expressing love; "negative Duty" might be a suggestion from an NPC to disobey orders. For my purposes, there's no indication of what the player should do: the player can choose how to react, if at all. This means I'm treating the moment of decision as a player flag, giving the player opportunities to address the character's personal issues.

    There's no explicit synchronization between two player's values, but the plot twist mechanics cause the situation to snowball. If your Duty value was invoked (central command issues an order to execute the space pirates) and I'm in another scene where the pirate queen expresses her love for me, our internal values may come into conflict, based on each of our decisions. Do I let the queen escape? Do you order me to execute her and I obey? Do you do it yourself, without asking me? Do I convince you to not execute her, but take her back as a prisoner and request a more lenient punishment?

    I kind of like this "serial synchronization" approach. Instead of force-linking two internal conflicts into every external conflict, let the conflicts slowly collide.

    What I tend to do when running carry (which, as Jeffrey says, is very much about external events impacting internal conflict) is find out situations that touch on the Burdens of whichever characters are in the scene, and then turn it over to the players (via the stakes resolution mechanics procedures) to say how those situations will impact them. The procedure is that, once a conflict has been identified, the GM sets the losing stakes for the characters, but each player sets their own winning stakes. This is reinforced by a mechanic wherein conflicts that have to do with your Burden allow you to bring in more resources in order to win them.

    Say Adam's character has a Burden "Must prove himself" and Bob's has a Burden "Can't let go of the memories of those he kills" (both of these seem to be internal conflicts, how you're describing them - am I right, Tony?)

    As the GM, perhaps I frame them into a situation where the Sergeant, who has been fairly antagonistic throughout the whole game, leaves the two of them alone with a pair of VC operatives and says "when I come back, I expect them to be dead."

    Adam here gets to set his winning stakes. Let's say he says "Ok, If I win, I grit my teeth and shoot em both, clean dead. This is my chance to prove that I can be a good soldier."

    And say Bob says "Holy crap, I can't deal with the guys I kill at night in the jungle, let alone in cold blood in a shack. If I win, I talk Adam's character into helping me stash these guys out in the rice paddy, and we'll lie to Sarge."

    And, in this case, I would probably call this one a conflict between the two characters, as their intentions here are opposed. If they weren't opposed (say, Bob's winning stakes were that, if he wins, he makes himself watch Adam's character kill them without flinching), then I would set losing stakes that hilighted their Burdens. Like, for Adam, I would say "If you lose, you take so long steeling yourself that the Sarge comes back, chews you out and then shoots them himself."

    Tony, how does this strike you?

    There is a lot simpler way to look at this. Tony, you're worrying about picking locks vs. kicking down doors when you haven't even checked to see if the door is already open.

    Let's say there are two characters with two internal conflicts and you are trying to cook up some external sitaution that will throw those internal conflicts into relief. You can either:

    1. Just throw all kinds of external situations reflecting only one of the internal conflicts at the characters and eventually one of them will go 'oh yeah, hey look'. This is the trad gaming route, and it works marvelously and fuck anyone who says different.

    2. Concoct with the players' help situations that they feel express the internal conflicts, then combine the two concurrently or consecutively. Then when one guys' conflict is "in the fore", the other guy isn't scratching his head, he knows what's what and can enjoy the scene either as a supporting player or as an audience. This is the hippie narration passing route and it works marvelously and fuck anyone who says different.
