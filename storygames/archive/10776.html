\n I'm running Primetime Adventures for the second time ever, and my group is on fire. It is rocking so hard it's hard to believe. Screen presence and fan mail are genius. We do not have to change a thing to be having a wildly successful game.

It's just that conflict feels like it could rock even more, but I'm not entirely getting it. We very often have trouble "finding" the conflict in the scene, even when it's populated full of amazing characters who want very different things. Either we're all so totally on the same page that we just know what has to happen next, or all the possibilities of what could happen are so equally awesome that nobody can invest too deeply in any one outcome: it's just as interesting to lose as to win.

The show is called Folk Noir. It's a glorious ripoff of Fables / Shrek / Thursday Next, in which fairy-tale characters (the "Folk"), driven from their ancestral homelands ("Story"), live a secret life beneath the noses of mortal ("Reader") society. It's a detective show, centered on Charming Investigations, a private-eye office founded by ex-prince Sam Charming (an NPC). I've put character details and episode synopses below.

So for an example of how conflict is hard to "find," consider a scene from Episode 3:

Red (Riding Hood) and her ex-boyfriend Gray (The Big Bad Wolf) have been kidnapped by bounty hunters Bo (Peep) and Brick (the eldest Pig). The rest of the cast -- Baba Yaga, Mr. Bridges the troll, and Timmy the lit major -- face off against Regina Hart (the Queen of Hearts), a thoroughly criminal-seeming woman they've spent the better part of two episodes investigating. The team wants to rescue Red; Regina Hart wants to procure an item Gray is carrying. They agree to work together.

We played through this (fun and thrilling!) plot scene without "finding" the place to use mechanical conflict. You'd think it would be something about agreeing to work with a criminal. But the whole thing just felt so right. Of course this was the part of the show where the good guys and the bad guy team up against a secondary bad guy! The power of the trope sang to us! We couldn't put any of that stuff at risk: everyone at the table agreed it was way too awesome. So in the end I wedged in a halfhearted conflict about the terms of the agreement to work together, and it really just didn't matter much.

"Work in their issues!" I expect to hear. And I know, I know... I feel like I know that in my head, but I'm just not grokking it in the moment. "Does Timmy convince Regina he's important even though he's not magical?" Meh. "Does Mr. Bridges trust Regina not to entangle him in his criminal past?" Vague. "Does Yaga use reason or fear to drive her end of the bargain?" Weak.

Sometimes we get it. In the follow-up confrontation between Red and Gray vs. Bo and Brick, at stake for Red was: "Do you lose Gray?" That clicked beautifully, since this was her spotlight episode and she'd just started to trust him again.

But on the other hand, Red and Gray's prior scene was completely conflict-free! It was classic: two ex-lovers, captive together, who can't possibly escape unless they learn to trust each other again. And we set up the scene, and we roleplayed it... and everyone at the table wanted Red to learn to trust Gray, myself included. It was Red's spotlight episode. Of course something like that would happen. Her story arc demanded it and we made it so.

We are operating at a crazy-high 95% level of fun right now, but this conflict thing nags. Help me hit the final 5%! How am I failing to find mechanical conflict and uncertainty in all these otherwise wildly satisfying scenes?

Adam C.

Show: Folk Noir

Protagonists:

Baba Yaga
Issue: She wants to be loved instead of feared -- but fear's such an easy tool...
Edges: Witch Magic, Bad Reputation
Connection: The Bear of Russia (a fellow Folk from the old country, he alone really understands how far Yaga has come from her wicked ways)
Nemesis: Sam Charming (Yaga's godson. She has made him into much of who he is today, but he's a narcissist who takes all the credit, and takes her help for granted.)

Rosalind "Red" Hood (Red Riding Hood)
Issue: Unresolved relationship with her ex-boyfriend Gray (the Big Bad Wolf)
Edges: Werewolf, Hot Chick
Connection: Grandmother
Nemesis: Gray (She was drawn to the bad boy, but he never told her that lycanthropy was a sexually transmitted disease. Now she hates him, though her new instincts are telling her otherwise...)

Mr. Bridges (Troll)
Issue: His criminal past is catching up with him
Edges: Troll Strength, Criminal Past
Connection: Gremlin, a street informant
Nemesis: the mysterious leader of the Cartel (a gang of criminal Folk who want him dead for knowing too much. But he doesn't even know what he knows!)

Timothy Geisler (normal human literature major)
Issue: Inferiority complex -- he's discovered the world of the Folk, but he's not magic like them
Edge: Literature Expertise
Connections: Penny (the normal human librarian who likes him just as he is), Guinevere (unattainable badass and bartender of the Round Table pub)

    \n It sounds like everything's rocking pretty well, so take anything I say with a pretty big grain of salt.\n
    all the possibilities of what could happen are so equally awesome that nobody can invest too deeply in any one outcome: it's just as interesting to lose as to win.
    \nThe standard advice about that is, when in doubt, stand up for the best interests of your own character, because someone should.

    As for the kidnapping... I think this sort of situation was handled fairly well in Lost, when the Hot Chick and the Charming Scoundrel were kidnapped. Here's some of the things they did:

    * Kept them in separate cages, but within sight and shouting distance of each other.

    * Worked on them individually. When Bo shows up to feed Gray, and Gray puts the old suave moves on her, how does Red feel about that? Can Red bring herself to make some moves on Brick? If Gray talks Red into making a move on Brick, and then Gray has to watch the result, how does he feel about it afterwards? Lots of potential for dramatic reversals here.

    I'd be working Red's Grandmother connection like a redheaded stepchild. She's all too happy to help Regina get ahold of Gray -- she hates that guy more than anyone, and for some very good reason. Conflict between Red and Grandma over Gray will make everything better, I think.

    As a general recommendation, I think finding conflict with this sort of thing is about finding things that the characters and players really care about, and then placing those things in harm's way.


    Cheers,
    Roger

    \nThe issue of Mr. Bridges is a little problematic to me, because it's about external forces rather than internal. So his criminal past is catching up with him. Okay. What does it mean for him? Is he just trying to forget his past and move on? Why? Is he trying to redeem himself? How? Why?
    \n
    \nAll his Edges and Connections, and Nemesis, are connected to that past. Is there anyone or anything he cares for that could be put at risk by his past? Suppose this person or thing was placed in danger, and then the bad guys offered him the help he needs in exchange for his assistance in a job or some other shady favor? Or maybe someone else is killed wounded trying to protect the Troll from the Cartel.
    \n
    \nFor Geisler, he is the one with the doubts. Conflicts should be created so that he believes he can do some good. Match his against overpowering foes, in situations where only him can help, and let him fail or succeed, further advancing his issue in either case.
    \n
    \nBaba Yaga wants to be loved. By whom? Everybody? However, she's also used to using fear as a tactic to get what she wants : old habits die hard. Create conflict that could be solved by being friendly and nice, but that could be solved much faster and easier by being mean and scary! Put people she likes at stake, like the Bear. The Bear's life is at stake, she has to work fast. But will he approve of her resorting to her old habits just to save him? But what if she doesn't and puts his life in jeopardy for the sake of her breaking of old habits, will he forgive her? Could she forgive herself?

    \n Yeah, Adam, it sounds to me like you're trying a bit too hard to work the conflict system in when you don't really need it. Your group seems to have an advantage, in that you're naturally "in tune" with one another's storytelling instincts to begin with. Milk that for everything that it's worth, and save the conflict system for when you have deep, abiding conflict of interest between characters and want the suspense of an uncertain outcome.

    Posted By: Adam CerlingI think we do understand that not every scene needs conflict resolution mechanics. But I think we sometimes want the "suspense of an uncertain outcome" more often, and ironically our very in-tune storytelling instincts interfere with that.

    It occurs to me that maybe I need to be playing these characters off one another more. They have a unity of purpose that makes the range of outcomes in any given scene kind of binary.

    \nThat's what I meant by "deep, abiding conflict of interest". The conflict mechanics simply don't work if the characters don't want mutually incompatible things from one another.

    Like, in your scene with Bridges / Baba / Tim and Regina... I don't see a deep, abiding conflict of interest between any character. I instead see an overwhelming, abiding unity of interest between them. They both have a strong motivation for Red and Gray to go free. Gray isn't there to say anything about whether he wants to keep the item he's carrying or not. So, no conflict. Later, after the rescue, when Regina says, "Hey, pony up this item, we made a deal," and Gray says "Hell, no," it gives the PCs the opportunity to take sides and thus engenders conflict potential.

    Same thing with Red and Gray's prior scene - both of them want to go free, and you said they needed one another to do it, so there's no conflict of interest there. Of course they're going to work together. It'd only be a conflict if one of them *could easily get free if they left the other behind*, or something to that effect, *and* one of them had a strong reason for being tempted to do that. That situation would also have squarely touched on the trust issues between them.
