My mind was, it's fair to say, blown when I found out a little about 'story games'/narrative games as it seemed the kind of approach I'd always been looking for many years ago.
\n
\nAnyway, I have a question about 'modern' games. I'm looking to try something as a re-entry into actually playing. Can anyone recommend me a story game that will help me understand and have fun using these new concepts for me? I want a game where character death is NOT an issue. I always thought when I started playing, if I'm trying to emulate Sword and Sorcery fiction, why is there a chance my PC could die? I want a game that is explicit in saying no, your character won't die unless it's crucial to the story. I'm also looking for a game with a system which helps me to set stakes - what I want is for the characters in the game to risk things all the time - to have consequences for their actions, to have to accept complications when they are involved in struggles - but not have to risk the death of the characters - to have other things which make the play interesting.
\n
\nI'm especially looking for a game that will work well with 'heroic' fantasy, sword and sorcery, Robert E Howard-type material. Where do you think would be a good place to start and will ease me in gently? I am quite happy with the idea of ditching my traditional-game methods of playing.
\n

Does everything come from the players - I mean all the initiation of conflict? Does the GM describe a situation and the rolls to decide the outcome only made by the players? I just don't see how this is handled in the Pool where it seems everything comes from the players and the rolls they alone make. This is the type of thing I'm unsure about, and why I was perhaps looking for a game with a structure and systems closer to what I would describe as a traditional game, or shoudl I just say to hell with it and try something very different?
\n
\nI hope this does not sound too clueless and that someone has a suggestion for me.

    \n I'd recommend reading Dogs in the Vineyard to just anybody who comes from a gaming background. Too bad there actually is a (small) chance of a player character dying in DitV (although it usually boils down to the player choosing death over a different outcome). Still a recommended read.

    I suggest Primetime Adventures. Anti-traditional, easy to understand, takes you by hand step by step, plus unlimited support available online since the game is a "classic" already - and makes it especially easy to bring non-gamergeek friends or family into the game. You can even do a Howard-ian sword sorcery serial with it, if you so wish.


    Then, there's this little game which is specifically geared toward old school Swords & Sorcery, with no unwilling character death mechanics:
    On Mighty Thews

    \n Hi Richard, and welcome! I suspect you'll get a lot of wildly different suggestions in this thread, which is all to the good. Pick and choose and check stuff out for yourself.

    Based on what you've written I'd suggest taking a look at The Shadow of Yesterday, which seems to meet most of your checklist. It's an excellent game that is unapologetically traditional in some ways, and a little radical in others. You can set death as the possible outcome of a conflict, but it is always an explicit and meaningful choice. It's very human, if that makes sense, so playing truly superheroic dudes isn't its forte. It's highly player-driven and very, very fun.

    \n I second Primetime Adventures. I jokingly call it "Narrative GURPS", because using this one game you can pretty much create a game of any feel, any genre, as long as it can be viewed through the lens of a TV show.

    PTA will pretty much lay down what this crazy hippiness is all about, and it's a kickass game to boot. I've run or played in something like 3 full seasons/campaigns, a number of mini-campaigns, and a bunch of one-shots, and it's *very* rare that a session is dull when everyone is invested. It's also easy to find or make players for this game.

    From there, you can decide if you want to descend deeper into the patchouli-oil-and-dreadlock abyss of games like My Life with Master, or stick with games that bridge the two worlds like Dust Devils.

    \n Hey Richard, welcome to story-games, and welcome to story games.

    If I understand this correctly, you're looking for: Sword and Sorcery, stake setting (which, to be clear, you understand is different from simply conflict resolution, yeh?), and no chance for meaningless death.

    Survey says: The Shadow of Yesterday. It has all the features you requested, plus, as a bonus, you can read it online for free, just follow my link to the wiki (you can also, if you want, read it and pay for it).

    Primetime Adventures can also do the same thing for you. However, it would be Sword and Sorcery as seen through the lens of the telly. The stories will be structured like what you would expect from an ensemble cast serial program.

    Some of the other games suggested come close. In a Wicked Age has Sword and Sorcery, but it has no stake setting (and will break if you try to use it that way) and probably won't feature meaningless death. Nobody mentioned it, but Sorcerer and Sword will feature the same qualities as In a Wicked Age. And Dogs in the Vineyard will feature stakes setting, no meaningless death, but isn't Sword and Sorcery.

    Now here's the thing, The Pool will also do what you want (minus the stakes setting), so do you want answers to your questions regarding it? Because, I think, if you're playing these games with friends of yours, things will go wrong initially. I can almost guarantee it (not just with The Pool, mind, but with any of these games). Just like things went wrong the first time you played Basketball, the first time you drove, the first time you did anything. But if all of you are walking in saying, "hey, this will not be perfect the first time we play, but we'll get better over time," then I don't see why a game like The Pool shouldn't work for you.

    \n It kinda depends on what you want from the game. Different suggestions:
    \n1) HQ - This supports good Nar style and its hard to die if you save your Hero Points for those critical situations
    \n2) SoY - I love this game. It's hard to die, but it can happen.
    \n3) PTA - PTA is a great game, because play is about the story, and not about whether you can get a critical hit.
    \n4) Donjon - In this game, players have a 1 in 6 chance of adding a secret door to the dungeon, not a 1 in 6 chance of finding one if one exists. I dunno the lethality level though...
    \n5) Otherkind - This has an interesting way of dividing control over outcome and narrative authority.
    \n6) Trollbabe - Any failed roll can be re-rolled, but you have to risk something you love.ii So, you can't die, unless its not worth what you would lose if you re-rolled and failed...
    \n As you can see, what you asked for can be re-interpretted in different ways depending on what you really want.

    \nOtherkind was mentioned above and has stakes setting and no death except if a player chooses so. It actually fits the description of what you desire pretty well: players outline what they want to do and establish some negative outcomes that could happen (whether they succeed or not). Dice are rolled, then assigned to the different outcomes: putting a high die in the general success category means achieving your goal. Or you could put it towards avoiding one of the negative consequences. But you have to choose which is more valuable to you and your character (and what's a more interesting story and such).

    It's also a heroic fantasy game, but much less swords and sorcery and more romantic fantasy (or some other fantsy subgenre). But I think it could be adapted easily enough.

    Unfortunately, the original PDF seems to have disappeared from the net, but this this outline of the system has the stuff you'd want anyway. A lot of what's missing from there is the stuff that'd detract from S&S anyway.


    The other suggestions people have given are good, too, though.

    \nHey, I was hoping someone else would pick that up, because I'm no expert on The Pool, but, uh, nobody did.

    The only rollers are the non-GM players. The GM never rolls. However, should any player (GM or non-GM) feel that a conflict is under way, or would like to initiate a conflict, they can call for a roll. In your example with the NPC jumping out and attacking the PCs, that would be an instance of the GM calling for a conflict roll.

    However, as you say, these are the types of things you are concerned about, indicating to me that you have quite a few other, unspoken, concerns regarding this game. My recommendation is, maybe read some actual play threads at The Forge about The Pool. They'll most likely give you a good idea of how to make the game sing, and hopefully also, indirectly, address a lot of your concerns. I would start here: [The Pool], and I'd draw your attention to Ron Edwards' posts as well as his links to other threads. I find he's particularly good at explaining how he plays The Pool. That being said, there's probably quite a bit of jargon being thrown around there, I'm not sure if it would be too much in order to understand the threads...\n
    Posted By: PandelumeI would be really grateful to have my questions on The Pool answered.     \nEh, I think of it like most roleplaying games. The non-GMs have a character that they think of as theirs. They would use the Monologue of Victory to address the issue raised in their broadly stated intentions made before rolling. They may do this by introducing elements to the narrative, or they may do this by describing the results of the actions of their character. But in the end, I expect their focus to be on their character and what their intentions were for that roll. So, like, I wouldn't expect them to make themselves fail to obtain their intentions in a Monologue of Victory (nor would I expect intentions stated before rolling to be a failure for their character).

    When I think of players bidding for the right to tell their story as opposed to controlling a character I think more of games like Universalis.
