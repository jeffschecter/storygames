    \n But to answer your question: as far as I know, I'm the only person who's attempted an actual, necessary-and-sufficient-conditions definition of the term "story game." Like to hear it, here it go:

    A story game is a game in which players can directly make things up as part of an imagined story, and things the players make up can in turn influence the interactions with the game rules.

    Obviously that's really just a reading of Brand's maxim that an RPG is a game where the fiction is part of the rules. I've tried to fold in a little of Vincent's old semi-definition, specifically the bit where players can interact directly with fiction without going through rules. That part seems important.

    D&D4 meets this definition. Of course, it is also a roleplaying game, which as a category is a subset of story games. Exactly how to characterize that subset is a much harder question, and maybe not one for this thread.

    \n As other have already said, "story game" is so broad a term to be really all-inclusive. It's not so much about what a game is, as about the direction from where you look a it: you call a game "storygame" when you are interested in using it to create stories, and when you want to discuss it in this forum... :-)

    I think that the real question is another: is D&D 4th edition coherent?

    Because all I hear about are techniques. Techniques that somebody says are similar to techniques used or created in "dirty hippy games". But these techniques weren't so distinctive to these games in the first places, many if not all (conflict resolution, fortune in the middle, narration sharing, etc.) were used in "mainstream" game before, and the innovation was in the way they were refined and combined into a coherent whole.

    Is D&D4 a coherent whole?

    [edit: I am derailing a little the thread here, because the coherence of a game has nothing to do with it being a story-game. Old D&D was a story game. Hero System too.]

I LIKE it, but it's not a story... well, not the kind of story I'm looking for in a game, anyway.

    \nSo, is [4e] a story game? No. Not the kind I was looking for, anyway. Is it a bad game or one I wouldn't play? Are you crazy? Of course I'd play, it's D&D, man!
    \nBut I come to this site looking for something else... A way to apply the rules of narrative consequence to a mutually-satisfying game.
    \n(I'm not looking for a model of reality. I'm not looking for an exciting system of GM-empowering encounters. It's cool, but it's not why I'm here.)
    \nI want to find the freedom to tell my stories while giving my fellow players an equal chance to tell their own, mixing it all together into something greater.
    \nI want to find the rules that make a great story available to support and encourage newcomers.
    \nI hope I can still find it in the forrest of [4e] arguments.
    \n
    \nThe real question, I suppose, is: Does [4e] belong on this site?
    \nWell, for old time's sake, I can't say I mind it squeezing in.
    \n
    \nBut I'm just gonna post about it on this thread and not worry about the rest of 'em.
