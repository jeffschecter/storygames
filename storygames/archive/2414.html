    Merten and Balbinus got it.

    I wasn't trying to provoke people, build fences or anything like that; I'm kind of surprised people read that into my post. Apparently the word "opposite", for some, has connotations I wasn't aware of. I shouldn't have started talking about subsets (I'm not really interested in the complement of SG's - which would just be a list of all games that aren't SG's); I meant opposite, being well aware that the term would be hard to apply, and thought it might be interesting to discuss what scales/dimensions - and what centre points/origo - different people would use to define "opposition". Just as an intellectual exercise, for fun, and perhaps as inspiration for some experiments in gaming.

    For example, someone might conceivably say: "Well, if we use D&D 3ed as the origo, and draw a line from Story Games through D&D 3ed, at the other end of that line we'd have a lot of games where narration was split up into a thousand discrete statements, all having the same importance and length - since Story Games often use 'one conflict roll pr. scene', and D&D 3ed has several task resolution rolls pr. 'scene'". Or someone else might say: "Let's use children's play as the origo - that's what everyone knows. Compared to children's play, SG's more often have set goals and end states, and tend to focus on conflict between characters. On the opposite side (of children's play), you might find a game with no end state whatsoever, having no conflict at all - perhaps just a conversation, or a game in a static world of no interaction"..

    (The reason I asked is that I've made a lot of games lately, and one of them seems to me to be the opposite of a Story Game. It has hardly any player influence whatsoever; the plot is completely predetermined, to the point that players are reading bits of pre-written text along the way to describe events. The characters are in a confined, dark space, and can talk, but hardly act. This set me thinking: What other forms of not-at-all-Story-games are there?)

    To help Matthijs out a bit heres my two cents (and they are mine not Matthijs').

    Over the past year the norwegian game-designer scene (as represented on rollespill.net) has been growing in leaps and bounds. And as time has passed there has developed a divide in play-philosophies. And spearheaded by Matthijs there is a group of game-designers who have developed a style of play that does not fit with the story-games paradigm (as far as one can say that there is a paradigm). This has recently been dubbed the Holmestrandtrend (or Holmestrand school right now cause it sounds cooler) and the games made by this highly induvidualistic group share these common denominators.

    They are very experimental in that they break with traditional RPG structure both in form and in theme. (this could fit a lot of the SGs as well)
    The games try to convey a state of mind and an experience rather than a situation or a dramatic story.
    These games are invariably rules light, yet the few rules they have are very strong and serve a spesific purpose.
    Narration rights are very often distributed through dice rolls, game-specific narration cards or left totally in the hands of a narrator or narrator function.
    These games very often move the rules up from the table or away from the SIS and into the social sphere of the players playing the games.
    On very specific trend is that they have little or no explicit use of resources/currency and there is a heavy reliance on freeform techniques.

    This form of games takes more inspiration from the swedish Jeepform and the danish con-scenario models than it gathers here or ove at the forge. LARP and LARP theory also affects this outlook. The design philosphy seems to be one more of intuition than one of structured design, but for this I have just conversations with Matthijs to back me up on.

    As of late both Mathhijs and me have wondered whether this may be a cultural phenomon between two very diametrical outlooks, me being the more american free-marked kind of guy, with him being the norwegian social-democratic hippy feelgood person. And I would venture a guess that this might be what matthijs is fishing for here. Without putting you all in any boxes you do not want to be in, or chasing you back into the trenches. Because if there is such a divide, the big next question is how can we exploit the friction in this to create even greater games.

    But here are a few things I believe right now.

    1. There are differences between U.S. culture and Scandinavian culture.
    2. However, you'll always be able to find USAns who are more in tune with the Scandinavian mindset, and Scandinavians who think more like USAns.
    3. Some of our cultural differences are mirrored in the assumptions behind the games we create & play.
    4. It's impossible to perfectly define the differences. Any description will be in general terms, and it will be very easy to find counter-examples. That doesn't mean the differences don't exist, or that it can't be fruitful to discuss them.
    5. Some games are such extreme examples of their respective cultures that they seem almost pointless for players from another culture. Others are very easy to transfer from one culture to another.

    Part of it is that "Story Games" themselves tend to break down into several overlapping categories, and there's quite a bit of drift in the use of the term, even among the indie games community that has started using it. Generally it seems to include:

    -- more focused (and often shorter) versions of traditional roleplaying
    -- more "Nar" versions of traditional roleplaying
    -- more freeformy games (the Munchausen/Once Upon a Time tradition)
    -- games with different kinds of authority distributions (variations on the GM-player thing)
    -- games with non-traditional subject matter (copier repair men! a single conversation!)
    -- games that draw heavily from non-roleplaying traditions (board games, Indian dance)
    -- etc.

    I mean, after all, Burning Empires and Baron Munchausen may both be "Story Games", but they're not really part opf the same design tradition, except in a very, very general post-roleplaying sense (they both were written by authors who hail from the roleplaying tradition).

    So, yeah, most Nordic stuff would probably be considered "Story Games" by this community, though the indie folks tend to come from less of a larp background, so they're haven't been any major indie games larps that I know of, though maybe Prawn might count. But, it seems like the opposite isn't true: "Story Games" wouldn't necessarily be recognized as kindred games in Nordic countries.

      (Kaare, I found the topic of culture of religion vs. playing Dogs a very intresting one. I'm not sure if I have anything intresting to add in regards with this topic, but something about playing stereotypes is certainly clicking at the back of my brain)
      Posted By: MatthijsWrt playing Capes wrong: Capes is one of several games (along with the Norwegian hippie-style narration game "Inntil vi synker") that, to me, is an example of elements that differ between our cultures. The strong emphasis on conflict just to have your say, the use of competitive resource management which is meant to make the game fun for everyone - these were very alien to me when I first tried it, and still feel really weird. The game just makes me want to ditch all the rules and sit down and tell a story together. If you don't really want conflict, it's a pointless game. Much in the same way that, if you play with conflict and tactical resources and power battles, "Inntil vi synker" will be torn apart like a thin veil of gauze in a knife fight.
      I'm not sure sure if I can tie this to the the competitive US culture - I just don't know it well enough to even assume. I do know that we have big problems with playing a "Story Games" because their approach just is too different from what we're used to ("we" as in my play group in the Census thread, which could be extented to a large group of 25+ years old Finnish roleplayers/live-roleplayers). I'll try to illustrate with some more or top-of-my-hat "opposites":

      Playing a Game vs. Interacting in a virtual world
      Empowering Players vs. Forgetting about players, empowering characters
      Seeking to build a Story together vs. Not caring about the story, just interacting in a virtual world
      Playing collaboratively with a lot of player-player communication vs. Not playing collaboratively, minimal player-player communication
      Using Rules to facilitate player-player communication and to work as safety net vs. Relying only on the social contract only
      Dynamic setting based on player input vs. Static setting, virtual world
      3rd person view vs. 1st person view

      And so on. I'm not saying that these are absolutes and most games probably don't hang in these ends of the spectrum, though I'd instinctly pick out the Game vs. Character Immersion -division here, regarding to our little microculture. I'm wondering what's the take on that in Norway?

      I think all the "opposites" in Character Immersion side can be rather easily traced to LARPs (and some trends that existed before them) around here, but commonly apply to tabletop playing as well (moreso to players with LARPing experience) - and one could take steps back to early 90's and see how things have come to this point from the humble days of Red Box D&D. This change/evolution is what I'd call culture, in microscale. I'm not sure it can be tied up to cultural differences between, say, Scandinavia and US, though the topic is very intresting.
