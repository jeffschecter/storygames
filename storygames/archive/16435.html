    \n Lacuna has been called the GM's Rorschach test. Everyone runs it differently due to some deliberate incompleteness in the setting.

    The way I run it...

    I like to start the Mystery Agents in a drab waiting room with stale magazines, decaf coffee, and something quirky like the elevator music version of Back in Black coming from the speakers. I take them through a breifing with a Green level agent that gives them the details of the patient, their crime, and possible hooks into their thought processes or environment. Next is the trip to the operating theater where they meet their control agent and conduct the dive. From there they get a little psychobabble and go under, the team leader (usually chosen at random) goes to green level and gets a tropical island and a surface impression of the patient and then heads to blue level (usually via a dream-like elevator) to act as a beacon for the rest of the team to lock in on (if they make their rolls.)

    Here I throw down the basic look and feel of blue city, I always have a map in my head and it always changes on each dive. Rorschach inkblots make for good maps. I've also used the human brain as a road map. The city is mallable, but the players know that they need to get into the mind (literally) of the patient. If I'm industrious, I'll have pregenerated events and locations where the patient might be. I'll also have a custom static table for 'random events' has the players interact with blue city. I also have some idea of what the hostile personality will look like when it manifests. After all, that's why the agents are here.

    How it plays out is left to the players, some check out from doing to much and scrub the mission, some nail the HP easily. I've had players play it cool like psycho-dragnet and others go full Scott Pilgrim. I generally give the HP some dreams powers that I foreshadow in the mission briefing or as the players navigate through blue city.

    I hope that helps. Players can burn through their heart rate slowly or like jack rabbits and you have to pace yourself as a GM. 'Winging it' really is the best advice since (to me) it is a very sand box-y game. In general, I try to bring the weird and slightly unnervy feel of a dream that is out of control.

    \n My games have gone better when I've made the Blue City very much like a normal city and a normal place, and let the Static do the work of introducing weird, surreal, and frightening things. The snag I ran into early on was going so balls out with the surreal and strange that the players felt totally adrift and couldn't connect to the game. They sort of threw their hands up.

    I like to come prepared with some ideas about the HP, and I come up with the different Static levels and some minor details about what will be introduced.

    \n Spoilerth.

    The first time I played was the best. We were dropped in with no preamble or memory of what we were doing. We looked down at ourselves and realized we were wearing matching suits with little "?" pins. Also we were in a car in the middle of an outdoor cafe. No one looked hurt.

    Truly, you only get one shot at the players having a total lack of knowledge. Starting in media res with no explanation makes the players feel the same shock and strangeness that their characters feel. Also, you get to start right away, which works so well in a one-shot.

    \n If none of the players have played before I like to offer them "The Full Lacuna Experience" (like what I ran for Mathalus) in which everything is removed from the table but their agent sheets (the wonderful bubble-test ones), #2 pencils, 5 dice each (3 white, 1 red, 1 black), and the stack of Japanese 1-yen coins I use to track Static. I have most of the characters pregenned, players choose their skills and whether to use their own age or the provided age.

    I start In Media Res, and let them figure it out as they go along. I've started them in Hotel Rooms, or together in a car in the middle of a sidewalk cafe, or in coffins on a conveyer belt into a crematorium furnace. I typically use a page of prep listing Static sources and events, a location list, and a few reminders for myself. So far we've never gotten to The Girl or the Spider Men, although they're on my static list.

    Others have had great success with lots more prep: briefing files, photos, handouts. I admire that, but have done fine without it. It is indeed a GM Rorschach Test.

    \n Lacuna is one of my favorite games. The advice in this thread which is in the list of links Carl posted, is good. I especially recommend a soundtrack.

    My personal advice is to wing most of the scenario, write a lot of static events (or don't write a defined list- I drew from a crazy handout I wrote and had an event at every single static increase, but some people like it a little less crazy than that). I also found that adding static gaining conditions like "a player goes on an out of character tangent" serves as a good way to keep the game frantically paced.

    Also, if you want to look them over, here are handouts I made for my campaign, and here are my static notes, which some have called "completely insane, but I think that's just the right mindset to be in for a game of Lacuna! (They were made by clicking random article on wikipedia a bunch and writing down interesting words. I then used those as inspiration for static events.

    \n In the past I've fallen between Carl and others. If it's a con I prefer to show up in full agent get-up and basically walk them through an agent orientation as they fill out the sheets - this is all about setting up an authoritarian control. Cut out side conversations, ask folks to remove all unapproved materials from the briefing table, etc (see http://www.story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=1817 for my inspiration on this point).

    From there I drop them into a car crash and force them to act when a policeman arrives and they have no documentation. There will be a folder (which I've also prepped) with a mission briefing on the dashboard, or in a trash can, or whatever. It's basic, straightforward - if you want you can drop hints that the target isn't actually a serial rapist or whatever, but it's fine to just let their trust in authority run wild. The first time I ran it they assumed the target was a major drug dealer just because I said he was 'intoxicated' on the mission briefing.

    Then there's a break, a bubble sheet, and an actual break for both of us.

    Then you drop them in and shit goes wrong. Control is high as fuck. They land in different places. They're targeted by a teenage rebellion. Their target isn't actually a criminal. And so on. The game is seeing where they go with it.

    More details on my write-up here: http://story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=15201&page=1#Item_5
