\n This type of post has probably been done here before, but I'm a greenhorn so I'll get one going again.

I came to gaming fairly late. I grew up in a Christian Conservative household in the 80s and 90s so I never even saw a 20-sided die in person till I was in college. I remember spending a large amount of time studying the Advanced D&D ads in my comics though, the Ravenloft ad with Dracula stands out in my mind as a particularly intriguing image. I also remember seeing a GURPs ad and not really understanding what it was talking about. I played Magic in college a handful of times but it never hooked me, I loved looking at the cards and the flavor text, but the game itself wasn't my thing. In 2008, I was 3 years out of undergrad and getting ready for my Master's when I decided to pick up D&D 4e. I'd recently suspended a WoW account because I got bored with it. I find once I figure out the mechanic working underneath a video game or the pattern of progression my attention wanders. With 4e I delved in head first, DM-ing before ever playing it from the other side. I was the only person in my group of friends who was brave enough to take on that role. I screwed up a lot those first few sessions, but I just kept reading up and working on putting together little adventure exercises. I played about two campaigns and by the end of the second one, I was exhausted. I had graduated with my Master's at that point and was now teaching 3rd grade in a public school. I found planning as a DM for 4e was the equivalent of writing lesson plans for my class, and when it was time to relax on the weekends the last thing I wanted to do was write lesson plans for fun.

It was around December of last year that I discovered Microscope and then Fiasco and it all changed from there. I'm a big fan of stand up comedy and improv (Mr. Show and Upright Citizens Brigade in particular) and I saw a lot of improv work present in these game. I fell in love with the idea of GM-less or GM-ful. Finally, I could play for once dammit! More recently, I've come back around to the idea of a GM (through reading Apocalypse content among other things). I also enjoy discovering material outside the fantasy genre. I'm currently running a Primetime Adventures campaign heavily influenced by Twin Peaks and Reddit's NoSleep community. I bought Itras By the other day and can't wait to delve into it. Game Poems are another weird little treat for me. I don't know if I would ever go back to D&D at this point, at least not as a DM.

So that's how I came to be here. Would love to hear from others.

    \n I was an enthusiastic protogamer long before I actually owned any games or knew anyone who played. I spent hours sitting on the floor in Walden Books at the Southwest Plaza Mall reading Gamma World or the Warhammer Fantasy Roleplay career compendium while I waited for my mom to finish shopping. When I was a little older and we weren't playing Phantasy Star on the Sega Master System, my friend Doug and I would frequently reinact scenes from the Rankin and Bass version of the Hobbit or the Ralph Bakshi movie Wizards. Unfortunately, Doug's family moved, and I didn't know any other kids who shared my interests. I completed the aptly titled Lonewolf game books several times by myself.

    In middleschool, I saw some kids playing Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles, and I was blown away by the weapon drawings and the idea that you could play an animal OTHER THAN A TURTLE!? Amazing. At some point in 8th grade, I saved up and bought Champions; I memorized the ideas of OCV, DCV, NND, RKA, ED, PD, Variable Point Power Pools, and a stunning amount of other minutiae; and, I made about a million characters that I never played.

    Toward the end of my Freshman year in highschool, I joined the games club and discovered there were others like me. (Including my friend Eric, who, with only vague knowledge of published roleplaying games, created a hysterical homebrew with his brother. They came up with my favorite spell ever . . . hair to glass.)

    \n When I was 10, my uncle gave me a copy of Star Wars Adventure Journal #4 as a Christmas gift. I had never heard of role playing before and the book wasn't a rule book. It was a supplement with articles, new items, short stories, and all sorts of stuff related to the game that was not the game itself. There was even a choose-your-own-adventure style story you could roll dice to. I kept my ear to the ground for more about this game but never came across the core rules in person. I eventually bought a copy on eBay a few years ago. D&D 3.0 came out in '99 and I bought a copy of the core books.

    My first game was in 2000, the parents of a friend of mine were gamers in college. For his birthday, they brought their college DM into town for a big family game of D&D. Since it was his birthday, he invited me over to play with them. That was an AD&D game. Later than year I started playing in a regular 3rd Ed. D&D campaign with people from another town. I've been gaming ever since. Just a few months ago I finally got a chance to play the old WEG Star Wars game for the first time, 18 years after I got that copy of the Star Wars Adventure Journal.

    \n FIRST "RPG" game? Playing Dungeon on a terminal attached to the mainframe at my dad's employer in, oh, 1977. He'd stick me in front of it when he had to go into the office on Saturdays.

At a break, my dad asked them about it and was told they were playing "Greyhawk"--a minis games, but "it's cool! Your guys get better if they live!" (OD&D; Brown-book D&D).

    Dad ending up meeting folks to play at the shop a few times, then got me Blue Book D&D some time later (1978/79?) because he was told "it's a lot simpler for kids." Heh.

    By 1980/81, I had the Basic Set and was well on my way to maneuvering for AD&D Player's Handbook and DMG. The rest is history....

    \n I'm another late bloomer. I was vaguely aware of D&D (the red box) back in grade school, but I didn't play it or even understand what kind of game it was, and no one else seemed interested, so it didn't really mean anything to me. Mostly I spent my time with my Atari and my computer games.

    In high school, the guys I hung out with played AD&D2e and invited me to join them, and I spent a good chunk of time doing that. I liked the hanging out with friends, I liked learning the system (something that we all did, both to be more effective and to be able to hack it into doing things it was not meant to do). I wasn't a huge fan of the setting or the kind of game it was or the system, and so it was something that I did, not something I loved.

    Then in college, I got to play other games for the first time, and Call of Cthulhu blew my friggin' mind with how much fun it was. And from there, WEG's first edition of Star Wars, Cyberpunk 2020, and eventually the WoD games. Playing a wider variety of games = better gaming. Plus, USENET was a big deal back then, so now I was reading/participating in huge discussions about gaming with people from all over the world, and that had a huge effect on me, too.

    \n In the summer of 1976 I was 12 and was reading Savage Sword of Conan, and saw an ad for the Dungeon Hobby Shop. I sent off for a catalog, and my younger brother and I started ordering miniatures (just because we thought they were cool). A one page flier came with one of our orders for a game called Empire of the Petal Throne. It sounded really awesome, with weird aliens, super technology/magic, and a cool looking map. I bugged my parents to buy it for me (it was $27.50, which I looked up using an inflation calculator last year to find was the 1976 equivalent of about $100) until they finally relented and got it for me for Christmas that year. I spent all Christmas day devouring the book, loving the artwork and the weird writing, and trying to figure out how you played it.

    When I got back to school after Christmas break, I got some friends together to play. We really had no idea of what to do, and made tons of mistakes, but had lots of fun. I went to my first convention that spring (1977), Gencon South in Jacksonville, Florida, and my friends and I were shocked to find that EPT wasn't the only RPG out there, and that lots of people played this game called D&D which we hadn't really heard of.

    John

    \n Gaming in general started with the Commodore 64 during my kindergarten years. My father had a healthy side-business smuggling C64s into Hungary from Austria and selling them to pawnshops. Those beasts were just too powerful for us socialists to own, so it was put on the CoCom list of military equipment not sold by the Western bloc to non-allied states. Thus the C64 was in high demand on the Hungarian grey market. Anyway, my dad thought he'd invest some of the money he got from smuggling the computers through the border to actually get one for the family. From that time, my older brother and I were glued in front of the television for many hours playing games we didn't understand a single word of.

    I left early for high school (I attended a 6 year long high school). One of the last things I remember of primary school is the birthday party of one of my buddies. He tried to play something called MAGUS with us which I later learned was the most successful Hungarian RPG, kind of a darker D&D clone. The party was big and somehow I ended up playing some completely different game during that afternoon, so I left primary school without ever learning what a pen-and-paper RPG is and no chance to get hooked on it for another 5 years.

    During high school some of my friends were roleplayers. We played some Star Wars d6 very briefly in the early years but it didn't catch on because playing in the school was forbidden and we didn't have another place to get together in the afternoons. I forgot about it quite quickly and the roleplayers returned to playing in separate groups. While I was already a gamer, roleplaying was still the hack-and-slash action of Nox in my mind until the summer of 2001. I was pretty much a sci-fi geek by that time and had influential exposure to fantasy, although still not understanding much of the English dialogue in CRPGs and having no literary experience with the genre. My friends were fans of The Lord of the Rings though and decided that the forthcoming movie adaptation of this classic is worth to be celebrated with a MERP campaign. We started playing MERP quite regularly. After high school was over in 2002, we still kept coming together playing MERP, Star Wars, Oriental Adventures and any other RPG we could get our hands on. My brother and I also started to play D&D3 with college buddies who we thought might find roleplaying interesting which also concluded in friendships still intact.

    So in the end it was Peter Jackson deciding to adopt Tolkien's genre-defining epic that got me and by proxy another half dozen of my friends to become roleplayers.

    As for how I got here: we entertained the idea of developing our own RPG about mutants running from anti-mutant-surveillance agents for a while and somehow I ended up reading the Forge a lot (no idea how I got there). Story Games was mentioned there around the time of birth of the site and I joined because the threads seemed more interesting than Forge topics.

    \n I've been playing video games all my life, pretty much - some of my earliest memories include a PlayStation. And I'm a nerd, so I've always been kind of aware that pencil-and-paper RPGs were a thing. At a con a couple of years back, I picked up a copy of the Dungeons and Dragons red box, looking maybe to run a game but also kind of just so I could say I'd read D&D, you know?

    So I read it, and I found it a bit... not what I wanted. I'm not really a fan of high fantasy at the best of times, to be honest, and something about D&D left me cold. Can't quite explain it.

    But it's really all John Rogers' fault. Rogers is one of my favourite writers - he's head writer on Leverage, and he's got a really great blog about writing. Just as I was getting into Twitter, he tweeted that he'd just backed a Kickstarter for something called Our Last Best Hope.

    I went on over, checked it out. It was like an RPG but not like an RPG in all the ways that D&D made my brain hurt, and Kickstarter is pretty addictive, and it turned out I could get a copy of the book pretty cheap...

    Well, if that hadn't hooked me, the next bit would. I emailed Mark, who wrote OLBH, suggesting that the system would be pretty cool for running a superhero game - the structure of The Avengers is pretty close to a disaster movie. And then he asked me if I wanted to write it as a supplement for the companion book, so I went kicking around the Internet to find out a little more about this whole story games thing... And I've basically been here ever since.

    \n I started with wargames, not sure when I first gone one (Tactics II), I think I was about 8. In junior high, I looked at D&D on the shelf, and went with Tractics instead.

    Then in fall of 1977 my best friend had a weekend birthday party. Friday evening he opened presents. One of his presents was the Holmes Basic D&D boxed set. I decided to watch that evening, but started reading the rules while my friend DMed. I stayed up all night reading the rules.

    Saturday morning I invited everyone to roll up characters. Oh, and I was already hacking rules (*). It didn't make sense to me that weapons all did the same damage. Having no basis, I suggested that damage be based on the cost of the weapon (to which point, someone asked if they could use a warship - being the most expensive item on the price list). I forgot what my ruling in the end was on weapon damage.

    The players launched into the dungeons of Geomorph populated with monsters and treasure from the realms of Monster and Treasure Assortment (in 1977, the boxed set came with a set of Dungeon Geomorphs and Monsters and Treasure Assortment not a module). We continued playing all day, sitting in the back of my friend's mom's station wagon on the way to and from Battleship Cove (which was the intended feature of the weekend, we did enjoy it, but D&D definitely took over the weekend). In the end, I think we ran through several sets of PCs after they attained 4th level and ran off the end of the rules.

    Of course I was hooked.

    My gaming really kicked off when I was invited to go to one of MITSGS's conventions and the friend who invited me had lined up 16 players before I finished registering and making my way to a table. Poor little shy Frank... I managed, I managed so well a couple of the older guys complimented me and suggested I should start coming to MITSGS on Saturdays, which I proceeded to do.

    Frank

    p.s.

    (*) I should note I actually had been hacking wargame rules. Not too long after getting Tactics II, I remember designing an alternate game board. I also tried my hand at game design with a game depicting the events of April 19 1775 (with the game board literally drawn on a pine board...). I also remember looking at some of the wargame rules before I got Tractics and thinking slope of armor should be a factor. Sometime in 1975 or so, we started getting together with my mom's best friend's family to play games. I ended up doing some wargame playing with their oldest son (which in retrospect is interesting I didn't get introduced earlier to MITSGS considering he was president of the club (not necessarily at that time - and maybe he wasn't in college yet) - the real funny thing was when I was in grad school at RPI and he became a professor and joined my Cold Iron campaign).
