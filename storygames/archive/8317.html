\n
\n The recent genre redesign madness challenge got me thinking about what genres are left without an RPG that covers them. It seems like every kind of story you'd want to tell has a system for it. But that can't be right can it? So, if anyone has a genre or type of story they think can't be covered, they should post it here. If it turns out there is a game in that genre, then you should post about it. Also, generic systems only count if you think they'd work well for the types of story/genre. (For example, wushu's game mechanics could be used for almost anything, but it's especially good at wuxia style action).

I'll open up with one: racing. Other than the old car wars (which wasn't even really racing) nothing seems to cover this. I mean, there're video games, but that's about it. (Actually, I think there was a game called gorkamorka that did this, but I'm not sure it was an RPG).

    \n Also, if it's permissible to step away from just a content perspective, Systems basically shape the social creative space. Playing Sorcerer & Sword vs. In A Wicked Age... is a totally different social and creative experience even though they both do "Swords and Sorcery." That's why those "What's the best game for genre X" discussions are ultimately useless because they can only be answered once we understand what kind of play experience the asker is looking for.

    So, I don't think we're tapped out in terms of what's already been done.

    \n There may be a game for many genres, but that doesn't mean that the best possible game for every genre has been developed. There's always room for a new, better game to be developed. Additionally, genre also doesn't tell you everything about the game you're going to run. There are subgenres and sub-subgenres and varying approaches to each of those subgenres and a huge variety of moods, themes, and stories. In short, even if there is the "best" Steampunk game, there will always be room for more, different Steampunk games.

    So, I suppose my answer is that I do not believe that this is a useful or meaningful question...

    \n I'm not enough of an RPG maven to know which genres are lacking, but I can't pass up a brainstorming thread. So...


    Good satire (Not silly whacky fun or social issues drama.)

    Chamber game, to be heard being played in the background, as an accompaniment to mingling.

    A gamist game about escapism

    Circle of life game for narrativist nature lovers

    A game about a collection of twins with sympathetic connections, one set on Earth, the other set traveling across the galaxy

    A game about a community of artists trying to invent a new kind of art, building a consensus about what makes it special, while also trying to transcend it and one-up each other.

    We joined Our consciousnesses in one body. If We fail to disperse soon, Our other bodies will perish. But We have discovered selfishness has no limit. Every consciousness lost is memories lost, skills lost, traits lost, etc. Every body lost is relationships lost, opportunities lost, resources lost. We want it all.

    A game about real strangers, what they throw away, and why.

    Giant monsters in a war zone, slice of life

    Albino sun worshippers love an eclipse

    Risky roadtrip. For instance, teenagers smuggling heroine in a stolen vehicle with a body in the trunk.

    History of X. A game for marathon sessions, where breaking the record for longest session is both part of the game and proof that it is fun.

    A Scrabbly game of story paths.

    Joke genre. Not about making jokes, but about the sorts of stories that jokes imply. Bars with a constant stream of impossible characters who want free drinks. Husbands and wives who love to make each other miserable. A rabbi, a priest, and a preacher take shelter in a house of ill repute. Etc.

    After death (e.g., What Dreams May Come rather than Ghost)

    Mr. Bean, Earnest, Spongebob, etc. Good-hearted buffoons screw up everything, then fix everything.

    Extraordinary character (Con, Spy, whatever) goes into hiding (w/ yuppies, Amish, whatever)

    A game about superstition, in which superstitious play is enhanced and rewarded.

      \n Now that the contest is over, I can join in these sorts of speculations a bit more.
      \n
      \nEvery time I broach this thread's subject with most gamers, I get the response, "but if you just ignore the rules," or "with really good roleplayers, you don't need the game to...". Of course, if you have to dodge the game to play the story you want, are you really playing the game?
      \n
      \nThe stories I'd love to play, but haven't found a game to do it:
      \n- Learning-curve stories (be it sports, boardgames, racing, or whatever), like Initial D, Hikaru No Go, Capeta, and others. Getting a +1 to the skill really doesn't cut it.
      \n- I used to say friendship stories, like Yotsuba&!, Honey & Clover, etc., though that may have change if I can get a translated version of Yuuyake Kouyake.
      \n
      \nI could make an argument that many genres that have been done over and over by rpgs have not actually grasped what the genre is about, leaving me frustrated. Some entries in the contest created new gaming experiences by moving closer to the genre.
