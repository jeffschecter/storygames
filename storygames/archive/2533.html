\n At this year's Knudepunkt I spoke to a lot of people. All of them were doing interesting stuff. There were the Jeepformers, the people running a school with LARP as a teaching method, the TV people using LARP for a children's program, the Hamlet-inspired Fastaval scenario writer, etc etc.

And it struck me that I'm fortunate enough to understand both those people and the people here (and at the Forge) - but there are a lot of brilliant people here, on these fora, who would probably love to know more of what's going on outside of the English-speaking world. I know John Kim has been to Knutepunkt, and I know Matt Wilson has some Danish ties... but other than that, there's not much communication going on.

Which is a shame. So here, at least, are a few links to relevant stuff in English.

Lifelike, the Knudepunkt book for 2007
Telegram, the blog of Jeepformer Frederik Berg Olsen
The Jeepform blog, not updated very often, but worth a read

    \n Jason,

    I don't LARP.

    So when people talk deeply about LARP I will sometimes read it because I find it fascinating, but I don't get into it as deeply because it isn't my thing.

    Where it crosses over (immersion issues, anyone?) I often pay sharp attention to the Nordic schools and their many good works. But there are also many ways in which what they are doing is a different discipline, and so I don't talk about it a lot around here, or follow it as deeply as our particular sub-community's conversations, because it isn't what I'm doing.

    Now, maybe I should. Maybe I should try some Jeepform. It'd probably be awesome and teach me a lot. I know there is more out there in the Nordic countries and with those crazy Finns that I'm missing because I don't pay as close attention to it... but the truth is that when a lot of the best work is being done on something that isn't my personal focus I often just don't have time or energy to be into it in the same way.

    Also, I think there probably is more silent crossover than we know about. I have been a reader of Polyfem and Interacting Arts for sometime. Mo reads more of them than I do. Neither of us comments much, however, for reasons like those listed above. Similarly Mo recently found out that lots of Nordic type folks read Sin Aesthetics, despite the fact that few (any?) leave comments. So I think there may be some degree of "sure I read that crazy cool stuff, and even use what I can in my field, but I don't talk about it on their community because I'm not in their community" action going down on both sides of the fence.

    Posted By: Brand_Robins
    So when people talk deeply about LARP I will sometimes read it because I find it fascinating, but I don't get into it as deeply because it isn't my thing.
    \nThe problem - and I'm guessing, here - is that the large proportion of the most vocal (at least in national scale) RPG enthusiasts in Nordic countries do LARP and that background has had a very strong influence on tabletop playing as well. The two "communities" of LARPers and tabletop roleplayers overlap in that vocal section of players (though not as much when counting the whole lot of players), whereas in US, I presume, the overlap is much smaller and the most vocal RPG enthusiasts come from more tabletop-oriented background. So whereas we (as in Me, and probably some others) see LARPs and tabletop playing as similar activities done in slightly different medium, people from elsewhere see LARPs and tabletop playing as different activities all-together. I'd think it's hard for us to talk about roleplaying without talking about both LARPs and tabletop at the same time, where as for other folks it's hard to talk about about the together.

    I talked briefly with Ben when he was back here and quite frankly, I felt that we were talking about different things, him trying to map what I was talking about to his experiences and vice versa (what's that "game"-thing, anyway?). I'm not sure if that was mutual, but I'd guess that the unwanted reaction is certainly based on something - like getting The Nordic Immersionist Guy coming to camp nerdly and telling you that guys, you're missing the point of immersive playing and telling you about how LARPs work.

    On the other hand, there's too much discussion on That Nordic Thing as a set of techniques and whatever and too little discussion on how the playing style(s) evolved to what they are and what kind of role the rather tight-knitted playing communities on the LARP side have been playing on that. It's like talking about the tip of the iceberg without covering the stuff that's it's build upon, that now rests beneath the surface. Or that's my take, at least.

    \n There's nothing like actually meeting people face-to-face and talking to them, of course. You get a whole different feel of where they're coming from. And there can be little doubt that most people will talk more to, and about, people they know; I'm much more likely to pimp a friend's good game than an unknown person's good game, for example. This is probably one (of many) factors that keep different groups/gaming cultures from interacting - the people we know show up on our radars as big red dots; other people are little pink smudges.
