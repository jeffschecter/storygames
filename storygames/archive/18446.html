\n What stuff should we watch, read, play?

1.Feel free to respond here in-thread to another link posted.

a. If you're looking to start a conversation, perhaps take your comment and create a new thread with it in the Stuff to Watch category.

b.Otherwise, to prevent too many comments in the thread, number your response (first response: [1]; second response [2]; third response [3]). If you see that you're the second person to post, "Wow, that looks cool!" to a post here, then put a "[2]" at the top of your post. Three posts ([3]) is the limit: The fourth person to post should just take their comment and create a new thread, to carry the discussion over there further.

Why?

Because these threads are great: We can link to little things being talked about on the web in general, on blogs, or other forums that might be noteworthy, without having to dedicate a new thread to each and every one (although that, too, is fine if you want to discuss the item further or "open up a discussion").

Responding to these posts with a comment or two is understandable (other times there might be no comments, just cause the link was self-explanatory: nothing to say, lots of heads nodding in agreement). But we don't want to create a huge discussion in the middle of the thread: Some folks get a lot out of these threads, and don't want to look back to see the "10 latest posts" just to see that it's a heated discussion of something from a previous page.

Cool?

Cool.

    \n There's now a faithful-to-the-book film version of Radio Free Albemuth by Philip K. Dick. The film's producers are kickstarting to finance a limited release of it in theatres.

    As one of the Kickstarter rewards, I've written a version of Left Coast (my game about Californian science-fiction writers).

    Left Coast is a GM-less no-prep game that takes about two hours to play. I'm really happy with its setting creation system, which helps you create an eccentric group of friends and family to make the writer's life difficult. This version of the game has been designed to tell a short story about the author and what happens when weirdness begins invading her life. It's very loosely themed around a Radio Free Albemuth-esque dystopian society.

    Mostly I'm excited about it because I've had a mental block about publishing and finishing games for years now, and this is a huge step forward for me.

    If you're interested, you can find out more about Left Coast and the Radio Free Albemuth kickstarter here: RFA bonus--Left Coast.

    \n It may not be quite "new," but I recently discovered a cooperative card game called Hanabi.
    Each player gets a hand of 5 cards that they cannot look at.
    On your turn, you have three options:
    -give a clue to another player
    -discard a card
    -play a card

    There are tokens that you all share, and they are consumed when you choose to give a clue.
    If you choose to discard, the group gets a token back.
    If you play a card, and that card winds up being an illegal move, you get a strike. Three strikes and the game is over.

    It's scored as a group, so you all want to make the most legal moves possible.

    It makes me wonder if secret information like this is ever used in so bold a fashion in RPGs. I know in Pantheon, we all play not knowing what the scoring mechanism is until the end, and in some games we discover our characters through other players. Are there any other examples?

    (P.S. If you own a copy of the Hanabi and Ikebana edition you aren't using, I am interested.)

    \n [Chuubo's 2]

    Finally started reading the May backer's draft of Chuubo's Marvelous Wish-Granting Engine and wishing I'd done so much sooner. What a fascinating game. It's all the beauty and whimsy and strangeness of Nobilis in a setting that's accessible to anyone who's ever seen a Studio Ghibli movie, but which scales effortlessly all the way up to epic fantasy and high weirdness as long as you retain the hopeful, coming-of-age core. The advancement mechanics alone sell me on the system: they're tied in to virtually everything, Burning Wheel style, and they reinforce the genre like crazy.

    It's a beautiful game, and I hope we see all of it. Dr. Moran called the last supplement, The Glass-Maker's Dragon, the best thing she's ever written, and the people who've played it seem to agree. Granted, $32,000 in 18 days doesn't seem terribly likely, but we can hope.

    Affliction: If I do that samurai move where I end up on the other side of someone with my sword extended, they may fall over dead or unconscious even if there\u2019s no physical way that move could have happened.
