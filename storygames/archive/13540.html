\n I've recently released something I've been working on.

The Hold 'Em Scene Generator

It's basically a quick scene generator that using the conventions of Texas Hold 'Em poker. The three cards of the flop determining the location of the scene, the basic situation unfolding and the intentions of those involved in the scene. The turn card escalates proceedings in an unexpected direction, while the river determines how the success or failure of events in the scene will affect the unfolding storyline.

To cater to a general audience, I've created the first generator to build stories in a medieval genre/setting.

I've sketched some outlines for the following settings: western, sci-fi, psychological horror, modern supernatural romance, and a few others...but I'm wondering what other types of settings might draw people's interest. What genres haven't been done to death? What genres have been done to death and are really in need of a revamp?

    \n I've tried mine out a few times, and it's a direct evolution of a scene generator I used a few times for "on-the-fly" GMing in the late 90s.

    It seems to work reasonably well, but it sometimes has a tendency to produce "WTF" moments. An intense combat scene, suddenly becomes a romantic conflict, a verbal argument in a back alley suddenly becomes swarmed with inquisitors seeking to purge your soul of evil...

    While I like to think of it as a great producer of "story now" (it brings unplanned events into the narrative and forces players and their characters to push against a random universe)...it might be actually an example of "story after" (the events occur and often seem to be non-sequitur, it is only through later analysis that a pattern and story can be identified).

    Basically, the Flop lays three cards on the table, Location (a scene that determines the potential bonuses gained), Situation (the surface events of the scene, and the potential effects of failure in the scene) and Intention (the opponents motives behind the scene and what you'll have to risk to proceed in the scene). On the GM has the three cards, he (or she) may allocate them between the three categories in whatever manner seems to fit the story.

    The Turn applies an Escalation (a new twist in events and a new type of risk that must be also be taken for players hoping to succeed in the scene).

    The River applies a final difficulty for the scene, and determines what type of actions will find it easier to successfully resolve the immediate events. If you draw a low river card you'll have an easier chance of succeeding but the payoff won't be as good...conversely if you draw a high river card, the chance of success gets harder but the payoff is proportionally greater.

    Each scene has a chance of success or failure, with each success adding points to a pool to indicate how far you have proceeded in the story and each failure either stripping points from the pool or raising the bar on the number of points needed to proceed.

    It's actually based on something I was brainstorming for a solo RPG.

    I've tossed in a few ideas for turning the process into a meta-game, allowing players to bid to add their own narrative into scenes, or bid for the best chance of success through the scenes, but at this stage I'm trying to keep it as a fairly generic oracle for use with any gaming system.
