    \n I don't know of many indie publishers / designers in the Netherlands, and/or in the Dutch language.

    There is Queeste (translates surprisingly into something like "Quest" in English, first started in 1979, and apparently still going), http://www.lulu.com/spotlight/queeste and http://queeste.ijlbode.nl/ (in Dutch), though it seems to have moved to be more of a public domain thing nowadays. I vaguely recall a game called "Schimmen en Schaduwen" ("Shades and Shadows") from a group from Belgium.

    All the games I (and people in my gaming circles) play are in English (I'm Dutch).

    In the past (1985) there was a Dutch version of the German game "Das Schwarze Auge" (these days also available in English as "The Dark Eye") while in 1988 a Dutch translation of the red-box Basic D&D game was produced. Neither of those had a very long life (and I already played AD&D (1st edition) as well as other games like Traveller and RuneQuest when those translations appeared on the market).

    While there are a number of original Dutch boardgames, as far as I know there are no only those two original Dutch tabletop roleplaying games I mentioned above, and nothing more recent than those (apart from some homebrew stuff). (edited because of contradictions).

    The Dark Eye started as a D&D clone in the 80s, but they kept developing the world over almost 30 years. It took a little while to introduces the background and get it all started but now it is one of the deepest backgrounds around. Comparable maybe to Glorantha or Song of Ice and Fire. Many german players who are into indie games really dislike it, saying it goes into too much detail. That aside it really is impressive as a body of work. There is a bi monthly "newspaper"/magazine with news from the world of Aventuria. There are big campaigns detailing world changing events. If you are willing to get into it and maybe approach it a bit like a series of fantasy stories or a daily soap that makes it rather enjoyable. You start to care for some of the factions in the world and all that. They are getting better with providing room for the players to change the world too.
    Still when you are getting into the Dark Eye you are buying the metaplot and the background as well, that is the defining aspect. A valuable skill is to know what to use and what to leave out for your own campaign, where to put the emphasis and what details to use.
    The setting began rather kitchen sink like, a bit like Pathfinder's, where you can travel into "genres". Aventuria has genre regions as well and you can usually play characters from there by clichee and get the right idea. The viking like guys are like vikings and the renaissance ideas in the civilised region aren't too different from historical ones. Dwarfs, Orcs and Elves all work like you imagine them by clichee. But there always is a deeper layer of background, with different Orc and Dwarf cultures and all that. Points where the clichee is broken. But you only need to go there when it touches your campaign.

    Here are the overview maps http://www.dasschwarzeauge.de/fileadmin/downloads/offiziell/Das%20Schwarze%20Auge%20Kartenpaket.zip of Aventuria. Most cities do have maps too and for all the regions and most places there is description in sourcebooks.

    \n What I always found fascinating about "Das Schwarze Auge" is that it popularised a type of play that is probably most accurately described as "coasting" or escapism in its most blatant form. A kind of "renaissance fair at the gaming table"-style of roleplaying. It has established the notion of the GM as a storytelling guide in its truest sense, taking the players along for a ride and presenting this "magical realm of Aventurien" to them with its ongoing storylines and decade-long history.

    This in turn led to different expectations and desires when it came to playing indie or non-traditional RPGs among German gamers. On the one hand, you have "traditional" German gamers who care most about new and different settings and backgrounds to delve into and experience through a storytelling guide. On the other hand, you have people who like to tinker with rules and game concepts, but don't have this strong motivation to focus on narratives and having their adventures be *about* something the way many English-speaking indie gamers seem to.

    And while I think the main reason why there are relatively few indie games translated into German, is purely economic; I also think that gamers with the means to get a game translated and published, don't really see much overlap between German gaming sensibilities and most indie games.
