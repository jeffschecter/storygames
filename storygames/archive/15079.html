I\u2019m only interested in the illustration.

Name me a game that can do this. I want to play Willy Loman\u2019s underprivileged, worn-out cousin. I want my character to wear these clothes, especially the hat. I want him to be mocked by his over-aggressive kid while chugging along in his outdated automobile. I want that visage of lower struggling middle-class despair, and I want to feel the pain.

Can this be done? I think this may be a horribly stupid idea. But this face will haunt me in my dreams tonight (that, and the hat).

    \n I'm not sure if Joe was serious or not in recommending the Rustbelt (he was either being serious and funny or just funny; it's hard to tell with Joe sometimes) but there's some neat things about the Rustbelt.

    First, it's really really easy to get it out of the grim, gritty, postapocalypso thing. The book even tells you how to do it.

    Second, although it's written that violence (and most especially the threat of it) is on the table at all times, the game still works when no violence or threats of violence happen. There are three "damage pools" -- Blood, Sweat, and Tears -- and the Tears rules (especially how they intersect with the Psyche rules) are interesting enough on their own that Blood (and the violence associated with it) need never come into play for the game (and story it generates) to be engaging. If violence is off the table (or, more appropriately for the game's structure, marginalized because we are not playing violent characters, and not playing in a violent setting), conflicts will be social. Getting into a lot of social conflicts in the Rustbelt system means having to cave a lot, or getting so overwrought that you're effectively incapacitated, or venting your frustrations in inappropriate, embarassing, and/or vulnerabilizing ways, not to mention plenty of regrets about letting people down, lying to them, making an ass of yourself, etc.. Which leads to plenty of self-justification on the parts of the characters, and/or engaging in various vices to cope, both of which lead to more social problems, until finally the characters are up against the wall in all the ways that matter, things can't escalate any further, and something's gotta give -- whether that's the character, the wall, or the forces pushing him against the wall.

    In other words, it would be an awful lot like Death of a Salesman and other stories of that kind.

    So, yeah, I think it would work. Being the game's author, I'm of course biased, but I really do think it would work.

    (Reading back over this, when I say "violence," I really mean "trying to kill people." Non-lethal violence -- such as drunken fist-fights and domestic abuse -- would be totally appropriate in this sort of story.)

    \n Thanks for all your comments and suggestions. I just wrote a long, rambling post about the escapist nature of our hobby, the importance of fantastic elements, why fantastic literature can be meaningful and powerful literature despite these non-realistic elements, and how for decades serious literature has been mostly defined as and demanded to be meaningful interaction between adults in the contemporary world or something along these lines. Then I clicked the post button and got an error, which probably saves you from a lot of pseudo-intellectual bullshit resulting from me trying to clear and order my thoughts while hitting the keys on my laptop and thinking about why the face in this ad created such a strong resonance in me.

    Basically, Mashall Burns had one part of the answer I was looking for:\n
    Posted By: Marshall Burns
    In other words, it would be an awful lot likeDeath of a Salesmanand other stories of that kind.

    So, yeah, I think it would work.
    \nI\u2019m still not sure if it would work as a game, though. We play games because they are fun; they\u2019re games, after all. Miller\u2019s plays can depress you like hell; yet, they are still \u201cfun\u201d, for some strange value of fun. But if you take away all the buffering fantastic elements and try to do Salesman as a game, and make it so it leads you to that bleak place instead where the fun is \u2013 wouldn\u2019t that be kind of self-defeating and in vain? After all, some of us play to get away from there. (Shreyas, Alex \u2013 not trying to troll here or to play the wealthy idiot; I\u2019m not wealthy, at least.)

    Would you want to play something like that?

    Apart from that, we already have a game of Fiasco lined up for the near future, and I will give Rustbelt a look.


    (Oh, and, Steve: I don\u2019t think there could be a reward in this bizarre game for feeling the pain of that character. It would need to crush you like one of Jack Womack\u2019s novels; there would be no place for any kind of reward. I still think I may be an idiot.)
