So I started a playtest of Savagery (pdf) last night. While we only finished characters, we did have a meta-argument about the rules, which suggests that the basic idea of the game is sound.

So by meta-argument, I mean we had an argument about the rules as an example of the rules:

"Zack seems to think that he just did a feint, but I think he just did a beat. What do you think? ... I know you're going to evade, but what kind of attack is that?"

Realize, feint, beat, evade, and attacks in general are all descriptions of social interaction - so they refer to the conversation as it is going on.

And, yes, I evaded both questions, the later by pointing out that the inflection of "What do you think?" matters - a passionate inflection means a libido attack (like strike), or possibly an ideals attack (like beat), while a calmer one leans more towards a beat or maybe a feint (as a reasoned attack).

So far the game does everything I hoped, and perhaps a bit more. Perhaps I should do more RPG design under altered states...

- Mendel S.

    John,

    Thank you, I'm heartened by your enthusiasm.

    I'm familar with Burning Wheel, Shock, and Contenders, though I wasn't when I designed Savagery (indeed neither Shock or Contenders were published then, and Contenders didn't exist at all - since I had the ill-fortune to do my 24-hour RPG design during the grand act prior to the Ronnies.)

    The Duel of Wits works very well for skilled people debating in court (royal or judicial), there are different extensions to cultural and psychic conflict (with the Infection / Propoganda rules and the Psychologist rules in Burning Empires), but none of these really capture the emotional side.

    In a Duel of Wits, the conflict is really about whether someone is convinced - whether the people, a judge, or your own mind, but that's not how most arguments people have end. Savagery is meant to handle the brutal emotional beatings we will give each other - in the practice fight I set up for the playtesters it ended up being a dominate versus grapple blood match, with rising desperation as it became apparent that the next good blow was going to break someone.

    People are much more easily hurt than convinced.

    That being said, as far as looking at The Duel of Wits for possibilities, most of the maneuvers map thematically as much as anything else. It actually makes me curious if Entice could be a valid Duel of Wits manuever - especially in the more seductive applications. The last Duel of Wits I played might have worked out better if offering mead had been something more fitting than an Obfuscate.

    I'm not familar with Fury of Dracula, but given how fighting styles work, I don't think a card mechanic would help (other than index cards with each of your styles on them). The core flow is that you choose defense and lock your fighting style, then you choose your attack in ego order. Repeating each bout.

    As far as the player's acting as antagonists, in retrospect a modified troupe system, where each player has a foe who builds the people they encounter. The random antagonist works fairly well, but I fear it will be a bit boring after a while. I'll be putting the troupe style approach in the next version - in addition to the GMed and non-GMed versions (I generally see no problem with GMs doing less work - after all a game where the GM has equal or less to do than the players tends to be more fun for the players.)

    Thank you again for reading. I hope to post summaries of later playtests, as we get to them.

    - Mendel S.
