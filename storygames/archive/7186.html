\n In the "What games scare you" thread Dave (DinDenver) mentioned that the games of Sorcerer he'd seen all seemed "squicky." Whenever people talk about Sorcerer being too dark or think the game is about reveling in depravity I point to this setting I came up with the first time someone asked about lighter Sorcerer. I would love it if someone played it. Bonus points for playing it with actual children.

Enjoy!

Sorcerers are children on the cusp of adulthood. I would say the core age range being 12 to 15 but could see going as low as 10 and as high as 18. At least one element of the character should represent their move from the care free days of childhood into a more adult role in the lives of others around them. A 12 year old with a new baby sibling is a good example.

Demons are fairies. Feel free to pull from the full range of folk lore from mischievous household sprites to burly trolls. In general bound fairies protect and help their \u201cmasters\u201d while probably causing lots of trouble. Mischief and Mayhem are common Desires. Knowledge a close second.

Sorcery is rooted in childish and irresponsible behavior. Summoning a fairy might involve stealing your mother\u2019s favorite antique tea cup, filling it with honey and burying it in the mud. Binding might be done via challenging the fairy to ever escalating dangerous dares (climbing a high tree perhaps?). Banish, Punish and Contain are of course the opposite. Shutting that window and going to bed like you\u2019re supposed to could be the basis for a Punish or Contain, for example.

Humanity is the ability to handle adult responsibility. You lose humanity when you act childishly, irresponsibly or selfishly. You gain humanity when you take responsibility for your actions, accept consequences and generally behave like an adult. At zero humanity you are forever a child emotionally, never really to take on the world and its challenges as an adult.

Jesse

    \n It's very common for people to react with surprise to my Humanity definition. They're used to the idea of Innocence that gets lost as the child transitions into adulthood. I really dislike this idea because it makes every story about childhood a tragedy. The Innocence interpretation of Humanity is also antithetical to Sorcerer's design as Innocence is often portrayed as something that can be force-ably taken away and Humanity in Sorcerer is all about what the character *chooses* to do. The example scenario in Sorcerer's Soul that uses Innocence as a Humanity definition actually does break this rule and its never sat well with me.

    So what's the incentive? I get to be irresponsible AND play with fairies! Cool, huh? What's the down side?

    Yes, the fairies are meant to be troublesome and destructive via their playfulness.

    That's why the real world relationships of the Sorcerer are about a thousand times more important in this setting. How much fun is it to see your mother crying when she finds out you've lost the teacup that was the last memory of HER mother? How cool is it when your best friend gets blamed and punished for something you or your fairy did? And when your baby brother, who YOU were supposed to be watching, gets kidnapped by the trolls, how fun is it then?

    "It's all fun and games until someone gets hurt," is EXACTLY what this setting is about.

    Jesse

    \n Here's my notes on Drugs for Contacting and Sacrificing for Summoning.

    Drugs & Contact: The underlying principle here is that the Sorcerer does something which endangers himself while heightening his capacity to commune with the fairy world. In the case of kid Sorcerers this might be analogous to staying up very, very late or traveling into a very dangerous area. \u201cHits\u201d of the drug map onto, perhaps, hours stayed up or miles traveled. Like drug use, when the Contact ritual is over the Sorcerer rolls the number of dice added against an appropriate stat (Will for staying up late, Stamina for traveling, etc) and if the stat fails then that stat is impeded for a period of time and permanently if the \u201cdrug dice\u201d scored Total Victory.

    Sacrificing & Summoning: It isn\u2019t likely that ten year old Sorcerers are going to be spilling the blood of their baby brothers in the name of the goblin king. To that end here are two different takes on sacrificing for purposes of gaining dice on Summoning rituals.

    The first method is using sacrificing with the possibility of retrieval. The idea here is that whatever is being sacrificed is not killed but simply given over to the demon and possibly whisked away to the Otherworld. Mechanically this works identically to the method described in the core book. This is still a very dangerous option because there are no guarantees in Sorcerer. Just because it\u2019s possible to get back whatever was sacrificed doesn\u2019t mean it will be. Also, this option tends to take over the story very quickly as turning over one\u2019s baby brother to the goblin king is rather a big deal.

    The second method involves sacrificing an item of emotional importance to someone who is not the Sorcerer. Rather than sacrificing his baby brother, the Sorcerer can sacrifice his baby brother\u2019s teddy bear. Mechanically the item owner\u2019s Humanity is rolled vs. the Sorcerer\u2019s own Humanity and the victories are rolled into the Summoning roll. This roll is subject to all the usual role-playing bonuses with the dice being added onto the owner\u2019s Humanity pool.

    \n My notes on how to handle adults:

    Adults function thematically different across different kinds of stories. As such it is worth giving them some special consideration. Here are some options.

    Thematically Active \u2013 Adults are present and active in the narrative and are not treated in any special way other than the normal thematic considerations of the material.

    Thematically Absent \u2013 The story concerns only kid-to-kid interaction and the adult world is presented only in vague and shadowy terms. Adults and their actions have no meaningful impact on the narrative and adult life seems far off, confusing and inaccessible to the main characters. Characters tend to talk about interactions with adults only in terms of their life functions, \u201cMom and Dad went to a party last night,\u201d or \u201cMy teacher said I\u2019m not trying hard enough.\u201d

    Thematically Skewed \u2013 Adults are present and active in the narrative but are thematically weighted in some manner. One such weighting might be that all adult characters are either antagonistic or ineffectual. Another weighting might simply be that all adults automatically gain two bonus dice in any conflict with children characters. This lends adult characters more of an authoritarian presence in the fiction.

    A super scary option would be to treat adults like Angels from Sorcerer's Soul. They have no Will score because they can't be influenced by children. They have a Grace score because they help children grow up. They have a Goal because they want their children to become something greater than they are.

    In addition to the above some attention needs to be given to how adults relate to the Mentor Descriptor for Lore. The basic setup assumes that functional members of adult society are non-Sorcerers. To that end it is recommended that having an adult sorcerous mentor implies that such a character is, at least initially, an outcast in the adult world. The creepy hermit who lives alone in the woods, the lonely janitor at school everyone avoids, or that great aunt no one\u2019s talked to in almost a generation are all good examples.

    \n And finally some cute, cuddly little demons

    Mug

    Type: Passer
    Appearance:
    Mug is a goblin bundled up in clothing to appear more like a squat, stocky boy. He is particularly fond of long coats and hats pulled down low on his head to cover his pointy ears and goblin eyes. He is almost always frowning.

    Telltale:
    Mug has pointy goblin ears sticking out from under his hat, yellow eyes and sharp teeth.

    Stamina: 4
    Will: 5
    Lore: 4
    Power: 5

    Desire: Power
    Need: To give a beat down.

    Abilities
    \tArmor (Goblin Hide), Special Non-Lethal Damage (powerful punch), Hold, Cover (Kid)

    The Orb of Kwill

    Type: Object (Fairy Artifact)
    Appearance:
    \tThe Orb of Kwill is a sparkling crystal ball glowing with pink light from within.

    Telltale:
    \tThe Orb of Kwill glows with a pink light from no apparent power source.

    Stamina: 3
    Will: 4
    Lore: 3
    Power: 4

    Desire: Knowledge
    Need: For the things that have been seen within it to be told to others.

    Abilities:
    \tMark (confers), Perception (those Marked)(confers), Hint

    Alora

    Type: Inconspicuous
    Appearance:
    \tAlora is a tiny woman wearing a mutli-colored dress composed of flower petals. She has delicate gossamer wings.

    Telltate
    \tAlora leaves a slight sparking trail behind her as she moves.

    Stamina: 2
    Will: 4
    Lore: 3
    Power: 4

    Desire: Attention
    Need: To be followed into the night and frolic under the stars.

    Abilities
    \tCloak, Travel (flight), Transport (limited by Will instead of Stamina), Boost: Lore
