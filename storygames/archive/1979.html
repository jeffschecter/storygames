Quoting Chris Pramas of Green Ronin:

"The new core city book, the Pirateâ€™s Guide to Freeport, will be a 256 page campaign setting with no rules material at all. In other words, a pure setting book that can be used with any fantasy RPG you like. Weâ€™re then going to be offering a series of companion books in print and PDF that will give you game info for various systems. The first two will be the True20 Freeport Companion and the d20 Freeport Companion. Others will follow."

I'm kinda hoping for a TSOY Freeport book, actually. I'd play that shit. We should convince one of the SG folks doing freelance work to get on proposing that to Green Ronin. Though one of the cool things would be feeling like you could run a Freeport game at a convention or at your local store and then whip out whatever cool system you like, seducing the d20 crowd to our Kool-Aid drinking ways. I imagine there's already some folks planning to play Burning Freeport or maybe even FATEport.

Green Ronin has always made the smart move of presenting Freeport as a kind of modular setting component that could fit itself into most D&D-style fantasy worlds. But this is the first effort by a large game publisher (that I know of) to present a setting separate from any rules set that you could use to run it (as opposed to those kinda lame books which include rules for a half dozen very similar systems).

Granted, it's going to be a setting custom-built to run fairly traditional roleplaying campaigns, so it's not completely adaptable to running, say, Freeport: Breaking the Ice (though pirate dating would be fun!), but it's an interesting publishing model that sorta moves roleplaying more towards the domain of fan fiction. Here's our setting! Now use whatever system you want to tell stories in it!

How do folks feel about this? Interesting idea? Silly idea? Bog-standard idea?

    Personally, I like the idea, to an extent. Let's, for a second, imagine that Freeport gets really, epically big in the RPG world.

    On the one hand, it can provide common ground. GURPS-heads and d20-heads and TSOY-heads could conceivably all discuss their Freeport campaigns together. The GURPS guy could be like, "have you explored th Alchemist's Cove much? There's this really cool adventure seed in it..." And then the TSOY guy is like, "Well, actually, my group plays a bit more like X than like Y". Suddenly, GURPS guy and TSOY guy have found common ground to talk on. GURPS guy knows a bit more about TSOYs playstyle, and he learns they really aren't from different planets afterall.



    On the other hand, what it does is makes sure d20 people keep playing d20 products, GURPS people keep playing GURPS products, FATE people keep playing FATE products, and there is little cross-over. One of the big draws to trad games is their dope-ass settings and races and classes. If they are all available to all systems, then it stops people from ever trying something new. And that is potentially a Bad Thing.

    The idea leaves me cold, but most because for the character-centric play that I enjoy, the tome of setting is cart-before-horse thinking. I don't want to read a setting and then install a character into it; I want to create a character and then develop the setting from which that character springs. Note that by 'setting' I mean lists-of-important-NPCs-and-locations-and-how-they-relate, usually presented in an exhaustive manner. I prefer my "settings" to be evocative before they are prescriptive -- like TSOY's Near, which says "Maldor has lots of feuding nobles" and leaves it at that, rather than drawing out a map and saying who's got the steel mines and who guards the forest of ship masts and blah de blah blah. All of that is useless if not directly destructive to the way I prefer to play. I'm not sure if Green Ronin is aware of the extent to which their "rules-free setting" is actually rather constrictive.

    On another note, I think it's worthwhile pointing out that Freeport was not created in this format, but has developed into it. The original Freeport was written for... whatever system it was originally written for (D&D 3?). It got a lot of adherents in that form, and became established in the gaming world. Now it can stretch out a little into other systems (one of which is GR's house system). I would be highly skeptical of a product presenting a new setting in such a format. I don't think you can start there; you have to come to it with a fanbase first.

    A few random thoughts because it's 1 in the afternoon and I still haven't eaten breakfast:

    1. People who play what they play because they already play it. Also known as "Design What Doesn't Matter" or "We already know what an RPG is" or lots of other terms. While most of us disagree on whether its a good thing or not or if you'd want to design for it or not, we do seem to have some agreement that there are a lot of RPers out there who mostly get books to support the way they already play, rather than to learn to play a new game. For these folks this kind of setting seems to be a potentially good idea, as it fully supports the way they play -- stip-mining settings for ideas and adventures and cool bits, while not having mechanics that they'll not use anyway. If they aren't going to use your mechanics, why put mechanics in the book?

    Big issue with this, from a marketing perspective, is getting these people to realize that's what they're doing when they're used to buying books with "crunchy bits" only. Green Ronin's companion vollumes may work for this, as players are also used to the idea of needing to buy multiple books to get a complete game, so, we'll see.

    2. Ain't it funny how much setup counts. Much as they're not the hotness they used to be, I know a lot of folks around here who gave love to Exalted in the way back, and who fairly recently were loving Weapons of the Gods. Exalted, as of Scavanger Sons, had probably 250+ pages of setting, and WoG has to be damn close. But Exalted spread it out over 3 or 4 books at the start, and WoG had it written by Rebecca and (kinda sorta) tied to character and world generation. So those go down with our crowd much more smoothly. And Burning Wheel has that much setting info, but it's built into the game mechanics so that one becomes the other, and this is awesome.

    3. Cliche and Not. Now, pirates are pirates, but I think its worth considering when we start getting ready to bad-mouth big setting books that the ability to run a game in any setting without having at least a big chunk of information on that setting has to do with how generic or familiar the setting is. A lot of Indie games make heavy use of external, mass culture tropes in order to drive their gears, and this is awesome because it gets the game rolling fast along channels we all understand. But it can be problematic for those wanting to get into exploration of settings, cultures, or ideas that are not part of "what we all know (at least kinda)."

    So while I don't know that a big setting book is needed for pirates, I would very much like one for when I'm going to be playing in Warring States China with people who've never even watched Crouching Tiger, or for any game set in India where the folks at the table only barely know what naan is. (Or even with those that have some idea about the area, but not enough to do anything beyond the generic and obvious.)

    Also, having a strong background in a setting is sometimes needed for a developer. Thor and Luke have both said, in the past, that to make a full set of lifepaths for Burning Wheel you have to have a pretty intimate understanding of the culture you're burning. The work I've seen folks do on Burning India games pretty much confirms this for me. So, if you have to start somewhere, and if you want to do it for something non-generic or non-pre known, you need to do a lot of research and work into the backgrounds of stuff. If the research and work you're doing is into fictional stuff you're making up yourself, well, you may end up with a document that has a lot of things traditionally seen as a setting book. And at that point, why not offer it up as well? Not even as something necessary, but maybe as something extra for those that want it?
