    Hiya Mike! Thanks for the write up. I'm gonna port your questions/comments here to answer them.

    \n
    \n

    I think some more limitations on cross-class talents are in order... in this game, the player of the longrunner urged everyone to take 'Do two things at once (if one is running)', which made them an unstoppable parade of destruction. Maybe like niche protection: you can't have a cross-class talent if someone in the party already has it?

    \n
    \n

    I have a question for you about this one, Mike. What level were the characters?

    \n
    \n

    Maybe add another combat action: 'Cunning plan: use your gear, the terrain, etc to gain some advantage in the combat. E.g. "I use my specialist equipment: bombs to turn the terrain into hazardous!"' Really, I just want a placeholder (and suggested mechanic maybe) for 'something else not directly about damage/movement/awesome tokens'. At the moment, there can be a temptation to just stick to the listed combat actions, because they are listed as 'the things you can do'. Adding one that encourages creative crazy tactics (as seen in both my games so far) would be cool.

    \n
    \n

    Crazy tactics? Planting bombs? That's totally a Showing Off action to me. How is what you're looking for different from Showing Off / Assisting?

    \n
    \n

    I don't think you need all that stuff about connections between arenas, beyond a little paragraph about how they might be one-way or ranged only or require a roll or something. Categorizing them as you have doesn't seem to add much.

    \n
    \n

    You're probably right.

    \n
    \n

    Some of the talents are way, way more effective than others. Is that a choice or are you still balancing them. Of particular note: the longrunner's two things at once if one is running (do anything then move) compared to warrior's charger (move then get an attack).

    \n
    \n

    I'm groovy with some talents being more or less effective than others. Just so long as every single talent is effective and fun. Not-fun or not-effective talents have to go.

    \n

    Further, if all my players conspired to take the same talent, because they thought that it was the UberTalent, I'd start by raising an eyebrow as high as it could go as ask "Really? Are you sure you want to cripple your party by not having any other abilities?"

    \n

    Then, if they insisted, I'd go right on to create monsters that could more easily counter that single tactic. Like FAST + FLIGHT. "You guys really are fast and dangerous. Too bad no one took a flying fox, because this wyvern can really blast through the air above you. Did I mention that 'The Air Above You' is a seperate arena? Yeahhhh..."

    \n
    \n

    Note for Eric: do you intend that there is no real way to boost up your defense to get away from a really mean attack? The players were concerned (mainly when I one-shotted their warrior who was sitting on 15 or so awesome tokens, I think) that they had no defensive option in that case. There's something in that, but I had a feeling that this might be intentional on your part... can you let me know which it is?

    \n
    \n

    You're right. That's totally intentional. It's mostly a whiff-prevention device. The best defense in RBH is to kill your enemy before they can kill you.

    \n

    I would have laughed my ass off at the dude playing that warrior. "So, the dragon wasn't all that impressed with your pelvic-thrusts, eh? Guess you should have attacked or something."

    \n

    Good stuff, Mike. I'm eager to hear what level those characters were. It'll inform my opinion on putting in more niche protection.

    \n

    Ah, I see. I don't go into explicit details of it in the rules, but I've always imagined that the arenas and their connections are just as volitile as the narration allows them to be. If someone locks a door, that could kill a connection. If someone blows up a bridge, that could kill a connection too. Might even kill off an entire arena, if the bridge were it's own arena.

    \n

    I recall a recent playtest where one character climed a tree, and declared the top of that tree to be it's own arena. So I had the big monster come into the Forest Arena that the Tree Arena was contained in, and had that monster try to knock down the tree. I made a Size roll of some sort of the monster. IIRC, I failed the roll for the monster. But if I hadn't, I would have erased the Tree Arena from the board and moved the PC's miniature into the Forest Arena.

    \n

    I encourage you and your groups to be just as creative with your arenas and connections. I would offer you this rule of thumb: Re-draw the arenas and connections as often as necessary, without the assumption that they are stagnant and unchanging during combat.

    \n
    \n

    Re: all going for particular talents...

    \n
    \n

    Well, I'm not sure what to tell you here. All I can really say is that I would have politely discouraged all the players from taking the same talent. But, at the same time, I want the option to be available. I mean, if they think that power is kewl, just imagine if they'd all taken Homonucleus? Being able to take two different actions in two different arenas is way more powerful than just a free movement action. Combine that one with a free-action talent and you've got one crazy-powerful character.

    \n As the player of the Warrior who got killed in one shot, I would like to explain why I was pretty bummed when my character got taken out. Firstly, I didn't realise that the shaman could even hit me. Mike just said "Oh, this guy has a ranged attack. Cool!"... roll... spend some tokens... "You're dead." Secondly, I didn't understand that my character wasn't actually dead. So to me it seemed mighty unfair that I got taken out of the game in one hit. Once it was explained that I was probably going to be okay after the combat, then I was cool with the insta-death, and a lot less gung-ho after that...

    As for all taking the same skill: The game is one of the ones where (not counting a range attack advantage) if you move into an enemy region you open yourself up to attack.Not having any defensive capabilities means that if the GM has a bunch of awesome tokens moving into killing distance is effectively suicide. Being able to close on the enemy and fight them in the same turn is incredibly useful. However, I'm not suggesting you fix this at all. RBH is in essence a free form board game, and as you said, if the PCs come up with some uber-tactic the onus is on the GM to find a counter, and vice versa.

    Oh and a correction to Mike. We all hit Level 3 near the end that's when we jumped on the "move and fight" bandwagon.

    Great game Eric, thanks. And thanks to Mike for running it.

    \n For niche protection:

    It does seem kind of... boring... if everyone takes the same talent.

    How about some kind of spacing/timing limitation? I'm thinking of something along the lines of:

    --You cannot select a talent someone else has unless you are higher level than them.

    --No one can choose the same talent as another player at any given level (ex: If Bob takes "Homunculus" when he reaches level 2, no one else can choose that when they reach level 2. This kind of parallels the character creation rules.)

    \n Actually, that was I mentioned the specialist gear. I was thinking precisely of magical items. It seems like a perfect fit!

    You want to sneak into the orc camp? One of the dangers is being caught by a patrol... so you use your ring of invisibility to get a +2 on that roll, but once you're in and a fight breaks out, there's no unbalancing effect to worry about.

    The magical items act as a way for the characters to act cool and dazzle bystanders outside of combat, or help them get in and out of it in interesting ways (e.g. the good old "feather fall").

    Know what I mean?

    (And, to tie them together: the whole arena modification thing could be another neat use of magical items treated like specialist gear.

    \n The game already has magic items... they're just called Talents. For the real classic D&D feel, just tie each talent to an item. The Longrunner ability to move and attack is obviously Boots of Speed, for example.

    To make magic items special, though, I would tie them into adventures. You need to slay the dragon! The only way to hurt the dragon is with the Sword of Woe. Go get the sword! What's it do? It lets you hurt the dragon.

    Then at higher levels, maybe you need to fight an invading army. Gotta get the Thunder Hammer! What does it do? It lets you fight a whole army as if it was a monster.

    Stuff like that. No simple +1s or whatever, but rather special plot-device type things.
