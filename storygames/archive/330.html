Combat System Sketch: Traps!

shreyas
March 2006 edited March 2006 in Story Games

So, uh, the idea behind this is to basically have a neat combat system where your decisions have clear systematic consequences, even when you are "just" jockeying for position or staing, "I am not left-handed either!" or any such thing.

How It Works

Every "action" in this system is underlyingly offensive; by doing anything at all you are threatening your opponent. We accomplish this by saying that an action "sets a trap," which, you hope, your opponent will trigger sometime in the future (by taking some action), or that it will constrain his choices if he tries to avoid it. "Trap" is used fairly loosely here; if you take an action such as Climb the Crumbling Stairs, then you may be setting a trap with the trigger and effect, "When your opponent uses any Approach action, you can roll Athletics to try and knock him down, which causes the Approach to fail." (You see here that Approach has become a category of similar actions.)

Put another way, all actions are defined by when and how they are dangerous for your opponent. Some (the Stairs example above is obviously one) are also defined by when they are possible to use.

What You Do

When it's your turn, you take exactly one action, and along the way you resolve and traps you sprung. Then you move on to the next character.

What This Needs

For this to work in a sane way, you need a closed set of generic action categories, and probably a good deal of other stuff. That's why it's a Little Idea!

The Cool Bits

I think the actions that are specifically connected to the environment are the best bit about this, here, and the potential that it has for inserting idiosyncratic actions that allow you to do Funky Stuff: Approach by Flight: This is like Approach, but you don't set off traps that are based on Athletics.

Naturally there aren't non-trapping actions or any such thing that disrupts the tempo; even "I swing a sword at him" is clearly a trap that triggers on "any action that doesn't prevent the sword making contact."

    I don't like designing inescapable things, so I would probably include just one action that isn't in the trigger set of any other action, calling it "Surrender" or some such. Maybe Surrender allows you to choose exactly one trap and trip it, and all the others are disarmed, and you've lost whatever was at stake for the conflict - I'm assuming that a stake was set explicitly earlier. So when you've run out of good options, you can surrender and at least get away with your dignity.

    The hidden assumption I have here is that, while the game process is very deliberate, I want this to look very energetic and fast-paced, descriptively. Is it even possible to take no action? I suspect that it isn't, or if it is then it is nearly always the least optimal choice because of actions like "hit with sword" that have negative triggers.
