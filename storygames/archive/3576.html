    \n Games that rely on fiat to determine the difficulty of performing a task/action or achieveing a goal? Okay. In D&D 3x, there's a table of suggested skill DCs, but no hard and fast rules for determining such a DC other than application of good judgement. Both Storytelling and Storyteller are also good for this (they have a recommended default difficulty, but ultimately what a player needs to roll to succeed is up to the Storyteller). In Fudge difficulties are ranked using entirely subjective adjectives, thus difficulties are. . . uhm. . . entirely subjective. Risus, works almost exatly like Token does, recommending a small list of difficulties and leaving the deterimination of actual difficulty in regard to a given action or goal up to the GM's discretion. Ditto the D6 System (which is were Risus nabbed much if its core mechanic, actually). This list could go on, though I think that for the purposes of satisfying your curiosity, this should be sufficient.

    \n James, I think you reversed the words in Joshua's sentence. He wanted to show you how to do it without fiat, rather than have you show him how to do it using fiat.

    Chances are me and him crosspost that sentiment.


    One thing I like to do, re: fiat, is to have both/all sides of the conversation be able to make a judgement call about difficulty/importance.

    In Perfect, the GM says "On a scale of 1 to 10, how much does this matter to you?" He (defaulting to a he, because I'm refering to myself more than anyone else, here) then asks himself the same question. He combines the two arbitrary judgement calls (his and the player's) to get a single lump sum.

    This sum is how many points he has for the scene. A similar (read: identical) mechanic could be used for determining difficulty ratings.

    That's just one way to de-fiat difficulty ratings.

    \nFor what it's worth, it doesn't have to be a question of "do you trust your GM." I've had situations when I was the GM and didn't trust myself to assign fair difficulties. In games like D&D that require a lot of prep-work it can be awfully tempting, when players decide to do something completely unexpected, to assign high difficulties to things in order to stall their progress in order to buy yourself some time to prepare. Conversely, when a player decides to do something really cool, you're naturally going to want them to succeed, so it's tempting to lower the difficulty for that action. Either way, it eventually devolves into a game of "guess what the GM wants," and that is most decidedly Not Fun for me, as a player or as a GM.

    \n And to take a crack at your questions:

    Why is reliance on GM fiat when assigning difficulties bad?
    It's only bad if it isn't fun for you and your peeps. For me, it is bad because you can't play all-out. Putting complete control over adversity in the hands of one person with unlimited power just isn't interesting.

    Why would another method be better?
    Again for me - and I recognize a whole universe of preference out there - knowing that all my friends, including the GM, are playing as hard as they can is engaging, challenging, and fun. Show me a system that requires this and I'll show you a game I can get behind. Prime Time Adventures is a great example of this, because the ecosystem of reward in that game dries up if people are not engaged and playing aggressively - it literally signals you when you should stop playing and watch a movie or something. The GM has a lot of power, but it is sharply demarcated and never arbitrary.

    What method would you suggest and why?
    I really enjoy games that distribute that authority a bit. There's no reason for a single auteur to have so much power over a group narrative other than tradition. I love being the GM, and I love telling my friends a story - I love it so much I want everybody to have that power at the table.

    Why is reliance on GM fiat when assigning difficulties bad?

    \n
    \n

    It means that the GM can't bust balls. It means that, when the GM wants to turn up the heat, it's a completely arbitrary decision. If they do it at the wrong time, it feels like cheating to the players, so it becomes a mind reading game instead of a storytelling one. And if the GM doesn't do it at all, the players don't feel like they're doing anything. Because they're not.

    \n

    Ultimately, in such a game, the only rule that matters is "Make the GM happy and you'll get what you want". I've played that game and I bet you have, too. I've been the beneficiary of that game and occasionally on the shitty end of it. If you want to see it really in action, watch a group where there's a couple in the game and one of them is GM.

    \n

    Secondarily, it encourages the GM, as a rule system, to direct the players with in-game events, making it easier to do what the GM wants than do other stuff, thereby diminishing the input of the other players.

    \n
    \n

    Why would another method be better?

    \n
    \n

    Because if you look at the authorities vested in a traditional GM, you'll see that they equate to several conflicts of interest. The GM is responsible for the players' fun, where that means making things not too easy and not too hard, the precise definition of that having to do with the meaning of the conflicts to the characters, the relationships between the players, the mood folks are in, etc.

    \n
    \n

    What method would you suggest and why?

    \n
    \n

    In my published games, the authorities are distributed to different players at different times so as to avoid conflicts of interest. If your job is to offer opposition, you offer opposition as hard as you can. If your job is to show approval for someone's ideas, you're not responsible for opposition. If your job is to play a protagonist, you play the guy as hard as you can (or as hard as you want. Sometimes, you don't like the character and want to see them suffer.)

    \n

    It's very likely you've had fun with games that use a traditional GM. I certainly have. What's going on when that's happening is that the GM and players have communicated something really well, probably when and what kind of opposition to give. If you can figure out how to reliably reproduce that communication, that's a rule. Write it down and now you can have fun all the time!
