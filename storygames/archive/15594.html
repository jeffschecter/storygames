    \n Just off the top of my head, Apocalypse World, Bliss Stage, Mouse Guard, The Mountain Witch, Silver & White, and Danger Patrol (Beta) do that for me, along with a bunch of others, and those were all made by a much smaller design team with none of the resources of WOTC/Hasbro. Maybe it's naive, but I have faith that consistent and fun play isn't too high of a standard to demand of a game, especially the biggest game in roleplaying. To compare to another fighty game, I'd just like something slightly more consistent than Agon, which doesn't seem like too much to ask.

    I don't think 4E failed because its design perspective was too focused; I think it failed because it wasn't consistently fun to play. I picked up the core books, got excited about it, and tried to play more-or-less by the guidelines in the books, and by the third session we were already ready to switch back to something else. I think the history of indie games shows that when you ask people to do something that's actually more fun or a different kind of fun that's on par with what they're already doing, they don't need that much convincing. There just wasn't much there that arose to meet our enthusiasm and the work we put into it, and --from what I heard -- the dedicated players who played a bunch of 4E had to put in a ton more work just to make it fun at the table.

    On the extendability issue: how many hacks, custom moves, and mini-supplements exist for AW now? It's not easy to nail that every time, but it's not rocket surgery. They just have to nail it once every 5+ years. I definitely don't envy the pressure that Mike and the others feel, especially when there's a bunch of folks like me that complain that they're doing it wrong, but come on. Maybe it's the company-mandated design process and design-by-committee and all the other crap that entails that's getting in the way -- I suspect that plays a major part in it -- as well as the crazy guidelines and goals (like John posted above: it has to work as a Facebook ap?), but making D&D consistently fun shouldn't be as hard as it's been the past few years.

    \n30 ways I would improve D&D;:
    \n(based on Wizards of the Coast's goals, not my own personal preferences)
    \n
    \n1. Bring back the OGL.
    \n
    \n2. Sell PDFs and Print of Demand versions of every single thing ever released with the D&D; name on it.
    \n
    \n3. Convert all this content into OGL.
    \n
    \n4. But require that to use the OGL, you must be a D&D; subscriber!
    \n
    \n5. Since Pathfinder is already OGL, flip the tables and reintegrate Pathfinder back into D&D.; Make Pathfinder part of D&D; rather than competition. Do the same for Mutants & Masterminds.
    \n
    \n6. Hire market research and usability testing experts to work with our designers. Asking existing fans about products meant for new fans is biased. Same with asking existing fans about what appeals to old fans. Implement strict procedures for recording unbiased playtesting data, reviewing input against our goals, rolling out changes, testing the results, revise, repeat. Get ego, personal agendas, non-rational/emotional decisions out of the process!
    \n
    \n7. Fans can make their own version of D&D;! Pick and choose the options you want from any edition. Upload your own cover. Create your own credits page. Write your own intro. Choose from a wide array of binding and paper options. We will mail you your own personal unique copy of D&D.;
    \n
    \n8. We will give you the option to sell your own version of D&D; through our website and we will split the profits with you. Now all your players can buy a copy.
    \n
    \n9. Stop developing software. License software. Reach out to the D&D; fans working for Google. Use Google Plus and Google Docs as our virtual Table Top. Use Google's Schemer to find players based on availability and playstyle. Don't create out own social network tools.Use Facebook and Twitter. Use what people use. Don't create costly proprietary tools that people are unfamiliar with.
    \n
    \n10. Open up the D&D; Online Database to 3rd party content. Create tools that let fans rate this content so that the stars rise and the crap sinks. Include a DM warning option to label content that may be good but game breaking. Tag content based on edition and play style.
    \n
    \n11. Forget editions. Instead the next version of D&D; will be a toolkit anniversary edition celebrating all of D&D; over the last 40 years.
    \n
    \n12. Instead of editions, we will have a stripped down version of D&D; that fits on 20 pages or less than you can make characters and play in 15 minutes. All the different edition advanced content will be available as modules.
    \n
    \n13. Instead of organizing modules by editions, organize it by playstyle. Want D&D; to be more tactical? Use these modules. More story based, use these modules. More immersive? Something in-between? Mix and match and let us know what works best for you.
    \n
    \n14. No more editions. Now there are just optional updates.
    \n
    \n15. Create a section on Deviant Art just for D&D; Fan Art. Users can vote on their favorites to be included in future products.
    \n
    \n16. Forget teaching games via rule books. Have them but don't rely on them. Most people learn by word of mouth. Embrace it. Teach people how to play via YouTube videos. Via audiobooks. Via classes at gaming stores. Teach people who to teach others!
    \n
    \n17. Create easy to learn family friendly D&D; boardgames that can scale into full blown RPGs with the right modules.
    \n
    \n18. Convince Hasbro that profits from D&D; video game licensing should be included as part of our overall sales.
    \n
    \n19. Make a lot of this content free to use for as long as you are a D&D; subscriber. Experiment with Free to Play + micro-transactions. Experiment!
    \n
    \n20. Now that we have the D&D; video game rights back, make it a requirement that all D&D; video games give you the option to import / export your characters for play in the tabletop versions.
    \n
    \n21. Keep supporting local gaming stores with D&D; Encounters. Create training seminars for the stores and DMs.
    \n
    \n22. Encourage local gaming stores and gaming communities to run more frequent 1 or 2 day mini conventions. Teach them how. Find space they can rent. As retail stores close down, conventions keep growing in popularity. People are social. Encourage this. Give people places to go, to meet, to play.
    \n
    \n23. Get the Hunger Games & Harry Potter licenses. But don't just create a Harry Potter RPG. Create a game where Harry Potter is an adventurer in the D&D; world. I know this is easier said than done.
    \n
    \n24. Create custom versions of D&D; presented by Vin Diesel, Wil Wheaton, and Felicia Day. If Felicia Day is in charge, what would her D&D; look like? What would D&D; look like if it was written by an 11 year old in China?
    \n
    \n25. Bring back Choose your own Adventure books but in App form. Let people use these Apps to build their own fully formed characters to play in the D&D; RPG.
    \n
    \n26. Create mobile games where you can play your D&D; character solo in-between games. Earn the chance to gain extra treasure at the cost of starting the next game with more damage or other costs. When you are done playing, the game emails your DM the results of you side adventures.
    \n
    \n27. Create apps you can play D&D; solo when you don't have a group to play with. As they go up in level, tell them about people in their area they can play with. "Congratulations, you just unlocked level 5 and acquired a vorpal +2 sword. We noticed in your profile that you prefer tactical play. There are currently 3 gaming groups within 20 miles of your location who are also level 5 and like tactics who have an open spot in their games. Would you like us to introduce you?"
    \n
    \n28. Make sure all these variations all still feedback into the bare minimum basic D&D; rules at their core. Expand the family tree that is D&D; while keep the roots strong and something we keep coming back to as leave grow, die, and grow again.
    \n
    \n29. Test all of these ideas ruthlessly. What sounds good on paper make be a failure in practice. What sounds mediocre on paper may be a huge win. Test, test, test! Stay objective. Kill your darlings. Experiment. This just the start. Listen. Learn. Evolve!
    \n
    \n30. Once we reach 50+ million propose to Hasbro that we create a D&D; Cartoon. Use the same formula as the new My Little Pony: Friendship is Magic. Hire the Penny Arcade artists to design the show!
    \n
    \nNote: some of these ideas were inspired by Ryan Dancey and Epidiah Ravachol.
    \n
    \nThis is just the start.

    If I did want to tackle this impossible task, rather than trying to create a modular game (which I think tends to increase game-design complexity since you're trying to design multiple games at once, and it's unlikely they'll all be well designed) I'd probably try to design multiple smaller games under the D&D umbrella. I'd try to make character creation simple for each one, but not directly translatable, so that switching to another game ("hey, let's use the tactical squad game for the arena battle that you guys got yourselves into!") would require using the chargen subsystem in a way that would put you in the right mindset to play the new game on its own terms.

      \n Man, this thread.

      Modular D&D is easy. I mean, it's work, but it's not hard.

      Get the basics down: six abilities, three classes (four if navigation/orientation/movement has to be a basic thing), experience levels, fighting.

      Everything else is modular options. For each part of the game, there are several canonical options that all the published products will make reference to, and then you can also write your own for when you assemble your own version of the game as per John's suggestions #7 and #8.

      For abilities, you have a number of basic options, like:

      Ab 1: Desperate scoundrels. Roll 3d6 in order.

      Ab 2: Heroic heroes. Some version of 4d6 drop the lowest and arrange where you want.

      Ab 3: Tournament (or whatever). Point-buy system. You need a point-buy system version for Living games and tournaments and whatnot.

      For skills, you have options like:

      Sk 1: No skills. Freeform it, whatever, consult your DM.

      Sk 2: Proficiencies. Either you have a skill or you don't. Roll your ability or less on a d20 to employ a skill successfully.

      Sk 3: Detailed skill system. Just like 3rd + 4th editions, with extended skill challenges and variable DCs and blah blah blah. You can still make desperate weaklings with lower point totals, and mighty demigods with higher point totals.

      Etc. for everything else in the game. The miniatures battles stuff is canonical for tournaments and whatnot, but is also an optional book. Feats and 4E-style powers are optional modules. So you start with OD&D or B/X, essentially, and add things until you get the edition you want. Some things will have to be tweaked throughout the rules to get everything nicely compatible (saving throws, xp/level numbers, etc) but fuck the complainers, as long as each thing is only as complicated as it absolutely has to be. Grognards can stick with their OD&D and their B/X, but newer players who prefer that style will just play the basic D&D rules with no modules. "We're hardcore, we play D&D with no optional modules, man!"

      When you go to play you will simply have to say which modules you are playing with. I know, that is complicated because y'alls are lazy-ass mutha fuckahs, but we will cater to you! That is what the official settings are for. Cramming Dragondudes and eladrin into every single setting is a dumb idea, and so is trying to make desperate greedy scoundrel dungeonfucks play the same as noble heroes defending the many points of light.

      So when you buy the Ravenloft setting, it says:
      Ab 1, roll 3d6 in order.
      Sk 2, this setting has proficiencies.
      No feats, no psionics, no primal magic. Arcane, divine, and martial classes only.
      Humans dominate, dwarves, elves, and halflings allowed.

      And when you buy Dark Sun, it says:
      Ab 2, way better stats.
      Sk 3, this setting uses the advanced skill system, and uses it for dangerous wilderness exploration especially.
      All gonzo powers are in.
      The list of races from the original Dark Sun stands, no others.

      And if you want to play Dark Sun without feats and skills, you just say so. If you want to play Ravenloft with skill challenges, you say "we're playing Ravenloft with the advanced skills module."

      Conceptually easy. Lots of work to implement. Not actually hard, in terms of whether it can be done or not.

      And frankly, I don't see any other way to even remotely reconcile the various editions. They are not the same game and will never be the same game and people play different editions because they want to play different games. By making the skeleton compatible with the various slottable modules, you at least have a common language and understanding between everybody. It's already several games, and trying to make it one game isn't going to work, so the only feasible alternative is to make D&D into a range of gaming. Not one game, not several games, but many interchangeable shades of gaming.

      \nI've been mulling over what the 'core' of D&D is over the last couple of days. What makes D&D different than other fantasy RPGs? Certainly it has had many competitors over the years, though more imitators than serious alternatives. I think it's hard to be totally objective here, because if you've read any comment sections of articles about the 5e announcement, you'll see a bunch of screeching, anonymous knobs defending each edition as the one true D&D. Who's right? No one and everyone. I started pondering about the game's core qualities when I was first playing 4e and feeling that it had stripped out a lot of what I thought made D&D distinct, and it is distinct, even if a lot of its qualities aren't exactly intentional. It's got a huge amount of character hanging off of it. It's an ugly mutant that has undergone a lot of changes but has survived. It sort of works. It's like a lovable pug, a breed so inbred that it sneezes on its own eyeballs but somehow remains lovable to many people.

      The core components of D&D, to me, are:
      1) A class system with complimentary, often mutually exclusive roles and different tactical requirements and gameplay opportunities. I think everything in D&D flows out from the four basic character classes of fighter, thief, cleric, and wizard.
      2) A complex rule set for combat that may not be immediately accessible to new players, but that offers good tactical variety and makes heavy use of the classes' differences and abilities. I think making a rules-light combat system for D&D would kill it. Just because something is complicated doesn't mean it's not worth learning.
      3) A unique magic system with a mix of spells, both combat and utility, that add flavour to the roleplay, reward improvisation, and add choices to the combat. A lot of the spells in D&D have developed an identity of their own, which I think is overlooked. Spells like magic missile, fireball, stinking cloud are iconic, in a way. I seem to be pretty much alone in this, but I think the memorization rules are great, too, and create a unique type of gameplay.
      4) Also iconic, the D&D bestiary. You've got the proprietary beasties like Mind Flayers and Beholders, but even the dragons and the giants have been given a readily identifiable character in D&D and have influenced their portrayals elsewhere. There are a lot of flat out dumb monsters, too, but I think that's something to embrace.
      5) This one is a little iffy, but it's one of my favourite, weird aspects of D&D: Alignment.

      One might argue that levels and experience points, magic items, ability scores and character races are fundamentals, too. They probably are, but I think they've always been pretty half-assed, no matter how much the extant editions have relied on them. I think these could be heavily altered and the game's heart remain intact. If you remove or dilute the core components I list above, however, I think the character of the game is greatly diminished.

      \n The only business transactions that are naturally antagonistic are the ones you shouldn't be making. If someone offers you something at a price you deem worth it, where's the antagonism there?

      Within the larger non-zero-sum interaction of "I would rather have this purchase than my money", sure, there is a zero-sum interaction of "exactly how much money each of us can get". But to reduce the entire transaction to that is to punt all context out the window.

      My take on buying complicated entertainment tools like RPGs is that, if the cover price isn't a big deal for you, you just buy it and see, and if the cover price is a big deal for you, then you read the thing and assess it as well as you can before considering purchase. In the former, trust is unnecessary, and in the latter, trust is earned by the product itself. And if you do care about cost but can't assess a game from reading it, well, that's what an internet full of reviews is for, right?

      When I hear that D&D has broken fans' trust, all that means to me is that more people will actually try to assess their products pre-purchase rather than just throwing money at anything with a D&D logo on it. To re-establish the expectation that anything with a D&D label on it will be quality, there's only one possible solution -- make everything with a D&D label on it be quality. In the meantime, folks who want to throw $30 at some unknown thing that might be fun have a larger field to consider (Hi, Dresden!).

      If "make it all quality" in this case is an unattainable goal due to different audience members' criteria for "quality", then that is what it is, and I'd guess that there's nothing D&D can do about it.

      Posted By: David BergWe've never lacked the ability for one player to indulge in crunchy min-maxing while another stays system-lite to focus on roleplay or fictional positioning. The only problem is that if you put those two people at the same table,they will bore the fuck out of each other.Formalizing how everyone gets to do different things isn't going to help them do a single thingtogether.
      \nI\u2019m worried this part of the new D&D initiative is misguided. Good DMs already consider players\u2019 different tastes within a single group; single groups tend to codify themselves around a particular culture of play. It\u2019s already jarring to leave one D&D group\u2019s playstyle for another\u2019s\u2014everyone\u2019s been doing it differently forever and always\u2014so making this a conceit of the newest version could increase the cognitive dissonance dramatically. Acknowledging it is good, however, and catering to it might mean that the long-running culture of play is maintained with positive results.

      Posted By: David BergI don't follow D&D news, but it seems to me thatifthey're focusing on the relationshipbetween each player and the game, they're fighting a losing battle against video games. Focusing on the relationshipsbetween playersis necessary to make the most out of the tabletop medium.
      \nThis is an important point\u2014I\u2019ve been tempted to start a discussion about it. Table-top roleplaying games can\u2019t compete head-to-head with videogames like Skyrim if they\u2019re expected to deliver content on a similar scale. They can\u2019t compete with \u201cThe Lord of the Rings\u201d or \u201cA Song of Ice and Fire\u201d (books or films, take your pick) in delivering a sweeping, unified narrative. What they can do is facilitate communication, collaboration, competition, and character-driven content created by the players. Playing up what makes roleplaying games more than simply entertainment seems best for their lasting health.

      \n Here's an alternative: the D&D "Find Your Edition" Quiz. The D&D folks could pin down what each edition is good at and make a play preferences questionnaire that slots players into the best fit for them.

      D&D5, rather than a blend of 1e thru 4e, would be filling whatever niche 1e thru 4e don't serve. Adding another option to the menu. "Discovery and immersion", "the GM's plot", "Cthulhu with swords", "court intrigue", or whatever a lot of D&D players want but don't reliably get from any previous edition. (The key word being reliably. D&D2 gets used for all of my examples! But how fragile is success, and how much does the game really help?)

      That's what I'd do. I hope they don't, though, so there's more of a void left in the hobby for indies and the stuff I hope to publish. :P
