\n Fuck it, maybe I am a designer, grrr...

Anyway, I want a game. I want a specific game that does a bunch of stuff that a bunch of games that I like do. But no game does all the things that I want. Here's an example of what I want:

PRIMETIME ADVENTURES' scene framing
IN A WICKED AGE's reoccurring characters and continuity rules
BURNING EMPIRES' world/setting creation
LACUNA's set task number
DANGER PATROL's character options/focus
NOBILIS' epic feel
etc, etc, etc...

and superheroes.

There's some other games that I want to rip off and graft onto this monster as well, but I don't know if it's really going to work. I know that those individual mechanics work in their games, but I don't know if they'll gel with everything else. I don't mind sanding off the edges of the pieces to get stuff to fit and work, but is it really worth it, or should I just design from the ground up?

    \n There's nothing wrong with Frankenstein design.

    The key is to make sure those components actually hang together in a meaningful way. That doesn't mean they all have to explicitly touch each other mechanically they just have to be mutually reinforcing at the creative social dynamic. Look at Burning Wheel. Lots of little pieces all over the place but they support and reinforce one central idea. There's a reason all of Luke's books have a chapter titled: It Revolves On This.

    \n Take the best of what you want to see and put a playtest document together. And the play the hell out of it.

    That's the easiest way to see how it's going to work in play. Sure some things will be messed up. But seeing that happen will allow you to fix on the fly.

    \n I've been cobbling together my own fantasy system and stealing ruthlessly.

    So far my Frankenstein has bits taken from:

    The Shadow of Yesterday (attributes as pools of bonus dice)
    D6 System/Wheel of Fate (adding together pools of D6s)
    Burning Wheel and lots of other games (Beliefs etc.)
    Hero's Banner (the idea of contradictory goals)
    Primetime Adventures (connections and scene framing)
    Runequest / Basic Roleplaying / Flashing Blades (skill advancement through useage)
    Fate / Amagi Games' Metasystem (consequences as damage)

    I'm also thinking of adapting the company rules from Reign (which were designed to be fairly portable).

    I'm not considering publishing this, but we may end up using it for a longterm fantasy thing we've been talking about.

    Posted By: jessecoombsWhat worked, what didn't, and why?
    The hard truth: All the stuff I ripped off of John Harper and Jason (indirectly, from James West and Luke Crane too) and mashed back together works brilliantly. All the problems are with the stuff I added or took away. Maybe you'll do better. Hell, maybe I will in the next rev ("...he said, with unjustified optimism.").

    I think the trick is to carve your sources at the joints as much as possible, taking whole "modules" out as cleanly as possible. Identify the synergies, and the points of friction. Play to the combined strengths and the hidden strengths you've discovered in the sources, like the death spiral I found in Lady Blackbird and exploited in my work. And have a plan to minimize the effect of the friction or dike it out - as I did with all the recovery mechanics in all of my source games, which would bail players out of the death spiral I was taking advantage of. Then live up to your predecessors, which is the really hard part.
