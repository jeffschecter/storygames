Hi,

\n

After a lot of writing and playtesting, Paul Taliesin and I have finished writing a new storytelling game called Muse. It's a game designed for people with busy schedules. It attempts to allow you to play a fun roleplaying-like game with no preparation time required. There's no game master, so nobody has the added burden of coming up with a story on their own, and there are no exclusive characters (i.e. PCs exclusive to a player) and no character sheets.

\n

I'd be overjoyed if you'd read our game or even give it a try. Please let me know what you think of it.

\n

You can download Muse in PDF or ePub format at the official website.

\n

Here's a little blurb about the game:

\n

Muse is a storytelling game where players collaborate and compete with each other to tell an enjoyable
\nstory. Players will take turns, with each of them contributing to a single story. Play will
\npass from player to player in clockwise order, unless listeners jump in or object. Players will inspire
\neach other to create story elements, which will get combined into questions about the plot.
\nNo-one knows in advance how questions will finally be answered. The game ends when no more
\nquestions can be created and the last question in play has been answered. The player with the
\nbest hand remaining gets to narrate the story\u2019s epilogue.

\n

Sincerely,

\n

--Jonathan

    Sell me on this! What's the game about? Why do I want to skim this, much less play or buy (?) it?
    \nFirst off, Muse is free. :-) That's an easy sell, I hope!

    As for why you might want to play:\n
      \n
    • If you like gaming (roleplaying or storytelling games) and you find that you're lacking in preparation time, then I humbly offer Muse as a potential solution to your temporal woes!
      • \n
    • Muse allows you to do things you can't in a regular RPG. You can have all the main characters die in a terrible tragedy, for example. You can have only one main character, which opens up a whole range of new story genres that are hard to explore in RPGs (where you generally expect a party size of 4 or 5 people).
      • \n
    • Muse really helps spark and focus your creativity. You can actually use it as a tool as well as a game, to generate new story scenarios for use in books, movies or RPGs.
      • \n
    \n
    --Jonathan

    Posted By: Ryan StoughtonWhat would you suggest for online play? Is it even possible with Muse?
    \nOriginally, Muse used dice! To reduce the odds of a tie, use the largest-sized dice you can (d100 or even d1000 if you're brave). Basically, your hand will be made up of dice instead of cards. In a showdown, whenever you'd reveal a card you instead roll a die from your hand. Whoever has the die with the highest face value wins. If the players are tied for highest face value, then they set aside that die, and consider the next highest face value. If one player runs out of dice in this way then they lose. In the case of a complete tie, the players reroll all their dice.

    e.g. with d100

    Alice rolls: 99, 50, 30
    Bob rolls: 75, 63, 40, 11
    Alice wins (her 99 is the highest face value)

    Alice rolls: 30, 10, 3
    Bob rolls: 30, 5
    Alice wins (they're tied with the 30s, but Alice's 10 beats Bob's 5)

    Alice rolls: 30, 10, 3
    Bob rolls: 30, 10
    Alice wins (she and Bob are perfectly tied for the 30 and the 10, but she has an extra die)

    Alice rolls: 65, 49, 36
    Bob rolls: 65, 49, 36
    Tie (re-roll)


    I hope that answers your question!

    --Jonathan
