\n This stems more from traditional games than story games, but perhaps it applies to both.

In some systems, the characters are rewarded for things that the players do. For example, some systems offer bonuses for describing cool stunts. Some other systems award experience for things that the players do, such as initiating in-character interactions. This seems like a reward mismatch to me; it seems like the character should be rewarded for things the character does. The same goes for the player in my mind. Linking character advancement to character actions doesn't seem that hard to do. Linking player rewards to player actions seems tougher.

It seems possible, but I'm having some trouble brainstorming ways to do it, so I'm looking for some suggestions. What sort of rewards might be offerred to players? And what sorts of actions could you reward?

    \n I agree that a game should be rewarding to the player, certainly. I'd also agree that most (all?) rewards should be enjoyable to the player. I'm thinking about the idea that a reward oughta facilitate the thing that is being rewarded. (It's not an original idea, someone around here said it recently, I just can't think of who.) Assuming that conjecture to be true for the moment, let's think about experience. Experience, as the currency of character advancement, improves the character's abilities and helps them do more cool awesome in-game things. So, by this theory, experience would be awarded for doing those cool awesome in-game things, and facilitates doing more of them. Hopefully, that will be enjoyable to the players. A similar line of thought applies to bonuses, in that they facilitate character actions in response to player actions.

    On the other hand, there are things that the players do that contribute to the game too, on a reasonably independent from characters (i.e. meta-game level.) This theory would say that those contributions should be rewarded with something that helps the player do them more. So, if you wanted to reward initiating new scenes, you might award some currency of scene framing power. If you wanted to encourage party cohesion/goal sharing, you might reward "finding a way to make another character's goal important to your character" with some currency of "introducing suitable rewards into the fiction for another character to share in your goal."

    So, to refine the question, what I'm asking is: What are some (non-character) player actions you'd want to facilitate, and what kind of rewards would you use to do so?

    \n Well, the theory that spurred this thought was the idea that one way to make rewards effective is to have the reward facilitate more of the same behavior that earned it. So, following this theory, things that players do on the meta-game level, like scene framing, ought to be rewarded in a way that facilitates doing it more. So that's where the divide starts: according to this theory, if you want to reward character actions, the reward should help the character do them more. If you want to reward player actions, the reward should help the player do them more.

    Take D&D. You get experience for killing things (a character reward), which makes you better at (i.e. facilitates) killing things (a character action.) Now suppose you want to reward players for, say, initiating scenes. If you give experience, it ends up making them better at killing things, but doesn't help them initiate interactions. The reward may actually send them away from setting up conversations and toward killing things. So maybe there's another reward currency that would encourage initiating conversations. A similar thing might be said for any player actions independent of their characters.

    \n I can see some benefit to doing this, but it leads to a bizarre scenario in which intuitive relationships are violated. Accomplishing things in character, or practicing them, won't lead to improvement at related skills, but framing scenes will. This just doesn't work out. Players who prefer the in-character stuff won't be receiving rewards they value, and neither will the scene framers. No one ends up encouraged.

    On the other hand, the problem of the players who are already good at a particular player task and get more resources for doing it, resulting in game domination is a concern. I think there are ways to work with this. One way would be to offer resources that enable framing "cooler" scenes rather than more scenes. Of course, you'd have to deal with this on a per issue basis, as the same solution won't work for every player task.
