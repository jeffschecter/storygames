\n Several NW storty gamers gathered at ConQuest NW this weekend for the ConQuest Story Games Lounge. We had about 12 people on the peak of Saturday, which was pretty much perfect for the space.

In a Wicked Age was the star of the show. We ran and played four chapters before I left at 5pm, and every one of them was a home run:

In chapter 1, a mighty gladiator struggled between his desire to lay down arms, and his demon-fed bloodlust, while an eight year old prodigy, possessed by ghosts, was proclaimed prophet.

In chapter 2, a rich but foolish merchant paid the price for meddling with necromancers, prophets, and wizards in the matter of his son's mysterious illness.

In chapter 3, a canny virgin rose from the ashes of her father's empire to become voyeur-priestess in training of the warrior cult.

In chatper 4, a demon-binding monk taught a craven warmaster, a slave-cult leader, a millenium-old demon, and a jaded general the true meaning of "I kick your ass".

Apparently the game went on for quite a while after that because the next morning, the we owe list was filling the page!

Sunday was very quiet and laid back. Joe ran some post-apocalyptic mutant Beast Hunters, and did a demo of Perfect for Johnzo and I.

The lounge was a great success, IMHO. The space seemed a little tight at first, but was actually just right. The only downside was that we were located on the bottom floor past the miniature gaming. We didn't get many role-players wandering in, and I don't think we introduced any newbies to story games. What we did, though, was to make new and stronger connections among the northwest story gaming community.

    \n I wish I'd drawn into In A Wicked Age; I'll have that on my gotta-try list for GPNW.

    But I had a great time regardless. It was wicked cool to meet Ogre, Deke, and Chris, and to see Joe again.

    Saturday night I played a couple rounds of Jungle Speed -- Joe rope-a-doped me and Chris -- plus Ogre's Kegger. I had never, before Saturday, drawn on my childhood experiences in darkest, rural-est Canada while playing a game. That was a strange brain intersection, but it was totally satisfying.

    Sunday was the Joe McDonald Super Blast; I played in his Mutant Killers (Beast Hunters) and Perfect games. I am always ready for Beast Hunters or Gamma World. BH and GW is like ice cream and bacon for me.

    And Perfect, with Tony and Joe, was a gem. The world was so damned evocative and nasty and utterly believable. Pru stuck with me long after play ended. I found myself thinking all last night about her next crime and about her golden comeback -- someday she'll be a rebel dancer again....

    One Perfect question, if a minor derail is okay: is character isolation a feature of Perfect or was that due to our isolated character creation? I realized afterwards that the player characters didn't intersect at all, and I bet they could've had fun together. Plus, it would've been nice to have a counterpart who could bleed off the massive pile of Society Is Pissed Off chips that Joe was menacing me with...

    \n Hi all!

    I'm both glad people who played had a good time and sorry that not everyone got to play the games they were interested in. It's true that the space was far away from other RPGs, which provided it with privacy (good) and isolation (bad). If you don't want to make them public, you can whisper them to Shosuro Kando (RPG Coordinator) and me (Program Manager), or e-mail them to gumby [AT] tlucretius. net and anemone [AT] tlucretius. net.

We really want to make the next SG Lounge even better and learn from this year's experience.

    \n Christian, I did dirty things to your game. The result was Mutant Killers.

    The game was set in a post-apocalyptic wasteland, and the characters were people trained to hunt down mutants. The beautiful part was that they all ended up having crazy mutations themselves, so there was a lot of "those in power decide what is adaptation and what is mutation".

    Instead of the first four character creation steps in the book, there was:

    Step One: The Apocalypse
    Define how your character has been shaped by the harsh landscape of the apocalypse. Talk about the physical landscape, the mutations you've developed, your traumatic childhood, etc.

    Step Two: Tribe
    Just like in Beast Hunters, except all characters are encouraged to be from different tribes.

    Step Three: Your Enemies
    Just like in Beast Hunters, except all characters are encouraged to take traits that label other party-members' tribes as enemies.

    Step Four: The Caravan
    At some point, you got abducted by the big, black, armored van known as the Caravan. You received state-of-the-art mutant killing training. You learned... stuff, I'm sure. At this point, you define what you learned, what you proved, and what gear they kitted you up with.

    They all functioned exactly the same, mechanically, as the steps in the book.

    And then, instead of beast hunts, you were sent to track and kill dangerous mutants. The one they went up against was named The Mind Eater, and he had an annoying voice. They had a social conflict winning over his brainwashed guardian, a mental conflict finding the hidden entrance to his water purification factory, and a physical conflict fighting him and his thralls in the ventilation shaft system.

    Instead of getting tattoos, you eat a mutant's heart to gain his psychic powers. They all gained Mind Arrows MO+1 after killing him.

    It was a crazy, bizarre, awesome game. One of the guys was named High-Fructose Circuiteater, and his tribe was all about installing computer chips in themselves. Another was a famed Lepus rider, which means he rode a giant rabbit into combat. And the third was a little girl who spits acid, and was rejected from the Clean Gene tribe because of his sketchy genetic impurities.

    And that's Mutant Killers.
