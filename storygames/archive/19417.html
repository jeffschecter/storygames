\n About a year ago I wrote The Bureau, a simplified hack of Apocalypse World to play with my little sister.

Sadly, it seems it's still a little too involved for her - and, more importantly, she doesn't own any funky dice.

Having no time to prepare anything, and only two regular dice on hand, I whipped up this hyper-simple Apocalypse World-based fantasy game. (It's two pages, but could easily fit on one.)

I don't claim it to be anything revolutionary; just a back-of-the-envelope draft of something simple to play. But I thought I'd share it with you anyway. Enjoy!

The Silver Dragon's Tear

    Some very young children can easily play, while others, even heading towards the teen years, just don't seem to have the focus or the desire to play such games.
    I've been playing with a group of people in their early 20's (I'm 50 myself), and I have noticed many subtle differences in the way they play and perceive games compared to "our generation" (if I may generalize about your age).

    Firstly and perhaps most importantly, these millennial kids have grown up in a world where roleplaying (as a hobby and as an industry) is a well known thing in the world, something done by many types of people and emulated by computers. Although they're new to TTRPG, they have all been exposed to the basic concepts of RPG through computer games. This means it feels different to them than it did for us when we were starting out - for them it's just one more existing choice on the menu of avocations and social cliques, rather than the "cutting edge" of anything.

    Another difference, often noted in other contexts, is that kids these days do seem to have shorter attention spans in general. Personally I see ADHD as a cultural phenomena. Perhaps an adaptation to the fast pace of information. So in general I find you have to pull them in faster, do things in short bursts, take breaks, give them time to check their phones, etc.

    And do they visualize less? I kinda think maybe they do?

    My younger daughter (age 11) usually joins in the sessions with that (edited for clarity: the early 20's) group, but it's because she likes having a reason to hang out with "the big kids". She isn't interested in playing RPGs in other contexts - either solo or with her own peers. "The Silver Dragon's Tear" is a neat minification and I wish I could talk her into trying it. Or Wildlings. Or Michtim. (The lament of the RPG dad!) But online games like "FeralHeart" and "Minecraft" interest her and her friends a lot more. It's the constant visual feedback, I think.

    \n kurisu,

    Glad to hear! World of Dungeons is also a good bet, of course, although it's fair bit more complex and less flexible than this simpler game. (Which is just as much feature as it is bug, of course. But this game is very intentionally much less dungeon- and combat-focused.)

    I don't think the "danger" of death is as great here as you think it is. For a character to die, first they have to be targeted in some kind of challenge where the clear danger is a lethal threat (as opposed to an injury, like a broken arm or a wounded leg, or just the opponent having the upper hand and winning the day). Then the poor character has to fail badly enough to take a serious, lethal wound. (And this could be a good point to negotiate with the player - or the MC - since you can always interpret "defeat" as something other than a potentially lethal wound!) Third, they have to roll a 6 or less on their "defeat and calamity" roll. Finally, they have to also roll a complete miss on the "recovery" move.

    Now, let's assume that a character has already qualified for the first two conditions: they got themselves into a life-or-death situation and they managed to miss a critical roll. (And presumably didn't use the Six-Fingered Hand of Fate - thanks for the suggestion, Johnstone! Inconceivable that I didn't think of it immediately myself.)

    They still have to fail their "defeat" roll AND then fail their "recovery" roll. Even if they don't have any Qualities or Implements to help them, the odds of failing both rolls are only 17%. If they have a single Quality or Implement which help them, that jumps up dramatically! (To 8% or so.)

    That seems much better than World of Dungeons, from a survivability point of view. (World of Dungeons actually says nothing about death and survival, but it would be natural to assume that attacks deal 1d6 or 1d6+1 damage, which means that any successful attack has a 50% chance of "killing" someone, by reducing them to 0 hit points. A second hit would presumably make that even worse; whereas in Dragon's Tear it doesn't matter how many times you're "hit", only that you lost the fight.)

    But, on top of those odds, we have the Six-Fingered Hand of Fate, which would almost certainly be used on any "recovery" roll when death was on the line! (Of course, it's possible that a player already spent all of their Hand, but in most situations I'd expect a player to have at least one reroll left over...) That improves the odds even further. (For instance, someone spending one Hand of Fate reroll on both the defeat and recovery rolls only has a 4% of dying.)

    I hope that helps! I think you can play pretty fearlessly. And if you don't want to put death on the line, don't: make defeat just that - you lost the fight, you're captured, your nemesis got away, you're washed up on a distant shore the next morning, half-drowned, etc.


    Edit: As an aside, anyone reading through the list of names should notice that it includes references to Albi the Racist Dragon, our current D&D adventures on IRC, Inigo Montoya, from the Princess Bride, and Valavanora, one of the heroes of "Dungeon Girls", which is, in turn, one of the grandest stories of playing World of Dungeons with kids you'll ever find. That's in lieu of a list of inspirations...

    \n Good feedback, Davide, thank you.

    I feel that many of the Qualities have to do with social action (Sage, Wise, Tough, Cunning, Sneaky, Deft, Seasoned, Quick, Learned, Wary, etc - who's to say you're not Deft with words, for example?), but I'll see if I can add a few more without over-filling the page. I hesitate with Qualities like "beautiful", because they aren't something you can generally *do* anything with, just a passive descriptor of how others perceive you.

    As for the Hand of Fate, that's for a few reasons:

    * Erasing can be a pain in the butt, and ruin a sheet of paper, especially when a child does it.
    * Some people only use pen, not pencil, and therefore don't have the option.
    * The growing line of boxes and crosses is this game's nod to "experience": you can look at a character sheet and see how much that character's been played ("Ooh! She's an old veteran!"), which appeals to me.

    \n Awesome stuff, Dave! Thanks. Those are great lists! Excellent.

    The idea behind the Weakness is that we can announce it's causing you trouble, and you add a Fate box. But when you roll, the GM can reroll one of your dice. Let's say you're Lazy, and you're working on a sculpture to present to the King. You say you stay up all night, making it the best work you can! This is a challenge, so we get out the dice.

    Now maybe you say, "Oh, but I'm Lazy, so I guess that might hold me back. I'm adding a Fate box," or maybe someone else says, "Hey, aren't you Lazy? I bet that might hold you back." (I'm thinking the player has to agree to this, so you can effectively ignore the Weakness in heroic moments or when it really counts, but at the cost of future effectiveness.)

    Then you roll the dice, with a +1 for your Tome of the Master Sculptor, and they come up a 3 and a 6, for a total of 10+ (with your bonus from the Tome). "But!" Says the GM, "Your Laziness might set you back. I'm going to reroll that 6." And she does: the die is rerolled as a 3. Now your total is 3+3+1, or 7, so you get some kind of compromise. (If the GM had rolled a 1 or a 2, it would have been a total failure, instead.)

    Obviously, I'll need to reword this somehow. Hmmm... (For now I've just adjusted the text very slightly.)

    The idea is that the GM would reroll the player's highest die, and probably only if it's a 4, 5, or 6. But I thought the GM would figure that out on their own, rather than make it a rule.

    (Part of the issue is that I'd want to fool around with this in play first, to nail down some things, like whether other players should be able to call it into play of if you should always have final say. That's why I'd left it a bit ambiguous for now.)

    \n@Paul_T - The reason for suggesting two pages is of course symmetry (same as the Player's guide) and simplicity: you play with a single sheet as the GM, same as the player(s) do(es).

    About the Players' Guide: I took the liberty of making an alternative version. Check it out - https://dl.dropboxusercontent.com/u/3720638/The Silver Dragon's Tear - alternative Player sheet.pdf and feel free to use it of course (I can send you the doc if you want). I thought it would be nice if the Players would read instructions then flip the page and fill it out - no need to additional paper (unless they want to do something special...). This is why I left the lists in very light gray - so the Players can overwrite them with pens...

    Fixed two typos in it (Learned had an accent in your document - and ardor is without the "u", it's spelled ardour in your doc).

    Back to the GM's guide:

    As the AW's triangles (pc-npc-pc), I think here the combo could be something to provide from the start. When you put an NPC right there to make a triangle, it's stronger then if you form the triangle later on. So I thought: if you start with 2 out of 3, then you start with a bang!

    I would even go further than how I presented it before... I would make 3 tables (personalities, intrusions, tragedies) and strongly recommend that the GM would start by picking or rolling for 2 of them, and do the best to bring those two in game from the very start. Like, in the very first scenes, if possible, by tying these 2 together immediately.

    If the GM can do that, if he can fit the 2 pieces together, then there will be no Players just staring blankly at the results of a tragedy - there will be no mysterious intrusion without any hook to the story - and there will be no personality going blah blah blah for hours, before getting to the point.

    Instead, the GM takes 2 out of 3 - and you have a sharp start just with the work done by putting them together.

    And also, you have a path set for the next step: bring the third into the game with the Characters actions.

    i.e. if you start with tragedy and intrusion, as a first step you might want to set the players on the traces of a certain personality (might it be a good potential help, or an evil master that triggered the tragedy)...

    if you start with intrusion and personality, the next step is the tragedy that results either by the collaboration of intrusion and personality, or by the conflict of intrusion and personality... or the tragedy to avoid...

    if you start with personality and tragedy, you have a classic: your players are after the intrusion (be it the cause of more tragedies, or be it the solution to the tragedy at hand)...

    A lot of this is hinted in your work, but I always felt that if you give a clear structure (especially when it comes to games with short instructions and therefore with a lot of blanks to fill), things work out better. It's better to say take 2 out of 3 as a rule, and let smart GMs figure out their own exceptions, instead of leaving it up to the GM to remember to go and pick also another element...

    I have a bit of an hangover that makes my ideas look unclear even to myself - if the above makes no sense, just trash it :-)
