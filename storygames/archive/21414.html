\n Hi all-- I've been working on a game off and on for about a year inspired by epic science fiction that keeps human conflicts between long-held beliefs at its core. (Most directly inspired by the fiction of Alastair Reynolds, but there's shades of Ursula K. Le Guin and classic Star Trek in there, too). It's been through a number of revisions with my local group, but I'd love to have more eyes on it.

The game is a diceless, GM-less system inspired by any number of storygames but probably most directly by Microscope, Downfall, Ben Lehman's Polaris, and Lovecraftesque. Players collaboratively create a House (a group of near-immortal galactic wanderers) and the values its members have sworn to live by. They then create characters who have their own personal values. Gameplay involves improvising a story together by asking successive questions, building towards a Dilemma, a big decision that puts two values in conflict. How the characters resolve the dilemma might change them and their relationship to their values in the next session.

Any feedback would be appreciated, but I'm especially interested in whether the rules read clearly, especially around building the Dilemma: and if anyone happens to run it, where the pain points were and what your overall experience was like. Thanks in advance!

    \n I really like the clarity of the text (both in terms of writing and in terms of layout), and the attention paid to using little examples or sidebars to help people get through tough spots - those are fantastic!

    It's also nice to see a simple but interesting GMless "space opera" game; I'd say it's a fairly untouched niche, and I'm curious to see this in play.

    Do you want any feedback on the text and/or rules here? I'll assume you do - let me know if I'm wrong - and include a few notes here:

    * "Everyone should be thinking about how scenes are building towards a Dilemma between two Values"

    This sentence, while correct, made me think that *each scene* should be building towards a Dilemma. Might be worth rephrasing, if you can avoid that implication.

    * I was a little confused about the Roles and how they work in small groups. It sounds like everyone has a character to play, except for the Archivist (right?). But then, in any given scene, there is a Narrator, an Epic, and an Intimate, and, ideally, the people playing characters aren't any of those.

    It might be good to include a few examples of how the Roles get assigned, particularly with smaller groups. Is the Archivist only distinct from other players in that they don't have a character?

    * When you talk about constructing a Dilemma, sometimes you refer to placing a Value on the Dilemma sheet, and sometimes about "creating a Threat".

    As far as I can tell, "creating a Threat" just means choosing a Value to be involved in the Dilemma. Is that right? If so, the text is confusing, because I kept looking for what the Threat was and what an example of one looks like. (Your term "threatened Value", used just before that bit, was clearer.)

    Here, too, an example for illustration would really help! It's a little hard to imagine just from a read-through of the rules what it's supposed to look like.

    * "Voting: Continue passing around the role of Narrator as before..."

    This is a little confusing, too. How does this relate to "as before"? Earlier, we were playing out scenes, but now we're just voting. Does each "vote" or question act as a mini-scene, effectively?

    An example and/or some orienting description for the reader would really help here.

    * "The Archivist cannot vote"

    What happens if you have three players, and all have a character? Wouldn't the Archivist's vote be necessary here?

    * Flowchart/order of events

    p. 12 has a really nice chart explaining how to play through a scene.

    I think a similar chart explaining how to play through voting would be really helpful!

    As described, it sounds like it's both very similar to playing a scene and yet, somehow, also quite different.

    I also saw a typo somewhere (a missing verb, I believe), but I can't find it now, I'm afraid. (And it's not the one on the "summary of play" handout, which ends with the word "confl".) It wasn't that important, and it's notable that I only noticed one in the entire document - that shows a high attention to editing/proofreading.

    Looks like a wonderful game!
