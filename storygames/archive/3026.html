\n So I'm sitting down to edit a proof of Grey Ranks when it hit me - when I facilitated the game at Camp Nerdly, I ran it wrong. Not just forget-a-minor-rule wrong, but turn-it-into-a-walking-embodiment-of-the-Czege-principle* wrong. You're supposed to create Mission scenes for other players, not your own. It's fundamental, I wrote the damn thing, and I got it wrong. I've experienced this before. Happily, in this most recent case, I think it proved that at least the game isn't brittle.

Is this my unique failing? People who make games, have you forgotten your own rules?

*The Czege principle says that when one person is the author of both the character's adversity and its resolution, play isn't fun.

    \n This thread just gave me the idea of having a rules checklist during playtesting. After playing, when writing up notes, look over the rules checklist and check those that were used. Don't use the checklist during the game, as you might be encouraged to use some rules you otherwise wouldn't in order to check them off. Then use that to figure out what rules are necessary to the positive play experience, which ones were harmful, and which ones tend to get bypassed/get in the way.

    I don't know how practical this would be, but the idea seems a bit neat.

    \n Not knocking any games in particular, but I think it's obviously a design principle to try to have the rules... I don't know how to put this... suggest themselves. That is, if you can build cues into the rules as to when they're supposed to come into play, linked to other rules, you tend to forget to use them less.
    \n
    \nThe example that I often give is with reward point pools. Where the rule is, "When you like something that somebody does, give them a point" or such. I find that often, even if this gets used occasionally, it also gets forgotten for long periods of time. Using tokens and such helps as reminders. But what works better, it seems to me, is to have one specific time during some process where the rule is, "And now, if this has happened, you can do X." The one event becomes a cue to use the other rule.
    \n
    \nSo, for example with Jason's case, perhaps the process of setting up scenes can be more ritualized (I don't know how it works now), such that part of the process says something like "You then set the scene for the player on your left, unless somebody else spends a point to request that you make the scene for them instead." Vague example, I know, but hopefully you get the point. By having the rule as part of a continuous process - and not just dangling out there by itself - you make it more likely that it won't get forgotten in play.
    \n
    \nWhat is more, often in doing this, you discover a better way for the rule to work, hooking it in with other mechanics in a synthetic way that makes for additional effects that you hadn't planned on originally.
    \n
    \nAnyhow, FWIW, I call this idea Rule Cues. In Universalis, for instance, you can't just do a Complication whenever, there's a very specific condition that, when triggered, requires a Complication (when you try to cause a change in a component that I control with a component you control). This makes Complications flow naturally from the elements in play, a bonus effect to the fact that people tend to recognize better when a complication should happen. The trigger condition - trying to change a component you do not control - is the cue to use the rule.
    \n
    \nIt's not always easy to find a good cue. But try finding ones if you can, and you'll find that such rules tend to get forgotten a lot less.

    \n You really like that point, dontcha, JD?
    \n
    \nHow about wrong meaning, "In direct contradiction to the rules as written, which is what we were hoping to play by?" I mean, this is the designer himself trying to playtest his own game. I think that "wrong" applies just fine in this case.
    \n
    \nMark: I don't know if it's an imperative sort of thing? What you describe is one potentially very effective way to deal with the rules, but not the only one. All I'm saying is that, if you put a rule out there all by itself, often it'll get forgotten in play. If you feel that the rule has good effects, then you might want to consider using a cue to make sure people use it.
    \n
    \nOr perhaps get rid of it. Or maybe it should be "optional" in effect. Consider the encumberance rules from D&D; as the classic example. When a player says, "I grab that, and take it along" in theory that's a cue to do the proper math. But in practice, since the rule must be enforced without any mechanical prompt - and the fact that it's often irellevant - this rule gets set aside. Whether or not encumberance adds to your D&D; game is going to be pretty local to groups (do they even have it in 3rd ed+?)
    \n
    \nIt's usually quite easy to remove rules from play, if you don't like them. It's much harder to get a rule into play if it's not been used. You tend to think, "well, it's been fun so far... why bother with another rule?" A rule pretty much has to "prove" it's worth.
    \n
    \nFor those who find no rule able to prove it's value... there's freeform.
