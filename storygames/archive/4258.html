    \n The rules for Bringing down the pain govern pacing rather than the question if success. Since the system's numerical range is effectively capped from 0 to 7, and practically from 0 to 6, you can really do any of the maths in your head. The fudge/bonus/penalty dice mechanics also lead to two small number additions or subtractions and a comparison of these results in some cases.     The work in all this is in the open phase, where you discuss who does what, and in managing the characters' intentions. But even that gets easier once you realize giving a conflict and coming back for a new goal later can be a superior strategy in comparison with changing intent in mid-conflict.

    Tracking Harm is easier than it looks, it's only one track with six fields, where gaps close after each BDTP.

    \n Hey - your friend is right in that if you get a statistically likely roll, Bringing Down the Pain is not a good way to change the outcome.

    Here's the purposes it serves:

    - To let you change the outcome of a statistically unlikely roll (you roll a -3 and the other guy rolls a 2, for example) when it's important
    - To let you hope that you can change the outcome of a statistically likely roll (by entering into BDTP, spending all your resources, and getting that great roll that harms the other person significantly.)
    - To make killing characters take more than a second.

    \n Official ruling, forever and ever:

    - Player characters can die without BDTP. I know, harsh, right?
    - Named Story Guide characters cannot die without BDTP. You're thinking right now, Clinton, WTF? But...
    - Story Guides should be cautious about throwing around death. Don't do it often. It is useful, though, when you want to get a PC into BDTP.
    - Seriously, if you're a player and your character dies without you going to BDTP, you gave implicit permission. There's few BDTP situations so harsh that you and all your friends, all pushing as hard as possible together, can't get you out of.
    - You can always say "I kill that guy" as your intention or stake or whatever anytime as a player. You just won't quite get there. You'll deal a fatal blow, knock a guy over a cliff, get her at sword-point, or whatever. You'll put someone at your mercy. But if you want to kill them, you have to go through BDTP if you're a regular player.

    \n My personal take: Sometimes you don't want to kill. "I want to humiliate him by showing his dashing swordplay is only for the courts and not for real soldiers." "I want to give him the Mark of the Moon Goddess on his cheek with my dagger." are both good intentions that resolve story-wise by fighting (or rather, with fighty abilities), and don't go to killing.

    Also, if your intent is "kill", you're still free to narrate a dashing back-and-forth of attacks, feints, counter-feints, stunning blows and last-second rebounds, scathing remarks and witty banter\xe2\u20ac\xa6 those aren't just handled in the die roll. As you choose an ability for a check or conflict, you just state what kind of action will be instrumental in achieving intent.

    Regarding Harm as the result of simple conflicts: Your intent can be "I want to break him with my staggering blows." or "I want to hobble him by cutting his achilles heel" and I as SG would interpret that as your opponent getting a broken result on his harm tracker if you succeed against him. This isn't in the book explicitly, but as the SG can set harm as the result of a failed ability check, why not do it in combat.
