    \n I'm not the designer, obviously, but for #2 I'd think it's remaining successes (IE winners - losers).

    For #1, this is actually a mechanic I don't really like, since it's so vague. It shows up all over...
    Basically, I guess, when you set up a roll, frame in your mind at least two outcomes - one is the 1 die, which will be just barely successful. 2 is quite successful, and probably really cool looking.
    At 3+ I'd also offer some additional narrative bonus (like they end up in a good advantageous position, find something helpful, impress some on-lookers, etc...).

    \n The way I usually shorthand it when explaining it to folks is, "At 3 successes or more, you're starting to get into Matrix bullet time stuff."

    I tend to look at those success numbers as absolute. I mean, consider this: your GM comes at you hard, lays down a lot of Pain, gets 5 successes. You push yourself, tap into your Madness, your Exhaustion, and nail that with 6 successes. While the relative degree is 1, that doesn't mean you were "just competent". You got six freakin' successes! Cue the fight between Neo and Agent Anderson in the subway, man. He punched through a pillar to try to get at you, and you moved crazy fast to get out of the way.

    \n The rules text in the book specifies successes, not net successes (winner - loser) and that's how I've always played. Iago (the author) nicely explains above, and I completely concur with that and have had excellent results running it that way dozens of times.

    But the rules are just a tool that you can reshape to fit your hands, so if you want to do something else instead, no one is likely to show up to husk you and cast you into the whispering vault.

    Average humans roll 1 die, the very best roll 3, so 3 successes is as good as humans can do. I point that out to players and give them free rein to narrate what they think 4, 5, 6 should mean.
