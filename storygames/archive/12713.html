\n
\n
\n
\n\nChristopherWeeks \n\n\n

\n
\n Over here, Eero summarized some methods for game-producers to earn income in the post-file-sharing world that came out of that thread. I've been wishing for a long time that I could supply patronage to various creators that I value. I'm not rich, so I can't make a huge difference by myself. But I do buy games that I want to support and donate money to people like Tarn Adams of Dwarf Fortress. And I've been lightly following the development of Indie Fund for indie video game developers for a while.

If there were going to be some kind of distributed patronage entity for indie RPGs, what would it look like? How would it help? Whom would be helped? What would the investors (is that the right word?) get back? What stipulations or agreements between the investors and the investees would make sense? At what scale would this have to work?

Is this just crazy? What would such an organization have to look like for you to help finance it? What would it have to provide to help you make games?

    \n I think that the main issue of making something like this distributed is to make it rewarding to participate in. This means low threshold of participation, clear results, ability to influence the matter perhaps, interesting developments and so on and so forth. It can be creator-originated by having the creator court people and gather his own patrons/audience as we've already seen done now and then (there's that guy with the blog who designs in private but lets his patrons into the process or something like that, right?), or it can be started by the patronly motivated, who then direct their pecuniary power towards philantropy pleasing to them: those designers they want to support.

    It would be pretty rad if somebody started an association or foundation or what-do-you-call-it where the whole idea were for the participants to patronize roleplaying by their own donations and arranging collection drives. Sort of like the Lions Club for geeks. In fact, somebody take the Lions Club recipe and refocus the culture into a gamer/artist/hipster thing while turning the philantropy towards gaming, and I'll be there like the wind.

    Giving out minor grants wouldn't be so sexy, but it would be quite the rush if you could get enough pecuniary power into it to be able to pay somebody a monthly grant, for example. Then the foundation members could make a call for design papers and pick a promising designer to patronize. Perhaps have them report on their progress monthly to keep some overview on the project, and end the grant when the game's finished and published. This is such an obvious model that I'm actually pretty sure that it's been speculated about before somewhere.

    Ultimately the difficulty is that this is no easier than traditional publishing in many ways: while you don't need to market the product to the public, you still need to market it to the patrons. This gets a bit easier with a long-term foundation that develops contacts and prestige among the patronly inclined (I'd say "among the elite", but in the future patronizing shouldn't be the prerogative of the rich and the powerful) and routine in money-raising, but it's still basically just as much a full-time job as publishing is. The artist to be patronized can do the work, but then he's not out there working on his design. Or you can have somebody else do it, but then that guy has to donate his work, or he has to be paid, eating into the efficiency of the system. I imagine that this is the main reason for why we don't have this sort of thing going on already in gaming - this is such a cottage industry that you could get people to jump for ridiculously small sums of money compared to other arts, so if it wasn't so much work to set this up, it would have already been done.

    Still, if one were to start a fund and situate it culturally as a money bin to put money into when you feel bad about your piracy, I could see that having legs. It just needs absolutely impeccable organization and lofty goals to make it seem the worthy operation that it is. Wouldn't hurt to have some fund-raising dinners or something, too.

    For the record, I'm totally on boat if somebody wants to patronize me. One rich patron or a bunch of small fry, makes no difference. I'll make you a character in my next game Eleanor's Dream, in fact, for the right price. Heck, for the right price it can be Christopher's Dream or something, what do I care.

    \n What about a publication co-op with a collective "tip jar". Different designers could pool their efforts to get their games to market, sell yearly game bundles, and profit share.

    I think I like the idea of buying stocks better than pure patronage. What if players purchased "stock" in the form of a gift card from a designer. The designer gets the money now to invest in game design and publication and, at a later date, that card could be redeemed in merchandise. When a designer announces they are working on a new game, they could make a public stock offer to collect money for that game's design and perhaps make those shareholders eligible for personalized copies of that specific game. You could then buy stock and gift it to others and/or trade it for stock in other designers. Designers could even hold shareholders meetings (meetups really) at conventions; being a shareholder would guarantee access to your designer, sneak peaks, playtest sessions, etc. Buying multiple shares could give you greater rewards as you are basically pre-ordering multiple copies in advance; game stores might be willing to buy multiple shares of a hot offering to get copies early for their store. Games could also give a list of shareholders in the credits, in the same way that some games give a list of playtesters, who purchased stock at the initial announcement to fund the project. I dunno, its an idea...

    \n I could also see a group buyin model where you get together about 30 people who all put in $20 apiece. Designers sign up and, after a given amount of time, the committee judges them all and votes on which one gets the pooled money - the catch being that you get one vote for each $20 you donate and when the book is published you get one copy for each $20 you donated. This would be a fun contest, allow people to go to press with a reasonable amount of already made sales, and allow those with buyins to wield their power collectively to make a game happen. For designers, it provides competition to produce the best product to win a block of sales - enough to make going to print a reality.

    \n Honestly, I think you need to start by finding game designers who think additional funding would help them get their games finished. Speaking personally, I know that money isn't the main thing preventing me from finishing games and I suspect that, for many designers, the issue is time (since they have full-time jobs and families and the like), which is something that money can only sometimes help with, when it replaces the need for working additional hours on something to make ends meet.

    I know Elizabeth got that $1000 grant to work on Blowback and that was really helpful for her, so there is definitely places where money can help, but I imagine that they are on a designer-by-designer basis. Sure, we all like money, but you really have to be careful -- like any grant-making agency -- that the money you're throwing at people actually helps them make progress.

    \n I think that you can still do acts of patronage without "supporting the artist" like some 17th century king or noble.

    For example, I have a friend who has recently flexed his wings with game design. But he simply doesn't have the cash (or the time to raise the cash) to do even a limited POD print run.

    So I stepped up and am going to fund a small 50-copy print run of his book. He keeps all the money, and pays me back for the cost of the book when the money for orders of the book come in.

    Then, with the rest of his money, he can either grab some dinners, or else fund his own next print run.

    Posted By: AndyI think that you can still do acts of patronage without "supporting the artist" like some 17th century king or noble.
    \nThis is 1000% true.

    Ben Lehman and Vincent Baker funded my very first booth buy-in at GenCon '08 as an act of quiet patronage. I've been hyper-conscious of the need to pay it forward ever since. Same with the DivNull Lark and my transparency blog. Plus I'm going to do layout for a new female designer's first game for free, and I'm helping another get their text into shape.

    I guess my point is, the community here is incredibly, profoundly generous to people who give back generously. There are lots of ways to do patronage, including support that isn't specifically financial\u2014 offering help that would normally cost the person a lot of money is something that happens fairly often around here. While money is a big barrier to a lot of folks, I think that Jonathan's right and there are larger barriers that could perhaps be solved with a different kind of patronage. (Not that I can write Jonathan's grad papers on religion in China for him so he can finish Geiger Counter, but you get the idea.)

    I've watched a friend's kid so they could finally get some writing done. People have sat down and given me an incredibly valuable crash-course in document structure. I know this isn't strictly what the OP was talking about, but if the idea is to support people in their artistic pursuits, it's something to think about.
