    \n In past play, I've seen them hand-waved as crossbows. That worked just fine.

    To make them distinct, choose one or more of the following:
    • limit which classes can use them
    • make them unreliable (lower their proficiency bonus or give them a recharge requirement)
    • make the ammo expensive
    • change the rate of fire (either no reload / slower reload / recharge to reload)
    • increase their damage (gah, don't, really)
    • change the defense against them (instead of AC, make it Reflex)
    • change the critical to something cool (like ongoing damage)
    \nSome of these will change the balance of your game significantly.

    \n The thing is, in 4E, the weapon has absolutely nothing to do with which defense it targets. It's all about the power you use. Some rogue powers, for example, target AC, and others target Reflex, but you're using a dagger all the same. And one's reflex can potential be higher than one's AC, so reflex defense isn't quite the same as a touch defense from 3E.

    Reflex represents dodging and such, while AC represents general defensive ability, being able to stay out of line of sight, presenting a smaller target to your opponent, protection from armour, etc. Remember that it's not about dodging the bullets, it's about the shooter hitting with them in the first place, and we're not talking relatively accurate modern firearms here.

    \n If you want to introduce guns in a way that doesn't totally change the balance of weapons in the game, treat them like crossbows or give them a quirky encounter or daily power or something. You can try to play balance games by making them more powerful than crossbows (ongoing damage is one way) but nerfing them in some other way (reload isn't much different than crossbow, which is why I went with a recharge). Matchlocks and flintlocks are a pain in the ass to use and a lot of people at the time wanted to throw them away and just use their recurve bows or crossbows, which were easier to use, were more reliable, had better range, and were more accurate. Civil War muzzle loaders weren't that much better (I mean, they were a vast improvement but had similar problems).

    If you want to fuck balance for thematic reasons, don't sweat it and just up the damage or add side-effects like ongoing damage. A good Winchester rifle is going to be a game-changer. It's going to be faster to fire and deadlier than a crossbow, with much better range.

    Metal armor is still going to be an effective defense against firearms. The Reflex defense stuff was a fun diversion though.

    \n I don't know if we're rambling on after Ryan's gotten what he's needed out of the thread.

    For the game I played in, we hand-waved guns as crossbows because it wasn't important. We wanted guns in the setting, which was a little steampunky, but it wasn't the main focus of play in any way. No one used a crossbow because why not use a gun, which was "cooler"? Noise of guns was never an issue (or a disadvantage).

    Any discussion of advantages and disadvantages based on realism will eventually have to tackle the essential question: Are these guns actually better than crossbows? A lot of people at the time (I mean 17th Century) didn't think so. They were curiosities, at first. They were finnicky. Technology improved and for that and many other strange, historical reasons, people chose guns over crossbows. But don't just assume that all firearms are better, especially if you need stealth, reliability, speed, and so on. Matchlocks in particular *sucked*. I'm not an expert on this stuff, by any stretch of the imagination, but I did a little research for my Oxford 1605 campaign. <=)

    In the end, you choose the "truth." Decide whether or not you want guns to be more dangerous than crossbows and what you perceive the disadvantages are. Choose some rules from the bullet list I offered above and put together a package for players to play with. Change it if it doesn't work.

    OK, so I definitely don't want the realism that drove armor out of the military world. I want a bit of a sparkly damage bump, but not to get into the guts of what works or doesn't work in D&D.;
    \n
    \n
    \nTo wax pedantic for a moment, its a myth that firearms drove armor out of the military world. The centralization of armies in the hands of kings, the increase in the size of those armies, the slide down the social scale from aristocratic warriors to lower class soldiers, and the development of commerce and finance (and the focus on profit and expense that brought) is what drove armor out of the military.
    \n
    \nBluntly put, its just not cost effective to spend a fortune protecting the lives of expendable peasants.
    \n
    \nThat firearms were increasing in importance at about the same time is coincidence, although largely driven by the same algebra. Learning to fire a arquebus was vastly simpler, less specialized, and able to be performed by a wider range of people than learning to fire a bow.

    \n Ralph and Adam are right in terms of realism. In fact, if you feel that damage of weapons in the game should be proportional to something like kinetic energy, then actually guns should do less damage than crossbows. For realz, yo.
    \n
    \nBut, that said, assuming that the world in question is still magical, fantasy, that sort of thing, then firearms should in fact be adjusted to be what you want them to be. For a Tolkienesque approach, make them dangerous not only to the target, but to the user. Thematically, you could have it where a character has to sacrifice other "real" skill to degrade himself to using such brutally simple weapons. Just an example.
    \n
    \nFigure out what role you want firearms to play thematically, and make the rules fit that theme. Trying to make them "realistic" in D&D would first require that you remove the hit point system...
    \n
    \nForget about it, and just make them fun in the context of the game. Make them deliberately unrealistic if you have to.
