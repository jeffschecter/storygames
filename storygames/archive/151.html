Setting/System Disconnects, and How To Fix Them

Josh Roby
February 2006 edited February 2006 in Story Games
My wife and I lusted after and bought the Blue Rose rulebook immediately after it was released because the game setting was powerfully intriguing. For those of you who don't know, it's a setting based off of "Romantic Fantasy" which, in short form, is Girly Fantasy Novels. So there are psychic powers, lifebonded animals, an emphasis on romance, winged cats, and the like. The centerpiece of the setting is the Kingdom of Aldis, an enlightened nation based on principles of acceptance of difference and equality of all people -- male, female, straight, gay, of whatever race and creed et cetera. It's like the fantasy version of the Federation.

This thread is not intended to comment on the viability or appopriateness of that setting. If you want to talk about how the book obsessed about gay people, go start your own damn thread.

This very intriguing setting was then hitched up to a d20 variant, True20. So the setting and color and focus of the game kept talking about making peace between agitated parties, spreading acceptance and equality, and living up to your ideals. And the system gave you tools for killing monsters. In a game that was supposed to be all about how you related with your fellow man, there was little in the way of support for creating, building, and manipulating those relationships. There were some rudimentary social mechanics, but they were, well, rudimentary, and had more than making people do things than convincing them of what you believed.

My wife and I have since talked about using a modified Dogs in the Vineyard dice system to play in the Blue Rose setting -- thereby allowing the characters to roll into a village in trouble and set things right, or confront big bad necromancers not on the basis of my +2 sword but on the basis on my beliefs (maybe backed up by my sword).

In a parallel case, I wrote up a little microgame called Shadows and Strings that attempts to take what the World of Darkness promised (dark supernatural conspiracies from the shadows of the modern world) and actually deliver (as opposed to pumping your vampire powers to kick someone's ass).

Has anyone else experienced similar situations where what the book promised was not supported by the system it offered, and you either munged the system or created a new system entirely to get you what you wanted and hadn't got from the book?

    Usually I don't. If I'm not playing a system as-is, I'll usually generate my background from non-gaming sources or whole cloth; and adapt some system to fit. I think the closest case of this was my Vinland campaign. I started from RuneQuest because I liked the basics for the feel of the campaign, and also it had a decent Vikings sourcebook. However, I ended up making a lot of adjustments to the rules. I've got a page summarizing that:

    http://www.darkshire.net/~jhkim/rpg/vinland/about/rules.html

    Mostly those were big simplifications down. I added Whimsy Cards which were pretty popular. I replaced completely the Wealth system and the magic system.

    By the way, I checked out Blue Rose a while ago and wrote a capsule review -- putting it at above average but pointing out various problems. Since then I've playtested the system, and was even more disappointed in it. (I've also read some Mercedes Lackey, which I hadn't when I wrote the review.) Anyhow, I've got some material here:
    http://www.darkshire.net/~jhkim/rpg/bluerose/

    As for better systems for it... Hmm. Dogs in the Vineyard seems like a poor fit to me. The inherent logic of the system is that if you can't get something by talking, you can always escalate to violence to improve your chances. That's not true to my understanding of the Romantic Fantasy genre. There are times when you need to use violence, while at other times using violence is absolutely the wrong answer and escalating is a mistake. In Dogs, characters engage in violence and risk death by choice via escalation. In Romantic Fantasy, characters usually engage in violence when they are forced to, though they shine under pressure. Put another way, I feel it's an aggressive, conflict-rewarding, masculine system.

    That said, I'm not sure I know of a base system which would work well for it. One thing is that Romantic Fantasy as a genre has a central protagonist and a bunch of strong supporting characters. This suits a structure like Ars Magica or Buffy where there are strongly differentiated PCs rather than the more typical ensemble of equals.

    At this point, I can't stand that kind of disconnect. So I write a system. Unfortunately, this turns out to take months at best.

    I think the issue is this: figure out what the setting you like is about. Then look at how designers answer what their game is about and look for a match.

    Dogs is about the consequences of decisions about violence as a moral authority. Shock: is about challenging the social ethics of the players. The Mountain Witch is about loyalty in the face of contradictory desires (which, after all, lead to suffering). D&D is about tactical application of resources to gain more resources to be spent strategically on tactical resources. My Life with Master is about being a terrible bureaucrat to retain your humanity.

    And so forth. Find something that matches. I bet something does.

    Answering Joshua's original question, I'm experiencing this disconnect right now with the Legend of the Five Rings (3rd edition) campaign I'm running right now. While the back cover blurb proclaims, "Honesty. Courage. Compassion. Courtesy. Sincerity. Duty. Honor. In Rokugan these are more than simply words," the system that actually deals with these concepts is under-developed. There are plenty of rules and kewl powerz but for reinforcing (or enforcing) the Japanese samurai/bushido mindset and behavior of the setting, I think it falls on its face.

    Really, if the the designers had gone the route Pendragon did with the knightly virtues I think it would have been a lot better. As it is, you really have to dig through a poorly laid out (although pretty) set of rules to find out what's up with Honor and Glory and such, and then when you get there, the rules for them are, IMHO, uninspired. Those things should have been front and center, and there should have been no doubt whatsoever that is what playing a character in this game setting is about. Instead, you get pages and pages of stuff that mostly winds up being modifiers to your to hit roll.

    My players (bless 'em) are doing just fine in terms of having their characters behave according to the tenets of bushido without implementing those rules (in fact I find myself ignoring them in play, so little is their actual affect on the game).

    It's a shame because so much could have been done there. If the game were truly about the tenets of bushido, I'd've thought the rules regarding those things would have been front and center in the system. But any such space is taken up by yet more kewl powerz.

    Anyway, we're going to finish this campaign out, but I very much doubt I will use the L5R ruleset again (if I use them again) without implanting a strong set of Pendragon-ish type rules to deal with bushido and how following it's concepts affects the characters in play.

    So, thumbs down to AEG for missing the chance to really tie the setting to the system here.
