    \n I hope I don't sound like too difficult, but, well, for one, I'm not really on the market for another 200+ Euro device. I just bought a (REALLY cool!!!) laptop in the last six month, so my gadget account is somewhat empty. (Of course, this might well change when I get my hands on an EEE or eReader or whatever and suddenly realize that my life has been incomplete without one. ^_^ )

    But, besides monetary issues, a large problem I have with the whole ebook thing are the rights aspects. I don't want to license my books. I don't want my file format or my reader telling me what I can or can't do with a file. I don't want all that hassle and I don't really want to support that. So a Kindle is right out and while I'll look at other ebook readers (Like I said, I love the digital ink technology.), at the moment, the best thing for "difficult" people like me is a small-ish laptop.

    Maybe this will change. I certainly hope it will. But we're not there yet and there are a couple of things that would need to change for me to enter the ebook reader market.

    \n I own a Sony prs-505 reader. I bought it soon after it was released in the US, and have absolutely no regrets about purchasing it.
    \n
    \nI've tried reading ebooks on laptops and other small portable devices (clie, etc) before. This blows them out of the water. Form factor wise, it's about as close to holding a book as it gets. Everything else is either too big (laptop) or too small (pda/cellphone). The battery performs amazingly as well. A PDA at a few hours can't hold a candle to a device that can do 4k page turns off a charge. The eInk screen has this kind of neutral almost paper like appearance to it that makes it much more pleasing than looking at the typical small form factor LCD screen. It also doesn't get hot, or make any strange whine/hiss noises either, which is a nice bonus.
    \n
    \nI've been aware for ages of the huge supply of good material available for free at Gutenberg and similar sites, and have even read a few from there. But the experience always left me wanting. Sitting at my desk, or laying in a funky position on the couch is not the same as curling up with a book. With a reader, you pretty much get that. In the past, I've purchased a few books that I knew were available on Gutenberg, just because I hated reading on my laptop. For a while, I was using the library system and their online hold functionality, but the drive back and forth became annoying. For me, the Reader made for a good solution.
    \n
    \nThe different devices often do have their own proprietary native formats, but they take non-DRM'd standard formats like PDF, txt, and html as well. Also, even as the native formats are usually proprietary, they're rarely closed; there are free and legal tools for conversion to the format. I use libprs500 on Mac for my conversions to .lrf (the Sony Reader file format). There's stuff out there for the mobi format, as well as a host of others.
    \n
    \nThe only "bad" news is, these devices usually don't render PDF designed for screen very well. If I try to read RPG PDFs I've purchased, probably 3/4 have text so small it's practically unreadable. Resizing on the fly is not such a great option now, either (I've heard upcoming firmware to my device might solve that). In my mind, this problem is really less that of the device than it is of current publishing practices that tie style to content. For electronic text publishing formats, I think there needs to be a push towards a new solution (yes, I know about and use LaTex) to separate the content and style of such documents. I mean, imagine if CSS had never done that for the web...ugh...
    \n
    \nAnyway, short summary, if you like reading and have the cash, I'd highly recommend looking into them.
    \n
    \nOh, one final thought. I know there's lots of independent publishing thinking in these parts, so just imagine for a second, if these devices were to proliferate. You'd have an awesome new medium to deliver your product (game tomes, novels, short stories, comics...) to, direct.
