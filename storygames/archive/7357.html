\n I think it's really cool when there's a moment of silence or contemplation in a game... when everyone just draws back for a second and thinks, "woah". Also, having one of those "woah" moments interrupted by someone saying, "Yeah! AND THEN A SPACE MONKEY ATTACKS YOU!" is pretty disappointing.

Do you have moments of engaged silence in game?
When do they come about?
How do you sustain them?
How do you signal to other members of the game that this, this moment... it is one of those moments? (ie, what's the social contract of silence, and how is it enforced?)

    \n Okay, first things first: If someone is introducing space monkeys then not everyone is going "Woah."
    \n
    \nI've experimented with this, and I don't know that "sustain" is something that I've seen done in any way that didn't feel gimmicky. Usually, the important part (for my money) is to flag people to consider it as a possible "Woah" moment, and then let them choose their own length of silence, using social cues more deeply encoded in our makeup than any game is likely to supplant.
    \n
    \nExample: I had a game in which I was using a "raise a finger for silence" rule, and someone said (of a rival political figure in a decadent court) "Oh, that man will be the death of me!" I raised a finger, and one by one around the table people went from "Hey, what's Tony pausing us for?" to "Oh shit ... what if she's right?" Once everyone saw that everyone had got it, any further silence would (in that case) have been unnecessary.

    \n In the original version of The Believers I wrote for the Role Playing Poem Competition, there was an entire minute of silence in the middle of the game so the players could contemplate what had happened so far. I think this was what Jason was alluding to.

    But when we playtested it, Jackson Tegu noted that as the person who was watching the clock, it was difficult for him to really relax and take advantage of the silent break.

    So in the revised version of the game, each player simply places a hand on the table when they are done contemplating. The game continues when everyone has a hand on the table. I playtested this again recently with a small three player game, and it worked much smoother. And the break still ended up being around 45-50 seconds long.

    After having played The Believers several times, I think this enforced break is a powerful part of the game and sets the mood for the alien visitation to occur next.

    Hey, I just remembered a "best practices" of my own relating to silence.

    I was playing in this Sorcerer game set in your average North American high school in the suburbs. I was this conflicted senior: gay, but in the closet; film geek, but dad is forcin' him to join the track team; brilliant, but a complete outcast. And there was this moment where my ex-girlfriend (the only one in the school who I'd come out to) threatens to tell everyone in an act of petty revenge, and I knew that my character would have this savage, SAVAGE biting remark. But I'm not as quick as he is, so I said "give me a minute". People threw out suggestions, but I said "give me a minute" again and they held their piece. And then I got it (!), and I resumed character and delivered this powerful, grabby line. And it was way better than whatever would have filled that space, and it worked because I commanded both their patience and their attention.

    I think the two lines were along the lines of...
    Ex-girlfriend: Hey, I really like this guy in the drama club, but he might be gay. I was wondering... since you're gay... maybe if you could... figure it out?
    (long, pregnant thinking pause)
    My emo kid: Listen, you're the expert on dating gay guys, not me.

    Which doesn't read like anything much, because it's mostly context and inflection... but I tore her fictional ass to shreds with that one.

    To clarify, this is an example of when *commanding a silence* was crucial, not of a specifically effective technique for doing so.

    It may be that "mucking about" is a less than polite way of expressing yourself in English. :-)

    I would, of course, talk to my players in a polite way, telling them that the game will be focused and serious, and ask them to refrain from breaking up any silent or serious scene with jokes. After they give me the OK on this, we will commence play.

    Does that sound ok to you?

    And by the way: I do not consider it part of the "social contract". People tend to talk too much of this "contract", and putting too much of the game method into it. In my view the "social contract" pertains to playing for fun in a RPG, and playing for victory in a boardgame. That's about it. The agreement to play low-key and serious is part of the game method, in my view. :-)
