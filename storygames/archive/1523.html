The Purpose of Applied Theory

Christian_Griffen
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Play Advice
The point of applied theory is to consciously do things with a purpose.

We can apply gaming theory in two fora: design and play.

So what is theory in this regard? It's a framework for understanding the dynamics at play. It consists of definitions, categories, and patterns that allow us to talk about what, why, and how things happen at the table. These frameworks are not "true" -- they are just more or less useful in understanding what's going on and predicting as well as helping to bring about certain events and dynamics.

First you understand, then you use that understanding to play or design with a purpose.

What's the alternative? Just play or design without clearly understanding what you're doing or why you're doing it (not in the abstract "because it's fun" way, but in detailed decision-by-decision patterns). For some people, this is attractive for a variety of reasons. Just like most people don't want to understand magicians' tricks or study literary or musical theory for fear of losing the magical enjoyment of the respective media. That's a valid play preference.

But I believe in designing with a purpose, and I personally prefer playing with a purpose.

For play, once I understand what I enjoy and what dynamics enable me to play that way, I can focus on that and play with a purpose. If my whole group does that, we've achieved a focus and intensity that I contend is near-impossible to attain without the understanding that underlies playing with a purpose. It does take away some of the magic of the early days of my roleplaying, though, and I won't get that back, ever.

For design, once I understand how play dynamics work and which mechanics, rules, textual and optical elements, etc., support certain dynamics, I can design with a purpose. My design will be more focused and will contain less white noise. If this design is then used by a play group, I contend that the group will achieve a focus and intensity it would have a harder time achieving with a less-purposeful design (because the white noise gets in the way).

If you ask me about any aspect of one of my designs, if I did my job right, I'll be able to give you an answer that shows that I had a clear reason, one that ties into the dynamics of play that I want to encourage or enable.

Combine the two for a cumulative effect.

In conclusion: Whether or not you want to play with a purpose is a personal preference, and telling others that "they don't understand their own fun" shows a lack of understanding in itself. But design with a purpose is the professional responsibility of any designer.

My hope is that, in all these discussions about "theorists" and "anti-theorists," this point of view might help to defuse and clear things up a bit.

    Totally. I think most artistic development follows this pattern:

    1. You do whatever-it-is (in my case, scenic painting, 2D drawing) out of interest or desire. Maybe you have some talent, maybe you have a lot of talent, whatever.

    2. You get some training (in my case, undergraduate level courses). You learn technique and, dare I say it, theory (perspective, color theory, proportion, etc). It's hard, because you're learning new stuff, you're learning new ways to think about your activity, and you're trying to make the stuff in your head have something to do with what you're doing on paper. You may actually start doing whatever-it-is worse, because you worry or think about all the theory and technique stuff, where before you just did it.

    3. You practice, you learn, and you absorb the material. It becomes part of how you approach the activity, and now your level of talent, whatever it is, in supported by what you've learned from your critical thought about the activity. You've synthesized, and your output is better than in 1 and 2.

    This is basically how I feel I have developed since I started critically thinking about playing RPGs. I think the addition of the social element of the activity adds nuances to the process, but it's a fundementally similar, if not the same, basic track.

    Instinct is merely internalised experience. I rely heavily on instinct, I don't kid myself that it comes magically from nowhere though, my reliable instincts come from experience and my most reliable instincts from understood experience.

    Anti-theory in the other threads is merely shorthand for people opposed to GNS theory, many of them are active proponents of other theories. There are people who are opposed to any theory or the very concept of theory, but I think that has relatively little to do with the merits or otherwise of any given theory under discussion.

    Otherwise, Nathan summarises things well. It goes like this:

    Unconcsious incompetence. You're shit, and you don't know it.

    Conscious incompetence. You're still shit, but now you know you are. Ouch.

    Conscious competence. You can be not shit, but you have to think about it in order not to be. Ouch again.

    Unconscious competence. You're not shit and you don't need to think about it anymore.

    Stages two and three are pretty painful, but this is a commonly recognised learning model for a wide range of activities. I see no reason to think this doesn't apply to roleplaying. If theory helps you get to stage three or four, go for it.
