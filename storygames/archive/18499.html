    Sometimes a protagonist wants to manipulate another character. Sometimes an antagonist wants to hurt a protagonist. In order to move the story forward, sometimes characters have to affect change on other characters. This is when we reach for dice. The dice help us tell the story of how these actions play out and what their consequences are.
    An action is an attempt to use one of the character\u2019s verbs to alter the course of the narrative by impacting another character. This is done by applying new adjectives to characters or by removing existing adjectives. Since each adjective represents a fact in this fictional world, you can change the world\u2014ever so slightly\u2014with every adjective you add to it or subtract from it. This is a system for manipulating characters, sometimes subtly, often violently.
    p.86

    \n Yeah! Man, I really think maybe there was something wrong with our play, because every tone I think about this system, I get do jazzed about it, but we just couldn't make it work somehow. Anyway, here's the cool part!

    In order to make an adjective sticky(lasts until the end of the scene, like unconscious), you have to give the GM one of your spent push dice(extra dice you can spend to add a die to any roll; refreshed each new scene). To make it locked(lasts indefinitely until circumstances change via special adjective removal rules, like crippled, or even dead!), you have to give her two!

    Same rules apply to the GM, so there's this lovely back-and-forth, push-and-pull aspect to the game, where in order to really affect the outcome of the story, you have to make yourself vulnerable to real, lasting harm.

    \n I tried Technoir over Skype, voice only, it was a single session.

    The dice system didn't slide into the narrative very well for me. It's a similar disconnect I have with Dogs in the Vineyard. I love the setting, the set-up, the chargen, the stories we want to tell. But once we hit the dice, the mechanics are all that happens, or they override how I see things actually happening in my mental image of what's going on. Then we end the conflict and jumble things around so it makes sense again.

    That's my personal experience with it. I still have the book, I may mine it for how Transmissions are set up and the whole mystery engine or whatever where NPCs become more important as PCs interact with them, that's neat. But the fixed adjectives thing just relied too much on "being creative with word play" for me. Again, the same trouble I have with FATE, so that's my baggage. I prefer mechanics that drive fiction, or tell me how they do things in the world.

    I hope the above is at all coherent.

    \n Here's my favorite fleeting/sticky/locked example: "Disarmed".

    Fleeting: You've knocked her gun out of her hand. If she takes a round, she can pick it up and use it again, but she can't shoot at ya until she does.

    Sticky: You've knocked the gun out of her hand and off the side of the building into traffic six stories down. She can buy a new gun or otherwise arm herself between scenes, but for now, she's outta luck.

    Locked: You chop off her arm.

    Adjectives are real in the system, as real as the cyberwear in your body, your experiences as a child soldier in the Coltan Wars, or the stolen motorcycle you rode in on. In my experience, the best way to give the system oomph is to play up the impact of adjectives on your Heavies (and especially Mooks!), and have them give/retreat/surrender when it narratively makes sense, not just when the odds are ridiculously against the NPC due to stacked adjectives. And only one negative adjective is enough to take out a mook, three is enough for most heavies. If the GM doesn't treat adjectives as impactful, the players won't either, and the game's just sorta mooshy and unfun.
