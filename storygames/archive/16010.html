    \n Depends on the game, because different games require different operative parts to be present in a character for the game to be functional. A beginner-friendly game is one where you never ask them to "make a character", but rather merely guide them through a set of discrete, concrete steps that result in a character having been realized to whatever extent is necessary for the game at hand. Thus, a beginner-friendly game will either not require much realization from characters, or whatever amount of requires, it reliably accomplishes via its character creation procedure.

    Conversely, the worst game from this viewpoint is the one that relies on an intuitive, higly developed sense of what "creating a character" entails. These games are almost always immersive traditional designs where you spend a lot of time e.g. buying powers for your character, but this process is ancillary to what you should really be doing: the game fails if you don't have a complete and entertaining personality to play when the time for that comes. If you're running one of these, I recommend explaining to the player that he should first develop an imaginary image of his character, and then go through the many layers of "buy things from lists" with a firm image of his character concept in mind: pick whatever things at first glance match your character vision, move through quickly and hold onto a strong image of what your character is like. Tell about it to the other players, too, so they know to play to it to whatever degree they're able and the game allows.

    \n A. Give them a partially generated character with 3-4 decisions remaining. Have one of these decisions be their name, and at least one decision be mechanical.

    B. Give them a pre-generated character to play for the first session (if this is a campaign), and then offer them the opportunity to design a new character for future sessions, now understanding how the system works.

    C. Have everyone play with pre-generated characters.

    D. Play a sample scene and sample conflict out before creating characters.

    \n Mease19, that's the mental prompt I've used for a lot of my players over the years.

    Especially the ones who come into a game and just want to play a clone of a specific character from popular culture in an inappropriate genre (or those who keep playing clones of the same pop-culture individual over and over..."What's Vin Diesel going to be in this game???").

    It breaks people out of their habits and gets them thinking a bit...but usually not so much that it confuses them (If it confuses them, is this really the kind of person you want to game with).

    \n I was at a convention last year running an Apocalypse World one-shot, kind of as a last-minute stopgap.

    We'd divvied up playbooks, and people started filling them out. I asked if anyone needed help, and showed a couple of people how to follow the steps on the sheet.

    When we'd finished, and just before we started doing Hx, I remembered it'd be a good idea to introduce ourselves as people too. The woman accross the table from me introduced herself and said she'd never played a roleplaying game before. She'd made her character from the rules on the sheet, with no trouble at all.

    Maybe she was just awesome, but I think mostly it's that it's not that hard if you just make clear steps with simple decisions at each point. Choosing is easier than inventing.

    Posted By: McdaldnoA. Give them a partially generated character with 3-4 decisions remaining. Have one of these decisions be their name, and at least one decision be mechanical.
    \nThis one's cool too. I reckon it's structurally equivalent to classes - any degree of preconfiguration can be a class.


    So, to combine the two together, create a set of characters that are almost-half-configured and have associated traits. Players pick two archetypes, combine them, and then add some polish - assign the last couple skills and make the traits make sense together. In a system that already pre-assigns pretty heavily, you'd have to do some reverse engineering to decompose the classes and make it work, but it's still possible. If you wanted to preserve the classes, though, you'd have to use a more naive combination and pre-assign characters within classes and then combine archetypes for personality hints.
