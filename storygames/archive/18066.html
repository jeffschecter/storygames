\n Last night, I got together with some of my non-gamer friends to, simply, "play a game". I trotted out a few options, and then said I had a game experiment in mind which was more involved, if they were interested. I pitched it to them as "creating a pilot for a new teen supernatural drama", and then pulled out a Fiasco playset I made especially for Monsterhearts, It All Ends in Tears. They were sold pretty much immediately. One of the players seemed on the fence, and then read through a bit of the playset and said, "Oh, man! So much dysfunction. Ok, I'm in!"

Here's how it went down, for people curious about the setup process. Next, I'll post what we came up with as a result, and then finally ask you for some points of advice on where to go next.

We spent most of the evening essentially brainstorming through the setup of the game, not unlike a PTA pitch session, and only played through a handful of scenes. We were interrupted on a couple of occasions by some unfortunate emergencies, and had to stop the game, then start up again. People were having so much fun riffing off the elements provided them by the game (the playset options, the Skins, and the Sex Move / Darkest Self descriptions all gave the players solid inspiration) that I had to work hard to rein it in and get to actually playing.

So, step by step (and much more to come later)...


*** First step: the playset setup.

Here are the Elements we generated between pairs of characters. We ended up with a lot more Elements than originally planned because a fourth player joined in towards the end, so we rolled eight more dice to tie his character in (I figured rolling a small number of dice wouldn't create enough meaningful choice). We also, on a couple of occasions, rolled for details from the playset when we needed inspiration or felt like another detail would be helpful (essentially using the playset as a random generator when we weren't sure what should come next).

A Quick Note: To help guide the process, I chose a playbook for an "MC character" (the Queen) so I could go through the setup with the players and explain what choices would be made at any given point. However, in the end, Cordelia the Queen remained an NPC, not a PC, unless another player (who had to cancel) joins us next time.

Relationship map at the end of the Setup


*** Second step:

Choosing Skins. We choose the Vampire for Cassius, the Werewolf for Flinch, the Mortal for Lilith, and the Queen for Cordelia.

(Obviously, we chose the names after choosing Skins, not before--i.e. at this point in the process. But using the names above [i.e. in step one] seemed easier then writing this all out with placeholders like PC 1, PC 2, etc., and then making you, the reader, figure out which corresponds to which afterwards. So I included the names we ended up with in the character pairs above.)

Here are the relevant details we got from the Skin choices:

Cassius (Vampire)
* Someone saved your unlife (we quite naturally and intuitively combined this with "a murder by accident, and the person you thought you killed")

Lilith (Mortal)
* Has a lover/True Love

Flinch (Werewolf)
* Has been observing someone for a while, their scent is unmistakable to him now... (we decided this obviously tied in to "stalker and object of powerful obsession")

Cordelia the Queen, once again, turned into an NPC at this point. We rolled a Reputation for her, and chose "scandalously promiscuous".


Reading through the Sex Moves and Darkest Selves inspired everyone quite a lot during this phase, as well.

We then talked about each Element in turn and decided how it would be interpreted. (For example, Flinch and Lilith are "infamous exes". So, is Flinch still her lover, or has she rebounded to someone new since the breakup?)

Next up... the result of the brainstorm/setup/pitch.

(If you want to, take a shot at what this setup would suggest to you, we can compare notes afterwards!)

    Main cast:

    * Cassius, newly turned vampire
    * Flinch, bisexual werewolf in his last year of school... once again
    * Lilith, Flinch's old flame

    Secondary characters:

    * Cordelia, most hot/popular girl at school and lacrosse team captain, as well as the daughter of the mayor. Close friends/rivals with Flinch. She knows he's some kind of monster but accepts him anyway.
    * Jenny, annoying cheerleading captain and part of Cordelia's gang. [Appeared in play, not from setup.]
    * Joe, a senior and a jock, feared by many. Not too bright. Looks up to Flinch (and, according to Flinch, smells like vanilla cream). [Appeared in play, not from setup.]

    * Mr. Jacobs, the new ethics teacher. [Appeared in play, not from setup.]
    * Mrs. Lloyd, the old ethics teacher, disappeared mysteriously
    * Slayde Cooper, the Mayor, Cordelia's father. A large, imposing man built like a bear.
    * Slayde Cooper's wife (unnamed so far) [Appeared in play, not from setup.]
    * Cassius's single mother, local waitress (unnamed so far) [Appeared in play, not from setup.]
    * Lilith's single father, used car salesman, rather clueless (unnamed so far) [Appeared in play, not from setup.]

    Backstory

    Lilith and Flinch were an item when doe-eyed Lilith was still in middle school (and Flinch was already well into his rather drawn-out high school career). However, when Flinch realizes he's got this monstrous rage in him, he decides he has to push her away before he hurts the one person he loves.

    Flinch breaks up with Lilith in a horrible, humiliating, and very public way. Their breakup is now legendary at the high school. ("Infamous exes")

    Flinch and Lilith have some history we haven't entirely established: something significant happened between them in one of her father's cars, a dented old Buick with a broken trunk (which is beginning to smell rather disgusting, but the lock on the trunk is broken and it won't open).

    Closer to the present time, Lilith meets Cassius, this cool, beautiful but somewhat distant kid who doesn't seem to fit in. She falls in love with him and they start getting involved with each other. (Lilith chooses Cassius as her lover.)

    One day, his thirst for blood pounding in his ears while starting to get physical with Lilith at her house, Cassius almost loses control and sucks the blood out of Lilith's favourite pet. She doesn't know yet; she just thinks her pet is missing. ("The corpse of a dead animal, half-eaten")

    Meanwhile, uses his heightened wolf-senses, Flinch catches Pervy Pete, the janitor, taping the girls' lacrosse team in the showers. Rather than turn him in, Flinch blackmails him, saying he won't tell anyone so long as Pervy Pete gives his copies of the videos he makes. They start working together, taping the girls after each game. ("In the wrong hands, graphic pornography")

    Flinch discovers that Pervy Pete has all kinds of weird occult paraphernalia in his cold, run-down home. ("Into the really weird shit")

    The Story so Far

    About half brainstormed due to setup choice, and half played out in actual scenes

    In the opening sequence of the show, we see Flinch and Cordelia driving down a dark country road just outside of town in her dad's bright red convertible. They're heading home from a party, and Cordelia isn't paying too much attention to the road.

    Cassius, a new vampire, is desperately trying to figure out how to deal with this bizarre thirst for blood. He's out in the Shady Elms, near Gus's Gas, feeding on squirrels and whatever else he can find.

    Unfortunately for Cassius, he happens to be crossing the road in the dark when Cordelia and Flinch are speeding home. They run right into him, and Cassius is thrown onto the edge of the road, basically dead. ("A murder by accident...")

    Cordelia, terrified, decides to get out of there as quickly as possible, and they speed off. Flinch, however, is bothered by something: that kid's smell just wasn't right. Stranger yet, he smells like his former lover: Lilith.

    Flinch returns to the scene of the crime alone later that night. "I... need... blood", Cassius whispers, dying, in Flinch's arms. Smelling his lover's scent on him, Flinch is unable to resist, and lets Cassius sink his teeth into his flesh. The fresh blood revives him, saving his life. ("Someone saved your unlife")

    This is the first time Cassius has ever fed on a human.

    Cassius is not seen again for some time after that, but Flinch develops an obsession with him: who is this strange creature, and why does he smell like Lilith? ("Stalker and object of powerful obsession")

    Meanwhile, Flinch realizes that he forgot his laptop in Cordelia's dad's car. When he recovers it much later (the mayor's wife gives him a call and tells him she found his laptop), he's glad to have it back... until he notices that some of the video files of the girls' showers are missing. ("In the wrong hands...")

    At School

    On his first day back at school, Cassius wanders into the very brightly lit cafeteria (I asked him: "When and where do you feel most uncomfortable and powerless on a typical school day?"). After bumping into Joe (a senior jock), Cassius is surrounded by Cordelia and her cronies. Cordelia is taking a real interest in him. He manipulates her, convincing her to leave for "a walk" with him, alone. This doesn't go unnoticed by anyone, especially Flinch, who follows them out.

    Their conversation is interrupted by Mr. Jacobs, the new ethics teacher, who demands to know why Cassius hasn't been in class, and asks for a doctor's note. To avoid Mr. Jacobs, Cassius returns to the cafeteria and forces himself to eat garlic bread until he is painfully sick.

    Flinch confronts Cordelia, telling her to stay away from Cassius (who, it appears, he's developing quite a crush on). Cordelia clues in to this, realizing that Flinch may have strong feelings for Cassius. (An MC move due to a failed manipulate roll.)

    Cassius, on the other hand, wants more of Flinch's blood... and he's not afraid to blackmail him to get it.

    (generated by "Need... to get respect by showing them they shouldn't have fucked with you" -- Flinch wants Cassius to show him some gratitude for saving his life instead of being so maddeningly cold, Cassius wants Flinch to let him feed again, and threatens to tell everyone about the hit and run if he won't)

    Meanwhile, Lilith ponders the disappearance of Mrs. Lloyd, the old ethics teacher, and decides she needs to find out what happened to her. ("Need... to get some answers about the disappearance of a teacher")

    Back in the cafeteria, "Pervy Pete" the janitor shows up and, in front of everyone, leans over to Flinch and winks, "Big game tonight, eh?", while tapping his backpack full of recording equipment. (Referring to their usual practice of setting up cameras in the girls' showers after lacrosse matches.) Flinch tells him to stay the fuck away from him...

    \n How do I prep? My experience is limited, however this is what I've done in the past:

    - Talk to the players in between games about what they'd like to do with their character (not so much grandiose character goals, but what they want to do _right now_), then have 'hooks' ready to go that will allow them to do that. I've not used them, but I imagine that MC love letters are a more subtle means of getting at their intentions than just coming out and asking them.

    - I think about how I can connect the players' goals to each other. Lilith's ambition to be class president is fine. Lilith wanting to be class president and finding out that Cassius has something that she needs in order to get it is a plot.

    - I check the players' skin moves. If they picked them they want to use them. My fear in MCing is being called upon to make a hard move and not having a clue, so I think about potential hard moves in case they fail.

    - Using the above, I work out my 'anchor scenes' - these are the scenes I'll go to if the players run out of steam. If the game is buzzing then all you need to do at the end of each scene is ask one of the players "So, what do you do now?" but when it's not, you need somewhere to go. It's nice to base them on reincorporating elements that the players have forgotten so you can drop them in anytime. I find that the game keeps buzzing better if the players feel empowered to create (like they were playing a more GM-less style game) for the surroundings and the other characters. "Hey, where can my character go out at night?" "Wherever you like." "Is there a bowling alley and is this other character there?" "They are now!"\n
    what jumps out at you? Where would you "and PUSH there"?
    \nWhat jumps out at me is the disappearance of Mrs Lloyd. This doesn't really feel that incorporated so far and Lilith doesn't have a great reason to care beyond any of her classmates. I'd push that.

    I wouldn't worry about pushing the Lilith/Cassius/Flinch triangle - the players will naturally focus on each other and push that plotline themselves. Tangled relationships will naturally spend their inherent tension and work themselves out over time. This love triangle is at the core of your game and so you want to postpone its ultimate resolution as long as possible. You do that by putting other stuff into the game to allow the relationships to be the subtext for a while and allow the big relationship changing events to emerge from the other stuff, rather than restricting the game to only using the relationship to feed itself.

    Pervy Pete doesn't sound like he needs any pushing. I think your group have taken him into their hearts.

    \n I started writing this before your last post, so I'm just going to answer your earlier questions. The big game tonight - or the aftermath - seems the most obvious starting point.\n
    What questions would you want to ask of the players?
    \nPushing the disappearance of Mrs Lloyd - 3 questions, ask one to each player, doesn't matter which:
    - What secret of yours did Mrs Lloyd discover? Why did she not already reveal it?
    - What was Mrs Lloyd doing to get you into a good college? Why were you depending on this?
    - You met Mrs Lloyd out of school in a dark bar late at night. Why didn't she recognise you? When she made a pass on you, did you accept or not? What did her husband do when he found out?

    \nTake some advice from Play Unsafe by Graham Walmsley. Don't be afraid of being obvious. I'll tell you the problem with going against their expectations here - and putting a stranger or one of their missing parents in there - the players won't know how to integrate it into their understanding of the story. When someone doesn't understand the story, they won't want to add to it - they become passengers, waiting for you to show them the next sight on their tour. I've tried to do that - tried to defy expectations and throw something completely unexpected in there. People said "Oh" and them promptly failed to develop it any further. I could have carried on developing my subplot on my own, but then you're just one guy sitting in a corner playing with himself and we all know what that is.

    I understand what you want - you want the wide-eyed look of mind-blown amazement that people get when they're told something they didn't expect and is awesome at the same time. You want to surprise them. You can do that, but don't do it by throwing them something that will be completely unexpected. They won't be able to engage with it. Instead, give them what they expect, but put a little twist on it. It's the body of Mrs Lloyd, but she suddenly jumps up and takes a swing at one of them. She's a ghoul or a vampire or a zombie or something else. There's a body of a huge dog in there - and its wearing a piece of jewellery that Mrs Lloyd always used to wear. etc etc. Give them a twist, but make sure you communicate to them how they should incorporate it into the story.

    \n Good general advice we tend to use in our * World game sessions is the MC writes every player a love letter. This love letter has some flavor text setting the mood and helping the players slip rapidly back into character and an associated roll which serves to instantly introduce some sort of problem, conflict or interaction for the PC's.     Dear Grace (the Ghoul),

    10+ You fight back the Hunger and regain total control of yourself. A close call.

    \nThis might have been a reflection of my own experience with MH campaign (as opposed to one-offs) where the player wanted something a bit more Veronica Mars so investigating into the dark underside of the school and town featured a large part.

    I should mention that IMO investigations in MH should be entirely optional. Just like another other hooks, they're there if the players want them, but they should never feel railroaded into it (though there could legitimately be consequences down the road of them ignoring it). MH is a game about sorting through screwed up people and screwed up relationships. Investigations are an optional spice rather than a staple.

    Getting into the meat of it. You can do an investigation entirely improv if you like (read Trail of Cthulhu The Armitage Files for a lot of thoughts on that), but I preferred not to. How did I handle it?
    - I would decide if I wanted to establish the 'final truth' beforehand or not. Usually, I did, but occasionally I was happy to lay down hooks, but not worry too much about them until the player was definitely biting on it.
    - Either way, I would establish several facts which would then be given out as the player bit on hooks. Once they've digested some of them, I'd reflect on how their mind is working - maybe adjust my own final truth - and then work out what was missing, What did they still need to know to reach the final truth? These kind of investigations don't typically get resolved in a single session, so there's no problem laying down lots of material off the cuff in one session and then take a breather to work out how it might all fit together before the next one.
    - I'd also have in place several red herrings (my god, why is all my terminology aquatic?) in order to maintain some surprise at the reveal. Depending on how the player's thoughts were progressing, I could swap out the intended culprit for a red herring part way through if I thought it would be better.

    Let's give you a specific example from my own game:
    - The final truth (I decided to establish this from the start) - one of the students was murdered by being drowned in a toilet by the nerdy (but secretly quite psychotic) home room teacher in a fit of rage because they mistakenly believed the student vandalised their prized vintage car. (I completely stole this from a tv show which I knew the player hadn't seen.)
    - My starting facts:
    -- the boy had disappeared
    -- his body was hidden on campus
    -- he'd been drowned, though he was nowhere near water
    - I connected each fact with a hook (though in truth I could have - and indeed had to - supply facts through different means to react to what the player was doing):
    -- the victim had disappeared (hook: he was still wandering around school as a ghost, though he can't speak and is scared of the player because she can see him)
    -- his body was hidden on campus (hook: there's a bad smell coming from the old groundkeeper's hut on the far side of the playing fields)
    -- he'd been drowned, though he was nowhere near water (hook: the player's mother was the chief at the local hospital, the player could find out from her or looking at the autopsy report)

    -- a hot-tempered sports teacher who often had run-ins with the boy because he was terribly unathletic
    -- a Queen student (also captain of the lacrosse team, weird how these similarities pop up) who thinks the boy has been stalking her and the player has seen that the Queen has extremely violent reactions when she feels threatened (such as beating up a potential attacker and then trying to get the player to come with her to dump him deep in the woods).

    \n So, some thoughts about the dynamics of the game so far:

    I started with a group of total non-gamers. They were excited about the concept, but totally uncertain about the process of play. Since they were somewhat cagey, I decided to just pull out the playset ("It All Ends in Tears"), and we started creating Elements. They got excited by that right away, and once we were done I pulled out the playbooks, which they felt a little more intimidated by, but by now they were hooked.

    I made a conscious decision to lead by example, but not to explain rules or limitations until it became necessary. (For instance, they know that there are basic moves, and how they're triggered but not their details. They know their Strings on one another give them an ability to influence each other, but not the full list of ways how. We figure those things out when they come up -- I use jenskot's Monsterhearts cards, so I can just pass along the appropriate card when a move is made). In particular, I very consciously avoided saying too much about what they can say and what they shouldn't say: instead, I just frame scene and make moves and then ask, "what do you do?", and we take it from there.

    To start from the beginning:

    The brainstorming was very active and everyone engaged very strongly. Once we started playing out scenes, however, the players got a little timid. Cassius was trying to avoid conflict or difficult situations, Flinch liked to watch things from a distance without interfering in them, and Lilith's player is still a little shy, taking a long time to deliberate on decisions and often taking the "safe" choice whenever possible.

    As we played through the scenes of the second session, everyone started getting into the game a lot more actively, and developing confidence about how the game works and how the various tools work. I can see their confidence developing as we go along, and by the end of the session people were starting to spend Strings without my having to prompt them first.

    Overall, the players are more comfortable brainstorming together than getting in-character and playing out scenes, but this is quickly changing. The result is that a lot of the scene framing is much more consensual than I've seen in most RPG circles, with players often stepping up to suggest details or actions on behalf of another character, and using "GM powers" in general. For example, when I was about to describe Lilith coming out of her "gaze into the abyss" trance, the player of the Vampire said, "Ooh! And then I'm there, she feels a hand on her shoulder!" This made for a great moment, and we went with it even though in the fiction we had absolutely no justification for the Vampire's presence in her house in that particular scene.

    I'm looking forward to seeing how the group dynamics will evolve further. The Mortal is the first to score an advance, and seeing what the player chooses to do with it may be a big hint about where we're headed next.

    Meanwhile, I'm wondering whether I need to up the drama (the tension is building, and it could be fun to ramp it up, see some blood and tears - maybe a fight, a demon, or some terrible discovery, a monster appears...) or whether I need to hold back the drama (because, as @EpistolaryRichard points out, the love triangle we've got going has a lot of tension in it, and it might be more fun to draw that out a bit rather than letting everything implode right now).

    In any case, my current plan for the next session is to open with the party at Cordelia's house, which is sure to be interesting.

    Playing this game has also made me appreciate the "seating chart" more, and I may do some of that. Heck, maybe I'll make a "seating chart" for a formal dinner at Cordelia's party! She's filthy rich, maybe she's into Murder Mystery Dinner Theatre, or just paid for some catered meal before the debauchery starts.

    (Now that I see it on the screen in front of me, though, I'm liking the "Murder Mystery Dinner Theatre" concept more and more...)
