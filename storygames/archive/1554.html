So, obviously the most common position is that people on this forum switched from mainstream buying to more indie buying -- but their number of books and money spent went up. So at least for this crowd, indie gaming is dominant but it isn't cheaper.

Still, the minority positions are evident in all cases.

I'm not sure if there are any greater conclusions to draw.

    John, there were a number of people who answered in the Fewer Mainstream Games / More Games / More Money vein. There was a significant minority that answered No Mainstream Games / More Games. I wouldn't dream of actually labelling the purchase and play of indie games the one and only cause in all of these cases, but the trend does seem to suggest that purchasing decisions were altered after exposure to indie games.

    You, to be perfectly fair, were not in the majority in that you purchase the same amount of Mainstream games (and I can totally respect that). Lots of others, though, do appear to have dropped other styles of gaming. The roughest trend here is that players buy fewer mainstream games but more games overall (and therefore more indie games) after exposure to indie games.

    Now, it's totally a straw poll, and I'll be the first to acknowledge that. It's why I'd like to see more numbers from other places. However, to 'do it up right' with some very solid results would take a whole lot of effort and probably wouldn't be worth it. So I'm very careful not to make any conclusions, or make any statements where my only support is this poll. I do, though, think that this poll is at the very least suggestive.

    The important thing for me, John, is making good games for people to play.     I'm also just simply interested in the trends of my own hobby. Indie gaming as we know it today wasn't even possible ten years ago. Now it is, and I'm glad for the change, but I wonder what more changes might be coming. I am curious what roleplaying will look like when my hypothetical kids are in their twenties. And so on.

What does that mean for the kinds of games that will be available to me in years to come? What will I need to do to create and sell my own games? And so on.

    As best I can tell, it is supportive rather than incestuous by and large. People don't seem to buy stuff they think isn't cool, if they did that would be incestuous.

    It would actually be weird if people didn't buy each other's stuff, given that they will most likely have seen all the work going into the new game and had plenty of time to get jazzed up about it. Ideas that nobody likes tend not to get developed anyway, if something gets enough support to get finished I would expect it to get bought by those supporters as a rule.

    As for conversion, I think that a sizeable minority of the hobby has been poorly served for years, making do with games that were not really designed for their tastes. Some people in the hobby want games that help create stories, and trad rpgs are the best tools they find for that task, but trad rpgs aren't really designed for that so it's not that great an experience always for those guys.

    If they are lucky enough to encounter the indie stuff, I would expect people in that demographic to embrace it and not look back.

    Plus there will always be those like me who like both, who play a whack of trad but occasionally crack open the indie.

    I doubt there are that many going the other way, starting indie but eventually jacking it in for the joys of trad gaming. Mostly I doubt that as people are far more likely currently to initially encounter trad gaming. If indie ever gets really big though I would eventually expect there to be some frustrated indie gamers who would then go around looking for a more task orientated and thematically agnostic experience.

    So I think there is a degree of conversion, I think that's natural and I think it's a good thing. Conversion is people who were making do with games that didn't quite do what they were looking for instead finding games that do exactly what they're looking for. That's a good thing.

An incestuous community can be extremely supportive of its members, but it isn't very open to outsiders. I don't think the indie community is very incestuous, but I think it is something to watch out for.

    For example, while many indie people have in principle called for marketing to non-role-players, GenCon is by far the biggest marketing event, and most of the other channels also seem to be aimed at traditional role-players.
    I doubt there are that many going the other way, starting indie but eventually jacking it in for the joys of trad gaming. Mostly I doubt that as people are far more likely currently to initially encounter trad gaming.     And there are plenty of indie games for them to go to for that, like Perfect20, Coyote Trail, In Harm's Way, and others. "Indie" does not mean "conflict oriented" and "thematic".

    I think that by its nature, indie games are always going to be experimental -- but the broadly successful experiments will be adopted (and possibly mangled) by game companies. We have already seen a lot of story mechanics in non-indie games -- by Robin Laws, Allen Varney, Rebecca Borgstrom, Greg Stafford, and others.
