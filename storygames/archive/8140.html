\n So! I've been thinking about buying some old Dragon Warriors books, since I used to play them when I was 14-15. At amazon.com they were insanely expensive, but at amazon.co.uk I found some of them for 1 penny each. (Which means that postage is almost 30 times the price of the books - oh well).

I played them with my 7-year-old son, just for fun and to see what they were actually like after all these years. I have to say that I hardly remember anything from back then.

A few things surprised me:
- The very Arthurian feel. The scenario in the first book is based on the myth of Arthur, with the names changed. There's a Christian clergyman who helps the players get to the dungeon. Old oak trees, king sleeping underground. I didn't remember this at all - I thought it was a standard D&D/Conan fantasy world, but apparently not.
- The blatant marketing: In book 1, you roll your characters randomly. If your Strength etc are good, you can pick one of the two fighty characters - but if not, you're obviously supposed to pick one of the two magic-using characters. They're listed in book 1 - and described in book 2
- The slightly boring basic combat rules. In the first encounter (with some wolves), the wolves have 0 chance of hurting Knight characters because of their armor, and only 1 in 4 chance (IIRC) of hurting a Barbarian character even if they hit. A lot of dice-rolling with no real effect.

I liked it, though - it's easy to play, and my son seemed to like it, too. He only went in with two characters, which was obviously not quite enough to fight 5 spectral warriors (total party kill). Also, he's used to either choose-your-own-adventure books or pretty freeform role-playing; this book is somewhere in between, and he wasn't always sure what actions were allowed.

    \n Dragon Warriors got me into gaming as well (well not quite, I'd gamed before, but they were the first system I really felt passionate about), I used to scour the used bookshops looking for copies, and I remember how happy I was to finally owe them all. (And so much better than the Fighting Fantasy RPG

    \n Dragon Warriors was my first RPG too: this was mid 80s South Africa where the only roleplaying store was in a dodgy neighbourhood of Joburg where nobody's Dad would take them, so we ended up running with a greasy tattered copy of the first book that a mate had got from his nan in the UK for about a year. Quality: it had a comic to illustrate combat! Burning Wheel so could use some of that combat-as-a-comic style

    You can get the not-so-greatly-scanned original texts of all 6 Dragon Warriors books from here. You can also get a great series of gamebooks that are set in the campaign world of Dragon Warriors on the same site.

    I'm gonna try pick the new edition up at Dragonmeet: just for old times sake really. I ain't got any young kids to hand that I want to intro to the hobby, and the mechanics are pretty much unchanged and are rooted firmly in 80s idioms, so I don't reckon I'll see any new Dragon Warriors play.

    \n I had books 1-3 in the late 80s - it's a very British game - knights and barbarians - putting the class into class system. Unemployed and living in the shadow of Hadrian's Wall in Thatcher's Britain, our DW games were riddled with social injustice and tension just like the country was. DW spoke to me in a way D&D never did !

    D&D seemed flavourless by comparison. Points of light - DW was much more about this than Basic D&D. Go beyond in the wild and there is weirdness and danger. Desperate men on some suicidal quest, stumbling wearily into a disease-ridden hamlet where wickedness abounds and the locals want to hang and quarter them. DW Elves are so hardcore fae, none of this 'well they differ by having a better dex, pointy ears and shoot arrows' bollocks - they are so Other, so distanced from humans. The setting was great - anywhere that did have any sense of social order was on the edge of crisis and impending war. Religion played it's part - not just having clerics being healing batteries - but being a force for good and disharmony, wielded as a weapon to subdue the populace or as the last hope of the wounded knight, alone against the creatures of night and shadow.

    Apt that they re-release it as the country slides into another recession, warring in a far-off land.

    \n I just got mine, I think the back cover lurb says it all :

    Here superstition covers peoples'lives like autumn mists cover the moors and terrifying monsters with bizarre powers lurk in the shadows. The king is a weakling, barons scheme against each other, and lordless knights, back from the Crusades without the honour or riches they were promised, roam the countryside in search of adventure, or prey.

    Something like a proto-version of Warhammer's Old World but as a "mature" version.

    What's interesting is that in the foreword Dave Morris says that what's make DW shine is not rules or setting, it's flavour. To quote him :

    "In literary terms, if D&D is Eragon, then DW is Jonathan Strange & Mr Norell"

    \n I've had a chance to read mine a bit more now and it's shocking how much

    (a) it brings back fond memories

    (b) I could never ever run it as written

    B is not a matter of rules (although some of them bug me looking back at them) but setting (the rules about not being able to sell magic items make no in-game sense at all) and most of all intent. The idea that I as a GM would take someone's character background and change it without their say-so such that they were suddenly a childhood kidnap victim with a strange tattoo ... no way. Just too much "GM messing with the players because his story is more important than theirs" for me.

    Also, I haven't sat down and compared this to the Corgi editions but there are surely less illustrations. Like no diagram of lighting in corridors, or falling from cliffs, or opening doors.
