\n Anna Krieder's Polaris supplement (it's unfair to call it a minisupplement: It's a full blown supplement) is called Thou Art But a Warrior. Instead of being set in a polar never-never land, it's set in Iberia during the fall of the Caliphate of Cordoba (also called the Reconquista or the Crusades.)

So we've just finished our game, John and Tony and Alexis and I. It's the second session. We're all staring at each other, not saying anything, and there's this great big deep sigh that wells up from within all of us. It feels good. It's catharsis.

Tragedy is supposed to provoke catharis through pathos. Catharsis is the audience feeling relieved at the end, pathos is real torn emotion. Thou Art But a Warrior has it in spades. Like, this scene earlier on, where I just set up Sharif (John's guy) and his daughter Aisha and I go "wow, wait, I don't have to antagonize this at all: his plans have failed, he's stuck, he's got no good options." So I just wait, and watch him struggle with lying and betraying his faith or telling the truth and betraying his pride. And John sits there for maybe five minutes, not like "I can't think of anything" but just totally in-character "oh my god I don't know what to do."

See, Polaris doesn't do this on its own. Polaris is about bathos (overblown emotional melodrama). And that's cool, and it's good, but man it isn't as awesome as Thou Art But A Warrior. It's not just a supplement for the game: it's a completion of its promise.

I love it so much.

    \n To repeat what the other three said, our game of TABAW was amazing. There were several things I really liked about the game. The setting is wonderful. I don't have anything against playing games set in the real world, and in this case, it made the game more meaningful for me-- there weren't any flying carpets, just the occasional reminder that we were playing in a time that was real, and that unlike Polaris, where you can sustain this false hope that since it's a magical fantasy world maybe things will turn out okay, here, you really are going to fail. Your character's civilization will fall. That you are sitting around a table today is proof of it. The game gives me a stronger emotional connection to that period of history, much like Steal Away Jordan also does. Since I like history, I'm inclined to like both these games.
    \n
    \nThe discord really works. It's another thing that contributes to the feeling that you will fail. Near the end of the game, Ben's character kept trying to win the battle inside the city, but he couldn't. He was a military genius, but discord had passed 15, and thus the new restrictions were in place, and we couldn't ask to win the battle. The most we could do was ask to make it to another part of the city intact. The discord also works as a great pacing mechanism. It shortens the game a bit, and it means you don't have a long period where one guy has gone out and the other three are still waiting to cross over. It also meant we had an interesting spread of endings: John's character sunk deeper and deeper into corruption until he finally topped out and converted to Christianity, which made him an NPC. He later was killed as a traitor by Tony's character, who then quickly died in a suicidal attack against the invaders, sacrificing the very last of our forces because he'd rather die than give in to the inevitable. My character was a doctor-knight who had 1 weariness; he lived, but was so appalled with himself that he gave up his sword, renounced his faith, and became a wandering doctor's assistant to his friend. Ben's character ended with one zeal still remaining, so instead of giving up, he spent the rest of his life in north Africa, convinced that he could raise the support to retake the peninsula. I think some formal explanation of what sort of ending you get based on your zeal/weariness would be useful, but even witout it we all ended up with what I felt were appropriate endings for our characters.
    \n
    \nThe "Man of Reason" rule also works well for keeping the game grounded while still leaving the option open for supernatural stuff. We never felt the need to do away with it, but it was nice to know I could have if I'd gotten a really cool idea. I didn't see any conflict with the transition scenes, either. Those were supposed to be unmistakable signs of God's will, which isn't covered by "Man of Reason". Also, our transition scenes were all very personal things which each character witnessed by himself. We didn't have any other manifestations of God's Will. It turns out that the setting is so rich on its own that it didn't even occur to me to introduce a four-winged animal.
    \n
    \nOverall, I thought the game was excellent. I enjoy original Polaris a lot, and I wouldn't want to never play it again, but if I could only play one from here on, it would have to be TABAW. That moment of silence at the end, where we all looked down and almost sighed, is the sort of thing that makes roleplaying worthwhile.

    \n The whole purpose of having every protagonist start with the Ability Man of Reason is to limit the scope of the game, since this is part of a historical setting. This is specifically to avoid gonzo, over-the-top stuff like \xe2\u20ac\u02dcthe angels descend from heaven and decimate the Christian army with flaming swords!\xe2\u20ac\u2122. That\xe2\u20ac\u2122s not to say that sort of stuff isn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t fun, but it prevents people from really engaging with the emotional suffering of the protagonists, as well as detracting from the feeling of fictionalized historical realism.

    That being said, it\xe2\u20ac\u2122s impossible to have a game take place in this setting without addressing religion. So I used it as a tool to heighten the tragedy of the setting. Can you think of anything more tragic than your god siding with your blasphemous mortal enemy?
