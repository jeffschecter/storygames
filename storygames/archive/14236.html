\nAs I work out the playtest draft of my latest idea (a cyberpunk operative type game if y'all are interested), I came across a snag regarding the GM's power. The issue arose from a simple question: should the GM have veto power. This power seemed most relevant when a player does something that seems, well, illogical and against the story in such a way that it bordered on "power-gaming." Though a few carefully crafted sentences in the rules seemed to iron out the issue, it got me thinking: do gamers automatically assume that the GM has veto authority?
\n
\nWhat are your thoughts? To open this, what powers do we intuitively seek in our GM?

    \n Well, veto over what? And in what situations? It's different to say the GM can veto any character action -- 'your character wouldn't do that' -- versus saying the GM can veto a narration of success by the player -- 'that wouldn't actually work' or 'that contradicts some other thing you don't know about the fiction.' For example, most traditional games don't allow the GM to do the former, but absolutely expect them to do the latter.

    Posted By: k.grazwhat are the basic unwritten rules of being a GM?
    \nI don't think there are any unwritten rules, other than "If your players don't like it they'll stop being your players."

    Veto rights are usually written - 1st edition D&D and a lot of other eighties games made the DM sole arbiter - veto anything; 3rd & 4th edition D&D put the bulk of the rules in the players hands, curtailing DM authority.

    For player expectations, I think in the eighties they usually thought "GM is God" but most of the people I've played with in this millennium will argue with the GM. (Often making me longing for the days when the GM was sole authority - I'd often rather have our characters debilitated or killed then have to sit through a rules argument.)

    \n "power gaming" has a lot of different meanings in a lot of different contexts, so that explanation doesn't really explain anything for me.

    So in your game, a player can say something that may be disruptive. That's fine, that can happen in virtually any game.

    But what you really have to handle is how to identify what is disruptive and what isn't. That's the main question you have to ask before you even get to the question of what to do about it. "GM veto" is one method, there could be others, but unless you know what the boundaries of the problem are you won't know whether your solution (whatever it is) will handle it.
