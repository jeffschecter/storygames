\n I was brainstorming a list of prerequisites for a really great session, and I was kinda surprised to realize there has to be a woman participating. I am a man, and I have noticed the social dynamics are unavoidably different when a group is all male. I should make sure it's clear - I am speaking of the actual people participating, not of the characters. I believe this effect is a handicap to good play, roughly equal to how difficult it would be to write good drama with the constraint that all your characters are male. I assume there would be similar problems for an all female group, but I'm really not qualified to say. So, now I'm wondering - am I not the first to come to this realization, and if not what has already been said on this?

I realize 'really great session' is subjective and largely a function of my own preferences.

    \n In my experience that's sort of true for certain types of rpgs, while not so for others. For clear examples of the opposite ends of the continuum, it is my experience that My Life with Master and other similar drama games are basically always better with a mixed crew, while hardcore D&D tends to be better with an all-male group - although I should clarify that that's not due to the maleness per se, but rather merely because all the good/committed/interested D&D players around here seem to be men, and more often than not the social reasons for a mixed D&D crew are of a nature that distracts from the game. Even disregarding the demographic bias and assuming equivalent skill, though, I wouldn't assume that D&D would benefit particularly from a mixed group; it's simply not a very gendered game. (To be clear, that observation goes for the highly specific type of D&D that we play around here; it could be different for others.)

    Also, the specific gender quality and relationships of the people involved matter; I've historically gotten basically similar outcomes from gender-mixed groups and highly empathetic male groups with a good personal chemistry, for example. I would not be inclined to characterize the social and creative effects of mixing genders as entirely unique, but rather merely the simplest and most robust way of enriching certain facets of play.

    \n Ok, I blame my poor english. But, come on, "society is uncomfortable with two or more genders in it."? How come you get that from my words up there?

    Anyway. First, stop assuming I'm speaking from the perspective of a person who isn't comfortable speaking with people of a different -allow me to take this further- gender/sexual orientation. In fact, take sex completely out of the issue for a moment, though it looks like the focus of the discussion. I dare you all to think gender neutral.

    If you have known your friends for a long time and/or in deep, there are probably too little stuff you're not open about, maybe not everyday, but at least you talk about all kind of intimate stuff when it really matters. You can joke with these people in ways you wouldn't joke with a stranger because you know what kind of things they will forgive you and what not. These people are in your comfort zone.

    Then there are the people you know through your friends and that, since you don't know them too well, you can't be sure about their sensitivity over certain issues that can be the meat of the jokes between you and your closer friends. When these people, you, and your friends are together, that's a mixed group. The tone of the jokes and subjects being talk about in a mixed group is adjusted to the level of intimacy you have with the person you know the least, at least until that person shows signs of integration that lets the group relax, or until someone forces the integration through some daring act of humor that happens to work.

    Until that happens, most people of the group will be acting A BIT out of their comfort zone (in spanish, the term A BIT is really crucial, it means that something isn't definitive, nor applies to every case, may be subjective, not the truth in every case, etc. perhaps now you get what I meant)

    Ok, now add all the gender issues you want, that will make the comfort zone bigger for some people and smaller for some others. But as you can see, it doesn't really make a difference, except in people's minds.

    Then again, let me talk again of the thing that I really find interesting about this discussion: any game can act not only as a "social lubricant" but as a temporary refuge for people who are feeling out of their comfort zone. These people tend to focuse more in the game and people focused on any game actually make the experience of playing it better, hence anyone playing a game in a mixed group (think of the gender-neutral definition I'm trying above) usually has a better game experience. Oh, note that I'm saying "Game Experience", not "a fun afternoon with your friends" both things may intersect and feel like the same but are completely different. You can all roll laughing on the floor while trying to play D&D in a totaly gonzo session, that's "a fun afternoon with your friends" but not the fun as intended by the game designers.

    EDIT: Another possibility comes to my mind, that everyone in the group is taking the spirit of the game seriously. It's a rarer experience for me, I admit, but completely possible and may happen more often for some groups.
