Y'know, this notion ("people can only play RPGs if they commit five hours a week for twenty weeks ... plus if they learn all of Monty Python ... plus ...") keeps popping up in the occasional discussion. Then joshua smacks it down, and I seldom see people actually defend it, but it ends up popping back up again in another place.

Much fun as the Whack-A-Mole game is, the whole pattern made me think ... is this actually an argument for the restrictions that really objectively exist, or is it a plea for others to recognize the restrictions we want to believe exist? It seems to be offered more as an article of faith or (even) hope than as an argument of reason.

So I thought to myself ... why would any gamer want to limit the number of people they could possibly try to recruit to a game? Phrased that way the answer is glaringly obvious: recruiting people to a game is immensely scary stuff.

Many gamers complain that they don't know enough potential players ... if not for the game they're currently running (which is usually barely-sufficiently staffed) then to try any game that doesn't meet with the approval of everyone they could try to recruit. "Oh, I'd love to run PTA, but Michelle doesn't like the idea, and I know so few potential gamers that I can't run a game that doesn't please every single one of them."

So gamers know they should be recruiting as many people as possible. But, because recruiting's so scary, they don't want to recruit very many people. The solution is simple: limit the people you believe it is possible to recruit.

In this thinking: There is no point to talking to Eddie about gaming, because he's a father of two and even though he loves gaming with a passion he couldn't possibly dedicate the same evening eight weeks running.

There is no point to talking to Mary about gaming, because she has never played before, and even though she is totally looking for something social to do with friends every thursday night for the next year, she isn't a gamer.

The only person you have to approach is George, who has already played games for twelve years, wants a game, and is ready to commit his evenings for the indefinite future in order to get it. Oh yeah, that's a hard sell!

Some gamers (myself included, if I get lazy) do not make even the most minimal attempt to recruit people to play with them ... they passively wait until an already-convinced gamer falls into their laps. But it's okay, because they believe that the already-convinced gamer is the only type of gamer that can be recruited, anyway!

Convention play proves that PTA (for instance) can be run in a four hour slot and create a spectacular experience of play. Under The Bed can be run in less than half that time, and has glass beads (shiny!)

It is no harder objectively to get people together to enjoy such a game than it is to get them together to play trivial pursuits. There is no greater real barrier.

I totally get that it is emotionally harder to get people together for such a game. It is harder to ask someone to play Under The Bed than Trivial Pursuits. But it is (to my experience) no less likely to produce results. We believe that it's less likely to produce results, because it gets us off the hook about asking.

    Wait, wait. Let me understand your argument. We imagine that we want these things (long campaigns, etc) just to create restrictions so that we can avoid recruiting people?

    I think that the annecdotal data I have on gamers doesn't really support this, but, data being lacking, let's take a real example - what about me? I recruit people to play less restrictive games all the time. And I recruit more "hardcore" gamers to play long-term games. I mean, isn't it just possible that some people want the long-term game?

    I don't even want to get into the health of long-term games, and whether or not they're something that should be sought - we can debate that elsewhere. Couldn't it merely be that the cigar is just a cigar in this case? That, in fact, people really do want to play long-term, high-committment, games?

    To follow Josh's analogy, isn't this akin to the difference between occasionally playing bridge, and having a bridge club that meets regularly and requires substitutes if you can't come, etc? Like my mother's club?

    In fact, I think that the reason for the requirement of committment, Monty Python Knowledge Skill, and all of that, isn't so much reticence to recruit others, so much as it is creating social restrictions. In fact, the whole phenomenon could be seen to be a reaction to other cliques in high-school. That is, if I'm not in with the jocks, or with some other group, I can create a sense of superiority for myself by creating a social atmosphere in which I have the key knowledge that makes me the good member. If I can recite Monty Python better than you can, then I'm the Uber Geek. And you can't feel part of the group until you adopt our tribe's ways. You have to subscribe to our notions of what is important, to appeal to us for approval.

    Standard social stuff. Geekdom is, largely, a matter of smart people creating their own social restrictions based on understanding of some esoterica. And extraordinary committment to activities that are not simply social. You are not one of us if you aren't willing to make the committment to play every week for four hours or more, and to learn the sacred text of Monty Python.

    No, of course this doesn't make it any better a reason to restrict people than if we were doing it because we were afraid to recruit. But I think it's substantively different. Does the impetus to play long-term come directly from wanting to require social committments to make groups restrictive? No, I think that's mixing up cause with effect. I think that D&D said that games shalt be indefinite, and that we fell in love with that idea. Once we got going, however, it was at that point that it became a geek empowerment (meaning it gives you geek power, for whatever that's worth) tool.

    I hope that I, for one, have gotten over the need to use long-term gaming and such as a way to create a social environment where I'm the top dog. But I do still want to play high-committment RPGs, and I realize simply that not everyone is going to want to make that committment. That said, that doesn't stop me from recruiting outside of the gamer gene pool. I don't get a lot of luck there (this should surprise nobody, the gamers have to some extent already self-selected themselves), but I'm not failing to attempt to recruit there out of fear or anything. In fact, I find that short-term committment games are good for getting people interested, and determining if they are, in fact, the sorts who might want to play longer term games.

    Like getting them to simply play a night of bridge, before trying to recruit them into the high-committment bridge club.
