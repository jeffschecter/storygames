    \n Hard to say what you're looking for. Stars Without Number is sci-fi, and not quite a retro-clone, but it has well designed advice. The "killer app", as they say, however, is the random tables for races and planets and cultures and whatnot. Doesn't replace creativity, of course, but creates plenty of inspiration, even on the fly. The "Hard Light" adventure module even has a random "sky tomb" generator plus random trappings based on the various "ages" of the extinct alien tomb-builders. Full-on "sandbox" support.

    Because exploration requires something to explore, and that a lot of work, especially if a significant chunk is either not important or will simply get passed over. So, you know, randomly generate an environment, clean it up, and tie it together. BAM!

    That sounds like a tool that would work for me, at least.

    Posted By: jjafullerCan anyone recommend some advice
    \nUse your computer!

    1. Google image search for inspiring places and situations. Use the images during play.

    2. Make Skyrim, but cooler.

    to explain: Skyrim has an amazing diversity of landscape and architecture and situation. It's a wonderfully rich sandbox with potential delight wedged away in every corner. Alas, it never breaks through into a immersion because there is very little interplay between the situations the player confronts in the game, and the player also has very little control over how their character resolves any of those situations.

    Happily, all of Skyrim's deficiencies are things that tabletop can do really really well! So I'd want to start at the exact same place as Skyrim, cramming my initial world with every setting element and situation I could think of, and then see what happens.

    3. Check out the recent threads on The Kanban Campaign and Opportunity Cards for sandboxy game management techniques.\n
    Posted By: jjafullergames that structure play in a way
    \nEver tried out Traveler's sector-generation system? You make a few die rolls that turn into facts about planets and facilities in the sector, and those become a jumping-off point for a a fully realized setting. (edit) But it sounds like Stars Without Number does this harder.

    \n Okay, talked to my friend and GM who's kept up on 5E a lot better than I have.

    It seems that WotC is going to try to make 5E work for both the player that has a full-fledged, detailed & stat-filled character sheet, and the player that has a piece of paper with only 'Fighter, Strong, Big Sword' on it. Or, something to that effect. I think.

    How they're going to do this, we don't know, but we are intrigued.

    jjafuller, I think I know where you're coming from. My group played a lot of 3E, and has played some 4E in the guise of Gamma World. You've got these neat powers that are very specifically defined, and that work together in certain ways for neat combos and such. When they're sitting in front of you, and there's a clearly delineated combat grid map, I tend to think in terms of 'what can my PC do with these powers' and 'how many squares am I away from X' and 'what's the move/attack radius of y', as opposed to 'would failure or success be more interesting here' or 'what would make this story cooler'.

    Caveat: This very well may be a personal failing, rather than a failure of the 3E/4E systems. While we don't see that behavior much in our Star Trek FATE game, I think that may be equally attributable to our GM asking the latter questions, rather than setting us up for the former.

    (In a way, I think your initial bit about 'immersion vs. abstraction' should be reversed; 3E/4E was immersive, feats for everything. 5E seems like it might be the other way around.)

    But yeah, in a long-winded way, I think you're right about 3E/4E. But what to do about it? Good question. I'd suggest giving Risus a try. Free, with simple task resolution, it depends almost entirely on the creativity of the GM & PCs. It might not have that much hand-holding - but the Companion does,and it's worth every penny.

    I also agree with johnzo - image search is your friend!

    Hit up your local library, or university's English department. I guarantee you, if you say to an English Lit professor/librarian 'I'm trying to learn to describe things better, what works would you recommend?' you will have made someone's day. Find a writer you like, and steal their style shamelessly.

    I think I might be able to offer some suggestions (hopefully helpful).
    \nIn my experience 4e works the best when invested in the combat system. The rules of the game really only sing in combat. That supporting rules exist, but generally situations out of combat only exist to provide a sense on continuity between battles. This natually happens because the tactical combats in the game take so long, the RP, exploration parts of the game end up getting smaller and smaller so you can make sure to keep getting your three fights per session to mantain the leveling pace. Another pressure comes from skill challenges that while a nice idea do not generally work out the way I think they were intended to. In effect I see them as taking fun exploration/rp activities and crushing them up into a series of die roles that abstract away a lot of the detail of what is happening underneath.

    The least succesful 4e games I have run have offered many rp opportunities, with a description of the environments and other things that I would focus on in other dungeon crawl games. This resulted in players becoming disengaged from the system, and either chomping at the bit to "skip the boxed text" or pulling out the cellphones. And really, this wasn't even boxed text. Before a combat players generally just wanted a mechanical explaination of things in the room. Appearance didn't matter, and puzzles were skipped because they can be hand waved by skill rolls. So, room descriptions would end up something like, "brightly lit, these squares hinder, you need to make a save if you move over here. Roll for initiative."

    The weird thing is, is that we play games like Fiasco and Dread, everyone has a good time and it is completely different. So I wouldn't say it was specficially the agenda of the players to seek out the crunch in games.

    \n Jeffrey,

    I suggest playing some 4th edition D&D.

    Seriously. Using the following playgroup and playstyle modifications:

    Playgroup - makes sure everyone is on the same page about creativity, especially but not limited to the DM. After all it only takes one person blocking to sap the creativity from the group.

    Playstyle - make characters as normal (but it need not be too strictly), and then largely ignore the power mechanics - just keep the powers themselves. So a first level infernal warlock could:

    At-will: create crackling bolts of eldritch energy and scour someone with your anger and pain made manifest in fire. (Eldritch Blast and Hellish Rebuke)

    Once per encounter: create a great claw of sulphrous darkness that moves as your hand. (Diabolic Grasp)

    Once per day: sheath yourself in a black ice. (Armor of Agathys)

    As you play you use these powers to affect the situations during and between encounters - to resolve things (yes, even combats) roll an appropriate skill. The DC is determined by the situation by the DM (as normal), so things like channelling your fiery anger at someone ought to make it easier to Intimidate them and so on. If you fail things no doubt get more complicated, you get wounded, winded, or otherwise hindered - in either case the situation can then change either through using powers, different skills, or DM actions.

    The goal here is to use the restriction of powers as a creative stimulus, not as an out to avoid being creative.

    Sure some of this may seem a drastic departure from the standard playstyle, but if you look carefully enough it really isn't a departure from the rules.

    - Mendel

    \n For 'games that help you practice exploration', I'd recommend Conquer the Horizon, a tiny game that is awesome and is all about a group of colonists (essentially) exploring a brand-new-to-them landscape. The mechanics of the game are competitive -- you are trying to find resources that you can exploit to some particular ends -- but you get a competitive advantage if you are able to easily elaborate and build on existing locations, people, resources, etc.

    Microscope is also a possibility, but I'd suggest approaching it with an extremely explicit goal of focusing on the worldbuilding/descriptive elements, as it naturally trends towards focusing on situations/narrative instead. But there is really no reason you could not use Microscope to build a sandbox, by focusing on creating a wide variety of interconnected situations, and the mechanics will definitely put your creativity on the spot.

    Of course these are both high-scope games, relative to something like 'how do I get better at making a single dungeon seem really interesting?' I can't really tell whether you are more concerned about your ability to create a compelling world-at-large, or to make the immediate environment more compelling for your players. (Obviously the two can rely heavily on each other.)
