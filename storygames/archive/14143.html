\n There was a discussion on Bill White's facebook and I figured this would be a good place to bring it to bear. For those whom this is an old argument or old discussion please enlighten me and send me links to books and site and posts to read. I do understand the idea of character as an alternate identity, part and parcel to the story but I'm uncertain how that challenges the idea as it has seemed to in the past.

Story - an account of imaginary or real people and events told for entertainment.

People, characters are at the heart of our hobby, our art, and no character can be brought to life without a player. There are time when the only even happening in the plot are the characters meeting. The story unfold from there. When the author or screen write tells a story, what is she doing? She's not only describing the weather and what paintings are on the wall, she's giving life to the characters through action and word.

How then is playing a character in improv or an RPG any different? Authorship does NOT separate one from the character. Authorship separates one further from the mysteries the author unfolds. There would be no story if the players didn't detail their characters involvement in the setting and events. And no plot moves forward until we see what the characters say and do in relation to the events and one another. That is clearly authorship.

A game supporting immersion simply means that surprises about ones own past and pacing mechanism must be kept at a minimum to give the player the most room to explore the character and briefly live like her. Walk in her shoes.

While players may seek after an immersive experience, it is by far not a creative agenda (and I mean that in a broad sense of the term, not necessarily tied to the Bog Theory). As with everything in a game, it should be part of the social contract. How far are we willing to go to suspend disbelief. For each group that answer is very different thus why we discuss line and veils. I say this to my groups all the time, remember that line and veils are not just for taboos like rape, torture and incest. Players have means of playing they prefer - the balance of rules, GM and player power; groups agreeing that in a group dynamic the good of the group outweighs the good of the player; how much table chatter there will be, etc. That's all part of lines and veils.

Some games support certain modes of play. D&D has a group dynamic clearly predicated on balance and fairness; Apocalypse World tells the Master of Ceremonies to make the players believe in the world; Fiasco allows you do determine your own successes and failures; while Burning Empires focuses on the effects the characters lives have on the larger story.

Immersion is just a way to tell your character's story - first person rather than third person.

Thoughts?

    This where I think the term Immersion begins to boggle me.

    You write: "What I find myself objecting to, though, is the suggestion that it's possible to be simultaneously immersed in character while also being outside of character and looking down on the story and appreciating it."

    But doesn't that beg this re-phrasing: "What I find myself objecting to, though, is the suggestion that it's possible to be simultaneously immersed in character while also being able to take a sip of diet soda or slide the pizza slice away from you as you begin a speech in character."

    Or: "What I find myself objecting to, though, is the suggestion that it's possible to be simultaneously immersed in character while also being aware you have to get back home by 10 o'clock to watch a show with your girlfriend."

    Or: "What I find myself objecting to, though, is the suggestion that it's possible to be simultaneously immersed in character while also being able to reach for the dice as you get ready to roll them."

    There is the bar for "Immersion" the way that some people use the word that seems not to exist outside of the Turku School. And I'm not saying the Turkus are wrong for reaching for that bar. I'm saying it's a strict discipline that I've never seen in practice anywhere I've ever been in any game session anywhere. Every game I've ever participated in people are constantly moving back and forth between engagement with their characters and being aware there's -- you know -- a real world outside of the fictional elements being verbalized at the table. I don't see any way around that.

    \n For starters, Immersion is poorly defined at best.
    http://www.liveforum.dk/kp07book/lifelike_holter.pdf

    My best attempt to define it (which is in an article that will be published this summer - I'll post links to it as soon as it is) has been to break it down into smaller player motives. Here's what I wrote:

    Immersionism focuses on deeply experiencing the character, to the point of minimizing the sense of self. This usually comes through a combination of the Embodiment, Flow, and Catharsis motives, often within the context of a strong Exploration interest. The focus on being the character often includes the Protagonist motive as well \u2013 it\u2019s nice if your character has a visible impact on the world. Some Immersionists value Crafting either before or during the event as part of the overall experience, though Exhibition is often viewed as a purely player motive and discouraged. Other player-focused motives, such as Fellowship, are often discouraged as well.

    The motives I'm talking about (which are the focus of the article)
    1.\tAudience - Experience a satisfying narrative.
    2.\tCatharsis - Experience emotions through the character.
    3.\tComprehension - Figure things out. Solve problems and puzzles.
    4.\tCompetition - Win at something, or at least enjoy the act of competing.
    5.\tCrafting - Create non-ephemeral things (costumes, props, documents, etc.).
    6.\tEducation \u2013 Take away new knowledge or understanding as a player.
    7.\tEmbodiment \u2013Make decisions based on character background, knowledge, and motivation.
    8.\tExercise - Enjoy physical activity and movement.
    9.\tExploration - Experience the fictional setting.
    10.\tExhibition - Show off (costumes, props, acting chops, mad skillz, etc.) and get kudos.
    11.\tFellowship - Enjoy time with friends (also includes flirting and such).
    12.\tFlow \u2013 Enjoy losing oneself in the moment.
    13.\tLeadership - Be important to the player community.
    14.\tProtagonist - Be important to the story and impact the game world.
    15.\tSpectacle - Experience the awesome stuff (pretty costumes, elaborate sets, funny NPCs, etc.)
    16.\tVersatility \u2013 Collect important things (spells, lore, favors, etc.) and have the right thing at the right time.

    -----

    Some games include explicit techniques to drive story that occur outside the personal characterization by the player. Many hardcore immersionist players balk at such techniques. For example:

    The bird-in-ear technique used in jeepform games. This is a technique where the GM speaks out an internal monologue on behalf of a player's character in an attempt to guide the game toward certain kinds of interactions - for example, having a character speak of his budding attraction toward another character. This level of external control of character thoughts is deeply offensive to many immersion-focused players, but it can be a powerful technique in helping to create a better story.

    The fan mail technique in Prime Time Adventures, where a player is encouraged to reward another player for creating awesome stuff in the fiction. This is a less invasive technique, but it's something that makes you think as a player rather than as a character, and many immersion-focused players find such a thing breaks them out of the flow of ideal immersion.

    ----

    I don't think anyone has ever tried to say that immersive characterization can't lead to a good story. I think it's a matter of priorities.

    A narrative-focused player may make a decision that is "bad" for their character, both from a strategy perspective (essentially the gamist perspective) and from a characterization perspective (basically the immersionist perspective) because that decision will make the story more awesome.

    Also, a narrative-focused player is more likely to accept outside influences on their characterization. Often among immersion-focused players, there is an unwillingness to accept outside influences on their characterization. (However, discussions of this often flip-flop between discussions of the Protagonist motive and the Embodiment motive, which I think are different things that get overlapped and confused in discussions of this.)

Roger suggests as much with his post above. There's this notion that the Agenda of Narrativism is to make these choices and then step back and appraise them.

    The Agenda of Narrativism is address Premise. Period, full stop.

    That's it.

    Did you get a chance to address Premise? You did? Bam! Awesome. Let's move on.

    That's it.

    Addressing Premise has never been an intellectual exercise. It's visceral and emotional. You're wife finds ritual tools your closet -- ritual tools you're using to control a Demon that is keeping your daughter alive. Your wife confronts you. What do you tell her?

    That's it. You're about to address Premise. Because everything is complicated and you're about to make a statement with your characters words and actions about what kind of father and husband he is.

    But even that sounds too intellectualized. You don't need to be aware that you're doing that. What you need to be aware of is that the Game Master is portraying the wife and she's confronting your guy and you need to respond in some way...

    And if those are the moments that turn you on most in play -- whether it's Jack not sure if he should buy the magic beans, or Sam not sure if he should kill Gollum, or whether your someone on the Galactica not sure if you should rig an election to make sure to save the fleet -- if those kinds of moments are why you showed up, then your needs are being met, your hitting your Agenda and your having a great time.

    You don't need to sit back and judge how it went. You needed to just do it.

    Is everyone seeing this? It isn't about patting yourself on the back that you addressed Premise a moment ago. It's about addressing Premise in the moment you're doing it. For some people that's fun and compelling and the best reason for playing. That -- "addressing Premise", not "having addressed Premise" -- is the Creative Agenda!

      \n I'd like to throw in a history perspective wrench.

      The term Immersion did not come from tabletop play. It came from larp play. And I think that's important. Because it relies on some of the differences between the two.

      In medium to large group larp play (20+ players) one can go a really long time, possibly the whole event, without interacting with a GM, or even a GM-planted NPC. The GM is, often, not a strong presence in the flow of the game. They create things up front, like the setting and the state of affairs at the beginning of the game. But after that, the influence can often be invisible.

      In such an event, it is possible for the player to approach the game from a perspective of "I am going to spend all of my time just being my character as much as possible." There is no narration to listen to. There is no off-screen time. There is just you, as your character, experiencing what is happening and interacting with it.

      Cranking that type of play up as much as possible is what lead to the term Immersion.

      Immersion in GM-led tabletop play is simply not the same thing. When you're narrating your actions instead of actually doing them and rolling dice and eating chips that your character is not rolling and eating, then you're having a vastly different type of experience from the experience that lead to the term Immersion being coined.

      You can be engaged. You can be making decisions from your character's perspective. You can relate to your character's emotions and feel them as your own. You can even do so to the point that you feel a flow state - loss of time, loss of sense of self, etc.

      But it's different.

      So it kind of bugs me that the term Immersion has been adopted by the tabletop community in the last couple of years.

      Not that I'm saying I'm a hardcore larp immersionist. Cause I'm not. But the type of embodiment that lead to the development of the term is part of the difference between larp-style play and tabletop-style play.

      It's a term from a different medium.

      I guess it's like when we talk about scene framing in a story game. We know that scene framing is a term from cinema. And we know that we're translating that term into a different medium. So we know that the result is going to be something analogous but different.

      But we treat it more like the term Focus in cinema and photography. Cinema and photography are different media, but Focus works pretty much exactly the same way, because still cameras and movie cameras handle light coming through lenses the same way. The only difference is that cinema can do a sliding focus - changing focus from background to foreground, for example - because cinema uses time as an additional factor that photography does not use.

      But translating Immersion from larp to tabletop is more like translating scene framing from cinema to tabletop (as an interesting side note, scene framing doesn't really happen in most larp, because you can't count on the players having a shared perspective to frame from). It's analagous, but different.

      And I think that leads to some of the complaints and disconnects. Some tabletop players want a larp-like immersion experience. They want to be their character all the time as much as possible. They don't want to think thoughts that aren't their character's thoughts more than maybe 5% of the time.

      And I don't think that's an realistic expectation. I don't think it honors what is good about the tabletop medium (which differs from what is good about the larp medium).

      \n Rob, I think the culprit is just lazy speech. My summary of immersion in tabletop:

      The structure, going from large to small:
      - engagement in the social activity
      - engagement in the game specifically
      - engagement in the fiction specifically: this is the broadest level at which the term "immersion" is used
      - immersion in the fiction specifically via one's character
      - feeling the character's feelings or seeing through the character's eyes

      Flow means doing any of these without interruption for long enough that you "lose yourself" in the experience.

      "Immersive gaming" generally seeks flow for one of the many types of immersion.

      Is any of this really controversial? It's just a matter of whether anyone takes the time to pinpoint the thing they're talking about in any given conversation.

Film and photography both involve, at their core, capturing light as it moves through lenses. And they share elements, like concepts of foreground and background. But they have different strengths.

      I think the biggest unique strength of larp is "simultaneous action" - having multiple independant things relating to the same game happening at the same time. The actual same time - not in succession, as you would have if you were jump-cutting between two scenes in a movie. A player is aware that something else is happening that they are not participating in, because they are participating in something else at that moment (again, not in a wait-your-turn narrative sense, but in the "those 2 folks are over there talking about something right now, but I'm over here doing this at the moment instead" sense).

      And I think that simultaneous action is related to the difference between tabletop immersion and larp immersion.

      In larp immersion, I can be acting out my character basically all the time for 48 hours straight. There is no turn-taking in the way scenes play out. I'm always being my character in whatever is going on. Other people are over there participating in a different scene while I'm over here participating in this one, with no turn-taking. I may be watching someone giving a speech, but I'm doing so as my character. If I'm not there to hear the speech, I just don't hear it.

      In tabletop play, generally, I spend at least some of the time in audience mode while the GM is interacting with another player in the group. That audience time can be deeply engaging. But it's still audience time. It's time spent receiving someone elses story, rather than acting out my own. The scene can focus on someone else's action but I can still enjoy it as the audience. And I think this is a great strength of the tabletop medium.

      I'm not denying that tabletop players can get deeply in character. That they can make decisions from their character's point of view. That they can experience emotions through their characters. That they can experience a sense of flow while doing so.

      But I think that the expectations about how that should look and feel should be tuned to the medium. And the tabletop medium is a bit different than the larp medium. Not completely different. But different.

      \n Bill, I think GNS is flawed - its too blunt an instrument, and its arbitrary. ClintonRNixons post seemed more right to me, from that dissection thread.

      For instance, I dont think Ron gets the things that people enjoy about a game like D&D at all. He has identified one or two things that the 'space aliens' seem to enjoy, and then arbitrarily decided that they are the most important for everybody he has slapped the 'gamist' label on. One of Rons favorite words is synecdoche and I certainly think he has committed that with his treatise on gamists, and probably GNS as a whole.

      With his label 'narrativism' which is 'consistantly addressing theme'. Well, a lot of indie games are 'about something'. They do have a premise, and if the game is well designed, you cant really play it without addressing the premise because everything about the game, from the characters, the situation, the types of problems and resolution mechanics it provides... all conspire to ram the premise down your throat whether you consider yourself a narativist or not.

      But other games arent specifically 'about something' in the same way. To consistently address a theme in those types of game, the players would have to actively and overtly conspire to do so in a way which to me would seem highly contrived. Those types of games might just be about the promise of drama and action in a cool setting, like Apocalypse World for instance.

      I guess what Im trying to say is that trying to identify yourself under one of these three arbitrary labels is a bit pointless. System _does_ matter, in as much as a well designed game will deliver on whatever aspects of play it has been designed for. And individuals certainly have a preference for certain aspects of play -- they will choose games that deliver those aspects for them. (or maybe they will just select the games their friends like, most of all)

      A particular person might enjoy a range of aspects that spread across all three GNS labels, and a particular game might employ a similar range of aspects. And for specific players and designers, the aspects which are most important to them might not be the ones that Ron has selected as being the most important for his three categories. In my opinion at least, he has certainly blundered with 'step on up' for 'gamist' players.

      I also think a lot of the confusion comes from players not really being critically self aware about identifying exactly which aspects they like, and the same for designers of games who either arent really aware and/or arent able to support their desired aspects properly. And theres a lot of ignorance about how many aspects of play there are even available. A lot of players roleplay the way their friends taught them to and so on, and thats just all there is to roleplaying from their perspective.
