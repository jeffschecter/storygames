\n It's late. I'm tired. And this is probably a terrible idea, but...

What are the best, most useful bits and bobs of both Fate Core / Accelerated and Apocalypse World, Dungeon World, and Monsters of the Week that could be cobbled together to make a good, clean, quick little system?

Something like Fate Accelerated for *World games. Yeah, I know about Simple World, but that's not working for me.

The basic resolution mechanics would be pure *World, with the 2d6 roll + modifier and the 10+, 7-9, 6- scale intact. Only players rolling and something interesting happening as the result of every roll would certainly be kept. Along with the majority of the GM advice and GM moves.

From Fate Accelerated, I think the Approaches, character Aspects, and Conditions are the most useful ideas. Though I'm not sure about the band-aid of Aspects for everything nor the meta-currency of Fate Points. There's something to be said for the finality of a roll result standing and moving on with the fiction instead of rolling then starting a negotiation for the result.

I like the idea of Playbooks from *World games, but think using them strictly as classes is too limiting. Something more along the lines of Worlds in Peril's use of them as Origin and Drive / Goal could work. But that would lead back to having to write new Playbooks for every genre, setting, whatever.

Maybe something like combining character Aspects and Playbooks. Along with the character Aspect the player comes up with a move for that Aspect, akin to spending a Fate Point through that Aspect...

Not sure. Just noodling.

Any idea or suggestions on how you would cobble these two together into a narrative-focused chimera system?

    \n I've been thinking a lot along these lines recently. Add to this Cortex Plus which is also bringing some neat tricks to the game (like Relationships, Initiative system, Doom Pools...).

    Basically, *W/Fate are quite different in some aspects (pun). Fate Core/FAE is a generic system that lets you play whatever setting/game you want.
    *W is always customized to a specific setting with the playbooks.
    Also, as GM, it seems to me Fate is encouraging more a collaborative GM-style, whereas AW draws a clear line between GM vs players.

    This makes it difficult to merge both in a single game. So I'd recommend:
    1. Based on Fate (character sheet, dice mechanics), hack AW ideas into it
    2. Based on *W (Playbooks), hack Fate mechnics/ideas into it
    3. Create an entirely new system (this is what I'm attempting at the moment, silly me)

    Good things about Fate:
    - Aspects (along with Compel mechanics, Fate Points, Temporary Aspects)
    - Fate dice! (they look nice and have very similar distribution to 2d6; nice to use them for *W)
    - Unified mechanics that let you stack anything (skill, boost, advantage, compel, team work, stunt...)
    - One scale that's used for everything (difficulty, dice roll, skill, damage, bonus...)
    - Fate Point mechanics (bonus, narrative, aspects)
    - Stress and Consequences
    - Setting independent, adapt to any setting easily
    - City Creation (Dresden Files)
    - Modes (introduced with Atomic Robo RPG, should work very well in AW direction)
    - Advancement works well in long campaigns
    - Quite crunchy while flexible and story-oriented

    Good things about *W (as far as I know, don't know it well):
    - Narrative freedom, less dependent on dice mechanics (if you want to do something, you do it)
    - Playbooks (quick, setting specific, vivid characters)
    - Moves
    - Very simple mechanics
    - 7-9 rolls (a lot of intentionally ambiguous results)
    - Contructing Fronts, Threats, building soft/hard moves
    - Hard choices
    - Elimination of railroading
    - Generally, superb GM advice
    - (probably much more I'm not familiar with)

    My advice for Option 1 (based on Fate, hack *W into it):
    - most of the AW GM advice is very generic and adaptable to Fate
    - enhance char sheet with nice playbook style creation options ("choose 2 of these")
    - let "Success at a Cost" happen more often and give AW-style choices
    - Use AW-style Moves instead of Fate Stunts
    - Use Fate Modes (Atomic Robo RPG), incorporate ideas from AW moves
    - become a more dangerous, threatening GM, use AW for Creating Fronts
    - as GM, stick to AW advice on no preparation/railroading

    My advice for Option 2 (based on *W, hack Fate into it):
    - Use Fate Dice (i.e. "0" instead of 7-9; "+2" instead of 10+)
    - Use Fate mechanics for handling damage/harm
    - Rewrite Playbook moves/details as compellable Fate Aspects
    - As GM compel the hell out of your players' aspects!
    - Introduce temporary aspects (possibly also as temporary moves)
    - Use situation aspects
    - Use Fate Stress and Consequences

    On Option 3 (build own system):
    I'm playing with self-made rules for a year now and keep on tweaking them. To be honest, I seem to be enjoying more and more Freeform narrative play. So, things becoming more and more simplified.
    - As a character sheet, I use something probably closest to FU (Freeform Universal)
    - I play it mostly freeform/narative.
    - If I need dice mechanics, as a GM, I set a "gut feeling difficulty" between 10%-90% and roll with a d10. That's it.
    - I love using random table wherever applicable


    Generally, with games like AW/Fate/Cortex Plus, it's less the system mechanics but the style of roleplaying that makes the difference in my opinion.

    \n There are some obvious blank spots here, but...

    You start with 3 aspects and may add 2 more during play, which have mechanical weight to the extent that they grant narrative permission to take particular kinds of actions with your approaches (like in FAE).

    You start with your arrangement of -1, 0, 0, +1, +1, +2 in the 6 approaches.

    You start with 3 stunts. Stunts might look like playbook-specific moves, offering their own specific meanings of rolling 6-, 7-9, or 10+ to do a particular thing, they might grant a +1 to use a particular approach in a particular way, they might modify the rules in a particular situation (like when you dish out or receive stress/consequences), or they might grant some sort of once per session superpower.

    There are three moves.

    You attempt to place a consequence on someone.

    You purely defensively attempt to resist a consequence being placed on you.

    You do anything else risky (maybe not physically dangerous but where failure could make things worse), roll 2d6+the appropriate approach based on how you were doing it.

    6 or less: things get worse
    7-9: success at cost or limited success (and per discussions in Fate-land, to approach something forcefully is to have approached without primary concern for being careful, clever, flashy, quick, or sneaky, so limited success would involve some side effect of the lack of one or more of those things)
    10-12: a good success

    The total limit of modifiers on a roll is 7 (whether +7 or -7).

    When you act consistent with your aspects (i.e. you're rolling for an action for which you'd be able to invoke your aspect by paying a fate point in fate or for which the GM compelled you) and fail, you gain an XP. If you decline a GM compel, you lose an XP (but you can't go below 0). XP can be cashed in for gains to your approaches, adding stunts, or modifying your aspects.

    You have three stress boxes that work like in Fate. If a given stress box is marked, you have to take a consequence instead. There are -1, -2, and -3 consequences; if you don't have the slot free for a consequence, you have to take the next worse available one. Whenever the consequence would impair your action, you take the penalty for it when rolling; they're cumulative. The consequences you put on others are counted +1, +2, +3 and likewise cumulatively count in your favor when it makes sense when you're making a roll involving the other.

    When someone would receive a consequence but has no free consequence slots, they're taken out.

    \n The more I think about it, the more I like the idea of a World of Dungeons single resolution mechanic, aka One Move to Rule Them All. Then simply giving GM advice where necessary to differentiate between the "moves". As this is one of the hang-ups some more trad minded gamers have with *World games, keeping the kick-ass resolution mechanic while also smoothing it out and streamlining things seems like a good idea.

    So something like this:

    Roll 2d6 + modifier.

    10+ you succeed; do what you wanted.
    7-9 you succeed at cost; do what you wanted but there is a twist or setback, GM move.
    6- you fail; GM narrates the outcome, GM move.

    along with specific advice for the GM on how to adjudicate attacks, defenses, and investigation differently would be a good way to go.

    But yeah, FAE's Approaches are spot on for me. Mostly because they do more than tell the player and GM how strong the character is, rather they're also characterization tools. This character is more Careful than anything else (i.e. Careful has the highest bonus) is a lot more informative than "Shoot +2" or "Dex 18" will ever be.

    To me, that kind of melding of characterization and mechanics is exactly the kind of thing to pursue, along with the melding of narrative and mechanics that *World games already do.

    \n What is the fictional trigger for that move?

    World of Dungeons works very well with one move because "when you attempt something risky" works really well with encompassing dungeon crawling: it's a series of sustained risks. It also keeps the conversation well anchored: here's the risk you're taking, here's whether it succeeds or fails.

    If you tried to condense the FAE procedure to one move, it reads as: "When you try to do something and you are opposed by someone or something....".

    When you're dealing with a wide scope, everything from investigation to combat and back again, it may be tougher, or introduce different concerns. I think a bunch more weight might fall to the GM: particularly, issues of scope become tougher: when the players want to do X, what level of fictional resolution are we working at? How many rolls will it more or less take?

    (I'm saying 'may' not as a rhetorical device, but because I honestly don't know and don't think it's necessarily a showstopper. Maybe it'll be totally cool.)

    \n I might be reading you wrong @liblarva, but it sounds to me like one of the things from FATE that you're longing for in *World is the variety and specificity of skills, which construct each individual character piece by piece rather than in playbook-sized chunks.

    To that end, I'd turn each skill into a Move and use the FATE skill-selection procedures to fill out characters with Moves, and there you go. You have a FATE menu but you resolve actions *World-style. (If you keep *World Basic Moves as is, I think you're basically screwed on the specialization front, because the Basic Moves are too broad.)

    I know you said you don't want a giant list of Moves, but I imagine the problem isn't the triggers ("Stealth" becomes "When you try to avoid detection" -- easy) but rather the outcomes, right? And I agree, I wouldn't want to have to flip through 20+ different "pick from" lists. So what I would do would be to decouple Move triggers from Move outcomes. "Stealth" doesn't roll on a "Stealth" list, instead it rolls on either:
    - an outcome list for a broader category of actions, e.g. something about advantageous position (which could also be used for Acrobatics or other tactical movement skills)
    - an outcome list for a broader category of intentions, e.g. something about tricking people (which could also be used for Manipulation or other "get one over on someone" skills)

    In this method, some *World outcomes get less specific via the text, but then possibly more specific via the trigger. If you start with the combo of "it works, but with strings attached" and "I was attempting Stealth", you probably don't need too much more from a specific outcome list. A generic list of "strings attached" might do it.

    The mechanics handle those unique inputs the same, and outputs one of a wide range of results, a la Dungeon World. The players' side is collapsed into one basic mechanic, but the GM's side is not.

    Then the GM narrates the different outputs as different fictional outputs.

    This is a fairly standard approach that's used in a range of games, none of which result in the fiction becoming irrelevant.
