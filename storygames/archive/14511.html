\n I hadn't thought about this much before the thread asking about whether story-games silences women and people of colour. I was thinking about multiple reasons that it might be manifesting, but it occurred to me that some games are pretty unfriendly to blacks... and who knows, maybe I'll come across some asian unfriendly ones too.

First one, D&D. Traditional western fantasy is already white centric, it's got such language as white knights and black hearted - an idea that the lighter your skin, the more pure you are, and the darker the more corrupt you are. That as a start might make some blacks uncomfortable, but Drow take it to another level. Here you've got a race of people who are immediately identifiable as evil based purely on their skin colour, which is apparently a representation of how evil they are inside/a punishment for being evil. I imagine if I were black, I wouldn't like hearing that. Take it a step further, the general consensus is that good Drow PCs are unwelcome in games - maybe it's because people don't like good Drow Rangers who specialise in scimitars, but still there's some implication that Drow are supposed to remain the evil race, that they're inherently evil and can't possibly be good. There are some other examples, but I think that's the most pointed one. I figure in addition to blacks who just don't feel their culture is represented well in fantasy RPGs and therefore wouldn't be that interested in them, there are some who might have tried it out and decided to head elsewhere once they run into some concrete manifestations of black is evil stereotypes.

Vampire's another example. Mainly due to being strongly associated with goth culture. Goth aesthetics pretty much exclude blacks - you're going for pasty white skin with black makeup (which acts as a strong contrast to the pasty white skin). If you're black I suppose you could just go the black leather and spiked collar route, but it's not something I've ever seen. Although I'm sure there are a few exceptions somewhere. There's nothing in the setting that's particularly exclusive, but I also don't think there's much that's particularly inviting either.

That's 2 of the top 5 RPGs (I'd say top 2, but Vampire at #2 might be arguable) that aren't set to attract blacks, and even have some elements that drive them away. There's also something of a further unfortunate effect this might have. White supremacy isn't accepted anymore, the KKK keep their identities secret (at least to my knowledge anyway), so if you're xenophobic, you'd have to find some other group. Even if you're not with like minded xenophobic people, if you see a group that coincidentally doesn't have any of the people you dislike, you might gravitate towards it. This isn't just random speculation - I've seen it happen with MMA, a sport that coincidentally was white dominated for a while, happened to get a bunch of white supremacists following it because it happened to not have many black people in it, unlike football and basketball.

So I'm thinking it might be something entirely different and specific to SG, or it could be that it's more to do with the hobby in general, that it has some conditions that could attract people with bigoted attitudes, and that it comes out in some subtle fashion in discussions.

I'd single out Kindred of the East (mentioned above), Gypsies obviously, the Italian vamps as noted, basically VtM works heavily on stereotypes and that has its inevitable issues. Werewolf too is a bit iffy.

    JD has a point that much of gaming is derived from the pulps, and the pulps were hands down straightforwardly racist. Like he says, we can still love them but that doesn't mean we accept them uncritically.

    To be honest I suspect the issue is more one of invisibility than these particular games. How much gaming art includes characters who are black where their being black is not somehow part of that character's concept? How often if a scenario needs a guy the PCs contact for info is he a Rajinder Patel rather than a John Harris? Unless, of course, his ethnicity is again integral to his concept? How often does Astroman take off his crime fighting mask to reveal his secret identity as Jim Woo? Unless, of course, Astroman's powers are somehow related to something Chinese.

    I'd suggest that invisibility is a greater issue than evil elves having black skin. If the iconic character illustrations in a game included a black PC (which in D&D they might for all I know) that would I think be more important than allowing the odd good drow.

    But then of course there's the risk of tokenism and condescension. Tricky stuff. That said, I do think there's a real danger in saying things like " I imagine if I were black, I wouldn't like hearing that" which is ultimately a form of appropriating someone else's experience, however well meaning. If we want to know what someone black might think and we are not ourselves black, we have to ask someone who is black, and then realise that the answer we get is representative of nothing more than that person anyway.

    Put another way, you could imagine (if you're not Scottish) how someone Scottish would feel about a particular image or representation (say Fat Bastard in the Myers' films). In doing that though you're assuming my experience for me. You could instead ask me how I, as a Scottish person, feel about it. That's better I guess, but the trouble is all that gets you is what I think. I can't speak for any other Scottish people.

    \n I think the origin of some of this was quite honest.

    4) Drizzit was the exception that was supposed to prove the rule rather than shatter it.
