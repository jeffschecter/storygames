Spend enough time on the Hero Games boards and buy enough of their products, eventually you run into the questions of "Why can't they do color books?" and/or "Why can't they afford better art?" These topics pop up a lot on the forums there. Ostensibly, the answer from the company is that color just isn't feasible ("We'd have to charge over $100 per book"), and that they can afford the art that they can afford.

See, then I think about, say, Artesia and Burning Empires. Glorious, full-color hardcovers produced by one- or two-man operations that cost $40 (Smylie was selling Artesia for $30 at GenCon; the same GenCon where I bought Champions 25th Anniversary Edition for $100, a B&W book with a plain blue cover that will likely rip away from the binding if I handle it too roughly).

Now, I've heard about the profitability of various indie games, e.g., Vincent Baker talking about how he makes a much better margin than he would if he licensed DitV to Mongoose. Still, I also know that most indie publishers have day jobs, Smylie and Crane included.

So... does the indie profitability/viability I've read about take into account the author's time? I know that Luke worked his ass off getting BE ready for publication. Does that $40 price point make him a profit given the cost of his own time? Or is he writing that off, thereby avoiding the theoretical $100 a mid-tier company like Hero Games, which has employees to pay, would need to charge?

Basically, I'm just confused why hardcover color is within reach of both the big guys like WotC and the indie guys like Smylie and Crane, but not the middle-zone of a company like Hero Games. Or is HG just playing things very conservatively to avoid becoming the next Guardians of Order?

    The big companies have economies of scale working for them. The cost per copy goes down noticeably when you're doing a bigger print run. Also, if you're going to sell a lot of books, you can afford higher up-front costs.

    The indie publishers bring in much more money per copy by cutting out the middlemen. Hero Games are selling through the distribution system. Both the distributor and the retailer have to get their cut of the profits, so Hero sees only a fraction of the money you pay at the store.

    That said, I wouldn't be surprised if a lot of indie games aren't showing a profit once you factor in the creator's time. They're doing this as a hobby, so if they're covering up-front costs, it's sufficient.

    January 2007
    Mike, you're confounding a few issues.

    The first is career vs. business. Adept Press is a profitable business, meaning it makes back more money than I put in. Assessed as a business, it's grossly successful. I just received word that the last print run of Sex & Sorcery is sold out. No problem - one call to the printer, a quick check on price, the machines hum, and I pay for it easily based on from the income the previous books brought in. Been doing it steadily for five years now - four Sorcerer books, Elfs, and It Was a Mutual Decision. Oh yeah, and Elfs is going through its umpty-eth print run too (I lost count).

    None of that has a damn thing to do with making a living, as in paying for rent, food, house, wife, kids, what Zola the Greek calls "the whole catastrophe." The key to a business is whether it's profitable, from lemonade stand to Standard Oil. Whose living it pays for, if any, is a side issue. People bring up the making-a-living thing in order to cloud the core issue that independent RPG publishing is often profitable on every scale, from book to print run to fiscal year, based on actual customer sales, and that traditional RPG publishing is not. Other companies may pay a living wage, before they hit Chapter 11 (again), or because they feed in money steadily from outside sources like one's wife or some inheritance and call it "investment." I'm not impressed.

    The second is this whole "time" thing. It's a hard pill to swallow, but one's time is worth nothing. People are taught not to say that in business seminars or self-help groups, because it's a disadvantageous thing to admit when you're negotiating for a paycheck, but it's true. Time is only valuable insofar as the work itself is good.

    OK, with those out of the way, I can point to several reasons why a good-looking book can make money.

    1. Seek out upcoming, skilled contributors who don't mind working for a little less. Provide contact information for them on your site and in the book. Pay them up-front and without delay (it's less, but it's real; a helluva lot of big paychecks promised to artists never actually become artists' money!). If it goes well, work with them again and recommend them widely. Example: Jeremy McHugh did his first illustrated book work for me, in Sorcerer. He is now a successful commercial artist with his own studio. The core reason is his skill and professionalism - but he specifically emailed me to say that my support as a client played a big role in his early steps to success. That kind of interaction cannot be bought, faked, promised, or finagled. Learn to do it.

    2. Stay alert to the fan base for those who offer crucial work: layout, covers, and more. I pay for all these things, but not by running after some big-name artist and moistening his asshole in hopes he pities me. Be good to people, pay them up-front, and they'll offer real skills at lower prices. Example: I was pretty surprised to get the layout work for the Sorcerer books - Paul Mason commands astounding fees in his real-life job; he wanted to do the Sorcerer books and told me so, offering his work for less.

    3. Learn how to research and bid for printers. It's especially crucial for pricey traditional technology (when I began publishing, current POD capabilities simply did not exist). Fight like a dog for the best prices, and choose based not only by price, but by the printer's honesty, which saves far more in the long run.

    4. Establish the market prior to printing - make sure people want it and will buy it, based (perhaps) on sales of PDFs and (definitely) on the buzz of actual play. The core is honesty - is the game good? Do people really want it? The advertising-man's dream world, in which sustainable demand can be created by hype, does not exist. You can't make a game successful, but you can make a successful game. Be sure first.

    5. (more important for traditional print) Learn how to figure out the breakpoint between number of copies and cost of printing each copy, relative to the amount that can reliably be sold in six months to a year. That last is important. Too many people are seduced into printing many thousands of books, thinking they're getting a deal, and all they end up with is unsaleable stock, which must become mulch. The POD equivalent is, "only print as much as you are certain you can sell pretty soon."

    6. Practice mutualism. Again, integrity is the key. Really help others and go the extra mile. Don't cut snake-oil deals like offering to sell books at a discount to people who help you out, or offering "cheap" advertising rates. Pay people who help you. Advertise for games you honestly like for no charge. The payback is diffuse and unpredictable (in terms of how), but it is also real and consistent.

    7. Utilize the internet for its real virtues, not its ego-driven ones. The real virtues include its potential as a fanzine, a letters page, an acknowledgment center, and a meeting-ground for people with common interest (i.e. your game). Foster those instead of your Whoo-Hoo Young Hot Designer Cred. Distinguish between those who really help you and those who decide whether you're "good enough" to be allowed to talk to them (fuck those people).

    8. Learn to abandon the sunk cost in a project that's gone poorly for any reason. Similarly, learn that no project is sacred. If you don't wanna do it any more, then don't. In line with that, learn to avoid sucking on the tit of internet-club fame based on promising any particular new game in the future.

    9. Learn to think one step at a time, in terms of product. Will this work at this time, for this book, in this way? If it does, only then think about the next step (in the sense of "think" rather than "muse pleasantly").

    10. Do not publish or design in any fashion aimed to make stores or distributors happy. Their interests are aligned with yours only to a limited extent. Direct sales are your money; store sales (more accurately, distribution-based sales) are minor gravy at best. Learn who your friends are, in that biz, and work only with them.

    That's all off the top of my head. I imagine there's more I'll remember later. If all this seems like common sense to you, then bear in mind that I can think of no non-independent RPG publisher who does business according to these numbered points. Therefore it's not about having some secret. It's about not being bloody stupid and not clingint to 1980s-mythology of RPG publishing.

    Tim Kleinert cut no shady deals with the artist for The Mountain Witch, to date probably the single best-illustrated RPG in history. He did it all up-front, all by these very points you see above. The Mountain Witch paid off for itself in real sales. The lesson is straightforward.

    Best, Ron

    Julian hit it.

    Let's say I produce 100 books for $500 with overhead of $500 for art.

    Big game company produces 1000 books for $3000 with $500 for art.

    So far looks like Steve Long is ahead. But...

    The books are largely the same, so we each charge $20. But the distributor for HG is taking $6 and the store another $10. So they're making $4 a copy sold. SO ther gross revenue is $4000! Net profit $500.

    I, on the other hand, am raking in all $20 per copy sold. So my revenue is $2000 and my profit $1000.

    Now, why do the indies get away with this? Because we skip distro, and sell many less copies of the games. The distro system makes it possible to sell to a much wider audience...but that only works if there is a much wider audience. We're assuming an audience ten times as large, and the indie model still is more efficient. And more likely to sell out in the long run.

    So Luke can actually better afford to take a chance with color, because his margins are so much higher.

    Now, all of this happens to ignore that Luke actually sells through distro (and I think Mark does, too). But you see where the efficiencies are to be gained with small press. Luke, I believe, sells to distro only when he's got his other costs already covered with sales at, say, GenCon (which also costs money).

    Is there an extent to which the author's time is not factored in. Well, did I factor it in for the authors of the games above? Don't be fooled, many of the people you may think are full time game designers may actually be their entire company, and may actually have day jobs as well (or angels bailing them out). Some few do make it, but for them, yeah, you have to add in that to the calculation. Basically the profits are what they're paying themselves for their time, and the same with indies.

    What's more, with indies like me, I'd be writing this stuff anyhow. Can't stop myself. So it's less like "Oh, that time cost me" and more like, "Oh, I spent all that time writing, I'd better make some money from it if I can." The impetus to publish because of this is huge. The time involved is a "sunk cost."

    Credit where it's due, please or at least where it's also and originally due. I've had Sorcerer books in stores the entire time they've been out, since the core book's release in July 2001.     I suggest looking at it the other way around: starting with the hobby activity. The hobby activity may not be a famous or money-heavy cultural feature, but it is a fixture. It's apparently renewable from generation to generation, even in the face of technological alternatives. This has been the case since right around 1970. Don't think of D&D inventing role-playing. Think of it as the first attempt to monetize it.

    Is that hobby active and spunky? Yes it is. What is its ability to support a real industry, i.e., a set of production and commerce activities which actually remunerate those who invest in it? The answer is, it can support a cottage industry at this time. Small tradesmen who arise right from ranks of practitioners, a high level of mutualism, a tendency toward barter, high contact between user and creator, very minor middlemen who do not own the product, and a lot of informal communication about which products work best for what, and which tradesmen (of any kind) are most reliable. Not a lot of money ... but it fits the criterion of actually paying back those who invest in production and distribution, and also rewarding those who invest as consumers, because the product is reliably what they want in each given case.

    That industry exists today. I think it existed for a little while in the late 1970s, too, until perhaps 1985 at the latest (maybe earlier). In the interim, nearly everyone hared off after some stupid myth which I think had its origins in D&D being carried in Waldenbooks, and which people promulgated as "magical thinking," if we say role-playing is the hot new hobby taking the nation by storm, it will be so. This led to what amounted to a distributor-driven circle-jerk for approximately twenty years, in which retailers ordered what the production guys provided, and vice versa, with the distributor's profits being maximized, and the actual customer being cut out. (This is also, in my view, why actual game design froze into a blend or stew of Champions/Hero/GURPS, RuneQuest, or rather BRP, and post-Arneson D&D the games from companies like White Wolf and AEG are still nothing but pastiche of those games, in terms of mechanics and assumptions.)

    A few bright lights flickered on. I think Greg Stafford deserves credit for Prince Valiant, which he basically did on his own albeit within The Chaosium. Erick Wujcik is of course a main man, with Amber; and so was Mike Pondsmith with Cyberpunk. A number of others too. Note as well that when any of these folks and anyone else tried to match companies like FASA and White Wolf with the supplement treadmill tactic, they sank like stones (no surprise that FASA went under too, later). It was that fucking myth, you see. A few companies could look big and grandiose with their external capital keeping the ship afloat, and convincing consumers that they were "successful." It is a flat tragedy of economic concept - not true market forces - that people like Mike Nystul, John Nephew, and Mike Pondsmith believed it, such that The Whispering Vault, Over the Edge, and Castle Falkenstein did not thrive on the basis of the games' actual innovations and value, as well they could have.

    Because the real hobby activity didn't become apparent until the internet developed into its current form. All of a sudden games people thought were "dead" turned out to be very much alive in play. All of a sudden homebrews that had nothing to do with the crap-wad of pastiche design appeared. The real hobby became visible, although it was hampered by over twenty years of cultural training that "D&D is the flagship," as well as the more recent, brainless, White-Wolf-reinforced notion regarding "roll vs. role."

    So Mark - look at the hobby activity. Look at the resurgent actual industry (which is to say, us, here, now). Remove all the cant about "the industry," which never was any such thing, merely a hunk of wannabe promises and repeated cant, with no reliable money ever involved.

    Best, Ron

    You know, I do a lot of panels. One thing that always bores the shit out of me on panels is old timers griping about "the way it was" or "the way it wasn't." I've heard endless permutations of the "there is no industry" or "the industry was crushed by card games."

    I always just shrug at these stories. They make nice bitch sessions, but I'm not certain how useful they are.

    RPGs are not some magic product that needs beans or holy water to grow and flourish. There's nothing special or unique about the RPG industry or the gaming market. It has its winners and losers and its ups and downs, and it has its hordes of failed amateurs and crumbling businesses. Just like every other industry.

    Now, admittedly, there's a fantastic DIY aesthetic and community in the RPG industry in particular -- akin to film and music. But the key to it all is just common sense. You don't even need to do market research to have common sense! Set your limits for time and commitment to your idea. Develop a product within those limits. Set a price point for your product which you think is fair and that covers costs. Promote your product while also adhering to your initial limits. If your promotions are good and your product is interesting or sticky or lucky, it will sell and you'll make your money back. If you want to push on to future projects, bank your revenues and invest them into your "company." If you want to cash in, do it. Go to the Bahamas or something.

    As a general rule, the amount of time and energy you put into your project will be commesurate with your return. There's a point of diminshing returns, of course. Just as there's a point of minimum effort to get the project off the ground.

    It's not that hard to be profitable! (Though I do concede that it is hard to make a living.)

    But it's really not any more complicated than that. Set your expectations -- business, hobby, second income -- and price your product and your time accordingly. Work hard, get lucky and you'll see some returns. If not, change things up and try again.

    And of course, it goes without saying, be fair, honest and pay your bills on time.

    But worrying about the vicissitudes of the RPG industry or perfidy of distro or whatever just isn't worth it. Cut your own path!
