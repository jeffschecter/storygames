Constraining Freeform Abilities

Brendan
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Play Advice
Andy said, in Open Ability Lists and Awesomeness:
"The term we use is "No Set Abilities" or "No Set Skills"... My group really has a problem where all the abilities are not set, and not offering suggestions, simply saying "Choose whatever you want! The possibilities are endless!"

And they are, it's just that it's really hard to decide what is appropriate, which stalls the game for us. "
This brought me back to the Story Deduction discussion, where we all raved about constraints and how they can help spur creativity when confronting a nebulous problem space. Andy's group's problem begs for constraints.

Ways I've seen this addressed: Capes, of course, has Click-and-Locks, which tighten things up nicely--even if you don't want to just grab one from the book as-is, you can take "Firestarter" or whatever, scratch out "Nova Flame" and write in "Magma Eruption." You've narrowed the problem space and made it your own. Zing!

Vincent has the "can he shoot?" discussion starter for Dogs, as suggested many, many times on the lumpley forum. Breaking the Ice has the word-web, which isn't tightly bound to a list of abilities, but serves some of the same purposes. And of course, the old-school constraint is "here's a list of fifty powers, pick five," which isn't really what we're going for.

Other examples are welcome too. What I'm really interested in seeing here, though, is new ideas on constraining an entirely open trait system--not on balancing them against each other within the game, but on kick-starting your brain to generate interesting and useful descriptions of your character (/ship/gun/personal fairyland). Hint: flavor text don't cut it.

    Brendan, flavor text is what you're asking for.

    Stuff on a character sheet is player resources with a thin-to-thick layer of color that serves to characterize your guy.

    This became very obvious to me while doing up the list of skills for Full Light Full Steam. Who could do what when was a matter of dice. Strictly speaking, you didn't need any names for the skills at all, and players could just say, "I'm rolling my fourth die pool now." What the stat names provided was color, to contextualize and characterize the PCs and NPCs.

    For an interesting alternative example, Bryan Hansel's Game Chef entry Pinnacle Empty Quiver is about commandos. In order to get the skill list, he went to the US and UK commando webpages and just grabbed all the key words. The skill list he came up with looks very functional, and is hugely evocative.

    I'm going to toss 'qualifiers' in again. If you have a completely open skill/trait system, then the skills or traits that you choose should reflect three different 'level's of interest:

    * How the character is different from the 'normal folks' of the setting.
    * How the character is different from the other characters.
    * The priorities of the player and the group in general.

    This doesn't mean that you should min-max within the group. For instance, in Dogs every character has 'Guns' as a trait. That's what differentiates them from the rest of the population. Their relationship defines who the player conceives of them being, and there may be some differentiation between characters - he's the talker, he's the shooter.

    Josh BR has the right of it (mostly) - you're really just covering over purely mechanical operations with colorful labels. But since those colorful labels determine how the players perceive the objects on their character sheet, they're also very important, mechanically speaking.

    This is a very incoherent post, so I'll write something else later.

    The upshot: Open traits should be those things that make your character stand out - from the setting, from each other, and in the player's mind.

    The best way to constrain an open trait system, IMO, is by providing LOTS OF EXAMPLES. Maybe in the form of the character sheets of 3-5 sample characters, or in the form of a full page of nothing but examples that the game designer finds appropriate.

    See, most indie games, because of a lack of playtesting with "real gamers" (someone outside their really supportive gaming group), missing a blind spot, and perhaps in some cases laziness (but I think it's mostly the blind spot thing), don't provide enough examples yet for things that are generally "open", whether the openness is traits, skills, settings, or themes. Mant just say "work it out, you can choose anything!"

    Personally, I can do this, but I love to see some guided examples. Not just a few, either, LOTS. A huge span of them. Seeing one I like, and twisting it a little to make it my own, is the best way I can make the most of openness.

    I'm hoping that more people, when writing their games, offer lots of solid usable examples instead of just one or two. This is, not entirely unrelated, my pet peeve with most technical documentation: Very few or no examples of using command-line commands to make commands that work in the real world...

    -Andy

    Having run into the issue where descriptive terms are only as good as what the GM and player agree the description _means_ and _can accomplish_, I tend to ask "Is keen better than clever? If you can fly, how high, how fast, how long?" As a GM, I pin those things down quickly with questions like, "What do you WANT it to be able to do?" As a player, I want to be able to do everything that would be reasonable for my character. "I'm a hooligan. I have a tricked out skateboard." OK, unless I specifically say otherwise, I want my GM to assume that I can ride that skateboard and that I can do some above-average tricks. "I'm a secretary." OK, I can alphabetize. I can file. I can take dictation, answer phones, arrange for travel for my boss, order meeting snacks...without having to pay for individual skills and being incompetent outside them; I've got the whole package, and I like that model.

    When we did the first edition of Fate, we included no skills list at all, as we were all about the openness and freedom of letting people skills on their own, convinced that it would be far more colorful and evocative.

    We were smoking something. The absence of a skill list came up over and over again in people's responses. it's not that they needed to be entirely handheld, but rather that they needed _something_ to uses as a basis for creating their own skill lists.

    Our solution for the second edition was to commit a whole chapter to how you might come up with a skill list. it drew from functional category models, like 5 point Fudge, as well as from the utility model from BESM, and the final result was....OK. It still skewed too fantasy, is a little too rigid, and honestly, it didn't open up enough of the guts of the system to the GM so I think it was a valiant effort, but ultimately a failure.

    Most tellingly, as we work on a setting-specific product, I would say that about 60-75% of our work has been in the playground around skills.

    -Rob D.
    PS - on the other hand, Aspects being totally open ended and flexible works out just fine because the mechanical constraints on the play out pretty organically, though even that has some room for improvement

    I think the trick here is define the traits in terms of fictional input into the SIS, rather than in terms of utility. Capes and Polaris both do this.

    In other words, every ability is always usable. It's just that, when you use your ability, it's up to you the player to bring it into the play in some way.

    This is a totally alien way of thinking about resources, I know. But I think it's a much more productive one*.
