\n I've been mulling over the idea of a game about mercenary pilots (a la Area 88 specifically) I've sought out (and bought) a couple of games that purported to provide this, but they have fallen quite short of what I'm after. One had some interesting ideas but these were very under-developed and the organization was a dog's breakfast. The other was a wargame first (not that I don't like wargames, but that's not what I want here).

My game about mercenary pilots has to have:

1. Pilots who are larger than life. Who have Drama going on all over the place, and Problems that can only lead to a fiery end literally and/or figuratively.

2. Aircraft that are an extension of the character. They're on the character sheet. You give them a name and draw nose art for them. They're that important.

3. A dogfight system that is tactical and has the right air-combat "color" ("I pull a 4G yoyo and drop onto his six, 'Fox 2!'"), allowing for move and counter-move, but is not a hex-and-counter wargame, and requires no map (but minis of airplanes to show relative positions and whooshing noises are just fine). A dogfight system that links it to the story of the pilots flying the planes. Maybe a system that treats all conflict as a dogfight?

4. Group creation of the squadron and some management of its affairs (negotiation of contracts, maintenance and supply issues, etc) that isn't just bean-counting and making lists. It has to have an impact on the main characters - the pilots.

5. Drama and conflict that plays out on the ground as well as in the air. In fact, when the pilots are in the air, they should be carrying their dirtside burdens with them and sometimes (often) working those problems out in the air, in combat.

How can I achieve these things?

    Posted By: NovembreOut of curiosity: were the games you boughtMercenary Air SquadronandIn Harm's Way: The Wild Blue? I've been eyeing those for a while... I'm interested to the topic, but less than convinced of the execution.
    \nYes, in fact they were. Mercenary Air Squadron is a decent game, but it is a boardgame, an air combat simulation. That's fine for what it is, but it's not a story game, or even a trad RPG. In Harm's Way: The Wild Blue is an RPG but suffers from very poor organization, scattershot and incomplete rules, and under-developed ideas. Granted, some of those under-developed are the gems to be plucked out and shined up I think.

    IHW:WB proposes group generation of the squadron, and this bit is certainly worthy of snatching. The "troupe play" idea in the book is woefully underdeveloped, meriting only a paragraph that really only suggests the idea rather than showing you how to fully implement it. The dogfight system is a subsystem of the main task resolution system (or maybe it's the other way around) and does a piss-poor job of explaining itself. The rules mention certain things but give no explanation of what they are; for example, aircraft characteristics are given but not explained; damage from missile impacts is "exactly the same as cannon fire" - but cannon fire is not explained anywhere. The maneuvers are interesting to look at but you don't get any context in how they are used - what maneuver counters what maneuver? There is a provision for ground based troops in action, but this feels unfinished as well and is a dilution of the game's main focus. A large percentage of the page count is given to chargen, but is mostly based on working up a large skill list you will likely never use. Naturally, there is a long list of equipment, right down to sleeping bags and flashlights that really isn't necessary. It's not a game you can play without a lot of piecing together the designer's intentions, or a lot of reading between the lines and working with standard RPG assumptions.

    But, like I said, it has some snatch-worthy ideas, squadron group-gen and troupe play being the most noteworthy. I think these are fairly easy to develop more fully.

    One of the things I find most difficult to conceptualize with this idea is how to story-game a dogfight that doesn't handwave energy-based air combat maneuvering. The maneuvers in a dogfight are checking and trumping each other, and the planes' design and performance, plus the pilots' skill, are key, and all factored together. Modeling this naturally lends itself to a wargame-style simulation, which is what I want to minimize. I think the trick is in there somewhere, somehow making this blend of performance characteristics, skill, and tactics story-gameable.

    And, if this is to be a more focused design, how do you deal with the game mechanics outside the cockpit? Is the dogfighting system a separate sub-system of the main system (or indeed, the other way around)? Can a game like this have a unified mechanic? How can it tie into the characters and their aircraft in an integrated way? Does this design start with the characters - the pilots - or does it start with the dogfighting?

    Here's an even deeper question - should a game like this, with such a focus on the tactics of air combat and so forth, even be a story game (is it better off as a wargame or simulation-style RPG)?

    Posted By: Mike MontesaHere's an even deeper question - should a game like this, with such a focus on the tactics of air combat and so forth, evenbea story game (is it better off as a wargame or simulation-style RPG)?
    Personally: Yes, most certainly.

    After all, being a story game doesn't preclude having tactics or even elements of simulation. Like, look at SotC, it's (IMO) certainly what many people here consider a story game, and yet it features a tactical-yet-abstract combat system, complete with positioning and all that jazz, right next to something like the Aspect rules which make things like personality and style of the pilot matter to a great degree.

    IMO, the biggest thing to avoid is turning it into a simulation game. I don't see this game getting improved by, dunno, dozen of finely printed pages with "realistic" stats for a bazillion of realworld and fictional airplanes. Much better to make "airplane creation" an integral part of the character creation process where planes are described by traits/aspects/whatever, thus making them unique and integral to the character that flies it. (And by "unique" I also mean stuff like some planes having traits like "Mass-produced" that could be used for repairing them or getting new ones.)

    IME, relatively abstract rules that nonetheless shape play in a certain way are often better then rules that are very explicit, detailled and ... "concrete". (At the very least because it's easier to adapt them to cover various situations, like out-of-plane and in-flight.)

    I haven't read Area 88 for a very long time, but my memory is that the protag is a mercenary who accrues debt (in the form of damage to his plane) faster than he gets paid. That's a pretty helpless situation. But the important thing is that the protag can't get out, and has to keep doing this. So it could be that being a raptor for hire is the only thing that earns enough money to bribe the police to keep her secret out of the hands of the military; or the pilot had a dream that the day he stopped flying, his family was killed.     \n

    OK, in super-brief, this is how dogfights work. (You might know this, but I really like talking about it.) This is pretty much true up until the middle of the Viet Nam War, with the advent of the F-4 Phantom and the MiG-21, when guided missiles became more significant.

    \n

    Energy is either kinetic or potential. Kinetic energy is how fast you're going relative to the air around you. Potential energy is in the form of fuel and altitude. By throttling up you can increase your speed, and by falling you can increase your speed. When you go up, you turn kinetic energy into potential energy. When you go down, you turn it back. Sooner or later, you'll turn it back in. Ideally, you do that in a controlled fashion.

    \n

    Maneuvering means "getting behind the other guy." Some planes have a gunner in the back, but that adds a lot of complexity to the plane, so that guy had better be pulling his weight in other ways, like being a bombardier or radar operator or something. It's not really practical for an escort (fighters that cover the payload, like a bomber or cargo craft) or interceptor (fighters that are sent up to shoot down the cargo, bombers, or whatever) because it adds a lot of weight and the structure to carry that weight, plus bigger wings to lift it, plus a bigger engine... So most of the time, you get behind the other guy, where the relative speed between you is small and you shoot a lot of bullets in the hopes of hitting something important. Sometimes that's something in an engine, sometimes it's structure, sometimes it's the guy in the bubble who was going to get a date with that girl this weekend.

    \n

    The trick, of course, is in getting back there. The more energy you have, the more maneuvers you have at your disposal. Here's a nifty site about the options you have. In a particular game I've played \u2014 I can't remember the name of it now \u2014 "energy" equals your hand size, and you play a card blind against your opponent to try to get the advantage.

    \n

    SO what you might want is something like a rock-paper-scissor mechanic, where certain maneuvers trump others, and the number of maneuvers you draw from is based on your energy. You get extra maneuvers for being flying against a rival with whome you have history, and you also get extras for a rival competing with you for a kill.

    \n

The top level is strategic/fleet level conflict that determines where the tactical battles are going to take place. This level is about gaining territory and winning hearts and minds. It has some board game elements (markers on the planets in the system). The middle level is the tactical combat - starfighters in dogfights and capital ships pounding on each other. I'm toying with this using maps and miniatures, but I'm staying well away from vector physics and riduculous speed/movement rules that ruin most of these games for me.     In my game, these levels are played in order. You make strategic decisions as a group, that creates NPCs and relationships (hearts and minds) and leads to tactical battles. In the tactical battles the PCs will often need to sacrifice and take risks in order to gain tactical advantages. This results in suffering to their relationships that gets played out in consequence scenes at the personal level.     \n Now I'm at the point I think where I can try to start figuring out how it all works. This is what I'm worst at - mechanics, and where I'm most likely to throw my hands up and walk away. But here's what I'm after, in sort of a brain-fart mode of just pitching things out there:

    1. Something that captures the move and counter-move of air combat. This is key for me and this game must-have it. I'm working on a system with a selection of maneuvers you can use depending on how much Energy you've got. For example, a Break (a hard turn) costs maybe 5E (I'm just making up some numbers), a Scissors costs 10E. A Break trumps a Break but not a Scissors, a Scissors trumps a Scissors and a Break, and so forth (I've got a basic list of maneuvers and a matrix of what trumps what). Then, once you've successfully played a maneuver on your opponent that he can't trump, you can take a shot at him (and depending on range and other factors you might hit or miss of course). Taking the shot also requires Energy, which you may have burned up just getting in position for the shot, but that's what energy management in a dogfight is about.

    2. That Energy you're using in combat in the air is an innate characteristic of each character. The plane he or she flies adds its own E rating to what the pilot has to use in the dogfight. You regain energy each turn of air combat by using Thrust, which is also an innate characteristic of each pilot (representing motivation) and each plane (representing its actual power). There may be other innate characteristics pilots and planes both share (for example, Structural Integrity, representing how much stress and punishment a pilot or plane can take; Afterburner, an extra boost of Energy that helps in a pinch but taxes you severely) . Basically, the planes add to the pilots' characteristics depending on the model (and having differences between the aircraft based on their actual performance is also a must-have for me, even if this is abstracted in the most simple way - an F-16 is an all-around better aircraft than a Mig-21 for example).

    3. Play goes in rounds, a ground scene focused on one character (but which can and should involve other players) dealing directly with the spotlight character's issues. Energy may be spent or gained depending on what happens and at the end of the ground scene, there is a mission in which everyone participates. When the mission is over the next character gets his ground scene, then there's another mission, and so on.

    4. A character's story arcs with their Energy. Once it reaches "Buster" (modern aviation slang for maximum speed) it's going to end. If the character hasn't successfully dealt with their personal baggage (somehow reflected mechanically) the end reflects that. If they've resolved their issue, the end reflects that too. Likewise, should a character's Energy drop below Stall Speed (which I can't get a grip on how to work it yet) they're going to crash and burn. Stall with unresolved baggage and it'll be a really bad ending. Stall out with your issue resolved and it's simply tragic (come to think of it, resolving your issue and then getting killed in the Mission is perhaps even more tragic). Some way to pull out or eject from this fate would be good. You can go Buster or Stall Out in a ground scene or a Mission scene, but the effects of these two always carry through to the end of the Mission scene, and if the character survives the mission, their story ends one way or the other and they get written out.

    5. The "Contract Negotiation" for each session of play is set up at the beginning, with the players allocating certain available resources to their squadron. This represents the company suits and staff in the head office in Pretoria deciding who gets what and what they have to do with it. I think it would be cool to have these people be actual minor characters involved in the story as well. In the next step - "Mission Package" - Players then allocate their share of the resources to their pilots' for each mission to be played that session (in the form of armaments, fuel, and repairs to their aircraft - or even new aircraft if it comes to that). Of course, if a pilot hasn't got enough money in his account to pay for everything (despite its availability, he may have to do without). If the game is a campaign, then the performance of the squadron is evaluated at the end and affects what resources the Suits are working with in the Mission package stage next time around.

    I'm going to try and cook this for a while and see if I can get it to gel for me.

      \n So, I'm actually working on this now, and I need a little direction. I'm trying to figure out how to set up the mechanics in the context of overall game play. Here's the idea:

      1. A character's story arcs with his Energy. When it hits Buster (maximum) or Stall Speed (minimum), the character's story ends, for better or worse.
      2. Each player gets a Ground Scene, in which their character deals with their Baggage in some way, followed by a Mission Scene in which everyone participates. For characters who have gone Buster or hit Stall Speed in a Ground Scene, the next Mission Scene is also their final mission.
      Note: For this, I'm considering making one player the Aggressor, the person who plays the characters in opposition to the spotlight character. Yes, this makes it a GM-less game and I'm not sure if that'll work. If I go this route, the player paying the Aggressor needs to get some benefit from it.
      3. In Ground Scenes, spotlight characters can gain or lose Energy (which they'll need in the Mission Scene) by wagering it on conflicts directly related to and addressing their Baggage.
      4. In Mission Scenes, characters use Energy to attempt to complete the Mission. Success means an Energy gain for everyone, while failure means a loss of Energy.
