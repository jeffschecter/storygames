    \n We call Settlers of Catan a board game, yes, but we also call it a Eurogame, because "board game" is a very large category that can include things as variant as Diplomacy, chess, Monopoly, and Chutes/Snakes and Ladders, and because we want a way to describe things in a way that will help us make sense of the very large category.

    Likewise we can divide roleplaying games up into several possibly overlapping categories. Yes, the categories are used for identity politics and cooler-than-thouness, but they're also used to convey useful bits of meaning, too.

    To answer the original question, I prefer the most accurate answer depending on what I'm talking about. These are the denotative meanings of these things in my idiolect:

    Indie games are published by the person who wrote them, or by a company owned by that person. This makes FUDGE and Mouse Guard not indie RPGs, and it makes GURPS and Rifts arguably indie RPGs, but them's the breaks when you taxonomize. "Indie game" isn't a useful indicator of quality; it's a description of the publishing process.

    Small-press games are published in small print runs, usually measured in the hundreds rather than the thousands. Also not an indicator of quality, also a description of the publishing process.

    Story games are RPGs where the rules treat story as primary -- games with a Narrativist agenda rather than a Gamist or a Simulationist one. The mechanics often concern themselves with pacing: the rules change depending on where you are in the story. Fiasco is a story game because game play is divided into Acts, and the rules change depending on whether you're in Act 1 or Act 2; and because the mechanics introduce rising action/increasing tension with the Tilt and explicitly handle the denouement.

    Roleplaying games are games where a major point of the game is to identify with at least one character and part of the activity involves considering what that character would do and acting out the role. Arkham Horror is not a roleplaying game, despite players having named characters, because the character histories are just color and don't really affect gameplay.

    Experimental games are games that try new things. If an experimental game succeeds, after a while it stops being new.

    These are not all the same thing: If I recall, Colonial Gothic is an indie game and a small-press game (author-published, small print runs), but not a story game (it uses a modified d20 system, which doesn't take narrative structure into account) or an experimental game (because it's using d20). None of these should have value judgments associated with them; the problem is that, often, they do -- there's a certain STORY GAMES GOOD, OTHER GAMES BAD mindset that will read that example as me saying that Colonial Gothic is bad because it's not a story game.

    So which one I use depends on what I'm looking for. If I'm in an unfamiliar game store, I'm likely to ask for "the indie games"; if I'm talking about the games I like to play, I'm likely to mention "story games."

    \n I call a role-playing game exactly that: a role-playing game.
    (and if it's a story-telling game, then I call it that)

    Sometimes I need to explain further, in answer to questions like: But what kind of role-playing game?
    - Then I answer that it is a classical role-playing game; with a GM leading the play-group, and players with each their character, working together to meet challenges and solve mysteries, or maybe trying to backstab each other while avoiding challenges and "solving" mysteries.
    - Or I answer that it is a modern role-playing game; with players engaging in a bit of character exploration and narrative joy, within the framework of a method that regulates their game-play.
    \nAnd if they want to know more ...
    - asking me: Which role-playing game?
    - then I name the game, gives the theme and genre of it, and how it may entertain and/or engage them ... and such a direction of conversation tends to have me give them some anecdotes from my long and varied game-life.
    - or asking me: What is this "role-playing" actually?
    - then I talk about interactive drama, created there and then, with players acting out the main characters in a weave of action and reaction. And I talk about immersion; the magic and make-believe of being someone else, acting and reacting on their behalf, and sharing in the intellectual and emotional developments of this fictional being. It's like having a great movie going on inside your head, and inside the heads of the other players, and making up this movie as you go along, with the tools given by a really powerful method of interaction ... and such a direction of conversation tends to have me give them some anecdotes from my long and varied game-life.
    \nI like role-playing games. I like talking about them. So I'm quite happy when I get to answer such questions.

    Maitrsinh, independence has nothing to do with commeciality; or, rather it has an effect on commerciality, but isn't defined by it. It means that the creator o the game is the publisher. When it's produced by several people, they're all in control of the production of the part they created. That means they're answerable only to their own conscience, business plan, and sense of value. The result is that Luke owns and publishes Burning wheel by the thousands and makes his living with it, Vincent publishes Dogs in the Vineyard in batches of several hundred and it pays for the birth of his last child (which, if you don't know, is quite expensive), and Fred et al. Published FATE for free, developing it into a commercial thing only when they'd established a huge fan base.

    \n

    What that means is that they have the freedom to create, market, and publish in the ways they see fit because they're not materially answering to anyone but themselves and their players.

    \n

    The absurd comment made elsewhere that Luke isn't indie because he's successful is ignorant of both the definition and Luke's trailblazing for indie publishers. He's just where many of us plan to be in several years. On the other hand, Brad has passed up that freedom to Bad Hat, which makes Diaspora "small press" but not independent. And Agon is both small press and indie, but is not a story game, as it's concerned almost wholly with competition between players.

    Isn't Andy's definition of "Story Game" something like, "Role playing games and the things people in 2006 say aren't role playing games that you use to tell stories"? I mean, that says it all to me. I don't care who publishes it. I care about what qualities the play has. I want effective tools to do the stuff the game's about, and I want to know what the game's about, and I want it to be about something I care about. The major distinction is that, when you're appealing to a broad or entrenched audience, it constrains what the game can be about, which means a large publisher is unlikely to make something that appeals to me. But it doesn't mean that any given small publisher makes something I want.

    \n

    Like, I don't really like 3:16. A lot of other people do. That's not because there's a problem with the game, but because it's about something that I can't engage in sincerely (I think). It's the same reason I don't like D&D (though frankly, I've had more fun with 3:16).

    \n

    I've had a great time playing Mouse Guard, on the other hand, and it's from a medium-sized publisher, written under contract with a designer who had no control over its publishing.

    \n

    Philippe, I'm pretty sure you're having a hard time finding a word for that thing because it's several different things that often overlap but don't necessitate each other.

    \n I target my language to my audience.

    Posted By: hans chung-ottersonI'm thinking this: would it be fruitful, rather than trying to classify different types of games based (largely) on publishing methods, to instead try to distill & understand & label different "schools" of design?
    \nI think it would definitely be.

    Plus, it has the advantage of not needing to cover everything immediately. You can just group together two or three obvious titles that "belong together," give that group some kind of name, and hey presto -- instant classification, useful for discussing those two or three titles plus any others that share elements with them (in the "it's a bit like a 'foo' game, but without the dice pools" sense). It's much, much easier for people to agree on the similarities and relationships between a handful of specific games than it is to try and place hundreds upon hundreds of games released over nearly forty years into a few neat categories.

    Once you've got the groundwork laid by establishing neat, narrowly-defined and easily-identifiable classifications, figuring out sensible ways to arrange them in larger groups should be easier and less prone to misunderstanding.
