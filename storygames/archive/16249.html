\n Just because I'm in a weird and slightly bored mood today, and all the game ideas I can think of are horrible, wrong or depressive:

Sometimes games are blamed for being bad and evil influences on people. Let's say you were to make a game that people would actually play, that would make them do or feel bad things.

What would that game be like?

(Note: It has to be something people would actually try out - so no "conflict resolution through putting a nail through your eye" or similar.)

    \n I'm personally pretty concerned about using game design for evil. I've even given some talks about it in a few conventions and such over the years.

    The basic issue in real-world evil game design is that commercial interests are not the interests of the people. The best interest of everybody, I think, is to grow into self-controlled, wise people who strive to be the best humans can be. The best interest of the corporation that sells you your entertainment is for you to grow into an insecure, easily influenced and passive slob who can be easily milked for yet another round of money by selling you worthless knick-knacks. This conflict of interest is inherent in all culture industry, not just games. However, I personally think that we have seen some of the most chilling examples of captivating, dependency-inducing entertainment originating specifically in gaming culture. Soap operas are pretty tame in comparison to some computer games that strive to be integral elements of a person's lifestyle.

    I do not wish to derail this discussion by naming specific games that strive to impose unhealthy lifestyles and modes of thinking on people. No matter what I'd name-drop, there certainly would be a counter-anecdote from a person who got much happiness from the game in question. If it makes the above argument easier to accept, I suggest considering it purely as an abstract hypothetical: given that we have the tools as game designers, could we not create games that teach people to be worse human beings? Maybe no such game exists today, but that doesn't lessen our responsibility in considering the consequences of our actions.

    \n I've repeatedly entertained ideas about an RPG that is to app games (Farmville, Echo Bazaar, etc) as D&D 4e is to MMORPGs. Something that brutally exploits various psychological conditioning techniques (like variable reward schedules) to make it super-addictive, and things like bonus XP when you post about your adventures (with a link to the game's official website where the basic rules are available for free) on facebook or twitter or whathaveyou. The kind of game you can sit down and play for ten minutes on your lunch break with a friend.

    And, in addition to the rules (and a few discreet banner ads) there's a button to donate as a way of showing appreciation for the game. And when you donate, you get XP equal to the dollar value of your donation. Not that you are paying for XP in a TTRPG because that is silly, of course, but rather that you are giving yourself a reward for contributing to the game's success.

    \n The "magic circle" thing in games can definitely be dangerous, in that you can convince people to do just about anything in the context of "a game," even if it's pretty emotionally abusive. That's definitely where I see the greatest potential for evil game design.

    Here's a somewhat evil game (IMO), disguised as something interesting and quazi-therapeutic:

    1. Don't tell the players the rules in advance.
    2. Each player writes some true secret and shameful thing on an index card.
    3. Shuffle all the cards together so it's unclear whose card is whose.
    4. Place the cards face-down on a table between the players.
    5. Flip over the top card, read it out loud, and collectively ridicule the thing on the card.
    6. Continue until you've done this for all the cards.
    7. Watch the faces of the players in the game and see who turns the most red and flustered.
    8. The End

    \n "I dare you: the RPG."

    Everyone creates a character. This can be as close to your real life persona or as removed from it as you want to. There are no stats or numbers or anything; you describe who your character is and what they do and how they do it in plain prose language.

    All resolution in the game is based on actually performing the actions. To do it, you do it: if you're going to try to pick a lock to sneak into someone's house undetected at night, you actually have to pick a lock and sneak into someone's house undetected at night.

    If you don't think you can do it, you dare someone else to. They can up the ante by daring you back; the only restriction on dares is that each one has to be more extreme (for which read "evil") than the previous, as determined by the entire group. I actually want it to be something like bidding in an auction, where people keep pushing the stakes higher; when everyone else is out, the person who pushed it the highest has to follow through.

    Playtesting will remove the bugs from this game.

    \n Posted By: Eero TuovinenI'm personally pretty concerned about using game design for evil. I've even given some talks about it in a few conventions and such over the years.
    \n
    \nThe basic issue in real-world evil game design is that commercial interests are not the interests of the people. The best interest of everybody, I think, is to grow into self-controlled, wise people who strive to be the best humans can be. The best interest of the corporation that sells you your entertainment is for you to grow into an insecure, easily influenced and passive slob who can be easily milked for yet another round of money by selling you worthless knick-knacks. This conflict of interest is inherent in all culture industry, not just games. However, I personally think that we have seen some of the most chilling examples of captivating, dependency-inducing entertainment originating specifically in gaming culture. Soap operas are pretty tame in comparison to some computer games that strive to be integral elements of a person's lifestyle.
    \n
    \nI do not wish to derail this discussion by naming specific games that strive to impose unhealthy lifestyles and modes of thinking on people. No matter what I'd name-drop, there certainly would be a counter-anecdote from a person who got much happiness from the game in question. If it makes the above argument easier to accept, I suggest considering it purely as an abstract hypothetical: given that we have the tools as game designers, could we not create games that teach people to be worse human beings? Maybe no such game exists today, but that doesn't lessen our responsibility in considering the consequences of our actions.
    \n

    As well as the Stanford Prison and Milgram experiments there are other game-like things that expose a lot of ways that people are mean to each other.

    \n

    Jane Elliott's exercise is intended to expose the ways that we are conditioned towards racism, and is basically a game. It very strongly encourages players to be emotionally abusive to one another inside the circle, without making it explicit.

    \n

    The trick to it is supposed to be that it exposes those indirect techniques\u2014you start to notice them outside of the magic circle. You start to notice the rules working even when you are personally benefiting from them, and to maybe be uncomfortable with that.

    \n

    I think what I'm saying is that large parts of society are actually already games designed for evil. "Gamification" is in some ways a more honest description of what's always been going on. It's part of what makes games so interesting and important to me.

    \n I recently read about a larp that actually was about replicating the prison experience (Kapo in Copenhagen). I think it fits the original criteria of "a game that people would actually play, that would make them do or feel bad things."

    A quote from the article: (http://www.solmukohta.org/index.php/2012/Book, pp10-1)
    "And then started the integration phase. I was, together with my fellow apprentices, abused both verbally and physically, forced by the old prisoners to do hard, meaningless work. Whatever reply I gave to a question would be the wrong one. No work I did would satisfy my master. I would be punished for things he made up. And even though I soon realized that the easiest way was to try to obey, on the inside I was filled with hatred, spite and a sense of injustice that made me nauseous. But I learned. I learned the rituals, the cycle, the work, the dynamics in the camp. At some point I was severely punished for losing my master\u2019s glove. I was gangraped Ars Amandi style, bad enough in itself, but for me, the truly humiliating part was being forced to caress a metal stand while calling it by the name of my in-game boyfriend. I broke down. Completely. And then they comforted me.
    ...
    I, as Elin, was surprised to experience a new feeling: The need to do good, the need for them to like me, the need to be a part of the group. I, or a part of me, experienced a real Stockholm syndrome. After this incident I became the most adaptable builder, with a loyalty and a pride I couldn\u2019t have imagined, in or out of character, only minutes before. The meaningless rituals (like carrying around danger signs just to put a nail in a wall) suddenly made sense. And a few hours later, when new prisoners were to arrive, I thought \u201dI\u2019m so going to show them!\u201d And I was at least as mean to my own apprentice as my master had been to me. Or maybe worse."

    And it was reported as a positive experience by the article writer (learning about herself, exploring the dark sides of humanity etc). Too hard core for me, though.
