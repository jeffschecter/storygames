  • Andy
    December 2006 edited December 2006
    Fred: I think you comments are spot on there:
    Implicit in all of this, though worth stating explicitly, is that this is a continuum.

    I like my games pretty mixed, with both storytelling and roleplaying elements. But I *need* those games to have roleplaying in the majority in order for my gamer-itch to be fully scratched (this seems to hold true all the way up to 49% storytelling). It's no mistake that Spirit of the Century (and thus the Fate engine) is still what I'd call a role-playing game, but it has a heavy left hook in the story-telling arena.

    I've found that games where it's, say, 75% (or more) storytelling that I end up feeling a little hollow about the experience and not as invested in my character and others' characters. Weirdly, this means that if the focus is more strongly on the story, I end up less involved in the story.
    Otherwise, I hear what he's saying, but I've been a little put off lately by folks trying to define what is and isn't a roleplaying game. By his statement, the instant a player picks up their character sheet to look at "what I can do here" (looking at the laundry list of abilities, skills, powers), he is making decisions as a player and not a character, and thus the game becomes a Storytelling Game.

    But yeah, rather than semantic fiddling, I'd rather just open the idea up to a continuum; "This is an RPG where the players can sometimes step outside their characters to influence the adventure" vs "This is an RPG where the focus is experiencing the world through feeling immersed in a character's skin" or the like. Or that "This game plays out like a cross between a board game and a roleplaying game".

    -Andy

    December 2006
    Posted By: Andy
    Otherwise, I hear what he's saying, but I've been a little put off lately by folks trying to define what is and isn't a roleplaying game. By his statement, the instant a player picks up their character sheet to look at "what I can do here" (looking at the laundry list of abilities, skills, powers), he is making decisions as a player and not a character, and thus the game becomes a Storytelling Game.
    The term "Roleplaying game" is like a huge umbrella holding a lot of different activies in it; some activities end up being in the far ends of the spectrum ("roleplaying" games in sense of working only through a character and "storytelling" games in which the player only controls a character like any other resouce), but most falling somewhere between these two far ends. There could be other ends of the spectrum as well.

    "Roleplaying game" is a thing of history, the starting point, from where a lot of different stuff has evolved, for better or worse. The name sticks because of the common ancestor. I think the distinction, like stated in the referenced post, is an important one, but it also should be noted that the intresting, new stuff can be found from the gray area in between the ends of the spectrum.

    Did I just state the obvious? I feel like a politician.

    Something Martha Wells' LJ pointed to - Ursula Le Guin's article in the New Statesman: Imaginary Friends.

    Curiously enough, most of these "lifelong" children's books are fantasies: books in which magic works, or animals speak, or the laws of physics yield to the laws of the human psyche. When there began to be such a thing as books written for children, in the mid-19th century, fiction was dominated by the realistic novel. Romance and satire were acceptable to it, but overt fantasy was not. So, for a while, fantasy found a refuge in children's books. There it flourished so brilliantly that people began to perceive imaginative fiction as being "for children".

    The modernists extended this misconception by declaring fantastic narrative to be intrinsically childish. Though modernism is behind us and postmodernism may be joining it, still many critics and reviewers approach fantasy determined to keep Caliban permanently confined in the cage of Kiddie Lit. The voice of Edmund Wilson reviewing J R R Tolkien is still heard, bleating: "Oo, those awful Orcs!" There should be a word - "maturismo", like "machismo"? - for the anxious savagery of the intellectual who thinks his adulthood has been impugned.


    -Rob D.

  • Rich Stokes
    December 2006
    Posted By: LarryHow was the Serenity RPG? I hadn't heard anything.
    It's a very vanilla system. Nothing particularly good or bad imo. iirc the system is from MWP's Sovereign Stone game and is fairly similar to Savage Worlds. Pretty book with full colour interiors. Sold like fucking crazy. I seem to remember reading somewhere that Eden said that they'd sold the same number of Buffy books in their first two years as MWP sold Serenity books in the first 3 months.

    There are a number of issues. Some of the rules are somewhat unclear, and MWP were very tardy in issuing any kind of official explanation of how anything worked. The book was printed with no index, which is annoying but not so bad for a relatively short book. A lot of people were quite rightly galled that MWP felt that it was more important to include a full page advert for their upcoming Dragonlance book than a character sheet. The book also promises several downloads from the MWP website which, along with the character sheet took over a year to appear. The book has that "slightly incomplete" feel that a lot of 90s games had. You know, the whole "we'll release a core book and then when people say such-and-such needs more detail we'll release a supplement for that". iirc there are no rules for actually piloting a spacecraft or pulling Wash's crazy stunts, for example. I don't know if it was ever the intention to do a huge gameline to fill in the stuff missing from the core book or just typical rushed product release that left it that way, but either way there wasn't any real extra stuff released anyway.

    The setting stuff is all written from Mal's point of view, and takes great pains to point out that he's no genius and doesn't know all the ins and outs of exactly how everything work. Which kinda leaves you in a "It's not important/make it up" position, which is exactly where I'd have been without shelling out 20 quid for the bloody book.

    The game has a couple of neat features you don't usually find in more trad/non-hippie systems. There's a pretty standard Karma/drama/hero point mechanic that works nicely. Or would, if the system didn't allow them to be traded in for XP (Which means players never actually spend them to do all the cool stuff they can be used for because they're naturally inclined to hoard them for XP). The disadvantages you can buy at character creation don't give you extra points to spend, instead giving you the chance to earn Karma points for playing them out during play. Also the players create their spaceship as if it were another character, which is also pretty cool.

    Overall it's not terrible, but it's not really what I wanted. At the end of the day it doesn't really do enough of the heavy lifting and relies on the GM to fill in a lot of blanks. It's probably no more effort to just take Your Favourite System and run the setting with that. I have no reason to think that the BSG game will be and different

    That any help?
