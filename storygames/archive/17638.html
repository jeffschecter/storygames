    For me, the only games that had traction and stick in my memory are the ones where all sides were pushing, and pushing hard. If even one person flinches, that crack will break things apart.

    It kind of helps to have a "we are not friends, we are engaging in a thing where we owe it to each other to push hard" ritual or something to start you off. The scene structure helps too. Push hard in the scene, use the phrases to push the other player(s) around, don't worry about social consequence until after. Break between scenes and talk about it.

    I find it more off-putting in that game to play with weak or yielding opposition, unless I'm convinced they are up to something.

    The long and the short of it is, you have extremely powerful tools at your disposal, and you are never railroaded. But you will absolutely have to compromise and give in. The trick is making the Mistaken give just as much, and grab as much good as you can in your descent to ruin.

    \n Cool.

    Make sure to have a list of the key phrases in hand. Hans linked them.

    Ask to go slow and build up fluency. Several people in this thread have provided good sentences that you could borrow, in requesting that support. Ask if you could all spend fifteen minutes using the phrases in an example scene. Don't play with them if they say no (not to be petty, but because they're refusing to teach you how to do an emotionally vulnerable thing they want you to do with them).

    Speak up if you feel trounced. A good sentence is, "Hey, I'm feeling overwhelmed and trounced, as a player. Can we fix that?"

    Relish the tragedy. This is a character who might end the game with a cursed demon tentacle arm, with a dead husband, responsible for putting a demonling on the South-District throne. If "but only if" was used right, though, it'll also be a character who slayed demons in droves, who rescued a child at great personal expense, and who kept the library tomes out of the hands of the abyss. Be excited for the prices you'll have to pay. This isn't a requirement for every game, but certainly Polaris.

    Play with people you trust. It sounds like your trust here is shaken. Either rebuild that trust with these players, or find different players. The bad experience you had wasn't necessarily their fault (they might not have understood your needs), and it wasn't necessarily your fault (you might not have known how to ask for support). But certainly avoid that same bewildered/trounced dynamic that you had last time.

    Whenever I would yield or pause too long while trying to figure out what recourse I had, suddenly 10 things happened and all of them were Not Okay.
    \nI assume this isn't literal -- like, you paused, and so the other guy kept talking nonstop, adding more and more things? Because that's just incredibly poor faith/play on their part, particularly if they were aware that you were just learning the game.

    If on the other hand you mean, your responses tended to be a form of stalling, and every time you would stall your opposition would come back with some new development: that's an issue to address within the game, rather than the social level. It's very important, in my experience, to match scope with your opposition, so that the 'back and forth' is approximately even, in terms of impact on the story -- if one of the players is constantly leaping ten steps forward, while the other takes a measured pace back, then you won't end up with a very satisfying story. It's not tragic if good steamrolls over evil, or vice versa.

    Negotiating that scope is probably worth being explicit about, with first-time players, though ideally it is something the players will work out organically as play proceeds -- that way, different scenes can operate at different paces, etc. Also, scope-creep is often an issue in Polaris ('but only if I stab him in the eye, mortally wounding him!' --> 'but only if you are then thrown into prison for six years while your family is murdered!' --> 'but only if, one night, the stars come down to me in prison and whisper in my ear their secret perfect names'.) It's easy to end up with a scene where actions are taking place at all sorts of different levels, and for an inexperienced player (or non-cooperative play-group) that can easily be abused to screw over different parties.

    Of course, scope of action isn't the only important thing -- equally key is scope of emotion/investment. So sometimes your opponent might narrate some sweeping epic event that takes place over several days and your response could be a single, brief moment. It all depends what you want. At its core I think the game is kind of about this: figuring out what really matters, to you and to your opponent, while trying to keep them in the dark about the same thing. The worst result as a Mistaken in Polaris is narrating some Super-Horrible Bad Thing and then noticing that the Heart player is kind of shrugging and smiling to herself -- that means you just gave them something for free, because you misunderstood what matters to them about their character. But the point of that interaction is not to beat the other person, exactly -- it's just a mechanism for the players to discover these things about each other, in a narratively competitive environment.

    \n Sorry you had a bad time.

    The intent is that you'll have a reference sheet for key phrases in front of you at all times until you've internalized them. Memorization is not a part of the challenge of Polaris.

    Thinking on your feet, though, is. If you're not a thinker-on-feet the game will be hard.

    Interrupting each other is just rude. I don't think that there's a rule against it or anything, but that's because I didn't imagine it would be a problem.

    --

    Here, I will write a new key phrase for you: "Wait wait hold on a second." Take a break, wherein you can ask questions about the current state of play, think about what you want to say next. This phrase interrupts the game where it is. When you have got your head together and gotten advice, return to play just as you left it. If you responded to an interruption with this phrase, finish what you were saying before you were interrupted.

    Here's another one: "Can we play this straight first?" Play a session of regular Polaris before doing whatever hack.

    Consider those official errata.

    --

    Here's another thought.

    In BW, the outcomes of a particular conflict are relatively constraints, by chance and system. It's reasonable to expect that you'll win this fight, lose that one, that reading a book won't lead to the death of your family. Most conflicts in Burning Wheel are _safe_, not in that you won't lose, but in that you understand what's at risk going in. In Polaris, the only constraints on conflict are what the players think is cool. So ... that's a lot more scope and responsibility in terms of play. Nothing is ever safe. It can be a really hard adjustment to make, and it isn't for everyone.

    Not sure if that goes anywhere. Just a passing thought.

    From what you've said it sounds like you haven't read Polaris--and you probably don't remember this from the pitch way back when--but Polaris has tragedy baked-in. It's not a tone-neutral game. The end for the protagonist is always either death or turning into a demon (or in our setting a cylon or cylon sympathizer). It's an actual rule of the game.

    Almost everyone I've seen have a terrible time with Polaris were pros at normal player+GM games, because there are a lot of ways that Polaris might look like one of those games on the surface but it is designed completely differently. It sometimes looks like a duck but it's not that duck. The wrong instincts kick in.

    For example it's easy to mistake the antagonist for the GM and just go along with what they say as being established world facts and feel like you're powerless. But in fact the protagonist and antagonist are entirely equal in their creative power. If the one player introduces anything the other doesn't like they can always jump straight to "You Ask Far Too Much". I know we had this discussion during the game but I was pretty sure even then I wasn't getting the point across. There can be a lot to absorb.

    Like I said in the post-game thread, the game is not really competitive:\n
    The real mistake (and this is a teaching failure on my part) is to think it's competitive. Bold, underline, italicize this part. It really, really isn't. If you think it's competitive you'll be making decisions for all the wrong reasons and the game will spiral into frustration. I think both you and Dustin caught some of this.

    \tThe ideal case (and this is true for just about all story games) is that both sides play equally hard, and push each other to interesting results. It's not competition, because neither side is trying to actually win. The protagonist player doesn't actually want to skate through the game unchallenged, and the antagonist player doesn't actually want to crush the protagonist like a bug. Neither is any fun.
    \nIt may look like we're being competitive because the antagonist is pushing for bad outcomes and the protagonist is pushing for good outcomes, but actually we know it's going to turn out badly for the protagonist in the long run. The real question becomes: how does the hero deal with all this bad stuff? They go down but how do they go down? What do they fight for? Do they stand true to the end or cave and become a mockery of their own ideals? We're playing in good faith and trying to explore that. Like I said in the first thread, the antagonist is trying to find things that matter to the protagonist so we can see how far the protagonist would go to try to save them (really, I could copy and paste a whole long chunk of that thread because that's what it's all about).

    Because of that it's important that the protagonist player not think of the character as their property the way you might in a traditional adventure game. That's not the dynamic at all. The protagonist is the center of the story and we're all watching what happens in their life, but we all have a lot of input because it's the one story we're all playing together (when that character is up). We're constantly intruding on the character. The flip side is that it's essential the protagonist player makes their character an open book to the other players: the whole point is to get in their head and understand their drama. If we don't understand them or can't see how they feel about stuff it's moot.
