\n
\n The word "immersion" is hard to define. People often mean different things when they use the term; and everyone's immersion is different.
\n
\nThis thread is for subjective reports on immersion in role-playing games. No technical debates in this thread, please! Just answer these questions:
\n
\n- What does it feel like when you're immersed while role-playing?
\n- What sort of things help you achieve immersion?
\n- What sort of things stand in the way when you want to achieve immersion?
\n- What sort of things can break your immersion once you're in it?
\n
\nIf you like, tell us of a time you were immersed!

    I ask this because... there's a lot of aesthetic to imply in this thread, and some psychology too. Stuff that touches the mind structures that "recognise" (re-co-gnosis, from greek, gnosis = knowledge + cum = together + prefix re = more than once, so it's "putting the stuff you know together more than once") patterns you may immerse into.

    They are the same keys that triggers those mental images of a "real" scene when you read a book or listen to a story.
    -IF- your knowledge of things is in someway brought to resonance with what you are perceiving, "immersion" is triggered.
    It's possible that those four answers you're waiting for are not exactly conscious. This is probably the reason why "immersion" in not so easily pictured.

    \n For me "immersion" means a complete focus of attention.
    When I'm feeling immersed in a game, it means the game has my full attention and is more important to me (in that moment) than anything else. It means I CARE about what's going on.

    Things that help me achieve immersion are anything that excites me or holds my interest, but mostly it's dramatic weight in the narrative. If cousin Molly's life is riding on the roll, I can be immersed in a byzantine die rolling exercise.

    There is only one thing that stands in the way of my immersion (or breaks it), and that's boredom.

    There is a constant battle for my attention between the game and the world outside the game. Immersion is when the game wins.

    \n It's easier for me to think of things that prevent my immersion than things that create it.

    One thing that buggers it up for me, for example, is using miniatures. I can see how it's useful (or vital in some games), but it severely gets in the way of my imagination. And once my imagination is in free-flow, uninterrupted by other stuff going on around the table, that's when my immersion is strongest. We played 4th ed. D&D the other day and I was deeply immersed until the first encounter, at which point out comes the battle-mat and the figures. And that was that.

    Another thing: one member of my gaming group is always offering cakes around. They're on a plate. Everyone can take one whenever they like. Instead, the GM gets the plate shoved under their nose right in the middle of some important exposition. Cakes are good, for sure, but not at the expense of loss of immersion!

    \n For me, "immersion" always and only means "being in character" or, similarly, "forgetting that it's all fantasy." If that makes me schitzo, then we thank you for the compliment (it's sad to see other humans trapped with one personality--what if it's an asshole?!?).

    I have felt most immersed in LARPs:

    * Once, portraying a wolf (NPC) in a boffer LARP, I ended up alone because my pack was all (stupid and got) killed. Wandering the fringes of the camp sites, trying to stalk individuals that I thought I could take down, fleeing bands of hunters who noticed me and decided they couldn't brook some mangy wolf sniffing around the edge of the village... Yeah, that was VERY fun, very immersive.

    * At a Dragon*Con theater LARP, I was extremely moved by a scene that a woman and I played out... pretty much without anyone else looking on. It was a logical result of play up until that point, given even more frisson by the fact that the game was ending badly for the PCs (Call of Cthulhu; failed to figure out the bad guy; and so Hound of Tindalos emerging to kill us all). We were both near tears, with fear and some actor's equivalent of "love," and it didn't really even matter if no one else was watching--we were watching each other in the moment (and the organizer's passable special effects), riffing off each other, and it worked VERY well.

    * Pretty much any time after nightfall on moonless nights, going ANYWHERE is rife with tension and, yep, even fear. One doesn't go wandering alone, at the very least. And basically having to sneak back to my cabin to get anything (or retire for the night) added a special angle to the immersion: I have to be as thoroughly prepared as one would imagine a night adventurer would have to be, in such a world, before I stepped out onto the village path. Might not be able to make it BACK there, later, if the shit hits the fan!

    I have managed similar immersion in tabletop, at times, but oddly enough only with horror-themed games. I recall being quite spooked out during an all-night Chill game, but I was rather young then (maybe 13 or 14--25 years ago).

    Then again, I'm a Stanislavsky Method actor, so "immersion" has only ever had one meaning for me. And the crux seems to be achieving some echo of the emotions that are plausible and appropriate for the situation, not merely PORTRAYING them. Most tabletop play doesn't let me stay in character enough to achieve full immersion, between having to manage system, deal with real world interruptions (mine and others), and think strategically, be it in terms of story arc (with these hippie games) or in terms of game resources (with more "trad" games).

    You want to BREAK my immersion?

    * Crack OOC jokes about last week's Lost or similar. Although, actually, I have all-but-mastered ignoring this stuff and ostracizing the perpetrators, so it only "shakes" my immersion, it doesn't break it.

    * "Been There, Done That, Got The Chainmail" - There's a class of LARPers who feel that they have to somehow retain their "coolness" by acting like anything and everything in-game is something they've seen before, could beat with one hand behind their backs, and is best addressed with snarky comments and wisecracks. I've actually said to one particularly jaded brat, "Hey, man, sounds like you should just quit the game, if it's so boring and old hat. We'd all be glad not to listen to your bullshit anymore." He had at least the decency to blush and go off with his other (almost as jaded, and practicing HARD to match up!) OOC and IC buddies... to plot some kind of OOC, PVP gank. Which reminds me:

    * Playing a political game with a combat monster; showing up for a combat fest with a non-com. I like to play out of type, too, at times. But it gets REALLY silly to see yet-another "tough, secretive assassin" hanging on the fringes of what is ostensibly a court of powerful and connected people. The "loner type" whose put all build points into combat and, as such, can't accomplish shit in the ACTUAL game play. And so what does he do? Trump up the thinnest excuses to gank someone, because "his guy would do that." To which I'd reply, "which is why your thug wouldn't be admitted within a half a mile of these gentles!" and look for an in-game authority to lock him up. (I find the irony of putting "the loner" into a isolated cell all event just too rich!) The non-com who shows up asking everyone to just get along just gets dropped, no muss no fuss (no lost Armor Points). But he's still immersion-breaking, if only because he can show how a somewhat thin premise for conflict isn't worth bloodshed. (So what? This is a fest--we came to let blood, ya Gandhi wannabee!)

    * Get into a long debate about some annoying niggle of the system. I tend to walk away from these, now, when not forced into a Hold. If I'm in a Hold, I give him or her a TINY bit of time to deal... then bitch and mock the number-cruncher who's wasting 20 or 40 people's time to sort out 1 or 2 points of damage!

    * Technology. In tabletop, you want me to drop totally out and restart cold... let your fucking 50-Cent ring tone go off. In LARP, drive your fucking SUV through the middle of the medieval village during game-on, because you're late AND too much of an asshole to carry your own shit to your cabin. (I've flattened folks' tires for doing this--you've been warned!) I even dislike when organizers have to use walkie-talkies to confirm stuff with each other--if I'm asking OOC, then fine; if I'm asking an NPC and the GM isn't sure... well, then, the NPC isn't sure at the moment: "Ask again later" is preferable t:
    "Hang on, Dave--*SSSHHH-TKK* Hey, Chip; who's the person whose got the treaty?" [wait] "*SHHH-TKT* "It's the Lord of Fortenfart's page, Blarfed." *SHHH-TKT* OK, thanks." *SHHH-TKT* "What?" *SSHK-TKKT* I SAID THANKS!" *SH-TK *Ah, OK."
    ... what did I ask about, again? God... do I care anymore, really?

    Sorry so long--slow day at work, and the memories started flowing.

    \n What I generally think of that I might be tempted to call Immersion is when everything is obvious. As a player, it's when what my character would do in any given situation is obvious to me. As a GM, it's like that, except for the entire world.

    Rulesets help immersion when they're robust enough to cover all the situations, but simple enough that I can answer any questions about them off the top of my head, rather than having to look it up. Settings help when they're rich with easy-to-remember detail and full of relatively consistent color, so like the rules, if somebody asks a question about it, I can come up with the answer right away. Characters help when they've got simple and straightforward motivations that nevertheless manage to be interesting to implement. That sort of thing.

    \n My immersion could best be described as my ego being set aside for the "ego" of the character. It includes perceiving the fictional world as the character, with his understanding and ethics.

    This is a state of mind I aim for in all role-playing games. Without identification with character the game is flat to me. A nice narrative is not enough to make my game. There has to be a character in there for me to identify with, to make the game interesting, and for the game to really take off I have to experience immersion in that character, preferably in communion with other players.

    Narrative in itself is better experienced through the magic of storytelling.

    Conflict-resolution in itself is better experienced through the magic of great board games.

    Character-identification and immersion is the prerogative of role-playing games.

    Thanks for the thread! Nice one!
