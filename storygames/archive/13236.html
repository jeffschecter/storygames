\n First just to cover all possible bases: If, somehow, you're one of the people coming to my wedding or you know someone who is, don't mention this. Keep it a surprise. Thanks!

So, since my fiancee (how do I do the little accents?) knows that I'm a nerd, obviously, she's come up with a novel idea for the bomboniere (the little gifts with sweets in them) that we'll be giving away on the tables. They'll be a d12, basically, with the candy inside. We made a sample one and it looks awesome! But instead of the usual numbers, we have 12 qualities that we believe are going to be important to our marriage. She's asked if I can come up with some sort of game for the people at each table to play. It should be quick to play (ten to fifteen minutes), it should be team-based and it should be fun and simple and something that the largely non-roleplaying group can enjoy. I'm a bit stumped at this, so I thought I would ask my extended brain on the internet for help. What kind of game would you come up with?

The list of qualities is:
- Brave
- Hopeful
- Wise
- Generous
- Honest
- Humble
- Loving
- Faithful
- Patient
- Kind
- Strong
- Reliable

    \n Totally out of my butt, inspired a bit by Do: Pilgrims of the Flying Temple, and not team-based.

    You're a bunch of snow white-ish dwarves. You roll to see what your dwarf's quality is.
    A dwarf baby is trapped in the mine! Take turns describing a complication that makes it hard to save the baby (a "Yes, but..."), and how your dwarf uses their quality to help save the baby.
    Example:
    "A baby's trapped in the mine. You must rescue her!"
    Player 1: "Yes, but the baby is trapped by a rockslide. My dwarf is generous, so he donates his tools to help save the baby."
    Player 2: "Yes, but your tools are broken. My dwarf is reliable, his tools are in good shape, so he uses those to dig through the rocks."
    Player 3: "Yes, but he doesn't properly calculate the leverage, and more rocks fall. My dwarf is wise, so he uses trigonometry to figure out the best place to dig."
    Etcetera.

    Go around the table once. Anybody who's stuck can ask for suggestions. The last player saves the baby.

    Yeah, it's lame. I would never play it. But it might be an ok wedding diversion.

    \n Are there a lot of sweets? Because it would be cool to use those as some sort of betting tokens...
    \n
    \nTo keep it simple, you could take it in turns around each table to roll one of the dice and then everybody has to put forward a statement showing how they embody that quality, e.g. you roll 'Loving' and everybody has to say "I'm the most loving, because...", then those seated at the table vote on who wins that round by passing a sweet to them. Play for as long as you like or for as many sweets as you have; if a quality repeats, then the guests compete to see who least embodies that quality, e.g. "I'm the least patient, because...". Keep it light and funny (keep the champagne flowing!) and everyone will have a raucously good time.

    \n Create two fictional characters, a married couple. For the Valentine's Day game I made for my wife their names were Mango and Honeydew.



    Each player rolls against the list and tells a little story about some problem that happens in the marriage when the trait is missing. And the other player has to guess which trait it is that's missing. Guess right and you get one of the treats.

    Paul

    \n No idea for the game, but know your audience. While weddings are a big deal for the people getting married, most people attending the wedding spend their time screaming at near strangers over bad music played too loudly while eating overcooked food. Most of these people are really looking to do one of the following through most of the wedding:

    drink a lot.
    hook up with a stranger.
    people watch.
    gossip.
    find a way to slip out the back and do something more interesting.

    If you can find a way to incorporate some of that into a dice game about virtues, you'll have a really memorable wedding.
