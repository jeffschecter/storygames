Engaging The Players Through The Game vs. Around The Game

Jesse
November 2006 edited November 2006 in Story Games
Hello Everyone,

So, I went to GenCon SoCal briefly on Friday night and all day Saturday. While I was there I got to observe part of a Spirit of the Century game; character creation on Friday night and then part of actual play on Saturday. Now what I'm about to say is going to come off as very harsh cricticsm of Spirit of the Century so I have to be clear up front. I have not read Spirit of the Century and I have not actually played it myself. My comments are strictly based on what I saw happening at that table and even then I was doing one or two other things (like reading Wilderness of Mirrors).

Here's what I think I saw. I saw a character generation system/session that rapidly generated a lot of pulp flavored color and got everyone grooving on each other's character's look and feel. This is not a bad thing. This is, in fact, the best thing I saw and after watching this I had high hopes that Spirit of the Century might actually be a game that interests me.

Then I saw the actual play. And it seemed to me that the game kind of walked out the door. What I saw was a bunch of great personalities creatively riffing of each other's color that was generated during the character generation process. I saw a lot of creative use of key catch phrases. I saw laughing and smiling. I'm not denying anyone's fun here. But what I didn't see was a game. I saw a bunch of people talking and then (without much structure at all) sort of employing an overly complex version of a coin flip to occassionaly decide whether something should go left or go right. Not once did I see any tension, decision making or cross-table player engagement happen over the deployment of the resolution mechanic. All the fun here was because people were doing something I call Engaging The Players Around The Game.

Contrast this with something like Dogs in the Vineyard. A player puts forward a Raise and attached to that Raise is a bit of fiction. Right away there are decisions to be made. Since you know the value of the Raise you know how much "weight" your bit of fiction is going to carry. If you push up a whopping 16 you know it's going to be powerful. What do you want to carry that kind of power? Some witty dialogue? A gunshot? It's important. The other players recognize its importance.

Then across the table there are more decisions that must be made in return. At the game level: How do I beat a 16? At the character level: What traits do I want to apply and how? At the story level: Are the stakes really worth escalating to the next level of violence? And probably most importantly at the inter-play communication level: What do I want my See and bit of fiction to say about their Raise and bit of fiction.

I call this Engaging The Player Through The Game. Everything that the players are meant to connect over is rooted in decisions proposed by and mittegated through the game itself.

Now I would like to say, I saw a potential mechanic in Spirit of the Century that MIGHT be used to the ends I'm talking about. It's the rule that I think is called Compelling An Aspect. (Again I haven't read the game so this is coming from observing the game). So my understanding is that the GM can offer up a resource called a Fate Chip to get your character to do something specific. If you want to refuse you have to pay a Fate Chip. This is how I WANT this mechanic to be used:

Let's say I'm playing a detective (Jack Bloodthorn) with the Aspect "There's always a Dame..." Bloodthorn is walking up to the house of a key witness when he hears gunshots inside. Then there's a scream and he sees a woman (not the witness) being driven off in the back of a car speeding out of the front drive. The GM offers up a Fate Chip and says he wants Bloodthorn to chase after the woman 'cause, "There's always a Dame!"

See what I'm facing now? Part of this is the get a chip/pay a chip decision but I'm also being asked what the safety of the witness is worth vs the safety of the unknown woman. The offering of the Chip is an underscore or exclamation mark around the decision. It signals to everyone at the table, "Hey this is important!" The Aspect demonstrates WHY it's important. And my decision to take a chip or spend a chip demonstrates just HOW important it is. Engagement THROUGH the system.

My problem was that I didn't really see this potential use of that system deployed and I didn't see much of the rest of the system organized around its use in the manner i describe. Note: For all know this was never the intended use of the mechanic anyway so criticising it for failing to be central is not really the point.

This is turning rantish and I don't mean it to be. My point is that red flags go up for me when it seems like all the fun is centered around just appreciating everyone's witty commentary and personality. I can do that without a game. If I'm playing a game then I want to connect and socialize and have fun with/through/about the game.

Does this make any sense?

Jesse

    I have a suspicion regarding Fate 3.0 and the tendency to forget about the fate point issue:

    There are three different flavors of resolution: Simple rolls against fixed numbers (checks), complex rolls on both sides (contests), and chained complex rolls (conflicts). It's easy to only use the first two kinds of flavors and gloss over the third. For example, I had all the players roll once to determine if they fall under the roaches' influence. This should have been a conflict where a number of wins in a row are very important because of the roll-up feature on the damage track. You really want to hit high marks repeatedly to force your opponent to concede, which you'll probably achieve by using your own or tagging your opposition's aspects.

    I'd need another session or two to find if this suspicion holds true, but if there is a conflict or two for each character to participate in, aspect tagging/compelling is much more part of the system than with the single roll resolution flavors.

    The things that SotC needs (and lacks IIRC) is advice on building a potent conflict mechanics wise, i.e. NPCs that hold up against a group of characters, and how to use the rules strategically as a GM. That is, in many games where such the use is optional, if there's no cue in the rules as to when to use the mechanics, the players simply forget to use them (even if it's to their benefit to do so, or they'll get rewarded for doing so). In HQ, using Hero Points to bump can only be done at one point in the process. But even then I've found that their use drops off if I don't explicitly say every time "That's a Marginal Defeat, bump to a Minor Victory?"

    Maybe if one remembered to verbally cue players in FATE when coming up with the total modifiers... I'll have to think about it. Making it a standard part of the process, like I do with augments in HQ (you don't have to augment, but it's clear in my games that it's an exception not to have any augments).     Again, the problem is, in part, because the uses are optional. In D&D combat, you roll for initiative, you roll to hit, you roll for damage. Where options exist, they're questions of whether to use spells or something. When you have mechanics that are instituted completely at the option of the player, especially where he may be uncertain if they're applicable, in play I've seen players just skip these steps. Sans a will to push for a win, and a willingness to play out the Aspect wether they get a bonus for it or not, why bother doing the extra math?

    Wow. If only this topic had mentioned Spirit of the Century in the subject line -- I would have taken notice of it much sooner!

    Here's my opinionated, personal take on things:

    * If you have a scene in Spirit of the Century, and exit it, without having interacted with aspects in some way, that scene was either totally superfluous or you've missed something. If on analysis it proves to be superfluous, why in hell did you have the scene? If you've missed something, it may be because you didn't zero in on the opportunities to invoke, tag, declare, assess, or compel. Very often the GM has missed an opportunity to regard a player's behavior as compel-reward-worthy. Shame on him.

    * When the original poster says "What I saw was a bunch of great personalities creatively riffing of each other's color that was generated during the character generation process. I saw a lot of creative use of key catch phrases. I saw laughing and smiling. I'm not denying anyone's fun here. But what I didn't see was a game," he is professing that he is blind, but wondering why he didn't see something. Or to put it another way: "creative use of key catch phrases" IS THE SYSTEM IN OPERATION.

    * Let's look at this from the perspective of a TSOY game where I have, say, the Key of Masochism (I'm looking to get hurt in fights for XP). If an outside observer watches me get into fights a lot and get fucked up, while I silently mark down some XP for what I've done, that observer might easily conclude that there's nothing but an "overly complex coin-flip" involved in my behavior. But he's flat wrong. I'm not going to engage in that path of behavior without having that particular Key under my belt. Its presence created incentives for behavior. That's system. That is in fact, engaging me THROUGH the game. Aspects work in a nearly-identical manner -- but when characters have just been created, it's hard for the GM (who's the gatekeeper on players receiving rewards for that -- some more thoughts on this shortly) to stay on top of what everyone's aspects are. Which flows into...

    * The players have a RESPONSIBILITY to say to the GM "hey, look, I acted in accordance with this aspect, it restricted my choices interestingly and dramatically, I should get a fate point". But that responsibility isn't always communicated to the players, nor acted upon. Let's look at TSOY again, since I have a recent experience playing in Jason Morningstar's TSOY game at MACE. Jason said up front to us that we should be driving hard at our keys, and self-regulating whether or not we're due XP for certain actions. Worked fine, especially for a Con game. I did my various Key of Doom and so forth actions and got XP for it that I was responsible for recording, personally. So here's where I alter what I was saying earlier: while the text suggests that the GM is the gatekeeper of Fate Point rewards for compels, there's no reason -- especially in a con game of SOTC -- for the GM to reserve that role solely for himself. He should feel free to delegate to the players -- to tell them, hey, if you have a scene where your aspect makes trouble for you, grab a fate point from the bowl in the center, don't ask me about it. You should be gaining and spending FP in every scene if possible!

    That said:

    * If I ever say in the game, you are having bad wrong fun if you are bypassing the Fate Point economy, I should stop making Fate games, because I've just become a dick. Fate is founded on a philosophy of "use what you like, set aside what you don't". We don't exercise the "tyrrany" of EVERY PART OF OUR PRECIOUS PRECIOUS SYSTEM MATTERS AND MUST BE USED on purpose. As a Fudge-based game, that's an ethos that is preexistent in that community, and as such we're aggressive about encouraging drift, customization, mix-and-match use of what we put out there. We don't enforce a damn thing, and we shouldn't; the attitude suffuses every edition and version of Fate we've done for the past five years.

    * And given that, it's fucking hilarious to me that the empowered players of the indie movement go nuts when confronted with a game that says you don't use all of it all the time in every way provided. I mean, come on, guys. Really? Some games are do-whatever-you-want friendly, and aren't worth engaging in a great hue and cry over when one group has the fun without using every intricate detail of the system. Cope, already.
