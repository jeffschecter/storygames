\n (First, strike memory of "ACTION CASTLE" from your mind. Good.)

So I log into a chatroom, where various people are. Joanne45 responds:

> You enter into Swordslinger's Hall. However, your master is not there.
> You have: 1 wooden sword, a small bag of gold
> You can go back outside, or upstairs to the library.

I type in what I want to do ("go upstairs"). Joanna45 leaves (she has to get to class) but James122 [1] continues:

> The library is nearly empty, except for Archmagus Tomas, who is flipping through the pages of an old tome by the fire.
> You can go back downstairs.

I talk with Tomas (via James122) briefly, and then go back downstairs. Norman18 signs on to the room, and she re-describes the downstairs, in a consistent fashion [2] with the above.

I continue for a few more scenes, and my brief adventure ends: I find my master slain, and take my revenge on Tomas.

...

Things to note:

[1] is because there is some guidelines/system for sharing the rights to be the GM/World in the situation. (And probably similar rules for whomever is theplayer.)
[2] is aided by some fashion of continually tracking/saving the information that's built up in a reasonable fashion.

Overall, this seems cool. It's kinda freeform, but with simple constraints applied (certainly to actions and world depth, and perhaps to the depth of the plot as well). This is obviously replicating the kinds of stuff that IF or MUSH can be made to do, but there's no need to program what's done by human intuition; but what's more, all of this interaction is between live people, not between live person and a previously created construct. The "fiction" created can appeal to everyone taking part, not just to the player.

In short: I think it's cool. Any co-brainstorms here?

    \nYeah, like in Waiting/Tea. Those aren't that hard to build, with a little bit of playtesting to get the list right. It's kinda like Og, where you only have a limited vocabulary.

    And you could decide what's actually an object in the game and what's actually color. Like, if you try to take books from the library the GM/World could respond, "You flip through a few of the books, but they are either supremely boring or in a language you don't understand."

    \n Because I've had a card game design fixation for, like, the last year, I think it would be fun if you had a card-based story game based on the limitations of early parsers. Each card would have a word on it, you'd have a hand of cards, and to take an action you'd have to "play" the command you wanted to take. So you'd have cards like, TAKE, EXAMINE, GO, SWORD, SAY, NORTH, WITH, DOWN, ROOM, OGRE, HELLO.

    Now, the story game aspect would be that this worked sort of like Donjon. If you play TAKE and SWORD... you get a sword. If you GO DOWN, you get to narrate the next room.

    Maybe words would be categorized and color-coded, so if you played LOOK you get to deal out some direction cards or whatever. Maybe when you gain items, you play them face down in front of you, and if you forget which card is which, you have to play INVENTORY to look at them. Maybe they'd all be in one big deck. I haven't decided.

    \n On one hand, the limited set of verbs might be overly constraining, or overly large, or may "give away" some things. However, I think this would be more fun as story-creating/exploration, rather than as a puzzle, and Waiting/Tea manages to hit a good balance of safe objects/verbs.

    Also: constraints => good. Like a quickie setup: the verbs are MOVE, ATTACK, and TALK - and that's it. And allow the people around there to have different potential states. (Maybe if you TALK+TALK+TALK to Lord Vicious, he'll lay down his sword. Maybe TALK+ATTACK+TALK will do it, but maybe he'll just attack you. Maybe ATTACK+ATTACK+ATTACK will disarm him, but maybe you'll run him through and you'll lose the chance to bring him in alive.)
