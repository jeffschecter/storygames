\n GUMSHOE is a hybrid system, with one half providing distinctive mechanics for investigative, and the other a simple resource-based ruleset for when randomness is fun.

This was always designed so that the investigative side, and some other aspects of the system such as Drives, could be bolted on to other games. I've found since GUMSHOE, that we use GUMSHOE as a verb sometimes when we get bogged down in rolling for clues or personal interactions - as in "let's GUMSHOE it." - a sort of informal hybrid. Our first release will be a Pathfinder/GUMSHOE crossover (PATHSHOE? GUMFINDER?) which has just completed playtesting. Playtesters are divided about 6:1 between great joy and blind hatred of this monster.

I'm considering other systems to hybridise; BRP, FATE perhaps. What system would you suggest, and why?

    \n I suspect that guidelines for older rulesets that tend to the simulationist would be more useful than the more modern, more narrative systems (such as anything FATE-based). Given the natural link between Call and Trail, I suspect that something for CoC or (perhaps more preferably) the newer generic Deluxe BRP would be very welcome; I also suspect that something for "Old School" movement games might also be interesting and welcome (such as Labyrinth Lord, Swords & Wizardry, and other related games). Finally, something for the grand-scale "generic engine" games might be welcome: HERO, GURPS, EABA.

    \n I (and my co-conspirator) have used the resource-management side of GUMSHOE to very good effect in con one-shot scenarios with pre-gen characters. Specifically in a short-story (1-2 hours tops) format.
    \n
    \nWhat we did was to kick out the technical skill names and substitute significant character traits. In order to spend from a pool, you had to bring the trait/theme into play. Oh, and we ditched the cumbersome damage system in favour of extra damage to the pools of losers, and loathsome in-fiction consequences of failure.
    \n
    \nEffect: A strong mechanical reward for delivering a rounded and diverse presentation of your character's themes and personality in the short time allotted to the game.
    \n
    \nOf course this has nothing to do with the investigation bit, and short-story investigation can hardly help feeling hasty, I suppose. So, just to illustrate something else that the pool system can be good for.
