\n I'm talking about a game that's not about superheroes, per se. I'm talking about the costumed heroes who were in comics before there were superheroes. For a basic working definition, let's say that "real" superheroes fight supervillains who are legitimately dangerous and relevant to the issues that the hero has; like, how Magneto is relevant to the X-Men because of the race-war thing, and the Joker is relevant to Batman because of that complete-lack-of-sanity-and-reason vs. uber-rational-control-freak thing. Even if you disagree with all that (and I am NOT interested in debating it, especially in this thread; you can debate it with someone else somewhere else if you want), it should at least give you an idea of what I'm shooting for.

So, I'm talking about the costumed heroes who were in comics before the superheroes. I'm talking about the Bat-Man, in the early, early stuff. I'm talking about the Sandman, the original one with the gasmask and pinstripe suit. I'm talking aboutDr. Occult and Rose Psychic. I'm talking about the Spirit. I'm talking about the Question, even though he came out in the '60s and some of his opponents were kinda supervillain-like. These guys all dealt with ordinary crooks (at the time). It was sort of an outgrowth from detective comics, but the introduction of the costumes and powers changed things quite a bit, making them a genre apart from detective comics. (I would even include The Shadow in this genre, even though he's radio)

So, they're not detectives, per se, nor are they superheroes, per se. They're more like vigilante detectives in costumes. But, then, they don't necessarily have costumes; Dr. Occult and Rose Psychic didn't. I've heard people use the term "mystery men" to refer to these kinds of characters, but that's already in use to refer to a specific comic book and the film based on it, so no dice there.

I also want to take that stuff, which (excepting the Spirit) is usually not very dark (in presentation, at least; the idea of a guy in a pinstripe suit & fedora and a GASMASK bursting into the den of thieves and putting everyone to sleep with gas is friggin' scary), and darken it up some. Give it an intravenous injection of Raymond Chandler.

Any ideas?

    \n Jake,
    Okay, I've got a better answer now. Here's a sort of run-down of it:

    The world, as depicted in the game (in the early 1930s, in a big American city somewhere), is morally gray. Crime (in the legal sense) does not necessarily equal villainy, and villainy does not necessarily equal crime. The innocent should be protected from the wicked, but innocence is something that you have to sorta squint to see. I want to explore that.

    When somebody takes the law into their own hands, puts on a costume, and fights crime, they're making a moral stand. They're saying, "This is what I believe is white, and this is what I believe is black." What's their standard? Who are they to be judge? When might their judgment break down? I want to explore that.

    Everyone in the game (the heroes, the villains, the ordinary folks) is driven to some degree by some combination of grief, hopes and dreams, and the wish to protect their loved ones. These things make them stronger. But what happens when these things become conflicts of interest? I want to explore that.

    There's a certain perversity in dressing up in a weird costume and using it to provide the power to impose your will on other people. No matter how you justify or rationalize it, it's still kinda creepy. Add onto that the fact that only the heroes are dressing up; the villains are ordinary. I want to explore that.

    I see that tagline, "Thrilling tales of justice!" as having a bit of irony to it. I see it emblazoned, sensational and desperate, across a sheet of pulp paper, which is known for its relatively high amount of acid. That little bit of acid, so to speak, I want to explore.
