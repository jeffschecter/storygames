\n I blurted these two terms in another thread and was curious if they resonated with anyone else.

At first thought, I imagine the Non-Actionable Setting is the background material for what a game is about. For example, "Dogs travel from town to town, ridding them of sin." If you deviate or break any part of the non-actionable setting, it becomes a fundamentally different game.

By that reasoning, actionable setting are any bits that can be changed. Character motivations, plans, personalities, etc.

Any of that make sense, or am I just rambling in my own brain? :P

    I talked a little about this in my blog post on genre sim, breaking setting down into formulas with variables. So to use your terms, the non-actionable setting is the premise of the formula, and the actionable setting are the variables in the formula. So to use Blowback as an example, the non-actionable setting is "Spies set up and sabotaged, doing odd jobs while trying to get to the bottom of what happened." The actionable setting is the menu of Clients, Mooks, and Bosses, etc.

    \n I'm with Vernon, except I'll go one step further and say that all Settings can be distilled to a rather brief statement, and everything else is just Color.
    \n
    \nLike, in The Rustbelt, the Setting is "a place where hardship and desperation are the norm, and there are forces actively encouraging people toward corruption." Everything else -- the ghost towns, the Expanse, the accents that I use when portraying NPCs -- is Color that you can switch out without affecting the play of the game on anything but a cosmetic level. (Which isn't always a shallow change; it can be quite profound) I've even done three of these to prove it: Mosswood, a fantasy thing; Frontier Suns, which is Firefly with the serial numbers filed off; and Heatwave, a sort of Guy Ritchie stylized crime movie thing.
    \n
    \nSo, if you're playing a game set in Victorian England during a hyperbolized version of the Industrial Revolution. Your Setting is probably, "A place where the gulf between the rich and poor is wide and widening, and recent developments in technology have upset society on every level." Any stuff about the Queen, steam engines, gin-addled teenage factory workers, and so on is Color, and can be swapped out for other stuff.
    \n
    \nAnother related thing: Color and Setting can both become Situation when a character becomes Positioned relative to them. Situation is the total collective elements that characters in the fiction are Positioned relative to.

    \nThis strikes me as a very personalized definition - because no two people are going to have exactly the same view on what a "fundamental" difference in the game is, even within the same gaming group. Your examples don't even seem quite consistent to me. For example, take your suggested Non-Actionable Setting "Dogs travel from town to town, ridding them of sin." This is primarily a statement about character behavior -- i.e. the Dogs choose to travel, and their goal is to rid a town of sin. If the players change their characters' motivation, they might well change this fundamental aspect of the game.

    Many games will leave the setting more-or-less intact, without a change that could be viewed as fundamental. However, some games may change the setting. Taking your example of Dogs in the Vineyard. Let me pick two examples:

    1) A demon gets a victory over the Dogs PCs, and suddenly they find themselves 150 years in the future, among the Faithful of a near future world - where they have to deal with the influence of virtual reality games on the Faithful and so forth.

    2) The PCs decide that a town really needs them, and settle to become the permanent guardians of that town rather than wandering.

    Are either of these fundamental changes?

    To take a non-hypothetical example, I ran a campaign of Lacuna recently, in which a number of the PCs ended up deciding to leave The Company and take up with a rival NGO. That was a very major change from the original premise, and yet I thought it was also a very interesting direction for the game to take.
