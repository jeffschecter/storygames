    \n I've started freelancing for a couple of companies, and have been dealing with a few others recently.

    (...and I hasten to add that while this is only my skewed perspective, it's a valid perspective to add to a well rounded picture...)

    There seems to be more talk of retaining the existing players, or capturing the players who fell away from D&D because they didn't like the shift to 4E, or because there's something they don't like about the way a specific system handles "situation X". In this light, there are a lot of "designers" who are simply cobbling a few old and new parts together, or even just veneering a new surface on the same old mechanical underpinnings. No real innovation in what they are doing, and they aren't really seeking to change the gaming world, but they're a solid presence keeping traditional gaming alive (or maybe they think they are desperately trying to prevent it's inevitable demise).

    I guess that's probably where the Old School Renaissance crowd comes in. Instead of reaching out to new players, they're trying to lure back in the old players.

    It's not the most effective business model, but it takes less risk than forging new paths through the creative wilderness. It's effectively saying, we can't really compete with the advertising juggernaut that is WotC, but we'll be there when people want something more, or something a bit different.

    The irony is that many of these games and gaming accessories seem to be turning a decent income for their creators. While the true innovators are trying to work out why the hobby isn't following their lead...

    ...maybe I'm just editorialising too much.

    Posted By: thadrineI do not know about the rest of the people here, but I convert at least one D&D player a month.
    \n...which is cool, expanding people's options is a good thing to do, but it doesn't do anything to "grow the hobby" in terms of bringing in NEW players.

    The most valuable thing any of us can do to get more people gaming is to run games for strangers.

    To elaborate a little further on a point Mike made, right now there are a number of companies who can do great-looking introductory products, and create systems designed to ease new players into the hobby. But currently, WotC is the only publisher equipped to support the regular creation of new GMs. The fact that anyone who might possibly be interested, even just casually, in trying out this RPG thing, knows that on any given Weds night they can go by the shop and drop into a game where we're specifically ready to help new players learn, that's a huge thing that no one else has been able to manage on a national scale, with the possible exception of GW, whose product is similar but not nearly as complex as an RPG.

    I know the Story Games community has a complicated and occasionally ambivalent relationship with game retailers, but is is one place where the receptive and progressive stores can hopefully meet the DIY crowd halfway and serve as incubators for new players. If you want NEW people coming into the hobby, you need a transition zone between the kinds of social activity they're used to and the rarified space of dedicated gamers. Good community stores, and less regularly, large-scale venues like ComiCon, PAX, etc, are the places that can happen.

    -Jim Crocker

    Jim, this is true, and it's how I view retailers. On the one hand, if I sell through a retailer, I effectively lose the sale, making just a couple of dollars on a copy. On the other hand, if that sale is to someone who wouldn't have bought it otherwise and leads to a player bringing it to his home group, who gets excited about it and either talk about it on the Internet or buy their own copies, it's a net benefit. It's a core tenet of the indie RPG phenom that the best publicity for a game is enthusiastic players, after all, and stores can make enthusiastic players. I have no doubt that in-store play and sales are more effective overall than promotional materials, and they're certainly more cost effective \u2014\xa0if I print posters, for instance, I pay for them, rather than vice versa. And who gets excited about a poster, seriously? Jim's got a lot of posters in his store. They make a nice ambience, but I don't actually see them. Games, though, people who like games see games.

My objective is to find their fans, because even if they know about roleplaying games, they don't think they can get a Dispossessed/Use of Weapons experience out of RPGs. I want to show them differently.

    \n I think many hobbies and arts are as concerned about getting new people into them as RPGs. Specifically younger new people.

    There's just a ton of competition for people's ears, eyeballs, participation and dollars in many fields of entertainment whether you're looking at the commercialized side or no.

    Aside from manga, the average age of the comic book enthusiast grows every year as fewer and fewer people enter that particular field of interest. Ditto rpgs. Ditto bowlers, model train enthusiasts, wargamers, bridge players, opera, symphony and theatre-goers fergawdsakes. I have a particular interest in theatre and if not for the massive reinforcement, often mandatory, by educational institutions to spread the meme of live scripted performance I wonder whether anyone under the age of twenty would know who Ibsen was fifty years from now.

    I don't personally care about the growth of the rpg industry. I've done the things I need to do locally to reinforce the particular flavors of the hobby that I enjoy. That's enough for me. Sure I'd love it if there were a million more people playing games like PTA (to name just one game I think has broad appeal), but only because I think it's an activity that many people would really enjoy if given the opportunity to try it, not because I care about the "industry."

    To address the OP specifically, I read the EN World forums daily and browse the WotC forums occasionally. This is an ongoing concern for them, and they discuss it at least as frequently as the indie community does. My off-the-top-of-my-brain theory is:

    1) They have more to lose.

    2) Most (not all) of their populations are not as active of proselytizers and/or they are often not as comfortable playing with new people so when they see their player bases erode (expressed in the singular event that the group they've been playing in a seven-year campaign with just broke up), their relative inactivity previous to that event causes them at least as great of anxiety and they bemoan the lack of growth in the hobby.

    \nThat's something I've been thinking of with the OSR and how they're almost doing it right. The first video games that really created the market where people continued playing them and wanted more and more complex games were actually very simple. Pac-Man and Space Invaders are incredibly well known games with constant pop culture references, and they were incredibly simple games. They also had a lot of hardcore appeal with trying to get the highest score possible. Over time video games became very complex to the point that they needed entire tutorial levels (every Final Fantasy game now takes easily half an hour before you can start playing the game fully, while you got started right off the bat in FF1), and similarly with RPGs you have to spend hours or days reading through the book, making sure everyone else has too, then making characters and spending time preparing adventures (even if it's a pre-made one). There's a similar disinterest among most people to put that much time into video games as there is for RPGs, and it has to do with not having enough time.

    For RPGs to draw new people in, they just have to go back to the way they were before, where 30 minutes was very long, rather than short, for character generation, and the rules were very simple. Simple casual games are putting iOS forward as the biggest competitor Nintendo has ever seen in the handheld market. People start with the simple games like Dizzypad and then occasionally move on to more complex or hardcore games. Those simple games that most people go for are no more complex than Pac-Man, but simply more polished. A game that's as simple as D&D was, but more polished and with rules that make sense to people who don't come from a wargaming background would do the trick. It would also I think have to do something video games don't already do better.

      Specifically, in regards to creating characters, I contend that the perception of such procedure as "quick" is an effect of selectively remembering only parts of said procedure as such.       In general, I've been struggling long time with the "old school" mentality that older was simpler, even while I now agree that "simpler" is often better. While arguably simpler than their most outrageous direct derivatives (3E D&D being an egregious offender, and 4E approaching it closely), "old school" RPGs only appear "simple" to the already RPG-conditioned mind of RPG enthusiasts. Mentzger/Moldvay Basic D&D, which was deliberately designed to be a simplified and accessible version, still manages to be way more intricate and complex than so many later works; it's only a quick and simple pick-up game to dedicated hardcore D&D-ists (which a majority of consummate roleplayers probably are).

      \n Creating a Character in Basic D&D meant rolling AT LEAST nineteen dice (much more if you use other more common creation methods like the "roll 4 take the 3 highest" or "roll 12 stats and choose 6") hit points included, then you had to calculate 6 sums, then you have to consult 6 tables and more, and then often you had to start again because the character is unsuitable (too weak or doesn't have the necessary scores for the class).
      And then you start rolling again for spells you can know, starting money, etc.
      You have to choose AT THE START your career, without knowing what this mean (it's like having to choose you college specialization when you are 5 years old), when you choose the class and you don't know the game.
      If you have a magic user or a cleric then you you start to choose the spell, and read all the rules about every single spell (every spell is a game exception)

      This is simple only compared to Rulemonster or other more complicated versions of D&D (don't let me start on AD&D...)

      As a reference, this how you create a character in other fantasy games that give you that "fantasy adventurer" feeling:
      - Trollbabe: choose a number from 2 to 9, then describe your character. Stop.
      - S/lay w/Me: choose 2 things from a couple of short list, say who you are and what you are searching for, stop.
      And I am sure other can list many, many games (even traditional ones) much more fast and simple for a novice player.

      In the "old times", when I played Basic D&D, the effect of many prospective players was evident: at the end of the character creation process they were bored, exhausted, and stopped making thoughtful decisions because they wanted to PLAY, finally... and some times all this "work" really scared some people who refused to play.

      But nostalgia of youth could make even AD&D appear as a "slim and easy system", I suppose. (but the process takes time, it' for this reason that I think we are seeing the OSR only now...)

      \n hans, I think where you're getting hung up is that while Basic D&D offers a few apparently-simple choices, the ramifications of those choices are really important and not at all obvious.

      So, sure, if you don't have strong opinions about what you want to play -- roll your stats, roll hit points, roll starting money, get your basic equipment, pick a spell if you need to, and go.

      But each one of those choices has a lot of ramifications. Suppose we're rolling 4d6, drop the lowest, and arrange to suit. Well, the best place for your attribute scores depends on what class you want to be, so it's not simple. Or, well, we could just roll 4d6, drop the lowest, in order, but then you have to choose your class based on your attributes, or despite your attributes, and both of those approaches have consequences later on. Every one of those apparently simple choices has long-term ramifications.

      So someone who knows the game, and who more importantly has already studied and understands all of these relationships, *can* create a character really quickly. But if that player has the slightest inclination to ponder his options, you'll be well over half an hour. And for a novice, there's a lot to understand, so if we're talking about barriers to entry, well, those barriers are pretty high. I noticed that with D&D, as with Magic later on, a lot of the new players came to the game not by reading the books and trying it, but by being recruited and taught the basics by other players. And I can't think of many players for which this is not the case: that their introduction to roleplaying games came not because they picked up a book and read it, but because they sought out or were found by other roleplayers.
