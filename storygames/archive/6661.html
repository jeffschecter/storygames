\n So I've got a copy of the DMG in front of me. The other books aren't available yet in stores that don't keep track of release dates.

Already I want to mess with the system... mostly the reward structure and give some more player control over the world. I'd also like to add something like a detail mechanic...

Inspectres ripoff to get players adding detail:
On any d20 roll of 11 the roller describes an extra minor positive detail.
On any d20 roll of 10 the roller describes an extra minor negative detail.

Level cap of 12th, after which you gain feats.

However, you can only earn 6 levels through the standard XP system. The other 6 levels must be earned off of the LIST.

The LIST (I'll add more options as I think of them):

1) Lead 3 sessions (handling out-of-game-level organization of the sessions).
2) Wrote 3 secrets, 3 contacts, 3 goals, and 3 problems in contact with the GM.
3) Helped bring out secrets, contacts, problems, and goals of another player character.
4) Created a nice bristol board sized map for the game (can count up to 3 times)
5) Plays an NPC connected to another player.
6) Backstory: Wrote up the events of two adventures prior to the game that involved other player characters as well as your own (in coordination with those players). Also connected pre-adventuring life to another player character.
7) Scribing: Wrote up a session's AP events.

    \n E6 was a viable thing because 3e didn't really work so well at other levels. Above 9th level or so, characters had too many options and the game got too complex for the DM to prep easily. Below 4th level or so, characters felt too fragile and they didn't have enough options to make them interesting. Somewhere around level 6 was the game's "sweet spot." This is acknowledged by the 4e team in their design blogs.

    I get the impression that 4e makes an E6 unnecessary. The whole game, from level 1 to 30, is in the sweet spot.

    But, if you want a fundamentally different kind of play than 4e provides, house rule it and have fun. I'm considering some Narr twists for my upcoming 4e campaign, myself.

    \n So much speculation, so little play.

    Here's what we used in the GPNW D&D4 game on Sunday:

    - No limits of any kind on Action Point spending. You can pass them to other players, too.

    - You hit a Milestone when you choose to push on to your second encounter (not at the end of the second enconter) so you get your AP right then.

    - You can spend a standard action to do Aid Another even when it's not your turn. This counts as your standard action for the round. You don't have to be adjacent to aid a skill roll, but you do have to be able to see and/or hear your ally. If you spend an AP to do this, you don't roll, you just grant an auto +2.

    - You have a Passive Athletics value (skill + 10) that lets you Climb, Jump, and Swim at that result without rolling. If you're under attack, in dangerous terrain, or in a threatened space, you have to roll.

    - Switching the weapon(s) you're using is only a Minor action, but it does provoke Opportunity Attacks.

    Posted By: buzzPosted By: Adam DrayAny bets on when the first multiclass house rules hit EN World or some other prominent 4e site?
    \nMearls mentioned a few months ago that you could technically assemble a PC from any powers you wanted, as they are all theoretically balanced. So, ditch any class, and just give your PC any powers you want in accordance with the level progression table. Poof! Point-buy D&D.

    I was just having this exact conversation with myself tonight as I rolled up my first new D&D character in about 10 years. (Triin Galfour, 5th level infernal warlock) I looked into multiclassing and I was actually a bit shocked to see how limited it is in the canon - 1st level at-wills only, which without the class features tend to be pretty weak. I wanted to make a sort of point-blank warlock/rogue who was all stabby and had things like Dire Radiance and life-steals that made his melee presence unbearable. Adding in the stabbiness meant two feats out of four (the weapon proficiency for daggers and the multiclass feat), and even then I could only use the stabby power once in an encounter. Pointless.

    So I started comparing across classes, and I came to the same conclusion: While I'm sure there's some totally rad munchkin combos out there, powers are a) pretty low-key until you get into the Paragon levels (and even then, the power curve's pretty smooth), and b) while the flavor is different, the mechanics pretty much stay equivalent between powers of the same level and usage.

    So yeah, I think I'm going to do some testing and probably ditch power restrictions, with the obvious advantage of having powers in your class interacting with your class feature.

    [Edit: I'm also unsure why they kept skills at all, since the list is so incredibly short and they don't interact with powers. It'd be more mechanically smooth to roll them into some sort of free attribute-based feat or power.]
