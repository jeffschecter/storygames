Posted By: J. WaltonLike, if I was going to run Scarlet Wake, it would be hard for me to get in contact with the designer and give him feedback and, even if I did, there's no reason for me to think he would take my feedback and use it to make a better draft somewhere down the road. While labeled a "playtest draft," Scarlet Wake is basically a free game now. And I guess my current impulse is to say that I personally like that approach to playtesting better, playtesting without future expectations, playtesting that is basically just play. It can still be critical or explorative, but I'm often critical and explorative when trying "finished" games.

I think we can see evidence that other folks are already embracing this approach in some ways. When people run Geiger Counter now, sometimes they send me feedback, but often times they're just doing it because they like the game and want to play it. So it's not so much that I'm suggesting a new approach to playtesting that I discovered, but that I'm watching how people approach Geiger or how I approach Danger Patrol, say, and feeling like -- especially with free products -- the line between play and playtesting isn't very distinct. And, honestly, I think that makes playtesting a lot more fun, even if the process of game development is often slower, because the focus is on enjoying the game rather than fixing the game. Fixing the game becomes a byproduct of play rather than the purpose of play.
\nThat totally makes sense; and I consider abandoned playtest docs to be free games with the last caveat mentioned in my original post (abandoned in the hope someone will fix or hack it). This is, cynically, my view on the type of playtesting you describe:

- If it's going to eventually be finished and my playtest doesn't matter to the designer (in the sense of helping him or her improve it), I'm going to play something already finished, that's polished NOW, and wait for the final version.
- If the designer can't be bothered to get playtest feedback from the people playing their game, then the game clearly doesn't matter to the designer. Why in God's name should it matter to me?

I also think the line between playtest and free game is only as malleable as you describe it being for Geiger and Danger Patrol because of the way you and Harper market those games: you explicitly refer to them as "Playtest documents," you say "This is a free game I'm working on and might publish when it's finished." Your description of Geiger a while ago as the beta of a free game implies that it is a free game for the foreseeable future.

That said.

I feel like you're interpreting what I said as saying that playtesting is inherently un-fun: that couldn't be further from the truth. There's only been one time I was playtesting a game and REALLY not enjoying it; I went to the designer and said "Hey, I'm not going to do this playtest any more, because this game is totally not fun for me" and their response was "I need your feedback, you said you'd playtest my game, so you need to show up and do it." That was a real edge case, though. Much of the time when I'm playtesting something critically, I'm enjoying myself, too. It's a different kind of enjoyment.

My point is that playtests, if they are REALLY playtests, aren't free. You're paying for the game with your time and your feedback. That doesn't make it wrong to have fun; but it does mean that you're playing with a responsibility. I do think you can have fun and focus on fixing the game; but even if your playtesting experience is super low-pressure, you're still playing it to see what you can improve, right? Because if it couldn't be improved further, it wouldn't be a playtest document, it'd be a finished game.

ALSO! Sometimes things that are hard work are really fun and rewarding. I love postmortems after playtesting. I guess basically, my feeling on playtesting goes with my aversion to free games: if I'm getting something for value, I'm going to pay for it one way or another.

    \n I allays kind of feel that digital play test copies should be free, but i mean something specific by that.
    \n
    \nI think with playtesting it's critical to understand what you are play testing.
    \n
    \n1. Are you checking the ground level mechanics, seeing if they are a sound foundation for play?
    \n
    \n2. Are you testing the balance of things built upon that foundation, details like are swords over powered, or do social fights take too many rounds?
    \n
    \n3. Are you an outside group testing how well a draft version communicates the game?
    \n
    \n4. Are you getting a nearly finished product that maybe needs an editing pass?
    \n
    \n1 & 2 I would only consider doing with my own groups, maybe a little bit of 2 with select groups that i know personally. this is manly because some of the best feedback you get at this stage is just watching people play.
    \n
    \n3. I would only consider as a free digital down load, because there is value in both the work put into the game and work it takes to play test and offer feedback.
    \n
    \n4. Is where preview copies or "ashcans" come into play, I think these can work as products at conventions and such. I think Vincent moved quite a bit of the Apocalypse world previews at game storm this year.

    \n No worries, Daniel. It is TOTALLY about being a playtest document. Let me put it a different way.

    There are more than a few published games I'm itching to play: Bliss Stage, Doctor Who, Nobilis, A Taste For Murder, more Annalise. And there are so many of my friends' games in playtest I want to play! And then there are the games in development by 2SP that I need to play (and am also excited to play, but I think they count differently). So that means there is a ton of games on my radar right now.

    With that many games competing for my time already, I'm not likely to look twice at an abandoned playtest document. Basically, it boils down to bad marketing: if the person who created it doesn't care about it any more, then it makes me wonder just how much I'll get out of it. I mean, when playtesting, I'd rather play a game in active development any day of the week, because even if it's not fun, it's at least useful to someone.

    So it boils down to superficiality, really. I'm much less likely to take a game to prom if even its mama thinks it's ugly.

    \n You brought up some interesting points, Elizabeth. As a designer with a game that I am getting ready to put out into playtesting again, if someone had agreed to playtest my game and then decided not to, I would definitely want to know why. Even the decision not to play is an important one.

    For example, 3:16 is one of the games I've had the opportunity to playtest. I ran though one mission. The others in my group were ready and eager to keep going. Me.. I wanted nothing more to do with the game after that. I think this is mainly because I was so tired that night, but it was also because 3:16 is a "downer" game. You happily kill monsters on other planets until you discover your superiors are conducting universal genocide. Emotionally, I just couldn't handle that at the time.

    So, if I had someone tell me they didn't want to play, I would definitely conduct a form of exit interview to find out why. The emotional impact of a game is extremely important.
