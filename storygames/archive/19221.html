\n Part research, part academic exercise.

First, what is an OSR game to you? Beyond just stating "a game designed to emulate X game from the last century," what is the difference experientially?

For those of you that enjoy them, what draws you to OSR style gaming? What draws you to them? What need to they satisfy that you would choose that game over other, more modern games?

For context:

The first game I ever played was a reverse-engineered homebrew of AD&D. We started with a DMG and a Monster Manual we picked up at a yard sale and basically built the game backwards from what we could glean in the charts and examples given there. Oddly, we were almost dead on.

I've played a lot of games since then, but after a few beers I got to discussing those old, first games again. I got a bit of nostalgia and dug out my old books. After a bit of reading, I realized that I had no interest in actually playing oldschool D&D again, but I'm very interested in trying to distill the feel of those old experiences into something I can build future designs around emulating. To this end, I understand that my experiences may not be other people's experiences, so in order to make this a more well-rounded experiment I want to understand how other people experienced it.

Thanks in advance, I look forward to the discussion.

    \n What makes it feel old-school to me? It's mostly uncomfortable chairs, bad potato chips, cold cans of soda, sarcastic in-jokes, and a lot of combat scenes with magic spells and swords and monsters and so on. And also no one needing to be anywhere to do anything tomorrow morning.

    Some of that can probably be recaptured (and the sarcastic in-jokes never really went away), except for the "no one needs to do anything tomorrow" part, so...yeah, it ain't gonna happen. Time marches on, you can't go home again, the bodies of your enemies will eventually float past, etc., etc..


    More usefully, old school games had a certain level of "disposability" to them -- no one had to give a crap about any character, so it was okay if your fighter died. Just roll up a new one and join up again, dude. There was no need for fancy motivations or explaining the setting or thinking long-term, because all that mattered was the map in front of you and the monsters behind the door you were kicking down.

    \n About the game and mechanics I can tell you this: there's a before and after skill lists. I'm from the generation who learned to play with skill lists, and it seems like before a lot of that stuff was either resolved through GMfiat, a long argument (involving logic, "common sense", physics, thermodinamycs and quick calculations on both subjects) and other more or less practical solutions.

    Veteran OSR players still play this way despite the existence of skills, while players from my generation (who have not played with veteran nor learn their techniques elsewhere) mostly rely on skill rolls, use less scientific arguments, roleplay just enough to add a narrative/color contribution to the scene and might be less cautious in general when dealing with the fiction, often throwing their characters in trouble just to see what happens next.

    The line between both play styles is quite blurred because old school techniques have actually passed down from player to player and GM to GM. So, while the first games I tried relied on skill lists (where all you had to do to solve a situation was to look up on your character sheet for anything that you could roll for on the present situation, and/or come with an excuse for rolling it), I learned to try caution whenever was possible and how important was to keep the fiction logical. Like, not allowing the GM to get away with, say, putting a gas stove on a spaceship and have an NPC cut the gas feeding tube, ignite it with his swords and TPK our party with this improvised flamethrower (actually happened and we bickered the GM to death after that).

    \n I started with the Blue Box in 1979 at a tender age. I've been doing a lot more boardgaming in the past decade or so, but am beginning to explore the idea of OSR again.

    Here's the basic difference. OSR is not a commercial product. 3.5 and 4.0 are, and Next presumably will be too. Now before the flame jets start, what do I mean by that?

    Quite simply, OS/R (the original and clones, for convenience) is not structured in a way that makes it easy to consume. It is typically very difficult for beginning OS GMs to judge encounter difficulty, encounter length, play session length, or even play session SUCCESS until a) they get some experience and b) they know a particular group well. New school products have solved these issues very well (and whether you like that solution is up to you...that's not something this post will address). In short, the new school has made it possible to have four-hour play blocks at your local game shop. These games will generally be fairly enjoyable, especially to new players, since expectations are generally clear, feedback is clear and immediate (the players typically have a feeling of "getting somewhere") and the experience usually provides a strong sense of closure toward the end of Hour 4. The mechanics tend to minimize group friction and group quirks--to put it another way, you can usually run them with a group of complete strangers and things will go fairly well. As icing on the cake, players are given a lot more specific options and powers, have fewer apparent restrictions on their character choices (half-dragon? no problem!!!) and generally stay alive quite a bit longer. Logistics, healing times, and other "mundane" things seem to be much more important in OS than in new school.

    OS/R games often involve the Wall of No. That is, players are typically forced to make hard choices and give things up. They may not be allowed to play certain races or even classes in a particular game. Attempting to play things like a half-dragon will evoke rich laughter from traditional OS GMs. Players will never be able to progress as high with an elvish magic-user as with a human one, because elves have so many inherent advantages. They are largely limited to the outlook of their class and even their alignment. Characters that end up super powerful will be very very weak for a very long time. Fatalities and serious injuries, the kinds with real game effects (draining weeks of time if nothing else) are quite common. In general, players HATE this...until eventually some don't. But NONE of this is particularly conducive to a successful commercial product, especially one that relies on getting new players (the whole player base turns over every five years or so, give or take).

    So OS/R is a lot looser, with a lot more potential for awesomeness and for total failure. OS/R can be a total cluster when you run it at a tournament, or are trying to finish room #4 of a 30-room dungeon before 2 AM, or when you need to prep something fast and are looking for some guidance in the rules (heh). But when done well, with a group that works well together, it's a literally transcendent artistic experience.

    To the OP:

    1. Nostalgia. It's not the only reason to play old school, but it's one.
    2. Challenge. Unlike most narrativist games, OS games really can facilitate a "step on up" style of play, if you cooperate with them. Strategy and tactics are important, and player skill is rewarded. This does have to do with the absence of skill lists, as Warrior Monk pointed out. Players who grew up before the advent of skill lists are always trying to come up with clever gimmicks to overcome opposition (through fictional positioning), rather than simply rolling the appropriate skill.
    3. Suspense. TallPaul is dead on about this one. In my Red Box game, players were constantly on the edge of their seats because PCs could die at any moment. You really have to experience this to understand it.
    4. Easy to hack. You can easily create new races, classes, spells, items, and subsystems without breaking the game. This allows for the kind of zaniness that PW_Shea describes (but really, WTF man, that sounds more like a William S. Burroughs novel than D&D).

    \n Personally I consider the OSR a current of cultural history, the same as I consider "Forgite analytic design" or "traditional mainstream of rpgs" or "TSR in-house design culture". I encourage anybody to read up on the discipline of cultural history if you're confused about what kind of thing a "current of cultural history" is

    This means that I am objectively speaking in the same camp with Jason on this: you can't point at specific rules, degree of commercialization, or any such easy analytical yardsticks when "defining OSR". The OSR people themselves have come to this conclusion by now, I understand! They used to have these big discussions about who's OSR and who's not, and they were mostly bullshit when people tried to define it by "has to use retro-clone rules" or "has to be playable with old D&D" or "has to pertain to stuff published pre-80s". The only feasible and realistic way to define a cultural trend like that is to look at cultural influences: OSR is the things that take influence from other OSR things, to whatever extent that they do. The first OSR things, in turn, were mid-00s bloggings, discussions and products that harkened back to old school D&D - this is again not an analytical definition in that not everybody who happened to harken back to D&D in mid-00s gets to be OSR; only those initiatives from which the thing actually later named OSR are counted when defining the roots of the cultural trend.

    By this measure I can easily say that my current D&D campaign is both "Forgite" and "OSR" at the same time: it takes ample influence from both, in ways that could be fully documented if I cared to. Neither of those concepts are rationally speaking some discrete boxes where you put things.

    However, in the spirit of the actual question, perhaps I should say what I find interesting or relevant in the OSR: there are many things in it, but the part that I feel is interesting and relevant to me is the challenge-based, goal-oriented, fiction-based conflict resolution using, rulings-structured old style D&D. So I guess that if I had to go purely by feel instead of cultural history heuristics in determining whether something is or isn't OSR, that gauge would be whether it's usable in my D&D campaign. If it's irrelevant to my D&D due to whatever, then I'm probably not feeling it. Old school Traveller can still be OSR according to that cultural history heuristic, but it's not relevant to me in the same way a OSR D&D adventure module is.

    \n For me, OSR and Story Games are different sides of the same coin, and that coin is fidelity to rules.

    The difference is, SG is about "what are the rules we need to get this story experience?" with the corollary that any rule that doesn't support the story experience desired gets tossed.

    OSR, it seems to me, is about "What story experience do we get if we follow the rules exactly as written?"

    Note that this isn't quite the same thing as "Let's go back to the playstyle of early RPGs," although certainly there is quite a bit of uncovering forgotten or misunderstood aspects of that playstyle. But as a veteran of that day, I can certainly speak to how a lot of rules were ignored, or bent, or used completely contrary to intent--at least at a lot of the tables I played at, because we were trying to make a story experience happen and used the rules we had to hand.

    This is as it should be--it's a renaissance, after all, and in the historical era called the Renaissance most people didn't put on togas and start only speaking Classical Latin. (At least not in public.) I think it also explains why OSR people and Story Gamers tend to grok each other, or even overlap.

    Some of what Eero talks about--especially the challenge-based play, especially the "player challenge" aspect--certainly was a common feature of the early playstyle; but it's also an emergent feature of the rulesets themselves, as many have discovered.

    FWIW, Eero, I think playing Black Book Traveller could get pretty OSR, although it's a different variety of the same. (The combat system was ridicudeadly.) Certainly the minigames--the trade & speculation in particular--and the default sandbox playstyle make it a lot more OSR than not.

    So...what would make a game OSR to me would be, emergent playstyle from fidelity to the rules, coupled with a...lack of an opinion from the rules as to what that playstyle would be, if that makes any sense. (Plus a more challenge-based, player focused gameplay.)

    \n From Philotomy's Musings which are in many ways the closest thing so far to this hypothetical principles of D&D document.\n
    There are many interpretations of "the dungeon" in D&D. OD&D, in particular, lends itself to a certain type of dungeon that is often called a "megadungeon" and that I usually refer to as "the underworld." There is a school of thought on dungeons that says they should have been built with a distinct purpose, should "make sense" as far as the inhabitants and their ecology, and shouldn't necessarily be the centerpiece of the game (after all, the Mines of Moria were just a place to get through). None of that need be true for a megadungeon underworld. There might be a reason the dungeon exists, but there might not; it might simply be. It certainly can, and perhaps should, be the centerpiece of the game. As for ecology, a megadungeon should have a certain amount of verisimilitude and internal consistency, but it is an underworld: a place where the normal laws of reality may not apply, and may be bent, warped, or broken. Not merely an underground site or a lair, not sane, the underworld gnaws on the physical world like some chaotic cancer. It is inimical to men; the dungeon, itself, opposes and obstructs the adventurers brave enough to explore it. For example, consider the OD&D approach to doors and to vision in the underworld\u2026

      \n There are some pretty big differences between how the Primer characterizes play and how I do it myself, yes. I don't consider those differences massively important in terms of creative agenda (that is, to me it seems that Finch plays for very similar reason to mine, when I consider the totality of his work), but rather more like matters of game design strategy, and taste in execution - it's simply about how one best accomplishes the creative goals with the tools one has on hand. My play is not mechanically orthodox old school D&D, as should be pretty obvious, while Finch cleaves very close to traditional mechanics; I think he would disagree, but to me it seems like he's mechanically conservative for the sake of being conservative, rather than selecting the best ideas from among all options. Not that there's anything wrong with that, I should hasten to add: I'm very much a tinkerer, designer type personality, which is often not true of the big OSR writers; it seems like a very natural choice to stick by what you know instead of wasting time and risking ill results by being extremely open to mechanical innovation like I tend to be.

      The reason for why I use relatively ample rolling pertains to achieving better realism in execution of problem-solving: there are certain things that are not true to life (a central concern for me: does choosing this procedure for resolution make our problem-solving more true to life, or does it take it out into some self-referential rules-mechanical la-la-land?) when they are consistently left up to player decision-making. For example searching, diplomacy, lock-picking, bravery, remembering to pack your underwear are all examples of activities that rely on character competence that cannot really be captured very well by choices a player makes in the comfort of the game table: we cannot measure character alertness by player alertness, I think, and thus would rather roll to find out how alert a given character is being at a given moment in time.

      It should be noted that there is another very good reason for shying away from character abilities, one that I think Finch doesn't address in the Primer: trad style (or middle school, which might be clearer a term in this context) roleplaying games routinely over-emphasize character abilities in a formalistic manner in their resolution schemes (I mean the tendency to reduce every imaginable task into a question of whether you've invested skill points in that thing), which is not true of most early flavours of D&D; a big, very valid reason for shying away from the entire panoply of modern skill rolls and such is that you get a more true-to-life resolution by abstracting away individual differences than by deriving every possible roll out of character abilities. For example, the traditional search for secret doors is 1/6 per searcher per time unit per area to be searched; this is much more realistic than e.g. the 3rd edition search skill check, which presumes that human beings have some sort of trainable searching faculty that can make such a difference as to make a task that is impossible to the layman a virtual certainty for the trained searcher. The OSR reluctancy towards universal skill systems and such may well be interpreted in this light, it's not necessarily the case that everybody underwrites that Finch model of replacing character abilities with player abilities.

      (One mechanical watershed that I've fiddled with lately is the notion of removing the ability array entirely from D&D. As some of you might know, my currently most-used house rule set for D&D features extreme probability weight for abilities: practically all rolls are on the d20, and all rolls get bonuses on a 1:1 basis from ability scores, making it one of the most radically ability-emphasizing D&D variants imaginable. My friend Olli Kantola, on the other hand, has been running a very ability-sparse game, sort of similar to Swords & Wizardry except even more minimal, and I have to say that I've liked some aspects of that; I could well see myself trade in some of the good points of the extreme ability emphasis for the good points of dropping abilities altogether at some point in the future, just for the change of pace.)

      In practice I should emphasize that I don't think that Finch is wrong in drawing attention to player skill - it is an important argument, even if I do not think that it is the only argument. Just like with so many other design considerations in D&D, you have to strike your own balance between system elegance, practical utility in the moment, realism, character differentiation, opportunity for player skill, and probably a couple other "virtues" that I'm forgetting right now. For example, it is not the case that searching for traps in a trapped corridor would be strictly up to a random roll at my table; rather, the procedural principle is that players can "call it" by describing their search correctly, and succeeding in that (happens rarely, but it is possible) gives automatic success without needing to roll. Rolling for tasks that can be "called" like that is theoretically a back-up mechanism that ensures for us that characters get a fair chance whether the situation at hand is possible to resolve by player insight or not. (This isn't a trivial division to make: you can't, or at least I can't just divide all tasks on the spot into ones that are best resolved by player skill and ones best resolved by character skill. For this reason I've settled upon a combined approach that allows each task to be resolved in either way.)

      As a practical example that has been established by so much precedent that it's basically stone-solid at this point: if a slime or giant spider or whatever lurks in the ceiling of a corridor or room, waiting to jump upon any passerby, a player may obviate all mechanical risk by explicitly stating that they are observing the ceiling as they proceed; it's automatic success in spotting the threat. However, if a player merely calls for a generally careful approach (without specifying that it's the ceiling they're concerned about), a Wits check is used to find out whether the character spots the danger, and how soon. (If the approach is "non-careful" due to e.g. being in a hurry, the roll is either more difficult, or the spot is automatically failed.) The "calling it" rule cannot be spammed abusively or set "on automatic", though, as it's relative to standard precautions: players can determine that their characters will "always look at the ceiling as we advance", but that will at most make the spot checks a bit easier rather than giving consistent automatic success. To properly get a free pass by calling a danger in advance, you need to specifically call it case-by-case, which is a separate consideration from ordinary procedure. (Not that ordinary procedure can't provide auto-successes in things like spotting ceiling threats: define your rate of progress as enormously slow, or establish that your character observes only the ceiling, and I will allow that your character really can't miss noticing any irregularities that are possible to notice by naked eye. Ultimately you can avoid dice rolling by sufficiently belaboring the fictional positioning to the point where failure is no longer credible.)

      My experience was a different sort of drift. A sort of megadungeon from Philotomy's Musings (thanks Adamwb), my first and longest-running dungeon was literally alive, healed itself, and was magically more powerful than most deities. This was really all just a way for me to explain away my neurotic need for control over the play environment. At first. All we needed to start was (1) a one-level dungeon (it grew to 23 levels over the years), and (2) a nearby village, in that order. Initially, neither I nor my players gave any consideration to any larger sense of world or history. Of course, we were 14 at the time.

      But as the "drift" started to occur (which I perceive as partly aging, and partly a growing tolerance or desire for "stronger stuff"), we didn't shy away from the "stupid monsters". We didn't omit things. I kept adding things on as they were published. We went from OD&D to AD&D. The dungeon got deeper, the monsters got weirder, and the world got bigger, and bigger, and bigger. But our play got *deeper*. We simply accepted the weird nature of things in the D&D universe (why do owlbears exist? I dunno, why do platypuses exist?) We accepted the game's ontology and used it as a backdrop or foundation for emotionally-charged adventure dramas with lots of roleplay and human relations stuff. It was all very psychodrama and much more "Narrativist" than my 20 color-tabbed notebooks and eidetically-memorized knowledge of the GM's Guide might lead you to assume. The best way I can describe it is that we found a way to "grow" a Narrativist approach on top of a densely Simulationist world. And I agree with Eero that those "stupid monsters" can indeed be very creepy if you play them straight.

      Now I'm uncertain whether you'd call that type of play "OSR" or not.

      To me the acronym OSR brings to mind (a) haphazardly smooshing everything together in a single unlikely place, and (b) not paying much attention to any world beyond the dungeon. Oh, and (c) badly typeset photocopied supplements sold in ziplock bags.

      \n One of the differences I notice is one of attitude on the part of the players when it comes to playing OSR games.

      I started playing Basic D&D in the Fad Era of its success. I think I was ten years old or so.

      At that time, well, those old games were what we had. We looked at them a bit like superior versions of Choose your Own Adventure books. We used or ignored mechanics as we chose, and pretty inconsistently at that. A lot of times, we mostly just freeformed and used the books and rules as inspirational material at best.

      When I started looking at this OSR thing that was happening, part of what I saw was people not only using the old rules, or picking one of many early playstyles, but also committing to playing those clones/variations much closer to the way the things were written. It was a conscious decision to actually use the mechanics to experience what sort of game play they produced.
