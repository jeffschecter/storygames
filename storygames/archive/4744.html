    Posted By: Andy1) How long did it last?
    \nThere were two big GURPS episodes and one brief MURPG episode.

    The first GURPS episode was several years ago. It lasted around average for our campaigns, around 6 months of weekend play. The second one was a few years ago, it lasted around 4 months of weekend play.

    The MURPG episode was actually one I ran. I set it around 100 years after the events in Thief 2, made everything a bit more 18th century, powder rifles and so on, and the characters were Keepers. Essentially they were stealth fantasy superheroes. I had kind of forgotten about it until I started typing. It lasted about 4 months but we didn't play every week, it was our 'off game' when everyone couldn't make it. "Oh really, the Keeper isn't around for some mysterious reason, there's a big surprise."

    Posted By: Andy4) Did you hack GURPS in any way to make it more "Thieflike"? Did everyone use 150 point characters, etc?
    \nNot really, except insofar as the skill/advantage/disadvantage list we chose made it pretty clear that:

    -- skills were broad
    -- combat was basic, and could be deadly (limited Extra Hit Points, etc.)
    -- running away and hiding was a viable option (availability of healing potions, etc.)

    I believe everyone made 100-120 point characters with limited disadvantages, primarily because we were not used to the options of the system and we tend to like increasing character power over a campaign, even fairly sharply.

    Those of us with characters in common between the first two campaigns remade the characters as (I think) 150-160 point characters, which included a nice "time passes" XP bonus at the end.

    I can't quite remember the numbers...

    Oh, I forgot one thing: we also used a GURPSification of the Masterbook drama cards. Textfile here, I think. Basically this gave you bonuses in sticky situations, introduced goofy subplots, it was not considered a huge narrative input device.

    For MURPG's Keepers game, I altered the range on the attributes, indicating that instead of human peaks being 3 for most stats, I made human peak 7 for most, and permitted them to buy up higher, being magically trained and all. I also broke down the broad Actions (Black Ops) into individual actions (Stealth, Ambushes, etc.)

    Posted By: Andy5) What were the characters?
    \nHey, I found the Website for the game. Keep in mind this was before my game websites got cool. This is actually kind of embarassing looking back at it. Thanks for saving this, Internet. Fuck you.

    Ahem.


    First game:

    Let Me Tell You About My Character: Fitz Hurstwood, sleazy newspaperman. In the very first level of the very first game, there's a single document that you can find that appears to be a newspaper or broadside article. I took this and ran 90 yards for a touchdown, all false modesty aside. He was idealistic in that he believed in The People but cynical because individual people were all slime. He actually was quite nastily manipulative, blackmailing many people for information by gaining secrets by skullduggery and stealth, and threatening to publish them. I recall from the first game when Fitz was tailing a Hammerite courier from one temple to another, he came around front, tripped the courier, nailed him with a blackjack, stole the letter and also the courier's money to make it look like a robbery. Then he dropped all the money in the poorbox at another church, and went and got drunk with what he kept for himself. He did it all for a girl who was a chief gardener for a powerful nobleman, who kept after him to give up the paper and get a real job, and was horrified the more successful he got, knowing he was doing it all for her and although he never could tell, she was hating what he was becoming. Also unbeknownst to him, he was a harbinger of the Metal Age, and so powerful pagans hated his guts.

    My wife played Angel Fontaine, torch singer and pagan-style alchemist. Although we never talked about it until afterwards, this set us up immediately to hate each other and we did, with exuberance. The GM later told me that this actually got a bit in the way of the game at points, because she would acquire information and then refuse to share it with the group because of me. For laffo-s, she could talk to animals and was very clumsy, which always led to hilarity when she would spill a new potion all over someone.

    My buddy played Hugh Brian. "Never trust anyone with two first names." He was a fence by day, who also brokered information for a twitchy, potion-addicted junkie type to the wardens of the city. By night he robbed, his position made him able to frame others easily.

    We added and then dropped (as another player who I forgot to mention before came and went), a sort of Ralph Steadman-esque caricaturist who was a drunken nobleman's heir who gave inflammatory information and filthy, vicious cartoons to my character to print. Together we took the game down a psychotic tangent when we decided we would expose a nobleman's sexual debauchery and subsequent attempt to cover it up with bribes to various persons, including a wicker craftsman who made the crib for the child resulting from the union(s)....the GM had no idea what to make of this but we threw everything gleefully into turmoil.

    The first game was just with the four of us, and as you might guess from the character description, it was more of three intertwined games as far as plotlines went.

    This Is also a start on 6 and 7.

    Posted By: Andy
    6) What was the first starting event in the first session?
    \nThe starting event in the first session of the first game was that a dreadful murder occurred after one of Angel Fontaine's shows, involving a rare coin someone passed off to her in her tip box. Naturally my character was involved because he wanted to print something about a hot girl in danger (FOX News had barely been invented at the time, actually. I am that good.) I think the fence was Angel's contact for finding out more about the coin. We all had our own individual problems as well, but in true classic mystery/thriller fashion, what started as a minor but shocking crime escalated into a mysterious force that threatened the city, dunh dunh duhhhhhh.

    Angel was basically a decent person, so she wanted to stop this from happening, the bad guys wanted it to happen in secret so they hated that Fitz was yapping at their heels every few minutes, and the fence was basically cajoled into helping by his two associates.

    Oh, uh, I answered 7 here too.

    The second game, you can get a bit of the plot (and side plots) from the website, the news stories, etc. Essentially a mysterious jewel was stolen that had previously been stolen from the clock dude and had terrible occult powers and was awfully cursed. Bloody mayhem ensued. Since we had added someone with a personal stake (clock guy) and someone who was a do-gooder (noble girl), they were happy to jump right in. There were still a lot of individual plotlines, which turned out to be sort of problematic because we hadn't really identified who was interested in whose spotlight time in the group at that point in our history and there was a lot of boredom while waiting to play.

    MURPG was mission based. Hey, something is happening over there. Go and look at it and report. Oh noes, it threatens the balance. Go and fix it and if you have to do anything make it look like an accident.

    Posted By: Andy8) How much "Thief backstory/In The Know" events/characters/items/places etc appeared (the boneyard, etc)? What was the reaction to them?

    9) How much did stealth play a part in the game? If "a lot", how did you keep it interesting?
    \n8 - Lots. The fanatics loved the references, nobody else really got them, but because as you know the game world is very compelling, they were interested in them independently of recognizing them from the game.

    9 - Lots. The way we kept it interesting in GURPS was by using freeform GM-description and player-strategizing to get various bonuses, using limited equipment while in dangerous areas (equipment was often handwaved for people who weren't Fitz because they had money and the whole game took place in the City).

    In MURPG, the whole game is basically about finding situational modifiers, "stones in the setting" to help you with your resource allocation, so I had a fairly extensive list of stealth modifiers prepped out, including shadow levels, surfaces, etc. If faced with a very difficult area (tile floors, bright lights, LOL Hammerite cathedral), the characters would develop subgoals (cut the lights, knock out a sentry or two in other areas, etc.) to make it more manageable.

    \n The thing with stealth that I tried clumsily to do with MURPG (and which I would work more on if I went back to it) was that you want to set up a gradation of failure modes, all the way from "Thought I saw those curtains moving...nah, probably nothing" to "Oh my god, who are you and what are you doing here?" I tried to divide the failure modes into four parts:

    1 - Someone saw or heard something but if they don't see or hear something else in a certain period of time they will just write it off to drafty hallways, "must've been rats", etc.
    2 - Someone saw or heard something and they're confident it was real, but they don't know exactly what it was and although they're more alert, and might even pass on some warnings, they're not raising the alarm. "Something's wrong...did you see anything over there earlier?" and checking doorlatches and so on.
    3 - Someone saw or heard you and they take active steps to search for you, but they don't have a clear picture of what's going on. They may go and get help, or they may not, but the search at this point is precautionary and investigatory, it is not a pursuit.
    4 - Someone saw or heard you and they pursue with vigor and, if applicable, raise the alarm.

    This way we didn't run into the 'oh shoot, we blew that situation, now we have to hack our way out' problem, failed stealth situations could (as in the games) be repaired somewhat.
