    \n I've played it.

    If the setting is too dense, work with a subset. Pick 3-4 civilizations that you think provide an interesting tension and a nice range of options. If you want to bring in other stuff, you can introduce it a little at a time. The fact that everyone's working for the Patent Office means that characters can quit or get reassigned to other teams without much trouble, so people can bring in new characters from the new civs if they like.

It takes longer to explain the concept of "pick whatever stats you want; high stats will balance later" than it does for them to actually do it.     \n Hey, Aik. Thanks for the questions. Here are some answers.

    On Capabilities: You are correct. Max them out as far as your Import allows. This is intentional. Having one capability at 7 is not much less powerful than having all five capabilities at 7. There are some alternate rules on the wiki forums if you prefer. Here's my philosophy, quoted from that discussion:\n
    If you're worried about people abusing the rules, my approach with SA was to make abusing the rules the norm, and encourage everyone to do it. If the game is still balanced at that point, then everything's fine. Of course, things break down if not everyone goes to the same lengths of "fold, spindle, and mutilate," which is why I tried to make it pretty easy.
    \nWhen my players use Themes, I typically get the "big idea" of it from them, and then describe as GM. Sometimes there's a little negotiation as to exactly what happens. Sometimes they want to describe it, which I also like. I go with the flow here, which is why there's no specific rule about who gets to narrate.

    Plot Immunity is a "save my bacon" ability, but the other Themes are intended to make the game bend and flex in short-term ways. They work well as flags, but they're intended as a form of story control. Most of my players save them for when the shit hits the fan, but using them early and often works well too. I love when someone says "Hey, Colin, give me three level 2 Complications. I'm taking some Twists to save for later."

    Bringing Down the Pain is a great way to treat extended conflicts, and I wish I had known it better when I wrote SA. You can do similar stuff using the "fold" rule (page 102 at the bottom), but it's not as explicit.

    I always go with realistic stats. If you want a short fight, put in weak guards, but if your Doomguard citadel posts incompetent guards your players should be asking why (and you should have an answer ready, because your players might pull out Comprehension). Because of this, I almost never see my players try to fight their way past guards. They size the guards up with Nanotech, realize they might be outgunned, and send the high-Meta guy in to talk the guards out of their lunch money (and/or pants). If your players would rather blow some Stringtech reserve, that's their call. Most guards (at least in my games) don't have a "stab everyone on sight" order and won't take the conflict into the physical realm unless provoked.

    Your second-to-last paragraph is basically correct. Each timeslice, each player picks a target and rolls when their turn comes up; you only roll defense if someone's actually coming for you. You don't immediately counterattack, you just get to roll when it's your turn. I can see how the flowchart led you wrong there; I'll see if I can make a less misleading one and post it on the wiki. Page 102 may describe things better for you.

    Does all that make sense? Any other questions?

    \n The very distant outliers in a civilization (for instance, the Mechanican "naturalists" who never replace any part of their bodies) are ignored when it comes to a civilization's capability scores. This applies for both higher- and lower-stat characters. A single individual, or even a small group of people, never changes a civilization's scores. Check out the rules for selling your heritage on page 78 for an example of how this can be handled for high Capabilities.

    The Stringtech 8 guy using his fists would probably count as pulling his punches (no pun intended). However, he could easily say that he has some sort of deadly short-range effect on his fist that makes his punches Stringtech 8, and thus be multiplying by 8.

    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
