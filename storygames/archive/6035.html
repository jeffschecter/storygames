\n This is inspired entirely by Jesse's fine work with Sorcerer; I was going to entirely hijack his thread but I managed to resist at the last moment.

This post is about what I feel is the single most widely-misquoted, widely-misunderstood rule in the entire Sorcerer book:

Page 74:\n
The big issue for the Sorcerer player is strictly a courtesy standard. It is that your character must be personally, emotionally involved in what's going on.
\nNote what it says, and what it doesn't say.

It says your character must be personally, emotionally involved.

It does not say anything about the player needing to be personally, emotionally involved.


In the general case, I think this is absolutely the right way to approach RPG rules. Make rules about what the characters must do or must feel. Eschew rules about what the players must do or must feel.


Cheers,
Roger

    \n Oh I don't know about. The text in the Sorcerer book I was talking about does indeed talk about the players finding reasons for their character's to be involved and retro-fitting their character concept as needed. However, I personally find it very difficult to do that, if I myself am not personally invested in some manner, even if that investment is different from that of my character.

    Also, I suggest taking a look at the Handbook section of the game Dirty Secrets. That stuff in there is very much about what the players need to be doing to make the game work. The one bit I hone in on is that he players need to have a personal working theory of the case at all times and play the various Characters with that theory in mind. If they don't do that, the game won't work. It's very easy to think that just sitting back and applying the rules will magically lead to surprise ending. They won't. They will, however, if you play from that "working theory" point of view.

    Spione works on a similar principle. If the players don't understand what The Cold is and how Maneuvers are meant to "spiral" in on The Cold then the game won't work.

    \n I have to agree with Per and Jesse. If narrativism is about exploring issues, then the player (and naturally by extension, the character) has to be emotionally involved in the outcome, at least to some degree. That's what gives Sorcerer an edge that a lot of "personal horror" games lack: it explicitly functions only when the players are involved. Their characters are only extensions, avatars of whatever issue the player is exploring in game.

    This is why Ron, I believe, explicitly cuts out direct mind control in Sorcerer: it cuts the player away from the character. Suddenly, the game has gone from Story Now to being a shared hallucination. And doing so risks making it difficult for the player to reconnect with the character, and use it to explore those issues. The character becomes "damaged goods."

    Only in a Simulationist sense can we talk about the character "being emotionally invested" in the outcome. Under those premises, the character is a (mostly) complete separation from the player. And even then it doesn't work well: consider how many times we see players willing to die before they lose control of their character. And why? Because a big appeal for people who game is to treat this fictional block of stats like it's an extension of themselves. But that only goes so far, and then stops. So, it's not really rational to talk about "character investment" when it's the player who has to invest emotion in the outcomes.

    \n Roger, the one sentence you quote is out of context. When you said "It does not say anything about the player needing to be personally, emotionally involved" this implies to me (and others I think) that you think Ron is saying that the player need not care.

    In fact Ron does _not_ write this, and Ron is famous for being surprised when people think he meant something he did not write. He relies a lot on saying something once and letting the reader understand through context. The rest of the book assumes that the player must care about the fiction and so, to his mind anyway, that applies here unless he says otherwise.

    What is radical about that paragraph on p74, is that it says "...assume that the character does care and work backwards from there..." That is a reversal of the common style of play that expects the GM to put out hooks that the character's care about. In Sorcerer, it's the players responsibility to create the hooks themselves.

    \n Let's see if I can clarify at all.

    These are sentences that I believe occur in the Sorcerer text:\n
    The big issue for the Sorcerer player is strictly a courtesy standard. It is that your character must be personally, emotionally involved in what's going on.
    \nThese are sentences that I believe do not occur in the Sorcerer text:\n
    Players must be personally, emotionally involved in what's going on.
    Players must not be personally, emotionally involved in what's going on.
    \nIf I have implied that Sorcerer contained either of the above sentences, I apologize -- this was not my intention. I'll suggest, once again, that the text is entirely silent on the matter.

    Several people have stated that I've taken this out of context, which I don't think I have, but just to clear up any confusion, here's the section in its entirety:\n
    The players
    The big issue for the Sorcerer player is strictly a courtesy standard. It is that your character must be personally, emotionally involved in what's going on. Players of role-playing games often complain that their characters have no reason to stick around and help one another. To the best of your ability, avoid this problem by assuming that your character does care, and work backwards from there to give him or her a reason. Look at the Kicker, there on your sheet. You and your GM have agreed that it is a priority for your character to exert effort to cope with it, and that means it should be a priority for you, as a player, to direct the character appropriately. Or if the Kicker is not immediately applicable, come up with a liking or a disliking for one of the other characters, and resolve to stick around until you can act on it. This is not to say, of course, that your character must always care -- but the default should be that he or she does.
    \nThat's it -- the entire context. Reading as charitably as I possibly can, I don't see anything in there which states or implies anything about the emotional involvement of the player.

    So -- where does that leave us? Well, to start with, it leaves us again with the text of the book. As much as I like discussing other roleplaying games, the roleplaying experience as a whole, the human experience as a whole, and bowling, I'd like to at least try to keep things focused on the topic at hand.

    Is it plausible that the author would simply omit, or leave to inference, a rule about player involvement, if such a thing were so fundamental and necessary to the play experience? It's possible, sure. It doesn't seem particularly plausible to me, is all.

    The players of Sorcerer include some of the most imaginative, creative, and innovative people around. They can pretend to be wizards, demons, murderers, and thieves without themselves being any of those things. It seems bizarre to me to suggest that, for some reason, they are incapable of pretending to be a character who is personally, emotionally involved without themselves being personally, emotionally involved. Give the players some credit. They are capable of creating good stories that they are proud of.

    So... is this, in fact, THE most understood rule? No, probably not -- I think hyperbole got the better of me. Let's just say it's among the misunderstood rules of Sorcerer -- as evinced, I would suggest, by this very thread.
