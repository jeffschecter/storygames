    \n As far as having NPCs with motives but taking the load off the GM, the existing games I know are mostly a mixed bag on that front. But that may not be suitable for the sort of fiction you're aiming for; I dunno.

    I find that "PC1 wants A from NPC1; PC2 wants B from NPC1" is a good place to start.

    \n OK, David and Paul, I've decided to be brief in explaining the issues I'm having with my game. If you have questions, I can give you additional context. Thank you for your willingness to help

    My game gives Advantage(s) and Disadvantage(s): in certain circumstance for different fictional elements and occurrences.
    Broadly these are as follows:
    Situational: these are things that can affect the narrative temporarily in any given scene. They consist of things like the following (this is not an exhaustive list):
    Location, physical obstacles, features of the environment, and relative position of the characters and NPCs
    Circumstances and factors that affect the behavior or psychological state of character(s) or NPC(s)
    Sudden changes in a character\u2019s or NPC\u2019s capacities or status; momentary conditions or injuries
    The state of affairs, and/or combination of circumstances and factors acting on the character(s) or NPC(s); anything that could affect the players or NPCs given the situation
    Any contextual details that affect the fiction or come into play
    Character Traits consisting of a Weakness and Strength. Players choose these and they are, and work similar to, aspects in fate core; albeit in more restrictive and realistic fashion. They can be called upon to affect the narrative temporarily in any given scene.
    Physical Harm: these are any ongoing or permanent physical conditions or injuries that affect the narrative.
    Mental Harm: these are any ongoing or permanent metal conditions or injuries that affect the fiction.
    Social Status: these are any ongoing or permanent social conditions that affect the fiction in regards to relationships, social developments, social circumstances or other social states of affair that can affect the fiction.
    There are also two types of special categories of Social Status that are tracked as well:
    Trust & Distrust: any temporary, ongoing or permanent factors that affect the trust or distrust of an NPC in relation to a given PC; for example, a PC being caught in a lie by an NPC
    Leverage: any temporary or ongoing factors that give the PCs or NPCs leverage in a given relationship; for example, a PC knowing something about an NPC that can be used as leverage to make the NPC do something (e.g. threatening to tell the NPC\u2019s spouse about an affair the NPC is having)
    Secrets: any temporary, ongoing or permanent physical, mental, or social factors that are secret or hidden from the PCs

    The main problem is that there is a lot of moving parts and that these things are difficult for the GM to keep track of and remember, and if the GM asks the PCs what factors on their character sheets could result in an Advantage(s) and Disadvantage(s) it can be cumbersome, interrupt the fiction somewhat and take the PCs out of the story.
    Different Advantages and Disadvantages are currently tracked as follows:
    \tPCs track (on their character sheets) Character Traits, Physical Harm, Mental Harm, any ongoing or permanent social conditions other than Leverage and Trust & Distrust.
    \tGMs track (on their NPC sheets): NPC Physical Harm, Mental Harm, Trust & Distrust, Leverage and Secrets.
    GMs also track Situational factors, with their memory or a sheet of scratch paper; as well as NPC sheets, which have things like NPC Motives and Goals, Relationship to PCs, a Notes section, etc. Finally, the GM has a relationship map that charts the different webs of relationships in regards to PCs and NPCs.
    I have not explained how any of the other parts of the game work so you are missing a lot of context. If you have any questions please ask. As you can see there are a lot of moving parts. My goal is to simplify and make the system more elegant, cut down any interruptions in the fiction, and make things easier for the GM to manage. Thank you for your help. Any ideas or suggestions you have are greatly appreciated

    \n A few quick first impressions:

    - I like that Social Status effectively has hit points.

    - I like that Leverage and Trust/Distrust exist. I think constantly updating them to reflect temporary factors would be really hard, but noting ongoing or permanent things seems manageable to me. I'd put those on the relationship map itself.

    - If a player's character has leverage over an NPC, or that NPC trusts them, and these things are actually useful to the PC, the player is probably not going to forget them. If they were tracked numerically, then that number would need to go somewhere, but if it's just conferring Advantage, then "write it down if you want, players" has you covered. This is my takeaway from many sessions of asking players in Delve to write down which powerful people like them how much. It was never necessary.

    \n Here's a brainstorm:

    Each PC gets 4 slots on their sheet (primary or secondary character sheet). Each slot is for an NPC role, meaning, a very broad type of relationship you have with an NPC. You then take the 4 most important NPCs to your character and slot them in where they fit best. Below each one is listed factors relevant to that role. Maybe that's leverage, trust, and perceived motives for each, or maybe it's more tailored per role -- I'm not sure.

    So, anyone who makes it into your Top 4 NPCs is documented in some detail, and these details are updated by the player whenever relevant. When a 5th NPC rises in importance to knock someone out of the Top 4, the evicted NPC is deprioritized in all ways, including spotlight and accounting. We won't expect to see them as much, and we won't be so careful to remember exactly where they stand in relation to everyone else -- close enough is close enough.

    This system of replacement is completely unnecessary if the whole game is pre-structured around a tightly-knit cast; in that case, the main point of my brainstorm would be that the Role label contributes to that structure. For example, if the four slots on the sheet are labeled Dependent, Benefactor, Nemesis, Idol, then we know this character's relationships are about a combo of need and intense personal like/dislike. This could be true for all PCs, or this could be a sort of chosen PC type or playbook. Maybe I've got Dependent, Benefactor, Nemesis, and Idol, while you've got Co-Conspirator, Secret Lover, Unworthy Heir, and Victim. Or maybe the 4 roles could be roles the PC actively seeks to fulfill for their own aims, like Patron, Guardian, Ambassador and Lackey.

    Tracking Leverage over your Patron or Trust with your Secret Lover seems worthwhile to me in a way that tracking those same things with every NPC might not be. Maybe each role could even get away with defining one core element to the relationship that role describes -- when it comes to your Guardian we care about Loyalty, when it comes to your Benefactor we care about Favor, when it comes to your Nemesis we care about Perceived Advantage, etc.

    If the game isn't pre-structured, "I want to make that guy my Patron!" or "I want that guy for a Co-Conspirator!" would totally be a thing, and there could be rules about how you establish those relationships, or fail to.

    \n Thanks guys, I haven't read your comments because I don't have time right now, but I will read them in a couple hours. Just to explain a little bit more how the game works. Nothing is tracked with hit points or numerically. Everything is tracked fictionally, with a fictional description. Situational elements, physical harm, mental harm, social harm, leverage, trust and distrust, are all handled in similar ways. They all have three lines under there heading. These three lines can have descriptors written on them. These are the ongoing and permanent conditions and or conditions and injuries. For example, under physical harm, written on one line, might be: "broken arm". All these ongoing or permanent conditions, as well as temporary ones, effect the fiction, if applicable. For example, in the above example the PC would be unable to fight with their broken sword hand, and we have to fight with their offhand and which would affect the fiction. Additionally, all of these ongoing or permanent conditions are given advantages and disadvantages by the GM if they are applicable to the situation. This happens every time dice are rolled to resolve conflict and the GM makes a judgment about what type of disadvantage or advantage is applied given the context of the fiction and how it relates to the condition or injury. Again, this happens for all categories of things above. So the GM chooses to give either bonus or penalty of minus one or plus one if the advantage or disadvantage is what is called: noteworthy. The GM chooses to give you their bonus or penalty of -2 or +2 if the advantage or disadvantage is what is called: major. The GM does this for all the categories of that stuff above on the fly and gives every bonus or penalty according to the fictional context and its relation to the narrative.

    \n I'm happy to have fiction lead to other fiction for it's own sake. Broken arm means I can't swing a sword? Awesome!

    \n I think that, in general, it's economical to move away from "here's a way to track all possible factors" and towards "which factors are MOST important right now?"

    Listing all the little injuries and advantages and things which have come along so far is tedious and not guaranteed to create exciting gameplay - often that kind of "homework" fails to pay off in terms of fun at the table, and then it can be frustrating to keep doing it when it's not delivering obvious fun.

    Something which helps you identify the pivotal elements at the correct moment, and bring them into play - that's easier and more effective, both.

    Dave's suggestion does this, by forcing players to prioritize NPC roles, and then further forcing the GM to follow along. Everyone is focused on a clear "target".

    You can do the same with Traits or Assets or whatever else.

    Not the *best* example, but a decent one:

    AW's "seduce/manipulate" move asks you, "What leverage do you have on this person?"

    There's no need to track matters beforehand - when you engage the move, that's when we ask each other the question. But if you're planning ahead, you'll want to maneuver appropriately, because you know you will be asked when it comes down to it.

    \n If you're doing this in a PbtA kind of way (the "moves" are things that characters do in the fiction, which interface with mechanical feedback), then think carefully about what you want the players at the table doing, and whether it feeds into the theme or not.

    For example, if you have a "Sneak" move, what does it do and what is it for?

    Do you want it to be something you use as a last resort, or a standard part of intrigue play, which you must partake in just so you can "keep up"? Is it dangerous? Is it reliable?

    The moves will be a little bit like "levers" the players can push in play. If they have goals which are hard to reach, and obstacles that keep them from reaching those goals through "free play" (or whatever other processes you have), they will want to reach for these "levers" sooner or later. Design the moves right, and you'll get a decent result. I'd advise to design the moves so that they are tempting - they are very likely to get you good information or leverage, and advance towards your goals - but also land you in deeper trouble.

    Ideally, everything you do in your attempt to climb the ladder of chaos (tm) also:

    * pushes someone else down the ladder (forcing them to react)

    and/or

    * brings a new problem with it (for you)

    and/or

    * creates an opportunity for another party

    This way, the action continues unabated, and only grows as the various levers are pushed and pulls, and the game develops a momentum of its own.

    Using the idea of the "sneak" move, again, I would consider how those three factors can play into it.

    * Pushing someone down? Hopefully, sneaking can lead to important and valuable things (including your victim's secrets) being stolen or revealed. It could also let *someone else* in to the victim's information or valuables (e.g. the spy or thief leaves the bedroom door unlocked...)

    * Bringing a new problem could mean that you are spotted while doing the sneaking, whether by the victim or a third party (usually more interesting). Perhaps someone now needs to die to keep your activities a secret.

    * Creating an opportunity for another party has already been hinted at above: someone now has leverage over you or your victim because of the action which has taken place. But there may other options - for example, the sneaking party distracts security, giving another actor an opportunity against their victim. Or the sneaking requires them to open themselves up, instead (leaving their own hidden business unattended or unwatched).

    These are the kinds of things I would look at to create "intrigue-type" actions in a PbtA design.

    Keep in mind the *scope* of the moves, too. If "sneaking" is one move, it's something which can be engaged quickly and bring results. This could be perfect - especially if you want the sneaking to happen 'off-screen'! But maybe you want more detail, more time spent, and more decision points. In that case, you'll think, rather, in terms of multiple interlocking moves which interact to create opportunities for sneaking and appropriate consequences.

    Keep in mind, though, that moves by themselves aren't sufficient. You should have some kind of overarching vision about how the players interact with each other: are they collaborators, competitors, only occasionally crossing paths, or what? That'll shape how you structure all the details underneath there.
