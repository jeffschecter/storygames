    \n I don't think I'm qualified to enter this discussion, but I think part of the problem with Sim-favoring rules, to the extent I understand them, is that they're very--for lack of a better word--gross (as opposed to fine or subtle).

    I don't think I have ever played "Sim-style", at least not very often. But I have a huge desire to play a game set in a fairly historical 13th Century Europe. Not to overcome challenges; not to tell stories with a tidy little moral at the end. Just to be there.

    The trouble is, I don't know any games that really make this work. A very large number of RPG's--such as, say, GURPS--have elaborate rules for combat, or for how many batteries a Cloning Vat requires, or how much something costs in such-and-such time period. But there are few, if any, rules for individual psychology (of PC's or NPC's) or rules for determing how cultural institutions behave.

    Exploration of character, exploration of setting - perhaps even exploration of situation---all of these are essentially left to the fiat of individual players, beyond a pretty gross level. (Your character is 5' 4", is moderately bright, and is afraid of spiders.) If I'm here to really experience being a peasant in Medieval Bavaria, the game isn't doing much of the work.

    Thus, maybe the thing is to isolate (a) what people want to explore, (b) figure out how that thing behaves, and then (c) make interacting with that thing entertaining and game-able. I would guess that for people who want to explore humans or their institutions, something deeper and more subtle than a d100 would be required, but I'm not basing that on anything.

    Two things I think feed into what John is talking about that you either do not see, experience or value:

    1. The joy of gambling. Yes, if you go to a poker game with fifty dollars in your pocket and lose all fifty, you come away from the game feeling grouchy and down and kicking yourself that you sat down at the game at all. If you win five hundred, you come away walking on a cloud and feeling like you're fifty feet tall. Similarly, when your 007 character takes a risk and loses, thereby dying, being maimed, captured, losing the bad guy, etc. it feels bad. But if there is no chance you will die, be maimed, be captured, lose the bad guy (whatever the stakes are), then there is no joy when you avoid those consequences and come through like a badass.

    Yes, yes, we can all set the stakes to whatever we want, but when watching a Bond movie, it's the action that gives us the thrills. On one level we know that Bond is going to be awesome, on another level we feel thrills when the false suspense that he might not be this awesome is resolved. In a game based on an action property, to get the thrill of action, there has to be some suspense. There has to be some gambling. As a result, sometimes you are going to lose and feel bad.

    2. A rules set that "gets out of the way", either due to player skill or designer artifice, is one that is easily tweaked by play groups to hit the (often very narrow) sweet spot of fake tension and real tension. I know this thought is verboten, System Matters, blah blah blah. But there's a big difference between: Ian Fleming Bond novels, John Gardner Bond novels, 60s Bond movies, 70s-80s Bond movies, 90s Bond movies, and the Casino Royale film. A system that gives me a big muddled grab bag from which to assemble my Bond game is exactly right for a big muddled property. Something really clear and tight and coherent and precise is totally fucking wrong for me and would fall over in a big quivering pile of suck.

    \nI don't think a game needs to go the physics route to support Sim. I think what you need is to design the rules in such a way that the group doesn't need to jocky to determine where and what the creative limits are, as you folks are doing in the James Bond example.
    \n
    \nFor instance one thing I like about Heroquest, even though I've only played Mike Holmes Narrativist reinforcing method, is keywords. Keywords tell me in a mechanical way what a culture is about, they affect my ability to do things in the world, and I can choose to have my character reinforce his culture or play against it. This makes the world more real, and my character more real. My ability to do things in the fiction are not artificially created on a meta game level, but on who my character is. What I mean by this is my ability to do stuff doesn't derive from my class of cleric, from strength + trait, or everybody rolls a d6 for conflict resolution, but from who my character is in the world. If he's from Crazy Barbarian tribe, he's got big freaking sword, knowledge of the Ice lands, crude humor, frost resistance, foolhardy, etc. This means that I'm more effective when I use my big freaking sword, to do dangerous things, in the Ice Lands, while making jokes about my opponents shriveled member. That reinforces my character, his culture, and the world.
    \n
    \nMake the game so that the fixed fiction of the game book is what I need to use to manipulate the created fiction of the game and then I think you are supporting Sim well.

No, Bond would not get hit in the balls with a knotted rope. Not in my 007 game. This is the "constructive denial" thing I talked about in one of the other threads.     Mechanisms that give GM override powers can support Sim. This basically tells everyone at the table that one of the GM's responsibilities is to protect the integrity of the Sim-space, be it verisimilitude, rulespace, or whatever you're using to get your jazz on.

    Mechanisms that give players a trump card for the rules (fate points and the like) can support Sim. Did you just whiff a roll that you know you should have made ("Zornan the Barbarian NEVER misses when he's angry!)? Spend a Reality Point and change the die roll to an automatic success. And any other player can spend a Reality Point to counteract yours if they think you're doing something bogus.

      \n Whoa, lots to answer here.
      \n
      \nFirst, while I think that Genre Sim is relatively rare, as I then say, I think that games like FATE do it well. So it does exist as a newer phenomenon in greater quantity. Put another way, if you like Genre Sim, I think that right not is a good time for you. Systems to support it strongly are appearing.
      \n
      \nAlso I have been called on that statement, in terms of whether I can back it up, and I should say that this is just my own observation. The point I was trying to make is not that people do not play genre sim (much less should not), but that as a proportion of sim play I think it historically has taken a back seat to the prevalence of more immersion-driven sim. That is, where something like James Bond does not seem to support Genre Sim, that's because it intends to support Immersion Sim.
      \n
      \nThe border between Genre Sim and Immersion Sim is like where somebody says, "I know that James Bond always gets the bad guys, but without a chance to fail, it doesn't feel like I'm there and in the action." That's stilted, sure, but hopefully you get the idea. That's not to say that Genre Sim can't have a chance to fail. It means that in each of these something different is being cherished at the expense of something else. In Genre, we do more to promote the genre, at the expense of the feeling of "being there," whereas in Immersion sim we sacrifice the story looking like a James Bond story for the feeling that we're "being" James Bond.
      \n
      \n
      \n
      \nNow, does the whiff factor support immersion sim? Tough question. It's going to come down to whether the people playing think that the level that the factor is at is "realistic." I'm not saying that James Bond is, in fact, well designed to deliver what it intends. Merely that this seems to me to be the goal. It man, in fact, fall short of even that goal.
      \n
      \nAnd is that possibly in part because of the rules also being from an era that assumes some gamism in play (meaning that the system promotes multiple modes, and thus incoherence)? Probably. James Bond, you have to understand, was pretty remarkable for it's time in how it made much of any attempt to model genre. Top Secret, actually, had started down this road with the inclusion of Fame and Fortune Points. That's a very rough beginning. But it's a tiny move from "Gamism Spy Dungeons" to something a tad more like the genre. James Bond was a huge leap forward from that. Tremendous. Just including rules for car chases, for instance, meant that they would occur in spy play (whereas before, as I say, the play was all about traipsing across quadrille rendered maps with rooms with contents descriptions). Rather, some people were doing this stuff despite these systems to be sure. But the systems were trying hard to keep up. Top Secret at least allowed you to have your character behave heroically while he still had points. As opposed to those dungeon crawling cowards who run as soon as they're out of magic missiles.
      \n
      \nUnderstanding a Genre Sim focus, games today can go even farther down these roads, and do.
      \n
      \n
      \nAnd, yes, some people playing freeform moved that way to avoid any gamism intrusion on their sim (just as some moved that way due to the same cause in order to get narrativism in play).
      \n
      \n
      \nAdam, for you, Uni might produce sim. For you playing with me, it might produce narrativism. This is the really difficult part about saying that Game X supports Mode B best. Because Mode B is produced by the players, and, as JD would tell us, no system survives contact with the players. You can only talk in gross generalities of what it "probably" does for "most" players. The only thing you can say for sure is, "Game Y supported Mode A for me."
      \n
      \n(This is why many have moved on from GNS to discussing Creative Agenda. It's less important that the system produce Mode C than it support activities in play that produce any coherent agenda, whatever that is. What mode does Universalis support? We can go around and around about that. What I can be proud of - if I can - is that most people who play it seem to "get" what the play is about, and everybody plays along those lines. Which neccessarily is informed to some extent by the players and how they actually play. With Uni, you don't seem to get players going different directions, even if it's not precisely one direction provided by the system. The group picks a single way to play. This seems to produce a quality experience most of the time. The players provide the fun, we just provide a method by which to agree to how to get at it. One of the qualities of the system is the extent to which it facilitates getting to this agreement. If you can get there by "just playing" that's optimal. )
