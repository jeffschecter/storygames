\n So, I decided to enter the 200 word RPG challenge this year. I am pretty happy with the game, especially as far as "200 word" games go. I have received some good feedback and would like to expand and tweak some things.

The game is a collaborative monster creator, with some boardgamey mechanics on top. Instead of writing about it, I will just post it here as it is only 200 words.

-------------
Liber Monstri

You are scholars writing a guide to monsters. You have travelled for months collecting rumors about different beasts. Now, you meet again to write up your findings.

Rumors: on scraps of paper write monster aspects: looks, abilities, origins, weakness, etc. Combined, all players should have at least 30 rumors. Mix them into single pile.

On your turn draw a rumor. Narrate it, recount the tale of the person who encountered the monster. Put the rumor in the center of the table. Play continues clockwise.

All new information should build on what is already established. Contradicting information needs to explained. Other players vote, majority wins. If vote is against you, the monster is proven not real. Discard all the current rumors. Next player starts a new monster.

After narrating you can declare the monster complete by naming it. Collect all the current rumors and put them in front of you. Next player starts a new monster.

After the rumor pile is empty, roll a d10 for each monster in front of you. Roll equal or lower than the number of rumors in this monster to score a point. Most points wins.

Feel free to write down and share your creations. #LiberMonstri
------------

I see this game both as a story game and a tool used for creating monsters for your traditional campaign. This is why I included the hashtag at the end, in the future I would like to collect the best of those creations from around the web and release an omnibus of them for free, so people can add some new creatures into their games.

As I see it now, the game's main target are traditional roleplayers. A kinda sneaky way of introducing story games to this crowd. This is why the scoring mechanics exist. It is a bit of a failsafe for two reasons: 1). establishes points to help traditional players decide when a monster is "finished" (I think story gamers wouldn't have a problem with that) and 2). establishes randomized/percentage based scoring to prevent very gamey gamers from taking any monsters whenever they can, thus spoiling the game for everybody else. I know that the randomized scoring is not very neat (especially among the board gaming crowd), but it is a narrative game first and foremost, so I am not worried much about that.

Saying that, I am not very keen on this scoring, or any score keeping for that matter, but this is the best solution I could come up with in few words. What else can I do to win over traditional rolplayers to give it a try?

For the expanded version I want to explain the rules better (examples would be a must) and I am thinking about adding couple different tables of monster rumors/aspects (probably by genre - fantasy, sci-fi, cthulhu, some more classic demons etc.)

I would like to know what you think about the game? Are the rules understandable to you guys? What would you change (scoring/end game advice would be most appreciated)? Would you rather have the game with a theme (I am thinking monks in a dark fantasy/s&s world after a cataclysm that spawned many new monsters) or be like it is now, genre neutral?

At this point I am looking for any advice/brainstorm. After couple "playtests" (and I am using this term very loosely) I found the game to be actually quite fun, especially for creating Conan/Cthulhu inspired monsters and would really want to finally take one of those small games I designed for challenges and make it into a fully fledged thing.

Thanks in advance!

    One thing strikes me:

    Since each player draws a rumour and then describes the monster, it means that monster creation is fully random. I would prefer to see some triage happening in there; some player-decision-making. For instance, have each player draw *two* rumours, and then pick one to use, placing the other one back in the rumour "deck". This way we can avoid some of the weirder/uninspiring combinations, and "edit" some of the monsters in specific directions.

    \n Scoring as it is now gives zero incentive to passing a monster rather than completing it. Sure, you only have a 20-30% chance of scoring a 2-3 rumor monster. But your alternative is 0% instead and the next player gets 30-40%. Pretty bad move! Turns go "try to add 10% of a point to the global pool, then decide whether to take all the points from the global pool". Well... I decide that yes, I will take the points instead of not take them.

    Similar problem for voting NO currently... you have a scoring system which incentivizes players to always vote NO. Why would they vote YES and give someone else points?

    You need some points-reason for a player to pass a monster, and you need some points-reason for a player to vote YES on a monster, or else your points are just sitting there providing perverse incentives.

    \n Example (clunky) mechanics which might incentivize the right things:

    For N players, get some tokens for monsters, and get N other types of tokens for voting.

    When a player completes a real monster with R rumors, she takes R monster tokens. When she passes a monster with R rumors, she takes R/2 round up monster tokens.

    When voting, the player to the right of the active player first secretly decides YES or NO, then the N-2 other players vote YES or NO. Anyone who agreed with the secret decision gets a voting token of a type which will be associated with the secret decider - always get that type when that person decides. If a majority of non-deciders vote NO, the monster is imaginary etc.

    When a player takes a turn, she first secretly decides whether she will try to complete the monster (write it down!), then takes a rumor, narrates, vote happens. Then reveal whether you tried to complete.

    At the end of the game, determine N partial winners, and whoever wins the most times, wins for real. There is a partial winner for each player - add up your monster tokens and voting tokens of that person's color, whoever has the most is the partial winner.

    Tell the secret decider to decide for realz, no metagame, and note that their decision either way doesn't hurt their chances of winning! (Technically they could try really hard to metagame and be a tiny bit successful but not much at all if I've done it right. Also outlaw people talking about any alternate algorithm they might use when they're the secret decider.)

    \n Multiple monsters allowed open at once. On your turn, either start a new monster or contribute to an existing monster. If there are fewer open monsters than number of players, you must start a new monster. When you start a new monster, take 2 Hipster tokens. When you contribute to a monster, each other monster gains 1 Hipster token, then decide:

    - Just take all the Hipster tokens on the monster.
    - Discard all the Hipster tokens on the monster, then attempt to complete the monster. If you succeed, name and take the monster. If you fail, discard the monster and take Hipster tokens equal to the number of NO votes.

    When you complete a monster, you may choose to trade Hipster tokens equal to twice the monster's rumor count for Monster tokens equal to 1 less than the monster's rumor count.

    When you vote, if you vote YES and succeed, take 1 Hipster token. If you vote NO and succeed, discard 1 Hipster token. If you vote to complete a contradictory monster or vote to falsify a perfectly coherent monster because of Points and Winning, feel sad.

    At the end of the game, get 1 point for each Monster token and each monster you have. Tiebreak on Hipster tokens. Tiebreak on largest monster.

    \n In my experience, when you have a game that's about creativity, scoring systems don't grip people too much - they can feel like a distraction. So it's best to keep them simple, and then only include them in the game if they help with some other aspect of the game (e.g. pacing, spotlight, focus, or something else).

    I like the idea of adding character wounds or limiting a player's ability to add rumours - those are interesting!

    Limiting the genre/scope of the game is always a plus. You could do it freeform, or by rolling or choosing from lists of options. Generally, however, limitations breed creativity, so it will only help the game. Your main problem here is dealing with the 200-word limit!
