\n This is based on my own personal perception (not necessarily preference as they are always evolving) and interactions with people.

I think there are two areas where you can really find dividing lines between gamers currently. I share these with you because I think they illustrate ignorances on the two sides and may help people here better understand where story games are not working for some seasoned gamers.

The first area is around immersion and stance.

There is a spectrum from a player totally inhabiting the skin of his or her character and interacting with the game fiction entirely through the capabilities of that character (well call this the left side) to the player being completely external to the character and the setting and manipulating those elements from the outside (well call that the right side). Very roughly, I'd say that Spirit of the Century is maybe somewhere in the middle of that line.

Your trad player generally hangs out somewhere on the left side. If you ask him or her to go too far to the right, you pull them out of their feeling of immersion, of being in and participating in another world.

This desire for immersion is often tantamount and it can be enabled by an authoritarian GM with absolute power. Thus your trad player will suffer some of the negative elements that go along with that immersion to varying degrees. Gaining the player power needed to prevent a GM from abusing their power may damage the immersion so much that it isn't worth it.

However, good trad groups on the left side know that immersion is enabled by strong communication among the group, pre-agreed upon conventions and a good system.

I know a lot of this is not new. I share it with you because I get the feeling from what I've read here that a lot of you are on the right side of this spectrum and you've gone there because of the abuse you see inherent in a system that limits player power to their role in the setting.

The horror for the right-side gamer is a total loss of power and a lot of you, it sounds like, have actually experienced this.

The horror for the left side gamer is a total loss of immersion and the feeling of being outside the world.

From these two horrors, I suspect we get a lot of the angry, dismissive, critical and generally negative feelings between the two camps.

I would ask that we try to separate our horrors when we look at gamers on the other side of the spectrum and really try to understand why they play the way they do.

I also recognize that there are many gamers who can move up and down that line. I tend to be in the center left. I'll definitely play in a one-shot on the right of the line (say, Inspectres) but I wouldn't want to participate in an entire campaign of Primetime Adventures for example (though if I had unlimited free time, I would without hesitation).

The second area is conflict, but I realize this post went on way too long.

    \n It's going to be interesting to hear how others reflect their own experiences on yours, Olman. Don't mind if I do! Thus:

    Personally I'm totally down with the idea that the player's role in most games is to "play his character", to put something with many varieties rather roughly. I've actually written about this now and then, about how important the character-playing role is for how many rpgs generate teh funny. Most significantly, it seems to me that the parts of the alternative scene that actually seem insightful and fun in play rather agree with me on this: roleplaying games tend to predominantly be fun when you've got a player playing a character, which is to say, a player deciding what a character does. There are plenty of rough drafts of games that don't do this, but the finished products are fewer, and fewer still are the successes in roleplaying without character-play.

    If the above seems to be phrased in a funny way, it's because I want to emphasize the idea that "playing a character" is a family of techniques, not an ultimate definition of good roleplaying. I'd be symphatetic towards a reasoned claim that all fun and worthwhile games involve playing a character, but that's mostly because character-play is so amazingly powerful as a tool of play that we've yet to get any consistently competitive alternatives. Much ink has flown on "parlor narration" and "conch passing" and "narration games", for instance - these are games that construe the basic player activity as telling a story instead of playing a character, which has proved to be rather controversial due to how many people (myself included) seem to find such games only pale shadows of roleplaying at its full power.

    However, while the above would seem to superficially put me on the left side of your continuum, it also seems to me that the understanding of character-playing you expose is ultimately of a different order from character-play as mere technique; if I understand you correctly, you're saying not only that character-playing is effective technique, but also that character immersion, achieved by character-play, is a desirable goal of play - indeed, that it is the very apex of left-side play. This matches my experience among "Forge-games": while the great majority of these games use character-playing intensively, they are also remarcably uncaring of player immersion; most great games of the Forge subculture involve complex shifting of gears when the players engage different techniques in turn, including character-play and other methods. As a simple example, consider conflict narration: the typical procedural solution for a narration-sharing conflict resolution in Forge games is for the players to first maneuver their characters into a conflict situation via character-play, by making choices as their characters; however, when the conflict is resolved, the player is expected to discard the singular technique of character-play for a moment to narrate the flow and consequences of the conflict fairly and with the interests of the fiction in mind. In hindsight we can see this as a pretty clear way of resolving the old traditional gaming problem issue of my character vs. our campaign: instead of asking a player to compromise the integrity of his character play or the integrity of the overall fiction we ask the player to switch between different techniques and viewpoints on play. Whenever you choose how your character will act you do with absolute character integrity and whenever you're narrating general events, you do it impartially.

    That's a rather large difference in goals, values and techniques - on this I agree with you, Olman. I don't know if I recognize the authority abuse angle, though; trad gamers in discussion often recognize an authority struggle or suspicion of GM authority as the underlying cause for why Forgers don't like the same games they do, but I think it goes a bit deeper than that: I just don't care about immersion, really, by almost any definition of the word that is specific enough to not be useless. I have no trouble switching gears in play, it's no different from separating IC/OOC knowledge. And I definitely don't think that my emotional relationship to the events of the game is dependent on being in-character all the time. For these reasons I just don't find the loss of IC integrity a very big price to pay for the exciting possibilities offered by demoting character-play into a mere technique - a valuable technique with immense power, but still just a technique that is engaged as necessary.

    What we have here is basically an introduction into the good old immersion discussion. It's a good one, to be sure, although it does easily bog down into trying to pin down what the word means. For me it's not a controversial thing, though, as immersion rarely shows up as a concrete goal or means for my game design. Insofar as there is immersion to be had in my games, it's as the successful result of an interesting game, just like it would be in a boardgame.

    Anyway - a great post, Olman. I wouldn't mind seeing others start threads for a personal overview of how they see the trad/alternative thing right now. Seems like a much better way of getting your viewpoint down than the long debate-like thing we've been doing.

    \nWhat you say about the left side of the spectrum definitely matches my experience - both of living in that part through the '90s and of discussing the matter with gamers. I think that it's a core part of the whole traditional gaming thing, explaining many of the inclinations therein, that the game is secondary to the fiction: it's OK to ignore the rules, it's OK to play all games in the same ways, it's OK to develop a strong gamer identity, because what you're really doing is a journey of mastery over a skill-set of imaginauting and not just playing a single game here and now. It's a journey you make by improving your skills, developing a long-term team, perhaps investing a lot in a single campaign... and the reward is a strong experience of being there, what we might call "robust Exploration" or "robust fiction" in Forge terminology. What you do with the fiction is somewhat secondary to being able to create and maintain it consistently, and any game design or external sources of fluff are only there to facilitate this. This totally makes sense to me even if it's not what I'm currently interested in myself.

    In comparison, my own deal with roleplaying games is totally all about the game nowadays. I don't know why this is, but I strongly suspect that it's due to my improvement as a writer - I no longer reside in the desk drawer writing territory like I did as a teenager, I no longer have to express my art as a GM - today I'm near utterly disinterested in waking up in the middle of the night, having a great idea for an adventure, writing it down and then playing it with friends. I don't pick campaigns to play, I pick games, and when I want to do fiction for fiction's sake, I opt to write, instead. What carries my roleplaying is the idea that the imaginary process/environment (called Shared Imagined Space in Forge theory) can be utilized in many interesting and delightful ways for making interesting games: you can have a game that out of the experience of play weaves a story, for example, or you can have a game that'll give you a rich tactical skirmish combat environment, or you can use the fictional environment created by the tools we know for other exciting purposes. The issue of forming a robust fiction, however necessary, is somewhat secondary as a concern for me. This could be because I don't have any routine problems with doing it or because I have lower standards in my immersion than the traditional gamer - whatever the cause, the practice is that I almost always choose to use gaming technique, mechanics and procedures that flout the fiction in favour of benefitting a game. IC/OCC firewalls do not matter to me, speaking in-character does not matter to me, having the rules break down in the middle of play isn't that big a deal... the fiction is no magical thing to be protected, it's a simple pre-requirement for playing the game itself.

    As for the game part, there are plenty of reasons to go for roleplaying specifically instead of other types of games. The virtues of game in general are, however, illustrative of what I like in roleplaying: for example, I absolutely adore the controlled chaos of having a system of procedures simplify cooperation in ways that allow us to work together in creating something without anybody actually having to coordinate it. I like being able to simplify reality into a stylized depiction, like games do. I also like how games allow us to strive and learn in a controlled environment. Plenty of reasons for appreciating a game, and combining that with the fictional possibilities of roleplaying, you get plenty of opportunity for this medium of ours.

    I don't think that my preoccupation with the game part is that uncommon in the end. I wouldn't have any idea, but I suspect that old '70s roleplaying would have mostly held a similar idea of the purpose and requirements of the fiction. At least I don't encounter a lot of texts complaining about the robustness of the fiction and how it gets broken - all that comes in later, through the late '80s, as roleplaying gets occupied with performing a story and heightening the artistic quality of the experience. I am pretty convinced personally that this was something of a dead end in certain ways for the hobby - roleplaying is a medium for art and game, but the artistry is not in the fragile, hypnotic illusion of being there, it seems to me, but rather in the interaction and raw communication between the participants. It's like cave painting, not photograph.

    \n The issue of setting fidelity vs. getting something interesting going is one that I have a lot of interest in myself. I wrote about this in Solar System in a way that I think is pretty well applicable to most narrativistic adventure games out there. According to myself, you need a robust fiction, meaning a credible fiction, because otherwise the events in the game have no impact on the players. However, I follow, you also need to ensure that the actual game progresses towards meaning. I chose to call the balance between these two needs "dramatic coordination" in SS, making it a specific task for the Story Guide (the GM-equivalent of the game). According to the Solar System good dramatic coordination means recognizing the genre fidelity of the campaign and making choices of scene framing in a way that pushes the drama to whatever extent is allowed by the genre the players are doing. (For the purposes of Solar System, an ostensibly universal system, all campaigns have a genre - it might be more or less realistic, but we never lose sight of the fact that we're making a story.)

    In practice dramatic coordination means forgoing dull choices when you can make exciting ones without breaking genre expectations. An example I use in the text is about when a player character is wandering alone in the woods after killing his brother's wife: insofar as it would not seem hopelessly contrived, the SG would be expected to continue the game by framing a scene where the character stumbles on a hermitage, some faeries, a king's reeve or perhaps his brother: just like a movie, we do not expect the SG to frame a scene where the character just wanders around and doesn't encounter anything interesting, even if that would be the most likely realistic choice. Even in the most raw, ugly realistic genre we would still skip over the part where the character wanders around for days, meets some cowherds, recuperates in their humble cottage for a month and then returns to the city to clear his name; we'd just note that this was what happened and frame directly into the next scene that actually matters for the story.

    Solar System is not every roleplaying game out there, but for the purposes of that game I'm pretty happy with the above heuristic: you suffer dull realism only to the extent required to give the campaign the realistic frisson expected by the group for this campaign. A superhero campaign will be highly stylized and involve a lot of dramatic coordination, while a historical costume drama will have considerably less while still being nowhere near start reality.

    It should be noted that the above discussion concerns the ways a GM chooses to exert his powers in scene framing - that is, in choosing what matters are dealt with next and what things are experienced by the player characters. It's a big sector of a GM's overall power, but it's technically separate from his backstory authority - the ability to invent the setting and situation the characters find themselves in. In this regard Solar System is very traditional: the SG has full freedom to create a backstory situation as he would, and he's expected to stick by whatever he invents, but he's not forced to do this in advance: insofar as this particular game is concerned, there's no meaningful difference in whether the SG decides about what's beyond the next valley before the session or in the middle of it as long as he makes the choice according to the right criteria. Specifically, "dramatic coordination" I describe above is a separate power and responsibility from "backstory definition" - you're not supposed to mix up the two by changing the backstory in the flight to make it more interesting; that's beyond the scope of dramatic coordination, which only concerns introducing the backstory into play without undue delay, not changing the backstory to artificially make it as awesome as possible.

    Anyway, that's just something that came to mind from the discussion of Lost and X-Files. Solar System is pretty representative of your average Forge-style adventure game in that while it recommends strong dramatic coordination, it does not advocate No Myth - that being our special jargon for a fluid backstory where player characters "just happen" to walk into the same city no matter which road they take. If one wanted to phrase this as reality vs. arbitrary staging, I guess you could characterize it as "enhanced reality": we skip over the dull scenes and assume coincidences that will bring the interesting stuff into play sooner, just like a movie script would, but we don't take liberties with the characters and backstory to artificially tailor drama.

    There are of course games that take more or less radical stances on this issue, as with everything else, all to the benefit of whatever goals the designer had in mind. Personally I can enjoy No Myth games, too - it doesn't bother me that we're not pretending that the setting exists outside the immediately necessary dramatic context, as long as this choice of dropping the setting serves an useful purpose. Zombie Cinema is like this, actually: in that game the backstory is extremely sparse, it really just consists of whatever has been brought into play so far. You're still not supposed to contradict prior narration, so it's not quite at the extremes of postmodern mindfuckery possible

    Also: I hate "whose vision of the story willl we go with". I don't find that interesting at all myself. Luckily it's very rare outside rought drafts, and it seems that not many games like that get much traction. Houses of the Blooded comes to mind, mainly.

    \n There was a discussion on NerdNYC a while back about this sort of thing, and one of the conclusions we came to was that, like in fiction, there are different points of enjoyment. The Forge vocabulary uses the term "exploration" to be practically synonymous with "roleplaying" (as in the activity, the hobby, as a whole; not the act of portraying a character). I think that's a good word that describes a lot of what we do as gamers.

    Anyway, given that view or RPGs, a bit of a dichotomy emerged: some people seemed to be really excited about the idea of exploring an environment, a world, a place. The character is the "interface", but the real goal of play is basically to go around and look at things, sometimes from a distance, sometimes by getting your hands right in there. You're literally "discovering" a place or time, feeling your way around, like a child exploring a new house or park. At some extremes, this point of view might be parodied by a nature documentary--we just look at things and learn about them.

    The other side of the discussion was focused around people who were more interested in the exploration of "Character": who's who, why are they that way, how will they change, what decisions will they make? Many dramatic critics, literary theorists, and authors say that exploration of character is at the basis of good fiction. Here, the interest is in personal action, personal belief, decisions and consequences. A parody of this kind of focus might be a television soap like "All My Children"--every scene is an interpersonal conflict or revelation of character, and, really, the events could take place anywhere, it doesn't matter.

    This interacts in interesting and unexpected ways with the "immersion line" you have drawn, walkerp. And I think that's why Eero's point that character-play is a technique and not an end in itself is very important. For instance, I've met players who are really into the exploration of "environment", who really don't care what the character is about at all. The designer of MYST once said (wish I had the link) that the game was inspired by D&D, except without the concept of an "avatar" or "character" for the player to embody, because that just puts you at another remove from the place you're discovering. Why pretend you're someone else exploring a place, when it's so much more primal to just explore it yourself?

    I've also played with some gamers who are really into "old-school renaissance"/basic D&D-type play, and love their games, where they go through dozens of characters, all thrown into the same dungeon. They love that exploration aspect, but they really aren't terribly attached to the characters they play: they laugh with glee when their characters die and roll up new ones.
    '
    Meanwhile, someone really interested in exploring character might also fit anywhere in your spectrum: a "left-side" player might be like a LARPer or Jeepform player, who wants to sit in a closet for four hours and just experience "being trapped". They want the emotional experience of a character's plight--playing a game where their character could die at any time would totally throw off their "immersion". A "right-side" player might be more like a GM in a traditional game: they want to throw characters into situations and see what happens to them. ("Jake chose to sacrifice his dignity in order to keep his job. Interesting! Now let's see what happens when his old flame shows up and offers him an ultimatum...") They might really enjoy maneuvering a character through a series of transformations, and, while they are doing so, portraying the character. But they're never trying to fool themselves into thinking that they ARE the character.

    I'm not sure why I'm typing all this, but I think a lot of these dichotomies can be useful to understanding different styles of play. What I like about that is that it gives me tools to understand different gaming styles and then to be able to jump in and enjoy them. That's really valuable! I don't believe any of these dichotomies are really just a one-dimensional line, but if we treat them as simplifications we can look at until we see something better, they can be useful to us.

    Incidentally, that's the main draw of "indie/new school" games for me: an effort has been made to outline what is fun about them and how to engage that. My main complaint about "traditional" gaming is that it is full of assumptions about what it is, and it's really difficult for me to figure out how to fit in and enjoy the game.

    Posted By: walkerpI think there are two areas where you can really find dividing lines between gamers currently. I share these with you because I think they illustrate ignorances on the two sides and may help people here better understand where story games are not working for some seasoned gamers.

    The first area is around immersion and stance.

    There is a line between immersion and stance, yes, but I do not believe it is the same line as the one you find between classical rpgs and modern rpgs.

And it's very easy for it to get into the sort of territory where it's incredibly frustrating for a player, either because a) they feel like their actions don't matter, or b) the game devolves into a whole lot of meaningless rolling without anything interesting going on (e.g. "Roll to climb the tree. Oh you failed? Yes, you can try again. Roll again. Failed again? Sigh... roll again.").

      I've seen in several (different groups) this problem very quickly eradicated by the introduction of conscious conflict resolution and something like overt stakes-setting. In particular, it's interesting because in those groups we didn't switch to a different system (e.g. an "indie" game), but just made this one change, and suddenly gameplay was faster, more reliable, more fun, and no one felt like they were being screwed over anymore.

      I hope that helps describe what I'm talking about: as a player or GM in that situation, the difficulty of making sure the non-fun stuff happened at the table was drastically reduced. Players didn't need to worry about somehow manipulating the GM or reading their mind in order to get what they want, and the GMs could stop worrying about whether they were being fair or unfair. Suddenly we found ourselves less worried about dealing with the social issues.

      Now, yes, for some players that particular rule might reduce their sense of immersion. But even removing the "overt stakes-setting" bit, and letting the GM handle that in his or her mind ("invisibly"), would greatly reduce the problem I've mentioned above. (I'm pretty sure a lot of "trad" GMs have come up with this trick on their own over the years, too.)

      And here's where I have an interesting question:

      walkerp, you know how you said that you're very much on the "left" side of the immersion line you've described? And how you don't fully understand what "right" side players get out of gaming?

      I'm pretty sure that you run games as a GM from time to time. What's the draw for as a GM? What do you enjoy about a game you're running? After all, you can't just "walk around and look at things", can you?
