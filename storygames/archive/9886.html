If people get excited about the game, you can point them to Lulu or wherever to get their deluxe print edition.     Precisely my model for (eventual) release of GLASS:
    GLASS is a hard or soft bound book with all the bells and whistles and a lot of pre-generated, ready-to-use elements.
    shotGLASS is JUST a rules reference, no bells or art, from which one would have to build elements.

    This is also basically the TriStat model, but the "core book" is, IIRC, $2 (printing costs). I got one handed to me for free at DragonCon some years back, just to promote the sale of full games like BESM and Mechwarrior (?).

    Finally, I know quite a few folks who release all manner of digital media under Careware (proceeds go to a charity--Jason, that's your cue) and Donationware. It works well enough, it would seem.

    Finally, if you *really* think "free is toxic" then releasing Print+PDF on Lulu for printing costs only can make something seem to have real value, actually HAVE a value add (a usable book and PDF) but not be utterly free, just rather cheap compared to printing it oneself. And then you don't have the bandwidth load for hosting.

    HTH;
    David

    \n MJ, it's hard for me to offer advice or suggestions without knowing more about your motivations and intentions for this game.

    Why CC? My sense is that CC is only useful if you want to encourage people to adapt and publish their own materials based on your work, like John does with Danger Patrol and Lady Blackbird, for example. Is this a game you're assuming other folks will want to release supplemental material for? If not, I wouldn't really bother with CC, because it doesn't do much for you. People can freely play your game without needing to have the rights to it.

    Why donations? Is this something that you're willing to give away free? My sense of "donation"-based products is that it often signals that a creator either doesn't want to go to the trouble of selling the product or is worried that it might not actually be worth money (because it hasn't been playtested extensively, for example). If it's the former, then sure, donations can be easier to deal with than sales, but you're less likely to make much return. If it's the latter, then maybe you shouldn't be asking for money for it at all, y'know?

    \n Just one point about Donationware--the motivations for doing so are far from binary, as you seem to suggest. Off the top of my head:
    * Developed for some other remuneration or use, now fulfilled, and so why not give it away? Exs) A program that was done for a CompSci class; LARP rules for a single, paid event which can be abstracted for other events.
    * Charitability: it was fun to make, it works great, it makes people's lives better, so the maker wants it available to all, though wouldn't be adverse to thanks in the form of financial compensation. (Note, I've also seen Donationware setup as trades--we do it in our industry, trading ad space in publications.)
    * And the aforementioned effect to keep it from being perceived as "free and therefore toxic junk." It says, "look, this is free but it's worth something--$5 or you tell me."

    I'll note that NPR runs as "donationware." It's creative content that is free and worthwhile, but they don't demand you pay to hear it (i.e. it's not moved to XM Radio or iTunes or similar). [Yes, this is muddled a bit by once being largely taxpayer funded, which got a lot of its infrastructure off the ground... but not any more.]

    Anyhow... you merely spoke of your sense (i.e. perception) of such products. I'm just giving Marcus another data point on which to base his eventual decision.
    ---
    While we're on the subject, have you considered crippleware? It's annoying, but you could offer the game without some key chapter or without a large percentage of its (say) Skills or Powers or Classes or Races whatever reference component your game needs (NOT procedural elements--you want it to be playable but incomplete, content-wise). And just say something like, "For the full list Skill and Powers, five additional Races and Classes, and a set of Alternative and Optional Rules, send $5 by PayPal to ...."

    The game is playable, they get the goodness from it, but if they want to fully engage the setting, they have to shell out. Might not work for your particular game design, though. Some games have very little reference material (especially here at SG) and, thus, can't be "crippled."
