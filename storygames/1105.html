Lxndr
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Meetups / Conventions
So, as Mike mentioned both last night on IRC and today in the GenCon 2006 thread, a lot of us already take Wednesday off for travel purposes, or because we need to be there for the booth... and is it really too much more of a hassle to take off Monday and Tuesday too? For some of us, maybe not, especially with enough planning in advance. If you can do that, you can even leave Saturday, and turn GenCon from a four day event (or less if you, like some of us, leave early on Sunday) into an eight or nine day event. Four days in the frenetics of the con, four or five days of a more sedate "pre-convention".

Is anyone else twigged, intrigued, and enthused by this idea? I certainly am!

    Okay folks, two object lessons:

    • The GenCon train plan: People talked about what a good idea it would be in theory, and then waited around for a critical mass of people to have already committed in practice. It never got off the ground.
    • The Go Play meme: Folks just started doing shit, without waiting for approval or even critical mass, and made the pieces fit together by constant feedback. It self-launched, accelerated, and headed straight for the stratosphere.

    I know which model I would recommend. I'm going to get in touch with the Embassy Suites and figure out with them:

    • How/whether one could join their outside-convention prices for saturday-tuesday nights with their convention prices (through GenCon block purchase) for wednesday through sunday.
    • What the grand total cost would be for a suite for that week.

    • TonyLB
      August 2006
      Okay folks, there's clearly enough interest to get something going. I'm not planning to wait until there's proof positive that it will be the biggest thing since sliced orcs. I've been wracking my brain to make a unit small enough to self-start (i.e. if this is all that happens, that's cool) but open enough to bootstrap (i.e. if more than this happens we get synergy), and I think I've got it roughly figured. Here's what I intend to do:

      I will be looking for people to fill slots in "Team Victory," an intentional gamer posse. It will (I suspect) consist of: Three returning developers (two of whom (including me) must commit to paying and enjoying their share of the full nine day experience), three aspiring game developers (two for the full nine days) and two monkeys/fans/cool-folk-without-games (both for the full nine days).

      The goal of Team Victory is that any single member of the team will be competent, confident and equipped to pitch, demo, run and sell every single one of the games that the team represents. That's at least six games, and possibly more.

      Reaching that goal will be a year-long projet, which will not begin but rather culminate when we reach Indianapolis. Over the course of the year we must provide brain-power, word-of-mouth, logistical support, etc. For instance, we will have a vested interest in making sure that our aspiring developers get their games done, tested, edited, printed and delivered on schedule ... Team Victory cannot achieve victory if one of the games isn't even at the convention.

      Likewise, returning developers will be working to provide new, better demo packets, pregenerated scenarios, and other equipment for the team, and to collaboratively refine the material being provided for the games of others.

      Let me reiterate, because this is mutualism gone crazy-in-the-head: For the purposes of GenCon, if you are a member of Team Victory, you are responsible for the success of every single game. You're selling for six. You're prepping for six games before the convention.

      No, we won't be splitting the sales receipts evenly. Get real, you hippies.

      I might, however, be inclined to cut the non-selling monkeys on the team in for some small commission on my sales. But if enough people tell me "Oh, no, that's cool ... it's an honor and a privilege just to be on the team" then I will certainly take them at their word and pay them nothing but honor.

      The days before GenCon would be spent in basic convention skills (for instance, I could use a thorough, non-stress orienteering run around the secret skyways of the mall, a chance to be the first to plunk down seventy-five cents for a storage locker, etc., etc.), in showing off how well we know the systems by running them for each other (not to learn the systems from scratch ... by then we should already know them) and of course just to have a freakin' blast.

      And that's it as far as the self-starter package. If Team Victory is the only group that ever gets off the ground then it'll just be the eight of us totally pwning everyone else in salesmanship, preparation and mutual spirit.

      But there's nothing that I'm recommending doing here that someone else couldn't do just as well (or better) with a different group of eight people. So if Team Victory ends up being one of many groups then I can immediately imagine a lot of great inter-team events that we can organize in the days leading up to the convention. At the most basic, we should get away from our own team-mates and play games with other teams. After all, we'll be primed to show off our mastery, and an appreciative audience is always nice.

      If we wanted to get more sophisticated, we could actually have mock booth experiences. We could take turns wandering by, and letting team members try to draw us into demos, pitch us on solid product, and herd us to the cash register, books and wallet in hand. I can think of ways to make this both good training and good fun. For instance, how'd you like to be handed a card "You are just the boyfriend ... your girlfriend is totally into these games, but you feel clueless and adrift. On the other hand, you do have a fairly good amount of money and the desire to gain some cred, if anyone comes across as a human being in a way that you understand" and then cut loose on an unsuspecting sales-staff?

      But all of that is my little daydream fantasy of what happens if the team idea takes off. I think it's extensible. But I'd be perfectly happy if all that ever happens is that a bunch of us get together as Team Victory, and become the most potently concentrated force for mutual indie games enthusiasm that the world has ever known.
