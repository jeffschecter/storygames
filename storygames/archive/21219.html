\n I've read and reread the rules of this game. It seems really good! I'm going to play it this weekend. Most of my questions have been answered by reading really carefully several times. But a couple of questions remain. Hopefully someone here has played it and can answer.

1: Can the scene end without the knife being put down? Pretty much the only example in the book is of a demand which is "I must marry a daughter of the Li family". That's a demand that can't be met within the same scene, normally. Does that mean that the scene can end and the receiver keeps the knife until the marriage has taken place? Is that why taking the knife with wirework can be useful? Or is the example intended as "You must promise that I can marry a daughter of the Li family"?

2: The book says "If you take the knife, you get to re- spond as though you were given it by the person you just did Wirework with." This doesn't make sense! You are only given the knife accompanied by a demand. If you take it, there's no demand, and none of your options make sense (like "accept" or "escalate"). Is this supposed to be used to take the knife to be able to make a demand yourself? If so, I'm assuming you're taking it from someone who holds it, since otherwise you don't need wirework to take the knife. And in that case is the demand on the original holder nullified? I don't understand this thing.

I think those are my only two points of confusion. I'm super excited to try this game!

    \n 1: The promise interpretation is correct.

    2: Wirework is triggered by a knife exchange; the implicit demand here is, whatever demand was conveyed when the triggering knife exchange occurred. So for example if you walk into a McDonalds and say, \u201cI want thirty cheeseburgers,\u201d and hand the cashier the open knife, and they go to wirework, and you win, the demand is still \u201cgive (me) thirty cheeseburgers\u201d but the tables have turned, and you\u2019re the one who decides if you get those burgers or not.

    3. After you\u2019ve defined a set, it\u2019s still \u201cyour set\u201d, you\u2019ve just said what the details are about it. It\u2019s in the middle of the table so people can see and use it. You\u2019re sharing, but you\u2019re not communists.

    I think I understand it now. Awesome. So by taking the knife I get to decide the answer of the opponent. I can choose that they accept my demand. That's pretty cool and makes sense.

    What tripped me up about the set cards was this sentence: "Blank set cards remain the property of each player until they are defined". That suggests they are no longer the property of the player once defined, thus not owned by the player. Just a thought if you ever revise the rule book.

Possibly this is me being squeamish, but I don't think I want to steal blank set cards in this game.

    \n Played the game, and it was really good! I hope to play it more in the future. One part still really threw us, though. Escalating to bloodied.

    So you hand me the knife and ask me to do something, then I stab your sheet and ask you to do something, which if you accept will mean I also accept. The text implies that this means I'm also doing physical violence to you. This feels really weird! I'm having trouble imagining a situation where this makes sense. And it gets more difficult since you will die before the end of the scene. So I'm limited to asking things that you can accomplish right now.

    An example of this mechanic in use would really clear it up. As it is, it always felt impossible to escalate from unsheathed, because it never made sense to (conditionally) agree to someone's demand and stab them at the same time.

    The ultimatum is also a bit confusing, but we never got there.
