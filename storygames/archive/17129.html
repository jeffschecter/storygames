\n Bill and I wrap up our Dexcon game discussions with a look at a scenario I ran of Cthulhu Dark. Entitled, 'A Head of His Time' the scenario placed Andrew Mellon and his family in the midst of the controversy between Joseph Duveen and Bernard Berenson over the correct attribution of the painting, Adoration of the Shepherds. I've been toying with this scenario for years and somehow Cthulhu Dark made it work the way I hoped it would. The episode is here.

    \n I tend to run combats as with any other opposed conflict. Here is a genuine example:

    Graham: Okay, so you're attacking him. What do you want?
    Player 1: I want him to drag him out of the hall.
    Graham: Right, and what do you want?
    Player 2: I want to sacrifice myself to the creatures.
    Graham: Right, so do an opposed roll for that.

    It's implicit, in this, that once you've rolled, you can't try again. Once Player 2 has begun sacrificing himself, Player 1 can't continue trying to drag him out of the hall. Well, he can, but mechanically, it's been decided, so narratively, we know he's going to fail.

    Combat gets complicated in Cthulhu games. I've once had a conflict between Investigator A trying to shoot themselves and Investigator B trying to stop them. You don't want to handle that using To Hit and Damage rolls. They just don't work.

    Oh, here is an important thing. The central combat rule is technically true: If you try to fight a creature, you will die.. But that's only if you're trying to kill the creature. You can fight your way past a creature or fight your way free from a creature's grasp or perhaps even fight a creature back.

    Thanks, Mel. It helps to write all this down!

    Oh, here is an important thing. The central combat rule is technically true: If you try to fight a creature, you will die.. But that's only if you're trying to kill the creature. You can fight your way past a creature or fight your way free from a creature's grasp or perhaps even fight a creature back.
    \nI think we handled the fights along the lines you indicate. In the two 'fights' in the adventure, one was to escape Berenson and his ghoul servitors. The second was to rescue Andrew Mellon. In neither case was the enemy permanently removed as a threat. The escape split the characters, leaving them hunted in chthonic chambers; while the rescue left the enemy vowing revenge.
    What was interesting is that some characters will be more prone to fighting due to the character concept; other characters will be more likely to hide or to flee, while still others will freeze in panic. So the action of the character is just a special effect of the die roll. For some, their escape is done through fighting their way out. Others just run.
