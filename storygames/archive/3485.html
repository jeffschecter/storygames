\n In The Power of Class-based Systems I got redirected to look carefully at the benefits of letting people create their character through a cross-product of two independent axes ("Feisty" x "Princess" = "Feisty Princess" = "Cool!") Then in [MB] Stat up characters in the new system, I put that to use in helping players to make their characters more quickly, by combining two different types of (essentially) Keys to give them a powerful synergy that motivates and inspires.
\n
\nSo, the players are all taken care of. Lucky fuckin' players. Now what about the poor, long-suffering GM? Can the same process inspire the creation (and structure the play) of a situation?
\n
\nI'm thinking, at its most basic, that this would be something like "Okay, I'm pulling ... hmm ... "Rivalry" and ... not "Romantic" ... not "Social" ... AH! "Competitive!" Absolutely! A rival gang is trying to muscle into your territory, pushing drugs on the street-corners that you guys work to keep clean, and generally being thuggish in all the wrong ways. Time for you to set them straight and show what a street-gang is really about!" And then Rivalry would have a set of keys that pay the GM for introducing certain things, and "Competitive" would have a set of keys that pay the GM for introducing certain things.
\n
\nBut, see ... I can only give that example, or any example, by assuming a certain set of independent axes (in this case "What the type of the conflict is" and "What the arena is") and I am totally unsure what the right set of axes is. In fact, I'm pretty damn sure that in this case these axes aren't even really independent. Can one really have a rivalry that isn't competitive?
\n
\n"Abilities" x "Persona" has decades of testing to show us how it works, its strengths and its weaknesses. I generally think that it is, if not THE right way to do characters, A right way.
\n
\nTo the best of my knowledge there is no substantial exploration of what axes one should use to break out situation creation. What ways are worth exploring?

    \n Make the players do it!

    One thing you had in prior Misery Bubblegum playtests that's worth restoring to the current version is huge, bright-red Flags in the form of each player stating their character's Issue for the session: "I want to find a real friend" or "I want to get a prom date" or whatever. And then you'd combined that with ablative traits to give an actual count-down to when you were finally allowed to determine success-or-failure on that Issue. That goes a long way towards structuring the session not just for the GM but also for the players, and it's something I've missed in the last few playtests.

    Ithink it'd be fairly easy to come up with a deck of goal cards -- each one designed to be evocative rather than restrictive, e.g. "friendship," "a date," "acclaim," "triumph," etc. So each player would have three things in front of them: a Persona, a Role, and a Goal. That would make coming up with proper adversity stupidly simple, I think.

    You can even restore the ablative element of the system, which is utterly in keeping with the high-school theme of "transcend yourself": You have to discard either your Role or your Persona (or both) before you can have a conflict about whether or not you attain your Goal. (I'd suggest not requiring a conflict to do the discards; just make it a free choice). Then the card economy will kick in to cue people when they're in a strong enough position to try for their Goal, and the desire to get into that position will structure the session.

    \n Taking John's excellent suggestion about "rivalry" being better than "a rival" and so on:

    Friendship -- draw a card every time you give the players a choice between...
    - being seen with their friend(s) or ditching them
    - helping their friend(s) do something questionable or stopping them
    - taking an opportunity that means leaving their friends behind or rejecting the opportunity in order to stay with their friends think of the last scene of Good Will Hunting here)

    Apprenticeship -- draw a card every time you give the players a choice between...
    - spending time patiently learning from their mentor or blowing it off
    - following their mentor's advice or disregarding it
    - taking on a new challenge (succeed or fail) or refusing to try ("Try? There is no try...")

    Rivalry -- draw a card every time you give the players a choice between...
    - taking an arguably unfair advantage to win or risking defeat
    - fighting/backstabbing/undermining their rival outside the arena of competition or "saving it for the game"
    - devoting everything to victory or putting some other relationship first even if it might mean losing

    Hatred -- draw a card every time you give the players a choice between...
    - defying their enemy and taking a beating or running to save their skins but not their dignity
    - sinking to their enemy's level or not being able to strike back at all
    - recognizing they have something in common with their enemy or denying it ("No - no - that's impossible...")

    \n Sydney: I think I would prefer to give rewards for what the GM does, rather than how it is supposed to impact the players. Remember the "Take a card when you shock two or more people" key-punch in Naughty? That one gave us trouble in play, precisely because every time you try to do it you have to poll the table before you can take your card.
    \n
    \n
    Evil Overlord List, #85: I will not use any plan in which the final step is horribly complicated, e.g. "Align the 12 Stones of Power on the sacred altar then activate the medallion at the moment of total eclipse." Instead it will be more along the lines of "Push the button."
    \n
    \nOr, as my sensei always says, "Mo' simple, mo' bettah."
    \n
    \nBy contrast, I offer a "Rivalry" key that looks more like this:
    \n
    \n- Demonstrate an advantage that another cannot attain
    \n- Refuse any solution but total victory
    \n- Demonstrate a vulnerability
    \n
    \nI think these would often lead to the same sorts of questions you list. For instance: the GM is motivated not to offer vulnerabilities that would give the PCs a fair advantage in the arena of combat ... but she's also motivated (by cards!) to show some vulnerability. So what does she do? She shows a vulnerability that the PCs couldn't possibly take advantage of without being scum-bags. And so on and so forth.
    \n
    \nThe advantage (to my eyes) is that as a GM I can do any of those things unilaterally. I can say "Kyomi is an unstoppable biker-chick engine of destruction ... even hardened gang-bangers fear her. She has challenged you to a fight ... and she has a clear advantage in that arena that you cannot possibly match," and just take a card.
