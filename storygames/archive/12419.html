\n Time to start a thread for July.\n
Again, our "official" (scarequotes!) rules summary for these threads:

-----
1. Feel free to respond here in-thread to another link posted.
a. If you're looking to start a conversation, perhaps take your comment and create a new thread with it in the Stuff to Watch category.
b. Otherwise, to prevent too many comments in the thread, number your response (first response: [1]; second response [2]; third response [3]). If you see that you're the second person to post, "Wow, that looks cool!" to a post here, then put a "[2]" at the top of your post. Three posts ([3]) is the limit: The fourth person to post should just take their comment and create a new thread, to carry the discussion over there further.
-----
Why?

Because these threads are great: We can link to little things being talked about on the web in general, on blogs, or other forums that might be noteworthy, without having to dedicate a new thread to each and every one (although that, too, is fine if you want to discuss the item further or "open up a discussion").
Responding to these posts with a comment or two is understandable (other times there might be no comments, just cause the link was self-explanatory: nothing to say, lots of heads nodding in agreement). But we don't want to create a huge discussion in the middle of the thread: Some folks get a lot out of these threads, and don't want to look back to see the "10 latest posts" just to see that it's a heated discussion of something from a previous page. Cool? Cool.

    \n [Feminist Geek 1]

    Neat writeup! While I'm glad she's writing about geek feminism, I wish her thoughts were a little more concise and focused less on clothing and cosplay. Although I am going to contradict myself by saying I love the Star Wars corsets. Two of my favorite things, combined into one!

    This, I felt, was one of the strongest statements in the interview, and is something that I agree with when it comes to sexism in geek culture:

    The problem with this narrative and how it functions in conversations about geek misogyny is that the hypermasculine standard that leads to geek men feeling disenfranchised while growing up is the result of a patriarchal culture. By becoming misogynists, geek men actually reinforce the sexist standards that led to them getting beat up or made fun of as kids. Patriarchy is still to blame. And the inability to recognize this, not only by the individual geeks who become misogynists, but by critics of geek culture, makes sexism in these communities difficult to diagnose and counteract.

    Basically, its hard to counteract sexism in Geek Culture because its hard to counteract it in Mainstream Culture. Yep.

    \n [Albion 3, which is symbolically appropriate]

    Oh, OK. Briefly then, for here.

    "Celtic fantasy roleplaying in a once and future Britain."

    In the day after tomorrow of our own world, climate change and social upheaval bite more strongly and quickly than almost anyone expected. Society collapses; lots of bad things. But a bunch of Celtic revivalists, new agers, environmentalists, etc organise to form the Druid Order and rebuild society along their chosen lines. That was a couple of centuries ago, and you're members of a fantasy-type society build on Celtic ideas and other bits of British history: druids, bards, warriors, magicians, crafters, musicians... The kingdoms are at peace, but every so often one hears rumours of bandits, or conspiracies, or monsters from the otherworlds. Threats are growing beneath the surface. Guess who gets to meet them?

    Rules-wise the basis is d6 dice pools, counting successes and exploding. It's quite a traditional design - aiming to do the different bits well so that they're easy to handle and mesh with the feel I'm after. That's the theory...

    This is my multi-year magnum opus get out of head thingy. Part publishing, part therapy!

    Oh, you might like http://www.silverbranch.co.uk/albion/gallery.htm

    Oh 2 - this thing is 256 pages in Letter format and when it's finished I'm having a nice snooze.

      \n [1 Rules of Writing]\n
      Posted By: Tim C KoppangIn terms of roleplaying, though, I often lift the "one place" restriction.
      \nIn terms of writing in general, and also roleplaying, I'd instead be lenient with the term "one place" and what it means.
      Like "Going to soccer practice" can count as one place, even if it involves rummaging through the garage, wading through a traffic jam, and looking for a parking space.

      "The road to Traitor's Tower," which wanders through the dark woods and narrow ravines, could be one place - so long as it is tonally and thematically coherent as well.

      A montage happens in one place, even if it's happening all over the city - the lighting, the sondtrack, the tone and the narrative space are unified, even if Rocky is jogging up the steps to city hall and in the gym and at home.

      IMO.

      \n [RPG podcast survey 1]
      I'm only through the first few pages but the Education Level and Earning statistics desperately need to be compared to statistics for podcast listeners in general. I don't know if statistics on podcast listeners in general exist, but until you can correct for the (presumably) self-evident fact that people who listen to podcasts are more likely to be educated and more likely to be wealthy, these categories aren't going to tell us much of anything about RPG players. (Not that that is the only goal, but at some point the author draws a conclusion concerning RPG players and education... if you're going to do that, the analysis needs to account for other factors to at least some degree.)

      (Edit: and while I'm at it, in the last section the author suggests that 'The Game Master Show' does better among women listeners -- but it actually has a lower percentage among women's listeners than it does among men. It just doesn't show up on the women's list because the women's list is a top 11, not a top 20.)
