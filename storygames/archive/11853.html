\n
\n
\n
\n\nLuke_Wheel \n\n\n
\n
\n\nMarch 2010 \n edited March 2010 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\nHook: Do you have what it takes to be a roleplaying game designer?!!?!@?!?!?!?!? Do you love games but hate having a social life?!?!?!?! Do you want to earn the adoration of five middle-class, educated white men?!?!?!?!?!?
\n
\nThe Contest: Produce a finished RPG in ONE YEAR!
\n
\nThe Requirements: During the contest period you must produce a concept and a design. You must then playtest said concept and design. Then you must write your game. Then playtest your game again. Then rewrite your game. Then playtest it again. Then edit it. Then playtest it. Then lay it out. Then playtest it. Then fix all the broken shit you missed before. Then edit it again. Then polish it and abandon it because even though it's still broken and worthless, you can't work on it any more.
\n
\nThe Deadline: You only have ONE YEAR from the day you start. Start any time, but once you kick off this contest there's no going back -- win or lose.
\n
\nThe Prize: Your prize is a complete, polished pen-and-paper, tabletop roleplaying game that you designed! In addition, you'll earn the approval and admiration of up to five white men in their 20s or 30s! Even better, your mother or girlfriend will make disapproving signs and wonder why you're wasting your time on this when you could be doing something more productive. Best of all, should you complete the contest and meet your deadline, you will be awarded a small, greasy dollop of Self-Respect that you can use to smear on yourself to ward off future bouts of Fear of Failure.
\n
\nMeet The Judges: Look in the mirror! You're the only judge! How cool is that?
\n
\nEntry Fee: All of your free time and as much money as your ambition can stomach.

      \n That's a fair opinion Luke has there, when delivered without unnecessary bitching.

      My own take on the perennial topic of game quality is that vigorous critique combined with offering access to the tools and methods necessary for creating quality are the best vanguards of continuing high standards. In other words, I rather think that bitching about bad games by name in public is effective in not only putting a rogue designer (ironic, that one) straight, but also in developing and explifying the community standards on which the critique is based. This still has nothing to do with game contests, I just don't see any connection between the two topics aside from the totally unsubstantiated idea that the people doing contests would somehow turn that same energy on long-term game development if we only found the right influence. Might as well claim that actual play reports are the work of the Devil, being as how they turn hobbyists onto creative writing instead of the good work of game design. There is just about as much connection in my eyes.

      Putting that aside, though, I'd love to see Luke put his social image on the line by coming out and listing designers and games faulty of underdevelopment. I have my own opinions, but I suspect that my judgment of quality is much more case-subjective and amenable to the postmodern publishing environment than Luke's, so it'd be interesting to see where he finds underdevelopment. Heck, I can totally tell right now that the Solar System booklet I've been selling is totally underdeveloped by traditional book industry standards: it's not a book, its planned shelf-life is just a couple of years, it was written quickly and in retrospective, it's intended to be replaced by new treatments whenever anybody gets around to writing any... that sort of project is so different from traditional notions of quality that in many ways it's smack dab in the middle of any perceived cluster of underdeveloped games.

      I do understand if nobody particularly cherishes the chance to say specifically who and what this trend of underdevelopment consists of, but to my mind it'd be an immense step forward in addressing the issue (which has been around for a while now) if a thread like this started by calling out the people responsible instead of deploring the sorry state of the hobby in general. After all, if we have this impulse of commitment that makes us care about the scene of indie game design in general, then wouldn't it be fair to address the people you're trying to manipulate directly? Seems that way to me, even if it might bruise a few egos.

      --

      It might be interesting to look at how I view this trend of underdevelopment myself, incidentally:

      To me it seems that this trend exists - as Luke says, this community produces underdeveloped games. However, I don't see this as much of a problem, for several reasons:
      • The changes in social communication have turned game design into a hobby this decade, a hobby where you can participate on many levels and in many ways. Rough draft design is the equivalent of writing fan-fiction; it's not intended to be published by a real publishing house, it's just something you do with your like-minded Internet pals. In time the best fanfic-writers will maybe go into real writing, and that is good. But accusing fanfic of reducing people's commitment to real writing work seems ludicrous to me; it's just the nature of the culture today that people not only consume, but also create, often in rather amateurish and fickle ways.
      • Looking higher up the totem pole, one might argue that the real problem is not that drafts of unplayable games clog the Internet; the real problem is that this community has started producing and publishing real games intended to compare with quality product that are of a more uneven quality than they used to be in (some idealized) past. To me it seems obvious that this sort of thing has started to happen because the community has grown and the number of designers has increased, which also means that there are more first-time designers, and therefore more bumbling. One might argue that the culture supports bad design practices, and I would agree to a point - but putting the fault entirely on that isn't necessarily something I'd accept without thought and discussion. Are we seeing established and successful designers abandoning quality in favour of quick esteem-boosts from subquality game design, or are we seeing naive beginner designers making mistakes? The latter phenomenon has become more pronounced because designers (beginners especially) are better connected than before, and thus a game that would've been quickly forgotten alongside its creator ten years ago will get some short-term traction on the forums of today. This might make it look like there is more underdevelopment today, but it might be that we're just seeing the old mistakes brought to light better than before.
      • Finally, the world and our relationship to texts is changing. Game texts are no exception. Unless somebody explains to me what "underdevelopment" even means in relation to game texts, I can't begin to form an opinion on whether I find it problematic. Is it just that games are published today in different state of polish than in years past? Is that it, really? I'm asking because that seems like a pretty minor issue in this day and age: when the requirements of publishing sharply decline and the amount and type of product people buy or otherwise consume is in flux, is it really a negative thing if people experiment with different standards of quality and expectation in their roleplaying products? Of course it sucks if somebody publishes a product that looks and feels like it fulfills some certain standards against which it ultimately fails, but what about products that set new standards? Luke says that underdeveloped games suck, but even sucky things might have a fair place on the market - in music, literature, comics and even filmmaking low-quality garage operations have a niche as a place for cheap innovation, outsider art, learning and other things. How is Luke's argument against underdevelopment different from a Hollywood professional declaiming shoe-string reality television because it makes the art form of film look bad?
      \nNow, I have to say that it sucks to see a game that's not all it could be because the production team failed in some way. Misery Bubblegum is not as good as it could be, to pick an example of a product where the production process has clearly left a large blind spot that causes real trouble for the customer. I'm not saying that this sort of thing does not happen, but is that, specifically, what is meant by underdevelopment here? Dragging game design contests into it seems like underdevelopment is more than just failing in the production process, more like it's an intentional strive to create things that are sub-par in comparison to finished product. But how is the latter a bad thing? How is it a problem if I put some rules ideas on my blog and tell anybody interested to take the stuff and run with it? I understand that Luke doesn't like unpolished, unproduced design floating in the Internet, but is there some reason for that aside from just not appreciating the stuff yourself? To pick another comparison (I've been going through these, I know), isn't summarily judging against draft design culture and for concerned polish similar to judging against punk music and for professionally produced pop music? There used to be a time when punk was condemned for intentionally forgoing the publishing standards of days past. If other people find playable (or otherwise enjoyable, for that matter) material in going through a different development cycle than your own, where is the problem?

      Luke's answer to the above sorts of musing seems to be that our audience is in desperate need of mature, polished, considered design. Offering them an entryway into the underground of non-guaranteed hobby design will frustrate them and tire them out, directing them to other sources of entertainment. This might be right for a certain type of audience. Am I wrong in questioning whether everybody is interested in this audience, and would it be an unlikely proposition to think that the two phenomena can live side by side? I still think that it sucks to buy a game at Gencon and find out that it's underdeveloped, but connecting that to something like design competitions where the product is intentionally delivered in a context where it's obvious that it's not competing with traditional quality in mind - what is that about?

      (I should note that I find nothing wrong in Luke speaking up for what he likes and what he believes to be best for the community culture, even if I'd like it if he were more considerate in the way he speaks. I'm just pointing out that the seemingly universally acceptable call for more polish and disdain for unfinished, flighty design culture might be hiding assumptions and ignoring values that might be important to consider for every one of us in thinking of Luke's mission. I don't really know what I think about rough draft design culture, but if the comparison to punk music holds water, then I think that there are rather good reasons for desisting from condemning other hobbyists just because they want to do game design that doesn't account for your own standards of taste.)

As for Luke's cross-post, didn't account for it in the above - but rest assured that no Internet stupidity is intended, I'm taking this topic as fairly and seriously as I can.

      \n I think that directing your creativity towards building upon what already exists is a fine sentiment. It's partly a matter of methodology, though; game texts are used radically differently in traditional and Forge-style indie gaming, and one of the differences is that in the indie approach it's often easier to express your creative work as a new thing, a new game, even if it is largely built upon work started under some other title by some other designer. It's easy to think up things to do relating to an extensive game like D&D, while "supporting" a smaller game will become forced pretty quickly. This is especially the case for the many people who appreciate complete transparency in the goals, rules and methods used in playing a game, myself included; I simply won't be bothered by playing Call of Cthulhu anymore, no matter how hacked - I'll rather construct a new system to directly tackle whatever it is I want to accomplish rather than trying to work around the existing baseline. A certain type of relationship with the game text (very boardgame-like, as I intimated) leads you to a situation where you find it easier to learn new, clearly written game texts than the opposite process of adapting more and more layers of methodology on a single base method. Starting from the very basics every time with a critical eye, that's a big part of what makes the indie design movement powerful in design methodology.

      That being said, I don't want to be absolutist about it, I'm definitely on the side of promoting the good work and not wasting your time on being special for special's sake; it's strictly the resulting quality of play that I'm interested in. This is why I've been promoting TSoY lately, I think it's the best pick out of the field for what it does. I'll choose between creating a new game and supporting existing effort based on strictly merit.

      It is true that commercially this means fracture to some degree as old games inspire new games which inspire even never games. Reducing the effect is sure to be an interesting challenge, and I think that it's being partially overcome by the way people play more different games in shorter campaigns, buy more and in general treat roleplaying games more like boardgames, not labels you staple to your chest for the next decade. Indie gamers of today are likely to have many, many more independent game texts in their library than your average D&D gamer, but they won't have the stack of sourcebooks, adventure modules and other support material the D&D guy has - ultimately it's going to be a wash, I think, with both buying and using about the same amount of product. It's just different type of product.

      Whether the small games we see today in the indie field are likely to have lasting impact, it's difficult to say. They're not major works by any standard of cultural history, not in the sense that D&D or Paranoia or Cyberpunk 2020 or other such classics of the form are. I would perhaps compare these games to short stories: not many are going to have lasting impact on their lonesome, but when perceived at a distance we'll be able to group them together, see similarities and treat the entire phase of design as an unified work, albeit one with dozens and dozens of authors. Sort of like pulp literature in that way. The sort of large game you design for five years and support for another 20 is in comparison more like a novel.
