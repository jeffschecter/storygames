    What a great question.

    Much of comedy is about status: sudden drops in status are funny. This is why Paranoia works as a comedy, I think. The Troubleshooters build up status and, suddenly, get the carpet pulled away from under them: either the Computer robs them of status or their fellow Troubleshooters do. Best Friends is funny in a similar way.

    I'd imagine that Joshua's Baron is also a status thing. He has a very high status (he's a Baron) which keeps suddenly dropping (he does stupid things).

    So, you can engineer situations which are likely to be funny. Joshua's rushing-round-fixing-the-Baron's-mistakes is one. Paranoia's everyone-undermines-each-other is another. You could also try anything else which is likely to feature status drops: Victorian ladies on a picnic attacked by monsters, maybe. Something like that.

    And there are also character archetypes that are often used in comedy. The bumbling idiot (Joshua's Baron) is one. There's also the aristocrat, the smart-arse and the dogsbody. You see them in everything, so they'd probably work well in games.

    But, yeah, I'm not totally sure, and I'm interested to hear other takes on this.

    Graham

    One feature that seems to pop up in our comedy games here is player character backstabbing or antagonism. Since we normally play very cooperative, often heroic characters, comedy games seem to inevitably involve PvP between selfish, grumpy, conniving, cowardly, or greedy characters. Though if one player pushes it to far (and is not willing to hose his own character), that can become un-funny as well.

    This is another key feature of our humour games: they're funny when everyone is willing to make fun of their own character as well. They stop being funny when someone only wants to make fun of other characters.

    All in all, it seems to me humour games are necessarily player-driven, though the GM sets the tone. You really have to go with the flow while harnessing player energy.

    Posted By: Graham WalmsleyJack, it's interesting to note that, although you say it's the players that make the comedy in Great Ork Gods, there'sclearlysomething about that game that sets the stage for comedy.
    Yes. It lets the players make the comedy.

    The really important thing to realise is that rules aren't funny. Or at least, they can never be funny more than once. They've told their jokes and now you're bored. Players on the other hand, can be funny every time. You just need to give them a situation that fosters that.

    GOG does that in three ways:

    1. It lets the players play, and makes it clear that silliness is what is expected from them. Every time they rise to the challenge. I sincerely believe that people are inherently funny; I've never met anyone who couldn't make other people laugh. This part is more difficult I think than it seems: Paranoia and Toon are two cases in point - neither of them are quite as funny as they could be.

    2. It rewards players for stabbing each other in the back. This is important, possibly very important. At least half of all humour comes from laughing at other people in one way or another. People laugh when other people fail; this not only provides humour in itself but because people are already laughing it provides momentum for other people to laugh. Great Ork Gods lets you revel in this by pitting you one against another.

    3. It doesn't attach you to your character. This ties very closely to the last point. If you fail at something that doesn't matter to you, you can laugh with the other people laughing at you and it becomes funny to everyone; fail at something you really care about and it's not very funny at all and other people laughing at you is only going to make you angry or upset. By making characters disposable, GOG lets you laugh at yourself and laugh with others when you fail. This is vital to stopping the game going sour.

    You can't make people funny; and you can't write repeatedly funny material - jokes don't keep on being funny - you need to tap into the humour that players bring to the table and let them express it. You do this be empowering them to be funny; not by making them be funny.

    After running InSpectres today, I got thinking about comedy in roleplaying again.

    Jack is absolutely right that actual jokes in the material are at best only funny once, and the real trick is to get the players to be funny.

    This works in two ways - first, you need to enable the players to be funny, and then you need to encourage them to be funny.

    Enabling involves things like allowing liberal use of director's stance (letting players introduce funny ideas they have), which InSpectres does ably with both the Confessional mechanics and by giving narration to the player in sucessful (and even unsucessful) die rolls. This power to improvise and control narration and even the introduction of plot elements also naturally creates a highly unplanned, chaotic and thus more humorous story. I think this is also where discouraging attachment to characters can come in - it's easier to laugh at people we have no affection for, including our own characters.

    Encouraging ties chiefly into two things - a Situation (eek! Forge terms) which provides material and impetus for funny things to occur, and reward systems which directly reward the player for being funny. In addition, you can simply tell players that the idea is to be funny, but this only works so well on its own

    For Situation, it can be as basic as having zany Settings and improbable Characters that encourage players to not to take things seriously in response. More advanced constructions are things like Paranoia XP's placement of conflicting demands on the player - by giving the player multiple mutually incompatible priorities, it encourages players to tie themselves in knots trying to fulfil them all, which encourages humour. This is given extra depth by the inter-player interactions. In InSpectres, the Stress mechanics encourage the GM to pile setbacks and problems on the players, leading inevitably to (hopefully humorous) meltdown.

    in terms of reward systems, both Paranoia XP and InSpectres hit upon remarkably similar systems to directly encourage the roleplaying of silly/humorous characteristics (Ticks in Paranoia XP, which earn you Perversity points, and the playing of Traits applied by other players through Confessionals in InSpectres, which earns you Franchise dice).

    There's a lot more to analyse in both games - the Perversity system in Paranoia XP in particular is interesting and worth of analysis.

    Things that kill comedy include slow pacing, including having to look up rules or any rules with long search and/or handling times. In addition, too much investment in the outcome can kill comedy - you have to have your players in a state where they are willing (and glad) to accept failure. Failure is much more amusing than success.

    Hey Iain, you're emulating Shaun of the Dead, right?

    Survival instinct interferes with furthering relationship agenda = funny. Conversely, relationship agenda overriding Survival instinct = funny. Competing relationship agendas amid unspeakable horror = funny. Group survival instinct overriding collective relationship agendas = funny and awesome. You need to put extreme, ridiculous stress on the dichotomy and it'll be comedy gold.

    Maybe you rank the other player characters in order of intimacy and worth to you (giving you some base-line in interaction perhaps), and author an issue/agenda with each. Invoking the issue gives you some sort of mojo.

    Ignoring the issue is the only way to be effective on the survival end of things. But if you don't invoke it regularly, the relationship becomes less important and less mechanically useful, and without strong relationships, you become weak and less determined to survive. So it's a dubious, no-win infuriating situation, with people you either love or greatly dislike, and zombies. Seriously, set that up right and you don't need anything else for it to be hilarious.
