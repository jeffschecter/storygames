\n So over in the MOVIES thread "Children of Men" keeps coming up, so let's talk about it a bit here.

In the main, I liked it. I didn't love it -- some parts bugged me more than others -- but mainly, yep, good movie.

In the broadest sense, I'm not sure I really agree with the underlying premise of the movie: People can't have kids and they FREAK THE HELL OUT. I mean, yeah, it's the conceit of the movie, so I'm going to just give it to them. But in my heart, I don't think it would play out like that.

I think it would play out like this: PARRRR-TAAYYYY! There's no rainy day futures to save up for, so let's screw the planet as hard and fast as we can before we burn out. Let's dine upon condor eggs while we nuke Everest just for the view. But that's just my take on it.

I liked the way the government was portrayed. I feel like they started off with a question: What would it take to make a crazy militarized hardcore government response justified? How about the end of the world and everyone freaking the hell out? Yeah, that works for me.

I kinda wonder what the movie would be like if it was set farther out in the timeline. Like, what if the youngest known person was 50, and most of the population in their 70s? It's not the movie they made, and I'm okay with that, but still I wonder.

The only part I really especially didn't like was the really cliche apocalyptic thing of, alright guys, here we are just driving down a country road in the middle of nowhere, and then suddenly HOLY CRAP all these pissed-off guys are just jumping out of the trees and attacking us for some reason. I mean, what are they doing out there? Having a picnic? Why are they so pissed off for some reason? What the heck is their deal? And what are they going to do after the car drives off? Go back to their picnic?

Is there ever a time when beating the hell out of someone with a car battery is not awesome? I do not believe so.

As far as stealing lessons for RPGs from it, I think there's some to be had here. It does a good job of telling the most interesting story that exists in the fiction. It does a fine job of keeping NPCs as NPCs (by which I mean: the payload of the Escort Mission of the movie is just cargo.) It handles a basic Mission structure (or Quest, if that's better) without feeling too terribly railroady.


What does everyone else think of it?

    \n I dug the hell out of Children of Men, but I'm not quite clear what we're arguing about here.

    For me, it's a wonderfully assembled film that raises a lot of questions, but offers relatively few answers. I've no problem with that. It's building a world as much or more than it's building its story, and I found that compelling in its way. I mean, its quest-story is pretty complete and effective, but its world-building is really wonderful. It does a lot of showing, a lot of implying, a lot of evoking \u2014\xa0and while I can agree that the conceit that the world becomes ultra-violent without kids is an assertion that's open for debate, I don't think the film has any need to explore the alternative. It's making an assertion \u2014\xa0that the lack of a future for humanity can make people violent and unruly, just as a lack of individual futures (be they economic, political, or other) can do the same \u2014 and building on that. It's a fair argument, I think.

    This film is a major influence on my forthcoming apocalyptic game, Razed, in which the apocalypse is here, it's just not evenly distributed. I've watched it a lot lately and may just go watch it again, now.

    Cheers,
    Will

    \n I'm with Will and also Tulpa. It's an excellent film, with no easy answers. Luke points out some flaws in the film, which are important, although I don't think they're fatal.

    \n We don't know what happens and there is no implication he is even going to die until we are shown his wound.

    When evaluating media it's important to focus what is actually in the text. While this runs against the grain of Althussarian 'symptomatic' readings of texts, I think it's fair to say that speculation on the absences and gaps in a text is just that, speculation. Often, this kind of speculation is of the variety that is irreflexive. If you look at what the text is not, you can find whatever it is that you are looking for because you are no longer looking at the text itself.

    The world in Children of Men is not saved. Humanity might be, after a fashion, but only if they shed that which is hegemonic: patriarchy, state terror, Middle Class White Guys, the choosing of security over liberty, etc. Only Kee makes it to the boat, as Caron himself says: "The fact that this child will be the child of an African woman has to do with the fact that humanity started in Africa. We're putting the future of humanity in the hands of the dispossessed and creating a new humanity to spring out of that." Clive Owen character is necessarily a middle class white guy, it is he occupies a liminal structural position, neither dispossessed nor ruling elite. Thus, he acts as the vessel and threshold by which Claire-Hope Ashitey's Kee is able to cross over. He is able to move (and move Kee) between the two worlds of the hegemonic and the subaltern. Still, he has no place in Caron's utopia for the same reasons. He is a narrative reagent that get used up.
