\n Generally games feel pretty open. I feel like my general options for what my character can do are fairly wide, and not particularly constrained. This is usually a good thing. But not always.
Does anyone have any suggestions for how to create a feeling of restrictiveness in a game? A sense that there isn't a lot that one can do, that one's hands are tied and the choices are limited?
Does anyone know of any games that already exist that feel particularly restrictive? If so, why do they feel that way? Is it a good thing or a bad thing?

    \n Hmmm, yes. It's easy enough, as the GM, to make things restrictive by saying, "No, you can't do that" but that sounds cheap and lame. I suppose the other route is to have a very specific list of the things that are allowed, and everything that isn't on the list is forbidden.

    I'd like to be clear: the restrictiveness isn't supposed to be fun. It's supposed to be lame and crappy and annoying. The players should be thinking, "This sucks! I want to have more options!"

    Of course, obviously, that sucks. The idea is then, after sucking for a brief time, certain things in the game will start to change, mechanically, that will open up more and more options. But unless it feels restrictive to begin with, I don't think the building freedom will be significant.

    \n I"m reminded of Ralph's Robots and Rapiers (if he'd only finish it). I have no idea what the state of the game currently is but the idea was that you are playing robots on a long forgotten amusement park planet designed to function like a swashbuckling adventure. Over the eons some of the robots have started to develop an awareness of the fact that they're just machines following a program and slowly gain free will.

    So the idea was that early on the GM was intended to create a very linear, pre-plotted swashbuckling scenarios. If you did something that wouldn't follow the plot of the scenario the GM could literally just say, "No, your programming takes over, you do this instead." The thing of it is that there's a mechanic representing how free-willed your robot had become. So if you didn't want to do what the GM told you to do you could roll. Early on you'd fail a lot and would more or less live out the GM's adventure, but the idea is that as the game goes on you'll start succeeding in those rolls more and more and more until you don't have to follow the adventure any more.

    But now you know you're robot on a forgotten world that's running in a endless pre-programmed loop.... What do you do?

The main advantage there is that the GM gets to go, "Oh no see, it's not that I'm not letting you do that, it's right there in the rulebook."

    \n I'm only pimping Verge because I just rewrote it and it's fresh in my mind. What I am describing below isn't even in the current version of my game, because it was too constraining.

    In prior versions of this game, you started a scene by figuring out what you wanted your character to do and how that changed the network (the giant statted relationship map that the game uses as the central vehicle of play). There was a lot of game business going on, right there on the network. Players moved dice around that network like it was a RISK board. Now, because the network was so much in focus during the game, it became the game and the role-playing filled in the cracks.

    As a result, play became very restricted. You sorta had to think about what you wanted to do. The RISK-ish elements of the game meant that you couldn't interact with every node of the network in a meaningful (mechanical) way; only the nodes adjacent to other nodes you controlled. Players naturally ignored the parts of the network "far" from their character. By increasing the number and complexity of mechanical points of contact, and limiting mechanical effects to a local section of the fiction (as defined on the network), the rules naturally limited the number of choices a player had and constrained them in a pretty amazing way. It was cool (because players didn't have to pay attention to the whole, complex network all at once) but it was also constraining role-play in a way that I didn't like. Ultimately, I nixed that element.

    \n Another game you should check out is Waiting/Tea. It's a small game written by Jonathan Walton in PUSH 1.

    The game is inspired by simple Interactive Fiction games. It stars two characters. One is the daughter of a lady who starts play at the gates of a temple. She is waiting for a proper invitation inside. The other character is a young monk who has been told by his master in the temple to make tea. Making tea requires him to fetch water, which is outside the temple. He needs to find a bucket for water.

    In Waiting/Tea, you have a small number of actions you can take. This list changes in each map area that you visit. You're always free to talk. So in the room with the bucket, you gain a "get bucket" option. Once you have the bucket, you lose the "get bucket" option but gain a "drop bucket" and "give bucket to " option. You get the idea. It feels like a small version of an old Infocom game like Zork.

    The game definitely constrains the players.

    \n Another very common way to restrict players is to make their characters whiff a lot. Seriously. This means that what you can try is not restricted, but what you can accomplish is. D&D is great at this -- especially versions pre-4e. You start out a low-level schlub and you fight kobolds and other really weak little creatures. You know you have no chance of fighting anything worthwhile. If a 15th level devil shows up, you run like hell (ha!) or maybe even die without a chance of escape.

    Being wimpy restricts what you can do. At 1st level in D&D, you don't know a lot of spells, you don't have good feats for fighting, and your arsenal is free of useful magic items. Then the DM dangles an opportunity for XP and treasure in front of you, and you reach for it, because you know it will make you stronger and less whiffy.

    \n Jason's "restriction through description" tool is something I've been working on too, as well as being evident in things like IAWA-style Oracles and the like. I imagine we're going to see a lot more of that type of thing soon. I wrote a game for a contest Jason ran in which monsters were defined purely by some words and a number. So the description reads, like:\n
    Kayongo is a plague spirit \u201cderived from an ancestor who was a diviner and wishes a living relative to continue his profession.\u201d Twisted by dark science, Kayongo now blasts visions of possible futures into the minds of all those who encounter it, often involving their deaths or the destruction of things they care about.

    &#149; \u201cMy Own Future is Dark\u201d x 2
    &#149; \u201cI Will Live to See the Ruination of All I Know\u201d x 2
    \nIn play, then, besting Kayongo requires describing at least 4 distinct things happening, inspired by the plague spirit's traits (two traits, each with a power of 2). For example, I could describe my character receiving a vision of the future in which he returned home to find his entire family gone and his village burned to the ground, expending one point of "I Will Live to See" (leaving 3 total points of traits left for the monster). In that game, monsters are not killed so much as endured, which is an interesting model, I think, for monsters that act less like D&D beasties and more like ghosts and other things that fuck with your mind and emotions. The monsters don't have an impact based on the dice they throw but on how they change the ways in which players describe things happening.

    I keep hoping we'll see mechanics more like that in fully fleshed-out and published games, because I may or may not get around to doing it myself.

    \n Another way to do would be to allow restrictions to generate in play. A lot of "trad" games do this already, at least the way they're run by some people.

    You start with all these options. When you try one, you roll to see if you can succeed. It's like there's a bunch of doors in a room, and you can try to open any one you want.

    If you roll and fail, though, that avenue, that door, is forever closed to you. You can never open that door again, you will never be accepted by that group of people again, you will always be defeated by that rival, etc.

    And so, the claustrophobic sense of helplessness as your options narrow can grow, especially if the odds of success are really low.

    Your "breakout" mechanics could be as simple as some way to reroll or retry failed contests.

    \n I've been looking over these things, and I can't help but think, what about social/peer pressure?

    No one is truly forced to do what their peers suggest. There are certainly consequences for it, but it feels a lot more restrictive than it is, even keeping the consequences in mind. That's because being social is such a strong force in human psychology.

    With that in mind, having a good sense of incentives and punishments are a way to push without disallowing other options. Of course, incentives and punishments can be risen above. It's probably hard to bribe Bill Gates with a hundred bucks, but there are plenty of people on the street who would do a little something for that much cash. At the same time, someone who has accumulated power or resources may be able to avoid or not be bothered by punishments. For example, with peer pressure, the risk of not fitting in is overcome by developing relationships with multiple social groups, something that could be played out over time in a game.
