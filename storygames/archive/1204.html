    That's a fair description from the designer's standpoint, Jason, but I think in the usage of players describing a game, "broken" describes a more specific thing. I'm going to try and describe it by CCG-analogy and example, and then wave a stick at a possible definition.

    In Magic: the Gathering, when the Artifacts Cycle block (this name is telling) was current, there was a card called Tolarian Academy which gave you blue resources based on the number of Artifact-type cards you had. In the same environment there were a lot of Artifacts with extremely low resource costs, and Artifact generators, and a number of variable-cost blue spells whose effectiveness increased with the resources put into them.

    Tolarian Academy was broken in this environment. In constructed play (one in which one brings a prepared deck of cards to a tournament, rather than purchasing new cards at tournament and playing with a deck made from a subset of those), it divided the decks present into (mostly) Tolarian Academy decks and decks specifically built to resist Tolarian Academy.

    This means two things - the Academy focused play around itself undesirably, and it sharply divided play based on its presence: if you were playing to win, you had to take account of it, while if you were playing in order to display an interestingly-built deck or simply enjoy the challenge of opposing multiple different opponents, you simply didn't really get to play; you'd be eliminated too quickly.

    That's broken, from a player's standpoint (or, from my standpoint) - disruptive to the normal or expected state of play.

    A broken game usually falls under one or more of these defining traits:

    1) The game doesn't provide the experience it promises.
    2) The game is not functional* without drift on the part of the group (add/ignore/change rules).
    3) Only one or two viable input strategies work, the rest are meaningless (see Shreyas' example)

    People also point to specific mechanics or subsystems as broken, which suffer in a similar fashion on a smaller scale, without necessarily breaking the whole game.

    *"functional", being here, the group playing is able to come to consensus about "what happens" (Lumpley Principle) without having to guess it, bullshit, or flail about.

    I think a game is broken when it contains so many broken mechanics or broken mechanics of such importance that it ceases to be playable as written. I think that's a high test.

    I think a mechanic is broken when it does not work as intended, not in a small way but fundamentally does not work.

    Concrete example. In Orkworld there are rules for winter hunting. If you apply the rules as written, the clan dies out due to lack of food. The mechanic is broken because it is supposed to govern how the clan gets food over the winter but the numbers don't add up and the clan always starves. You cannot use the mechanic as written, it has to be houseruled or ignored.

    Is Orkworld broken? IMO no. The mechanic is a one off and isn't that critical to the game.

    If, however, you have a supposedly gritty mercenaries game but your damage mechanic means that people can soak up .50 cal bullets without a problem, the game is broken because that is a key mechanic and directly undermines the playability of the game. That incidentally is drawn from an actual play experience I had, where we abandoned the game as following the rules led to situations of such absurdity that we had either to make up new rules or play something else.

    The term gets used way too loosely. I think mechanics, and games, can be broken but usually I think it is used simply to denote something that does not work as someone would wish it to. IMO something is only broken if it actively interferes in actual play with achieving the outcome it is intended to produce.

    To add, one thing that definitely is not broken, but which is a common use of the term is where a mechanic has an outcome that is not in line with one's preferences.

    Example time. I don't personally like Extreme Vengeance much, it supports a style of play that has little interest to me and generally there just isn't much in the game that does much for me.

    And that's fine, not everyone will like every game. Others love it and that's fine too.

    But, a really common mistake is for someone who has preferences like mine to say "EV is broken, the results it generates in play just aren't realistic". Well, that might just be because it's not about realism.

    I like verisimilitude. I like stuff to feel realistic. That a mechanic does not generate an outcome that feels realistic may well mean that that game will not work for me, but it does not make it broken. Confusing that leads I think to the most common misuse of the term, this game does not do what I want it to therefore it is broken is a non-sequitur.
  • PaulCzege
    August 2006
    If you, as a designer, know your game isn't good, then I would personally encourage you not to sell it as if it is. And harsh as this sounds, it's not important to me as your colleague, or me as your potential customer that the game is as good as you've been able to make it, or that it contains good elements, or that you just needed to move on. If the hobby is to overcome this past decade's slide into beautiful unplayability we need two things:

    1. well designed games that are fun to play
    2. a relatively reliable mechanism that helps gamers identify well designed games that are fun to play

    And that's it. And the thing is, what we currently have for #2 is pretty fragile. It's all word of mouth and reputation by association. Selling a game that isn't good as if it is places a stress on #2 that we're currently not managing all that well. And the thing is, there's absolutely no reason to put yourself in the position of doing it but creative denial (which isn't good for you as a developing designer) or personal pride. If your game isn't good, but it's the best you've been able to do, go to the community for help making it good. You do a disservice to your own critical design faculties by trying to convince yourself to put it up for sale as if it's good before you know you've made it good, and to your reputation as a designer and credibility with customers by actually doing so. There's no reason you have to put it up for sale before you think it's well designed. And there's no reason you can't move on to new projects without ever putting it up for sale. I have done so myself on more than one project in which I had invested considerable design time and for which I had relied upon the time and energy of close friends for playtesting and feedback. Did I feel guilty for having used the time and energy of my friends with ultimately nothing tangible to show for it? Yes. A great deal, in fact. But did they begrudge me for that? No. Because they know this isn't a "for sale" competition. And because they knew I'd benefited artistically from the project.

    When you put a game up for sale the implied proposition is that it's well designed and fun to play. If you know that's not true then the only way to honestly put it up for sale is to somehow directly address the assumed proposition by asserting the actual reasons someone might buy it (e.g. cleverness, historical or scholarly significance, utility relative to some other game, etc).

    Paul

    I think that everyone is agreeing that broken is often used like railroading is in RPGs, to simply mean something that makes the game unplayable for the player in question. Yes, its loaded, because when you say something is broken, you mean for people to take it as meaning unplayable for everyone. So you're trying to say here that the thing is so bad for you, that it must be bad for everyone.

    IOW, this use is rarely actually correct.

    The boardgamers have a higher standard, more like Max's where the game has to be actually unplayable by the rules to produce the effect intended. Some mechanic simply doesn't do what it's supposed to. It's not as hard a line to discover as it might seem at first. Even when the output desired is aesthetic.

    If a mechanic tends to produce only one strategem, then the game isn't broken, techically, but unbalanced. Tolarian Academy is unbalancing, but you can still play the game. If, in fact, there is but one strategy that everyone uses, as in the game tic-tac-toe, then the game is a "known" game.

    Broken is unimportant for purposes of whether or not to sell the game. That is, a game should neither be known,broken, unbalanced, or otherwise seriously flawed. So even if the problem doesn't in a technical sense indicate that the game is broken, that's not the guidline for publishing. "Hey, I fixed the game, it's not broken any more!" shouldn't be the cry of the designer who thinks he's finished with the product. Like Paul says, it should have been playtested, and it should have been fun in play for almost all of the time played.

    No, it doesn't have to be perfect, no game is. If there are some small, localized, hitches in play, the game may be good enough for publication. The thing to look out for are problems that tend to repeat in playtest, or look like they will repeat. Once you've gotten to the point that all of the problems can be attributed to the fact that it's humans playing, and not to a systemic problem in the rules, then you know you're ready.

    Mike

    Yep, that's what broken is about.

    Just to give a concrete example of what broken really means, here's a broken resolution mechanic.

    Difficulty TN = 13 to 20, as determined by GM
    Player rolls 1d6, and adds his skill rating to see if he exceeds TN.
    Max skill rating is 6.

    If, in fact, the goal of the mechanic is to always produce failure, then the mechanic is not broken, but can be said to be grossly inelegant. Because you could simply say that all attempts to do anything fail as your mechanic, and avoid the search and handling time.

    There's another term to add to the list, inelegant. I think that the names for problems are pretty complete:
    - Broken - mechanic can't do what it intends. Doesn't just produce aethetically questionable results, but simply doesn't produce the set of results it intends to produce.
    - Unbalanced - most or all players will select from a relatively narrow range of options, because others simply don't provide the same level of benefit
    - Known - like unbalanced, except that there is a known winning selection, meaning that all players will choose this option
    - Inelegant - the mechanic tends to take too much work (seach and handling, calculation, etc) for the benefit produced
    - Kludgy - the mechanic is cobbled together from existing elements to fill a gap, instead of there being a stand-alone mechanic designed specifically to deal with the need well (a kludge often refers to a rule created post publication to fill such a gap in the rules - as such, a kludge found in a published game is way ugly)
    - Incoherent - mechanic tends to produce play of an agenda that conflicts with the overall intended agenda
    - Ugly - mechanic produces aesthetically undesirable results, or does so in an aesthetically unpleasant way
    - Crock - a crock is a "handwavey" sort of rule that produces the desired range of output but in some other non-ideal way. For instance, having all weapons do 1d6 damage in BD&D was a crock.

    One I created:
    - Incestuous - a feedback mechanism that's too close between rewarded act, and what the reward promotes. Often means imbalance, but other problems exist, too. For instance, giving a "Win a Contest" point every time you win a contest is broken, because no contest will ever be lost after you win one (or ever, if you start with one or more of these points).

    I'm sure there's others that I'm forgetting. I also think we could check at boargamegeek.com for other designer terms like this.

For example, if you go to Boargamegeek.com, and use broken, you'll get criticisms based on this definition. So people will challenge you saying that perhaps the mechanic in question is "nearly broken."

    That said, the geek hasn't achieved anything near a consensus on the exact meaning of these terms, so even there you'll find variable use.

    The point is that you should either establish the jargon meanings of the terms, or skip using them altogether, and use the longhand for them.

    So, yeah, outside the Forge, here, and similar, I wouldn't use Incoherent. But used at the Forge or here, I think it's largely a good tool. If you don't believe in the concept of incoherence, then don't use the term to describe anything. If you find a text to be incoherent in the sense that it's not understandable, then use another term. Because, like it or not, if you use Incoherent here, you'll get people thinking that it's a problem with the game's creative agenda. Just as if you use the term railroading, you won't have people envisioning players on trains.
