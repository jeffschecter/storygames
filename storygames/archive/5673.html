\n I've been working on a character sheet for a game where players take the role of larger-than-life denizens of Gold Rush-era San Francisco. They come from disparate backgrounds and have questionable skills. But they are all looking for a way out of the Barbary Coast. I want the character generation to feel like answering a series of questions and bring in as much feel and slang as possible. I'm looking for the relatively open-ended feel of a game like Dust Devils or Contenders, where anything is possible given the constraints of the genre. It's also important for the characters to have direct links to other PCs and NPCs to make each scene matter. The Barbary Coast was a lot of people in what was then a small city, and I want the feel of "everybody knows everybody".

Take a look at the sample character below and tell me what else you want to know about this character. I can work just about anything into the mechanics, but I would like some suggestions to help make these characters come alive!

--

What\u2019s your name?

\u2022\tKelly O\u2019Malley

What do people call you?

\u2022\tShanghai Kelly

What are your marks?

\u2022\tFists: 4
\u2022\tWits: 2
\u2022\tGuts: 4
\u2022\tCharms: 3

Where do you come from?

\u2022\tIreland

What do you do?

\u2022\tFight

Who do you know?

\u2022\tChin Poy, leader of the Sue Yop Tong
\u2022\tBig Bertha, the Madam of Montgomery Street
\u2022\tSamuel Tetlow, local politician on the take

Where is your hangout?

\u2022\tThe Loose Rafters, a bar off Portsmouth Square

How are you going to get out?

\u2022\tMake enough money to buy a ship and sail to Vancouver.

    Gold (use green or gold colored tokens if you have them)
    -For opportunities
    -Use Gold to get ahead in a conflict

    -Cause the character to sink deeper into depravity
    -Get rid of Gutter points by sacrificing for an ideal.

    \n If you want to stress the nebulous nature of those relationships, you might want to consider putting that into char-gen.

    For each person you know:

    "What do they owe you?"
    "What do you owe them?"

    or another way:

    "Why do you know them?"
    "Why do they know you?"

    or even just:

    "What do they want?"

    which can be used to drive whether the character is supporting or opposing the player's character.

    I want to call Gold "Glitter" so that I can have "Glitter vs. Gutter." I like how you're trying to make them conflicted stats, where high values and low values have different advantages. I'm thinking something like Poison'd where it's Glitter vs. Gutter for above-the-board tasks, and Gutter vs. Glitter for underhanded stuff.

    So you've got player-initiated questions like "why are you broke?" that are really cool, but can't be quantified - it's a spontaneous offer. What if the whole thing was set up that way?

    Everybody starts out in a holding cell after getting a good old fashioned oak towel rub-down from the bulls, maybe after a raid on some brum cribs or something. And the entire character generation occurs in character, as these yeggs get to know each other.

    PLAYER A: What's you name?

    PLAYER B: O'Malley.

    PLAYER A: Kelly O'Malley?

    PLAYER B: Yeah, what's it to you?

    PLAYER C: I heard of you. You're a friend of Big Bertha, aren't you?

    PLAYER A: Friend may be a stretch, but I been in her crib. We're mates, you might say.

    PLAYER B: Oh man, there's a guy up in Portsmouth Square who'd like to talk to you.

    PLAYER A: That'd be Samuel Tetlow.

    PLAYER B: The very same. Says you owe him a tidy sum. Says he's going to kill you. What's your game, O'Malley?

    PLAYER A: I just want out of here. I'm angling to buy a ship, head north...

    So you'd need a way to take notes, establish facts, maybe have limits or a way to know when you were done.

    Thinking with my practical-at-the-table hat on, I wonder whether it might need a _little_ more structure. Certainly the GM and perhaps one or more of the players would need to be taking notes. And the players would need to feel comfortable and empowered enough that the things they said in a conversation would be getting turned into "law" on their character sheet.     Perhaps the GM could be a good facilitator here to ask questions and start conversations.

    \n You could still have questions that need to be answered, but disengage the answers from character ownership. So if, in conversation, I say your character O'Malley has gambling debts, that becomes part of your guy's backstory, and you add it somewhere - maybe under "Why do I want to get out?" or maybe under "Who is my enemy?"

    Under this scheme, the scene would continue until everybody had a fleshed-out character in front of them. Maybe there are five questions and everybody answers four. Personally I wouldn't worry about ownership issues at all, but if it is a concern, add some kind of veto: "No, you heard wrong, buddy. It's Kowalski who owes me." You'd want to limit this because, ultimately, it's lame.
