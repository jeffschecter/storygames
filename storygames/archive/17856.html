\n
\n
\n
\n\nRob_Alexander \n\n\n
\n
\n\nJanuary 2013 \n edited January 2013 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n Out for a walk in a perilous wasteland, you find a wizard trapped in a burning pit of fire. You rescue him, and he offers you a boon. He will summon into being a fantasy rpg. Not just any fantasy rpg, but the fantasy rpg. The fantasy rpg that haunts the edges of the your dreams. The ideal fantasy rpg for you. This heartbreaker will not break your heart; it will make it sing with joy.

You can have anything you want. A Burning Wheel .com that advocates and teaches the one one right way to play. A Pathfinder Society that promotes the game and organizes play. A cover painting of Andy K. curled up asleep in a woolen sweater, a faint smile playing on his lips.

But there is a cost. Many fantasy rpgs will be consumed by his spell - they will cease to exist, they will never have been, and no-one will be able to play them again. The wizard doesn't know which ones, but casualties could be D&D, Warhammer, Burning Wheel, HeroQuest... any fantasy rpg at all. If your dream game cannot can replace many old games, you will inflict great misery on the world. With great power comes great responsibility. Choose wisely.

What do you ask him for? Tell us the big picture, or one detail, or one pattern. Put words to your most heartfelt desires!

    \n Hmmm.

    What I've been calling in my head "kid in a candy store" character creation: Think D&D 3.5's prestige classes and feats, Burning Wheel's lifepaths and traits, Dungeon World's playbooks and moves, Exalted's splats and charms: a whole mess of options, each of which screams "play me! plaaaay meeeeeeeee!" But, at the same time, some 'pregen' options... or maybe like Capes, dual-part pregens where you pick two things and combine them, like "Exiled" and "Knight" or "Grizzled" and "Wizard", and you're good to play? A setting that's a mixture of broad, impressionistic strokes and small specific colorful details, with enough material to work with for people who aren't feeling creative that day but enough blank spaces to work with for people who are. A setting that gives you as many questions as it does answers, full of unexplored horizons and great mysteries. Random tables. No, more random tables than that. Still more. A few.... okay, now you're good. Err slightly on the side of epic, gonzo, and over the top, but only slightly. Keep a straight face, though.

    And you learn real magic when you hit level 8.

    \n Clearly the Perfect Fantasy RPG is just a helmet that transforms the wearer into the Perfect Fantasy GM.

    Failing that, I'd want:
    - easy generation of fun adventure situations
    - easy generation of plot threads to glue the adventure situations together
    - easy tools for the GM to take whatever they come up with and turn it into fun adventure situations
    - an excellent fantasy world created down to the last detail, with a digital interface to browse at whatever level of detail you'd like
    - a quick method for updating the world so everyone who ever plays it can hear of you and your deeds
    - a giant trove of secret useful facts, patterns, and metaphysics
    - a simple method for getting those into play
    - instructions specifically for playing first sessions, conventions with strangers, one-shots, short campaigns and long campaigns

    and then, as no other games exist, I'd also want:
    - customizable char-gen & advancement, allowing crunch or no crunch
    - customizable levels of lethality across subsystems, including combat, traps, starvation, drowning & falling
    - optional resolution methods with and without physical cues like die rolls
    - parts of the setting which accommodate any type of adventure fiction, from political intrigue to gladiator bouts to unearthing ancient ruins

    If all that having my cake and eating it too defeats the purpose of the question, and I'm just supposed to pick what I personally like best, then the answer is my game Delve, but, y'know, finished. Start as nobodies, rise up through adventuring, magic is a big deal, learning secrets is a big deal, monsters are unique and scary, power comes with costs and risks, play methods prioritize "being there" immersion, internal causality rules, players work together, players can pick between quests or invent their own. That's most of what I like in fantasy roleplaying. Being a badass lightning-calling god-killer is fun too, but I'd survive without it.

    * does not focus too tightly on one specific situation or setting (all of the games mentioned are fine in this regard)

      Simple for teens to learn even if the do not have any RPG playing older friends to learn from.

      Given the premise to only have one game left, I\u2019d mix these three up in some good proportion. Perhaps humans from our 2013 planet earth can travel to the fantasy realm, as can people from other realms. (Think \u201cNarnia wardrobe\u201d.)

      Everway already had a device for this, but it stipulated that every accessible realm must be a pre-gunpowder fantasy realm. Which was a fine stipulation to make, but if it\u2019s the last game on earth\u2026

      \n Wouldn't want other people's fun to disappear just so I could have my fun... But the ideal fantasy game for me is pretty much the one I already designed, a game that:\n
      • Uses a moderate number of ten-sided dice for most rolls with a roll-high mechanic
        • \n
      • Encourages interesting actions and generates evocative detail naturally through the mechanics of the game
        • \n
      • Is 'realistic', in the 1980s sense of the term: relatively gritty, with deadly combat and a good (though not perfect) degree of historical feel
        • \n
      • Is immersive, helping the players get into their roles and helping the GM breathe life into the gameworld
        • \n
      • Approximates an early medieval level of technology and social structure, but isn't sexist or racist
        • \n
      • Assumes player characters are adventurous \u2014 meaning the system can't be so gritty and dangerous that no one would want to do anything interesting
        • \n
      • Tends towards more complete than less, with pre-existing rules to cover most common game situations
        • \n
      • Gives players, as well as GMs, some control over the narrative
        • \n
      • Presents magic that is mysterious, somewhat unpredictable and non-formulaic
        • \n
      • Allows anyone to learn any skill, but still has room for innate talent
        • \n
      • Doesn't include a pre-designed game world. (There are already many compatible game worlds on the market, and I didn't want to tie this game system too strongly to any particular world.)
        • \n
      That game is, of course, Blade & Crown.

      \n I've been reading my answer to this last year\n
      I'd kick the wizard back into the pit. I'm having enough fun making my own ideal fantasy rpg, many thanks.

      But comments here are way more helpful than whatever the old geezer could do. Lifepaths? checked. Burning wheel for character and setting? checked. Rulelite core? checked. Random tables ad infinitum? checked. Random tables that can be consulted without interrupting play too much? checked. Relationship tree with fronts included? checked. Stuff to ignite your creativity but space to use your own? checked. Emotional matrix? yeah, it's included in the random tables, silly. Mc guffin generator? hmm, it could be. Translation in english? sorry, that'll have to wait until I finish the damned chimaera. Moves? well, I'm adding a bit but not exactly hacking that much of AW here. A crunch that produces fluff on play? well, that's the whole point!

      Yes, I'm defintely kicking the wizard back into the pit.
      \nAnd this\n
      Clearly the Perfect Fantasy RPG is just a helmet that transforms the wearer into the Perfect Fantasy GM.

      Failing that, I'd want:
      - easy generation of fun adventure situations
      - easy generation of plot threads to glue the adventure situations together
      - easy tools for the GM to take whatever they come up with and turn it into fun adventure situations
      - an excellent fantasy world created down to the last detail, with a digital interface to browse at whatever level of detail you'd like
      - a quick method for updating the world so everyone who ever plays it can hear of you and your deeds
      - a giant trove of secret useful facts, patterns, and metaphysics
      - a simple method for getting those into play
      - instructions specifically for playing first sessions, conventions with strangers, one-shots, short campaigns and long campaigns

      and then, as no other games exist, I'd also want:
      - customizable char-gen & advancement, allowing crunch or no crunch
      - customizable levels of lethality across subsystems, including combat, traps, starvation, drowning & falling
      - optional resolution methods with and without physical cues like die rolls
      - parts of the setting which accommodate any type of adventure fiction, from political intrigue to gladiator bouts to unearthing ancient ruins

      If all that having my cake and eating it too defeats the purpose of the question, and I'm just supposed to pick what I personally like best, then the answer is my game Delve, but, y'know, finished. Start as nobodies, rise up through adventuring, magic is a big deal, learning secrets is a big deal, monsters are unique and scary, power comes with costs and risks, play methods prioritize "being there" immersion, internal causality rules, players work together, players can pick between quests or invent their own. That's most of what I like in fantasy roleplaying. Being a badass lightning-calling god-killer is fun too, but I'd survive without it.
      \n
      And after trying my old idea I'm giving David's idea a go. I'm using a lot of the lessons learned with my previous design, some of David's design goals, sone ideas from a pc game called Realm of the Mad God and as much of Whitehack as I could find.
      -I'm halfway of making a deck to generate both hexcrawls and dungeons, another to make NPCs, one to make monsters, one for towns and one for magic. the material generation part is covered.
      -Instead of balancing classes and races, I'm using RotMG approach: you start with 3 of the simpler classes and one race available, and unlock the rest little by little. The game is supposed to be lethal, so each time your character dies you get new options for your next character. The longer it survived, the more new options you get.
      -Not all PCs need to die; players can retire them so they become NPCs that they can still handle if the GM agrees. Like, your old fighter can become a bartender that introduces your new character to your old friends.
      -Setting is supposed to be emergent. Players will certainly add new facts of it through their actions and assumptions about things they have seen. The GM just has to write this down, embrace it and build upon it. The world map is built as players explore it, monsters and people meet can be wrote down on the adventurer's diary, to later become "popular knowledge" that future characters can know and that GM can reuse. The same can go for magic, which can be totally unknown at the beginning.
