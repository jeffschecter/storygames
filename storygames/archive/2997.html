    \n I think it is important to provide some context without going into a stupid play by play mode. I usually try to add some relevant details that illustrate things that were important about the session, and these will always vary. Typically they are metagame things, people's choices, how the system worked, powerful moments, whatever. Stuff that is reflective and useful to a reader considering playing the game, highlighting stuff that worked (whether social or systemic) as well as stuff that didn't. I think the very very best AP reports are really AP dialogues, where players and GM discuss what happened over multiple posts. So I also try to open it up to conversation.

    \n From my perspective (long-time dissatisfied traditional gamer, cluelessly enthusiastic indie gamer), what I'd really like to see in Actual Play posts is... actual play.

    When I read an AP that says, like, "We played an awesome session of System Y last night where Bob's character ended up blowing up the planet, but overcame his fear of commitment in the process," that's cool and a lot of fun to read, but it doesn't really help me much* when I'm trying to pick a system to check out and pitch to my limited pool of potential players.

    What I'm interested in is how System Y enabled that sequence of events to come to pass. I want to see the mechanics in action. I want to see how Bob declared this goal and rolled these dice and spent that resource and then Mary jumped in and added this and this and here's what happened.

    Obviously, it's unreasonable to expect someone to type up an entire session's worth of that stuff, but a paragraph or so detailing the mechanics of one key scene can go a long way in communicating how a game works. That's why I think actual play podcasts are so awesome. Reading reviews of DitV made me go "hmmm", reading AP posts of DitV made me go "OK, that sounds pretty cool." It wasn't until I listened to Fred Wolke's DitV AP podcasts that I realized that I must obtain and play this game at any cost.

    I do realize, of course, that selling me on a particular game system isn't necessarily what AP posts are for. Sometimes the purpose of an AP post is just to go, "We played this game the other day and this is what happened. Aren't my friends awesome!?" There's totally nothing wrong with that, and I do enjoy reading those kinds of posts. But you did mention that you wanted to review games through AP and I think that's a great use for them.



    (* Not that I think that someone posting an AP experience has any responsibility to help me with anything.)

    \n Hey Don,

    For me, a great Actual Play post centers on what you learned. Did the mechanics surprise you? Did the play experience differ from what you were expecting? Why do you think? Did you learn something about what makes a game engaging? Or about structuring collaboration? Or functional social dynamics? Did you unlearn something? How?

    Explain only what you need to about the players, mechanics, and game events to make your point.

    And, since you're blogging, end by asking a question you're honestly interested in discussing, or by expressing appreciation for some aspect of the experience.

    Paul

    And to avoid hijacking the thread let me just say that what I'd like to see in an AP--particularly in a podcast--is player perspectives interspersed with excerpts from the play session. Sort of like some wargame replays that you may have read in The General or other wargame magazines where the players first wrote down what the moves were in a partucular turn, then described why they made the moves they did. In the wargame replays, the players often kept a journal during play. So, in the replay, the reader would get to understand the perceptions of each player about what he or she thought was happening. That journal entry could be compared to the other player's entry for the same turn. Classic 'fog of war' type issues--even in a boardgame with nearly perfect information--except enemy intent.
