\n This has been eating my head in the last two weeks. Is it possible to reduce encounter design and balance to something more versatile and reliable but fair?

I mean, the whole objective of the encounter balance procedure is to limit the DM budget in a way that she can present a fair challenge to the players given the level and amount of PCs. The procedure allows the DM to select between easy, medium, hard and deadly encounters and limits the amount of creatures and challenge she can present to the players for each encounter, making things fair. So far so good, I'm not really against this procedure, it works wonders and I get the point of it.

However, with the gameplay I'm used to, in practice the procedure takes so long that it can only be done at prep time and the results are as reliable as a weather forecast; it can either turn into a TPK of a boring rollfest. And this is taking into account all sorts of tricks like interesting terrain, enviromental hazards, monster strategy, combos, etc.

I mean, it's exactly things like these that make this part of game math so unreliable for me: the right amount of goblins vs the right amount of PCs of the right level that should become a fun medium encounter turns into a deadly one because some obstacles are added and a bit of strategy is used. A deadly encounter with a legion of lemures turns into a kinda boring rollfest since no one can even reach the PCs and it doesn't make sense for them to have an strategy.

There's more to each PC and creature than just their numbers. This is what ends up making the normal encounter balance procedure not too effective for my gameplay style. So I'm wondering if things can be done sort of, in reverse?

Like, what if I take note of the PCs stats and according to that I give proper stats to any monster I'd like to showcase/make up, ignoring the original ones in the MM?. For instance (I'm no mathematician, so feel free to correct me), if a player has a +5 to hit, she has 50/50 chances to hit anything above 16. Dial it down to 11 for easy, up to 20 for deadly, anything in between for medium and hard.

This is actually how the D&D math already works, the only thing I'm eliminating here is assuming an X lvl PC will have enough bonuses to hit a CR Y monster, and instead looking at the PC actual bonus and decide "ok, then this monster will have this AC"

Dunno if it's clear enough, let me know if I can clarify this in any way. But if you think it is clear enough, does it sound too crazy? Considering all PC bonuses and stats, can balanced encounters be built easily? Do you think it would be more or less fair? What potential downfalls you foresee, is there any way to avoid them that doesn't include going back to the original procedure?

    \n What you suggest could work, assuming the crunch landscape is generally simple - which is probably not a fair assumption in 5th edition. Given a simple enough interaction of various special powers and such, though, it would be possible to simply calculate appropriate stats for monsters from the PC stats. I think it's only theoretically feasible in the real situation (5th edition D&D) here, though, and will fail in practice due to actual character effectiveness coming from a variety of complex sources rather than simple to-hit bonus and armor class.

    More generally, your problem is fundamental to modern D&D. I personally ultimately resolved it by refusing to take the responsibility: let the GM merely tell the players that there is a hydra here, and let the party bard or whoever fish up a stat block, and let the players decide if they can "take it". Much easier (and crucially, more interesting) than for the GM to take responsibility for the encounters fairness (or worse yet, encounter outcomes). The balancing thing didn't work in 3rd or 4th edition, so I would be surprised if it works in 5th either.

    \n Indeed. As Eero says, the question here is somewhat philosophical. Is your intentation to remove all "unbalanced" fights/encounters from the game? If so, what are you willing to sacrifice in order to make that happen?

    For instance, your suggested solution - if a functional version could be made (I'm not 100% sure it could):

    * Devalues player decisions, tactics, and strategy. If they are clever in positioning or strategy, shouldn't they benefit from that? If they make mistakes or play foolishly, shouldn't they suffer from that?

    It's not an obvious question!

    * Devalues character builds, magical items, and abilities. Do I just keep increasing monster hit points when one of the characters finds a really powerful weapon? Do I give the enemies better to-hit bonuses when so-and-so gets magical armour?

    If so, what's the point of getting better weapons and armour?

    * May create problems if character become "unbalanced" - do I adjust the monsters' AC so that the Fighter finds it hard to hit them? If so, then what happens to the poor magic-user, who can't hit them at all?

    The standard D&D answer seems to be to populate a world (or dungeon) with a variety of monsters, and then allow the players to fight different monsters as appropriate to their abilities and power level.

    That seems to me to be the most sensible and robust way of engaging the system, and it's the sort of D&D I would prefer. I don't *want* all my encounters to be predictably "balanced" - the fun is seeing how the players deal with varying levels of challenge, and how that creates a variety of situations in play.

    What else can you do?

    I suppose you could create a tier system, instead of D&D's escalating bonuses and numbers: when you fight something below your level, you get better odds of success, and when you fight something above your level, you suffer penalties.

    That would allow you to create a game where you can create an "optimal" set of stats for enemy monsters, for balance. Now, the player characters never advance their abilities at all - instead, their scale/level increases, allowing them to take on harder monsters despite their unchanging stats. All encounters can now be "balanced", but at the cost of unchanging stats/numbers/bonuses. Instead, the "colour" changes - you reskin the monsters with each "advance" or "level up".

    If you were going to create a "balanced" game, that would seem to me to be a better way to do it.

    \n Note that:

    Games which do not use enemy "stats" at all (like Apocalypse World's moves/rolls) allow you to maintain perfect "balance", if that's your goal: the players are always rolling against the characters' stats, instead of against monster stats.

    Another idea:

    You could create a system for creating mostly balanced encounters, so long as you're willing to have the occasional "easy" or "hard" encounter.

    The way it would work would be to pick a standard encounter level you feel pretty comfortable with, and keep it. Reskin it for different monsters, make small adjustments, but don't deviate it from it much at all.

    However, each time the PCs have an easy victory, bump up the enemy stats or numbers one notch, and then use the resulting stats for the next encounter.

    Each time the PCs suffer a defeat or struggle too much, bump the enemy template down one notch.

    That's something you could keep using over and over, as it's self-balancing. You'd have very little variety in encounters, however!

    \nThis describes a simple control system, like how a thermostat works. You want a thermometer to result in very little variety. But there are other control systems which are known to oscillate, go off the rails sometimes, etc., you could draw inspiration from those.

    Begin with: every encounter, bump up enemy numbers by d = 1/4 of a notch.
    Every time the PCs blow away the encounter, set the new d to (d + 1/4)
    Every time the PCs have a hugely difficult time, set the new d to (d - 1)
    Every time you notice this system is producing fairly static encounter difficulties over time, do something like increasing 1/4 or decreasing 1
    Every time you notice this system is completely out of control, replace it with something new or just restart it

    \n An observation about the encounter-spicing: my experience is that GMs generally feel much more pressure to be original than is warranted by the player experience. In the D&D context, it seems that players are generally quite happy with what the GM may consider to be normal, boring, by-the-book content; it's not by-the-book from their viewpoint, because they're not the ones with the book. I have experienced this on the both sides of the table.

    This being the case, I personally find it very sensible in most rpg situations to sacrifice GM creativity to gain some other virtue. In this case it seems that you're saying that you could achieve your desired amount of game balance by simply being less ambitious with the "personal touch" and sticking closer to the content the books offer. Because I do not believe that the players really value your personal touch, it is easy for me to recommend that you just drop it and let the game speak for itself.

    Of course that personal creative touch is often a big thing in the GM being interested in the game at all. After all, at a certain point of creative constraint the GM just becomes a big, fat computer replacement. The solution to that, I've found, may be to let the most boring player at the table do the GMing, freeing the GM to kibbitz with the other players from the other side of the screen while the pedant does the numbers [grin].

    \n Would systematic fudging on the fly work for your group and style? Like, keep the monster stats secret and reveal them only as you go, which implies that e.g. monster HP never needs to be revealed before the monster goes down, and monster morale just happens to break just when it might get iffy otherwise. Of course it just so happens that the monsters go down when they need to; they can be a bit tougher or weaker as needed, in real-time.

    Take that plus a crisis plan or two (like, introduce a storyline that justifies mysterious divine interventions "three times before the judgment comes", and then use those to save the party if you mess up and send them against something too hard; you get a cool side plot as a bonus if you ever are forced to save them three times, too), and you should be able to keep going for a long time even with the strictly-theoretically-useful encounter balancing system.

    The reason this sort of thing tends to work for most fantasy adventure gaming groups is that "I experienced exciting things in the game session" is perhaps the single most common, most powerful player value in roleplaying; if you recreate your own position as the "bringer of fun" (as opposed to "functionary of the rules"), the odds are that the other players will be entirely happy. They'll get their excitement, probably much better than if you tried too hard to balance things; after all, you'll be able to run the game on overdrive, essentially, when you have fudge factors on your side. They'll get to slay dragons or whatever, and it won't end in tears.

    I'll note even if this suggestion works short-term, it's IMO a bad long-term solution because of the incidious effects it has on player expectations; you basically can't go long-term illusionist without infantilizing the players as participants, is my experience. But if you're just looking for a temporary fix to get the campaign over with, so as to build something new on a more solid base, then that might be the smart thing to do.

    But then I readed some PbtA and some good advice here in SG and realized those were the perfect moments to narrate the NPCs doing something cool, unexpected or lucky.     -I can be open about monster moral, my rule of thumb is that if it's not cornered nor insane, it will try to run when it's down to half HP or less. (are rolls for moral more fair than this? I could go for those, np)
    -Tactics and stats I can prep, I just hate to use the encounter balance procedure even with online calculators. (I end up fudging whenever I use it)

    -I can come up with some stats right before the fight and stick to it. I can come up with some stats when needed and stick to it. If anything has been stated before, we stick to it and follow the consequences. (There are a lot of threads about relativism of this fudginess, but let's say it's fudgy and leave it at that)
    -I can inform the players if the fight is obviously easy or way above their CR before it starts. (it makes their decisions count, I suppose it's fair)
    -I can inform the players of possible consequences they haven't foreseen because my narration can be at fault. (uninformed player decisions aren't fair in my book)
    -If a mountain goat is specially trained and/or it makes sense it has supernatural abilities, I don't consider reskining, say, a manticore and watering down it's stats as unfair. (It's, you know, reskinning. It's meant to me creative, surprise the players. otherwise a single mountain goat can't give 5 level 3 players too much of a challenge)
    -I use a maximum of three tricks per encounter, between things like a creature's special ability, good tactics, item use, traps, etc. I may have more prepared though since most can be quite situational, or even improvise one on the worst case, if the combat is on it's 3rd turn and nothing else is going on. (a fudgy but calculated risk: if players can deal with it, are still at top of their HP, won't suffer more than 1d6 damage and it scares them a little, then this last fudge paid for a whole uninteresting encounter)
    -I keep mountain witching the players for their input, using everything on their character sheets as flags for my prep, asking them where they want to go and prep for that, stealing their ideas and throwing away my prep if those are better. This is as far away from railroading as I can get. I love it too when I don't know where are things going as a GM

    WM, you really, really, really don't want 5E, you want 4E. Its encounter building and monster math is much more transparent, and you'll pretty much never have the kind of accidental TPK or whiff-fest you're worried about. 5E is inherently kinda swingy.

    You\u2014all of you in this thread, AFAICT\u2014also have no idea how the math of 5E works. You talk about irrelevancies, like whether the PCs will have a decent chance of hitting a particular monster. There are monsters in the high teens in CR that have ACs of 15 or 16. Bounded accuracy, remember?

    In fact, the biggest fallacy here is the idea that if a monster's CR is "too low" you can't throw it at the party. That's also nonsense, precisely because of bounded accuracy. If a monster has a low CR, but has the exact ability you want, use it anyway, just use more of it. Not too much more, though: Goblins are 50 XP, right? Let's say you have 4 5th-level PCs. A dozen goblins is 50 x 12 = 600 XP, right? No, because the table on the bottom right of page 82 of the DMG says to multiply that number by 3 for their numerical advantage. Suddenly, although still only worth 600 XP (if you're using the book's XP system), they are as difficult as an 1,800 XP encounter with fewer monsters would be. That's only just shy of Medium for our hypothetical 5th-level party! (Even easier way to build encounters: choose a single tougher monster. Don't worry about the CR too much, as long as it's not more than 1 higher than the PCs, but make sure it has a decent amount of HP. Then given it a number of mook type minions equal in number to the PCs plus 2.)

    Because the other thing you have to understand is that difficulty in 5E isn't really supposed to be about any single encounter. It's about endurance. It's about the Adventuring Day. You see, the DMG isn't kidding that the system is designed around a long rest only every 6-8 encounters. (And short rests every 2-3 encounters.) Many, many people totally miss this aspect of the system, and try to run exciting adventures with only 1-2 combats per long rest... and then wonder why there was an accidental TPK!

    But I enforce that expectation fairly strictly, and it's why if there's a fight where the PCs only take, like 5 damage on average apiece before slaughtering all the monsters, that's fine... because those HP won't heal themselves. Or, if they kill all the monsters before taking a single point of damage... but only did so by blowing an important spell or two? Fine!

    5E works best as a tactical and strategic exercise, just as much as any old-school game. But it doesn't care about light sources, food, or water. The resources you have to manage are Hit Dice, spell slots, and other expendable character abilities.

    Lessons learned so far:

    -Solitaire monsters are not recommended for a good encounter. At least it would be good to pair them with an enviromental danger or obstacle course for the PCs to run through.
    -Different monsters with different synergic abilities make for more interesting encounters. Otherwise, give the monsters a couple tactics or a single use item they can change the tide of the battle with.
    -Encounter calculators online work quite fine for monsters. Monsters are absolutely not a problem now for me. NPCs with classes are.
    -Spellcaster NPCs should never battle without cannon fooder around. There are too few things for them to do with their bonus actions and damage spells will often be too much for a single PC.
    -As a GM, whenever your monster's or NPCs HP and/or numbers fall to 1/2, start retreating, unless the monsters/NPCs are unable to reason. It's believable and makes for a good change of pace admist the battle.
    -A battle where the tides don't change works as a preparation for a really good battle, but not as a replacement.
    -When PCs reach 0 HP, they just fall unconscious and will roll their death saves each round. That puts TPKs further than I was used to think, but requires the GM to be creative about what happens when they wake up. Story can go on, it doesn't have to end in a single combat for the same PCs.

    \n As statisticians posters on a role playing game forum, I believe the thing to do is obsess over the outliers.

    Anyway back to "balance" - for 4e GM-less-ish, what we did frequently is set up "sounds too hard" encounters with novel success conditions.

    Some examples were:

    * get everyone through the obstructions/enemies to that hole in the ground on the other end of the map
    * don't leave the area for X rounds
    * break these four crystals
    * don't let the minions beyond the walls

    This showcases less-used powers/skills and player ingenuity and allows a defeat to be really smoothly turned into obvious story consequences rather than a TPK (a safety net we wanted for that campaign). On average these novel-end-condition encounters were less straight-up balanced but had a kind of dynamic equilibrium I really enjoyed.

    The other interesting thing we did (again during 4e days) was set up a global clock tied to long rests. There was an invasion or something that was going to happen and several mitigations (reinforcements from allies was one) that we could put points into by succeeding at various themed encounters... so go ahead and take as many long rests as you want, the invasion clock will advance each time and you won't be making any progress toward mitigations.

    The way this made balancing easier is that the encounters could be made easier, less chance of TPK, and the focus becomes players maximizing ratio of encounters defeated to long rests taken. Bringing it back to novel success conditions, I guess.
