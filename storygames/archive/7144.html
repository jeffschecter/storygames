    \n Actually, I have this game in my drawer with the working name "Blindfold Tryst". I don't know yet if it has any legs, but it specifically involves blindfolding one player while the other acts as a story guide. The idea is that not having any visual distractions would presumably make for a different imagined experience (including time-distortion, potentially).

    I haven't played significantly with impaired people (that I know of), but I'd go for written mediums if I was deaf, myself. Being blinded here and now wouldn't affect my roleplaying so dramatically, I guess, although it would certainly affect everything else.

    \n Hey Nathan,

    I know half a dozen visually impaired or blind gamers and the ones I'm closest to are really into L5R and D&D 3.5 right now. One girl is a huge Ars Magica fangirl although i don't think she plays regularly any more. In terms of techniques, the L5R and 3.5 guys both use laptops, screen readers, die rolling apps and spreadsheets for their character sheets.

    I wrote a small game that is intended to level the playing field between sighted and non-sighted players - Ghosts. Everything is resolved by touch and I've not had a chance to try it yet.

    \n The closest thing we got was one of my brother's characters, who was a mute cleric (actually he took a silence vow...). We agreed he'd have to remain mute regarding his character. So he could speak to declare actions and stuff, but not to communicate with others in any verbal way. It was a damn pain in the ass, hard as hell, and tons of fun to both play and watch.

    I am wondering how would actually put a gag on him to make him completelly unable to talk would work...
