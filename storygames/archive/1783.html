Games you have to play to understand

Jason_Morningstar
November 2006 edited November 2006 in Play Advice
This is a little subjective, and your mileage will certainly vary, but some games are easier to grasp after a thorough reading than others. It often seems that there is some emergent property that manifests in play that makes a game great that is not captured in the text.

Sorcerer was like this for me - the text was a brick wall, but in play I can see how it all works to bring the ... word Nathan has forbidden.

The fan-mail currency in PTA is another example of an elegant, critical system that is not at all obvious on first read, but makes perfect sense when you see it in action.

You could probably argue that Keys in TSOY don't really demonstrate their power until they are bought, used, chained, and bought off in play. There's always moment of dawning awareness as a new player grasps the potential of Keys.

I'm not convinced that devoting pages to explaining the cool things that will happen when you actually play would be useful in any of these cases. Actually playing, of course, solves the problem.

So my questions: What are other examples of games that really must be experienced to be appreciated, and what are games that demonstrate what makes them mechanically interesting on first reading? Are some qualities too variable, subtle, or complex that they can't be accurately captured outside of play? If so, how can this be communicated to a reader? Should it be?

These intangibles are not drift, they are specific game play things that happened solely because the game was set up to run the way it was. Most likely the author of the game was already aware of those tangibles, sometimes their 'deepness' is discovered and promulgated by other people (like Spiritual Attributes in The Riddle of Steel, probably the best example of that).

    In any case, it's my strong opinion that the game should have come right out and laid those intangibles right in the text by means of sidebars, text boxes, further explanation and the like. Often fans of the game can describe the cool intangible elements of the game in a way that is readily understandable, and will get a viewer (in the case above, "The person who started the Sell Me On thread") actively looking to use and exploit those intangibles.

    Do you see what I'm saying? I'm not all "author sucks for not writing good" here, I'm saying that RPG texts can very definitely talk about the emergent factors that the game's rules or structure lead to (even in broad brushstrokes), if nothing else than to whet the player's appetite for play, or to get him to realize that This IS (or isn't) The Game She's Been Waiting For. But few texts do so.

    I hope I'm not off base here (LMK if I am Jason), but again I think that there are intangibles that emerge through play, but the simple fact that some fan dude can tell me in two minutes about "his actual play campaign" or about what he thinks the game does well, and I can see the shadows of those intangible things (now made tangible because this dude told me what happened at his table)... well, I start to think, "Why don't more people put this kind of thing in their game text?"

    (aha, now average reader/listener has a frame of reference. He hasn't played the game himself yet, but now has experienced secondhand an actual play event or two that solidified the idea above. So it gives him more to look forward to in his own game)

    So in the above, sure it's not anywhere near having Mark's actual experience. But his little speech above is definitely a middle point between "reading the rules" and "experiencing it for yourself".

    Think of a medical book (none of us are surgeons, but still).
    1) Here is a book that describes where all the organs in a body are, and proper surgery techniques.
    2) Here is the actual experience of performing several successful surgeries.
    That's a big jump there. Medical students spend a lot of time in lectures and tutoring and the like, picking up Actual Play (as it were) from doctors, getting the kinds of lessons that bridge numbers 1 and 2 together until they're ready to take on #2.

    Now, I'm not meaning to imply that playing games are like medicine or whatever, but that "bridging effect" between textbook and actually experiencing the event firsthand... there's a huge gap in pedagogy there normally, and I'm personally seeing what I can do to bridge that gap . Japanese roleplaying Replays (the script-format actual play sessions) are one huge step to bridge that gap over in Japan.     II'm going to suggest that we're talking about different kinds of "understanding" here. There's a cognitive level of appreciation and there is a gut level of experience. The trick is that the two work together much more than we often acknowledge, and so different levels of "knowing" happen from learning something, experiencing something, or from doing both together.

    To take this to a non-RPG context for a moment: I just traveled to India with Mo after I'd spent 10 years studying Indian history, politics, and religion. We spent a lot of time in India with people she was working with as well, who had a very different take on India than either of us did as we were both there to experience it.

    In the first week I was in India I learned absolutely nothing that I had not already known. However, I learned a huge amount that I had never felt. All the intangibles became tangible and gut punched me so hard that I was often left (literally) breathless. My understanding and experience of India is now on a whole different plane than it was before.

    Mo, otoh, had been trying to study India for the few months before we left, and was having a hard time remembering all the strange names and bizarre concepts. Then we got there, we got down in it, it got her in the gut and suddenly it became clear in her head. She both learned and felt in India, and as a result came ot understand it where she'd had no real knowledge of it at all before. She ended up on the same page with me about India, but her initial experience of it was very different.

    Many of the people we were working with, OTOH, never tried to learn about India. They only experienced it, only felt the culture shock without any understanding of what was behind it. They came away with an understanding of India all right, but it was very different than mine and very different than Mo's -- because they had gotten only one side of the knowing.

    So, back to game: A well described game text can give you cognitive appreciation of what is going on in the game, but it won't give you that gut level feeling of it. In some games with rigorous and straight-forward systems this isn't always important, as the experience of playing the game is pretty much exactly what you might think from you cognitive understanding. In other games, however, the gap between the two kinds of knowing can be massive.

    Look, for example, at the Pool. If you read the Pool it seems like there is hardly anything there. There isn't a lot to cognitively appreciate, as the whole thing takes up like one page of not-small font text and is completely straightforward. But when you play the Pool and get down into it and have Idea rolls flowing and people blowing their whole pool and screaming in horror at the ruin that is their character, you get a whole different level of appreciation of the game.

    So what I think Andy is talking about is games that, for whatever reason, don't even give you a proper cognitive understanding of the game before you start. This is a problem, and real big one, and I think we can all admit that. What Jason is talking about, otoh, is games that you can understand cognitively with no problem, but when you get to the gut level of understanding from actual play get that experience like I had in India -- everything you knew abstractly kicks you in your gut and makes you feel it very concretely.

    I'm ambivalent on this one, for a couple of reasons. First, I think there's lots of merit in presenting the rules of the game in a very dry mechanical form, and letting people discover the feel in play, because if you clutter your text with "Why the game works with this rule" that can often make the reading harder, and people miss the actual "how to" that's critical. In a purist sense, I'd say that I personally prefer very little explanation of "why it works" and just lay out the procedures for me.

    Now, that said, RPGs have those moments where people have to make decisions on when to use the mechanics and other just judgments. As Matthew's example points out, circumstances of play might lead to the wrong conclusions about these things if the text doesn't instruct on these judgments to some extent. So I also understand the POV that says that we should explain better.

    Part of the problem, however, that it's almost a contradiction in terms to be explicating an "intangible" isn't it? I mean we call them intangibles precisely because it's so hard to put your finger on just what they are. Trying to describe them might be as confusing, or more confusing than refraining.

    Moreover...a lot of this amounts to "write better explanations." Which is a hell of a lot easier said than done.

    Andy does have some interesting ideas that might work. Actually I'm personally for laying out the procedures sans commentary in one section, and then later having a section on design notes that explains why things are designed as they are, and what the design intends to do. Note that this is precisely what the designers of wargames do, and it seems to be pretty effective.

    That's a flawed analogy, of course, since wargames have no judgment calls. The one place where you do need to have examples and explicate in RPG texts are where the ambiguities of participant choice arise. If the rule itself doesn't make crystal clear what the player prerogatives are about, then you have to at least give some inkling. These textual clues are akin to the "strategy tips" you sometimes see in some non-RPG rulesets, where they say something like, "Note that it's often best to simply do X here, but there may be exceptions..."

    I think it's a fine line. I'm quite sure it's just as possible to over-explain as it is to under-explain. I'm also just as sure that the latter occurs far more than the former, because it's easier to just hope that the players will put the mechanics into action and "Get it." But if you go into it with an eye toward setting the level correctly, I think you're doing about all you can to try to make the game as understandable as possible in this regard.

    Because you can't just will yourself to be a better writer. And, worse, what's clear to one reader is fuzzy to another. So there's no system out there that's so clear that you'll never have to give any clarifications. Just do the best you can with it.

    Mike

    On what's been said:

    I'm new to this thread, but I do need to say that everything Andy's been saying may as well have come out of my own mouth as well. Over at Evil Hat, we are explanation *fetishists*, (c.f. my examination of the questionnaire in Don't Rest Your Head or, well, nearly all of Spirit of the Century).

    When we started revising Fate for the version that's in SotC, we went down the wrong path a lot. An absolute ton! But thanks in great part to Lenny Balsera's involvement, we kept pushing back towards a target of "okay, that may be what you've written, but how do you actually play?" If the text didn't match the actual play -- if it didn't presage it, underline it, and to a great extent match it, it was something we needed to turn a deep, critical eye towards. As often as not it ended up with something changing in the text -- or even the system itself -- to better match and illustrate actual play techniques.

    This isn't rocket science, but it is exhausting and time-consuming. I don't think we can honestly, reasonably expect all games to try to hit that mark. And sometimes it's a design decision *not* to hit that mark, as I believe has been suggested earlier in the thread.

    But there's a reason why you playtest, beyond simple mechanics validation, that I think gets missed. IMO, you should be factoring those actual play experiences back into the book. Examples should be based in things that actually happened in your games. Realizations that you had about how the game functions should exist in the main text or, at the bare minimum, in sidebars or "behind the curtain" type stuff.

    But you also have to recognize that sometimes folks just won't feel it until they drink whatever flavor of kool-aid you're purveying. The comments people are making about Dogs in the Vineyard most fully show this to be so. Dogs definitely hits my "lots of real, actual play, sink-your-teeth-in, see-it-from-all-angles explanations in here" goalpost. And Vincent wisely stops the explanation right where he does -- right at the doorstep of the door of playing it yourself so, the moment you open that bad boy, the things you can't turn into text, the last few scintillae of game-magic that only ever happen experientially, will hit you square in the eyes.

    As a game designer I feel like it's my responsibility to get players to that doorstep. Getting them to the same town isn't enough; nor to the neighborhood; nor to the street; not even to the house. To the doorstep. That's where it's at.

    On the original mission of the thread:

    Dogs is amazing because it forecasts all of the incredible stuff you're going to encounter, but still leaves room for you to be amazed when it happens.

    Exalted has a bewildering array of charms that I find overwhelming -- until I sit down to make a character, and then it's like shopping for candy with a rich man's credit card.

    The Shadow of Yesterday and Sorcerer (among others) both have powerful setting reinforcement tools dressed up as standard, "generic" RPG features -- keys and descriptors, respectively. Once you see them in play, you sit up and realize that they are where the setting lives, not in some dusty thick tome of endless paragraphs of trivia.

    Mortal Coil's prices on magic -- creating setting on the fly, but never truly fully controlling what you add -- man, it creates real-feeling, new-smelling, exciting magical laws for your universe, and in reading the text, man, you can just gloss right past 'em and not realize you've just seen the most important thing in the book. Similar things *could* be said about the passions, I think, but I haven't done any long-term Mortal Coil play, so this is more theory than observed data.

    Rolemaster's brutal damage system, with its wacky crit tables and chances of sudden, ugly, hilariously untimely death looks like a fun read but lousy play until it gets in motion -- and then you see its nastiness turn bold cinematic heroes into devious, sneaky, let-do-it-quick-and-skip-the-fighting Black Company type mercenaries. Say what you will about it, but *man*, that's another powerful setting tool built in right there that you end up experiencing as you play it.

    Agon's ideas of the range strip and it mattering whether you have dice in your left or right hand can seem a little bizarre on the reading of it, but when you take it into play, you realize that these are best fit tools for putting you in the moment. Swinging your sword and blocking with your shield by rolling dice from the same hands that hold those implements in-character? Awes-- er, fantastic. Strategic mastery of range being a crucial, yet abstracted aspect of battlefield play? Rock on.

    It's weird when I read on of these threads and realize that everyone has a different idea of the subject than I do.

    I see as a significant dichotomy between emergent features of games and situations where you need someone who's played it to explain it to you. The former usually portends well for the quality of the design, since you get a higher ratio of good-experience to rules count. The latter I tend to think of as a flaw: "* designer not included."     As far as emergents go, there is the slight danger that in reading the text you won't see them at all, and a single sentence point them out is worth it's weight in gold. My analogy is always to Go, which has 5 rules, which is enough to produce a deep enough game that there are between 40 and 50 ranks of players, where every rank will consistently defeat the rank below it.     Ultimately, I think what helps most is some quick pointers to the emergent properties of a ruleset. I think(?) that PtA and TSoY both do this about their respectives, and if they don't they ought to. Contrariwise, Tony goes on for a whole chapter about the economies in Capes, which are a big part of the emergent properties.       Well...Game Theory is all about how the rules of the game cause behaviors. Strategies are precisely the response behaviors to the rules.

      Now, there are some "optimal strategies" that everyone follows (usually becuase they're obvious). I think this is what you're referring to, Tim.

      I think that the array of strategies that people can employ in situations where the best strategy isn't known are, in fact, an emergent quality of the game. But so are the optimal strategies, yes.

      I mean this quite literally, from a Game Theory POV. That is, let's take the game Feng Shui, with it's rule that you get penalized if you announce an action in a boring or repetitive manner. Everybody knows that the single way to respond to this is to make your announcements fun and innovative, and (when enforced), the rule produces the desired result by giving better rewards to the player when he follows the desired behavior. While the color of the announcement has no effect itself on the outcome of the game, the selection of that color has a mechanical effect, and so even color is affected by system.

      Often this is less obviously mechanical. Subjective "roleplaying rewards" for instance. Or even just the group's social reinforcement schedule. But, insamuch as these behaviors are the result of the structure that the group decides to use, I think you can say that it's an emergent property of those structures.

      Mike

      P.S. Where the term "Strategy" is well defined in Game Theory, the term Tactics is less used, or ommitted entirely. When used it generally means a strategy used to solve for a short portion of an overall game. In that context, then, Strategy means a plan to employ certain tactics in sequence where possible. In fact the term "Strategic Move" refers to a move early in the game that intends to fix the game situation later so that only certain strategies will work then (these could be called tactics, too).
