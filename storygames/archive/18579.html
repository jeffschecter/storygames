    \n Color first is about really imagining the look and feel of the fictional world and working from that to mechanics rather than the other way around. It makes sense, since many of us are more excited about the color of a game (the imagined details and style of the imaginary world/fiction) than anything else. Not even all of it, but just specific stuff to get ahold of.

    The examples from when this was talked about on the Forge were mostly centered around finding specific images and riffing off of them to make characters and build setting for any particular game. If you ever played Everway, it does this very specifically by giving you "vision cards"

    Some might say this is just the 'concept' phase of character creation, and to an extent they're right. But look, I teach visual art students all the time. Most of us think in generalities. It's what keeps us from having to make a new mental file for every car and truck we ever see. It is why artists train by drawing from a live model. No matter how good your mental model of something is, nature still surprises you with its specificity. Color first is about finding a specific vision that brings things to life in the game.

    I pretty much do this for characters all the time, now. Looking for images and then riffing of the specifics of an image or a few images to make a character gives me some great stuff. I ask myself cool questions like "why do they have that," or "why are they wearing that, or "why is that expression on their face."

    You know how AW asks you to define your look/outlook up front and gives you the specific lists of names and bodies and eyes? That's very much color first.

    \n Paul, it's true, I'm mentioning the problematic case where the rules adjustment is visible and the fictional explanation isn't implicit. There are plenty of other cases. It's pretty normal that (a) no one notices the extra dice and thus no one asks "where did that come from?", or (b) everyone imagines "where that came from" in a way that works for them, with no communication required.

    W-Monk, I think numeric representation doesn't really become "simulation" until physics enters the equation. Example:

    I'm atop a small staircase, fighting a badguy who's completely encased in magical armor which cannot be cut, but which doesn't do much to blunt the force of bludgeons, and is also very bulky and heavy heavy. I'm getting my ass kicked, but my back is to a wall, while his back is to the stairs. I'm imagining the situation, tackling it with all the cleverness I can muster, and I have an idea. "I'm going to push him down the stairs! If I can just force him back an inch, his giant boots should give trouble finding purchase on the narrow stairs. If I hit him above his center of gravity, he should be leaning back when he loses his footing. If he then falls, the armor should provide no protection against the injuries from repeated impacts of tumbling. He should wind up a crumpled heap, struggling to get up under the weight of his own armor, and I can then walk up and slit his throat. I'm a genius! Now let's see if I can pull it off..."

    My inspiration is based on physics, so I need a physics-based rule set to reward me for its specifics. Each bonus to damage or penalty to a balance check or whatever is a reward for my cleverness. A game whose numeric quantities are broader and based on something other than physics, like "acting With A Plan gives you +1", does not offer the same rewards. A lot of narrative-first games will say something like "succeed and inflict a fictionally applicable Condition from the list". So the plan above would mean I can hope to inflict the condition "stunned" on Big Armor Guy. If that's good enough for me, great, I can use a simple rule set that doesn't concern itself with things like inertia, balance, force and distance. But sometimes that's not good enough. "Wait, I can also inflict the Stunned condition just by kicking him in the balls? Okay, so my brilliant idea was worth nothing."

    As for tapping an untapped market by offering games that don't force players to wrestle with physics and look up all sorts of detailed special cases, I certainly hope you're right! I think having a crunchy system wherein the player can deal more with rules than with other players is actually a solution to the creative extrovert barrier, and I've heard that D&D at big cons is a lot of rolling without talking. So I have some doubts about how much my taste (and yours) reflects the market.

      \n Well said, I know exactly what you're talking about. I had to deal with that, and here's how I did it:

      First, really get buy-in to "playing this game seriously". Seriously doesn't mean "no jokes", but it does mean that whenever someone distracts from the game, they're ruining the fun. If your group doesn't want to put some effort into focusing on the game and having a fun game, and would rather simply have a fun evening regardless of the game, then I'd give up right there and play a board game or a very light-hearted RPG.

      If they can get behind the idea of serious gaming, next I'd pitch Delve's style of play. "Let's do the immersive pretend-you're-there thing! Not just for drama's sake, but also because that's how we'll solve problems -- as if we were there." I love this style, so I'd go on about how it's awesome, and see if I could get buy-in. If your group doesn't get excited about that, then Delve is the wrong game. I'd play something else -- what have you guys had most success with?

      So, finally, if they really want to do the immersive style and take it seriously, then I'd tell them "here's how we make it work". I'd spell out exactly how "integrity of the fiction always trumps anyone's personal advantage" will get us where we want to be. Again, I need buy-in to this idea before proceeding. This can be a very long and philosophical discussion about past play, what's worked, what hasn't, etc. The goal is twofold -- to convince the skeptics that (a) even if they have their doubts, it's worth trying, and (b) there is actually hope for success (I'd offer my 48-session game of mostly excellent play as proof).

      So now we have a framework for evaluating anything anyone does at a given moment. The only way you can achieve advantage in the game is by first establishing what's what in the fiction and then working with that. Players can't make up fictional facts, just as characters can't invent their surroundings. Remember, you are your character!

      Is this counter-intuitive at first? For your group, of course it would be. It would feel really strange to do what the comics say. "Instead of just announcing an action, I'm supposed to share my intent first and make sure I'm not missing any fictional details? WTF? I'm not gonna spend all game asking the GM's permission!" I would take this as a teaching moment:

      You'll say, "I'm gonna do X, cool?" a million times, and a million times the response will be "cool" or "if you want to, but remember Y", and at the end of the session you won't have had any arguments. It's worth it.

      The one hang-up is that you give the GM an opportunity to foil you. The GM is not trying to foil you. The GM gains nothing from foiling you. The GM is trying to help. Which is what you're asking for: not permission, but information. Shared information is the foundation of shared imagined stuff.

      Perhaps that GM role needs to be highlighted more in the comics. The GM is always on the players' side. The GM just needs to do certain things in order to provide adversity for the characters. Otherwise, the whole group is in the "make a plausible and usable fiction" project together, with equal voices.

      What do you think? Does this address your player dynamics? Whoever's the alpha should use their alpha-dom to enforce the values that this endeavor was predicated on. If someone wants to be the clearest and most communicative and most plausible player in the room, then good for them.

      As for physics and genre, I think they're always related. If you ask me "what type of action movie is this?", one of the bases I'll be sure to cover is whether people are flying through walls and surviving, superhero-style, or whether the action is plausible in terms of real-world physics. You can tell how important this is when a movie stumbles from one end to the other, like when Die Hard starts out pretty down to earth but then the end of Die Hard II abandons plausibility for a neat special effect and a lot of the audience goes "that's stupid". I think we have names for these different genres -- "gonzo action" vs "gritty action", perhaps.

      Delve leaves physics-fidelity up to the group out of necessity, as no ruleset can do a better job modeling the real world to the players' satisfaction than the players themselves. What the game provides the players is not novel and unexpected takes on physics. (Until magic gets involved. Which is why magic is a big deal.)

      Genre-emulation, on the other hand, can be faithfully modeled or given unexpected and novel twists or left up to the players or a mix of all of the above, I think. But as you're designing mechanics for Vengeance and Righteousness and Turning Points, you still have to answer the question of "who deals with in-fiction physics?" Is it up to the players entirely, or do you structure their conversation with procedures, or do you include some good old-fashioned measurement and simulation?

      I had a friend who liked Vampire tell me that "the rules handled the arbitration and causality stuff, leaving me free to focus on genre and mood". I've heard the same said of Cthulhu games. The problem I had in those games was specifically the problem Delve addresses -- despite whatever genre conventions applied, players were trying to problem-solve, and they were using the tool that had been highlighted for them: their character.

      So if you want to avoid the physics-sim trap in your genre RPG, I think you'd better make it abundantly clear what the players' tools are for tackling the situations of play. If you throw a threat at my character, it's second nature for me to treat this as a challenge, and start looking over my character attributes and gear to form an optimal response. If what you want me to do instead is provide a response that is, above all else, colorful, then please get rid of attributes and gear and give me points for being entertaining. (This is what I do for my supervillain game, Within My Clutches.)

      \n I think I have an answer to your question, K-Bob:

      Here's how Fiasco handles physics-sim. Each point below is a subset or special case of the preceding point.

      - Why are we playing this? - To make stories that are like (among other sources) Coen Brothers movies.
      - What should we do now? - Something that'd fit well in a Coen movie.
      - What would/should/can happen in the fiction now? - Something that'd fit well in a Coen movie.
      - Can my character physically do this? - If a character in a Coen movie could.

      So if your guy falls off a 3-story building and you're wondering whether he's alive, the answer is that, well, if that happened in a Coen movie, he might die, or he might live with some awful/comedic disfigurement. So which is it? Well, the game never pretended to give you any tools for deciding such things, so clearly it's up to you. Just pick.

      The whole point of physics-sim is to support a play style in which such things are contested and thus it's not okay to let someone just pick. By "contested" I mean that part of the fun is (e.g.) facing death and disfigurement as hazards that you can avoid through skillful play, or prices that you pay for taking risks, etc.

      In a phys-sim approach, the question is: a character fell out of a 3 story window: What mechanics do we use and what does it show? Okay, let's go to the mechanics.

      In [whateveritisI'mtalkingabut], it's more like: What outcomes are suggested by the source materials? Do we even feel the need to roll? If so, how to we weight the possible outcomes?

      . . .

      On a side note, I tend not to love competition or gaminess in RPGs and Story Games. I like challenge, but that's a bit of a subtle difference.
      I think this is important. In most games, players apply their skills, which are challenged in some way by that game's particular demands and constraints. In RPGs, that challenge can be provided by the rules, or the fiction, or the other players, or any combo of those.

      Most physics-sim games strike me as an attempt to use rules to quantify and gamify the challenges presented by the fiction. I think that's a large part of their appeal. When they fail to satisfy that appeal, it's because the rules and the other players impact the challenge in other ways, from rules exploits like Peasant Railgun to player judgment calls like the GM not letting you roll.

      Met with such failure, I think there's a spectrum of responses. At one end is a desire for fewer judgment calls, and play supported by rules that refer to other rules rather than to nebulous fictional stuff. At the other end is a desire for fewer fiction-independent rules incentives, and play supported by fiction-communication techniques that don't require gamey rules. I think these ends of the spectrum tend to map roughly to "rules first" and "fiction first" play.

      Similarly, "fiction first" and "color first" often go together.

      So I guess that's my take on "color-first vs physics-sim". If you want play to be about fiction and color, you might actually seek out a physics-sim game for that reason! If the fiction and color you like cares about physics issues*, and the rules model physics well, and your group has a clear agreement on how to apply the rules, then you might get exactly what you want! But if not, you may then seek out something from the "freeform" or "rules-lite" or "genre emulation" families.

      *So who likes fiction and color about physics issues? Certainly not everyone, but I don't think it's a tiny niche either. Many of us enjoy action/adventure fiction which challenges protagonists to problem-solve physically challenging situations. We can also enjoy the color involved in detailed descriptions of character actions and scenarios, in games where those descriptions are always offered because they always matter. Coen Brothers color in Fiasco is great too, but scratches a different itch (for me, anyway).
