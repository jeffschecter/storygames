    Hey Andy,

    I have a filing cabinet. It's four drawers, 24" deep. Two of them are devoted to game stuff. My filing system is pretty informal, but I do try to do some color coding at the folder level. Grey folders are for printouts of game rules. I have some labeled "indie games" that contain many games (e.g. The Pool, etc.). And I have some labeled by game title for longer game texts that I've spent some time with (e.g. Sorcerer, InSpectres, from when I got them as pdfs). Two manila folders labeled "cast" contain 25+ years of characters I've played in various games. (Character illustrations, mostly from long ago games of AD&D and Champions, are all together in a separate manila folder. I'm not sure why but I've always done it that way.) Beyond this, the coloring system breaks down. If I've run a game scenario for more than a single session, the maps, relationships maps, prep materials, notes, printouts of rules and whatnot are all collected together in a single folder, usually labeled by the game system. Notes and rules drafts and playtest materials for games I'm developing also each get a separate folder.

    Paul

    I greatly miss having access to a complete GBC comb binding setup. The whole lot of my RPG printouts are neatly comb-bound, including "hardcover" editions of Donjon and MLWM. Covers color-laser-printed at Kinko's. (I need to re-do Trollbabe and Paladin in laser, they are not so hot in inkjet.) Stuff like, say, all the InSpectres supplements get bound into a single book with dividers. You can buy consumer-grade systems at office supply stores, but they don't make 'em like they used to.

    Also, there's a special type of folder colloquially referred to as "red ropes" in the legal world. They are largish brown-red pocket folders that accordian-fold out to hold a bunch of regular manilla folders. They are super-handy for organizing all those manilla folders by theme. (I've got one for D&D paperwork, another for every other RPG.) The best thing is you can carry them around town as sort of an impromptu briefcase.

    I use manilla folders for everything and store them in a file cabinet. One trick that I got from the book "Getting Things Done" (a really great productivity/organization book, by the way) is to get a handheld label-maker from Office Max and use that to label all the folder. That makes them easy to read in the file cabinet and they look really professional, so I can take the folder to the tabletop and it makes me look more organized than I am. I also try to recycle stuff frequently if I'm not using it, and I tend to keep a lot of digital media right where is, in a special folder on my computer desktop.
