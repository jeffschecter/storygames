    Is there a place to find monsters? I'm willing to wing some things, but not most monsters.
    \nThere are monsters throughout the Bloodstone Idol section, as part of that adventure. The Red Book is meant to be a starter version of the full game, which is still in development. The name is meant to evoke "Red Box" basic D&D, which also included a module and a few monsters. For more content, you can join the Adventurers Guild to get previews of the full game, or you can get the free Hack version, as Chris suggested.

    For what it's worth, it's pretty easy to adapt and create monsters once you've run the game and have the hang of how monsters work. The real trick is in matching the numbers of opponents to their levels and that of the PCs- that's an area where the Red Book rules don't give a lot of guidance.\n
    Are there more classes besides Bard, Cleric, Fighter, Paladin, Ranger, Thief, Wizard? Maybe someone has posted some somewhere?
    \nThe Red Book only uses the basics- figher, thief, cleric and wizard, and I'd suggest starting with that until you have a feel for the game. I think other character playbooks are available, but many of them will be designed for the full DW rules.\n
    I don't own Apocalypse World. Is it open game content, and you can create your own version a la Dungeon World?
    \nIt's worth getting AW if you don't have the game (even if you just get the PDF), because it's a good game. You shouldn't need it to play Dungeon World, however.

    Posted By: chrisshorbI don't really get how combat works, and I have a feeling I'm looking at it in the wrong way, i.e. in a D&D way, not a DW way. Can someone maybe give me an example of how a combat between a fighter and a goblin would go?
    \nSo this got a little out of hand.

    Keep in mind that a lot of running DW (or any game) is about style, so the way I lay it out here is not how everyone would do it. I think it's mostly correct though.

    -----------

    Ozruk the Angry has entered the domain of the Grimtooth Goblins.

    Dwarf Fighter: level 1, d10 damage, 8 HP; str 17, int 8, wis 11, dex 9, con 15, cha 13

    Goblin warrior: Level 1, 5 HP, 1 Armor, 3 damage (swords, spears) (from "Goblin Advanced Guard" page 91).

    GM: Ozruk, as you stalk forward toward the gates, you hear a shrill battle cry from behind a barricade ahead and to the right, perhaps 30 feet away. Behind it stand several angry-looking Goblins, hoisting their spears threateningly.

    Player: I raise my shield and ring it against the ancient battle-axe of my father, roaring back at them as I move forward.

    GM: Okay, you start forward across the narrow stretch of clear stonework between yourself and the barricade at the gate. The floor is littered with the bones and old weapons of previous intruders. What do you do? [Move: Show Signs of Doom]

    Player: Whatever, I'm killing some Goblins. I charge at them!

    GM: All right, that sounds like a Defy Danger move, as the Goblins now launch a rain of spears at you.

    Player: Those little weasels! [rolls 2D6, adding nothing for his 9 Dex]. Hmm, I rolled a 7. That's not good.

    GM: Right, so you're successful in charging across the killing floor, but as the first volley of spears begin falling around you need to stop and take cover. Do you want to hold here and take advantage of your shield, or charge ahead boldly? [Defy Danger "hard bargain"]

    Player: Charge ahead! Goblin spears can't stop Ozruk!

    GM: Alright, there are four goblins attacking, doing three points each. What's your armor, without your shield?

    Player: Ouch! So yeah, my chainmail is 2 armor, so I guess I take 1 point from each attack, for 4 points total. That's half of my hit points! I guess I should have taken cover.

    GM: Live and learn, right? At least you are now at the barricade, which you can easily get around. The Goblins turn to meet you, clearly afraid of your ferocity but rushing to meet you anyway [Monster Move: Charge!]

    Player: They should be afraid. Now that I'm within striking distance, I'm brushing their spears aside and cleaving skulls!

    GM: Sounds like Hack and Slash. The Goblins in front are attacking with short swords, while those in back are stabbing with their spears. Make your roll.

    Player: [Rolls 2D6, adds 2 for Str] That's a 10!

    GM: Good roll. Do you want to expose yourself to attack to do extra damage?

    Player: No thanks, I'm already wounded. [rolls 1D10, plus 1 for his signature weapon] They take 7 points.

    GM: Okay, the lead goblin takes your hit, your axe slicing through his armor and killing him in one blow [7 - 1 armor vs 5 HP]. His companions attack but you sidestep the blows, and your enemy's falling corpse. What's next?

    Player: You said these guys are afraid, right? Can I tell which one them looks like he's in charge?

    GM: Try Discern Realities, and see if you can tell in the heat of battle.

    Player: [Rolls 2D6, adds 0 for Wis] I rolled an 8, which gets me one question. "Who's really in control here?"

    GM: Okay, in the frozen moment after the first Goblin falls, you read their faces- the two in front look a little shocked until the guy in back throws a worried glance at the gate behind them and then yells something. Clearly he's the leader of this band, but the real boss is the big guy inside.

    Player: Right. I'm clearing a path to the guy in back then. Hack and Slash? [Rolls 2D6+2] I got an 8, and [rolls 1D10+1] 9 points of damage.

    GM: So another one is going to go down, but they're hacking and stabbing at you as well. YOu take three attacks of 3 points of damage.

    Player: With my shield and chainmail, I have an armor of 3, so I take nothing. I shake off their attacks laughing!

    GM: It's a glorious day for Ozruk! The remaining Goblins begin a fighting retreat to the gates. The one in front leaps on your shield, trying to bring you down.

    Player: to hell with that! I'm scraping him off with my axe. Hack and Slash? [Rolls 2d6+2, gets a 5] Crap, total failure!

    GM: The Goblin evades your attack, digs his sword into your shield and throws his weight on it, forcing you to let it go before you fall under the blade.[Move: Use up their Resources]The Goblin in charge takes advantage of the moment to drive his spear at you, doing three points, and then backs off.

    Player: Argh, without my shield that actually hurts [3 - 2 armor]. I'm down to 3 HP. Still, they're on the run. I'm killing the Goblin standing on my shield and looking for a spear. [Rolls Hack and Slash, gets a 9]. I do [rolls 1d10+1] 10 points of damage. I guess I still take damage right?

    GM: Yeah, the Goblin thrusts low with his sword, stabbing you in the leg for 3 points before you sweep his head off his shoulders with your axe. There are spears all around. What do have in mind?

    Player: I'm down to 2 hit points. Hmm. As the last Goblin runs for the gate, I'm picking up a spear, ready to throw, and yelling at him to surrender to me or I'll skewer him.

    GM: Okay, he's a reasonably intelligent enemy, make a Parley roll.

    Player: [Rolls 2D6 + 1 for Cha] I got an 11! Sweet.

    GM: He turns, hesitantly, hearing your threats and lowering his spear. "Promise to let me flee this place alive and I will tell you what lies beyond the gates".

    \n Steve of course nails it!

    Thanks for your interest, Chris! DW Basic is the red book, the PDF is the same file as we used for printing the interior of the book so there's no color, since we printed the interior of the book B&W. I can understand the confusion, sorry for that.

    The monsters in the Bloodstone Idol should get you started. If you want to move beyond those, here's an easy recipe: take a monster from the Idol, change each stat by +1 or -1, as you feel is appropriate, and give it all new moves. There you go, new monster, easy! I'd lean towards leaving Armor as is most of the time and if you bump up one stat reduce at least one more. Always lean towards paper tigers: an enemy that hits big for a little while is more interesting than one that just won't quit doing 1 point of damage.

    As people have mentioned, the Hack version (which is a little out of date, beware) has more monsters and classes. The Guild has even more! You'll also see a Paladin playbook download in your download section, that has a Red Book-updated Paladin.

    Is there anything particular about combat that concerns you?

    \n Yeah, it definitely feels like a missing piece to me; the GM has to learn by trial and error how much a group of characters can handle. Can a 1st level fighter take a level 1 Goblin? Sure, most likely. Can he take on four and survive? Assuming every creature can do damage (within the bounds of the fiction) numbers matter.

    I may have suggested this to you before but it seems like levels (if you keep them) should be tied in some way to the number of creatures and their other stats. I'm sure I'm retracing well-marked paths here, but using level as an abstract ranking of the overall challenge might be useful. A single, threatening skeleton (1 armor, 3 dmg) might be Level 1, while a small group of the same would be Level 3. That same skeleton wearing armor (armor 2) might be Level 2.

    Of course you could also draw on the whole "gang" concept from AW, which I'm sure you've already considered and possibly rejected. A group of Goblins is just the same one Goblin, but with gang bonuses worth +1 armor or +3 damage, etc. the ability to act on multiple fronts at once. This also gives the DM freedom to interpret the fiction more broadly- "you rolled 12 points of damage? Okay, three of the Goblins fall before your sword!"

    \n I'm all for monsters not having levels. What monsters do, fictionally, is what is important - the number really doesn't mean much; although, I suppose you could use monster levels as a component of moves. Including monster levels, on the other hand, begs lots of questions from GMs who are familiar with the complicated balancing schemas of other games (e.g. AD&D 2nd/3rd edition) and assume that it is a crucial part of DW. I think I'd rather not have a level than have a lot of people waste effort on trying to balance encounters numerically and how to design new monsters according to the numbers (when all the good stuff is in the description/instinct/moves). If nothing else, it saves you having to answer a bunch of questions.

    The way I've come to understand the rules (and I'm basing this on the Red Book rules, since I haven't looked at the playtest drafts lately) a character can take damage from all opponents that are fictionally positioned to do damage, unless a move (or some other rule) says otherwise.

    For Hack and Slash, the move says the character does damage to one opponent, if he rolls 7 or better, and he takes damage unless he rolls 10+, in which case they can choose to take in exchange for doing more damage. So in my example above, all four Goblins miss when Ogluk rolls 10+, and he kills one of them. On his next attack, he again kills one, but the three Goblins all get to do damage to him in the process.     I tend to think some things naturally swarm, and should be treated collectively, and this should apply to Dungeon World monsters too. Like a goblin by itself, unless it gets into an ambush or other advantageous situation, is never going to take on a well armed human. Similar to a bunch of nameless mooks or henchmen or whatever, these guys only pose a danger in groups. So my feeling would be if you hackNSlash some Goblins, you treat a group of them as a single entity with regard to dealing and taking damage and running away. Damage dealt to a group is sort of to the groups cohesion rather than sepcific wounds to specific individuals. But keeping track of individual's HP and damage dealt seems clunky and unneccessary

    \n I've had success in some of my designs using a basic damage level and then a (smaller) modifier for the number of enemies who can engage in a fight.

    For instance, let's say your monster does 6 points of damage, +1 per additional.

    So, one monster gets you for 6.

    But three of the same monster hits you for 8 (6+1+1).

    It's relatively simple, and can be calculated quickly, but still keeps it feeling "heroic" without making it a non-issue.

    But that's just one way: I'm not 100% sure what would be best for DW.

    \n Like John, I like monster levels as a guideline to how tough the monster is. When I'm going to quickly throw down a game, I look at the rough party level, then skim through the monster pages in the Adventurer's Guild version looking at monster levels for for ordinary opposition and a couple of higher level for tougher challenges. The level doesn't correspond exactly to Character level, but it doesn't need to. Monster level is just a rough guide for how tough the monster is compared to other monsters; it's a shorthand so I don't have to read full statlines for all monsters when I'm skimming. Removing them would hinder my "prep", in that it might mean that I have to do some rather than just run it as a pick up game.
