    Posted By: noclueWill, awesome episode. Particularly the latter half when you start discussing failure conditions and how they should resonate with character Beliefs. It felt like an epiphany moment for Jesse and CK.
    \nI certainly was an epiphany moment for me.

    I'm all about aesthetic design. When someone hands me a simple rule that will allow lots of permutations for storytelling, I always go, "AH-HA!"

    One complication of compromises is that they can spin out of control in a gazillion ways -- with everyone discussing them with their own eyes on what makes sense. The notion that you'd have compromises that are "realistic" outcomes an especially big trap, sine then you're playing that strange game of five people bringing in more and more detail to a fictional event that never happened but are trying to prove their case as to what would really happen.

    To keep the compromise element focused on Beliefs means all one has to do is say, "Okay, what's on the PC's character sheet?"

    I did this a couple of times in the session we played after the recording of the podcast. We'd finish a conflict with a compromise, Jesse's eyes would do that thing that says, "Oh, man, compromises, how are we going to do this?" and I'd ask, "What are the PC's Beliefs?" and wham-o, the notion that the compromise would be found in the limited orbit of the Belief made finding an inspired compromise inspired and quick.

    \n The issue for me is that I understand the GM's mandate of "Challenge the PC's Beliefs" but I didn't (don't) have a firm grasp of how to do that with the tool-set provided in Burning Empires. Most of the games I play these days Dogs in the Vineyard, Sorcerer, Primetime Adventures, etc work purely on character-to-character confrontation. Basically those games consist ONLY of Duels of Wits and Firefights, so to speak. So the way I challenge a character in those games is I have an major NPC get in their face about it. And we do that over and over from scene to scene.

    But because the GM only gets one (optional two) conflicts per Maneuver that read to me like I only get to challenge one Belief a maneuver. Which I knew HAD to be wrong. The Building Scene is the weird unfamiliar tool in the box because it rarely deals with direct confrontations between Major Characters. And I could never figure out how as the GM I was supposed to challenge a PC's Beliefs with Building Scenes, largely because the GM has almost no say in the content of PC's own Builder. But then I realized that isn't true. I get to set the failure condition. That's why I called it "Back Door GMing" I'm not throwing something AT the player as a confrontation but I AM attaching a price tag to it.

    That's weird and unfamiliar. Interesting outcomes are dirt simple from a direct confrontation because either Character A or Character B gets their way (or some compromise there of) and the story moves forward. But making a "Finance" roll interesting from a Belief standpoint is really, really hard.

    The issues are even more complicated when using GM Building scenes because I could never figure out what should be a builder vs. what I could get away with in a Color scene. That's because I would find myself in this awkward situation of wanting to do something that would probably be a Building Scene but if I rolled for it and failed it meant that the players didn't get challenged. It felt like I had to roll to introduce a Bang.

    For example, in Sorcerer I would just say, "You try to use your credit card and it's declined. You check your financial records and they've been ruined." That's a bang. No roll needed. But in BE that's VERY OBVIOUSLY a Building Roll very likely Finances or maybe even Security first linked into Finances. But, if I fail those rolls what happens? It's hard enough making Failure of those rolls interesting for the PCs but what the hell does Failure on those rolls for an NPC mean other than, "Oh you don't get to have this cool Bang."?

    Jesse

    \n Luke in your example the PC is present at least for the Close Combat/Surgery part. Which means we get to see their reaction. So even if I fail, it's still a big deal in the fiction.

    The problem I had was for Building Scenes that were attacks on PCs but required no response or reaction from the PCs unless the Building Scene succeeded. Failure usually render the whole thing a non-event. For example let's say I'm trying to financially ruin a PC. A direct attack correct. But I'm rolling Security and Finances. The player is obviously aware of this because they're sitting right there. But the character likely isn't because this is some fiscal hacker dude working at a terminal. So I roll and fail.

    The whole point was to see what the PC would do if their finances were ruined. But I failed. So it's rendered a non-event. At no point does the PC ever have to "jump" in response because it's not allowed to develop to the point where it would require their attention. It might as well not have happened at all.

    And since you're usually curious about this sort of thing, no this situation didn't happen in our game. However, what did happen was that I didn't do very many Building Scenes because of this phenomenon. I felt paralyzed by building scenes. I didn't want to even attempt them because of the risk of having them simply go no where.

    Also, I'm willing to accept that this is a failure on my part to understand how to use a Building Scene. Which is why I keep saying in the podcast: I thinking Building Scenes are key to making this game work.

    \n Jesse, you need to realize that every single piece of fiction you author is a weapon.

    In the example of the valkyrie squad Belief: It's a colour scene for your NPC to band women from the sodalis or whatever. If the players want to challenge it, they can spend their building rolls to counter you.

    In a building scene, do exactly the same stuff you would do in a colour scene to introduce fiction that the PCs cannot ignore. Even if your roll fails, that doesn't mean your attack is a failure, because you still have all the colour stuff that you can introduce. If you try to kidnap a PCs relationship and you fail the roll, that doesn't make it a non-issue.

    You guys also discussed fiction in Firefights. It's true that the narration isn't going to affect the rules in the Firefight, but everything you narrate can be used again after the fight to get you an advantage.

    Posted By: Luke WheelI'd like to offer a response to Jesse's assertion that a failed Observation/Security roll goes no where in my example.

    Failure is a result that must be colored like any other. So I fail the Observation test. I say, "You're being followed." I establish an incontrovertible fact in the fiction -- that happens to have unfavorable implications for me.

    I can leave it like that for another turn and then come back to it later and build on it. But more than likely, a player who is being followed will react.
