    \n "Annotated Edition" makes the most sense as a name, as that's what it is.

    There are two ways that Kickstarter projects account for shipping costs. One is to calculate it as part of the pledge level, the other is to ask the backers to each pay a given amount on top of their pledge to account for shipping. This latter method is almost a necessity if you want to account for differing expenses in global shipping. It's also not unheard of for a project to state that shipping is simply not included in the totals, and the backers will be asked to pay for it separately later on, when it's been calculated exactly.

    Given that one might end up doing extra payments for shipping anyway where physical products are concerned, I don't think that having physical books be a stretch goal add-on is that confusing. It just means that those who want the physical book pay their $25, plus whatever Ron states as the printing+shipping cost of the physical book. This is no more complex than having a pledge level for physical books, and then requesting extra payment for shipping. It's a bit unusual, of course, but then that seems to be the overall tone here anyway.

    Really, the basic reason for why Ron's project seems a bit peculiar compared to ordinary Kickstarters is that there just isn't very much complexity involved here. All the pledge levels are masquerading add-ons, as the core product only comes in one flavour and everything else is non-exclusionary (that is, it makes sense for a given customer to want any combination of them). The entire thing could be expressed as a single pledge level ("$1 and up: pay at least $25 to get the PDFs") with a price list for the perks you get for larger donations.

    \n I've not seen the video yet, so looking at things from a "what Lisa spends money on" point of view:

    Yes, I'd pay for annotated pdfs. I don't give a shit about the cover art, but from your point of view, that's not relevant. I've plunked down $25.

    Now, while I'm maybe willing to pay for shipping for the hard copies, I'd really rather you set up two more levels of the kickstarter, one for national shipping, one for international. First of all, that is what I'm used to from other kickstarters, and I am judging your kickstarter by the others I've supported. It's probably a safe assumption that I'm not the only one doing this. Second, I want to know how much money I'm paying. This is important for things like budgets, including budgets for other kickstarter projects and for whether I bump up my bid because you've added a cool extra. Third, I don't see how I can use Kickstarter to pay for postage in advance when I don't know what that'll be, and the thing about Kickstarter is that it makes paying easy. You want to make it as easy as possible for me to give you my money. (Seriously, after some disaster or other, the charity I donated to was the one that didn't make me jump through hoops. Make it easy for me to throw money at you.)

    As for why you need extra stretch goals: Don't leave money lying on the table. Let's do a quick compare and contrast:

    I love Jenna Moran's work, and I supported her IndieGoGo project for something like $9. All of her add ons came with that $9. I didn't need hard copy, and I had no reason to give her extra money. She didn't have a huge number of add ons, and could easily have gotten $25 out of me by adding them as things I got by pledging more. I'm not going to spend more than I have to to get what I want. There are a lot more IndieGoGo and Kickstarter projects clamoring for my wallet, and spending only $9 on hers means I can justify spending $15 on someone else's. (And that's before we get to other expenses like, oh, supporting my FLGS.)

    Fate Core? I was panting to throw money at this. I was panting so hard I kept checking the site to see if I could do it yet, and groaning in frustration when I realized I'd miscalculated and it was a day or so early. $1 for preview of the rules. $10 for all the pdfs. $30 for hard copy of the book. This is a good set of levels -- I'm in for $30, and a friend with more of a cash flow problem is likely to find $10 because it comes with All the PDFs.

    But, this project has gotten $20 more out of me, because, well, now there are three books worth of material. And, I get it all as PDFs, no matter what. I don't have to spend dime one more. But, for $20, I get the second book in hard copy as well as the first. Easy -- I don't have to calculate probably postage. I know what this will run me. I can pay $30 more for hard copy of the third book, and I may. It all depends on the stretch goals. And Evil Hat is very good at choosing stretch goals and pacing them. I supported the Dinocalypse novels kickstarter even though I'm not that into the concept because it got to the point where Evil Hat was offering 7 pdf novels for $25, which was just Too Much Stuff -- that doesn't take up shelf space -- for me to turn up my nose at.

    Evil Hat is also good at crunching the numbers to make sure that offering more stuff doesn't mean the project slides from the black to the red, and that is something to be aware of, and a valid reason for going easy on the stretch goals.

    Also, look at the Monsterhearts kickstarter. The video and the text make it very clear what the game's about. The stretch goals were well paced, right down to the final, semi-silly one that I knew probably wouldn't make it, and that was okay, given what it was.

    Will you have a thousand backers tossing money at you far beyond your most optimistic estimate? I don't know. But, you need to be prepared for best case scenario as well as worst case. There are a couple of projects out there that I think would have gotten more money if they'd had more stretch goals. I could see the cash flow tapering off as the final stretch goal was reached and a new one was not added.

    Now, to be fair, it may be that the folks running those projects accurately gauged what they could get folks to shell out. But, I suspect extra stretch goals would have helped.

      \n Uh, I'm pretty sure that the extra shipping thing is just a free instruction in the text, not an enforced rule. This means that Ron can just instruct people to not pay any shipping costs until the stretch goal is reached, at which point everybody can add that to their pledge. Were it me, I'd even phrase it such that getting your complimentary paper copy of the book depends on you paying the postage, clearly and unequivocally. (That is, it's not "it would be nice if you paid a bit extra to cover postage", but rather "you can get the book on paper by pledging $X extra, so as to cover my postage expenses". ) This way there'd be no griping afterwards if somebody decides they don't want to pay more, or whatever.

      (The recent Glorantha book Kickstarter did all this, basically, and it didn't seem that complex to me. At one point they decided to split the book they were selling into two volumes, which meant that the shipping expenses for American customers increased by $5 each, while those for international customers didn't. They just told everybody to add that amount to their pledges to cover the shipping. I presume that if you don't pay the instructed amount, they wouldn't ship your book; a sketchy thing to do when you've already sold the book itself, but I guess the implication in this kind of crowdfunding structure is that the customer is responsible for the shipping expense, even if it changes as the project evolves. After all, you could just withdraw your backing if the project changes in a bad way in the middle.)

      In other words, Ron: add that postage information in there ($X for Americans, $Y internationally, or whatever), but also advice the backers to not mind that to begin with. Then later you might get to declare that oh thousand jubilations, we managed the stretch goal, so everybody remember to add that postage expense to your pledges. Remember that you can change your pledge amounts in Kickstarter at any point, so it's no problem for the backers to add the postage later, as long as they're following the proceedings enough to notice the request.

      As far as I know the shipping costs are calculated as part of the total collected; I've never heard of Kickstarter supporting a "side fund" where you could put part of the contributions aside and not have them count as part of the collected total.

      \n Perhaps I should explain that trending thing a bit:

      The Kicktraq "trend" is just a mathematical function that draws a straight line from your current speed of acquisition to a theoretical end-point. In other words, it shows you how much money you would make by the end of your project if the speed of acquisition remained constant from now until the end of the project. There's no secret knowledge or complex prediction going on in there. Saying that the total is "currently trending towards" some high value is technically quite accurate, but a layman will then come in and think that this is some sort of prediction when it's not. I don't quite know why the trend is pushed as an indicator in Kicktraq, except that it's easy to compute and it gives you nice headlines when you can scream about how this or that project is currently trending towards 10 billion dollars.

      The "projection" feature, on the other hand, attempts to do some more complex prediction about where a project is going to end up.

      \n Hi Ricardo,

      I appreciate the thought, but I hope you can see that it puts me in an impossible situation. Either I help with explanations and people say, "Oh, so you have to explain it," or I don't, and they wail, "But you aren't helping!" I guess I have to choose which way to be damned.

      However, I really think the Kickstart is fairer than the vibe you're referencing. I don't think that offering interaction, examples, and community conversations about an innovative game is the same, which is to say, offering less-than-good content and continually promising to make it good.

      Because it was and is innovative, Sorcerer in text is hard for many to grasp no matter how straightforward the text could be. It's evidently the case that a lot of readers have trouble with it, but then with only a little bit of conversational focus, discover that, "Oh wow, it says what you're saying, right here!"

      I'm offering a variety of means to heighten that focus, across a lot of people at once, and a person can participate in that at what strikes me as a very reasonable cost. I'm not even requiring full price for each of them, because they stack for $10 after the first $50, and the books come along with the deal too. And if you just want the books with the new covers, then you don't need to pledge for those options at all.

      Your final point is valid. I like the phrase I hit upon earlier in the thread: "a" Sorcerer Kickstart, not "the" Sorcerer Kickstart. It's not actually a recruitment drive, it's a fundraiser, in-and-out. It blew up on me a little when I shifted to book fulfillment, and fumbling the pledge rewards interface causes me some problems (learning as I go ...), but the basic philosophy is still the same - yes, mainly targeting the people who do know and want to enhance their enjoyment of the game.

      I appreciate your candor, and I don't want this to be a defensive post. I hope instead that it's a chance to let you see where I'm coming from.
