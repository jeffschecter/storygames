To sell this kind of stunt in UA, I have to rely more on my authority as GM. In practice, that authority comes from the players' trust in me, which of course is something I have to earn.     I think that difference between HEX and UA is part of the reason that my UA games have far more structure than my HEX games. Players still have a lot of freedom in my UA games, but their responses tend to be more predictable than the responses of players in a HEX game. That predictability may make the adventure feel as if everything is scripted, when in reality it's not.\n

      \n

    • You can earn a Style Point for playing one of your character's Flaws in such a way that it affects the game.
    • You can earn a Style Point for making a good movie. This is the subjective one. Anything that pleasantly surprises or entertains me fits the bill. Also, in this year's scenario, the players were given the assignment to focus on action and keep the audience on the edge of their seats, so they got Style Points for that, too. It's easy to figure out if you're adding action to the movie. I try to give examples of things that keep an audience on the edge of their seats (cliffhangers, when you accept failure for your character, when you the player decide to put your character at a disadvantage), but it's hardly an exhaustive list.     The "no" is that it's very difficult to pull this off if you have to fight the game system. For example, in HEX, it's fairly difficult to kill a PC, so character survivability isn't much an issue. This frees the player to concentrate on adding to the story rather than saving his character's butt. I often tell players up front that HEX is not a tactical game, and that becomes obvious soon after the adventure starts.

    Unknown Armies is different. It's a gritty horror system. Even though it's not tactical, in the sense that the direction in which your character is facing is unimportant, it's still a horror game. My definition of the horror genre is that characters are engaged in a struggle just to keep what they have -- trying to come out ahead isn't even an option. UA reinforces this mood, and if I undermine that, I compromise the system's ability to tell the kind of story I want to tell. So in UA, players have no choice but to spend much of their time fending for their characters as best they can.

    I mentioned at the top of this list (post #2) that I can only constrain the PCs in a UA game if I win the players' trust. Even then, the constraints that I can impose are limited, or I'll come across as a bully or a cheater.

    But in a game system where character survival is less of an issue, the answer to your question is a resounding "yes, that's all there is to it."

    Yeah, your point about fighting the system is a big part of the hurdles I'm seeing. I think it extends all the way into the realm of expected possible outcomes, whether those are clear from the system or not. Like, what do I expect to happen to my character if the GM mind-controls them? There's a lot that factors into that! Mechanical quantities, the described nature of the NPC doing the mind-controlling, any important decisions on the horizon that I'll miss out on if I have no agency, etc. Constructing a safe space for this sort of thing seems to require at least three different elements in harmony -- system, meta-level communication, and the state of the fiction. All 3 need to give us the thumbs-up before we can enjoy how mind-control makes for a good movie.

    I think part of what makes this stressful and error-prone for a lot of GMs is working backward from the plan without first nailing down the process. Because you, Todd, already know that "take control of your character and give you style points" is a thing you might do, you can set the players' expectations on this correctly, and scan your ruleset for compatibility with that. Whereas a GM who ad-libs that same solution (grab control of character, give style points) in a moment of "this would make a good movie!" inspiration is more likely to run into unanticipated hurdles.

    As a wannabe game-designer, I think there's a lot of value in nailing down such processes for GMs in actionable form. The techniques for (e.g.) making a good movie, the steps to set group agreement on how the techniques will be used, and the mechanics to support those techniques in play.

    The original request was for me to give examples of how I mind-control someone or have them trigger a trap in such a way that's palatable to a player. OK, maybe that's a different question entirely, but it feeds into the one above.

    Example of mind control: This year's HEX adventure features a Big Bad who's very skilled in sorcery. I decided that his brand of sorcery also includes mind control, even though there's no canonical support for that conclusion.

    This Big Bad is present in only two scenes: a car chase early in the adventure, and the climactic scene at the end. The PCs will definitely meet the Big Bad at the end. In contrast, there's no requirement for them to meet him -- or even discover he's present -- in the car chase. However, if a PC takes an action that will have him encounter the Big Bad in the car chase (for example, jumping onto the truck that's being chased and climbing inside, which is were the Big Bad is located), then by GM fiat, he'll meet the Big Bad and the Big Bad will mind-control him.

    The mind control is hinted at, but not explicit. I describe the Big Bad's piercing stare and the fact that his eyes are entirely black. I toss the PC's player a Style Point and say, "You can't bring yourself to look away from those eyes. The audience sees a close-up of your face. As they watch, your eyes also turn entirely black." Then I cut back to the other PCs to build suspense. When I come back to the mind-controlled PC, he isn't forced to do anything. He just stands there. He can use an action every round to regain his will, and if he succeeds, he can do anything he wants. (Without exception, a player who breaks free always concludes on his own that the best course of action is to get out of the truck, which suits my purposes perfectly.) Or other PCs can use their actions to try to rescue this PC from the truck. Either way, the mind control is broken as soon as the Big Bad is off camera.

    Or so it seems.

    In the climactic scene at the end of the adventure, I toss that same player another Style Point. (I use poker chips for Style Points, which is why I'm saying I "toss" them.) Even though that PC may be nowhere near the Big Bad, the PC's eyes again turn all black and he starts to attack his comrades. Again, the PC can roll every round to break the control. The mind control typically lasts for no more than two rounds, which is enough for my purposes. It's more for drama than anything else. This time, when the control is broken, the break is permanent -- that character will not be targeted for mind control again.

    The astute reader will notice that I apply constraints to my control. The character can't stop the control from starting, but always an opportunity to end it. There are multiple ways that other PCs can help end the control, such as by temporarily incapacitating the mind-controlled PC or by targeting the Big Bad. The control lasts only long enough to serve the story. It always happens early enough in the climactic scene that the mind-controlled character, once free, can still contribute meaningfully to the story. Almost invariably, the meaningful contribution is some sort of payback on the Big Bad for having mind-controlled the character in the first place. Which is both dramatically appropriate and supported by ample literary precedent.

    I follow the same considerations when I use GM fiat to have a PC spring a trap, get captured by bad guys, or suffer any number of other dramatic-sounding inconveniences.

    It's the start of the a round of the final climactic scene. All the PCs are engaged in a huge fight with the Big Bad and his minions. The Big Bad won't be standing for much longer.     So I turn to Ted first and ask him what his character does. Normally when this happens, I tell the player, "OK, then for dramatic reasons, you'll go last this round. I'll come back to you at the end." I'm not penalizing the player when I do this. I want to make sure that every player gets an action in the final round, and Ted's success would make that impossible.

    Unfortunately, I couldn't guess what the other five players had planned. My fear was that by the time we'd come back to Ted, the other players might have taken out the Big Bad and won the day. Then that deprives Ted of his chance to shine. So I rejected the option of the "dramatic delay." Instead, I decided to turn this into a teaching moment.

    I said to Ted, "Ted, do you realize that if your plan works as you described, none of the other players will have anything to do for the rest of the round?" Ted looked around the table as if he were seeing the other players for the first time. When he said, "Oh!" I knew the light had gone on.

Ted thought for a moment and came up with an alternate plan that worked rather well and still left bad guys standing. It turned out to be the final round of the combat, and all of the PCs got an action.

    [Ponders] Now that I think about it, David, I don't always constrain a player before he tries something. There's one case where I'll do it afterwards, but usually as a suggestion to the player, not as GM fiat. Here's an example.

    In one of my recent sessions of the Hollow Earth Adventure with the car chase, one of the characters is a weird scientist who can invent or modify devices to do all sorts of pseudoscientific stuff. During the car chase in this particular session, the scientist's player had his character somehow modify the car that he was a passenger in and that was chasing the truck. (I say "somehow" because the movie audience didn't see the modification. The player declared that his character was under the dashboard and that only his lower extremities were visible. Consequently, neither the audience, nor the other players, nor I knew what modifications the character had made.) The player told me he'd tell me when the modification takes effect. I'm fine with that approach.

    Later, another character announced that he was pushing in (activating) the cigarette lighter in the car. The pseudoscientist player jumped up at that point and said, "And the car accelerates like a rocket!" It was a cool dramatic effect.

    Later in the scene, the player used the car's cigarette lighter for a second pseudoscientific purpose, I forget what. No problem there. But when he tried to use the lighter a third time in the same scene, I told him, "That'll be the third time the audience will see the lighter do something unexpected. My fear is they're starting to get bored of the lighter. Since your character can modify any device, instead of using the lighter to do what you wanted to do, can you use something else?" The player obliged.

    Game mechanically, my request made absolutely no difference. The new device did exactly what he wanted the lighter to do. But the player understood why I was asking him to do what I did.

      \n It occurs to me that the imaginary audience is a proxy for the other players, but in a way that makes it safe. The imaginary audience isn't going to judge you - it's less intense than trying to perform for your fellow players, even though that's the practical consequence.

      The other thing that occurs to me is that having a fixed outcome relieves a very particular sort of pressure from the group. In a 'no fixed outcome' game, we're all hoping to produce an outcome that's also a good starting point for what happens next, we just don't know what it is. (Either that or a terminal outcome that is somehow satisfying.) We hope it will emerge naturally. But there are lots of ways for it not to happen - the group getting split up, inadvertently leeching the dramatic tension out of the scene, hitting an unsatisfying terminal outcome either mechanically (e.g. unexpected player death) or narratively (a character's driving motive is obviated, etc).

      This makes me wonder about the 'world of constraints' and what they do to the game. A GM could say, "You're not splitting up, ever. I'm not going to split you up, and I need your help in coming up with reasons and excuses to stay together, whatever's happening." The GM fudging the dice is a sort of invisible constraint, "I don't want you to die just yet, because that would be a failure to produce a good next starting point." And also, I suppose, some constraints are achieved mechanically - a very non-lethal combat system makes it hard to kill players accidentally, GUMSHOE's take on finding clues.       My own past failures to take control of the story in ways that weren't violations stemmed in part from the combo of (a) I had certain things that I wanted to happen, and (b) I didn't have all of those things written down and planned out with the requirements to make them happen.

      Accordingly, when an opportunity arose for the players to put a Plan of mine in jeopardy, or when they in fact started down such a path, I was often a bit late in recognizing this. "Hey, cool, the players boarded the badguys' ship! Now they decided they could use it to sneak into enemy turf! What a neat idea! They're smart. Oh, crap, wait, I can't let them go to enemy turf because my Plan requires them to get to Haven!"

      So maybe (b) is the difference.

      Maybe if I had an outline in front of me that said "PCs go to Haven", I'd notice the first sign of deviation, and slip in, "The badguys set fire to their ship while fleeing it," before the players could start investing in plans of infiltration.

      Or maybe not.

      I'm inclined to think that's a big part of the equation, but not the whole thing.

      Maybe, if you had such an outline in front of you, you would recognize the need to be upfront with this; OK, players, here's what will happen in this scenario; you will all go to Haven someway, before actually confronting the enemy on his home-turf.
      ________________________________________
      EXAMPLE:

      In a convention tournament I wrote and arranged some years ago, I had a clear idea that it was not to focus on where the characters went; it would instead focus on what happened to them, and between them, while going there.

      So we, the GMs, told the players up-front that the scenario was not about them taking alternate routes; There is only the one track to follow! The track was well known, so we had a map of it, telling them of all the terrain dangers beforehand, and which beasts in the mountains were dangerous, and how to treat them.

      And finally; we told them this was a scenario about doing the right thing, and (hopefully) succeeding in it.

      All the scene-framing were written beforehand, every single scene, so the GMs would be free to concentrate on other issues

      It was, of course, railroading galore!

      So; what was it all about?
      Why on earth railroad players like this?
      What could possibly come out of such dumb-ass game-play?

      Well ...
      - the characters were villagers, and the village was in dire danger. The spirits were against them. So they had to go to the old altar in he mountains, and make a blood-sacrifice of a living goat there, before the next full moon.
      - so the characters were chosen to do this, them being young and healthy, and very well equipped for the trek.
      - and one of the characters had this brother, that was "slow" (mentally retarded). The elders sent him with them, telling them to let no harm come to the simpleton (and he was strong, so he had no problem trekking).

      The GMs played the simpleton, and was instructed to play that NPC as much as possible (the heavy railroading made this easy; taking focus away from route-choices and scene-framing). They were told to touch the other players a lot, giving hugs and commending them, as the dumb one. Creating emotional responses and relationships between the dumb and his peers from the village. Those relationships were what the whole game was about.

      The GMs were of course warned that all this touching would be awkward at first, but were instructed to persist, and to find different ways of doing it, and being simple and happy by doing it. And if some character/player tried to shy away, they would sulk and go into tears, and saying that "Jobool don't like me! He never hugs me! I am dumb, and he don't like me! For being dumb! No one really like a dumb one!" - etc. And then, if given a small touch on the hand (or something, perhaps a compromise proposed by the GM), he would declare his great love for that character! Instant forgiveness! Persistent relationship-building, massaging the group dynamics through the childish simpleton, was the cornerstone of the scenario. And it was important to portray the simpleton as childish; the innocent "spirit of their village", which they were to protect.

      And then, coming to the mountain top, with the age old altar, where they were to sacrifice the goat to save the village, they had lost the goat (Dang! Isn't that typical!). It had been eaten by a dragon on the way (pre-planned scene, and nothing they could do about it). They continued after loosing the goat, of course, having no choice but trying to plead with the spirits (but no spirit ever heed such pitiful pleadings). The moment they got there, and no pleading worked, the GMs were instructed to let the dumb say he wanted to sacrifice himself for the village.

      That's the moment when the scenario really happened! Relations blossomed into full fruit, emotions flared, and they (predictably) balked at the idea! But the dumb one insisted that he had always been given gifts in the village; "Give me gifts! Good life! Lots of porridge! Me happy!" He had never been able to give anything in return. "Now, this my chance to give back, to mummy, and village! I never live to be strong, without village! You give gift of life to me! I give life back, as gift!" And the GM was instructed, after a time of debating this, to lay down on top of the gaming table, and instruct the characters/players to cut his wrists: "I lay here, on altar! You kill me! I bleed for you! Love you!"

      The death-scenes in these sessions were, without exceptions, scenes of high emotions and tears. The dumb one laying on the table, getting cold, getting fearful of "the dark", dying slowly while his blood dripped for the village, and the characters/players stood in circle trying to comfort him; a lot of touching, holding hands, hugging, tears and repressing tears (to be brave and smiling for him), soothing voices ...

      Of course they succeeded! He was sacrificed, and the spirits were appeased. I believe it was the toughest success any of those players had ever had. It was a scenario that focused on the tension between individual and collective, and what meaning there may be in sacrificing yourself for a greater good.

      It is one of the strongest scenarios I have ever written and led.
      ___________________________________

      In this scenario it was easy for the players to contribute, due to them knowing what the field of play was. They even contributed with scenes where the characters discussed alternate routes to the mountain, inventing age old routes that had been used before, but concluding that they would be too dangerous now ...

      David; my point is;
      - if it is crucial that the characters go to "Haven"; SEND THEM THERE!
      - if you want them to be in on it; TELL THEM!

      Don't muddle about. Concentrate your creative energy, and the energy of the players, on what matters to the real drama.

      I forgot to mention that underway to that mountain, the simpleton saved the life of other characters in several of the groups. This was a scenario were players, when loosing their characters, had to leave the game-room. The players were informed this would happen, if they lost their character. It's a technique I use from time to time, to make death a bit more "real", in scenarios were I want an extra acuity to the inter-play. This made some players very much in debt to the simpleton, and they could have offered to stand in his place, of course. They did not. None of them. I believe that is a testimony to the strength of his arguments and convictions.

      If a player really had offered to take the place, and sacrifice his character, it might have spelled for a totally different ending. It would have been fine with me. When planning such things, it is important to set a limit to what you decide beforehand. Some things must be be left open, to be the result of inspired and spontaneous interaction. I seldom decide the outcome of any individual encounter. The altar-scene was loaded with arguments for the simpletons sacrifice, but it was not decided that it would end that way. A player character sacrificing himself is fine with me. It would make for a strong scene anyway. The player would have had to take the place on the altar, and be center to the death-scene, and that would have been a strong scene to play out too.

      But the arguments made it otherwise. Thinking through arguments beforehand has given me some nice and sharp edges to some scenarios. It's a great thing to do; having NPCs discussing with the PCs, meeting them with really strong logic, or really strong convictions. It's great fun too!
