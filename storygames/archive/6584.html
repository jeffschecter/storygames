\n I'm working on an ars ludi post and I need some help.

Basically I want to suggest indie games that an average dyed-in-the-wool D&D group could try and succeed at. Insert usual caveats that all groups are different, people like different things, etc -- we're shooting for a best guess.

Succeed is the key word here. It doesn't do a lot of good to say "ooh, go play Shock! It's rocks!"* when that's such a big leap from what they're used to that they don't have a good time and never want to try anything experimental ever again.

So far I'm hovering on:

Agon -- Includes mechanical combat that feels like a traditional game (you can run around fighting monsters, your spear does X damage). Lots of resource management and advancement, so the players might not initially notice they are competing in a very un-D&D like way. Players can also ease into steering the plot, framing scenes to get advantages, etc. One downside is abstract defeat, which may prove slippery at first.

In A Wicked Age -- A farther leap, but wins because it includes an adventure seed to get players started (would you normally choose to play a virgin sacrifice? No, but the oracle prompts you). Even if they don't immediately grok negotiation they can fall back on just inflicting damage and learn as they go. The GM sets scenes and can use NPCs to provoke situations so the players aren't dropped in the deep end.

There are some games that seemed like a good idea but I'm rejecting for this purpose (they would be in the advanced course, but not the starter kit). For example Truth & Justice: great game, but being able to bring in any trait as a bonus if you can explain it is probably too big a jump for players used to literal rule systems. It's likely to feel too easy to gain an arbitrary advantage.

So what games am I missing? Suggest some (or reject the ones I've got) and tell me why.


* Yes it's true, Shock rocks.

    Posted By: JesseIf they are going to have a problem with "trait as a bonus" you're going to have a tough time.
    \nIt's not so much trait as bonus that's the issue, it's negotiable amount of bonus depending on how many traits I can wedge in with description -- that's where I've seen new players get bogged down either wondering if they are cheating or thinking the whole thing is just arbitrary ("well, I'm Determined, so, err, I guess that's another +2"). Compare it to something like SotC where there's a cost to bring in each trait.

    It's just a very different system than D&D, which has little or no negotiable bonuses, just literal situational bonuses (I am 10th level, my sword is +2, I have surprise).

    At least that's been my experience.

    \n I'd suggets TSoY or Burning Wheel as initroductory games, otherwise my approach is to pick those lovely indie-mechanics and add them to D&D instead, infiltrating the game. I've done this quite succesfully with my own campaign, though my players didn't need any convincing.

    For example you might introduce Keys from TSoY as a way of gaining XP - I know somebody already did this, but I can't remember who and where.

    You can also add the Gift of Dice in this manner: Create a pool of d4s. For any d20-roll, another player can give a d4 as a positive modifer. In order to give the die, the players must fulfill a criteria, this could be some narrative element etc.

    Also in my campaign about teenage wizards at The Great School of Magic in Glantri, players gain minor spellboosts, if they describe how their master taught them to cast the spell. Is adds flavor and focuses on the various Noble Wizard Houses and their ways of doing magic.

    Furthermore I changed the mechanic "Aid Another", which is normally used in combat, where you spend an action to give a fellow a +2 bonus. Now you gain a +2 bonus to a any skillcheck, if you play out a non-conflict scene, in which you gain the assistance of another character. We have several library-scenes, where they discuss exams, ancient tomes and the latest gossip while studying, as this gains them the necessary +2 modifiers for some otherwise very difficult skillchecks.

    From Mortal Coil we added the MagicTokens, that allow you to state a fact about the setting. We use them for interplanar adventures, where the players can add all sorts of cool details to the setting.

    So borrow mechanics from other games, add them one by one, and let your players know, that these houserules are added to make their characters more cool and give them more options - as none of the added rules can be used against them.

    \n Hi!
    \n I think TSoY is a great example of a game that straddles the line between full on story game and task-based tactical game. Also, a lot of stuff is accessible, but it still forces the player to voice what is important to them and then go get it!
    \n Barring that, I have had tremendous luck introducing trad gamers to DITV. I try and frame the game concept as a cross between playing a Paladin in D&D, playing Cain from that Kung Fu TV show and/or one of the good guys from the A-Team. I mean you wander from town to town saving the town. A comparison to the Blues Brothers might work too. The only resistance I ever got from running ditv was from people who did not want to play a dogmatic jerk. There are techniques you can use to assuage those fears, so let me know and I'll hook you up if you want to go that way, k?
