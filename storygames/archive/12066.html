\n So, I happened across this on YouTube:
http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wH1wXRvhSNA&feature=related

He's talking about video games, but I would argue that this also applies directly to tabletop rpgs. I think it also summarizes why the ubiquitous "indie game about relationships" has never really worked for me. I can front load a cool situation wherein the duke is my brother, he's holding my sister hostage, and that's the MacGuffin that gets things rolling. I can pretend that I care about my sister, and to hate my brother for being a big a-hole, but until I've had meaningful interactions with those characters in the game (usually over the course of long term play), I don't feel any more attachment than if I was after the magical doodad in D&D.

I dunno. I found it somewhat revelatory. Food for thought anyway.

    \n He makes very good points. Basically, a relationship in a game only works if the player develops it himself, not if it's pre-set.

    However, with RPGs there are some interesting counterpoints...first of all, we make our own characters, our own protagonists.

    If Infamous tells me I should care about my sister, but I don't, that sucks. However if I made that character and decided he cares about his sister, I probably did it for a reason. Flags and all that.

    Second of all, yeah, it works better when you meet the NPC at the same time as the protagonist does. But then, in a videogame, again, the choice whether the protagonist is going to bond with that character or not is left to the game. In a RPG, the choice is the player's.

    So it doesn't fully apply. But worth thinking about.

    \n For what it's worth, I find that player-defined relationships tend to carry a lot of weight. If my whole character concept is a guy who's trying to rescue his wife from terrible slavery, I've bought into that so hard it's pretty unlikely I won't fight tooth-and-nail for her in the fiction.

    I've seen this again and again in games I've run. In my last Dogs game, I had a player write up a few sentences about an NPC he didn't like, and why. When I had the NPC show up in play, the character felt such intense hatred towards him that they nearly killed each other in the very first scene where he was introduced. The NPC ended up dead; the player character barely survived the experience. But it was clear that for this player defeating this other guy who had just showed up in play for the first time was so important he was willing to lose his character over it.

    \n Paul,

    I've found that that is absolutely true with people who trust they are going to get a return on that investment. I've found that a lot of gamers have been burned in the past by either simply having that material ignored ("Hey GM, my character's all about his sister's pending marriage to man he disapproves of!" GM: "That's great, so you all meet in a bar....") or abused ("Hey GM, my character has an autistic daughter and it's putting stress on her marriage...", GM: "So you come home to find you husband and daughter murdered...."). The result is that these people learn to stop investing. When they are asked to create material like that, they do so because they "have to" and then they fabricate something that they know is emotionally safe and that they can stand a safe distance from.

    I absolutely agree with the basic premise that just listing relationships is not enough. They have to mean something to you out of the gate and that requires a bit of honesty and self-reflection. I also think having those relationships questioned, challenged and re-evaluated mid-play is perfectly fine and indeed a great idea.

    On the other hand, maybe I'm just easily emotionally imaginative. I created a character who had a lost love in his back story in the Diaspora game I'm playing now. The GM (knowing me very well) had her show up. He played her as a bit distant perhaps barely remembering me. I felt really bad for my character. I can easily imagine and empathize with having your illusions shattered like that.

    \n Actually I think he's wrong. Like utterly, utterly wrong. (And I think he mixes up sympathy with empathy. For ease of reading, I say empathy when I mean a direct overlap of emotions between myself and somebody else. And I say sympathy when I mean a imagined construct of what somebody else must be feeling.)

    When he says that in a game I need to care about the secondary characters the way the protagonist does, I just shake my head. That's like saying I need to feel I am looking at real lava to worry about my protagonist falling into it and dieing. It's completely ridiculous.

    I do think though that roleplaying games can succeed in the kind of thing he seems to argue for, but mostly because of their very flexible nature. I think we react and connect with fictional characters because certain details, certain contexts and descriptions are recognisable and ring true to us, personally, and trigger the kind of reaction that goes beyond mere sympathy to explicit empathy.

    And with RPGs in general, and Story Games in particular, you can do that. You can develop the kind of personalised character, context and situation that makes you able to feel as the fictional character. In fact I think that many gamers naturally gravitate towards that kind of gaming experience. Most gamers want to empathise with their characters. Which is one of the reasons why games where you might play far outside your comfort zone (e.g. violent bastard pirates or subservient housemaids) feel squicky and weird to some gamers. They naturally assume that character empathy is the goal of every gamer.

    The fun thing about RPGs is, that you start off sympathising (as in imagining) what your character is going through and - if the game is as well-tuned to your personality as most honestly-played games tend to become - you find the distance between your character's emotions and your own disappearing. That is if you let it occur. Meaning the player's compliance is the last and final (but far from the only) barrier that keeps you from empathising with a character.
