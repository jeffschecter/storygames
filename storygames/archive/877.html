This thread (design elements for a "gateway" game to introduce people to RPG/story games) got me thinking. Here's a sketch of my thoughts so far.

As a reminder/for those not following the other thread, the design elements as discussed there boiled down to approximately these:

1. Numerous hooks into popular culture. Whatever story is used follows a well-known and easy-to-follow genre format, such as a quest, a murder mystery, or television procedural.

2. Does not call itself a "roleplaying game" anywhere, but it is one anyways.

3. Has rules that can be grasped as quickly and easily as Scrabble's rules, which fit on the inside of the box. The game uses a minimum of jargon, and what game terms it does use, are well defined.

4. The game is bought in a box, and all that is needed for play is included.

5. Can be played by any number of people, from two up to six, possibly eight.

6. Fun begins when the box is opened and does not generally let up. Can get up and running into play in 5-10 minutes

7. Extensible in interesting and fun ways, by the buyer or through add-on sales, but not crippleware.

8. Can provide a satisfying play experience in short chunks of time. Can be played and enjoyed in one sitting of two hours or less. The game can be continued through further play, but doesn't have to be.

9. Doesn't require one person to act as rules referee and player of every character not played by the other players and creator of situation and color and setting authority.

10. There's no 'character sheet'. All character attributes can be represented with the physical tools that come with the game.

OK, so here's the sketch. I call the game "Gateway to Adventure: A game of collaborative adventure storytelling" (fulfilling requirement 2). The format is board-and-card (fulfilling requirement 4). The setup consists of setting up the board, placing the packs of cards in the clearly indicated spots, and assembling characters. Character creation is also done via cards, drawing for things such as role (fighter, leader, brains of the outfit, fixer etc.), abilities, equipment, background, strengths, weaknesses, and a dilemma/goal for each character (not necessarily all of the above). These cards would replace the "character sheet" and sit in front of each player (fulfilling requirement 10).
