Why I'm psyched about Charles the Bald

Matthijs
July 2006 edited July 2006 in Story Games
Some of you may remember Charles the Bald is Superf***ed*, my entry for the 2005 Iron Game Chef competition. I've been playing it a lot lately, and I'm thrilled. Here's why.

First of all, it's smooth. It works just the way it's supposed to, which makes me immensely proud as a game designer.

Second, it's taught me a lot about player feedback in groups. In CtB, every turn, you award other players tokens for their stories, and you have to tell them why. In some groups, this is used spontaneously and just for kicks, which results in over-the-top stories where players compete to come up with hilarious and shocking episodes. In other groups, players use this feedback actively to make each other better storytellers. So in some groups, players might go: "That was cool when that guy's head exploded!", while in others, they say: "I liked how you used that flashback scene to show the king's motivation". After just one round, there's a group consensus about what stories should be like.

(Has anybody heard about the forming, storming, norming, performing model of team development? That's exactly what goes on in CtB).

Third, win conditions and rules encourage good storytelling. It's very easy to make games where you have to choose between telling a good story and trying to actually win. In CtB, you get a good score only if other players agree your story's good. If you try to get away with rubbish just to place tokens on the map - you'll lose hard.

Fourth, it's a great channeling game, where one sort of desire/motivation is channeled by the rules into a very different form of behavior. Players come to the table with a desire to win. That desire is channeled by the rules into story-telling behavior. (I like channeling; it's both theoretically interesting and practically useful).

Fifth, it keeps challenging you, every turn. The cards say whose story you're telling, and how it has to end; the board tactics give you choices about where the story should take place; and the group consensus says what sort of stories will work in this group. These are all creative constraints, none of which are easily predictable. There's no planning - it's your turn, now go!.

Sixth, people are giving me all sorts of feedback. From wanting to take home the playtest versions, to giving me good advice on further development, to having dreams about the game and incorporating bits into their own designs.

This last part is how I really know I'm on to something. Plagiarism doesn't lie.



* Note: The version currently on the web isn't up-to-date. The most important changes are that schtick narration has been removed, and there are only five or six locations on the board. Anyone interested in a newer version, contact me.
