    \n Everway (spelling corrected!)
    Wizards of the Coast
    1995

    The GM in this game was VERY strong. They could basically make everything up as they went along. It was a diceless game. It used two decks of cards to add randomness, a fortune deck and a vision deck.

    The fortune deck included 36 card that could be pulled when some kind of task resolution came up. The GM interpreted what it meant.

    The vision deck of 90 cards included suggestive scenes with interesting questions to spur creativity. Again it was interpreted by the GM.

    Sounds like this game required a very creative and active referee. The cards did not mechanically make anything happen but just suggested things. In this way I wonder if cards are really part of the system. The GM making shit up with no set limits on their power is the system. The cards were just ancillary. Still it was a seminal game that broke the mode of role play games and suffered the economic penalty for that reason.

    \n Fairy Tale Assassin League
    Chris Engle/Hamster Press
    2013

    In this game all the players are equal. Most play a fairy tale hero in modern day Las Vegas. One player runs the bad guy. The games is all about how the heroes defeat the bad guy. It uses character and location cards to make a game board. It uses action and scene cards to guide play. Players want to win action and scene cards to win victory points to determine who gets the last word in the game.

    There are 4 scene cards guide play through a coherent story.

    There are up to 54 action cards that have suggestive pictures and phrases. Players have a hand of five action cards. They draw back to five cards as they use them.

    Each turn a player plays an action card or scene card and make up what happens next. Other players play a card and add to or alter the story. Players roll to see who gets the victory points but actions generally happen regardless of the roll.

    The cards are suggestive, like they are in Every Way, but each player has full creative power. The cards limit choices which helps people be more creative (one of life's little paradoxes). The cards are central to play because of this limitation.

    \n Magic the Gathering
    Wizards of the Coast
    1993

    Not a role play game but definitely the most influential of card games.

    Players build decks by running on the treadmill of collectibility. The cards carry the rules of play and play is all about gathering power and using to kill your single opponent. The game has ever expanding rules which without resets become unplayable.

    The cards and card play are everything with this game. There isn't really isn't any story.

    The useful thing here is rules on the card.

    \n Gloom
    Atlas Games
    2004

    The players are trying to kill off their characters as fast as possible by playing cards that describe describe events. The cards are clear plastic and lay on top of characters cards. A neat mechanic.

    The only story part of the game is that players are supposed to narrate how the disaster happens. This is not mechanically meaningful so if people skip it or half ass it doesn't really matter.

    This is a cool little game but I'm not certain it has much to say to role playing. It is all about hit points rather than story.

    Currently $10

    Skills (and spells) are divided into four types, each type is assigned to a suit in a deck of playing cards. You draw four cards and may play up to all four on each task - if the suit matches, you get the face value of the card, otherwise you get 1.     \n Two-Fisted Tales
    Not sure of original release date. Second Edition came out relatively recently. It's $10

    This one's an interesting combo. Everyone has 8 stats, these are added to or subtracted from by skills and traits. A lot of times you just add everything up and if you beat the target number, you succeed. But sometimes you roll two ten sided dice - add the result of one and subtract the result of the other. Both ten sided dice can "explode" when a 10 is rolled. You also have a hand of cards that can provide big bonuses, especially when played together, based on your Luck score. These don't regenerate until the end of the session. Everyone loved their Cards in this game so much, though the balance between hoarding, delaying the end of the session, and getting grouchy with a fellow player for getting tired and proposing ending the session before you spent your limited Cards, it didn't work perfectly.

    \n Untold: the RPG (2010)

    You didn't even hear about this one, did you? That's right, because you blinked, and missed that someone printed approximately a hojillion copies of this hand-building card-based battle and RPG game, whose rules were printed on fold-out handouts inside each deck, and where there were expansions for each faction/race, and you could read all about it on the website UntoldTheGame.Com, go ahead, click it, I'll wait!

    Because I love each and every one of God's abandoned creatures, including this game, I'm gonna tell you all about it. Basically you had a pool of points which you used to purchase traits, equipment, and so on, each of which was represented by a card. Each card had rules detailing when and how often you could use the card. Like maybe your gun you could fire once per round, so when you used it, you flipped it, and then at the top of the round you flipped it back.

    There were a lot of good kernels in this game, like the advancement system - each card was marked with how you got them (like, you have to go on a quest to get this magic sword, or you have to be trained to use this martial arts style) and story-gamers would like the "implied setting" that came with just cool-looking pictures and awesome-sounding names for things, including locations. I also like that the GM had a budget by which he filled out the challenges the PCs would face.

    But in general it was really underexplained and hard to follow.

    \n Deliria (2003)

    I'm pretty sure this game fell into the "brain damage" category at the time, but now that you don't believe in that anymore (lol), you should read it because it's really interesting and ULTRA fiction-first in how character creation and the system works. Basically, you draw a single card from a deck of playing cards and add the value to your skill in order to see if you beat a target number. You can add additional draws through spending luck points called Fortune, and certain magical effects permit additional draws, or "draw and keep the highest" results and things like that. I'm not super thrilled with this one because really it's just a d52, it doesn't do much with the cards as cards but whatever.

    \nDeadlands
    Pinnacle Entertainment Group
    1996

    Uses a normal deck with a black and a red joker for some subsystems.

    Character generation: you drew two more cards than the number of attributes that the character had, and could threw away two of the cards. You had to keep deuces. The cards were then converted into dice types that became your attributes' score. Jokers gave some special background. The deck was reshuffled before each draw.

    Initiative system: you rolled your initiative roll and the better you rolled, the more cards you drew. The game master then counted from joker down to deuces and when a player had that card, they were able to act. If two people had the same rank they were ordered in initiative by their suits. Special abilities could manipulate the cards. Black joker was thrown away with the player's highest card. The deck was reshuffled when there were no more cards (I used two decks so there were no waiting time if we had to reshuffle during the initiative phase).

    Magic system: the magicians were poker players, and rolled with dice against their magic ability to see if they got to draw any extra cards. A really bad roll gave backlash. They then drew at least five cards and tried to make as good poker hand as possible, often starting with Ace high. The better hand, the better effect of the spell. Red joker was wild. Black joker gave backlash, and the backlash table was consulted by drawing a card. The deck was reshuffled before each draw.

    \nOnce Upon a Time
    Atlas Games
    1994

    A storytelling game where all the players strived to fulfill their individual goals. These goals were drawn at the beginning of the session. The winner was the one who reached their goal first, which finished the story. Before they can do that, they had to involve all their cards on their hands, that they also drew at the beginning of the session. The cards had a picture, a short word that explained the picture - a word that had to be used during the narration - and a theme. When the word was mentioned, they played the card so everyone could see that the card was used.

    Only one player narrated at the time, but could loose the right in three ways: by leaving the word over to the person to the left, by narrating in a non-fairy tale like way, or by being interrupted. When the narrative right was lost, that person had to draw a card. If the narrator choose to leave the word over, that person was also allowed throw away a card.

    You could be interrupted in two ways. The easiest way was to use interrupt cards that could be played when the narrator played a card with the same theme as the interrupt card. The slightly harder way was when the narrator mentioned a word that you had on one of the cards. This created a tactic of establishing cards on beforehand. If you had a princess on your hand, you could make her important in the story but not playing your card. When the next person took over and told the tale about the princess, you could play your card and take back the narration.

    I'm in the process of developing (playtesting is done, just need some updates) my game Disaster Strikes! which uses playing cards. The deal is that each player has a hand of cards and play one with a stat to beat the GM's card plus difficulty number. Face cards can be used to provide instant bonuses, but every time you play one you get a complication. Aces are a get out of jail free card. Suits each represent a particular mode of action (power, skill, creativity, sacrifice) and you get a bonus when your description matches the suit of the card you play. Finally, whenever you play spades (sacrifice) you get a complication just as though you played a face card.

    \n Munchkin
    Steve Jackson Games
    2001

    This is really a numbers game with no built in narration of story, but reading off the silly stuff on the cards sort of makes a crude narration.

    Player have a hand of cars with either monster, curses, or silly weapons on them. The player taking a turn "kicks down a door" (the start of the narration?) and draws a card. If it is a monster they compare their present level to the monster's level. If they are higher, they win. If they aren't , they may play more cards from their hand to get their number up. Other player may play cards, in a pile on manner, to help or stop them from winning the fight and gaining a level.

    The cards are funny and there are many expansion games that add new joke cards. They don't change the game though. This game was and I believe still is the cash cow of Steve Jackson Games.

    \nPsychodrame
    Fr\xe9d\xe9ric Sintes, Magali Coulombi\xe9
    2010 (pdf only)

    It works similar to Dogs in the Vineyard but with cards. I will only describe the fast resolution system. What is noticeable is that a conflict is played out as a dialogue. Each turn, you may draw cards from attitudes, emotions and traits but only from each group once. There are four attitudes - confrontation, manipulation, flight and aid -, six emotions ordered as three pairs with two opposites in each, and traits that defines who you are and the relations to the other characters. All these things have numbers and those numbers tell you the amount of cards that you can draw each turn. You can only draw from one source once during a conflict, so you can't draw from, for example, Confrontation twice or more times.

    What you use to draw cards from colours the dialogue. If you draw from the attitude Aid and the trait Cop, you will aid with help from the experience as a police officer. Each turn, you must either forfeit or draw cards. If you draw cards, you must play one or more cards of the same rank that are higher than the previously played cards. Single cards are always beat by two of a kind who are beat by three of a kind, and so on. If you forfeit or can't play any higher cards after drawing new, you will loose the conflict.

    (I can't remember it to well, but I think it works as) The more card you have on the hand when the conflict is over, the more backlash you will suffer from the conflict. The looser must also pick up the previously played cards. If someone forfeited, they can first throw away their own hand and then pick up the played cards.

    The backlash consist of raising the numbers. If any number reaches ten, the character is written out of the story. The game ends when two characters have shared this fate.

    I don't know if it's a houserule, but we played with two decks that both had jokers.

    \nSpectrum
    May Contain Monkeys
    2013

    Each player use their own deck of playing cards. All actions are resolved by card draw + Trait to meet target number. Black cards represent Shadow, linear time, the characters' physical and mental capabilities, and skills. Red cards represent Spectrum, the characters' time travel powers.

    Shades (normal, linear-time people) can only use black cards, they treat red cards as 0. Black face cards add +1, +2, or +3 and draw again. Black Jokers give automatic, dramatic success.

    Time travelers (including PCs) can use both red and back cards. Red face cards add +1, +2, or +3 and draw again. Black face cards subtract -1, -2, -3 and draw again. Red Jokers give automatic, dramatic success, Black Jokers give automatic, dramatic failure. Narratively, if the character succeeds with a red card, it's not their skill that saves them, it's because time slows down, they knew what was about to happen, etc.

    For time-travel actions face cards and jokers have additional special effects.

      \n House of Cards
      2012
      Neal Stidham

      A Tarot deck (or modified deck of playing cards to account for an additional rank of face cards) is used for several different mechanics. Character generation uses the Major Arcana only, and only the Minor Arcana are used for task/conflict resolution. Players who wish their characters to take an action play a card; actions are keyed to the four suits, and if the card played is of the appropriate suit, it may be replaced in hand immediately. Non-suit actions may still succeed if numerically sufficient, but are not replaced immediately. Characterization is reinforced by allowing players to replenish any remaining cards at the end of a scene for roleplaying pre-defined personality attributes. Personality attributes may reciprocally aid in relevant actions by adding their values to played cards for dramatically appropriate actions. Hands of cards also represent characters' health/magic/durability, and harm or trauma cause hands to deplete.

      \n Sacred Steel
      Neil de Carteret
      2004 (Dev release - there was never a final one, as far as I know)

      Cards were either marked with a success or a failure (50/50 mix) - the card sheet in the PDF had green check marks and red x's. Each player (plus the GM) has a separate deck. Draw a number of cards equal to your appropriate trait, attempting to get at least one success. Bonuses and penalties affected the number of cards drawn, and opposed checks were also often used. Decks would only be reshuffled if they ran out.

      While this could have been done trivially with dice, the intent was that having a deck guaranteed that luck would even out over the course of a session - if you started out with a bunch of lucky draws early, you'd definitely be getting more of failures as you reached the last few cards of the deck (and vice versa).

      \nHot Guys Making Out
      Ben Lehman
      2012

      Each player has a small hand of playing cards. Each takes turns; on your turn you play one card from your hand and narrate the next part of the scene according to what card you play. Different cards allow for different kinds of narration, both in general and for specific characters.

      The Tulip Academy Society For Dangerous Gentlemen
      Nick Smith
      2011

      This game uses playing cards differently in several different ways. At the start of an episode you draw a single card to determine the general theme of the game session, and for each scene after the first the player hosting the scene draws two cards to determine its location and who is present. In a Conflict of Science, each participating player gets a hand of 8 cards, and they take turns playing progressively higher cards until one can't play one that's any higher. In Conflicts of Art they also get hands of 8 cards, but play them face-down, and you can call a player on their bluff. In Conflicts of Fencing each duelist gets a deck of 10 cards, and when the other player plays a card, you have 3 chances to flip over a higher card to win that volley of the duel. The winner adds any cards used in a volley to their own dueling deck, until one player gets all of the cards. There are some other bits and pieces, but that's the essence of the game.

      Ryo Kamiya
      2008 (English Version)

The scenario runs in turns, and during each turn the players have opportunities to move from room to room. Upon arriving in a room the player flips over its card, and the value of the card determines what's inside.

      \n I love card mechanics. I've made cards that simulate dice for games I've run, and I've made my own-card based system. I'm currently working on one that uses the tarot deck. I would LOVE to talk more about it at this point, but it is still very much in development. That said:

      Fortune's Fool
      Pantheon Press
      2010

      Uses Tarot cards (the inspiration for me to try my hand at it) for resolution and character creation. The skills are kind of tough on the characters, but the idea is great: While building your character, you are assigned Major Arcana. When you pull these cards for resolution, Fortune Shines (exceptional success). When you pull Major Arcana that aren't assigned to you, Fortune Weeps (exceptional failure). Also, your race (the game is set in a fantasy renaissance Earth) determines which suits favored you. If you pull a suit that favors you, Fortune Smiles (either a good success or not-so-bad a failure depending on the number of the card). If you pull a suit that doesn't favor you, Fortune Frowns (either a scant success or a poor failure, again, depending on the number of the card).

      What they do really well are called Fate Twists. Players get ways to manipulate the deck. Maybe they can turn Death face up in the deck so you always know where it is, or maybe they shuffle certain cards into the top three or five of the deck.

      \n Campaign in a Day
      Chris Engle/ Hamster Press
      1992

      This was my first published Matrix game. I made a hundred copies of it in a three booklet set and sold them over the next three years.

      The game included a set of cards with action words on them. This was the matrix of the game. It included ideas central to war game campaigns like: Move, retreat, morale, tactical advantage, supply problems, weather, etc. The card were laid out on a table next to a terrain board or game map (I billed them as miniatures games back then). The players would make strategic moves on an area map and fight battles using a quick set of miniatures rules (not important to the present discussion). Campaign actions were done by "arguments".

      Each player in turn would step up to the laid out cards and pick one to be the ACTION they were doing, another to be the RESULT, and three cards to be the REASONS to support the argument. Players who argued later could disagree with earlier arguments using the logic "Yes and" "Yes but" or "No actually". The game used a skilled referee who listened to all the arguments and helped decide where these logical conflicts existed (if the player's were not explicit). Players then rolled a dice to see if their action happened. The roll could be better or worse if the referee though the argument was strong or weak. Players in logical conflict rolled for their arguments in a sudden death contest. The last one standing was the winner.

      The weakness of this game (aside from a lack of production qualities) was that bad dice rolls blocked player creativity which kills energy in the game. Also it used a very skilled referee who could listen to ten arguments and then be able to tell which were in conflict and which weren't. I happen to be very good at that so I didn't realize that most people aren't.

      This was a competitive game so in that way was different from the story games of now - that are much more collaborative. Much closer to the war game roots of the hobby.

      \n Itras By
      Martin Bull Gudmundsen & Ole Peder Gi\xe6ver
      Kolofon Forlag 2008

      Itras By utilizes to sets of cards: the chance cards and the resolution cards.

      *The resolution cards* are similar to those used in Archipelago III. They are drawn when the character attempts something dramatic/difficult/for which randomization would make the story more interesting. They\u2019re interpreted by one of the players (the player whose action it is chooses which player should interpret the card) and the results are incorporated in the story by the whole group.

      The cards read \u201cYes, and\u2026\u201d, \u201cNo, but\u2026\u201d, \u201cYes, but only if\u2026\u201d, \u201cHelp is needed\u201d and similar, with a little additional text on each.

      *The chance cards* can be drawn once per session per player, at any point in the story. They\u2019re interpreted by the player who draws them. They\u2019re designed to infuse the game with surrealism.

      Some examples:

      \u201cCut Scene: Jump forward three hours. Describe what conditions the characters find themselves in. You\u2019re not allowed to describe what has happened in the meantime.\u201d
      \u201cNemesis! This card awakens the character\u2019s Nemesis. In some way, this arch enemy affects the situation. Exactly how is up to you. Doesn\u2019t the character have an arch enemy? Well, now she does.\u201d
      \u201cWhat\u2019s in its pockets? The character has something in her pocket which might be useful. What could it be?\u201d

      \n SAGA System (Dragonlance Fifth Age and Marvel)
      TSR
      1996

      This was a diceless heroic high fantasy game that was not AD+D, was thought to be very good, and which didn't win in the market place. I suspect that people seeing a fantasy card game wanted Magic the Gathering and were not satisfied that this wasn't that. (My guess I have no data to back up this notion).

      The game comes with a deck of 80 unique fate cards divided into nine suits with number rankings. A player has a hand of cards that they play as they encounter challenges. The high number wins. The cards also included positive and negative states (I don't know what that means) and cues to aid role playing (info to help the GM narrate what happens). The number of cards in a player's hand sort of represents their hit points but since players can hold back their big cards till the end they are at their most deadly when they are nearest to death. Very dramatic!

      This game came out at the very end of TSR's life and looks like it would be in direct competition with WotC's Everway game so when they bought TSR, that had to have an effect on this product line.

      This is a good example that the big companies can make good product that can be ignored by the buying public as much as indie designers can be ignored. They just spent a lot more money doing it.

      \nPsychosis: Ship of Fools
      Chameleon Eclectic
      1993

      Psychosis billed itself as game in which your perception and memories cannot be trusted (but to describe any more than that might be a spoiler). It uses a tarot deck divided into minor/suit cards (used for challenge resolution) and major arcana (which each have special effects potentially unique to a campaign). Suit cards are dealt to players, with each suit representing some sphere of capability: strength or brute force (staves), agility or dexterity (swords), intellect or knowledge (coins), intuition or empathy (cups). Cards with higher numerical values beat lower-value cards in challenges. You may hold some cards in your hand and/or some face-up before you in your "trace" (which allows you to use them without discarding them, but tips everyone off as to what you can do). If you run out of cards in your hand, you may draw back up to 5 (minus however many are in your trace). If you draw an ace, you may draw from the major arcana.

      Interestingly, "Psychosis is played with cards and only cards." Players aren't given character sheets and aren't allowed to take notes, really trying to drive home the "unreliability of memory" theme.

      Untitled
      Paulo Rivas
      2011

      I ended using a slighty modified version of the Whimsy Cards to make a different unpublished/untitled game: you divide the whole deck among the players, choose any setting or genre you want, brainstorm one location for each player, a setting-destroying danger, three minor other possible problems and one hope. The game starts with the player who holds more cards, or roll the dice.

      First player then uses one card and sets up a challenge to another player he chooses, using the phrase in the card and any of the previously brainstormed elements: dangers, locations, hope, etc. The player then explains what her character does and how it reacts. If he resisted she rolled dice, if succesful, the challenge was overcome and the First player keeps the card. If not, PC is still in trouble and the player who faced the challenge gets the card.

      Turns continue clockwise around the table with players setting challenges and rolling to overcome them or just accepting the card. Next turn players get to use two cards, then three and so forth. They could use all in a single challenge or make more challenges to different players on her turn. No PC dies until the end of the game, though their problems can become more and more difficult. Some cards also help players get out of a challenge or give them a second chance at overcoming it.

      Game ends when one player uses all her cards on its turn; her PC survives the whole deal and cards are re-shuffled for the epilogue. All players roll dice and draw that number of cards from the deck. Except for the winner, everybody rolls the dice to see if they survive and then everybody uses the cards to tell how it ended for her character.

      In playtest it tends to produce nice stories when all players are more collaborative/story oriented and a total gonzo-fest when they get competitive and try to make fun of each other. Either way was hilarious.

Each of the deck's 51 cards presents a different theme that the GM and players, working together, can weave into the game\u2019s narrative, as well as related rules effects. With Plot Twist Cards, everyone can help tell the story and enjoy being surprised by new and unexpected turns of fortune.

      GameMastery Plot Twist Cards add a new storytelling element to roleplaying games, allowing players to bring their own twists to an adventure\u2019s plot. Each card provides a minor story-altering idea along with a few options players can either use as presented or take inspiration from to craft similarly themed suggestions that affect in-game events. Every Plot Twist Card can be interpreted in countless ways, allowing players to suggest minor alterations to a game\u2019s events that a GM might add or adapt however best fits the story\u2019s needs. Such alterations might bring about an added bit of luck to improve the situation for the PCs or up the ante to create even greater challenges\u2014it\u2019s all up to the players and their Game Master!

      \n About I Am Zombie and how it works.\n
      Here\u2019s an example: Dr.Offenbach likes to dance and put things on fire. He has a Pyromaniac card, which besides the traits of Explosives, Stealth and Rage, has unique advantages and disadvantages. He knows how to start a fire without leaving any evidence and how to combine household supplies to make combustibles. Very useful indeed. However, his disadvantages are that he absolutely loves to start fires obsessively, can\u2019t resist in fact, and that he always plays with fire, if only with his lighter.

      So, Dr.Offenbach is passing down the street at night with his party and sees a school. What is that school special for? Thats right, fire safety protocol misconduct. What does he do? Sets the school on fire. Narrator can give him a chip, since he used his disadvantage to commit a major crime, but which also puts the whole crew at risk.

      Dr.Offenbach got a Vengeance Chip (Crazy Dice) of a red Mayhem color. He can use it to turn one red botch, a 1, into a red success. Extremely useful thing, when you can turn a failure into a decent success. Chips can also be traded among players. A sign of a strong cooperative team is no hustling when trading.

      I wonder if "Add to narration" is a little to wide.       \nRetrocausality
      Ettin Productions
      2317 (but not really)

      This time-travel game was developed for a Something Awful forum contest to make diceless RPGs (I think). In general, you draw a card from the deck when you attempt an action, aiming for either a target suit or a target number (adding modifiers to cards based on your skills and such).       \nBust
      Cold Blooded Games
      2006

      Played with a standard deck of playing cards, setting-neutral. To attempt to succeed at something, draw 1 or more cards, with more difficult actions requiring more cards; you "bust" (and thus fail) if you go over 21. Aces cancel out the highest card in your hand, Kings force another card to be drawn, and various contextual modifiers may adjust your draw.
