\n Apologies in advance for the GNS talk.

Over in the Gamism and death in CoC thread, John Kim pointed to a thread on the Forge where Ron Edwards said something like, "Exploration of Situation is one of the only ways I enjoy Sim" (the Forge is blocked from current locale, so I don't have an exact quote).

This made me think about my own gaming, which, particularly in my HERO group, is basically all Sim, and in which I see various issues that sometimes bug me, e.g., illusionism.

It also made me think about how most of the excellent gaming advice I read lately tends to be either awesome hippie Nar talk, awesome Gamist D&D talk, or else (what I consider) lousy old-school System Doesn't Matter talk... which I always end up relating to Sim, wrong as that may be.

So, where's the teh aw3som3 advice for running the big swath of mainstream games out there? What advice will help me bring the awesome when I'm playing HERO or GURPS?

My initial offering: A recent post on John Kim's blog pointed to a general article on "The Impossible Thing Before Breakfast" over on Places to Go, People to Be that paraphrased Ron's idea of "GM as bass player."\n
In this resolution of The Impossible Thing, the referee controls the story in the sense that he sets up the world and the situation, and so decides what the story is about and where it begins; but he does not control how it ends, or how it reaches the end, because that depends entirely on the choices made by the players expressed through their characters. In the end, the story will be as much a surprise to him as to anyone else.
\nAnyone have any other ideas or pointers?

    Anyway, having thought about this some more, two things come to mind:

    First, Marco, your integrated scenario design points to the priority of plausibility. I.e., tailoring the scenario to the PCs, as well as tailoring the PCs to the scenario/campaign, adds to the feeling that the game "makes sense." This would seem very important to maintaining the "Dream," as it were. I know that the most die-hard Sim guy in my HERO group is often bugged when settings or situations lack consistency. Ergo, I'm thinking that anything that bolsters the integrity of the experience is good Sim.

    This is obviously less important with Gamism, as exemplified by the typical D&D "module." The dungeon itself is kind of a ridiculous concept, but it makes for great challenge-focused play. Of the flip side, Nar would seem to care more about whether the situation pushes everyone to address premise. Setting integrity takes a back seat.

    Second, that you're going to need at least a little plot in addition to your situation. Given that, in your typical GURPS, HERO, or BRP game, the players are not empowered by the system to create plot for themselves (if anything, it can be antithetical to typical Actor stance), the GM is going to have to do it. Both the essays on the JAGS site and some of the better D&D adventures I've played point to having some sort of overarching plot or timeline that marches forward unless the PCs act to thwart it. "Plot" here translates to, as a D&D article on WotC's site put it, "What happens if the players do nothing." The nice thing about this is that since it's based on the non-involvement of the PCs, it's not a "story" that the GM overtly/covertly pushes the players through. It's just the gears that continue to turn in the background.

    This thinking also jibes with my poor experience using Nar techniques with my HERO group (though I was also doing a poor job of them, too). My players were totally stymied when faced with a bang, but are totally happy going off on tangents if that's what their character would do.

    Or am I just totally off?

    \n Hi Buzz,

    The article's not about playsibility--it's about alignment. It's about prioritizing alignment between Players and GMs (the real people) in the traditional model of play. That is, the Players and the GM are, through the vehicles of character (PC) and Situation (run by the GM but effectively co-created) discussing what they want out of the game. What's fun. What's not fun. Where it should go (in general).

    This will result in plausibility. It isn't the primary driver (i.e. the GM says "You will be super-spies going into China to steal cloning technology." A player says "That's dull, but--hey, plausible. I'll be James Bond."--this would not be the likely output of the process since it'll stop at "that's dull.").

    Secondly, I'm bothered in Sorcerer games when they lack consistency (not really a problem--but, eh, it happened). If DitV produced inconsistent situations regularly I'd be bothered with that too. I like the games. Am I another Sim-Guy or is consistency common across all your modes? (trick question: I am a Sim-Guy* and consistency is valued across all modes).

    Here's my advice:
    1. IMO, casting things in GNS terms is going to screw it up for you if you want "fun Sim." I don't think it's geared that way. It can, you know, work for "people who like that sort of thing"--but that sort of thing is never anything good (like "I'm going to Sim being an elf ... so I sat around all day imagining I was, like, an elf--wasn't fun--I must not be Sim"). I'd do my best to drop that mode of thinking about the game and the system (and, hey, you can feel good--Hero has never been classified--it could still be kinda Nar!)

    2. The term Plot is toxic. Either get rid of it in your thinking or "take it back" from the GNS-analysis that has likened it to railroading (of some degree). The essay linked to talks about Situation (and I, personally, use the word Plot and Situation interchangeably as in "Here's the plot for my kicker"). Get rid of it. If you are working on a Hero game, come up with 'Situation.'

    Don't believe either, for a minute, that players are not empowered by the system to create situation themselves. That's what the essay is about. The players can suggest the situation-foundation in Hero, JAGS, or GURPS. That's immediate creation of situation right there. They define adversaries (enemies) and focuses of conflict (DNPCs). They don't have the power to define large-scale situational elements during play (i.e. determine "who dunnit") but they don't in DitV either. I think that's a red herring.

    3. I suspect that anyone who is "stymied" by a "bang" has some problems going on. Maybe they don't have a real good grasp on the language you're playing in (presumably English)--or only do things when they think GM (or alpha-participant) wants them too--something severe like that.

    On the other hand, I've been stymied by situational changes that seemed random or uninteresting or hard to get a handle on for some reason. I've seen stuff happen in the game that looked like the GM wasn't on top of their game and was floundering. I've had trouble figuring out how to *help* in situations like that.

    See, when you make players 'those guys' (who are, you know, Sim-players) and game events 'bangs' (which, in your mind, are Narrativist) you put things into a GNS box that's, IMO, more or less supposed to result in failure. And amazingly, you get it. I doubt the players are stupified by the idea of "an in-game choice with no preordained outcome." I suspect they were frustrated by *something* else that was going on and not the pure "bang-ish" nature of the event. After all, how could they be: absent railroading, most any roleplaying game will be composed of these (the theme-focus is a possible point of interest here--but, again, if you just made a theme up without an relevance to an ongoing situation that's not necessarily functional narrativism either).

    Basically I would suggest the following things:
    1. work your preferences into the pre-game zone. Talk about them before play starts. If you have stuff you want to introduce, do it with disads and tell the GM how you want it introduced ("Here is my DNPC--she is my partner and she's been captured by the Triad's Kung Fu master who wants to marry her. Worse, she's kinda attracted to him--but he has this bad idea that if he defeats me, he'll win her heart--").

    2. Stop thinking of people as Sim-Guy (unless it is how you think of yourself). Don't think in terms of Plot (unless you can use it to mean situation--in which case Sorcerer Kickers have a plot--and I think they do. So do DitV towns.).

    3. Break your play down into basic techniques: As the GM, how will you provide adversity? How will you maintain consistency? Is the action congruent with what you think the Players want? Is it congruent with the characters? Are the characters "fit" for whatever action you have decided on. Are the characters provided with real and important choices? Do the players care?

    These are all techniques that can apply to ANY GNS mode--so get rid of modal thinking.

    These are all techniques that are supported beautifully by traditional play--so get rid of "revolutionary vs. traditional" thinking.

    Basically: think outside the (three) boxes.

    -Marco
    * I'm willing to be a Sim-Guy. Someone has to be. I think the methodology is extremely clunky.
