\n Hey everybody, imagine a dozen people stuck in a stinking tin can for two years. It's space travel! I'm angling for a pressure cooker environment, where each character is assigned a sort of "problem track", starting at nominal, and progressing through stressful to unbearable as conditions deteriorate for them. This last step is where the real mayhem occurs, and it should be destructive in some way - to themselves, the mission, others, whatever. Venting EVA suits, smashed control panels, punches flying, people losing their shit.

I have a few tracks, but I'd like some more, and I also want to fine tune my stages. I'm open to suggestions. Anybody with insight into how various manias and dangerous mental states emerge under stress, please chime in.

Foreboding \u2013 Anxiety - Panic attack\t
Homesickness \u2013 Depression - Suicidal depression
Obsession \u2013 Compulsion - Manic frenzy
Intolerance \u2013 Condemnation - Personal hatred
Carelessness - Bad luck - Deadly mistakes
Disrespect \u2013 Contempt - Insubordination\t\t
Suspicion \u2013 Paranoia - Psychotic delusions
Disagreement \u2013 Bitterness - Furious violence
Hypochondria \u2013 Illness - Self-injury
Attraction \u2013 Lust - Jealous rage

Maybe something with regret - guilt - ?

Thanks!

    \n Hey, cool idea, Nathan! That makes a continuum from "the virtue that got this crew member on the craft" to "when virtues become vices " to "when vices become aberrations" to "when aberrations become horrific."

    There's something... I dunno... creepy-good-fun about having players choose Virtues which will, inevitably, twist into motivations to do risky or horrible or irresponsible things. Even better if you can pull a Dread: TFBOP trick and not let the players know that's how the system works... or, at least, for the first game (folks will figure it out, when half or more of the group is rotting away due to perverted or over-stressed "virtues"). So a Virtue would be, like, a sort of "power" unique to each character--gives some bennie or allows for some kind of special action--which, in time, corrupts into a compulsion.

    All-the-better, if there's more "exits" than one from each state (my notion of Forking, above) because, even on replay, a given Virtue's perversion isn't predictable (there's no metagaming of "I'm gonna take Alert so I can play the delusional guy at the end game!"). HOW you'd choose one Fork over another is an exercise for the reader--err, designer.... You know, if'n he likes the idea and all....

    \n I'm not quite sure what form this will take, but it will be very simple, because I want it to be something you'd use in a classroom with 12+ participants. So the Grey Ranks model won't really work. I think the model will be push and push and push until someone cracks, then clean up after them until someone else cracks, until there's just a handful of lonely, sad people with no way to get home. You know, like high school!

    I'm thinking that it will be designed as a distributed thing, where each group of participants plays a crew as well as mission control for another crew somewhere else, connected via Internet asynchronously*. Mission Control can attenuate the craziness somehow, but they have to choose how to allocate their attenuation resources. Like "OK, the inorganic chemist is dissing the mission commander, and she's perilously close to telling him to fuck off. Let's soothe her, bring her back into the fold, calm her down before she explodes." As a player you don't have any control over your character's state of degeneration, and that spiral into catastrophe is a clearly marked path. MC will be balancing that, trying to keep the worst human problems contained so the mission can be accomplished. Meanwhile the crises just keep piling up. I'm just thinking out loud.

    *Hilariously, regular text chat is not a bad simulation of a 4-5 minute communication delay.

    Posted By: Jason Morningstarwhere each group of participants plays a crew as well as mission control for another crew somewhere else
    \nIt's--as ever--your baby... but ICK. I generally eschew games where I play two diametrically different roles; too much cross-over/back-and-forth makes for flat play on both sides. I'd lose my manic feel, when shifting to an MC role, and I'd prolly not play an MC "stright" with the knowledge that I've got to get back into mood on the next switch. Then again, I'm very Method in my RP.

    However... the notion of two TEAMS--one the crew, breaking down; one mission control, trying to shore them up--has some real merit, I think. As a LARP, it would ROCK: one or two laptops per room; crew and MC never in same room; two GMs (with their own walkie talkies/cell phones, to coordinate); and a discrete goal (end game, after X hours) which the crew wants to complete... but which the breakdowns are threatening.

    Then, you have a neat counter-balance of both duties and abilities: the crew Does Stuff, with each passing time period (some sort of maneuvering or key experiment or duty for each player, on an every-15-minute schedule, just like a Mission Plan?) ratcheting up the stress. The crew CAN help each other maintain... but put some mechanical reward in place for NOT doing so, so that MC is highly motivated to step in. Tough to think of a way to do that--maybe mini-games (sodoku to do "navigation checks," build a three-story house of cards for "hydroponics maintenance," remove all the body parts from an Operation game, to fire maneuvering jets) that should take almost all of the time allotted, which reward succes but cause "pile ups" if failed. Enough pile ups, and things slip: ratchet up the stress, move folks along their crack-up track, cue frantic Mission Control calls to try to get them back on schedule. Hell, each crew member could have a whole chart of things they have to get done, on schedule, to avoid gaining Stress.

    OOO! And controlling the laptop(s) becomes a part of the whole overarching story and conflict! What happens when the now-paranoid Comms Officer thinks MC is out to get him replaced? Will the now-Apathetic Captain actually perform the duties necessary at each of his mission stages (gravity assist maneuvering, midpoint turn-over, decell burns, orbit injection, landing)?

    Damn... I think there's a killer one-shot, con LARP in these ideas, even if they don't mesh with your particular design goals, Jason! Figure some light, push-pull mechanics for stress v. support, have conflicting agendas bubble up from those mechanics and duties (and rewards for achievement), and turn loose a group of, say, 10 to 20 (2 to 5 MCs--not many needed, as there's only a couple of laptops--and the rest various crew roles). Such scalability is KEY to getting a con LARP to go off, as one never knows how many sign-ups you'll get.
