\n In a conversation I had with Joshua A.C. Newman at Dreamation, he said something that has stuck with me:

"You have to make a lot of shit before you make something good"

I may be paraphrasing here, and we were talking half about art and half about game design, but in my own endeavors it has been particularly encouraging to remember that nobody is awesome right off the bat and that everyone ends up with failed playtests, broken mechanics, and projects gone wrong. Nobody becomes a gaming rock star overnight, and those that think they do probably need a swift kick to the ego. Or help with their psychosis.

Anyway, I want to hear about some of those Chernobyl projects. What were some of your grand ideas that, in retrospect, were completely absurd? Tell me the story of the ashes of game designs that have crashed and burned.

One story I have heard so far was from a vampire LARP that my friend designed for months from scratch. He made the mistake of failing to playtest before a large session... And fifteen minutes into the game people started dropping out like flies hit with mustard gas. People began to come up to him, asking to play something else because they just weren't having fun. He has since moved on to better things, but says that moments such as those are important learning tools and, ultimately, made him better at what he does.

    \n The best artists I know throw out at least 3 ideas (sometimes dozens) before getting what they want. When I was an illustrator, a motto I had was "make mistakes faster." You're going to make mistakes no matter what. Might as well embrace it and make them as fast as possible.

    I don't know about my worst idea but I've had plenty of bad ones! Or at least something was bad about them (sometimes it's not the idea but the execution).

    These ideas didn't work out for multiple reasons...

    1. Play D&D backwards. Start at level 20 and every adventure you lose a level as you play through your past.

    2. Reverse Call of Cthulhu. The players do everything they can to drive their character crazy and the GMs job is to keep them sane.

    3. RPGs to confront fears. I consulted with a few therapists and designed a game where you took minor fears and created fictional analogies that you could safely confront. We had a process where world creation was inspired by how we dream, so every element in the world was really an aspect of yourself in an effort to inspire internal introspection on your fictional journey.

    4. Severance. Inspired by the TV show the Wire. You play multiple characters, multiple sides of a conflict revolving around layoffs. You play Human Resources and the employees in a company that needs to lay off 30% of its work force.

    5. Smash. A miniature wargame where the punishment for failing rolls was roleplaying! Every time you failed in combat, you had to roleplay for a length of time determined by the extent of your tactical failure.

    6. Blackout. You play normal every day people facing the sudden realities of an all out alien invasion. The mechanics were very cool but I found the situation to be a drag. Characters lacked agency. It felt more like an exercise in roleplaying fear and running away a lot. Even in horror games, you generally can be proactive. You could be proactive in this game as well but a lot of time was spent building characters that felt very real (the character sheet was composed primarily of real life demographic and medical information) that in play it felt more realistic just to run away.

    \n Holocaust video game "a survivors tale" you played though a number of historical stories of people who were killed at the camps, each story ended with a characters death, the last story you play through is one of an actual survivor.

    I did not put much work into this idea.

    Something more akin to the op's question would be my game Switch and Tip, the first abstract game I released as a pdf. Gordy it was terrible. I was trying to make games that used the fewest possible components.* This one was on a 3x3 grid and each player had two pieces. ill spare you the details but the game simply does not end with evenly matched players.

    *this was actually a very educational process, it gave me a much better idea of how games work.

The idea was that the game would get nastier and uglier as it progressed from fantasy to uncomfortable reality and that the mechanics would break down and be remade as the game progressed. I was burned out on D&D at this point, and the campaign was my personal, "Fuck you." In retrospect, this was an awful idea, as the result was less "I'm having a moral crisis" and more "Why the hell did I lose my flaming halberd?"\n
