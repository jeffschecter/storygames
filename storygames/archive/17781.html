\n I've put up a poll on immersion on The Big Purple which has had 137 votes so far.

Please mosey on over, join the discussion on the thread and vote :-)

I think the results in so far are very interesting and in particular show that around two thirds of immersively-oriented gamers find that their immersion is broken or strained by "rules making me make decisions that are nothing like any decisions my character would be making", whereas comparatively few find it troublesome to be "feeling that we\u2019re all just making it all up as we go along" and about two in seven find it troublesome to be "expected to decide things my character would have no control over or no way of affecting".\n

    \n@David_Berg, what do you think of dividing the two joys of gaming you mention into "passive imagination" and "active imagination"? Or "experiencing the fiction" versus "creating the fiction"?

    GNS claimed to identify "creative agendas", and I've seen it said in a forum debate that immersion is not a creative agenda but an experiential agenda. It seems to me that when people "get their hippy on", if you'll collectively pardon the expression, they're interested in shared/collaborative creativity. When people are gaming immersively, they're not being consciously creative in the same way. They're interested in the experience of the fiction.

    EverythingIsTwo discusses this collective creativity here, with a particular egalitarian slant.

    For me, the key difference between storygames and conventional RPGs is the emphasis in storygames, for players, on active creativity, whereas in conventional RPGs there is oftentimes no emphasis on active creativity on the part of players, which may be good or bad for those players' immersive experience of the fiction, depending on how their individual psychologies work. My belief is that for most gamers on the whole it's good (from the limited point of view of the experiential agenda), but there's at least a significant minority for whom it's bad (for that same experiential agenda).

    I am well aware, of course, that Stanislavsky theorists would regard the actor (who is ideally in a state of dual consciousness-immersion) as being creative. Bella Merlin refers to (what is basically) immersion as a "creative" state, IIRC. But I think it's very different to the conscious, active creativity involved in storygames. I think it's a passive, reflexive, subconscious creativity.

    When I want my active creative kicks, my own general preference frankly is to get them from GMing. I'm perfectly happy to play storygames as opposed to conventional RPGs - write-ups here of games I played and fairly much enjoyed at Indiecon 2012, some of which were storygamey - but I guess I find them a bit of an ambiguous middle ground in between playing and GMing, i.e. not my personal ideal.

    Personally I think this is part of why one particular hardcore fan of storygaming described Montsegur 1244 as a game of "four mini-GMs", which is of course what I've done with my free-to-download storygame CHIEFS too.

    To me this is the nub of why the distinction between storygames and conventional RPGs is interesting, even if inevitably there's a whole spectrum from focus on "my character" to focus on "our story", and games don't often occupy the extreme "our story" end, and many games are somewhere in the middle. It's not a question of what's your creative agenda, but of whether you have a creative agenda at all - of experiential agenda versus creative agenda. The fact that someone can be genuinely and uninhibitedly enthusiastic about both as goals of play, but recognise that (for that individual personally - not as a universal truth for everyone) they are best achieved in different games, seems to me to be a very positive thing.

    I would like to see a world where the hardcore storygame crowd can recognise the value some gamers see in conventional RPGs for experiential play, and hardcore experiential gamers can recognise the value some see in hardcore storygames for collaborative creativity, without the misconceptions getting in the way that have muddled a lot of RPG-related discourse over the years, and still do. Misconceptions shown by people, for example, using "trad" as an insult (this I have seen, only 2 months ago), or some of the "funny" things that get written all the time on my favourite trad-centric RPG site. I'm not pretending to be entirely saintly/neutral when it comes to tribalism myself, but I do at least try to behave myself...

    That's sort of my manifesto for 2013!

    \n@JDCorley, re the second paragraph of your post and the example of exposing the love triangle, consider this:- it might be very inconvenient to your character that the love triangle be exposed, and they might be horrified at the prospect of it, but you might be using out-of-character knowledge to engineer what you consider to be a more satisfying story.

    Changing the example slightly, say for instance you know, out of character, that the characters Billy and Bob both love Sandra, but Sandra and Bob are about to be separately deported (both to the US). Billy wants to persuade Sandra to turn fugitive and hide out with him in the wilds of Scotland. You don't know this in-character. Your character, Johnny, has a sports car, which Billy asks you to borrow, promising to pay you back. Johnny owes Billy a favour, but the sports car is brand new and it's a big ask from someone who's not very credit-worthy. You could say yes or no; your instincts, as Johnny, are to say no. But you, as JDCorley, might decide to come good on the favour Johnny owes Billy and lend him the sports car, as if Billy can drive fast enough he can make it to Sandra before the immigration officials deport her. Bob will be there too. Billy doesn't know about Bob being with Sandra. You, JDCorley, consider it would be a massive anticlimax to the story for Sandra and Bob to be deported together before Billy makes a stand to try to persuade Sandra to turn fugitive. So - it's not an internal choice for the character. Or, even if the character knew everything you know, the character might not care whether the love triangle plays out or not - all he cares about is his sense of honour in honouring a bet, and his pride in his new car and fear of it not being returned to him. That's his personal dilemma - honour versus love of possessions - and either choice is plausible, but you choose honour for reasons that would be alien to Johnny's psychology.

    It's not that one choice is "more plausible", it's that each meets the baseline minimum that it could be considered believable, but one "feels" to you like your character's natural choice. Supposing you're experiencing dual consciousness-immersion, you may have a sense of what feels "natural" to you as your character, but also be aware in an out-of-character way of the desirability that the story reach a climax and not an anti-climax.

    \nThere's generally speaking no conflict [more strictly, no immediately obvious conflict] between a choice that's believable to an outsider (e.g. any other player at the table, or the GM) and what's good [or rather, seems, outwardly, to be good] for the story.

    There is, however, a potential conflict between your "inner truth" of the character and the needs of the story.

    If you are deeply immersed in character, if you are thinking "as" your character and not thinking "about" your character, if you are thinking "for" your character and not thinking "of" your character, if you are not consciously considering what you character would do next, but are simply assuming their personality and letting it speak through you, then you will (from time to time at least) have an "inner truth" of what your character will do next. Not of what your character "might believably do", but of what your character "will do".

    Denying that inner truth is counterimmersive.

    Moreover, a skilled professional - by which I mean an actor, a director or especially someone who trains actors - may be able to tell whether you are acting (cf. roleplaying) according to your "inner truth". Certainly, that is the impression I have from reading about actor training in Russia based on Stanislavsky's teachings.

    Likewise, whilst the rest of us may not be able to consciously differentiate between "action according to inner truth" and "action which is believable but not dictated by inner truth", we may be able to subconsciously intuit some flaw in roleplay executed intellectually rather than simply from the heart. We may be able to discern some minor flaw when the gamer is not plugging into his inner truth, though probably we can't put our finger on exactly what the problem is, and usually it's so minor a problem that it's hardly noticeable.

    Of course, the biggest problem caused by a discord between roleplay and "inner truth" is the effect it may have on the fluency of the gamer's portrayal of his character and especially on his ability to suspend his disbelief in "being" his character. If you have broken with your "inner truth", you have broken character psychologically. Your character concept is no longer whole, and may need time to heal. Above all, your ability to identify with your character - in the sense of seeing yourself as the character - is shattered. This may lead to hesitant roleplay.

    Thus, to me it feels "false" and almost like cheating if, having found the "inner truth" of my character, I then roleplay differently to some other end.

    An example of when I did this might be, for example, when my proud, obstinate warrior bowed out of a duel with another PC in a Middle Earth-based game. I knew that the game was more important to the other player than to me, and as I lived a long way away, it was difficult for me to attend regularly. I knew that whoever's PC was killed would probably have no further role in the game (the other player would have taken it the wrong way, and I had precious little enough enthusiasm for that character, far less any of the other alternatives that the GM was prepared to open up to me). So I bowed out of the fight and made up a plausible in-fiction reason. It was within the bounds of believability. But it was totally against my "inner truth". At that point the character could only really become an NPC. He was dead to me. I surrendered my character sheet to the GM without waiting for instruction to do so. I suppose it was likely I would have had to abandon the character anyway, as there was no place for him in the party really after he bowed out of the duel. But if I had carried on playing him, I would have had to rebuild the character concept and my sense of immersion more or less from scratch. It would have been harder in a way than starting with an entirely new character because the character would have had that broken "inner truth" in the background.

      \n@JHKim, I can certainly accept that at times you make out-of-character decisions about how your character develops between "scenes" which may not involve any distortion of your "inner truth". You are skipping from roleplayed scene to roleplayed scene via your internal narrative. If on the other hand during a roleplayed scene you lose immersion and make an out-of-character decision as to how your character develops, you are not breaking your inner truth because, having lost immersion, there is no inner truth there to be broken. Then add the "dual consciousness" concept to completely muddy the waters...

      Regarding the rest of it, it may be only internally that you're "breaking from character" if you choose a seemingly believable course of action that goes against your "inner truth". How does you internally breaking from character directly affect the story for the other participants? It doesn't. There may be an indirect effect there, but they probably won't really notice it. And if you can rationalise your choice and persuade yourself it was believable, then your "internal story" may be essentially untarnished too, even if your immersion takes a knock.
