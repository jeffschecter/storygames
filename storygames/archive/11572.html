\n I want a word that describes two things at the same time: Creation and discovery.
\n
\nI'm writing a game where facts (about the fiction) will emerge during play. During a scene, things will usually come up that - in hindsight, after the scene is over - can be defined as a "fact".
\n
\nI don't want to tell people to "create" these facts, because then they'll be spending a lot of time during the scene thinking "I have to CREATE a NEW THING".
\nI can't tell them to "discover" these facts, because then they'll think the system provides the input.
\n
\nAny tips?

    Posted By: David BergSo, are you looking for a word to describe the Scene Director's choice of what to note down?
    \nHm. Yes, I guess I am - after the scene, the director should basically ask the group (including himself/herself): "Did we - the players, the audience - find out something new about the mnemosites?"

    So, something like this: The players play out scenes, where they might reveal facts about the mnemosites. Afterwards, the director decides whether any facts have been revealed, and notes them down.

    The reason I like "reveal" is that this game is about exploring our collective imagination, about seeing what's already there. This is based on the (Keith Johnstone/impro) idea that creation can and will often come from just looking at what your mind produces - which is different from telling it to produce stuff.

    \n I'm going to agree with Johnstone. Post-factual questioning has a better taste.

    Ask for a scene involving X (mnemosites, in this case), then question the play-group about which elements should be carried through into further scenes (living behind red lines, etc). The players only appreciate the relevance of their creation until after it has taken place.

    I would warn against rampant post-modernist thought, but I'm sure you'll make it work!
