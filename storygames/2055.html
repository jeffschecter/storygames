Character Creation Baggage or "Ooh! It says here I can lift a ton!"

Mark_Causey
December 2006 edited December 2006 in Play Advice
So, I'm of the opinion that good streamlined (indie?) gaming makes games where, when a character sheet is involved, there is nothing but useful flags and/or player chosen expressions of not only their character but what they want to get involved in game-wise.

However, a lot of D&D style gaming includes things in character creation (or later, during 'leveling') that aren't explicitly part of the player's choices. For instance, you may have chosen to be a Druid, but you didn't ask for an Animal Companion. You may have chosen Ventrue, but part and parcel of that comes certain 'abilities'. Heck, even in the Shadow of Yesterday or FATE, I may have chosen early on to get a particular ability but now I don't want it or never use it.

These situations are usually remedied by giving options for customizing classes or getting paid back for getting rid of these parts of your character. There are nearly endless forum posts and books filled with these options explored and permuted. I'm not worried about this because I don't consider it a problem.

However, I still remember at some point facing a challenge in a D&D game when I realized that something I'd added way back when for some flavor or class ability also granted me some small thing that helped solve a problem and made a cool scene. That is, it's something on the sheet that leads to a cool scene that the player enjoyed (in my mind, a flag) but that no one knew about (making it invisible).

So, how can my favorite games get me this thrill again? How can I be surprised about my character all over again?

My first thought is to have other players give you abilities mid-game, on the spot. But, what if Andy thinks it would be totally awesome for me to have frog-like leaping and I don't? That creative energy will be wasted, and we'll have started a tangent that didn't lead toward awesome gaming (either because Andy's idea sucked or I'm a complete moron for not wanting frog-like leaping). This could even lead to worse situations: "Wait, I thought you had frog-like leaping! That's the entire point of this scene! Man, this sucks."

Any ideas?

    But, they're not "discoveries" then. They're surprises in that you're creating facts nobody knew about on the spot. But Mark's thing seems to be discovering a use for a previously enumerated dimension that you hadn't noticed before. So it wasn't in this particular case that Remi was making up new stuff. In fact, at the time in question I can't say that he did at all.

    In Remi's case (and the case JBR is talking about) it was a question of changing situation. That is, the character remained the same, but as the situation changed, some already extant ability gets a new take on it because it's considered from a new perspective.

    There's an implicit dilemma: Do I focus on just having few notes about the character to make sure the game sticks to what it's good at? Or do I allow the game to enumerate the character expansively, so that these moments of serendipity can occur?

    I tend to lean to the latter, actually. Rather what HQ does well is to give mass level focus in the keywords for the character, but then to create micro-level breadth on the ability level. Is "Swordsman" an ability that leads to thematic play? As opposed to the Warrior keyword? Well, it can be if the situation is such that the character's ability with the sword is challenged somehow.

    Call these things not "invisible flags" but "potential flags" or "waiting flags." They're just waiting for the situation to morph somehow into something where they will, in fact, be interesting to play around. The GM can hammer on the more obvious personality traits, relationships and keywords, etc. So he can maintain focus that way. But the individual situations can still be surprising, when the player discovers that some as yet uninteresting listing on the sheet suddenly stands out in a given situation.

    A fully fleshed out HQ character can have more than 60 abilities on the sheet. Yet somehow the forest essence of the character is not lost for all the detail trees.

    Now, again, that indicates my bias somewhat. I play a lot more "meandering" than many focused story people do. But I think that it's good to try to have both focus, and extra danglies on the character that can become important spontaneously.

    I think this come from two places, Mark:

    1) multiplicity of interpretation - Very, very often, putting one thing down on the character sheet has multiple repercussions in the fiction. The character sheet is just a labor-saving device to help our feeble human memories, after all, and it uses a lot of shorthard. So we put down "+5 Hide" and it means that you can hide in a number of different places, and maybe that you're good at finding other people hiding, and your hiding has effects on other characters, and so on. In crunchy traditional games, many powers (feat, skill, advantage, whatever) has multiple discrete effects; in looser more indie-flavored games, many stats are loosely-defined relationships or concepts that can be interpreted many ways. Increasing the individual effects of a power or opening up interpretation of a stat will increase the frequency that this happens.

    2) combinations and permutations - The more stuff you have on the character sheets, the more likely it becomes that two or more of them will interact in interesting ways. Burning Wheel and FORKing, Exalted and Comboing, and World of Warcraft tactics are all examples of this, and a large part of the tactical element of these games is in finding useful and exploitable combinations. (Note that some of these combinations involve taking one thing off of your sheet and combining it with one thing off of my sheet, and producing a teamwork-based combination.) Now, this happens more often the crunchier the game is and the more individual items go onto individual character sheets. The number of potential permutations goes through the roof (and it's quite possible to exceed the players' ability to keep track of them).

    Does all that sound accurate to what you're talking about, Mark?

    There are several answers that come to mind on this one, all in random order:

    1) Make a game where it doesn't matter. If Andy wants you to have it, you get it. In this kind of game you don't have total character ownership, and so you have to trust the other players to be down together. Now, you may still have the ability to spin the ability (i.e. "I never use it because my father made me promise I would not become Frog Man"), but if they grant it, it is there.

    2) As above, but if they force an ability you don't want onto you, you get a bonus token or something that you can call in later. Like, if you have two of them you can block the new suggestions, because you've gone with two things you didn't like already, or if you spend three you can remove an ability you don't like, or whatever.

    3) Use a currency system like Universalis, where you can express you interest, disinterest, or blocking by spending tokens to keep the character in bounds in as much as you're willing to spend for it. In this situation other players may still be able to overcome you and give something to your character, but only if they want it enough to a) spend and b) know that they're putting something on the character you don't want.

    4) Do a round-the-table where you call out for a cool new ability and each player at the table (GM included) comes up with one. You choose the one you like most. Maybe you have to then give the player who came up with the cool thing some reward or bonus.

    5) Do up a story-chain style list where you put your character on a branching power path (like a stunt tree in Exalted), but then don't get to chose your powers yourself. The other players chose from the list, and get to send you down one path or another based on their choices.

    6) Roll dice on a random table. Okay, that's getting away from the other players choosing though, so....

    Josh's stacked abilities issue is another way to go without having to have the other players able to manipulate your character at all, but might be able to propose different combinations. In games like HeroQuest and Burning Wheel I've often seen one player suggest FORKs or augments to another player for their character. (i.e. "Hey, based on that pose you could totally FORK your Dance into your Sword roll!" or "Wait, don't you have basket weaving as one of your abilities? You could totally add that to your courtship because you just brought her a basket full of flowers!")

    Now, the thing is that this is sometimes quite subtle. Some systems don't put any one flag all that particularly in the foreground in any way, and you have to discern what is a flag and what is not from some background noise of other abilities.

    But that's the point. If I see a relationship like "Hates Brother" that's likely a flag. But not 100% sure. Rather, it's a flag, but is it the one the player wanted to draw attention to. Perhaps it's his "Feels Odd Around Father" that's the real flag, and the brother ability was just tossed in as an afterthought to make the father ability have more meaning.

    You can't really know for sure before play in a system like HQ. Oh, in Sorcerer you have a kicker that stands out like a huge flag, and stuff like the reason the character became a sorcerer. But, barring that sort of systemic information on what's a flag, it's guesswork to some extent.

    Oh, sure, you can ask the player. But interestingly, I'm not always sure that with such a system as HQ that the player really knows at start what it is particularly about the character that they'd most like to play around. I often get non-committal answers. Meaning that there are no really prominent flags.

    Moreover, presumably the player chose the abilities in question because they interest him to play them in some way. That is, to some extent, every single ability is a tiny flag.

    So what I think were' talking about here, Mark and I, are flags that have been identified as interesting, and character resources (or other facets) that have the potential to be flags at some point in the future. If, in fact, a character's Swordsmanship ability becomes centrally important at some point, isn't it now a flag? Even if you stumble across it, once you can see that issues that pertain to X ability are things that the player is looking to play around with, doesn't that ability constitute a flag going forward?

    As for "furling" flags, that seems to match Fred Volkes idea of indicating areas about a character which you're specifically not interested in investigating.

    So the way I'm seeing it, and with the danger of extending the analogy taken into consideration, I'm thinking that most or maybe all abilities are "furled flags" that can become unfurled at any time. Heck a player can look at his sheet and say, "Man, that Eat's Well ability sounds like fun. Can we do something around that?" That's unfurling that flag. Or it can become unfurled in play due to situation.

    And then once unfurled, they can become furled again, when, say, a player has resolved the issues important to him surrounding that flag.

    I aim for this in thematic character driven play, and have hit it with reasonable success in the past, so here's some AP. I find that the key is to imbue the character with lots of potential meaning, so I can use it later on as needed, and even rediscover it. In practice, I did this in a play by email game that was largely freeform, very mythic in tone, and had lots of player power. Its over now, but while it played, it was a slow game, with posting maybe once a fortnight, or once a month. During character creation, I did heaps of research into stuff that could relate to the character. I sat with my book of symbolism and started with an entry tied to the base idea of the character, and then just started reading, jumping from entry to entry as one was referenced by another, and jotting down notes as I went on things that captured my interest. I did the same with names, word origins; I got into the history of nursery rhymes for further inspiration, and war songs (the character was kind of a personification of war crossed with a Valkyrie, only with extra stuff as well). I used this to build the character, with bits and pieces taken from all over the place.

    Later on, when the game was playing, posts would come from the GM that would make my brain hurt with their goodness, and Id sit and wonder how on earth I was going to respond. At those times, I could drag out a reference book and look up a concept that was important in the game at the time. Often Id get that Oh, that makes so much sense, the symbolism of red roses simply has to change in her magic as she steps closer to facing her own guilt and towards redemption/destruction* discovery of something new in what had been there before.

    Partly it was a process that helped me forget details that I could rediscover, but more importantly, it was a process that helped me build a strong framework of potential meanings that, in collaboration with a GM who really got what I was doing with the character, I could draw on to twist new meanings. And these potential meanings were drawn from broader symbolism in the types of stories the game involved so that the other players could send them back to me with spin, and I could build on them myself because they didnt just rely on the setting of the game.

    I think a key element was that I didnt try to create meanings for symbols from whole cloth. If I had, and Id forgotten what stuff meant, as was inevitable, that meaning would have been lost. But if I say my character has an ambiguous relationship with red roses after betraying the race of unicorns who live in the Land of Red Roses and who she was sworn to protect, and tie that in to the symbolism of red roses in real world cultures, that meaning still exists even if I forget, and it can be communicated to other people without the boring info dump, while at the same time it tinges the concepts of betrayal and protection with things related to red roses. And if I say shes kind of like a Valkyrie, it makes sense to say that one of her powers is the ability to dance on sword tips through the middle of battle, then I get some damn cool visuals and a neat power. And later on, I get to discover that if she can do this, shes pretty unkillable by conventional means, which can give her a complex if shes got a death wish following the aforementioned betrayal of a sacred trust. If youre going to play a game of theme and meaning, I think its easier to use the language that already exists where possible, rather than create a new one. Otherwise, you end up failing to communicate, even with yourself.

    In this game, the GM could make me clap with joy with an oblique reference to something that was relevant to my character in a thread relating entirely to another character, because it indicated to me that there was a commonality of issues between my character and the situation the other character was in.

    Claire

    Imagine a game with a web of traits that are on the surface abilities (e.g. "Strong"). Further imagine that those traits get stronger when the player puts them on the line by making them an issue to be resolved (e.g. "Am I stronger than my father thinks I am?") Puts them on the line, because once that issue becomes resolved the trait will morph into at least one trait upon resolution depending on how powerful it was during gameplay:

    Strong ("Am I stronger than my father thinks I am?") ----Power 1 (Resolved by failing father and being ostracized) -----> Alone

    or

    Strong ("Am I stronger than my father thinks I am?") ----Power 2 (Resolved by failing father and being ostracized for allowing him to die -------< Alone and Masochistic

    etc.

    If all traits evolved in this way, and gameplay was about playing your character at different times in their life, you could have instances where you discovered why you became Strong.

    But also, the point I guess, is that you can have Dead End traits. That is, you have an ability you want. But you could make it a Flag by assigning an issue to it and be rewarded for it. Furthermore, if you had a trait that you didn't want anymore, you could make some issue about it "Why do you have those tattoos when you know you won't get a job with them?") and transform it into something you did want to get to eventually make into a Flag.
