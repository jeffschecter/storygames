\n As I was moving into my new appartment, last September, I found myself facing two facts :

a) I did not have enough shelf space to hold my RPG collection (about 60 books strong, not counting PDFs), or rather, I didn't want to dedicate so much shelf space to it, wanting to leave room for board games, literature and non-fiction.

b) I keep buying new RPGs faster than I get to play them. Less than half my printed collection has been played, and most of it just once or twice.

So, I decided to stop buying new games. Or at least slow down.

I figure, if I keep the number of games on my shelf small, keep the rest in a box in a closet, and rotate the titles whenever it starts looking stale instead of buying something new, I might be better at resisting the consumerist urge...

So, what's on my shelf these days? I'm down to twelve nine three games! Just keeping a handful of games there that I'm likely to play in the near future and perhaps trigger curiosity from the casual board gaming crowd that comes around.

The finalists are :

Hero's Banner and Shock: Social Science-Fiction : Two games I love, have some experience with, but have yet to try with my non-gamer friends and/or flatmates (I have so far tried Capes and Inspectres, with mixed results due to inexperience of the other players and miscommunication of the participants expectations.)

A Thousand and One Night : I never played that one, but the rules are simple, and it would be a nice excuse for me and my friends to dress up as the Sultan's courtiers. Since several have a background in theatre, I think it's a strong selling point.

Are you subject to the same consumerist urges? What are you doing to fight them, if anything? If you had to restrict your library shelf to a handful of games at a time, what game would find themselves there right now?

    \n I find that, for the past several years, I buy games mostly to support the designer or publisher (often one and the same). I couldn't justify buying any more games if my only arguments were "I want it" or "it has a unique play experience." All that said, the single biggest motivation for me to purchase a game would be a guarantee that I'd have folks to play it (as long as it's not D&D; 4E).
    \n
    \nI've played almost every game I own, at least once... but, yeah, there's maybe a dozen that I own and have read, but have never played, even at a con or in a demo. Include card and board games, and that number goes up a bit more--I have another half dozen or so that I bought mainly via charity auctions at cons, where I'm mainly spending to donate (I often bid against myself, if I think I'm getting something too cheaply... and to shame others into bringing up the average sell values). I see every such purchase as FAR AND AWAY a better investment than the beer I'm likely to spend it on otherwise.
    \n
    \nAnd I *never* cull gaming stuff, ever, never. I have every OD&D; module I ever bought (and all the books, of course, including Survival Guides). I've got old CityTech box, original Illuminati... I think the only things I've lost over the years were the original Top Secret box, James Bond RPG box and a few boxed modules, original Gamma World box, and Gangbusters. Best I can figure, I must have left them at my Mom's and they got given to the Salvation Army...?
    \n
    \nGoing forward with my consumerism:
    \n* Mouse Guard Boxed Set - MUST buy, if only for the dice!
    \n* Killer Bunnies Red - I won the base set, Yellow, and Purple in a con auction... but you need Red to use Yellow or Purple! Wonder if I'll ever play it....
    \n* Are You The Traitor? - Been meaning to get this party game from Looney Labs for a while, but its price point is JUST high enough to keep being postponed.
    \n* Fiasco Companion - See first paragraph: love to support BPG; easy as hell to get a Fiasco game to make at cons or pick-up games.
    \n* Mechaton Revised (if it ever happens) - See first paragraph. Also: Legos (another consumerist urge--show me a set with a per-piece price of 5\xa2 or less and I almost HAVE to buy it, like a moral imperative!)

    \n I still buy plenty of games (for the value they give me, they are relatively cheap) BUT...

    - I'm more reluctant to buy new games at conventions without playing first or reading reviews.

    - I buy less games from designers who don't buy games or support our community in some way.

    - If I want to buy a game for research or just to read, not for play, I'll buy it as a PDF.

    - I used to buy every game my friends released. But as my friends have increased and my budget tightened I look for new ways to support them. I'll promote them, help with playtesting, organize games, recommend them (if I think the game is solid and the person I'm recommending them to would be interested). And if I think a friend's game is amazing but isn't something I will play soon, I'll try to buy it as a gift for another friend.

    - I frequently purge my bookshelf of games (or any books) I will never read or play again. I either give them away to friends or donate them to local convention swap tables.


    Generally speaking, outside of supporting my friends, my consumerist urges are mostly under control. I buy what I will use (use doesn't have to be the traditional use... I'll buy games to lend to friends who can't afford them). But I will say that I've started listening to the Yog-Sothoth radio podcast and the number of Trail of Cthulhu scenarios that sounds absolutely fabulous is starting to become a bit staggering! The Artimage Files blew me away (I'm excited to run it) and I'm interested in more of their work. My wife already owns an entire shelf of Pelgrane Press material that I may have to ransack!     \n It's been a personal quest of mine over the past couple of years to get rid of as much stuff as possible. I apply it to everything in my house, but mostly to books, because that's the bulk of what I own (thanks in no small part to having worked in several book/comic/gaming stores). In fact, I've gotten rid of over 60 boxes of books since I started the purging and now have a standing rule that if anything comes into the house, something has to go out. Keeps clutter at a minimum and since I downsized apartments last year I have less space to work with anyway.

    The RPG weeding was a tough slog, but I eventually instituted some tough love and ended up keeping the stuff I was reasonably sure I'd play at some point or had rules-sets I was particularly enamored of. Here's the list:

    - All of the Midnight books and supplements. This is my favourite RPG setting ever, bar none, and even though I hate d20, I think it would probably adapt quite nicely to Burning Wheel or something similar. Now it's just a matter of finding a group that wants to commit to playing a long-term serious campaign in (what is essentially) Middle Earth 100 years after Sauron won.
    - Amber Diceless & Shadow Knight. One of my favourite systems. I love the way that player/character rivalries are set up during the character creation auction.
    - All my Planescape books. My second favourite setting ever.
    - A Song of Ice & Fire core book & campaign guide. I love the novels; I love the setting; I just wish it wasn't so intensely rules-heavy. I'll figure out some way to hack it eventually.
    - Two full feet worth of WFRP1 books. I admit in a lot of ways this is pure nostalgia, seeing as it was one of the first really intense roleplaying experiences I had, but I can't help loving it and I'll never get rid of them.
    - Six or seven random Exalted books, which I'm keeping for the fluff.
    - The Paranoia 2E box set, which is full to bursting with modules and supplements.
    - Iron Kingdoms Character Guide & World Guide. I understand from a business perspective why Privateer killed their RPG line in favour of Warmachine, but I'm not gonna pretend I didn't weep a little when it happened.
    - Call of Cthulhu 6E HC.
    - The Extraordinary Adventures of Baron Munchausen.
    - The Nocturnals supplement for M&M. (I love Dan Brereton a lot.)
    - 3:16.
    - Fiasco.
    - Apocalypse World.
    - Savage Worlds Explorer's Edition.
    - All of Burning Wheel.
    - Agon.
    - The Armory's 30 Sided Dice Gaming Tables.

    I also buy PDFs, but I'm not gonna include them--they don't take up space. I don't really buy RPGs from any of the big guns anymore, but I don't have a problem shelling out for the indies. I guess my consumerist urges still hit me in that regard, but as an independent writer I understand the importance of having people support your work. Besides, indie games are cool.

    \nThat all sounds very familiar.

    One quick plug I'd do for the minis gaming end though is that it tends to inherently have more "hobby end" stuff to do.

    When I met some older, usually historical inclined-, minis gamers, I found ou that is extremely common. However, there seemed to be less angst about. Heck, there's even a great deal of humor about ( see various Larry Leadhead comics for examples).

    Part of what I suspect works for those guys is that they've really changed ( or simply hae a different) idealized version of what gaming is.

    For example, they had a regular once per month club meetup, kinda like a Minicon. In addition they'd have a couple of bigger local cons they'd go to where they'd put together a presentation scenario. In between, they'd hae occasional more casual meetups, where someone would basically host a presentation game.

    Not everybody did all of those things, but the presentation games projects between other meetups bcame items for individuals to work on and have fun with.

    Some of these guys really put lots of time money and effort into this stuff, for things that only might be played a couple of times. But it was a happy thing, not a bummer, the way a big collection of unplayed or only slightly played RPGs seems to be for a lot of us.
