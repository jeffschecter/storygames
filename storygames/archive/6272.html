\n I'm not sure if this is even the correct place to put it. If not then please strike it with my apologies Andy!

My geek side is suffering. We gamers rally hard to welcome everyone. We rally to make sure we are all having fun. We rally to make gaiming more welcoming to non-gamers. But not for me anymore. I think we are too focused on catching new gamers that we are beginning to forget our current friends and current gamers.

In the gaming community I have noticed that we say on a pretty constant basis via boards and emails:

"We should always invite everyone to our games!!" (and yet I don't get an invite to theirs and then get nailed if I want to host a game at my house for only a select few.)

"We need to be more open minded to other's ideas!!" (and yet I get told that what I've suggested is 'not cool' and/or 'stupid' and then I am ignored.) Not to say I can't be uncool or stupid. Far from it. When I see someone else mention THE SAME EXACT THING, they get no retaliation for their thoughts and words. And then further discussion takes place about the topic until all are happy and hugging again. But not me. I sit staring at my screen like a confused puppy wondering if I've read what I see correctly.

(There are plenty more examples but those are the two that stick out to me the most.)

I've asked this question once before too... should I just become a gamer wife? I know that my being female, a gamer, and someone's wife have caused issues in games before because of the simple jealousy of it all. (e.g. why won't my wife play?! Eric what did you do that got Lisa gaming?) And thus my continued arrival at the table becomes uncomfortable for others. I leave the table and I hear tales of how great things are. For the record, I've always been a gamer and I have the scars to prove it. I am relegated to playing only at my table, Mark's table and Andy's table as those are the only places I feel welcome anymore. And you have no idea how much that makes me happy guys! Seriously.

Or... now seriously think about this one... is it that you've just remembered that I'm a witch? Now this one funny enough I can actually understand. I've had lots of people not invite me into their homes/lives anymore after years of knowing me. Somehow they find out about my religous beliefs (or figure it out as I've never hidden it!) and I'm out of their lives.

But I thought we gamers were above all that petty bullshit. Am I wrong?

I've talked to Eric about this and he's noticed this trend as well but can offer me no advice as he's not sure what's going on either. Has anyone else noticed this?

Or is it me and I just have less gamer friends than I thought I did? Do I expect too much from my fellow gamers? I look upon all of my fellow gamers as intellecutals and interesting people to talk with about all sorts of things (not just gaming).

What's happened? Is it something I said? Is it something I did? Is there something on my face?

So come on guys let me have it. Let me know what the hell is going on. If I've offended, let me know! I'm a witch but I'm not psychic! I'm a big girl and I can take it. I just want to know what the hell is happening in my corner of the gaming world. I miss it and want to be part of it again.

Thanks.

Lisa P

Commonly, though not universally, those short-comings include poor ability to interact socially, which is not left at the door when gamers get together. Tom loves something, and sharing it with Jill would give Jill a deeper understanding of Tom as well as introduce her to something she might love too. That impulse is happy and healthy and normal. But when you start using language that denies people's independence and self-direction, and when you start talking about that sharing as if it were indoctrination, then it starts to get offensive and weird very quickly. But it's important to understand that the base impulse of sharing is a good one, and that what needs to change is the mindset/approach. So something like "the same way she'll get you jogging with her friends in the morning" might give that necessary paradigm jolt that lets them see how weird their approach is, without disrespecting that impulse to share their love.
