    \n Well...!
    - the dice need to matter: they need to be, largely, the final arbiter of certain types of outcomes. If it's up to the GM or whomever to decide the outcome of conflict, the dice are just there to fill in the details.
    - the dice can do a lot of work for us. In the case of AW's Moves, rolling the dice (poorly) can create a whole chain of events, or at least the inspiration to move things forward without having to come up with every little thing on our own, which is exhausting.

    \n I have never tried it, but I saw somewhere a technique based on asking generative questions.

    "You see something on the side of the road and you suddenly feel sad. What does it remind you of?"
    "You really hate X. What did she do to you?"

    You assume a fact, then generate a backstory for it.

    \n I've working in this issue some time. First I tried coming up with a group of tables to roll the dice and mix the resultant words to add a complicate narrative piece into the fiction already established, but the whole process of dice rolling and checking the tables disrupts greatly the flow of the game. This is something that fits better in the preparation stage.

    However, defining all the elements that will intervene in the game from the start kinda takes away the element of surprise in the story, with to me is a key factor for enjoying the game. Right now I'm going again for the tables, but instead of rolling the dice, you just pick up a few words from each column and add it to the fiction whenever is needed.

    In the playtest of the previous encarnation of this system I found out that, while anybody inthe table can come up with an interesting scene framing, not everybody has the same disposition or quick thinking to pace it right, some people can even panic at the thought of coming up with something interesting for everybody, some are more inclined to take the process as a joke and stage the most ridiculous scene they can think of. So these lists act both as constraints and creative help.

    Now, throwing a lot of inpirating words together doesn't make the trick. The words need to be grouped, each list should remain short and in some cases you can have a list of 30 things separated in three columns and allow to pick only one thing on each column. That reduces the time we take comparing all the different options.

    On the system I'm working on now, lists should be small enough to fit half the page of your character sheet, leaving the rest of the page for skills/ aspects/ inventory/ whatever. And perhaps the back of the page for the social relationship tree. : )

    The only game I can recall now that uses an actual mechanic to introduce random twists on the story is Maid RPG. Players pay points to introduce a random element to interrupt scenes (often to prevent other players to achieve their goal, since this is more of a competitive game). However thay have to roll a fixed table to determine the nature of the interruption, and perhaps after a while, the "random missile hitting the location" joke can get old.
