Immersion v. Isolation

Kuma
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Story Games
From ye olde blogge poestte: http://blog.kumapageworks.org/blog/?p=139

So we've looked at what I'd call immersive play and what it means in terms of the AGE Model - the convergence of the various spaces of the game, such that movement or information from the various spaces is not disruptive to the experience of the game. The player enters a state of flow - or a sensation of being the game instead of simply playing the game.

While immersion is easily accomplished with computer games - any game is a candidate for immersion: play a few particularly balls-to-the-wall games of chess with your Ukranian boss and you'll see what I mean. (I never had a chance of winning, but I did have him worried from time to time.) I'd argue that RPGs, while quite capable of an immersive experience are more difficult for immersion to form, simply because of the sheer volume of different agendas and intents that are around the gaming table. It's easy to get lost in a computer game when it's you alone in your head with a copy of Call of Duty. It's much harder to mantain that flow when you have multiple people involved, some of whom might not be interested in attaining this kind of state.

With that in mind, I'd like to address something that John Kim said on his blog (and in various forum threads) over the last few days, and to introduce the idea of isolationist play.

In contrast with immersion play, isolation play is an attempt by the player to engage one (and only one) space exclusively, with a minimum of interference from the other spaces. John talks about the concept of 'mask play', and references it in his article on the Threefold-Model:

'Mask-play' is the most complete way that the player can enter the game-world. Think of it as a virtual reality: when the player looks around, they see the game-world. They look at other players and see the characters. They look in a mirror and see their character's face. Only by doing this, by shutting out as much of the real world as possible, will the player be able to let their normal personality take a back seat, and allow the personality of their fictional character to take over. I can't describe what that actually means because it doesn't happen often enough to be analyzed, but personal experience makes me think it's worth striving for.

Wallis, J. 1995. Through a mask, darkly: Connecting players and roles. Interactive Fantasy, 3, p. unknown.

In this passage, we see that Wallis is talking about engaging the emspace completely and solely, "by shutting out as much of the real world as possible". That means no playspace, no gamespace. (Of course, you always have your own personal aspace with you, so shutting that out is impossible.) So this is where I'm drawing isolationist play from - the idea that the player isolates themselves in a preferred space and sees all of the action of the game from that point of view.

John's own comments support this:

"My experience has been that highly immersive play is seen as problematic in most traditional RPGs. Immersively played characters will frequently break the rules: by splitting the party, by questioning other PCs, and by failing to follow the meta-game cues for the direction of the adventure. In the past, I've had multiple fights with the GM because my character went off to do something which broke the assumptions of the adventure. The stereotypical case is when an NPC approaches the PCs with a job offer. A player who suggests refusing the offer is quickly labelled a troublemaker."

In this example, John is talking about looking at a situation purely from the character's PoV in the emspace - no information from other spaces is considered. John calls these 'meta-game cues', but really what we're looking at are the other spaces involved in the play of an RPG. The first and foremost of these is the playspace - as a player, John knows that the GM is likely introducing an adventure hook. (For the record, I'd like to say that I think that John's apparently had a boatload of crappy GMs. I mean, who has NPC walk up and offer jobs anymore? Soooo 1987.) This information from the playspace is ignored, however, and a decision is made purely on the prima facia of the emspace circumstances - the character refuses the job because he wants to, or because the guy looks shady, or because his credstick is bad.

    Cont'd:

    The problem (one of the possible problems) with isolationist play is that the states of the other spaces must coincide so that they are frictionless with the remaining space. You have to agree ahead of time that you're operating solely on emspace information. Given that understanding in the playspace, the GM would be better off with an open, GTA approach to gameplay, driven by the actions of the characters instead of driving a narrative foreign to the emspace. When the intervention of the rules is necessary, there should be understandings on how that information is dealt with and re-incorporated into the emspace where the players can then act on it.

    There's likely going to be a negative association with folks on me choosing 'isolationist' as a name for the play method - but I think that the term is apt and differentiates it from the definition that I've worked up on immersion. Emspace isolation isn't the only mode - it just happens to be a common one. A group of casual gamers might engage in a sort of weak gamespace isolation, not engaging in in-character play at all.

    It's easy to see where the difference between the two states would be lost - both immersion and isolation deal with single space. But the root cause and the nature of that space are different in the two approaches.

    The difference is that while immersion is low-friction between all of the spaces, isolation is low-friction within one space, but high friction between that space and the other spaces. Working within the emspace is easy - involving the other spaces is verboten, or at least frowned upon.
    If we think of immersion as the set of spaces facilitating low-friction play, then isolation is just immersion with a set of 1.     Immersion (as I'm looking at it) means that the gamespace, emspace and playspace are all mixing easily, so that the disruption required to break immersion has to be large - the player is manipulating information from the playspace (the GM's input, the input of the other players), the gamespace (any rolling or other mechanical bits are done 'by rote' or with a minimum of friction) and the in-character information. A major disruption like needing to look up the stats for a medusa, not accepting 'fudging', and the book being in the other room, would break immersion, but possibly only temporarily. Of course in an immersion state that sort of disruption might be less likely to occur as well, because the players might want to preserve the state.

    Isolationist (and yes, I'd love to have a better word - about the only other one I've found so far is 'perspectivist') play requires that all negotiation be completed inside the chosen space - information from the other spaces is either disruptive or disregarded - one or the other. In the 'perfect' isolationist play, all of the players are working in-character, using only the information available in the emspace, and disregarding the necessities of, say, the playspace - they don't worry about whether Player B will be offended or whether Player C is getting enough spotlight - the emspace is as it is.

    No, I'm just saying that it's a valid form of social intercourse. Whereas it seemed from before that it wasn't being included as form of social intercourse. The importance of any given part of play is only as important as the participants make it. So, if no one cares whether we stick to the prepared material, then no, we don't have to worry about it. If only one person cares, and everyone else doesn't, then the one person should hopefully bend on this point rather than making everyone else worry about it.

    Here's an illustration of my particular issue. Suppose I and another player (Martine) are in a game, and we prefer that everyone stay in-character during the game. Our enjoyment is largely dependent on this. Now suppose you have two other players, Mark and Carol, who are more focused on other quality: say that the game follow adventure-story-style pacing. So, Martine and I are happily having an in-game conversation, when Chris and Carol interrupt to say out-of-character "Look, this is getting dull. Can we get to some action please?"

    Now, people's comments often seem to be that Chris and Carol are always justified in pursuing their preferences -- because by speaking out-of-character, they are being "social" and are speaking about the fun of "real people". Whereas from the rhetoric, if Martine and I pursue our preferences and try to keep things in-character, we are guilty of "putting fictional characters above real people's fun". That seems nonsensical to me. Our fun comes from being in-character, and we are people who are just as real as Chris and Carol.

    To compromise, yes, Martine and I should to some degree accept breaks from the game which Chris and Carol prefer. However, the converse is also true. To help the other players' real fun, Chris and Carol should try to stay in-character more and pursue their interests without fewer out-of-character breaks during the game. Being more "isolationist" can enhance the fun for other players.
