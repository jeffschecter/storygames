    \n This actual play report is for a game that is not yet released. Please feel free to disregard this thread if you have no interested in pre-published games.
    \n
    \nThe actual play in this first session is rather brief as some time had been spent creating insurrectionists (i.e. player characters) discussing the current state of affairs in the city and how to springboard the first scene.
    \n
    \nThe Cabal
    \n
    \nGerald Birch- An ambitious scheming rabble rouser with a dark secret. Gerald is the only member of the cabal to not work in the Saracen's Head. He is the cabal's firebrand. Gerald is not trusted by Gregory and has yet to convince Jim that he has the best interest of the cabal at heart. Edward finds Gerald to be a fascinating and much welcomed client due to his ability to work crowds and his apparent commitment to the cabal.
    \n
    \nGerald is being played by David.
    \n
    \nJim Black - A recently freed Negro slave. Embittered by his treatment at the hands of his former owners, Jim works for the owner of the Saracen's Head, throwing out troublemakers and other undesirables. He is the cabal's renegade. Gerald looks down on Jim, viewing him at best as a tool to be manipulated. Gregory wants Jim's respect as he is impressed by Jim's physical prowess and apparent lack of fear. Edward thinks of himself as a father figure to Jim.
    \n
    \nJim is being played by Ben.
    \n
    \nGregory Strevell - Resentful stable boy from the Saracen's Head. Gregory supplements his meager earnings by pilfering the saddle bags of rich clients. He is the cabal's infiltrator. Gerald has nothing but disdain for this scrawny youth. Jim is friendly towards Gregory but finds his lack of self-respect to be an undesirable quality. Both he and Gregory share a strong disliking towards people in power. As with Jim, Edward sees his relationship with Gregory as being somewhat paternalistic, Unlike his relationship with Jim, Edward is confident of Gregory's ability to cope with almost anything that life can throw at him.
    \n
    \nI am playing Gregory
    \n
    \nEdward Mowry - Careworn and kindhearted owner of the Saracen's Head. Edward inherited the inn from his parents. He is the cabal's saboteur. Gerald thinks of Edwards as the de facto leader of the cabal due to his role as the employer of the both Jim and Gregory. If he can control Edward, he can control the activities of the cabal. Both Gregory and Jim consider Edward to be an exception to their philosophy of all people in power being bastards.
    \n
    \nAdam is playing Edward.

    \nSession One: Part I
    \n
    \nTo prepare for the current story we all discuss how things may have changed since we completed the last story with our previous insurrectionists. We decide that their bravery will have had a positive influence on some of the citizens.
    \n
    \nSome weeks have passed since another revolutionary cabal freed a handful of political and religious prisoners and held back the city watch (a.k.a. mercenaries) from slaughtering several families of refugees. Inspired by the heroic resistance of their peers, the cabal mentioned above has upped their activity against the corrupt authority of Duke Clermont and his council of patriarchs.
    \n
    \nIn order to kick start this story, I recommend that the first scene begin with someone trying to get back a letter that my insurrectionist has stolen. None of us know what the letter says. We'll decide that later on.
    \n
    \nA messenger from the Duke is staying one night at the Saracen's Head and Gregory has stolen a letter with the Duke's seal from one of the messenger's saddle bags. Recognizing the seal but unable to read the missive due to his illiteracy, Gregory plans to reveal his discovery to Edward or Gerald. But fearing Edward's reaction and not trusting Gerald, he decides to keep the letter secret for a few days. The game begins with three members of the City Watch, accompanied by the Duke's messenger, barging into the Saracen's Head, demanding the return of the letter and any information on who might be responsible for the theft.
    \n
    \nNervously fingering at the folded letter in the waist of his breeches, Gregory eyes the front door and waits for the right time to make a dash towards it. Edward is mediating between the Watch and the regulars of his inn while trying to persuade the Watch that there must have been some misunderstanding and that the letter was most likely to have been lost rather than stolen. Expecting the worst, Jim moves slowly towards the cudgel behind the north most corner of the bar. Gerald sits quietly, evaluating the scene, confident that the Watch have probably bitten off more than they can chew.
    \n
    \nWe all discuss what our insurrectionist's might be in this scene. Adam wants Edward to give the appearance of trying to keep the peace while actually distracting the Watch and the Duke's messenger from noticing my insurrectionist sneaking off. This is a contingency challenge because the result of his draw will have probably have an impact on the result of my challenge.
    \n
    \nWe all discuss reasons why Edward might succeed, e.g: "He's probably quite confident given that he is in his own home, surrounded by people that are loyal to him" and why he might fail, e.g.: "The Watch are in a potentially dangerous situation, they're not going to stand in the middle of this inn without keeping an eye out for potential attackers or people trying to sneak away". We also mention what results we each believe would be more interesting for the story and all the player-narrators except for Adam add red or white beads to a small bag according to what results we each believe to be right for this challenge.
    \n
    \nAdam draws some beads and get a success with positive consequences. Because this is a contigency challenge and he has drawn a success with positive consequences, I will get some exta red beads when I draw to see what happens when my insurrectionist tries to sneak away.
    \n
    \nAs Edward tries to convince the Watch that it must be a mistake, the Duke's messenger becomes more tetchy and some of the regulars are aggravated by the way he's staring at them. The senior member of the Watch is regretting their hastiness and considering how to remove themselves from the inn before things become ugly, but the Duke's messenger is certain that they will apprehend the guilty party if they apply enough pressure to regulars and the bar staff.

    \nSession One: Part II
    \n
    \nMy insurrectionist is trying to sneak away because he has no desire to be hanged or possibly tortured for his crime. As before, everyone except for me adds red or white beads to the bag according to their own particular reasons. But I also get the benefit of some extra red beads due to Edward's distraction and because Gregory is an infiltrator I get to draw an extra bead (infiltrators are adept at moving unnoticed).
    \n
    \nI make the draw and due to the extra bead provided by taking a forte related challenge, I can choose between one of two results: success with negative consequences or failure with positive consequences. If I go with the first result, I'll probably ask that the other members of the cabal consider him a potential traior for leaving them to handle the Watch on their own. In the end, I choose the second possibility and go with the result of failure with postive consequences.
    \n
    \nWith most of the attention on Edward and the Duke's messenger, Gregory sneaks towards the door. Jim glares at him with a mix of sympathy and repulsion. Sympathy for the trouble that Gregory has got himself into and repulsion at his cowardice and for leaving his friends in the lurch. With only a few feet left before he has a clear path to the doorway, Gregory inadvertently nudges a table and sends a tankard crashing to the floor.
    \n
    \nThe whole world seems to hush as everyone in the inn turns to spot Gregory trying to leave. The brief silence is interrupted by an accusation shouted by the Duke's messenger and a demand to apprehend the detestable little thief. Some of the inn's regulars stand up and clench their fists with menacing aforethought. The senior member of the Watch notices that his fellow Watch members have moved their hands towards the hilts of their swords.
    \n
    \nBen wants Jim to see some action this scene and as the inn's doorman and the cabal's renegade he's ripe for sorting out any problems with a little violence. Once more a challenge is underway and because Jim's forte revolves around solving problems with violence, he gets to draw an extra bead as I did. Everyone except Ben, adds their beads and Ben draws a result of success with positive consequences
    \n
    \nNo one can agree on who threw the first punch, but everyone who was there remembers Jim bringing his cudgel over the head of one of the Watch and choking him with it at the same time as using the poor unfortunate fool as a shield. Barking directions to his cabal, Edward manages to move Gregory and Gerald towards a hatchway behind the bar. Jim shoves the Watch member he is strangling into the fray as a randomly swung chair leg from an unknown assailant connects with the back of the messenger's head. The last thing he sees before leaping into the cellar are two members of the Watch battling their way to the inn's door while their colleague is on all coughing on all fours and being kicked in the ribs; the messenger lies prone, blood flowing from an open wound above his left ear.
    \n
    \nWith the scene at an end, all the player-narrators are rewards some influence points. Adam is voted as the player-narrator deserving more influence points for his involvement in the scene and everyone that took part in a challenge is given additional influence points depending on the virtue their insurrectionists were using for their particular challenge.
    \n
    \n[End of session one]

    \nSession Two: Part I
    \n
    \nThis session began with all the player-narrators recapping the most salient points from the previous session and putting forth some possibilities for the current session. Recalling that the previous Captain of the Watch had been killed by an ahriman in the final session of an earlier game, we all agreed that the letter stolen by Gregory was an offer of employment to a son of a dignitary from a neighbouring city and the offer had been made because of rather handsome bribe from the aforesaid dignitary. We also agreed that aiding and abetting the thief that stole the letter and the death of a the Duke's messenger in Edward's inn was grounds for the loss of the Saracen's Head and that the cabal's civil and political rights would be extinguished by a bill of attainder.
    \n
    \nOutlawed in their own city, the cabal considered how they might us the letter to their own advantage. One of the insurrectionist offered up the idea of blackmailing the Duke, but the suggestion was scoffed at by the other insurrectionist who couldn't imagine the Duke being intimidated by a bunch of amateurs. In the end, the cabal decides to go with Gerald's suggestion of selling the information to an influential rival of the Duke.
    \n
    \nThe player-narrators discuss whether or not to have a chain of scenes in which their insurrectionist's have to gather information and persuade the right people so they can get an audience with Jonas Michellson, the Duke's most powerful rival on the City Council. We agree to skip to the meeting and begin the next scene in the house of Jonas Michellson, head of the most wealthy banking family in the entire city.
    \n
    \n[align=center][/align]
    \n
    \nJonas is a bald man in his early fifties. His calm demeanor is rooted in a cold apathetic aloofness that slowly weakens the resolve of the cabal. Although we know he is a moroi, our insurrectionists are not aware of this fact and explain away the feeling as a product of the unsual circumstances. The cabal's firebrand and saboteur, Gerald Birch and Edward Mowry, try to negotiate a deal with Jonas, while Jim and Gregory say nothing, feeling out of depth and slightly uncomfortable with the situation.
    \n
    \nDavid's insurrectionist is taking this challenge with the help of Adam's insurrectionist. Both Gregory and Jim could assist but Ben and I decide it wouldn't be appropriate considering the characters of their insurrectionists and the current circumstances. Beads are added to the bag as in earlier challenges and additional red bead is added because of the help that Edward is giving Gerald. David gets to draw an extra bead because Gerald is a firebrand and as such he is the mouthpiece of the cabal. The result of his draw is a failure with negative consequences.
    \n
    \nDespite the best efforts of Gerald and Edward, it soon becomes apparent that Jonas is not going to buy the letter without reading it first. Although he does seem to be keen on toppling the Duke, it probably won't be on any terms hoped for by the cabal.
    \n
    \nI tell the other player-narrators that my insurrectionist is becoming agitated by the negotiations and is about to do something rash. David declares that his insurrectionist will try and stop mine and we both agree that it should be Gerald's challenge to stop Gregory rather than Gregory's challenge to get past Gerald. Because David is taking the challenge, he gets to make the draw and narrate the outcome, but we will both be given influence points at the end of the scene.
    \n
    \nConfounded by the back and forth of a negotiation which seems to be going no where fast, Gregory takes out the letter and steps towards Jonas. Gerald notices what Gregory is trying to do and he moves to intercept him. But it's too late and Jonas has the letter.
    \n
    \nThe cabal consider taking the letter back by force or intimidation. They abandon these plans upon remembering the armed bodyguards standing on the other side of the only door in the room. So they wait for Jonas to finish reading it. Carefully folding the pages of the letter, Jonas evaluates what can be done with the letter and makes the cabal an offer: Persuade the dockmaster, Hugo Culley, to put aside his animosity towards Jonas and vote with him on a proposal to freeze the Duke's assets while he is being investigated.

    \nSession 2: Part II
    \n
    \nAfter returning from a brief visit to the local shop to restock our supply of refreshments, we discuss what kind of person Hugo might be, and we are awarded influence points for participating in the previous scene and using our virtues. David is given additional influence points because we feel that his contribution was better than ours.
    \n
    \nHugo is less than pleased to have his busy day interrupted by four thugs claiming to be affliated with the Duke. His mood takes an even bigger dive, when Jim decides to kick his table over and tell him that if he dares to vote against the Duke they'll make his life a living hell.
    \n
    \n[align=center][/align]
    \n
    \nAt this point our skills at negotiating have been found sorely lacking once again, Edward drew a failure with positive consequences (Hugo isn't going to have his men break their arms). Ben noting his insurrectionist high propensity value in vengeance decided that Jim would lash out and once again an insurrectionist who has no business being at the negotiation table has probably set the cabal another three or four steps back.
    \n
    \nThe cabal are convinced that Hugo probably won't vote with Jonas, but he might vote against the Duke if he thinks the Duke has sent over some ruffians to try and intimidate him. They'll need to go far enough to annoy Hugo and they'll need to be believable as agents of the Duke. But they can't go too far and succeed at frightening Hugo. Lucky for them, Hugo is not a man that is easily unnerved. Unfortunately, they are unable to tell from his reaction how he will vote and after they are thrown out of the docks, they nurse their bruises and come up with another scheme to make Hugo hate the Duke as much if not more than he hates Jonas.
    \n
    \nAlthough we failed to convice Hugo with our rubbish attempt at pretending to work for the Duke, we haven't run out of options. We can't repeat our last tactic, but there's more than one way to skin a cat.
    \n
    \nIn the middle of the night, the cabal's infiltrator Gregory, sneaks amongst the crates, coiled rope and timber of the city's dock, his destination a small cargo ship. Gerald, Edward, and Jim are nearby, speaking with the few remaining dock workers to distract them and give them some faces to report back to Hugo.
    \n
    \nThe virtue I use for sneaking on to the ship is Gregory's anxiety. He has quite a high propensity for anxiety and I know it will mean lots of extra influence points at the end of this scene. More importantly for here and now, I and the othe players can imagine Gregory's anxiety playing a part in the way he moves around the docks.
    \n
    \nBeads are added to the bag and I draw a success with negative consequences.
    \n
    \nHaving successfully boarded the cargo ship unnoticed, Gregory looks for a good place to start a fire. He does his best to remember the advice that Edward gave him: "find somewhere that the fire will rapidly spread from but not so rapidly that you won't be able to escape." Setting the fire is an easy task for Gregory and getting off the ship is no obstacle. Less easy to deal with is the weight of guilt and responsibility that will come to rest on his shoulders as the fire spreads across the docks, destroying equipment, ships, cargo and worst of the livelihoods of everyone that works there.
    \n
    \nThe scene ends and we are all awarded some more influence points and we all decide to spend some of them making Hugo a significant character.
    \n
    \n[End of Session Two]

    Posted By: khelekThe AP post shows that you all played a coherent story; but without a little bit of meta-game info this could have been any system or game in the world.

    Are you going to share any of the Meta-game aspects about what was going on? What makes it about Defiance... did the characters have to act a certain way..?

    \nI hope the additional text will help you see what makes Defiance different from many other roleplaying games. The game is called Defiance because the heroes are insurrectionist trying to inspire a frightened and/or apathetic populace to fight back against the corrupt authority that is oppressing them. The insurrectionist all possess certain virtues which make them stand out from their neighbours: resentment, concern and vengeance. If a player narrator uses these virtues when her insurrectionist faces a challenge, she will be receive more influence points than if she used another virtue, e.g. anxiety or contempt.
