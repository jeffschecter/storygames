\n In another thread, Airk notes:\n
I remain a little mystified by retroclones, because I have no actual fondness for anything mechanical from early editions of D&D - and I cut my teeth of Mentzer. It was simple, sure, but I didn't really feel like it did anything super exciting or clever or interesting. It gave us a way to resolve killing monsters, and some cool magic items and stuff, but I... just don't really understand the love, I guess?
\nSo I thought, why not give him some ideas of what to look at here, because I totally see interesting mechanical features in that there game. But, out of threat topic - and therefore, a new thread.

So, I think that original D&D has interesting game design in it. I am a bit of a designer myself, and an aficionado of game design, so I'm confident that I know my business when I say that D&D has unique bits that are interesting for anybody who likes unique game design.

On the other hand, it's obvious that many people disagree. I think that I know why, because I was most lukewarm about D&D for like 15 years before I really got into it; I am, essentially, an outsider to the game when you compare to some real hardcore "D&D only" gamers. I'll speculate a bit:

I think that the issue for many is that they got into the game too young - D&D is a wargame, IMO creatively better suited for 30-somethings than children. Of course the game has its virtues as a childhood game as well, but when the way you look at the game has been preconceived through teenager eyes, it's difficult to look at it in any other way, right? I can imagine that if I had played more than a couple of miscomprehending one-shots as a child, I'd find it impossible to drop those associations about what the game's like, too.

Then there's of course also the fact that the publishing company has done its damnedest to dumb the game down, and also the tribal identity issues where people cherish their status as non-D&D gamers (just like others cherish their position as part of the D&D tribe). Lots of reasons for why it's difficult to see the virtues of the game in a plain light.

Anyway, that wasn't my main point. I wanted to put down some observations about what is interesting and clever in D&D's mechanics. Consider the following points:

* Hit point mechanics give the game an abstract, resource-based conflict resolution clock that runs down with organic task resolution events. This is exactly the same idea that e.g. Heroquest does with its extended contest mechanics, except the implementation is arguably more to the point. (It's also the original, for what that's worth.) This basic chassis is something very powerful, very functional, and often repeated in other games in various forms.

* The concept of "hit dice" (and "level", its specific application to PCs) as a single-number streamlining of a given monster's or character's mechanical gravity in the game is an elegant, to the point abstraction that does much more work for maintaining the economy of the game's structure than many, many other games do. You can immediately see how something like e.g. Runequest crumbles into an incoherent mess of near-inapplicable rules mechanics once the designers accept the idea that it's OK to expect the GM to track and care about 20-50 numbers regarding each individual NPC. D&D in comparison gives you the concept of universal mechanical weight: this monster is "1" HD worth of meaningful to the situation, this other one is "5" HD worth, and so on. This mechanical basic premise is precisely as elegant as your average complexity-controlled story game.

(Nota bene: Tunnels & Trolls does the same thing with its monster rating, and arguably the details of the implementation are even more stream-lined there, because it's more feasible to run a T&T monster with the single-stat approach than it is for a classical D&D monster. Nevertheless, the idea appeared first in D&D.)

* The game has a general social situation resolution system that is not conflict-based; rather, it has an initial mechanically formal part where the GM establishes a social stance for the NPC counterparty, and then the players get to negotiate what exactly a "helpful" NPC of this or that type is willing to do for them. I find this approach interesting and different from the more usual ways of mechanizing social encounters.

* Contents of play are, rather uniquely to the RPG tradition, created procedurally with a heavy randomization element. The method combines heavy reliance on on-the-spot aesthetic intuition with carefully standardized prep-work. The GM does not preplan or control the experience of play, yet nevertheless proceeding with play is effortless. Is this not a wonderful and clever accomplishment, even today?

* The game features an experience system with objective awards for accomplishment and a bold, even radical effectiveness curve for developing characters. Both of these elements are, perhaps surprisingly considering the game's stature, very rare in other games. They contribute to an unique game experience.

* The game's structure supports one-shots, campaign play, character stables, constantly shifting game groups, a player range from 1 to 20, with low-performance and high-performance players playing together. This feature of ultra-flexibility alone makes D&D worthy of the most careful consideration for anybody trying to actually understand social footprints of games and how the game's premise and methodology contribute to setting the hard constraints within which the game necessarily operates.

* The game has a precedent-based approach to the concept of "rules", with a central referee judging and tracking precedent for the game group. This is entirely unique in the rpg field, and a different paradigm to both traditional GM-fiat and boardgame-like games with fixed rules. Even if nothing else entices, surely an unique, even bizarre look into a world where "game" and "rule" mean entirely different things is of interest?

There's probably other interesting stuff in there as well, but those are what occur to me off the top of my head. I'll also note that this is not some sort of superficial apologia; all of the above are elements of D&D that I have specifically discovered and delighted over myself, and adopted into my design toolbox for future game development. I've learned a lot from older D&D, and it's such an unique game that surely others can as well.

    \n Yeah, my angle was more to consider what is relevant about D&D today. The historical significance is, I think, trivial - I don't think I've ever seen anybody suggest that D&D was not a formative influence for the entire medium.

    But setting aside the historical interest, I don't think that D&D, when read constructively and applied smartly, loses to many games at all in terms of sheer inventive uniqueness. I say this as somebody for whom old school D&D is not in any way a baseline for comparing other games to; for me that baseline is formed by the second generation of games, the "traditional" style of game as I like to call them: if those games are what a "normal" roleplaying game is like, then D&D is just absolutely one of the weirdest games I've ever played. It wouldn't need to go very far to be outside the hobby (as defined by e.g. Runequest) altogether. It sort of doesn't surprise me at all that I didn't get it in my teen years - how could I, when I'd learned rpgs from texts that have an entirely different conception of just about every fundamental of play.

    This weirdness is only emphasized by what Paul notes: due to historical accident, D&D's game design philosophy, the "school" of it if you will, hasn't had a very high profile in the rpg scene of the recent decades. The immense success of the one single game has played its part, but the end result is that there just aren't that many games similar to old-school D&D in their deeper bits. (You might have better luck looking at wargames, in fact - I don't really know.) This, in part, supports the relevance of D&D to this day: while one may argue that e.g. Champions has been superseded by progeny that captures its ideas better, D&D definitely has not been. (Or it there is some game out there that totally supersedes it, do please let me know - I want to check it out.)

    \n For classic (B/E) D&D, I think part of what makes it so interesting is how bare-bones it can be: classes are very broad archetypes, and there's almost nothing in the way of skills or special powers (outside of spells). So this gives rise to the "Player Skill" interpretation of the game, which can be a very creatively empowering style of play for the right people. Compared to the (potentially contentious) interpretation of later editions as games where you're confined by what's written on your character sheet and what options the rules give you (and it can't be said that these editions of the game are not option-rich, at least), it's a game where anything not explicitly spelled out is something that you can try, if only you can conceive of the idea yourself.

    While this is nothing new to a lot of people who prefer freeform and/or rules-light gaming, for gamers who only really knew roleplaying from the succeeding generation of games (I'd wager I'm probably talking about many of the same ones that Eero just referred to as "traditional"), I think it was their "rediscovery" of this style of play by reading older game texts that was, at least in part, responsible for the OSR boom in the first place.

    Conversely, I feel like I owe a shout out to the concept sometimes called "Gygaxian Naturalism" and how it uses game mechanics to model a world, something that really came into full bloom with the first edition of AD&D. While it creates a web of sometimes arcane procedures and restrictions, almost 30 years later I still find something fascinating when I see lists of "No. Appearing," how many noncombatant goblins there might be in a warren, the chance they might be riding worgs, what sort of petty goblin treasures they have, and things of that nature. If nothing else it was a wildly ambitious attempt at something.

    I've always been a sucker for games with lots of random tables, and I think part of this even feeds into the "Player Skill" style of play, where even the GM can't necessarily prepare for everything, meaning she has to (gets to) be as quick on her feet as the players are in reacting to unexpected situations, and which I might go so far as to argue even creates a level of disconnect that can serve as a cushion against over-application of GM force (things like railroading or fudging for the sake of a preconceived plot).

    \n I like that early 3 point alignment better when viewed through a war gamer campaign lens. It could just as easily be Red/Blue/Green, which be more traditional in war gaming.

    For those who aren't following, Red and Blue are traditional war game factions. They're generic stand ins for Side A, Side B in a scenario. ( I assume the choice of those colors comes from the Napoleonic Wars, with Red associated with Britain and Blue associated with France).

    A scenario might simply describe a set up like:

    Red is advancing in march columns down the road which runs across the board with the goal of existing intact at the opposite end through the town of Braunstein on the western edge. Unbeknownst to Red, Advance elements of Blue's Advance guard have thrown up pickets east of Braunstein and have sent back word to Blue's main body, off the board west of Braunstein. As the sun rises, Blue's main body will be arriving piecemeal to throw up a more active defense in and around Braunstein.

    For this, it doesn't matter who exactly Red and Blue represent, and to an extent, it doesn't matter what the war in question is. It's just a classic scenario.

    In those games, occasionally you'd have a Side C, or Green. Green is usually a problem for both sides in some form, having their own goals which make them annoying as either allies or enemies, as appropriate. In a scenario, it would be set up already what Green's goals were, and everyone would have a fairly good idea why the other sides would find Green to be a pain in the butt.

    In a campaign game where you get to choose your army's alignment, choosing Red or Blue gives the basic info to other members of Red or Blue, Hey guys, I'm basically on your side. At most we might have some personality differences or slightly conflicting personal goals, but generally, we're sympatico.

    Choosing Green could mean almost anything. You've likely got some personal goals outside of Red's and Blue's overall goals. And every Green player might have wildly different goals form one another, and maybe wildly different capabilities. If you're team Red or Blue, what you want is either to bring each Green entity/nation/faction over to your side, or, failing that, to keep them essentially inactive. You definitely don't want them to flip and join the other major faction, your enemies.

    I'm sure you can see how some of this gets weird when you start to apply it to individuals, and when you throw in concepts of L/C, G/E.

    Following up on the war game aspect a bit, old forms of D&D were also set up to allow a for a kind of dual-level/parallel sot of war game campaign to be played, and integrated the two, especially in the context of something like a war game club.

    The dyed in the wool war gamers could play a Diplomacy-esque war game, getting together sporadically to fight out the table top army versus army battles, with those players making alliances and betraying one another as needed, with alignment being a rough guide to that. If I was setting that up, all of the national/kingdom leaders would be fighters.

    Going on simultaneously, you have the character players going around, doing loot-and-level operations, building their characters up as useful wild card mercenary types of any class they chose, leading their own small warbands, and offering their services to whatever leaders they wish as time goes on.     \n Some interesting stuff here, but nothing that sounds particularly exciting to me. (Note: I don't have any antipathy towards D&D, but neither do early editions hold any place of special magic for me.)

    * Hit points as an abstraction are basically so bog standard at this point that I don't find anything of noteworthy value in them.
    * Hit dice have always seemed like a weird, awkward approximation because really, how many hit dice a monster has doesn't actually have very much coupling with how dangerous it is. Later iterations of the game with the concept of "challenge rating" do this better, IMHO.
    * Maybe I'm ignorant, but I don't feel like there's that much uniqueness to the power curve either? And actually, the power curve in D&D is ALL OVER the place depending on what edition you are playing (Do you inexplicably stop gaining a meaningful amount of hitpoints at level 10?) what class you are (Sucks if you're a fighter, bro) and so on.
    * I do tend to forget about the social resolution system; Actually, I'm not even sure if it was IN Mentzer. I forget. It's been a long time. This may be one of the rules I am forgetting, but it also seems like one of the weirdest rules to value in a game about player skill.
    * I also disagree that the game has a large amount of randomized content. I mean, yeah, there are random encounters with 1d100 orcs or whatever, but the basic method I've understood to be used for this stuff since forever is the GM creates a dungeon. In detail. With what's in each room (not just monsters, but like "this is the kitchen, and it has these features...") and possibly also custom wandering monster tables if he wants it to be anything resembling a coherent experience instead of "Okay, I guess the random encounter in this dungeon full of orcs is 2d4 fire beetles, because... I dunno, maybe they're pets?" It has always seemed to require a fairly substantial amount of prep that could then be supplemented by random stuff. Which to me is exactly how most other games that aren't on horrible plot railroads work. Not much of an achievement at all.
    * The game "structure" supports different types of play only with heavy kludging, IMHO; It has all the same problems with drop-in-drop-out play that any other game has unless the party retreats out of the dungeon at the end of every session, which is neither implied nor obvious. High and Low performance players are a historical source of friction. One shots are dubiously supported and require all the usual sorts of awkwardness (pre-gen characters, etc.) I'm not seeing anything unique here at all.
    * I'm really not sure what you mean by a precedent-based approach to rules. Isn't this basically GM fiat, only you're supposed to stick with it going forward? I guess this is basically how I've always played with regard to edge cases, because it just seems like best practice (and also obvious - why would I want to fiat something different each time?) and therefore not that unique?
    * Alignment, to me, is kindof a failure, because it's completely up to the GM and the group to make it useful. If there had been any sort of instructive text saying, I dunno "Make sure your party is composed of people of compatible alignments" it would be "useful" except for the fact that that's not really any better than saying "Make sure your group has reasons to work together" and is tied to a weird and potentially extremely problematic idea that people and races(!) have inherent goodness or badness. And the game doesn't even do this - indeed, it more or less implies, even if just by admission, that you can pretty much choose whatever with respect to alignment, therefore defeating the only purpose I've ever found for it. It's NOT a "simple social contract" because there's nothing in the text that makes it a social contract. It's just a weird descriptor of your character. Heck, the Moldvay rules explicitly say that players can pick whatever alignment they want, and they don't even have to TELL the other players what they picked. How is this useful as some sort of social contract?

    \n I think what you find interesting and relevant depends on where exactly you are creatively yourself. I find it important to emphasize that my appreciation for the theoretical features of D&D (the main ones of which I listed earlier) does not rise out of ignorance of modern design; rather, it is precisely because D&D offers its own, alternative solutions to certain esoteric issues that I find it so interesting. The novelty factor alone becomes an immensely important thing once you've drawn all the inspiration you can from your current set of influences.

    However, if another person is "working through" a different set of issues in their roleplaying, or developing games for different concerns, it is possible that the features of D&D do not offer them particular insights. Like other artistic mediums and crafts, roleplaying is not linear, understanding does not grow sequentially. We travel, sometimes in circles.

    This subjective perspective brings us to the idea that what matters in artistic influences is utility, not some kind of objective importance: if somebody gets value of out engaging with whatever, even old D&D, then that thing is "important" because it helps them advance in their work. There is no need, and no way, to prove that importance generally, except by the simple statement: these are the things I need, and this thing has them in it.

    \n Many prominent retroclones do indeed prioritize faithfulness to the old rules. Although the scene does have a lot of nostalgia in it, I think that there are three other reasons, too, for why the retroclones resemble the original rules so much:

    1) The retroclone was originally created specifically to enable access to the old rules in a situation where it was difficult to get the original texts, at least legally.

    2) Often the retroclone is basically an assembled set of houserules from an avid player of D&D. This does, predictably, mean that it's mostly the same as D&D. This phenomenon is encouraged by the adaptation-predicated rules methodology of the game, and the fact that people want to retain cross-compatibility with other players of the same game. All this encourages limiting the "retro-clone" to minor forms of innovation rather than just making it entirely different. And of course, observation bias kicks in here: if the game text ends up too different from D&D, we stop considering it a retroclone, and then it disappears off the radar

    3) D&D is, as discussed, very different from most roleplaying games. Somebody who wants to make a game that preserves its qualities will, necessarily, retain many of the distinctive features. In fact, if you're less than certain about what it is that entices you about D&D, perhaps it's best to stick to it as much as possible? Just change the things you know annoy you, and keep the rest as is, so as to not lose the underlying excellence of the game.

    Those, plus the nostalgia factor, explain the retroclone phenomenon pretty well, I think. Some of the clones are more about OGL-pirating the old texts, some are house rule compilations, while others are simply conservative game design careful to preserve the fundamentals. All of these motivations contribute to making the retroclones an endless parade of minor variations on the base D&D texts.

    (I'll note that I don't mind the retroclone phenomenon myself; I view it as one of the ways that the players of the game have for discussing house rules. There are stranger things under this sky than a parade of nigh-identical game texts, with differences that matter a great deal to the players of the game while being largely obscure for outsiders. It's like wine tasting, pretty much.)

    \n Being the self-described King of the Grognards I suppose I should take a stab at this. Before I do I think there are a couple things to bring to the front of the discussion (most of which has been brought up in some way already, but deserves focus).

    1. Subjectivity. Pretty much everything suffers from it. There probably are NO objective truths in anything we're talking about. Only varying degrees of couched, parametricized, and warranted subjective preferences. As such it's important to remember that the question isn't 'which mechanics ARE good/interesting/unique/useful', but instead 'which mechanics do YOU FIND good/interesting/unique/useful'.

    2. Goals. A thing only 'is' good or bad in so much as it serves to achieve a limited goal in limited ways. This is a subset of subjectivity, but I think deserves special mention in the context of this thread. What I'm meaning is that if someone says mechanic x is good or bad, what they're saying is that it seems good or bad to them as a means to achieve some specific end that they find favorable or unfavorable, and that opinion is likely formed as a result of experiences which may or may not have validity. Which leads us directly to number three.

    3. Bias. Pretty much everything suffers from it. Nearly everything we talk about is probably going to be tainted by our overall experiences and perceptions...both in general and specifically. As such those with generally positive experiences will be able to point to specifics in a more favorable light, while those with generally negative opinions will critique more harshly. As always, any time we can transpose those (a person who loves the game pointing out flaws, or one who hates it pointing out gem flecks) it should deserve special consideration as likely significant.

    4. Place in history (both broad to narrow, and narrow to broad. While I agree that, for the purposes of this discussion, it's beneficial to go beyond 'it was first' or 'it was the only', I think it really is beyond important to acknowledge. By sheer virtue of this fact almost every single mechanic and aspect deserves a certain level of reverence and respect. If you would argue otherwise then you're essentially claiming that Wright Brothers or da Vinci had no interesting or meaningful mechanics either (because after all, every single aspect of flight is better now, and more successfully accomplishes any given goal within that general field). No...EVERYTHING about D&D is amazing for this reason alone.

    Ok, so with all that said and done, here's some things that I love about early versions of D&D (say BECMI/RC or 1st edition AD&D). I'm only talking about each one briefly, but since I've spent so much time analyzing them in the past for game design elements I can extrapolate where necessary.



    *D4, D6, D8, D%, etc - Yes, these existed before D&D, but their inclusion as a set to achieve random results of nearly any subset of numbers is nothing short of genius without which there would be no traditional rpgs. Yes, there are pluses to systems using only one type of die as well, and there are other systems that required sets which came after, but the idea to have the entire set (and to use them all BOTH generally and specifically) is iconically D&D.

    *3D6 attribute generation - The infusion of normal distribution into character creation is astoundingly brilliant and central to the nature of D&D as a somewhat statistically valid simulationist experience. After all, D&D is as much about math as it is about storytelling.

    *Class system - The idea to have one's role within the group generalized like this is fantastic. Not that skill based systems (or other types of games) are any less awesome, but it's an entirely different concept. The wargaming roots of it are glaringly obvious, and bring a multitude of limitations and flaws...but it also creates an experience which is largely uncapturable outside of a class-based system. In fact, I appreciate it so much that I've been lukewarm or even hostile to skill sub-systems and feat sub-systems being included in D&D as a result. To do so completely alters the experience. Either be a class-based game, or don't...hybrids muddy the purpose.

    *THAC0 - So first off, it wasn't done as well as it could have been to achieve its goals. Admitted. However, it came close enough that given the utility of a universal mechanic of this sort I'll let it slide. Variance by class, improvement through experience gain, etc...this is just a nifty idea without which you fail to achieve the flavor of an early D&D game.

    *Name Level - The entire concept of reaching a level of renown and skill at which you gain holdings to administer, and pretty much every aspect of that (followers, strongholds, etc)...just about brings a tear to my eye. Very few other games attempt to run the gamut from penniless vagabond to king and even to eventual immortality. In early D&D it's not merely an option, it's damn near integral.

    *Box set tiers - This is the whole Basic/Expert/Companion/Master/Immortal breakdown. While it was likely done more for costs, time and business reasons it actually lays the groundwork for some vital differences in gameplay that sadly never fully materialized into a coherent framework. I think it's one of the greatest losses between D&D and AD&D.

    *Weapon Mastery - I'll be in my bunk.

    *Immortality - Still in my bunk, but now so is the Dallas Cowboy Cheerleader squad.

    *Dwarven anti-magic - The idea that dwarves are inherently resistive to magical energy is pretty fascinating. It blends so well with the whole 'enduring creatures of the earth' thing. Again, this was never developed enough, and I think it evolved in the wrong direction entirely. Within the early editions, however, it's a mechanic that reinforced the flavor/story brilliantly.

    *Training requirements - HELL YES! It's something so obvious and yet most non-simulationist games completely ignore it. In a class based game where increased skill/aptitude is the point the manner of those gains is intrinsically tied to the greater story. This is essentially the 80's cinematic training montage of RPGs.

    *Racial attribute modifiers - This is one that's absolutely central to game flavor, but that never was fully evolved into what it should have been. Even so, D&D isn't D&D without mechanically different races.

    *Age attribute modifiers - Often ignored by many, this helped reinforce the simulationist flavor of D&D.

    \n The connection that I personally see between D&D and wargaming is in the creative agenda and subject matter: D&D is a game where the play group sets up a commando operation, with a group of elite commandos penetrating a hostile base to achieve a mission. The players establish a team, equipment, strategic and tactical objectives, and the referee sets those up for the opposition, too. Then the scenario they set up is gamed, to see how it goes and to make any necessary judgement calls midway. The milieu is fantasy-medieval, with fighting-men and magic-users as the main operational resources, and goblins, traps and dragons as the sorts of things you may expect for opposition.

    Look at that description. How is that not a war game? The fact that the game uses nigh-revolutionary methods of play (although by no means entirely unique; referees and fog of war are known from more elaborate wargames) doesn't make it a not-wargame, although it may mean that some people won't like it as a wargame because they're used to wargames handling differently. Nevertheless, it's a wargame in the sense of being in the general medium and engaging the creative challenges that wargames deal with. It would perhaps be appropriate to compare to live-action roleplaying: D&D is wargaming in the same sense that larping is roleplaying. (Which is to say, not everybody thinks that it is, but most do; it depends on what you think is at the heart of the form.)

    Regarding tactical decision-making, D&D has plenty of it. It's just not encoded into the rules, which is actually not that uncommon for old-fashioned wargames. I mean, where are the encoded decisions in a game that gives you rules for moving Napoleonic regiments and resolving charges? You're presumably going to use those rules for a whole scenario where you decide where those regiments move and who they charge, which is where the decision-making comes in, but none of those things are in the rules: the rules just tell you how to move and how to charge.

    (This is actually about a wider issue that concerns the whole form of wargaming: is the game about simulating a situation and gaming it out, or is it about providing a fun boardgame that has a historical theme? If the former, then it is entirely normal that the game's rules do not encode and ensure particular tactical choices and challenges. If the latter, then the game has to provide its own abstract game of skill, with the background world relegated into mere color. It's not just D&D where one may question the game's nature, all wargames may be similarly challenged for their lack of, well, in-built game.)

    Similarly in D&D the rules tell you how to move and attack, but it does not particularly ensure that there are meaningful tactical choices involved in those decisions, because it plainly assumes that these choices will emerge from the way those rules are applied to the simulated environment. Once you add the rest of the ingredients you get many tactical choices such as whether to attack or retreat (turn by turn, note), try to hold a chokepoint (notably, the original text is silent about in-melee movement; it assumes that the referee handles it), when to cast your spells or other special attacks and who to attack. The players need to choose how they arm themselves, whether they use formations of some sort, where they move, who they attack and so on.     \n That is one of the many rules-development concerns for D&D that keep me up at night, Paul. (Kidding. Mostly I just think about this stuff before I fall nicely asleep.) I like to call it "linearizing the statistics", because the base conceit is about interpreting everything as flowing to and from a single number.

    I've ultimately come to the conclusion that while HD can serve as the default basis for hit points (obviously), attack bonus and saves (as it does), the armor class is one thing that cannot and should not be linearized under the D&D's mechanical context. The way I've solved that for my personal "stat block format" is that I've dropped AC from the stat block and instead just give an in-fiction description of the defensive abilities, which I'll then reconvert into AC in the moment when it's needed. In effect, AC joins all the "special qualities" of a monster in the description block, leaving HD as the single relevant numerical descriptor of a monster.

    (What do I win by doing this? Mainly strict adherence to conservation of detail, which is a cornerstone principle of my D&D development anyway; a big starting point for my starting to fiddle with the D&D rules in the first place was an attempt to reduce the clutter and lay down a precise framework for how much and what kind of mechanical detail is utilized in the procedures of play. This is why character sheets grow bigger as characters gain levels in my homebrew rules - you literally gain a "budget of mechanical consideration" as you grow in levels and become a more important character for the campaign.)

    I should also mention that the "attack routine" (how many attacks and how much damage) is another default element that cannot be linearised into HD. Once you've swallowed the idea of dropping an explicit AC listing, though, it's no big deal - just read it off the descriptive portion like you do with AC.

    The end result is exactly as Paul says, a one-number monster stat block. Here's an example:

    Ghoul, 2 HD
    Ghouls are malnourished semi-undead humans ridden by a cursed appetite for human flesh. Most are cunning, some are truly intelligent. They are both more robust (+1 to AC) and more agile (+1 to AC) than humans, but are otherwise similar (base AC 20). They can attack ferociously with both claws (normal damage) and by biting (half damage), and do not use weapons, armor or tools of civilization. Ghoul claws and fangs carry a paralyzing agent, save or become immobile for a while.

    The description block can run a ways, obviously - ghouls, for example, have seen a lot of play over the years, and thus there's a lot of minutiae in my head that comes into play when I run them. Nevertheless, there is technically speaking only one mechanical number relevant to them, everything else is fictional detail that provides circumstantial mechanizations where relevant. I added some mechanical numbers in parenthesis in the above example to pin-point where the information is, but technically speaking it's all a fictional description first, and it only becomes mechanical conceits via referee decision. The rule is that "thick skin" gives you a +1 to AC, not that ghouls have AC this or that - you get a thin-skinned ghoul (perhaps somebody who became a ghoul just recently), you get one with a lower AC.

    Out of academic interest, it should be noted that Tunnels & Trolls has its own version of the linearization conundrum. While the game has a very primal mechanical environment for monsters, the concept of special mechanical monster powers does exist.     \n
    * Another view is the "wargames" as played by military commanders in training (possibly throughout history!). You take on the role of one side of the battle, and another player takes on the part of an impartial "referee", whose job it is to make rulings and to bring their knowledge and experience to bear on the game as it develops.

    The expertise of a smart "referee" is considered, in this paradigm, to be entirely superior to any existing ruleset.

    That is the type of "wargame" which D&D is perhaps derived or evolved from. Looking at it that way, the lack of rules in certain areas - and incredibly strong focus on rules in other areas - suddenly makes a great deal of sense.
    \nTo a very great extent. There's a bit more in D&D's ancestral DNA ( including quite a lot of debate in different types of kriegspiel approaches), but yeah, that's the gist of it.

    Worth noting, there was always controversy over whether an "expert" trainer/referee actually knew what the heck they were talking about in making their rulings, even when the refs were trained officers teaching younger officers.

    No big surprise then that recreational gaming with the same approach creates tensions when the ref/Gm also has this authority.

    After all, if we aren't sure whether a trained, experienced field officer knows what the heck he's talking about when making a ruling on things military, why would one expect an 11 year old GM to be better? \n
    I'm going to take a wild guess that this is related to the "Game"/"Simulation" debate.
    \nTo some degree. Some of the debate among war gamers has to do with aspects of handling time/playability as well. Some of it has to do with "fun" and where people find it. Some of it has to do with "game"y rules producing wildly a-historic results ( a classic one being vastly higher casualty rates than real battles).\n
    However, my favourite take on D&D is the variant where the player roles are asymmetrical: the referee (DM) is attempting to present a "Simulationist" view of the game world (although somewhat selectively: at least, where it is possible to do so without reducing the game into an un-fun proposition - most D&D groups aren't interested in accurately simulating wounds and infection, for example, nor settings where there are no monsters nor treasure), by crafting logical sequences of cause and effect in order to create a sequence of gameplay. Meanwhile, the players are equally committed to the "reality" of the imagined fiction/setting, but doing their best to perform competitively within its limitations.
    \nSo much this.

    This is very much in line with the thinking of the niche of war gamer hobbyists I'm talking about.
    \n
    It might be considered "good sportsmanship" at some tables, for example, to play within the limitations of your character's intelligence or knowledge (e.g. your character doesn't know that it is possible to create gunpowder, or the formula for doing so). In such a group, the limitation of avoiding out-of-character knowledge is accepted precisely because it makes it more challenging to play the game, and the players enjoy that particular challenge. (Much in the same way that I would accept the rule not to use my hands in a game of soccer, whether the other team can see it or not.)
    \nAgain, dead on.

    There are a couple of other ways it might come out, especially in war games.

    1) Team games where individual team members all have broadly the same goals, but then have sub-goals that makes co-operation tricky. Often the sub-goals are hidden from team mates, or are up to the players whether they reveal them or not.

    Example: It's WW2 in Western Europe. The Allies are all in agreement that Nazi Germany must be defeated as effectively and rapidly as possible, minimizing Allied casualties whenever possible. One of the Allied players is DeGaulle, who has a sub-goal/personal goal of prioritizing the recapture of Paris. the other Allies have decided to bypass Paris and isolate it, and have built plans to do so. What does DeGaulle do here?

    2) Vague and contradictory goals, often without an obvious "scoring" mechanism. Players will need to prioritize them using their own judgment. Post-game discussion and analysis, based on how players attempted to follow these goals and the results of those attempts define Win/Loss/Other.

    Example: You need to bring the enemy to battle and defeat their local forces, but in such a way that you continue to have an effective fighting force to deal with other enemy formations that could be brought into the local theater later. How much are you willing to risk in open battle, and at what point, even if winning, do you withdraw to maintain your force-in-being for later operations?

    3) The GM/Scenario designer may build in "bait" events or opportunities/traps unknown to the players at the beginning of play based on the above points, or even simply random, unlooked for occurrences that players may deal with as they choose.

    Example: Your army is risking a desperate gamble to take the fighting into enemy territory for a change of pace. Your army is undersupplied, and entering unknown territory, but if the gamble succeeds, it can change the entire character of the war. You're the force's primary reconnaissance commander. Your forces have been scouting away from the main body, and have good intelligence that an enemy supply depot nearby has material your force desperately needs, and is largely unguarded. Certainly this material would vastly improve the fighting capacity of your force in the longer term, but capturing the depot will take time and move you away from the main body of your army. Do you go for it?

    If this knd of challenge revs you up as a wargamer, you're basically the wargamer equivalent of a "narrativist"\n
    I'm not familiar with the debate, but my guess is that such a more nuanced view might obviate the debate entirely (although it may still come up on minor matters, such as whether the group finds Armor vs. Weapon modifiers interesting, or not).

    \n I didn't play D&D growing up in Sweden. I played Swedish roleplaying games in the Basic Role Playing (BRP) tradition like Drakar och Demoner (fantasy game) and Mutant, later played Kult, and Vampire 2nd Edition before I found the Forge and all those games. To be honest, without having played it, I always looked down on D&D (the game, not the players) because it seemed so shallow, focused only on combat and levels and HP. Even though I enjoyed combat in my games, I expected RPGs to cover more situations, and felt that D&D was just a power fantasy where the solution to every problem was violence. (I was wrong.)

    I've always been interested in minimalism, more recently in the ideas of "omit needless words" from Elements of Style, "Perfection is achieved, not when there is nothing more to add, but when there is nothing left to take away." (Antoine de Saint-Exup\xe9ry), and the decluttering movement of getting rid of things you own to create more space. When I read Moldvay's B/X D&D after seeing it mentioned here and playing it a bit online, frankly mostly the Basic part, it felt like I found this chiseled stone that contained exactly what's needed to run the game. I don't have the time or patience anymore for 300 page rulebooks, especially games that don't give you any clear direction of what to do during a session. When I say B/X, I actually mean only B (Basic), but B/X is a nicer acronym so I'll keep it. I haven't looked as closely at X.

    What I liked about many Forge games (say InSpectres, Dogs in The Vineyard, My Life with Master) when I found them was that they presented a clear situation and gave you a minimal set of rules for handling that situation. B/X is the same, in that the game says that the session starts as the group enters the dungeon and ends when they leave, and it gives you the tools you need to run a dungeon crawl: combat, task handling (save vs attribute), light, food, monsters, treasure, and so on.

    One similarity between the Forge games I listed above and B/X is that they all give the GM the tools to generate a situation as part of prep. InSpectres has a table to create a mission seed, Dogs has the town creation guidelines, MLwM has the master creation rules. B/X has a set of tools to create a dungeon where you roll on random tables to place monsters and treasure. Then all four games tell the GM to present the immediate situation and back off, not steer the group in a particular direction to present a GM-prepared story. It's up to the players what they want to do. The GM gives information and applies pressure in all games, but there's no planned story with checkpoints that the group must follow in a certain order, something that was common in the other RPGs I played growing up. Instead of the Dogs town, you have the dungeon and its inhabitants, and they all either want to get rid of or ally themselves with the PCs. As others have said, this is even based on a random reaction roll, so even the GM doesn't know exactly what will happen in the dungeon.

    You could add things to B/X if you wanted to make the rules more detailed. You could add hit locations or an exhaustion system, things I had in other games I played growing up, but going back to Saint-Exup\xe9ry I feel that would take you further away from perfection rather than towards it. Also part of my minimalistic interests is low level B/X play, where you have a very limited set of rule-based options (low chance to hit, low HP so you can't mess up and take many hits, one spell per day), which forces the player to go beyond the written rules and try to navigate the fiction in a way that gives them a chance to succeed. I love how you don't have to balance any encounters to fit the group of players and PCs. It's perfectly fine to put a dragon and a giant treasure in an adventure for level 1 PCs. If they are greedy enough to try to steal anything they might get toasted without standing a chance, but the situation is not designed so that they *have* to fight the dragon as the case might be in a traditional checkpoint-based adventure where the dragon is step 3 of 10 on the way to defeat the evil wizard in the final chapter of the story.

    The retroclone I've played the most is Whitehack, which is similar in many ways that make it easy to run modules from other games, but has more free-form classes and magic, and other innovations. I've grafted in the magic system from Drakar och Demoner (the Swedish fantasy game I played in the 80s) because we use modules from that game, which works perfectly because the scaffolding in the base game is strong enough, or modular enough, that you can pull out one subsystem and replace it without affecting other parts. When I find a situation that Whitehack doesn't handle, I can search OSR blogs and forums for an answer, for example how to handle encumbrance for a horse, how do to best handle sneaking as a group, and so on. Because the clones are so similar, you can easily adopt rules from many different OSR communities, which gives you input from a range of people that may play clones that focus more on different things.

    As I said, I'm inspired by Moldvay Basic and Whitehack. What I've read of Mentzer or BECMI, or AD&D, doesn't feel as minimalistically nice. I guess that instead of searching OSR blogs for rules ideas I could browse those games first, but they fall into "300 pages, my eyes glaze over" so I haven't made an effort to get to know them closer. Since I'm mostly interested in low-level play, they include a lot of stuff that just isn't necessary when that's your focus. It doesn't mean they are bad games, and a lot of people find them more complete games because they handle more situations.

Part of the fun is seeing your characters get mutilated and then trying to bypass or figure out how to get through with the next character. A very high level of "PC lethality" has to be combined with repeated visits to the same location, scores of henchmen, or stables of PCs.

      \n I believe in the referee-GM; if it's just an illusion, and we're fooling ourselves, then it is one that has successfully mislead many clever people I know, myself included, for hundreds and thousands of hours of play.       * The GM has made judgement calls on various situations to the best of their knowledge, taking input and arguments from other players as relevant.
      * This process has actually allowed us to achieve certain truths and insights about the tactical and strategic case studies we've engaged in during the course of play.
      * The above points have held true both when I've been GMing and when I've been a player in games that have attempted this.

      I have, of course, also been a player in games where the referee-GM has not been attempted, and in games where it has been falsely posited (while the GM is really not doing it), as well as games where it has been tried and failed (due to lack of skill and understanding with the proper procedures of play).
