Selling with sex

Clinton
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Directed Promotion
I was reading a discussion elsewhere today and someone mentioned how music sells itself with sex these days: you're as likely to buy the new Christina Aguilera album because you are aroused by her appearance as you are to buy it because it's good. (It's pretty good.)

Why haven't we applied this to RPGs? I look around and see a group of moderately-attractive to very-attractive men and women. I probably lead the pack on this, but not by that much. Why are we not putting our faces and bodies on at least the back of our RPG books, or better, the front? Can you imagine me, recently doused with a bottle of water, my white dress shirt untucked and unbuttoned and a pair of snappy black dress pants with a sword in hand on the cover of The Shadow of Yesterday? Of course it'd sell more. Jason Morningstar is definitely a good-looking guy: why isn't he lying undressed in a bed of well-placed fake roaches on the back of the Shab-al-Hiri Roach?

Has this idea been had before and discarded, and if so, why? I think that it's the key to our future success.

    I think for me a part of it comes down to the way that RPGs are used. I feel like when I pick up a game, its real value comes in how I use it with my fellow players, and while the designer has helped enable the fun, the creative work they do is an enabler rather than the direct source of the enjoyment I get out of my engagement with the material.

    I don't play with the book. I use the book as a launching point to play with my friends. It has something to do with the multiple stages of creativity involved.

    Would I be more likely to buy a book that had a sex-tastic cover with the (female) game designer on the cover? Perhaps, but only if I was already inclined to buy the book to begin with. A sexy author picture wouldn't make me buy a game that I never expected to do anything with and looked like it had horrible design. Then again, I might buy it for historical documentation in my academic studies, but that's neither here nor there.

    Hmmm, I don't know. Avalanche did this. Their first few books were covered in normal gamer art. Then they started producing books with loosely-themed fantasy "bikini-babes" on the cover and their sales sky-rocketed (or so I have been told by a few retail sources).

    To be honest I can't say that I'd want all of us on covers of books. Sex sells in the extreme where I think even the most attractive of us run the gambit of the real. I could be wrong but there aren't many Marc Anthonys among us. Not many of us sport six-packs, have personal trainers, tanning salons and air-brush artists spinning our image.

    On the other extreme there are those whose appearance on their book may, dare I say, hinder sales.

    I have to say that the folk that did Midnight Seduction did this. It also happens to be a game about sex so I guess it's taking the idea to the extreme
