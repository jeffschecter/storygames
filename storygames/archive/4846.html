\n
\nOK, all that said, I'd be interested in views on how much background -- especially historical information -- is necessary/helpful/downright problematic. I'm an historian by trade and training, but don't see RPGs as textbooks, and try (others can assess the success) to keep the history in games accessible and confined to what really does help play. When moving into supplements, though, there is a question in my mind as to how far to go, especially because prospective contributors are proving often much more keen on historically-derived info-dumps with some story plothooks than, say, a scenario or a really interesting NPC whose story and actions illuminate the historican context in a 'show don't tell' way.
\n
\nAm I being over-concerned? Would people be quite happy with lots of extra historical background from which they can pick and choose? Or would this begin to deaden the game, turn it from being a fun exercise in consensual fantasy into glorified homework? Given that I am beginning to sketch out what I want to see in the next issues of the BBC, I'd be grateful for opinions.

    \n It really depends on the kind of play style and experience you're going for.

    Personally, I like to make shit up during the game. I want to have evocative details and a general backdrop, but I never want to be in a position where I'm going to have to say, "So last session I said that the ruling group in the fourteenth dynasty was the Umar Council, but actually that dynasty had a king, and so we need to redo this and that because your plans hinged on evoking the glory of the old democratic days." Or something.

    That said, I know plenty of people who want to have enough details to feel like the fictional world exists outside of them and their characters. They DO want to have to memorize stuff, and the possibility of being wrong, because it then means something when you get it right. Plus it feels more authentic.

    So really, it all depends on what you're going for there.

    \n I like details that give texture and feel, and great currents of history, but I care much less about dates and names. It's important to my understanding to know what the majoy forces at work were in the period of interest (real or fictitious), and what daily life felt like to various people in society. But nothing makes my eyes glaze over in an RPG like a 12-page chronology of the setting.

    Personally, I prefer analysis and explanation to information; the sentence "The Golden Horde's material culture is constrained by the necessity of carrying all their possessions around by horse," is way more useful than an equipment list ever could be.

    \n

    I prefer not to have to think about how I can use a thing; the game product should be a game product, and each piece of information should be packaged inside something immediately and explicitly useful for play. Every loose, unencapsulated fact is another thing to forget and ignore.
