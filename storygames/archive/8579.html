    \n Very varied age range, that. 8-9 is a different beast from 12-13. Some of the oldest girls might like Vampire. Princes' Kingdom would suit the entire range theme-and-setting-wise, but the rules are way too fiddly for everyone to grok them. Possibly Shadows.

    Mostly, make sure they can choose cool and powerful characters that do things, but also make sure they can do girly stuff if they feel like it. They might want to be sneaky fighters, they might want to be beautiful princesses. Expect more focus on social than martial victories - let them roll dice to see who can persuade, manipulate or control an NPC at some point, for example. (This is based on my experience playing with girls that age).

    \n First off, I'd ask the kids and let them pick from a giant pile of games. I don't think any games are really out of reach if the youngsters are motivated. 12-year-old girls might really get into 3:16 and I am in no way joking.

    We just played Mentzer D&D last night (with adults) and it was great. One of the basic D&D clones like Labyrinth Lord, maybe. I've had the weird experience of running D&D 3.0 for a mixed group of tween girls and boys, and here's what they seemed to like:

    1. Making characters and describing them.
    2. Shopping for equipment.
    3. Rolling dice.
    4. Reacting to strong descriptions and situations.

    Everything else in 3.0 just got in the way, but they loved to fantasy color and rocked out, which is why a Basic clone might be a good choice.

    \n For the younger age range of 8-9, it's not about gender stereotypes for me. I don't care if my girls want to play The Princess Game or straight up DnD. However, I do want to minimize the violence, or at least the graphic or murderous aspects. Non-fatal comic-bookish violence would be OK. My girls love superheroes, princesses, talking animals, and faeries. They're going to be drawn toward those interests. If there was a My Little Pony game, guess what? That's what they'd pick.

    When they're older, they might become more interested in grit and grime and so forth. But right now, that's just not where they are. My 8-year old's eyes glaze over at the mention of DnD (though she likes the monster art). Faery's Tale, though, grabs her attention. I'm not being prescriptive about what other 8-year olds would want, but I have little to no agenda about gender appropriateness in games (although admittedly, I'd lose SAN if we were to play My Little Pony). Where it would likely come up might be more me steering her away from, say, a Barbie Princess kind of game.

    \n I'd agree that what is most likely to appeal to a particular group is largely going to be influenced by the types of books, movies, games, or TV they're currently consuming. I don't think the system matters so much as the subject. At the moment, the 'Twilight' book series, is huge with the 10-14 year-old-crowd, especially girls. Thus, anything involving vampires is likely to be a hit with anyone who's read the books. In other words, find some setting the children want to play in and then go from there. IMO, that's a much easier task than trying to guess which RPG will work for your particular group.
    \n
    \nIn the after-school RPG club I run, 4E is a big hit with the students (all but one who are 13-17 year old boys) because it plays a lot like WoW and that's something most of them are in to.* My experiences with my 6-year-old son have followed along these same lines. I started trying to play Faery's Tale with him but he didn't really like the premise. So we moved on to Marvel Super Heroes, with him playing Spider-Man because we've read a few of the comics together. That he could totally identify and get in to. Fast-forward a couple of months: We're now listening the the Spiderwick Chronicles audio books and suddenly he wants to play Faery's Tale.

The one exception is the youngest kid in the group (14) who's read the rules and plays his rogue to take full advantage of his movement and sneak attack abilities.

Mechanics aside, the core idea was to have the main characters be all super-talented girls from Miss Schiffer's Academyrs, but with each player also having talented ( but less well-rounded) non- Academy Girl characters on the side ( along the lines of Ars Magica multiple characters/troupe play).

    I never did complete it, but the core idea was there: female characters are at the core of this game, and that's the focus that always comes back.
