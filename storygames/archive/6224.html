    \n Brand's Top 10 Graphic Novels Ever

    The Pride of Baghdad by Brian K. Vaughan. I don't know if 50 years from now this would still top the list, but fuck -- its about a fucking war that is on right fucking now, and handles it with guts and balls.

    V for Vendetta by Allan Fucking Moore. Only fucking man who makes the list twice, Mr. Fucking Moore. He's a god, worship his dirty beard.

    Astro City by Kurt Busiek and Brent E. Anderson. Series, whole thing is good. Also about as close to real bright four color superheroes as the list is going to get, because super heroes are a drain upon the whole medium.

    Lone Wolf and Cub by Kazuo Koike. (Yes, cheating, as there are 25 of them -- but its one series.) Blood and Samurai mother fucker! This series has more guts, glory, and sex than just about anything ever made in the weakness that is America.

    Fabels: Legends in Exile by Bill Willingham. Series, gets even better as it goes. Plus Snow White is red-fucking-hot.

    Sleeper: Out in the Cold by Ed Brubaker and Sean Phillips. Series. I'm only through 3, and so far they're all good. Really, how can you not love a book in which a woman seduces the main character by kicking a man to death in front of him? You can't, that's how.

    The Fountain by Darren Aronofsky. The movie had problems, the graphic novel is beautiful. Just shows that movies are for morons.

    The Complete Maus by Art Spiegelman.

    Watchmen by Allan Fucking Moore. I feel dirty about putting this here. Everyone puts this here. Fuck them, it barely made the list.

    The Sandman by Neil Gaiman. Series, mid range novels the best. As with all Gaiman's stuff there is brilliance that kind of meanders off before it goes anywhere ethically meaningful.

    \nThink Like A Mountain is a really powerful comic that really made me think about radicalism and direct action in a way that no other media had. The letters pages alone were worth the price, hopefully they made it into the graphic novel.

    Both collections of The League of Extraordinary Gentlemen were smart, funny and gripping.

    I personally loved David Boring as it felt like the zenith of Clowes' work on the topic of sensitive, lonely, artistic self-rejects. For me, a bunch of his earlier stuff delivered a smack to the face, a jeer and footsteps away down a dark alley. Boring is still pretty bleak at times but has a shape, a slight turn to it, as if Clowes has made his peace with something. [edit: for some reason, I consider Ghost World a different thing, and in no way inferior to Boring. Perhaps this is the zenith of his Male reject period]

    I loved the first Persepolis and understand the others are just as good.

    Possibly underrated - I don't know, as I don't follow comics much anymore - is Supreme - Story of the Year. It's both a straightforward, fun, classic supers story and at the same time a commentary on these sorts of stories and postmodern on its own terms - without, to my mind, these goals stomping on one another. It's an extremely elegant success.

    \nThieves & Kings even if the series may never finish. This is particularly good if you're into beautiful fairytales with a dark twist. If you like games like The Princes' Kingdom or Zorcerer of Zo, you'll *love* these books. The Green Book (Volume 2) is out of print, but is available used on Amazon.

    Anything by Los Brothers Hernandez; but in particular, Love & Rockets; and specifically Jamie's Hoppers stories. IMO, the best way to start down that addictive road is with the new(ish) 3 volume "Locas" set which begins with Maggie the Mechanic: A Love and Rockets Book. Love and Rockets is so damn good, and so damn important to the history of graphic novels. Given it's long, involved, and non-linear history; it can be a bit intimidating, though. Fantagraphic actually has a page on "How to Read Love & Rockets." And seriously, anything by either of the brothers is amazing. I just happen to have a long-term creepy fanboy crush on Maggie (yeah... I know... she's just a drawing...). The "New Tales of Old Palamor" by Gilbert is heartbreakingly beautiful, too (and if you're a comics bibliophile, the volumes are damn desirable).

    [Response to the inevitable "I thought Love and Rockets was a band:" No... Bauhaus was a band. L&R was a joke.]

    IMHO, you can never go wrong with Chris Ware's Acme Novelty Library (or any of his stuff, really). Yes, it's been hyped to death, but I think it truly is deserving of all the attention it's garnered over the years.

    If you like the style of manga but think the majority of the content is juvenile and base, check out Buddha by Osamu Tezuka. Pretty amazing work, especially in the visual storytelling area.

    \n I'm totally all over this one.

    Criminal by Brubaker and Phillips is possibly the best comic being put out today. Rotating story arcs about the three sons of men who were part of the same criminal crew, the criminal underworld they live in and the people that populate them. Stories of anger, vengeance and loyalty. You can get the first two trades, Coward and Lawless, right now, and Bru and Phillips just started a new numbering scheme for the singles. You really want to pick up the singles as they have DVD 'commentary' style back matter extras that aren't collected in the trades.

    Scalped, by Aaron and Guera. The other possibly best comic being put out today. I don't want to give too much away about the book because there's a bit revealed in the first issue about the main character that some might consider spoilerish. What I will say is this: Dashiell Bad Horse ran away from the native american reservation that he grew up on as quickly as he could. It's been ten years and he's been forced to come back. To see his mother whom he resents, to work for the crime boss of the res whom he hates, to confront his own demons.

    Sharknife is awesome. His enemy is named Orcasword. This is the best thing ever.

    Anything by Paul Pope, but I would recommend 100%, a romance comic set in a dystopian, safety-over-liberty New York City of the future. I adore Pope's artwork, and his writing can be heartbreaking. Reading his comics is like hearing that perfect pop song that encapsulates what it feels like for you to be in love.

    Grant Morrison's The Invisibles. A tale of rebellion and love that is really hard to encapsulate. I really do think it's as good as, or even better than, any of Alan Moore's more high-brow work.

    Warren Ellis' run on Stormwatch and the Authority. Superhero comics about superheroes actually trying to make the world a better place. Energetic, fun, and smarter than hell. Read Stormwatch first to get the whole story.

    Joe Casey's run on Wildcats and Wildcats 3.0. The best superhero comics ever published. I'm not trying to be hyperbolic. I really do think that these were the pinnacle of the genre. Beautiful artwork by Sean Phillips and Dustin Nguyen, intelligent dissection of the concept of the superhero, and what happens to one when there is no longer a world that needs saving.

    Starman, by James Robinson and Tony Harris. Jack Knight, collector of odd pop culture antiques and son of golden age super hero Ted Knight, Starman, is forced very much against his will to assume his father's mantle and along the way explores concepts of legacy, responsibility, and family. Think a DC version of Spider-man, only even more relatable and human. DC is releasing this in Absolute editions starting later this year.

    The Nikopol Trilogy, by Enki Bilal. A fantastic head trip of a story about the future, a rebel leader, the blue haired albino woman that he loves, and the Egyptian god that has possessed him. Funny, strange, and beautiful to look at. I keep this book in my bedroom just so I can flip through it from time to time.

    Carla Speed McNeil's Finder. Sci-fi comics about culture told through the eyes Jaeger, a man who has been lost to his own culture and is just trying to find a way to live. His wanderlust draws us through all of these fantastic societies that highlight very human issues. Totally captivating.

      \n While there may be plenty of repeat advice, I may as well recommend them still:

There are also short stories on the character.

      Scott Pilgrim- What if you put a slice of life story about a loser into a world where video game logic is common place? You get this.

      And while I'm at it, here's a couple of Manga titles I'm currently enjoying:

      Monster- Done by the author of Master Keaton, the story first starts in Germany in 1986 where a doctor decides to do the right thing and stop the practice of "who is more deserving of his talents" except the life he saved afterwards wasn't so innocent as he thought. A really good psychological thriller that I occasionally joke is "The Fugitive's European Vacation".

      Kurohime- In the exact opposite of the last book, is Kurohime. Taking place in a weird mashup of the wild west, medieval europe and japan, a young gunslinger named Zero is looking for the legendary gunwitch (one who can create witch bullets to create monsters or wild magic spells) Kurohime, who saved his life when he was a child. He quickly finds her, and not at all of what he originally thought she'd be like. The setting, odd ball characters and crazy fights are really worth it, while I do feel it's fair to warn that the first two books feel very formulaic and scooby doo-ish but after that the author wisely starts shifting things around.

      \n Cerebus is the title that started me down the road to being a CBG.

      I was big game and in 1982-1983, a shop called The Dragon's Den opened up in my neighborhood in Sacramento, CA. They sold both comics and games.       \n Moving outside the US and UK...

      My real introduction to comics came through the European classics Tintin and Asterix, both of which are available in many public libraries, if you can snatch them out of the hands of kiddies. Really great stuff.

      Also, if you're interested in something kinda obscure (in America) and different, the Hong Kong classic series Chinese Hero has recently been released in English-language trades by an upstart company called Dr Master. It hasn't aged as well as Ma Wing Shing's later work, such as Storm Riders, but still pretty interesting. You'll probably have to request it at your local comic shop, though. Ma has inspired all Hong Kong comics since and the work of Jim Cheung, who not does a lot of stuff for Marvel, is about 75% inspired by Ma Wing Shing's trademark illustration style.
