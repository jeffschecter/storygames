\n Even if a game doesn't have a GM, there is still usually at least one player who has a lot of authority in the group either because they've played the game before or just because they own the game. That's okay, it happens in board games, too.

A chess newbie who tries to move a rook diagonally is corrected by the more experienced chess-player. That situation might be discouraging or enlightening, depending on the personality of both the newbie and the veteran. It could make the difference whether the newbie becomes an enthusiastic chess-evangelist or a chess-reject. (I've met both, personally.)

So, even in an RPG without a GM, there is still often one player who hasn't played much, and one who has played more than the others. When I was playing Shab-al-Hiri Roach at GenCon last year, there was definitely some facet of the "GM" role as one of the players taught me the rules n' stuff. It faded after the group became acclimated to the system, but it was there briefly.

I'm just wondering if there is some deeper analysis of this "default GM" role. If there is some practical advice for would-be default GMs, that'd be awesome. Just coming straight out and saying:\n
"Hey, you bought this game, so you're probably going to have to teach your friends how to play. Here are some tips on how to teach them."

Tip 1: ...

Tip 2: ...

Tip 3: ...

...
\nHell, writing it from that perspective might make the regular rules text more clear, too. Rather than teaching the reader how to play, you're teaching the reader how to teach others how to play. You've got a cool setting and a cool system, so the reader will inevitably become a champion for your game. You can arm that champion with the tools to instill the same enthusiasm in his or her friends. Kinda like viral marketing, actually. Neat.

So yeah, is there anything like that trundling about on discussion forums or existing games? I've heard the term "alpha player" in passing, is that the same thing here?

    \n I call that facilitation, myself, and often fall into that role, especially with my own games. Notes on how to do it effectively, included in the rules, is a great idea, because it is something that happens in GMless games until everybody is up to speed. You see it happen in regular GM'd games as well, if there's a player who has more experience with the system - that person might drive play and set the tone a bit. Oh! And orienting your entire game text from the pedagogical POV of a teacher is brilliant. That's a fucking great idea.

    \n Hmm, negative examples.

    Making assumptions about players' interests, background, ability, or enthusiasm.

    Explaining why when you should be explaining how. Vice versa. Explaining only one when when you should be explaining both.

    Remaining hands-off when being hands-on would be beneficial for everyone.

    Forgetting the rules.

    Omitting important communication and group formation steps at the beginning, like introducing yourselves, warming up, and having a chat about lines and veils.

    \n Hey Tony, you already did that in Capes Lite. Note, Dogs is also written in a similar way.

    Personally, I dislike the "you are reading the book, therefore you are the GM/Facilitator/Host/etc" approach. I think it leads to badwrongfun social structures. Also, it cuts down the number of people who will want to read/buy your book. Now, you could probably do it "right" in such an engaging way that others will still want to pick up the book (see Dogs), but it's difficult. I'd rather write the book for all players, rather than writing it for all-players-who-might-be-GMs.

    \n A fellow on my livejournal sent me a link to the Munchkin Rigged Demo, a scripted demonstration of a game of Munchkin, complete with special promo cards to incentivize potential teachers to get the product and show it off, thus creating more players. I've no idea how successful the product is, but it seems like a great idea at least.

    The more relevant comment he made was that learning by teaching was a great way to transmit game-information.

    I suggested a corrolary: If a game is too hard to teach, it's probably too hard to learn.

    Of course, that's like saying "A is A" since the act of teaching by definition involves a second party learning whatever information the teacher is giving out. If there is no learning, there isn't any teaching. You know what I mean though. If you can't make the system easy enough for your potential teachers to internalize, it'll be that much more difficult to spread its popularity.

    I'm wondering what the gaming podcasters on the forum have to say about this, since many reviewers like Paul Tevis spend their time describing and teaching games verbally, without any visual aids.
