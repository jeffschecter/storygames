\n This month, Rudy's take on at risk youth and superheroes.\n
It\u2019s your senior year of high school, and you don\u2019t know if you\u2019re going to graduate. You and your friends have been cutting class to sell dope. The Mexican gang that controls your neighborhood is pressuring you to join, and you\u2019re considering it. Meanwhile, your brother\u2500the only one you\u2019ve ever really looked up to\u2500is sitting in a county jail cell for armed robbery.

Still, your Urban Outreach mentor, Mr. Jameson, insists that you\u2019ve got some hidden potential. And he\u2019s right, but in a way that\u2019s nothing like what you could imagine. He\u2019s been assigned to you by the System to make sure you don\u2019t fall through the cracks, but his true agenda will turn your life upside down.

Mentors is a role-playing game for three players. In this mashup between Finding Forrester and Unbreakable, you\u2019ll discover a youth\u2019s true potential as it is revealed through a Pictionary-like mechanic.

    \n Okay, so I read this.

    It was significantly less racist than I expected from the introduction! So uh... congratulations?

    I have the following questions to ask about it, though, with regard to race issues.

    1. Why, in the introduction above, is the gang described as Mexican? It has no bearing on anything in the actual game text.
    2. In my country, talking about "inner cities" is well known as a coded way to refer to black people without actually doing so. What's the intent behind the use of the term here?
    3. Why in the examples of play are the players called "Mike, Rudy and Hank" (names which connote whiteness), but the player character is called "Fatimah"? Is this idea of a group of white people gathering around to play characters of colour intentional? Was there any reason not to give one or more of the players a non-white-sounding name?

    I am also kind of unsure about the message of a game in which we are encouraged to see social workers as corrupting forces.

    I like the Pictionary mechanic, though! That's good.

    \n Hey Meserach
    \n
    \nFirst off, thanks for reading my game. That actually means a lot to me.
    \n
    \n1. What I was trying to communicate here was sort of "what the character was thinking." We don't know the example Probationer's race, but from that line, it might be assumed that they don't identify as Hispanic/Mexican (and therefore might be uneasy about "the Mexican gang" trying to recruit them). Just a way of highlighting some racial tension without actually giving too many details.
    \n
    \n2. The place is definitely called Inner City for a reason. When I made this game, I wanted that term to conjure certain images for the reader. Certainly, I wanted it to cause people to think about the area as being predominantly minority. I want that kind of knee-jerk reaction. This game is meant to provoke uneasiness about race and social class, and I think that using a loaded term like "Inner City" encourages people to actually stop and think about the ethnicity of those involved. Ideally, even though the game does give the option of making the Mentor a relative, it's kind of intended that they be of a different race or social class than the Probationer. That separates "their world" from the Probationer's even more, and makes the idea of them using the Probationer even more, well, insidious seeming. In short, the term is there to make people uncomfortable.
    \n
    \n3. As for the examples in the text, I straight up just used our own testing group for the names, like William said. Fatimah was my Probationer in one of the games we played. I'd love to see an all-white group portraying POC characters- I feel like that would make things extra uncomfortable, which as mentioned in #2, is the point.

    \n Hey again Thomas,
    \n
    \nI see keeping the players on edge as a good way to nail down the game's atmosphere of, well, racial and social tension. I think that if people are a bit uncomfortable with the game going in, it will come across in small (or maybe not so small) ways when they play their characters. I feel that's key to experiencing this game at its fullest; having people in that very particular mindset is (I think) the only way that the interaction between the Mentor and the Probationer will feel like I want it to. When you play the Probationer, it should feel like you're being condescended to. When you play the Mentor, you should feel like you're being condescending. That relationship, involving that kind of "passive," indirect abuse, is what really makes the Mentor a true "villain" in my eyes- not his scheme to take over the world (or whatever).
    \n
    \nAlso, I think that kind of relationship is somewhat inherent in the source material (movies like "Finding Forrester," "Precious," and "Dangerous Minds" give me that kind of feeling anyway). Actually that's not entirely true. I guess the weird feeling I get is more when I hear people talking about those kinds of movies, as opposed to watching the movies themselves.
    \n
    \nDoes that make some sense?

    \n When I read this game, I never saw the racism. Maybe it's my ability to not read too much into social ideals (aka, care too much about skin color, since calling someone a different race implies they are a mutation of the original source). I saw this game as a game about manipulation and more importantly, manipulating mailable minds who haven't been trained to see someone's true intentions. To me, this doesn't even need to include race as a possibility for tension, but is totally fine having it in there to inspire people unaware of the game's true intentions. A lot of the games you guys are testing out seemed to be very meta once investigated, and I like that.
