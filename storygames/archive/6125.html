\n My colleagues from the Experimental Gameplay Project, Kyle Gabler (2D Boy) & Kyle Grey (EA Tiburon), along with a small handful of other indie videogame designers have started Experimental Gameplay Apparel. They sell t-shirts for the indie videogames they've made which come with free copies of the game on the t-shirt. The games themselves are freely available online (with perhaps 1 or 2 exceptions; those shirts come with demos instead of the full game). It is essentially the webcomics business model, where you give away a product, and then sell means of identifying with the product.

You may have clicked on the link above and realized that the store is not open yet. I say to you, this does not matter.

The shirts (with games attached) are already available nation wide in the US via Target stores for $12 a piece.

This shocks and amazes me as a feat of indie games gumption.

    Got me thinking, some, too: Wouldn't it be cool if there wereLong Live The KingT-shirts (a set, I'd guess)? They could show the cards' cover art on the front, with the victory conditions and privileges (special rules) on the back, for each role. In general, I can see a lot of traction for rule-bearing T-shirts in LARP games.
    \nOnce when browsing Cafepress, I found a set of T-shirts intended for a wedding's rehearsal dinner: each identified the wedding guest by their role. "Father of the Bride", "Sister of the groom", "Best Man", etc. And I thought, naturally "I could make a LARP out of this".

    But paying $15 or so per LARPer was beyond my budget, and I don't know if my friends would each chip in $15 for one night's entertainment and a T-shirt that wasn't much use afterward.




    But you could totally give a really basic set of rules for the LARP, and then have each character have one "exception based" rule, like in a card game. So if the conflict rules involved playing playing cards from a hand, maybe my T-shirt lets me see your hand first, or restricts the cards you can play, or has some other funky effect.

    Or doesn't have a mechanical effect but establishes limits on player interaction: "Once per conversation, this player can demand a truthful answer from you, and you must answer them truthfully." "Any male character finds this character extremely attractive." Etc.

    I think these rules would best apply to the person not wearing the shirt, but instead apply to people interacting with the shirt wearer. To avoid having to read your shirt upside down.

    Maybe make a line of shirts which maps to various common functions in a popular LARP (I'll use Vamp, Were): Obfuscated, Crinos Form, Majesty, Etc. Your shirt tells your role to others who might be playing, as does their shirts; and the system does like what you said (i.e. read what I can do to Role FOO on his shirt, he reads what he can do to or ask of me from my shirt text--heck, you could even have different rules for if I've snuck up on him, on the shirt back).

    Anyhow... this would make a good new thread: Make a Game That Uses T-Shirts. Game Chef 2009, anyone? Imagine the revenue stream, if your game was geared to con-play, with groups in the scores, and a ton of roles available as shirt!
