Before Christmas, I ran a one-shot LARP, with a werewolf theme. It was very rules-light (any conflicts were handled by GM fiat or player negotiation) and it went rather well. There's an Actual Play report on The Forge.

I want to discuss one part in particular. At the end, some of the PCs (decided by a vote) changed into werewolves. I told those werewolves that they had five minutes to gather their pack for the final attack: by running into the convention hall and persuading as many people as they could to play werewolves.

It came off really well. They rushed off and, five minutes later, fifteen werewolves burst into the room, howling. I stood on a chair and shouted out some basic combat rules I'd worked out. And then there was a very quick combat, in which the werewolves died and some of the humans survived. It was a wonderful moment: I've got a very clear memory of fifteen (ish) werewolves lying on the floor after the battle.

Oh, wait, here's a picture.

I'm currently planning another LARP - the Meat Loaf one - and wondering whether to do a similar stunt.

Here's my problem. Correct me if I've got the theory wrong here, but this is illusionism, right? I'd decided before the game started that there'd be a humans-vs-werewolves battle at the end and railroaded the game to reach this battle.

Now, to be fair, the battle could have been reached several different ways. As it happened, the players decided to stay where they were and mount a defence, so the battle was a good ending. But if the players had gone hunting the werewolves, they'd probably have found themselves surrounded: and I'd have used the "final battle" ending. Hell, if the player had wanted to negotiate with the werewolves, I'd have let them: and then a rival pack of werewolves would have attacked them to end the game.

It's also important to note that I didn't tell my players about the ending or that there'd be any sort of surprise ending. (I prefer it that way. I like to surprise them.)

Now, looking through some of the past discussions on illusionism, it's often thought to be a bad thing. Some discussions compare it to cheating on your wife. Others acknowledge that it can be useful, if you tell your players what you're doing (which I didn't).

So help me out here. I'm not that well versed in the theory and I think it could be useful here, especially in designing my next game. Do I accept that I used a nice, high energy stunt to end the game? Or did I use a dysfunctional technique which I shouldn't use again?

Graham

    Bullshit on "the GM has an event which will happen in mind, and it's going to happen, one way or the other" being dysfunctional. It can be applied dysfunctionally, sure (like when this skanky GM I had in college fiatted that my character had to be enslaved to his . . . even after I killed off the character and started a new one). But it can also be a really powerful way of creating plot that the players own in a different way from plot that they invent themselves.

    Epic tragedy, for example, always ends in Doom. That doesn't mean that all epic tragedy games are dysfunctional just because we know something about what will happen in the game's future.

    It sounds like you gave the players plenty of agency in how your event was going to happen. You didn't decide, "Okay, these people have to fight these other people, no matter what." It sounds like you had an event in mind, but you were ready to contextualize it and give it meaning based on what the players were doing. A story isn't just about the things that happen, it's about the meaning those things have to the people experiencing it; a game isn't just about the actions that you take, but also about what those actions mean to you. In the context of our story-game-zombie-children-things, you can let people own the narrative acts, or the meaning of those acts, or both. It sounds like you chose to define what the act would be (a fight) but let the players construct the context and meaning of the fight, and that sounds just fine to me.

  • Brand_Robins
    May 2006
    Illusionism = railroading without the players knowing they're being railroaded. Or at least, with the GM trying to hide the fact that they're being railroaded.

    So the GM who says, "All roads lead to Rome, but you get to chose your road" and lets you take your road, isn't an illusionist. The GM who says, "You can go wherever you want" but always makes the characters end up on the Via Appia into Rome, is a big illusionist.

    At least, that's how I've always understood the term. The "illusion" is "the illusion of choice."

    Railroading isn't dysfunctional if everyone is having fun, and if everyone knows that they're basically being guided on the grand tour of thrills and chills or whatever. In a con game, railroading is endemic, and many very popular, enjoyable and successful con games are run as such.
    I think most people expect a big finale, which does sound like more fun then a fizzle; even less structured con games usually have a constrained opening scene and a constained climatic big fight/showdown whatever at the end.
    In a LARP, a lot of the fun is in the player interaction - the play, rather than the story; it's fine to relax into play, knowing that someone else is responsible to move the plot to the big finish. Unless that ending feels forced, intrusive and jarring. But that's where a GM's skill and flexibility comes into play.

    Oh, and in a big Con multi-player game like a LARP, people damn well know there's a railroad. So no illusionism (per Brand's helpful definition), therefore, functional and Forge Glat Kosher.

    My take has been (and probably always will be) that illusionism is risky. Out and out railroading is decidedly risky, and probably not maintainable when playing with adults.

    But illusionism can play out really well. Or, on the other hand, it can play out really really poorly. It does take a fair degree of skill, and once you have the skill it's very hard to avoid the temptation to use it.

    And one of the possible saves from a discovered illusionism is a deflection into participationism. In fact, I'd argue that a lot of players make that jump on their own - they realize, and even come to expect, that the GM has something he's working towards, and is willing to provide them with latitude about how they get there.

    The danger is that a player will discover the illusion, and feel betrayed. And then it's really hard to save their fun, now in this game, or possibly ever again. I think that ultimately the question over whether illusionism being inherently dysfunctional revolves around how great you think the chances are of this, and how much you fear that danger.

    My take on illusionism/participationism is that it can be very functional; of course it is, since almost all of the GMs around me practice something like that. In the best versions of this, I see that the players gladly follow the key thread that's out there, because that's where the fun stuff is; in exchange the players have enough flexibility to apply their own take / solution to issues, and at times pursuse self-driven side-plots (but this isn't always appropriate). Things I've heard include "I've got to sit down and figure out the plot for the next episode", "this next ep will be more heavily plotted", and "I hope my character isn't messing up the plot you've set up" (I think this was when I didn't have one set up!).

    The above is participationism, I suppose. And I'm pretty sure that's what you have: players who've come to expect that the GM to have a more or less planned thread in mind, with a likely ending, and they expect the GM to keep secrets like your ending. Your part of the bargain is to not be too obvious or narrow minded with the ending: if the players have achieved real, lasting peace by virtue of some really cool sacrifice or something, then it would be wrong for a GM to still press on with the plot thread despite what the players had done; that's probably when a participationy game goes off the rails, as it were. But the GM has a lot of leeway in setting up a scenario where everyone has buy-in into a very likely end, such that there's almost no chance of avoiding that ending, and that's okay.

    One risk that could crop up is that a player goes in earnestly, and participates along, but ultimately is not 100% engaged in the thread that's there. Perhaps he was hoping for werewolf court intrigue. (???) So he's disappointed in the thread you provided, but that just happens sometimes. I think it's acceptable, and most RPG players understand the implicit social contract of participationist play.
