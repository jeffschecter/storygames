\n I recently came to the conclusion that if I had to get rid of all my RPGs except one (or shove them into storage and just keep one on hand) it would be Dragonlance 5th Age. Mainly because it's easy to learn, and works just as well for someone who likes tactical crunch like GURPS or D&D as someone who just wants to tell a story and do method acting and gets incredibly bored with anything to do with numbers. Wide appeal and doing it well being the key point.

Then I saw this; FATE Power Cards and there seems to be the same thing going on - a (potential) wide range of appeal, while doing it well.

That's an interesting thought to me, because there's this notion that seems to go with buying new RPGs - that I'll eventually find one that I'll like and someone else will like too - of finding a holy grail game that works well for everyone. Maybe it's a pipe dream, but it's worth exploring nonetheless.

I also think about the OSR, and pretty much the first thing that comes up - rolling stats. It seems to me that the entire problem with the system is that element. Take it out and replace it with something better and it's pretty tolerable. I figure for me the issue is that with things like racial class and level restrictions, at least the constraints are fixed and I can work within them, but bad rolls to start with can bone me no matter how hard I plan, and good rolls can benefit me far better than any planning could. On the flip side, there may be another (hypothetical?) person who's content to let his characters die until he rolls one up that has solid scores and settles for that. Rather than planning it's laziness and patience, and the reward comes as well.

The thing we have there again is a system that offers elements which appeal to quite different people, and overall leave them happy.

So what about this thought; of a system that you could pitch to almost anyone and have them say they'll play it, and they'll actually enjoy for the system and not the company. Possible? Desirable? Does anyone think it exists?

    1- "I, the Narrativist player, would like this tactical game a lot more if it were better explained!" Possible. Depends on whether such a player *also* enjoys tactical play.
    2- "I, the Narrativist player, would enjoy this very complicated narrative game design if it were better explained!" Extremely likely - games like Spirit of the Century and Burning Wheel are complicated enough that a lot of hand-holding would mean players are better-informed about what's going on in the game, and thus better able to a) give it a go and b) decide if it sounds interesting.
    Oh, and the phrase "Narrativist player" here just means someone who prefers that sort of play above others.

    So, Tyler - which one did you mean? I really do think that strategic/tactical games and dramatic/emotive games tend not to have a lot of common ground. It's not impossible to cover both, of course, but it's tricky, especially if mechanics for, say, social combat and for firefights start diverging - there may be a tactical element to escalating a conflict in Dogs in the Vineyard, but the tactical thinking ultimately serves the emotional/dramatic tastes of the player, rather than serving to create conflicts that are tactically complex.
    ALL games have some hint of tactical or strategic thinking to them; that's how their economies and reward cycles function. But obviously some games make the strategery a part of both "using the rules" and of "what happens in play".

    \n A few thoughts going all the way back to the beginning of the thread:

    First, in any group endeavor, especially artistic endeavors, you are not going to get everything you want, period. You can't even get four friends to have a favorite restaurant, why would anyone in the history of mankind think that you can always get all of your tastes fully satisfied in a group working together on something as extensive and varied as your typical roleplaying campaign? Nobody thinks that who has thought about it for five seconds.

    So the question becomes what are you willing to give up? Take the dislike of stat-rolling in OSR-style games. Maybe you don't like it. Okay, but will you do it in return for something else that game does really well that you do like? Will you do it in return for playing with this group that you do enjoy? Will you do it if you only have to do it once or twice in a game campaign? This is the nature of compromise, this is the nature of all shared enterprises, right down to what you want on your pizza.

    \n I think looking at system is the wrong approach. It's not the system that makes or breaks the game, it's how it's applied.

    What I want in a game is characters in conflict, making tough, meaningful choices. You can do that in GURPS, you can do that in Storyteller, you can do that in Nobilis. I am minimally interested in winning by proxy when my character thumps the bad guys. You can do that in GURPS, you can do that in Storyteller, you can do that in Nobilis.

    Now, some systems are going to be better suited to some creative agendas than others, but this really shouldn't come as a surprise to anyone on this board.

    By comparison, something like stat rolling is not even on my radar. If you have stats, you have to determine them somehow, and rolling works as well as anything else. If I have to roll stats to get meaningful, difficult choices, I'll roll stats without a second thought.

    And while you can't get *everything* you want, it's certainly possible to get *most* of what you want. My friends don't all share a favorite restaurant, but I can easily find four whose top five restaurants have significant overlap with my top five; also, I have some friends I know to call when I feel like rodizio, and some friends I know to call when I feel like Neapolitan. The notions that friends always do everything together, and that not inviting someone who will not enjoy what you propose to do is a sort of ostracision, are geek social fallacies: if I limited myself to only the restaurants that *all* my friends enjoy, much as you propose to do with your universal game, it would be a pretty miserable experience going there every time.

    \n Think of it more differently, it's not about once you've already got the friends. Think about meeting someone new, and deciding which restaurant to take them to. So there nobody has to worry about having the meal be too expensive for them, while at the same time someone who's used to eating at more expensive restaurants doesn't feel they're taking a step down due to the ambiance, quality of service and the food.

It'd be nice to have an RPG like that too, where if you want to introduce someone to a game and don't want to spend half an hour picking their brain to figure out what it is they really like, and in doing so make them decide that it's just not worth the trouble, you just decide the game, show it to them, and you can be reasonably certain that they'll have fun with it and find something they like.

    \n Tyler: Excellent!

    I've been meaning to write a lenghty rant about how we need a new set of categories/labels whatever to sort RPGs by. It would also be useful to dispell all these silly "rpg vs not rpg" arguments imo. Frex Settlers of Cattan, Jungle Speed and Parcheesi are all boardgames. They couldn't be more different, but nobody has a beef with calling them boardgames.

    But with RPGs we hit this brick wall where people start discussing whether Fiasco, WHFRP3 and D&D are actually the same kind of game or not. Age, players and time is an excellent point to start from, but how about other categories such as:
    -"GM-full/GM-less",
    -"Genre" "Setting" etc.
    - skills that the game promotes: "Tacticall Skills, Strategic Thinking, Improvisation..." etc.
    -Co-op? Competitive? Freeform?
    -categories: Adventure, Exploration, Economy, Negotiation...
    -techiques & mechanics: bidding, dice rolls, card draws, roll & move, scripting...
    -language dependency, length of text/manual, level of complexity, quantity of rules...

    Boardgame geek does this and while it makes matters more complex and multi-layered I think it's an excellent way to describe games. But people are lazy. It's easier to call WHFRP3 "not a RPG" because it has cards and tokens. Sigh.

    So: yes!

    \nRPGgeek (the sister site to Boardgame Geek) is a start, but it doesn't feature such in-depth writeups as BoardgameGeek, and a lot of them are incomplete. But look at this writeup for Mouse Guard.
    "Anthropomorphic, Kid-Friendly, Low fantasy", "Core rules", "Description based, narrative, uses d6s, dice pool, trait/aspect based".
    If we close an eye to the fact that a lot of those tags are bullshit, it's a pretty concise and valuable list that tells you a lot about what kind of tastes Mouse Guard could satisfy.

    Designers and publishers including such a summary of the game's contents would also be a big step in this direction. The only way to figure out what a game is like before buying it now is to read review and actual play reports, which can often be incomplete, subjective etc. Why not stick a bunch of this info right on the book or box. Like a game statblock. It would be easier to figure out what tastes it caters to, what it includes, how it's supposed to play etc.

    I don't know. That's just my opinion.

    If I had the skills I would build something like Last.fm or Pandora for games. Games would have tags in two or three categories and could be sorted either by these tags or popularity or "relatedness" to other games. Then if you said you liked games with dice pools and D&D, you'd get a suggestion for Storming the Wizard's tower or whatever.


    Also: An interesting thing Graham is doing with his Cthulhu Dark project is "globules". You've got a very light and basic core system that could fit on half a page or something, maybe a page. And then you get a lot of these rules variants, plug-ins and add-ons that are a few paragraphs each (most of them at least). It's almost a build-your-own-game kit. Would that be something you're looking for?
