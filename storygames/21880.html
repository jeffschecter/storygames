hamnacb said:

What is the difference between 'spicy roll' and 'princless play' from the player's perspective? How can they work together or why not?
@Eero_Tuovinen?

\nI think that practices similar to the "spicy roll" have been used a lot in a princess play context, and successfully too. They evidently go together well.

They're not the same thing, however, assuming I understand the "spicy agenda" correctly. The way I understand it is that it's a new name for participationist GM-story railroad. The name comes from using task resolution dice rolls as a dialogue technique; rolls are interpreted in a vague fashion where they essentially become an improvisation aid for the GM, and occasional justification for player pushback - spice on otherwise diceless fiat resolution.

Given this understanding, it's a classical comparison between a technical framework (what has occasionally been called "technical agenda") for structuring a game, and an actual creative goal of play. The "spicy play" is a method of defining player roles and activities during play, while "princess play" is what you're attempting to achieve. The former seems to me rather well suited to achieving the latter.

Some specific features of spicy play that have the potential to work for princessing:
* All sorts of descriptive rules for defining characters exist, which helps you establish an interesting character identity. These descriptive rules are clearly under-utilized by the resolution system, which is a good thing, as you aren't forced to optimize the character towards some specific task unless you want to.
* The GM does not have an intrinsic motivation to challenge and break the player characters; he doesn't even need to care about them very much in his GM role as long as they don't stray from their assigned story roles. Plenty of space for the players to strut their elf-stuff.
* The task distribution between the GM and character players frees the character players from having to care about things like "what happens next": they're free to focus on princessing, which is ultimately an aesthetic, non-strategic activity that requires a background context (which the GM provides) but doesn't necessarily feed back into it.

On the other hand, there are totally Spicy games that are not very princessing-friendly. Call of Cthulhu is my go-to example of a Sim game - also a very Spicy-friendly game - that is very much not an ideal platform for princessing. The game presumes that the player characters are much less interesting than their setting, requires them to be rather ordinary, doesn't even let them be conspicuously good at what they do... it is in many ways the opposite of a typical princessing game like say anything from Whitewolf ever.\n

    \n DeReel: princess play is not the same thing as being an actor. Princess players generally see strong inborn meaning in the character roles they inhabit (I would say "immerse in", but that's another terminological kettle). I agree with Yuka about much of the payoff being internal. There are social rewards to princessing as well, but you are your own first audience when doing this stuff.

    For example, one of the players in our gaming group has a clearly observable interest towards playing macho, honorable warrior type characters. He usually picks characters like dwarves, paladins, noble savages or similar in various games. His favourite character play moments, the "princessing opportunities", revolve around tough male bonding (boisterous laughing, drinking, wrestling with bears), justice (avenging wrongs, giving bold speeches before battle) and similar themes; he generally likes situations where it's pretty obvious how a brave warrior type would tackle them. We tease him regularly about how he tends to play to a type, even in games where princessing is not particularly well-supported. He plays a "tough Irish cop" in Call of Cthulhu [grin].

    From my observations this player rather enjoys princess play, but it's not because he likes to Act in character - it's this specific type of character that appeals to him, not the process of acting by itself. I realize now as I'm writing this that this guy happens to incidentally also have a theater hobby, so he does in fact apparently like acting as an artform, but believe me - I can distinguish when a player is playing to his type and when they're simply taking a character and running with it.

    These "typecast" princess players aren't nearly the entire population of gamers amenable to princess play - I think that it's a pretty common creative agenda that most of us can enjoy. However, they illustrate the psychology well: to be truly entertained by princessing you actually need to find the role-inhabiting process somehow personally meaningful. The character concepts you find meaningful in this way will change over the lifetime of most people, and some people always have a wide interest in various kinds of roles, but it's always important to be true to whatever it is that you find relevant yourself.

    I myself used to have an inclination towards magical and elfy character concepts when I was starting with roleplaying (I was a "pretends to be an elf" kid, I guess), but then I became a GM for so long that I'm only slowly coming back to grips with what I actually like to play. I think that I would need more and more consistent opportunities to be a character player in Simmy games that support princessing to really get to the bottom of this personally.

    \n I'm presuming that Princess Play, like many sorts of sim play, requires psychological buy-in from the players. So like, if you like the pre-gen you're handed, maybe there's no problem there. But if you don't, then you won't "act it" and "express it" the way you would a character you actually want to play. I'm not sure to what extent Princess Play can encompass a "cold" portrayal.

    If I get what you're saying DeReel, it's that you could fail to achieve satisfying Princess Play by, for example, trying to play someone charismatic who can't mechanically charisma their way out of a wet paper bag, and I think that's true: mechanics can reinforce or hinder your expression of your character. I'm not saying that character optimization and character expression can't coexist, just that decoupling them doesn't have to be an impediment, and can actually work to the benefit of either or both. Particularly if the primary draw of PP is passively psychological, then it's probably more beneficial to eliminate the possibility of failure hindering it than it is to rely on success bolstering it.

    As an aside, in the Japanese roleplaying community there's an entire defined technique dedicated to this sort of disconnect, called "Makenai Roleplay" (something like "not giving in, not losing"): it's the practice of trying to use roleplay to spin bad mechanical outcomes into something that still maintains the ideal of your character.

    \n I myself think that princess play is a specific type of (or perhaps vessel for) simulationist creative agenda, yes. To be clear, this is an experiental conclusion and not structural speculation as such: princess play feels very much akin to some other types of play that I think are Simulationistic. They all blend together seamlessly in the right conditions, really, which I guess might just be my imagination because it's suspiciously precisely what GNS theory would predict [grin].

    Others I know of... at least these come to mind (and yes, they also have weird names):

    Dollhouse play is when you enjoy building fictional stuff and parading it around. Point-buy games like GURPS get into this occasionally, and Ars Magica is basically just an extremely ambitious "let's all build a wizard castle together now" dollhousing project. This differs from princess play mainly in the way the relationship between the player and character is construed; dollhouse play is less absolute about the intimate relationship, it's less about "I need to be an elf" and more about "let's make a cool elf together" or "let me show you this cool elf I made".

    Substantial exploration is when the game has a basis in prior art and focuses on discussing that prior art from various fresh perspectives. Call of Cthulhu, some MERP motivations, various Star Trek and Star Wars games... My own Chronicles of Prydain campaign runs on this + princess play + some plot stuff, pretty much.

    GM story hour is the railroad plot stuff: the game delivers a first-person perspective on a story that is hopefully narrated skillfully. Horror games in general often run on almost exclusively this stuff. Dread, Dead of Night, etc.

    Mechanical monkeying is a thing that comes up in the heaviest type of Sim games, such as the classic point-buys and Ars Magica and whatnot. I think that it's a rare type that is difficult to get right, as it requires the players to be successful in crafting the system itself as a chassis of poetry for the actual subject of the game; if there is no specific and particular beauty in the way you solve the point distribution issue in simulating the Noldor vs. the mortals or whatever, then you're just wasting time with mechanical overhead instead of engaging this type of Sim value-add. You need to make mechanical discovery, and many systems aren't even capable of offering that.

    I'm sure that there are other varieties of Sim, of course - this is just a list of what I've experienced personally over the last decade with a sufficient impact for me to have noticed it as being structurally discrete. The smart monkey might notice the relationship the above list has to the Forgite "elements of Exploration" analysis, which I do not believe to be a coincidence.

    \nI'd say that it's helpful for the player if you understand yourself and know what you like. It's difficult to stumble upon a character that's fun for you to play for its own sake by pure accident.

    A strong princess play character is not built on abilities or premise so much as they have a theme: Wolverine is "the best at what I do" with all the macho teeth-grinding that implies, for instance. Many people play essentially on instinct, but it doesn't hurt if you understand why you like Wolverine - that you're intrigued by the warrior lifestyle of a self-satisfied badass with nothing left to prove.

    (By the way, this "theme advice" is a staple of traditional rpg text ritual content - the shallow parts that we skip when we read them because they're all fucking same always. Trad games often pay a lot of respect to princess play, even if the concrete support is usually haphazard. I've never really understood why you actually would want to start your character creation by freeforming up a character idea, but this often useless or even harmful conceit is actually useful for a princess play orientation.)

    Once you have that concept, there's probably all sorts of game mechanics involved in giving it concrete expression in the given game. The real challenge during play is about realizing the character in action, however: how does the wolverine-ness of the character actually get portrayed in the activities of the game? Here it would be ideal if the game actually wanted you to wolverine all over the place, otherwise it's just going to be disruptive as fuck.

    Every princess play character concept essentially comes pre-matched with its own "core activities" or "emblematic situations". Ideally the game "supports princessing" by having some way of achieving those situations. If this is the case, then Wolverine gets his turn at some point: maybe it's a combat game of some sort, so if you wait enough, the GM gets around to it, and you get to spout the tagline and turn the room into red mist. (To perhaps wallow in the societal displeasure this causes afterwards, or whatever your specific Wolverine take is about.)

    My theory is that princess play is performative: while cool-down periods are practically necessary, they must be book-ended by opportunities to actually perform the character archetype, or there's no point to playing the game. It would be wrong to say that it's the only fun part, but I believe that you will have more fun playing Wolverine if you get to do the things Wolverine does: you try to solve things with violence, and hopefully there's a Cyclops there to hold you back from doing it when inappropriate; you flip all spiritual all of a sudden as you reign in the raging beast inside you; you become a righteous avenger in response to the circumstances. The individual games have nuanced interfaces that the players use to actually perform their character roles, so doing these things may imply dice-rolling and giving thespian speeches and whatnot, but the real unifying theme is that you get to rub your character vision all around the play space - that's the creative reward.

    The question of what good princessing looks like is an interesting one, as it depends on how a given game is structured - what it expects the players to do so that it flows well as a joint creative exercise. It may be the case that it's actually important to the game that your princessing entertains the other players in some way, for instance. Often rpgs face the challenge of having to entangle a sometimes very solipsistic player character concept in a meaningful way with other complex material prepared by some other player. For example, a superhero game may actively rely on both the player doing Wolverine in a vivacious way and him willingly entanging Wolverine with whatever it is that the GM has brought to the table.

    From this viewpoint I'd say that good princess play is about flexibility and an ability to engage with the game outside of the narrow perspective of waiting to be let loose. I know that I enjoy playing a princessing game like D&D 4th edition with people more if they have a rich, varied and flexible interface between their character concept and the external world. It's much more interesting to GM for a princess character who actually reacts to most situations in a colorful way instead of just sitting there like a zombie until their specific fetish gets trotted out. Wolverine is actually an example of a character who can be a drag in this regard if the player's Wolverine vision is that the only thing he ever does when not murdering somebody is drinking beer - that's something you got to plan around instead of expecting the player to support you, in many games.

    It is worth remembering that princess play doesn't usually form the entire creative payload of a given game. You usually have other things to enjoy in there as well, such as plot, some clever mechanical monkeying, or whatever. The opportunities to throw out cool powers and spout one-liners are just part of the entertainment. Your princess play will be more enjoyable if you can enjoy those as well instead of sitting impatiently and waiting for your opportunity to be Wolverine the whole night.

      \n Okay, so like, to expand on my feelings about the Czege Principle.
      There are two big things that I don't feel like can be overstated enough that very much color my perspective here.
      1) I'm not really into conflict-based fiction in general, so for me trouble is a tool instead of its own ends. My default paradigm is slice-of-life/iyashikei, where trouble is extremely rare.
      2) I approach play completely as an author, not from any kind of in-character stance.

      For me, what makes trouble interesting in roleplay is not solving it. It's telling the story of how your character solves it. I don't have any interest in challenge-based stuff where the players are having to figure out how to solve the problem. I'm solely interested in stuff where the focus in a scene involving trouble is the character solving it, where whatever the player chooses for the character to do to solve the problem works unless they say it doesn't.
      Scenes where characters are in trouble are scenes where very specific things involving how a character solves problems are being conveyed. Very specific emotions, even more specific than many other sequences that are more nuanced and portray several different things at once.
      So thus sequences involving trouble are extremely tight in their choreography, on such a level that it's often easiest to just establish it yourself and solve it yourself, then have the other participants help out by adding details and fancy descriptions. The kind of "improvise within these narrow parameters" like I described in the player expectations thread. Those types of sequences also very much have a major sort of "Follow my lead" built in, with basically the other players riffing off of what the spotlight player is saying and doing and establishing.

      Trouble established by other people just has too many failure points as far as potentially turning out unsatisfying through not giving you the chance to convey the emotional content correctly, so thus, for us, the reasonable way to address this problem and make the rare occasions where characters get in trouble interesting and enjoyable is to hand it over completely to the player whose character is going to get in trouble, so that they can make sure everything is as tight as it needs to be for the scene's dramaturgical intent.
      It varies from player-to-player how much the rest of the group adds to the sequence, but even in situations where the rest of the group is adding a lot, it's still ultimately set up and resolved by the same person - the spotlight player.

      Basically, the most important thing of support needed from the rest of the table is a deep investment in the fiction. When you start basically monologuing out your character's experience of a trouble or conflict, all eyes are on you. Everyone is watching in rapt attention, looking for places they can add additional details, and most of all excited to see how you play out the sequence, how you execute the scene.

      Dreamer said:

      Regarding the czege principle, I think it says more about player preferences than anything quite objective, though it seems to be taken as an objective thing by a lot of folks. It could be that it's a preference for a majority.

      \nIt's not really subjective or objective so much as it is a characteristic of conflict-centered roleplaying. The causality goes roughly thus:

      1) Roleplaying games are inherently social, as implied by the fact that you bothered to gather a bunch of people together in the first place instead of drawing the curtains and playing alone.

      2) An understanding of play that focuses on conflict is common and fruitful for a variety of purposes. Conflicts are important, even to the extent of sometimes being the only part of play that actually matters.

      When you bring those two observations together, the Czege principle necessarily follows: if your conception of conflict did not imply a social understanding that shares this core content of gameplay between multiple players, then either #1 does not hold (and you therefore should drop the excess players as a complicating factor), or #2 does not hold (and therefore how you handle conflicts doesn't actually matter, because they aren't the actual point of play).

      In other words: the Czege principle is not universal, because there are rpg-like activities that are performed alone (e.g. fiction-writing), and there are rpgs where conflict is not of crucial importance, and there are other reasons for a bunch of people to gather to play together.

      You could, if you wanted to, generalize the Czege principle so it applies to non-conflict rpgs:

      The responsibility for the central conceit of your game should be distributed between the players, for otherwise you are creating something with no inherent justification for gathering a gaming group together in the first place.

      This generalized form can then be applied to Emma's playstyle, too: once you recognize what the other players are there for actually, then you know what is the interaction that needs to be preserved at all costs. Evidently conflict is not it for them if they're happy with a forum-rpg style solipsist character authority. (Alternatively they might have a highly implicit resolution system - e.g. the players debate the outcomes in advance in the abstract, and this is the "conflict" where you want and need other players to participate.)

      \n On the other hand, once you realize that this is even a possibility in what the game and the other players except of you, then... at least you have something to work towards? I'm about as far from a native princess player as you can get, so I do get the astonishment of everybody questioning the sense in even doing it, but the truth is that it's highly compatible with many traditional game forms, and can be fun. Maybe not the most devastatingly fun that I've ever had, but nevertheless something constructive to be doing, and clearly something that may be the most important single activity to engage in the entirety of roleplaying for some people.

      For an example of making the best of a situation, take Call of Cthulhu. It's (potentially, predominantly) a Sim game, and while it's nowhere near focused in princess play specifically, it's not actively contradictory either. In our on-and-off, painfully traditional campaign much of my own amusement and participation in the game has been simple princess play. Being as how the game otherwise would be mostly acknowledging the GM's clues ("yes, let's next go to that place that you mentioned, so we get the next scene"), it's not like I have anything better to do than some light-weight princessing.

      (Yes, let's talk about that game a bit here. You see, the campaign in question is also essentially an exercise in Spicy dice game mechanics. The GM has pretensions about sorta-old-school "let the dice land as they may" play, but at least to me it's obvious that the dice are there more for moment-to-moment narrative inspiration. A good example of a game that, for me, is mostly princess play + Spicy dice.)

      Not having anything better to do in CoC than princess away, my first character in the campaign became a biker, veteran of the Korean war (the campaign is set in the American west in the '50s) with some criminal connections. The entertainment about playing this character is getting to talk about '50s biker culture, drug trade, veteran issues and his shaky mental health situation (made worse by certain mythos-related breakdowns as play progresses, of course). For example, I found it mildly entertaining that it was specifically my character who had a contact with a veteran friend with explosives expertise who could come in and solve a mythos problem in the traditional way; the scenes related to that not only let me do some minor thespian stuff with myself (playing both the PC and their contact, basically), but also handle the minutiae with the explosives, which the rest of the group were completely unprepared to handle in a manner approaching realistic.

      (To clarify: it is completely obvious to me that had my character not been in the adventure, the explosives and whatnot would simply have appeared on their own. "We go buy some dynamite" and then "we use the dynamite to blow up the entrance" and that's that. It would be a misunderstanding to imbue any kind of plot relevance to that play content.)

      When the first character took an extended sanitarium stay at one point, as one is wont to do in CoC, I created a second character. This time I went with a clever and academically accomplished young woman who happened to be the mildly-infatuated assistant of the local Gandalf-type NPC at the University of Arizona. Playing her was most fun when she did anthropological field study, wrestled with her mythos-skepticism and tried to keep the too-curious-for-his-own-good professor from outright throwing himself into insane worship of the old gods for anthropological purposes. Basically, doing what the character existed for.

      For both characters it has generally been much less entertaining to have to handle the adventure-related things that are not character identity relevant. This is clearly princess play attitude speaking here: when the character doesn't react in an interesting and unique manner to what's going on, I'm not being entertained by being that character - I could just be giving instructions to any generic space-holder.

      Pay attention, if you please, to the relatively high-brow nature of my princessing entertainment with these characters: this is what I mean by CoC not being particularly supportive of princess play. I think that the reason that I'm even able to do it in a game that insists on a historical setting and overall realistic characters is a deeper than average interest in cultural history and humanist arts in general, meaning that I know enough about the setting to embed a sufficiently colorful and specific character in it. My biker-veteran character, for example, wouldn't be interesting if I didn't know about American biker subculture and its historical underpinnings; the GM certainly doesn't have the technical means in CoC to somehow grant the player the necessary background for princessing effectively in the setting, so you're on your own and you're only going to be as entertained as you can be on your own strengths.

      To illustrate: another player ended up playing a young hoodlum sort of character. We helped them conceptualize the character a bit by determining that they're specifically a greaser, a type of youth subculture prevalent in the '50s USA landscape. However, to actually be successful in princessing the player would actually have to have the vision and wherewithal to discover something interesting in their own character to portray and inhabit; somebody who doesn't know what a greaser is, and is otherwise entirely clueless about American 20th century history (and their own for that matter), simply isn't going to benefit from something like "you could be a greaser, how about?" It is an entirely empty label to them.

      The type of princess play I describe there constitute about 50% of my entire participation in the campaign, it's all like that - chatting about what my character thinks about things and how they do this or that thing that needs to be done. The other parts are 10% pacing stuff (keeping the investigator party on track and moving the way the GM expects us to) and 40% general audience/support/kibitzing commentary. I even did a whole 4-session adventure without a character of my own, so the princessing isn't crucial, but I have to say that it is a core part of the game having any point at all - if nobody had characters in this, it'd become painfully thin.

      Also, consider this: setting aside the difficulty threshold for princess play that CoC causes by its relatively impenetrable setting and style, the gaming procedures are precisely the same as in any other traditional game. The vast majority of roleplaying games are systematically, moment to moment, precisely as princess play friendly as CoC. This means that while they do not actively obstruct, they certainly do not support it either. Historically concerned design towards princess play has been very sparse, particularly as the whole indie revolution basically bypassed this type of creative concern entirely. It is obvious that there are all sorts of things one could do to actively help people towards successful princessing - more than the vapid advice about "developing a character concept" or "giving your character a tick" that game texts like to offer.

      \n I would say a completely hermetic character protection is atypical. It is logical, though. Forum play of the type "you can't do anything to another player's character without their prior consent" operates on the same principles. This doesn't mean that characters never face something that narratively resembles trouble, but the player is ultimately in control in some way.

      I think that consequences have to be carefully modulated somehow for princess play to be safe and fun. Maybe the consequences are commonplace and their nature known in advance, for example, in which case the princess character concept can incorporate them as future possibilities. Or maybe the player gets a veto or choice about what kind of failure or price their character will pay. Or maybe the players work hard to understand each other's character concepts so they can avoid upsetting them.

      Without some sort of modulation Wolverine is constantly on the mercies of the whims of the other players, and what's worse, the game often actively celebrates ruining him. It's great that he lost a battle due to bad dice rolls against an enemy that shouldn't conceptually be a match for him. It may be great for some creative agenda, but not for a princess player who's specifically committed to a character vision where Wolverine is the best at what he does, not some random goblin with the dice luck.

      Interestingly I don't think that Call of Cthulhu is much of a problem in this regard despite being a rather character-disruptive game. You know your character's likely going insane or getting injured or killed at some point, so it doesn't need to come as a horrible disruption of your princess play concept. It's not a good vehicle for power fantasy (although our GM does facilitate that much more than I would), but it's just fine for misery tourism as long as the group has the sense to let a player take the lead in orchestrating the demise of their own character. Arguably that's one of the big creative payoffs of the game: knowing that you're a Lovecraft protagonist, and getting to organize your own march towards your inevitable doom.

      Old school D&D in comparison is awful: it's intentionally a refusal and corruption of heroic fantasy tropes. It encourages hope, yet crushes it. Any control a player has is fleeting. Having players dislike the game because it is so very cruel towards princess play is the most common single creative agenda conflict I experience with the game.

      \n Yeah, I think that tea-party play is another one of these "stances" towards Sim play like princess play and dollhouse play. It's not exactly the same, but as I speculated earlier, I don't really think that you're actually forced to choose just one - it's entirely feasible for a single game to shift between these various activities fluidly as play goes on. Some games are very focused on just one or two of these, while others are more generic.

      So yeah, add that one to the list I was making earlier of different Sim activities. Definitely counts, good call. There are games that distinctly set up tea-partying - I'm reminded of Ars Magica myself, with its periodic wizard conclaves.

      The main difference between princessing and tea-partying is, I think, that the former assumes an archetypal character activity context, while the latter specifically presumes a social scene framework. Your character identity might not have a relatable interface for social scenes at all, so you can't princess with them at a tea-party, but it's also possible that tea-parties are a structural part of what the game even is (like a Vampire LARP), so everybody's going to join in regardless of character concept.

      This means that if your character's princessing activity specifically is having tea-parties (or club raves or orgies or whatever), then there's no meaningful distinction for you between getting to play a tea-party scene or getting to play a princessing scene: the tea-party scene works just fine for expressing your character. A literal princess character in most fiction will be like this: having a tea-party or a royal ball or something like that will usually work to highlight their character and show them in their element.

      Compare, on the other hand, to Wolverine: he has to occasionally participate in a tea party (also known as "X-Men social group-building activity"), and depending on the type of party he's probably not going to be princessing there. The scene may well still be fun for tea-party reasons, but it's not going to be fun because it enables Wolverine to be Wolverine. At best it's an opportunity to showcase Wolverine's unique social style, but if that's not what interests the player in Wolverine, then it's not princess play for them.

      (For the comic book geeks: yes, Wolverine has his own social interface style in his solo stories. I can see princess playing him at a tea party, provided it's a Japanese one where he gets to be the broody white guy samurai. He also has this brooding working man's alcoholic stance that fills in for an actual social life. That's the real Wolverine, though, and not the armchair-theory type we're discussing here.)

      \n That's more or less what I was getting at though: I think that it meshes perfectly fine with story now play (or if you want to put a more general spin on it "playing to find out what happens") because it's part of a continuum or spectrum rather than a matter of starkly defined categories.

      I would propose princess play is more akin to a stance than a philosophy or goal of play. It's not quite pure Actor stance, since a part of it is maneuvering your character into different story beats: this is where Wolvie bucks Cyclop's orders, this is where Wolvie violently slaughters everyone who threatens a little girl, this is where Wolvie shows off his past as a samurai. There's an element of Author stance here, in that the player is moving their character into position to make those beats.

      With characters or tropes whose whole deal is their arc, this can also include a subtle version of Director's stance where you as a player build the fiction to include stuff like The Guy Who Knows Wolverines Secret Past and The Teammate He Has Awkward And Ill Advised Sexual Tension With. It's usually expressed as an explanation for some sort of mysterious behavior that the character engages in, and meant to be an extension of Their Thing.

      "I put a coin before the shrine of the Sea-Bitch Umberlee, looking out into the dark waters before me. Faedamious once lost someone to the sea, and while he is not a man of faith he hopes that their soul is at rest."-Look guys, I just inserted by necromancer's dead son into the narrative.

      "Where were you Logan?"

      "Tyin' up loose ends Professor. Tyin' up loose ends. His claws are caked with dried blood, but it is obvious he is not satisfied."-Look guys, I just inserted a mysterious quest for vengeance into the narrative.

      I agree that it's important to construct categories for different types of play so that folks can easily ascertain what sort of play is their thing and talk about it openly and honestly. With that in mind, I think that it's most productive to talk about this sort of pattern as a continuum and figuring out where you fall on the continuum by answering these questions:

      1. What's more important to you during play: facts about your character, or questions and answers?

      2. Do you want your character to change significantly during play, and if so how much do you want these changes to be under your control? How do you want the system to enforce this, if at all?

      3. You do you have a plan for where your character is going, or are you in this to see what happens to them, and how they change as a result? How invested are you in both of these?

      So categorization via sliders rather than boxes, essentially.

      \nThis might not be helpful, but here's how the technique vs. agenda divide generally goes in Forgite analysis - I know you've seen this kind of thinking before:

      "I imagine a person and wrap this imaginary personality around myself..." - technique
      "... to satisfy my personal issues with that character type." - agenda

      "I imagine a person and wrap this imaginary personality around myself..." - technique
      "... to provide authentic automatic-writing input into the game situation." - agenda

      "I write fan-fiction about Wolverine..." - technique
      "... to satisfy my personal issues with that character type." - agenda

      People often have issues with identifying the creative agenda in actual play. Some think that this is because "creative agenda" doesn't exist (as a coherent thing) or cannot possibly be observed, and the various people who think that the concept makes sense are just all looking at different kinds of technical cues and projecting their own biases. My own favourite explanation is that because the creative agenda is a psychological process going on in the mind of a game participant, we simply don't have any means of directly observing it, and thus the only way to talk about it to each other is by comparing experiences and classifying similar psychological mechanisms together under one label - and that always boils down to "hey, I've seen that" or "no, I haven't ever experienced that, so it might not even exist". It's a tricky subject.

      This being the situation, I can only hide behind the definition: what I tried to name with "princess play" is a certain kind of creative agenda I observe (or think I do, at least) in the wild, associated with certain techniques (role-inhabitation). If you can see the technique part, but doubt that the agenda part exists, then I don't really have any clever arguments against that. Maybe you're right and I'm just imagining some instances of role-play to be engaged in for different purposes than some others. As you say, the technical idea of "playing a role" is used constantly for all sorts of purposes in actual play.

      Maybe this hypothetical question would be interesting: when a 5-year old girl does role-immersion play around, say, being a princess, and gains great delight in being called pretty (or otherwise acknowledged as a princess) by adults as they flounce around in a costume - what do you think the creative agenda is there? What's going on in her head, in terms of play satisfaction?

      You might answer that we don't know, because all people are different and this little girl might have one of many different motivations. My current theory, though, is that she's probably engaging in recontextualizing of the self by imagining an alternate identity, being entertained by the way a different social mask makes them feel, and that this is a pretty typical form of the Simulationistic agenda mode - it's basically a major answer to the question of "why would I want to pretend to be an elf". I also think that I can differentiate this particular type of creative agenda from some other reasons somebody might have for taking on a role, the way you usually would distinguish between creative agendas in practical observation.

      Another hypothetical question might also help: if I hypothetically design and play a game revolving primarily around princess play as I understand it, do you think that I'll find any players who'd like playing that, and what kind of creative reasons they might have for liking it? Would they have coherent reasons? (In concrete terms a "princess play" game would involve unique and detailed player characters engaging in various situations that enable different characters to have different specific reactions; there would be both GM-initiated and player-initiated situations, and expressing the unique individual reactions of each character would be a major part of play.)

      By the way, an excellent example of a game that I suspect could be very princess play -friendly just occurred to me: Wushu seems like it does almost nothing but princess play. I can't even figure out why somebody would want to play that if not because they're entertained by princessing.

      I don't know why you don't think that you can distinguish between princess play and other reasons to role-immerse, but one reoccurring reason I've noticed for somebody not "seeing" a creative agenda is that they don't have sufficient similar experiences themselves, and thus don't understand what they see. This tends to lead, I think, to mischaracterization of the agenda: you observe some play and try to figure out what the players are doing, what they're getting out of it, and you miss the real reason because it's outside your personal life experience.

      The other explanation would of course be that the distinctive psychology that I posit for "princess play" does not actually exist, and thus the situation is precisely as you say: we have a whole bunch of examples of role-immersion used as a technique of play, but because the type of creative agenda I posit as an underlying explanation does not exist, it's no wonder you can't distinguish it from some others that clearly aren't anything like the princess play I try to describe.

      I'm also hamstrung in the creative agenda discussion by having the Forge concepts etched in my brain that "creative agenda" is a group endeavor, not an individual one, and that one might best find it by looking at long-term reward cycles, not moments.

      \nYeah, but creative agenda being expressed as a group activity is not the same thing as it existing only as a social phenomenon. I know that Ron's traditionally against psychological explanations and likes to stick to socially observable phenomena, but for me it seems obvious and necessary that a communally expressed creative agenda only exists because it has psychological antecedents: the people engaging in this social behavior have internal lives that are somehow satisfied by this behavior. Or, in other words: a person has a "creative agenda" that can cohere with the creative agendas of other people via successful play.

      We could use carefully separate terminology for the different logical forms that creative agenda takes, but traditionally it's just been called "creative agenda" being expressed in different ways. For example, we tend to say that a book has a creative agenda, although we don't really mean that the book is a group of people expressing empirical social cues - we just mean that the book in question contains instructions of play that are most functionally used in service of that specific creative agenda. Similarly we say that a person "has a creative agenda" despite that person not being a group of people expressing social cues - we simply mean that the person has the necessary psychological drive for potentially engaging in play that expresses that creative agenda.

      I don't think that anybody's ever argued that creative agenda exists only as some sort of interpersonal thing that does not require psychological antecedents. At least the way I've understood Ron on this (he's the one most psychology-averse in theory terms, from what I've seen) is that he's unwilling to speculate about the specific psychological antecedents for methodological reasons, and due to his specific philosophy of science. He doesn't claim that you could take a bunch of lobotomy patients or zombies and get them to express a creative agenda, which would be the natural implication if creative agenda was not a psychological phenomenon as much as a social one.

      Accepting that creative agenda has psychological underpinnings that we can speculate on, in the way psychology as a science generally speculates about the human mind, doesn't of course mean that I reject Ron's technical program - I agree with him about social expression being the only realistic means of observing creative agenda directly. We can talk about our introspective feelings regarding e.g. princess play, but we can't evaluate each other's internal state of mind; in practice you need to set up a real play environment and see if people respond to opportunities for engaging in princess play. If they do, well, apparently they do have a compatible creative agenda.\n

      In my experience, momentary or fleeting princess play is common, and says little about long-term group play, while ongoing individual princess play is less common, and can be integrated or not in a variety of ways depending on what else the group is up to.

      \nI absolutely agree. That's actually the major reason for why I even gave this type of play a name and started investigating it actively - there's a chance for a productive artistic program here, in figuring out whether princess play is common, enjoyable and something you would want to specifically design a game around. Considering focused and specialized princess play is an opportunity to create a new type of modern rpg design, you know?

      The way I see it, historical traditional roleplaying texts have been endemically infested by princess play, but the support for it has been relatively covert. For example, games will generally advertise themselves as being for the purpose of roleplaying and having fun by being your character (princess play), but in practice the mainstream trad rpg has carried a heavy adventure gaming structuration that runs contrary to this promise. The notion of a D&D gamer creating a boisterous character who runs into the dungeon, drops some flashlights on the way, and then dies is a historical constant: it was just as true last week for Hamnacb, last decade when it happened to a nice girl I was teaching to play challenge-based D&D, and in the '80s when AD&D 2nd edition was completely in the throes of confusion regarding whether the players are supposed to wargame or character play.

      So I have this pet hypothesis that while princess play might not be as oppressed by the historical institution of traditional roleplaying as some other styles of play, it's not quite being dealt with honestly, either. We've brushed on the relevant attitudes earlier in the thread, in fact: it's easy for gamers to question whether something that doesn't ascribe to a goal-oriented adventuring program reminiscent of old school D&D has value or is healthy. Is it good if we simply let a player goof off doing their princess stuff? Shouldn't there be challenge embedded here somewhere? Isn't it a masturbatory power fantasy if we (the plural GM here, hi) sit down, shut up and let the player perform their character for us? These are very traditional, very old questions, I think; I see them underlying theory discussion in the '80s already.

      Something like that could explain why you don't see entire groups doing consistent princess play. I also believe that princess play is not the most natural exclusive focus for a game, so there's probably going to be other stuff going on as well alongside it - the GM's plot, for instance - but I also think that the vast majority of groups engaging in princess play probably don't view it as an intrinsic value. The way I've seen it, it's more something that people allow to exist. Compared to the GM's plot railroad the princessing is an entirely submissive activity: the GM's plot gets discussed as a theoretical concern, it gets prepped for, it gets goal-oriented structuration around it, it gets criticized and worked on. Meanwhile princessing is a voluntary part of your average simmy trad adventure campaign, something you're allowed to do if you feel like it - or maybe aren't, depending on the GM and whether you've understood timing correctly and so on.

      I come back to Ars Magica often when thinking about princess play, as that game's naturally grown culture has an amazing amount of relatively honest princessing activity going on. The AM player base, as publicized at e.g. the publisher's forums, is quite overt about the idea that the player character is the player's long-term project, the player chooses what kinds of content their character engages in, and neither the rules nor the GM nor other players are allowed to break another player's toys unilaterally. The player-originating character building and decorating is consistently a major part of play, apparently more important than plot or wider setting for most campaigns. One could do worse than looking into how Ars Magica is played in the wild if one desires to see relatively intentional, pure princess play in action.

      Paul_T said:

      What would it take to talk you into expounding a bit on your ideas of how the Five Elements of Exploration interact and serve functions in a Sim context?

      \nFor those unfamiliar with Forgite theory jargon: the Five Elements are a generalized conceptual break-down of what happens in the simple act of roleplaying. The claim is that when you engage in a meaningful unit of play, your game will always feature Character, Setting, Situation, Color and System, and that these elements can be analytically separated from each other and observed in isolation to understand what essentially happens in the complex act of play.

      It's a rather complex topic taken all at once like that; I've found concepts like "princess play" or "GM story hour" useful precisely because they are discrete technical clusters one can work with in the specific. I think that's part of why we've had all the conceptual trouble we've had with grasping Sim on an abstract level over the years. Sim overall, in general, is predicated on the idea that there is something in the Shared Imagined Space - in the fiction - that has human attention for the sake of the thing itself. What that something actually is gets strongly shaped and framed by our cultural understanding of what a "game" or "roleplaying game" is.

      If you will, take this understanding/definition of Simulationistic psychology for a test drive: Sim play concerns ideation - inventing or reconstructing - of human-relevant concepts. The play process - the Exploration - exists for the sake of observing the ideas imagined by the players; it makes sense because the types of imaginary concepts involved in Sim play are generally intricate and cannot be properly observed in isolation. You only understand it by seeing it go, which is why you gather a bunch of people to simulate your ideas in various ways.

      For example, a player interested in Wolverine - or masculine violence in general - will desire to play games that involve thorough scenarios that embed Wolverine in them, because this is a way to see Wolverine move and act. A still picture of Wolverine is a shallow understanding of the concept in comparison. A movie is better, or perhaps a video game. In the right circumstances, with the right kinds of ideas, a roleplaying game can be absolutely superior, as it enables the players to fine-tune the ideation in a way that would be impossible for less interactive and player-directed arts.

      How this relates to the Elements of Exploration is that the ideas generally involved in Sim play do not form complete Exploration packages; they are rather literally individual elements of it. This means that there is room in Sim game design for different arrangements. Wolverine, for example, is a Character concept, with merely subtle requirements for the other Elements; while you cannot really meddle a lot with Wolverine without ruining the player's ideation, you have wide leeway with the other four Elements. Thus you can create many different games around Wolverine.

      Other idea complexes are more expansive and thus more fixed. Dungeons & Dragons Sim, for instance, is a rather ritualized package: you get together a bunch of people who have childhood memories of playing D&D, or who've watched a lot of Youtube shows about playing D&D, or who are fans of the Order of the Stick, and then you provide them a simulation of the experience of playing D&D. Everybody does what they're supposed to do, and the game goes like it's supposed to go. There's not a massive amount of leeway in how the Elements relate to each other, and what the Elements each have to be - not unless the players start taking individualized creative risks and breaking out of the simulation.

      Call of Cthulhu is similarly expansive as an idea: veteran roleplayers desiring to play CoC will generally expect certain types of Character, in a specific Setting, with the same Color they always have, and similar Situations... even the CoC System for all that it's horrible seems to retain a lot of nostalgia, probably because it's been such a central vehicle for the idea complex of "playing Call of Cthulhu" over the years.

      In reality, though, these rpg-related examples of Simulationism in action are a bit of a cheat: they are self-referential cycles of roleplaying motivating people to ideate about roleplaying. Most Sim play, and most authentic Sim play, does not usually revolve around reenacting a full complex of ideas like that, because they're not roleplaying games reenacting roleplaying games. Rather, the typical way the creative concern in a Sim game seems to go is that you elevate one specific idea and build everything else in relation to that.

      A Star Trek roleplaying game, for example, is a clear concept: the point of the exercise is to take the Setting idea of Star Trek and "make it go" by throwing in your own, basically arbitrary ideas about Character, Color, Situation and System in service to the idea of substantiating or demonstrating the Setting. That same game-playing activity often has room for other creatively relevant elements as well, of course: the Wolverine guy can bring Wolverine into the game (as a Klingon bridge officer, perhaps), for example, which is a value add collectively speaking: we get to see Star Trek in action, and we get to see Wolverine in action as well.

      If we assume that an act of Exploration consists of the five Elements, and that Simmy ideation commonly exists for all five Elements (I believe this to be the case), then the theoretical "perfect" Sim game would be one where you select every Element to fit your own motivations: the Character we observe is one with great personal meaning to you, in a Setting you find interesting, with Color that delights you, a Situation full of intrigue from your viewpoint, and a System that highlights the most interesting things as far as you are concerned. Of course this sort of thing isn't normal; I'd rather say that you're doing well if the game elevates a single Element that is of interest to you, and selects the rest to conform to it.

      Character is second-most important: the players have D&D backgrounds, video gaming backgrounds (that are D&D backgrounds, really), and the 4th edition rules specifically ask them to pick a character class they like, and exemplify that in a heroic campaign. Their mission is to dwarf away (or Celtic champion away in this case, to be specific) as hard as they can. The high fantasy campaign structure enmeshes the players' chosen Characters into the plot to allow them to each substantiate their own type of heroism. Princess play abounds, obviously, as the players interact with the GM's story hour in the ways the 4th edition system facilitates.

      Paul_T said:

      Why do you feel confident about the way you're partitioned "plot", Situation, and Setting? Essentially, it seems you've placed the importance of the canonical plot as a Setting detail.

      \nThis is quickly becoming a general discussion of Big Model theory. Should be its own thread, properly speaking.

      Your typical rpg plot railroad is substantially neither Situation or Setting, as I understand it; rather, it's merely a technical tool that is used to create, organize and convey either, both, or something else. Basically the existence of a plot railroad in the game is a System element saying that "in this game, the GM tells us what happens next." A game that doesn't have a fixed plot simply doesn't have the prepared GM plan for the order and content of play.

      The reason that the plot itself is not an Element of Exploration is that the Exploration process properly is a relatively low-order social activity; Exploration as a concept doesn't refer to GM prep or the general sweep of play during a session, but rather to the smallest atomic process of activity by which the Shared Imagined Space changes. From this perspective the plot doesn't "exist" as a cohesive entity; there's simply a System conceit that assigns certain responsibilities for delivering Setting, Situation, Character and Color to different players, to be established in a certain way. In pre-plotted games whoever does the plotting is generally the one who delivers initial Situations. In a sense the "Exploration" is merely the stage on which the play happens, and the stage doesn't know or care about what happens back-stage: as long as every element combines in a functional way, so that Exploration manages to occur moment to moment, there's not much more to be said by merely observing the stage. Who knows where all this Situation is coming from?

      Creative agenda works at a higher level of concern compared to the activity of Exploration; for agenda purposes we're not asking whether the SIS manages to change from moment to moment, but rather what purposeful plan that Exploration is following. In Sim play the purpose seems to be the presentation of substantial ideas on the stage of Exploration. The Elements of Exploration come into that only insofar that the ideas a player might wish to present on the stage of Exploration cannot appear there unless they are expressed by one of the Elements. If your creative concern cannot be expressed as Setting, Character, Situation, Color or System, then what is there to be done?

      The canonical plot our Prydain campaign refers to, or any railroad plot in general, is a mechanism that helps us determine what the next Situation in play is going to be. In some other game the GM might ask the players "what does your character do next" in a strategic-level sense, and the answer would completely shape the itinerary of the game session; in this game the GM instead refers to his notes and tells the other players what the next scene is going to be, or at most offers the players a chance to jump to a different track.

      The reason for why I don't analyze Situation as a major creative concern for the campaign is that in truth the actual situations the game moves through are relatively arbitrary, gaining their meaning by the way they showcase Setting and Character elements. This is partly due to the nature of the high fantasy epic: iconic situations simply aren't ultimately that important. Even climatic high points of the campaign like e.g. encountering the Horned King on the field of glory gain their significance not because they're canonically iconic (which this particular scene is not), but simply because they're major opportunities for revealing Setting or Character. In our campaign the combat encounter with the Horned King was the most intimate event the heroes ever got with the man/creature, and that opportunity to showcase the King's identity was why the scene was so important. That, and the miniatures skirmish combat related to it, which I did account for as a genuine Situation concern in my analysis.

      A Sim game that had a true Situation focus would need to be less generalist about the plot... A wuxia game, for example, could have a specific creative concern over the conduct of swordfighting duels. The game would revolve around those. That would be what a Situation-based creative agenda would look like, more than what we're doing. Our Situation concern is overbearingly over whether a given scene is going to be a combat scene (implying battlemats and miniatures) or not, and that isn't even the most important thing going on most of the time.
