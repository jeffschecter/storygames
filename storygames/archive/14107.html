\n Quite simply, how do you make a huge multi-session dungeon interesting? I'm thinking of something like the Temple of Elemental Evil which I'm adapting for my current Dungeon World game. How do you avoid room after room of boring fights? How do you make the dungeon come alive and become a threat all on its own?

    \n I'd say, give the dungeons noticeably "themed" areas, and variations within that theme. Like...

    "Okay, the old troll in the Undercity told us that the kobolds have the key to the Upper Vaults, so we'll need to hit the Kobold Mines and find either the Big Chief's quarters, or the head sorcerer's quarters, he wasn't sure which of them had it. Their storage chamber would be lucrative to hit, too, but will have a lot of guards and traps, do we want to risk that while we're there?"

    is much better than

    "Well, we've killed everything and looted all the treasure in this branch of the dungeon. What's the closest intersection on the map that has a passage we haven't explored yet? Oh, god, we have to go all the way back to room 7? Okay, basic marching order, everyone."

    And give the players enough information to make their choices meaningful. If I can choose between going left and going right, but don't have anything to base that decision on, it might as well have been a straight hallway, yeah?

    \nOh, and stop the boring fights by taking them away.
    \nIf you are using DW, take all the Monsters and Traps from ToEE and group them into fronts. Attach the fronts to spaces or areas in the dungeon. Areas that can be personified the same way as in Old School Hack. Ask what does each Front want from the players? What don't the players want to lose (including their lives). Hit them there. Make the monsters actions meaningful and driven by an emotion. Make the players descions in the dungeon have a ripple effect elsewhere.
    \n
    \nAsk lots and lots of questions of the characters to flesh out the dungeon rather than telling them. :)

    \n What helped me a lot was to stop thinking about megadungeons as dungeons, but rather a world. Small dungeons with a handful of rooms, those are dungeons. They can even survive being (relatively) linear.

    I personally wouldn't treat it as a single threat (unless in the sense that the architecture itself is out to get you): Megadungeons are countries, cities, ecosystems. They should be absolutely non-linear, with many paths everywhere, some easy some hard. Think of them rather the same as the surface world ("So the goblins live here and the skeletons are here."), except it's underground-flavoured. Characters should spend days and weeks in there. Themed areas are definitely a way to do that and DW fronts should help you there, I believe. I think it's possibly more important to have a relationship map of parts of the dungeon than a detailed geographical map of it.

    That and also try treating it as a mythic underworld, the id-cellar, the dark side, through the mirror and down the rabbit hole. A dungeon is strange, weird, creepy, crazy. It has its own rules. Don't be afraid to let your imagination go wild.

    For inspiration: Try getting the Dungeon Alphabet or some other Old School resource thing, random tables are awesome to give you some starting seeds. Check out Zak's blog (D&D with pornstars) or Philotomy's oD&D site, they both have some interesting articles on dungeons.

    \n One simple change I would implement is that all corridors connecting sections of the dungeon should be much longer (I'm thinking that you should add about 5 miles between most areas). This separates encounter sites and lets each one be more independent. It also lets you camp out between areas rather than treating sessions like the TV show "24"...

    \n Apply all the techniques of relationship mapping and thematic conflict to your dungeon: who wants what? Who is in love with whom? What's the relationship between the goblins and the shriekers?

    I like every bit of the dungeon to have a bit of history, some story to tell. Every trap has a history: who set it up? Why? Who were they trying to catch?

    Ideally, learning about this kind of setup gives the characters some interesting leverage over the dungeon itself: maybe if they can steal that treasure over there and give it to those monsters over here, they can start a war between these two underground tribes, which might allow them to sneak into the ranks of this other fighting force and bribe their way into an audience with the Goblin King...

    \n Temple of Elemental Evil is full of factions, that's good.

    One way that we keep it interesting is by having players map and work together on creating the physical artifact of the game.

    The main thing is to have a goal - why are they in the megadungeon? If it's to get rich and famous, that will create one set of priorities. If it's to gain insane magical power, that will create another.

    \n Go find a copy of Ultima Underworld and play it. It's a little dated now, but it does the "themed areas" thing Ben was talking about and has quite a few small communities you can interact with in meaningful ways. Very neat. (I've heard that UU2 is also very good.)

    Paul's comment about the history of the dungeon is also spot-on. It's one thing if your dungeon actually is random-roll, but if it feels like random-roll, that's no good.

    Make sure you have places for the group to pick up new PCs, and good reasons for the new PCs to join. Reasons beyond "My goddamn elf failed his final goddamn saving throw. Hold on while I roll up a new guy."
