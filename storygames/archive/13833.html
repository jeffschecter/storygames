    \n What I'd like to see:

    - YA mystery adventure RPG: for "teen sleuth" type stories covering the gamut from Encyclopedia Brown, to the Great Brain, to the Hardy Boys/Nancy Drew, to Veronica Mars: no supernatural need apply, please.

    - Reasonably gritty, non-medieval, no-real-player-magic-need-apply fantasy for doing sword-and-sandle-ish type gaming: I don't want platemail, and I don't want bastard swords, and I don't want magician artillery. Give me spears, round shields, short swords, knives, sandals, and city-states.

    - Reasonably gritty, TV-oriented "science fiction" that unashamedly isn't high-science, but isn't full on Space Opera, either (for example, Stargate).

    I'm a bit tired of games that build around the premise of providing characters with "kewl powerz", whether they amount to magic, or psionics, or feats. I don't want "awesome turned all the way up to 11" so much any more. I'd like games with nice, simple, robust systems that provide for a return to "heroic adventure" where the PCs are mortal, afraid of getting hurt, and have to work for a living.

      It was easier when you ran it almost exclusively with crooks, tbh.

      I suspect GB! was an example of a transitional game in a lot of ways, despite it's relatively late publication date. I could see GB! harkening back to earlier, multifaction, multiplayer games like the Braunstein games. In text, it was much more like the then-developing/developed unified party model of play.

      The modules didn't really clarify, either. Mad Dog Johnny Drake, the included tutorial module, is basically a CYOA walk-through for the GM and players, and probably ends with a deadly, satisfying shootout with a John Dillinger stand-in.Trouble Brewing, the first module, is a setting/campaign module designed to set up starting characters as movers and shakers in Lakefront City, regardless of profession. Trouble in Harmony is a fairly convoluted mystery to solve. Death on the Docks is an attempt to go back into the setting and bring in some historical based plot elements while still keeping in the spirit of the game, but shows off some of the weaknesses of the game's class-based character system. Death in Spades is another mystery, with a fair amount of gunplay, riffing off the subsystems developed in the original AD&D Ravenloft module. The Vanishing Investigator is a horribly railroaded, NPC-riffic affair that should be purged from memory, save for its attempt to deal with creating an adventure for slightly higher/mid-range level PCs.

      The text does suggest that players will be at odds. First there's the advice that the GM never create adventures or adenture hooks for Criminal PCs. Crooks are expected to always be active, creating and making their own breaks. The lengthy section on crime certainly gives them plenty of options to explore.

      There's also the way the classes are divided up. You have your Law Dogs ( FBI, Local PD, and Prohibition Agents), your Neutrals, but probably generally Law supporting types ( PIs, Journalists) and then everybody else ( Criminals). The very particular XP gaining rules are interesting in this regard: Law Abiding types gain XPs primarily for solid, convictable collars, neutrals for that plus a few other key things, but crooks only get it for cash earned.

      Finally, there is a fairly detailed example of play, involving crook PCs and pursuing Copper PCs. More than any other bit of the text, this suggests that some sort of multi-factional play was supposed to be part of the game. This especially was noteworthy to me as a kid, since it ran directly counter to how I was taught to play D&D, which was my base model for RPGs at the time.

      [Sorry for the vast digression y'all, but GB! was the second RPG I ever owned, I played the hell out of it as a kid (probably more AP than any other game, ever), and it still ranks in my top 3 favorite RPGs of all time]
