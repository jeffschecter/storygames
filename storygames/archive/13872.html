\n I've been playing around with RPG design for a few years. Until now, my games have just been designed for use by my local gaming group (and my own obsessive enjoyment). However, recently I've decided that I want to attempt to develop one of my games into a more polished, complete product, and possibly to self-publish it.

The problem is that I have a small pile of unfinished games that I like more or less equally. All of these games have been played, but all are also very far from completion and most don't even have a real design document. This means that each of these games would take a significant time commitment to complete. I don't mind this, but I do want to be confident that I've chosen the right game to devote my time to.

This is where I'd like feedback. I'm going to give a brief synopsis of each game and some of my concerns, and I'd appreciate it if others could offer their opinions. I know commenting on such limited information isn't ideal, and I don't expect profound insight. I'm really just looking for a nudge in the right direction.

Here are the games that I'm considering:

- Danse Macabre. Danse Macabre is a largely freeform game about confronting death. Based on medieval Memento Mori art and more modern work like Bergman's The Seventh Seal, the game brings characters from all different social classes into contact with a personified Death. Pros: This is my most complete game, and I have a rather clear idea about how I would expand upon the design doc in the finished text. It has also provided some really interesting, compelling playtests. Cons: It's very jeepy and it's form and subject matter could potentially be off-putting to a lot of players. I'm not sure if it's likely to generate much interest.

- Escapist. Escapist is a game of parallel universes. In one universe, the players portray mundane "real world" characters, struggling with disillusionment and delusions about their abilities and self-worth. In another, they portray epic adventurers in a heroic fantasy world. Each player's two characters are mechanically and conceptual linked, so that they struggles and failures of the real life characters strengthen their fantasy counterparts. Pros: I see a lot of room for expansion here. The mechanics are unconventional without being too "out there." This has become a favorite for my players. Cons: The fantasy portions use a dice-based resolution system that is fairly simple, but still involves enough math to make me concerned about game balance. Parts of the game are in flux and there is no solid desgin doc yet.

- Six Page Manual. This is goofy, improv-heavy parody of jeepform/"serious personal issues" RPGs. It's supposed to have a party game feel. Players roll character concepts from a chart--most of which are of the "mundane life with one big, sensational person problem" variety--and role play those characters. However, as play advances more and more ridiculous/extreme concepts are added to the original, and players have to juggle all of their character concepts as convincingly as they can. Pros: I have had a lot of fun both writing and playing it. Plenty of room to add bonus content via more charts. Cons: Pretty fringe in every possible way. Character charts are unfinished. Goofy writing style could be a turnoff. (Will have to add appendix that explains the words in conventional English.)

- Shade. Designed because I wanted to run a ghost game for Halloween and was unhappy with how most RPGs handle ghost stories. You begin the game as a human character who can be killed and become a ghost during play. Self-contained with each session contained scenes for five life stages: Childhood, Adolescence, Young Adulthood, Middle Age, and Old Age. Each player begins play with five cards, each of which includes a personality trait that character possesses. After each life stage, each player with a living character loses a card. (So characters wear down over time, becoming narrower and more extreme, withering away.) However, each card also contains a power that a ghost could possess--mysterious footsteps, cold spots, supernatural stains, etc. When/If your character dies, you flip your remaining cards and your character becomes a ghost who haunts the living PCs with the powers on the cards. Pros: Uh, hard to say. Cons: Hasn't been playtested much. Definitely needs mechanical work. Would require designing and printing physical cards.

- Isolation. A game based on stories/film/tv episodes about characters who don't know each other but are trapped together in a small space with a timer counting down to disaster and a traitor among them. The mechanics are loosely inspired by party games like Werewolf and Mafia. Characters draw unique roles--like hero, traitor, survivor, bounty hunter--and keep them concealed from other players. Most of the mechanics involve drawing cards from a hat for tools, events, etc. and in character bartering. Pros: Lots of subterfuge. Cons: This is a competitive game, and I'm having trouble balancing the mechanics to make them as fair as possible and to prevent cheap/exploitative strategies from becoming dominant while still keeping them as simple and intuitive as possible. Also, I hate the name. Need something more exciting.

    \n William,

    It's been my experience that one should only complete the games that they can't not finish.
    Walk away from them all for a week. See which one screams to be looked at, tinkered with, polished, unveiled.
    One of those games will haunt your soul if you don't finish it. Figure out which.

    Alternately, design the game your friends are most excited to play, because they're who you'll be playing with.

    \n William,

    The way I'd pick one to complete is to consider which project represents the currently most compelling opportunity for developing myself creatively as a gamer. Is playtesting one of them going to develop my gamer skills in a way that I'm hungry to develop them? Is doing art direction for one of them going to involve something creatively exciting?

    You can complete any of them if you put your mind to it, but figure out which of them entails the most personally compelling learning opportunities, and that's the one your energy will bring to its most powerful realization.

    Paul

    \n My proposal is to hold up "Danse Macabre" next to "A Flower for Mara" and ask if it would be a good compliment to that game. If so, you have functionally the same audience and you can dismiss that concern. If that is the game that you have the most progress toward and that you find personally rewarding to develop, work on that one. It may not earn you a ton of cash, but it doesn't have to in order to be satisfying.

    \n I like the concept for Escapist, I see in it a cycle where failures in the mundane then empower the alternate and successes in the alternate empower the mundane, ultimately resolving the character's issues. Correctly balanced, that could make for a really good short form (one to a handful of sessions) game. It seems the most likely to succeed. Shade on the other hand seems to have the strongest mechanic (losing cards and becoming more narrow/extreme) and is the one I would be most interested in trying.

    (re: Shade, I'd love to see a game with this mechanic morphed into a damage system for social combat. Sort of the reverse of gaining conditions in Mouse Guard, you would have cards which you discarded when you took damage. As you take damage, you get slowly painted into a corner where your remaining card is how you have to react to resolve the conflict.)
