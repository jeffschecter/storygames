\n I've spent a couple of hours today chatting with the owner of my favorite FLGS (Planet Harry, great store.), about the GW/Black Industries news and gaming in general. He was tired and down and taking stock, so the talk turned out pretty gloomy, with him casting a rather dark picture on the current gaming business here in Austria. We talked a bit about the lack of real "entry level" game, how the trend to bigger, prettier hardcovers isn't a good or successful one and how many corporations are making big mistakes in how they produce and market their games.

...

As you can imagine, I took that chance to talk a bit about Indie games, and while he wasn't really sold on the idea of stocking a couple of them, he seemed interested when I offered to lend him a couple of my Indie Games to check them out, so I consider that a success. (We also talked about other stuff, like daydreaming about a shop with infinite money would look like, his plans with the shop in general and how he plans to spend his next vacation, so it wasn't all gloom. It was a nice talk. )

I also told him (And here comes the real topic of this thread.) about my long-standing plans to run a couple of games at the upcoming convention he's organizing and how I failed to do that the last four of them. (I'm shy and lazy. Bad combination for con games. ) But he gave me positive feedback and made the idea of telling complete strangers about those games I'm all hot for and maybe running a demo or two look not all that horrible and scary, so I went away strengthened in my resolve to finally actually do this.

And it made me think about the Indie games I had and what I wanted to demo and talk about. Some of the games I have make for better demos then others. Wushu/Roanoke can be played with almost zero prep, while something like Reign needs a bit more time and work. A Mechaton game can be played for a while, but even so any given round looks interesting and gives a good impression of the game as a whole. On the other hand, something like Dogs might need a longer bit of play to showcase "the Awesome".

So, I've been thinking: What Indie games make for good demos? And which of them are available at IPR? (Because I kinda sorta hope to also target Harry with these demos and maybe get him and other FLGS' to stock some of them, which is probably easier with IPR then something that only come directly from the author or Lulu.)

(BTW, with "demo" I mean something less then a typical oneshot. I can't see myself running a whole game there unless there is lots of interest or no interest at all, so I've been thinking of doing short intros between 15 minutes and one hour when people are interested.)

I've been meaning to buy some games from IPR anyway and now I even have an excuse to do so.

Aaron

    \nIn a Wicked Age could work within 30 minutes. To highlight the ease of the system, you could decide on an oracle before hand, have a player pick four cards as you're going through the basic resolution mechanic and start framing one scene. If done right, it could be a fairly organic evolution. And you'd also be demonstrating how quick scene generation can be with the Oracles. Especially if you ask each player, "Would you mind playing X?" as each card is turned to speed up the process.

    Wushu. Love Wushu. I'd likely recommend one sheets with various settings - fantasy, sci-fi, pulp ... what most other RPGs sold in brick and mortar stores spotlight. Gauge interest on a particular setting, choose three traits and go. I'd likely avoid motivational traits and just stick with the standard 5/4/3 split and have a combat related skill as 4, 5 for high action.

    Mechaton. I just got it yesterday, so I'm only now building mechs with the basic Legos I have lying around. Can't comment on that yet.

    If you have only a single player hanging around doing nothing, how about Beast Hunters? I just got it this morning, but I wager a quick 20-30 minute challenge can be whipped up if you already have a particular Beast in mind.

    \n Re: Dogs

    The thing about Dogs is to not let people think too much. They have to act from the gut.

    Sooo, what I do is I set out their dice right in front of them on a sheet that tells them when they can roll them. "Roll these dice when you talk or shoot," "Roll these dice when you touch or hit someone," "Roll this big pair of d10s when you make this an issue of religious law rather than brotherhood," and so on.

    Then I hit 'em with the pitch from the front of the book, about the whoring son and the angry father (your brother) and I say "WHAT DO YOU DO?" They do something wimpy, and I say "We don't get to the dice until you do something I can't ignore," and then they do and we're off to the races.
