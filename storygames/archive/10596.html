\n After looking at it and leafing through it 100 times in the local game store, I decided I should probably go ahead and buy Burning Empires. I've now read it pretty thoroughly (I need to do it again, some of the subsystems need a second read-through). The greatness of this game cannot be appreciated in a brief glance-through.

The number of previously impossible or unadvisable things that this game pulls off is amazing. Here are some thoughts of mine.

* The three-phase setup for worm takeover of a planet is great. Although I do have a question. The description of each of the phases says that Infiltration happens first, if it's successful enough, Usurpation happens but if it's not, Invasion happens. Nevertheless, in the game structure diagram it indicates that the campaign has three phases. Does that mean all three of the phases happen in every game, or if we kick the worm's ass in Infiltration enough, can we force them to skip straight to Invasion, as Usurpation wouldn't work?

This is an interesting question to me as it's quite possible to come out of the World Burner with a world that's resistant to infiltration and invasion but vulnerable to usurpation, and strategically it would make sense to make every effort to avoid that second phase if it could be done.

I also think that this phase system (whether it is branched or linear) can be used in all sorts of situations. Figure out the bad guy's plans, and what sorts of things they're up to, subdivide them, then start working on a Burner that will create different environments for those plans. My Forgotten Realms work would be greatly assisted by this. So would all those "collect the plot token" games out there. The cool thing in those situations is never the tokens, it's the bad guy and the environment which he and the players must contend with. Burning Rod Of Seven Parts anyone?

* The "you must identify an objective other than survival or killing in a fight" rule is devastatingly concise and useful in all sorts of contexts. What I like about it, and the rest of the fightin' system, is that it still permits (and in some cases even encourages) winning by routing the other fellow. Unlike a lot of systems that try to impose other objectives on combat through mechanics, an enemy soldier is still an enemy soldier, not just a narrative obstacle. That is fucking great.

* You must have a connection to someone "on the other side". HAHAHA. That is hilarious and wonderful.

More thoughts on this as they develop, this really is a terrific game all the way around.

    \n So I'm thinking a breakdown of how Infection actually works would be helpful.

    1) Players pick a Maneuver. This is usually driven by the desire to hit the other side's disposition, but sometimes it isn't. Like, you can't attack the other side 'til you've performed an Assess, for example. Or you really want to earn up some downtime and get some close skills to go ahead and pop. But setting those aside, assuming the players have a full range of maneuvers to pick from, we take two things into consideration:

    * Who has lots of Artha RIGHT NOW? Because this maneuver's artha doesn't get rewarded 'til after the maneuver

    * Whose Belief(s) and/or "when last seen" is going to be easiest to engage in a way that feeds back into the skills available for the Maneuver you're considering? IOW who is going to be making the FON roll? Who is on deck?

    The GM's choice is a bit easier, and IME typically comes down to who has the most Artha headed into the maneuver.

    2) Players do the stuff they need to do within their scenes to feed dice to the on-deck Figure of Note's player. That is, provide help, linked or connection dice to him from the maneuver's skill list. Meanwhile, the on-deck guy is working at making sure he actually IS the "most important character", probably by instigating a conflict. Players who are NOT the FON chase the living shit out of standard Artha-generating BW play, so they're ready to go to bat on a future Maneuver roll.

    Meanwhile, the GM is doing the stuff HE needs to do within his scenes to feed dice to his own on-deck FON. This one's easier: just get the FON's all involved in scenes. Usually easy, sometimes not. THe GM also can fuck around with the players' plans, typically by directing narrative attention *away* from the guy angling to make the Maneuver roll.

    The players then have to balance several things at once:

    * Earning Artha for themselves
    * Keeping the on-deck FON on-deck, i.e. not spotlight-whoring
    * Making sure they're doing what they can to feed the on-deck FON dice (i.e. helping within the chosen maneuver's skill list)

    3) When the scenes are over and everyone's out, reveal maneuvers, determine the suitable skill, gather dice, and spend Artha. Now if the players are doing it right, the "suitable skill" will have been aimed at by the FON on deck all session long. You KNEW you were going for an Assess, so by GOD you have better been doing Assess-y things.

    But that part, right there, is where things slip sideways for a lot of players. They look at the maneuver only after the fact and ask "huh...well, I made an Investigative Logic roll...I guess that means I should be making that Assess roll..." And guess what? In most cases, they weren't really aiming to make that roll. So it's no wonder they can't link the maneuver's fiction to the maneuver's outcome.

    4) Play out the sequel. This is really the only point in the transaction where they kind of prompt you to tie the Maneuver's fiction into the larger Infection. But that's way, way after the fact.

    \n Sorry, poor choice of wording. By utopia I meant the kind of setting you describe. I'm cynical like that.

    If you want more reactive style play out of a World Burner/Infection mechanic, focus on these areas:

    Figures of Note: If the GM FoNs aren't external baddies or are also enemies of the Vaylen, that means their agendas are wrong, ie they allow the Vaylen to invade. If the Forged Lord is an implacable enemy of the Vaylen but is also a GM FoN, the PCs have to fight against him to stop him from inadvertently allowing the Vaylen to win. That means internal conflict. So, remove that.

    Relationships with the other side: Same thing, really. This is to emphasize the insidious nature of the Vaylen, and how they take over your friends and family and you never know until it's too late. It also works to emphasize how decisions as to the fate of the planet will be hashed out by people who know each other. So, remove that too, or y'know, use it like Superman has a relationship with Lexx Luthor, 'cause they already know each other. Whatever.

    Beliefs: An effective Belief basically states a problem and proposes a solution. "I must be the next Forged Lord, not my brother! I will gain the support of the church." Beliefs that challenge the status quo get you Artha, defensive Beliefs are defective. So, you could remove Beliefs. Or, you could be up-front about the threat, and have players write Beliefs focused on it, or perhaps on their own personal lives instead of on social issues. "Lexx Luthor must be locked up, I will discover his secret headquarters!" or "I want Gwen Stacy to go steady with me," but not "The mayor is a fool, I will replace him with a robotic double!"

    Factions: These don't actually come into play until somebody goes after them. Having an Organized Crime faction is an opportunity: if you go after it, you can get the Disposition bonus, but if nobody brings it up, criminals don't become a threat in the game. You don't have to have any factions, in which case nobody will get "sidetracked" by them, and you can concentrate on threat vs society.

    The framework of creating a world and then using Disposition and Maneuvers to threaten it doesn't inherently create a setting in conflict with itself. Even ignoring all this stuff I just wrote, you can still make the kind of setting you're describing.

Keeping the Usurpation Phase Figure of note alive is a good idea during the Infiltration, but after the beginning of the Usurpation Phase Prequel (Page 405) after the FON makes the roll to add to disposition, he or she or ... giant frog thing is just the same as everything else.

      Maneuver rolls are made by "The player whose character played the most prominent role in the preceding scenes.." (Page 430). The GM side "tests for the figure of note whose presence or machinations factored most heavily into these scenes" (again Page 430).

      Therefore any player may roll for maneuver. The GM is limited to his figure of Note characters, but he can choose whichever one he used a lot in the maneuver.

      And therefore Figure of Note isn't a big deal. Players can be one or not, and still affect each maneuver roll via making the roll, or helping with the roll.

      Posted By: Paul BAgony: The biggest stumbling block IMO to really rocking Burning Empires is reconciling how the Infection roll *actually works*.

      Intuitively, every gamer I've ever met assumes that the actions in the fiction impact the Infection roll. They do not! In fact, the players earn dice based on table behaviors, not in-the-fiction stuff. It's weird and wacky and tripped us up for at least two games.

      So, basically, disregard what happened in the fiction. Look instead at what happened at the table (who was the important player? who helped whom?).
      \nWhen I was playing a lot of Burning Empires, before we chose our Maneuver, we'd sit down and make a list of all our goals.

      The goals might be things like:
      * Investigate the source of the narcotics spreading in the Red District.
      * Trace the paper trail on the Church Operation.
      * Disappear Ortiz.
      * Get my new factory up and running.
      * Get General Leaf released from prison.
      * etc.

      Everyone got to add their goals to the list. Some might have been on the list for a few sessions, and some might be new. As a group, we'd select a handful of goals (usually two) to pursue for that maneuver. Then we'd ask ourselves which maneuver best fit the goals we'd selected. That would be the maneuver we'd go with. (And if the group didn't agree to pursue a goal that was very important to a particular player, that player could still choose to pursue that goal during the maneuver rather than helping the rest of the group).

      And occasionally, we'd choose a maneuver and then select goals that fit that maneuver.

      Either way, that little ritual meant that our actions in the game would always mesh with the maneuver we'd selected. We knew that our Conflict Scene had to relate to one of those goals. It also meant that everyone took a moment to really think about what their personal goals were for the maneuver so that they really knew what they wanted out of their Color, Interstitial and Building Scenes.

      Incidentally, I think that was a big reason we were able to blaze through two maneuvers each four-hour session we played.
      1. We were all very focused and there was very little game downtime as players figured out what they wanted to do next.
      2. We didn't try to squeeze too much into a single maneuver. We were very comfortable deciding that a particular set of goals could wait until the next maneuver.

      Incidentally, this process helped in another way as well. By staying cognizant of our goals, any Color or Interstitial Scenes that we didn't require for the present maneuver could be used to set up for future goals.
