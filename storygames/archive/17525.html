\n I'm hoping to get some feedback on a basic die rolling mechanic that I derived from AW.

My goals:
- To keep it simple and clear (a la AW)
- To make it so that PCs will generally want help or additional resources (Assets) to ensure their success
- To allow for those additional resources to have easy and quick representation

The basic mechanic:
Roll a number of d6s. To each d6, add your relevant stat. Every 6 or higher is a success. A roll of 1 is always a miss.

If you get:
No successes = GM makes a hard move ("Your sword strike is deflected by the goblin's buckler and it swipes its claws at your face, take damage.")
1 success = GM makes a soft move ("Your sword strike is deflected by the goblin's buckler and it swipes its claws at your face, what do you do?")
2 successes = GM chooses between a hard bargain, a worse outcome, or a terrible choice ("You can strike the goblin with your sword, but if you do then the goblin will get a swipe at you before it expires. What do you do?")
3 successes = GM offers full success ("You strike the goblin's head off its shoulders with your sword, huzzah!")

By default, PCs will be rolling 3d6. Additional dice come from Assets, each of which has a number of six-sided dice on it. An Asset can be used to add its full number of additional dice to the roll, but then the Asset's die pool will be reduced by 1, after the roll.

Every player can remove a die from the die pool that he or she would roll to add +1 to all remaining dice. This must be done before the roll.

Out of 10 verbs to describe possible actions, characters will have +2 on 2 verbs, +1 on 3 verbs, and 0 on the rest.

I'd love to hear any feedback on this system. Thanks!

    \n Sure! It's not exactly for a hack, as it is for a whole new system-y game-y thing I'm dabbling around with.

    Setting: A giant sentient city has come to a pristine, untamed, vicious, beautiful wilderness and has started to eat that wilderness. One player plays the city, one player plays the land, and the other players play Pawns, the people caught in the struggle who will ultimately decide which half of the fight wins. So both the city and the land are attempting to woo the Pawns over to their side of the fight.

    Mechanically: So, things are hard for the Pawns to do. Not impossible, but not easy. They've got their own assets, from when they started the game, but assets dwindle in utility as you use them, so that's just a temporary solution. If the Pawns want to get anything done, they're probably going to have to start working with the city or the land. The players of the city and the land each have big ole piles of dice that they can assign into assets, on the fly, and then grant to the Pawns. So the city might say, "Here you go! It's a corps of our steam-powered police force, come to help you kill that monster!" The land might say, "Here you go! It's a giant monster, come to help you smash those steam-powered clanky bastards!" The Pawns get to keep the assets until they're used up. But as the Pawns use assets from either the city or the land, they actually sway themselves towards those sides, making it more and more likely that they will gain permanent Scars or permanent Brass, with Scars connecting them to the land, and Brass connecting them to the city.

    I can get into more granular detail, but hopefully that's enough to explain what the general game is like.

    What I'm hoping to get out of this system is what I described above. Something easy, without too much math or fiddliness; something where the Pawns will likely need the help of one of the two god-beings, the city or the land, to succeed; and something where the city and the land can easily and quickly create the assets they want to give to the Pawns.

    \n As for the "linear" comment, what I mean is this:

    If each die has an equal chance of scoring a "hit", then adding one more die always has the same value to the player. Roll enough dice, and you're guaranteed to succeed.

    In a non-linear system, though, each subsequent die you add is less effective. Add enough dice, and it's hardly worth it anymore: it barely increases your odds.

    One example would be like this:

    Instead of your verb reducing the difficulty number, it changes one of the dice you roll. For your "zero" verbs, just roll your 3d6. For a "+1" verb, change one of the dice to a d8. For a "+2" verb, change one die to a d10, instead.

    So now you can have a "+5" verb (giving you a d30 or something), which is nice--almost a guaranteed "hit"--but still doesn't mean you're going to get what you want if the other dice roll poorly.

    If assets "stack" the same way, you have a similar effect for additional assets: they help, sure, but only to a certain point.

    (That's just one example, there are many other ways to do this without changing die types.)
