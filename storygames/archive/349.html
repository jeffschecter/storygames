    Stakes Resolution

    In its simplest form, Stakes Resolution has three steps:

    1. The players involved agree to the stakes. This agreement may or may not be spoken. This agreement must come before the mechanic is engaged.

    "If you lose, your brother goes into town and shoots the woman."
    "If you win, your brother does not go into town and shoot the woman."

    2. The mechanic is engaged (add skill points, roll dice, draw cards, use drama, etc.) which results in one outcome being selected over another.

    "I won! He doesn't shoot her."

    3. The final outcome becomes part of the fiction. It "happens" in the world of the game. All the players abide by the outcome, as stipulated in the stakes.

    "Your brother looks at you as tears well up in his eyes. 'I don't know how it came to this' he says. He puts the gun away."

    More examples of Stakes Resolution:

    "If you win the fight (by reducing the monster's hit points to -10), it dies."
    "If you lose the fight (by having your hit points reduced to -10), you die."

    "If you succeed at 10 climbing rolls, you will have climbed the cliff."
    "If you fail a climbing roll, you fall back to the bottom and take damage."

    "If you make your Investigate roll, I will tell you something that will help your character."
    "If you fail your Investigate roll, I won't tell you anything."
    "If you fail your Investigate roll by 10, something very bad will happen to your character."

    The key to SR is that the players agree to stakes before the rolling happens. They essentially enter into a contract that says they will abide by the outcome of the dice, as established by the stakes. Nothing else may modify the stakes once the final outcome has been determined -- UNLESS the players agree to a new contract and begin the process again.

    Maneuver Resolution

    With Maneuver Resolution, the players do not enter into a contract before the mechanic is engaged. Instead, the mechanic determines whether an attempted maneuver "happens" or not. The outcome and effect of the maneuver is determined by another process in the system, and may be modified or even negated after the roll.

    Examples of Maneuver Resolution:

    "I split your ugly face with this axe-handle! Raise with 12."
    "Oh no you don't. I duck under your swing and it misses me by a mile. Dodge with 12."

    "I put my sword through the goblin's neck! I hit for... gah... 3 points of damage."
    "Your blade bounces off its shield and just nicks the goblin's skin, leaving a trickle of green blood behind."

    "I want to climb the cliff."
    "It looks pretty tricky. DC 25."
    "Okay. I'll take extra time for +2. Climbing roll of... 20."
    "Ah, close. Just when you get to the top, your hand slips. You skitter backwards and clutch a root to stop your fall to certain death. You hang there in midair, legs kicking."

    So, obviously, Maneuver Resolution is sometimes embedded in Stakes Resolution. Sometimes it isn't. Unlike Stakes Resolution, players are not required to agree to anything before the mechanic is engaged, and the final outcome can be modified or substituted with a different outcome, as determined by another player or system.

    So, those are our terms. Feel free to ask for clarification if something doesn't make sense.

    As you can see, SR is concerned with outcomes (of any scale). The "moves" performed to get to the outcome may be strongly or loosely connected to the outcome, depending on the mechanic.

    MR is concerned with "moves" performed in the world of the game. The final outcome or effect may be strongly or loosely connected to the moves, depending on the mechanic.

    Check out Nathan's blog for more about SR and MR.

    John Kim: I'm glad you brought up the vague SR example. That was very much on purpose. Having perfectly clear and precise stakes are not a requirement of Stakes Resolution. What's required is that the players involved agree to the stakes as established -- before the roll.

    So, yes, this is Stakes Resolution:

    "If you roll a critical success, something very good happens to your character.
    If you roll a success, something good happens to your character.
    If you roll a failure, something bad happens to your character.
    If you roll a critical failure, something very bad happens to your character."

    But ONLY if the players agree to it before the dice hit the table.

    I think I see what you're getting at about player knowledge now. And I agree that it is kind of a gray area. I think Nathan's answer is interesting, but it's reaching up to social contract as opposed to a specific resolution method.

    Let me put it another way, and see if this helps: I think almost every single roll in a game of D&D is Maneuver Resolution, even including combat rolls. I mean, yes, the D&D combat system is a kind of stakes resolution -- specfically winning or losing a fight (-10 HP) = death. However, players don't usually know how many hit points a monster has, or what its capabilities are, and even if they do know, the DM has the ability to modify them before combat. AND the DM can add anything he wants to the conflict after it has started, including more monsters, an invisible healing ray for the monsters, or whatever.

    Given "open" combats like that, D&D can't deliver true Stakes Resolution. The players think they're agreeing to the terms of the stakes when they go in, but those terms can be manipulated during resolution without their knowledge or consent. So no SR is happening.

    In Maneuver Resolution, can you narrate your own failure? Theoretically, it's possible, but is it possible in practice?

    "I want to climb the cliff."
    "It looks pretty tricky. DC 25."
    "Okay. I'll take extra time for +2. Climbing roll of... 20."
    "Crap! Okay, just when I get to the top, the rock I'm holding collapses and I tumble backwards, but my lightning fast reflexes save me and I grab a root about ten feet down, my feet dangling beneath me. Whew!"

    The trick is that since stakes were not negotiated pre-roll, they have to be negotiated now. Presumably, the GM is the "neutral arbiter" of consequence, but we all know it doesn't always work that way. If you let the player narrate his own consequences, someone (the GM, other players, whatever) has to "judge" that the player's narration doesn't essentially let him win the stakes that were never pre-negotiated.
