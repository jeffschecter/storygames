\n So, it's summer, and me and my friends finally have some free time. As gamers, we were raised playing D&D 3.0/3.5 E(we're all in our first twenties), and we really would like to play something that captures that kind of feeling and 15-years-old sense of wonder (it might be argued that 3.0 D&D was poor in real sense of wonder, as everything is too shiny and encyclopedical, but we really were amazed by it, back then). On the other hand, i think that a game with simpler and more streamlined mechanics, that allows for a lighter GM prep and is geared to work on short adventures (3 to 5 sessions? maybe just a bit more) might really prove convenient. This time, we're not looking for intense emotional play (i do have polaris for that), but for some old-fashioned fun, for that "WOW!" you get out when you're 16 and playing your first D&D adventures. old AD&D retroclones might prove interesting, but we never tried AD&D, so i fear a big part of their charm would go wasted on us...

As the people on this wonderful forum always have plenty of advice and know lots of hidden gems, is there some game i really should try that goes in that direction?

    \n Fantasy heartbreaker time? I guess a lot of us are persecuted by this same question. How many games were written as reinterpretation of D&D? How many as a tribute and attempt to recapture the old game? How many more as an attempt to "fix" D&D?

    Also, as others have said elsewhere, D&D means different things to different people, there is no quintessential D&D experience. So I guess if you want something D&D-like, the question I want to ask is "what is D&D to you?" You say you want that feeling of wonder...That sense can never be fully brought back, I think. It's just a feeling you get when you play for the first time, when you're fresh and young. I don't think a game by itself can provoke that, not really.

    If we're talking retroclones, I think Lamentations of the Flame Princess looks damn great. You can find the thread on the front page.

    If we're talking reinvention, I think Storming the Wizards' Tower does an amazing amazing job when it comes to fighting monsters and exploring a world. The pdf is still up for free, I believe, but it's a beta text with mechanical problems. Still, plenty of people have run it successfully. You could try to give that a shot. I think STWT really hits on all the points you list. It does require some prep but it's not a lot of it. And the mechanics can be sort of confusing at first but they're really very straightforward. Best of all, I think it's really good at provoking wonder, as much as it can.

    I would also like to pimp Tony Dowler's "Apocalypse D&D", "Dungeon World" or whatever it is called now, which is basically AD&D filtered through the Apocalypse World rules.

    And if you want something that's basically D&D without being D&D there's tons of stuff out there, including Dungeon Squad, Red Box Hack and others.

    I bet others will have a bunch of cool suggestions, too.

    \n My regular Monday Night group plays Pathfinder. As a referee, I like it much better than 3.x, as I think it retains what's good about the system, pares back much of the baroque cruft, and also streamlines in some really spots ("Combat Manoeuvres" for example are very nice indeed).

    If you're willing to play from PDF rules only, it's also nicely inexpensive: rulebook, plus monster book, plus, GM guide, plus advanced player guide, all for 39.96 USD.

    The latter two are not at all necessary: the GM guide looks to have some quite useful resources in it (the statted up NPC blocks, for example, are a really useful), and the advanced player guide provides more classes, spells, and what-not, but you can easily work with just the first two, for 19.98USD.

    If you need campaign/adventure resources, Pathfinder/Paizo provides a wealth of material there, as well, all available in PDF. The non-core material pricing in PDF is slightly more expensive, but still a good deal.

    My regular Wednesday Night group is playing 4e at the moment, and is having fun, but it's not the same feel as old-style D&D, nor are we really looking to get that feel.

    For my Friday night, sporadic, "fathers and sons" game I'm going to be using Labyrinth Lord: solidly old-school, "rules-light" in comparison to anything D20.

    I had a look at True 20 and related systems from Green Ronin, but frankly, I prefer Pathfinder, and I don't think it's measurably more complex.

    \n Another vote for DungeonSlayers. It's free, rules-lite, and has a ton of resources available for it, including rules on firearms, new monsters, an alternate combat system, etc. Characters can be made in five minutes, the rules can be understood in another five minutes, and there's a bunch of 1-page dungeons from levels 1 through 15 or so, just waiting to be defeated. And its FREE! And if you happen to speak German, there are more resources on their original German-language site, like more spells, potions, etc. Good, good stuff.
    My friends and I have been playing this for the last couple of weeks, after months of my telling them we should play. We even managed to bring back one of our friends that had previously been lost to the morass that is World of Warcraft. Of course, we have been playing a mildly hacked version (added a bit more complexity to character abilities), but otherwise it was just a compiled version of all the info I could gather from the English version, the errata (of which there is impressively little), and the German bits I could successfully translate. Still, with their basic version, you CAN NOT go wrong.

    \n Boy, I'd have to vote against Pathfinder if you're looking for 'simper & streamlined' - it simplified stuff from 3.5, sure, but that's like saying "it has less fat than mayonnaise does" - it's still insanely complex. We're just finishing up a campaign that we've been playing for over a year now, and I've become so frustrated with it that I've started doing time-and-motion studies of our play to figure out just where the hell the time goes each night. Last Wednesday each player took, on average, 10 minutes to take their turn. The big time sinks were usually looking up rules. "There's a purge invisibility spell here." "Well our rogue needs to be invisible to be effective, let's dispel it." "Ok, but we don't know who cast it, so can we target the spell since we know its all around us or do we have to know exactly where the spell is cast?" So we spend 10 minutes studying the text of the dispel magic. And then there's the what-bonuses-can-stack-with-what stuff: "Wait a minute, how is your AC so high?" "Well, I've got this and this and this." "Wait, does that doesn't stack with that." "Yes, it does, it's a this bonus instead of a that bonus." So, 10 minutes recalculating our barbarian's AC, only to find, in the end, that yes, the player was not cheating. EVERY FUCKING TURN IT'S SOMETHING. The GM ensures us that the end is a few nights away, otherwise I would have quit by now.
    \n
    \nOTOH, if you're looking for crunchy tactical combat, Pathfinder's your baby. And the complexity sneaks up on you, just like with 3rd ed - at 1st level you don't have to worry about how Dispel Magic works or what stacks with what. We're 14th level, that's where it's untenable. I'm going to sign up for another tour of duty with our next campaign, and probably play until we get to around 8th level or so. (When you get to the point where everyone starts getting Fly spells cast on them it stops feeling like heroic fantasy to me. That's also when Dispel Magic starts becoming popular.)
    \n
    \nI don't really understand nostalgia for 1st edition AD&D either, but I guess that's because I was the one who got sick of looking up stuff in charts and got my friends to play The Fantasy Trip instead. *There's* an idea for simple-and-streamlined, if you can still find it!

      Posted By: snej
      I've got doubts about the DungeonSlayers magic system, though, mostly in the way spell-casting doesn't consume any resources apart from the cool-down time. What worries me the most is that it seems a first-level healer can cast Healing Hands every five seconds indefinitely, recovering about 5HP every time. That seems ... broken. Or at the very least, a severe case of the Cleric Problem. (Maybe the problem's self-correcting, in that no one will ever voluntarily choose the boredom of playing a healer?)
      \nMy group has kind of noticed this with the magic system. I think what the designer was originally trying to do was to make it so certain spells would make sure the spellcasters would have as much to do as the fighters and scouts. Its definitely more cinematic, like a video game when it comes to magic, rather than making magic users useless.
      At least with the healing spells, the way we play it (with the normal rules), is that magical healing can only be applied to a single target from a particular source once per day. So if you have Healing Hands and Healing Light, you could heal one person twice in a single day. If you only have Healing Hands, you could only do it once.
      In my house-rules version (which we're currently playtesting), the cooldown period is replaced with a Recast value equal to the Cooldown of the spell, at least of 2 (even for spells normally with a cooldown of zero) and no more than 20 (for spells with a cooldown of 100). This is a penalty to future casting with that spell, reduced by 1 per round waited. Spells that can only be cast every couple days are kept the same.
      Of course, this still makes spells fairly powerful when characters can cast them often. The idea we're tossing around now is to have the Recast penalty apply to the spell for the rest of the day, or require a MND + AURA check as an action to reduce the penalty by 1. That would be a bit more balancing, but we're still tinkering with it. But it is an option.
      Otherwise, DungeonSlayers works pretty damn well as an easy to learn and play game. We're actually now about to bring one of our regular player's wife into the fold, and she's a complete newbie.

      EDIT
      ...and one of our players did choose to be a healer. His rolls are kinda crap sometimes, but he has come in VERY handy, and the fighters owe him their lives.
      And now that he figured out Arrow of Light works against ANY target (not just creatures of darkness), he suddenly has some offensive capabilities.

      Posted By: jdfristromI've got to ask - why *not* D&D 4e? Don't most people say "It's streamlined and simpler, but it's not D&D"?
      Haven't played it myself, but that's what I hear.
      \nThe people who say that are generally the people resistant to change, I've found. A lot of the complaints against 4E are the same as those against 3E when it was first released: not "real D&D", too much like a video game ("Diablo on paper"), etc. In some ways, 4E actually feels a lot more like old-school D&D than 3.x ever did, and in other ways, 4E is completely different. In the end, 4E is D&D because it's about fantasy adventurers who kill monsters and take their stuff, and because WotC decided to stick the D&D label on it.

      Either way, sure, 4E is streamlined and simpler, but not to the degree that Albi_SG is looking for. It still calls for a lot of GM prep, and is geared toward longer campaigns. Look to the various responses in this thread to see a whole variety of more appropriate options.
