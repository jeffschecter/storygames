    \n Wow, great to see some talk of Roludo over here! (As you might know or might have guessed, I'm one of the Roludo founders, the major caretaker of the Roludothons, and one of the organizers of the Grand Roludothon.)
    \n
    \n@Orly...
    \n
    \n[RE: The Roludothons] While story games have not been the focus of the Roludothons, I gotta say they have been really well represented. Each time, there's been at least two story games on the schedule, and the diversity of player has been pretty great (i.e.: it wasn't the same 5 people sitting at the table for each event.)
    \n
    \n[RE: Organizing an event] The renting fees for the university, unless you're a student association, tend to be a bit pricey from what I can tell. A better bet would be the CEGEPs, where the classroom rent for 75,00$ per day, plus a fee for the security guard if you want to hold the event on the weekend and/or late into the night. If you want to hold the event during the summer, however, you're out of luck, and you're going to have to settle for a community center. Either way, for a first time event, my best advice would definitely be to go for that loft of yours. Until you know how many people will attend for sure, renting space is a financial risk. (Heck, even when you do know, it still is.)
    \n
    \n[RE: The underserved community] Yep, pretty much. This is actually a big part of why we created Roludo to begin with - and right now we're thriving. The community keeps growing and diversifying, and it's great. If you do get an event underway, be sure to come over and announce it on our forums - there's a good chance you'll get yourself a few more participants.

    \n Like Walkerp said, I don't think anyone over at Roludo would feel like Allons Jouer is some sort of hostile competition or whatever. If that's okay with you guys, I'll ramble on a bit about us, because I think the philosophy behind Roludo is something you guys might appreciate. (That, and it'll calm any worries about "competing against Roludo" you guys might have.)
    \n
    \nOur goal was never to be the be-all end-all of gaming in Montreal, at least not in a centralized manner. Our official mission statement, roughly translated (our official documents are in french) pretty much sums it best: our goal is to promote networking and discussion amongst the Montreal gaming community (which means both Larger Montreal-based gamers and people interested in gaming with Larger Montreal-based gamers.) As far as we're concerned, as long as people flock to Roludo to get their own thing on the community's radar, everything is working out like we want it too.
    \n
    \nThe Roludothons are more a matter of community members getting together than it is an official mini-con. We don't publicize it, and we don't actively try to draft participants. As long as there's enough games offered to meet the demand, we're happy. The only issue is that it takes around 25-30 participants for us to be able to rent gaming space while charging an entry fee that isn't too high (according to us: 10,00$), and that our free gaming venue (my home) gets a bit cramped when the number of participants go over 20. I think this is the so-called "critical mass" Walkerp was talking about. At some point, you might get too big to host your event in a free venue, but not big enough to make space rental float financially.
    \n
    \nFinally, there's the Grand Roludothon. In our vision, a big con is essential for the Montreal community to have decent networking and discussion among gamers. Our initial plan was to support Draconis as much as we could by encouraging people to run stuff at the con, and be ready to pick up the mantle if they decided to drop the con gig. They did, and we stepped up - hence the Grand Roludothon. It's a way for us to act our official mission statement, albeit a pretty big and time-consuming way. This is why we're doing a con.
    \n
    \nAnd of course, we'd rather not see other gaming events around the time of the Grand Roludothon. Truth be told, we're putting in tons of hours and taking on a fairly big financial risk to make this thing happen. So, of course, we'd like for the Grand Roludothon to be the big thing in Montreal in June when you're a gamer. It's a bigger pay-off for our efforts, and a bigger safety net for our financial risk. But we also think that supporting the Grand Roludothon instead of doing a smaller event around that time of the year is a win-win situation. When we created Roludo, we strongly believed that we could develop a sort of symbiotic relationship with every other gaming event / group / organization. We thought people could join the community, make us bigger and more dynamic, make us more awesome. In turn, we could be a rallying point where they could advertise and draft gamers for projects of their own, build momentum by piggybacking our own. It hasn't been a year yet, and we're happy to say it's working pretty well thus far - both ways.
    \n
    \nOkay, I'll stop my rambling now. :-D

    Posted By: Orlyfriendship. I know that last one sounds silly, but honestly, I think one of the evenings should just dedicated to mingling, socializing and bullshiting.
    \nNot silly at all, it's important for building the spirit that makes people want to keep coming back. Which might sound mercenary, but hey, we want to game with friends, so allow some time for people to meet and become friends without having to be focused on a game. That said, don't mandate it as social time only, no games allowed. Just build it into the weekend schedule\u2014here are the scheduled gaming blocks, and here's some unscheduled time\u2014and let people do as they please, even if that means some games break out.

    Our Friday Night Feast at Go Play NW grew out of the impromptu gathering the first year, when people arriving on Friday afternoon for the weekend naturally grouped up to go find dinner and hang out. We've formalized the dinner part (although it's still ticketed as a separate optional event, in case people can't make it at that time or for whatever reason would rather do something else for dinner) but the goal is still the same, to give people some time to just hang out and socialize before plunging into the heavy gaming of the weekend. And by this point we've now got impromptu post-con dinners happening on Sunday evening.\n
    Posted By: Jocelyn RobitailleAnd of course, we'd rather not see other gaming events around the time of the Grand Roludothon.
    \nThis is also important, especially when you're starting out. Check the calendar, look up the dates of the various big cons as well as some of the smaller ones and in particular the local ones, and look for an open weekend that doesn't overlap other stuff. Last year we lost at least one person because we didn't know about PaizoCon, which was scheduled for the same weekend. Not a big deal, someone's always going to have some kind of unfortunate conflict, but still we'd rather avoid that kind of conflict whenever we can\u2014which is why we also made a point of trying to avoid Origins, which also happens in June.

    One other note, we formed a non-profit corporation because our initial host, Seattle University, wanted to deal with a single organization rather than 60 individuals trying to book dorm rooms for the event, and we figured if we were going to be handling other people's money then for legal reasons we'd be better off as a non-profit corp. I Am Not A Lawyer, the laws will be different in Canada, funding things as an individual through Kickstarter may avoid that sort of necessity, but it's worth checking out. I'm sure Roludo can help you out with determining whether that sort of thing is necessary for Allons Jouer.

    I think it's cool that you're stepping forward to organize this. I've never been to Montr\xe9al, despite growing up in NH and being of French-Canadian descent, and if my financial situation were at all in better shape I'd consider attending. Good luck!

    \nI've been compiling some data relative to Roludo, and I figured some of it might be of interest to you guys - either as a way to get to know Roludo better or as a form of primitive market research for your Allons Jouer Montr\xe9al plans.
    \n
    \nBoth Walkerp and I have claimed on this thread that Roludo was very pro-story games. While checking the numbers for our Roludothons (our in-house day-long gaming events), I figured I ought to verify that claim. It turns out that 27,8% of the games played were story games. If you take the board gaming and war gaming events out of the equation and look only at RPGs, this number climbs up to 37%. In terms of players, 42,5% of the participants who attended at least one Roludothon played at least one story game.
    \n
    \nFor the record, part of those numbers come from projections, because our fourth Roludothon is taking place this sunday. However, unless a game gets cancelled out of the blue or someone fails to show up (which hasn't happened much thus far), the above numbers should be pretty accurate.
    \n
    \nHopefully, this'll be interesting to you guys.

    \n Posted By: jackson teguwhat criteria did you use to define a "story game"?
    \n
    \nHeh. I totally knew I would get called out on this.
    \n
    \nBasically, I went with pretty much every game which did not follow the traditional structure of RPGs, especially with regards to task / conflict resolution and with the way control over the fiction is distributed between the GM and the players.
    \n
    \nConcretely, this definition ends up including games from the whole Burning shelf even though they're pretty much their own thang, and excluding [small press / indie]-but-traditional games such as Little Fears.
    \n
    \nIt's not perfect as far as a definition, but it works.
