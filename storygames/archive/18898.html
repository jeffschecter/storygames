This Week's Controversial Focus of Debate: Rolling for how well you did.

\n I was reading through the recent Cthulhu thread and thinking back to my days playing exclusively "trad" games.

Do you remember how, in a lot of trad gaming styles, you'd say, "Ok, I'm going to [perform some non-crucial task]," and the GM would reply, "Ok, roll to see how well you do it"?

The key here is that there is a roll based on your character's skill level (in D&D, maybe a Dex roll or similar) for a situation that has no apparent conflict or very little story-relevance. It's pure task resolution, and often with very little (or nothing at all!) at stake.

For instance, the PCs decide to pose as servants in the King's fort, hoping to pick up on some news or gossip while they're there. They headmaster asks them to mop the floors or prepare bedrooms for guests, or some similar menial task. The GM has everyone roll, to see how well they perform the job.

Or maybe the PCs are coming in to land at headquarters. The GM tells the pilot, "Ok, roll to see if your landing is smooth or bumpy."

Two PCs say they spend the afternoon sparring with each other for practice. "Let's roll to see who fights better!"

The party has made camp, and is trying to get some rest in preparation for tomorrow's adventure. One PC is preparing dinner for the others. The GM says, "Ok, roll your Cooking skill!"

The outcome of the dice roll is then used to inject some Colour into the narration. ("That evening, the rabbit stew is stringy and tasteless, and you all go to bed feeling a little unsatisfied, with grumbling stomachs." "Kenny prepares the bedroom nicely, with all sheets tucked in, but the room prepared for the Duke is a little shoddy, and you miss some dust under the cupboard, which the Duke's handmaid is sure to notice.")

Some other examples:

* In the procedural/investigation genre, sometimes the GM has a string of clues the PCs must follow. In a scene where a clue surely MUST be found for the game to progress, the GM might have everyone roll, and then give the clue to the highest roller. This gives the characters a bit of a pecking order, or more characterization: "Jane is the smart one; she's always the first one to spot the clue!"

* Sometimes, these rolls can become very important in retrospect. The heroes tie up the villain; the GM asks them to make a "Rope Use" roll to see how well they tie him up. They roll pretty poorly; later, when the villain turns out to have escaped, the roll is a natural justification. Of course he escaped! Stupid Jim didn't tie his bonds securely enough!

How do you feel about this kind of roll?

From a modern design perspective, there is a trend in game design to say that this kind of thing is not an efficient or useful game procedure: dice rolls and mechanized procedures should be for important, focal elements of play. (Typically conflict resolution.)

When I'm playing Monsterhearts or Dogs in the Vineyard, there's no rolling of this type.

Looking back with the wisdom of hindsight, is there something to be explored here, or is it a good thing we've left this behind?

Although, as a designer, I winced a number of times while writing this post ("Oh, the pain! Why would you roll for that?"), I realized that, as a player, I don't particularly mind this. It's fun to have a little detail to throw in once in a while. Maybe the character who's in charge of the cooking misses three rolls in a row, and now we all laugh because apparently the group's chef is a terrible cook - it becomes a source of jokes and banter for the rest of the game.

What's your take?

    \n The main thing I would say (to have a conversation with myself for a moment) is that this kind of procedure, unlike conflict resolution, is very dependent on precise odds and curves.

    For instance, it sucks to roll for landing your plane if the system you're using gives you a "critical failure" 5% of the time. Likewise, it's weird to be rolling to bake a pie if you can often achieve a result of "Legendary success! Best in the country".

    So, if you're going to roll for this kind of thing, you need a finely-calibrated random process, which is not an easy thing. Some tasks, after all, are more random than others, and it's hard to field everything on the same scale. I remember, as a trad gamer, constantly looking for ways to tweak probabilities so that task resolution outcomes would "feel right" in different situations. In several systems, I used different dice (more variable -> less variable) for stressful situations like combat and routine situations. On this level, things never quite felt right: real life (and fiction) doesn't work on a consistent bell curve.

    \n Task resolution is an useful tool for providing color. We roll, and then riff off the result to create some choreography for whatever it is that's happening. Solar System, for example, leaves the door open for doing this: you just want to see how this thing goes, let's roll for it and see. (Of course the game also leaves the door open for the player to make the choice post-roll to create a resource off the roll, or for the SG to riff additional outcomes off the degree of success, so no roll in the game is entirely devoid of substantial consequence unless everybody chooses this to be the case.)

    Also, certain games use this task resolution more subtly, to establish arbitrary detail that might become pertinent later. For example, considering your PCs masquerading as servants: I can totally imagine how I might ask for something like that in D&D, in the spirit of its "conflict resolution through short loop task resolution", as I've termed it. I wouldn't know yet while asking for that roll whether it'll be important, but just moments later I'm running a scene where the majordomo berates the guy who rolled a critical fumble, and then that might blow their cover. The great thing is, this outcome will have come about seemingly arbitrarily and randomly, there would be no single player at the table who'd have maneuvered for it to occur. The DM wouldn't have the moral responsibility for it, either - you rolled a 1, that's why it happened! All in all, quite useful, and quite impossible to achieve without having a practice of asking for those task rolls whether they matter or not. This type of play simply requires a constant stream of fictional positioning to be established; it'll be too late if you start asking about the quality of the housework when the majordomo random encounter is already in place, as at that point you're forced to establish the detail retroactively, and it's much more difficult for everybody to separate player and character knowledge ("My character totally would've dusted on the highest shelves, too!").

    I don't feel that using task resolution rolls as a tool is a problem, and I never have - I always (as in, I think I wrote my first opinion piece on the matter in -04 or so) felt that the popular task vs. conflict dichotomy in the discussions during the last decade was idiotic, and mostly caused by an incapability to see beyond a false comparison. (Comparing task vs. conflict is a category error - not the topic here, though, so I won't go into more detail.)

    Where the problem comes in, and where task resolution gets its bad reputation, is specifically and exactly in obfuscatory conflict resolution practices: the GM lets you roll the dice for a task resolution, and then resolves the conflict utilizing his implicit narrative powers, and that might break your own implicit expectations that this roll you did should matter somehow. Once this practice (the GM fiats conflicts while obfuscating with task resolution) is firmly embedded, it opens the door to other GM hygiene issues like playing favorites, railroading, and that whole panoply, simply because now the GM has a massive amount of extra power that nobody at the table quite perceives or understands, and no oversight whatsoever to what he's doing with it, except perhaps some lukewarm rulebook advice about how he's supposed to make sure everybody has fun in the game.

    As you can see, the above explains why task resolution is not universally reviled: if you play with a GM who does not do the power-grab over conflict resolution, or does not abuse the unwritten powers that gains him, then you won't necessarily see any problems with traditional task resolution. Good for you. Even better for everybody, now that we understand the issue, we have no difficulty creating games that utilize mechanized task resolution (I'm specific here because, as I like to say, all games have task resolution - it's just non-mechanical in some), should it be an useful concern. We have the language and the theoretical understanding, and task resolution is slowly gaining a more varied and interesting place in design, as opposed to always being this cat's paw for the GM's pretentions of puppet mastery.

    \n Interestingly, in my D&D you totally have a 5% failure rate in landing a plane. This is because of what I call "enchanted probability": because we want to play exciting stuff, everything the player characters do goes through a wringer that exaggerates unlikely events somewhat. This is why all sorts of million-to-one chances actually have a 1-in-20 to happen, should a player suggest the possibility. Good examples of this are random encounters: despite every effort of every sandbox GM ever, it is absolutely impossible to provide realistic odds of interesting encounters while simultaneously running an interesting game; thus we instead constructively pretend that 1-in-6 is about right for whether my characters stumble upon that goblin hunting party during their 6-hour trek between two towns.

    That is to say, it's too simple to claim that task resolution inherently requires some specific approach to resolving tasks. It's exactly no more and no less than what it sounds like: how and for what purpose do you resolve individual tasks in your game? In one game you might want to get totally realistic odds for landing a plane successfully, while in another the case is that when the game has already progressed to the point where a plane is being landed, the only sensible thing to do is to resolve the task by a 50/50 coin flip. Just like you can resolve conflicts in different ways and for different reasons, you can do the same for tasks.

    \n Great feedback, everyone.

    Eero, as always, your take on this is insightful, fair, and very thorough.

    But I also like the analogy of the "let's roll some dice!" as a "fishing expedition", though also often one which does not bear fruit. (What do you with an average, middle-of-the-road, "Rope Use" roll?)

    When it does bear fruit, however, it can be dramatically effective. (Particularly in a comedy genre - or just with a group that enjoys having a laugh or two as they play. "Ha-ha! Joe the Bugbear failed his hunting check again! He'll be watching the camp from now on!")

    It can also be effective simply as a procedure of play: people really *do* enjoy engaging the gears-and-bolts of a new game, rolling dice, consulting oracles, drawing cards, and so forth. I've seen many a player's eyes light up when someone says, "Ok, roll those dice!" Now, I'm not 100% sure whether that effect would be felt as strongly in a really engaging, driven game. Maybe that effect is a symptom, rather, of a bored player, or someone who can't figure out how to engage with a certain game?

    Dave, you make a great point about any downsides/strange results rolls may have in a given system becoming just THAT MUCH more noticeable when the rolls are used in this kind of routine manner. That's the main problem I have with this technique, as I mentioned earlier. I remember, back in my excusively-trad days, constantly trying to adjust the probabilities of various rolls and never quite being satisfied with the results: no matter what I settled on, it would feel right in Situation A and then horribly out-of-whack in Situation B.

    Anyway, great discussion, thanks everyone. (Not intended as a closing remark; I hope the discussion continues.)

    One detail which is interesting is what Matt brings up: some games are amenable to this kind of treatment, and others are completely allergic to it. Highly drama-driven games (particularly games where important relationships or issues are intended to be on the line with every roll of the dice), games with very detailed resolution procedures (e.g. Dogs in the Vineyard), and games which require resource allocation/expenditure from anyone interfacing with the resolution mechanics (e.g. the Pool, any game where you get X rolls per scene, and so forth) all are pretty hard to engage in this manner.

    I remember noticing this when I designed a variant of TSoY which used the Pools (Reason, Instinct, and Vigor) as resource Pools a la the Pool (i.e. they could fluctuate up or down, depending on success or failure). It worked great... but it was now impossible -- or at least senseless - to engage the mechanics for "Color" purposes, as we could do in TSoY, where rolling the dice, on its own, doesn't cost anyone anything.

    \n If you grok the math and the outcomes right away, then you can also grok the tone and genre you're playing with, e.g. "1 in 20 chance something crazy happens = excitement trumps realism". If you don't immediately grok what task rolls do, though, you can wind up with some rude surprises. I've definitely seen groups get in the habit of making lots of task rolls, and then wind up making them at times when they really aren't okay with any given result.

    I wonder if it would be possible to ditch the stupid immediate outcomes (extreme roll produces failure at something routine) but keep the useful fallout? Arbitrary events as fodder for subsequent play needn't all be unexpected failures or successes right?

    Maybe instead, routine task rolls could be made to determine synergy? "When you do this thing you can obviously do, roll to see if happens to mesh well or poorly with the immediate circumstances." So if you know how to light a fire, you aren't going to roll badly and somehow forget this skill, but you could provide an unfortuitous signal to a nearby hostile. I like this general idea, but involving immediate work on the GM's part to invent "so how does that mesh well / poorly?" is probably a bad idea. Something more akin to the simple factoids produced by traditional task rolls would probably be better. "The branches here produce smelly smoke, which the wind carries into the forest," for example. And then that can either matter or not as the game proceeds.

They just DO things sometimes, like try not to fall asleep or try to untie a tricky tangle in the rope in their backpack, or what-have-you. Even a good GM ends up leaving people out of the action for a little while here and there--I don't think it can be a good thing in some groups for players to feel comfortable having their character take just a bit of the spotlight for just a moment to do something that doesn't impact the main swing of the session but still makes them feel included instead of just present.

    \n Here's an idea -- make plenty of task rolls, but each one is preceded by a GM declaration:
    "Pass / Fail"
    "Degree of success"
    "Degree of failure"

    "Pass/Fail" might or might not include degrees, but the point of the label is to announce that the character might succeed or fail. The roll will then establish this as accustomed. "Degree of success" means the roll will only establish how masterfully the character succeeded. "Degree of failure" (how masterful is your failed attempt?) will be infrequent, as most sane characters won't attempt tasks with no chance of success, but I'm sure it'll come up occasionally.

    The result of each roll will then be a bit of data for possible later use, just as with traditional task rolling.

    I'm sure groups do this informally already, but having 3 labels to specify between might expedite things.
