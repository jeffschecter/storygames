Are we too close to the flame, industry-wise?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nNeil Gow \n\n\n
\n
\n\nApril 2008 \n edited April 2008 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n I've been looking over some of the 4e threads and the horror that some people have been showing regarding the business practices of WotC with regard to their licence - and how this horror is being transfered into some aversion to trying the game when it comes out.
\n
\nIn a previous gaming existence I was very close to some of the inner workings of some CCG companies.
\n
\nOne of the companies was very open about some of its procedures and its chief designer participated it various fora, kept an online Q&A; and was easily accessible by players. Obviously he didn't answer ALL the questions asked of him, but when he could, he did.
\n
\nAnother company was almost impenetrable. I ran their entire organised play for half the world and I was never able to speak to anyone other than a single member of staff. Playtests were top, top secret. Noboby knew anything apart from the official line.
\n
\nGuess which company was held in the highest contempt by the players? Yup, it was the open company. The designer, sadly passed away now, was wholly LOATHED by the player base. The financial and marketing decisions of the company were questioned in the finest minutae and indeed a number of players quit the game stating their disagreement with the companies business practices as being the reason. The closed company, despite their product being perennially late, their promotions being dire and their communication being virtually zero, was simply ... there.
\n
\nI think of this and harken back to the days when I just went down to the local games shop, looked over the shelves, saw what was new and bought it. I never realised when a game was late to market. I never thought about whether it was a trad-game or an indie-game. I certainly didn't think to ever contact the designer, speak to the company or try to influence the way the game developed. In some ways these were simpler, happier times.
\n
\nAre we too close to the flame?
\n
\nOr are we just selective in the way that we use this more readily available 'inside information' to inform our purchases? Are we breeding a generation of ethically conscious games consumers or are we just 'giving it to the man' because we have found a weak spot in his armour?
\n
\nPersonally I think it is probably the latter. After all, I have yet to see (direct me if I am wrong) anyone questioning a company about the policy they have on say, recycled and/or sustainable paper for printing, the use of vegetable inks, whether they bank with a sound financial institution, what their policies are on childcare, healthcare, work-life balance or maternity/paternity rights?
\n
\nWhat implications does this have for independent publishers? Are their business practices going to come under similar scrutiny?
\n
\nOr is this just a case of using the information that is presented as and when it is presented?
\n
\nCheers
\nNeil

    \n I think the internet and the collapse of the information float has its hands in that as well. Back in the days when communication was all by letter, and we found out what was new when it appeared in the store, we didn't have all that visibility into various companies. They could run fine, or run themselves into the ground, and we'd never know until the company came out with, or stopped coming out with, their latest products.

    These days, we have forums; we have forums about that company on the company's website. We have enthusiastic people who hit refresh every day on their favorite company forums to see what products they're working on, what they're talking about, amassing knowledge about what's going on under the hood... and we also have the internet cranks with too much free time on their hands, who feel that they are owed private knowledge, and when given some of it they use it to promote whatever halfassed conclusions they'd already reached.

    When you think about it, it's the same as the movie industry, or anime industry, or comic industry before the internet (although of those three, the comic industry had the most "pen traffic" before the internet, I'd wager).

    Personally, I'm with Pramas: In this day and age, it makes sense to go open, or stay closed, but not some halfway stance between the two. I'd prefer it'd been closed until they figured it all out, then released it to the community, rather than the halfassed "sorta open/sorta closed; every employee has their own take/model on how it should/might work", etc.

    And it seems that they're feeling harassed, or at least pressured, by the fanbase to talk about this stuff more: Fans will be fans, both the enthusiastic ones and the cranks. WOTC should be used to it by now. A policy of going Closed wouldn't do any more harm (to themselves, anyway) until they got all their shit sorted out. Or, a policy of going Open, through a few spokespeople/articles, then completely ignoring the buzz generated by those comments (letting the enthusiastic fans stew, and not giving any more food to the cranks) would have been a less stressful stance to take.

    These are interesting days.

    Personally, as a fan and consumer, I plug my ears as much as possible to prevent getting influenced by incorrect speculation. It also enhances the surprise of the final product. It's the reason I'm trying to stay away from IRON MAN trailers before they reveal like the whole goddamn movie in a series of 1-minute clips. :-)

    -Andy

    Posted By: AndyFor larger companies like WOTC, though, I think it's an interesting topic.
    \nHere's something to consider:

    In the video game business, a lot of industry information is made readily available to anyone who's interested. You can read official publications, such as articles, interviews, and press releases, and you can read unofficial information like blog posts and message board threads (though these are obviously far less reliable). Sales figures, revenues and profits, and development status (announced, late, cancelled) are all presented for public consumption, and work-in-progress demos and videos are easily accessed if you're interested.

    Nonetheless, most gamers don't care. I'm convinced of that. Of the millions of people who bought Rock Band or Call of Duty 4, few can tell you who developed the game (though some can identify the publisher), or what the significance of the developer-publisher relationship is. This information is out there, but most gamers just aren't that interested.

    I'm of the opinion that the readership of online news sites, such as RPG.net, is pretty marginal. Of the millions of people that will buy D&D 4E publications this year, relatively few of them will have read about it on RPG.net or EN World or whatever, and few of them will actually know about the development process.

    On the other hand, the indie scene doesn't have the presence or the marketing budget of a larger publisher. Indie games don't sell millions of units. We rely on the Internet to get the word out. When the first edition of Dread was released in 2002, I sold pretty much every copy due to a few posts and reviews at RPG.net and The Forge. No one else knew about the game unless they met me at a con. Though the playing field has changed, the indie scene is still fairly dependent on that customer-developer relationship.

    I guess the point that I'm trying to make (with an alarming lack of factual data to back it up) is that a larger proportion of indie game consumers are involved in the discussions about indie game development. Does that make sense?

    \n PR is certainly a tricky question when it comes to complete disclosure.
    \n
    \nBut I think that's all we're talking about here. Each company described above was approaching publicity in a different manner. The closed company used an air of mystery and impregnable importance while the open company used a "friendly, open" approach, both of which are simply what the more odious pundits might call "spin."
    \n
    \nThe real question is two fold: which approach was ethically sound and which approach helped the company meet its bottom line?
    \n
    \nThe open and honest-seeming game designer died, we are told, but how did the game do? Was it a financial success? Did that matter to the designer as much as other concerns?
    \n
    \nThe closed game had loyal fans, was that because it simply gave people what they want? How well did it do? Were those involved comfortable with the business they did?
    \n
    \nI don't know. Certainly there is some strategic merit to keeping secrets, but it would seem the package is often just as important as the product, especially where a vice like gaming is concerned. Sometimes word-of-mouth is the packaging and sometimes its simply clever guerilla marketing.
    \n
    \nGuerilla marketing is actually the problem. Story games designers cannot afford to take a direct approach, they are not as mighty as their competition (I mean HASBRO? You got the chops to go up against HASBRO? Since most story games are self-published or small print runs, even Steve Jackson Games can give them a run for their money.)
    \n
    \nSo, you play to your niche. You expand your niche, either by playing it canny and acting like a resistance movement or going to your fan base and involving them in the process. It comes down to the personality of the game and the designer. I know that sounds like a cop-out, but, really, the secret of guerilla marketing is always to find the thing that people aren't doing and do that.
    \n
    \nSo is it really a question between disclosure and open content? No, that's someone else guiding your argument. What's the option you're not being offered? What is it that nobody's mentioning? What can you do that the more powerful companies either don't care about or can't see? That's what you've got to tell people.
    \n
    \nUnfortunately, I rather think its games like Prime Time Adventures and With Great Power... that have the most promising "message" and each other game system, whether it's Life with Master or the Secret Lives of Gingerbreadmen will have to reach out to its audience in its own weird way... and it better be weird, relative to the audience, or the big guys will already have gotten it covered.
    \n
    \nBest of luck. Each game's gotta find its own niche. I hope this makes sense.
