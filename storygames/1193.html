As requested, I've started a new thread for anyone who wants to talk about this editorial by Mendel Schmiedekamp, to which Andy responded initially in the latest Threads To Watch thread.
Posted By: AndyBut I HAVE seen people taken to task for having their heads above the clouds, arguing for the sake of discussion, in a shifting, illusory realm that no longer has anything to do with nuts-and-bolts gaming on any level; Using Actual Play as a rallying cry to keep this hazy illusory junk tied to something that could actually be observed or used.
When I read this paragraph what I see is a claim that "Actual Play" is one of many rhetorical tools deployed when someone else is talking about something they don't personally understand. Just like accusing that person of having their head in the clouds (see Aristophanes "The Clouds," to keep our Socratic context.) While I didn't agree with a lot of what Mendel said -- or I guess, a lot of the assumptions that seemed to underly what he was saying -- I do agree that this rhetoric can be wielded inappropriately, and that the general claim that theory can or must be "grounded" in Actual Play to be a bit overstated.

I guess the point I want to make is, it's hard to tell the difference between someone who is engaging in "hazy illusory junk" and someone who is expressing thoughts that you don't understand. (See: general reaction of RPGers to Ron Edwards for years and years.) This leads on the one hand to people who think super-abstract philosophy is even more awesome, because they can't understand it -- on the other hand, it leads to people who assume that anyone saying anything they don't understand is somehow acting superior, purposefully appealing to the former crowd, and doing nothing of any value. In a culture that favours the latter opinion, and provides strong rhetorical tools to support that stance (like, say, "your head is in the clouds" or "show us the Actual Play"), these positions tend to become entrenched. I'm not saying that this is what is currently going on. But I recognize the danger from other places, and I don't think bringing it up is all that crazy.

Theory and empirical evidence (i.e. Actual Play) have a complicated interaction. If you think Actual Play must always come first, or is somehow more real or important than theory, then I too can drop some names -- I think Thomas Kuhn would like to have a word, for starters, and the list goes on. If you think Actual Play is useful to help explain and elucidate things, I absolutely agree, but that doesn't mean it's the only way -- sometimes you can just say what you mean right off, and it's a lot faster. Maintaining a healthy balance between the two seems the most sensible. And part of that is making sure that when you ask for Actual Play, it is because you actually want to know what this person is talking about -- not because you secretly think they're producing junk.

    Daniel,

    Kuhn is a great person to mention, because his analysis on shifting paradigms shows that a paradigm is replaced by a new one when it turns out to be superior to the old one in addressing actually existing problems.

    That is, new theory needs to prove its value by reference to actual issues that old theory has a hard time explaining. Because all theoretical frameworks are tools for analysis.

    The same thing is true for RPG theory. People can talk about "RPGs are like Levi-Strauss' rituals" in the abstract all they want, that does nothing for me up to the point where they show how using that analytic toolbox enables us to better understand actual play (which, incidentally, eventually happened).

    A related issue is that you can take the boy out of actual play, but you can't take actual play out of the boy. We're all shaped by our play experiences, and our theories are inevitably based on our experiences. By sharing our experiences of actual play, we can begin to truly understand each other.

    So there's this whole swath of theory that's basically just about classification, attempting to create"a taxonomy of roleplaying," where various things get sorted into various different imaginary boxes based on whatever point a given taxonomist is trying to make. GNS is a great example. Push/Pull is another. I would argue that most of the "theory" stuff that we do is basically just categorization and the resulting discussions about whether Exception A fits better in the 300s column or the 400s column. This is us trying to be descriptive. Here, examples are clearly needed so we know what it is that we're talking about. Otherwise, mass confusion and misunderstanding is bound to result (as we've seen with both GNS and Push/Pull).

    One of the other major swaths of theory is us trying to be prescriptive, saying that if we do X in this case instead of Y, that it'll be much more likely to lead to result Z. This is where theories or other ideas are applied to try to speculate on what might happen in certain circumstances. This is damn hard to provide examples for, especially if we're trying to uncover new varieties of play or solve traditional problems in new and unexpected ways. The best we can usually give are hypothetical examples. I do think that there is wisdom in pushing people to make their speculative ideas solid enough that people can play through a scene or two and see if what occurs is actually what they expect. Playtesting is often 20 times more useful than hours and hours of thinking, as I'm sure most of us have experience with. But it can take a while for people to pull something playable together, so it's important to not expect instant results either.

    In order to be useful to a community of game designers, theory need not have any connection with Actual Play. It must have a connection with Potential Play (Actual Play being a subset of Potential Play). We are not (merely) here to describe what is going on at the table; we are (also) trying to uncover or invent new ways of playing, i.e., new potential play and the means of actualising it. Sometimes the best way to do that is take some piece of abstract theory that you feel is interesting, play around with it, and discover whether it yields new ideas about play or not. The success or failure of such an enterprise cannot be known in advance. Immediately asking for Actual Play examples will nip these enterprises in the bud.

    As an actual example consider my old thread on Shared Imagined Space / Shared Text on the Forge. Does it get more abstract than considering the difference between the fiction and the uninterpreted 'text'? Yet, this discussion has in the end informed my design of Shades, because it sensitised me to the difference between accepting a statement made by a player into the 'text' and accepting the statement's accurately describing the fictional world.

    From my point of view we have:

    Gaming Hypothesis: 1) A gaming proposition put forth, without any direct connection to its correspondence with factual or experiential data, as the basis for reasoning, discussion, or as a premiss for drawing a conclusion.
    2) A supposition or conjecture about gaming put forth to account for observable data; a supposition that will draw a conclusion in regards to known facts, which creates a starting point for Gaming Theory.

    Gaming Theory: 1) A systematic statement of game rules, applications, or principles to be done or a method of doing it.
    2) A gaming hypothesis that has been confirmed or observed and is accepted as accounting for results.

    So Gaming Hypothesis is the abstracted discussion of an idea or item without observable outcomes, without Actual Play. And Gaming Theory has been or can be tested readily, and the tests can be repeated to confirm the the theoretical idea.

    An artist, scientist, or anyone else who has an unsupported idea has an hypothesis. Theories may be tested through experimentation. Experimentation in this venue is actual play. The extension of all this is...

    Gaming Proof: a theory that has been confirmed through repeated testing under controlled circumstances.
    ---
    Gaming Hypothesis is very useful, but actual Gaming Theory should involve some rudimentary form of testable premiss.
    Example:
    Gaming Hypothesis: Olfactory input can influence immersion in role-play.
    Gaming Theory (1): Scratch-N-Sniff stickers on Character Sheets will enhance the player experience.
    Gaming Theory (2): Burning incense during gaming sessions impact play.
    Gaming Theory (3): A pile of rotting pork in the center of the table will generate horror and disgust for my Zombie Apocalypse game.

    The theories are all actionable. And may lay a foundation for rules or structure relating to your game.

    http://blog.kumapageworks.org/blog/?p=138

    There seems to be some semantic leakage here when we're talking about theory. Gaming theory (not game theory, BTW - that's a whole different ball of wax, and people should be more careful about using it, IMHO) - is a theory in a different sense than scientific theory. Those two senses are delineated well in the OED:

    The first sense is theory as in "theory and praxis" - 4.b. That department of an art or technical subject which consists in the knowledge or statement of the facts on which it depends, or of its principles or methods, as distinguished from the practice of it.

    The second sense is that of scientific theory - 4. a. A scheme or system of ideas or statements held as an explanation or account of a group of facts or phenomena; a hypothesis that has been confirmed or established by observation or experiment, and is propounded or accepted as accounting for the known facts; a statement of what are held to be the general laws, principles, or causes of something known or observed.

    Gaming theory is not falsifiable in the scientific sense. You cannot reliably prove that, for example, players have (and are driven by) knowable Creative Agendas. Part of the problem is that in order to accurately label their creative agendas, they would need to be familiar with the model, and you'd lose the control on the experiment. You can't prove that the three identified agendas are the only three, or that they are some fundamental basis by which others are created. You just can't. So the idea that actual play can help 'prove' the validity of one theory over another is out the window right from the get-go, and comparison to scientific theory is just plain bad mojo.

    The best that gaming theory can do is describe what is seen in practice in gaming and do so accurately and fully. That's the first sense of theory, and the one that most gaming theory follows, including my own AGE Model. In that sense, you can judge the model on how well it fits the observed act of gaming (actual play), but even if the model correctly describes actual play, it doesn't 'prove' that the model is somehow a causal agent for the play, and that by making conscious manipulations to the model, you can change actual play. It doesn't work that way.

    Yes, you can build games that use the model causally - but that only proves that you can use a model to create directed action, which isn't all that shocking. Yes, if people want to believe in the power of Theory X, they can alter their behavior to fit the model. But that doesn't make the model 'right' - it just means that people are capable of altering their behavior to fit a particular pattern if they see benefit in doing so.

    Which is why GNS has never rung true for a lot of people - they're perfectly happy doing what they're doing, and see no large upside from altering their behavior to conform to the requirements of GNS. Of course, there's the facile notion that these folks are incapable of changing their behavior, but that's pure bullshit. If there was a significant upshot to people buying into GNS (and GNS-derived games) - if it made them pretty or famous or gave them a 10" dong (metaphorically speaking, mind you), then people would adopt it. But it doesn't, so people don't.

      It's the conceptual linkage between actual play and scientific method I don't like. Actual play as "beta testing", or "feedback" is fantastic and essential. But unless you have more controls in the experiment, you can't be sure what you're observing supports your claim broadly, or simply for the specific individuals you play with. A large number of actual play examples, with a wider number of groups, over a longer period of time, carries much more weight when trying to prove or disprove a theory on gaming. A small number of actual play examples is annecdotal, and certainly interesting and useful, but ultimately not all that much better than fictitious play examples when discussing game theory.

      Hey there,

      First off, I shouldn't have made a comment about hostility, I should rather have queried whether I had correctly understood the direction of your remarks. The hostility query I think was inappropriate with hindsight so sorry about that.

      I agree actual play examples are not the only means, I simply think they are a particularly powerful means. That said, I shall reread your editorial as I would be delighted by the addition of other powerful tools.

      With respect to requests for actual play, I tend to ask for them on one of two grounds:

      1. I simply cannot understand the point someone is trying to make. In that case, the request is not an expression of incredulity, it is a recognition that communication is failing and an attempt to find another way to illustrate their point. Sometimes people use different language for very similar things, an actual play example can sometimes illustrate that what someone is talking about is something that in fact I am familiar with, but which they were describing in language that for whatever reason I found unclear. Thus it can be a way of reapproaching a point so as to better understand it. Necessarily then, I only do that where the person's point seems to me to be worth considering but where I think as presented I am not able to properly consider it. It is then of course a matter for them whether they wish to bother illustrating it or move on and either is fine.

      2. Separately, if someone is making assertions that I find incredible or unlikely, actual play examples can be useful. I don't overformalise this, people can come back and ask why I think it would be useful after all. But, it does change my views. I was originally firmly of the view that the Decipher LotR rpg was pretty much unplayable as written. However, some of those fond of the game pointed out to me that they played it as written and it worked well for them. Really on that example no further data is needed, I had made a strong assertion - that it was unplayable - and the existence of any actual play data to the contrary pretty much proved me wrong.

      I don't see asking for an actual play example as a way of putting down the other guy, I see it as an opportunity to educate myself. If say Bankuei posts about how a technique has worked for him, and I can't see why it would be useful, asking him for an actual play example might help illustrate to me why that technique was useful and whether it might also be useful to me.

      Now, sometimes I do challenge people (though not very often at all I don't think). If someone is making an assertion I regard as being driven more by dogma than reason, I will ask if they have actually experienced that thing in play. But that's not that common, partly because it's generally obvious when that is happening and partly because I get little personal value from people who are looking to grind their axes. Usually I would only challenge someone in that way if I did not myself have actual play experience and was concerned to understand whether they had a real point or were simply gunning for the game. An example of that would be if someone were repeatedly posting about how rule x in a game is broken, I think it is fair to ask them if it has actually caused them problems in actual play, particularly if people who are playing are not finding it a problem.

      As for is there anything I would not accept, the only thing I would not accept is an absolute statement that contradicted my own actual play experience because as a matter of logic a statement cannot be absolute and have exceptions. So, if someone said "you can never have fun running Pendragon, it cannot be enjoyed" I would know that was factually wrong because I have enjoyed playing it. That runs both ways, I accepted I was wrong on LotR because a single person saying that they had run and enjoyed it necessarily made my position wrong as a matter of logic. However, if someone said "almost nobody can enjoy Pendragon" I would find that surprising and unlikely but in the absence of better evidence I couldn't simply dismiss it, they may be right after all.

      Generally though, I ask stuff because I want to understand someone's perspective better. If I ask for an actual play example, it is because I think it will aid communication in some way, it's not a judo trick to take them down. When Droog on rpg.net challenged people for actual play, that was fair, if only because in the absence of actual play it was questionable whether the discussion had any purpose or was simply theory for its own sake.

      I don't think it's a rhetorical attack, whenever I ask someone if they have actual play experience I hope the answer is yes. I would far rather find myself wrong and faced with an interesting new idea than be right and no further forward.
