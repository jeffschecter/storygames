\n Hey People Pimping Games at Gen Con,

I started a thread over at the Forge to get the ball rolling on the much-loved 15 minute demo. Or, in the case of some of you lusers, the much-loved 15 minute demo that lasts 45 minutes. So let's discuss and hone to razor sharpness, there or here or wherever.

I'd also love to hear from people who are buying games - if you have fifteen minutes to sit down with somebody and get shown a game, what do you want to see? What don't you want to see?

    \n I responded over there, but even with the stressing out I prefer this forum. So, duplication:

    I'm orienting on a SOTC demo as a freeform template having to do with:
    - 5 minute "tour of the character sheet"
    - 5 minutes of "make sure you hit all the cool features of aspects"
    - 5 minutes of "fudge factor to absorb overspill and enthusiasm from the prior 10 minutes"

    Materials on hand need to be:
    - 12 to 16 fudge dice
    - 3 character sheets (Mack, Sally, and Jet, to maximize the love triangle; one on one demos focus on Jet and his "First on the Scene" compellable aspect, one on two Jet + Sally thanks to her "Jet's in Trouble!" and his "Sally Save Me!" aspects) in transparent sleeves
    - 3 vis a vis pens

    Personally I want my demos to run *less* than 15 minutes whenever possible. There are too many games; a smaller time footprint should help share the love better.

    DRYH will have a similar setup.

    My question, ultimately, is "does it need to be more than that?"

    \n I played a bunch of demos at the Forge booth last year and the one that stands out most in my mind was Jason's demo of The Roach. I actually ran and found my friends and dragged them over so I could go through the demo again with them. The NPC cards that let me keep track of which characters were in a scene and allied with which player, the short comic that explained the setting so I could read it while the other players were getting 1-on-1 time with Jason, and, of course, the quality of the game itself.

    \n I'm vaguely working on a new Cold City demo for this years. Those who played the one from GC last year will remember that it falls into basically two conflicts between two characters each. That's good in that it allows people to watch and see different parts of the overall mechanics in action. What I'm striving for now is a situation where everyone is taking part at once.

    My mind, though, is a blank when it comes to a really cool moment for the demo to take place in. My thoughts were:

    4 characters, all with opposing Hidden Agendas
    They have just killed the adversary
    They are in a room surrounded by valuable esoteric technology (was thinking of an abandoned floor mill/grain silo on the border of the British and Soviet zones of Berlin)
    Soviet MGB troops are heading up the stairs on one side of the building
    British infantry are heading up stairs on the other side
    Go!

    However, it just doesn't seem to do it for me.

    The demo last years worked, despite the split in conflicts. It ran to time, it was fast paced, demonstrated all the key aspects of the mechanics and gave a flavour of the game. Anyone who played it have any further feedback on improvements that could be made?

    I'll agree that the Roach demo was fun: it captured the game and made me want to buy it. As a buyer, I want a demo to be enthusiastic, to take place in a prototypical moment of that game and I certainly don't want it to last longer than 15 minutes when that's what it's been advertised as. There were demo that went on way longer than this and if I wasn't personally interested after the first five minutes, then it felt like it was really dragging.

    So, I think the things I value most are concision and clarity. Keep it short and keep it to the important basics.

    Cheers
    Malcolm

    \n A suggestion: demos aren't the whole story, either. I've played a fair number of demos, but they've almost never influenced either my opinion of the game, or my likelihood to buy it. Two exceptions: the demo of the Matrix games sold me. And I was on the fence about Shock:, but bought it because of the demo [and it turns out i wish i'd read the book instead of done the demo, because then i'd've known the game wasn't all there, and be buying the new edition, instead of having the original, but that's a separate issue]. Unfortunately, i've most often used demos after i've already bought and read the game, and couldn't figure it out--a use that there should never be a need for. But, again, that's sort of a tangent.

    See, for me, the fact that, as several have observed, you can't cram all of the coolness of most RPGs into 15min means that a demo really isn't satisfactory. There are two ways that the "incomplete" demo falls short. From the consumer's/player's perspective, it's not satisfactory, because while it may give me a couple cool mechanical hooks, it won't give me an appreciation for the game as a whole. From the producer's perspective, it's not satisfactory because what you think is the cool awesome in your game may not be what i think is the cool awesome, so you miss a sale because you don't even tell me about this awesome bit of your game, because you don't have room for it in the demo.

    However, there are very few RPGs that are so complex that all the cool bits can't be *explained* in 15min. Heck, most of them can be explained in 5min. Now, i realize that for some players, this just isn't the same as actually playing a bit of a game. But i can speak with authority for myself: even if they covered the same exact content, in most cases describing the mechanics to me is going to be *more* effective than showing them to me in play. IOW, i'm almost the diametric opposite of Gregor Hutton and Caesar_X: i want it all, not just the highpoints; and i'd rather have it explained, than shown. And i've met other people like myself, who're pretty much scared away from the Forge booth by the aggressive demo atmosphere.

    Luckily, there's a really easy solution that i'd like to encourage: refine your ability to explain/describe your game clearly and concisely, as well as your ability to demo it effectively. And then give the potential customer all three options for discovering whether the game is for them: a short demo, a shorter (but presumably more comprehensive) description, or a chance to browse the book. In the past, my experience at the Forge booth has frequently been that the game creator can't concisely explain their game, leaving me frustrated or forced to just read the book and figure it out for myself. Or perhaps they can, but don't even try, instead responding to "tell me about your game" with "it doesn't really come across in the explaining--let me show you with a quick demo". Please don't do that--if i want a demo, i'll ask for one; if i ask for an explanation, it's because i want an explanation. Some people learn better from the concrete experience of play; some learn better from the abstracted description of play--it's a learning style thing, and transcends the medium or the topic.

    \n Weird thought on demo design from this morning.

    A demo is not a teaching session of your game. It's a trailer for your game. You know all those movie trailers that essentially give away the entire movie? Don't do that. Instead, you want a demo that holds out tantalizing goodness and makes the audience want to line up for more.

    This means that you shouldn't feel frustrated that you can't get your entire game into the demo. That's not the point. Just get those couple moments of coolness in.

    This may be an obvious point, but it just came together in my head, so I thought I'd share.

    Seth Ben-Ezra
    Great Wolf

      \n I have a strict policy of demo-ing games before buying them, so demos are a big deal to me. Usually I demo games I'm already excited about, but at Gencon this year I was unsold on several games I was almost sure I was going to buy by confusing or boring demos. A good demo (to me) is a snippet of play which does three things: it shows off what's cool about the game, it lets the players do something cool (or otherwise feel like they're having fun), and it gives me some idea of what it might be like to GM the game. Some demos that sold me were a Dogs demo which was basically just a couple of conflicts resulting from the "your nephew has been stealing from your brother to hire the shopkeeper's prostitute wife" situation from the book. The dictionary of mu demo was a single scene with each player, followed by an explanation of how the two plotlines would connect, was another winner. The most successful demo I've played in was of Inspectres. I was never that interested in Inspectres, and my friends and I only went to the booth to get our indie passports stamped. Somehow I found myself demoing the game (basically a very short adventure with pregens, the plot apparently cooked up on the spot); it was so much fun I walked out with a copy.
      \n
      \nThe kind of demo I hate has no play and is basically a brief lecture about the game and how it works. Although well-written rules are a pleasure to play with, I still find them a chore to read. Getting lectured about the game is like a chore with no payoff, and even if it sounds fun, I still have no idea what playing is actually like, not even the snippet from a play demo. A lecture 'demo' at this year's gencon actually unsold me from a game I was almost sure I was going to buy. Another thing I really hate in demos is when the GM shuffles dice back and forth, telling me to roll this or that and sort of half-explaining why, leading to a sort of dice vertigo when I completely give up on trying to follow what the rules are like. If this happens, the game is a much harder sell, because I can only judge it on non-rules aspects and remain suspicious.
      \n
      \nLastly, in games with simple, clever chargen, a stripped down chargen session can really add a lot to a demo - this was done to great effect in the Shock and 1001 Nights demos.
