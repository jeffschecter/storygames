At long last! I am super excited to be announcing that Monsterhearts is at the editor, and an IndieGoGo campaign for the game is live!

Monsterhearts is a story game about the messy lives of teenage monsters.

It's based on the Apocalypse World engine, and it tells teen monster drama stories in the vein of Twilight, Buffy the Vampire Slayer, True Blood, and Jennifer's Body. It works great for one-shots and short (3-6 session) campaigns.

The IndieGoGo campaign is a pre-ordering campaign. You can purchase the PDF, the book, other goodies (postcards, posters, game resources), and the chance to creative direct some additional design work (con scenarios, a new playbook).



If you've played Monsterhearts before, give a shout-out and tell us about your session and how it went!

If you've got questions, feel free to ask them here or on the IndieGoGo campaign site.

If you want me to tell you why cheesy teen monster dramas make for amazing story gaming material, ask!

    \n This is one of my favorite games.

    I feel like the AW engine has been focused very well, which i to say, taking away things that don't serve teen horror drama and adding new rules and mechanics that do.

    Playing Apocalypse World is great for playing adults who are bad communicators in a world gone berserk. Monsterhearts doesn't feel like that - partially because the big scary things, the big "unknowns", are the characters themselves. Certainly there can be other weird stuff going on, but the characters cause a huge amount of damage just trying to get their basic emotional needs met, and that drives things forward at an excellent rate.

    The principles have been rewritten beautifully to push play in thematically correct directions. My personal fave: Accept people conditionally. This in service to the principle Make the PCs feel unaccepted. It's a fun (and potentially heart-wrenching, for all the right reasons) game to MC.

    I'm glad that this will soon be on offer at every con (because i know you SG folks!) & i'll be able to play it even oftener.

    \nJohn,

    I'll take a stab at answering some of these as well, though Paul and Steve covered a lot of what I would have said.

    Twisted Romantic and Sexual Entanglements

    This starts in character creation. Each Skin has a "your backstory" that gets read aloud and establishes some Strings before the game even starts. For example, The Ghost's backstory is:\n
    Someone knows that you're dead, and how you died. They gain a String on you.
    You\u2019ve been inside someone\u2019s bedroom while they were sleeping. Take a String on them.
    \nStrings are emotional and psychological leverage that you have over people. You can spend them to do a list of things: boost your rolls against them, subtract from their rolls against you, offer the carrot (XP) for them to do what you want, threaten the stick (having to hold steady) if they do what you don't want, etc.

    In play, you earn more Strings by turning people on. Here's the move, along with its explanatory notes:\n
    When you turn someone on, roll with hot. On a 10 up, take a String against them. \u2022 On a 7-9, they choose one: give themselves to you, promise something they think you want, give you a String against them.

    When you use this move, you have the opportunity to step outside of your character role, and speak like an author would. Describe your character\u2019s pouty lips, how the sweat rolls off their strong brow, and how they look silhouetted against the pale moon. Unlike other basic moves, this one can be triggered without a specific action being taken. If the player describes how arousing the scene is, without their PC actually doing anything other than standing around and looking sexy, the move can still be triggered.

    This move implies something about sexuality, and particularly teenage sexuality. We don\u2019t get to decide what turns us on. When you make a move to turn someone on (with a character action or with scene description), the other player doesn\u2019t get to exclaim, \u201cWait, my character is straight! There\u2019s no way that\u2019d turn them on.\u201d That\u2019s a decision that we as players can\u2019t make for our characters. The dice are going to be the ultimate referees of what is and isn\u2019t sexy for these characters. Their own sexuality will confuse them and surprise them; it\u2019ll show up in situations they didn\u2019t expect to find it in. Regardless of the results of the roll, however, each player still gets to decide how their character reacts. Being turned on by someone doesn\u2019t imply or demand a reaction.
    \nFinally, lots of moves introduce super problematic and messy and interesting "romantic and sexual entanglement" prompts. People have sex moves, and Steve's comment described two of them. My current favourite sex move is written by Jackson Tegu, for The Selkie (which gets released if the campaign hits $5k): "When you have sex, it counts as being underwater." A number of The Selkie's moves are triggered when she's underwater, so her sex move is just weird and symbolic and creates lots of possible combos.

    In addition to the sex moves, lots of the Skin-moves that you can pick are focused on relationship dynamics and sexuality. Each Skin is both a kind of monster and a kind of lover/person. The Mortal gains Strings on people for forgiving their base nature ("Sympathy is My Weapon"). The Witch gains Strings on people by taking their things ("Sympathetic Tokens"), and maybe later using them to cast hexes with.

    And, finally, the text puts a lot of effort into teaching you how to bring teenage sexuality - in all its confusing and messy glory - to your gaming table. My favourite section in this regard is Queer Content. It's a couple hundred words that unpacks why queer content is important, how to introduce it, and what to do when you introduce it. It was written with the help of a queer trans friend of mine. I'm really proud of that section.

    Downward Spirals of Chaos and Horror

    There are a couple different answers, but the most important one is Darkest Selves. Each Skin has a Darkest Self, which can be triggered in a couple different ways: as a 7-9 option on certain moves, by the MC making a hard move, by hitting 4 harm and choosing to become your Darkest Self rather than dying.

    You're a monster, right, but you're also just a kid. There's a difference between being a monster and being a monster. When you become your Darkest Self, you become a true and despicable monster. The Werewolf burst into wolf form and strives to prove its dominance via bloodshed. The Fae begins a personal quest to "re-balance the scales of justice." The Mortal begins to burn bridges and expose the secrets of all of her friends. The Ghoul begins to devour its hunger and kill anything in the way of it.

    You can escape your Darkest Self pretty easily - there's an escape clause built into each one. In play, most people like to revel in their Darkest Self for a while. It's kind of like a permission: go crazy, do damage, get vengeance, embrace your inner monster, no-holds-barred.

    As an example, here's The Werewolf's Darkest Self:\n
    You transform into a terrifying wolf-creature. You crave power and dominance, and those are earned through bloodshed. If anyone attempts to stand in your way, they must be brought down and made to bleed. You escape your Darkest Self when you wound someone you really care about or the sun rises, whichever happens first.

    Posted By: Paul T.
    Could I play this in a more "grown up" setting? Like, more Vampire: the Masquerade, less High School Sweethearts?
    Or would that kind of ruin the slant of the game?
    \nPaul, there's a section in the book that answers that very question! Why don't I just quote you it?\n
    After Graduation

    While the default assumption in Monsterhearts is that the PCs are teenagers who go to high school together, this doesn\u2019t need to be the case. It\u2019s fine to have the game focus on twenty-somethings who work together, or a sports team, or some similar departure from the high school milieu.

    If you choose to shift away from that default, it\u2019s important to know what the high school setting and teenage protagonists were providing. Otherwise, you might lose some of the magic that keeps this game alive and interesting.

    The PCs need to be in the midst of a major transition. They need to feel alienated, both alienated from their own bodies and alienated from the world at large. They need to be put together in an environment of petty social politics.

    The game flourishes when PCs are passionate, dramatic, catty, loveable but unlikeable, confused, and divided against themselves. It doesn\u2019t have to be teenagers who share a home room, but that\u2019s the assumption that the game is written under.

    On page XX, there are instructions for creating a homeroom seating chart, and using it to create compelling drama. If your game doesn\u2019t take place in a high school classroom, find an appropriate equivalent to the seating chart.
    \nSo, in True Blood, everyone is in their twenties (or 120s, in the case of the vampires). But they totally act like teenagers, all of them, all the time. Monsterhearts works perfectly for playing True Blood.

    Playing Vampire: the Masquerade totally works, if you start with the premise that vampires are passionate and irresponsible beings always one step away from a nervous breakdown. Which, you know, has been accurate in the two Vampire games I've ever played in.

      \nWilhelm, the "freak show parties" aspect of the genre, where as a series goes it just introduces more and more types of monsters, turns lots of people off. The text says its fine to have two people playing the same Skin, but not to have more than that. But, there are other ways to avoid "freak show parties" as well. Here they are:

      Let Two People Play the Same Skin

      The text specifically talks about what to do when two players want to play the same Skin, and says that it's totally fine so long as you have the ability to make an extra copy of the Skin. There's no listed answer on having the entire group be the same Skin, though, if there's 3 or 4 PCs.

      I would personally suggest having no more than two of any Skin. That way, each character can easily be mechanically differentiated from one another. Having a Fae who is "fae-blooded" with Guide and Unashamed and having a Fae who "stole the gift" and has The Constant Bargain and Lure is great. These characters have something in common, but are totally different than one another in background and play-style. Adding a third Fae to the mix, though, would create a pretty crowded Venn diagram.

      Use The Mundane Skins

      The Mortal's whole deal is that they aren't a monster, they're someone caught up in a dangerous and codependent relationship with a monster.
      The Queen is intentionally designed so it can be played as a freaky, powerful cult leader... or simply the local cheerleading captain. It can be mundane if desired.

      Using these Skins allows you to avoid the "freak show party." Two Vampires and a Mortal is a really common set-up in the genre. It's the heart of both True Blood and The Vampire Diaries, even though both shows lavishly heap additional monsters into the mix. Two Fae and two Mortals would be a great set-up! Two Ghouls and a Mortal and a just-a-regular-popular-kid Queen would be a great set-up too!

      The mundane Skin options allow you to keep it to a single type of monster, without having everyone playing the exact same playbook.

      Play With Fewer Players

      Another option is to play with fewer players. If you have an MC & two PCs, then you've got the perfect mix to play Let The Right One In (a Vampire and a Mortal). You've got the perfect mix to play some weird sexy version of The Grudge (a Chosen and a Ghost).

      Multiple Things At Once

      The Skins aren't all about "monster races." For example, The Chosen is about being a monster-hunter, The Queen is about being the leader of a popular clique, The Infernal is about making deals with the devil, and The Witch is about being a witch and casting hexes. But there's nothing preventing those characters from being a faery (little f) or a werewolf (little w).

      If you want to have four vampires, you'd just need to find out how at least some of those characters might also fit the description of a different Skin.

      A really good example of this is in The Craft. The show is ostensibly about four Witches, but one of those witches is trying to absorb all of the power of an elemental being, and becomes corrupted by that being's power and influence. So, really, she's an Infernal. She's making bargains with dark powers. She's an Infernal with Soul Debt (Bargains: The Power Flows Through Me, Elsewise Power) and Dark Recruiter. The same is true of Willow in Season 6 of Buffy the Vampire Slayer. She's still a Witch, but she's clearly switched playbooks and is now an Infernal, as she's borrowing uncontrollable power from some freaky dark power dude.

      So, yeah: there's lots of tools for avoiding the "freak show party" syndrome.

When people posted about the game (to Google+ or their blog) and I happened upon it, I'd engage them and share my own experiences. Oh! And I released an extended example of play at one point. That generated lots of positive buzz.

      How I found key supporters/champions...
      I did lots of early design work in public, via the Apocalypse World forums, where I engaged playtesters and talked to them about their experiences. I shared song lyrics and movie quotes that captured the genre for me, and invited others to do the same. Early on, Monsterhearts had a little discussion community. The game got lots of playtests, both internally and externally. It saw over 100 playtests with over 100 playtesters. So, the community carried momentum throughout the development process. Finally, when a frequent playtester said, "Hey, I'm going to do another round of playtesting, are there updated files?" I took that as a motivating kick to revamp the game and get them something beautiful. Carl Rigney asked and he got a newly-minted Rigney Edition in time for Big Bad Con. Jamie Fristrom and Steve Hickey asked and they got a newly-minted Fristrom-Hickey Edition in time for their late Autumn games. Those people then went on to earn me lots of referrals and support during the IndieGoGo.

      How I arranged interviews...
      Hans and I are friends. He writes for a gaming news site called Giant Fire Breathing Robot. Arranging that interview seemed to fall together pretty naturally. I think he asked me first. Someone named Paolo posted about the campaign on his blog, and I happened upon it (through searching "monsterhearts" on Google every few days). I posted a comment there, and he followed up by emailing me and asking me if I wanted to do another interview. I said sure! Finally, JBMannon backed Monsterhearts, and I noticed that he has a podcast called Gamestarter (that's about Kickstarting games). So I messaged him on Twitter and asked if he'd like to interview me. We recorded a podcast episode, which tragically got eaten by technological mishap. So, there's no discernible pattern here, but following up and talking with people who post about the game proved useful.

      How I got lots of random IndieGoGo traffic...
      To be honest, I have no idea. They chose to feature me in the Games section, tweet me as their Daily Project, and include me in one of their weekly newsletters. I think it's partially attributable to having a sexy cover image and icon image. I think it's also attributable to the fact that lots of games get funded on IndieGoGo, and so they've recognized how important gaming projects are to their customer base. So, (1) have a sexy image, and (2) cross your fingers.

      All in all, the best practices that I might offer, if you're looking to replicate this success:
      1.) Foster a sense of community during the game's development
      2.) Dialogue with your key playtesters
      3.) Ride on the coat-tails of Vincent Baker's success
      4.) Have a sexy cover image
      5.) Engage people whenever they talk about your game
      6.) Design a good game
      7.) Release some meaty previews along the way

      Posted By: reactionHmm... I'm a little bit disappointed that the next level is "Joe does something that he may well have done anyway."
      Any chance of a bonus "The Tattooed" skin?
      \nTo be honest, I wasn't originally planning to set any milestones beyond $10k. I thought that we might crest that figure, but that we certainly wouldn't exceed it by much. When I set the $11k milestone, it was with great skepticism as to whether it would be met. I was 92% certain that the $12k milestone wouldn't be reached. (Now, I'm not sure.) So, yeah: I ran out of milestones because this project was too successful. The notion of getting a tattoo to commemorate this success is a cool idea that Morgan suggested, so I ran with it.

      Kickstarter/IndieGoGo inhabit a weird social space, especially milestones/stretch goals. They're all things that the designers are really, really hoping that they'll get an opportunity to do. They're all things that the designer would like to do anyways. Kickstarter/IndieGoGo campaigns inhabit a space of not exactly being a strictly retail transaction, but not exactly being a community endeavor. This kind of stuff has been weird for me to process throughout this month. I'll probably do a blog post about it after a period of further reflection. But in general: all of Kickstarter/IndieGoGo exists at various points on the "they were maybe going to do it anyway" spectrum. I was probably going to finish another Skin at some point, some day, even if The Angel hadn't been unlocked. Now it's definite and it's given priority, but its previous likelihood of completion was more or less unknown (including to me).

      This stuff is weird. Like, different people have acted as champions as the project has approached different milestones. Matthew Gagan rooted for the game as it approached the $5k Selkie milestone. Others rooted for the game as it approached the $8k charity milestone, and the $10k games milestone. Sam! rooted for the $11k Angel milestone. Morgan is rooting for the $12k tattoo milestone. In each of these cases, the language has taken on a tone of community endeavor and collective triumph, which is... super weird and interesting. I've noticed it with other Kickstarter/IndieGoGo campaigns too (especially Flatpack, most recently). It's not bad, but it's... weird and interesting. It's being treated as something greater than a buying choice, which it (a) totally is, and (b) totally isn't.

      So, yeah. The $12k milestone was a fun little idea, some icing on an already gigantic and successful cake. I probably won't get a Monsterhearts tattoo if the target isn't reached, because I am a student who's swimming in debt and I've just been forced to spend 3 months not working on account of an injury. But, then again, maybe the milestone won't be reached and I'll still get a tattoo of the game at some point. Because the art is sweet and it would totally look hot as a tattoo.

      As for another Skin: I'm totally not going to commit to another Skin. I'm already workshopping The Selkie for Jackson, writing The Angel, writing The Serpentine for a perk reward, writing a mini-series of 3 three interlinked scenarios for a perk reward, committed to 4 Skype games as perk rewards, hand-crafting 15 zines, making several mix CDs, laying out the game, coordinating with printers, figuring out all the post-campaign-who-ordered-what stuff, traveling to Portland to do order fulfillment on 250+ books, traveling to San Fransisco and staging a Monsterhearts release party as per the Summoning Ritual perk, and more. Simultaneously, I'm a full-time online student. Any more commitments taken on for this project would lead me to burnout.
