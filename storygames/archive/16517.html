\n Hello Story Games, I would like some suggestions.

I do funny voices. When I GM games, I do a LOT of funny voices. They seem to be entertaining to the people I play with and I like doing them.

Here's my thing, though! I have like a bazillion dude voices up my sleeve, of all different types. But I have only a mere handful of lady voices, and they tend to be either sorta half-assed or are funny caricatures.

So, right now, I have a bunch of free time and I might be running a game with a lot more NPCs than I have in my B/X D&D game, so maybe y'all can give me some suggestions for female performances in the mediums of film and television and youtube clips that I can study in order to stock up on some good female impressions?

(And yes, as a dude I do feel a bit weird asking y'all to help me sit around at home learning how to do better impressions of women. If only I was an actor, then I could stick to male roles! Until somebody offered me the role of bearded lady...)

I'm looking for voices especially that:
a) are quickly recognizable as female,
b) are stereotypes to a degree, in that they conjure up an image of the NPC fairly quickly, but aren't crappy or offensive caricatures,
c) have other "hooks" that make them memorable and differentiated from other impressions,
and
d) are not super-hard to imitate.

But I'll take suggestions that don't meet all (or any) of those, too. It's an open thread, fire away.

    A good source of inspiration in general is the Speech Accent Archive at George Mason University.

    \n

    I'm an opera singer among other things, and I've played with voices a lot in my GM'ing. I find it important not to use falsetto/try to sound "like a girl," because it at best sounds like a Disney heroine or a poor caricature. Try finding a comfortable spot at the highest point of your normal speaking range that doesn't make you sound squeaky, and go from there.

    \n

    Some examples I'd recommend:

    \n
      \n
    • This Morley Safer interview with Katherine Hebpurn. It's classic Kate, very distinctive, and something that can easily be riffed on without squeaking.
      • \n
    • This supercut of Nigella Lawson "talking dirty" could be worth watching. Her voice is quite low-pitched and very expressive. Especially good, of course, if you're looking for posh.
      • \n
    • Along the same lines, Michelle Dockery (Lady Mary from Downton Abbey) has a lovely, lovely voice. Her accent in this interview with Letterman exemplifies her actual speaking style, but any clip of her from the show would also do.
      • \n
    • Kathleen Turner at most any point in her career. Here's Jessica Rabbit.
      • \n
    \n

    I will now stop.
