    I definitely sit if I need to fiddle with mechanics, or if the scene is more (for lack of a better word) intimate. I definitely stand if I need my energy level up, or if I want to convey that a character is dominating/imposing.

    \nI'm exactly the opposite.

    As a GM, I tend to stay seated, and if I do stand up (to stretch my legs or whatever), I remain in the same spot. I think this is mostly a matter of practicality: I want notes and dice and whatever else close at hand so that if I need to do something with them as a GM, I can get right on it. I like being easy to find if someone needs me: getting eye contact with me is trivial if I stay seated where everyone can see me, you know exactly where to look.

    But as a player, I spend most of the session standing, and I move around a lot. I'll lean up on walls, move to another spot at the table, pace back and forth, sometimes as part of play and sometimes just to burn off energy, because I'm not a big fan of sitting still in one place for long periods of time. (I'm not entirely sure how I manage to do it as a GM...I suppose GMing gives me lots of things to do that I can burn that energy off with, and so it doesn't feel like I've been in the same position for most of the night?)

    \n When at home I usually sit to GM, but at conventions I tend to stand.

    I think it's mostly because the room we play in at home is too small to permit a decent pacing circle, and I certainly can't walk around the table. In a lot of games at home we usually settle into our favourite couches, bean-bags, hammocks and other reclining devices. But this sets the scene for a particularly relaxed game. For more formal games we sit around the dining table (especially if we're using miniatures, other props, or have eaten a meal relevant to the game being played).

    Most of the conventions around here are played in schools or universities and as a GM you can set up the room according to your needs. I tend to set up a room so that the players are gathered in a table at the centre with all of the necessary props and accessories centred between them. That way I can walk around them and pace. I don't know but I feel it increases the tension when you can pace around the players. It also makes it easier to quickly access a certain player when they have something detailed to add to the game, or if there having trouble with the story or rules.

    Posted By: James_NostackI will never forget the time I playedMountain Witchwith Jenskot at Recess one time. I was like, "Whoa, what's he doing? It's like he's GM-ing, but he's... taller! And moving somehow, as if using his legs!"

    After a great deal of study I concluded he was using this technique called "standing" but it seemed too advanced for me. Maybe someday.
    \nThat was a fun game. It turned out better than I expected. Although veered a bit on the light hearted side with the tengu providing comedic relief.

    I tend to stand when the action is intense. Sit when things are calm and reflective. I also GM standing longer the more players are in the game. It's easier to maneuver around and keep the action flowing. If I don't want to stand too much, I will often re-arrange where the players are sitting. The louder players farther away, the quieter players closer to me.

      \n I was going to say "duh, I sit," but then I thought about it, and realized I often stand for practicality or emphasis.

      Emphasis- when I play as The Master in my Life With Master, I often stand and move around the table as I talk. Or last night, as a dragon's gnome minion, I stood up, moved over between two of the players, crouched a little bit, then started talking to them.

      Practicality: If two players are having a conversation, and I want to converse to yet a third but not interrupt them, I might move around to where the other player(s) are. Sometimes when running a combat with a battlemap, standing lets me see over my screen better, and I sometimes get in the habit of just standing through the whole fight, but these days I usually sit.
