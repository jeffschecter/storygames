\n From Books to Boards: How Barnes & Noble Got Into Games
http://geek-news.mtv.com/2012/01/24/from-books-to-boards-how-barnes-noble-got-into-games/

My friend Matt, who writes for MTV Geek, interviewed Barnes & Noble's VP of Toys & Games and their Lead Toy & Game Buyer.

Topics discussed:

- B&N's history with board games
- Challenges selling games
- How many stores are selling games & plans for expansion
- What B&N looks for when deciding what games to buy
- How B&N categorizes games
- Which categories sell best
- Which games surprised them in terms of sales
- How B&N playtests games they are interested in
- Who buys games
- In store promotions
- Training store employees
- Tracking buzz coming from Origins, Gen Con, Essen Spiel, and BoardGameGeek

Great interview!

    Posted By: Emily CareLots to think about there.
    \n100% yes!

    One thing that's amazing is how accessible these people are and how cool they seem!

    In the Vegan world, there was a controversy over Earth Balance (vegan butter) so a vegan podcaster (who doesn't even have too much of a following) called Earth Balance up and offered to interview their VP to clear up any issues... and they agreed!

    One thing that stands out about the B&N interview is how they categorize games. Roleplaying is not the focus or even something many people understand as a category of game. RPGs have minimal floor space dedicated to them and tend to by hidden away.

    But if your game falls into the Strategy, Card, Board, or Word game categories, it's much easier for a place like B&N to sell them. Specifically Strategy games are their big sellers. They also break down Strategy games into \u201cadventure strategy\u201d and \u201cabstract strategy", with abstracts not selling well as adventures.

    It seems like they care much more about the play value, clear rules, presentation, and price of the game rather than the quality of the physical components.

    \n "It seems like they care much more about the play value, clear rules, presentation, and price of the game rather than the quality of the physical components. "

    I think that it has more to do with "perceived value." Munchkin befuddles me as far as clear rules. You really need someone else who has played it to teach you.

    Along the same lines, based on what Hasbro usually pushes out for the Christmas rush, you'd think that the people in charge at Hasbro really could care less about games,but the head guy at Hasbro who makes decisions on games, (Mike Hirtle, I think), is also a huge fan of intricate, well-produced games. Unfortunately, he also works for a company that looks hard at the performance of the bottom line, and what can be placed at places like Walmart and Target..and so his work really requires him to temper his opinions, and make sure that their products make money at $20 price points.

    There was a point a few years ago that Hasbro did try to create a "hobby line" of games...but the margin just aren't there for a monolith like Hasbro to spend any effort on.
