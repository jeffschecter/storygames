Escalating and Non-Escalating Stakes

Josh Roby
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Play Advice
Posted By: JasperWhat do you think the effective difference between escalating and non-escalating (flat?) are? Is escalating always more interesting, or can the finality of a flat bid/trade have its own purpose?
Jasper asked this of most everybody (so join in) and I thought it probably deserved its own thread.

To me, I think escalating stakes is an improvement over flat stakes, but that's not exactly saying much. Escalating stakes certainly avoids the grind-a-thon nature that defined most of my prior gaming, where every session was pretty much the same as the last, just with different window dressing. However, I find purely escalating stakes to be more than a little exhausting, and only appropriate in short-run to medium-run games. Dogs being a prime example, I can't even conceive of going for more than three or four towns (and the dice system starts to break down after six or so).

I think the optimal stakes frequency, if you will, is something more like a sine wave. You need some ramping up to conflicts and whatnot, but you also need some settling down time that allows the players and characters to wind down, reflect, and maybe pursue kinds of character development that is only available in a less-pressured setting. We have two approximations to the sine wave -- the flat line (non-escalating stakes) and the straight positive-slope line (escalating stakes). The flat line approximates best over a long period of time and the positive-slope line approximates best in the short-term, but the flat line doesn't have the variation needed to maintain interest and the positive-slope line never lets up to give the characters and players time to unwind, relax, reflect, or develop outside of always-pounding conflict.

I think we'll see some new developments on the horizon for manipulating stakes in a fashion more amenable to long-term play. I think PTA probably stands out here as a good precursor. The television-series motif allows players to access mostly unconscious understanding of the ebb and flow of plot significance because they base it off of their long experience with television shows. All we need to do now is figure out how to bottle it and start injecting it into our game designs.

    Non-Escalating Stakes, Escalating Resources, Escalating Consequences
    1) Rescue a little girl kidnapped by kobolds or they'll eat her!
    2) Find a locket stolen by goblins and hidden in a series of crypts so that the town will be safe!
    3) Defeat a necromancer at the bottom of a dungeon and retreive his magic doohickey to save the kingdom!
    4) Kill the Evil God to forestall apocalypse!

    The stakes -- survive and get a mcguffin -- don't really change, but the increasing power of the main characters and adversaries (and the consequent destruction weilded by the adversaries) change the experience. When done well, this creates a pretty entertaining campaign. When done poorly, this creates Power Creep.

    Mike -- yes.

    Jasper -- they are essentially the same to my eye, just easier to illustrate in the macroscopic scale. Girl Eaten/Town Burned/Kingdom Falls is easier to lay out than Lose Advantage/Lose Hit Points/Knocked Out of Fight.

    So while it's nice to have that episode in the middle of the season that doesn't push the season metaplot, but just lets the principal characters be who they are and do something that's not fucking earth-shattering for a change (the macroscopic), it's also nice to have big significant fight scenes followed by in-character conversation and a little humor (the mezzoscopic), and even after downing the first badguy in a long-form combat to chase the second badguy across the combat map before downing him (the microscopic). Every stage of the game should not be "like what we just did, except more."

    Or a different example from a different angle: you're the GM, right, and the PCs have hacked their way into the dungeon and just killed a Level 10 Hobgoblin. If the next encounter is a Level 11 Hobgoblin, and the encounter after that is a Level 12 Hobgoblin, and then the Level 13 Hobgoblin... you get what I mean? Exclusive escalation eventually dulls the benefit that escalation is supposed to provide you in the first place.

    Joshua,
    I think the optimal stakes frequency, if you will, is something more like a sine wave. You need some ramping up to conflicts and whatnot, but you also need some settling down time that allows the players and characters to wind down, reflect, and maybe pursue kinds of character development that is only available in a less-pressured setting.
    I went to a play-writing class where the teacher plotted "Tension" against "Story Time". The ideal graph, she suggested, was approximately a sine wave: tension goes up, then goes down to let the audience rest, then goes up again, then down, etc.

    So I guess what I'm saying is that your sine wave applies in other types of storytelling as well. And, by extension, the sine wave doesn't just let your players and characters wind down: it's a function of telling a good story.

    Incidentally, her graph went up over time. So, at the start of the story, it went up a bit, and then came back down. Then it went up a bit more, then came back down, but didn't quite hit zero. And so on until, at the climax, the tension shot right up and then crashed down.

    Trollbabe handles this by the concept of "scale" - you start out with conflicts/stakes that affect one or a few individuals and, between sessions, by player request, increase the scale (small group, town, country, world, from memory, but probably incorrect memory). But it's a specific rule that the scale only ever goes up, not down. The consequence, I imagine, is that once you've had a world-scale conflict, you stop - those particular characters, that particular story, are at their natural end.

    Joshua, check out With Great Power. . . the first chance you get. The game is all about story structure as it applies to melodramatic superhero stories. It's the only game I can think of that's deliberately built to use the rise and fall of action in the way that you describe.
