\n FLGS GUY: "So Pandemonium Books (FLGS & SF/fantasy bookstore in Cambridge) just bought Your Move Games (FLGS in Somerville)."

ME: "Interesting. I've noticed that, recently, Your Move has had a much better selection of small press games than Pandys." (Implicit question: Are you gonna try to preserve that / learn from them?)

FLGS GUY: "Yeah, that shelf certainly looked really nice, but it seems like it was the only thing the former manager was really concerned about." (Implicit response: Focusing on indie games, which didn't really bring in the money for Your Move, contributed to the demise of the store.)

ME: "Huh." (Thinking, in my head, that the only reason I didn't spend $100 a month at Your Move is because... I already owned most of the indie games I wanted. And the reason we run SGBoston at Pandys is that Your Move doesn't really have a great atmosphere for playing in the store. Still, I feel bad that the store that was really invested in supporting indie stuff wasn't better supported by local Boston area indie games fans. Clearly there could have been a better way to combine our powers like Voltron.)

    \n Often, it's a matter of investing time to maximize profits. I used to run a game store (10-15 years back), and even though we could make a little money from weird role-playing games, our time was much better spent making much more money from Magic cards. Time is limited, margins are small, costs are high.
    \n
    \nExample (the numbers are just illustrative): Bob runs a game store. His monthly costs are $10,000 - rent, hired help, phone etc. He needs to make more than that every month. Say he buys stock for $20,000 and sells it for $40,000, paying $10,000 sales tax.That means the store is barely surviving from sales of >$1000 a day, even though Bob and his two employees work full-time (and Bob more than that).
    \n
    \nSpending two extra hour a week on 10 different indie games (contacting them, keeping updated on what's in stock, ordering stuff etc) for a profit of $50 means they're not spending that hour selling novels and CCGs for a profit of $100 or more. In addition, ordering from a major distributor is a fairly smooth process - just write some numbers on the order sheet and fax/email it over to your contact person, and you've got all your items for the next two weeks ordered. Indie games, not so smooth; they're mostly their own distributor, and they're not professionals, so you don't know what to expect.

    \n A complaint I've read quite a few times in recent years is that, with the death of independent record stores, people lose the ability to have that personal, immediate interaction with people who share their interest. Yes, you can get the same records online, but you might miss out one a different record or hearing a local band, because no one is there to recommend something to you.

    It seems fair to say that retail establishments for entertainment niches or subcultures (indie music, role-playing games, model trains, etc.) serve functions other than simply selling products to customers, right? They're places to create community, in a way that Best Buy or Walmart is not.

    So I totally get what Jonathan is saying. Whatever the failures of these stores as businesses, its a loss to the local hobby community when they close.
