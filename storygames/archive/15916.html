\n I have some bad GMing habits. I am disorganised. I have bunch of bits of paper and I mix them up when I'm playing and have no idea where things are. I find it hard to remember new systems are rely on my players to help me with them. I forget the names of NPCs, their accents and habits. I forget exactly what's happened in previous sessions, or even 15 minutes ago. I try hard, but I suspect I give some players, or characters, more attention than others, usually because I'm enjoying the interaction. I think, but I'm not sure, that there is too much NPC - PC interaction in most games, and not enough PC - PC interaction other than long planning sessions. I think I go too easy on the players.

Other than that, I think I do a decent job. What about you?

    \n OK, so after being snarky I'm honor-bound to put some skin in the game.

    I'll cop to disorganized and unprepared, for a start. My deeper weaknesses are:

    1. Letting my NPCs be all reasonable and accommodating and indecisive and shit, rather than giving them all strong motivations and goals and playing them accordingly. In my effort to let PCs be the center of the story, I hold back an d have NPCs do little more than ask, cajole, scold or complain. Thus they come off as weak, stupid,m incompetent and whiny--not sympathetic and not real drivers of the action. The players generally pick up on the NPCs' lack of resolve and walk all over them, push them around, respect no one, and play their PCs like entitled dicks. Which would be fine if the NPCs would actually push back in a meaningful way.

    2. Failure to be firm about rules-interactions, parameters for success/failure, etc. I'm not a full-on railroader; that would require me to have actually planned a plot. I just developed the habit of hedging my bets, "forgetting" to think of a target number in advance, for instance, just in case I don't like the way things are going and need to nudge them this way or that. I think this is largely a defensive reaction to 1, above.

    Playing Apocalypse World with Hans Otterson GMing, and Sorcerer with Jesse Burneko GMing, has taught me a TON about both of these. AW and Sorcerer make 2 pretty much impossible, and the way Hans and Jesse have run them are both great models for averting 1.

    Peace,
    -Joel

    PS. It occurs to me that the thread might have maximum value for all involved if our posts included ways we're learning or improving?

    \n 1. In the heat of battle, I tend to make stupid decisions. Not just tactically (I kind of suck at tactical play), but I often don't push hard enough, or I blank out and pick some course of action that is totally boring. Hell, I do this as a player, too.

    4. I'm super-organized and, if anything, I over-prepare. This is generally a good thing, but it has bitten me in the butt. Could also be related to #3 above.

    \n When I start a new game, I think about the procedures I want to use in it. Sometimes they work out great, sometimes less so. However, I often let the successful procedures fall by the wayside for no good reason, and fail to re-implement them in later games -- instead going after a new bright idea.

    Most of my NPCs are men. Unless I roll for it, or make up a randomized list of male and female names I default to a male NPC. (And because my players tend to be 50% women, they do notice, and point this out.)

    I know putting even just a tiny bit of forethought into an NPC will make it a whole lot memorable. (Just name and a picture stashed for future use would be plenty.) I still tend to make them up out of thin air, which makes their quality very uneven.

    When I'm improvising I tend to forget to firm up some details in my mind, leading to consistency being streeetched a fair ways sometimes.

    I crave validation.

    Sometimes when I mess up the consistency I should just say "Um, I messed up there." but instead I try to cover it.

    I suck at portraying travel.

    Sometimes when I should just make a ruling and continue I feel an irresistible urge to explain and justify the ruling, when no-one really cares.

    EDIT: I can't do accents at all. I have soft spot for my players even when I should just bring the hammer down. I drink too much coffee when running games. I'm bad at taking notes -- and even when I implement a procedure for it that works great I tend to abandon it.

      \nI struggle with this as well. I'm always updating my NPC Beliefs for BW just as session starts.

      I kind of know where this comes from, at least for me. Back in Ye Bad Olde Dayze, when I was running 2E, then 3E, effective prep was kind of this nebulous thing. Often prepping meant planning a railroad (even if only because "hey these are the encounters I planned you guys"), but if there wasn't any prep, there was nothing to do. So it became this very stressful and potentially time-consuming thing.

      I really need to fucking get over it now that I know exactly what I need to do to prep effectively. But it's really hard to get over that sense of "what if it's all a waste of tiiiiiiiime?" or "what if my prep locks me into ideas about what should happen and then the game sucks and my players hate me forever".
