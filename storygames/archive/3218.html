    Guns, Guns, Guns by BTRC... an entire book, in three editions, entirely about calculating the damage of firearms based on real-world information. Take the size and type of projectile, the amount of propellant (which eventually converts to joules of energy), length of the barrel, etc and plug all of it into a bunch of basic formulas and some lookup charts that summarized complex formulas... and you get damage, range brackets, and I believe the mass of the weapon and other things. It's been a long time since that kind of thing mattered to me, so I don't recall exactly. But I do recall writing the author (Greg Porter) about my difficulties in deriving the exact formulas from the charts, and the possibility of some of the numbers being wrong. Drove me crazy, because I was trying to create a spreadsheet to do all the math, and my curve-fitting software couldn't account for some of the numbers, which turned out be heavily rounded for simplicity. That really drove me crazy... rounding for simplicity was for wimps! I had Lotus 123, gosh darnit!

    \n

    I suspect SJ Games of using it to create the gun charts in GURPS.

    \n Sweet merciful heavens ... that Synnibar thing just totally obfuscated the square-cube law. And that's an easy one: "Multiplying something's size uniformly in all dimensions by X will multiply its surface area by X-square and its volume (and weight) by X-cubed." Instead they make a temporary swap-variable of "Multiplier" ... apparently to confuse and frustrate.
    \n
    \nMaybe it's just because I grew up with it, but I found Champion's Advantage/Limit system for fancifying powers to be fairly elegant. You would divide the total points by the sum of the limits, and multiply it by the sum of the advantages. That way, the more you limited it, the less impact each new limit had (adding a 1-point limit to a power that already had 3 points of limits would mean that you divided by 5 instead of by 4 ... which isn't anywhere near as much an impact as dividing by 2 rather than by 1), but likewise buffing up a power did not become an impossible exercise in geometrically increasing price.
    \n
    \nIt was made a bit wild and wonky because the limits and advantages were measured in quarter-points ... so you'd be dividing (fairly frequently) by things like "one and three quarters." Divide 57 points by one-and-three-quarters, then multiply it by one and one quarter. It can be done, but it's not either fun or efficient (much better, in fact, to simply say "Okay, you start with four points of limits and four points of advantages ... if you add to either one then you must multiply the cost by the advantages and divide it by the limits" ... that way the calculation above becomes "57 points times 5, then divided by 7" and I get 41 (rounding up) much more quickly)
    \n
    \nBut, even so, the fundamental math was pretty damn elegant, and some of its unintuitive complexity was clearly necessary to make that work.

    \n The funny thing is I and exactly one other person I game with actually enjoy computing hardcore orbital mechanics. Meaning everybody else can be guaranteed to not give a fuck and get bored by it. (Interestingly, this includes physics majors.)

    There was a good amount of this in Transhuman Space. Okay, I probably embraced more math than was actually even in the books. GURPS Traveller: First In also features an astonishing amount of mathematical forumlae. I've got a bunch of physics crib sheets, spreadsheets, and software for computing all kinds of things like delta-V for interplanetary travel, Hohmann transfers, spin gravity, orbital period for an object of arbitrary mass, and so forth.

    Basically, it's "math homework."

    This kind of stuff is really pretty splendid for solitaire world-building, where you don't have other people waiting around for you to work through all the math. But there's no surer way to get people to lose interest in the game than breaking out the graphing calculator and getting into some heavy physics porn.

    What's really weird about the Traveller thing is that they've gone to great length the make the sci-fi tech subordinate to easy play handling. All jumps conveniently take a week, it conveniently takes about a week to do your business in-system... for pretty much anything, you can just say "Oh, it takes you a week to do that." Really elegant and board-gamey. And then they drop this crazy math in, which seems to have no direct impact on actually playing the game. It's a pretty classic case study in a game drifting to Simulationism as new material is released.
