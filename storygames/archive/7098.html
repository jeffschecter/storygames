\n
\nWHAT THE GAME IS ABOUT
\n
\nAll the players take on the roles of made-up famous novelists. Let\u2019s assume that all these novelists have been well-acquainted for about 10 years, and also that, when it comes to their art, they are talented, popular, and critically acclaimed. None of these novelists have finished a book in quite some time. Why not? Due to the unspeakable horror of the literary life, they have become too distracted.
\n
\nSince these novelists are talented, popular, and critically acclaimed, readers everywhere are champing at the bit for them to come out with a new book\u2014but there\u2019s not enough buzz to go around, and whoever gets a book out first will steal the others\u2019 thunder and get all the glory. Enter all manner of passive aggression, undercutting, sabotage, backstabbing, aggressive aggression, intrigue, and shaky alliances. Who can cut through the distraction, survive the warfare, and keep the others down long enough to get their book finished first?
\n
\n
\nWHAT\u2019S SO HORRIFYING ABOUT THE LITERARY LIFE?
\n
\n*Your floors become strewn with page upon crumpled page of horrible, horrible prose that you are ashamed to have originate from your hand. You will burn them presently.
\n
\n*One of your best ideas, it turns out, completely ruins the last act of the book, and must be cut out.
\n
\n*Book signings. Not so bad in concept, except when people have you sign their arm and return to show you that they have made it a tattoo. Or when you sprain your wrist and people curse your name because you couldn\u2019t stand the pain long enough to sign their copy.
\n
\n*Your family makes unexpected visits when you are in the best possible spirits to get some work done. Now you can\u2019t, because you have to show them around Brussels, or Rome, or L.A., or wherever it is you have decided to live in search of inspiration.
\n
\n*You run out of coffee at precisely the wrong times.
\n
\n*You write a sentence that suddenly seems familiar \u2013 have you accidentally plagiarized it? You search your library feverishly trying to find a clue to its authorship, and all the while horrible thoughts race through your head. You will be sued! Your career will be ruined, your credibility dashed! People will pelt you with stones from the street!
\n
\n*You discover a copy of your first novel at a bookseller\u2019s, in the bargain bin.
\n
\n*Your fictional characters begin manifesting at the top of your stairs late at night.
\n
\n*You re-read your current draft and realize that it is AWFUL. It could not have originated from your hand by any stretch of the imagination, surely. You will burn it presently.
\n
\n*You take up smoking again. You run out of cigarettes at preceisely the wrong times.
\n
\n*You are so overwrought that your behavior has become so unpleasant that your friends hate you. Because of this, you hate your friends.
\n
\n*For some reason, the television set is on. What you see on it inevitably enrages you, and yet you cannot find the will to stop watching it. You will waste the entire afternoon berating talk shows and soap operas. You even pick up the telephone and say something on a call-in show that you will regret.
\n
\n*A distant cousin that you cannot remember will see that call-in show and call you about your remark. Then he comes to visit.
\n
\n*You are so overwrought that your spouse and/or lover refuses to remain in the same room with you. Because of this, you hate him/her.
\n
\n*Your fictional characters begin manifesting in your kitchen, where they drink the last of your coffee. They then raid your cigarettes. Finally they go to sleep on your couch without extinguishing their cigarettes.
\n
\n*Parties thrown by other literati, where you are compelled to mingle with authors you do not recognize, do not know, or wish you did not know. All conversation revolves around how incredible my next book will be, inane or outright inhuman reviews, how other authors are losing their touch, ineffectual publicity, paltry royalties, and, of course, the unspeakable horror of the literary life. Everyone is boring, but you wonder if this is merely because you are boring.
\n
\n*You realize that you are getting older every minute, which can only mean that your talent is slowly dwindling. Maybe it\u2019s already gone.
\n
\n*You receive a letter from your publisher wondering when the new book will be finished. You will burn it presently and dance around the ashes.
\n
\n*The preliminary sketch of what will be the cover art for your new book is hideous and throroughly inappropriate. Furthermore, it is too late to do anything about it.
\n
\n*Interviews, in which you will be asked questions you have answered a million times and questions for which you have not prepared an answer. The former will bore you to catatonia. The latter, while rare, will stun you so sharply that you will be unable to do anything but stammer, grunt, and sound like a fool on NPR.
\n
\n*You are so overwrought that you have driven away everyone you care about and who cares about you. Because of this, you hate yourself.
\n
\n*At some point, you must either finish this damned book or admit that you are no longer a writer.

    \n Hey, that's very cool. I compared your trait lists to the authors I know personally and it totally works.

    FAMOUS YOUNG ADULT NOVELIST
    Unpleasant traits: Apprehensive (double), erratic, brusque, repetitive
    Pleasant traits: Hip, affluent, fashionable
    Peculiar Trait: Enjoys shopping at The Gap

    FAMOUS SCIENCE FICTION NOVELIST
    Unpleasant traits: Self-righteous (double), tactless, paranoid, callous
    Pleasant traits: Dependable, focused, motivated
    Peculiar Trait: Praises Fox News

    I'd suggest the "double up on one prominent trait" thing be mandatory. In general I think 5/3 traits is too many, and the die economy doesn't feel right, but playtesting will tell and I love the idea.
