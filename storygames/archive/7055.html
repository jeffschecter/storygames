\n Raph Koster, in A Theory of Fun, writes:

"Games are not stories ...
Games tend to be experiential teaching. Stories teach vicariously.
Games are good at objectification. Stories are good at empathy.
Games tend to quantize, reduce, and classify. Stories tend to blur, deepen, and make subtle distinctions.
Games are external - they are about people's actions. Stories (good ones, anyway) are internal - they are about people's emotions and thoughts."

I read that and was like, hey wait! But on further reflection, those points ring pretty true to me, even among the poncy edge case games where you roll to overcome your grief or burn your birth certificate as a resolution mechanic. Is Koster right, or is he full of shit? One of the things he argues in his book is that there's no compelling need for games to try to emulate stories, and that stories can learn a lot from games perhaps. But I like vicarious learning, and empathy, and subtlety, and internal emotion and thought. Are those things to be sought after? Are we already reaching for them? Is it a fool's errand? What's next?

After some wrangling between them, the outcome tells us what particular action the collective protagonist will take in response to a question, but the action happens off-screen after the scene has ended.     \n I think that the quoted text, when applied to the thing I do, is pure and utter bullshit. Not only because the "division" is wrong, but because the categories themselves are false.

    What I want is play.

    The joys I acquire from play are legion in number and in type, and multiply unceasingly when those of my friends are included. Categories, when applied to this thing, are nice ways to point at clusters of joy, to say "you can get these five kinds of awesome stuff all at once if you play in this way".

    But taking on the pretense that any categories are exclusive and absolute in themselves is a nice way to say "My neat little categories are more important to me than helping you get good play".

    And, you know what? Fuck that.

    \n Theory of Fun is a pretty cool book, but he does cheat a bit to support his fun==learning premise.

    That said, he's fairly on the mark from the perspective he's coming into it from, which is really from the play of games angle. In broad strokes, it is the assertion that a game of Chess does not need to tall a story to be a satisfying game, and that using chess pieces to tell a story will not necessarily make for a fun game.

    Where it gets weird, especially for this crowd, is that we often view the creation of story from a more atomic level. That is to say, we embrace the _actions_ that create stories (creation, plotting, telling, collaborating, whatever) as being on the same level as any other action (moving a piece, making a tactical decision) so that when we create a game with those pieces, it create story. That's awesome, but from Koster's perspective, that's more of a curiosity of the playing pieces we choose to use. That some games create story in this fashion is no more surprising (and no more impactful) than the idea that a game about putting together food ingredients might make bread.

    I feel like this is one of those things better expressed with a diagram or some kind of Algebra, but i hope the gist of it is clear.

    -Rob D.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarOK, that's cool. His framework doesn't apply to us. Can we talk about the poles of experience Koster outlines (objectification vs. empathy) anyway?
    \nIf that's what's most on your mind, love to! This may not be quite what you're thinking of, but hopefully, it at least won't be hostile to the thing you're trying to get at.

    Treating the fictional world as a collection of playing pieces and objects means that we can get all the kinds of good stuff that regular game-type games provide. Treating them as expressions to be empathised with can make for story stuff, but it can also lead to session as a weird means of communication - which is why people thinking about this kind of thing often also feel the need to stop and think for a moment about play-as-therapy.

    Switching some piece of fiction from one of those kinds of thinking to another can yield interesting moments and enjoyments of other kinds; when your knight (an expression of you in the fictional world) is sitting around and their page (an object sometimes used to facilitate expression) suddenly says something inadvertently poignant as a mouthpiece for a GM, they might make the leap in your head between object and expression, and once you act on that, the transition permeates the group.

    Going the other way, you might deliberately "step back" from your character, making them an object in your head, so that you can employ the now-object in a wider expression; when your send the knight to die, you're not expressing yourself through the knight, but with the knight as a tool.

    These are just meandering thoughts on that... Kind of looking at it as a "stance-to-fiction" thing.

    \n This is something that I've been thinking about a lot lately.

    I've gradually moved toward designing games that are not about making stories in the the traditional sense. They don't really support good narrative pacing, arcing fiction, etc . . . What I've found is that such games do produce stories, or at least drama in some sense. They produce the same kinds of stories that you get in sports or video games. Little mini-stories about an interesting series of events within a constrained and regulated framework.

    Think baseball. It's like the bottom of the 9th grand slam to come from behind and win the game. There's certainly drama in that, and I think it's some type of story, but it's completely generated by the structure and procedures of the game.

    I got into this mindset a couple of years ago when I learned how to play D&D "correctly" for the first time (I maintain that many, many people get bad impressions about the game because they play it wrong) . We took Rules Cyclopedia D&D and ran it as a complete world simulator. No concern for narrative structure, just pure simulation. I was amazed by how satisfying that kind of play can be and it's filtered into everything I've done since.

    I don't know . . . a lot of this comes down to the specific meaning of terms (story, etc . . .), but I would tend to agree with the Koster quote. That whole book is very interesting IMO, even though it focuses mostly on video game design.

    \n For Koster's values of "game" and "story", his premises are entirely consistent and (barring some weird edge cases) perfectly accurate. The major thing that makes them a poor fit for the sort of games "story games" is dealing with is pretty clear, too.

    In Koster's world, a game is a closed system. It contains all the fictional content and all the "rules" in a fixed, unalterable form when the player encounters it. All the player can do is explore the game as it exists.

    In "story games", the game is an open system, a mechanism, if you will, that takes player input and transforms it into new rules and new fictional content. Indeed, a fair number of our games don't even work as Koster games - they are intentionally incomplete systems unless they get user input and rule-making. Even highly exploratory games - things like Spore or Sims - only allow the user to manipulate a defined set of elements. You can do anything you like, as long as it fits into this framework. I think highly System-Matters, coherent TTRPGs come about as close as anything in the tabletop world to a Koster game, but they're still a good distance away.

    Similarly, what counts as "story" in our medium and other media are only superficially similar, but we could be here til doomsday if we get into that swamp.
