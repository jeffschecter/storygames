\n In an unfortunate, un-Zen thread, some confusions about copyright came up. Rather than discuss there (where the OP is quite unwilling), I am making a new thread.
---------
In the US, a creative work is not "owned" by the creator, it is protected against infringement of sales for a given period of time, after which it releases to the public domain. Over the years, that time's gotten longer and longer (now it's Life + 25 years, IIRC), but the Framers never intended IP to be locked away or lost forever--quite the opposite, having seen the results of English laws which had steadily resulted in lost works.

I quote (from a Thomas Jefferson letter (highlighting mine):\n
If nature has made any one thing less susceptible than all others of exclusive property, it is the action of the thinking power called an idea, which an individual may exclusively possess as long as he keeps it to himself; but the moment it is divulged, it forces itself into the possession of every one, and the receiver cannot dispossess himself of it. Its peculiar character, too, is that no one possesses the less, because every other possesses the whole of it. He who receives an idea from me, receives instruction himself without lessening mine; as he who lights his taper at mine, receives light without darkening me. That ideas should freely spread from one to another over the globe, for the moral and mutual instruction of man, and improvement of his condition, seems to have been peculiarly and benevolently designed by nature, when she made them, like fire, expansible over all space, without lessening their density in any point, and like the air in which we breathe, move, and have our physical being, incapable of confinement or exclusive appropriation. Inventions then cannot, in nature, be a subject of property. Society may give an exclusive right to the profits arising from them, as an encouragement to men to pursue ideas which may produce utility, but this may or may not be done, according to the will and convenience of the society, without claim or complaint from anybody.
\nSo note: if you get an ISBN for your game and send it to the Library of Congress, it will be on shelves waiting for public domain release, indefinitely. In a similar fashion, after Life + 25 years, anyone who wants to setup a press may print, distribute, and sell it (barring some estate blocking, viz Tolkien estate).

It could be argued that taking action to physically remove the work from the public space necessarily grants others the right to provide that work, again, so that IP is not lost forever but preserved to educate, inspire, and enlighten all, forever.

    I presented a basic argument, summarized as follows (if I'm following it right):
    * It is presumed that an idea is of value to the world and its people.
    * We want ideas to be disseminated, to provide maximum utility (maximum "good," to be sloppier).
    * To ensure that, we protect IP for various periods of time--providing a creator monopoly--to incentivize taking the effort to create and distribute.
    * An idea that is presumably valuable is not "taken" when it is learned by another--the idea remains the creator's (or rather, remains everyone's) and more importantly, that physical manuscript is still locked tight int he creator's safe.
    * Should a creator wish to destroy an idea, that is preventable via copying.
    * It is believed that destruction of useful ideas is to be avoided, for the good of humanity.
    Therefore, should a creator attempt (or even risk) destruction of an idea, it is a moral duty to preserve the idea, using whatever means are available and affordable.

    This was of interest to the Framers (the most-easily-found quote I provide above; there are several to read; and, no, they might not all be "right" but they sort of had the idea for this country) because they had seen quite a few travesties of IP injustice, including loss of works forever (unforseen death, fires destroying masters and copies, etc) and enforcement of monopoly on works far beyond the life of the creator (backed by a form of extortion by the bookmakers of the time--this was a bigger issue pre-cheap-press).

      \n
    • Copyright does grant a limited (albeit extending) monopoly over the work copyrighted.       • \n
    • After that it goes from the public domain, irregardless of the existence of a copy in the library of the congress or not.
      • \n
    • If a work is out of market but still under copyright protection nobody can't touch it without negotiating the rights from the holders, be it the corporation, the creator or its estate.
      • \n
    • Creators have no way to prohibit something from going to public domain after the time passed (well, except if they're called Disney) or have any creative control over it after they're dead; the Amber stories written after Zelazny died are an example of this.
      • \n
    • Consumers, the community, or whatever you may want to call it, has no legal rights over a copyrighted work, other than the right to use it if they bought it (and resell, etc.) and the right to wait until it goes to the public domain.
      • \n
    \nWhat do I think:\n
      \n
    • Creators don't exist in a vacuum, we "steal" ideas from each other, be it in the same medium or different ones.
      • \n
    • Many (if not most or all) creators benefit from public domain material (e.g. illustrations in from old books). Lack of public domain material hurts us all, because it limits what we can do1. I stopped gaming in 2002 and just a few months ago I become involved again, right now I'm reading Polaris, IAWA, Shock, Mortal Coil, Agon, PTA, and DRYH, if any of these authors had decided to make these unavailable, I would never had the chance to buy those legally (or even read a copy, as here in Brazil there isn't many owners of indie games).

    What the Framers intended is only part of the picture. In the late 1800's, copyright law went through a pretty drastic reform, shifting from publisher protection to author protection.

    The Berne Convention admits a "moral" copyright for the author. That is, an author owns a set of rights to something as soon as he expresses it in a fixed form. Those rights give the creator protections over the integrity of the work. This is what makes it possible for Heart to get McCain and Palin to stop using "Barracuda" on the campaign trail. Heart doesn't want those people using their song; they feel it violates their work's integrity. If art is a form of free speech, then protecting how your art is used is how you keep your free speech from being subverted.

    The Berne Convention is a standard used internationally for copyright law. The U.S., in its crazy maverick way, only uses the parts of the Berne Convention that it likes, but it does admit certain moral copyrights. Where some countries grant copyright to an author as soon as she creates something, the U.S. provides exceptions such as "work for hire," in which the default assumption is that your employer owns what you made (without an explicit license necessary).

    Removing one's own art from the public view is not censorship. Governments censor. Authority and power censors. Making a choice not to say something is not censorship. In any case, you are certainly not protected by the Constitution against the "censorship" of some guy not selling his RPG anymore. Seriously now.

Publishers needed profit protection and got copyright laws in place to stop piracy. Authors then made almost nothing (not much different than today, but it's a lot better, especially in creator-owned arenas, eh?).

The money aspect was still there, but there was more. The Berne Convention solidified many of those ideas and a lot of nations signed into agreement to uphold a minimum set of creator's rights. The strictest moral copyright laws oversees make it impossible to give up one's ownership of art accidentally. No work-for-hire contract will steal it away.
