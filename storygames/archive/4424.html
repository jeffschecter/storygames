\n Over in this thread, Malcolm Sheppard posted, in response to my statement, "once we hit the dice/cards/tiddlywinks we're not in conflict anymore as people":\n
Posted By: Malcolm SheppardIt's too safe, at least from the standpoint of an immersive, emotionally invested play style. As a GM, part of my role is to create uncertainty as to whether or not I am emotionally invested in the desire to, for instance, kill another PC. This encourages the player to make a similar investment. The game mechanic takes on the subtext of being an expression of will. The results are about how that will matches up to the narrative environment.
\nAnd:\n
Posted By: Malcolm SheppardSo I stand by what I say. The instrumental approach *is* passive. You et thrown a bone or beg along approved channels within the system to construct a narrative simulacrum -- it strings words together to sound like a story but there is no real obligation to give it sentimental weight. Real emotions are dangerous, but if you seek that type of play you go in conscious of the risks. There are of course degrees of commitment as well.
\nAnd also:\n
Posted By: Malcolm SheppardAn instrumental approach means that we primarily define the event in terms of a game systems resolution. A narrative simulacrum has the signs associated with narrative but not the substance. There is no shared sentimental investment in the outcome that approaches the in-world investment of the protagonists. The dice roll says my character is unhappy but I do not taste any unhappiness. I may be disappointed at the negative game effects but I am neither immersed not sympathetic to the character.
\nAnd finally:\n
Posted By: Malcolm Sheppard"Narrate this and you get a cookie" is passive and possibly lazy. It merely looks otherwise because people are moving their jaws to get said cookies. The assumption -- the destructive, vision-limiting assumption -- is that cookie handling is the sole axis of reward, whether it's a cookie for picking good feats for a cookie for pulling the narrative levers for a cookie.

    \n So, to make sure I've got all this straight (feel free to check me on all this): Malcolm, you favor an "immersive, emotionally invested" play style (although you later said immersion isn't required). You consider this mode of play to depend on suspense created by ambiguity regarding the intensions of other participants, and the willingness to risk "real emotions." You consider the "instrumental," or "defined primarily in terms of resolution," approach to games to be a detriment to this style because it creates shallow stories with real emotional investment. This seems to primarily be because of a focus on "Narrate this and you get a cookie" type mechanics. Am I doing OK so far?

    About ambiguity of intentions and emotional risk: Are you saying that you want to intentionally leave group consensus regarding roleplaying (consensus on how to approach the activity, not consensus on "what happens") unsettled, so that you risk unpleasant emotions between the real, human people at the game table? Like when you say:\n
    Posted By: Malcolm SheppardPeople seem to be so deathly afraid of being unhappy, even for a moment, that moments of genuine pleasure are stale and forced by comparison.
    \nyou're talking about real unhappiness with your fellow players?

    I'm not saying that its ever possible to completely avoid frustration or bad feelings in a group, just that you can define a social contract to minimize it and provide a framework for dealing with it when it arises. Are you saying you prefer to leave that social contract undefined and at the mercy of covert power games or incompatible intentions?

    I hope I'm making a decent amount of sense. I'm finding your posts confusing because while a post like this one:\n
    Posted By: Malcolm SheppardNot long ago I was feeling uncomfortable with my D&D game because I like to play it as pure tactical wargaming. I was so committed to that vision of play that I say all kinds of problems with our campaign: railroading, inconsistent rulings and so on. But I really love my friends. I stuck with it, communicated and realized that I needed to test the waters with a new playstyle. It worked for me and in many ways, I learns that the dissatisfaction from delayed ratification made the result taste very sweet indeed. That was me plodding and stumbling my way to option #2.
    \nWould seem to contradict the impression that you oppose working out the social context for gaming with your fellow players, the very thing that you responded to with this line of argument was my and Shreyas' affirmation of this insight of Vincent Baker's: basically, when we engage the resolution mechanics, we're not in conflict as players, because we've already agreed to arbitrate the situation by the mechanics. This is pure social contract stuff. Just an acknowledgement that social contract exists, rules themselves are no substitute for an understanding of your group's contract, and making clear the distinction betweem fictional gameplay events and the real interaction between humans. If you seriously understand this and object to it, then I can only imagine that you favor an undefined contract where subtle manipulation, bitter misunderstanding, and hurt feelings are all possible and unchecked. Which confuses me a great deal.

    Now, while I'm waiting for the verdict to come back on those interpretations, let me try to tackle the critique of "instrumental" games. You're saying they're shallow and passive because players just show up and expect to get rewarded "cookies" for saying stuff. This seems to be a problem for you on two counts: because the cookies are awarded for slavish reproductions (and therefore pale shadows) of stories, and because they create an expectation for instant gratifications. Let's approach these separately.

    Regarding the first, I don't think there are many games that actually do this. I understand that the old "Pantheon and other Games" from Hogshead has a scoring system based on introducing appropriate elements, like in the haunted house scenario you get points based on narrating horror/mystery tropes. And I'm not very familiar, but I think Mutants & Masterminds has something like this in terms of handing out brownie points in exchange for accepting Superheroic plot complications. There are some games that give points for "whatever" based on group enjoyment: PTA of course, and Shadow of Yesterday, and Sorceror. But that's about it, in terms of my admittednly limited game experience. Heroquest is a game that uses brownie points, but they're awarded in a more or less trad XP-like fashion. Capes is a game that's frikkin' made of brownie points, but you have to fight for ;em, tooth and nail. ANd they're arranged in an elegant economy of advantage tradeoff that's as far from "narrate for cookies" as East is from West.

    In terms of instant gratification, that seems a gross simplification (though a popular one) of the spectrum of Story Games. Next to the "play in an evening" games like the Roach or My Life With Master, we've got Burning Wheel, and there are plenty of middle ground games like SToY and Sorceror, which support the short-term, but range into the mid-term. Sorceror is especially modular this way: you resolve a character's plot arc, take stock and decide if you want to keep going, and if so launch into a new plot arc. Sounds pretty elegant (I haven't yet played it). It keeps play going for as long as it's interesting, resolving when things feel right and neither quitting prematurely or dragging on interminably.

    As I said in the previous thread, the granularity of either narration OR mechanics is a red herring. I think you're assuming that play following the "instrumental" approach is of necessity less emotionally invested. That it's completely protected from the threat of creative failure, and so is passive and flat. I'm here to tell you that's not necessarily the case. I've experienced a ton of passive (for me, maddeningly passive) play from my buddies in traditional systems, and my most exciting and emotionally invested roleplaying experiences have been in indie systems with different players. That's purely anecdotal, but it's the data I've got to offer. Intentionality with regard to social contract has nothing to do with dull, lifeless play. I personally find that it frees me to really plunge into a game with abandon, to go for the narrative jugular.

    Your mileage may vary. I think you're off base in your correlation, though.

    Posted By: Malcolm Sheppard"Narrate this and you get a cookie" is passive and possibly lazy. It merely looks otherwise because people are moving their jaws to get said cookies. The assumption -- the destructive, vision-limiting assumption -- is that cookie handling is the sole axis of reward, whether it's a cookie for picking good feats for a cookie for pulling the narrative levers for a cookie.
    \nMalcolm is both right and wrong here. At its face, his conclusion here is correct. In many online discussions, there is an unspoken assumption that cookie-picking is the core of play. And, yes, cookie-picking doesn't lead to good play.

    But here's where he's wrong, or at least possesses a misperception---most games don't assume cookie-picking is the core of play. This all relates to the concept of the Fruitful Void, the idea that the real heart of play is entirely non-mechanical, and that the rules only serve to focus attention on that non-mechanical issue.

    Look at DitV. The heart of the game relates to judgment (and the toll that kind of power has on individuals over time). The mechanics are set up to give the PCs overwhelming power. At that point, players have to decide when judgment is called for, and when mercy is required. This is entirely non-mechanical.

    The assumption that cookie-picking is the center of play is often expressed by people who don't have much experience designing games. All of the successful indie publishers understands this point, at least intuitively. The issue with online discussion is that much of online discussion revolves around the discussion of individual mechanics. This often leads to misperception, as game designs are systems of mechanics. To fully appreciate game design you need to discuss how different groups of mechanics interact with one another, not how individual mechanics work.

    So his conclusion, by itself, is correct. But it's wrong to apply that conclusion to all indie games and designers. Malcolm, what's your response to that?
    _______

    On the issue of emotional investment, that's hard. I believe there's a skill to recognizing what sort of content will connect with people, both yourself and others. I doubt that sort of thing can ever be systematized. Certain guidelines can make it easier, but it can never be guaranteed. It's a matter of individual skill.

    \n Hmm not sure if this is on topic, but one thing we found in our limited PTA play (I've only done PTA 4 times) is a high danger of conflict that no-one cares about. By no-one cares, I mean both the "no one cares at all" and the "both outcomes are equally awesome, so I don't care which happen." One or the other were alarmingly common, maybe as much as 1 in 3. Include in cases where there's a conflict which only one or two people actually care about the outcome, and the others are just throwing in cards in order to have something to do and the frequency goes to almost 2 in 3 in my experience.

    The problem wasn't so much a case of cookie-seeking though, and more of a case of rules guidelines saying there ought to be about one conflict per scene. Sure, the rules allow for no-conflict scenes, but they are discourged. Sometimes the producer calls a conflict because he's "supposed to" even though noone really cares. I suspect this is similar to the case of someone invoking a conflict or doing narration merely because they are looking for a mechanical cookie.

    (The other problem for me in PTA is "safe negotiation" over stakes. If theres an event you really don't want to see happen, you can veto it during the stakes selection. You are explicitly directed to find outcomes which are acceptable to all participants no matter which way the encounter goes.)

    However, I don't see the phenomenon in my favorite hippie game, Polaris. In Polaris there are never conflicts that noone cares about (or they get resolved almost instantly by "and that was how it happened"), you can have scenes with no conflicts at all, and things aren't safe. I've gotten hit by statements while heart that induced a moment of panic "OMFG I can't let that happen, what am I going to do?" The brilliance is the panic only usually lasts a moment, since it gives me tools to fight back.

    Posted By: noclue

    Why would you want to have a chance for unacceptable outcomes? By definition that would be...unacceptable.
    \nWell the standard argument/example is chance of crappy character death. If there's a small chance of a character death which everyone agrees would be a crappy outcome, it adds a "charge" or "sense of urgency" to everything people do. Proper use of resources becomes important, etc. etc. I don't really find this argument personally very persuasive, but I can see how it might make a big difference for some, and I think the basic idea is tied to this thread in terms of some games becoming "too safe" and thereby losing an element of emotional energy.

    In other words, it solves the problem of people not caring about the outcome of conflicts. If Universally Crappy Outcome is on the table as a possible result of every conflict, then every conflict "matters." You gain more emotional investment, at the price of risk that the Crappy Outcome happens.

    However, my particular point in this case was not only is PTA safe, but its "obviously safe." A player can pretty easily veto anything that causes discomfort. About the only cost is it prolongs negotiation over stakes. I think Polaris solves this problem by not making safety obvious. If the mistaken or heart narrates something "unacceptable" it's going to take clever negotiation, sacrifice of previous narration, or the expenditure of limited resources to avoid the dreaded outcome. Usually there's a way out, but a) it can take a moment to figure out a solution and b) there's always a chance, no matter how small, that you're stuck. It also works better than the Universally Crappy Outcome option, because there's always at least one player who does want to see the thing happen.

    To be more concrete, let me give an example from my last Polaris game. I was heart, and the mistaken narrated a But Only If... that my character had through incompetence accidentally killed her favorite squire. This was "unacceptable" to me - my vision as heart of the protagonist was that she was very competent, and the prospect of her killing off her squire at this early point in her story just was too much to bear. So for a couple seconds I was scrambling in my mind to find a way to prevent this horrific outcome. I decided to burn a You Ask Far Too Much, rendering me more vulnerable to a horrible event later on, but for now I'd staved off the terrible outcome.

    \n Addendum:

    I think the word "conflict" carries a lot of bizarre baggage as well.

    Narrativist games are about CHOICE. That's the whole definition. That's what "addressing Premise" is in actual practice.

    I think many people, when they see the word "conflict," assume it means hitting someone over the head with a tire iron, or who "wins" an argument. But this isn't the case at all.

    In the Sorcerer & Sword game I ran at Gateway, one player made a Humanity roll because his character branded his face with the mark the Old Gods put on their human slaves thousands of years ago to tap the powers of Lore and bind a demon. Another Player had her warrior, born into a family of warriors, break her family's heirloom sword to get a more powerful, demonic sword so she could live up to her family's expectations. Those are cool character moments that would echo for the rest of a campaign involving those characters. They were about choice. They were also about conflict -- the internal conflict a character faced when choosing how to get what he or she wanted.

    I'm not sure if the word "conflict" is the right one anymore. I know how to expand the definition beyond "they fight" but I see a lot of people getting hung up on it.

    Revelation of Character is REALLY what we're talking about. If a guy draws a gun on someone else, why he does it, how he does it, and how far he's willing to go in the next 60 seconds is what the conflict is about.

    That's what a "conflict" in PtA is about, or a Humanity check in Sorcerer, or the Conflict and Trust mechanics in The Mountain Witch and so on.

    So, to tie this back into the original post, the purpose of conflict rules and mechanics is to arbitrate the result of a the character's revelation of who they. In Dogs, a man says, "Well, I'd draw a dagger on you because of what you just said," and what matters is that now we know that whatever that issue is is worthy of drawing a dagger for that character. In PtA the producer says, "The King has asked you to swallow your pride and do his bidding. I say your guy blows his stack in front of the servants the minute the King is gone," and soon we'll find out whether our guy blows his stack or not. In "The Mountain Witch" a ronin might be going down before a fantastical creature, and the other ronin could assist him -- or not -- and now we know more about where these desperate strangers stand in relationship to one another. In a game of Sorcerer, a man is given a choice of saving a child or sacrificing him to gain the power he needs to finally get the vengeance he has sought for four sessions of play -- we roll Humanity when he chooses to see if he has changed. Bonus dice in Sorcerer work the same way -- the strongest points of play are when a Player reveals something interesting about the character through choice -- those always get dice at my table.

    I don't see "cookies" at all. It's all about revelation of character producing moments that could then go ill or well for a character. The rules arbitrate the result of that revelation.

    \n Sometimes even the option of escalating to Conflict, and having that option not taken, can be really cool quasi-mechanical reinforcement of interesting character choices. The other day in my BW game, we had a conversation between three Elves. I as GM was playing an NPC Elf, who is a Relationship of one of the PC Elves, and both PC Elves were present. The three got into a pretty heated debate that was just about to turn into a Duel of Wits, but they were actually able to reach\xe2\u20ac\u201dthrough sheer, old-fashioned roleplaying\xe2\u20ac\u201dan equitable agreement on how to deal with the situation at hand. What's particularly interesting about a resolution like that in Burning Wheel is that any promises or agreements made in that scene are systematically non-binding. It's assholeish and discouraged to r\xc3\xa9n\xc3\xa8ge without reason, of course, but without a Duel of Wits, you're NOT formally bound to any stakes. So I could have had the NPC go back on his word, and either of my PCs could have done the same.

    But nothing in the game rules forced us to roll dice when we didn't want to. In essence, we had a diceless Conflict, that still lead to interesting results.
