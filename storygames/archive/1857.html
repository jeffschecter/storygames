The Most Important Thing In D&D...

Mcdaldno
November 2006 edited November 2006 in Story Games
...Is located on pages 147-162 of the Player's Handbook II.

It's a section on The Adventuring Group.

It talks about how to set up a party that has a cohesive background. It details different ways that a party could come together (they lived in a city that was destroyed, and are the only ragtags who are in a position to get vengeance, or they formed a mercenary charter, or...) and gives players advice on what to firm up BEFORE designing characters and what can be built up in play.

It talks about how characters can help each other - the wizard picking spells that will help the fighter, etc, etc.

It gives mechanics that can be used for improving the team - Teamwork Benefits are essentially like a party-wide feat. If a character has Concentration 8 and everyone has base will save bonus +2 then you can get the Steadfast Resolve teamwork benefit and characters "use their camraderie and shared experience" to stay strong together.

It includes advice for creating ways to work as a team.

.........Cohesive group? effective group dynamics? Stuff that incorporates teamwork, common skill focuses, and commonalities? Advice for working together, so that it's not just SOME GUYS, it's actually a REAL PARTY?

Seriously!

Thinking back to an old d&d campaign - cold north, low magic, orc hunters.
If we'd seen this, and said: "It's a charter group. Let's actually work towards getting the Expert Mountaineers and Foe Hunter Teamwork Benefits. Let's use these tactics for working together." Wow, the game might have worked so much better.

I... I just think it's a shame this stuff is in the back of a crappy book hardly anyone uses, instead of front n' center in PHB (one).

    Joe, given the importance you attach to this, would you create house rules in your own campaign to promote and enforce it? If so, what would they be? If not, why not?
    Here's what I'd do:
    *Group planning/tone setting/expectations discussion, wherein I tell them where the campaign is headed and find out what works and what doesn't.
    *We establish what the party is. A chartered mercenary group? a family? childhood friends? people brought together years ago, while fighting snakes on a plane?
    *Collaborative character generation. Ideas hashed out before 4d6 get rolled 6 times.
    *I'd kill off alignment because it can divide groups.
    *I'd ban certain races and classes that didn't fit in. Often, these classes/races would be: elves, halflings, druids, wizards and paladins. I'd allow classes from other books.
    *Group keys or group feats might be introduced, like the ones in hte PHB2. Not sure.

    Ultimately, the chapter in the PHB2 I was talking about.. it does what it sets out to do.

    Joe, I guess the basic idea I'm trying to get to here is that there's a school of game design that holds that when it comes to things that a game designer thinks are really important about a game -- the way you think this particular topic is important -- then those important things should be a part of the game rules proper.

    It looks like you're happy to leave it on the "advice to players" level, which is certainly not an unusual approach.

    In contrast, consider something like the character generation rules in Amber Diceless. I think that's an example of how the rules can really bring their full weight to bear on the players with respect to how they collaborate and generate the party.
