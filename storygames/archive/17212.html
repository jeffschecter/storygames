\n Last week we played a fun game of Monsterhearts but what stood out to me was how awesome Mo and Brand are (they were in town visiting NYC).

Specifically, their playcraft (a term Mo used over dinner).

By playcraft, I mean player skill. The kinds of things Graham Walmsley teaches in his book Play Unsafe: http://goo.gl/shRlk

Their player skills were incredible. I barely had to do much as a GM and these are all skills I try to bring to the table when I play. They:

- Listened to others
- Build on other people's ideas
- Offered ideas when people asked for them
- Didn't push their ideas when no one asked for them
- Seized the spotlight when it was appropriate
- Shined the spotlight on others
- Shared the spotlight
- Tied their characters to other protagonists
- Patiently helped other players with rules
- Remembered and reincorporated small details brought in by other players
- Advocated strong for their characters
- But sold for other players, even if it meant having a lower status
- Cared about cause and effect
- Yet when we hit a bump in the road, where the fiction went awry, they helped pave over the holes and got us back on track
- Sold their character's emotions
- Had strong goals, logical motivations, and made their intents clear
- Played transparently and honestly
- Were quiet and loud as needed
- Had weaknesses and vulnerabilities
- When another player needed space, they gave it to them
- Absorbed the rules and pushed them hard
- Looked for fictional loose ends and tied them together
- Built and broke patterns and escalated them
- Changed who their characters were based on the game's events
- Showed good pacing and helped the game move along
- Weren't selfish

I could go on!

I would play with Brand and Mo anytime.

    That is an excellent list.

    To summarize something that I've learned as a player is that if you are constantly thinking about how your character feels about the situation they're in, and again now, and again now, and again now, and expressing it with the tools available to you in the game (dialogue, or description, or skills, or expenditures, etc.) then that is sort of the big broad general umbrella of "good play".
    \nIf you're trying to boil down that awesome list of specific and inspiring examples down into a diamond sutra of hyperfocused mantra, I think "how does my character feel about this?" is going just a hair too far. I would expand it to 2 questions: "How does my character feel about this?" and "What are we trying to do here?".

    The answer to the second question will remain relatively constant. But there are 2 reasons to keep it in mind. One, because it is inclusive of the group and the sharing part of shared fiction creation - it leads to some of those group-oriented things on the list in a way that the more inner-focused first question can sometimes lead one to forget. And two, because the interplay between the two questions can lead the player to more easily embrace choices that are sometimes not the most obvious choice for the character, but add more effectively to the group fiction and still make plenty of sense for the character.

    In other words, just focusing on one's own character can, in the worst case, lead to turtling. Adding in a second notion that focuses on the "us" can reduce the likelihood of that being a problem.

    \n A theme running through some of these is 'playing the story' through playing the character rather than just 'playing the character' through the character. I'm interested in the tools that games provide the players to allow them to see the story rather than just see their own character. As well as your basic character goals (Fiasco Needs, Forsooth! Motivations) "This is how you can help or frustrate my character", you're seeing games come out with character targets as well (Oaths in Durance and Forsooth! for example). "This is where I'd like you to push my character." Alongside that you have games like Archipelago that provide a common language for players to provide quality control over one another.

    The other thing that caught my interest was status. People will bang on and on about status in theatre improv. I'm generalising horribly here, but generally higher status characters are more proactive and therefore more critical to the success of the overall story. It's natural for an actor, as they become more confident, to graduate to higher status roles so that they can do more to influence the success of the story and newer actors have a chance to learn without to be more reactive. But then you go and put a great actor in a low status character and (so long as they stay in low status rather than pulling a Jeeves) they're supporting the story rather than leading it, it can end us as quite special.

    \n I think "Playing the story" isn't what play craft is about. "The story" is the same way as the character just a limited perspective.

    I think playcraft is about playing the players.

    To me it been like at first you only see you character. The you widen you perspective a little bit and see the story and the character is part of the story. And then you lift the perspective and see that story is not the real thing, what is real it is the players and what they experiences.

    Playcraft is about, for me, to see the players. Not the just the character. Not just the story To rather then to try to create a good story or good character, look at the other players, try to get inside their skin and try to create a great experiences for them. Story and character are just tools. It is the players that is the real thing. They are what the game is about. The play is about playing for the players (yourself among them).
