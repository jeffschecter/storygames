\n Okay, I'm looking to make a puzzle that is played out with playing cards, during the course of a game. So this is not a directly competitive "You both play cards, high card wins" conflict-resolution scheme, but rather a "You've got a situation that starts off (a) random and (b) not fully known and you use a sequence of moves to try to transition it to a state where it is ordered and fully known."
\n
\nExample: Solitaire. It uses two techniques ("A card can go on top of the card one higher than it, of a different color", and "Cards may stack up on the aces one after the other, in the same suit") and makes a fairly nice puzzle.
\n
\nMy problem is: I'm stumped on other ways that cards can be structured relative to each other. I don't particularly want to remake solitaire. So, a little brainstorming would be a great help to me: How else can cards relate to each other? What kind of "moves" can be used to change a structure of cards? What kind of end-game ordering is interesting to shoot for?

    \n Totally off-the-cuff response: Orientation and pattern? Maybe the color of the prior card laid down determines the orientation of the current card being laid down: red = horizontal, black = vertical. Suit determines location: spades = above, hearts = right, diamonds = below, clubs = left. You shuffle and deal out 36 cards according to that scheme and end up with a crazy pattern of cards.

    Now I'm really spitballing: The goal is to get them (a) into a 6x6 grid or (b) all facing the same way or (c) both. Turning cards can require you to manipulate adjacent cards? With a cascading effect? Flipping cards does something else?

    \n Speaking of stacks:

    I was working on a Frankenstein card game where the playing area was a grid of four-card stacks. This formed the "graveyard" where your minions would move around to dig up body parts for your monster. In order to dig, you would move your minion onto a stack and reveal the top card. If it was a body part you wanted, your minion would carry it back to your lair. Your minion could only carry one body part at a time though, so if you didn't want the revealed card, you had to leave it behind. It was kind a memory game, with a slightly more ghoulish theme. :P

    \n Wow, yeah ... and that overlap is nice too. The cards fit together with a half-card offset, so that any one card is partially covered by two cards, and itself partially covers two others. It engenders inherent relationships between the cards: card 5 and card 6 in row one are more related than card 5 and card 7, because removing 5 and 6 uncovers a card in row two.
