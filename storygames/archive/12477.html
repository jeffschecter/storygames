\n Hi Story Games,

I don't have anything up for an ENnie this year so I feel it's safe to offer some, um, commentary.

ENnies are awarded based on fan votes logged at the ENworld website. Thus, it's essentially a popularity contest. There's no committee of judges, no reviews, no criticisms or analyses, just votes.

ENnies are awarded annually. Only games published in the previous calendar (maybe +/- a few months) are eligible.

Thus they seemingly measure the groundswell of popularity over the last year.

But look at the "Best Game" category this year:
Dragon Age:\u2008Set 1 (Green Ronin)
Hero 6th Edition (Hero Games)
Pathfinder (Paizo Publishing)
Shadowrun \u2013 20th Anniversary Ed. (Catalyst Game Labs)
Wild Talents 2nd Edition (Cubicle 7)

Only Dragon Age and Pathfinder* are actually new games. The other three are simply new editions. Shadowrun was published in 1989. Hero System was also first published in 1989. Wild Talents was published in 2006.

Allowing 20-year-old properties to participate in an annual award, puts newcomers at a huge disadvantage. Unless your game has a massive installed user base, you can't compete.

I like awards, but I think combining the popularity contest aspect with games that have been around for 20 or more years renders these awards irrelevant. We're not even looking at the most popular new game. We're looking at the most popular new edition. Have tabletop RPGs become so ossified that we just eagerly await retreads? NO! You're all well aware that there's a thriving community/industry/hobby all producing interesting material. The ENnnies structure sides with the big guys, the old guard, and lets them step in as they please to squat a fart on the efforts of other publishers.

I think some simple, common-sense rules for eligibility could render the awards incredibly relevant. But I doubt we'll see any change. It's fun to give Warhammer, Shadowrun and D&D awards every year! Okay, that's not fair. There have been years when Green Ronin has deservedly swept the awards. But they weren't up against some of the biggest audiences in the hobby during those years.

Not that you asked, but thems are my thoughts.

-L

*And yes, even though Pathfinder is a retread of 3.5, I think it qualifies as a new system.

      Posted By: jenskot What do you do if you don\u2019t get enough entries for a category?
      \nIf a category doesn't have enough submissions, that category is elminated and the submissions move to a similar category. Take miniatures for example: if there aren't enough miniature products submitted, there probably won't be a Best miniature Product category and what miniature products are submitted would likely get moved to Best Aid/Accessory. Something like that.\n
      Posted By: jenskot What negative impact, if any, does merging categories cause?
      \nThe main potential problem I could see is if products from the collapsed category don't have a good place to go. That could lead to a category having two sets of disparate products. Like with the above example, it would be harder to compare/contrast the qualities of two different GM screens than a GM screen and a miniature. They're both aids/accesories, but there serve two very different functions. It would just result in a complicating factor.\n
      Posted By: jenskot Why weren\u2019t more entries for a category submitted?
      \nI think you mean this is more of a rhetorical question. Each year I've followed the ENnies prior to becoming officially involved, the submissions seem to have shifted around from year-to-year.       Posted By: Luke WheelThis judge discussion makes me yearn for moderation.
      \nThat side-discussion did kinda take over the thread.

      Personally, I'm a little uncomfortable with this jump:

      Premise: "I think that some of the categories are broken, because games with longer histories have a far greater chance of winning*." (either legitimately through fan votes, or just a higher population of people IP-spamming the vote box).
      Premise, Cont A: "Changing some of the categories/adding a category or two would totally correct the problems I see with the awards."
      Premise, Cont B: "The votes are a popularity contest."

      (note, on the side: "*": It's not that "Game X 3RD Edition" has a better chance of winning against "Our" (Indie/Story-focused/Hippie/Hyperfocused/whatever) games, but against All New Games, regardless of where it came from)

      Response, Focusing on Premise Cont B: "Instead of requesting the awards get fixed, look at the judging process: The method in which judges are chosen is fair**. The method in which the judges choose the games is fair**. The popularity contest that ensues is largely based on the decisions of those judges, which again is a fair process.**"
      (note: "**": I believe that the judging process IS fair)

      Rescponse, Conclusion: Therefore, we should focus on staying within the (imo broken) system, spam-voting for the judges that are halfway in our pockets and will pick indie games!!

      That's just disturbing. (IMO)

      I'm all for making use of an irrevocably broken system, "playing the game" when there are no alternatives and no hope of changing the system. This is much the same way in which I address and play (successfully!) the NY stock market, for example.

      Andy's Conclusion: But these are the ennie awards, not say "a government" or "a company". Can we not presume that if we find whomever is responsible for the system, that we could perhaps suggest to them that next year they think about some categories that are fair to all new games: "Best New Game", "Best New Edition" (incidentally JD totally sold me on that idea, I think it's great)). Things like "Best New Game" and "Best New Edition" would bring ALL games, not just "indie" games, to a platform where they can more evenly compete with games that have been around in some form for 5, 10, 20 years. The "popularity contest" will continue, but I think more people (Luke included) would find it a "more fair" or at the very least "more interesting" popularity contest.

      For an enterprise as small and perceptibly changeable as the Ennie Awards, I am far more in favor of:
      1* Shouting loud enough to get heard on the issue of New Game vs New Edition, until "an internal vote" (or whatever the process the Ennies need to add/change awards) is called.
      than
      2* Stuffing the ballot box of judges who like My Life With Master, who will vote for "us"/vote for "our games", instead of looking to fix the actual problem we perceive.
      The former fixes the awards in general, and would prop up more awesome new games outside of this little community that we would never otherwise have heard of. The latter just props up the paragons within our little community for yet another Nomination.

      In the end, if "1" is an impossibility, I'd rather simply ignore the judging/voting process than engage in 2, which feels unclean and ultimately futile. I'd rather sit on my fucking hands than use them to stuff ballot boxes with "insiders".

      -Andy

      \n I think a core issue here is that the titles of the categories aren't very informative. People look at Best Rules, or Best Game and there's no way to tell from just those name exactly what the judges mean or how they differentiate between them. To get that you have to dig around on the ENnies page or search for threads like this one where the judges have a better ability to explain their decisions.

      Unfortunately, I don't know that there's an easy fix to this. You can't exactly go around calling each award by names like 'Best Individual Rule or System that Provides Consistent and Clear Direction for Play, and/or Adds an Innovative or New Approach to Play'. If they were all that long we'd have to sit through another hour of that damned ceremony.

      The judges have clear direction on what belongs in each category, rules to follow for what to select, but also have a lot of room available for personal interpretation. Perhaps the biggest failure isn't in the categories themselves, but in the difficulty for people on the outside to see what those guidelines are. In future years it also might make for a good series of questions to ask the candidates that pop up everywhere: What do you think Best [insert favorite category here] means, and how would you judge it?

      I don't see what Luke is saying here as sour grapes - Mouse Guard wasn't the first game he entered or had nominated, we nominated Burning Empires the first year I was a judge - I think it's a suggestion coming from someone who's trying to provide some context as to where he's coming from.

      The awards aren't perfect, but I think they try hard every year to fix the problems people have, and I think that a lot of what are seen as problems are really from a lack of understanding (that, of itself, IS a problem). If people don't complain then no one knows what needs to change. Similar complaints have lead to the creation and rearranging of categories a lot in the last few years, and asking for what you want is really the only good way to get it.
