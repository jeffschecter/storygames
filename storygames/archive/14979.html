\n Okay, so I have some friends that have expressed interest in me running an RPG for them sometime. Awesome. They've also really gotten into Game of Thrones recently and expressed interest in playing in Westeros. Extra awesome. Here's the situation, though:

I am perfectly willing to hack Westeros into an appropriate system, but does anyone have any ideas as to what that might be? Something relatively easy but with a set of rules that encourages Machiavellian interplay?

    \n I've been wanting to do something similar and tried running the Song of Ice and Fire rpg a while back but we dropped it because the rules were so annoying even after I hacked them. So, why I don't have a direct answer I have put some thought into it.

    If you like parts of Song of Ice and Fire rpg and Fate one option would be to use Wheel of Fate which has a similar system of roll and keep d6s and add them together. You also get aspects. Skills are determined by the GM and can be tuned to the setting so you could borrow the essential skills from SoIaF rpg.
    http://evilhat.wikidot.com/wheel-of-fate

    If you like house creation but think it is too complex the group rules from Reign work really well and can basically be applied to just about any game. We used them for a D&D4 game.

    Houses of the Blooded probably comes closest to a SoIaF game. It would be fun to try and figure out which houses are associated with which traits or you could just leave that open. Houses probably lends itself best to Machiavellian roleplay.

    \n First of all, Sons of the Watch is a very PVP-scenario. I co-wrote it, and have run the 3 Musketeers version of it upwards of a half-dozen times, so trust me, I know what I'm talking about. Run it as a separate intro game if you want, but don't try basing a campaign around it.

    That said, BWG might be a good fit. Yes, yes, BW is all crunchy... but not really. Don't use the subsystems until you're ready. Plus, there's basically no magic in Westeros (the only characters who seem to have true Faith are Thoros and Melisandre), so there's even less complexity.

    Also, Fight! and Range and Cover are really only for major engagements. Everything else is a versus test or bloody versus. If you really stick to that, BW is not a very complex game.

    \n One of the best games I've played was a Westeros-set game with three people. It was fairly light on system-specific rules (although we did use the licensed game's character creation). I think what makes a game "feel" like Westeros is partly a fairly complex background story that the players get to unravel as they play and characters who are willing to be grimly pragmatic when the situation demands it.

    I'd suggest riffing off of Dogs' town creation for the game's history (possibly developing it further should the game be more than a one-shot affair). As a GM I think it's important to hit the right notes when it comes to the setting, to encourage the kind of uncomfortable compromises that the characters are forced to make. So that means keeping in mind the inherent injustice and accepted cruelty of the setting, as well as the power-hungry nature of most principal characters. Westeros is an ugly and - as others have noted - an ignoble place.

    I'd also suggest giving the characters one strong and driving goal, that they should strive to hit again and again. Although I'd shy away from hard-wiring it into the rules. Something like a reasonably long-term Kicker (from Sorcerer) might do the trick. (In our game it was the mysterious death of my character's favourite uncle, at the behest of her father. The other player had been named guardian of my 14-year-old character, so he was drawn into the bloody and dangerous mess somewhat against his will.)

    System-wise I'd actually recommend something traditional like WFRP (2nd), as its unpredictability and whiff-factor fits with the setting without it seeming forced.
