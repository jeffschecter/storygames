\n I ran a bunch of games at Dreamation this year, but the only game I got a chance to play was Kevin Allen's Trouble for Hire, which was a blast: a GMless game where players trade responsibility for playing the 70's action movie anti-hero protagonist as well as the villains, allies, bystanders, and background. There's even an "editor" role that lets you recap, reframe, jump-cut, and do other meta-level structural functions. It was really neat, and the underlying game-mechanical currency was very elegantly fine-tuned. I'm a fan of this game.

What all did the rest of you get up to?

    \n I ran a few games over the course of the con, and even got a chance to play a little.

    Friday morning: I administered Our Last Best Hope, first time playing the game. It was compelling, though I missed one of the important procedural steps in the text.
    Friday Afternoon: A game of Spark, which I was lucky enough to have Epidiah playing in. The game went perfectly and the economy was flowing smoothly.
    Friday Evening: I administered Shock, first time actually playing the game. I was happy to use a variant recommended by Joshua to suit the con format. The mixture of Faster than Light travel, Fragmentation and Colonialism led to some interesting scenes, though I didn't pull my own weight.
    Friday night: We didn't have enough people for 1001 nights, so I jumped into Bills excellent Dungeon World game. It ended in the wee hours of the morning, but it was very much fun.
    Saturday Morning: We didn't have enough people for Archipelago, so I played a game of Project NPT, followed by some setting creation for Spark.
    Saturday Afternoon: I got to run my first game of Dogs in the Vineyard, with one of the four players having never played an RPG before that point. The initiation scenes resonated, especially with the one character who got the trait "I shot my daddy dead in church". I will admit that it was a tad rough to use the resolution mechanic for the first time, but it worked out great.
    Saturday Evening: I administered a 9 player game of Microscope, using my little hack of the game. While we had some rather severe tonal whiplash (The fracturing of the god of life from mortal worship in one, Wiley Coyote in the other), it was a worthwhile experience overall.
    Saturday Night: I ran and oppressed during a game of Dog Eat Dog. We played it straight, and it was quite a rich, short experience.
    Sunday Morning: I facilitated a kickass game of Durance with Rob Donoghue participating. We got a disturbing number of pairs, and a tripple on our very first roll.

    So yeah, good times overall. That's not even counting the socializing going on.

    What's the quick take-away from your experience? Would you do it again, and what would you do differently, if anything, to make it even better?
    \nOoh, man, that's a lot to answer there. Quick, huh? Let's see how I do.

    We were able to deliver on the goal that we set for ourselves: create a complete story that reacts to the players choices organically and nurtures character development. In the course of accomplishing those goals I discovered a whole new set of goals that I have for development.

    As for what I would do differently, I would love to have an extra GM for every session whose sole job is to handle administrative tasks. Run character sheets around, answer questions about rules, and coordinate stuff between GMs.

    We worked hard to make the different tables actions feel like they were part of the greater web of the event, and I feel like we could have done better there. I don't know how to conquer that yet, but we'll get there.

    And the bane of most LARPs: pacing. Man, pacing was a giant pain in the ass. Some tables would complete their goals before others, and it was hard to find ways to keep those players engaged without resorting to filler material. We need to work on techniques to get that to work better.

    I would also limit player choice a little more when it comes to character selection. I made the insane decision to allow any official MWP datafile be used, which meant that we had some players struggle with trying to fit their favorite character into a cosmic battle. While I grew up reading Spider-man fight Thanos on the regular, a lot of our players had a hard time squaring that circle in their heads.

    I would definitely do this again. Hell, we're already planning on doing it again. (Really, really early stages of planning, but planning) I'd love to have the same guys along, if they're all willing. Mark, Frank and Tim were all heroes and really drove the game into amazing directions.
    \n

    I also got to run a game of Eternity, my soon-to-be-finished (I swear!) GM-less, long-form epic story game with a few awesome playtesters. We had a great time, and they came up with one of the most beautiful scenes that I've seen since writing the first draft of the game two years ago. I wish we could have played a few dozen sessions of that...

    \n The other KristaCon GM team, the Bulldogs! group, did actually have 4 GMs and only 3 tables, with the fourth person being a rotating "floor GM" \u2014 enormously useful for me to have help laying out maps, getting more index cards when needed, etc. In practice we kind of scrapped this towards the end and had either some co-GMing going on or took breaks and played\u2014this was also useful, as it gave us a player's-eye-view of how the campaign felt. Brennan, Brendan, and Michael McDowell were great team members and we are also already planning more goodness in the future\u2014I want to revise this campaign and do it again, and also do something similar with Mouse Guard.

    Aside from GMing Bulldogs! I ran Monsterwarts, as noted above, and a new Burning Wheel one-shot. The BW game worked better than it should have considering how little work I put into it. We didn't touch any of the detailed subsystems, because I had six players, many of them completely new to Burning, but as usual the Hub shone through amazingly well. I will definitely run "The Three Trials of the Seven Pilgrims" again, with only slight revision.
