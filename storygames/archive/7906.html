\n I plan of having PTA available at a small house con on Saturday, the day after tomorrow. I have a few things I plan on doing if I get a group to play:

One, I plan on presenting my "resume". I'll run over the games I've produced or starred in and go over their premises.

After that, I will have a few pitches in mind. For these pitches, I'll make sure to have plenty of the details that bog down a con game/one shot game already resolved (since gamers tend to geek out when doing a pitch in the games I've produced and have to be focused like lasers or cameras), and hopefully, with buy-in and last minute alterations, just need to "cast" my show.

Finally, if that doesn't work out (which I'm fine with), I'm going to have everyone take out note cards and answer with their favorite show in a genre I mention. Of course, I'll take genre requests, too. I'll make sure that everyone is familiar with the shows mentioned and don't despise them, and then I'll take two or three and make a show out of it, e.g. True Blood meets Battlestar Galactica or Dexter and Burn Notice meets Heroes.

How do you or have you handled short time prep for PTA? Success stories welcome, failure stories with morals (like Aesop) are welcome.

    \n Limit your pitch to 1/4 or less of your play time. If you're going to get anywhere you need to be playing not pitching.

    I usually start with a quick round of what genres do we all like, then a idea of tone/grit for yet to be decided setting. Then I mash those two ideas together and start pitches.

    Obviously avoid any setting that requires alot of backstory (I've been bitten by this with scifi, as folks love to make up details about the setting that rarely end up in play).

    I generally trot out a few pitches that have worked in the past (maybe 2 or 3), but I always generalize them enough that the players can take ownership.

    Supervillian re-hab
    Interstellar Cryoship waking up
    Fairytale cop drama

    Other than that it really amounts to trying to read the table and keep people on track.

    Jeff

    \n Or you could do it 'Morningstar style' and simply enforce a setting from the top. This works remarkably well, even if it makes the game a little more traditional in structure.

    On the other side of JD Corley's suggestion, only allow them to add positive input to the pitch, no negative input. You will be required to make sense of the input and find stuff that connects it all together.

    Don't play with more than you are comfortable with. 4 or 5 is my limit.

    \n Yeah, I think if I were to run a one-shot PTA game I'd come with a general setting and go from there. The nice thing about this is that you can provide cool and useful materials - a map, a list of names, a list of locations or whatever, stuff to spur creativity and give a sense of place. And it's fast. Like:

    "OK, we're playing sentient helper robots on Mars after all the settlers have mysteriously vanished. Here's a map of Mars; which of these settlements were you in? What was it like and how were you treated? What sort of help did each of you provide?"

    So you immediately get over the initial hurdle of generating a setting by consensus starting with nothing. There's still tons of room for players to make it their own, but they are starting from something. Plus you are guaranteed not to end up with some ass mish-mash you are uninterested in as Producer.
