    A GM Screen can be nice, depending on the game and your group's preferences.

    \n

    Some generic things that would be nice:

    \n

    dimensions: wide enough to have plenty of space, but not so wide that you can't reach across the table. At its widest, it shouldn't be more than about two arm lengths. Length with that width, should be enough to give each player about 2-3 feet of space for their playing aids.

    \n

    A raised lip would be good to help keep dice on the table.

    \n

    Inset cupholders, preferably versatile enough to hold a soda can or a Big Gulp. I doubt there are many gamers who haven't had a beverage ruin play aids or books.

    \n

    A plexiglass top for putting playmats and other aids underneath. Alternately, something like a nagahyde tabletop, that can be written on and erased from with wet-erase markers.

    \n

    If it's possible to do all the cool stuff, and still have the table be easy to assemble and disassemble, that'd be awesome.

    \n For one, check out poker table designs--similar needs. In particular, consider breaking away from the four-sided or rectilinear model. Most games benefit from a table design that allows everyone to see each other equally easily, so look at octagonal or hexagonal designs rather than long rectangles (assuming you want to be able to play with 6 to 8 people). Or heck (i.e. "duh!") a round table.

    In general, I'd like a gaming table with a "sub-level" of the table (or a lower-lip around the edge) for putting random crap that would otherwise clutter the table (books, pencils, etc that isn't in use). Basically a table top on the table top, with about a hand's-width of space between the two (or more). Cut circles into the upper table, and you've got drink holders that *should* prevent most spills.

    I don't like the die-rolling pit idea, because sometimes you need to move dice around, and I like sliding them (no question that the value's been changed, as there might be if one has to pick them up). I *do* think the slightly raised edge would be nice--better still, make it padded like the edges of some bars (perhaps not as generously, though--soft for forearms to cross, not elbows and forearms to rest on). And the plexiglass top is a cool idea, too--get some dry erase markers and you don't even NEED to put maps and such under it (but you still can).

    I'd avoid drawers and cabinets and such for the reason Jason mentions--it should be more versatile--and also because I play fairly regularly at a table with similar... and I'm going to fucking kick it to splinters if I knock my knee on the drawers again. It will also make the costs go WAY up, compared to just a "dual-top" design like I mention above, which has 90% of the same functionality, vis a vis avoiding table clutter... unless you play a game that requires four or five or more 9"x12" hard back books.

    \n[I doubt I would ever buy a specialized gaming table, but what the heck, here's my $0.02 anyway...]

    - I'm diggin' the idea of a round table, though I would make it so that either the legs could be folded/disassembled or so that the table could be split in two, to make it easier to move through doorways.

    - Drawers would be nice (deluxe model, maybe?), but I agree with David that you could get the same functionality out of a dual-level design (standard/economy model?). But with drawers, I would only build one level (like the Emissary), otherwise you risk people hitting their shins.

    - Here's what you do next---you make the top level a ring with a removable center piece. That way, a customer can buy multiple center pieces for different functions. The default could have a felt surface for rolling dice, but you could also sell a dry-erase piece, and maybe a clear piece (for placing maps or whatever underneath). I think this would be a win-win situation---the design would be more versatile for the user, and would probably lead to more sales revenue (no one's going to buy more than one table, but they might buy multiple center pieces).

    - So assuming the removable center piece idea, what you can do is design the center piece so that if you insert it one way, you get a recessed surface for gaming. But if you flip the center piece, you get a flush surface for eating or whatever. You could also sell a deeper center piece with a separate cover for those people who want to be able to leave all their gaming stuff as is between sessions.

    - For cup holders, you could make "deluxe" center pieces with cup holders on the edges of the gaming surface (that way, the cup holders disappear when you flip the center for eating/whatever).

    How's that? I think I covered most of what was suggested.

    [edit] Oh, and one more idea, which I'm not sure everyone would want---assuming a dual-level design, you could make the top "outer ring" maybe 8 inches wide and made of clear plexiglass or whatever. That way, players could place books or papers underneath and still read (and write?) on them. [/edit]

    \n Plexiglass is notoriously NOT scratch, scuff, or nick resistant. If I ever were to purchase (or more likely just build it myself) a gaming table I wouldn't buy one that had plexiglass as a feature. If I was going to use plexiglass to cover maps or draw upon or whatnot I'd go buy a thin and cheap piece that fit within the dimensions of the table. You're going to be replacing it too often to want a thick and expensive or specialty cut piece.
    \n
    \nI also want to second David Artman's idea for having most of the special feature in a couple removable leaves. Without the leaves the table could still be square or octagonal, with a drawer or such on either side, and then you add the leaves to make it more rectangular with the recessed surface or whatever other features. And the table still breaks down small enough to move rather easily.

    \n~Versatility (it can serve other functions than specialty gaming table)
    \n~Modularity (it has features that can be added on for specific functionality)
    \n~Round, ovoid, hexagonal or octogonal is preferred to rectangular
