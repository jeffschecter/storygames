    \n What two-player experiences (i.e., what games) have you played with other people who are not your wife? Also, were these other people experienced roleplayers or not?

    I find that two-player games take a slightly different mindset, depending on the game, than from group-oriented play. You have to get into a collaborative dialog with the other player much more than in other styles of play. If you don't establish that back-and-forth repertoire, then one player often ends up dominating play to the detriment of the other's experience.

    \n Two player games thrive on intensity and super focused play.

    Sorcerer or Perfect as a two player game is all about power dynamics and hard choices. It can get dark quickly.
    Breaking the Ice or Shooting the Moon as a two player game is all about nurturing a spark, the joyful chase, finding that elusive balance. It can get steamy quickly.
    Don't Rest Your Head or Syzygy as a two player game are both surreal and spooky, ranging from in-your-head psychological horror to phantasmagoric odyssey.

    Many of my most intense and awesome games have been two player. It's all about committing, hard, to an intense experience.

Back when I fancied myself a novelist I used to count the words I'd produced each day (a lot of writers do that, so I'm told) and it's that same feeling, "Man, we've still got 200 words to go, what are we going to write next?"
    \nI think maybe this is the problem. Are you trying too hard to make the story "good"? When I play S/lay w/ Me I just say whatever pops into my head based solely on the imagery at hand. The games are WEIRD but totally hypnotic.

    For example, I played a game in which I decided I wanted to see what happened when the Monster and the Lover were the same entity. The Hero was wandering around these vast underground caverns looking for a way out. I introduced the Lover as this kind of mate-devouring tribal shaman woman. And Sayler, playing the Hero, had his guy kill her current mate.

    What I did from that point forward was begin almost all of my goes like this, "You wander around the caves for about a week."

    After the first week he encountered the Lover among all her dead tribesmen. Without a mate she had been unable to feed or breed and so everyone had died. She had literally cried a river of tears and she tried to drown him in it.

    After the second week he found the Lover had turned into this almost spider like creepy crawly thing that tried to claw and bite him.

    After the third week he found the Lover a decayed corpse that collapsed as it crawled after him until finally her head popped off, rolled across the floor and landed at his feet where she spit acid on his face before finally dying.

    BUT! He used his Good Dice to "save the Lover from harm" and he incorporated into his go that he picked up the skull and brought it out into the sunlight of the exit he'd finally found. He then raised the skull, kissed it, and as he did so the Lover reformed and two left together.

    Afterwards, Sayler said, "That was disgusting.... in a totally sexy kind of way." And I said, "I'm pretty sure S/lay w/ Me is the game where you're totally convinced the story is the other guy's fault."

    Which brings me to my second point. I think S/lay w/ Me works best when you're more or less completely unconcerned with what the other player might think of your contribution. Just lose yourself in the fantasy and trust the other player will do the same. If you're both trying to either make the story go some where specific or trying to please each other in some way or trying not to step on each other's toes too much, it won't work.

    The sense of intimacy in these games comes from creative vulnerability. To be kind of crude about it just put your kink on the table and let them deal with it.

    That's my 2 cents.

    \n I've only played one 2-player RPG ever. It was about 20 years ago now and it was awesome. I ran the game, my mate Sid was the player.

    I've often wondered what made that game so intense and great to play for both of us, and the answer, I think was that the plot was so strong. The reason it was strong because it was lifted wholesale from a swords & sorcery fantasy novel I had just finished reading. I basically presented the plot of the novel, which involved a mystery to solve. The story was pretty directed (the PC was a loner who came across a caravan when travelling across a harsh desert - he had no choice but to travel with them for his own survival).

    So my tip for one way to make a fun 2-player game: Pick a novel or short story that the player hasn't read and lift the plot from it. Make sure it's a plot that has a mystery to solve and that the player is pretty much impelled to go along with it.

    You might as well pick a really simple rule system too. I'd favour The Pool.

    (In case you're interested the novel was Brak the Barbarian Versus the Mark of the Demons by John Jakes.)
