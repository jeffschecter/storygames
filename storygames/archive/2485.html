    My paranoia-fu is strictly 2nd edition, so a lot of the references may be dated. Having said that, here's some ideas.

    Have a couple of VIPs (High Programmers?) These are NPCs and are the only people who know the full rules of Fun Ball. They support different teams and hate each other.

    Each VIP has some high-ish (Green?) clearance guards. These can be PCs and if so they should have treasonous secret objectives (bump off the other VIP, bump off your own VIP, steal the rules of the game, etc)

    The teams themselves consist of PCs with orders to throw the game, eliminate rival players, or commit random mayhem.

    Have a couple of PCs who are not guards, but have an objective to "protect" one or other of the VIPs (maybe they're in the same society.) Bonus points for giving these guys rumours that implicate loyal guards in a murder plot.

    Have several "assassins" with orders to kill one or more of the VIPs, or star players on one or other team.

    At least two people should be trying to blow up the stadium. The bombs should look exactly like the Fun Ball.

    R&D want to test a new improved Fun Ball and have hired someone to switch their version for the real Fun Ball so it can be tested.

    Some people have to be in charge of crowd/stadium security. You can have lots of fun with this.

    The referee should be a PC. He knows none of the rules of Fun Ball. He is given a document with the rules, except most of the information is replaced with "THIS INFORMATION IS NOT AVAILABLE AT YOUR SECURITY CLEARANCE". Add some totally stupid rules added by some traitor. Remember that displaying any actual (even accidental) knowledge of the rules at a lower security clearance is treason

    Someone is a spy from another Alpha Complex (or some other plot device), and doesn't know the communists are fake.

    At least one of the "communist" team is a real communist. Another communist is in the crowd and has been given orders to meet up with his contact, but hasn't been told which one of the communists is real.

    The ticket vending bots (you do have NPC bots, right?) go berserk halfway during the match.

    Someone has hidden an important Maguffin inside the real Fun Ball.

    Have lots of secret socety recognition phrases and handshakes - but mix up the information so that about 2/3rds of them are incorrect and refer to other people's secret societies.

    Only if there is One True Ruleset that they're trying to discover.

    Like, if one High Programmer has rules that say "When a player drops the ball, that is a turnover, and play immediately stops. During turnover play, all touchbacks score double points," those rules don't work with each other.

    Likewise, if another High Programmer has rules that say "When a member of the defending team gains the ball, that is a turnover, and play continues until they are tackled or score a fulldown. Failing to set up for a new play immediately after a turnover is a procedural error, and the offensive team must submit a player to be executed as a penalty," those rules don't work with each other either.

    But there are two sets of rules that you can make by mix-and-matching (one where a turnovre stops play, and the next play must be immediately set up, one where a turnover shifts things into a different mode of play where touchbacks score double points). Neither of them will be completely pleasing to the High Programmers (you're not following all the rules) but they recognizably evolve in part from the rules the High Programmers had.

    Let's see. There are so many good ideas there that it's hardly worth me going through each of them and going "That's great". But they are.

    To answer specific things...

    Mike, there'll probably be twelve to fifteen players, so fairly small.

    Costumes: often people dress up specifically for LARPs. Having said that, I'm not a fan of costumes, so I'm always happy for people to just turn up. Commie jerseys or berets are a great idea. Or armbands, perhaps.

    System: it's not planned yet. But the interesting thing about LARP systems is how light they can be and still be workable. For example, there needn't be any conflict resolution system beyond GM fiat (which might be used once or twice during the game). Most of the system will likely consist of giving the characters objectives and perhaps powers (e.g. "Mutant Power Sensitivity: If another player uses their mutant power near you, you can tell what it is (ask the player)"; "Telepathy: You can send messages to other characters, without being overheard. To do this, whisper to the player."). There's a bloke called Iain who's interested in helping design the system.

    The idea of constructing the ruleset is nice (and workable). When I first thought of this idea, I'd thought of leaving the players completely in the lurch: they didn't know the rules and there was no way of finding out the rules, so they'd just have to be entertaining. I'd imagined them not actually playing a real game, but acting as though they were (doing silly dramatic leaps and dives; pretending to score incredible goals; and so on).

    If there actually is a ruleset - whether it's constructed or hidden from the players - then the players would actually have to play. It's definitely possible. I'm not sure.

    Do keep ideas coming.

    Graham
