A d20 Idea - Full conflict system.

Levi Kornelsen
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games
I hack d20 relatively often, for various reasons. There's a good twenty different full redesigns of the game on my hard drive, about seven of which are complete, and about five of which have seen actual play.

The reason *why* is simple - Instant audience, quick feedback, and the chance to inroduce new ideas to a really big audience.

I've been looking, like others here and elsewhere, at recent innovations like Spycraft, and making little mental jumps. One of those jumps is to convert d20 into a full-on conflict system. I've updated my Perfect 20 a few times, but I'd love to go even futher. So that's what I'm thinking about right now.

So. Picture this. Take d20, turn all the special features into feats. No classes. Bang all advancing traits (like saves) and turn them into skills. Make stats into the advancing features instead. Hack it down to size, stripping out everything complex. I've already done most of this work in various places, I'd just need to tinker-toy it together.

Conflict builds on the normal model. Roll to hit, roll to wound, difficulty to hit. Wound rolls like standard rolls, with the degree of success indicating the damage done. But no hit points - instead, damage is inflicted as a series of conditions (combat would be "lightly wounded", "Moderately wounded", "seriously wounded", and so on.)

Now, this is where it would go all story-game. You could plug any four skills into those ports, along with a series of conditions.

**So, a fight would be:**
To-Hit: *Warfare Skill*
To-Wound: *As Weapon*
Defense: *7+Reflex*
Toughness: *7+ Fortitude*
Condition chain: Health

**Sneaking into the wizard's tower to steal his toys would be:**
To-Hit: *Stealth Skill*
To-Wound: *Athletics Skill*
Defense: *7+Awareness*
Toughness: *7+ (As Location)*
Condition chain: Capture (in your case, you want to "capture" his toys).

...And so on.

Any thoughts?

    Okay, you want to sneak up. If there's no opposition, maybe you make a task roll or I say "great. done.". But let's assume that there is. There's a guard, he's gonna try to catch you.

    So, on your character sheet, there's bunch of little conflict boxes. You fill one in just like the bit with the wizard, writing in:
    To-Hit: *Stealth Skill*
    To-Wound: *Athletics Skill*
    Defense: *7+Awareness*
    Toughness: *7+ (As Location)*
    Condition chain: Capture (the guard wants to capture you)

    I, GM, do the same for the guard's side of the equation.
    To-Hit: *Awareness Skill*
    To-Wound: *Athletics Skill*
    Defense: *7+Awareness*
    Toughness: *7+ (As Location)*
    Condition chain: Capture (you're trying to take the thing he's guarding).

    I decide that the location favors neither of you, so the as location is an "average" number for both. You end up with:

    To-Hit: +7
    To-Wound: +10
    Defense: 14
    Toughness: 11
    Condition chain: Capture (get "hurt" enough and you're conrnered)

    We roll initiative. You go first, and attack, sneaking along. You roll to hit, succeed; you've snuck. How far? You roll to wound, succeed, and score a "Moderate" success. So I mark that off my Capture chain; you've made progress towards getting up there.

    My turn; I declare I'm using a feat, and will drop both my Defense and Toughness by three next round to get a three point to-wound bonus this turn. I describe this as patrolling aggressively. I roll to hit, and make it; I heard something or saw something. Then I roll to wound, and throw down a 20. with my bonuses, I beat you by a whole bunch; it's a "critical" wound - I've spotted you rounding a corner.

    The next round...

    ...And so on.

    Okay. Tactical choices are about deciding what's better for you right now - option A or option B. Move, and take the AoO? Stay put, and attack a foe that isn't your best target? Stuff like that.

    Conditions are useful to this because they're specific, discrete items. Which means that they can be referenced by feats - things like "Ignore a Light injury at the cost of X" and so on, creating new choices to make in place of the ones lost with the map.
