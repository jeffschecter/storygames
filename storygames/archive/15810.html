\n This is long.

Some of you may recognize me from a couple of years back when I posted regularly, while most probably won't. It doesn't really matter, though if your memory of my SG participation goes back that far you may recall a few posts I made that echo points I'm going to discuss now. I'd dig up the links (or try to), but I think the gist will get across here.

Anyway, I should start out by saying that I haven't played in a face-to-face roleplaying session since late 2000/early 2001. I played like a maniac in my teens and early 20s, often to the exclusion of other social interactions, so I have an extensive background in the hobby. My most recent roleplaying experience of any sort was the winter of 2010 during Snowmageddon, when Benhimself ran playtest sessions of Apocalypse World over IRC in the channel, #playnow.

I try to keep my toe in by checking out various and sundry new games as they appear and make a splash, but there's one thing I started noticing back during that Apocalypse World game: I don't really understand roleplaying games anymore.

People keep telling me how well-written the MC sections of Apocalypse World are and I believe them, especially since so many folks are playing and enjoying the game. Clearly something was done correctly. But I read them and I feel like I'm being exposed to some kind of alien text, or a heretical document that directly contravenes everything I know (or thought I knew) about roleplaying. I would come to Story Games during that period and read about things like "emergent gameplay" and couldn't wrap my brain around what that meant in practice.

When I started playing RPGs in the late '70s things were different. I'm not saying they were better, but there was a very specific dynamic in play: the DM/GM makes up a story or a locale and the players explore it. If there's a plot, the DM/GM has it all prepped out ahead of time, and while there may be room for improvisation to a certain extent, the path is well marked. Games were exemplified by tons of development before the first die is rolled. To put it another way: the GM is god and you are his playthings. Collaborative storytelling happened by accident, not by design.

That's really not how things work anymore. Well, maybe they do if you're still playing D&D in its various flavors, but when it comes to what people call "story games," which are what I'm talking about now, the whole paradigm has shifted. The GM is now a facilitator, not a dictator. Plot is organically developed through play. The players themselves bring much of the direction and drama to the table.

I recently read Play Unsafe, which had been recommended to me because of its "story game"-y approach to roleplaying. The takeaway I got from it was, "Don't do anything you ever did in a game played in the '80s. In fact, do the opposite, and all will be magically revealed unto you." So I didn't find it very useful, with apologies to the author.

The thing is, I really want to play these games. People are obviously having a good time doing things in this new way, but I'm stuck here with a kind of befuddled look on my face trying to comprehend even the bare essentials of this method. I'm so attached to the way things were done in my formative years that doing them any other way is a total mystery.

Bill White recently encouraged me to come to a gaming convention later this month and join in a playtest of his game, The New World. I'd love to, but I read his description of how gameplay develops in that game and I think, "I have no idea how to do that." I would be dead weight at the table, completely paralyzed by my inability to think about roleplaying in any other way besides how I thought of it in the '80s.

The obvious answer would be (to borrow from Apocalypse World): to do it, do it. However it's hard if you don't have people who are willing to handhold you through the process of completely relearning roleplaying. It's a total fun-kill for acclimated players, and I don't want to put any group through that. Plus a lot of you guys own cats, and I'm deathly allergic of cats and can't play at your houses, but that's another problem altogether.

So am I stuck? Is there some way for me to unlearn on my own and emerge, fully formed, into this new standard? Or am I condemned to be some group's fun-killer for however long it takes me to get my nu-roleplaying act together?

The title of this entry is a little misleading. I don't think I've outgrown roleplaying games. Rather, I think they may have outgrown me.

    \n When I play with people who are role-playing veterans but are new to story games (which sounds like what you're describing), I find they usually have an easier time adjusting to a game that is entirely unlike what they're used to, rather than something that looks like a traditional game on the surface but has wildly different expectations and norms.

    If it looks like a duck and walks like a duck, you're going to keep thinking it's a duck and all your duck-related reactions are going to kick in. If it looks like an early 19th century Ottoman, you may be able to approach it fresh and not think so much about all the time you've spent with ducks.

    I recommend something without even the appearance of a GM.

    \n It sounds to me like you're stuck, yeah. The world actually is like that, there is no guarantee that even relatively simple things would be possible. For you it might be trying to learn to play a certain game without having anybody to play it with in your immediate social circles; for some other people I know it's things like wanting to have a martial arts hobby but having no instructors in their town, or wanting to travel internationally but not having the time for it. I know here in Finland is currently considering abandoning his rpg hobby simply because he's moved to such an inconceivable hellhole of a town that nobody there seems to be willing to form a gaming group with him. There is no certainty that you, I, or anybody else in this world will find happiness in our endeavours.

    That being said, I've played a lot of story games with people who've never encountered anything like that, both roleplayers and non-roleplayers, and I can't say that I'd have felt the potential dead weight that difficult. You just need to play a game that can adapt to different players carrying more or less of the weight. This is a virtue in game design in general, there's no guarantee that all players will be equally imaginative, active and sharp at the table. Just tell your prospective group that you know that you'll be pretty passive for the first few sessions, but that you're willing to work on improving, so you should play a game that enables you to participate but doesn't require it for the game to function. Then just play, and ask your group to help out, perhaps, if they're better at it than you are.

    Furthermore, can't you be happy playing the types of games you know? Are they not fun for you? I myself am usually not tempted to play types of games where I don't have any natural affinity. For example, I'm quite happy with my hobby without participating in another larp in my life, more or less.

    Lastly, I understand that you hesitate to simply play, and would like to understand more about new types of games before trying them out in practice. I can tell you that my own traditional gaming experience fit very well with the Forge discourse in the early years of the last decade; I could understand the theory discussions and appreciate how the game designs from that era built upon various assumptions about roleplaying while discarding others. It seems that the majority of people can't understand those discourses at all, but for me learning to play "story games" was a natural process of reading theory, actual play musings and game texts. You've probably already tried this, but somebody who hasn't might try my method and simply read Sorcerer, Dust Devils, My Life with Master, InSpectres and whatever other games appeal to them alongside all Ron Edwards articles and any Forge forum threads that spark attention from 2003 to 2004. Definitely not a crash course for everybody, but for me it was an effortless route to understanding.

    \n Ah, also, I should recommend some games that are particularly well-suited to having possible dead-weight players. My experience is that games like Burning Wheel or Shadow of Yesterday or others with a vaguely traditional set-up are useful in this regard, as they have such a free framework that it's not difficult for the more passive players to voluntarily play companion characters to the real protagonists. As long as the group can build creative accord between the GM and one of the players, this type of game will run well and drag the other players along. The players who actually get their creative agenda on the table will be on the spot for the most of the time, while the more passive and uncertain players can observe, participate in secondary ways and look for a natural-feeling opportunity to come to the forefront themselves.

    Dogs in the Vineyard is like this as well, nothing much breaks even if one of the players is more passive and doesn't quite grog things, especially if he's willing to listen to cues from the others. On the other hand, games like Dust Devils, Polaris or Mountain Witch are not suitable for these types of situations: they apportion serious influence to the players and requires them to use it, it's going to reduce the enjoyment of the game if there are particularly clueless players participating.

    Of course, as Ben says, there's a danger of playing these games "wrong" by bringing in assumptions from superficially similar games. For this reason I'd suggest playing with an experienced GM who knows what he's doing with the particular toolset in question.

    \n Hey Sam --

    I feel you, and in some quarters of the internet (or the hobby) much is made of the distinction between traditional RPGs and "story-games." But I think that from a player perspective there's actually very little difference in what you are trying to do--or, at least, not big enough a difference to make a difference. Ultimately, as a player, your job is still to make your character try to do what you want, or perhaps make your character try to get what he or she wants. You take a chance, and you roll the dice. To me, it feels the same. Sure, the GM's role is a little different--more facilitative, less coercive, as you point out--and from my perspective that produces more satisfying outcomes at the player level, since the game responds to my vision of the character (maybe it validates that vision, or maybe it pushes back against it, but it still responds). But most games still work fine if, as a PC, you imagine what your character would want and try to get it.

    Here's actual play (AP) audio of a playtest I just ran of The New World with a group that plays mostly traditional games; we're in the middle of a D&D campaign right now that the DM is running on pretty traditional lines. Some people participate more, some a little less, but they figure it out and seem to be having a good time. And, once the game gets going, playing their characters is pretty much what they do.

    The meta-level stuff about setting up the situation that occurs in games like The New World doesn't sound that different to me than pre-game "what sort of campaign do we want this time?" discussion that takes place in some traditional groups. Granted, there may be more frequent shuttling back and forth from the character's-eye view to the meta-level, but it is just that: a shuttling. There are moments when what you are doing is pure role-playing: imagining the situation from the perspective of the character and describing an action or reaction that makes sense in that context.

    The difficult moments that you are afraid of can happen, but I don't think they should be a deal-breaker, like the "cat in the adage"--as Hamlet said self-reflectively--who let "I would" wait upon "I dare not." I remember a moment like that happening to my brother the first time he played With Great Power. But he just left the table for a few moments, got his mind right, and came back to play. Easy.

    To sum up: (1) not that different; (2) differences possible to manage; and (3) hippie gamers love everybody.

    \n Hi Sam! I'm not in your situation, but I've had analogous experiences when playing different kinds of story games. Here's some advice that would be really useful if you were me. I'm not sure how much of it will actually be helpful to you, but in some weird parallel universe where you are me, then this might actually be good advice.

    0.) There's nothing wrong with "traditional" GM/Player games! There are a gazillion of them out there. Play with friends. Have fun!

    1.) Oh man you are totally overthinking this. I bet your head is swimming with story game jargon about 'engaging the fiction' and 'shared narratives' and 'division of GM responsibilities'. Forget all that crap. All you have to do to play story games with friends is to be a little more willing to jump in and riff on what's going on. I bet that you used to do this all the time when you played RPGs, without even thinking about it. Like in D&D, when one player says, "My character ran away from his apprenticeship with an evil wizard", and you say, "Hey, my character's uncle was an evil wizard; I be they're the same guy!", and the other player says, "Yeah!". Boom! If you really have never done that - and that's fine, lots of people have fun playing RPGs without jumping in and riffing on stuff - then you can totally practice doing it in traditional games before moving on to story games.

    Here's the thing that totally blew my mind when I figured it out: In a traditional GM/Player game, you can jump in and riff on what the GM is saying. Like the GM says, "OK, you're in this restaurant in Tokyo," and you say, "Oh man I was actually in this really cool restaurant in Tokyo, it was in this little space under a train trestle, can it be like that?" and the GM says, "OK!", and you describe it. Depending on your play group, you can even skip the part where you ask for permission; just jump in and do it. (Or spend chits, or say the catch phrase, or whatever.) Again, you can practice doing this in traditional games.

    2.) It's OK to play it "wrong". You don't need to pore over the story game rulebook, trying to memorize every little detail, so that you will have a Correct and Accurate Story Game Experience at the table. A lot of those story game rules about fiddling with dice or cards or beads or whatever are just a pacing mechanic, so your game doesn't run for ten hours. As long as everyone is at the table for the same reason (create an engaging story), and is willing to jump in and riff on things, you're good to go. (I, personally, am really bad at this. I always obsess about the rules. Note to self: unclench, sit down, have fun.)

    3.) The goal of a story game is to create an engaging story. Well, duh. But it can be hard, when you're actually playing at the table, to stop thinking about how your character can succeed and/or survive, and start thinking about how you can make your character's story awesome. It's extra hard to remember to look around at the other players, and think up ways you can help make their character's stories awesome. But seriously, this gets easier with practice. It all comes back to jumping in and riffing on what's going on. Once you start thinking about how you can riff on things to make a better story, it gets easier to remember to do it in the future.

    4.) You can totally mix Primetime Adventures with traditional games. Every so often, when there's a break in the action & you want to get into some deeper character moments, slip a session of PTA into your traditional game. Have everyone make PTA versions of their characters, and do a session as though your game were a television drama. My friend did this with his Star Wars game, and it worked great. (Although it was hard to find a good Issue for my Jedi. "Maybe I'm Too Awesome" just didn't cut it. )

    5.) Just because a game is well-designed doesn't mean it's going to be fun for you to play. Like, I'm still blown away by how well designed 4th Edition D&D is. On paper, it's a great game. At the table, though, it just doesn't do what I want it to do. I bet the same is true of lots and lots of story games. When you read about how excited people are about a game, remember that a.) Maybe they're excited because it's fun for them to play, and b.) Maybe they're excited because they love how the game has been designed. Neither of those things are predictors of how you, personally, will like a game!

    6.) Seriously, play with friends. I mean, sure, go out and play with new gamers from time to time, but if they're not friend material, don't keep playing with them. Remember when we used to play with random groups of people, and we assumed everyone would want the same things out of the game as we would, and we thought we'd all get along because we were all gamers? Yeah no. Life's too short. Spend time with your friends.

    That's all I got. Hope you got something useful out of it!

    -Pjack

    I don't think that Apocalypse World is the perfect rules text (IMO Fiasco and Microscope are the current state of the art in text lucidity) and I don't think that playing over a low-bandwidth IRC connection is the optimal way to learn a game. It certainly wouldn't be for me. I need to have people model a game for me in real life before I can really get it.

    I've been playing story games like a maniac for the past few years and I very rarely learn a game from its text. If I'm interested in a game, I usually seek out someone who knows the game well already and learn from them. Story games, for me, are all about the things that are created collaboratively that none of the individuals at the table could have created alone. So they're impossible for me to understand in a vacuum.

    Think about how you learned to play RPGs back in the day: did you learn from reading the text in isolation or did you learn from other people? All that stuff about how the GM makes the world and the plot and the characters adhere to it -- that didn't arise strictly out of texts.

    So if I were in your shoes I'd follow your own advice: If you want to play these games, go and play them. You don't need to find a regular group. Go to a convention. Try lots of stuff. Explain that you're new at this to anyone you play with. I bet the vast majority will understand and help you along. There are lots of great game teachers out there who are eager to share their favorite game with you. When you're prompted to say something, go with the most obvious thing in your head. Don't worry about making beauty and art and novelty and originality; just contribute. Some of it won't be fun, but some of it might be!

    Also, I know it's very much easier said than done, but try to let go of the idea that you're a surefire funkiller. That's a self-fulfilling prophecy. It's a risk to step out of your comfort zone, but it's not a guaranteed disaster.

    Good luck!

    \n If you're just starting out with the whole "player-driven narrative" thing, an easy way to get going, no matter the game you're running, is to turn player questions back on the players. Example:
    \n
    \nPlayer: "What does the hotel room look like?"
    \nGM: "I don't know? What do you think it looks like?"
    \nPlayer: "Well, I suspect it's a double bed, and..."
    \n
    \nIt might seem like you're copping out as a GM, or like you're under-prepared, but from the player's side of things, it rarely looks that way. What the players get out of that is agency. They own that little piece of the story now, and every time you reference the hotel room, they get to interact with what they've made.
    \n
    \nAs far as being "that guy" at the game table, as Bill White said above, hippie story gamers love everybody. One of my greatest delights in running games is helping a new players get their feet wet in a new system, be it Pathfinder, or Fiasco. Worrying about how little you think you know doesn't do you or the game table any good. If you're too worried about it, you become "apology guy" who asks for minor forgiveness every time you have to ask for rules clarification. Relax. These games are meant to be fun, and if you're at our table, we'll give you all the help you need to get to the fun.

      Let me try an analogy:

      As a writer, you work from an outline. When you're writing your first sentence, you know how the story will end: this gives you some great ideas about what to write. "If it ends like so, then the first sentence should be like so."

      If you don't know where the story is going, how in the world do you know how to start it?

      Well, as a writer I'm sure you're familiar with the "other school" of fiction writer, writers who like the fiction to "emerge" naturally. Stephen King's excellent "On Writing" comes to mind, and many other well-known authors also like to write one step at a time, claiming they themselves are "surprised" by where their characters take them.

      Does that point of view make any sense to you at all? (King's book is really good, whether you like his fiction or not--and it's very short, too.)

      It's basically how these games work.

      Another related analogy might be some kind of writing exercise where you take turns with a group of people, each adding on to an existing story. The rules are: "We're writing a fantasy novel. We each take turns adding a paragraph, then pass it on to the next writer."

      How would you "play" this game? You can hopefully see that you can come to the table with an idea in mind, and craft an introduction based on that idea. But you also know very well that by the time the pen and paper comes your way again, you'll have to have discarded all those ideas and run with what's on the page.

      All the weird advice in Apocalypse World and Play Unsafe is guidelines for how to make that process easier for yourself. Because if you come to the table with an idea of *how things will go*, and then are forced to discard it because another player "spoiled" your vision, you're going to feel angry and frustrated. You need different tools!

      Writing from an outline, it sounds like, is your tool, for both play and writing. But you have to trust that there are other tools that are also functional.

      I'm probably gonna be the stick in the mud in this thread when I state my opinion on this: I don't know that the magic "just happens". You will fail a lot. It's okay to overthink it ... as long as all that overthinking isn't leading you into cul-de-sacs or paralyzing you.

      It seems, to me, like there are several flavors of new tech being explored. Some of it works very well by just doing it, some of it works well only after failing a lot.

      Economics tricks: This is where you've got games that mess with reward/punishment that has a mechanistic impact. Some of the games reward individual social behavior (fan mail), some of them reward good story-making (artha), some of them reward other unexpected stuff (like table teamwork, in Burning Empires). I think that if you can really focus on the economies built into these games and what behaviors are actually being addressed, you'll get a long ways toward figuring out the intent of the game. It's also super-easy to get misled, possibly via poor explanation or your own expectations, and try to shoehorn a game's economy into how you think it "should" work. I think economics tricks, I think Luke's games.

      (Re)Framing tricks: I think Vincent's games are really good at forcing the participants to frame up situations in certain ways via his mechanics. So like in Dogs it's all about escalating levels of force, because the whole game is strongly premised on "how much force are you willing to use to get what you want?" It's not about how good a shot your dude is, it's about how willing you, the player, are to introduce mortal violence into a scene. Or like AW: everything's about being cool, hot, smart, weird, etc. If you mapped IAWA's "with love/etc." stuff onto AW it'd be a much different game. So same thing, right? Figure out how the mechanics force the players to frame up situations and you're golden. Try to turn a fictional framing engine into a physics engine and you're looking at "situation" in entirely the wrong end of the telescope.

      Authority tricks: Games that decentralize or rearrange authority have IMO the most profound impact on how quickly the game experience vectors away from "tradition". Authority got hit hard by a lot of designers: Lehman, Newman and Morningstar all have different, excellent games that move or eliminate authority roles traditionally found in oldskool games. There are more subtle examples of this in other designs (fact establishment via -wise rolls in BW for example). The fact that your expected authority structures are upended can, all by its lonesome, be massively disruptive. Can be good, might be bad. I've had players react the MOST negatively to this. I think that authority tricks are where you largely run into "this is not an RPG" type feelings.

      Ritual tricks: This, to my mind, is sort of everything else. Lighting a candle, shaking hands, ritual phrases. It's all very soft and nonmechanical. I find it hard to evaluate, before actually doing it, what impact a ritual will have on play. I've had players react very poorly to this stuff, mostly because it feels weird or faggy or whatever. I think the point of a lot of these rituals is to get players out of their emotional comfort zones, so this reaction is to be expected among some folks. I've also had players really lap it up. Very case dependent. Proceed with caution. :-)

      There's probably a larger/different breakdown available but this is what pops into my head when I think about the subject of how storygame-style design differs. However you find you break this stuff down yourself, I guess what I'd say is most important is to really hit the difference *hard*, figure out how to leverage it, and play toward the designer's intent once you think you've figured it out. I have a beef with the designers who aim for "emergent behaviors" by hiding their design intent, but if/when it works it's a neat trick.

      For the record: I'm 42, I've been professionally involved in traditional roleplaying design in my past, I just had my first kid and I run my own business. NOT TOO OLD FOR THIS SHIT.

      It's been said repeatedly in this thread and at this point I won't argue the opposite, but the only real way for me to get "into" this type of game is just to play them with people as much as I can. I need to find those people, but at least the course is set.
      \nThe good news is, "emergent" story play is much, much easier than "emergent" story writing. Because when you're writing you have to kind fool yourself that your characters are acting on their own whims and leading the story. In a game, though, there's no illusion: the character really *are* doing unexpected things on their own, thanks to the players making those decisions for them.

      The way a lot of "story games" go is kind of like this... here's another analogy:

      Do you recognize that when you're playing an RPG, playing a character, you're improvising, making stuff up as you go along? You improvise your character's dialogue, your character's decisions. You improvise details about them: suddenly your character is going to a fancy dress ball, and you must decide, "Does my character like fancy dress balls?" You decide that, no, she doesn't, because she grew up hating the next door neighbours, who always attended fancy dress balls. You also improvise "plot", by making significant decisions ("I'm going to betray the Duke and murder his messenger").

      If that makes sense to you, can you imagine playing two characters this way in a story? What about three?

      GMing many "story games" is just like that: you have a small group of characters that you play, just like the players, while in a scene in the story. You make up what they do and why they do it, and how they do it.

      The other part of playing the game as a GM is "framing scenes": deciding where a scene takes place and who is there, then deciding when the scene is over (no wrong answers here, really). If the game gives you interesting characters with strong desires (like In a Wicked Age... or Dogs in the Vineyard), the game goes "by itself" after that, not through magical improvisation mastery, but by virtue of the good stuff that's already built into the fiction and the rules for resolving conflicts.

      For example, playing In a Wicked Age... is not unlike saying, "Hey, Bob: you've been planning to kill Jennifer, haven't you? And Jennifer, you want to seduce Bob, right? OK, the two of you are sitting in a bar at closing time, and everyone else has just left. Go!" Then you sit back and let them play it out. You can see how that works on its own, right? Well, the only difference is that sometimes you'll be playing one of your own characters in these scenes, too.

      I think the best way to learn is to play a game as a player, with a person who knows the game well and is enthusiastic about it. Then, either as you go along or afterwards, ask the GM how and why they're doing what they're doing. Have them explain it to you every step of the way, and it'll make sense: the bizarre rules and things in the books and on the forums take shape in the concrete reality of that particular game.

      Edit: It's not magic, it's just a different tool. Like how you write from an outline and Stephen King doesn't. But you could each learn to do it the other's way, if you cared to enough.

      If you'd like to get more insight into a particular set of tools, you can start a hypothetical game in a thread here on Story Games, have a forum member create a character for it, and then ask us to help you "prep" for the game. You'll get some insight into the process, and see where you're stumbling and lost versus where you find it easy and logical. I think a lot of people would enjoy a thread like that, actually!

      I believe that most "indie" games were designed by frustrated GMs who wanted to get away from plotting and outlines and wanted to give more weight to the players' decisions--wanted to be surprised more often by what happened in play, and to enjoy those surprises rather than being frustrated by them.

      Posted By: Sam!I do wonder what portion of my anxiety arises simply from the fact that I'm doing at 42 what I was doing at 22, minus the blind confidence of youth.
      \nTrufact: I would not have been in the right head space @ 22 to enjoy the benefits of this style of play. I've been committed to improving my craft since my very first game @ 11, but that improvement was along very traditional lines built entirely on personal experience, the wishes of my play groups, and my own half-baked ideas about the highest and best uses of the medium as I understood it.

      In 1996ish, I was hired by Precedence Publishing to create a new edition of their Immortal RPG. I built this GMless, card-driven, distributed-creative-authority thing and playtested it with them for several months. The whole time, like, I'm scared! And they're scared! The emotions surrounding playing this was were visceral and weird and just...very hard to figure out why we were all so freaked out this whole time. I was terrified of "letting go" of a central creative director. They were scared as players of having to build this, I don't know, cooperative creative superstructure that they'd never, ever even thought about before. Like every other publisher of the time, every rule of every game was built on the assumption of a strong GM who's just going to ignore and rewrite all the rules anyway. So bringing in this rules-driven, decentralized thing felt nothing like, well...it felt like nothing they could sell, ultimately. So we ended up scuttling that design and went back to ye olde trad trough. The actual 2E books ended up being a total fucking disaster. Worst design work of my life.

      My takeaway from that whole experience was that the emotional investment of everyone involved, even me as the creator, was so unshakeable that we couldn't figure out how to find the fun inside it. I had designed it largely as an ideological statement (some really old Usenet posts I had written about where the RPG hobby needed to move have circulated now for a couple decades; Graham was the last one to bring them up, a couple years ago) but, lacking a ready audience who'd already been sold on the benefits of the play, couldn't really keep it going. I feel like that same investment/fear continues in the minds of a lot of us old(er) pharts whose local audience -- their play groups -- still need to be sold.
