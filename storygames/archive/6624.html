    Posted By: Clyde L. Rhoer[1]Levi did you look at Disqus, or Intense Debate before using Tangler? If so what made you choose Tangler? I'm considering adding Intense Debate to my blog.
    \n[2]

    Slightly (but not in great depth). From what I could gather on my peek, neither of them "centralises" comments in the same way. They're both actually a little bit better as straight "comment box" setups, and have the weird potential functionality of going cross-blogging, but don't centralise the same way - and don't allow editing of comments in the same way.

    For myself, operating alone, the central tangler forum that goes with using the setup is a handy way to just check my comments instantly, all divided up nice and tidy, without clogging up a feed.

    In addition, let's say that you do install one of these things. If it's Tangler, you're welcome to operate out of the same "forum" I do... At which point, the comments forum becomes a semi-destination unto itself, and some very "meta" things could easily start happening. I think that potential is really, really cool.

    Posted By: Jonathan Walton Your buddy and mine, Eero Tuovinen, gives a pretty scathing critique of Keep on the Shadowfell. Clearly, some of his disappointment was related to his play group, and some of it could be fixed by some easy tweaks here and there, but, overall, some valuable concerns, I think.
    \n[2]

    I just read his AP report. I think it's a pretty fascinating example of a player with Simulationist preferences meeting Gamist rules that don't drift easily. For example, he says, "My theory is that it was because the encounter-based adventure structure did not allow for enough of vectors of influence from one scene to the next. What this means is that there simply was not enough continuity and long-term decision-making involved in the adventure for it to feel like anything more than separate sessions of skirmish."

    I'll bet 4e can do more of what he wants though, if he builds his own adventures. There's no reason he can't have the setting fidelity he prefers while engaging with the hardcore fighting mechanics. He can just prioritize setting and character and situation fidelity over Step On Up stuff and he'd be fine. There's not much support in the rules for doing that, but he has the experience to make it happen anyway. I see his group using lots of skill challenges and adding many house rules, but they'd have fun with it.

    His brother's comment below the post is especially telling: "It was not fun to try to run 'an old-school D&D adventure' for Eero, since he has such strong views how things should be run."

    Haha!

    \n[1] Take the time to read Rich Forest's comments.

    -Rob D.

    [2]

    Rich describes exactly the way we played in the long-lost days of AD&D 1st edition, and (by habit) the 2nd edition at first, until we gradually shifted to illusionism (this was caused by the lack of enough time to prep the game in the old style, and anyway the illusion stayed very similar to Rich's description until we dropped AD&D)

    The particular form of this "gamist" playing was "avoid combat, if you are not sure to win with the lowest expenses of resources". The very good players were the ones who could pass a room of gnolls talking, bluffing, threatening, or killing only the chief with a show of power to intimidate the others. Use of spells to avoid even the contact with the monster you killed became almost an art form. The people who did not do this but did fight every monster in every room were called "munchkins" (and it was considered good GMing to kill them when they did it).

    I am surprised about the surprise. I always assumed everyone played AD&D like this at least until the 2nd editon. (but I learned by now that most of the thing people think "everybody do when playing D&D" are very local and group-specific instead, so I should not have been surprised, after all...)

    \n Kevin Kelly on a concept called Scenius, or Communal Genius.

    Kelly lists four factors that are important in nurturing scenius:\n
    1. Mutual appreciation -- Risky moves are applauded by the group, subtlety is appreciated, and friendly competition goads the shy. Scenius can be thought of as the best of peer pressure.
    2. Rapid exchange of tools and techniques -- As soon as something is invented, it is flaunted and then shared. Ideas flow quickly because they are flowing inside a common language and sensibility.
    3. Network effects of success -- When a record is broken, a hit happens, or breakthrough erupts, the success is claimed by the entire scene. This empowers the scene to further success.
    4. Local tolerance for the novelties -- The local "outside" does not push back too hard against the transgressions of the scene. The renegades and mavericks are protected by this buffer zone.
    \nI'm trying to decide if this applies more, less, or equally to the Indie Game Design Scene and just plain old Gaming At The Table.

    In either case, I'm unconvinced of the validity of #4 :P, but it is otherwise dead. on.

      \n
      \n Brennan "the Chairman" Taylor just released a PDF ashcan of his mythic Anasazi village-based, baby-raising-and-monster-fighting game, How We Came to Live Here. I helped with layout for it and it's really very cool. It combines some hippy elements with some attractive crunch, in the form of special skills and abilities you gain by working your way deeper into the secret societies and clans that make up a village. It's a crossover game in the style of Spirit of the Century, basically, but with a much more specific cultural context and flavor, in a way that reminds me of The Blossoms Are Falling a bit.
