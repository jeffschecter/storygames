Playing Card Randomizers

MikeRM
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Play Advice
Cards have come up a couple of times in the recent dice threads, and I've been debating starting a "cards" thread. So here it finally is.

Matt Wilson uses cards in Prime Time Adventures (2nd edition) - I believe because they're more familiar to non-gamers, easy to obtain and easy to handle.

Fred (Vaxalon) is using cards, totally appropriately given the importance of the Trumps in the source material, for his Amber game.

I'm using them in both my games-in-development, City of Masks and Errantry. Perhaps my next design challenge is to design a game that doesn't use playing cards.

Anyway, there are lots of ways you can use them, and I'll kick off with what I've done so far in the hope that others will chime in with implemented and unimplemented ideas.

In both games I separate the face cards (J, Q, K) from the pip cards (A-10). That was inspired by someone's passing mention on the Game Chef forums (if I recall correctly) that he was using a split pack like this to replace the various types of dice. He didn't explain in detail, but you can see how this would work:

* d4, either packet, by suit.
* d6, face cards, denomination and colour.
* d8, pip cards, by suit, 6-10 add 4.
* d10, pip cards, denomination.
* d12, face cards, each with a value.
* d20, pip cards, denomination and colour.

EDIT: Of course, you could also do d3/Fudge really easily by denomination of the face cards.

Anyway, in City of Masks I use the face cards (plus the Jokers) to represent masks, effectively pre-made sets of attributes which players play both to and against. I use the pip cards to provide a set of random numbers from -10 to +10 which players assign to their characters' relationships with other players and the several factions in the game at the time of character creation.

In Errantry, the cards are used to generate random encounters, with a bit more sophistication than the old D&D monster tables. When you're in the rotating slot that runs the encounter characters (NPCs), you draw three cards, one face card and two pip cards.

The face card represents the encounter character from then on, so you can re-encounter the same characters in future scenes.

The pip cards' denominations act as a percentile roll which you look up on a table to see what kind of character you've encountered. It's not quite the same as rolling 2d10, because when you draw the second card there's only a 3 in 39 chance instead of a 4 in 40 chance of it being the same denomination as the first one. But it's a randomizer, right? It doesn't have to be statistically exact.

The suits of the three cards, and the denomination of the face card, combine in various ways to generate everything else about the character on a Solar System (TSOY) framework. It's actually quite cunning, and I'm absurdly proud of it, though it remains to be seen how practical it is.

Vax, Matt, anyone else want to comment on what you're doing with cards?

    I'm using cards in a work-in-progress called Saga. There, I also separate pips and face cards. But the way it works is different:

    You establish a stake for the conflict. You draw cards. You discard some. Then the pips are added and decide the stakes winner. The face cards, however, are lined up highest to lowest and compared. Each trick won that way allows a player to change the opposite character, such as inflicting handicaps, establishing influence, or even completely changing character traits.

    That way, due to drawing more cards and discarding some, you can choose in every conflict whether it's more important to you to win the stakes or to have an effect on the other player's character.

When you play a trick in your trump suit you can use all suits but your weakness as your trump suit. When playing a trick in your weakness you may never trump and the other two sutis just work as for regular whist.

    I like cards as randomizers because of the tailorability of custom decks. I've found most mechanics that use prepackaged cards require more pre-work to setup a deck than dice, for about the same results in play.

    So one thing to consider is "What can cards do that dice can't?"
    "What can dice do that cards can't?" when you get down to designing for an effect, remember you make too much play go into any mechanic, and all play will be too or for that mechanic (I've had a friend flat out despise Dust Devils because it silenced the game while people messed with card hands.)
