    \n Oh, it's in the step-by-step Combat Round rules on p.18 (23). Under (4) for successes it says:

    "If you succeed on FA/AA before your opponent then you can also change range by one step at the end of your turn. Move the PC(s) on the range chart and describe closing or retreating."

    So, if you're a player you just read it as FA. If you're the GM then you read it as AA. Note that it specifically doesn't mention NFA as that has its own rule: if you succeed you can just move (whether you get killed doing so, or not, is determined by whether you beat the aliens roll, or not).

    Also note that you change range at the end of your turn, i.e. you kill at your current range and then move. (Some of the alien special abilities also allow the aliens to change range in other ways too, which will surprise the PCs the first time they're used).

    The cool thing, I hope, will be when you get to play. You'll see the choices at each stage of an encounter based on where you are, what you were trying to do and what you (and everyone else) rolled. Those encounter steps are your guide to what happens.

    \n Thanks for the questions!
    \n
    \n>Do Officers have access to ALL the resources below their rank?
    \nYes (well, they cascade down to Sergeant). Normally you will progress up (and down) through the ranks and gain (lose) items as you go. I reckon the APC and TPK bombs are most likely to get used up. It's then up to the PC to decide if they ask for a replacement item using a development roll between missions.
    \n
    \nIf someone jumps up several ranks via a field promotion then we'd judge as a group, then and there, whether some of the items were available for this mission. For instance, the now-dead Major got fried in his Drop Ship so it's gone, right? But if the old Major hadn't used it then the new Major gets the keys before the next encounter, right?
    \n
    \nAnother option is to let your character die (!) and get a replacement PC with a lower rank. They'll come from the mother ship with new stuff for sure.
    \n
    \n>But you can have only one of those "readied" at the start of an encounter...and to switch between them requires a NFA roll?
    \nA default weapon has to be a weapon from the weapons list (Energy Rifle through to Shotgun).
    \n
    \nThe "One Use Per Planet stuff" isn't in your hand, it's just stuff you call on when you need it and doesn't make you drop your weapon. So you don't need to "ready it" you just choose to use it when you want it.
    \nThe TPK and Paradise bombs are grenades, so you can switch to them for free, like grenades.
    \nThe vehicles just have "special effects":
    \n-the Drop Pods just do their thing. When you get out of the Drop Pod what's your default weapon?
    \n-When you're driving the APC you're using an NFA roll each round, but when you exit it what's your default weapon?
    \n-When you're piloting the Drop Ship you're using an NFA roll each round, but when you eject what's your default weapon?
    \n
    \nEveryone needs to know what the default _weapon_ your PC is carrying for when they get out of the vehicle (because that could be at any range and that might be crucial).
    \n
    \nChanging weapons, by the way, can be a pain in the ass, since (i) you might not be successful at it, (ii) you might get killed while trying to do it, and (iii) it doesn't remove any Threat Tokens.
    \n
    \n>When an alien ability prevents all kills, does that prevent the loss of the token that a trooper success would have removed, as well as not letting them count the kills?
    \nWell, there are three Special Abilities that do this kind of thing.
    \n
    \n"Armour" allows me to say "no kills this round and all the Threat Tokens stay there!". The attacks just fail to get through the Armour. I'd use it as soon as the dice hit the table and I see how successful PCs have been. If I use it after you've killed then that's my fault for not calling on it earlier - it's not retroactive.
    \n
    \n"Stop Technology" I'd use after seeing what the PCs had rolled too. If I use it after you've killed then that's just stupid by me as the GM.
    \n
    \n"Regeneration" I'd use at the end of the round after the PCs had cause a load of kills, or before they remove the final Threat Token and end the encounter.
    \n
    \nOr was there another ability this was specifically about? (It was Armour I think?)
    \n
    \n>Can this be retroactive (for the abilities that prevent all kills in a round, for example)?
    \nNo. If they've been killed they've been killed. Unless you're using "Regeneration" which specifically details what happens (you keep the kills) but the creatures reform and the Threat Tokens are returned to the encounter.
    \n
    \n>Just to verify: range has no effect on Troopers resisting alien attacks. You are just as likely to take damage no matter where you are, right?
    \nRight. Aliens cause kills at all ranges on a successful AA roll. Unless you're at Far Range and the ability "Reduce Visibility" is being used.
    \n
    \n>A few character sheet suggestions (if you happen to make revised versions for download):
    \n>- You might to add a tick box for combat drugs just like armor, since everyone can use them once per planet
    \nGreat idea!
    \n
    \n>- Would also be good to have a place to list the orders and special powers a particular character could use (force weakness, e-vac, orbital bombardment)
    \nGood idea. Ralph Mazza also suggested cards that could be printed out and put on the table or held in your hand. I will try and make those and have them as PDF downloads.
    \n
    \n>Love the presentation of the strength/weakness on the character sheet. Very logical.
    \nThanks!

    I think that when you look at the rule wordings in the book in the context of where you are in play and what is happening in the fiction/at the table they are actually clear. When you play out an encounter following the steps in the book you'll find them guiding you through it.

    The exact wording of the present rules was something I worked pretty hard at and got a lot of feedback on. And I take Paul Czege's advice to heart about not double-guessing what people might bring in themselves from outside games.

    So, I understand why people might have questions and I'm happy to answer them. I think we'll have questions about any book, and especially ones that have some really different ways of doing things. But I'm pretty happy with the words in the book as they are.

    \n After a little brainstorming here's a quick house rule for leveling:

    - after every mission give yourself an experience tick
    - when you have two ticks immediately trade them in to level
    - if you have the highest kills for a mission, gain a level but do not gain a tick

    Given a reasonable party size I think that comes out close to the progression of the current system, but without the chance of really lopsided results. You're rewarding top kills and encouraging competition but not leaving everyone else in the dust (or making random leveling too much of a distraction).

I'm a big believer that keeping the in game fiction and the meta game situation seperate can actualy be an obstacle to truly experiencing the character. That's why things like playing in your home town in Dirty Secrets is such a powerful tool...or having the Transgressions in Spione being real things done by the real people at the table. Its those little connections that tie you the player to the character that can really make the experience come alive.

    For me random leveling does just that. Life as a grunt in war time is shit. Its a bunch of tedious crap interspersed with moments of terror...for little pay, and (especially for the 3:16) no real purpose. Random leveling gives us just the tiniest taste of that injustice. Just the tiniest bit of "what the fuck that's bullshit" at the meta level carries down into how we portray our characters at the in-fiction level. Its a GOOD THING if your players feel a little cheated...vaguely unsatisfied...as if the system just isn't fair. YES...go with that. Use that. Fuel your game play with petty grudges against the player who got lucky enough to level 2 missions in a row even though in the fiction he did exactly jack shit.

    Don't shirk from that uncomfortable feeling...embrace it. And don't worry about it being "backwards" because its supposed to represent growing competence, yadda yadda yadda. That's just simulation carry over thinking. Its not supposed to be anything...except a totally arbitrary mechanic for dishing out rewards injustly. It doesn't need to make "in-fiction" sense...embrace its meta-nature and bring it back with your to the fiction.

    That's why I find it appealing. A house rule that seeks to make leveling more "fair" so that players are more satisfied I think really misses the boat. Your suggestion probably won't break anything mechanically...but I think it would diminish the game pretty severely.

      \n None of these are actually questions about the rules anymore. They're about trying to second guess when and where you'd use things in the fiction, right?

      Here's my take on it.

      If you ever get to go to Spain you should. If you go to Spain, and I did, then you should definitley visit Barcelona. I can say from experience that it's a great city. If you're in Barcelona you should absolutely head to see Gaudi's Sagrada Familia, his unfinished cathedral. I'm afraid of heights so I didn't go up a tower, but that's OK because you can see really cool stuff in the basement, and you should go down there. Because in the basement they have this construction that Gaudi made. It's a model of the cathedral made of string and bags of sand. Each one models the load on each pillar and tower. It's a marvellous thing, and shows how Gaudi intrictaley planned the cathedral with all the loads and strains carefully measures.

      Consider the rules in 3:16 as strings and bags of sand. You can cut a string or fuck about with a bag of sand. But on your own head be it. The strings and bags of sand I put in the cathedral are carefully placed and fit together exactly as they are.

      Briefly, and my final word in this thread.

      Levelling: consider it as the player that levels up. Those that don't level still have that level available in the future. It is absolutely not like only rolling a 1 for Hit Points.

      0 Kills: the GM needs to grow balls and stick up for the aliens. If the PCs cause 0 kills then no Threat Tokens get removed.

      Regeneration: You killed them so you get the kills but now they're back.

      E-vac: only once per planet, highest rank only, need to succeed on NFA. Kiss all those potential kills goodbye. And the GM gets Isolate as an alien power if he wants it for a planet.

      Do not plan ahead, just play the game.
