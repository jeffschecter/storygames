\nWhile not a terrible game by any means, I'm fairly profoundly disappointed by this new title. Part of my disappointment is the disappointment of high expectations. Being a big fan of Dragon Age: Origins I wanted this game to excite me as much as the PC game has, and with Chris Pramas as the designer I figured there was a pretty good chance of getting just that. Unfortunately...no. At best the mechanics of this game are "functional" frequently they're downright "meh" and at worst...some really poor gamer math. Now I suspect that much of the weakness in this design is the result of needing to put out a licensed product on a time limit, I don't know that for sure, but it seems a reasonable assumption. Now given that they've indicated that this game became the best seller at the Green Ronin store for the year in the very first day it was offered it seems like it was a good business decision (depending, of course, on how much that license cost) but I'm interested in whether the game is actually worth playing.
\n
\nDisappointing Thing #1: I was really hoping for a detailed setting guide to Thedas, something that would take the amazing wealth of little details uncoverable in the game from dialogs with characters and read in books and organize it into a text that I could refer to long after the game is no longer installed on my hard drive. Unfortunately that was not to be. The setting information is scanty, and in some cases contradicts the PC game. Worse IMO is that it falls prey to the usual RPG setting text error of declaring the setting as if it was Fact-From-On-High. The beauty of the Dragon Age Origins setting is that so much of it is presented from a variety of perspectives, many of which are contrary...leaving the player (much as with real world history) to piece together their own understanding of events. Not so the TTRPG where everything is laid out in far less interesting "this is how it happened" fashioned. Verdict: I already know more about the setting from playing the PC game then the RPG provided.
\n
\nDisappointing Thing #2: The game is designed to be incomplete. The box set currently available is "Set 1" the book refers to Sets 2, 3, and 4 being on the way (though it doesn't indicate what those are). Why is this disappointing rather than merely annoying? Well because the rules for playing Gray Wardens...not included in Set 1. Yup that's right. The whole point to the PC game...not included in the TTRPG. Further, while the characters can level all the way up to 20, much like the old D&D; box sets, only levels 1-5 are included in Set 1. I suspect they'll be doled out 5 levels per set requiring a ton of book hunting and flipping to find the level information you're looking for.
\n
\nDisappointing Thing #3: Not only can you not play a Gray Warden, you are specifically forbidden from playing nobility...even the lowest rung of nobility, the knight. Yep...the rules are quite clear...you must "earn the right" to play a nobleman or Gray Warden <shakes head sadly>. So get this. In the PC game there are 6 possible character backgrounds. 2 of them are nobles. And within the first couple hours of play you are recruited into the Gray Wardens and spend the rest of the game as a Warden. But not in the TTRPG. So any PC gamer new to TTRPGs who picks up the game thinking of maybe continuing the adventure of their favorite character...nope. I still can't fathom that design decision. If I were designing the game, the ability to play the continuing adventures of your PC character would be design goal #1. Oh, and two of the starting characters are Dwarves from Orzammar...and you can't play them either.
\n
\nDisappointing Thing #4: Some of the most boring dice mechanics of recent years. So here's the system. Roll 3d6 add them up. Then add your attribute (-2 to 4). Then, if you have an appropriate focus +2 (yeah...there are skills...but the skills don't have levels...its just a flat +2 for having it). Roll against a GM set target number. Here's the big twist...one of the d6 is a different color (the Dragon Die), and the number rolled on that die is the degree of success. Also, if you happen to roll doubles then the number on the Dragon Die gives you "stunt points" to do extra tricks with. Kinda cool, but that means all the fun stuff you might want to do (like mighty blows, or duel weapons strikes) you can only do if you randomly roll enough stunt points. That's it. Quick, easy, functional...but hardly state of the art.
\n
\nDisappointing Thing #5: Ok...so an average attribute is 1. A good starting attribute is 2. And if you're a warrior you'll probably have a focus in your preferred weapon group. So you're rolling 3d6+4. The typical opponent has a defense (target number) of 12-15. Which means at 1st level your dedicated warrior has an 83% chance of hitting the weaniest creature in the bestiary but only a 50% chance of hitting one of the tougher ones. A less fighty character (say with only 3d6+2) the chance is 62% to 26%. Thing is...as you level up...you really don't get much better. Since the skills have no levels, the only way to improve is increase your stats. By 5th level you can only improve a stat by 2 points, but more likely you'll spread that around more. After that...there is no improving your chance to hit that I saw. Typical damage is 2d6+2 while most monsters have 16-22 HPs so you need to hit 2-3 times to kill a creature. That's like 3-6 attacks to put an opponent down. Pretty bog standard make initiative roll, make to-hit roll, roll for damage stuff. The only excitement comes from maybe, once in a while, rolling enough random stunt points to do something else besides say "I attack...again"
\n
\nDisappointing Thing #6: Ranges, special moves, and the like are all given in yards. So no abstract, creative, or clever positioning mechanics going on here. More bog standard moving minis around on a map and measuring distances.
\n
\n
\n
\nConclusion: 1994 called, they want their game design back.

    \n Joe the problem with the Dragon Die setting the degree of success is that you wind up in a situation where the harder a task is the more likely you are to get a high degree of success...if you succeed at all, you'll succeed big. Only in easy tasks is it possible to get a mediocre level of success. Say your rolling 3d6+2 if the difficulty is 12 you can succeed with any number on the Dragon Die. If the difficulty if 18, then you can't ever succeed with anything less than a 4 on the Dragon Die. No scraping by by the skin of your teeth on the harder rolls makes the system rather dubious to me.
    \n
    \nSkills as yes or no toggles seemed nifty to me too, when I was reading the character creation rules. But when you do the math its really annoying. First, any fixed add on a summed die pool has completely different effects depending on where the difficulty is set. Say you're rolling 3d6 +2 with another +2 for having the skill focus. If the task is Hard (TN is 15) then the focus means you only have to roll an 11 instead of a 13 to succeed. AN 11 is twice as easy to roll than a 13 so that mere +2 just doubled your chance to succeed. If the task is only Challenging (TN is 13) then the focus means you only need a 9 instead of an 11. A 9 is only 50% easier to roll than an 11. If the task is Imposing (TN 19) then you'll need to roll a 15 instead of a 17 with is about 5x easier to do. So, on the surface you'd think...ok having a focus helps more with the harder tasks than the middle of the road tasks...that kind of seems sensible. But then you realize that that effect doesn't really kick in until the rolls are whifftastic anyway (even with the focus you only have a 10% chance of making an Imposing check). Second it means that even though the characters are expected to grind through 20 levels...they never really get actually better at anything. They'll get better at more different things...but once they buy a +2...that's it. Third...from a character perspective it means all trained swordsmen are equally good...there's no variety. There's no phenomenal master swordsman. Once you're trained you're the same as everyone else...that strikes me a profoundly uninteresting.
    \n
    \nReading the dice in multiple ways had a bunch of potential. Like say...Rolling Doubles (which I think is about 1 in 3 rolls if I did the math right) lets you immediate take an extra turn for that powerful combo move. Or rolling high on the Dragon Die gives the player the ability to name one additional benefit that happened from the roll, or things like that...that would have been cool. But the implementation as is, is a definite missed opportunity.

If this was to be a gateway RPG for the PC gamers than design goal numero Uno should have been to capture the experience of the PC game. As a gateway game...IMO Epic Fail. Only long time TTRPG gamer grognards can be forgiving of the flaws in this game because they're the flaws we've been used to forgiving for years. For gamers who already think TT Roleplay is lame...they're going to look at this game and say "yup...lame...like I thought".

PC gamers are used to playing the characters the game allows. If I were designing this game I would have gone back to the last Blight so as not to trample on the subsequent PC titles, and set the game up to showcase the campaign of the Gray Wardens to end the Blight...cuz that's what Dragon Age Origins is about.
    \n

It would not have been hard to create a system that works like that...instead of works like a tired old hack of D&D.;

    \n Looking at the character sheets and sample material, I wonder if Chris and Co. are partially trying to recreate an "old school" feel, kinda along the lines of the Ye Olde Skool Renaissance, but in the streamlined style that Green Ronin are famous for (in addition to their stated goal of making an intro RPG). Perhaps GR are in some ways hoping to recreate the intro roleplaying experiences that many of them remember from the boxed sets of the late 70s and early 80s. I fully agree with Ralph that, ideally, what you want is a tabletop game that replicates and then extends the experience of the licensed property (and stand-out products like Mouse Guard certainly do this). Honestly, I don't think I'd want to work on the adaptation of a video game I couldn't personally play through, but that may not have been the case here.

    However, Chris did post that "I would draw inspiration from the computer game, but ultimately the tabletop RPG would be its own animal." Personally, I agree with you that this is probably the wrong decision for a licensed game hoping to bring new players into tabletop, that it would be better to replicate the video game experience more closely, so, in the end... yeah.

    \n Just found verification of my Olde Skool theory:

    I designed a new game to capture the feel of the Dragon Age world, but I did so very much mindful of the history of tabletop RPGs. There is a tendency these days to look back on the games of the 70s and early 80s and pat ourselves on the back about how far we've come from such primitive beginnings. I felt like there were still important things we could learn from those games, lessons perhaps forgotten over the years to the detriment of the hobby. With Dragon Age I was trying to take inspiration from the old school while still creating a modern design. I guess you might call it a neo-retro approach.

    From the interview in the Escapist, which also indicates, that, yeah, Green Ronin didn't get to play the video game much before it was released, but talked with the designers and got to see the artwork, which I assume means illustrations mostly.

    One thing that was different about this particular project was that I was not working with finished source material. BioWare was designing its game while I was doing mine. When we did the Black Company adaptation, for example, all ten books were out and were a ready resource. I didn't have the finished Dragon Age: Origins to fire up until very late in the process and some of the background material changed over the course of the design.

    To its credit, the CONCEPT ART for the game, as well as the FMV animation previews (the party attacks a dragon in the snow) was TOTALLY BADASS. I have no idea what happened between that and the game, though: I think most of the vibrant-ness of the art must have washed out into a dull grey as it was translated into the game. Like how Yoshitaka Amano's character sketches for games like FFVI (FF4 in the US) turned into 8-bit sprites. But the Green Ronin game has a lot of that art, it seems, so that gives it the edge in my opinion.

    The music was cool, save for the emo rap-rock at the end credits (as soon as the credits rolled I was in the kitchen, washing dishes). I plan on selling my copy as soon as humanly possible.

    ===

    Anyway, I shared the above not to be a dick but to make a point: Some people here seem to LOVE the setting behind Dragon Age, but don't like the mechanics. While everyone's got their opinions and all, I wanted to demonstrate that, as Walker P points out above, innovation isn't the end-all: Because I personally found the world of Dragon Age (the setting) about as innovative as every homebrew setting you've ever designed for AD&D back in Junior High School, but with more writers and better art. So while I am reading about the lack of innovation in the mechanics and nodding silently along, I don't jump to the conclusion of "...therefore it's bad." It might not be my cuppa, and it doesn't seem to emulate the more interesting parts of the CRPG (Grey Wardens and Nobles and Dwarves out-of-the-box), I don't begrudge their stated goals of "old school but new again".

    Time will tell if it puts butts in chairs. Even if it brings in dozens/hundreds of gamers who like the console game, who end up never venturing outside of the confines of the Dragon Age RPG, I still say that counts for somethin'. We'll check back in a year or two and see.

    -Andy

      \n I think Valamir's example is a good one, and it points out the subtle pitfalls in writing for beginners. I was trying to put my finger on it, and now it's clear to me.

      \nOn one hand, the game is aimed at beginners. On the other, there are a ton of TTRPG assumptions built into it.       \n

      \n
      \nThing get even worse on the GM's end. There are almost zero guidelines for how to match monsters against the characters. The GM assigns XP based on difficulty *after* an adventure. There's no mechanical way to judge how tough a situation is until after it's over, and even then the mechanic is "The GM decides how hard it was."
      \n
      \nThe random encounter tables in old D&D were great for beginning GMs - roll a die, look up the result on a chart, and that's the critter in that dungeon room. All of that randomness pushes the work into a purely world building, plot crafting, space. Even a complete n00b can do that - it draws on general skills most folks have before they crack open an RPG.

Today TTRPGing has to compete with: World of Warcraft, XBox Live, DVDs, Netflix, OnDemand, streaming video, You Tube, Web 2.0 social apps, texting and chat, hand held gaming devices, 3D movies, and a whole host of entertainment fomats that didn't exist in 1978-1986 when I was getting into gaming.
      \n
      \nThe key measure IMO is "how long till the fun starts". What all of those things I listed above have in common is near instant gratification and accessibility. WoW is tremendously layered and nuanced and complex to master...yet you can also start playing almost immediately from the time the game is installed. Its not just "Complex" vs. "Simple". I think the above demographic today has no more trouble with mastering complexity than we did back in the day. Instead its "degree of fun per unit of time". How long till the fun starts.
      \n
      \nHaving to sit and scratch your head and try to guess what the GM is supposed to do to make the game work...its doable...with sufficient will its totally doable. But I think far far fewer people today are going to be willing to do that than were willing to do it back in the day, simply because there are so many other fun forms of entertainment that don't take so long to get working. Not that there were all that many people willing to do it back in the day as judged by how small this niche hobby has always been.

      \nNow, as a reminider, I'm not saying all RPGs need to be targeted towards beginners in this way. I'm merely pointing out that if if Dragon Age was meant to be, as some have suggested it was...by my standards...epic fail.
