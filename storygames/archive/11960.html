\n 3 of my old gaming groups played Shadowrun non stop. They\u2019ve since merged, split up, mixed back together, moved away or gotten too busy to game.
\n
\nAs adults they have Shadowrun like jobs... computer hacker security experts, martial art instructors, forensics, special ops, government intelligence agents, elf posers, and peyote smoking ghost dancing mages. Ok maybe not the last 2. But not for lack of trying.
\n
\nWe\u2019re getting back together in the next few months to run a few games. They want to play Shadowrun. But they dislike the Shadowrun system.
\n
\nSome of them dig elements of Mouse Guard so I\u2019ve hacked together a Frankenstein amalgamation of a stripped down Shadowrun 4, Mouse Guard, PTA, and Blackout.
\n
\nWe played a few times and it\u2019s pretty neat. The GM creates obstacles like a Producer in PTA but instead of spending budget they increase how much money the mission is worth (you can even use money tokens as hit points on the obstacles). And every 1 on a d6 the players roll causes Glitches the GM can spend for special effects regardless if they fail or succeed (they usually succeed but with fun complications). And a whole lot more.
\n
\nThe problem is, they are wicked smart! They out think most situations. Last game they purposely failed the mission, used the failure to get paid twice, double crossed their employers, and perfectly covered everything up. They even tricked their employer into giving them a 5 star review on ShadowTweetBook before the double cross. I was blown away by their adeptness. This is why they get paid big bucks in real life.
\n
\nThey had a blast so it\u2019s not a major problem. But they also expressed they don\u2019t want to get away with double crossing employers all the time.
\n
\nWe since made it so the GM can spend Glitches to leave evidence behind. Which has helped. Even though the players may describe everything perfectly, the unexpected can still occur. And it\u2019s fun figuring out how that happened or what they missed.
\n
\nThey also mentioned they are essentially playing as themselves rather than as their characters. Obviously their characters don\u2019t exist. But if they were in their characters positions, they wouldn\u2019t be a Shadowrunners. They would get a day job. So we need to bake into their characters reasons why they have to be Shadowrunners and why they can\u2019t just get a day job. And use those reasons to make them more fuckups or victims who have no choice but to Shadowrun.
\n
\nThey even discussed coming up with some sort of character detail that explains why they can\u2019t hold onto their money for long vs. saving it away. Even if they can think of ways to get out of the Shadowrun life, they want something that keeps them there.
\n
\nWe still have the problem of figuring out why you would bother Shadowrunning if you can simply hack people\u2019s bank accounts. Even if we made it incredibly hard to do so (although hard is relative as Shadowrunners are essentially hired for those exact skills) there is still the issue of mind control. You can use magic to get targets to transfer money into your credtsick. Which gets boring quickly. But these are heavy immersion players and want in fiction reasons to patch these loopholes.

    Some of their complaints were that legwork, money/lifestyle, and contacts didn\u2019t matter enough. Legwork was easily solved by using successful tests to generate bonus dice or tools for future rolls and to have a space on the character sheet to note these bonuses (one player said\u2026 if it\u2019s on the character sheet, it\u2019s important, which is why we made space for the other PCs\u2019 names).

And Money is partly XP since you need it for gear, cyberware, bioware, and spells.

    Having meaningful personal goals that make you more human could help. Maybe a reputation system. If your reputation goes down from acting insane, the only people who will hire you demand discount prices.

    What Runners Do Shadowrunners commit crimes, usually for money. When a corporation or other sponsor needs someone to do dirty work, they look to the shadows. As \u201cdeniable assets,\u201d runners make advantageous\u2014and expendable\u2014tools.

    Runners have contacts, who represent other potentially useful people they know. Some of these will be other underworld types, such as gang members or hit men. Others may be ordinary people, useful for information or for \u201cspecial arrangements\u201d\u2014 for example, the corporate secretary who lets you know when the wiz research scientist you\u2019re supposed to kidnap will be leaving the building. The more you rely on any particular contact\u2019s skills, information, and resources, the more you\u2019ll owe them in the end\u2014even between long-standing contacts, money and favors are usually necessary to grease the wheels. A player character\u2019s relationship with a contact need not be friendly. Sometimes, the people that can help you out the most are those you like the least. The most important contact for shadowrunners is the fixer. A fixer acts as a middleman and can usually help the runners find gear, other contacts or work\u2014all for a fee, of course. A corporation or other employer that needs shadowrunners sends someone to a fixer to ask for recommendations. If a team of runners has a good reputation and meets the job requirements, a meeting is arranged to discuss details and haggle over payment. Because such matters are highly sensitive, anonymity is par for the course, and employers of this type are known simply as Mr. Johnson.

    Mr. Johnson may not always be a corporate representative. The world of Shadowrun is rich and complex, with many people and groups who may need to hire runners to accomplish certain goals. A criminal syndicate may hire runners to strike at rivals, a mage may hire them to acquire certain rare materials for magic use, or Joe Neighbor may need to find the terrorists who kidnapped his wife. Regardless of the sponsor, if a job involves doing something dangerous and potentially illegal, it\u2019s a shadowrun. Just like Mr. Johnson is anonymous, so may be the sponsor\u2014if the price is right, most running teams don\u2019t care who\u2019s paying. Misdirection is common, and Johnsons will often try to drop false clues, leading \u2019runners to think that they\u2019re actually employed by someone else entirely. Shadowrunner teams may even take the initiative, doing jobs of their own accord. For example, a player character may hold a grudge against a certain megacorp, or dislike how a certain gang treats people in his neighborhood. Maybe he decides it\u2019s time to get his criminal record erased. Other jobs may be politically or socially motivated; the character or runner team may be members of or regularly work with far left or far right political groups. In the Sixth World, everyone has dirty work that needs doing.

    Runners accomplish their tasks by working the streets for information, calling in favors and markers from friends and contacts in the shadows. They take whatever action their job requires: surveillance, theft, breaking and entering, even murder.

    Runners do these things because they are survivors. Many of them grew up committing crimes to get by, or perhaps they obtained special training somewhere and want to put it to use. Some may have extended families to feed and no other source of income. Many of them prefer the freedom of the shadowlife, controlling their own destinies as opposed to being a wage slave in some drab business park kissing corporate ass all day. Others enjoy the thrill of running, thriving on its risks. Finally, some are inspired to run by a sense of social justice; they want to damage the powers-that-be however they can while providing for the underclass. These runners are known as \u2019hooders for their Robin Hood outlook.

    Why Crime?
    "Why yes, Big Brother is watching. However Big Brother has ADHD, so I'm going to sit here drinking my soykaf like any of a billion wage slaves are doing right now. And then Big Brother will get bored. And distracted. And then I'm going to do\u2026 anything I want."

    One of the core conceits of the Shadowrun game is that crime is possible, and that crime pays. Given the wealth of potential satellite oversight (just look at Google Earth in 2007 \u2013 imagine the law enforcement version in 2070), and the incredibly daunting task that is cracking through somewhat decent encryption, it is entirely reasonable to project a future where getting away with any crime at all requires some sort of elaborate social engineering to pull inside jobs that play off of secret limits of the anti-crime system. But this isn't Minority Report or any other Phildickian setup, this is Shadowrun. And in Shadowrun: bad people shoot other people right in the face for money and get away with it to do it again.

    So here are some quasi-plausible justifications for that:
    \n
    Throughout human history the creation of data has exceeded the capacity to store it. It starts in infancy where a babe simply doesn't remember every single thing she sees, and it continues on through the Age of Bronze where not every conversation or every play gets written down, and it continues today. It could very plausibly continue in the Shadowrun future and for the sake of playability we're assuming that it does. The cameras in the world exceed the number of people who could watch them, and they collectively generate more video footage every day than can be stored on all the world's storage media.

    And that is amongst the things that makes crime possible. When you go to the bathroom, a computer is measuring the mass of your deposit. When you flee a crime scene you're being watched by every store front you pass. But likely as not, none of that information will actually be saved anywhere. Some of it may be, but it quite likely isn't organized enough to actually identify you as the perpetrator (of the crime or the leavings). More importantly, information getting deleted isn't really news. If 18\xbd minutes are missing or overwritten by elven pornography, that's not weird.
    \n
    Your SIN, your driver's license, your home owner's insurance, your medical records, and really every other thing about you are stored electronically in the Matrix. It's like your credit report today. And like your credit report (or wikipedia), pretty much anyone can put stuff into the data stream at any time. You can challenge the data in court and maybe get it changed, but by and large stuff just accumulates in the data stream. Because of the fact that things aren't always correct and some people are total tools, the system is equipped with failsafes to try to weed out incorrect data. Data which is repeated many times in many places (or in important or "trustworthy" places) is considered to have a high veracity. Data which shows up only a few times or in very sketchy places is treated as having a lower veracity. If data conflicts, the system automatically chooses to believe higher veracity information at the expense of lower veracity information.

    ...This can be used by criminals (that's you). Because of the complete lack of a central authority of Truth\xa9, you can actually create truths that happen to suit you. If you treat something as true long and loudly enough, everyone else will treat it the same way. While archaic considerations like "statute of limitations" are out the window, the fact is that if you can fool the world into believing that you've always lived in Nag Kampuchea for a while, the world will continue to believe it pretty much indefinitely. The world of 2071 has an extremely short attention span and you actually can reinvent yourself with sufficient effort.
    \n
    Why Money?
    \u201cWhy rob a bank? That's where the money is.\u201d

    Perhaps the most extraordinary claim of all in the annals of hackerdom is the idea that these people get paid in electronic currency to break the laws of society and change electronic records. The extremity of this claim is quite apparent: people are breaking the rules of society to change data records in exchange for being gifted with data records that according to the rules of society entitle them to goods and services. Why not eliminate the middle man and just hack the money records directly? The fact that people in the Shadowrun universe don't is highly indicative that they can't. And the reason for this is primarily because the monetary records themselves are very far away.

    Debits and Credits: Debt Slavery and the Credit Spiral
    \u201cWork your fingers to the bone and what do you get? Bony fingers!\u201d

    An interesting thing that happens in Shadowrun is that despite the fact that the characters are getting a brand new identity several times a month in some cases, they still feel the need to work for a living. And that's actually somewhat odd when you think about it in terms of modern finances. See in 2008 \u201cyou\u201d can borrow fairly substantial sums of money at any time at merely ruinous interest with no collateral. The threat of destroying the credit rating of your identity is considered sufficient of a stick to make these short term loan sharking operations solvent. In 2008, identity fraud comes with a certain amount of cash money automatically. Simply by virtue of trading the credit rating in for cash advances on loans that one has no intention of repaying, someone who is already committing the crime of fraud on their identity can gain a steady income from sketchy banks and loan houses until one is caught for the first offense.

    And yet, in Shadowrun that manifestly doesn't happen, because the characters are perjuring their identities again and again and they are paying money for the privilege instead of vice versa. What does this mean? It means that the credit system in Shadowrun is somehow set up so that taking a loan with an identity that is going to cease to exist long before the first payment comes due is not a no brainer. In fact, it seems that taking a loan is itself so onerous that characters just are not doing it at all \u2013 even though the campaign only takes place over a short period of the character's life and thus can be looked at as being in the disposable ID situation even if the character is a SINner. See, the campaign is likely going to end in a year of the character's life, so anything she ever has to repay at any interest in 13 months is just flavor text in any \u201creal\u201d sense. And yet, players just don't take loans. They spend money that they have already earned rather than drawing on the reserves of a speculative future to gain monetary advantages during the actual game.

    The primary reason for this is probably linked directly to the reason that people call corp workers \u201cwage slaves.\u201d See, when you take a loan in 2071 you don't just get a pile of money that you are expected to pay back. You actually sign up for employment and the corp gives you an advance on your wages. Wage slaves literally are slaves. Or indentured servants. Or whatever. They work for nothing except food and board, occasionally having the number of required work hours they are required to put in go up. And that's why player characters don't usually take loans \u2013 they would actually have to show up for work in order to get the money. Which is really just like their current job as freelance mercenaries except less awesome.

      \n We played again and it was amazing in surprising ways.
      \n
      \nNot only was the game fun, but the rules themselves triggered a 3 hours game theory conversation after the game! And this is with people who hate game theory or taking games too seriously. It was bizarre and fascinating to watch.
      \n
      \n1 person, who just came back from Iraq, stood up and tried to physically illustrate how all the rules would work in real life and re-enacted scenes from the game!!! He took the game's gear list and re-wrote large parts of it from scratch. Another person started discussing how the rules would need to change to capture the feeling of their favorite books or movies which had very little to do with real life. Another person started talking about how we needed more abstraction and game resources that they can tactically manipulate like a board game.
      \n
      \nIt was insane! I barely spoke and I'm pretty sure I didn't inspire the conversation directly.
      \n
      \nOur friend who was trying to make the game realistic wrote down a list of variables we would need to make it work. And it was a very, very long list. Then after back and forth he came to the conclusion that what you really need to know is what someone wants, what's in their way, what's helping them, what's at risk, and how are they trying to get it.
      \n
      \nSomeone else was making a case that combat needs its own system and that's its more complicated than hacking or persuading someone. Someone else then referenced a few books on hacking and negotiations and concluded that its all about perception. The more you know about a subject, the more is involved. It depends who is playing if you're talking about realism. And it will never be realistic only perceived as such. And perception is relative.
      \n
      \nAnd this led them to the conclusion that the GM can't be the one to set difficulties.
      \n
      \nOne person who just came back from combat training preferred an abstract system of glitches rather than having rules for reloading or other weapon inefficiencies or obstructions. His point is that a lot of people spend too much time talking about issues with ammo and there are so many more issues to consider that could go wrong. It came back again to its situational, depends on perception, and the depends on the group as a whole.
      \n
      \nThe conversation swung from "we need a 1,000 page book of rules or a computer to crunch all the variables" to "even if you had that it would probably be wrong anyway because wrong is situational based on experience and expectations."
      \n
      \nIt was really interesting. I had a lot of fun just watching!

      To these particular friends, playing an RPG is like playing a video game. They want their real life skills to matter but they don't want to see too much of the code behind the game they play.       Another way to look at it is that meta-knowledge and meta-rules are different. You can use meta-knowledge to shape your decisions without slowing in fiction play with too many meta-rules.

      \n We hung out again today and playstormed aspects of the game in preparation for a future session.

      They want everything to be fast and to not break their suspension of disbelief. We've already made it so the GM doesn't track much or roll for anything. The players roll determines who successful they are, their opponent is, and what consequences emerge from that action.

      We talked more about setting difficulties by group consensus. We tried out a few things and this worked reasonably well...

      Assume all default difficulties are Hard. And what Hard means mechanically will depend on the number of players. The more players, the harder Hard is.

      GM sets obstacle. Players steps up to solve the obstacles and explains how.

      GM asks (at first, later this should become habit requiring no prompting) if everyone thinks they will likely succeed. If everyone else (and the GM) think the player will likely succeed, they hand them a die. Otherwise, they slide a Money token into the money pool. The more people that think you will succeed, the more dice you have, effectively lowering the difficulty (which starts Hard) without actually lowering the difficulty. If they don't think you will succeed, then the mission is harder than expected, and is worth more Money at the end if the players complete the mission.

      This worked really, really well. What was even more awesome, if someone is unclear on what a player is doing and how (and thus not sure to give them a die or increase the mission's Money), they are encouraged to ask the player to go into more detail. Which helped clarify the fiction and get everyone more invested. And the GM isn't even always the driving force!

      \n Paul, It's very simple and we want to keep aspects of it familiar to Shadowrun for the people involved. Here is what we playstormed (which is very different than what we played when I started this thread which was more Mouse Guard like).

      Roll a D6 die pool drawing dice from Abilities, Traits, and Linked Help Tests. 4,5,6 = Hit. 1,2,3 = Miss. 1 also = Glitch.

      The number of Hits you need depends on the number of players at the table. Base difficulty scales because the more people playing, the more they can affect the difficulty. Players (GM included) affect the difficulty by simply passing you an extra die if they think what you are describing is plausible and would likely succeed. Every person who doesn't pass you a die, increases the mission Money pool by 1.

      Any extra Hits beyond meeting the difficulty may be spent to increase the effect of the roll. There is a list of options on the character sheet. You may also spend Hits to cancel Glitches. So you can go all out and do crazy amounts of damage but have consequences or you act carefully and use some Hits to remove Glitches. But... time is of the essence... the longer the mission, the less Money it is worth (remove 1 Money from the Money pool after everyone takes a turn).

      Gear gives you free Hits but Cyberware is extra Glitchy making it so that any Glitches you roll are doubled.

      If you fail a roll, you can give the GM a number of additional Gltches = to the number of Hits you failed by or burn out the Gear you were using or in some cases spend Money or you can just fail.

      Also, after you are out of minor wounds and armor, you start taking major wounds which increases how many dice you roll BUT increases how many Glitches rolling a 1 generates. So it's a different kind of death spiral. You are still effective... in some ways more effective as you take damage... but you generate more consequences.

      The GM doesn't roll for Threats (NPCs). They simply spend the Glitches just rolled for effects. The GM can use Glitches to cause a piece of Gear to jam or you need to reload a gun or you are disarmed or take damage or a new Threat is spawned (with Hit Points equal to the number of Glitches spent) or a whole wonderful list of bad stuff including the player being capture or leaving evidence / loose ends behind (which subtracts Money from the mission if it isn't cleaned up).

      The GM may also introduce new Threats by increasing the mission's Money by the number of Hit Points of the new Threat. Glitches allow the GM to do this without making the mission worth more money.

      Threats can be anything from NPCs to chase scenes to bomb countdowns to new twists to bad reputation to evidence of you that can come back to haunt you.

      And then after missions there is a lot of cool stuff regarding healing, buying new gear, and lifestyles that feeds into how glitches and money affects the game.

      Posted By: Paul T.What is "a turn"?
      \nThe GM describes problems and then asks each player what they want to do. Or the players can take the lead and tell the GM what they are doing and then the GM can introduce problems.

      Players take turns asking questions, testing their abilities, helping other player's tests, or passing (waiting). After every player does something (takes a turn), then a round passes and 1 Money is removed from the mission's accumulating Money pool (represented by a stack of tokens, coins, chips).

      This is awesome in play for a few reasons. It stops players from hogging spotlight. If a player wants to keep taking turns, then that means everyone else has to let them by passing, which means they are losing Money. It also reminds the GM to make sure everyone has a chance to do something. And the pressure to take action as the Money pool shrinks and grows is very strong... it feels thrilling... and emulates the fictional feel we want. \n

      Step 1)Ask myself if the shredder is definitely present (say "yes"), definitely not present (say "no"), or could go either way (move on to next step).
      Step 2)Ask the players why they care. Hopefully this will also shed some light on whether and why they expect the shredder to be there. "You're looking for a shredder now because...?"
      Step 3)If necessary, ask the players what they expect. If possible, do so without revealing that I haven't determined an answer yet. "You're looking in the admin office? How many companies do you think keep their shredders in the admin office?"
      Step 4)Make a decision, based on many of the same principles you use. I either meet their expectations, or break them in an interesting way that communicates a reason for the break. If their plan for the shredder points toward fun play, given what I know of their goals and my adventure, I usually say "yes"; if not, I usually say "no". After Steps 2-3, I can usually execute Step 4 instantly, as I've been weighing those factors (plausible? expected? fun?) since the initial inquiry was posed.

      Great list!

      I feel like I often do my own steps 1-5 in your Step 1. It's how I decide if I should say yes or no..

Which for many of my other groups is totally cool. But for this group instantly kills their immersion. That's why if I say no, I will then ask them why they were asking, and then offer them an alternative that is less immersion breaking.       \nImmersion is personal to each individual (I believe), which is why discussing it can be treacherous. That said, I can speak (to some extent) for some of my friends who love immersion.

      Many people find Dogs breaks their immersion but not my friends. They felt Dogs forced them to immerse more!

      But Call of Cthulhu breaks their immersion (when the basic system is adhered to and modules are run as is). They feel nothing they say or do affects what happens when they roll. Further, their character's level of skill described fictionally doesn't match what actually happens in play. You 1. describe a bunch of stuff and 2. roll but the two aren\u2019t connected.

      Games like PTA break their immersion because the resolution system resolves too much for them. That said, they like PTA from the perspective of co-authoring something cool as if they were a group of writers breaking a script. I suspect that would make Matt happy!

      Pretty much anytime fictional actions and consequences (fictional or mechanical) are disconnected, their immersion is disrupted. Also, even if they are connected, but they can\u2019t follow it naturally in the fiction (scene resolution resolving too much too quick), they have issues.

      Timing is important. They don\u2019t like rolling for social conflicts in D&D 3E because you roll once, and the conversation is resolved in a few minutes of real time. It happens so fast, it\u2019s not clear why you or anyone were convinced of anything. But they love Dogs social conflicts. It\u2019s almost real time! And you decide if you are convinced most of the times by giving or refusing to escalate (the fact that conflicts aren\u2019t just talking or fighting but can go back and forth is also a big bonus in Dogs for them). D&D combat also breaks their immersion, you can lose 75% of your hit points and its no different than losing 1% moment to moment. Also combat may only take 10 minutes in the fiction but 1 hour in real time, that also takes everyone out of the game. \n
      What are the hardest parts of running this game for you?
      \nThat\u2019s a huge question. I don\u2019t think I can cover too much of it now but I will tie this in with the above. What 1 person finds immersive may break someone else\u2019s immersion. That\u2019s a tough spot for the GM.

      But there is a big ugly problem with immersion. Sometimes what breaks immersion isn\u2019t the game but the player\u2019s own selfishness.

      We\u2019ve discussed this as a group and people freely admit it. Sometimes you just imagine a very specific amazing moment for your character, and it may not be realistic, it may not be entertaining to anyone else, it may not be consistent with your own typical preferences, and it may not fit with what\u2019s currently happening\u2026 but you imagine your character successfully doing this cool thing\u2026 before its your turn or even before you rolled the dice\u2026 and when things don\u2019t match your premature expectations, its easy to have your immersion broken and be disappointed. But it\u2019s no one else\u2019s fault. You did it to yourself. It\u2019s essentially the evils of preplay.

      Usually when this happens people fess up to it and it\u2019s a non issue. But sometimes people start rationalizing the behavior and it can become dysfunctional. When someone starts complaining about immersion, I do my best to understand exactly what the problem is. And sometimes it\u2019s not something any of us can fix, but something the player has to fix themselves.

      \nRemember when I was like, "Ask questions, don't make assumptions," and Daniel was like, "That feels like asking permission, which I hate," and I was like, "No, really, it's worth it!"?

      This is why.

      1. \u201cIs there a glass on the bar?\u201d

      2. \u201cI look for a glass on the bar.\u201d

      3. \u201cI grab a glass from the bar.\u201d

      All of these happen.

      Asking \u201cis there a glass on the bar?\u201d is the player outside the fiction asking the GM a direct question. For many of my friends, requiring them to ask this is too meta and breaks their immersion. And it can feel like asking for permission, the subtext is \u201cI want there to be a glass on the bar, GM can there be a glass on the bar?\u201d

      Stating \u201cI look for a glass on the bar\u201d is still in the fiction. It\u2019s describing a character\u2019s direct action while making the player\u2019s intent transparent (there not just saying I look at the bar but looking for something specific). Generally this is immersion friendly. It still carries risk and a level of assumption. You probably wouldn\u2019t have asked if you didn\u2019t expect something to be on the bar and hoping it would be a glass. The player is looking to engage with you and the fiction, if they find nothing, it\u2019s a chance to provide an alternative so they don\u2019t disengage.

      Assuming \u201cI grab a glass from the bar\u201d is highly immersive, they don\u2019t have to ask, they are there in the moment\u2026 it\u2019s real time, it\u2019s thrilling, and holy shit\u2026 this is happening right now! But as much as it is super immersive, it risks collapsing immersion if there is indeed no glass on the bar.

      As a GM (for me), \u201cI grab a glass from the bar\u201d is a gift. If someone is that immersed, and we want to play an immersive game, I want to keep them in that moment. Even if there is no glass, now there is. It\u2019s also much less work for me. I don\u2019t have to describe every last detail of setting minutia. The player did it for me!

      I can see problems if the player goes into too much detail\u2026 \u201cI grab a lip stick stained corona bottle with fingerprints of my dad\u2019s ex-boss.\u201d It\u2019s easy and safe for everyone to assume there is a glass on a bar, it\u2019s mundane, common, but beyond that it risks conflicting with what everyone is imagining and every additional detail comes with subtext of further assumptions that risk more and more miscommunication. It\u2019s definitely a balancing act. My players don\u2019t want the world to be black till the GM provides light. But because it is a group activity, even if illuminated, the details need to be blurry and only come into greater focus with direct interaction in the fiction.

      \u201cI grab a glass from the bar\u201d is a double edge sword. It can be incredibly immersive or completely throw you out of the game. For me, the benefit outweighs the risk. And the risk can be mitigated! It\u2019s an opportunity! If the GM thinks it is so important that there is no glass\u2026 then say why\u2026 make it clear! Introduce a twist! Break the expectation by further revealing deeper truths about the fiction that the players can immerse in. If there is no deeper meaning, if the glass doesn\u2019t matter to the GM, then why forbid it? What\u2019s really going on here? It\u2019s one thing if every detail about the bar was predetermined and written down beforehand\u2026 that\u2019s cool, it makes the game a huge puzzle for the players to explore, but if not, if the GM is just making it up, and there is no deeper meaning or opportunity for a twist, then why say no? For some, it\u2019s because they view their interpretation of the fiction to be more important that everyone else\u2019s. For others, it\u2019s a matter of training\u2026 so people don\u2019t form habits of making assumptions. But is that habit bad?

      What if it isn\u2019t \u201cI grab a glass from the bar?\u201d What if it is, \u201cI chop the big bad\u2019s head off?\u201d

      In combat, people usually describe actions\u2026 I \u201cpunch\u201d, \u201cshoot\u201d, \u201crun\u201d, \u201cnegotiate\u201d, not effects\u2026 I \u201cknock his teeth out\u201d, \u201cshoot him dead\u201d, \u201crun away and no one can find me\u201d, \u201cconvince him to surrender\u201d.

      Isn\u2019t making setting assumptions describing actions and effects? Why is that ok then, as long as someone is super immersed, and doing the same in combat not ok? Maybe it is. Maybe it depends on the stakes. Maybe it depends if we are just talking back and forth vs. using dice to determine effects.

      The danger I see is when someone preplays in their mind what will happen and won\u2019t accept anyone else\u2019s input\u2026 the dice or the players. This is true for the GM as well. Preplay is often responsible for railroading. And selfishness responsible for preplay.

      If we agree that the dice matter, then why did we roll if we don\u2019t accept either outcome of the roll?

      It\u2019s tricky. I don\u2019t know if there is a right answer. Immersion can be very selfish because it isn\u2019t completely shared. It\u2019s in your head. But that\u2019s not a bad thing (neither is selfishness if it doesn\u2019t hurt other people). Where you draw the line is personal.

      I\u2019ve largely sidestepped this by building the resolution mechanics so that failure is in the hands of the players. What you are rolling for is the consequences of your actions and the degree they affect the world. The only time you really fail is if you choose to by not accepting the rolled consequences. In actual play it seems to make all the difference. It also seems to be why the players are ok with talking conflicts in Dogs in the Vineyard and not a single diplomacy roll in D&D 3E.

      Players describe actions, GM sets difficulty but the player doesn't have to beat that difficulty. Say the difficulty is 3.       \n We're playstorming it for 2 sessions to determine what works or doesn't work, and then formalizing it. Informally, we agreed that fiction wise, the pain would be "your past coming back to haunt you."

      Actual Play examples:

      1. GM re-rolls Player's Hits (successes) after introducing a twist.
      2. GM has an NPC calls in a favor that puts the PC in a bad spot.
      3. GM has an NPC demand Money (right before the PC was about to spend it).
      4. Player chooses to automatically fails a test (usually when another PC needed help).
      5. Player describes or asks the GM to describe something horrible happening to something they care about (usually a contact).

      #4 is interesting because it isn't meta, the PC is making a decision in the fiction. #5 is interesting in that it is meta but felt less so (even though it isn't less so) because it was the PC targeting a contact, not themselves, even though it is themselves because it is effectively a trait on their character sheet (but it didn't feel that way).

      \nPlayer setting difficulties:

      We tried players adding details to determine difficulty several times. Players who are ok with some meta or are very opinionated about certain areas of knowledge LOVE it! Players who are not HATE it! It's very polarizing. And with people who love it, it works smoothly and the fiction is really enhanced by the added details. But the people who don't think that way shut down and feel alienated. It's divisive.

      Traits that hurt you:

      In games like Mouse Guard, you can use Traits to help and hurt yourself. Helping gives you bonuses. Hurting gives you penalties but helps you later in the game. Specifically in Mouse Guard, help later means earning extra scenes for the Player's Turn (essentially you can do more personal stuff after missions, like heal, make friends, buy gear). So my character has the Tough trait. Normally Tough means I can wrestle larger opponents, last long in fights, resist pain and more. But sometimes being Tough means accidentally hurting friends or being insensitive or pretty much anything where Tough disadvantages me. When I use a Trait against myself, it gives me a penalty to a roll now but I gain extra scenes later.

      Generalizing to any game, it's a system where you use a part of your character against yourself to gain an advantage later. Lose now, win later. But in some games, you can't just take a penalty for any reason. It has to make sense in the fiction. And its a great opportunity to roleplay flaws. This is where Traits come in and are related to the "Hurt" examples above.

      What we experimented with last night was very different than the above (and unrelated to Traits). You can help yourself now by getting lucky at the cost of being hurt later when your past catches up with you.

      Why does it feel cheap to help with stats and not skills?

      In a game like Mouse Guard, if you are making a Resources Stat test, anyone with Resources (which is usually everyone) can help you. If you are making a Pathfinder Skill test, anyone can help with a variety of Skills including the same Skill (Pathfinder) or related Skills (Scout, Hunter, Weather Watcher) or appropriate Wises (Wisdom Skills) where situationally appropriate (forest-wise, cave-wise, path-wise, winter-wise and so on). In all cases you have to explain how you are helping in the fiction. And help has a cost, if the person fails, you may also suffer consequences.

      Some players feel weird using Stats to help Stats. I don't understand it fully myself. But the reason using skills seems less of an issue, especially wises, is that they feel more specific to the character. Everyone has the same stats but not everyone has the same skills and especially wises. So when they help, they feel special because they are doing something unique. But just helping with the same exact Stat causes them issues. Which I don't think is a meta vs. non meta issue.

      Although when I've played with people with minimal RPG experience, this is a non issue (to be fair, almost everything is a non issue as long as non-gamers don't have to talk in funny voices, memorize pages and pages of rules, the game matches their comfort level for crunchy numeric calculations, and there is enough color to help them imagine the situations).

      \n So this is the post that brought me to these forums. I realize its four years old but I wanted to chime in on it anyway.

      One of our longest-running, and most beloved traditions is playing rpgs using ourselves as the characters. We've done it in nearly every game we've played at least once. This has a very interesting impact on their playstyles. What's more, the game ends up having a significant impact on their awareness of the fragility of their reality. Most come to understand that when faced with certain situations they may well be capable of behaving similarly to the characters in the fiction they enjoy. It also usually leads them to become better, more immersed roleplayers with characters they create next time. Having experienced acting as themselves, and understanding it, they are better able to act as __________, and understand that as well. It's the old Stanislovski vs Strasberg dichotomy.

      I bring this up because I'm currently doing two SR games where the players are running themselves as characters. I wrote a short novel of introduction to the world and game from the standpoint of the players (presuming they had never read a word of the game otherwise). In it I explain how they were essentially put into suspended animation/cryogenics (don't overthink it, merely a plot device) and awoke IN the world described in Shadowrun. Being from another time, and being hunted mercilessly if their identities are ever discovered, gives them the perfect backstory to becoming runners. They simply have no good alternatives.
