    \n Spione does John LeCarre psychological thriller. It will utterly fail you for doing counter-terrorism action-adventure blackops stuff. It focuses on the dehumanizing nature of being a spy. In particular the game focuses on two spies, always two regardless of the number of players. It is always set in Berlin between the 1950s and the 1980s. It's pretty constrained but does what it does very well.

    It works in round-robin manner with each player taking a turn to add one thing to the developing fiction. Seriously, just one thing at a time. The two spies are not necessarily guaranteed to the be the protagonists and have no plot immunity. They might both be killed out of the gate. The game simply revolves around their activities and it's perfectly possible for say, their boss, to be the "real" protagonist over time. Usually the game stays focused on the spies but that's a property of the groups commitment to developing them than a mandate of the game.

    Wilderness of Mirrors is much better at doing "mission" games where there's a goal and the group is operating as team to accomplish the goal. I have played Spione, I have not played Wilderness of Mirrors so it makes it harder for me to comment on it.

    Jesse

    \n Sure.

    The available characters in Spione are fixed. There are 14 guy sheets (representing the character's personal life) and 9 spy sheets (whiich includes everything from who the spy is working for all the way down into the target of their spying). The two people responsible for "running" the two Principles selects one of the guy sheets and pairs it with one of the spy sheets. The text refers to the genre of the game as Spy vs. Guy. The idea is that all you have to do to get effective Spione play is split the priorities of the spy between the elements on his Guy Sheet and the elements on his Spy Sheet.

    On the Guy Sheet is a list of Supporting Characters. A game of Spione ends when the final fates of those Supporting Characters is determined. There are two ways a Supporting Character meets a final fate. The first is using the conflict mechanic in which someone uses a double-card narration to permanently kill or imprison the supporting character.

    The second involves something called The Trespasses. Trespasses are pulled from the players REAL LIFE experiences and are moments where either they themselves or someone they personally knew did something, by their judgment, that was morally wrong. The principle players each pull a Tresspass out of a hat and incorporate it into the understanding of the character.

    At any point a principle player may choose to reveal his Trespass making it public. When that happens every conflict after that point one of his supporting characters WILL leave the game via mechanic called the fate deck (basically a draw of cards that determines a range of possible outcomes the player may choose for that character). This is the only way that Supporting Characters may have "happy endings."

    So there's a lot of group control over how long the game lasts. Lots of action? Lots of blood shed via the conflict mechanic? Drives the game to conclusion faster. If the Principle Player chooses to reveal his Trespass, again, game is ending rapidly. There are between 2 and 4 Supporting Characters on each Guy Sheet.

    All my Spione games have been single session. However, I've always felt slightly rushed in them. I think Spione works well in the two to three session range. More than that would be pushing it.

    Jesse

    P.S. Oh! To hack the game to other eras you'd have to come up with new Guy and Spy sheets. That's about it.

    \n Jesse did a good job of explaining the rules of the game contained in Spione, but it's only a part of the package. Spione is really something different from what it's commonly though as a rpg (even here in story games), it's primarily a book about the war of spies during the cold war. In history and in fiction. It's a book with an authorial voice, that take position in real-world matter, and it contain many points that a lot of people would find controversial. One of these is that was passed for "facts" during the cold war was in fact propaganda, fiction, and if you want to search for the truth, you have to look in the works hidden under the label of "fiction". THEN, at the end of the book, there is a game system to create with a group of friends that kind of fiction. It's not really paragonable to a rpg with a game setting.

    If you want something really different, and the idea of playing real people in the maw of history interest you, I suggest you play Spione (hell, I would suggest any game creator worth his salt should play Spione, if only to see its elegance and efficacy). If you want something more "traditional" where spying is an escapist fantasy, I suggest Wilderness of Mirrors.

    \n There are some "tricks" to be able to play a Spione game in a convention slot (choose the guy and spy sheets for the players - some are for shorter play, other for longer play - use CIA and KGB as spy agency, everybody know them and you don't need to explain what they are. and most of all, during the game, kill the supporting cast every chance you get) but it's impossible to get the full experience of play in a short time. The convention demo is done to show the simplicity of the system, how it's easy to learn, and get the players to the (usually tragic) ending. But the game shine when played at a more "literary" pace, building characters and connections with time.
