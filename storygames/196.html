On the Abolition of Character Generation

Joshua A.C. Newman
February 2006 edited February 2006 in Story Games
Sydney just typed what I've been on about for a while now: character generation and how it sows disappointment in the fertile fields of imagination.

To summarize:

You need characters if you're going to tell a story. Let's assume you're responsible for one, who's going to be a protagonist, though maybe we'd share them, or maybe something else. Doesn't matter.

That character is defined through actions they take.

That character's player has resources to use to give that player authority over certain areas of narration.

Traditionally, those resources have been defined by this challenge: make decisions now that will benefit you later. That's because it's how wargames work, and that's because it's a particular (fairly accurate) view of how war works: you strategically determine what resources you need, then you suffer through the mistakes, use your advantages, and use them both flexibly in the face of changing situations.

But there's no reason to think that way when you've got a character in a story. It's not how it works in a story. You have a character enter a scene. Maybe there's some description, maybe there's foreshadowing. But we don't know she speaks Farsi until it comes up andwe don't know she's hiding a pistol. Ironically, what we might be able to tell, given genre clues, is that she's going to have some sort of romantic tryst with her rival/sidekick and that she's going to retrieve the artifact at the end of the story.

Absinthe and Shock: already work like this to different degrees. I don't see any reason not to, given a Narrativist agenda.

    How about generating characters before the story starts (to some degree), but taking their skills, abilities, whatever as for what kind of story is to come? (Which, of course, a good traditional GM does anyway). So instead of trying to determine the point value of an ability (or of one "level" of an ability) based on how useful it is, in some unfathomable objective abstract, you give it a point value based on how often you want to see it come up.

    A: "Okay, I want my character to be a master super ninja of death. Like, +10."
    B: "But this is a romantic comedy game."
    A: "Yeah, it's just background. Like, it might come up once every few sessions."
    B: "Fine. One point per level, +10, that's ten points. What about the rest?"
    A: "Cooking +5, and I want it to come up all the freakin' time. 10 points a level."
    B: "Cooking in every scene, check."

    But even this method has the weakness that maybe you don't really know, beforehand, what you want to see in the game. So you should at least have a bunch of unspent points going into the first scene.

    Creating characters on the fly, conversely, is tricky. Tony Lower-Basch tried this in an early version of "Capes," as an experiment, pretty much at my insistence, and it created very scattered, unfocused characters -- but then a Capes character is defined by a list of 12 things. My draft "apocalypse girl" tries to get around this by giving each player a "core" and allowing characters to be defined by one or two attributes.

    The main contention that I have with removing character generation or minimizing it in such a way is that:

    1) It can thwart the players from being invested in the story. In traditional RPGs, the character is (mostly) your own invention. It's your investment in the game. When the players have other powers, such as scene-framing, it certainly gives them other opportunities for investment in the game - but it's also very nebulous - kinda like having a 20% share in 'liberty'. You know you're invested on an intellectual scale, but there's no proof of it sitting in front of you - nothing concrete to say 'this is my stake, come hell or high water'.

    Certainly not all groups and not all RPGs need this sort of investment, but I think getting rid of it completely is a minus, not a plus.

    2) Imagination works best within limits, in my experience. Ask someone to 'create a character - any character you want', and you risk mental vapor-lock. I see character generation as a way of focusing player's imaginations to the tasks at hand, to put them into a certain frame of mind for communicating with each other. Not to mention that character creation, even if it has some limitations on it, also requires that players think about their characters in way that they might not at first - if the game has an 'empathy' trait, the player has to ask themselves: "How empathetic is Torgo the Innkeeper?" And the answer might surprise themselves.

    In short - I see character generation as a tool, not an obstacle, to the creative process.

    ...

    As to this focusing players on making decisions that benefit them - again, I don't see this as a bug, but a feature. Especially if these decisions are in conflict with one another. Certainly, players are going to play to the protect their stake, their investment, in the SIS. Protecting these investments generates conflict, which is key. In some narrativist games, it seems like you have to go the extra mile to generate conflicts - everything's all about consensus. I think that a little extra friction in the consensus process is a good thing.

    With Verge, what I'm trying to do (and this isn't well covered in the manuscript yet) is have the group collectively define the Color of the Setting and create a Situation (I call all this stuff Story) before making characters. I imagine the group drawing a sort of r-map with corporations and technologies and ideas and people on it and connecting them with lines that represent events and relationships. They do this until the GM says it's "cooked," then they play.

    Any time during the Story Creation phase, a player can claim one of the people on the r-map as his character. He can change his mind at any time, abandon that character, and pick a different person who isn't already claimed. When Story Creation is done, then the players flesh out the characters. The character generation rules will require players to choose a Drive and various Drive-related Problems, some of which must relate back to stuff on the r-map. They'll have some freedom to add stuff to the r-map on the spot during c-gen. They will also pick their traits, which are Edges and Issues (nee Strengths and Weaknesses), Allies and Enemies, and Gear, some of which must be tied back to the r-map again. They'll be allowed to add stuff to the r-map now, too.

    So basically I have them creating Setting and Situation first, and those absolutely drive Character.

    Joshua -- it seems to me that you have a very limited view of what character generation is. You're suggesting that it is blind speculation about what will happen in the future, but there are a lot of other possibilities:

    1) Character generation can be creating flags which suggest to other participants what you want to have happen in future game sessions. i.e. Rather than speculating, you're making suggestions. For example, you can define Relationships in Dogs in the Vineyard, or even more strongly put down characters in your cosmos in Polaris. The adventures are then generated based on what's on your character sheet.

    2) Character generation can give you power to define what will happen in future game sessions. For example, when I take a Hunted (11-) in Champions, I am defining a villain who will show up in the campaign 62.4% of the time.

    3) Character generation needn't be about chosen design at all -- stuff can happen in it, such as lifepath generation like Traveller or other random creation.

    4) Character creation can also involve group interaction: like in Orkworld where the players collectively create a household, or Ars Magica where you create a covenant together.

    OURS, as opposed to "the published author's" for setting, and as opposed to "mine alone" for chargen. What's more, "ours" in a dynamic sense of something that arises from an ongoing interaction, as opposed to thinking it out beforehand.

    Clearly, there needs to be some "thinking it out beforehand" to avoid total confusion and lack of focus. Heck, I give this advice to every person to posts on the Forge that "hey, we played Capes, and it was kinda random": "Dude, just because you can start playing this game without having any idea of what you want to do doesn't mean you SHOULD."

    This thinking-beforehand can be hardwired by the written system ("you're heroic adventures who go into caves to kill monsters and take their stuff, go!") or brainstormed by the particular group (as in PTA pitches) or any combination thereof. But it does need doing.

    What I'm arguing for, really, is a much lower ratio of "thinking it out beforehand" to "developing it through play" than is traditional in the hobby. (World of Darkness setting, and GURPS point-build characters, much as I love 'em, are the prime villains in this regard, and very different from Dogs et al). I'm not really calling for the abolition of "chargen," or of setting. I'm not even necessarily suggesting that character generation and setting need to be short and sweet (though that often helps).

    What I AM suggesting is that character creation, setting, and every other sort of preparation should be conceived as sparks to light the fire of actual play, rather than as sources of heat and light in and of themselves.

    Well, I've never done any historical or real-world-present games "straight" with no fantastical or imaginary elements, and I think it's fairly rare in the hobby overall, so that might be answer right there -- but more profoundly:

    The attitude you take towards your material (setting, character sheet, other prep) is more important than the sheer amount of material -- although an imposing, impressive mass of material makes it harder to have the right attitude. If you take even a tiny, slender bit of material and say "this is canon, everything we do must conform to this," you're in trouble. If you can take a huge book of raw material (fictional, real history, doesn't matter) and say, "this is the starting point, this is inspiration, this is the spark for the fire we build together" -- you're golden.

    I see no functional difference between

    A: My sire is the secret Vampire Prince of Detroit!
    B: No, the book says it's this guy, he's the wrong bloodline, can't do it.

    and

    A: My dad is a deputy battalion commander in the 101st Airborne Division as they land behind enemy lines on D-Day!
    B: No, the book says it was this guy, he didn't have any kids as of D-Day, can't do it.

    Note also that "A" and "B" can be two people or two voices in the same person's head.

      To me the issue is that a table top RPG is a complex arrangement of art and desire where multiple players are building a work of fiction in real-time. It is for precisely this reason, in my opinion, that the old-style character generation was created (consciously or not). With a set of numbers that provide guidelines for roleplay, those less comfortable with acting or writing (aka, building a character on the fly) have a series of benchmarks to follow that tell them how to roleplay. The old style character creation helps transform a lack of imagination or social discomfort into roleplaying. I suppose one might consider it social lube or RPG lube. :)

      I think a games market should determine the games character creation method. A game targeted to people fully invested in the idea of real-time fiction should probably have a much more organic and story-like approach to character building. Games designed for younger players or games built for the twitch crowd should probably concentrate on teaching the player how attributes = cues for roleplaying.

      The character-creation-as-you-go-so-that-it-doesnt-blow-immersion thing is a problem for many game genres. Computer RPGs and MMOs go to great lengths to teach players how to use the controls and build their characters without killing immersion. I think they mostly fail because they dont really understand what immersion is all about (thats a subject for another conversation). Morrowind III is a good example of a game that more completely understands immersion and storytelling. Their character creation is all about your characters history and background and star signs, etc.

      Although this is a separate thought, it is a related one: I feel like it is this area the immersion, real-time fiction, shared imagination space thing its this aspect of table top games thats going to keep them around for a long time. It will be our childrens children who make the computer games that can compete with four people sitting around the table telling stories about their characters.

      This reminds me very much of my experiences with EPICS:

      http://edge.rpgnow.com/product_info.php?products_id=1807&

      In EPICS, your character sheet has very little on it at the start of the game, you can put stuff on it, but you're not required to. But you have a pool of points.

      When your character faces a situation, you spend that pool of points to add things to your character sheet. Such as:

      "'Well, naturally I know how to fly a plane, you bounder!' I say, tapping my pipe in the ashtray. 'All members of the Explorer's Club do...'." costs a little bit.

      "'Thank God I read a book one time on how to fly a plane! Quick, give me the controls!...um...these look different!'" costs more due to urgency.

      "'Ha! Safely land a plane with both engines out and the landing gear gone? Done it a thousand times! Why not blindfold me and make it interesting?'" costs a lot due to urgency AND the overwhelming skill of the character.

      You can also get points back into this pool by contributing to the game world or causing problems for your character:

      "'Are you crazy?? I don't know how to fly a plane! We're all gonna die!!'" gains you points because you are establishing a limitation of your character, for example. If the "Explorer's Club" hadn't been invented before you mentioned it above, you might gain back a few points for introducing it.

      There's a more standard "reward for roleplaying" section that relates to writing personality traits of your characters.

      Definitely worth a look if you are interested in design-in-play sorts of approaches to characters.

      Note that when I proposed this game to my group, they loudly protested. "How can I decide what my character would do if I don't know what he CAN do?"

      I say, "Well, you can just decide by what would be cool to you, the player..." That was not suitable, what they thought was cool to them, the players, was knowing what the character could do. Interestingly, A) this was even true of the players who hate character creation and consider it a blight, and B) nobody really plays immersively in our group.
