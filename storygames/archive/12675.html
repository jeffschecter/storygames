    \n Here are some writer's block tips that have always worked for me:

    * Change your font.
    * Change your workspace.
    * Sing along to some music.
    * Go get some exercise and fresh air.
    * Skip the problem part and work on another part of the project.
    * Work on a project that doesn't involve words (visual art, music)
    * Go out with someone interesting, not necessarily romantically, but take a few hours to hang out, eat food, and talk.
    * Work on a completely different project.
    * Play some boardgames, chess is a great logjam breaker for me
    * Get more sleep
    * Change the time of day you work

    \n I don't really know. If I don't feel like working on something, I don't. Unless I've set myself a deadline - but deadlines for fun projects (like games) I usually make sure that they've got room for procrastination and weeks or months of Nothing Going On.

    If I had to force myself to produce X amount of game material every day, well, I'd insist someone paid me plenty of cash.

    But yeah. I've had games lie idle for six months or more - this happened to both Draug and Society of Dreamers. They turned out much better for it. Some projects are fun and quick and easy; some are just too darn serious, or complex, or... what I think it is: Some projects are just at the edge of my capabilities; they're good because they're challenging, and I'm not sure I can complete them. They're the projects that just insist on being left alone for long stretches of time because something's not ready or not right.

    Posted By: J. WaltonIn my experience, the best cure for writer's block is playing the game, in whatever form you have, to whatever degree you can.
    \nHere you go. I didn't read the post as looking for solutions, but since the bandwagon has slowed down right near my stop here, I'll hop on.

    This is our development model. We are not writers by trade and so we don't sit down and write a game. And so writer's block is easier to dodge. We get a sketch down, just enough to play the damned thing. Then we play it, concentrating on the new material, and keep notes (and audio now). Then we refine and expand the hack, and repeat. And repeat and repeat and repeat. There is far more playing than writing.

    When I get writer's block is at the end, when we have to turn those notes into a text that teaches and acts as a reference. Fortunately there's another trick in here: organization, which feeds the "divide and conquer" methodology. Write a ToC, put your bits in place, and then pick bits to solve piecemeal. A trick within a trick, though: don't hammer on the hard ones. Kill the easy ones dead and, if one gets hard, hop right to another. It's better to keep writing than to bang your head.

    When all else fails I say "fuck it" and go to the beach with the dog.
