    \n what I found interesting in retrospect was how most contestants (myself included) interpreted 3-4 words that on balance have neutral-positive connotations into darker games.

    currency + memory + drug + palace = 3 positive tending words if you think of illicit drugs, 4 positive-tending if you think of medical drugs; sacred + thread + rose + inconsistency = 2 positive tending, one neutral, one negative tending. but palaces became crumbling or oppressive, currencies corrupting, memories lost or painful, etc.

    I suppose that this is partly a function of thinking of rpgs as about overcoming conflicts/challenges and so the game has a screwed-up starting point that characters fix.

    There were some more positively framed games. I'm interested in seeing the grassroots one, for example, and there were probably others I missed.

    commondialog, sure, I'd be glad to elaborate on that.

    \n

    So, back in the day (like, in 2001-3 or something like that), Game Chef was this shy young lady who didn't have a lot of friends, and was firmly in the business of experimentation. I remember that at that time, iron games were fiercely, dramatically individualistic. There were chess pieces whose movements tracked characters' emotional states, ice cubes used as timers, games with drinking and couch surfing...things were invented in iron games. And you could reasonably keep up with all of them as they developed. They were slick and short and a little rough around the edges.

    \n

    These days, I feel like the games coming out of game chef are a little more mature, both in their presentation and in the sheer age of their mechanical techniques. I don't percieve stuff being invented. It's a venue for experimentation and testing rather than innovation. It's basically unfeasible to keep up with everyone and brainstorm with them as they work (for various reasons, including volume, time, and the clearly shifting expectations for what makes a good gc game) - it makes the contest feel much more solitary, less communal, and the constant growth of the contest and the influx of new people contributes to that, 'cause they're not people you've been designing with for ages and you have to struggle to understand each other.

    \n

    I'm by no means decrying the new state of affairs. It's particularly super great that new faces are always getting involved in game chef, and I think it's a great environment to meet them. I appreciate that a lot.

    \n

    But it's really unfamiliar. It's not the neighborhood I grew up in.
