\n OK, let\u2019s say your character is made up of cool things you want to see happen. You always have five of them, like so:

SMASH THROUGH A PLATE GLASS WINDOW
SAY \u201cNOT ON MY WATCH\u201d
FALL IN LOVE WITH AN ENEMY
RIDE A ROBOT LIKE A COWBOY
PLAY DEAD

Every time you do one of your cool things, you cross it off the list and add a new one at the bottom, so you always have five. You also get some sort of mojo. In fact, it is the only possible way to get mojo. I don\u2019t know what mojo is but it lets you as a player succeed and be awesome.

Now one of the five is always a transformative point for your dude \u2013 in the list above, it\u2019s \u201cFall in love with an enemy\u201d. This one is worth triple mojo (or whatever) and is authored not by you, but by your friends at the table.

Pacing would be important, I think. Maybe it'd be wide open - you\u2019re in a fight, and you want to win. You jump through a plate glass window and grab one mojo and immediately author \u201cMoment of silence as stunned enemies survey my intimidating form\u201d, then inject a little color in the game, cross that one off, grab more mojo, author a new thing, rinse and repeat. They could come and go that fast, or you could milk them if you preferred.

Or maybe activating statements happens at a more measured pace. I'm not really sure what's best. Maybe authoring new ones takes place when they are all used up, so burning through them would be a little more difficult to reconcile, or more dangerous. This seems more fun, to have some elegant governing mechanism that would encourage players to have diverse and interesting statements, covering many situations.

Either way, you might find yourself get put n a bad corner and be forced to fall in love with the enemy at hand somehow, because you had to grab those three mojo to whip him. I'm not sure

I\u2019m thinking these are like hyper-keys, where the creating of, using of, and buying off of all happen very rapidly. They are the game\u2019s only source of effectiveness, which drives their use. You are constantly thinking of interesting, fun things you want to see happen and then actualizing them. You also have a hilarious record of all the crazy stuff your guy has been up to - "When did I set myself on fire, again? It's right here between 'Steal a baby' and 'discover a vein of silver'"

Does this sound fun and workable? Any thoughts on how this might play out, or unintended consequences?

    \n I've kinda done this, in a wild and crazy game called "Time Soldier X", that I wrote, played a number of times, and then never picked up again. This s almost makes me want to pick it back up again, but I don't have the time.

    Anyway, the "kinda". Here's how it worked. I had a list of things that were ALWAYS awesome, like 'splotions and stunts and people falling to their deaths. And I also had a running list of things people would just write down crazy stuff on, like "Fight i zero-gravity!" and "shrink ray!". Sort of like the stuff you have up there, except you couldn't use your own contributions to the list. Basically, you had this big pool of dice of different pretty shapes and colors, and you could roll as many as you wanted to, and if you won narration, you could cash in a bunch of new dice by incorporating all that awesome stuff into it. It was like a big, crazy, gonzo exercise in creativity, as there were lots more things you had to include in your narration, like weird equipment you built and circumstances depending on the number you rolled and WORDS OF AWESOME and ... Well, luckily the game was usually short. Could have used some moderation, but it was quite workable, and fun.

    Anyway, don't know if that was useful, but it might give you some ideas. *Shrug* Also, there were extra bonuses for escalating stuff, so if you blew up something that was larger than anything that had been blown up so far, you got a big die. Yah.

    \n Another thing that just struck me is that these are kinda like backwards flags: they're signals to yourself (not to the other players, as you've described it) of what you want to have happen. And not just thing to happen, thing you want to happen to your character, yeah? And the Czege Principle applies here a bit, I think. Just like how it's not that fun to create and resolve your own opposition, it's not necessarily fun to create and resolve your own awesome either. If you wrote these things down somewhere public and other folks could assist you in making them happen, maybe it would work better? Not sure.

    There's also the Mountain Witch Dark Fate trick, right? Where you give total narrative control to players as long as it has to do with This One Thing. There might be some mileage there too. Like, give players authority to drive towards the things they want to have happen, but I'm not sure, off the top of my head, how to make that work in this kind of context. The OUAT model of stealing narration kinda sucks in this regard. There would need to be a less jarring way of transferring authority.

    Also, if you don't accomplish something, can you cross it off for another reason? Like, maybe it isn't appropriate anymore. Now you're getting into IAWA list territory, yeah? Instead of characters that can potentially re-occur, you're talking about lists of things that may or may not happen.

    \n The part of this idea that gets me most excited is the part where other players up with something transformative for my character.

    I think I'd want to see:

    1. Other players write these Hyper-Keys (HKs) for you; or you write them, but other players have to make them happen.

    So, maybe one of your HKs is "getting thrown through a glass window", and another player can score by throwing you through it. Otherwise, there might be some danger of parallel solo play, where each player is just trying to run down the line of HKs they've written up.

    Some guidance on the scale and impact of a potential HK might be useful here, as well--falling in love with an enemy is a very different thing from breaking a window, for instance.

    2. Rewriting them should be more interesting, somehow. What if a new HK had to be a modification or twist on an existing HK?

    There could also be types of HKs, like "physical event", "change of heart", "revelation of the past", and you had some limitation on which ones you could tag, like one from each category per scene, or you can only play a "revelation of the past" after you've had one "sudden interruption", or something like that.

    \n One thing I love about Keys: They're broad. You have the Key of Cowardice, not the Key of Hiding Under a Table, so the stuff you do to ping your Key can always be new and different.

    The thing I see in your list is a lot of pretty specific actions. I write down "Smash Through a Plate Glass Window", and then I have my guy jump through a plate glass window. It feels kind of flat, since there's no phase in between where I have to interpret the Key and creatively apply it. I agree with Jonathan that this is edging into Czege Principle territory.

    Graham does something similar in Forbidden Kingdom, with his method for gathering dice. "Take a Wealth die when you show your superior firepower or knowledge." The conditions are still pretty broad, though. "Show your superior firepower" requires creative input from me in the moment in a way that "Hold your pistol to someone's head" doesn't.

    So, I think this idea could definitely work, but I'd personally like to see the hyper-keys be a bit more broad and less like a specific stage direction.

    \n Side Comment:

    One of the positives about something like this is that if you're using a heavily genre-related set up, you'll (probably) burn through tropes/cliches pretty quickly- which is good!

    First, it lets folks get the stuff they came for out of their system.

    Then, it encourages them to come up with variants, and lesser tropes/cliches that haven't been used before by someone else.

    By the time players have cycled through a bit, however, they should be pretty well on their way to creating their own unique take on stuff in the game being played by the actual group of people. they'll have moved beyond the mental collection they all came to the game with.

    Um, I'm not quite the designer other folks are, so here would be my implementation: Rather than write out stuff and cross it out, just write down the ideas and put them on index cards. You could have them be character specific or not as you see fit. No individual player could have more than five out at once. They can be character specific, or more general. Doesn't matter.

    Players have a pool of dice to use for the game. It's a total pool. That's all they'll get during play unless they refressh part of it by using the cards.

    The card gives both the creating player and the using player the dice from it, but you can't get both ( so if you pick your own, you only get the dice once).

    (Here I'm supposing that the players have ranked the cards from 5 down to 1 in value, but adjust as appropriate. Maybe the cards out on the table by a given player have to add up to a certain total instead, like 15 or 20. Whatever; Lots of possible variants there. I'm also supposing that players initial their cards for identification purposes).

    So anyway, yeah, that's the core mechanic. Limited total dice pool from the game+ use ideas on cards to gain more dice to use throughout game.

    \n Let's say, to hit your Hyperkeys, players must achieve them within the scope of a broader conflict.
    \n
    \nA player can't just say, "That's when I smash through the plate glass window" and earn mojo.
    \n
    \nFirst, the scene must include a plate glass window. This situation authority power is earned through game play.
    \nSecond, the character must position themselves to plausibly achieve the hyperkey. (i.e., Bad guy somehow is positioned behind the plate glass window).
    \nThird, the overarching conflict of interest must be resolved, in part, by the character smashing through the window. The genre trope is not just color, but important to the conflict of interest. (i.e., because the character smashed through a window, it contributed to his or her success in the broader conflict of interests.)
    \n
    \nHit those three things, with some type of player skill (tactics, number crunching etc...) and with a little luck, if they get them all then they get the Mojo.
    \n
    \nAlthough Mojo should be part of its own reward cycle, not part of the previous player skill cycle involved above. So you'd never spend Mojo to influence player skill required to get one, two and three. Mojo opens a door to a different Reward all together. What that is, i'm not sure.
