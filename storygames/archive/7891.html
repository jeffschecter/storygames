\n Earlier in the month, Rob posted a link regarding the use of story and character arcs in the TV show, The Wire.

Unfortunately, despite lots of experience GMing I've never focused attention on deliberately creating story or character arcs. I think that many have arisen naturally in the games that I ran, but not as a result of intentional effort.

My group is transitioning from a weekly to a bi-weekly game. My tendency as a GM is to sustain lots of story and character threads simultaneously, evolving naturally from the consequences of the players choice and the NPC agendas. Some of these get resolved and some fall by the wayside during play. But this tends to lack structure or unified tone, and I believe it often feels to the players like progress is difficult because few things are ever resolved permanently.

I'd like to increase the ability of individual sessions to stand alone as units of play (to give a feeling of completeness and satisfaction to the players), but still sustain interest in the campaign as a whole. I'm looking for suggestions on how to learn from the model The Wire, and other narratives, offer and implement them from the GM side.

So:
What are your suggestions for creating campaign, story, and/or character arcs?
How much do you try to emulate 3 / 5 acts structure or the rising action, climax, falling action of traditional narratives?
What specific suggestions do you have for keeping things tight - i.e. starting an arc and delivering a satisfying conclusion in 1 - 3 sessions?
How to work with players to create character arcs that aren't artificial feeling?

Your suggestions and experience greatly appreciated.

    \n I'd describe the traditional narrative in roleplaying games as hook - conflict - climax. Before these I have preparations, and after them I have epilogue and experience. I try to get a climax of sorts every time, a scene that triggers some feeling of completeness, but I don't succeed each time.

    By focusing on a possible conflict, though, and planning a bit, I usually do succeed. I don't do anything else than look into what elements the players would like to play around with, and give them some hook pertaining to it, and then drive the consequences (and possible motivations in NPCs) towards whatever climax there is to be had.

    Sounds like you got most of it in your fingertips already. Only need to organize yourself a bit, and perhaps ask yourself if the game you play by is good enough. My advice: go for a game with simple conflict-resolution, and build it out with sophisticated method (leadership, group dynamics, storytelling, etc.).

    Good luck!

    \n I have recently started seeing a lot of gamer stories and gamer advice highlighting the fact that, while a lot of gamers and GMs are very good in the moment, up to and including one complete session of play (one situation resolved, one "plot," one "story"), there is a glaring lack of capability in the long term: campaign arcs, maintaining interest, developing characters and setting, and so on.

    Look at successful television shows or radio serials (the closest medium and genre to ongoing RPG campaigns). One of the things you may notice is that every character and location of interest for the first two thirds of a season are introduced in the first episode. Only a handful of new faces and places are introduced in the course of that first two thirds, and if you go back and review the course of a season, you will find that those additions are introduced far earlier than you remember them being introduced. Random example from Babylon 5: how early is Zahadum, site of the final battle at the end of the entire show, introduced? That would be episode four.

    Additionally, long-running shows also tend to run on a relatively small cast \u2014 a handful of primary protagonists (AKA the PCs), but also a handful of supporting characters and a handful of recurring 'villains' \u2014 there are the odd one-off characters that only appear in one episode, but the bulk of the adversity and foiling you see on these shows are done by characters that stick around and keep coming back. In fact, you see the biggest fan uproar or loss of interest when new characters are injected into a long-running series (Lochley in B5, Ezri Dax on DS9, Riley on Buffy, the Ori on Stargate: SG1, and so on). The world of the series, the setting, is emblemized by the regular cast. Changing the cast is changing the show. And that can be done well (Ezri), or poorly (Lochley), and will be the source of never-ending debate, as well.

    To my eye, the lesson to take away from these examples is to develop a relatively small stable of characters and locations \u2014 somewhere around ten to fifteen \u2014 and then whenever you need a character or a location, look to what you've already got before you go and create something new. A lot (A LOT) of campaigns suffer from the "new chapter, new place" problem where the GM thinks that constantly giving his players a stream of new stuff will keep them interested. This is exactly backwards for most audiences \u2014 what people want is continuity, to get to know characters and places, build up history and associations, and inhabit that world in their imaginations. (This problem is also exacerbated by gaming assumptions often leaving antagonists dead at the end of an episode, but that's a whole different topic.)

    So how to make campaigns tight? Don't loosen them up with too much stuff to keep track of. Keep things tight by making sure all your parts and pieces are few in number and have many interrelationships. A new character or location custom-made for a conflict is almost always less interesting than an old character or location dragging its baggage back into the spotlight.

    \n I struggle with this GMing my regular, traditional game (In Nomine, if you know it - terribly broken system, but lovely setting and concept). Mostly it's because I do try very hard not to force particular outcomes or even particular story threads on he players, but have to compromise between that and plot threads that meander going nowhere interesting, or between players who have nothing to do and then just have their characters bimble endlessly until I have some plot show up.

    I think the key to the compromise is to aggressively introduce hooks, but try and leave their resolution open-ended - but making the hooks interesting to the character, unstable and critical in terms of situation, and having lasting, meaningful consequences. In short, Bangs. But it's still not easy.

    \n I've been running 3-session campaigns for a long time now (which evolved out of my desire to both keep things relatively short and reach a satisfying conclusion). Generally, when we create PCs we give them a purpose as a group which it is assumed they will try to fulfill. Sort of building an end in at the beginning. I ran two sci-fi campaigns recently where each had an assumed ending. In one case, the ending was pretty wide open - we knew it would end but not exactly how, and the answer to this question developed itself both in-game and out, through play and discussions with the players. In the second game I had an ending in mind but the campaign itself was mission-based, which meant the PCs had a built-in goal to focus on.

    So the focus of the campaign helped decide the characters which helped decide the goal, if you see what I mean. Once I have that, I can pace and present things to achieve that goal.

    Having the players on board with this is obviously good. I often find players quite willing to help drive things to resolution quickly if they know it's just three "acts".
