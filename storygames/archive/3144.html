\n
\n
\n
\n\nMatthijs \n\n\n
\n
\n\nMay 2007 \n edited May 2007 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n I love Kaare Bergs term "hippiemetoder", which he periodically applies to me, and I hereby steal it as a description of my game design philosophy.
\n
\nWhat are hippie methods?
\n
\nHippie methods are a collective term for techniques I use among others in the Itras By campaign (Norwegian), We All Had Names and the reality game Agabadan (Norwegian). They usually have these traits in common:
\n
\n[ulist]
\n[li]No numbers.[/li]
\n[li]No resource management.[/li]
\n[li]Minimum amount of bothersome mechanics.[/li]
\n[li]Communal creation. Players participate in what happens on several layers.[/li]
\n[li]Paper and cards rather than dice.[/li]
\n[li]Methods of play that can be tailored to the situation (new scene = possible new rules).[/li]
\n[/ulist]
\n
\nWhat else are hippie methods?
\n
\nIn addition to the above, hippie methods can use:
\n
\n[ulist]
\n[li]Immersionist techniques.[/li]
\n[li]Surrealist techniques.[/li]
\n[li]Techniques focusing on altered states of consciousness and views of reality (such as hypnosis, dreams, loss of identity, loss of memory, multiple identities, paradoxical/self-contradicting views of reality, altered time flow).
\n[li]Personal challenges, also outside of the game.[/li][/li]
\n[/ulist]
\n
\nThe hippie method attitude
\n
\n[ulist]
\n[li]Hippie methods are thought through.[/li]
\n[li]Hippie methods are positive.[/li]
\n[li]Hippie methods like people, especially creative people.[/li]
\n[li]Hippie methods help you be creative.[/li]
\n[li]Hippie methods are challenging.[/li]
\n[li]Hippie methods give everybody control.[/li]
\n[li]Hippie methods are a continuation of what nineties role-playing should have been.[/li]
\n[li]Hippie methods are unpredictable within given limits.[/li]
\n[li]Hippie methods aren't used in school.[/li]
\n[li]Hippie methods take in anything that's needed from other disciplines, as long as it's good shit.[/li]
\n[li]Hippie methods don't care if you think this is proper role-playing, dude. Chill out.[/li]
\n[/ulist]
\n
\nDo you have to be consequent?
\nHippie methods steal from everyone, and anyone can steal from hippie methods. That's okay. But if you're a player-oppressing resource capitalist who believes in score greed and resource tactics as the Right Way, then you're not using hippie methods, man. Then you're doing something else, and that's okay, too.

    \nSome techniques are intended to make the player enter an altered state directly. Most, however, focus on altering the character's state of mind - which will, of course, affect the player. Here are a few techniques from my Itras By campaign:
    \n
    \nSuggestive countdown ritual. At the start of the session, all players close their eyes and start a countdown - aloud - from 10 to 1. In between each number, the GM speaks a short sentence or a word, designed to evoke imagery, activate the imagination processes, get the symbol-processing parts of the mind to gear up. These phrases also point towards things that might happen in the session.
    \n
    \nAn example:
    \n10. A metal apple.
    \n9. A leaf falls from a tree, twirling in the wind.
    \n8. A paper falls from a window, twirling in the wind.
    \n7. You can feel the rain on your tongue.
    \n6. Who's that, flying?
    \n5. It's warm under the coat.
    \n4. The metal squeaks.
    \n3. The smell of coffee.
    \n2. The smell of roast pig.
    \n1. On top of the Moon Tower, Nindra rests.
    \n
    \nRue du Roi. Named after a street in Itras By where the surreal takes over. One player controls his/her character as per usual; the other three play the character's longings, fears and self-image, respectively. The main player is not informed of what will happen before the scene starts. The other players can control objects, NPCs and events as they see fit. Perhaps the weather changes to reflect the main character's fears; perhaps he/she sees him/herself in a mirror.
    \n
    \nFlashbacks from an alternate reality. The same scene is played several times, with a major NPC (confronting a character) played by a different player each time. All versions of the scene are equally true, and have taken place in the character's memory.
    \n
    \nText vs reality. In the game, there's a book chronicling the life of one of the PCs - events that have happened, that might happen, that never happened. At a dinner among strangers, with a pretty girl serving, the PC starts remembering a text about this speficic event. However, in the text, the PC performs horrible actions. While the player plays through the dinner in a "straight" fashion, the other players "read" (that is, invent) pieces of text from the book, that the PC remembers. So, for example, while he's eating the main course and trying to have a chat with the businessman next to him, he remembers how the book describes him raping the serving girl.

    Posted By: Ben LehmanWhat I'm saying is: If I have a game where, say, there is a GM but people "own" a number of characters which they all play, and the GM only has the right to play one character, and doesn't have the right to make any rules calls, can that still be a hippy game?     \nI see what you mean. Well, yes, it can definitely be a hippie method game, in my book. Hippie methods are very open to unconventional divisions of traditional GM functions and authority. The reason I talk about players and GM in the four examples (suggestive countdown, rue du roi, flashbacks from an alternate reality + text vs reality) is that they're all taken from my Itras By campaign, which by default has a traditional GM/player setup. However, many of the methods I've tried out there (along with other methods already in the game text) specifically change that setup, challenge it, play around with it.

    There are, for instance, "chance cards" that players can draw - each player once every session. One of those cards requires the GM to give his/her notes to the player to his/her left, who is then the GM for the rest of the session.

    The main thing about hippie method GM functions + authority is probably this: A setup where there'\xc2\xa8s a GM who controls just about everything (contents of setting, situation, resolution [GM fiat]) all the time is antithetical to hippie methods. However, a setup with rotating GMs, division of GM power etc is almost a requirement for hippie methods - it's part of what I mean by "communal creation".

    \n Thanks, and that's a central question for many people, I think. It is, of course, just a personal preference; at the same time, it seems to be one that I share with many self-proclaimed immersionists, freeformers, jeepformers and LARPers.

    Why I want to minimize the amount of resource handling in my games

    Resource handling systems are logistics I don't want. I understand how they work to make a story; I like playing games with them sometimes, for the end results they provide. I see how Capes can end up making players listen closely to each other and provide good antagonism, or why Nicotine Girls' smoking rules are cool. However, the end doesn't justify the means - in this case, breaking the flow of the game.

    Resource handling is, to me, a speed bump in the playing process. It makes me stop and think: "What if I use this point in this fashion? How will that affect what I can do in the future? What do I want to do in the future?" Instead, I want to think: "I need to do this thing. How can I express it? What actions should I take? What emotions am I going through?"

    Resource handling also makes it easy to fall for "story point greed" - if I'm not sure exactly what I want, I can at least keep my options open by maxing out on tokens I can use later. (Like choosing what job you want based on what you might earn, instead of what job you actually like). Why use or abuse the system? Because it's there, and I'm human.

    Finally, resource handling requires an added layer of explanation and understanding. When playing with (or designing for) others, if I use resource handling systems, not only do I have to explain the rules of the game, I also have to explain their implications. If not, players may easily have a bad experience the first time they play, because they don't get how the game works. "You mean just because I used the McGuffin point in scene 1, my protagonist has to become a slave to the Spider King in scene 5? Where does it say that?" "It doesn't. It just follows from the rules."

    Dice

    Dice, in and of themselves, aren't good or bad. However, they are very often used in resolution systems that tie in with resource handling systems, which I want to avoid. Other uses are for rolling up stuff on tables (which is old-skool); I'd rather use cards for that sort of randomization.

    Resolution systems

    Still thinking about those.
