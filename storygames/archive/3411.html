\n I'm starting to write Dirty Secrets, which will be full of awesomeness blah blah blah you've heard it all before. But, I've run into a bit of a snag. A major part of the book is going to be a tutorial section of sorts, where I explain how to use the ruleset that I've just given you. Things like "This is the kind of thing that you do with a Reflection sequence" or "This is what the Investigator needs to do to be pulling his weight at the game table". As I've been writing, I've found myself hovering in the third person. "Players must do thus-and-thus." This is proper for formal writing, but boring to read. I could switch over to "You must do thus-and-thus", which is less proper, and perhaps too informal.

So, I thought I'd ask the denizens of Story Games. What do you think? Which would you prefer in a gaming rulebook? Do you even care?

Seth Ben-Ezra
Great Wolf

    \n Indeed, game books are such weird little chimeras. Would it help to break 'em down into some general ways books are used?

    The way I'm seeing it, game books are equal parts:\n
    Fiction - For those who read the whole book from front to back. The fiction can literally be whole stories... but even if there are only a few bits of flavor text sprinkled here and there, the mind will make connections and fill in the blanks to form a larger narrative.

    Instruction - For those who want to learn to play. The instruction provides thorough explanations for the whys and hows of the game's system.

    Textbook - For those who already know how to play and simply need a quick reference for any questions that might occur during the game. I like to make my systems minimal enough that you don't need to do this, but tastes vary.

    Marketing - For someone flipping through the pages online, at their local store, or at their friends' house. The book has qualities that immediately hook a potential enthusiast.

    \nI suppose there's some overlap there, but that's just off the top of my head.

    \nAt this point I'm planning on leaving it in. It turns out that, so far, it's not a page only a couple of paragraphs:\n
    From: Misspent Youth draftSome Notes about Style

    In this book, I have chosen to take a conversational tone, both because Misspent Youth is a game that focuses a great deal on friendship and I wish to set that tone, and because I wish to avoid problems with third-person singular gendered pronouns. English does not currently have in common use gender-neutral third-person singular pronouns that writers can use to refer to people. Rather than refer to any unknown person as "he," as has been common until recently, I have chosen to speak to "you" instead. Furthermore, as the focus in Misspent Youth is on group cooperation, I will often be addressing the plural you. Often, when referring to The Authority, I have chosen to use "it," in order to de-personify The Authority. Where it has been necessary to use a third-person singular pronoun, I have randomly determined the gender.

    When I use words that have specific in-game meanings, I have capitalized these words. This is to distinguish between when I am using them as they relate to the rules, and when I am using them in their normal, everyday sense. For example, when I refer to The Authority, I am talking about the force that ruins the lives of our Youthful Offenders. However, if I mention that everyone has the authority to introduce elements to the fictional world you are playing in, I am not referring to a fictional antagonist.
