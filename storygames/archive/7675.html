\n I'd like this to be interactive (duh!). I am going to post some of my thoughts and I am going to be brief, subjective and kinda vague. Help me flesh it out. Ask me questions. Help me figure out what you find in this kind of book. Let's work together to flesh out what Don't Lose Your Mind is really all about. Please no answers, only questions.

Don't Lose Your Mind
by Evil Hat Productions

So I want to start with two one liners:
"This is a gaming book worthy of the RPGPundit's Scorn!"

and the less funny/ironic:
"This book drove me to want to play Don't Rest Your Head, like NOW!"

So Don't Lose Your Mind is a supplement for Don't Rest Your Head. Most of you probably know that it covers the topic of Madness Talents. DLYM is all about exploring one of, if not THE major theme in DRYH. I can tell you from the outset that this book is well done and once you've read it I suspect you'll agree you needed it. Can you play DRYH without it? Sure. Will your DRYH game improve by reading this book? Absolutely! Not only will you learn some "tricks of the trade" and inspirational material for the game you'll also get a tighter idea of the setting while remaining totally player focused.

The production quality is really VERY good. The cover art is stunning and evocative. The internal art is even more disturbing and representative than the cover.
The type is interesting and easy to read. The layout is indictaive to the subject matter - it's maddening. Its TOTALLY Mad City. It has ryhme and reason while really not making sense. The book is written so that both covers are the "front" cover and meet in the middle. It has this really interesting playing card deck motif that could easily be used for auto generating Madness Talents.

The book goes from A to Z and describes over a score of madness talents. Some flavor text that makes a really narrative game rich and descriptions on how to bring the talent into play makes these entries significant tools for playing the game and easier entry for people used to lists of powers. It's also vague enough as to make your protagonist different and fresh and gives the player a fuck-load of creative input.

The very center of the book house some tidbits of info on how to choose and use madness talents in your game. Some of the advice is like "duh!" for story-gamers while others really flesh out how it all works in the game such as using responses more fluidly (planning someone's demise instead of just punching them in the face). There is some really interesting and innovative stuff that will really enhance most any game of DRYH. It also gives ideas on how to handle Nightmares in ways that make them more than just villains, per se. I won't look at Nightmares, in the game, quite the same way again.

If there is a Exhaustion Talent book that is a fraction as well done as this book I will buy it in a heartbeat. Way to pump life into an older game and I sincerely hope it brings a fresh audience to an older indie release. I am going to reward the architects of a spectacular book by putting the game back into my convention circuit.

Great job, guys,
- Don

    \n Judd, Don't Rest Your Head has the rule for becoming a nightmare (when you lose your last die of Discipline, you become a Nightmare with strength equal to the dice you used in your final roll) but Don't Lose Your Mind has 26 examples of what sort of Nightmare might develop from its 26 Madness talents. So it not only has 26 Madness Talents, it also has 26 Nightmares, plus an excellent discussion of how to choose and use Madness talents. Fun for the whole soul-devouring family!

    I like it a lot, even if I've only used 3 so far, to good effect: H is for Hands, K is for Knife, T is for Teddy.
