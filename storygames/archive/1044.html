Preface: I am not an expert on literary movements, but (for a CS major) was exposed to a lot of this stuff while in school.

So, there's this postmodern group of writers called Oulipo, which is short for "Ouvroir de littÃ©rature potentielle" - "the workshop of potential literature". Writers including Jose Luis Borges, Italo Calvino, Umberto Eco, and lots more, and they create works using 'constrained writing' - where they spark their creativity by imposing additional restraints on themselves. Read all about them here. More on other, similar movements here.

Maybe this has been covered before - I'm sure you can point me to threads if it has. But to me it looks a lot like the kinds of things I see going on in our little community with 24-hour RPGs, Game Chef, Ronnies, the BPG Owlbear challenge, and others. I see game designers spurring eachother to greater heights of creation by imposing little constraints - "your game must be done in 24 hours." So, it looks to me like there's an unofficial Ouropo movement - "Ouvroir du rolisme potentielle" (my French is rusty, but I've seen 'roliste' refer to 'rpg gamer' before). We are the Workshop of Potential Roleplaying.

If you agree with all that, what do you think we can learn from Oulipo and other such movements? If you disagree, why? Discuss amongst yourselves...

    Although I'm a booster the Oulipo has produced many more constraints than it has works that use those constraits. In fact the development of constraints in some ways is a purer exercise of what they are about, and the purest constraint of all would be one that is virtually impossible to use. Not to say that they didn't come up with some cool works. I'd put george perec's life a user's manual and raymond queneau's 100,000,000,000,000 sonnets at the summit. Indie game designers haven't addressed constraints in a rigourous 'movementy' way and have focused more on elements that generate playable games. Maybe one could view the ghostly morgue of unfinished games at the forge, rpg.net, and folks' hard drives as a set of unmet constraints.

    another caveat in the link to rpgs/story games is whether constraints are mainly challenges to game designers, mainly challenges to players in the game, or both.

    But yeah, spread the word. I used two ouxpo inspired constraints in my game Heads of State. A card pattern method of generating mysteries from the oulipopo (potential detective literature) forms the basis of The Disappeared and I use a form of perec's method of generating contents of rooms in his life: a user's manual in my the Palace Gates.
