[Misery Bubblegum] Why external conflict?

TonyLB
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Story Games

Okay, so I'm writing Misery Bubblegum, which is all about social uncertainty among kids who are intensely tied up in what everyone thinks about everyone else, and how their actions impact others.

No problem there. Joe never gets (certain) resources for anything he does ... he only gets resources for things other people do. If he hopes that Charlene will notice him then he gets Misery points when she ignores him and Happy points (or whatever better name I think up) when she notices him.

Charlene's player has no direct knowledge of how Joe gets rewarded, though she may (if she's attentive) figure it out. Or she may make up some entirely different theory ("Joe gets points when he pisses me off by getting in my way!") that is also supported by the facts that she's noticed.

There's lots more details of that, but there's the general gist, and I'm pretty pleased with that section of the game. What it doesn't immediately explain to me is why there should ever be any external conflict ... at all. I'm not even talking space-aliens crashing the Sadie Hawkins dance (although I totally approve of such things) I'm even talking things like "Who gets the top rank on mid-term exams?" or such mundane (but perhaps vital) questions.

It is vital for Joe that Charlene be striving with real adversity ... that makes her decisions genuine, often contradictory, quirkily human. It's what makes his attachment to her actions painful and human and dramatic.

But, absent any external conflict, it's not vital to Charlene that she be striving with real adversity. What matters to her is that (say) Blake the Hotty is striving with a fractured family life so that his response to her attempts at friendship (and maybe more!) are quirky and human and contradictory.

So I need some reward to draw people to invest in their own stories as well as paying attention to the actions of others, or else the whole thing is just going to be silly and flat.

Now I think I know what losing an external conflict buys you: It lets you make new opinions/hopes/desires about the winner. So if Arima beats Miyazawa out for the Valedictorian spot, she gets to create her seething hatred of him, and use that to fuel her later attempts to unseat him from primacy in the school. And, to some extent, that's a benefit to Arima's player too ... he's got another player who is intensely interested in his story.

But I get the impression that I need to create some additional benefit for winning conflicts. Currently the only equivalent to "skills" or "abilities" in MB is "Hangups" ... which are things you're so insecure or obsessed with that you ground all of your emotional energy (good and bad) through them. I could see staking a hangup on a given conflict ... Miyazawa stakes her "Need for Praise" on the valedictorian conflict, and the like. Maybe winning the conflict lets you change/strengthen/control the Hangup in some way?

Why do players go out and fight such conflicts? What is it they hope to gain? How can I reinforce that in the system, and get them salivatingly eager to go out and win?

    But reducing the misery cost of the conflict assumes that failing a conflict has a direct misery cost, which I'm not at all sure it should.

    But I totally agree that failure should be the key to reducing the intensity of your Hangups. I am right with you there. Here's an alternate thought. Since losing a conflict has you buy an opinion about another person, maybe reducing the strength of a Hangup lets you modify the strength of the opinion you buy about them. Like, if you lose a conflict and would normally make a 4 point opinion about someone else, then if you also reduce the relevant hangup then you can make a five point opinion about them instead. Your opinion is stronger, because it was formed in the process of them changing you.

    Or, maybe, as you said you reduce the strength of the Hangup to reduce the extent to which you form an opinion about them ... "They're not really that cool ... I didn't want to win that skateboarding competition anyway. Skateboarding's stupid."
