\n I was sitting around in #playnow, as is my wont, and chatting with Potemkin about western games. We talked a little about the games we've seen, their good points and bad points, and a bit about my love for Italo-westerns, as well. At which Potemkin said something quite interesting:\n
I'd love a western game that lives on cinematic theory in the same way PTA lives off televisual tropes: describing camera shots, musical scores, mis-en-sc\xe8ne, etc., etc.
\nYes, that would be a good game, wouldn't it?

The thing about Italo-westerns is that they're dripping with style. Even the shitty, no-budget knockoffs that clot the bloodstream of the Italo-western period have managed to retain at least some of what made the greats by Leone and Corbucci such effective pieces of cinema.

For the most part western games seem to follow in the footsteps of Boot Hill, and the resultant product is far more reminiscent of Gunsmoke than it is of Django. I'm not saying there's anything specifically wrong with this, but it's not the roleplaying experience I'm looking for when I say "western game." There has to be a mechanism for rewarding the inclusion of cinematic style in order for a game to step up and become something truly different, otherwise both the games and the players will be content to do what they've always done, which is really only halfway there.
