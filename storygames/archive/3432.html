    \nHere is the original post...
    \n
    \nI've been playtesting my own game and the following incident occured.
    \n
    \nIn one scene the protagonists were staying the night at a religious compound. A player who was roleplaying a renegade (ex-muskateer) was woken by crashing sound and went to investigate. As the Chronicler (GM) I decided that we should find out if his character is successful in his investigation. We discussed why we thought his character might succeed, some reasons we thought that his character might fail, what would be interesting for the story, and he told us how his character was going to go about investigating the noise. After this brief discussion we all added various amounts of coloured beads to a bag (depending on what they thought should probably happen) and the player roleplaying the renegade drew three of them at random. The result of the draw was a failure with positive consequences. Which basically means that the player got to narrate how his character failed in his investigation, but something good happened for his character nevertheless.
    \n
    \nSo the player narrated a scene where his character got lost and accidently stumbled in on a priest having sex with a boy. At this point, I stepped in and used my authority as GM to veto the scene unless the player had a damn good reason for it. The player claimed he was going to blackmail the priest and rescue the boy. But it was pretty obvious to most of us at the table that the player wanted to narrate the scene because he thought it would be amusing to have such a shocking and controversial scene. As far as humour goes, I think that nothing is off limits. But there's a time and a place for all kinds of humour and this wasn't it.
    \n
    \nMy decision to veto his narration resulted in a sulk that lasted for the rest of the session and soured the experience for some of the other players. It was as though I had stepped over the line by refusing to allow a player complete freedom in how he narrated his characters involvement in the scene.
    \n
    \nThe rules of my game states that the GM can veto players narration and story suggestions if they do no fit in with the setting, theme, or if they contradict facts established in earlier scenes. Although the GM has this veto, the GM cannot tell a player how to narrate their own characters involvement in the scene. There is also rules for changing the GM during play. This means if players feel the GM is abusing his/her authority or the GM or players just feel like having a change they can go ahead and get a new GM from amongst the players at the table.
    \n
    \nKeeping the players sense of humour aside, do I need to change my rules about GM's veto? I like the idea of players having freedom to narrate their own characters involvement in a scene. But I feel that if a player narrates an inapproriate scene (e.g. a laser cannon being used in a high fantasy setting) someone should be able to say that it is not allowed.
    \n
    \nShould I get rid of the GM's veto and give the players total narrative freedom?

    \n GM veto isn't the issue here. This is a social thing - a player pushed for a scene you weren't comfortable with.
    \n
    \nIf this happens, I find the best thing to do is not to use GM powers to shut it down, but just to talk honestly to the other player as another human being. Say "I'm not comfortable with this idea for a scene."
    \n
    \nQuestion: what did the rest of the players of the table seem to think about the scene idea? Did they all look uncomfortable, or were they all wanting to go for it?

    \n Yeah, for starters, this isn't GM "fiat" it's GM "veto". That's an important distinction. Veto is just saying "no". No one likes that. OTOH, fiat can be a powerful tool for good. I confess I don't see the humor in the example, but it seems like it could have been an interesting thread for the characters to explore. Knowing nothing about the setting, I'd look to why the priest was engaged in pedophilia. Was he possessed by a literal or figurative demon? It's also interesting that the player intended for his character to blackmail the priest (an unethical act, as opposed to killing him or turning him over to the authorities) and rescue the boy (an ethical act). I would have pushed very hard on that dichotomy. Make it difficult or even impossible for the character to do both.

    \n MJG - You have run headlong into one of the hard and impenetrable walls of communal improvisation: that in matters of taste people differ. Sometimes you will have people that occasionally, or consistently, contribute stuff that makes you go "good lord, that's fucking awful". Their jokes fall flat, their drama is empty, their conversation meanders, and so on. And there's someone on earth who would think the same thing about you, occasionally, or consistently. There is no solution which improves your enjoyment except to limit their contribution, which they will rightly see as unfair since that solution limits their enjoyment.

    Let me say that again: there is no solution which both keeps the "shared narration" thing going and makes everyone happy.

    Actually this is a problem with non-shared narration too, but that's a story for another thread.

    Meanwhile, what most people said is good. I think, though, that the problem is that you're thinking of yourself as the only "leader" in the game.

    Every player in your game should be able to step up and say "Woah, hold on, this is really squicking me in a bad, uncomfortable way. Time out. I have a problem here, so let's talk it over."

    It shouldn't be a GM-only tool and a GM solution, because that requires the GM to read minds and know when other players are getting squicked. It should be something that's open to everyone, not as a tool in the game as players but as human beings who have a right to feel safe and non-squicked while gaming.
