    \n The Upgrade! and Doubt have probably been played more than any others. Doubt is a wonderful exemplar; The Upgrade is designed to teach some jeepform skills like Bird-in-Ear.

    Previous Occupants is good scary unnerving fun.
    Lady and Otto is a frustrating and informative rumination on "conflict resolution".
    The Mothers and Drunk are both really intense, thematic, serious games. Fat Man Down and GR are even more intense, thematic, and serious. They feel much more like thought experiments, and worthwhile ones, but are rarely played for obvious reasons.
    Robin's Friends and Summer Lovin' are fun, accessible and full of emotionally resonant play.

    Hasimir said:

    What is (the publicly declared) premise of Journey? What is the game (allegedly) about?

    \nThe actual plot (and advertised blurb when I signed in to play) is about a family in a post-apocalyptic wasteland following a charismatic stranger on a journey to a promised land. It's post-apocalyptic in the vein of The Road, in themes, motifs and mood.
    I believe the plot would actually be fine - even really good - as a theatrical piece, novella or film... The way it is delivered, though, is as consciously "railroaded" role-playing. I can't say it doesn't successfully engage its themes of desperation, powerlessness, disability, gendered expectations and more - it does. But it's about as subtle as starting a discourse about torture by actually waterboarding people.
    Scene content which would in itself be "edgy" but entirely genre-appropriate is delivered as theatrical instructions, but in the second person typical of game-books and manuals. I found myself in a role where, while not instructed to perform any horrible acts myself - in fact, my role was mostly about inaction - I had to sit through things happening which were against my character's best interests in all possible ways - ethical, emotional, relational. I was instructed to find reasons not to try and prevent those things from happening, despite being painful both to my character and to the real me, and following these instructions was doubly painful to me. Still, the content was so viscerally affecting that I couldn't manage to just disconnect emotionally.
    I understand now that I should just have halted play and walked out. But I was then a novice to Jeepform and to emotionally-charged larping. I recommend anybody who wants to set up a run please introduce emotional and psychological safety measures: at the very least, make an effort to communicate very clearly to participants that they are allowed to walk out.

    \n Metatechniques around player safety have come a long way since Journey was published. At least in the US a sort of lingua franca has developed for short-form /parlor/jeepform larps - The Door Is Always Open, Cut/Brake or Cut/Largo, and often the OK check-in and Lookdown (sometimes with regional variations in name and substance). The absence of these tools is remarkable, which is wonderful. Many games build from there with, for example, touch boundaries or other game-specific metatechniques around safety. Debriefs are now very common as well.
