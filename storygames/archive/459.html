    Oh, and Stripper Resolution (as opposed to _stripping_ ) works like this:

    * Establish Conflict, declare Stakes, etc.
    * Participating players hold up a bill of any denomination.
    * The player whose bill is taken by a stripper first wins the conflict.

  • Jeph
    April 2006

    I've always wanted to use this drama game where one guy pretends to chop everyone up with a sword, and everyone else pretends to avoid being chopped. When the guy up front swings high, everyone else ducks. When the guy up front swings low, everyone else jumps. When he cuts to his right, everyone jumps to their right. When he cuts to his left, everyone jumps to their left. If you ever do the wrong thing, you lose! Oops!

    Hella fun exercise that I've done in various drama classes a half dozen times or so. Really builds up energy and stuff.

    Expand it out a bit, and it could make a kickass combat res system. Like, add in another half dozen attack and defense modes, and then a rule where using the wrong defense is, like, a glancing blow, and the complete opposite of the correct defense is a critical blow... and special character abilities open up more attack and defense modes...

      Did someone say I Ching? If Black Iron Dungeon-Crawl: The Philip K. Dick Fantasy Role-playing Game was an actual game and not something I just made up for my girlfriend's birthday, it would totally use I Ching as a resolution mechanic.

      I've had a couple ideas for games where your chance of success is determined by the number of words you use to describe your actions, i.e., in the heat of battle, "hide!" is more likely to succeed than "shoot anything that moves", which in turn is more likely to succeed than "take cover behind that boulder and try to lob a grenade into the machine gun nest," but nothing ever came of them.

      I had another idea involving characters who may or may not have had magical powers as children. The game would alternate between childhood fantasy flashbacks where you'd create a crayon drawing of your character using magic to defeat supernatural creatures and present day scenes where conflicts were resolved with traditional dice mechanics, but you could get bonuses by sacrificing your childhood memories--basically, you'd pick a drawing, come up with a mundane explanation for what "really" happened back then, and then tear up the drawing and throw it away.     • Lxndr
      July 2007
      "21 in the middle" or some variation thereof. For those who don't know this game:

      Everyone effectively does the "1 2 3" of rock paper scissors, but instead holds out 1 to 5 fingers on one hand. Then, starting with one person, you count around the circle until you reach 21, cycling multiple times if necessary. The person who is at 21 is the 'winner'.

      This is one of the things I've been considering for my "humans provide the randomization, not tools" kick. I like it 'cause it's a bit more involved than a simple odd/even game, and gets the entire group participating. Also, it's more difficult to predict as you get more people, where you'll finally land.
