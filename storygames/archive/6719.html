\n So I am now even more convinced that Shadowfell is a completely awesome introductory scenario. Why? Because the pregen characters with their abbreviated character sheet and the quick start rules REALLY are an excellent short version of the game. I was somewhat disappointed, having spent this weekend pouring through the PHB, at how complicated the full system really is.
\n
\nThere's a TON of fiddly bits in this game and the full on character sheet is just down right scary. The things that stand out to me the most as stuff I would have designed differently:
\n
\n1) Cut down a bunch on the exceptions and keep more to the core jist of things. For example, the new duration rules are great. Instead of tracking rounds and minutes and such you just make a save at the end of your turn to see if you're still affected. At least that's the core rule. In fact, there are so many exceptions to this in the PHB powers that its hard to see this as a "core" rule at all. Some powers last till the end of your next turn, some last to the beginning of someone elses turn, some last to the third alternate tuesday when the moon is full. Ok I exaggerate, but why have exceptions at all? Tracking which power ends at what time has the potential to be just as irritating as tracking rounds. And THEN there are rules that let you change your initiative order (delaying actions and ready actions)...The shear number of words they had to spend explaining what happens to the duration of effects when you futz with your initiative order (seems like they were WAY worried about someone delaying till the end and then getting 2 turns in quick succession) should have been a clue to just not allow you to futz with your initiative order. The fact that they didn't include any of these rules in the Shadowfell game is a good thing (and should have been their second clue).
\n
\n2) Having bonuses come from a multitude of sources is a good thing. But when you add on to that that each type of bonus falls into a different category and bonuses from the same category don't stack...now you have an extra thing to track. Its not enough to know you got +2 from this and +3 from that and +1 from the other. You also have to track the categories for each...If they're all from different categories you have a +6...if they're all from the same category you have a +3. If two are the same you have +5 or +3 or +4 (depending on which two). I guess in the world of D&D; 3.5 munchkins its essential to have such rules to keep the dumbasses from breaking the game...but geezelouise its a crying shame that the rest of us have to pay the price for it. Since I don't expect any of my players are going to spend the hours of their lives obsessing over rulebook minutia in an effort to squeeze undeserved bonuses into their character...I'm of a mind to say "screw the stacking limit, if they get a couple extra plusses they shouldn't have had whoopty-dee" Perhaps the categories will become second nature after enough plays...they actually were pretty straight forward categories...but the learning curve is already pretty steep in the full rules relative to the quick start.

    \n 3) Changing attribute scores. On the surface I like the notion of increasing attribute scores...but it does mean that nearly the entire character is going to need to be recalculated every time you do it. Mostly you're going to be increasing scores when they change a modifier, and you're going to be changing modifiers that you use alot, so all of those powers and skills that rely on that attribute for modifiers are going to have to be recalculated. That seems like a lot of unnecessary fiddlyness to me.
    \n
    \n4) The range of basic actions you can take on your turn seems really unnecessary. It looks like they went down the list of stuff you could do in 3.5 with the idea of keeping as much as they could instead of scrapping all the junk and demanding an action justify its own existance. Things like the "Double Move" are especially egregiously obnoxious. See you get 1 Move action automatically, and if you want you can demote your standard action to a Move so you can get two Move actions. So if you want you can take 2 full move actions on your turn. --OR-- you can do a "Double Move" which has a bunch of other restrictions and seems to exist for the sole purpose of allowing you to add your two moves into 1 bigger move instead of 2 seperate moves so that you don't get hurt from rounding down twice when moving through difficult terrain. Christ on a cracker. So some slow character who has a odd movement rate can move through 5 spaces of difficult terrain instead of 4 on their turn, they added 3/4s of a column worth of text. GAH. The PHB is full of stuff like that.
    \n
    \nThe Charge Action is also irritating. I can already do a full move and then an attack...why do I need another special action called a charge? Now I have fiddly things to remember like the limit of what space I'm allowed to charge to, the requirement to move at least 2 spaces, and a freebee +1 to-hit (that last is especially annoying...why not just call it a "Combat Advantage" accept it as a +2 like all the other combat advantages instead of making us remember that this bonus is only +1). Now I know why the Charge Action is important...because the Surprise rules only give the surprising party a single action (basically attack OR move but not both)\u2026so if you want to move and attack as the ambusher you need to Charge. How much cleaner would it have been to just let the ambush round be a regular old full round, and if someone wants to charge they can just move and attack normally without needing special rules.
    \n
    \nThere are other actions like this that could have been greatly simplified (and mostly were for the quick start rules).
    \n
    \n5) Feats, the feats are pretty inconsistent. Some of them are like \u201choly crap, I can do all that just for taking this feat?\u201d Others are like \u201cBig whoop, why would I waste a feat on that\u201d. Allowing for the likelihood that some of the big whoops actually are more useful than I\u2019m able to ascertain just from reading\u2026some are clearly not\u2026like powers that only activate when a critical is scored. I\u2019m also not really sure what the feats add. You\u2019ve got Racial Powers, Class Powers, Powers from magic items, and Powers from Feats. Not all feats provide powers but many do\u2026that kinda sorta makes sense if there are some powers that are class specific or race specific and others that anyone can take if they take the feat\u2026but that\u2019s typically NOT the case. There are quite a few class specific feats that provide powers. Huh? WTF. If they\u2019re class specific just make them freaking class powers to begin with. The whole feat system throws a TON of unnecessary fiddly schtuff into the game, especially character creation.

    \n 6) Rituals I was initially very excited about. Take all of the spells that go boom\u2026turn them into attack powers. Take all of the spells that let you do cool things in a battle and turn them into utility powers. Take all of the other spells and make them rituals. You keep your rituals in your spell book. All seems sensible so far. But then they throw a couple of bizarre wrenches into the works. First some of the rituals take ridiculous amounts of time to cast\u2026ok, I get it\u2026they\u2019re rituals. But then clearly some of those rituals (like Knock) would have been better served as Utility powers\u2026cause who in the heck is going to spend that kind of time opening a lock? The bigger and far worse wrench, however, is spell components. Spell components have been around since 1E. They were obnoxious pointless and stupid then, and they\u2019re obnoxious pointless and stupid now. The difference is that then, you just ignored them. Now they are an integral factor into the way rituals are balanced. See in order to cast a ritual you have to spend gold\u2026oh, I\u2019m sorry you have to have purchased large amounts of spell components. Some rituals that seem to have fairly minimal one time effects can cost as much as 45,000 gps to cast. Huh? Lets see\u2026buy a kick ass magic item that\u2019s far more useful than that and works over and over again\u2026or spend way more for a 1 shot spell. This is the first rule that strikes me as \u201cno one ever actually playtested this rule\u201d in the game. The others\u2026okay\u2026they\u2019re a different design philosophy than I would have used but I can see the logic behind them. This, this is just dum.
    \n
    \n7) The magic item economy. Ok. D&D; isn\u2019t a simulation, I get it. But some things are just sensible. In order to buy something you have to have someone willing to sell it. When you pay that someone you pay them the gold and they receive the gold. Unless you\u2019re trading a D&D; magic item that is. If you\u2019re trading a D&D; magic item you buy a magic item for full price, but sell it for only \xbd price. Ummm\u2026come again? If I pay 10,000 gps for a magic sword, that means the seller received 10,000 gps for the magic sword. But if I want to sell the magic sword\u2026I only get 5000 gps for it. Presumeably then the NPC who bought it can sell it to someone else for 10,000 gps\u2026right\u2026so I\u2019ll just wait and find that guy and sell my sword to him directly\u2026how about that. Nope. The rules are EXTREMELY clear on this (to the point of having several text boxes to clarify)...that\u2019s REALLY dum.
    \n
    \nBut wait, it gets better. If you\u2019re a wizard you can use up valuable feat slots to gain the ability to enchant magic items. Of course you have to pay for the materials\u2026but get this, the materials cost the same as the full price of the item. So I can make my own magic sword\u2026but it will cost me 10,000 gps to do it\u2026the rules actually go out of their way to say that its better to just BUY the sword. Umm, why then would I waste a feat on being able to make one myself. And further\u2026why then would there be magic swords for sale. If it costs some wizard 10,000 gps to make a sword\u2026and they can then only sell it to me for 10,000 gps\u2026then essentially all their hours of labor was\u2026free? How does that work. Scrolls work the same way. Buy a scroll for 1000 gps or copy it yourself for 1000gps\u2026potions\u2026yep, same way.
    \n
    \nOk, I can see how they didn\u2019t want wizards to become money making machines by simply creating enchanted items and selling them like some merchant\u2026but my god, there are better ways of accomplishing that.
    \n
    \nConclusion: Ok, no despite all these negatives, my ardor for getting to actually play this game has not diminished. But it has disappointed me. The quick start rules are actually WAY better (except for the couple three things they referenced but forgot to explain) then the full rules. The idea that the designers may have actually created a fast playing streamlined engine of fun now has to be measured against a fair amount of head scratching...WTFs. But, that said, I suppose the number of WTFs pales in comparison to the mess that was 2ed or the huge sprawling morass that was 3E. But man-o-man\u2026to come SOOOO close and fall short can sting way more then just being totally off.
    \n
    \nI think what this means for me is that to have a hope in hell of playing this with my group I\u2019m going to need cards. Cards for all the powers, including the feat powers. Cards for all the rituals. And especially cards for all the basic actions (like second wind, and bull rush). There just isn\u2019t enough (any) space on the character sheet to write down any details of these things, and there\u2019s no way anyone is going to remember them.

    \n 4e is like Burning Wheel in one way: Don't try to play with everything in the book right out of the gate.

    Forget all those exceptions and fiddly movement rules and such. Like you say, they are there for the hardcore. You don't need them. If you do ever need them for some strange reason, then look them up. Otherwise, fall back on +2/-2 situational modifiers on the fly. After playing for a while, you might start to layer some extra stuff in, or not, depending on taste.

    The one thing that is hard to leave out for later is Triggers on powers. There are many different sorts (when enemy is adjacent, when enemy misses melee attack, when you take damage from a ranged attack, etc.) and tracking them is a pain. I think I'm going to make little Trigger cards and put them out in front of my sheet so they're easy to see and everyone can help monitor them.

    The weird item economy is indeed weird. If you try to make it sim-y ur head esplode.

    \n What John said. D&D4, for all its streamlined-ness, is still part of the school of design that says "Throw a bunch of rules at it and groups will decide what sticks." They're expecting play groups to act as a filter for a lot of that stuff, deciding which elements are fun and interesting and which stuff will be ignored. The quickstart may fool folks into thinking they've caught the Dread Indie Disease and only included rules that they expect people to actually use all the time. But that's clearly not the case, as you can see when reading the core books. It definitely sucks, if you come from a Diseased perspective and don't want to spend hours of your life figuring out whether you like XYZ sets of rules or whether they are, in fact, optional or whether they will cause all sorts of other problems when you take them out.

    But... for your purposes, Mike, where minor or even major advantages here and there don't really matter, where you can compensate for one lop-sided fight by making the next one tougher or easier or what have you, where you can renegotiate what an ability does after you've played with it for a bit, then it doesn't really kill your game, it just means you spend more time worrying about the rules than you'd ideally like, more time that you're used to in most of the Diseased games we've been playing. So yeah, really lame in some ways, but D&D4 was never going to be the crunchy version of Agon that many of us were hoping for, I don't think. Guess we'll have to hack that together ourselves.

    \n Complexity that doesn't add more fun to a game than it kills is the fecund kudzu of game design. It's like it comes from nowhere as you're working on a game. You identify a problem, and fix it with a rule. Lather, rinse, repeat, and pretty soon you've got live kudzu and dead fun.

    It seems to me the success of 4e depends on the answer to three questions:

    1. Are people going to want to play it
    2. Are people going to want to run it
    3. Are people going to want to design for it

    We played some of Keep on the Shadowfell yesterday. If the full game plays like Shadowfell, no doubt in my mind the answer to all three questions is yes. The great challenge of designing a face-to-face RPG is designing the social landscape. It's clear from just three combat encounters that Shadowfell nails it. Does the Dragonborn Paladin do her roar (or whatever the hell it is) if a wounded comrade will be caught in the effect? Do I use a Healing Surge now, or power through and hope my next attack takes the opponent down? The social concern of personally demonstrating effectiveness, playing for the good of the team, winning, losing, aiding each other, all collide and clash to produce meaningful personal and social rewards and consequences for fun gameplay. Shadowfell felt like a "just the good parts" version of my heaviest D&D phase (basically, when I was in high school). And there was no kudzu. Just fun. No small achievement.

    \n Ralph, a lot of the things that displease you were apparently far more of a problem in 3.X edition:
    2 - stacking and non-stacking was a huge problem; apparently less so now

    3 - changing attribute scores -- this was a huge problem in 3.X, because it would happen quite often in a combat, and essentially forced you to recalculate everything on the fly. It looks like this isn't an issue now, except once every other level.

    5. Feats have always been pretty inconsistent. Frankly, why bother with Feats at all? Fold that stuff into your class features or powers.

    7. The economy - apparently this has been ridiculous in the past too.

    Also, creating characters in 4e is enormously faster and more efficient than doing it in 3.X. Inspired by Ryan's E6 hack, I tried fooling around with basic 5th level PC's in 3.5, and figuring out all their stuff took a long time. Building NPC's and monsters was a huge drag. To make matters worse, I'm one of those guys who says, "Hey, they went to a lot of trouble to make these particular sub-systems internally consistent - let me figure out what the DC for this skill really ought to be, or let me figure out what a Wizard's Tower really ought to have inside it based on these rules." It was amazingly plausible, but also a huge amount of time at the design stage. In 4e, a lot of that stuff is much faster and easier, even if the implications are really bizarre.

      \n I just read through a friend's copy of Keep on the Shadowfell. I think the things that make me wary about it aren't the combat mechanics. Those seem extremely well thought out. Ralphs complaints about charge and combat advantage, for example, don't make sense to me, since the interaction of sneak-attack, shifting, and so on, lead to some really interesting tactical choices in play for relatively little complication. I'm talking about things like Kobold being able to charge, shift as a move, then use their free shift to get behind someone, setting up their buddy for a sweet charge and flanking +3, or the Rogue not being able to sneak attack charging someone's face, but if they can get to someone's flank and charge, they get +3 and their sneak attack damage. Sweet! The way they've broken up actions makes a lot of sense to me too, allowing for the same kind of board-gamey tactical decisions, and letting a lot more happen between rounds and interrupting.

      What dissappointed me about the text was that there seems to be very little advance, and in fact a great deal of backsliding, in terms of how to actually use all this stuff to make an adventure. I feel like 3rd ed. for all its faults, had a really cohesive set of rules. Anything a character did in the setting was covered by a set of rules. That creates its own problems, but it did mean that everything that happened in the game world was the result of interactions with the rules. 4e seems to me to be harking back to an older style of play, where a lot of actions outside (and inside) of combat are covered simply by Drama resolution between GM and players. 4e seems to have revived the concept of "roleplaying skill" in the gamist sense of using creative ability to think around challenges. This manifests in really concrete ways (the explicit encouragement of puzzles and riddles in the DMG, and their inclusion in KotS) and more subtle ways (there are no skill rolls called for during the Winterhaven scenes, just roleplaying).

      Now maybe I'm just still burned by this style of play, and maybe it can be awesome in the right situation, but to me all it reminds me of is hours of wandering around the same freaking village looking for the "right" NPC to talk to, knocking up against some bullshit puzzle in the middle of a dungeon, or being punished for not thinking of the "obvious" solution to whatever problem the GM has cooked up. In D&D3, all of that stuff would be covered by a skill roll. In D&D4, I feel like outside of encounters (skill challenges and combat), there's very little interaction with the rules at all, and it'll rely on the problematic concept of "player skill" to overcome obstacles. Perhaps the DMG clears this up, but that's the impression I got from the rules given in KotS, and the way that adventure was designed.

      Can anyone confirm that?

      \n Simon, I had the same problem you did when I ran KotS a few weeks ago. While the combat encounters were great, the adventure fell down in helping players find the next bit of fun. There were some bits of GM advice about "moving the story along" (the section of the book was even called something like that) that were somewhat railroady and the in-town parts of the adventure were organized poorly. They tried to help DMs by organizing the "investigation" part by topic -- each question that PCs might ask had its own section and under it were all the answers that different NPCs would give. In actual play, PCs tended to ask the same NPC a bunch of questions, and that made me jump around the book: I had to figure out who this NPC was, then jump to each question's section when they asked that. I'd rather have just had all the questions answered in each NPC's section.

      Reorganizing the adventure wouldn't have made it less railroady, though. With very little effort, the KotS book could have dropped adventure opportunities in front of the PCs without the senseless role-play surrounding figuring out where to go next.

      To get back on-thread, though, I think the DMG contains plenty of advice about this kind of stuff. It's not all perfect, as others have pointed out, but if the author of KotS had followed more of the DMG's advice, it would have benefited.
