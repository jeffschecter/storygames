\n Heya

I was at my local Barnes and Noble yesterday with my wife picking up some children\u2019s books for my daughter. While she is debating Peter Rabbit vs. Little Ms. Chatterbox, I wander over to the RPG section. I know there\u2019s nothing there I want, but I do like to peruse the covers and see what\u2019s being sold in my area.

There\u2019s a fairly large DnD section, to be expected. There\u2019s probably a dozen little adventure modules. There\u2019s some PHB\u2019s and other core books and some campaign settings. At the top shelf, something catches my eye. It\u2019s a beige cover illustrated with a purple sash and a curved dagger tucked in behind it. It\u2019s called Legend of the Burning Sands.

I remember well the CCG called Burning Sands from back in the 90\u2019s. I was huge into CCGs back then. I played nearly twenty different ones back then, so I pick the book off the shelf and check the back. I guessed it was produced by AEG, and the logo on the back confirms my suspicion.

I flip through the inside. It\u2019s attractive. The layout is clean. There aren\u2019t many illustrations, but that doesn\u2019t mean the inside was boring. I did find a brief reference to the CCG inside, so that made me happy. I compare it to the other books around and decide Burning Sands is fairly unique compared to what else is being sold in this area.

After flipping through a hundred pages or so, I decide to check the price. FORTY BUCKS!!!!! With my free hand I grab the DnD PHB: $35. I weigh each book in my hands and put myself in a buyer\u2019s shoes. The real difference between the two games is mostly color. You can create the Burning Sands experience for DnD by hitting Wikipedia and using your imagination. Plus, I looked at all the adventure modules for sale. Anywhere from $10 to $20. It\u2019s so much easier.

A thought works its way into my head. \u201cIs this how it ends?\u201d I ask myself. AEG is a venerable company, the inheritor of FRP. But I wonder how it can compete for entertainment dollars anymore with games like Burning Sands. $40 can get you a lot of entertainment, not just in RPGs but anywhere. Just in B&N there were gobs of graphic novels behind me. $40 will get you two or three of them. Maybe more. Just to my left were the Twilight novels. Thoughts of Xboxes and Wii\u2019s danced through my head. How can Burning Sands- which doesn\u2019t have the heritage L5R or DnD have- compete with all this? Is the mainstream RPG market doomed to dwindle into nothing outside of DnD? Is DnD Insider really the future of what our hobby will look like?

Anyone else ever feel this way when they look at gaming books at stores?

Peace,

-Troy

    \n Small press books in full color are either mass-produced, like mass-produced, or they're mad expensive to produce, especially if you're going to sell them at retail. Most niche-publishing books of similar format in color (think aviation stuff, but there are probably other examples) that go through retail have a price point of $60 at least, and the people who buy them get it - they understand that that's what a nice, full-color niche book has to cost. Mainstream gamers don't get this for some reason. They demand books be cheaper, but these days they're at least willing to pay roughly the same for black and white. AEG probably still won't make money on the book.

    The PHB is mass-produced, but not in big enough numbers to really bring its price down to $35. That's an artificially cheap loss leader right there.

    \nIt makes me think that creating the same game over and over again -- only altering the color -- is a poor model.

    There is very little differentiation between most RPGs. Sure, one generates a percentile result by rolling a twenty-sided die and another generates a percentile result by rolling two ten-sided dice. One has hit points and the other has hit points that result in minor penalties as they're ticked off. Etc. The color is different. The names are different. But the games are pretty much the same.

    However, I think some mainstream publishers are starting to push the boundaries. WotC is a great example. Whether you like 4e or you hate it, WotC clearly put a lot of thought into the experience that they want D&D to generate and designed the game to do that. It works differently than the other games out there.

    I haven't seen it myself, but it sure sounds like Fantasy Flight is doing something different with Warhammer Fantasy Roleplay 3e!

    Weapons of the Gods is a game from a few years ago that I also think made a serious effort to create a unique experience. It's not without its problems, but there's a lot of interesting stuff there.

    Sure, it's possible that mainstream RPGs wither and die. But it's also possible that these companies are going to have to actually design a new game to stay in business.

    \nArguing with other people about what THEY ought to value and how they ought to value it is a mug's game, whether it's you trying to convince me that RPGs are a rip-off, or it's me trying to convince you they're a great value.

    The product is priced in a perfectly reasonable way when compared to similar products in the marketplace that serve similar functions. If enough people don't agree that there's sufficient value for them to buy it, then they'll adjust, either by tinkering with the pricing structure, or altering their business model to deal with the smaller user base.

    There's also all sorts of biases that go into these arguments. If I run a 3-year Burning Sands campaign out of just the core book, that costs me 40 bucks. If I get Netflix for 3 years, that's north of $300, plus the cost of a TV and DVD player. Now, those are 'sunk' costs, of course, but they are a required investment. Then again, if I buy snacks at every game, is that a 'cost' of the game, or would me and my buddies have had pizza that night anyway because we all got together to squeal over Twilight? And does THAT count as a cost associated with the movie?

    The rapidly-changing media and entertainment market we're living through certainly makes things interesting (especially for me as a retailer of 'traditional' hobby games), and we're certainly seeing the midlist rattle out as the entertainment market deals with the lowering of entry points that result in a glut of product. But I think that projecting your own ideas about what constitutes appropriate entertainment value onto the entire market is probably over-generalizing.

    -Jim C.

    \n That's where my thoughts are with this too. If I'm coming to it cold, I am not considering making a $40 purchase on the assumption that I and 4 of my friends will be sinking fortnightly Sundays for 6 months into exploring this product. I am thinking: is this something I will play? (or even just:read?).

    Consequently I think a nice pricing approach would be to place it closer to other single-session activities, tweaking as necessary for shiny stuff (if it has a colourful board and miniatures in the set, then price closer to a high end board game. If not, price closer to a standard fiction book).

    The industry tends to price closer to an expert text than a mainstream book. In some ways I can understand why but I think it's doubly offputting: firstly the raw price and secondly the fact that you only pay that much for "special books" - coffee table or technical.

    If it's got the coffee table feel - luxurious, easy to open and browse - then okay, it might be earmarked as a present for that special someone into that special something, but it's unlikely to be picked up on a whim.
    If not, then it screams "serious business! not for casual users!" - which is a shame.
