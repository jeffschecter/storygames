\n I can live with a -1 or -2 in STR or DEX if I'm a caster type. I could go with a negative CON if I'm perhaps a little sickly, and that -1 WIS just mean's I'm foolhardy. -1 CHR is a little awkward, but it just means that my character doesn't care much for diplomacy, sure.

But I don't want to take a -1 INT, and certainly not -2!! Oh noes!! So why is that?

My current theories:

- I've got nerd hangups about the supremacy of INT.

- I want to avoid being pidgeonholed into "dumb character" RP, e.g. "Oh I failed that roll because I got distracted by my shoes." That shit ain't heroic.

- I'm a power-gaming min-maxing roll-player.

Other theories? Are there any stats where you'd rather not take a penalty to your abilities?

    \n The part I don't get about playing intentionally dumb is that D&D is fucking hard to succeed at without tying one hand behind your back already, so to me it seems like fucking with your party members for no good reason if a player plays dumb. "I know how we might all survive here, but I guess I'll just stay silent and let the party die - that'll show everybody how good I am at roleplaying." Obviously this is all dependent on higher-order details about how you play and so on (I'm not trying to naysay Rob or anything), but hike the level of difficulty and stakes high enough, and nobody feels like underplaying themselves. Players manage boundless stupidity and passive plays without any sort of conscious decision to do so often enough that adding any more makes their party seem like a travelling clownery. If players were capable of being too good, then I'd feel the need to down-play for variety and true-to-life characterization, but that's a miracle I've yet to see.

    Also, another Ability I use in my D&D homebrew is "Wits". It's sort of the other side of Intelligence: not about education, but about how quickly and accurately your character perceives and thinks. If you'll look at my choice of mental attributes (Wits, Education, Will and Charisma), you'll note that I've specifically selected a set that does not impose limitations upon the ability of the players to make the best choices they can: your character might be uneducated and often miss important details, and he might be slow and hesitate in the face of adversity, but at least in principle he knows just as well as anybody (you, specifically) what should be done in a given situation. He might not have the means to execute your brilliant play, but at least he knows what a stronger person might try. This logic of refuting Intelligence and Wisdom works for me, it is appropriate for the separation of character and player that is inherent to tabletop roleplaying: however much you'd like it, you are not your character, and the basic relationship is most functionally considered a guiding one: you are the guiding intellect and wisdom behind your character's actions. Your character won't listen to his intellect at all times, but you are not the one suffering from fatigue, hunger, injury and fear in a dark dungeon, so your relationship to your character is already irrecovably compromised: it makes no sense for you to try to pretend that you're your character. The best we can do is let you empathize with your character and suggest courses of action to him, and he will execute these to the limits of his abilities and lower urges.

    The above is obviously entirely different from an immersive philosophy where we want players to feel that they are their characters. From that viewpoint it might make sense for the players to intentionally put labels on their characters and then strive to play a particularly "wise" or "headstrong" or "smart" character. I just don't think that this works too well, or that it is very interesting in the D&D context: you're problem-solving all the time, adding a need to do the problem-solving with somebody else's brain instead of your own is just too much of a curve-ball. Drop the problem-solving and play it like a drama game, and I can see more sense in it.

    \n Oh, I should clarify the above by saying that players will, of course, develop a personality for their characters. This is natural and commendable.     \n OK, I've been lurking this thread since it's started, and it's given me some food for thought, and awoken some fond memories of dull-witted characters. I might be preaching to the choir here, but here's my perspective on this, based on my experience with 2.5E...

    It's a great time, (if a little heavy on the paperwork,) simply because all of these dense, obscure mechanics make great things happen at the table once you just accept them as tools, roll with them, and trick yourself into a suspension of disbelief despite the incongruities, metagame hangups and philosophical flaws that come to mind when doing so. Then again, I've always had a kind of absurdist approach to D&D, and I probably love to gamble with the life, health and sanity of my character more than most people would care to. I love using the numbers, tables and rules to just make a lot of silly stuff happen. There is so much info! So many things to play with that will give your character all the depth you could ever want, if you're just able to cut loose and drift with the gaming table's tide.

    To put my blathering into context, I'll relate briefly the story of Grompy, my most recent victim deceased character. He was a lawful-good ogre-mage shaman (I had this heavy backstory made up for him, explaining how the hell that combo came to pass, but I won't regale you with it now,) with an Intelligence score of 5, and 19 Charisma. For me, that dichotomy alone said so much about him, and it allowed me to make a really cool, surprisingly deep character that followed a charming and engaging arc towards enlightenment and acceptance... which culminated in him donning a bathtub as a helmet and flying headfirst into a dragon's mouth. And you know what? I was fine with that. He had been wandering in the desert in spirit-animal (flying squirrel) form for a few weeks, had a few epiphanies, and had just found his way back to the party in a way that gave some real closure to the way his moral and social codes had been evolving, and the heroic (Quixotic) moment with the dragon and the bathtub just felt sooo right, because I had this picture of him in my mind's eye that I had constructed over a long period of playing, not in spite of the simplistic, clunky stats, but rather based on their simplistic clunkiness, and how it inspired me. Long story short, he died a horrible death, spectacularly failing to choke the dragon with a bathtub, and we laughed and cried and ate crisps, in the highest of spirits, while I rolled up a new character. Grompy's animal companion, Belvedere the Boar, is now my pixie wizard's familiar.

    So, my method for the "cerebral stat" hangups, and D&D hangups in general: Take whatever mechanical meat you can get, make it your own, and perhaps most importantly, share your vision with the group. Unless your GM is running a meat-and-potatoes, static-character dungeon crawl, or unless you go too far and you're sabotaging the group's story arc with your vision, he or she will probably at least be able to meet you halfway. It might be hard for the more experienced and cynical gamers to get over the age-old problems like worn-out tropes and anachronisms, blatant mechanical fixes with no in-world justification, etc, but you have to turn a blind eye to these things if you want to maintain the spark of engagement that is the fuel for any game. Use your built-in "IGNORE"\xa0function, and just play it where it lands.

    \n I skipped from AD&D to Pathfinder. So I'm writing from the perspective of Pathfinder, and this might not be relevant.

    What does real-world intelligence correspond to?\n
      \n
    • Breadth of knowledge? That's mostly the class skill list and class skill points.
      • \n
    • Depth of knowledge? Again, class skill list. Also character level.
      • \n
    • Having a clue? That's Wisdom (and its associated skills Perception and Sense Motive)
      • \n
    • Cleverness and wit? That's usually up to the Player, not the PC he or she plays.
      • \n
    • Problem-solving? Ah! That's finally what "Intelligence" gives you in Pathfinder. With minimal dabbling in any field you can tinker and problem-solve with the results a professional or expert would expect.
      • \n
    \nSo my guess is that valuing Intelligence is either unrelated to game mechanics or because you (well, we actually) value PCs that have problem-solving ability.
