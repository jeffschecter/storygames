\n I'm looking for some tricks and tips, some new ways to do old things, some advice for GMs and Players. Help a brother out!

So this thread is not about playing RPGs with different languages.

It's about ways to explore language perhaps in a fantasy setting, and things that we could do to make the process of language more interesting.

For example, my D&D character can speak Human, Elven, and Abyssal.
My Shadowrun character presumably speaks Japanese and Portugese, with some English.

Normally, in games, this means:

GM: There is writing on the wall in Dwarven. Does anyone have that language?

or

GM: There are two people in the corner speaking quietly. Can anyone's character speak French?

With the usual success and failure conditions. If you have that right language that just happens to fit the adventure, a metaphorical doorway opens. Otherwise, it remains closed and blocked. So, traditionally, as a player I tend to choose "Common" and nothing else (cause it's too easy to pick the "wrong language for the adventure/mcguffin" unless languages are free or are defined by my character's background. As a GM, I never really have characters speak foreign languages unless they are doing so to enhance the mystique of speaking a language no one understands, or unless I see that someone took "Halfling" and I want to throw them a bone for choosing it.

So what can we do to make languages more exciting? Specifically:

1) What can we do in play to make different languages interesting?

2) As a GM, what way can we use them other than a "you have it or you don't" switch for puzzles and secret info and stuff?

3) What can we do to encourage players to choose different languages, without fear of choosing the "wrong one"?
-- (obvious fix: If your CoC explorer has "Farsi" written on her sheet, then by God have Iranian artifacts in the game. But aside from that, what else is there?)

Looking for creative suggestions, an exploration of how to make language and language choices fun and interesting in a game, be it D&D or Transhuman Space.

-Andy

    \n A few days ago I was thinking about how, in Nobilis, the existence of the Mythic World lets you solve almost anything socially: just switch perspectives and speak to the spirit of a thing. Raging storm? Ask it what it wants and offer to go get that in exchange for it abating its fury. That sort of thing.

    Then I thought, this would be a pretty cool system of magic: just what you could talk to, and how persuasive you were. "Nature" magic would be talking to animals and plants and things, "arcane" magic could be more inanimate objects and types of energy (and maybe the ability to encode the mystic tongues into written form), "Divine" magic would be, maybe, relationships and communities and abstract concepts? "Hey, Marco's Guilt, how's it going?" "Man, you wouldn't believe what this guy is up to. He doesn't listen to me at all." "Oh really?"

Or the thief who picks up the language of shadows to aid him in his profession. "Darkness, cloak my passage!" "What's in it for me?" "I'll extinguish half the lanterns I run into on my way back out of the building when I'm done." "Deal. Oh, hey, somebody's trying to sneak up behind you, by the way."

    \n language is so much more nuanced than it is presented in most RPGs. Its not just knowing or not knowing. We generally have three different vocabularies. There's language we can understand when we hear it, there's language we can use in speech, and there's language that we can write. They're not the same thing and people aren't equally good at them.

    Also, often a word or phrase can have two or more meanings, giving you a different translation depending upon the choices you make in deciphering the information. I think a lot more could be made of the potential for sensible but erroneous readings. You might think you completely understood and be completely wrong. You might know both (or more) readings and have to pick one.

I know I mostly want it to be handled as primarily by story, with some mechanical suggestions & guidelines, but I haven't figure out how to wrap my head around that yet. I'll be watching this thread.

    \n I find myself thinking of Call of Cthulhu (and Lovecraftian language use in general):

    You find an arcane tome - desirable information.
    It's in an ancient dialect of Hebrew - a difficulty level to interpret the text.
    If you get a weak success, you understand some of it but not a key element, which will bite you in the ass later - a risk.
    If you get a solid success, you can effectively use it - a reward.
    If you get a phenomenal success, you can use it and even generalize the info for other uses... but you lose Sanity, as your mind reels from the dark revelations - a trade-off.

    Is that sufficiently different from the binary state of "you know it or you don't" to make language mechanics interesting again? At the least, it's another augmentation of the non-binary language uses suggested above (non-comprehension -> misinterpretation -> partial comprehension -> full comprehension).

    \n Interesting uses for language:

    * Language as membership: In this case, language is a representation of a growing affinity for an alien culture. Example: 13th Warrior -- Antonio's character becomes more accepted among the barbarians as he picks up their language. Another example would be any case in which a subgroup identifies itself by its jargon -- if you can drop the appropriate jargon, you gain access to that group.

    * Language as understanding: Some story facts may simply be inaccessible without a matching language. I'd assume this would fall into stuff like social relationships (X owes Y something, which you can't know w/o speaking the language), language-triggered effects (magic, voice-activated technologies, memes that don't translate correctly, psychic constructs that work only in certain languages).

    * Language as obfuscation: Perhaps you can turn language into an offensive tool, a way to hide your intent. If you're not talking, the guy checking you out will have to resort to his own observation of you: You're carrying a sword and your shirt's covered in blood, I'll bet you're the murderer! But if you can speak the language (and/or use the jargon), then you could actually bypass objective observation and impose a subjective perception onto a target.

    * In Actual Play, I've always enjoyed providing unreliability of information due to partial understanding of language. This might be as subtle as not understanding nuances, or as hardcore as simply misreading "left" for "right".

    \n The two ways in which I could see language being used in a game in a more interesting manner:

    1) Assign spells/special abilties to having the language.

    Obviously, Clinton kicked open the door with Zu on that, but you also see it in one of the D&D magic supplements as a separate form of magic. But- let's take that a step further- what if only certain spells can be cast in certain languages? For example, Protection against Evil requires knowing Celestial? Then languages become this fascinating stepping stone to making strategic choices and a way of dividing up spells into groups so that no one can have a full range.

    2) Cultural markers

    As Ryan points out- not knowing a language, or speaking it poorly, can simply mark you as an outsider.

    But let's take it from foreign situations to simply current living situations. There's a whole lot of politics about whether you speak a second language in the US. And it swings from whether you're marked as an outsider to even how folks in your own group might consider you. Language is tied into culture and identity, and that opens doors for interesting stuff to happen.

    \n One of my favorite parts of our Blossoms supplement is the language section. It's not about speaking foreign languages though.

    We divided all speech into four types of language: Plain, Polite, Respectful and Humble.

    Plain language is used with close family or when drunk. It's about jokes, camaraderie, insults and threats. It can be used to discuss any topic except the health, well-being, mental state of, or concern for a superior.

    Polite language is the default mode of speech.

    Respectful language is used to speak to superiors. When using it, a character may not refer to himself or his own needs; also, the character may not curse, utter profanities or speak in anger or harsh tones.

    Humble language is used when the speaker is attempting to aid or help the recipient of the speech. When using it, the speaker cannot claim credit for an action, boast or brag, or speak in anger.

    Rules-wise, when entering into a conversation with someone, either side has the ability to call for an Etiquette test in order to set the level of language appropriate for the interaction, with the caveat that someone who is of lower rank, even when victorious in the Etiquette test, may not choose to set the level of speech lower than polite.

    Once the level of language is set, breaking the conventions incurs a +2 Ob to the social skill test he is roleplaying.

    Alternatively, the winner of the Etiquette test may choose to set the level of language to Verse. If you choose to take a conversation to Verse, you can ignore the speech constraints. However, violence and war are frowned upon as poetic topics and inclusion of such would be justification for a Disadvantage.

    The rules are pretty simple, but they create some very cool effects in play.

There are different levels of language that can be used in a Duel of Wits - informal, formal, poetry, etc - and each one restricts what can be said. So you can maybe convey the same information using different styles of language, but the way you do it is different in each.

    These are all dialect versions within a larger language, but the concept can be used between languages as well. The better you know a language, the more complex you can make your communications, the more complex the ideas you can impart. And of course, the better you know a language, the better you understand that society, because their social structure and their ideas about the world are all present in their language. Nowadays, you can learn a language through audio recordings and movies. In Ye Olden Days, you'd have to learn it from somebody else, and so you would learn about them along with their language.

    \n Hm. Let me explain a bit more.

    French used to be the most prestigious language in Europe. Educated people all over learned French. For a Russian aristocrat to speak Russian to another Russian aristocrat was an insult, because it was a peasant's language. They would speak French. This situation lasted a long, long time, pretty much picking up from when educated people all spoke Latin, and trailed off when the British Empire and the US came to dominate the world economically and culturally.

    So if you grew up learning proper French in this period, why would you want to learn another language? After all, anybody worth talking to speaks French! A slight exaggeration, of course, but that's the mindset. All the other countries in Europe can remember a time when somebody else's language was more important than their own, usually several levels of other languages. In Italy, for example, you have lots of regional dialects (like piedmontese), and then old Latin was replaced by the Florentine dialect, which became the model for modern "Italian" and then the French and the Austrians and the Spanish all conquered sections of Italy, and French was important world-wide, and then English, later. So it is not surprising that Italians (or most Europeans, really) would see the utility in speaking multiple languages.

    The British ushered in the age of English dominance. But they could (and can) still remember when somebody else's language was king. Even at the height of empire, knowing French (and Latin, and Greek) was an important part of an English education.

    Interesting Language Use 1: The Language of the Empire

    This mimics 1700 europe: each broad subject of conversation in high class circles has a language attached. Art and Music is discussed in Italian, Romance and Politics in French, Science and Philosophy in Latin, business in English, Diplomacy in German (or something like that: change according to your setting and taste). Now, you need proficiency in many languages to be a real social monkey: discussing of a subject in the wrong language is seen as uncouth and inefficient, and not only can give you minuses to your rolls, but could even cause miscommunications, since some languages lack the correct words for some subjects!

    \n While it was a big thread-drift, the discussion about the social ramifications of knowing a second language in the US contains a GREAT idea for use of language, in particular with other applications of it mentioned above:
    * Designate some languages as "socially stigmatizing" (Orcish or Demonic) and others as "socially promoting" (Elvish or in politics or arts; Gnomish and Dwarvish in sciences).

Thus, there's a stimulus to gain languages one might otherwise skip in favor of languages with more immediate benefits (like learning a bunch of monster's tongues to be able to deal with/persuade/con them).

    \nLots of good stuff here, and I'm going to work back through a lot of points on this thread.     \n
    \n
    \nConsider that even in everyday use of your best known language with other competent users of that language, that problems in interpretation occur. In fact, the science of communication tells us that it's not really a question of binary understanding of one another, but every attempt at communication is just a question of how badly it went. If you take my meaning. No communication is perfect, and most is seriously flawed in one or more ways.
    \n
    \nConsider, for example, that often we as speakers aren't really even sure what it is that we're trying to communicate. Our idea may not be well formed. Then there's the attempt to put it into words, which often fails to some extent. Then there's the attempt by the reader to obtain from the words transmitted the meaning. Which can be foiled not only by a lack of conveyed meaning from the broadcaster, but also by simple mechanical problems with hearing and other various impediments. How often have you listened to somebody and not really made out some word that you heard, and not asked for clarification for whatever reason, instead trying to discern the meaning of the sentence from it's incomplete context?
    \n
    \nThese sorts of things happen constantly, and, again, that assumes two "native" speakers.
    \n
    \nA real question with any language is whether or not the user actually thinks in the language. I can speak Spanish... badly. When speaking the language, I must always think in English, and then recall what the translation is, and then attempt to speak it. This extra step causes no end of trouble in terms of getting the communication across well. One generally becomes proficient in a language by starting with common phrases and can begin to "simulate" thinking in the language by using these. For instance, I'm fond of saying, "Mi Espanol es muy mal." Trans: My Spanish is very bad. But past this I have to go very slooooowly.
    \n
    \nEfficient use of a language is all about how quickly you can get an idea across, as well as how accurately. If you're slow and deliberate, you can often make yourself understood by taking a lot of time. Often, however either the transmitter or the reciever doesn't have the patience. I am reminded of a cab ride I had in Paris once where my wife spoke very little French, and the cabbie was from Spain, and spoke French as a "second language." We were unable to communicate at all that way. Using Spanish, of which both me and my wife speak a little, we were able to get a few phrases across, but couldn't really get the idea of where we needed to go. Fortunately we had an address, and the driver got us there... but the ride cost us $50 US in 1990. Our lack of skill in his languages offended him I think. In any case he certainly didn't have the patience to try to figure out what we were saying in our badly mangled version of his language.
    \n
    \nSo, in fact, I think that language use is entirely a social skill. Treating it as such is completely "realistic." In face language can be thought of as "The" social skill. Almost all other social skills are subsets of it. If you cannot communicate well, you cannot be socially apt in almost any other way (raw physical atraction, perhaps, aside).

Better yet, what often happens is that we'll reel forward in time a week in the new land, and players will spend one Hero Point and purchase the local language at the beginning level (13). Which represents a crash immersion course in the language. Or they'll even begin play with that language, under the assumption that they arrived there a while ago, and are sensible enough people to have aquired a smattering of the tongue.

    \n1. You allow no communication beyond sign-language and such obvious things as body language and expression. The problem here is that usually NPCs exist in order to deliver information to keep the game moving. Sometimes language isn't neccessary in this. For instance, I have a scenario in which PCs encounter cruel slavers, and, assuming that they free the slaves (I haven't seen them fail to do so yet), the slaves don't speak their language neccessarily. Over time, you're going to want the NPCs to be able to speak.
    \n
    \n2. You ignore the problem. Which takes one of three sub-forms, generally:
    \n A. You assume that everyone speaks the same langage in the world. Not very realistic, but simple, and internally consistent.
    \n B. You have a "Common" language that everyone speaks. This at times may seem implausible, yet even Tolkien uses it, and there are some real-life examples. The term Linga Franca applies here. It should be noted that, realistically, "common" languages tend to be the language of one dominant culture that has made it's use profitable for many to use (or even has forced its use on an empire).
    \n C. You assume a "babelfish" solution, meaning that there exists some magic or technology like the babelfish from the Hitch-hiker's Guide to the Galaxy. Adams comments about the existence of the babelfish proving that there is no God, are actually a subtle commentary on the fact that this is an obvious plot device meant to keep the action from revolving around issues of language.
    \n
    \n3. The D&D solution mentioned by Justin... you have characters all know so many languages that somebody will know the one needed in all likelihood. This has several drawbacks, however.
    \n A. It makes it a cultural assumption for all cultures that they are massively multi-lingual. I don't know about you, but I don't find that to be particularly plausible. Again, even Tolkien had his characters knowing multiple languages, sure. But they were supposed to be exceptional in this. Samwise doesn't know anything but his rustic version of Westron. Frodo does, but he's wealthy and educated.
    \n B. Worse, it assumes that there's only one human language, largely, as if all humans are from one nation. This is, in fact, embedded in the World of Greyhawk where "common" is supposedly the result of the vast majority of the map having been once under the sway of a single great empire. Possibly plausible, but even in the Roman empire nobody spoke Latin as their primary language outside of Rome. Yes, Oerth has other languages, but even these tend to be racially aligned instead of the more realistic cultural alignment, and seem to be spoken second after common. Put another way, in the real world, there are a lot of languages, and even Tolkien is working under the assumption that the area in question really only has the one human culture (Numenorean, for lack of a better unifying term). The orcs certainly have their own language, and speak Westron pretty much as a plot device as a second language. We would assume that the Easterlings have their own languages, too. So

    \n
    \nAnd that's because, frankly, usually langage is not meant to be central to play. As a corrolary to Renato's point, don't make language central by putting specialized mechanics on it, unless you really do want it to be central. That is, despite my reservations about the methods above to deal with the potential problems of language barrier, I'm for them if you really don't want the game to devolve into some discussion about language and it's effects, itself. Which I think could be dread dull, especially if undirected.
    \n
    \nThis is why I like the HQ solution, again... it means that language is mechanically included, and treated as anything else, so you may, if you wish, investigate how it impacts any particular contest, or ignore it at your lesiure if it's not your thing.     \nThe single simplest and, I think, most common use for language barrier in RPGs is as a privacy mode. That is, you have the two dwarfs, and an elf, then they probably all use common to speak to each other most of the time - but if the dwarfs want to speak to each other, and not let the elf in on what they're saying, then then can communicate in the language they share with each other, and which the elf doesn't know. This can be done for several reasons, but often is done mostly as a slight to the character left out of the conversation or just to annoy them. Sometimes it's done for tactical reasons. If you figure that the orcs don't speak your language, then you can feel free to shout battle plans to each other in the midst of a fight, and your opponents won't be able to take advantage. Etc, etc.

    \n "Translator," above, made me think of nice situational hooks around language (if using Mike's Point 1). When the pantomimes and shouting/speaking slowly don't work, for complex stuff, a translator is sought (could even be one of the PCs). The "fun" begins when the translator's agenda doesn't align with the party's--the translator is actively manipulating the conversation in one direction while leading the party to think he's following their lead.

    Even with the players (OOC) knowing they are being screwed, it could be fun! But I can imagine some serious plot hooks or complications resulting from a "trivial" exchange with a local merchant, via a translator, for supplies. >:D

    \n So far I have tried to integrate the languages more to the "feeling" of my setting.
    \n
    \nIn my fantasy custom-made setting, each Realm has its own culture, customs AND language. So if they are down at the Realm of Kaar, chances are they will mostly encounter Kaarite speaking NPCs, puzzles and stuff. If they are on say Lyon, they'd need to know some Lyonese... And stuff like that.
    \n
    \nSo they sort of know what to expect. And even while they do not know the language, I allow them to try understanding it by other means (asociation, body-language, etc). I like "role playing" foreign and strange languages as gibbering. That way they feel they are actually missing something.
    \n
    \nIf everything else fails, they do have access to magic (They can use Tongues and Comprehend Languages. I play DnD. Sue me!)
    \n
    \nNote that I also use that same setting for some written stories, and I like playing on the cultural differences.
