\n OK, we're well on our way to listing 95% of all movies, in this thread:
Films for Gamers

So much so that I speculate the following in it:
I dunno... maybe it's me and my gamer brain damage, but I can see nearly every movie fitting gaming, nowadays, what with the indie scene carving out so many unusual niches. (Ok, ok, NOT Kramer vs. Kramer... hmmm... but Contenders hacks pretty well to social conflict... and wouldn't It's Complicated or It Was A Mutual Decision map virtually line-for-line? DAMN, see what I mean?!?!)
\nSo, I'm starting THIS thread to see if folks can come up with a movie that's impossible to do, approach, or "get-at" with gaming (for whatever reason: be specific!). I'll start:

The Razor's Edge - MAYBE as a solo game, but any notion of group gaming the emotional struggles and angst of one dude as he travels the world, finally returning to where he started but with new insights, is likely to fall a bit flat, seems to me. Good for a rainy, depressing day with close, philosophical friends... but a "game" and not, rather, some kind of group therapy? I'm doubting it.

GO!

    \n Any movie where characters aren't driven by what they want or need, I'd think. That's going to be a very short list; actually, I'm scratching my head right now trying to come up with a movie that fits that description. Unfortunately, many action movies fit this description, as they are entirely plot-driven. They're also common fodder for gaming. :-/

    Really, any movie that doesn't focus on characters would probably not play out "like an RPG" anyway: where's the role to play? I think most documentaries, especially in the Michael Moore vein of agitprop, would be ungameable...unless you're playing Michael Moore! Do you "win" when you've made your point? When you've ratcheted up your audience's righteous anger? (Some sort of weird inverse Lacuna: you're trying to assault other peoples' heart rates!)

    A biopic would be hard to pull off: The character's "need" is sort of subsumed into the function of the movie to convey historical information. Like...what would a Walk The Line game look like? Would conflict arise from how closely the player hews to his portrayal of the character? Could there be some sort of "artistic license" budget you have to manage to tell an engaging story without being too shackled by actual events?

    \n There's a big difference between gaming a genre, and gaming a particular movie.

    Montage movies are pretty hard to get at. Like the Iranian movie with the name I can't remember, which is basically the same style as Koyanisqatsi, only it moves through Iranian history, from ancient times to modern day. I can sort of see a montage movie game, although the "playing a role" part is problematic - but playing a montage-of-Iranian-history game? Take turns framing the visuals of a scene from Iranian history, they must be in chronological order, try and keep it going for as long as possible. I could see that, I guess. That would be a parlor game, with limited conflict going on (OP David wrote: "gaming" but I'd rather just go from an rpg perspective). Playing Shock: with Issues for characters is still gaming a genre - or just gaming a type of story, even, since Shock: isn't exclusively sci-fi, unless you're using the specific issues of the specific film, and even then, you're not guaranteed of getting the same or similar feeling.

    As long as a game includes communication with language (I'm lookin' at you rpgs), it's going to have problems translating or recreating the experience of music or images without any narrative content. I could maybe see Wagner, but how do you game Shostakovich? How do you game a Picasso?

    So it looks like folks are bringing out movies that are either highly visual/aural or surreal or lack narrative structure of any kind. Some say, "Sure, I could play that" in response, but I am thinking that comes from (as said above) more of a "simulation" of the movie through conversation than anything close to a role playing game. Or another way: if, as Jason said, no movie is impossible to play, and given the range of cinematic weirdness out there (and its parallels to other arts, viz symphony, documentation, art), then it's not a large inductive leap to say "anything can be gamed, period." Opening a can of Spam. The color of clouds in May during a sunset. A D-minor scale. The shape of the color blue while it's screaming.
