    When I run Hero Quest with As You Go setting and character generation (Which I refer to as OtherWorld), it looks like zero prep. But the truth is that it's not. That is I have a couple of very generic situations that I've developed pre-play that are important to how this "scenario" plays. And, in fact, it does pre-define the setting in some very generic and minor ways.

    For example, the scenario you've played Thor is the "Black Riders" scenario. Basically here's my prep for the scenario as it's evolved over time:

    Scene 1: Players select biome or terrain type. Characters appear over horizon, or through vegetation. Somebody riding something arrives, out of control, and crashes. This somebody is being persued by the Black Riders.

    Scene 2: A few of the Black Riders (men in ugly spikey black armor, riding lizards of some sort) arrive on the scene. Even if the characters learn of the Black Riders in Scene 1 and are evading, the riders still catch them - though the character efforts may make the encounter different certainly. The black riders require the characters to convert to their dark lizard religion.

    Scene 3: More riders are on the horizon, too many to take on, but the characters find a gateway to the hero plane. Scenario ends with the characters suggesting some sort of heroquest to deal with the situation.

    Obviously I extemporize in the in between. As I find values of the characters (as the players make them) I find ways to incorporate them in conflicts in the scenario.

    I have a second scenario that I've used a couple of times now, involving the characters coming across a slaver about to whip a pretty female slave. Basically the idea with these scenarii is to present the player with situations where his reaction to the situation will neccessarily go a ways to defining the character in play.

    So, not a lot of prep, but a little.

    Mike

    Posted By: Thomas D
    When he first mentioned the game on his LiveJournal, I instantly thought it would rock in a Shadowrun game. I plan to use it in my group's next infiltration-themed mission they get from The Foundation. If I might ask, how did you adapt the various elements in Wilderness to SR? Did you use mission points and narrative control, or did you start ratcheting up the threshold for task resolution as the game session went on?
    I didn't really need to give the character's bonuses, and since this was the finale session of the game, I used mission points a little oddly. Basically, I paid them out per the rules (1 per player created fact) but then those became points of currency that the players could use for 2 things. First, they could use them to bring in resources, people and events from earlier in the campaign to help out, so our Okie nomad rigger spent one to effectively have his Trailer Park With Guns show up to help the attack on the front gate. Once play began, they were used for fairly tight narrative power, usually in the form of me saying "The out come of that will probably be X, unless anyone wants to spend some chips?" which did a wonderful job of bleeding the players.

    For the ratcheting tension, I gave myself a big, black chip every time the timer went off, and made it clear to the players that these were "I'm going to fuck with you" chips. These were, honestly, an agreed upon violation of player/GM trust, because when I spent one, it was understood that I was explicitly abusing my power to make things worse for them. This also worked out quite well.

    All in all, it really rocked, and it was also pretty educational. The one thing I realized is that I need to be pretty explict with the players about what facts gain points, because we sort of drifted from "planning" to "jam session" and back a few times - the curse of a painfully creative table. All in all,it worked out great. there aren't many game sessions that involve werewolf jesus, the holy grail, Rasputin, The spear of Longinus, a controlled nuclear reaction and and elf being beaten to death with a gold brick all coming up within a two hour period. I mean, outside of unknown armies.

    -Rob D.
