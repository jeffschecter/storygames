Designing for duration

D-503
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Story Games
One difference that strikes me between many indie designs and many trad designs (without for the moment worrying too much about definitions for the sake of all our sanities) is that many indie games give thought to the likely duration of play.

What do I mean by that? I don't mean the individual session, I mean they give thought to how long a given play experience, campaign for want of a better term, should be under that system.

Many go for closed story arcs, the idea is that you play out a particular story and if you wish may play out a related story later but that story is self-contained and done.

By contrast, most trad games in my experience don't really think about campaign duration. The assumption is that it is open-ended, potentially indefinite. I think that misses a trick, I think if desired campaign length was taken into account it might lead to better gaming.

Desired campaign length might well be open ended, fair enough, but if so that has design implications. Desired campaign length might be one night of play, again that has implications. A game might see a desired campaign length as being around two years of weekly play, this is the assumption behind DnD 3.5, and I think it is a stronger game for having given clear thought to how long it is likely to be played for and incorporating design elements supporting that duration of play.

I'm not sure if I'm being quite clear, so I'll try some examples.

Pendragon is designed for extended, but not indefinite, campaign play. It supports this through use of a defined timeline, through rules for families and continuing play through multiple generations, it has given a lot of thought to actively supporting the extended duration play it aims for.

DnD 3.5 is aimed as I said above at two years of weekly play. Accordingly, how quickly you level is calculated so that if you play on that basis by the time you finish you will have experienced each facet of the game, from beginning character to uber-PC at 20th level. Campaign duration is again an intentional design feature.

My Life with Master is designed for a single play session, and the rules support that and pretty much guarantee you a successful play experience in that timeframe.

Dogs is designed to provide satisfactory play with one town, though you can should you wish go on to another town if the mood takes you. Again, duration is factored in.

But most games don't do this, most games vaguely assume open ended play and then don't really think through the implications of that and so don't really support open ended play.

Note, I'm not saying open ended is better than closed or short better than long or anything like that, what I'm saying is that I think the expected duration of a particular game is a design feature that is not thought about enough and that has potential to make better games if factored in.

Not sure if this is quite the right forum for this, but does anyone get what I mean and agree/disagree?

    Techniques:

    Heavily front-loaded work (creating the characters, creating the world, playing through levels 1-5 to get to the good stuff) encourages ... nay requires! ... a long-term campaign in order to capitalize on it. Contrariwise, reducing that start-up cost increases your ability to play short games.

    Changing the nature of characters and situation, and particularly confronting extant conflicts in the situation is part and parcel of coming to the completion of an arc. You don't have to end the story once you confront the conflicts (you can continue with some new ones!) but you do have to confront the conflicts in order to end the story, at least in a satisfying manner. So a system which makes the main conflicts distant and untouchable ("Kill these three thousand orc minions of the evil overlord ... not that it will change the world, because he's got lots more!") make it easy to continue a campaign in the long term, whereas a system which drops the main conflicts right in your lap and expects you to deal with them ("This town is rife with sin. Fix it!") makes it hard to continue the campaign, and easy to finish it quickly.

    Those are the two that leap to mind for me. Damn fine question, though! I look forward to hearing other people's responses.

    On short and long games.

    Firstly, that's too simplistic a divide. Secondly, I think there are real benefits to an end goal, as in practice most open ended games tend to peter out in a rather unsatisfactory way. The answer to getting a great long game is having a meangful long end goal with interim mini-goals, not to just playing until the group gets bored or breaks up.

    Note, henceforth everything I'm talking is probabilities, there are no absolutes.

    Short games are more likely to produce satisfying play focussed on a particular issue or small set of issues. You get to grips with that thing, really have it out, play the hell out of it and you're done. It's like the tv miniseries, long enough for a bit of development but short enough to tell a single coherent story.

    One night games are like a movie, short and intense and you can only deal with so much by and large. That said, what you do deal with you get real focus on, you have no time to lose so you use your time well.

    Longer games have other satisfactions. Depth is one, a character developed over extended play tends (remember, no absolutes here) to be deeper and more engaging by virtue of having been developed in play. Of course, you can ignore that and play Bob the fighter for a decade and there are tools to help achieve similar results in more intense play, but length helps depth.

    Longer play can also help get an epic feel. Pendragon is great for this, you have adventures, your lands prosper or decline, you gain glory, marry and raise children. In time they grow up and you play those children, who live in a world literally shaped by the players in the form of the parent characters. That is depth, that is epic (a form of epic anyway), that is a tale that is hard to do (no absolutes) in a short and focussed game.

    Note, I make no judgements here about which is better, I don't think that's actually a sensible question.

    Longer games also need an equilibrium of sorts. You can't just escalate and escalate. This goes for both the narrative and for mechanics.

    If you look at DnD, the power from level to level is largely an illusion, as you get more skillz, the enemies get tougher to match. Compare to Dogs, where PC abilities go get bigger and badder, or MLwM where PC abilities change over time.

    I think many long term games tend to focus on external conflict first, and personal conflicts only peripherally. Options are much broader for external adversity than it is for internal/personal adversity. Personal issues only have so much play time. You can change personal issues over time, but then it just ends up being a string a little cycles rather than one "long" campaign. By focusing on external adversity, players can build personal issues slowly over time.

    Anyway. I think the rewards of longer games are as I said depth of character and a particular type of epic story is easier to do in that format. Both these things can be achieved in shorter games, but longer ones are well suited to that.

    Moving on, timefire makes an important point re DnD, and actually one of the many things I dislike about it. There is no real progress, merely the illusion of progress.

    So, what games do I think support longer term play well and what do they have in common?

    Pendragon has a defined long term story arc, support for character growth and to see a full and rich character life ending in death and then renewal in the form of the son. I think that is powerful stuff, incredibly clever and possibly the best long duration game design out there. Play a full Pendragon campaign and you see characters grow from boyhood to men to old age, you see a full life lived at the table. That is a special kind of thing, and while not for everyone that is hard to do in a shorter game.

    Bushido had a points build chargen mechanic. Your first character had enough points to build an average guy of his class, an average warrior, an average priest, and so on. When you died, your final honour score modified the points of your next character, so that if you died well your next character was built on more points. Thus players were motivated to get into the medieval Japanese mindset of putting honour before life and were rewarded at the player level for doing so. Actual character growth was capped, there were six levels and at sixth you were expected to retire and found a school or monastery or somesuch. Again then, we have a structure for longer term but not open ended play, your guy has room to grow, to become a power, to retire and your next character if you played well will be better. Clever stuff.

    Last example, Traveller. Classic Traveller that is, Little Black Book Traveller. Traveller did have an advancement mechanic of sorts, but it was minimal and hard to advance with, most characters did not in fact chance in terms of stats and skills during play except downwards through aging. In normal game terms, you didn't advance. I think that helped longer term play by removing the power creep issue, in Traveller you lived a life, much as one does in the real world. There was no character power to gain, except power within the game world in terms of wealth and influence.

    So, what do these games have in common? Each addresses the issue of power creep, Pendragon by killing you from old age and resetting, Bushido by forcing retirment at a certain point but rewarding you still for good play, Traveller by removing advancement.

    And that's what makes IMO, or one thing that makes, for an effective game design aimed at longer term play. You have to address power creep, and do it in a way that is satisfying.

    For shorter games, other challenges present themselves, but I'll leave those to those better suited to addressing them.

    Hey ... honest question, regarding the mathematics of this.

    Which of the following are easier? Which are harder? Which are just as easy/hard as the others?

    - (a) Getting people together for a self-contained three hour game.
    - (b) Getting some people (not necessarily the same ones) together for six three hour games, each self-contained.
    - (c) Getting the same people together for six three hour games, each self-contained.
    - (d) Getting the same people together for one game played in six three hour sessions.

    People keep saying "You can do more in a long game than in a short game," and on some levels (hours played, for instance) that's clearly true. And if, in your social group, all of the effort of getting people organized is front-loaded into getting them to sign on to anything at all, then it's just as easy to get them into a long game as into a short one ... the effort for (a) and the effort for (d) are identical, and (d) has the higher payout.

    Me, I find that (c) is a bit easier than (d), and (b) is vastly easier than (c).

    (b), (c) and (d) all get you the same amount of time played. I suspect that they achieve the same amount of fun, through different means (although I am tempted by the idea that (d) is less fun, and marginally open to the idea that (d) is more fun).

    If that social equation ... how hard it is to get various things going ... is different from group to group then it's going to hugely impact how people weigh the cost/benefit ratio.

    What I'm attempting in Errantry is to provide a mechanism whereby you can decide upfront what the (approximate) duration of your commitment is. The group picks a number of "boons" which you need to earn before you can complete your quest - essentially these are plot tokens. The smaller-scale structure of the game is episodic: you encounter a character and attempt to win a boon to progress your quest. Because there is considerable variety in the characters you encounter and several different ways of earning boons, I hope that this won't become tedious easily.

    My main goal was to reflect the genre I'm drawing on (medieval romance), which is essentially a variable number of episodic encounters in which resources are gained to complete the quest. But I think it also works as a metering system for game length, and combines short-term and long-term fun.
