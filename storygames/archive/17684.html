\n So I tried to run this with a friend or two yesterday. We got distracted and only really made it through character creation, but I got a question thrown at me that I didn't know how to answer and that I've been unable to successfully Google. Maybe you fine ladies and gentleman can help me sort it out. So, in the character creation steps it says this:\n
Look over the character classes and choose one that interests you. To start with everyone chooses a different class; there aren\u2019t two wizards. If two people want the same class, talk it over like adults and compromise.
\nNo prob, I've run AW, I've got my player-friendly-niche-protection speech all queued up, good to go. Then my player points out the marginalia I sort of skipped over the first time I read through skimmed the game:\n
Later on, if you\u2019re making a replacement character, you can choose a class someone else is already playing.
\nHis question was "So... we both can't be Fighters right now... but we can be later? Why?" I didn't have a good answer for him, because I'm honestly wondering that myself. My only idea about this is that, generally speaking, they'll probably be different levels so there's less overlap than there would be right after character creation but... I honestly don't know. Halp?

    \n It only seems like the niche protection is there in the first place in homage to fantasy game tropes. It's not like DW is a game of strict tactics that requires players to fill particular slots. As a practical matter, the game would work fine if all the players were clerics. But, a) fantasy game tropes are a big part of DW and important for narrative reaons so it makes sense that there is some version of "character class," and b) it's a hack of AW, and AW had playbooks -- playbooks were the logical place to graft the character class idea. But, you know, once the vibe of Gauntlet, D&D, Golden Axe, or whatever the group is going for is achieved with an unlikely band of diverse heros, there is no actual tactical harm in adding an extra fighter to the team after the wizard gets zorched.

    For the sake of comparison, playbooks in AW seem a little different to me. That game is about scarcity, so the niche protection in AW is neat because (among other things like color and exposition) it gives each player some social currency and encourages interaction for the sake of survival. So, to me, the playbooks serve different purposes in the two games . . . which isn't shocking since they really are different games.

    \n The paladin being only human is entirely intentional! Each class has the races that we think make sense. For some classes (fighter) that's all of them, for the paladin it's one, most it's 2 or 3 (with 3 being more common, I'd guess without counting).

    The reason you don't start with two of the same class is that out of the gate two characters of the same class are going to be more similar than we found people really want. Two starting fighters, before their first session, are both going to have special weapons, bonds about protecting people and fighting, and similar gear.

    Even after that first session, the character is pretty different. They're likely going to have a new bond. They're going to have history that we've actually all played out and seen. It's the difference between Gregor the first level fighter and Gregor the fighter who almost died to a shrieking fungus that one time and who might have the eye of a god or it might just be that those kobolds were lying I'm not sure (and who might still be first level).

    If you really want to have two of the same class, I'd recommend creating a second list of starting bonds, or writing some on the fly. It'd be kind of weird to have two people both say "The spirits have spoken to me of a great danger that follows ____." I mean, what do those spirits do all day but speak of great dangers, I guess?
