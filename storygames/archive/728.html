Freeform play: yes or no?

Faerieloch
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Story Games
So, something that came up in our BitV game that several people commented on was our desire and enthusiasm for freeform play i.e. play that is not directly related to a conflict or even to furthering plot. We wanted to just RP our characters and see what happened (with relationships, group dynamics, to flesh out personalities). It also came up in the G&S thread, noting how in the operettas, people often sang about what just happened, rather than moving on.

So I was wondering, do you like freeform play? Why? Why not? Do you want to freeform play even when the system isn't set up for it (like in Dogs where you have to specify you want it, otherwise play is centred around towns and their conflicts)? Do you prefer to RP within conflicts rather than outside of them?

--Nancy

    Shreyas, I think it's sort of a premise thing. The 'default premise' of roleplaying games, if you'll all allow me a massive overgeneralization, is knocking about, seeing cool things, and wreaking havoc to the situation you're presented with. That's at least been my experience with 90% of the games I played prior to discovering indie stuff. And stakesless, unstructured roleplay forwards that sort of play.

    Most of my play since finding indie stuff -- and a kind of play that I really, really enjoy -- defines a strong premise and then supports it with rules that hit that premise. Power-19 sort of design. Now, "free play" very well may forward that strong premise, but it's not play that is structured by those targeted rules. As such, it's sort of outside the rules in these games that depend on pretty strict adherence to the rules they present.

    As the GM of the game Nancy originally referenced, I'd like to make an observation or two about the way it happened in that game and why. I am also going to use the terms free play and formal play here, even though I agree with many in this thread that they're loaded terms. Hopefully by the end of the post you'll be clear on how I'm using them here specifically, and if we need to do further refining of vocabulary we can do it afterwards.

    First off, I had four of four players in the game who wanted freeplay in order to establish their characters and build a dynamic set of character interactions. All of them, I think, have an F based Character socket into game to one degree or another (KJ, Mo, and Nancy for sure, PJ seems to go there too, though I've talked to her less explicitly about it) and so having an emotional grounding and empathic connection with their character before the character hits crisis is very important for their enjoyment of the crisis. Because of this, they argued, the big turmoil and moral judgment of the towns would mean more to them if they'd had a chance to make their characters people before they judged their characters as people. I let them have what they wanted, because they seemed sure it was really what they wanted. This is pretty much my standard GM praxis: give the players what they want.

    Second, there is a single very notable differentiation between free and formal play in that game that can be pretty clearly delimited. When we are playing conflict driven play I am the GM and I push shit and move the game from conflict to conflict pretty relentlessly. When we are in free play, I am not the GM anymore. I have said, every time we've done extended free play scenes, "Okay, now I'm not the GM, y'all are in charge and can do whatever you want. If you want me to frame something for you, tell me and I'll do so, but other than that, you ladies are running the show." So you can tell that the play in those scenes is different than the play in the other kind of scene because in one type there is a GM authority and in the other there isn't. At all.

    Now, part of the reason for that is because of the structure and medium of the game. It isn't that this is necessary to all free play or anything like that, it happened because we were playing on a MUSH with time constraints, and after the initiation challenges we'd fill out the remainder of the time we had to play with some free play to explore the character's growing relationships in temple. So while the desire to establish character through unformalized play was there, the exact ways it played out was due to a combination of personality and medium, and not something essentialist to the divide.

    I also found it interesting that none of the players abused, tried to abused, or even (as far as I could tell) even considered that they could abuse their power in the free play where I was not the GM. They could have all gone and dunked Brother Kishkumen (their hard-ass trainer) in the river, and as I wasn't the GM authority I couldn't really have stopped them. But they didn't, and they didn't want to, because (I think) they are all as committed to the game and its tone as I am.

    Throughout the game when someone has asked me "Could I use this trait in this situation" I have replied "Don't ask me, if you think it fits, it probably does. If you want to ask anyone, ask the rest of the group." Now, there have been times when someone has used a trait that left me scratching my head, but when I'd stop to look at it and figure out what they were saying with it, it always made sense in the end. I like that, it gives me space to figure out the tick-tock of the PCs.

    Those points together have really confirmed something I've been noticing for the last while in my RP (which has included several GMless games to further the decision): the whole idea that we need a GM as authority to keep the tone and meaning of a game consistent is bunk. Now, having a GM with that authority is essential for some groups and maybe for all groups in some kinds of play. But if you can play hardcore Dogs with the players brutalizing their own characters and not have to use your "GMness" to keep them on the same page, monitor trait usage, or any of that stuff, it really does show that a group that are all committed to each other can easily distribute those GM tasks and make them work without the central authority.

    Which brings us right up to the edge of freeplay GMless play, which all those dirty hippies have been talking about for some long time now, but that's taking us off topic and so I'm going to stop here.
