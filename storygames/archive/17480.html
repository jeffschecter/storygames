\n In my on going effort to experiment and explore different game design approaches, I've been considering doing away with "Character Charisma" and allowing "Player Charisma" (read: role-playing) to have singular influence in social situations for the characters. This approach helps reduce a potential lack of clarity between how player role-playing and character social prowess interact together to affect the fiction, but comes with the caveat that some players may fair better than others for various reasons.

Thoughts?

    \n Are the charismatic players benefiting from their charisma? Then it should be reflected in their stats, not simply to describe the benefit, but to assess the opportunity cost to their other stats. In a lot of games, if you are letting someone who rps high charisma to benefit from their natural charisma but also from their character's high dexterity, strength, etc., then you've got a problem... unless they've paid for their natural benefit by purchasing high charisma through the game's mechanics.

    Simply removing charisma as a stat doesn't solve anything; it just removes the possibility of a fix -- while implying that the game isn't about charisma even though actually it is. The fix is to keep the charisma stat and make a correction to the character's stat distribution to reflect how the character is actually being played. Since it almost never happens that a character in play comes off exactly as originally designed, then there's got to be some way of enforcing or encouraging a correction.

    Yes. It can be unfair.

    \n As I've seen it, "Charisma" as a character stat in the first place is a conundrum. Depending on the gaming system, it can be used so little that no one cares about it all. Maybe you'd roll once to see if you convince a town guard to let you pass.

    But for games with more talking and interpersonal mechanics, the "charisma" stat is still surrounded by player roleplaying. If a player gives an actual convincing speech to the guard (/GM), then what is the meaning of the Charisma roll done by the character? The guard (/GM) is convinced. A failed Charisma roll would imply some interfering force that neither the player nor the GM can explain.

    So, it makes sense that for some games the roleplaying comes first, and the roleplaying defines the character. After all, the player group came together to play by TALKING; if some players are not sure how to do so in the group, they can be encouraged to learn. And if the group came together to, say, climb walls, fire BB guns, and shoot arrows, then that is also their prerogative.

    \n I think part of the purpose in having a GM roll to be influenced has to do with the multiplicity thing. I'm comparing TT to live combat larp (where player charisma is almost always dominant).

    In a larp, you have 12 different NPCs played by 12 different people, all at the same time. If you're trying to convince one of them to, for example, follow you to the edge of town so your friends can ambush them, then you actually set that up and do it. You choose your victim. You act out whatever diversion you think will get them to come away with you. You lead them away. You shank them.

    Part of why this works so well in larp is step one - choose your victim. Again, there are 12 NPCs to choose from played by different players. You can look them over, talk to them, and get a sense of which one you should try to lure away, and how you should try to do it. You might convince the shady looking merchant guy to slip off and talk about an illegal business deal. You might convince the mercenary you need a paid guard for a dangerous trip. You might convince the cleric you have someone who needs healing or wants to talk points of faith.

    By having all those NPCs to interact with, you have lots of opportunities for real life assessment and acting to play out in an interesting way.

    In tabletop, you usually just have the one GM representing all those people. Having some chance element worked in can be a good way to make things work out differently and inspire unexpected outcomes to riff off of.

    If you approach social skills as an opportunity to introduce interesting complications and inspirations, rather than gateways to be unlocked, then having a few dice to roll can add to the fun.

    If all you're going to do with them is decide whether or not something works like a social light switch, then it's probably best to remove them.

    \n Thanks for all the feedback, everybody. I see points and issues here and there. To me, it kind of seems like ditching the Charisma Attribute can the potential to work given the right group of players, but in general is probably not the best way to handle it for a game designed to work with most groups. Though, this does get me wondering about how different groups handle the overlap of player and character charisma.

    As a GM, I usually let the player decide how they want handle any particular social situation. If the player just declares what they want their character to accomplish (like, "I want to convince the guard to let me past."), I always ask for a more elaborate description ("How?", "I try to pass myself off as a former guard.") and then let them roll to see how well they do ("I rolled a 18!", "Okay, the guard doesn't ask too many questions and you manage to convince him to let you enter.").

    Otherwise, if the player starts role-playing (like, "I walk up to the guard and say, 'Hey man! Remember me? I used to be a member of the guard just like you. Is Josin still kickin', that old bastard?'") I will let the scene play out, and depending on how clever they're being (yes, my subjective opinion) I may let them get an automatic success, or call for that roll after all. If they go with role-playing and don't do a great job, but roll well, I'll retcon the conversation to some extent in order to make the success believable, or reduce the NPC's resistance to giving the player what they want. So, within my line of thinking, if the player puts forth some effort to role-play, they end up with a better chance of success overall. In a way, I use the role-playing as a social version of physical fictional positioning. So players might not have to roll if they role-play cleverly, in the same way a player describing their super-clever method to sneak into a fortress might not have to roll for it because their description and ideas are so clever or creative.

    How do you do it with your group? I like examples, if you're so inclined.

    \n Hi Zachary,

    Sounds like you want the roleplaying and the dice to both matter, in somewhat flexible amounts. I've GMed like this, and had the most success when I made the connections between the two explicit, and open for group input.

    Example:

    Player 1: (roleplays out an exchange)

    Me: Nice, that's pretty convincing!

    Player 1: So does he let me in?

    Me: Well... He has reasons not to. (roleplay that in, if not already done) Normally this would be difficulty 12 (on d20). But you've done a lot to convince him, so I'll move that down to difficulty 8. Sound good?

    Player 2: After all that, there's still a 1 in 3 chance he won't get in?

    Me: Okay, fine, 1 in 4 sounds better. Difficulty 6.

    (Obviously this depends on your larger system.)

      \n That sounds like a good approach.

      I've always liked the model where what you say determines *how* you're tackling the problem, and then the dice tell us *how well* that method works.

      For example:

      A. You want to kill an orc.
      B. You want to convince a guard to let you pass.

      How do you tackle this problem? What do you actually do?

      A. "I call out to it, draw my sword, and rush in, hoping to hack it before it hacks me!"
      B. "I offer the guard a bribe, saying, 'Hey, man, I lost my ticket. Here's the cost of one, why don't you let me through?"

      How well does it work?

      A. You roll well. You kill the orc. You roll poorly, the orc hurts you.
      B. You roll well, the guard accepts. You roll poorly, the guard calls security instead.

      Now, the *how* is always important. That's the fictional positioning part. If the orc is sleeping and you drop a statue on it, you might not need to roll: you win the fight. If you can come up with a bribe the guard has no reason to refuse (e.g. offering him a new, better job as a guard somewhere else), no need to roll, he lets you in.

      Along the same lines, same tactics just won't work. You can't attack the dragon with a ping pong paddle; no guard will let you in for a 25-cent bribe.

      Why do you need the rolling? Aside from game balance and similar concerns, here are three points that come to mind:

      1. Separating these two things conceptually, you allow a richer variety of playstyles. I can play the guy who's clumsy or untrained but still beats everyone in a fight because he's just lucky/brutal/blessed/faster/more experience/whatever. I give a description of an attack that might not work for someone else, but we all know that's just some colour for who the character is: the dice show the effects.

      Likewise, you can play a character who thinks she's really smooth, but actually is always under suspicion because she just can't get the local accent quite right.

      Or you can play someone who's dumb and inarticulate, and yet everyone loves her/him. (We all know someone like that.) Maybe a beautiful woman with a lousy personality still generally gets what she wants in your game world.

      2. Injecting certain randomized procedures can enrich our play. Maybe the game we're using creates interesting twists or interesting consequences when we engage the mechanics, or determines how many experience points you get, or what resources are at risk.

      Otherwise we should just play freeform, right?

      Injecting the unexpected into our games also gives us creative constraints and creates new situations we might not come up with if we were just freeforming. That can be a valuable goal for many games and many groups.

      3. In most fictional situations, there is enough undefined that we don't KNOW how things will turn out. When you say you swing at the orc's head, how tired is your arm, actually? How low is the orc's stance? Who has faster reflexes? We don't know, but the dice will tell us.

      Will the guard be tempted by your offer, or are they so loyal to their job that they'd rather turn you down? We don't know: this is just an NPC we invented on the spot. Does the guard value a bit of spare cash? Does the guard feel a strong sense of loyalty to the organization they work for, or have they been thinking about quitting for the last two months anyway? The dice will tell us.

      \n I think the breadth of responses here nicely illustrates that different solutions are appropriate for different games/groups/goals. Rickard, I'm with you, taste-wise, but I really do think it's a matter of taste, and I was trying to stick within the taste Zachary originally expressed, which is RP + dice.

      I think there are very good reasons why some groups prefer RP + dice -- some players like the concreteness and game-ness of having all their decisions impact mechanical quantities, even their roleplay decisions. In a game with any sort of strategic element, it's easy to wander over the line between player system-optimizing and character situation-optimizing. Forming a plan to attack from above so you can use your excellent Jump skill is essentially both. Whether smooth-talking an NPC also falls into that category really depends on the game, group, and goals.

      Zachary, I think that works as an example of fictional positioning with known outputs: if you move to the high ground, you can use Jump. The requirements for using Jump are pretty clear. Social skills tend to defy that type of clarity because instead of operating on a known, static entity (like, y'know, gravity), they operate on an NPC's brain. So, you can fictionally position all day -- giving the NPC reasons to love you, fear you, want to work with you, etc. -- but in the end it disappears into the black box of the GM's knowledge of this NPC and opinion of what would work.

      Many solutions exist, but all are matters of taste:
      - screw the GM's knowledge and opinion of this NPC; let the dice determine the NPC's reaction, full stop
      - screw fictional positioning for effect; resolve outcomes first, and then narrate in appropriate fiction second
      - screw formal game-play; let's trust our GM to decide, abiding by our shared principles for what makes a fun session
      - screw consistency; let's pick the best system (no dice/coin flip/4d12/etc.) for each situation on the spot
      - let's not be extreme here; let's combine a bit of all of the above

      If you do the first one, then you can treat it just like a Jump, with a long list of numeric social skills and pretty clear requirements about which you can use when.

      If you do the fourth or fifth ones, you can still have rollable skills, but you won't know when you'll get to use them.

      \n Paul_T nails it.

      Fictional positioning is the talking we do about the shared imagined space that sets up whether we go to the dice/rules or continue talking. Typically, we go to the rules when the next step isn't clear. Did I convince the guard to let me in? Did I manage to wound the goblin? Was it an overwhelming success, or hard-won?

      Now, let me paraphrase a smart thing Mike Mearls once said: "A good rule is one that makes the game more fun than it would have been without it."

      Ideally, the rules make determining that unclear outcome more interesting than it would have been if we just said yes. The savvy player's excellent plan is undermined by her PC's low Charisma. The timid player's weak plan goes over like gangbusters when her Cha 18 paladin rolls a 52 on their Diplomacy check; hijinks ensue!

      Now, if the rules don't make this outcome any more interesting, I can see why one would want to jettison them entirely and just talk it out; that's how we did it back in the day! That's why my recommendation tends to be, "You should try a different game."

      To bring this full-circle, my feeling is that leaving it all up to negotiation leaves too much room for group social shenanigans. It certainly can work, but it opens the door for all kinds of dysfunction that designers have worked to overcome for a decade or more now.

      \n One thing I seek in social interaction scenes of all kinds, from persuasion to seduction, is ways to establish or display aspects of an NPC. Sometimes having someone who is fully persuaded but still doesn't cooperate is a good thing for characterization. Here's an example:

      nWoD game, the characters are members of a crime family who, a few years previous, had been devastated by a horrible war with a drug cartel. Tons of people murdered, in jail, etc. Completely pushed the family out of the drug business. Player character goes to NPC who lost her beloved husband first when he had to kill someone and then again when someone killed him in retaliation. He attempts to convince her that taking a particular action against an obstreporous and potentially violent relative is in her best interest and the best interest of the family. PC gives a thorough, persuasive case and gets a success on his roll.

      I say "Her voice shakes, tears well up in her eyes. 'I won't start this shit again. You're right, but it's not gonna be me that starts this shit again. I can't, I just can't.'" What he got out of that was not her cooperation, but her capitulation. Everybody knew that this conflict could emotionally destroy her, and it also showed that when it came to war, she didn't have the stomach for it anymore, even if it would hurt her, or hurt the family.

      I put the NPC in his hands rather than giving him what he "wanted". If this were a conflict resolution system, the player would have just got what the PC wanted, and blah blah blah, boring. By putting in this unexpected result - she's persuaded, she knows what the PC is saying is right, but she can't bring herself to do what she knows is the right thing, that becomes an important part of her characterization, and what the PC does with that becomes an important part of his. (He was so taken aback that he seriously rethought his approach. I made an NPC cry?!?)

      I don't even know that the dice added that much to this decision. If I said "you can't roll for this, you have to just tell me what you say", it might have worked just as well. I'm not sure.

      To the discussion at large:

      For me the dice have always been about the unexpected. It's hard to imagine something suitably awesome and interesting with nothing to go on but your character back-stories. It's a lot easier when the dice start ruling out possibilities and a lot more interesting when the stats and dice create novel outcomes. Maybe your gut feeling wouldn't be to let your character take a bullet to the leg in this scene but the dice tell you that it happens. Now you're in a situation you wouldn't have allowed for if you were having this dream or writing this novel or, perhaps, playing this out in free-form. Maybe your natural response to any dangerous scenario is to talk your way out of it. The dice tell you that it didn't work, or at least didn't go as planned. Now you use your stats and dice to find inspiration for alternative solutions. There isn't anything wrong with games that rely on less randomness, but the more acting and creative writing you require of your players, the more limited your game becomes in terms of finding unexpected outcomes. You shrink the scope of what can happen to what your players are capable of doing or what they think up first. These limits play a role in any RPG, but the dice expand the range of possibilities by subverting everyone's expectations at least some of the time in a way that is agnostic to skill and experience. For me, this isn't about fairness to the players who aren't as good at improv acting. It's about maximizing the unexpected.
