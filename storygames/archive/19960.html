\n Hello, dear reader.

If you're reading this, you're welcome to join in. The game is simple (full rules and discussion HERE, but you can play without reading that).

The basic rule is: anyone can play at any time, so long as you don't take two turns in a row. On your turn, you answer the previous question with one sentence, and then ask a new question for the next person to answer.

There are only two other important rules:


Themes

* Each player may specify one Theme at some point during the course of the game.

Themes are important story elements or motifs we want to see explored and resolved. A Theme you name must be something already present in the story, not something you just made up on the spot.

A Theme will usually be a transformative character goal ("Desiree wishes to see the King overthrown and exiled") or an important question ("Who will inherit the throne?", "Will the assassin manage to kill Desiree before the night of the Masked Ball?", etc).

One of the things I'm curious to see is which feels/works better.

(If you have an idea for a classical literary Theme - "Will love triumph over loyalty?" - and think you can phrase it in a way which allows a resolution of that Theme, that would also be interesting to try. Go ahead if inspiration strikes.)

* There is a maximum of four Themes. Once we have four, no more may be created.

* The game ends when all four have been resolved.

(We may also do an Aftermath for each main character using the Oracle, if it feels appropriate, in order to create an epilogue. We'll see when we get there.)


Calling the Oracle

Any player can call for the Oracle, generally in order to help answer a question. However, since in real, live play, you could also interrupt another player in order to use the Oracle, there'll be one more option here:

You may also call the Oracle pre-emptively, by stating a condition which cannot be fulfilled without the Oracle's use. For instance, "If someone comes through that door later in this scene, we must use the Oracle to find out who it is!"

Add any preemptive Oracle conditions to the bottom of your post in italics.


The Oracle Chart

This is the new, modified chart. We'll see if we like it (if we don't, we can go back to the original chart, even halfway through the game).

0 to 2: A miracle! The very best of all possible things.
3 to 6: Things are bad, very bad indeed.
7 to 9: The Worm Turns: An apparently good outcome which, in reality, presents a dark twist.
10 to 12: An excellent turn; a very pleasant surprise.
13 to 15: Utter catastrophe! Your darkest fear come to life.

(I am secretly amused by my entirely unintentional re-creation of the Apocalypse World totals. And, if we were to use 1 through 6 instead of 0 through 5 - i.e., dice instead of fingers - we'd have decent D&D stats instead - a 3-18 range...)


Using the Oracle

To use the Oracle, post your choice of 0 to 5 fingers in invisible ink, like I do below. The higher the number, the better you want this outcome to be, and vice-versa.

Fingers: 4

If you select the line above this paragraph, you will see I decided to go with 4 fingers. The way to do this is to copy the following line into your post (and choose your number of fingers):

<font color="#FFFFFF">Fingers: 0,1,2,3,4,5</font>

Once we have three posts with invisible fingers, the next poster (it can be the same player who made the third vote) should select the invisible text in the previous three posts and announce the Oracle result.
