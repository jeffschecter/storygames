\n I think it's Trollbabe that did something like this, although I'm not sure how as I've only seen it mentioned.

Use the age of the character to determine things (using d%). Anything that's a factor of experience, knowledge and anything mental that benefits from experience; roll under vs age. Anything that's a factor of vitality, natural ability and generally anything physical; roll over vs age. Also, have it as a base stat that other things are derived from, such as number of skills or abilities.

    \n Age serves as your only stat in my game. You sit in order of character age, from youngest to oldest (stolen from Ganakagok). You get another word in your name for every seven years you live. When you want to do something that requires close attention with a time element involved, you can pull a number of beads from your bag equal to the number of words in your name. The beads come in four colors, typically yellow for things you intuit, red for things you feel emotionally, black for things you feel physically, and white for things you perceive intellectually. For each bead, you can add a detail about the scene of that type. So, to make a jump, you really need a certain number of red beads to notice the details you need to make it. If you pull a bunch of white beads, you can narrate all kinds of things you think about, right before you fall on your face because you had your head in the clouds when you should have paid attention to where you jumped.

    It does make elders unabashedly more powerful, which I had an initial reflex to balance. Then I realized, that actually makes a lot of sense for this game! And it helps promote the pattern from the seating arrangement that I stole from Ganakagok: the young kids go out and cause a bunch of trouble, that the elders then have to go fix.

    \n Just played in a Samurai drift of Pendragon. Not sure how much of this is drift or straight Pendragon.
    \n
    \nAge had a considerable impact, not only socially (the relationships between characters is radically different depending on relative age), but also on stats. Every year of age was an extra point to spend on a skill (counting up from the default value of 6) AND an extra point to spend on an attribute. Pendragon uses d20s, so, a forty year old samurai would have two or three decent extra skills and significantly improved all round stats compared to a 20 year old guy.
    \n
    \nNice and simple, but it just gave our older brother the edge, along with his social dominance.

    \n In my World of Darkness Larp Heartbreaker, one power trait of the game, Legacy, is determined by your years of life experience which is originally tied to your actual age (which is also initially tied to your physical age). You get experience points and get to do neat stuff with them, but they don't convert to being able to raise your Legacy. Instead you increase your Legacy by living longer, living an exciting life (cramming more life into your years), and acquiring the life experiences of others (at the risk of losing your Enlightenment as their values over-run your own). This manifests as a thriving "soul" trade, as magical containers holding fragments or even whole remnants of people's lives are passed around and consumed by power-hungry supernaturals or mortals, or merely saved for a rainy day.

    Your physical age had another impact, factoring in on how many qualities (regular traits) you could have, but that's a pretty standard use of age in a RPG. On the other hand, I haven't seen anywhere else where years of age are actually used as units of currency.
