\n Over the past decade and change, I've designed dozens of games. They are design explorations, requests, competition entries, and ideas that wouldn't leave me alone. Now I have an online library of play-ready (or at very least playtest-ready) games. So for the month of April I'll be showcasing a game a day.

A few years ago I caught myself about to argue with Robert Bohl about whether or not there was such a thing as a Shared Imagined Space. Instead of arguing, I decided to design a game to prove my point. Speechless is that game. Speechless is a game where players influence each others separate stories, without saying a word.

    \n The Reverse Engineering competition had designers build a character sheet and then design a game using someone else's creation. I was assigned Claire Bickell's character sheet. I took a strict approach to the contest, going as far as to keep the name of the game listed on the character sheet, Title Goes Here is a game of delving into a text (which could be any novel, whether or not the players have even read it) and constructing a mystical system of truth from that text. I was inspired by my readings in hermeneutics, semiotics, and various forms of Christian and Jewish mysticism to make a game where you play the very high stakes game of interpreting a holy book.

    \n In the midst of a geeky New Year's party I offered to design an RPG right there using terms provided by the attendees. I received a list of 17 words and phrases for things like "zodiacide" and "3-sided dice for thrown weapons". I was going to select from the list, but the host convinced me I should just use them all. The result is Christmas Ninjas, inspired by my housemate's obsession with Naruto, a review of Weapons of the Gods RPG, and a Sluggy Freelance's joke about Christmas Ninjas. Curiously enough this game required little revision from playtesting and for a while was a very popular convention game, especially with the addition of the Rocking Out rules. After all you aren't just playing panda and penguin Christmas Ninjas, you are also rock stars!

    \nComing of Age is an uplifting 1-6 player game, where you play people who are learning their potential and dealing with their issues in the midst of trouble. Coming of Age lets you create your own setting or use a setting sheet (not unlike Fiasco's playsheets). Some of the settings I've put together are Space Police Cadets, Squires of Sword and Sorcery, and Scions of the Superspy, in which you play the children of the Superspy who now must save the world because their deadbeat dad screwed up. And yes, since Coming of Age is a cooperative, GMless game it can be just as fun played solitaire.

    \n Back when the Doctor Who spin-off series Torchwood was in its first two seasons, some friends of mine asked me to run a Torchwood game, which I set in Ahmedabad based on the premise any Victorian colony of the Great Britain could house a branch of Torchwood. For the first season I tried running it with Prime Time Adventures, but my players just couldn't get as focused on their character issues as the action and suspense, so I found myself as producer warping the game to the point where I could practically hear the strain. For the second season of Torchwood Ahmedabad, I insisted on making a system to satisfy what my players wanted. The result is Prime Time Sci Fi, which hacks together four different games and some additional innovations of my own to make a five act TV narrative of increasing tension and stakes.

    \n To me, the cyberpunk genre is about living fast and dying in debt. Debts, personal and monetary, propel characters to stupidly dangerous risks and being faster, more advanced is the only way to stay on top. And the only way to afford the bleeding edge is more debt. Fevered Pace started as a cyberpunk game where you took out usurious loans to play for your equipment and cybernetics and the key abilities are how many times you can shoot, punch, or hack someone each second. Then I realized that debt can be more than money, and in a game with the brutality of cyberpunk, someone will need to take up the debts of those who die. That circle of friends, lovers, and family became the heart of a game that is Rent meets Burning Chrome.

    \nHomeworld Project is a game that originated from two ideas within my RPGnet column, Speculative Physics: Astral Space Opera and inverting the bell curve of dice pools to make extreme results more likely. Homeworld Project is a campaign length (up to a year or more), space opera RPG built on narrative logic. It blithely contradicts a surprising amount of common "wisdom" about designing RPGs, from social footprints to narrative structure, from dice handling to authority distribution. And after my last campaign of it has become my preferred way to play the Star Wars movies.

    \n For a while I've been intrigued by the parallels between the heist fiction genre and the genre of mathematical proofs, and Squaring the Circle is an Apocalypse World hack that merges them. In Squaring the Circle you play world class mathematicians who are also world class thieves. While attending conferences around the world you also engage in a friendly competition to solve open problems - near impossible heists, pranks, and cons, all in the language of mathematics.

    \nDrift arose from a design project I was working on for a friend's space setting where I was trying to drill down to the basics of Star Trek style technobabble action. One of the things I realized is that problems in this genre are solved in one of two ways: a clash of values or with scientific cleverness.     \n Pure Shoujo is a game I designed over ten years ago as part of a personal 52-pickup, where I tried to design a game a week for a year. It ended up being more like for a month. Of those games, Pure Shoujo worked remarkably well. It is truly relationship focused and demonstrates loose coupling in a powerful way. Faust and Friends is a variant of Pure Shoujo from 2007 Game Chef which stirs together demonic bargains and youthful social experimentation. Both games use a simple idea for their core, you must play a relationship well enough that when pointed out the majority of players agree that it has been established, but which relationship you can play toward is determined by random draws from a deck.

    \n Often the advice given to game designers is to quickly get something to playtest in order to see what works and what doesn't. That works for many kinds of games, but Brimstone Emissaries in the City of Thorns (BECT) is another kind. If iterative design is about climbing one of the mountains you can see, BECT is a game that necessitated crossing a desert to get to a new mountain to climb, at an intersection of state mechanics, shared authority, and journey narratives. I can't be sure BECT will be a successful ascent, but I do know there is something here worth exploring.
