\n Last Sunday three people played my game/scenario Enola Gay.

I wasn't one of them, funnily enough, and I got to sit aside and watch.

It's a 7-minute game where the three characters (Commander, Pilot, Bombardier) decide, in a fictionalized moment, whether to drop the bomb on Hiroshima. Two of the characters have known the mission from the beginning, while a third has just found out the full nature of the bombing run as the play clock starts. They have to decide what to do in the last 7 minutes before the point of no return.

If two characters are agreed on a course of action, the third cannot stop them.

It was quite intense from my perspective, probably in no small part due to the fact that this is the first of my games that's ever been play(test)ed, so I was nervous about that. I had to go sit at another table, and I didn't feel comfortable looking up from scrabbling notes for the whole 7 minutes.

The Pilot (played by Joel), had just found out the nature of the mission as play started, and argued with his Commander about what to do. The Commander held fast the whole time, while the Bombardier (Ben) sat on the fence and was quiet most of the time, until the Pilot looked to him for agreement on aborting the mission and turning around. He wavered as the seconds ticked down, and finally gave in, aborting the mission.

When 1 minute is left on the clock, the Commander has to give an order one way or the other, and he forcibly told them to continue the mission, and drop the bomb. They didn't. The last few seconds ticked down in silence.

After the clock runs out, on the way back from the mission, each character can say one sentence. The Pilot said something like "I'm sorry, Commander." The Bombardier paused, and shook his head. The Commander said something like, "You don't know how fucked you are right now."

And that was it. Joel said something about it being "intense", and there was a little talk afterward, but I don't remember it. If anyone who played wants to hop in and comment, that would be great.

It was nerve-wracking listening to it, and playing it too, I imagine.

Was it good? Was it fun? I don't know. As I was pitching it to people my behind-the-keyboard courage failed and I almost felt like it was a total mistake to even write the thing. One other possible player opted out "for personal reasons," which is very legit.

I will say, the game is not about judging history and those in it. It's about inserting ourselves into an incredibly charged situation (charged both for the characters and the players), and seeing what decisions we make, and what that feels like. Think of it as a roleplaying poem, I suppose.

    Posted By: Hans c-oI was hoping to mitigate this somewhat by having every player come up with a reason that their character follows orders, but whether that's successful is up in the air.
    \nRight, but implicit in that is the idea that the only reason someone would choose to drop the bomb is if they have the means to sidestep the moral question entirely -- eg. by an appeal to military authority, 'orders', etc. 'Why do you follow orders?' provides a rationale to play the dramatically-necessary pro-bombing role, and also encourages the kind of experience-what-it-is-like part of the game (where you may conceivably drop the bomb or feel powerless to stop the dropping of the bomb, entirely for personal or social reasons; eg. military hierarchy, running out of time, social browbeating, etc.) But it doesn't provide a rationale for the moral belief that dropping the bomb is the right thing to do.

    The general implication is that nobody would make a moral decision that is also the decision to drop the bomb (so you need to give them another reason, or the game will be flat.) But of course, lots of people did make those sorts of moral decisions, even if the context that allowed them to do so now seems extremely remote to us. I'm not really sure it's possible to elegantly provide that sort of context, though, short of players who already know something about the historical/military situation at the time.

    \n I think my comments may have come across the wrong way: I don't think this game needs to be about moral decisions to be interesting to play. The idea for example of substituting an ahistorical, invented scenario for the Enola Gay scenario seems like it's just designing some other game, largely unrelated to this one.

    Jonathan's response re: characters all deciding to drop the bomb is an interesting one. My initial impression of the game was that it wasn't the sort of game where you take on a character and adopt their worldview completely, but something closer to a character-as-surrogate approach where you put a slightly-modified version of yourself into a historical situation and see what it's like. The sparsity of the questions seemed to support that, but it could be that I simply misread the game, or (see last paragraph) that we both just read it differently.

    Now obviously the questions are still adequate to produce a different-enough version of myself that I could play a pro-bombing character -- I would just have to come up with a really strong answer to 'why do you obey orders?' and focus on that in play. My feeling was mostly that people would do this largely out of dramatic obligation, rather than personal preference, if they imagined that the game was about a moral decision-point. Including a question that aims at this moral context/experience would (I think) allow for a slightly wider variety of play.

    In general, I think for a 7 minute game, the fact that two groups might play it based off completely different assumptions is a major plus, and not something to be scrubbed-out or worried about. The question of what happens when different players within a group approach it differently is maybe a bit more of a concern.

    \n Yeah I admit after I wrote what I did about how there being "near-total consensus" regarding the dropping of the atomic bomb, I thought about going back and hedging further -- obviously I was speaking for my own small-c personal culture, which while North American is not US-American. I also very specifically said "emotional level" because while I feel there are definitely many people who can intellectually justify dropping the bomb as a military action, there are very few who would feel particularly good about the idea, and in the sort of 7-minute-pressure situation I kind of expect emotionality to rule the day. I think the suggested questions about knowing someone who has been killed is definitely the closest/most likely avenue.

    Posted By: Ice Cream EmperorBy comparison, circa 1945 I imagine there were a huge number of people who would emotionally, viscerally feel that bombing Japan was not only acceptable but admirable, even triumphant.
    \nYou are understating things.

    The 1945 American mindset is that the Pacific war is a long way from done. The Allies have demanded unconditional surrender, which puts the Emperor himself in jeopardy, so everyone expects Japan to fight on. That means the next move is going to be the invasion of Japan itself, which is going to be a catastrophe on the order of many Stalingrads.

    If you're an American serviceman, you are rightly terrified of this. You know that Iwo Jima and Okinawa have been corpse factories and that the home islands will be even gnarlier. You've heard that millions of civilian levies are set to charge the landing beaches with bamboo spears in hand and land mines strapped to their chests, and that the entire Japanese Army is dug in behind them. You know your enemy has got nowhere to retreat and they don't care if they live as long as you die.

    And then suddenly, thanks to these mighty silver superbombers and their mighty payloads, the war is over. You're not going to die! Your father / husband / son is going to come home safe! Imagine today if a million cancer patients all simultaneously received a miracle injection that put them in remission. Triumphant doesn't cover it -- how about utter ecstatic jubilation! Urban riots! Fucking in the streets!

    In this atmosphere, I don't think many Americans really gave a shit about what had been done to the Japanese in order to induce their surrender.\n
