    \n I don't own the newest version of the game, but I playtested earlier versions of it and it's doing some unique stuff that no other games are really doing, even though it was originally written back in Game Chef 2004 (like Mountain Witch, Polaris, Ganakagok, etc.)

    Games it's closest to: Ribbon Drive, Breaking the Ice, Polaris, a lot of those so-called roleplaying poems. I'd probably argue that it's the least traditional Forge-influenced game that's actually been commercially published, aside from maybe Sweet Agatha.

    Dev's design and play preferences are super interesting. If you haven't seen his series of notes on playing The Matrix, you're missing out. I wish there were more games out there, whether in print or just formally written up, that looked like the games that exist inside Dev's brain.

    Posted By: hans ottersonRight now I'm stuck between buying this andMaid. One of the selling points of tDatD, to me, is that it seems that the familiarity and physicality of having a chess board in the middle of the table will aid in getting my not-yet-roleplayer friends to roleplay with me.
    \nIf you're looking to break people into roleplaying, The Dance and the Dawn is absolutely your baby and Maid absolutely isn't.

    Or rather, if you're looking to break non-geeks into roleplaying, Maid absolutely isn't.
    The random tables, the game tropes, all of the neat bits about it... are entirely opaque to someone who isn't immersed in some combination of gaming & anime.

    If you want a beautiful game that's easy and interesting, that uses familiar elements (chess pieces) to break into potentially unfamiliar territory (collaborative storytelling! fantasy courtly drama! fairy tales!), then Dance & Dawn is your choice.

    \n Maid works well with non-gaming geeks and otakus, but I'd go with Dance and the Dawn any day of the week if your players' eyes don't light up at rolling a zombie apocalypse out of a random table. Both games are suitably simple and have short reward cycles for beginners to appreciate.

    The Dance and the Dawn requires you to play a girl, which might be an issue for particularly troglodyte players. The same goes for Maid, of course.

    \n Although (at the risk of getting ahead of the game) I've tried to encode enough into the setting/description/gameplay to be critical of the "fairy tale". YMMV; my games of tDntD tend a little towards critique/subversion. I'm curious how different people bring stuff to their own games.

    ...

    One feature of tDatD is that there's a decent amount of GM responsibility and authority (held by the Narrator role). On one hand, you may not want that much authority for yourself in your game; on the other hand, it could be a comfortable balance of authorship if the more experienced player takes on the Narrator role, with the other players focusing just on their characters.
