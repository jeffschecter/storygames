    \n I'm not sure what you mean. I don't track any of that on paper, but I do retain some of it from session to session in my head. For a really complicated game I might sketch out an R-map or have a list of names and locations to draw from. Some games, like Dogs, allow you to set up everything you're going to need - others don't.

    So, at most:

    For a person, I'll have their name and relationships
    For a location or item, just a name with some idea in my head about why it might be interesting
    For plot, maybe some key scenes I'd like to see but more likely nothing

    Is that helpful?

    \n It's tough to give an accurate reply, because invariably locations (for example) are just objects that are malleable by everyone at the table. Example: I set up a gang safe-house. I have a clear picture of what it's like, what surrounds it. At some point Clinton describes sitting in the game room with the guys holding him hostage and watching television. OK, now there's a game room. It wasn't part of my initial mental picture but now it is, and that's how things live and grow in the shared fiction. In effect they are just place-holders for imaginary content until someone chooses to flesh them out further.

    \n I usually give people and factions three details. Something obvious, something inobvious, and something contradictory.

    I also try to keep track of changes. So the NPC I'd thought of as manipulative might have a political scene. If the characters describe him as goofy, then maybe he's goofy and had been all along.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarLike everything else, just place-holders until they get buffed out. Sometimes I'll have a neat piece of backstory to breathe a bit of life into them - in the same PTA game I mentioned above, I included Unit 88, which is a real skinhead organization, and gave them ties to a real Cincinnati-based National Guard unit. So in that case I did about five minutes of Wikipedia homework to add some flavor and then set the result free in the wild.
    \nIt's worth noting that Jason also has an encyclopedic-brain full of color details, applicable to any game setting. This makes it a lot easier for him to quickly sketch out a unit of WWII paratroopers, complete with a detailed list of gear, off the top of his head. It's a skill that makes me envious, because for my part I have to research all that mess and write it all down.

    My notes are usually more verbose- a paragraph or two describing each character, including a bulleted list of any relevant game mechanics (stats, powers, etc.). My notes on places are generally visually descriptive bits of color, enough to remind me of the setting so that I can relate it to players.

    For plots, I tend to sketch things out in terms of scenes I'd like see happen. I usually have more listed than I'll actually use, with little or no relationship between the scenes. They're just colorful moments to throw into play as needed.
