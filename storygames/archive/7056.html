    Hi David, thanks for the question.

    \n

    Drunk should work fine with beginners, is fun to play, and could
    \nbe thought-provoking, depending on what one means with "fun". It
    \nrequired a small level of trust between the players, but since
    \nthere are only three of them, it should be fairly simple to create
    \nwith total strangers.

    \n

    Emily Care Boss, Paul Tevis and myself playtested Drunk at
    \nDreamation this year, Paul and I had never met or played before,
    \nand I think we still had a great time.

    \n

    Drunk is also very short to read, the structure is simple and
    \nsomething everyone can relate to -- inexperienced or not. I think
    \nit would make a good beginner scenario.

    \n

    Otherwise, it seems like the big springboard for jeepform over
    \nhere is The Upgrade! That is also simple, but not necessarily
    \nuntil you have played it. If you're at GenCon, we can definitely
    \ndo that. Or we could Skype about it. I know that at least Jason
    \nMorningstar, Clinton R. Nixon, Paul Tevis and Emily Care Boss have
    \nrun the game, so if you have access to any of them, then that's a
    \ngood start.

    \n

    Otherwise we can trade email or skype names and talk about it on the phone.

    Two answer David's second question:

    \n

    We are not in the habit of making N-player games. Generally, we are either keen on tying story to characters, or having few enough characters to make sure that the game does not turn into a LARP. N-player games are hard if you want to make good stories.

    \n

    From the top of my head, the closest you will get is Thorbi\xf6rn's Free Form Soap Opera or, again, The Upgrade! I guess one could make on hell of a game with 16 players in Lives Births Deaths, but I am not sure it would be an improvement over 6 players sharing the 16 characters as the default setup reads.

      \n Jason said it.

      For my part, acting out a role gives new dimensions to my role playing. Not just the visceral thrill of doing what you're describing, but also playing with body language and communication with others. It helps me feel more deeply what I play.

      Jeep is pretty different than acting or improv. It is like some beautiful marriage of the two. I love the techniques of improv, but I feel empowered by the structure of jeep and similar play, which often give you a loose story to act out. I love the directed fluidity of riffing off of others within a story, with all kinds of juicy cues that help us find an explosive or funny or touching story together. Jeep form uses some very smart ways of throwing you right into the emotional action of a situation. Right off the bat, you've got meaty, contentious issues to deal with and rich relationships with other players' characters to explore and get tangled up in.

      And, man, jeep form is so different from larping in general, that it's just not funny.

      There is a whole world of different techniques you can use. Many flesh out the interior world of characters, others give players new ways to collaborate and comment on what the other person plays, and all of them play with the forms of narrative. In one game I played with Tobias and Paul Tevis at Dreamation (Drunk & Disorderly), we played out the story of a drunk ruining his life and driving away his family, in which each of us played one of the three characters at different times, with a completely different take on each character depending on who was playing them. I'm not saying we can't do this in table top, but there is a facility to live play that makes this kind of thing flowing and possible, and powerful. I can't wait to see it in all kinds of applications.

      \n That's a beautiful description of it, Steve. There's a lot of support and room to work together in the Jeep stuff I've been involved in. When we ran Under my Skin at Dreamation, Bret had a great comment about that. He watched other folks do their scenes, and it seemed like they knew just what they were doing, giving "Oscar level performances" I think is what he said. (It was a great group!) And then when it came to be his turn he was like, "Oh, no! What do I do now?" But when he stepped into the scene, he slipped into the character and it carried him through.

      I've definitely had my moments of "What do I do now!" in Jeep, but that is what the GM is there for, too. Mostly you just play off of one another, but if everybody is floundering, the GM can toss in an idea or get things spinning again.

      Good entry level jeeps are The Upgrade which is extremely funny, possibly Flowers for Mara, which has a strong accessible if somber theme and likely Lives, Deaths, Births which I need to learn more about. Night of Nights is a small group larp--just 3 players, I think, as is Drunk & Disorderly, but that does have more intense issues. My first few were the Upgrade, the Mothers and Doubt. I recommend just diving right in and see if it suits you.

      Also, I hope I'm not coming across as dissing larps. I've only played in a few, but have had some great (and some just okay) experiences with them. I'm all over seeing what amazing things we can do with that form too. It's just very different, technique-wise, from larp. Jeep is like a hybrid of tabletop and larp in many ways, crossed with Brechtian theater. As if that makes it more clear!

      \n By request, swift results: Ditch'd, which I ran at Ropecon this Sunday, went quite well. The larp version was polite, quite subdued and enjoyably boring. For some players it was preferable: there was direct continuity, a chance to enjoy secrets (such as the constant eye-contact one's spouse had with his best friend - is he cheating or not?) and an equal chance to shine.
      \n
      \nThe Jeepform version, in turn, had some really strong extra scenes, gave some players (and one character, whose instability suddenly became apparent) a chance to shine a lot more, and expanded the situation's story - both what was in the game material and what new the players came up with.
      \n
      \nThe cast (eight in total) included some experienced larpers, one or two probably not that used to experimental live-action, one guy with experiences from psychodrama (which has many techinques that Jeepform also uses) and one player who attended The Upgrade with myself and Emily last year. So a suitably mixed crowd. Jeeping offered fun for more, but a significant minority did prefer the larp approach The minimalist nature of the scenario may have affected everything, as Jeep is most suitable for dramatic situations, but I think the experiment rather reliable.
