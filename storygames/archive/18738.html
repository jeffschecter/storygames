    \n If not game, then perhaps roleplaying system. I say this because most RPGs are collections if rules that modulate and regulate the way characters are described and how interactions occur within the fiction and between the players. Of course system is a very neutral and generic term.

    As someone who has done a fair amount of improvisational theatre, I can see a lot of parallels with certain styles of play. Similarly, as someone who has done a lot of wargaming, I can see plenty of parallels with other styles of play. As a sculptor and artist I can see similarities to working within a medium to achieve a desired result, and as someone who has designed a few games, I can see mathematical and social theory underlying the system mechanisms. It's interesting that different limbs of this beast we call "roleplaying" seem to have more in common with outside hobbies than they often have with each other.

    \n -It's a System, it has procedures that are triggered by specific situations and rules that put a limit on argumentation about the fiction.
    -It's Theater, each player gets to incarnate one or more characters and portrait them.
    -It's Impro, the script isn't fully conceived when it starts.
    -It's a Movie, every player has their own point of view of the fiction but it can focus on different things, go foward and backwards in the timeline and all at a speed that theater can't match.
    -It's a Conversation, you need to comunicate both in a verbal and gestual way to get on the same page of the fiction and then negotiate all the changes you make on each of its parts.
    -It's a Book, setting material inspires you to write your prep and gives you the vocabulary to describe it properly, then players write down who are their characters and the rest of the story is developed as a lineal continuum since all players have to take a turn in the conversation to give their input. Read any AP for proof of this.
    -It's a Game, there are either victory conditions established by the system or players get to fabricate them on play and even change them as the game advances. There's micro and macro-management of resources, there's tactical thinking and a playful social struggle to get specific situations happen in the fiction, there's dice-rolling, all of which are games inside the main game.
    -It's a Multimedia shared creation, players use all their skills to representate the fiction and add specific detail to it: they draw their characters, the maps, paint miniatures, sing and compose the songs or bring soundtracks from other sources, perform the characters, etc etc, all activities that have a ludic component of their own. Then players get to present that to the group and everyone else builds on that other pieces of the fiction.

    And then everything overlaps and mixes through the use of ritual phrases (Techniques) that indicate the switch from one into another form so everyone can follow and give the appropiate input in the appropiate time.

    \n The larp crowd seems to spend a lot more time answering the question "what is larp" than the tabletop crowd does.

    When I took a stab at it a couple years ago, the result was similar to what timfire is saying. Tabletop roleplay is a medium of expression, as is larp. And they overlap. I made analogy to the way photography and film overlap. Some elements are very similar, such as the use of lighting and framing and perspective. But film has things it can do by changing the scene over time, things like jump cuts and swipes that don't mean anything in a context of still photography (unless you get into funky art show tricks, maybe). And similarly, photography can pack a lot more density of subject into a single photograph because you have time to study it and notice all the details. So the way that film and photography interact with time is drastically different.

    Likewise there's a huge potential difference between the way a tabletop game and a larp deal with spatial relationships. Tabletop games almost always occur in a single confined, shared space with a small group of people who all hear everything that everyone says (unless you do the "go outside with the GM to talk about stuff" or "pass secret notes" techniques). And the medium greatly benefits from that intimate sharing of the imaginary space. Larp, on the other hand, tends to occur with larger groups in larger spaces with each participant having a more varied experience compared to the others. This leads to a phenomenon I call simultaneous action, which I consider to be the most unique defining aspect of the medium. It's the sense that, within the context of a shared constrained event, that there are parts going on that you are not participating in, but you recognize them as being part of the event you are participating in. The closest thing that tabletop roleplaying usually gets is when some games give you a sense that there are options available that your group did not explore that would have made the story turn out differently - but that's a different thing.

    \u201cWhat is a Role-Playing Game?\u201d
    Table-top RPG's (Role-Playing Games) have evolved so much over the years, it makes a
    single definitive answer too general to be helpful. An RPG might have a Game Moderator/
    referee (or it may not); it might have a single player or several players; each player may
    have one Character to play, or several, or they may all share just one Character! There may
    be dice, cards, maps, miniature figures, or none of these things.

    However, there are three things that all RPGs have in common: rules, fiction and characters.
    But other types of games have these as well. Even the game of Chess could be viewed as a
    fictional battle field where characters are moved according to rules. Chess has inspired
    other table-top war games and war games have, in turn, inspired Role-Playing games. So, what
    makes RPGs so different?

    The difference lies between Play and Game and their effect on Roles. Briefly and very
    simply:
    Play is open-ended, interpretative, without explicit predefined goals.
    Game is challenging, focused, has definitive end-states.
    Role is defined in social context as a set of connected behaviours, rights, obligations,
    beliefs, and norms as conceptualised by actors/players/Characters.

    \u201cLet's pretend\u201d is Play. "Let's Play "house/doctor/space marines". Toys promote Play. Simcity was described by it's creator Will Wright as a software toy; technically, it is categorized as a non-game.
    Chess is a Game (even though you might imagine the pieces to be robots or wizards). Poker,
    in all it's variations, is a Game.
    Calvinball is Play and Game; it has one rule: you can't play Calvinball the same way twice. You can win at Calvinball but nobody is sure how.

    RPGs are Play. They are also Game. They are a strange hybrid of the two modes.
    On a moments notice, perhaps with a single word or roll of the dice:
    - rules may be changed, ignored or even made up.
    - focus may shift from one character, theme, event or purpose to another.
    - goals and challenges can be created, achieved, failed or simply abandoned.

    Having said all that, RPGs are not quite Calvinball; they have a number of rules to
    provide structure, and winning is not necessarily an end-state.

    So, \u201cWhat is a Role-Playing Game?\u201d

    It is a Game (rules) in which one is Playing (fiction) a Role (character).
    I told you it wouldn't help ; )

    RPGs are guidelines for players to explore, interact and affect, and expand on fictional
    worlds through their Characters.
    The \u201chows\u201d and \u201cwhys\u201d are very much dependent on the RPG and the participants.
    So, what is this Role-playing Game.

    is "What is role-playing games?" (it should be "What ARE role-playing games" but your

    \n First, an aside, neither the GNS or the 'Big Model' are a comprehensive survey of real gamers out there. Flying in the face of it are the hordes of people playing out power fantasies as their own. It was good work in it's time, but it was primarily for self-publishers who had to sell by word of mouth. Who had to believe that the rules were them most important thing.

    Within the Forge and contrary to the GNS, I got a meme going regarding the 'act' for 'role-playing gaming' called 'Shared Imaginary Space'. In the land of the Forge, it was impossible to discuss the ramifications of that idea. I am very fond of the idea of Story-Games, but once again I see that as a redaction of creating and maintaining an SIS. (So it doesn't really come out like theatre; it's actually 'Lets Pretend' for grownups.)

    The biggest problem, by far, you will experience looking for grounding idea to base your advertising on is the name: role-playing game. Our game design hobby is both insular and hide-bound against one important fact: we don't own the term 'role-playing game' any more. It belongs to the Final Fantasies of the world, the Fallouts, the Mass Effects, the Diablos, and to the one that most closely resembles OD&D - your Worlds of Warcraft.

    Another Aside: I was learning computer programming back in the eighties when D&D crossed the critical threshold into common parley. Individually, we programmers threw an obscene amount of effort into turning D&D into a computer moderated game. My strongest and most honest opinion is that World of Warcraft became that game (for a while).

    If we are to advertise, we must look at our strengths and not our history. Those strengths are also the weaknesses of those who now own the 'role-playing game' banner: imagination and social interaction. There will never come a time where a graphics processor will trump the imagination or the SIS (largely because imagination is only sometimes visual). And, as yet, the internet has not superseded meeting someone in person.

    To this end, I have begun referring to the games of our hobby as 'spoken-word gaming'. Not for accuracy, but for differentiation. The biggest problem with sticking with the old-school terminology will be confusion with computer and console games.

    Further, after a market study in the nineties, my wife and I concluded the way to get into bookstores, grocery stores or any retail establishment was subversion. At the time, this meant writing the 'Goosebumps' role-playing game. We explored the idea to the point of requesting the licensing for that brand. Back then, publishers did not separate 'game rights' between board, card or electronic, much less SWG. My wife has an intuition for things like this and proclaimed Harry Potter as a 'Next Big Thing' after skimming the first hardcover on it's release week, so I've felt we had a small edge.

    During the Goosebumps and Harry Potter era, I worked in a bookstore and gained a good perspective on how ordering works. Simply, if the brand was hot, we ordered it, without regard to the format; posters, sticker books, comics, candy or anything. We realized one simple vector to get more gamers was to license. As fans, they would sweep everything, even our thing. And where licensing was impossible, to emulate. It was my idea that we should have a center core product line that these licenses and emulations would draw players back to.

    So I began to develop Scattershot. It has twelve core products covering most genre of role-playing games. It's been designed so that a simple or more introductory version could have flavour dropped in and new products turned out quickly, but professionally. Our basic advertising idea was simple: 'Did you like Harry Potter? What if you could BE IN Harry's world?' Since the licensed product would be ordered by pretty much every bookstore in America, we'd get broad exposure. With the hints of 'advanced' play, some of those people would come looking for the core product geared for magic or coming of age games.

    Before long, you would expect those bookstores to start asking for what their customers wanted 'next'. And bamf! We're in the bookstores. Now, things have changed a lot since then. Licensing in role-playing games is far more restrictive. I'm also betting you aren't marketing a multi-genre game system, but I'll leave our story as food for thought.
