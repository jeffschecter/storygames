\n I was just preparing Play Unsafe to be released, on Lulu, and noticed I've sold a copy already. So someone, this evening, searched for Play Unsafe on Lulu and bought it. Thank you, whoever you are. I'm deeply flattered.

Anyway. After several months, Play Unsafe is out. I've taken everything I learnt from improv and applied it to roleplaying games. It's turned out well.



And you click on the above picture to get to the book. But you knew that.

The chapters of the book are:\n
    \n
  • Play: How to make roleplaying games less like work and more fun.
    • \n
  • Build: Why we shoot down other players' ideas and how to build on them instead.
    • \n
  • Status: How to play kings and clowns, letting status drive your games.
    • \n
  • Tell stories: How to, you know, tell stories.
    • \n
  • Work together: How to, um, work together and play better with other players.
    • \n
\nAnyway, there you go. It's pretty good stuff and looks nice, too.

    \n Corollary to Christian's point is that the lack of a pdf option will incentivize some number of sales of the print edition. I'd guess that with a reasonable shipping cost, maybe 30% of folks who'd have chosen to buy the less expensive pdf will buy the print version if a pdf isn't available. So if you could somehow charge a price for your print edition that includes a per-unit profit margin of maybe three times the profit from what you could charge for a pdf, then not having a pdf version might be the right choice.

    But selling a pdf almost certainly has such low cost to you that it's hard for me to imagine how you'd manage a per-unit profit on the print edition of three times your per-unit profit on the pdf. Either Lulu would have to be taking a huge cut of your pdf revenue, or there would have to be some factor of insane downward market pressure on your pdf pricing keeping you from charging but a pittance, or there'd have to be a big, impossible to replicate for the pdf, value-add to the print edition that both justifies an aggressive price and isn't reflected in a comparably high production cost.

    Short answer: yeah, Christian is right.

    Paul

    \n ...fair point. I probably mean indie roleplaying games. Play Unsafe is about the same size and shape as many indie roleplaying games I own.

    Landon, it was a difficult decision to leave out "Yes, but...". It was partly because I dealt with "Yes, and..." in a sidebar, so that "Yes, but..." would be a sidebar to the sidebar. And partly that I didn't want to overcomplicate things, especially at the beginning of the book.

    And, then, I wondered whether "Yes, but..." really has much application in roleplaying. It's used in Polaris (in "But Only If..."), but apart from that, I'm not sure. Unless you count building on ideas and conflicting with them ("Yes, but your sword breaks on contact!") as a "Yes, but...".

    So I left it out. But that might have been a bad decision.

    Posted By: Graham WAnd, then, I wondered whether "Yes, but..." really has much application in roleplaying. It's used in Polaris (in "But Only If..."), but apart from that, I'm not sure. Unless you count building on ideas and conflicting with them ("Yes, but your sword breaks on contact!") as a "Yes, but...".
    \nI sort of see it as the constructive way to build on ideas while preserving the integrity of some game elements or keeping tilts from being too drastic - so you're right; as a middle ground kind of option, it may be out of place in the sort of gung-ho, "swing for the rafters" tone of Play Unsafe. But it's a useful habit to develop when you run into a moment where you might want to block outright, because at least with "Yes, but..." you're still building. It's safer most of the time, yes, but it keeps things going.

    That's actually another point in favor of a .pdf release - it lets the text be a living document that you can update for customers over time.

      Posted By: Graham WMark, I'd love some feedback on the layout.
      \nOkay, I just finished reading the book. Good stuff! I need to mull it over now. Anyway...

      I think p.47's sidebar, "An ordering guideline" is a good example of the spacing thing I mentioned, especially the first line. The justified margins are kinda having their way your text. It could be that this is just a confluence of justification, font-size, and Scribus's text-handling, but the effect is jarring for me.

      Next, look at the spacing between the bullets and text on the book's back cover and p.vi, and compare them to, say, p.28. Similarly, look at the spacing between the attributions and their associated speech on p.16. In the latter case, the attributions are closer to the side-header than they are to the text they label. Or, look at the "couplets" on p.38. The "breaks" (i.e., the second part of the sample sentences) are closer to the following sentence (to which they are not related) than they are to the sentence they are completing.

      I mention all of these because I think they can muddle the "interface" of the book. Spatial relationships between elements on a page help emphasize which ideas go with what. When these relationships are awkward or unclear, it impedes the ability of the reader to make use of the information presented. Consistency and clarity of spacing aids readability and is aesthetically pleasing.

      One other thing I would add is that, since the sidebars generally take up an entire page, I often thought they'd work better just incorporated into the text. E.g., "Don't plan ahead" on p.4 sort of looks like it ends on p.4, but actually continues for one paragraph more on p.6. I found myself often flipping ahead of the sidebars to make sure that I was finishing a section before diverting to the sidebar. But, since the sidebars are less asides than they are key points, it seemed to me that the text would have flowed better if they had just been incorporated into their respective sections.

      Granted, these are kind of nitpicky, but I do layout (albeit web) all day long, so I notice these things. I want to emphasize that this is a fine looking book. I'm just hoping to suggest ways it can be made to look even better.

      \n Hey Graham. I'm a graphic designer who is obsessive about typography. I took the liberty of looking at the preview of your book on Lulu.

      First of all, I'd like to say that I've seen layout done by people with degrees in design who haven't done as well as you have.

      The main problem I see is that you've opted not to use hyphens at the expense of your word spacing. There are ways to get rid of hyphens gracefully, but it requires layout software like Adobe InDesign or (I suppose) Quark, and the knowledge of a few tricks-of-the-trade.

      I'd suggest using a little more leading, and possibly a better typeface for text, like Sabon or Century Schoolbook. I'd probably use Univers or Franklin Gothic for the Headings. These choices won't change the overall feel of what you've chosen that much, just make them nicer and give it a professional sheen.

      Here's the sneaky bit on my part...I'm willing to pay you for the opportunity to polish your document.
