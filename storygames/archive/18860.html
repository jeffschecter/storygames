\n I was listening to the Nerd Poker podcast, and they were talking about switching back to D&D 2nd Edition from 4th, and it reminded me that in spite of buying all the 2E books as a kid I never actually played it as written - I just skimmed the books incessantly and uses them as inspiration for a weird mishmash of house-rules and freeform play. What kinds of adventures did you have with it?

    \n 2nd edition had several things that were unique and were positives or negatives depending on your tastes:

    * Exotic settings like Planescape, Dark Sun, and Al-Qadim.
    * Probably more rules support than any other edition. There were countless supplements.
    * "Kits," specialized spins on classes. D&D Next seems to be bringing back this idea.
    * Decide-for-yourself layer of rules depth. You could play with the main books, but if you wanted to have more in-depth combat, grab the Player's Option: Combat & Tactics book. Want to make your own types of elves and specialized classes? Grab the Player's Option: Skills & Powers book.

    I guess that the only part of 2nd edition that interests me from today's perspective are the settings, some of which feature pretty nice fantasy authorship; I suspect that they would be difficult to use for actual play for me (would need to design a game specifically to utilize them, I suppose), but as rpg setting literature they're occasionally pretty nice reads. The vast majority of everything else 2nd edition has proved to fail the test of time: the game design is either insipid or half-baked much too often, failing to provide either elegance or completeness. The contrast of original D&D on the bottom, Mentzer Basic sideways in the next slot, and 3rd edition on the topside leaves 2nd edition seeming entirely inferior, looking at it as a design with the benefit of hindsight.

    For the sake of fairness, some people give 2nd edition big points for modular complexity. I don't, but that's mostly because I don't particularly desire modularity from a game. Even if I did, though, I would find the level of consideration in the design completely unacceptable by modern standards (and probably the standards of the day as well, but that's more difficult to gauge), and would rather just pull 101 house rule documents off the Internet rather than subject myself to those Player's Option books and their ilk.

    Is it off-topic to ask what people didn't like about it? I got really curious when I heard that "least-loved" comment.
    \nCurrent D&D players generally fall into two camps: the OSR guys and the WotC era guys. (Yeah, 3.x and 4e fans bicker, but they share many traits in what they want from D&D.)

    2nd edition isn't old school enough for the OSR types, as it is about big damn heroes on epic quests instead of fantasy Vietnam (and a large number of rules and supplements, especially by the end of the run). 2nd edition isn't modern enough for the WotC era crowds, as it is a jumbled mess of decades worth of houserules rather than a coherent system like 3.x or 4e.

    2nd edition wasn't very well loved even when it came out. It was the edition where Vampire started outselling D&D. (That period and possibly the 4e period are the only times that D&D wasn't the best selling rpg on the market.) Not coincidentally, it was also the edition of TSR's slide into bankruptcy and getting bought by WotC. So there aren't as many people yearning for the old edition with nostalgia as there are for, say the editions from the early 80s when D&D was growing rapidly. Nor are there as many folks still around who have been playing continuously, as there are for 3.x or 4e.

    \n I remember 2nd Ed *feeling* very exciting, between the nice hardcover books, glossier design, better art, and endless splats. I also remember none of it hanging together in play whatsoever. The "Player's Options" and "Complete Handbooks" were fun to read but inevitably ruined each game they were brought to and were subsequently outlawed, by every group I played 2nd Ed with.

    That drawing of the dragon always caught my eye. Not because it was amazing art, but because it was so unlike all the other art. Instead of an epic battle of improbable dimensions (I've always wondered how a knight with a sword was supposed to believably defeat and kill a 35-ft long, several ton, fire-breathing monster!), it showed a group of "realistic" adventurers (tired, pot-bellied, mismatched) completing a believable "adventure": they'd caught and hung a monster. The monster was a dragon, but one you could imagine a group of such "adventurers" capturing: maybe 8 ft long, somewhere between the size of a dog and a horse, and looking a bit deflated.

    That suggested a much more realistic take on D&D, and one perhaps closer matched to the realities of low-level play.

    \n The difference is always utility, interface, realistic use cases.     \n There is something philosophically aligned about the complete handbooks and the binder monstrous manual. They are both first attempts to systematize the one major part of D&Dism that 1e didn't: freewheeling messy rules that incorporate whatever you happen to have lying around. And although it's demonstrable that in some ways it failed in the market I find it incredibly more appealing in terms of creativity, inspiration, etc., as well as my sense of ethics as compared to the much more strict 3e/4e style of supplement marketing. It's a part of why the OSR has been successful on its own terms: D&D is a messy game.

    In the core books there are all these rules marked as optional too, which weren't present in 1e. It was obviously something that was really exciting to the team at TSR, a recognition of something present in their game. I think it's strange that so much pain has been spent in later editions trying to rebrand campaign settings (especially FR) as less monolithic, giving permission for players to mess them up without knowing every little detail ever published, because the fact that there were so many books and moving pieces came directly out of this era of crazy bits-and-pieces use-what-you-like game design, and that rebranding took place within a culture of increasingly strict expectations of completeness, as 3 and 4 made big attempts at creating unified mechanical structures as well as unified marketing structures.

    The thieves handbook is so good in every way, also.

      A Visitor by Night
      Whenever two or more PCs are together, after dark, a pale blue radiance will suddenly spring into being nearby, growing rapidly brighter. It expands as a whirling, pulsing ball to become the ghostly image of an upright, detached human hand, which turns to point into the darkness and fades away.
      If the PCs look where the hand is pointing, they see a slim, distinguished-looking man, wearing gray robes and a red cloak that seem to swirl and shift by themselves, as if disturbed by an unseen, unfelt wind. The man\x92s white beard is so long that he\x92s tucked it into his belt to avoid treading on it. A few hairs as black as a raven\x92s remain around his lower lip, among their snow-white brothers. The man regards them gravely, and says, \x93Well met. I come in peace, to speak of war and danger\x94 It is the wizard Flamsterd who has appeared via his teleport ring and created the hand. If the PCs don\x92t look where the hand is pointing, Flamsterd will clear his throat loudly to get their attention. If that doesn\x92t work, he will simply walk right in among the PCs, stepping on anyone who\x92s sleeping.
      Flamsterd slowly looks around at all of the PCs present, and says, \x93I understand you are adventurers.\x94 He will wait for a reply, but whether or not one is given, will continue: \x93My land has need of adventurers. I am come from the Moonshae Isles, that men hereabouts sometimes call the \x93jewels on the hilts of The Sea of Swords.\x94 My fair land has faced evil aplenty in recent years, and its folk are weary and sick of death and blood-magic and blades. A new evil has come to our shores; evil never grows tired. I need you, and others like you, to fight this evil. Are you willing?\x94
      Flamsterd will quietly and politely answer PC queries. He will not bluster and cannot be pressured into bargains or admissions. He will say that the evil of which he speaks consists of \x93. . . brigands, undead, and darker creatures, all of them guided and goaded on by priests of the god Bane, The Lord of Tyranny.\x94
      If PCs ask for payment for aiding the Moonshaes, Flamsterd will reply that the High King has already offered them \x93coins, and more.\x94 (If the PCs did not read the letter that Panthras bore, Flamsterd will produce it now, offering it without the state security warning Panthras was obligated to offer.)
      Flamsterd will tell the PCs he can offer them his friendship, aid at times when they are in the Moonshaes, and magical tutelage in a single spell of their choosing, when the evil is defeated. If any PCs are Harpers, Flamsterd will call on them as Harpers to do their duty and aid the Moonshaes, invoking the names of any powerful Harpers that may be known to the PCs.
      If the PCs seem willing, Flamsterd will tell them to report to the Mermaid Sword, a caravel owned by the High King, at a particular dock three mornings hence. (Once again, the time period can be adjusted to suit the DM\x92s purposes.).
      If the PCs are unwilling, Flamsterd will shake his head sorrowfully, and sigh. \x93I see no heroes here,\x94 he will announce. \x93Indeed, none of you can even strike true with a blade!\x94 With these words, the wizard will vanish, using his ring to teleport away. If Flamsterd is attacked at any time during this encounter, he will utter these words and vanish.
      Flamsterd has enacted a powerful steel curse, once widely used in the North. This type of curse prevents a victim from successfully attacking with any bladed weapon, from a bill to a belt-knife -and all PCs present at Flamsterd\x92s visit are affected!! Attempts to
      use bladed weapons as clubs, or to strike them against walls, trees, and other immobile targets, will cause the blade to shatter into tiny shards with no damage to wielder or target (magical weapons also affected) at every attempt. Bladed weapons are simply useless to the cursed beings until a number of months equal to the level of the caster (in Flamsterd\x92s case, 21 months) have passed, or until the curse is lifted.

      \n 2e basically didn't know what it was doing at all - and there was a good reason although White Wolf only overtook them when they weren't actually publishing anything the creative energy all went to White Wolf. D&D, as most of us know, was a game set up for dungeon exploration - and AD&D 1e was basically Gary's Big Book of Houserules which he presented as rules. 2e was an attempt to move D&D from a game about dungeon exploration to a game the Dragonlance Saga would work in - while remaining entirely backwards compatable.       \n I think there were a couple of things about 2nd that were good.

      1) It was the first time D&D really started to look at diverse campaign worlds.       So 2E? Not the trailblazer that 1E was (sort of was anyway) and not as well thought out as 3E (which wasn't as well thought out as Pathfinder), but it did a lot of things that were important and cool.

      \n Hi all. Thought I'd chime in with my 2 c.p.

      IMHO, 2E made progress in that characters are more individual (wizard specialty, customized thief skills, NWPs, kits, etc.); there are issues that it retains the same 1E ruleset with its inherently high lethality, and GM advice to keep this down through fudging die rolls as much as carefully tailoring the adventure. The ruleset in general was designed under expectations of near-total backwards compatibility of the kind that mostly seem ridiculous in the modern era, where a D&D edition change is expected to be a ground-up redesign into an entirely different game.

      The GM advice that more or less assumes bad players (XP penalties for alignment change on the assumption its just 'bad roleplaying' rather than character development, railroading, etc). I think a lot of it you can put down to the designers assuming a majority of players would be aged about 14 - so that naturally a group will consist of a dwarf fighter who wants to kill everything and refuses to travel anywhere by sea because dwarves hate water, a mysterious hooded guy who refuses to tell anyone their class and steals everything that's not nailed down, a chaotic neutral wizard who insists on making all their decisions by flipping a coin, a hot drow priestess with three 18s played by the GMs girlfriend, and maybe a paladin .

      2E does have quite a few toxic ideas which are also more or less par for the course for the era; GM driven stories, illusionism, the idea the rules are fine because you can change them, the 'Stormwind Fallacy' (that picking good options when making a character is directly antithetical to roleplaying), metaplot e.g. in Dragonlance /Dark Sun. To an extent I think it also could be said to encourage cliche-driven RP, low-grade roleplaying as players tried to play mentally challenged or socially dysfunctional PCs, and it has some distinctively strange sorts of characters - like the 'True Neutral' and 'Chaotic Neutral' characters whose alignments were (badly) theoretically derived from the [3x3] alignment matrix idea. On the plus side it should get some points for starting to move D&D outside the dungeon to become a more 'epic fantasy' game, and trying to make the rules at least more clearly written though, I think.
      While it was the longest running D&D edition, 2nd Ed. is also in kind of an uncomfortable place because its hated by 3E people for being a disorganized pile of junk subsystems, people of a narrative bent for having bad DMing advice, the more staunchly traditional 0D&D/AD&D players for not being dungeony/Gygaxian enough and having a story/character focus and too much Elmore art, and D&D non-enthusiasts because of the same things as any other D&D edition (classes/levels/hit points).

      As time went on I guess even the designers got frustrated with the mechanical holes, and a majority of other systems from TSR were quite different; Gamma World-4 (early on, 1992) was deliberately quite similar, and actually the first Buck Rogers game as well I suppose, but Amazing Engine and Alternity were both drastically different to D&D. Amazing Engine in particular was (more or less as noted from designer notes in one of the Dragon magazines) very much intended to facilititate storytelling by being rules-lite and getting out of the way, in a fashion that AD&D didn't.

      \n You know, I think maybe it is a little anachronistic to fault AD&D 2nd Edition for being incoherent. I mean, there's a lot of talk in the OSR threads these past few weeks, about how OSR games home in on what BX, AD&D 1st Ed., or the white box did best, and winnow out the chaff. And, like, yeah, that's great and I'm into it, but that's a 21st Century sensibility. The design sensibility of the late '80s and the entire '90s was: Let's see how far we can push this puppy. And I think, just in terms of raw ideas, 2nd Edition is a resounding success from beginning to end. I hear you all saying, "They tried to make this system support too much that was too far out of the zone of what D&D does well," and I get that, I really do; but I think it's pretty amazing to turn that around and say, "Holy moly, it's pretty amazing that this game supported all that at all!"---even if it only did it partially.

      2nd Edition reeeeally took the game out of the dungeon like no edition before it. It did romance, it did deserts and decadence, it did spacefaring sci-fantasy, it did "dungeon punk" long before the term was coined, it did quasi-historical low fantasy in the greenback series, it did Kung Fu in Dragon Fist.... And everything was designed with a view to giving the players a chance to make almost any character archetype they could imagine (and scores no one would ever have thought up independently), as coherently as the system permitted. They never stopped churning out great ideas that could substantively and fundamentally change the way you play the game; I mean, look at Player's Option: Spells & Magic (one of my favourites).

      In other words, no, it wasn't perfectly coherent by Indy game standards, BUT it WAS remarkably coherent for what it was, and what was being done with it!

      Alright so..being positive...I used to love 2E. Main game I played through the 90s (along with Palladium) since I didn't like storyteller. We basically never used any of the modules and pretty much ignored the GM advice; PHB + the completes (Fighter, Thief, Wizard, Psionics, in particular) + Tome of Magic + 1E Oriental Adventures occasionally. Varying character generation methods (the GM disliked V), used proficiencies and kits though the GM didn't like kits either. For worlds usually homebrew, though we did a couple of Planescape campaigns and a shared campaign experiment with Spelljammer that didn't quite get off the ground.

      I GM'd more or less a one-off of Planescape last year that went across two sessions and was quite fun - the PCs were sent by one of the factions to a ship going through the astral plane to a prison planetoid owned by a rival faction, to free one of their factols. Along the way trouble ensued as a group of githyanki and their red dragon attacked to recapture another githyanki held on the ship, wanted by vlaakith the lich queen for reaching 11th level. The planetoid itself was matter which came originally from the ethereal plane, brought through the ether cyclone ; it 'wanted' to return, generating a large local gate back there, and left to its own devices would eventually have become the seed for a new Prime Material world (the Doomguard PC was appalled; he also went off and broke the clock tower regulating the planetoid by polymorphing its guardian modron into something). PCs fought some ethereal plane monsters (thought eaters) that'd feebleminded the guy they were sent to rescue and got out.

      Lots of good moments playing they game, many aren't especially system-specific though. A few I suppose:
      *the overpowered dwarf fighter PC decapitatated by an pirate with a vorpal sword, except that he also had a ring of regeneration...they stick his head back on and he hunts the guy because the blow also cut off part of his beard.
      *the 12th level or so game where the party did Dungeon adventure A Rose For Talakara, defeating the evil wizardess of that name (24th level) when she rolled a 1 vs. her own Mass Charm (bounced off someones' Ring of Spell Turning). Spoiled a bit by finding out that shouldn't have worked (area spells can't be turned).
      *Red Baron Mk I, someone's fighter who was described as 7ft tall but rolled "1s" for hit points all the way up to 4th level somehow.
      *my PC built with both a DIY race (half-nymph) and a DIY class (a 'lone wolf' thief variant)
      *my halfling gypsy-bard/psionicist in an online game (GM approved combination) who used Read Languages to read Common due to being illiterate.
      *a thief/bard with crazy thief skills in another campaign; the min/maxing here appalled the GM when the character died due to giant crab and he got a look at the character sheet.
      *the PC wild mage who generated some very silly combats due to wild surges, including a 'target falls in love with caster' on an evil warlock, a 'switch personalities with target' during a fight with the BBEG (the Witchlord from HeroQuest), and at one stage being polymorphed into a chimera trying to recharge a magic item using Nahal's Reckless Dweomer.
      *any number of horrible Deck of Many Things incidents.

      Also, the Monster Manual is excellent, with nearly every creature having ecological notes or adventure hooks built in.

      There are some things I enjoy about it mostly in hindsight after another 10+ years playing 3E/Pathfinder: multiclassing works better, the rules are often fairly ignoreable (less skill checks getting into everything), and characters with low ability scores are much less gimpy than in 3E. Also while the subsystems are a bit crazy, they do sometimes give less silly results than 3E (half-ogres picking pockets as well as halflings, old people being better at Spot checks, Str checks being only 20% better for the 18 Str character). Psionics is less like a clone of arcane magic, the classes are generally better balanced somehow, characters are more archetypal instead of the winning combinations being fairly strange things appearing emergently from the sea of weird options. If you use the Fighter's Handbook, fighters have quite a few cool tricks available without needing feats to do things in particular, and generally the damage escalation up levels is less so there's more incentive to try cool stunts in combat.

      Also, non weapon proficiencies and kits I do really like as a character customization tool; unlike skill-based systems none of the skills are really important/powerful, so that its usually fine to just take stuff that suits the character. Often the way skills don't have defaults or are more specific leave untrained use of NWPs open to a 'rulings not rules' approach that can be interesting. --e.g. say you don't have Survival (jungle) and you're looking for food in the jungle, the GM describes the red berries you find and asks you if you want to eat them, stuff like that. Likewise social interaction usually boils down to 'so what do you say?', maybe a Charisma check - Etiquette is just a fairly specific knowledge of protocol which would help in specific ways rather than resolving a social check completely like Diplomacy.
