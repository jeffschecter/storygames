\n I'm curious, as I try to hammer together a draft for Quadrivium, as to how others end up writing their drafts.

I have a mead notebook with me at all times into which I scribble endlessly during my daily subway commute (I carry it in a tooled leather cover my wife bought me in Venice just so that I can brag about it on the interweb - no really). When I fill one of these notebooks up they're a gordian knot of adventure notes, system design, rule fragments for various games, and random thoughts.

Which makes it pretty bloody inefficient trying to extract all the various bits that pertain to a single subject and synthesize it into a digital document.

I played around with updating a blog as a to start this synthesis process. But I keep going back to the notebook (the blog isn't with me on the T ride and the notebook is a habit I find hard to break) so this issue of transcription from handwritten notes to digital document continues to plague me.

I'm curious how other designers handle this. Do you spurn handwritten notes and only record things in digital format? Do you have to go through this same transcription process too? Is it your favorite part - does it exite you or fill you with dread? How do you manage your writing process and what secret ninja tricks have the veteran publishers developed and keep secret from the neophytes?

    \n The earliest ideas are pencil and paper for me. Then draft after draft as text files, then I switch to Microsoft Word when it gets to the point where identifying styles is useful. For Grey Ranks words in German or Polish had their own style, so that when Patrick laid it out in InDesign he could easily identify them and make sure the characters got encoded properly. Once the book is actually laid out, changes happen in the InDesign files, which are also versioned pretty much daily. There were 64 stand-alone drafts before it got laid out, and another 8-10 after.

    \n I compose in text files -- sometimes using Pages or Word or email or whatever but always saving to text files. I do this until I have like 10% of the manuscript and an idea of the shape of the book.

    Then I outline the whole book.

    I rename the text files to correspond to the index numbers in the outline. I create dummy placeholder files for all the gaps, and use labels to make the unwritten ones a different color.

    Once 90% of the text files are written, I start compiling them together -- usually to procrastinate from actually writing the few leftover sections. This all goes into one big text file for each chapter.

    Then I bring the text files into InDesign, style everything, mark what needs to be indexed, and so on.

    I'm seriously considering doing SoL in XML.

    \n Most of my notes actually come from chat transcripts wherein I bother one of my friends with my latest game idea and discuss it for a while. I'll collect these thoughts a bit and post them in my blog.

    \n For Sufficiently Advanced, I started with pencil and paper, jotting notes during class and such. (The life of a TA gives much time for such things, especially when the professor's lecturing.) After that I started an online development journal, putting the better parts of my notes online and getting feedback from time to time. The paper held game mechanics notes and very, very short versions of the civilizations (basically just their tech capabilities and their core values). More detailed descriptions of the civs got written in the online journal.

    When it came to putting an initial version together, all I did was take the important parts from the dev journal and write things to tie them together properly. Now that I'm in the late stages of work, everything gets done in InDesign. I never did an outline; I have a half-dozen outlines from old books that either never got finished or went utterly different directions from what looked good in the outline.

    The original version of Console went similarly, though the starting point was different. I wrote out the advice section first, with a lot of it on paper, and then borrowed my girlfriend's laptop to transfer and add. I still have the 20 or so individual Word files on various different console RPG clich\xc3\xa9s, though of course they got merged together in InDesign for the final version.

    I have written some items entirely on computer, including The Undying Lands (which I still feel is one of my best works), but they tend to be shorter projects. SA needed a shitton of think-ahead time, because I wanted the setting to hang together and make sense. Undying Lands was only 55 pages with a minimal and mystical setting, so logical cohesion was less of a problem.

    \n Notepad files and the occasional scrap of paper for rules notes and concepts. Copies and pastings from chat logs as necessary.

    Then on to Word documents as it progresses to a "final" form.

    I have a flash drive that often sits in my pocket, and often am near a computer (either on campus, or carrying around a laptop with me) so it's almost as good as carrying around a notebook, and faster than my fingers can guide a pen/pencil.

    Then I give it to someone who actually cares about things like layout and all the meaningless stuff that helps people get into the game, but isn't the game itself. That's best done by someone who actually thinks it's important, and I'm totally not that person. Especially interior art, ugh (also note depictive charts, illustrations, dice diagrams, etc., aren't "art" in this sense). I still kind of think I'm selling out my principles having cover art for Fastlane, but Fastlane needs all the marketing help it can get.

    In terms of formatting, I try to write it such that you come across textual information kind of as you'd run across it while playing. Like the first thing you reach should be the first thing you're expected to do in the game, the next thing is the next thing, etc., from character creation (assuming that's first, and it often is, unless you need a setting dump first), etc. etc.

    I also have several non-gamers read my texts over at various points in my process, because if THEY can't follow my game, I'm in real trouble.

    \n I'm a tech writer, so for my work I learn detailed complicated process-y things from people that understand them and try to make sense of them on paper. I write games the exact same way.

    I always carry a standard size sketchpad and 5-6 technical pens with me, which I fill with notes and sketches as ideas occur to me.
    When I've got an idea I want to refine, I brainstorm a few pages of mind maps for it.
    Then I walk around until I've got a particular chunk of the game in my head just the way I want it.
    I draw a mind map or flowchart of just that piece, making sure that each component at least appears on the map so it won't be forgotten.
    Usually the first flowchart is also my first playtest document (I do scenarios this way too).
    Then I write it out as a word doc.
    After each playtest or brainstorming session (i.e. me, a latte, and a long walk), I make a list of everything that came up.
    I go through the list and revise the word doc.
    At the same time I make a text file for idea dumping. If I get an idea that doesn't fit my current text, I drop it in the text file.
    At some point I print the word doc out and revise it in pen.
    If I REALLY care how it flows, I read it out loud.

    What I end up with a bunch of chapters representing different pieces of the game.
    This process has a lot to do with never losing a thought or idea through forgetfulness, a major problem on my part.
    It's also a reflection of my hugely visual brain, since at any point the definitive design document is actually a mind map that no one's every going to see but me.

    \n My ideas hit me in awkward places, like the shower. For a while, I was seriously considering getting a set of whiteboard markers for writing on the shower wall.

    During the evening, which is when I do 90% of my writing, I'm pretty much always within shouting distance of a computer. Most stuff gets brainstormed into .txt, later those chaotic ideas are written into a Word .doc, and it stays there through whole bunchs of interations until it's baked. At layout, the docs get imported into InDesign and final tweaks (add a word here, change the order of stuff there) happens for layout reasons. Then copies are frozen in stone and stored in the vault for safekeeping. Somewhere in that process it also goes by an editor.

    James
