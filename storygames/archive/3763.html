    \n Some issues that arose:

    Someone was a bit resistant to the "open questions" thing that Schizonauts asks. The game wants you to ask open questions instead of setting stakes. For example, you've got a gun pointed at the head of the bank teller. You don't ask "Do I shoot the guy?" you ask "What happens with the gun?"

    I had a hard time explaining to the person in question why I thought this was ok. He was saying that all of the freedom that open questions give you are present in closed questions.

    --

    Bret said that he likes to put in stuff that makes him uncomfortable, so I followed up with him by creating a frigid career woman / hooker-lady. That really weirded me out.

    --

    We ran into a situation once where I opened a scene with a violence trigger but then narratively there was no room for it. We got a little stuck. Fred suggests we try hard to drive to that point.

    Posted By: Robert Bohlall of the freedom that open questions give you are present in closed questions
    \nActually, I don't dispute that at all. The freedom is certainly there. The *sense* however that you're *allowed* to exercise that freedom is far more variable. I think that closed questions tend to put blinders on people without them realizing it. That's why I encourage open questions in Schizonauts rather than closed questions; the sin committed by closed questions is the degree to which they give the impression that certain outcomes are preordained. I want a more even playing field for the range of answers.\n
    Posted By: Robert BohlFor example, you've got a gun pointed at the head of the bank teller. You don't ask "Do I shoot the guy?" you ask "What happens with the gun?"
    \nDo I shoot the guy is actually a borderline question; you could see it as either one. A better example of closed options, as I think about it, is in classic stakes-setting: "Okay, if you win, the bank teller gets shot, if I win, *you* get shot" or whatever. It's determining what happens before the authority to determine what happens is determined. Closed questions skirt the edge of that, and that's something I specifically didn't want in how Schizonauts plays out.

    But let's go back to the borderline, "Do I shoot the guy?" Here's the problem: the answer to that is "Yes" or "No", the way it's phrased. Yes, you shoot the guy. No, you don't shoot the guy. Those sorts of responses are more heavily weighted, even if it gets storytold as "Well, you shoot at him, but the bullet deflects off the glass at lodges in your shoulder." or whatever. I personally feel when the question is instead "What happens with the gun?" people are more willing to go interesting places with it -- it's more likely to trigger some free-associations which are thematically appropriate for the game. I might look at "What happens with the gun?" and think, "You mean after the bank teller gets shot? Well, I think it gets lost from the evidence room and the shooter ends up walking thanks to a mistrial." I'm not sure I'd ever get there from "Do I shoot the guy?"\n
    Posted By: Robert BohlWe ran into a situation once where I opened a scene with a violence trigger but then narratively there was no room for it. We got a little stuck. Fred suggests we try hard to drive to that point.
    \nYeah, the language of the text needs to be a bit more on point about telling people, "When you choose a trigger of a particular type, the scene itself is predisposed towards that sort of resolution, colored by whatever the visitor is bringing in as well." So Violence triggers are scenes involving violence, Intimacy and Knowledge having similar behaviors.
