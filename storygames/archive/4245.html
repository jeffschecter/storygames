\n

\n\nptevis \n\n\n

\n
\n

\n
\n One of the common guidelines in our improv troupe is \xe2\u20ac\u0153a scene about an agreed-upon activity isn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t about the agreed-upon activity.\xe2\u20ac\x9d That is, a scene about two guys fishing isn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t about the fishing. It\xe2\u20ac\u2122s usually about the relationship between the two guys, and the fishing provides opportunities for, as Josh so nicely puts it, externalization.

In roleplaying games, on the other hand, games are often about what they\xe2\u20ac\u2122re about. D&D is about whether or not you kill the monsters and take their stuff. Burning Empires is about whether or not you stop the Vaylen invasion.

However, I\xe2\u20ac\u2122ve found that enjoy a number of games when they\xe2\u20ac\u2122re played in the former mode, rather than the latter. My experience with Polaris demonstrates that. The Esoterrorists is another interesting case. By removing the uncertainty of whether or not the investigators can find the clues they need, the system makes it much more likely that I\xe2\u20ac\u2122ll want to focus the game on the relationships between the investigators. (This my general approach to procedurals, by the way. Mysteries aren\xe2\u20ac\u2122t really about the mystery, as Dirty Secrets so nicely demonstrates.)

So when is a game about what it\xe2\u20ac\u2122s about? I don\xe2\u20ac\u2122t really know. Anyone else have any experience like this?

    \n Part of this depends on if you mean "game system" or "instance of a game" -- I don't just mean you Paul, but what anyone means by this.

    I have to say that my more recent games mesh with your idea -- our Burning Empires game was on the surface about a prison world being infiltrated by the Vaylen, but was really about what it takes to get people to compromise on their beliefs and how their relationships change. My last Unknown Armies game was on the surface about a group of folks saving the world while getting some revenge on folks who betrayed them, but ended up really being about how you treat the people you work with.

    I think I'm unconsciously (until now) working this into my next UA game, about a group of related Godwalkers who reunite after a central loved one dies, on said loved one's farm. On the surface, the conversations are about who gets the farm, but underneath it's really about who deserves to Ascend. I don't know if this will work, but it's an idea I've been toying with.

    But I'm not actually answering your question, because I'm not sure either. Maybe with "game system", PTA is about what it's about -- playing in a TV show. But then, is an individual game about what it's about? I don't know, and maybe it's different for every game or story.

    \n It's really pretty simple, James. Looking at the Burning Wheel example... are the players going to change the fact that the game "involves" battling the Vaylen invasion? Not without changing the game, making it "involve" something else, playing an essentially different game. But will they fill the void that is the question that Luke asks about the consequences of the actions that people make in a setting that involves a Vaylen invasion? Yes, this is where the players get to be creative, what the game is "about."
    \n
    \nSo Paul's example with Essoterrorists is apt - it certainly involves investigations, but it's about how the characters handle themselves about each other while involved in such investigations (the mechanics eliminate play being "about" whether or not the investigation succeeds). D&D is a bit more agnostic on this, because the setting is semi-ambiguous, and you can do a lot of things outside of killing and taking stuff without detaching from the mechanics altogether. So most D&D games are going to involve killing things and taking their stuff, and also be about how well the players do that. But it's easier to take D&D to some other place than it would be to, say, make a diplomacy game out of Essoterrorists, or to play a traveller campaign with Burning Empires.
    \n
    \nSome would say that's a strength of D&D, some would say that's a weakness, but that's all just preference for system specificity. In any case, D&D is more involved with, and "about" killing things and taking their stuff than, say, GURPs is about or involves anything in particular. So it's a spectrum.

    \n That's a whole thorny tangle. For one thing, a lot of games have messy, contradictory, and highly drift-y reward mechanisms. For another thing, it's been traditional to override a lot of mechanical reward mechanisms with social ones.

    I'd say that that's actually more or less correct - the main reward in D&D is getting to change your character sheet to be more powerful. Leveling up, getting loot, filling in the map of the dungeon. Mastery stuff.

    In "gradual improvement" games like WW, it's less about increasing effectiveness by changing the character sheet, and more about aligning the character sheet with your vision of the character. The reward for playing your character is that your get to play your character more. But games with that highly incremental style of mechanical reward often end up being more about some socially driven reward than the mechanical one.

    I think the way a game gets to be About Something in the Capital Letters way is by having a strong, identifiable, accessible reward system. Many "traditional" designs really don't want to be About Something - they intend to have that filled in by the play group through social reward. That's one reason they tend to have fairly weak or noisy reward mechanics.

    \n There can be layers of abstraction between what you do in play and what the game is about. I think Burning Empires was the first game that revealed this to me clearly. In Principia, I consciously put layers between play and what the game is about. Principia is about resolving abstract questions. But the things you do in play are not abstract. They're about this interest, this relationship, this conflict. But everything you do when you're dealing with these particularities has an impact on the questions. Eventually there comes a point where mechanically you answer the question and narrate what it means for the game world.

    So I guess my answer is that in Principia, it's during chapter-ending narration.

    In general, I think a game tends to be about whatever the players can't resolve directly. In D&D, they can't just level up. They have to do something to level up. This probably means killing goblins, and maybe carrying out some kind of mission or quest. So the game is sort of about that quest and sort of about becoming mightier. In Burning Empires, it's resolving the Vaylen threat but just as much about everything that earns you artha. In Contenders it's your dreams and hope and what happens to them when you try to become the champ. These just happen to tend to be the kind of stuff that lukzu spoke about.

    Does that answer your question at all, Paul?
