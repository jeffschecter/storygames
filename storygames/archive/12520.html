What do you look for in an RPG intro?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nDanielSolis \n\n\n
\n
\n\nJuly 2010 \n edited July 2010 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n RPGs are a little peculiar in that they often have a section in the front that spends a fair amount of time introducing you to the game you're holding. Sometimes it will also explain what RPGs and story-games are as a genre. Some intros are shorter than others, perhaps only a paragraph, while others are pages and pages. Some focus on the setting, others on the rules. Often there might even be a list of influences on this game's design.

My question to you is: What do you look for in an RPG intro?

    \n I agree with Justin, actually. I like reading about the setting.

    What I really want to read is the designer's vision for the game. So, in Shab Al-Hiri Roach, I want to read about universities and tenure. Steal Away Jordan, I want to read about slave narratives. That vision usually includes the setting, to a great extent.

    And then I want to know, briefly, whether it's GMless, how long it lasts, etc, etc.

    \n I'm not going to read about your setting unless you write good prose. And my idea of good prose is probably pretty harsh (I skipped the setting material in Nine Worlds because it wasn't engagingly written. Nothing personal, Matt, just my tastes). Even then, I won't read about it for more than a page or two. I want my setting to be attached to every rule in the book so that I learn it as I learn the game -- not in some special section where I have to study it.

    On the more general topic of the introduction: I expect to see a reasonably concise statement of what the game is, what the motor of play is, and what the point of play is. This expectation is usually sorely disappointed.

    And then, there's prefaces. The only RPG with a preface I've seen is Josh Roby's Sons of Liberty, with a preface by John Wick. It was awesome.

    \n In a Wicked Age was one of the first indie RPGs I ever read, and one of the things I liked straight off the bat was the brief but good introduction. What I liked about it was that it a) concisely explained what to do to play the game, along with what you needed, and b) instead of going on about how awesome, if misunderstood RPGs are, described it as an activity for cool, sexy adults with good food and a classy ambience. I've been trying to figure out how to get my non-gaming friends into RPGs, and I think that sort of attitude/description was helpful for that.

    \n I want to read:
    -- what your game is about
    -- why I should play it
    -- a quick summary of how you approach its premise (like, if it's Dread: "this game uses pulls from a Jenga tower to simulate the uncertainty of actions in a horror movie")

    Basically, I want you to hook me three ways: with the premise, with my desires/interests as a player, and with an appealing mechanical/procedural approach.

    Also, if you use the term "story games" to mean "indie RPGs that I happen to like" rather than "an online forum," I will find you and put a banana creme pie in your face. That's a promise. If you don't want to call something a "roleplaying game," just call it "a game" or maybe "a tabletop game" or, better yet, "a game in which you do XYZ." (Sorry, personal pet peeve.)

    Posted By: DanielSolisWhat do you look for in an RPG intro?
    A lack of one.

    Your cover and back cover need to pique my interest before I open the book. Once my interest has been piqued, I want to know what I'll be doing in play. If Page 1 is Step 1 of play, I'm happy.

    That said, if Step 1 is "GM introduces genre & setting to group", then Page 1 will have to cover genre and setting. So I may not be saying anything too radical here.
