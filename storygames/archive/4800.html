    \n It looks like a system designed to facilitate some pretty high-energy, adventure-y, pulpy action. Quick and lean -- dare I say Risus-like in some ways, but using descriptors in a way not entirely unlike Aspects in Spirit of the Century/Fate 3.0. If I had to boil it down to someone, I'd say "a quick dice randomizer with Fate's Aspects wrapped around it to tweak the numbers", and I would consider that both an accurate and positive review.

    Sidenote: A friend of mine played in one of the two games that you used this system for at AmberCon, and had a lot of good stuff to say about it.

    It's interesting, because it ties into something that I'd been pondering with regards to mechanics over the last couple weeks. That 'thing' is this: \n
    There are really two things that an "edge" in a game can do (by "edge" I just mean "that thing on the character sheet that lets you tweak things in your favor" -- Aspects, Edges, Traits, Supporting Characteristics, etc., etc.).

    1. They can be used to give you a intial, pre-roll boost to your chances of success, increasing your odds of being awesome. Primetime Adventures does this. Spirit of the Century pretty much does this. I think your system does this.

    2. They can be used to stave off or lessen the sting of failure. Galactic's "Edges" do this. I think that any traits you narrate in after a conflict has started in Dogs in the Vineyard do this. "Doom" in Conspiracy of Shadows does this. I know there are others.
    \nNow, my point is this: numerically, your end result using these 'edges' in either of the two ways above might be exactly the same, but they *feel* different, and that feeling probably seeps into the tone of the game, pushing either toward adventure-heroic or the survivalist-gritty.

    I'm not talking power level here. It doesn't matter if you're giving folks one 'edge', or five, or ten -- I think if they're implemented in the style of Mode 1, above, it's going to have a kind of "let's be awesome" feel, and if you're using them in Mode 2, it's going to have a kind of "let's survive this" feel.

    So I guess what I'm saying is that the game system itself is sort of built toward a "let's be awesome" tone.

    My friend mentioned, while talking about the action-adventure game you ran, and explaining the system to me, that you'd used the same system for a gritty 'world war two tank story', and my first thought was "I wonder if the system will be subtly fighting that tone."

    He said "but I think they're using it at a lower power level", and I replied that I didn't think it would matter, for the reasons mentioned above.

    I noticed that you've posted about that 'tank' game in another thread, and mentioned that it was a little more Kelly's Heroes than Johann's Panzer, and I wonder if maybe ... at least in part, if that wasn't because the system was whispering "let's be awesome" instead of "let's survive this."

    Maybe, for a gritty game with the same system, the thing to tweak to help you with tone isn't the number of 'edges' -- what you call Supporting Characteristics, but the way in which they can be invoked in the game. In other words, for 'gritty' -- you can only invoke them after the roll, if the roll wasn't in your favor.

    Or something; just random brainstorming on my part.

    \n I'm struggling with creating a similar effect in a d6-based system - my approach at the moment is to have "successes" which are roll under a number (the number varying based on the character's rating in the kind of conflict it is), and "consequences" which are rolls of particular numbers. At the moment, 6 is always a negative consequence and 1 is always a positive; the negative number gradually counts down the longer the conflict goes on, so it becomes more and more likely that you'll get a negative consequence, even on a success.

    That's not much direct use for you but it may possibly spark an idea in some lateral way. (Basically, I looked for something that wasn't going to directly depend on whether you got a success or not, but was strongly correlated.)

    \n Some thoughts on setting tone.
    \n
    \nFirst off some Supporting Characteristics do work to negate Effect Traits. In the Professionals game at ACNW we had "Takes a Licking and Keeps On Ticking", "Calm, Cool, Collected" and "Unshakeable" which came into play mainly after the characters had taken hits.
    \n
    \nYou could make it grittier by allowing SCs like "Takes a Licking--" to enable characters to act as another Effect Trait box. For a while, until they can get fixed up or a temporary ET wears off.
    \n
    \nIn the Professionals game the Reroll awards were aimed squarely at the action movie genre. We got them for cool action bits and character bits -- interesting shots or scenes in our movie. Award them for furthering the grit -- or whatever feel or genre you're shooting for -- and it should help things along.
    \n
    \nPower level/ SC number may not help per se but it more SCs does allow you to use them for gritty, tough bastard characteristics.
    \n
    \nTone is also set by level of opposition. If you're scrambling to win your conflicts and watching your ET boxes fill up, that sets it on Gritty/Survival to my mind .
    \n
    \nYou might add in more of the tappable SotC scene Aspect thang to give the game more of a tactical feel. Mods to Effect die rolls against you for taking cover, mods for smoke, fire, polka music, whatever.
