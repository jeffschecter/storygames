\n I have gotten stuck with a bit of advice I am trying to convey. Where the common wisdom has become "Say yes or roll the dice", I am trying to find a good way to say, "Don't roll the dice unless all outcomes will keep the story going". There are lots of times when we say, "That would be cool but I can't just give that to you...Roll" without thinking about the other possible outcome.
1, is there a better way to say it?
2, am I completely wrong?

    \n Ah. I see. I agree. That's why I stick to games that simply eliminate ability and task rolls and only feature rolling for conflicts between characters.

    But if your game still includes rolling for those things then I suggest those things are most interesting when they cut off (often tactical) options. Like when failing to pick the lock on the back door means you now have to contend with the 5 guards at the front door.

    So I suggest "confront the player with a situational choice" might be an appropriate phrasing.

    \n Not all mechanics give you one of two outcomes. That's actually one of the ways to make task failure better. Just plain failure gets boring quickly (but not immediately), so a high whiff game (or situation) will likely treat failure as blocking your contributions. If, on the other hand, you have a variety of ways to fail - many of which are quite distinct, then failure itself is interesting. Look at Elfs - where character's failure does not equal player failure.

    You could imagine a similar problem arising in a conflict resolution game where, let's say, your premise is "can you protect the people you care about?" What happens after the fifth consecutive conflict where yet another friend, family member or lover is killed in spite of your efforts. Baring some shift in your premise, you'll encounter the whiff effect just as much - the game will rapidly become about as interesting as repeatedly being told "Nope."

    In each case you could shift your interest. Not "Do I succed?" but "How pathetically silly are we." (I've had a d20 game drift that way beautifully after 4 consecutive failures on a basic climb roll.) Or "Can I protect?" but, "How do I cope with unstoppable loss."

    The real point is to only bring the dice in when they make the game more interesting, and not do so when they won't. Figuring out when that is, well, it's something we are always learning.

    - Mendel S.

    \n Hi Thor,

    All of this really is very system dependent. Some systems have Task Resolution, some don't. And so on... How to phrase these things really depends on how a system works.

    So, when you're writing it, it really depends on the system. With that said, I know my rule of thumb for when I play Sorcerer is, "When someone wants something to happen, and the someone actively doesn't want that to happen, then roll the dice."

    Now, some systems support that, and others don't. Here's an example from a conversation taking place over at the Sorcerer Forum boards...

    Let's review the basic rule: if Adam says to Belvedere, "Sit down!", and Belvedere doesn't want to, then there has to be a dice roll. For purposes of the example, let's say Adam is an NPC and Belvedere is a player-character.

    The dice are rolled, and Belvedere loses, to the tune of three victories for Adam. You're the player. Belvedere is your character. What can you do?

    1. You can sit down.

    2. You can do anything else, but if dice are involved in the "anything else," then you have a three-dice penalty.

    Notice, that for any result, because of the Sorcerer rules, the story "keeps going." The dice are only rolled because something is going to change. Someone is:
    a) winning
    b) backing down
    c) shifting the conflict in a new direction when they refuse to back down

    So, no matter what happens status between opposing interests has changed or continues to shift until the change in status is settled. That's interesting no matter what.

    I don't always know ahead of time when a die roll will make the game more interesting, but I do know that when conflicting interests meet that is interesting right then and there. I then introduce the dice to resolve a conflict because I want a resolution that a) takes the responsibility of the resolution off my shoulders, b) helps provide unexpected results (which the Sorcerer system does.)

    My rule of thumb, "When someone wants something to happen, and the someone actively doesn't want that to happen, then roll the dice," works, then, because once we start rolling dice interesting things are happening.

    What is the context of the system you're using?
