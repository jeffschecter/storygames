\n Over on Anyway, I said:\n
...why games succeed: those games get in the hands of people who want what they offer and deliver on what they promise. That's it.
\nAnd then Simon C said:\n
What do people want? Do people know what they want? How do people know when they're getting what they want? How do you best communicate what people will like about your game? How do you know what people will like about it?
\nSeems like a good topic for discussion, though I think we should start from the assumption that you've designed a game (at least the initial draft) that you, the designer, like very much. Trying to design a game based on other people's supposed preferences is the road to disaster. BUT! Now that you have a game, you're going into the revision, publication, and marketing process trying to figure out how to present your game so that it really finds its audience.

So, that said, how should we approach answering Simon's questions?

    \n First of all, I'm surprised, and indeed, dismayed, that you identify this topic as primarily being one of designers trying to sell/edit what they've designed instead of players trying to play well and enjoyably. "What do people want?" and "How do they know what they want?" and "How do they tell when they're getting what they want?" are crucial questions for all players at all tables in all games, no matter who designed the game or by what soul-chilling marketing they were sold. So I will address those questions first.

    I think one thing that's definitely of importance is that many preferences are decided on rather than discovered. I can be convinced to want new things, things I didn't previously want, or might not want in another context. This is something that marketing does, but not exclusively. Art itself can do this. When I listen to a new band, I might say, "wow, these people are fucking great, I must buy their back catalog down to the last crappy movie soundtrack single", without reference to what was put on an album cover or how many radio stations were bribed to play their song.

    But other preferences are not decided upon, they aren't chosen in any particular way. Some of these are best considered in terms of prejudices, since they are formed in the same way as prejudices.

    I think even beyond those questions is another question that is more germane to the play experience:

    What are the best practices for players regarding their preferences?

    The goal obviously should be openness and toleration to the extent they can be practiced, but recognition of the limits beyond which they should not go. How do we do this? Here are some tips:

    * Conscious selection of a new approach. Have you ever played hack-and-slash D&D? Try it. No, go find a game and try it, for reals, see how far you can get into it, what is good about it? (Shopping lists.) What is bad about it? (Arithmetic that accomplishes nothing.) When you get done you can go back to doing what you did before, write down your thoughts and experiences.

    * Try to express as specific a preference as possible for something extremely super-narrow, and why you have the preference. "I like red-headed female NPCs in my pulp adventure stories because I can vividly imagine them on a fictional pulp magazine cover, even if we're deconstructing a bit and they don't fit directly into a pulp stereotype."

    More to come?

    \n This is something I can speak to as a videogame developer. It's really fucking hard, and even "those games get in the hands of people who want what they offer and deliver on what they promise" isn't that simple, because both, say, Medal of Honor and Call of Duty deliver what they promise, but CoD delivers more and/or better. And then there's the brand lock-in you see with D&D - which is still the most succesful ttrpg, right? Are people playing it because they're promised something and getting it? Or are they playing it because everyone else is, because of social proof?

    But just on the "What do people want? Do people know what they want?" front:

    1) You can approach this by looking at successful titles and making them "like that, but better" - this is how Blizzard made Warcraft and World of Warcraft. They looked at the successful Dune 2 and the successful Everquest and one-upped. Usually you're going to fail with this approach, though: the title you're imitating is going to come out with a sequel about the same time as your title, and you aren't going to have the resources to compete, plus they've got brand lock-in.

    2) You can approach this with polls, surveys, focus groups. Another troubled path. People don't know what they want. They will tell you they want whatever their latest, favorite game has....and see #1.

    3) You can introspect. "I love this kind of thing, so I bet other people would love this kind of thing." That's how I came up with the swinging game mechanic in Spider-Man 2. It's problematic because you might be weird - what you like might not be what the masses like. In fact, because you are so deeply into games, you may find the trite surface amusements the masses enjoy unpalatable, and your deeper pleasures may be inaccessible to them! (For me, Spider-Man 2 was successful beyond expectations, but I don't know how much of that came from the game mechanics and how much came from a powerful brand. Otoh, Schizoid, another introspective title, did not sell as well as we expected.)

    4) You can try a "funnel" approach - this is what TV does. They film a crapload of pilots, and test them, and keep the one(s) that people like the best. To extend to games, make a crapload of prototypes. Follow up on, refine, and sell the one(s) that get traction. This is probably the most effective method, but it can have problems too. Some of your unfinished games may not be ready when you test them, and you might kill something that could have been really good because people don't like it - yet.

    So a mixture of #3 & #4 is probably your best bet. Make a bunch of games you love. Keep working on the ones that other people love, too.

    Of course, that's probably what you were doing anyway.

    \n There are many things that I want from a game that might be unfair to ask:

    I want an event that I can get people to gather for.
    Flower for Mara does this for a smaller set of my friends than D&D, for example.

    I want a game that will be a fun experience for people I don't know that well - basically a game that helps acquaintances become casual friends.
    For example, I've played boardgames where people are quiet and don't get to know each other, and roleplaying games where the whole first session was spent in rules confusion. Those fail versus 3:16 played as pure machismo.

    I want a game that doesn't get stale - I want something that helps casual friends become closer friends, if they're basically inclined to do so.
    Can't be fluffy bunnies every time. Can't be Settlers of Catan every night.

    \n I think Apples to Apples is a great example. It gives people something they know they want in broad strokes, and then uses particulars that have a high likely of being agreeable to anyone who bought into the broad strokes.

    Anyone who likes conversational play with opportunities for sharing and silliness can see that this game might be for them.

    Anyone who then tries it will find no barriers to entry in the mental effort required -- simple rules, easy decision-making. You can play it on a table, or a floor, or anywhere you can drop a handful of cards.

    Balderdash, on the other hand, caters to a much more specific set of likes. If you don't enjoy being creative, your turn will be no fun. If you don't enjoy playing detective and/or learning weird words, everyone else's turn will be no fun.

    I like Balderdash much better, but can find many more people to play Apples to Apples with.

    Cranium and Hoopla are interesting cases, as they includes something everyone will like, and something everyone will hate. It's a game of exchange, where the competition means that your suffering is ameliorated by seeing your opponents suffer too. As long as most of the game is fun, then it all works out in the end. Rather than attracting only players who like all their activities, these games seem to attract more players than specialized games like Balderdash (I have zero proof of this, just seems true among my gamer friends).

    I wish I could give similar assessments for D&D, Vampire, PtA, Dogs in the Vineyard, Burning Wheel, etc., but that is much fuzzier in my mind.

    \n Here's some things I think:

    Sometimes people need permission to like a thing. Most people don't like to be caught liking something that is agreed by everyone else to be crap. When a well-known designer makes a game, people have permission to like it. When a less well known designer makes a game, they don't, so they're more cautious about it.

    People choose their preferences based on what those things say about them. This is more true of music and other things with strong cultural markers, but there's still meaning in being a D&D kind of a person over a Polaris kind of a person.

    Possibly people don't know what they want. They say they want a game with lots of violence, simple rules, and a strong GM. Then they sit down to an introspective and complex GMless game, and have a blast.

    Context is probably important. If your friend tells you a game is great, you look at it charitably, and decide you like it. You see some asshole playing it, you decide it's a game for assholes, so you hate it.

    Sometimes you sit down to eat your soup, and they've changed the recipe. Any you're like "Shit!" and "I liked the old soup!". But if you trust your soup merchant, maybe you keep eating anyway, and maybe you find out that you like the new soup just as much. Maybe better!

    What if the people who need your game are not the people who want your game?

    Posted By: J. WaltonHow do we find the people who need our games but do not necessarily want them?
    \nEvery time I read this I want to poke my eyes out.

    Nobody -- nobody -- "needs" a game.

    There's a fairly compelling argument that all marketing is about solving problems. There's also an argument that much of marketing is convincing people they have problems that need solving. I'm not a huge proponent of the latter but the former has some merit, at least when it comes to marketing games. I think it's easy/lazy to couch RPGs in aesthetic/artistic terms, but it's kind of a marketing rabbit hole to try and chase taste that way. If you've got crazy money and a distribution foothold, you can go the Fantasy Flight route and market the shit out of visual aesthetics. I don't think anyone in this little pocket universe is playing at that scale, so that leaves us with gameplay aesthetics -- good luck trying to describe that!

    I think a really good place for many/most game designer-slash-publishers to start is to really think about what gaming problems their designs solve. I think at some level, the indie gaming community has already done the work of convincing people they have gaming problems that need solving, or that there are (passive voice here) problems that needed to be solved.
