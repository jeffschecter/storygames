    Don't be surprised if I ask a whole lot of seemingly thick questions.

    I guess what I'm most interested in right now is how to get players in a mindset that says "Go for it, take chances and roll with it because it is a one-off thing you're playing" but still encourages a style of play where the real people want to keep the other players involved throughout the whole play time.

    This is actually a kinda big deal to me with in regard to various kinds of rpgs/s-gs. I don't mind if a character or faction gets knocked out of play, but it's important to me that a real person who has shown up gets to participate throughout if they wish, with minimal barriers to doing so.

    \nMelbourne notes

    We were at Arcanacon for one day, so naturally I'm now an expert on the local gaming culture.

    Melbourne has at least three yearly cons, depending on who you ask;
    and many participants are at all three cons. This seems to give
    continuity to the community. Many people get the majority of their
    role-playing at the cons; in between, they play board games, but
    rarely campaigns.

    The GM is important. The title can be misleading; to GM a game can
    simply mean facilitating it. People often choose what games to play
    based on who's the GM.

    People write their own scenarios - but there's apparently no interest
    in publishing, although GMs are happy to send you their files on
    request. The drive is to run fun games for people in the community,
    not to reach out. However, we met several people who'd played many US
    indie games - one girl said her first gaming experience was with PTA.

    We played two full games: A CoC tabletop and a multiform.

    The tabletop was what I'd call a solid, fun, traditional freeform.
    Diceless, with pre-created characters; clear setup of central
    relationships - though there wasn't much focus on developing
    relationship dynamics, this being a free-rolling one-shot where we
    sort of assumed the characters would die anyway. The very competent GM
    portrayed NPCs, had all necessary info memorized, set up the
    situation, let us get ourselves into trouble (the characters were
    designed to make that quite easy), and kept events moving at a regular
    pace. GM fiat and basic impro skills were the (implicit) system.

    The multiform was a semi-larp freeform game. We gathered around a
    table, got character descriptions and looked at a map of the ship. The
    genre and plot expectations were communicated clearly: there would be
    a murder, and the characters were mostly stereotypes of different
    nationalities. (This is typical for con one-shots, where you need to
    be able to get into & portray your character quickly). Again, GM fiat
    and play culture were the system; we rolled dice a few times, but were
    never told the mechanics - I did notice that my roll of 5 was
    different from another player's roll of 5, but don't know why.

    The game was larplike in that me moved about, and acted out stuff -
    getting on my knees to drink vodka from a lady's shoe, for example.
    Many players were in costume; apparently there's also a strong
    steampunk community which overlaps with the freeformers. (The
    combination of rules-light games and opportunity for dressing up seems
    to result in more women in the community).

    Often we'd want to go to different "rooms" in the "airship". Either
    that meant a quick talk with the GM in the corridor, or the GM would
    temporarily designate a corner of the room as kitchen, laboratory or
    similar.

    Towards the end the GM said we had fifteen minutes left, in case there
    was anything we wanted to do. I tried to push the main character off
    the airship, but a die roll showed that I fell to my death instead. So
    I sat watching and taking pics for a little while.

    A general note: The games, being one-shot con games, did not focus on or promote any form of heavy immersion, complex characterization, emo porn etc. I'm not sure how that translates to the rest of the Melbourne gaming culture. There are, apparently, ongoing Vampire larps that probably cover this, but I don't know anything about it.

    Questions are welcome!

    EDIT: John Dalton, our capable and sociable GM in the multiform is apparently an active steampunker - here's his web page.

    \n As far as I could tell, most Arcanacon people were well into their twenties. One of our GMs had his daughter co-GM the game; I don't know how old she was, but probably late teens (I'm getting so old now that I have trouble guessing young people's age...) It seemed to me like the crowd there knew each other fairly well, so I guess they're regular con-goers who have been playing for years.

    Another clue is that the con (at least on the last day) wasn't really set up for newcomers; when we got there, there was no sign on the street outside the school area; people couldn't really tell us whether a game had started or not, whether it was full or not, or who was the GM... we often had to ask around a bit to find out basic info. So it's probably a place where everyone knows what's going on.

    ("Standard larp" doesn't exist. UK larp, French larp and Scandinavian larp are very different things. Danish larp and Finnish larp cultures are not at all the same. But after that obligatory pedantic comment...) From my point of view, the multiform we participated in had these traits:
    - Larplike: Some people dress up. We move about and portray our roles with fitting voices, accents and physical gestures.
    - Un-larplike: There's no attempt at a 360 degree illusion; that is, nobody tries to hide the fact that we're in a classroom (not on an airship), the diamonds we steal are hastily sketched on pieces of paper, etc. Much of our shared fiction comes from GM descriptions and other verbal cues. We roll dice for resolution.

    The scenarios aren't really distributed, which is a shame; however, the GMs are (so I hear) happy to send you their files if you e-mail them. That assumes, of course, that you know who they are, what games are good, and can find their e-mail address...

    \nNo, not wrong at all. :P

    And remember, most of this gaming is considered old school to us. A lot of this stuff was formulated down here in the late 80's.

    As for dice rolling, an Aussie freeform typically has none. If conflict arises between character, the designer will often have an order of priority for who would win in specific situations, these will typically be modified by who has done something strategically within the context of play to gain an upper hand. The exact nature of this strategic advantage depends on the designer's ideas about task resolution or story flow.

    Some common resolution methods include.\n
      \n
    • Giving characters a range of values for comparison if they happen to be involved in a certain type of conflict (these might be as general as "combat", "smarts" and "etiquette"...or whatever is suitable to the scenario designed). In most cases a player won't know their character's actual scores, but their detailed history will indicate a general idea of their potential. Such values are rarely numeric, and might be "Poor", "Average", "Good", "Very Good", or a rating from A to F (Where A is good and F is bad...lowest letter involved in a confluct wins)...very karma based.
      • \n
    • Simply making all characters equal
      • \n
    \nComplications to this are usually derived through the actions of the event. \n
      \n
    • If a character describes something strategic to the GM, their rating in a field goes up by a point.
      • \n
    • If a character manages to accomplish one of their goals in the scenario, a related field increases.
      • \n
    • If a character manages to get an ally or two on their side for an attack against a lesser number of opponents, the larger groyup might use the highest value and increase by 1 for every additional supporter.
      • \n
    \nIn most cases the initiator of the conflict describes their victory condition, the victim describes theirs, and then any onlookers offer their input to the GM. The GM decides based on everyone's input, then describes what happens. Each player's input is addressed in the description of the conflict outcome.

    It has only been a recent phenomenon for Australian freeforms to include dice or randomising mechanisms (such as Rock Paper Scissors), notably appearing after White Wolf's Minds Eye Theater appeared.

    A multiform is where dice tend to appear...the majority of the play is LARP-like, but occasionally a need for game mechanisms rears it's ugly head and the dice come out (or cards or other system specific resolution method.\n
    Also, can anyone speak to the effect that the one shot nature of these games has on design, gamemastering, and character play? And possibly also on returning player culture?
    \nTradiationally, well written Australian freeforms have a web of complex relations spread across all the characters. If you kill someone early in the piece, there is a good chance that you will alienate someone who is necessary to one of your goals, or one of your allies goals.

    The first third is typically working out where allegiances lie across the game, and who you can use to further your own agenda (typically, a character cannot fulfil their agenda on their own and they will need someone elses help to accomplish something significant). The second third is usually a complex game of helping allies with their agendas in exchange for gaining their help, some character impairment or elimination may occur at this stage. The final third is a climax, usually occur when one of the major background stories comes to the fore...once a plot line develops a critical mass of players behind it. There may be some kind of showdown, possibly a mystic ritual, a combat, a wedding, something dramatic that a lot of players are linked to in their back stories.

    Most characters have a stake in each potential climax (and their may be two or three potential climaxes to a scenario). If there are multiple climaxes, they may occur simultaneously and players need to make decisions for themselves as to which they involve their characters in. At this climax point, it's make or break and players make big sacrifices in the hope to change things toward their character's cause.

    Players tend to accept the unwritten rule that other characters shouldn't be killed in the first half of the session (after all, they paid good money to participate), but once the climax is reached, all bets are off.

    The majority of these games are one shots. Those that occur as an ongoing sequence are like movie sequels, the surviving characters from the first installment typically appear in the next...but a new batch of characters join them.

    I hope that helps.

    \n Traditionally (in Australia), Freeform is live action roleplaying without rules (or using a very minimalistic rules set), it relies very heavily on the social interactions of the participants to move the story toward a climax.

    On the other hand we have tabletop, which may use a standard set of rules or may use a set of rules of the GMs devising. Non-standard convention games have been designed as one shots in much the same way that we see people now producing many focused indie games (a focused set of rules specifically designed for telling a single type of story, with a scenario specifically designed to exploit the full potential of those rules, characters are typically (but not always) pregenerated). Table-top play using a standard rules set is something I'm sure you're familiar with.

    Multiform is a hybrid between these options.

    Different GMs/Designers will select different elements for their hybrid, which brings me to this earlier comment from Matthijs:\n
    Posted By: Matthijs
    The GM is important. The title can be misleading; to GM a game can simply mean facilitating it. People often choose what games to play based on who's the GM.
    \nWith different GMs choosing which elements of live play to incorporate and which elements of system to retain, different games can have a very different feel. Most GMs tend to develop a following and stick to a specific formula when presenting their games. People come to them expecting a certain play experience, and they try to deliver. We have much the same thing in Sydney. If people want to be led by the nose through an adventure where their characters WILL get screwed over, they go and play one of Frank's games (some people love this, and all the angst it brings...other people call it railroading and hate it). If people want something freewheeling and wild where they can betray one another on the way to persona glory or character agenda fulfilment, then they'll come and play one of mine. Neither of us run a very rules intensive game, but we pull the rules out that help us tell the story we want. We'll use a variety of systems that people recognise, but in most cases only pay lip service to the books, and often make up new rules on the fly to cover situations that the original game was never designed for. Pulling out a rulebook in this style of play is often considered a heinous crime.

    Some multiforms involve a lot more movement, some use a hybrid of miniatures and live action (notably a sequential series of multiforms called Raven's Nest, which ran at least twice a year over a period of over a decade in the late 80's early 90's, using miniatures and a scale city that was over two metres wide and at least 4 metres long from what I remember). It had very simple combat and skill mechanisms and a lot of deep political intrigue, with players often starting a game with three or four agendas and picking up more along the way as they interacted with the other denizens of the city.

    "What is Multiform?" is a very difficult question to answer because every GM has their own interpretation. What we call "Story games" or "Narrativist games" might be considered multiform to some. Other might only call it multiform if it involves props and live-action aspects.
