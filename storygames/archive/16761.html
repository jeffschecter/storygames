\n A note -- For the purposes of this thread, I'm looking at games in terms of an optimal number of weekly sessions:
One-Shot = 1-2 sessions ( 1 to 2 weeks )
Short Form = about 3-5 sessions ( about a month )
Medium Form = about 8-25 sessions ( 2-6 months )
Long Form = about 40-200 sessions ( 1-5 years )
Very Long Form = much more than that ( one or more decades )

I regularly run long form games (approximately 3 years of weekly sessions). I'm usually stuck using fairly conventional games for these purposes (Storyteller, d20, 7th Sea, etc.). I'm looking for games which work particularly well for that span of time, with a more indie/story game bent.

One I've played which straddles that divide is Artesia: Adventures in the Known World which has a Tarot inspired character growth system, which while a bit complex offers a tremendous richness to how characters change intentionally and unintentionally.

I'd like to hear about other options. Preferably with play experiences. For example, I'm not sure I'd believe that a game of Burning Wheel can take over a hundred sessions without the system entering into some unusual territory, but if you've experienced otherwise I'd love to hear about it.

I'm not interested in speculations or arguments why folks shouldn't be playing or designing indie/story long form games. If you really want to talk about it, make another thread and I'll offer my perspective.

- Mendel

    \n Burning Wheel is a great one.

    With Great Power is excellent for this. So is Smallville.

    In A Wicked Age, Dogs in the Vineyard, Houses of the Blooded, Blood and Honor, Universalis and Mortal Coil have mechanics that really shine in long-form games (the second visit to a town, the owe list, season mechanics and actions, the various fact lists, etc.) that simply don't do anything in shorter games, so playing them as shorter games is silly, don't ever do it.

    There's a bunch more, actually there's not that many strictly short-form story games OR indie games.

    \n My longest game of BW ("Beyond Grunweld") is just reaching session 31, so not yet a long form game by your standard.

    Abilities-wise, the characters started out very green (3LP, and decidedly non-optimal, especially in one case). They've now rounded out quite a bit, becoming solidly competent in the areas they're being challenged so far. From the data I have, I estimate that the characters are logging a usable test each every half hour or so. Advancement does slow down quite a bit once you reach that level - you need more tests to advance and the obstacles are much higher. Also, permanent injuries have knocked down one character in a few ways, so it's not uniform improvement.

    Just from a numbers perspective, experienced characters are still pretty easy to challenge. Another game (I'm a player in this one) we're playing 6LP elves that are vastly more potent than the Grunwelders, but we're still highly vulnerable when we're out of our comfort zones. The Grunwelders can kick ass against B4 mooks, but a couple of mounted knights or a persuasive bishop are still a major challenge.

    But from a story perspective, it still feels very fresh. The players are contributing a lot to the direction of the game, so I'm starting to feel the world deepening in a very exciting way - all the more so because much of what's going on is a ripple from something the players initiated.

    Though, we're nowhere near 100 sessions. What does your spidey sense tell you will happen?
