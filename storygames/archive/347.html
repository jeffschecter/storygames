John Harper wrote:
I don't think MR has a "pre-defined result". Because if you have agreed to the result before the roll... well, you're resolving stakes. :-)

Remember, this is stakes resolution:
"If you roll a 25 or better, you hit and do 2d8 damage."

So, maneuver resolution would be:
"Roll to hit. DC 25."
So I'd like to expand and discuss John Harper's suggestion of "Stakes Resolution" versus "Maneuver Resolution" -- which I like a lot and seems much clearer to me than many previous Conflict/Task definitions. As I understand it, Maneuver Resolution means that by definition the possible results aren't known at the time of roll. Though I think a better term might be "Principle Resolution" (i.e. resolve the principle first, and only later arrive at specifics) or perhaps "Abstract Resolution". Any thoughts?

Now, here's the tricky part about player knowledge. Let's take D&D3. Suppose that I am DM, and I have a monster which has Damage Reduction 10/+2. So if I try to hit it with a weapons with less than +2 magical bonus, then 10 is subtracted from the damage. So now the player attacks this monster with a +1 sword. Is this Stakes Resolution?

The answer seems to depend on player knowledge. So suppose the player doesn't know that the monster has damage reduction. He attacks, and rolls 2d8+5 damage for total 11, and the GM then announces that the monster seems barely affected. What's going on here? So there's a concrete principle that the DM is following, but the player doesn't know it. Then suppose the player realizes that it has DR after another PC attacks with a +3 weapon. Do his attacks now become Stakes Resolution, where they weren't before?

This applies equally to all sorts of other rolls. Suppose I have in my GM notes what the result of succeeding a Perception check at a certain point does. Is the roll Stakes Resolution?

    John, perhaps you're conflating two different resolutions -- the hit, and the damage.

    First Resolution: Do I hit Mister Monster? Stakes are Yes or No. Roll. Answer turns out to be Yes. Stakes resolution.

    Second Resolution: How much do I damage Mister Monster? Stakes are not really defined. Roll. Answer turns out to be hardly at all. Manuever resolution.

    I would argue that hit and damage rolls are one application of the mechanic, like a multi-step conflict in Dogs. Just because it's two rolls (or three with a crit threat) doesn't really make it separate mechanics. If that's a problem, imagine we're using a single-roll system like Buffy or Rolemaster, or in D&D a case where every hit always does exactly 11 points of damage without a roll.

    The point is that once the player realizes that the monster has DR 10/+2, then he knows what the damage result is. In all cases, the outcome of the damage roll is precisely defined, and follows strict laws. The only difference is player knowledge of what that is.
