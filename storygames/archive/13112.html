Again, our "official" (scarequotes!) rules summary for these threads:

-----
1. Feel free to respond here in-thread to another link posted.
a. If you're looking to start a conversation, perhaps take your comment and create a new thread with it in the Stuff to Watch category.
b. Otherwise, to prevent too many comments in the thread, number your response (first response: [1]; second response [2]; third response [3]). If you see that you're the second person to post, "Wow, that looks cool!" to a post here, then put a "[2]" at the top of your post. Three posts ([3]) is the limit: The fourth person to post should just take their comment and create a new thread, to carry the discussion over there further.
-----
Why?

Because these threads are great: We can link to little things being talked about on the web in general, on blogs, or other forums that might be noteworthy, without having to dedicate a new thread to each and every one (although that, too, is fine if you want to discuss the item further or "open up a discussion").
Responding to these posts with a comment or two is understandable (other times there might be no comments, just cause the link was self-explanatory: nothing to say, lots of heads nodding in agreement). But we don't want to create a huge discussion in the middle of the thread: Some folks get a lot out of these threads, and don't want to look back to see the "10 latest posts" just to see that it's a heated discussion of something from a previous page. Cool? Cool.
-----

    \n ...Ok, maybe i was the only one who didn't know about it, but...

    http://www.freakangels.com/

    Flooded London. Post-Apocalypse. 12 youth with ESP powers striving to make things better. Written by comic superstar Warren Ellis (at his finest) and with magnificent art. it's really worth a go: complex psychology and inter-group dynamics, an interesting rendition of what post-apocalyptic community's life would be (lighter than usual) and... well,a darn good plot.

    Also, it's interesting as a business choice: even though Ellis is a total professional (both Vertigo, DC and Marvel under his belt, he's still working for Marvel i think) and the artist is getting paid, they decided to try to disttribute the comic free on the net, and make money on collected volumes and merchandising. And it worked. everyday life for story-gaming, but quite uncommon in comics, isn't it?

    \nICv2 says Pathfinder and D&D; 4E are tied for sales...
    \n
    \nFrom...\n
    \n
    In the RPG category, the neck-and-neck competition between WotC\u2019s Dungeons & Dragons and Paizo\u2019s Pathfinder continued in Q3.
    \n
    \n
    \nI can't confirm the validity of this claim but ICv2 is a fairly trusted source. This may be true in hobby gaming stores but I don't think Pathfinder and 4E are tied elsewhere. Redbox 4E is outselling Pathfinder on Amazon (although Pathfinder outsells both the 4E gift box and player handbook 1) and from what friends in publishing tell me, 4E is on top at BnN and Borders as well. Although Pathfinder is doing incredibly well! And I've heard rumors at Gencon that their monthly subscriptions are in the 10-20,000 range for their adventure modules (rumors also say that total to date D&D; DDI subscribers are in the 40,000 range but no idea if this is correct or what the present numbers are).
    \n
    \nEither way it's incredible.
    \n
    \nThe previous conventional wisdom was that modules don't sell. Pathfinder is making a killing with modules.
    \n
    \nEdit: Lisa Stevens, Paizo CEO, confirmed that ICv2's reporting matches her sales data reports from Bookscan.

    \n Five elements of a story, via Rob Donoghue's blog.

    a. a character we care about
    b. who has an opportunity, problem or goal
    c. who faces obstacles and/or an antagonist
    d. who stands to win something (stakes)
    e. but can lose something he cherishes (jeopardy).

    It is possible for a story or screenplay or movie or episode to fail even with these five elements, but it is almost certain it will fail if a, b, c or (d or e) are not working right.







    I realized that we were using a, b, c and d in yesterday's Archipelago game. But it would have been even stronger had we defined e.

      Joe, that's "good play" to us because it's about story, I guess to many it's "not fair". I find that the first part of the article "Lie" isn't that bad. In fact, I think it's even the opposite of illusionism. Illusionism is about taking choice away. Changing the human into a tiefling is expanding the choices. Even if the reasoning is a bit screwby.

      The last bit, "Steal" is perfectly fine by all means. I mean, we all plagiarize all the time, no surprise.

      The problematic part is probably "Cheat", although I think he advocates it with good intentions. Cheating the system is a big part of "immersion" play "Right to Dream" call it what you want. Sometimes the dice break the illusion. If it's the DM's illusion, then we might have a problem, but if it's for the sake of the group's shared illusion then it's necessary. It might be abominable to me (and you), but people have fun that way. Cheating to make a fight more challenging is somewhat excusable in a game that's about challenges. No point in fighting a boring, easy fight where you already know the winner. Lowering the difficulty of the challenge is totally abominable to Step on Up and steering right back into Right to Dream, protecting the "hero" status of the characters (They can't just die!).

      Yeah, I've just written a bunch of stuff on Creative Agenda. I have a hammer and every problem looks like a nail.
