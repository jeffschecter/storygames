\n Just another little thought I'm throwing out there. This one is more hardcore theory than the Furious Five.

The thought is: the rise of games like Apocalypse World which move from "the GM (or another specific player) has final say when X occurs in the fiction" to a more egalitarian phrasing of "when X occurs" place greater emphasis on the underdeterminacy of the fiction in roleplaying.

To back up a bit: my sense is that Forge-influenced RPG thought has traditionally viewed the fiction as completely indeterminate, that the same fictional situation could be interpreted completely differently by different players and that some player ultimately has to step forward and use their social authority to interpret the situation and create a shared understanding. Bill White and I had a conversation on the Structured Freedom forum a while back and he asserted, quite correctly I think, that the fiction cannot interpret itself, meaning basically that the "proper" interpretation of the shared fiction is not objectively evident.

However: Vincent -- both in AW and is his Anyway discussions about fictional positioning -- seems to think that's not the whole story, and I suspect that he's correct as well. The fiction in roleplaying -- the things we communicate at the table that get cobbled together to determine "what happens" are not without shared meanings. Indeed, it would be stunning if we didn't have, to one degree or another, some shared understanding of what's going on because that's kinda the whole point, right? So I would argue that the issue isn't really that the fiction is completely indeterminate, it's just underdeterminate.

To paraphrase and flat-out steal from Wikipedia on underdeterminancy: knowing the meaning of the words and other communication that flows between us at the table IS SIGNIFICANT, BUT ONLY TAKES US SO FAR in resolving controversies. Due either to ambiguity or vagueness, the meanings alone may not always lead players to the same unique interpretation of what is occurring, but they do point the players in one or more directions.

So when Vincent throws down a rule that says: "when X occurs, do Y"; that's an intentional (?) contraction of "when you think that X occurs, tell the other players and prepare to do Y." It's basically telling you to make the smaller interpretive jump from the underdeterminate fiction to a shared understanding by invoking the rule (i.e. making a move), rather than assuming that the fiction doesn't help you at all and attempting to make a looooooong interpretive jump from meaningless fictional color to "this is a conflict now."

The latter attempt feels more arbitrary in play, right? Because it's not building on the shared understanding that already exists. It's like "Oh yeah, and then ninjas attack!" But in AW, even the most random insertions into the fiction or orders to roll dice are heralded by fictional (or in some cases, mechanical) predecessors: you threaten badness or totally blow a roll.

Now, clearly there is a limit on how determinate the fiction (or the rules, really) can be. Ultimately, Bill's right, it can't interpret itself. But Vincent's also correct that it can lead us much further down the road to a shared understanding than we might normally give it credit for. "Following the fiction" is definitely a thing. As for just how far we can follow it before one of more players have to step in and say "this is what this means"... I'm not sure we've discovered that yet.

    \n If I might venture to unpack...Whatever happens in the game's fiction needs to be interpreted, we must constantly evaluate "what's going on", so we can engage the rules. You know, clouds, arrows, cues and all that. I think what Jonathan is getting at that Moves bring us closer to a consistent shared interpretation of the fiction on a moment-to-moment basis than other game mechanics do.

    You know it's the old "higher ground" thing. Who gets to say my elf has the higher ground? How do we establish that in the fiction, how do we interpret it. In D&D I'd say I jump on a table and then maybe or maybe not the GM gives me a +2. It's a vague and unclear leap from cloud to dice. We might get into an endless discussion about whether or not my elf is on the table and whether or not it counts as high ground.

    In some other theoretical game, I spend a Tactics point and I get +2 for high ground. If I actually narrate what's going on in the fiction doesn't necessarily matter. I'm making a dice-to-dice mechanical thing.

    Moves, on the other hand, state that when X happens in fiction, we'll do Y. "When you seize the higher ground." Poison'd was a venture into that same territory. It's a concrete fictional cue that's predetermined. It brings us on the same page about what's going on in the fiction at the same time as engaging the dice.

    There is a question that a lot of games don't answer at all, which is "When do we go to the dice?". Trad games often have no instructions for this at all. A lot of games just say something like "when the result is unclear". Dogs in the Vineyard for example, tells you "when there is a conflict". When is there a conflict? Who decides? We must interpret the fiction. There's a big interpretative leap from what's going on in the fiction to actually taking the dice out. It's like, one moment we're just talking about what's going on and the next we're rolling and it's like a break.

    What AW is doing is that in a hypothetical, utopian game, those leaps are smaller, because the rolls emerge directly from moment-to-moment fiction play. When do I roll +cool? When I'm acting under fire.

    \n The first problem here seems to be that the debate in legal theory is systematically ambiguous about the concept of indeterminacy. An indeterminist about the law claims that nothing counts as law until it has been declared to be by an official with the requisite power. Because this sounds all spooky and left-wing, critics of the position on both sides of the political spectrum are fond of saying that indeterminists believe that the law isn't "objective," in the sense that the constitution and subsequent legislation don't even constrain what can or can't be counted as law. But this is of course an entirely different (and self-evidently preposterous) claim.

    The notion of indeterminacy first came up in a rigorous context in the early discussions of quantum mechanics, when Bohr and others argued that it's always indeterminate what the spatial positions are of certain subatomic particles. They certainly didn't mean that any subatomic particle could be just anywhere. It later became important in the philosophy of linguistics - W.V. Quine famously argues that when one is translating a radically unfamiliar language, it's impossible to come up with a single empirically optimal translation manual. But he didn't think that one's observations of what native speakers said are entirely irrelevant to what one should think that they are trying to communicate.

    So the upshot is that the word "underdeterminate" is being used throughout this discussion to mean what "indeterminate" actually means. And the larger moral of the story is, if you want clarity in theoretical discussions, don't trust the hack amateur philosophers who set the conversational agenda for America's inane and hopelessly broken legal system.

    End of sermon.

    \n I think the best way to frame discussions about various types of indeterminacy is via the various senses in which interpretations of a story can contradict one another. So someone who subscribed to a very radical form of indeterminism might say that two interpretations of a story which involve contradictory claims about what a text (or gamer) actually says could be equally justified (e.g. When the GM said to Fred, "You killed the troll with your axe," Fred's paladin both did and did not kill a troll with an axe). A more moderate form of indeterminism would deny this possibility, but insist that equally justified, contradictory claims might be possible about the background information that one needs in order to understand the story (e.g. that Sherlock Holmes both was and wasn't less than seven feet tall). An even more moderate version of indeterminism would deny this possibility, but claim that it's possible to make equally justified, contradictory assertions about the motivations of characters within the story (e.g. that Hamlet both was and wasn't faking his madness).

    Posted By: Paul T.Is it just the argument that we can bring this feature back into "modern" designs? If so, it's a good one, but it seems to me it's one Vincent's already made very thoroughly on his blog (with all those right-pointing arrow posts).
    \nBasically, yeah, although I think Jonathan's point is about how reliable the fiction can be as a guide. If you imagine a continuum between looking at something in real life, like the number on a die, or whatever, and someone making an arbitrary decision, looking to the fiction exists somewhere in the middle. I think the point Jonathan's making is that how reliable the fiction is as a guide depends on how we have all built it together - how rigourous we've been about establishing assent.

    To use my mime analogy again: When you see a mime ride an invisible bike, they don't just jump on and start riding. They'll brush off the seat first, waggle the handlebars, roll it back and forth a couple of times, then swing their leg way up and over the thing to get on. What they're doing is establishing your assent to the bike. They're saying "the seat is this far off the ground" "the handlebars are this far apart" "It's a bike, see?", so that when they hop on that thing and start riding, we know what's happening and we accept it.

    It's the same in roleplaying. Rules that help us establish and agree to the fiction first, that let us brush off the seat and kick the tyres, allow us to rely on the fiction a lot more robustly when it comes to resolution.
