\n So, I've been doing a semi-regular gaming night for a bunch of friends - I organized it over Facebook and there's a pool of about 30 of them. Whenever I have an event coming up, I invite them and typically a small handful of them (3-5) show up. For the last three sessions, when I attempted to do Don't Rest Your Head, I had, at best, one person showing up, so I figured folks might have just moved on to other things.

Then I announced that the next event was Lady Blackbird.

I've had a rush of responses so far because it looks like the game concept is hooking them in all the right places. The thing is now I'm concerned that there may be several more people coming than there are pre-existing characters for the game. This may be like the night I ended up doing a nine-person game of InSpectres (which actually did work out, though folks didn't get quite as much spotlight time as I'd have liked).

I'm not sure I'll have enough time to create extra PCs before it happens, so can anyone point me to any resources where there might be more characters for the game? o_0

    \n I played with 7 players.

    It wasn't my fave gaming experience, but it was a rollicking good listen-way-more-than-you-talk kinda game.

    If you're dead set on playing with that many players, try this weird thing i'm making up right now:

    1) split two characters (say, snargle and the bodyguard lady) into two characters each. Let's take the bodyguard as an example. One of the bodyguards leans on her relationship with LB, the other leans on her relationship with the captain (right? that's the bodyguard's other relationship?). And the two bodyguards have a close & complicated relationship with one another, that you devise.

    When we played, the relationships were much more important than the mechanics because, y'know, not enough time for all of us to both talk and engage the mechanics meaningfully. I bet you could, should you take this route, have the twin sets be mechanically identical.

    Now, i don't think this is a good idea.

    This is a last-ditch idea to weld a new weird part onto the game so that you can all socialize behind an awesome fantasy veneer for a few hours. If you guys want to actually, y'know, play Lady Blackbird, you should split into two groups.

    Posted By: John HarperInvite fewer people. Run it twice for two different groupings.
    \nYeah, this is likely what will be happening. I must say, though, that it's a testament to the appeal of this game that I got such a positive response from people so quickly. It's an awesome concept.

    I think I've gotten into the habit of "overstuffing the invitation box" as a means of getting what would be considered an average number of players. Typically a lot of people will be interested, but in practice only a few of them actually show up. I'm used to this because I usually run games my friends have never heard of, so whenever they come over to play they're always having to dip their toes in something they're completely unfamiliar with. As a result, they can be a bit intimidated and hesitant to put in an appearance sometimes (also, you have the standard adult/life/kids/job/schedule thing going on with them that makes it difficult for them to make time for leisure activities - or they just forget, which happens a lot as well; most of the folks I play games with are a lot of fun to have in a game but are extremely casual about concepts like time and attendance). That combined with the vicissitudes of Facebook Events has made for one of those situations in which I have no idea how many folks will ACTUALLY show up (though, granted, that seems to be a pretty common thing for Facebook Events).

    So it's just as likely that I'll have about the right number (or fewer) as it is that I'll have too many people. But I like to have a contingency plan, so extra folk who turn up will sit out this one or maybe play a different game at a second table and will get to play Lady Blackbird next time (those who manage to make it next time... sigh...). 0_o
