    \nBah, pre-empted my answer.

    But seriously, I've got nothing. I should by all sense, considering my current and successful highly heterodox OSR campaign that's completely based on module material, but it seems that I'm just happy with the original text. This is probably because I've worked hard to develop a methodology that spins uneven module material into all seven sorts of excellence in actual play; this process has simultaneously apparently blinded me to detail, as being critical isn't conducive to working with what you've got. As I see it, the module has plenty of weirdness to turn into awesome in the process of play as long as the group has the flow down.

    Well, this isn't so much awesome, but were I running this module in our campaign, I'd go ahead and connect it into a web of other otherworldly scenarios with a similar theme. There's a smaller one in Fight On! about a busted Venusian spacewreck, and then there's the Spire of Iron and Crystal, that sort of thing; by having an ample backing of stuff that I'm ready to drop in the players' laps I'll enable a wide strategic scope for them, with room even for mistakes. They could research the matter and end up investigating the wrong spaceship, or they could find hints or outright adventure hooks for further adventures in the same line, perhaps culminating in finding a stargate of some sort, to pick arbitrary examples of the types of interactions that are made possible by expanding the scope of your prep beyond the immediate location.

    \n The best part is to leave it entirely up to the players to choose whether to step into this new world, and to what extent. In our campaign these types of choices have been the major, major pivot points around which the game has revolved: when the players first chose to dare the unknown imperial hinterlands in hopes of adventure, for example, that was entirely on them once I gave them the chance. Similarly they've been messing with the unreliable planar magics of the Alder Gate lately, and it's all about strict player pacing: the players choose whether to do a familiar dungeon any given session or whether to go for new territory. It's the same choice as whether to go down a dungeon level or whether to abandon one dungeon for another, really: strategic choices are the brew of champions in D&D, it seems to me.

    Other modules that might be relevant in this context are Tower of the Stargazer (an unreliable method of interplanetary travel plus an entirely credible sage+library on the topic) and of course, Carcosa and Fomalhaut, both of which are in my own "campaign galaxy" as well-detailed worlds that explicitly exist a starship jaunt away from Earth (or other campaign world taking its place in the galactic scheme). A straightforward move would be to simply decide that the Barrier Peaks ship originates with the same Grey aliens that visit Carcosa so often - they certainly have that same pulp scifi feel, just a few details would need to be changed. I myself try to find these types of connections wherever possible, as D&D modules span such large memetic spaces that you gotta grab the similarities when you spot them. Like, no sense having two fantasy Egypts in your campaign world, all of them's pyramid adventures are gonna happen in the same place. Probably all desert modules in general, if the campaign mostly spends its time in more rainy regions.

    \n The legend as I've heard it is that Barrier Peaks was based on material from a first draft of Metamorphosis Alpha,which is essentially a big sandbox dungeon in space. Some of that material also made its way into the Gamma World DNA. So Barrier Peaks sits astride a really interesting place in the OD&D family tree. I'm not sure if that information provides anything interesting.

    One of the things I enjoyed running Gamma World back in the day was introducing the common day-to-day processes of a technological society to the players, having them start out as mysterious, and eventually become commonplace. For example, there were these blue and yellow cards that corresponded to blue and yellow doors. The PCs eventually figured out how to use them to open the doors. Then later, they turned up a black card (higher security clearance) and started looking for a door to open it with.

    What about the sentient computer, still functioning, but which only has control of part of the facility? The concept is similar enough to some fantasy concepts that you could present it in the style of a mysterious deity, or bound demon inhabiting the place. Not to mention the fragmentary sub-systems, some no longer sane, which have taken over some portions of the complex. Embroil the players in the inter-personal politics of a schizophrenic supercomputer.
