Ticket to Ride: The Countdown Clock

\n
\n
\n
\n\nTonyLB \n\n\n
\n
\n\nJuly 2007 \n edited July 2007 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n Okay: Ticket to Ride: Great board game with lots of little plastic trains that you place in order to complete routes between distant cities. Checkitout.
\n
\nThe more trains you have on hand, the more freedom and (in a way) power you have over the game. You can freely take on route-contracts, because you know that you've got the resources (given enough time) to complete them. As you spend trains, you inevitably weaken and constrain yourself.
\n
\nEXCEPT!
\n
\nShortly after the first person runs out of trains, the game ends. Anyone who was still hoarding their trains, waiting to deploy that power later, is left out in the cold. There is no later.
\n
\nAfter the first game (when people inevitably get caught with their pants down by the end-of-game) I've noticed that this acts as a very effective countdown clock: People keep an eye on the number of trains out, and they adjust their behavior accordingly. If all players have many trains then they make slow, careful moves for long-term benefit. As somebody gets closer and closer to the finish, players ramp up their own speed, making moves that aren't necessarily perfect, but which aim at their objectives and spend their own resources down to keep pace with the forerunner.
\n
\nIt strikes me as a very elegant mathematical solution to the problem of "climaxing together" ... how in a game with separate story-threads, it can be devilishly tricky to get the threads to all come to a boil at the same time. If each player has a thread of their very own, and they know that when the first player runs their resources down (which means ramping their drama UP!) it will mean the imminent end of the game, they'll make sure to keep pace.
\n
\nDoes this sound like it'd work? What kind of advice and/or feedback would people need in order to be able to keep tabs on these resources and to adjust their own actions to suit? What else could it do, beyond just synchronizing climaxes?

    \n Poker chips?

    I've used them in a few games (for example, HeroQuest) and they could work admirably well; their size is an asset as they can stack nicely (for cramped quarters) and be seen from across the table. The stacking feature makes them (imho) subtly more useful than a pool of glass beads or a pool of dice; it's strangely much easier to gauge relative size with a stack of chips than it is with a pool of little objects.

    Plus, if you play boardgames (or card games) poker chips will present themselves as inordinately useful. I don't play poker, but I bought myself an inexpensive set of chips on a scratch-n-dent discount (the case) and I use them heavily in my play of boardgames to replace cash -- it makes boardgames play soooo much faster and easier, it's astounding, especially those games that use paper money.

    \n I've got something like this getting set up in Ensemble. But instead of each player's thread winding down, there's a central thread that all players basically tap quite continuously, and they know that when THAT winds down, the story is over. So it's not a "first player down" thing, but it's still a countdown clock - and one that might (or might not) be as good, since it's a resource everyone at the table can participate in draining (or replenishing).

    Fastlane has a weaker form of this. Whenever a protagonist "burns out" the game is potentially over, but it's up to the group to decide. In every game that I've played that reached that point (including a few con games) we've always decided to end it after the first burnout, but it's not a mandatory thing like in Ticket to Ride. A player's character burning out is not something that happens without people noticing though.

    \n The game I'm designing, Keystone, does this in a way, by having each character have multiple destinies, with each destiny having a track that is furthered by playes bidding in to them. While you have multiple destinies, one of those destinies has a relationship to The Bane (the Keystone destiny) that brings the game to a conclusion. Since only one player can complete his/her Keystone destiny, the other players have to make moves that would either prevent him/her from ending his destiny as soon as he'd liked, or if that's not possible they need to be sure to push their story so it can climax before or simultaneously. In the playtest that we're in the midst of, we have two characters very much pushing to fulfill their Keystone destiny, so the energy level is ratcheted up pretty high and leading to some very exciting gameplay in the story and some very interesting metagame decisions.

    \n I don't know if this is useful, but in the retro space shooter Warning Forever, you get an infinite number of lives but only a limited amount of time. If you defeat the boss quickly, you get bonus seconds. If you die, you lose 30 seconds. There is a natural curve that develops as you defeat the early bosses, building up a safety net until it gradually eroded by the increasingly difficult enemies until eventually the clock runs out.

    It's an addictive, elegant time mechanic and one that I think could be emulated at the table.
