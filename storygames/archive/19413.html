    \n Depends on your jurisdiction. Specifically, how derivative works are handled in the local copyright law. The CC license here grants the right to produce and distribute a derivative work in clause 2/a/1. My belief is that this is equivalent to a grant of copyright under Finnish law, as the right to copy (and sell) is nothing more than a more specific type of right to distribute. Technically speaking the copyright is originally held jointly by the original creator and the creator of the derivative work, but the license explicitly has the original creator relinquish the substantial commercial right to the derivative.

    So the short and likely answer is affirmative: all significant commercial rights, commonly known as "copyright" in the US, are relinquished.

    Note that while the above answers your question, you might not have intended to ask that; if you meant to ask whether you lose subjective moral rights such as the right to be acknowledged as the original creator, then the answer is no - the license specifically withholds those rights in clause b of that same section, and in section 3 it explicitly affirms that attribution is reserved. Of course in the Finnish jurisdiction at least these are inalienable rights anyway

    Note that this assumes that the derivative work we're examining is actually a derivative of your game text in the legal sense of the term. This is not a given, as it is possible to create a game that we would understand as "derivative" without it being so in terms of copyright law. In that case the license itself is irrelevant, unless the adapter decides to follow it anyway.

    \n If you use the cc-by license you linked to for your work, other people will be able to use your work IF they attribute you as the original creator AND state that your work was releases cc-by. If they ALSO modify your work, they can claim copyright over the final result.

    So for example, if I like your game's combat system, I can write a game and if I put at the beginning of the book, or the combat chapter some text to the effect of "the combat chapter was originally written by Troy Costisick and released under a CreativeCommons-Attribution 4.0 license" then I can claim standard copyright over the whole book.

    If someone simply reproduces or republishs your work, then obviously no, they cannot claim copyright, because they didn't add any art to it.
