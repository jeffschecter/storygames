    \n In all seriousness, and as someone who has done somewhat related research back in my university days (though the thesis I was working on had more to do with marginalized social groups that gravitate towards certain hobbies to find camraderie), this is a very interesting topic. Particularly since gaming has been slowly evolving from a fringe hobby to something recognized as an actual cultural (and pop culture) influence.

    As someone of mixed heritage, the thing I have noticed a lot in gaming is how Euro-centric the games still tend to be (or perhaps I should say Western Euro/Northern Ameri-centric). I mean, it makes sense in a way, considering the roots of the hobby, and the majority of people who play such games. However, for many years there have been either sourcebooks for existing games or campaign settings that try to emulate other cultures or use the sensibilities of other cultures as inspiration (Oriental Adventures or Azteca for D&D), L5R, Qin, Feng Shui, Hong Kong Action Theater, and many, many others. Very few, however, really get the feel of the non-Caucasian cultures across very well, and typically they will focus entirely on "popular" stereotypes or features of such cultures (ninjas and samurai, shaolin monks, Triads, Aztec warriors, etc.)
    Again, this makes perfect sense when you consider the audience the games are being marketed to, but one would hope that as the games and gaming itself becomes more sophisticated, that the material and cultures provided would similarly become more sophisticated. This has rarely been the case. I think, in the long run, Qin and (strangely enough) the old Oriental Adventures for D&D have presented probably the best representations of Asia (and, similarly surprising the Palladium Ninjas & Superspies and supplements). There's been a rare few that I have seen presenting Africa or South America as anything other than the savage stereotypes (I think the Vampire Africa book was one of the better ones in recent memory), and precious little for anything else. Scion is another that breaks a lot of the boundaries, too.
    So, I'm not sure this would be an actual question, per se, to pursue, but at least a launching point. Are we unable to bring other cultures and ethnic groups into our games without using stereotypes, or is there a way to bring a more robust representation of other peoples into the game, without having to write treatises on cultural development and interaction?

    ...okay, I think that does it for me... I'll shut up now.

    \n To all the people who say minorities are not prevalent in gaming, I would urge you to reconsider. There are so many of us--not as many as there are straight white males, but that just reflects demography itself. The real question is: why do we not feel safe revealing ourselves?
    \n
    \nThe answer is that people are invariably incredibly bigoted or incredibly condescending. The second is somehow worse.
    \n
    \nSkin color and sexual orientation are only the most obvious targets--social class, poverty, education, dialect, accent are all vulnerable spots. How many times have I code-switched when entering a group of white, college-graduate gamers?--too many times to count. Their demeanor is so rigid and uptight, their self-worth and that of others so firmly invested in vocation and formal education--you have to cultivate a superficial veneer of "whiteness" or they quickly turn uncomfortable, feeling as if they have to either relate to you or reject you (bigotry versus condescension).
    \n
    \nIgnorant mainstream gamers erect huge barriers of fake weird bullshit based on their privileged, sheltered upbringings. Topple that, and minority gamers will be more visible because it won't be so bullshit just to exist.
    \n
    \nSo for the book, you're saying that your friend doesn't know what she's going to write about, but that she is putting herself in the position of finding out for other people? What are her qualifications for being a minority spokesperson? And how could one person speak for so many? I could see her compiling different experiences and enlisting the words and thoughts of a wide variety of minority gamers, that would make sense--but not a one person project.

I do not have a background that would make treatment of minorities a strong identity issue for me - I would wager that I'd have a fair shot in any "more liberal than thou" competition we'd care to have, but that simply doesn't translate into an exaggerated sense of minority rights because I don't have to deal with minority issues in my everyday life. I am well aware that I am in no way qualified to judge the American social niceties around the black minority, for example - a black man is a mere curiousity to me, incidental as much as hair color or a weird accent, simply because I don't have 10,000 black people with their own subculture and history living right on the south side of the tracks.

    The above perspective includes my gaming. I have no inborn experiences or personal interest in the issue of minorities in roleplaying games, at least not in anything more than the most superficial way. This naturally raises the question I broached: why should I care? Is there some hidden injustice going on in gaming that I'm not seeing? Is some minority of gamers being repressed by social mechanisms of gaming and I'm not seeing it? I certainly am interested if this is the case, I'm pretty big on human rights. But from where I'm standing it's up to the writer of this hypothetical book to convince me that I'm upholding some patriarchy or racialist regime in gaming even without realizing it myself. So that's why I answered the question the way I did: the first thing I would want from a book like this would be for it to convince me that there is a problem. Without that we do not have a minority rights topic, but rather just minority self-aggradisement. The latter is of course well and good if you're into that, but what I'd be interested from a book like this would specifically be the issue of right and wrong.

    Specifically, I want to emphasize that my suggestion was not intended in the spirit Dylan took it: I did not want to say that I refute the idea of minority rights or the significance of these people as individuals in gaming. My suggestion for the prospective writer was not a backhanded rhetorical declaration of how very unimportant minorities are to me. To the contrary, the question and challenge was genuine: I do not currently know of any reason for me to be interested in minorities in gaming. Last I heard they were doing just fine, as much as they do in their respective cultures, anyway. Is there some institutional repression or other concerns going on in gaming? If there are, write about those, because that's what I want to know about. The same goes for positive news, I suppose, although that was not emphasized in my initial suggestion: if minorities have the power to improve my gaming somehow, do tell.

    In a nutshell: I come from a radically different and more homogenous culture in this regard, and am not able to talk your minority-tolerant lingo facilely. I will sound like a jerk if I discuss this issue at all simply because I don't understand how serious this is to you all. Therefore, what I'd like from a book like this would be for it to show me that there is an issue to be considered, how I'm part of the problem (if that is what I am), and how I could be a part of the solution. I admit to being slightly sceptical, but that's just because I don't have that affirmative action viewpoint you get by living your life with a real minority problem. I'll believe it when I see it.

      \n I don't think I've ever seen a situation where there was an issue with minorities at the table where the issue was specific to gaming. I have seen it happen that the issue was activated by gaming: the, shall we say, social ineptness of a certain subset of the gaming population, but even then it's been ignorance rather than malice.

      (Here is where I flash my credentials, such as they are: I'm an out gay man. It's a lot easier for me to pass as a non-minority than it is for a woman or a person of color, but to be honest, it's repugnant to do that so I'm only likely to bother if my safety is in question or if a great revelation at the right moment will enhance a smackdown -- there was a magic moment some years ago at a boffer-combat LARP where one of my friends' teenage son was using some fairly homophobic slurs, and my friend said, "You know, one of the players here, one of the people you know, is gay. How do you think he would feel if he heard you saying those things?")

      What really pisses me off is an essentialist, reductionist view of people. "This is what gay gamers want." Well, no, frankly: I don't think what I want, except in a few fairly restricted areas that don't generally come up at the gaming table, can be clearly defined by my sexual orientation. I agree with wundergeek: "We're asking you to get rid of the things that make us feel not welcome." It really doesn't bother me that everyone in your D&D world or your Star Trek game is straight, for instance; a lot of that is fidelity to the source material. It *does* bother me if people in your D&D world or your Star Trek game are universally homophobic, or if homosexuality is used as an evil-marker, or if the only gay people are bad stereotypes.

      And then there's the problem of in-game versus out-of-game: what happens if you're playing in a setting inspired by Dune, where only women are Bene Gesserit? Or the Wheel of Time, where male Aes Sedai are all insane? Or in a historically-inspired game, where dark people are good and white people are Christian infidels? There's a qualitative difference between minority issues and discrimination in the fiction and minority issues and discrimination around the table, but there's no clear line between them and they bleed over into each other.

      All that said, in actual published gaming texts, I think it turns into an even thornier issue of marketing. In a published game, the publisher has to balance the tastes and prejudices of the target audience against his or her vision of the game. Including an explicitly gay character, or a romantic subplot in a campaign-setting metaplot, will cause some people to not buy the product. Including pictures of women in chainmail bikinis will appeal to a certain subset of the market; if the publisher leaves out those pictures, will there be enough extra sales to offset the reduced sales to cheesecake-seeking buyers? This is not likely to be easy to disentangle from issues of discrimination and minority treatment in the larger society.
