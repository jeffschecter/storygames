Last night, I heard a delightfully appalling description of Polaris that made me wonder what sorts of short descriptions of games you use. For example, I have described Dogs in the Vineyard as a game about crypto-Mormon mail carriers-slash-religious police. Or Promethean: the Created as a game about alchemical hobos trying to become real boys and girls. This description of Polaris was as a game about ice-fairies defending their world against space-demons.

\n

So none of these are necessarily accurate, but what do you say about people's games? What have you heard people say about your games?

    \n There are very few descriptions of my games... But this is how I pitch some of the games we offer in the IGL.

    Polaris
    You play knights, on the North Pole, then everyone dies. It's a beautiful game of poetic tragedy.

    Montsegur 1244
    There's an 18+ age limit on this game. It's a Danish historical game about one of the last groups of Cathars, trapped inside castle Montsegur, with Catholic crusaders surrounding them. The game ends with most of them burning at the stake, we'll follow them to see why they burned. Did I tell you we have an 18+ age limit? Right. It's a really creepy game, you will NOT have fun when you play it. But it's a really good game, my own favorite at the time being. If you hesitate even the slightest, pick one of our other games.

    Love in the time of Seid
    Imagine Shakespere and Wagner getting together to write a viking saga. This game feels sort of like a theater play or opera.

    Zombie Cinema
    This is the Finnish zombie movie RPG Zombie Cinema. It's not a general zombie RPG, but a zombie MOVIE RPG.

    Lady Blackbird
    Talespin RPG,but with steampunk and without the talking animals.

    \n I try to find the two big things the game is sort of about and preface them with "at the intersection of".

    Grey Ranks is roleplaying at the intersection of teen romance and genocidal war.

    The Shab Al-Hiri Roach is roleplaying at the intersection of petty academic politics and a telepathic ancient Sumerian God-King.

    Fiasco: Powerful ambition and poor impulse control.

    Dogs in the Vineyard: God-given absolute power and human compassion.

    The Shadow of Yesterday: Frailty and transcendence.

    Primetime Adventures: Hollywood and Vine.

    Then if the eyes light up, you can add a detail about the interesting die mechanic, or the time commitment, or some cool setting detail.

    \nThis weekend I ran Dogs twice for 8 new players (1 read the Dogs book).

    I revised the description to...

    Play teenage virgin gunslinger priests with ultimate power and zero life experience. Your job: judge good people for doing bad things for good reasons (at least they think they're good reasons). And fight demons (at least you think they're demons).

    Many had no idea what a paladin is so that was replaced by priest. And the demon bit, especially the idea that there may be no demons, made people REALLY excited!
