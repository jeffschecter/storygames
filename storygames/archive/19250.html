\n So a while back I was looking for a good system to run a Space Opera game in... Sadly I didn't find anything I liked that much, and my interest in a Sci-Fi game waned.

But recently I've gotten back into a sci-fi mood and have been renewing my search for a system.

I just recently came across Ashen Stars, which is a game using the Gumshoe System, now, I am unfamiliar with the system and would like to know more about it before I buy the game. So sell me on it.

Here's a bit of an idea on what I like in a system now... Rules-lightish, a minimum of GM paperwork, smooth fast combat, a focus on actual characters rather than just a bag of stats, skill-based, simple die mechanic

So is Gumshoe what I'm looking for? If you think so please feel free to say Why, and even give me a rundown of how the system works and anything interesting about it. Also, keep this on topic, if you have problems with the system please tell me, but keep it civil.

    \n I quite like Ashen Stars and Gumshoe in general.

    It is lightweight, runs smooth and the Ashen Stars universe is (to me) quite inspiring.

    Gumshoe IS a specialized tool to tell/play investigative stories. The method it chooses works fine but its the centerpoint of the rules. IMHO the combat rules e.g. are - while being fast - a little bit simple (and I dont need especially complex combat).

    IIRC there is no special focus on actual characters (although there are some pointers to it) and you will have a large number of stats (that can be handled easily).

    My conclusion - it is the system I would choose if the story I wanna tell is of the investigative type. Detectives in space - cool. Play stories like Firefly or Revelation-Space-Series .. better look for something else.

    \n I think Gumshoe meets all of your criteria. My problems with it are:

    1. Diviing up all the Investigative Skills during character generation.
    2. Rolling a 1d6 does not feel substantial to me.
    3. I find systems where you commit resources to improve your chance of success before the roll to be very frustrating. You can't help either over committing or under committing, and both are a waste.

    Next time I run Gumshoe we will spend pool points after the rolls.

    Other systems you might consider:
    Bulldogs! (Fate edition)
    Rocket Age
    Mongoose Traveller

    \n the GUMSHOE system is a lot like communism. Someone had a really powerful insight into why That Which Had Gone Before was totally f-ed up, but then proposed an alternative that just doesn't work in the real world. GUMSHOE makes great points about the structure of investigative adventures, but the actual rules system behind it isn't robust enough to support play. If you're in the market for a setting, buy Ashen Stars. Because despite what I just wrote about how terrible the GUMSHOE system is, I ran a GUMSHOE game for a year and will continue to buy their products--their settings and adventures are that good. But if you're looking for a system to use for your own setting, look somewhere else.

    \n I ran Ashen Stars for the first time last week at Dreamation. I quite enjoyed it! I think what I liked most was that my passing out of information and clues was not tied to specific rolls of the dice. This was particularly apparent in conversations with NPCs, because it meant the dialogue did not get interrupted. Ashen Stars seems to be GM-prep heavy--at least for me it was--the GM has to know what clues are out there in order to pass out the information. But almost none of the prep was wasted, because I built the prep from the list of character skills. For example, prep consisted of deciding what information does the skill 'Forensic Accounting' provide about the victim and the murderer, etc. The adventure became a vehicle for me to pass out the information I had prepared. Yet I've also ran Night's Black Agents--another Gumshoe game--virtually without prep and it worked fine. The basic difference in running the two games was that in Ashen Stars, the players used character skills to find clues. In NBA, the players used those skills to create information. I imagine you could run Ashen Stars the same way. Full disclosure--I'm also biased towards Gumshoe because Pelgrane has published some of Bill, my brother's, TOC and Ashen Stars adventures.

    \n Oddly, I found that GUMSHOE worked much better in the freer "exploring the conspiracy" framework of Night's Black Agents than in Trail of Cthulhu, where the book - while arguably not the mechanics - leans towards heavy prep by the GM.

    My current working theory is that it's because NBA encourages players' suggestions for spends more. And in turn, I now try to make all my clues in ToC 0-spend (that is, if you have the knowhow, you get the clue), unless I think up a particularly fragrant detail that's clearly DVD bonus material.

    I am not overly fond of the General Abilities in GUMSHOE. I don't mind the idea of pools and refreshes, but the way the pools get refreshed are pretty bland - and very subject to GM whim.
    In contrast, the methods for refreshing your ability pools in the related Dying Earth system worked to add flavour.

    \n I like GUMSHOE in general--I'm running my Trail of Cthulhu remix of Masks of Nyarlathotep in it, thanks for asking--and...well.

    I do like the system. I'm not a huge fan of the way the couple of Trail adventures I have disseminate info...sometimes it feels very, well, systematic. Schematic, maybe--you have what skill? Okay, CLUE.

    That said, I've found GUMSHOE sings much better when you let Investigative skill buys work...a bit like Creating and Advantage in Fate--that is, a way to inject content/awesomeness into the fiction. This encourages creativity, I find; there was an awesometastic Biology buy in my game that kept one PC from being eaten by a werewolf.

    Night's Black Agents pretty explicitly adopts this idea (the Tactical Benefits) & from what I can gather, so does the new time travel GUMSHOE build.

    My players mostly don't like the Stability rules (they're used to PCs being cold-blooded, and haven't quite bought my argument yet that PCs are cold-blooded, since they have freakishly high Stability scores), and some don't like the resource management parts of the game, although seriously, they're not particularly more onerous than DnD.

    The other thing people seem to not like is what happens when you run out of a pool in something. I think it helps to play GUMSHOE with a bit of an understanding of the metafiction. Like, if you're out of Pilot during a big chase, what that means isn't that you're not awesome anymore, it means this chase won't be decided by Pilot. Maybe the gunner needs to spend Firearms. Maybe the mechanic needs to soup up the engines. Or maybe the guy with Scuffling needs to...reduce your cargo payload. With extreme prejudice :-)

    As for running it off the cuff, I've been able to even in Trail; to a large degree in my Masks game (though to be fair, I do have a lot of material already at hand) and when I ran the Dreamhounds of Paris playtest. Of course, it helps if you adopt my attitude towards what an Investigative buy can do :-)

    GUMSHOE in action: a combat example, the most awesome Biology spend ever, and my Masks thread in general, wherein I sprinkle commentary about what was going on mechanically.

The system for general skills in GUMSHOE, by contrast, provides no benefit to gameplay. Resource management in D&D feels like you're making important choices within the game.     What it comes down to is this: When you pick up the dice in D&D, you're likely to get a feeling of anticipation. When you pick up the dice in GUMSHOE, it's a feeling of resignation.

    \n In our game of NBA, the PCs are under constant threat, which makes it very exciting. We don't really know what we're up against so we've been careful and have developed some pretty strict tradecraft rules which have stood us in good stead. We run away more than in any game I've played, even though we're pretty badass. In Ashen Stars, we were happier to go full frontal more often. It's a different vibe.

    @John, You don't have to allocate all your investigative points at the start, you can keep some back for a floating pool, and as you earn experience, this is also unallocated.

    I'm pretty sure the Stability below 0 meaning you can't use investigative skills is going to change in the next edition (which has been discussed by I've not heard anything firm on it). We now use it to mean you have to succeed a stability roll to use such skills.

    Generally when we play GUMSHOE, the idea behind an investigative spend is that the player gets some spotlight and something exciting happens. That can mean some useful information but quite often our spends are much broader than that, for example spending Intimidate during a fight to stop the enemy hitting you for a round. Asking for spends is a tricky one, and depends a lot on who is playing. In our group almost everyone spends when they come to what seems like a sensible point to do so, much as you would ask the GM if you could make a roll in BRP. But not everyone does this so I'll sometimes ask if an interpersonal spend is going to be forthcoming. I also use a lot of 0-point spends and this are for very small amounts of information and an indication whether any more might be available or not if a spend were to be made.

    So, for example, if the group goes into a room covered in a mess of papers, obviously some kind of personal memoire, I'll point to the person with Evidence Collection and say "It looks like there's something interesting here but it may take a while to collate it all", they are then likely to say "I'll spend and speed the process up." They might even say "Some of this paper looks a bit newer than the rest, see less dusty. I'll bet that's where will found out what the creep has been up to most recently, 1 point of Evidence Collection?".

    There are many different ways of handling points and it's about finding the best way that works for your players. I think the information on how to play GUMSHOE needs to go beyond the "don't stop the flow" stage to more practical information about handling the game in different groups.

    Regarding general skills, well I don't mind the GUMSHOE set-up. Dice feel like a bit of an interruption for me anyway, but I know that's not a very common feeling. Many players identify the d20 directly with their sword, and fair play to them.

    I'm not a massive fan of large random variation so I'm happy with a d6 and I quite like the difference between the action scenes which might fail and the information ones which don't. It makes for good fiction. That said, in Fantasy GUMSHOE we've experimented with different ways of handling such things, including requiring rolls for some non-core clue spends.

    I'm surprised often about how little players spend on rolls. I usually try to make sure, or do it another way. I blow through a ton of points in most games and take full advantage of the various refresh rules, some of which are more interesting than others. I used a variety of ways of refreshing Stability in my recent magic revival game and that seemed to work well, especially when tied into sources of Stability.
