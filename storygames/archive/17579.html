\n Hello, new to the site, looking at getting back into RPGs. I got into board games in a big way a few years ago, and wrote off RPGs as too much work for too little reward. Recently, I did some cursory research on improv acting, and discovered RPGs weren't working for me because I was playing wrong! Over the years, I had come to realize results were better when I let my players have influence over the game, but I still spent a lot of time negating when their ideas seemed inappropriate, or when I just thought they were lame. So I conducted an experiment. I got ahold of my son, and proposed a storytelling game. The only rules were that you couldn't say no to the other person's input, and whenever you said something, it had to add something to the story. It worked like a charm. So I am looking at integrating improv concepts into my RPG playing.

I'm not interested so much in improv acting as improv storytelling, though. It seems like it would be fun to stay in author stance most of the time, like when I'm telling stories to my kids. The main thing, though, is having a good play experience without having to do prep work. If you have been down a similar path already, your advice and recommendations are welcome!

    \n I've found Keith Johnstone's book "Impro for Storytellers" to be a valuable ressource for roleplaying. I highly recommend it, and it has some valuable points.

    Among my favourites are the commandments "be more boring" and "be more obvious". If impro storytellers (and roleplayers with storyteller authority) get into a pissing contest of saying the most super-special and awesome things, it's easy to get into space monkeys and nuclear-powered bananas. If you lay down that it's cool to work with what's already there, and you don't have to add a super-clever twist on your turn, it makes for richer, less strained telling and play.

    \nYou got Play Unsafe, which is pretty much Keith Johnstone's techniques converted into roleplaying games. I'm reading it now, and it's a must have. It's time to get it now because between Friday (23 Nov) and Tuesday (27 Nov), Lulu is also offering 30% off any order with code REDEMPTIO. If you want the pdf, it's slightly cheaper but I never got the pdf when I ordered it.

    Flow in RPG is about how you create an environment where everybody can feel safe and therefore willing to contribute in impro storytelling. The writer is into impro theatre himself, so you will probably recognize some of the techniques there. It's a good combination with Play Unsafe.

    You should also have a look at Play With Intent which lists a number of techniques that you can use while playing roleplaying games. It's mostly techniques gathered from Nordic LARP (live action role-playing game) and Jeepform.

    And speaking about Jeepform, have a look at their dictionary to find even more techniques. I find they rather hard to understand without placing them in any sort of context, but you should have a look at the scenarios that you can find on the site.

    I've created a few threads about narrating techniques:

    And, but, because explains how you can take what the players just said, add something to it and throw it back to the players. It also slightly touches the topic on how you can make the players spin off from each other's descriptions.

    If you want to create narrative challenges for your players, you should have a look at descriptive challenges, where the players have to include things to succeed with a task. I've been playing a lot with this the last six years and are about to create a game that purely is based on descriptive challenges. They are also called "banana descriptions". (Wee, I've found out that I've been mentioned on this forum! )

    What is your favourite narrating technique at the moment? is pretty much self-explanatory.

    I hope this is what you're looking for.

    \n It sound like you're on an interesting track. When I encountered jeepform, and friends who practiced improv, it made me all kinds of excited about new directions rpg could take. Play with Intent (linked to above) has some of those thoughts. Having the storyline that is normal to rp stabilized improv acting, to my mind. Gives you a known line of narrative to follow, and made it more accessible to folks less experienced (like me!). Improv folks are amazing! But it's nice to have some ground rules and share understanding of story to help guide a group to be able to craft a tale together. The simple approach you took with your son sounds right on.

    You may also be interested in A Penny for my Thoughts, which was written by Paul Tevis, an accomplished improv'er, and uses many of those techniques. A little game I put together that plays with shared story telling in a short form is here at The Color Game. You might also really enjoy Meguey Bakers' 1001 Nights: it uses some simple tricks to help players tell improvised stories in the mode of the Arabian Nights.

    \n Oh, yeah! As far as "rules about who gets to talk when", Penny for my Thoughts has some great ones! big_buddha, you play a character who's in the process of remembering what you've done, but you can only go so far before the rules tell you to ask two other players what you did next. There are no dice, the progress mechanics are really simple (basically, sometimes you earn pennies), and although I personally prefer to act scenes out, there's no reason you couldn't just stay in author stance the whole time.

    Another fun option is Swords Without Master, which gives you different constraints on what you can narrate depending on what kind of scene you're in, plus a die roll for input into the scene's tone. The whole thing plays out by passing the talking stick (the dice, actually) from GM to players, between players, and from players to GM.

    Another technique you might enjoy is something I learned from Archipelago II, which is giving authority over certain parts of the fiction to certain players (so if your son claimed "the oceans", then whenever the story moved to the oceans, you'd have to ask him what's there). This game is free, so you might want to give it a read-through.
