\nPart 1

So over in this thread: Games for Science

I asked about games for the science classroom. Lots of great suggestions and a kind donation.

So tomorrow I am introducing Shocks, Minutiae, and Issues. I already prepped the class a little bit about creating a science fiction story to address the intersection of social law and physical laws.

I am going to back up a little here. The class is a college level natural science class for pre-service elementary or secondary education teachers. The core tenants of the class are:
We teach fewer things with greater focus on concepts and student exploration.
We stress hands-on, inquiry-based activities to fortify in-class discussions and lecture.
We encourage students to do the teaching through in-class presentations.
We use a variety of assessment tools including testing, oral re-tests, student projects, class presentations, etc.
Where possible, we use field trips to strengthen student understanding.

An important point about how I teach is that there are no points just dynamically assessing understanding and mastering of topics/skills (known as Standards Based Grading, see here)

So the topic scale (see the SBG link) is something like this:
Topic: Social Law and Physical Laws with Shock
4.0 Can do all of 3.0 and discuss or demonstrate how and why social laws come into conflict with physical laws. Determine if some social laws operate under pseudo-science
3.0 Can do all of 2.0 and show evidence of social law and physical laws in science fiction and real world examples.
2.0 Can define social laws and physical laws. Can identify the difference between the two laws and know that both evolve over time
1.0 Can do all of 2.0 with some help

So this ranking will change over time since we will be address these things all semester.

    \nPart 2

    The class is 17 women and two men. In this group there is a wide variety of cultural, racial, and socioeconomic backgrounds as is typical of New Mexico (a minority majority state). One question I asked on Tuesday was how are kids like scientist. I followed that up with what happens between then and now. One of the most telling answers I got which the class all agreed with was the racial and gender bias in textbooks, classrooms, and teachers. This holds true in science fiction as well. Shock lets us explore/create science fiction while allowing the authors to envision a future they are in instead of being told they don't belong there or have a place.

    So why Thursday for this?
    There is a lab that's been heavily modified based on Wise and Bluhm, Scientific Observation and the Learning Cycle: Burning the Candle at Both Ends, Journal of College Science Teaching, 2008

    The setup is that we are present with an unknown artifact (it's a candle). Everyone has to make 20 observations about it. The language of the classroom is such that the words candle, wax, and wick are not in our vocabulary. There is a lot more to this I don't want to type it all out.

    \n Some thoughts/observations

    Last week when creating Shocks and such I wrote a bunch of examples on the board. One of the Shocks I wrote up was Genderless Society (from Left Hand of Darkness). The class went dead silent. That was a pretty funny moment. A couple people new the story. Others wrinkled up noses in disgust. I knew right away no one was going to take that Shock on.

    Today in one group a woman admitted to being geeky and playing World of Warcraft. She knew what RPGs were. I mentioned that I played these games. Another woman in the group responded with something like "Please tell me you aren't a LARPer." I laughed and told her no. Apparently the larp group on campus had a booth at the Welcome Back Day for UNM. I guess they were all dressed up to the hilt (it's a WoD larp) and apparently this weirded out a lot of the incoming 1st year students. I think that gets logged into the Barriers of Getting People into Gaming.

    Every time we have worked on the Shock project no one hesitates. Everyone jumps in and plays. I think the structure of the game really helps people get into the story.
    One thing (among many) I find cool about this process is watching folks who are underrepresented in all aspects of life getting a voice and seeing stories that matter to them come out through play. Shock also lets everyone have an envisioned future where they have a place which is lacking in most science fiction.

    The concern with it being stretched out is very real. Shock: is enthusiasm-dependent in a way many games aren't in that everyone's required to care about what's going on.
