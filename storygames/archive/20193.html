\n A relatively new member, @Raisin, posted an interesting question in another thread recently. I'm copying it here, since it deserves its own thread. Thanks, Raisin!\n

I don't know if WoDu is still hype those days, but I for one really love its concepts, even though I only ran it using group text conversations on Skype for some reason. Anyway, I'm going to run World of Shadows or some kind of Mohawk & Mirrorshades old school Cyberpunk game soon, and the Harm mechanic that Paul_T suggested helped me a lot dealing with the damage and hit points part, that I had trouble swallowing last time I ran the game, so thanks for that!

[...]

I'm currently doing an experiment to help me get better at structured improvisation (structured as opposed to on the fly improvisation which fueled most of my GMing life before, with sometimes great, sometimes less great success) : using the WoDu basics, I'm trying to design a setting using only mechanics to do so. Everything I write or say about the setting and game must be either through mechanics (like, one of the stat is called Sugar, it is used when the surnatural or the wireless net is involved, and is also the name of the drug people must take if they wish to keep interacting with spirits or surviving being connected to the net), or stated during my turn as the MC (when narrating, which here consists in being the perceptions of the characters, meaning I don't explain things more than the characters' would need to). Of course, the monsters' moves from Dungeon World is a great help to that effect, but as are old timey Random Tables that are the perfect excuse to populate various landscapes, or determine which kind of landscapes can be found in this world.

I started doing that when I realized how hard it was for me to GM Powered by the Apocalypse games with players that aren't familiar with "story games", since it actually provides great tool for narration, but not anything that could be used to be lazy (like, for exemple, a combat system that stays mostly out of the fiction).

Which brings another question in order to this not be a simple comment-question thing : does WoDu (and maybe PbtA games in general) give you more or less work as a MC/GM than a "trad" game? Did it help you improve in some way your gaming life, and if yes in what way?

    \n These labels are really a question of perspective, aren't they? Compared to jeepform or Kazekami Kyoko Kills Kublai Khan, AW is an extremely traditional game.

    However, to someone who has only ever played D&D4E and those legendary railroady Werewolf scenarios, Apocalypse World presents a definite shift in mindset.

    I think a lot of people have experienced a real change at their table when trying to play this game. (I've also seen people play AW while ignoring some of the principles, and it feels entirely like a "trad" game, as many of its unique features become stripped away and replaced with the local GM's particular procilivities.)

    I do agree that playing more creatively-intense games (which a lot of indie/story games tend to be) can be more tiring. A lot more tends to happen per unit of time (for instance, a single combat can take up an entire D&D or GURPS session, which may not even require any preparation, whereas a typical AW game requires the GM to bring a lot of ideas, situations, fronts, NPCs, and so on to the table), which can be creatively exhausting.

    I remember playing 8-hour sessions of D&D when I was younger (and recently I've sat in on some similarly long-winded "trad" games), but, for instance, when it comes to some of my more recent games, I keep sessions to a fairly strict 1 1/2 - 3 hours, because after that I see signs of burnout in all the players and the quality of the game drops rapidly.

    If your players aren't "playing along" (in AW, this means, among other things, answering provocative questions with strong, leading answers, and making strong moves in the game), GMing can be very tiring, indeed. When the players are on the ball, however, it is easy and free.

    In a "trad" game played by "trad gamers", I find most groups tend to sit in one zone of that spectrum all the time. They are comfortable there and they can play for hours.

    The more creatively open a game is, the more that will vary.

    \n I admit I enjoy the lack of prep, the only thing which stresses me out is having to make myself stop being presumptuous and prepping a story.

    In the past I used to prep maybe four or five hours a week for a game which would last maybe a little over half as long as that, often with massive derailments. I loved running sandbox games as it was a good way of adapting to player wants.

    Before discovering the PbtA games, I generally set up problems and gave them many tools to solve them without actually solving them myself. With Fronts, it's still possible to do that. I still have very loose prep here and there, but most of it is made so that the players fill out the form properly as we go.
    For Monsterhearts I wrote down eight rough session arcs which would introduce concepts one-by-one and then build to a season finale. I don't know what the antagonists (if any) would be or what any of the challenges were, but I had the structure down.
    For Dungeon World I would tend to think of a main setpiece monster like a King Mimic who pretended to be a populated tavern or a gigantic ettin who had been turned into a village for kobolds. Then the rest would fall into place going from wherever they were to wherever they needed to go, according to any missions they were given or looked for.

    Having players pitch things beforehand is quite nice, too. I had my players draw a natural feature, a civilisation and a mystery/danger onto a map so that we had seeds I could throw in. It was great as it drove investment in that world up a great deal.

    \n I'm lazy in one way and much more active in another. I'm always working to try and think of cool things, but I don't stress myself if I can't because I can always disclaim decision-making and crowd-source answers when I don't have anything good at the moment. I do this a lot. In theory you could do this with many trad games (though I think it runs counter to how many trad gamers expect a session to operate), but you can't really do that with the tactics situations that make up large portions of trad game sessions.

    So I'm lazy in that I can skip prep work and I outsource a lot of the details about things, but I'm more active in that I never break off my direct engagement with the players. My real laziness shows up when I forget a game's Principles and just MC as myself, though thankfully this happens less and less now that I've noticed my tendency to do it.

    \n Vincent's GM advice in Apocalypse World (AW) has really changed the way I GM.

    It takes a clear intention to stay away from railroading - I knew this also before but AW helped to focus what I
    - should prepare as GM
    - should do as GM during game-play
    - should avoid as GM.

    What I've maybe learned most from AW is to constantly apply pressure on the players, keep them on their toes:
    - Building fronts,
    - player-vs-player conflicts,
    - killing off people/stuff,
    - giving hard choices,
    -switching spotlight to another player on a cliffhanger...
    I'm using this type of GMing in different RPGs. It takes energy to GM like this. However, it is also very rewarding and gives the players really a thrilling experience.

    Another related thing I've adopted from John Harper's Blades in the Dark is to cut off the prep time and fast forward to the action.

    To return to the original quesion, do I become lazier?
    I think no. It has inspired me to challenge myself as a GM. That style of GMing is more exhausting and needs presence, indeed. At least I cannot sustain it for a whole day. But instead of slowing down the pace with fillers, I tend to keep sessions shorter in general, I think. Now, I tend to prefer a tight 2,5h session with pivotal scenes to an all-day session.

    \n Lazy? In a sense. I no longer spend countless hours prepping and laying plans that, let's face it, never survive contact with the players anyway. on the other hand, PBtA makes it harder and more intense to gm, because i really have to think on my feet, and constantly mentally prep thirty seconds ahead, imagining possible outcome based on player actions.
    I don't always succeed, but I'm no longer afraid to simply ask my players either. If anything, PBtA has taught me to trust that my players are as invested as I am in seeing great scenes unfold. Hell, if I have trouble thinking of a bad enough consequence, I can definitely trust the failing player to come up with something far worse than I would. I'm too nice.
    If anything, really, I find it harder to GM PBtA than trad games, because I have to be creative all the time. I have to react constantly and instantly, and I have to cut scenes on a high note, yet leave them wanting more. I get to use for more of my GM'ing techniques than in any other games. It's exhausting.
    on a sidenote, we get loads more story progression in three hours of PBtA than in ten hours of trad.
