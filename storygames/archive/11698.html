    \n That's my boy!

    I often defend Diplomacy as the best thing since sliced bread, but I have to say that this twisted social pressure thing that surrounds the strict 7-player requirement is just weird in comparison to most other games. Few are the social environments in which Diplomacy is played just like any other game; much more common is for the game to become politized against other programming options as the fans strive to convince others to join in and fill the quota of players. Verily, a pound of gamer flesh is the price you will have to pay if Diplomacy becomes a part of your game convention, say.

    The above is one of the big reasons for why I'm so interested in variants myself. If there existed more high-quality variants for different play situations, getting that seventh player wouldn't be that big a deal.

      \n I can! I already said that you had reasonable assumptions there! They were not the ones I would have made, but that's probably just a factor of different cultures and experience. For instance, I'll share a dictum here, one that has served me well in Diplomacy:

      The weaker your position is, the shorter-term should your reward expectations be, the riskier the gambits and safer the cooperation. You cannot afford to be lied to, but you have to be willing to lie to everybody yourself to get back the lost position.

      That sort of thinking would make me more prone to stab a bigger ally like Austria in your situation, not less. Your risk in trusting him is larger than his risk in trusting you because you're in the game more tenaciously, and you can't correct your position without taking large short-term gains. Conservative play is for when you're in the lead or when you're merely trying to survive as a 2-SC draw-stopper, not when you're still trying to crawl back into the game.

      I'm definitely not thinking that Mike's playing irrationally. I've seen irrational play here in the SG Diplomacy series, but that's not it, even it had dramatic negative consequences. Rational is not always the same as successful, that's all.

      \nI'm loath to comment on my own play, for fear of revealing some cognitive flaw or strategic blindness. But sticking strictly to the tactical side of the fall moves and considering only capabilities and potentials enabled by army and fleet movements and positions, a couple of things pop out.

      First, notice how Italy took it easy on Austria. The only place it attacked Austrian forces was in Tyrolia, which can be understood as a defensive move, maybe to cut support for Bud-Tri. Second, that Adriatic fleet that could have defended Trieste went instead to the Ionian Sea, where it's in a position to strike at Greece or swing into the Eastern Med. What's more, Army Trieste attacked the Turkish Army in Serbia supported by Army Albania. Why? If successful, the Italians charge out of Trieste, leaving it open to that same Bud-Tri. What's worth charging out of Trieste for? The answer: Bulgaria. If nothing else, Tri-Ser cuts Ser S Bul, making Con-Bul, Aeg S Con-Bul more likely to succeed. Coincidence? Or something more? But it's not as simple as Austria choosing sides in the Turkey versus Italy fight, as Mos-Sev reveals. I'm not privy to the Italo-Austrian consultations, but it kinda looks like that.

This suggests that France and Italy may not be merely non-belligerent toward each other but actually actively allied. It's a thin reed to spin out a wild theory upon, but it bears watching.

      As for stalemate lines, Bill has it right that MAO is easily defensible from both sides, which makes it an important bottleneck. It's technically lax to speak of stalemate lines here, but the basic tactical truth is that controlling MAO and getting to project force beyond the Gibraltar, in either direction, is often a game-changer simply because the ability to do so implies that you've bested France and can free up units from your lines for new adventures in the othe sea. Our particular strategic situation is atypical, however, in that the game is relatively resolved when I finally get to the Atlantic, so the normal dynamics do not work here: part of why Italian presence in the Atlantic is normally so devastating is that if it happens before the western triangle has been resolved, Italy is in an excellent position to cleave in between French and English interests. As this happens in the end-game here, however, there is much less overall strategic meaning in the situation. Heck, this whole (completely baseless) speculation about my French alliance proves the case, as normally the whole notion of allying with France after taking Iberia would be laughable.

      But enough of that for now, let's see whether the game ends before counting our chickens. I for my own part believe that we're seeing the end here. The server just doesn't think ahead, not being human, and thus doesn't "know" yet that Austria's dead. It will only make this determination once the build phase resolves, at which point it'll also probably check any victory conditions, such as this draw thing. It's possible that the server's been written to make these checks in the "wrong" order, but we'll just have to wait and see. Even should the game continue, I don't expect France to last too long against the Grand Alliance.

But he leaves Warsaw unguarded, and Austria claims it, while Germany bounces him out of Sweden. I take the Black Sea. I destroy the Russian fleet in Rumania at the cost of losing Bulgaria to Italy assisted by Austria. Meanwhile, the Italian Navy steams into the Eastern Mediterranean. By the end of 1902, I have been pushed out of Europe entirely, occupying only Anatolia. But I have the Black Sea, and Austria\u2019s capture of Moscow that year meant that Russia was now fighting for his life. Additionally, there were strains in the Italo-Austrian relationship, as evidenced by Austria\u2019s opportunistic seizure of Greece.

      With Russia crumbling and Austria growing, Eero started to worry that if kept on dismembering me, he would fall prey to Austrian adventurism. The seizure of Greece had shown that Daumantas was no respecter of persons, and Eero made sure I was aware of his disdain for Daumantas\u2019s communication style. He wanted to cut Austria down a peg or two while keeping me in the larder for later. So he let me have Bulgaria back so that he could retake Greece and push into Austrian-held Serbia. Meanwhile, Austria turned on Germany (busy in a three-way fight with England and France), which had hitherto been a staunch ally insofar as I could tell, and grabbed Munich. By the end of 1903, Austria controlled 7 SCs to my 4 and Italy\u2019s 6.

      My alliance with Italy continued into the spring of 1904, as Austria put paid to Russia and pushed into Berlin. I took Rumania from Austria. In the fall, I took Sevastopol, but Italy grabbed Bulgaria from me once again. Luckily, Eero messed up the convoy order that would have landed an Italian army in Greece capable of supporting the fleet in Bulgaria, and so his stab was less effective than it otherwise would have been.

      The Mid-Game: Breakneck Balkan Brouhaha

      1905 is a pretty desperate year for Turkey. I\u2019m fighting both Austria and Italy, and while I\u2019m holding my own against Austria, Italy is taking names. I seize Bulgaria, but the Italian fleet in Bulgaria slips into Constantinople while the rest of the Italian navy steams eastward. I make peace with Austria when Eero decides he can take us both on at once, and Daumantas takes Greece back from Eero.

      In 1906, I am definitely allied with Austria, and Daumantas and I have a blast coordinating our counter-attack on Eero. I maneuver toward Constantinople, trading Smyrna away to retake my capital city. Eero makes overtures: he'll withdraw from Turkey in exchange for help against Austria. But I also charge into Italian territory, seizing Serbia from Bulgaria with Austrian help. Austria in the meantime has been beaten back by Germany, and is grateful for my help. The next year, I free Smyrna and pass through Italian-held Trieste to seize the Italian city of Venice\u2014not so untouchable after all, Eero! I gloat privately.

      The end of 1907 marks the high-point of Austro-Turkish cooperation, though, as my erstwhile ally turns on me in Spring 1908. I make overtures to Eero towards a rapprochement. Meanwhile, Germany has beaten back Austria\u2019s incursion and has driven France out of Munich while England is on the ropes.

      The Mid-Game: Deceit and Destruction

      In 1909 and 1910 I take a pounding from Daumantas, losing Sevastopol and Rumania. But concentrating on me means that he is vulnerable to other powers. Eero slices off his pounds of flesh, grabbing Trieste again and then Serbia, and Mike (as Germany) sneaks into Moscow even as he continues to fight Brian (as France) in England, the Low Countries, and Scandinavia. It seems that Brian is rapidly getting the upper hand, and thus it is at this point that Eero broaches a deception scheme whereby he and I will pretend to fight in order to get our units in a position to attack France\u2019s exposed Iberian underbelly. I move into the Ionian in Fall 1910 to begin the deception, threatening Naples and Tunis

      In Spring 1911, I sneak into Tunis as part of the deception, while Italy sends fleets to \u201csurround\u201d me. I also get to push back against Daumantas, seizing Sevastopol back from him at last. Eero intervenes in the Franco-German War, helping Mike push back against Brian. In the fall, Mike also helps out against Daumantas, grabbing Warsaw while I help Eero into Rumania. We unveil the deception, and suddenly Brian has two fleets and an army materialize off his southern shores. Brian says, "Damnation." Mike tells him, "Welcome to my world, where pain is God and Eero is the pope."

      The End-Game: The Pope of Pain

      By the end of 1912, Daumantas has a lone army in Warsaw as the sole remnant of his once proud empire. Italy occupies Marseilles, and Turkey has Spain. I\u2019ve grabbed Moscow. The end-game is in sight. Eero has been talking up a three-way draw, with he and I sharing the laurels with either France or Germany. Eero has alos been trying to convince Brian to give up Iberia without a fight and concentrate on defeating Germany; Brian does not take this advice. He makes overtures regarding an anti-Italian alliance, to which both Mike and I respond positively. I tell Brian that if I get Portugal, I\u2019ll use that position to help him keep Italy out of the Atlantic. I am for the most part sincere, although I wonder about the possibility of France and Germany burying the hatchet. Still, I grab Warsaw from Daumantas and do a little reshuffling of forces to point into the Med.

      But Mike was kidding! He stabs France with vicious efficacy, grabbing the Low Countries from him in a single fell swoop when Brian redeploys south against Italy. Bitter, he swears loyalty to Eero and helps him stomp on my fleet in Portugal (over my request to support me into Italy-held Spain) while Eero helps him into Munich. Eero and I charge toward each other in the Med, and Mike and I start talking seriously about containing Eero in a post-France world.

      All our talk of an Italian solo has its effect. In 1914, Brian tries to defend against an Italian offensive rather than commit to being the tip of Eero\u2019s spear, losing Munich as a result and permitting the German amphibious invasion of Yorkshire. Eero and Mike work out a plan of attack that cripples France in the fall, seizing four of his remaining five SCs. Brian, cornered in Edinburgh with German forces all around, agreed to allow us our three-way win.

      In summary: I was mainly a punching bag for Austria and Italy in this game, surviving to the end mainly by luck. If Eero hadn\u2019t blown his convoy order, I would have been toast. That fortunate accident slowed Eero\u2019s attack long enough for me to make common cause with Daumantas, which marked the revival of my fortunes.

      I have come to regard Diplomacy as an instructive perversion: it turns every communication into a strategic instrument, making you ask, \u201cWhat is he really up to?\u201d in response to every message you receive.

Normally the mid-term strategy would be to work with the Austrian to finish Turkey on the premise that /neither/ Italy nor Austria can win the game with the Turk alive - and Austria can't even live to a draw, in fact, barring aberrant diplomacy. In this case I've found the Austrian impossible to work with on many levels despite long-term efforts, so I'm inclined to look into buffing the Turk into a major Power and ensuring his ascendancy into a draw that'll hopefully come to include myself through clever balancing work.

      \n Here, for posterity's sake, is Eero's 'Ride the Tiger' proposal:\n
      Italys suggested course of action in the early end-game

      Ride the Tiger

      I've been talking with the Turk and Germany (both leaked the anti-Italian plan, by the way), and there's a new draw proposition (IGT, leaves out Austria and France) on the table. I'm going to get some sleep next, so I'll lay out my line for you before that.

      I'm going to put in an accept for this draw, hoping that you'll refuse it, as you're not included in it - it's a loss, so the only reason for you to accept is if you want to end the game more than you want to win. In the latter case I have no use for you, so we might as well end the game in the draw. In the former case I want to give a strong signal of amity by accepting to the draw even as I don't actually want it.

      However, if you still have fighting-spirit, I suggest that your only chance of survival is to "ride the tiger": there is a strong consensus after the last turn's tactical upset that the safest way into a draw is for us to put you out of your misery. Germany in fact claims that he's intentionally feeding Turkey SCs to make him able to rein me in should I try for a solo victory; that's how clear the concensus is. It seems to me that you have no lifelines available from these directions, no matter what they claim to appease you. The only motivation anybody would have for supporting you is the one I have: you might be able to boost me to a solo victory.

      "Riding the tiger" is when you ally yourself with the board leader in an effort to forestall your own imminent destruction, putting yourself against the Great Coalition trying to stop the soloist. The idea is that you cooperate with the devil to fight yourself into survival. Ideally you'll also be able to get off the tiger without being eaten - turn on the soloist once you're in a safe enough position to demand a part in the victory, but not too late so as to not actually cause the soloist to win. In this particular case your goal might be to fight yourself into a 4-way draw, which is a honorable result and much better than a loss; it's also notable that you're just as much a loser whether you ride the tiger or not - not doing it means that we'll pull you apart with Germany, while going along risks giving me a solo victory, but also buys you a chance at improving your position against Germany. A loss is a loss whether another player gets a solo or not.

      To put this into concrete terms, I'm suggesting an alliance: I will not advance against your SCs aside from the Mar-Spa-Por line, but will rather move to help you against Germany with all reasonable means while also turning against Turkey in a bid for a solo victory. You will be able to defend youself and get a chance to grow, and should I play my cards worse than you, even a chance to turn on me at a suitable moment, redeeming yourself in the eyes of the world. Even if that never happens, you have the chance to drive the game so close to the brink that the Coalition /has/ to accept you as part of the draw, simply because they cannot hold me without your aid. I will accept a four-way draw if I'm stopped before 18 SCs and it seems that nobody is willing to deal for anything apart from the draw; however, you can't hope for a 4-way draw before I'm so close to the victory that Germany and Turkey will have to rely on your forces in keeping me from winning.

      You'll note that tactically I have no chance against Germany and Turkey at the moment without your active participation; you need to fight Germany and make him pay for any decision to interfere when I attack Turkey. Thus I not only need your willingness to not accept the current draw offer, but also your active cooperation. Diplomatically you might want to explain this all straight to those two, or you might want to give them a cover story - your call, as it seems from what I've seen that you'll do what you want anyway regardless of what I would like.

      Thus, your three choices: accept the draw and lose, deny the draw and get ripped apart by the Italo-German alliance, or deny the draw and ride the tiger.

      (If I'm wrong in my analysis here, I'd be interested in hearing about it. Seems like an interesting situation, all told, and we are potentially really close to the end of this enjoyable game. I've only ridden the tiger once myself, and I've never been the tiger )

      If you want to ride the tiger, retreat to Bohemia and refuse the draw. I also recommend keeping quiet about our cooperation for the first couple of turns to give us a surprise advantage. We can get you into Munich, eliminate the Turkish fleet in Portugal and start getting a few of my fleets on the Atlantic to help you out against Germany before they'll really suspect that we're working together seriously.

      \n Here's my two bits for the game:
      \n
      \nI had basically three things going for me, or against me, as the case may be: first, book-knowledge of what was supposed to be a 'good Austrian play' (hedgehog opening, aggressive expansion and opportunism whenever the chance arose), second, the willingness to calculate every possible tactical move (plain ol' brute force!), and the complete inability to think about the end-game in any other terms than an austrian solo.
      \n
      \nAnd the result of this was that, whenever I had clear lines between allies/enemies/resources to feast upon drawn, the game went rather swimmingly - for the most part, I just went with "go with whatever set of moves that gives you a chance at more builds and hope that the opponent is thinking about something else", and that kept me afloat for most of the game. However, whenever the situation was such that allegiances were more difficult to determine (for instance - the middle game when I had grown unexpectantly after having cannibalized on Russia, and was surrounded from all sides by countries that had no hostile intentions towards me), I immediatelly floundered.
      \n
      \nI think that was mostly due to the fact that I had a 'goal' (king of the world!) but no strategy - in terms of when and how I should adjust these goals, at least - to speak of. And so, all of my long-term efforts were directed at the same scheme of "eliminate Turkey, turn on Italy, grab whatever I can after that and win!". Yes Bill, I was out to get you! However, having eliminated Russia (with tears in my eyes, as you'll recall), I found myself having to turn again and again to Italy as my only possible ally against Turkey. The alternative was to eliminate Italy (not easily possible from where I was standing, for reasons explained further on) first and then build enough fleets to somehow crack the turk's armor... But that would have involved deploying most of my would-be resources on my southeastern front, leaving my posterior open to attack from anyone who thought that an Austria with Russia, Italy and Turkey under its control is maybe a little too big (i.e., everyone who's still on the board).
      \n
      \nAnyway. Here are the moments of the game I found the most important.
      \n
      \n* Germany building all those fleets. I think no choice made by any other country had as much of an effect on game (at least up to its middle) as this. This meant a couple of things for Austria - most importantly, it was now patently impossible to make a case for Germany and Italy dogpiling France, meaning that those italian units, instead of making modest gains against someone else while simultenously baring its side to me, was now bristling with units on its eastern front. "Against Turkey", of course. I tried again and again to have France commit to the Med - at least a token fleet, but that didn't quite work, nor did I expect it to. Secondly, this meant that Germany was now ill-equipped to deal with any major land powers - France, and as it turned out, yours truly as well. I'm fairly sure that Mike's choice here is what resigned him to the fate of "weird scandinavian hump" for the duration of the mid-game.
      \n
      \n* Me NOT building a fleet when I had a chance and instead going for Germany's guts. It seemed like the right idea at the time - I roll over Germany while leaving enough of it alive to act as an effective enough speedbump against France, get some builds, use these builds to crush eastern opposition. Plus, Italy made it ever-so-clear that if I were to build a fleet, any chance to work together would be ruined forever. And, as you'll remember, I needed Italy to crack Turkey's shell, so I went along with it... And ended up losing any goodwill with Mike, gaining two SCs and then having to defend them with four units in total, and leaving myself with a single fleet. As I soon learned (and took advantage of by allying with the turk), there is such a thing as a "Rule of Two" - a single unit in Diplomacy is completelly worthless against two in opposition. Two units, however, can make life difficult for a far larger enemy numbers, as long as the circumstances are right. An extra fleet is extremelly important for Austria for another reason, too - while you can't *take* the Balkans without armies, you can't *keep* them effectivelly (i.e., keeping the SCs held/units used to defend SCs ratio high) without fleets in the adriatic, ionian, or maybe greece.
      \n
      \n* The alliance with Turkey! Oh man oh man, was this ever fun. Not only did I get to try out the craziest tactical choices ever, I also had the opportunity to take things from Turkey and have him make no fuss about it AT ALL! Now, I didn't really have any grand master plan (aside from keeping SC parity with my ally) when I went with "Hey, I have a chance of losing SCs here... Why not give me Sev?" to Bill, but... The fact that the most headway I made throughout the game *fighting* the turk was while being *allied* to the turk still amuses me to end. Sadly, Bill eventually caught on (or caught it early, but thought it was better than having to deal with Italy, or some such). It was the "Er, could I have those back?" that ended our alliance.
      \n
      \n* A memory that isn't pegged to any particular event so much as to the general feeling of the whole game... When Italy asks you, as Austria, to commit to a specific path to "show that you harbor no ill-will", there are foul shennanigans afoot, and you must harbor the illest of wills that are humanly possible. This is double-hard as you usually need Italy for whatever plans you may have, but - seeing how you're pretty much the only reasonable expansion ground for 'er, it is not wise to turn your back. Ever. "Italy - keep a dagger close to the throat for best performance."
      \n
      \n
      \nAnyhoo! If anyone would ask me what the main reason for my downfall was, I'd have to say - the inability to think beyond one narrow pre-planned scheme. Had I even once bothered to think of an end-game where I wasn't sole victor, I'd likely have been able to, well, manipulate people better on the road of becoming sole victor actually take part in a shared win. As it is, I didn't even once use what I understand to be a fairly common phrase of "I spy with my little eye... a three-way draw between you, me, and that other guy". As symptoms go, it's pretty telling.
      \n
      \nAnd that is all.
