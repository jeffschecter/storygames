\n
\n I've been thinking about the game I want to design, deep down in the bottom of my messy little heart. Something that I'd look forward to playing, something that would inspire me and keep engaging me play after play.
\n
\nI want players with power, in-game and out-of-game. I want the theme of the game, from top to bottom, to be about power. Say you've got close-to infinite power: you want to do something, no matter how outrageous, you've got at least a fighting chance to succeed. What do you do with it? Where does responsibility fit into the picture? Is power inevitably corrupting? Or is it, ultimately, a matter of choice?
\n
\nI think in Sorcerer mechanics; it's my default in-my-head system. I don't do dice curves, or cards, and for this game, using a point system doesn't feel right. Just because you can push around the universe, doesn't mean it can't push back. I like the idea of variable error; it adds tension. I love the Currency system. But still, out-of-the-box, Sorcerer won't do it for this game. The idea of demons who have the actual power, and the bargaining for it, and Humanity as it works in Sorcerer won't do what I want to do.
\n
\nSo, let's talk about my idea for a game: EGO. You have the power to warp reality if you really want it bad enough. Tired of that guy who cut you off while he was yakking on his cellphone? Use your power to hurl him off the road. Fuck collateral damage! If he's still alive, let him handle the expenses; it'll be a learning experience about paying attention when you drive!
\nOr better yet, wait until you're both stopped at a red light. You get out, walk to his driver-side door. You smash open his window, grab the cellphone, crush it in one hand, and then you pick up his car and heave it off to the side of the road. And if you're a little leisurely getting back to your car, and the light turns green, who's going to give you any static about it? If they're dumb enough to honk their horn at you, or give you the one-finger salute, they deserve to end up right next to Chattering Asshole Cellphone Driver.
\n
\nOr say your tastes run into Biblical-style vengeance. Tired of that snotty little fucker on the other end of the Internet, the one who keeps on making those ass-chafing comments about Your Favorite Game/Movie/TV Show/etc.? Well, one day when he goes too far, you make your face appear on the screen, call him a waste of fucking skin, and you make his computer attack him, fusing itself into him like in that movie Tetsuo: the Iron Man. Yeah, it kills him, but so what? He looked young enough, and his parents are probably young enough to squeeze out a better model in no time.
\n
\nI'm thinking of going minimal on the Attributes. To be honest, I'm thinking one: Ego. If you want it bad enough, it will be done. But I'm also thinking of something like Spiritual Attributes from TROS. If you really have an interest in something, you can add your Passion score to the Ego score and roll to make it happen. More than one, if they apply. because let's face it: you only get real results if you're committed to getting results.
\n
\nNow, I'm thinking of having two ways to go about something: Regular and EXTREME. Regular is just when you want to open a really stubborn door, drive a car in treacherous conditions, talk out your frustrations with your boss and get positive results, or just pound some asshole who won't stay out of your face. It's ordinary, everyday stuff. You can whiff it, or you can succeed but end up doing yourself more harm than good. EXTREME is when you blatantly throw the laws of physics face-first into the mattress and go to town. Want to punch someone so hard you pop them like a zit? Want to do that to a building? Want lightning to smite your enemies? How about a plague of locusts, or rabid rats? Want to turn water into kickass bourbon? Want to turn that annoying skinhead prick into a black woman? Want to make someone your devoted slave? Well, you roll against a difficulty chart that I haven't yet gotten around to making up, and you roll against it, dice against dice, just like in Sorcerer. No matter how the roll turns out, you succeed in doing what you want to do, but if your roll sucked, you'll take fallout for it (negative Currency). Also, if you roll EXTREME, you have to roll your character's Ego vs. his/her Ego (+Fallout, if any); if you fail, you lose a point of Ego.
\n
\nBut you can regain it back. At the end of a conflict, if you succeed in your goal, you can roll Ego + [Spiritual Attribute] against your current Ego to boost yourself one point. Or you can use that point to boost any [SA], or buy a new one. Keep in mind: if you run out of Ego, you're in NPC or dead territory.
\n
\nAnd you can die in this game, multiple times in fact, but until you are defeated (your Ego is reduced to zero) you can't die permanently. You can reform yourself, but you have to go EXTREME to do it.
\n
\nYou can also regain Ego by canceling an EXTREME effect by another Egoist (PC). That takes effect immediately in-game.
\n
\nNow, that said, I have specific questions:
\n
\nIs Sorcerer a good mechanical basis for this game? Are there any better ones you out there can suggest?
\n
\nHow do I encourage characters to fail in this game, without minimizing the danger of the character's Ego bottoming out?
\nI'd like to tie it into the improvement mechanics, if I could.
\n
\nHow do I set difficulty levels for standard conflicts against a person, an angry mob, riot police, an army division, a solid door, a prison cell, a prison, etc?
\n
\nI want rotating GMs for this game. There is nothing that frustrates me more than one guy getting stuck with running the game, and the other players kicking back and waiting on that one person. I want the GM to have fun, too.
\n
\nHow do I keep the other players interested when their character isn't in the spotlight? Fan-mail mechanic, maybe?
\n
\nShould I set this in the "real world" or in a fantasy setting? Should I have the option for both?

    \n

    \n
    \nThe chips could either be spent during game to activate what the character thinks are good traits in his background for extra dice (Passions are always available for the player) during rolls, or he could wait until the end of the story, and use those bonus chips to see if he upgraded either his Ego, one of his Passions, or add a new Passion.

    \n Adding in a shared narration of failure *is* a mechanical option of course, mechanics are not just restricted to the dice. People have criticised the fact that in writing Solipsist I didn't trust the player to narrate their own failure, but I think it makes perfect sense for your game to not let them. You are talking (as I read it) about parallel consequences, you both succeed at what you want and a terrible consequence happens at the same time (with a variable degree of both), rather like the parallel intents from Shock's conflicts.

    Say you had a dice pool mechanic where you rolled D6s and looked for successes. Instead of making it 5+ you could say 1s and 6s were successes. The total number of successes determined if the power worked, and then the 6s go to the player to narrate the degree of effect, but the 1s go to the player to the right to narrate the degree of backlash.
