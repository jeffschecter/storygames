Story Templates (and Genres?)

Josh Roby
June 2006 edited June 2006 in Play Advice
In that other thread which I'll link to when I get around to it, I said something about how an accessible RPG needed to utilize a well-known and easy-to-replicate story template, like a murder mystery or a quest or a television procedural. Which got me to thinking, and now I want to brainstorm different basic well-known story templates.

The Quest - There's a McGuffin over there. There's obstacles between here and there. Go get it.

Murder Mystery - Somebody's been murdered! All the potential suspects are all conveniently trapped in one place. They're all connected and they all have something to hide. Solve the murder!

Pirates! - There's a treasure. There's a map to the treasure. There's a badguy who has the map. Get the map, follow the map, defuse the inevitable traps. Fight the badguy who inevitably followed you to the treasure. Claim treasure.

Police Procedural - Somebody was killed/raped/kidnapped. Investigate the crime scene. Follow leads to find interesting, colorful characters. Lean on civil liberties to collect evidence (cause you're hardcore). Confront and arrest the guilty.

What else? What does a medical procedural look like? What about a sitcom? What about a comedy of manners? What about... I don't even know what else?

    Graham, Rags to Riches only brings to mind The Secret of my Success for me -- can you give some other examples?

    Bildungsroman (Coming of Age) - There's this kid at the cusp of adulthood. He's jostled about by many different things in his little microcosm (school, bullies, parents, that special girl), and this causes him to doubt who he is and his place in the world. Then he's either assaulted by a traumatic crisis or counseled by a wise older person, and he confronts all the different bits of his world, orders them, and exerts some measure of control over his world.

    Fantastic Bildungsroman - There's this kid at the cusp of adulthood. He's in the midst of a confusing microcosm, but he's whisked away into a completely new world (or allowed to see a completely new world hiding within his regular world), where he must confront an antagonist of some sort. He then returns to his own microcosm and applies the lessons learned in the fantasy world to order and control his own world.

    Surprised there's fewer "team" type outlines here.

    The Heist:

    The main guy, a world-class thief, has a personal stake in his next job, embodied in the form of the Opponent. He gathers a team of quirky but brilliant specialists. There's internal friction when developing the plan. There's a "dry run" or minor side heist that has to take place. The internal friction makes it go nowhere near as smoothly as it should. Finally there's the big heist. The Opponent appears to stymie it...but either the team is able to improvise, or it was part of their plan all along. The team gets the money, internal frictions are ironed out, the Opponent is enraged, and the main guy is not quite satisfied with the money as expression of his personal stake.
