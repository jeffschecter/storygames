\n Brought on by a discussion here with Joe McDonald about creating games for those with vision impairments, how would you create a game specifically for a player/s with disabilities? One thing Joe mentioned was the idea of using cards instead of dice, or colors instead of numbers (such as for those with dyslexia or vision impairments that might make it hard to see pips or numbers).

So brainstorming, how could you design a game, role-playing or otherwise, for those with specific disabilities? Are there games out there already that could fit the bill or be converted? Is this something anyone has dealt with or looking into?

    \n One thing I would imagine would be to identify the medium limitations based upon the exceptionality. A game designed for someone who is blind would be a lot different than a game designed for someone with limited movement, for example.

    Pips on a die might work better than numbers because you could more easily "read" the die by touch. The idea of using cards as a randomizer is interesting, but how would that work for someone with vision problems?

    I also was thinking about role-playing games as audiobooks. Is this something that could be done? Especially if you are looking at a game where there is more storytelling than dice/pen/paper, is it a possibility?

    \n While not so much designed FOR folks with disabilities, GLASS has a specific status for folks unable to participate in live, boffer, full-speed and -strength combat: the Safe Character (SC). From the STD (Gold Candidate):
    Safe Character (SC) \u2013 An injured or disabled player who wears a blaze orange headband and does not wield Weapons. The player may not be the target of a Touch, Aura, Throw, or Projectile Attack; likewise, the player may not use Touch, Aura, Throw, or Projectile Attacks, though he or she may use Palm, beneficial Throw Abilities, or Stare Attacks or Abilities. The player\u2019s character is considered present.

    So they can play all sorts of roles--even combat roles--but are never at risk of physical contact (barring a combat swirling too closely around them--but that's what "HOLD!" is for).

    That said, unrestricted GLASS uses far too many Verbals for it to be easily played by someone with a hearing disability. Although a game type could be setup which only permitted Gesture-based Abilities (all Weapon hits are for 1 per hand employed, i.e. a two-handed attack does 2).
    -----
    In general, I think that being accommodating is preferable to designing with disabilities in mind or at the forefront. Techniques are rarely "hard-coded" into RPG designs, so there's a variety of ways that the disabled could work around issues with reading the text (screen readers, PDFs), manipulating the randomizers (use a web site and activate Read Aloud on results), and handling information (type into laptop and screen readers). One MIGHT get some good traction and adoption, though, by offering a Braille version (or running Braille intersperse between printed lines in all versions) of a game which has little to no handling elements to resolve mechanics--even if the blind are "reading" pipped dice, they still have to find them after a roll, to pick them up!

    Posted By: merb101how would you create a game specifically for a player/s with disabilities?
    \n

    That's a great question. I deal with accessibility issues as part of my day job (i.e. web development), so I usually start by asking which kind of disabilities are we trying to workaround. Besides sight/reading related disabilities, there are many other forms (e.g. learning, cognitive, speech, hearing) of impairments that cause different problems. This just defines the constraints we need to design around. For example if the game is supposed to be accessible to blind people one design choice could be "minimize written text as a game tool", so we could just abolish character sheets, drop out dice in favor of bidding mechanics using physical tokens, emphasize textual descriptions and metaphors, use game boards to convey information (the ideas behind chess boards for blind people are very useful). Even then early playtesting is essential: unless the designer also has the disability he will miss many pitfalls that blind people will find in a few minutes. Another issue is trying to improve the usability for a blind player vs. creating a game that's accessible to a all blind group. IMHO much of the material available to create accessible web design is useful to game design too, but some creativity is required to adapt the ideas.

    \n Michael,

    There are ways to reduce entry barriers. If a player has difficulty processing numbers, a game without numbers or with fewer interactions with numbers has a reduced entry barrier.

    There are ways to provide supports. If a player has difficulty processing numbers, having another player help with calculations would be supportive. A useful tool might be a reference guide.

    And I think this might be "providing supports" as well, but I'm not sure: If a player has difficulty processing numbers, you can reduce the impact this has on play by giving buffer time. Announce a calculation you need made right before taking a break ("Okay, orcs burst forward. Let's take a five minute break, and I'll need your Opportunity Bonuses.")

    A game designer has more capacity to affect entry barriers than a GM or fellow player, I think. A GM or fellow player has more capacity to develop/implement/suggest targeted, specific supports than a game designer does (because a game designer is not at the table/doesn't know the person and difficulty).

    I think the single biggest thing to be conscious of in designing/re-tooling/presenting/GMing games for people with specific disabilities or difficulties is to give them the opportunity to advocate for themselves, and expect them to be leaders in leveling the playing field. If someone struggles with math, they will know specifically what struggles they have, and if they're lucky they'll have found through experience tools and approaches that work for them. Empower them to tell you what is troubling them, and work with them to set up tools and options that enable success. And by "empower them", I mean be open to hearing suggestions, invite criticism, speak to people both in groups and privately, and expect the people you game with to be generally capable people.

    In short: As always, the solution is to be reactive to your players, their needs, and their desires.
