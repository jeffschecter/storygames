\n So, one of the tricky pacing issues I've run into in investigation RPG play is "when to move on".

The group's going for that "Aha!" moment where player cleverness ("I dust the bottom drawer for fingerprints!") and GM hidden knowledge ("You find some -- and they aren't human!") make a glorious match. The "Aha!" only happens if it is somewhat earned, occurring in the fiction as a result of some character action. Sometimes, the players will quickly do something the GM deems sufficient -- either looking where the GM expected, or looking somewhere totally unexpected that the GM then judges would work. (In this latter, the GM kinda gets to share the "Aha!" himself!) Other times, the players will just try stuff that wouldn't work, and frustration will set in. Still other times, the players will find what the GM expected, but will continue searching, not knowing what else they might find, not knowing whether the scene contain one "Aha!", or several, or none. This tends to drag.

The last time I played Trail of Cthulhu, I approached it with that "Aha!"-seeking mindset. I searched carefully and came up with a few neat ideas. I didn't make any rolls or employ any skills, I just navigated through the gameworld as cleverly as I could. No luck. Finally, the GM prompted the group, "Use your Investigative Abilities!" I said, "Sure." She told me what my Investigative Ability uncovered. She told me that I could spend some points for even more info. I was profoundly unsatisfied.

Now, however, I might approach this differently. Knowing that each scene contains The One Clue, and knowing that I can make a simple declaration to receive it, I might view navigating the scene as pure color. It's my chance to portray my character, and to help the GM portray her scene by shining the spotlight of my attention around in it. And then, when I've had enough of that, I can effectively yell, "Cut!" I can look at the group, say, "Ready?", get some nods of assent, and tell the GM, "We're ready to get some new info, parse it, and move onto the next scene." Except, instead of using those words, I say, "I'm using my Investigative Ability."

There's no "Aha!" moment. The resulting experience is more like reading an investigation than doing one. But maybe that's the point -- the investigation is just the plot skeleton upon which the real tale of the characters' descent into madness can be strung.

I'd be interested to see how this maps to others' play experiences, and to the designers' intent. I readily admit that I could be completely off base with respect to Trail of Cthulhu, but even if I am, I think this is an interesting option for RPG investigation.

    \n I don't have as much experience with Gumshoe as Graham, but I'll be running my adventure Castle Bravo at Dreamation as a final playtest prior to sending it off to Pelgrane, so if you're going to be there -- as I know you often are, Dave -- you can see how I run it. I have moved to a model that de-emphasizes using the mechanics as the primary interface between Keeper and player; i.e., the "What skill are you using?" approach. Instead, it's a much more traditional "Tell me what you do now . . . " sort of thing. Players describe their actions, and I decide whether a particular description counts as a specific use of their skill, and whether it counts as spend.

    Note that this means that my reaction to a player who does something cool, innovative, or unexpected -- i.e., something that's not in my notes -- is the same as it is to a player who's doing exactly what I expect: "You do X? Okay, Y." I recognize that this flies in the face of some people's understanding of how Gumshoe should work. For example, never again will I say, "Spend two Reassurance and I'll give you a bonus clue." And I'll be damned if I ever look at a player and go, "This is a core clue." Rather, you tell me what you do and I'll decide what the relevant game-mechanical effect it. As far as I'm concerned, the Investigative abilities are a pacing and spotlight-rotation mechanism.

    In running the game, at any given point in an investigative scene, these are the questions I'm worried about:\n
      \n
    1. Is there more information (a "clue") available in this interaction?
      2. \n
    2. Is there an opportunity for role-playing or otherwise allowing players to be cool with their characters if the scene continues?
      3. \n
    3. Does the currently active player need more spotlight time?
      4. \n
    \nIf the answer to any of these questions is "no," then I need to think about aggressively scene framing the next thing that needs to happen, even if that's just by shifting the focus to a different player and saying, "Okay, what are you doing now?" It's my job running the adventure to keep players from flailing around aimlessly; if there's nothing else for them to get from a scene, I end it. Conversely, if there's plenty more but they're unable or unwilling to go after it, that's why I've got Drives to hit: "Isn't it true that your Thirst for Knowledge would make you get down on your knees and examine the spores more closely?" Notice that if the answer is no, that's cool too: the scene ends, and whatever information could have been gained there is now either lost, which means that the players will probably be making decisions with incomplete information somewhere down the line, or it floats, which means I have to figure out a way to make it possible for them to get in another way. My preference is for the former, since I try to design TOC adventures so that anything you absolutely need to know hits you in the face.

    Either way, I'm stage managing things so that the flow of information and the opportunities for meaningful action and decision-making are apportioned among the players in a satisfying way. The pay-off is a final confrontation for which PCs are either loaded for bear or woefully unprepared, and either of those is fun.

    Let me add, finally, that I recognize that TOC is not generally a System Matters kind of game; my sense from reading what Robin Laws has written is that he doesn't really believe that system matters all that much. Ad-hockery is fine, one-off rules are fine. This can be deeply unsatisfying for those of us who enjoy tightly constructed games. For example, I think that ultimately treating Investigative skills and non-Investigative skills differently creates more problems than it solves, but I'm prepared to finesse that issue; there's a simple fix amounting to treating all skills as both particle and wave already implied in the rules. But TOC (and the players it attracts) does let me indulge my passion for quasi-historical narrativist-flavored sim gaming, so I'm prepared to forgive a lot.

    \n Hey Dave --

    More art than science, I'm afraid. The critical skill is listening to the players: when you say "what are you doing?" you have to be prepared to really hear the answer--and to go get it if necessary. This is a skill, and it needs to be cultivated. Whenever I listen to recordings of games I've run, I often discover moments where a player said something that got lost, and if I'd attended to it in the moment, it would have made the game better--at least for that player. So I'm talking about the kind of active listening that facilitates participation by everyone at the table.

    But adventure design matters too. I learned a lot at GenCon running TOC. The feedback I got from the players was that they hated it when it seemed like I was just "reading the story" -- all of the back story and "clues" and so forth. The part they liked was taking action, even though they hadn't uncovered half the clues necessary for them to fully understand what was going on.

    So I don't write adventures any more that require deduction in order to be at the right place at the right time or anything like that; if you miss a clue, shit is going to come find you. Instead, I try to write the adventure so that clues enact themselves around the investigators, to be appreciated or not by their active engagement in the scene.

    For example, I'm working on an adventure based on the death of occultist rocket scientist Jack Parsons in 1952 California. So the PCs are there at his wake, and all around them the mourners will be acting out their history of antagonisms and attachments that are the clues in the scene. Of course, the PCs appreciation (I mean, of course, perception and understanding) of those clues is contingent upon their engagement with the scene: they get access to the NPCs enactment of the clues via what they do and who they talk to.

To my mind, "I use Flattery" is insufficient; the player should actually role-play the attempt to ingratiate the investigator with the NPC. All Interpersonal skills should work that way. But a player without the skill should not be able to get the information keyed to the skill, otherwise why have the fucking skills? If it's vital information, it's a "core clue" and there should be multiple ways of getting it, or it should be a "floating core clue," delivered to the players with any sufficiently significant effort.     \nThis is a fun question. To extend Graham's answer a little bit, I'd say there are two functional reasons why ToC uses a clue-finding system.

    (a) To differentiate characters one from another. If you only get clue Clue X because you have skill A, and she only gets Clue Y because she has Skill B, then you guys can each say you've contributed meaningfully to solving the mystery.

    (b) As a pacing mechanism. If the situation is such that the revelation of Clue Z shines a clarifying light on Clues X and Y, serving to inspire and guide appropriate remedial action, a formal clue-finding system can be useful in specifying that situation. Arguably, DITV's town creation system is its "formal clue-finding system," in the sense that you lay out NPCs and point them at each other in light of the overall progression of sinfulness in the town.

    The difference in terms of pacing, of course, is that DITV embeds its clues in its NPCs, in the expectation that information will be revealed in the course of the Dogs' interactions with townspeople; TOC embeds its clues in skill-keyed scenes, in the expectation that information will be revealed in the course of the investigators' progress from one "crime scene" to the next. You will have to be the judge as to which system is more elegant and flexible; I will refrain from invidious comparison. But it should be noted that, structurally, they operate from similar methodological premises: figure out what happened before the PCs come on the scene, and then imagine the traces (e.g., clues, what townsfolk want from the Dogs) that this leaves on the situation at present, when the characters arrive.

    Of course, you could say that mechanically, there's a big difference because in DITV you don't have to break your heart fussing around with what skills unlock what clues; you know what the information is and you're dying to give it to the players so that they can confront the moral choice emergent from the situation. Your character sheet and abilities signpost how you'll deal with what you find out, rather than how you find it out. So the larger issue, and the one that speaks more directly to John's question, is whether or not it's worth it to make the big deal about "investigative skills" that ToC makes.

    I think the answer ultimately is yes, given the Lovecraftian aphorism about the mind and its fortunate inability to correlate all its contents. To me, the ideal Trail of Cthulhu scenario is a big complex unexplainable mess with ramifying complications and rhizomatic resonances that pop up throughout the adventure: "Why is this here? What does this mean? What the hell is going on?" The PCs' investigative skills orient them to the kinds of information that will come to their awareness as they engage with the scenario. In contrast to Dogs, in TOC what the characters do once they know everything hardly matters; the question is are they even able to proceed, given what they had to go through to find it out.

      The guy with Fibre Analysis might say, I'll spend some time in the lab analysing it and I might say, "It's just a piece of cloth from a red sweater" (no point cost) or I might say "You find some strange residue which you could put in the mass spectrometer but it would take some time and effort" (indicating it will cost at least a point).

      If the player goes with it (which they mostly do), I might say "The residue turns out to be a complicated organic compound. It's been subjected to some very intense processes because under the microscope it was difficult to discern any recognisable structure but you're pretty sure that it's actually some kind of grey matter, you know, from a brain."

      This information doesn't actually point to anything in particular but it does give a different flavour to what is going on and might be useful at some point, perhaps the PCs have to choose between exploring a warehouse or an abatoire and the supplementary clue indicates that the latter is more likely.

      The other way of spending points is for benefits. These are great when the players get the hang of them and are basically permission to make stuff up, create new connections etc. So you might spend a point of Library Use to:
      - find a book quickly
      - get permission to take a book out of a library
      - find a particular book that gives you a pool of points for something else, e.g. bombs for dummies might give you a one off 2 points in explosives
      - impress the librarian with your knowledge of the Dewey Decimal System so that she's distracted whilst your friends steal a book
      - at a pinch chat up the librarian and get a date with him
      - get the librarian as a connection serving as a pool of library use points, someone who can do research whilst you're chasing off chasing Hastur (is that three times in this thread now? Shouldn't he appear in the internet or something?).

      \n Simon: What my players have been feeling lately is that there's not much, mechanically speaking, either pushing OR pulling them to play a certain way. Roleplaying is a thing they do, and then event resolution is a thing they do. They're not fundamentally interconnected, other than the in-game-fiction dictating suitable skills (i.e. if I'm yelling at the crazy old lady to confess, I'm Interrogating).

      I think it'd be too easy to lay that at the feet of "bad players" or whatever, but I think mechanically driven play introduces another factor: Rules-based economies provide a useful feedback loop for some kinds of players. It's not (necessarily) about acquiring I Win points, it's about receiving validation/reassurance that you're doing what the game wants you to do.

      So, like, in ToC you have (for example) Pillars of Sanity. They're terrific! But right now they're just RPing flags. If you want to play toward them awesome, and if you don't then no big deal. They barely influence RPing decision-making until you've lost them all. So you have to rely on your fellow players to ooh and aah and let you know that playing toward your Pillars is cool. They also have to provide social-level validation to the GM that challenging those Pillars is cool.

      OTOH, you have Drives. Now Drives are very potent, since now you're faced with a really tough decision: Do I ignore my Drive and suffer the Stability loss? Is my Stability kind of shaky right now, and can I get some back by following my Drive?

      Granted many players simply do not care about/for the mechanics influencing their RPing. Some do. I'd even go so far as to say many/most RPers are more viscerally motivated by mechanics than character fidelity. So that's kind of tough to get one's head around in a game like ToC, which is very very hands-off when it comes to Playing Your Role.

      I think the virtue/economy thing has all the problems of in-game/metagame clash when those sets line up. If, on the other hand, your economy simply represents and describes the fiction, rather than imposing external incentives on it, then there's no clash.

      Dogs in the Vineyard has its external incentives, but if the players are willing to put a little effort into moment-to-moment reimagining the fiction in accordance with those incentives, there really isn't a big gap to cover. You "escalate from Talking to Physical" to get more dice, but then it's usually easy to say "arguing further won't work, let's try shoving the dude" without breaking the fiction or warping the roleplay too much.
