    \n I have not yet played the game with three, but I played it earlier this year with five and the game sang the same woeful song of utter desperation.

    Basically you are just adding a few dice to the mix, so you'd think that little else would change. But I saw some subtle differences.

    We were playing the (unpublished) JFK assassination playset, and five players easily allowed us to have several subplots going on at once. I was running it in a four-hour con slot and accommodating an extra player. So I just made sure each scene was a little quicker, and the pacing was fine.

    I suspect that three players will be less of a slow burn at first, since there will be no characters at the table who do not have a direct connection to each other. And perhaps more usage of NPCs and more time to stretch out scenes if you like.

    Either way sounds fun to me!

    \nFear the Deuteronomy Kid!

    (cross-posted with Chris)

    A three player game played straight goes by really fast - 12 scenes. That can be super fun! You cannot screw around. It can also be too tight to really dig into the situation if you are a bunch of lollygaggers..

    A five player game goes slow (by Fiasco standards), with the added wrinkle of two characters who are not directly involved with your dude at the start instead of zero (three player) or one (four player). That can also be fun, or it can drag if you play with knuckleheads.

    You can slow down a three player game or speed up a five player game by adjusting the available dice at the start of play (see page 52).

    So it is really a matter of preference, and the game rocks with 3,4 or 5. I facilitate 20-die five player games all the time and it is fine - you just need to be on top of things and maybe edit more aggressively than normal.

    Also: The Kennedy assassination (written by Chris Bennett) will be playset of the month in October.

    \n My strong preference is for three players. I actually like the fact that subplots are so costly to introduce in a 12-scene game, and that the end is always too close - I love the intensity, and the high narrative velocity, that that entails. I also like sessions under two hours, and I've only been able to do that with three at the table.

    None of the foregoing should be read as disagreeing with the nice things people have said about larger games, because four- or five-player Fiasco is definitely fun. But Jason's advice, that it gets more important that you get rigorous about editing scenes as you add players, accords 100% with my play experience.

    \n I played in two games with three players at GPNW.

    One of the big differences for me is that there's less raw material on the table at the start. This means that every Relationship or Detail has to count, and that the discussion between the setup and the first scene has to really bear fruit. With more people, you can dive into the first scene and just explore: "hm, a Klingon sword, let's push that button and see what happens." And it's not a real problem, because there are plenty of Relationships and Details and so if you don't get to some, it's not a big problem. With fewer things on the table, there's less inspiration for the first few scenes.

    Also, our first game in the session was kind of rocky and didn't really take off, while the second one had caught on fire by the second scene. I don't know exactly why, but I think there are at least two possibly contributing factors. One was the playset: we played "Boomtown" first, and I think that had the problem that we weren't quite sure what genre we were in. The western genre is pretty expansive - it covers "A Fistful of Dollars," "Unforgiven," and "Blazing Saddles" - and one of the things that I think is key in freeform games is to make sure everyone is on the same page as far as genre goes. (The game was never *terrible* - it was just that when the same set of us played the second game, based on the "Main Street" playset, and things took off like a rocket jet, the problems with the first game were obvious in retrospect.) The other was inexperience - we spent a lot of time talking about Rules and What To Do in the first game, while by the second game the mechanics had become pretty transparent.

    So if you have three, I'd say to make sure you've established precisely what genre you're playing in and that you each have a solid scene idea by the time you're done the discussion phase that happens between the setup and the first scene. If you have five, be prepared to be aggressive, or for the game to take longer, and think of interesting subplots that can be resolved in 4-5 scenes.

    \n In contrast, in a five player game it is actually a good idea to leave at least one element to be largely defined in play. You don't need to nail it all down in the setup, because that last element's utility will usually become glaringly obvious once you start to mess with the other four. There's enough stuff on the table it can start to feel like playing before you play. So if you've got "Object: Love letter", it is totally OK to leave that alone and see what turns up in the first half dozen scenes rather than defining who wrote it to whom, provided everything other element is kicking.

    Not so with three or four players generally.

    It is gratifying to see the differences in taste and opinion here. I really tried hard to make Fiasco a game you would integrate into your own culture of play rather than bend to accommodate.

    \n Colin, I think playing Blazing Saddles with Boomtown would work amazingly well. It's just that if one player is playing Blazing Saddles, one player is playing Unforgiven, one player is playing A Fistful of Dollars... it doesn't work so well. I might pitch it at a Story Games Boston game night, if I can clear up my Wednesday schedule....

    Figuring things out ahead of time can be a bug or a feature -- it's too context-dependent. In five-player games, I've had a lot of success just figuring out what the relationships are and leaving *all* the Details to be worked out in play. In a three-player game, that's a recipe for a really slow start and possibly for an unsuccessful game -- if you don't figure out what the incriminating photos are, and the Need and Location don't pick up the slack, then you're fumbling.

    Another point from actual play: in the game I played at JiffyCon, one of the details was "Klan outfit/hate paraphernalia," and that wound up never entering play -- we decided that the two people with the "Crime: Thieves" relationship were burglars, and when one was at a Klan meeting, he would notify the other as to who else was there so that she could burgle their houses, and she would return the favor when she was at an AA meeting. When you have enough details, you can let one of them just serve as background -- that detail really illuminated two characters -- without risking running out of details in play.

    \n@Jason Morningstar: The Klan paraphernalia informed their relationship, but it didn't make anything happen because there were no conflicts over it. By contrast, the other object in the game -- a secret recipe, which one character wanted so that she could sell it to KFC for $500K, and which another character wanted so that she could serve real fried chicken -- drove a lot of the actions.

    The risk of having a Detail turn into background like that is that you have only so many Details, and some of them have to spur actual conflicts.

    And then there's the unknowable question - if the recipe hadn't generated such delicious conflict in that game, would the Klan paraphernalia have come into play? Probably. Part of the reason it never came up was because we had more than enough to work with without it.
