    But that means there's a 30.76% chance (4 in 13) of "rolling" a six, not a 16.67% chance (1 in 6). It's your fun to have, if you like that, but it's a pretty big shift in probabilities.
    ----
    On topic:
    I think the dice pool system came about as a way to deal with scaling abilities without too much "auto-kill" or "auto-miss," which is a common problem in system like (say) Hero where one can have a 60 Strength guy punch someone with 10 Body--fist-shaped hole in chest. But a gray die is only 16% more likely to succeed and a white only 33%, and the act of summing the successes further mitigates that. it makes a nice two-dimensional way to scale--shade or exponent--which a non-pool system couldn't provide as easily.

    That's my guess.

    \n Wait, I have a revolutionary idea.

    So we have three shades, right - one that succeeds in 83.3% of cases, one in 66.7% of cases, and one in 50% of cases. And we have a potentially exploding die at a chance of 16.7% so we need to be able to tell these apart from a regular success.

    So, how about we take a regular cube with six equal sides - we can make it from any material, really, as long as it's structurally and statistically sound - then carve symbols onto it that map onto the five different cases above (obviously one case, guaranteed success but not exploding die, will have to map onto two sides of the cube).

    But then how to create suitable symbology, to make people from many different cultures and background able to determine success from failure? Ah, your resident genius has thought of this as well. I propose that we utilize symbology originating from the world's most universal language, mathematics. Most everyone in the civilized world will recognize the numerals 1..6, which will represent boundary cases one would have to reach to achieve the effects outlined above, depending on one's shade. However, to make the game accessible to a less mathematically advanced audience, I suggest instead that we represent the numerals as easily countable dots. In fact - and hold on to your hats, boys and girls - I suggest that we organize the dots in arrangements which, after having played for some time, will be instantly recognizable as de facto symbols on their own for easy recognition.

    I guarantee that this method is statistically in line with the game's expectation and provides a suitably close to optimal play experience to be considered "perfect".

    Anyone who'd like to use this idea can feel free. The genius is feeling generous today.

    \n Hi folks,
    \n
    \nSeems like you've all answered the question yourselves, but I'll throw my hat into the ring:
    \n
    \nPhysical: I like that the amount of dice in your hand is a physical representation of your character's skill.
    \n
    \nTactile: I like the feel of five six-sided dice in my hand.
    \n
    \nTactile 2: I like that an advantage or help is a physical item that is traded from one player to another player.
    \n
    \nIntuitive Difficulty: If you know that half of the dice you roll are going to come up positive, you have a fair guess at how well you'll do on a roll.
    \n
    \nManipulation of Results: I like how after the dice are on the table they MEAN something. That meaning is visible to all of the players. And that meaning can be manipulated by rerolling one, some or all of them. There's more depth to that option than simply rerolling one die.
    \n
    \nThere's probably more reasons, but that's all I have for now.

    \n 1.) Because Burning Wheel is an elaborate Shadowrun hack.

    2.) Using pools of dice gives a tangible quality to the tests, which reflects Burning Wheel's focus on rolling only for what matters. Is, or is not, something worth a die in advantage or disadvantage? It's coarser than, say, D&D's various pluses and minuses, but for good reason.

    3.) As much as I love a GURPS-like probability curve, the ability to see gradations in my success, accumulate successes over multiple rolls, and spend dice on effect is too useful. It rocks, in fact.

    \n Here's why I like the Burning Wheel dice pool system: Because even when you are going to resolve things with a single roll, the act of assembling your dice can become a detailed task that generates interaction, consequences and fiction around the table.

    After you've established the skill you are going to use, you plunk down the number of dice for its exponent.

    Then you look for possible FoRKs. There's some negotiation there, as each skill brought in potentially colors the possible outcomes. Maybe the pool grows a little.

    Help is asked for or offered, accepted for refused. More dice trickle in.

    The GM might have an advantage die to offer.

    The player then considers whether to spend Persona on the roll, to add a few more dice.

    Only now are you ready to roll.

    This physicality of assembling the pool can be used to great effect. As an example, in a Burning Sands: Jihad game, the players needed to make a very difficult roll. It was obvious they were going to need to do alot of prep work to make it possible. so I plunked an initial group of dice down in the center of the table "That's your starting skill". Then for the next hour or so we did lots of roleplaying involving linked tests. Dice got added or removed from the pool. Each time, I pointed at the pool and asked "are you ready to roll those yet?" It made for a very satisfying build up.

    \n I like BW/MG's dice pools because of the memorable failures they've produced in play.

    Twice, as a player, I've accumulated 10+ dice pools by pouring every resource I could spend and wheedling every FORK / wise / gear / circumstance bonus I could get from Wil.

    On both occasions I was directly addressing the character's Beliefs or Goals in a huge way. Both rolls would likely have completely transformed the character's role in the fiction.

    Both times, I was set up to either blow away the Ob or to be in good position for a Fate-powered reroll. And both times, I beat better than one-in-500 odds to roll just 1 success with no reroll opportunities, and fail miserably.

    I'm not generally mystical or woo-woo or anything, but it really felt as though the game was saying, "move on, look elsewhere." I've never had that experience with any other game. Only a die pool lets you generate those infinitesimal odds and then achieve them.

    \n In a sense, sure, open ended rolls are dice pools, however, my favorite open ended die scheme is a little hard for me to qualify as a dice pool.

    The scheme uses the normal distribution to produce positive or negative modifiers that are added to a character's ability and compared to a target number. For the particular scheme used, each standard deviation away from 0 is 20/3 (i.e, 1 standard deviation above 0 is +6, 2 standard deviations is +13, 3 standard deviations is +20). The way the dice are used is essentially to randomly generate the digits of a number between 0 and 1. Normally, 2d10 are rolled just like you would roll percentile dice (except 00 is 00 not 100). So the pair of dice produce numbers from .00 to .99. The roll is then compared to a chart that produces the modifier. At the tails of the chart, two digits are not enough resolution, so additional d10s are rolled to add more digits, for example, if a 00 was rolled, two more dice would be rolled, resulting in the decimal number now being between .0000 and .0099. The chart we use is rendered to have two digits past any string of leading 0s or leading 9s, I have used a chart that goes out to +- 40 (6 standard deviations).

    Here's a thread on the Forge with more details

    Back to Burning Wheel though, part of the key is just how BW uses the dice pools, and open ended rolls.
