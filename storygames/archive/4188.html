\n So, my employer finally had to cease operations today, which pretty much means no more new games for a while. . . that said, I got a few hundred extra dollars of severance pay and my bills are currently paid up. So. I want to grab one last game for some short, guilty pleasure, game play to fill the hours that I'm not actively canvassing for employment (I have my second interview with Time Warner on Monday, so the good news is that it looks as though this may not be a long process). That in mind. . .

Can you recommend me a short, self-contained, game that is structured to support one-shots or campaign play?

It doesn't need to be a Story Game, though given the 'light' nature of such games, I suspect that one would fit the bill better than a traditional RPG. I'm not looking for anything that requires a great deal of time investiture to fully appreciate (i.e., I'd love to grab Polaris, though everything that I've read indicates that it's all about extended play, not one-shorts or short, self-contained, story arcs). Nor am I looking for anything that requires much work to set up in preparation for actual play. The key selling points here are "short" and "simple". I guess it wouldn't hurt if it was in stock at the FLGS, either. The following is a list of indie game currently on the shelf at the FLGS (I've exempted a few that I already own):\n
  • Cold City
    • \n
  • Capes

    • \n

    • \n

    • \n
  • Mortal Coil
    • \n
  • Shock
    • \n
  • Sorcerer
    • \n
  • Spirit of the Century
    • \n
  • Truth & Justice
\nFinally, any kind of a gimmick mechanic is pretty much out of the question (I'm not looking to invest in $50 worth of 12-sided dice or anything). So, which of the above games might be a good way to fill a week or two of free time? Feel free to suggest something that isn't at the FLGS, if so inclined

    \n Yo, bias alert and all, but Dirty Secrets could fit the bill for you, if you're interested in hard-boiled detective stories. There's a prep system that you do at the table for the first session which takes about 15 minutes, and that's all you need. There are three different story lengths (short story, novella, novel) which range from 1-2 sessions out to 4-6 sessions. The main system is Liar's Dice, which might qualify as a "gimmick mechanic", I suppose. But, all you will need a couple of opaque cups and a bunch of d6s, and I'm guessing that you probably have those lying around, so hopefully this won't be a problem.

    You can buy from IPR (the print version is currently out of stock, but that should be remedied in a couple of weeks) or from Lulu.com. I have both print and PDF versions.

    I don't want to just sit around shilling my game, but if you're interested in the subject material, I really do think that the structure of this game fits what you're looking for.

    Posted By: jdrakehWhich reminds me -- the FLGS also has a copy of Legends of Alyria. How well does that game fit the bill?
    \nRelatively well, though not as well as Dirty Secrets. The "low-prep" ideal is one that I've tried to build into my games, because I don't really like to do prep. ;-) I'd say that Legends of Alyria is similar to a short run of PTA. By this I mean, there's the need for a "pitch" session to organize the initial situation, which then supports a few sessions of play. In my experience, this has been about three sessions.

    The shortest that I've managed to run Legends of Alyria is about three hours from prep to completion. You can read about it here. That required at least one experienced player, though, to be able to facilitate and to help blitz through opening prep.

    Although I've not done this, you could run a campaign of Legends of Alyria by stringing together storymaps, similar to a series of towns in Dogs in the Vineyard. If you do, I'd love to hear about it.

    Seth Ben-Ezra
    Great Wolf

    \nIt may be kind of hard to find. It stars Ice-T, Christopher Lambert, and a host of other decent b-movie actors (Thom Matthews!). Spoilers:
    \n
    \nIce-T is a big-time mob boss who happens to be dying, so he calls all of his various associates and underlings for one last meeting -- in a new federal prison the day before it is scheduled to open. When they arrive, he locks them all in and tells them that he knows they've all been stealing from him or otherwise screwing him over. He declares that all of them will have to fight to the last man standing -- and the last man standing gets to walk out of the prison alive, with suitcases full of cash and a clean slate. Immediately after this announcement, from the catwalk above the commons (where the subordinates are assembled), two of Ice-T's henchmen empty the following Rubbermaid tubs of their contents:
    \n
    \nTwo tubs of melee weapons (knives, shivs, brass knuckles, bats, etc).
    \nTwo tubs of various firearms (revolvers, semi-auto pistols, etc).
    \nTwo tubs of ammunition.
    \n
    \nIn that order. From that point on, for the poor underlings, it's all about killing everybody else and striking alliances to survive as long as they can. There's a shocking amount of character development and some very cool, low budget, fight scenes. For a cheap, guilty pleasure, movie it's very, very, good.
