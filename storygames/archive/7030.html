GM as prime listener

\n
\n
\n
\n\nTonyLB \n\n\n
\n
\n\nJuly 2008 \n edited July 2008 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n So I was thinking about the GM's role, particularly the traditional viking-hat GM, and what gives it its tremendous staying power. At first blush, you'd think that the gatekeeping features of it would be stuff that folks would be happy to be rid of: They want to do X, they get to do X, they don't need anyone to tell them that they are allowed to do X. Sounds all western and empowered and like that, right?
\n
\nExcept for one thing: "I want to do X, I do X" is a series of actions that take place without involving another person. "I want to do X, the GM listens to what I want and judges it and then tells me I can do X, I do X" is forging a link of connection. The GM has to listen ... they have to pay attention.
\n
\nWhich means that if you, the player, are worried that none of your fellow non-GM-players are paying any attention to the cool things you're doing, at least you know that the GM is listening. He has to be. It's his job.
\n
\nIndeed, the more "power" the GM has to permit or deny, the more attention he has to be paying. In Feng Shui I can sort of lackadaisically say "Oh, rolling on a desert cart while firing two handguns ... that's your standard +2 for a stunt." In (say) Amber, I have to be judging it more critically: Is this enough to overcome the bare one rank of Warfare difference between the targets? I mean, it was pretty cool, but sorta standard ... and yet, it fits into the whole John Woo setting that we're working with here ... I should get doves involved in this! Yeah, I'm gonna make this a standoff and start hinting at the presence of doves, and then if he does something cool with that then that'll be worth ... etc., etc."
\n
\nWith great power comes great responsibility, and in the GMs case can it really be described as anything but the responsibility to listen? The GM is the default audience that makes games work even when people don't know how to be a good audience for each other.
\n
\nAm I crazy?

    \n I totally agree with this topic's statements, but i'd also like to add that GM listening is beneficial for the player AND the GM. It's his JOB to listen, sure, but they can also turn that listening into great opportunities.
    \n
    \nI run most of my games with a loose outline of what I want to happen for my plot/story. For the rest, i kinda point the players in a direction and "listen". Most players think out loud about what they think might happen or ways that certain strategies may play out, giving a GM ammo. These statements would be surrounded by "wouldn't it be cool if XXX happened" or "Oh, i hope it's not XXX behind this door". The GM could make either the good or bad occur and get the same result: the players have a ton of fun and feel the satisfaction of guessing the outcome, even though the GM was just playing off of their own words. The players don't need to know that. ;-)
    \n
    \nIn short: Listening is THE best tool for a GM has for running a game.

    \n The validation you get from someone else really engaging with your input to the game is probably the reason we do this crazy thing in the first place. Really. IMO the social validation element is what sets tabletop RPGs apart from any other game activity I can think of.

    When I mention the "table daddy" thing, that's what I was getting at: It's a formalized presence at the table who's job is to provide social validation. I think it's not uncommon to see a play structure comprised of spokes (players) aimed at a hub (the GM), with no meaningful connections between each spoke. GM talks, Player 1 responds. GM talks, Player 2 responds. And so on.

    So my thesis goes, the reason the "designated validator" role in traditional RPG play is so durable is because that validation is coming from a parental/authority figure, rather than from a peer. I think a lot of gamers want/need that, rather than a (perhaps more mature) peer-to-peer relationship.

    Posted By: Levi KornelsenI think that you are very correct. Not only that, but I think this is one of those things that (to me) is obviously true as soon as it's said.
    \nThis was my reaction as well. It also does a lot to explain certain group dynamics. In many games, something isn't true unless it comes out of the GM's mouth (or, in GM-less play, until they've been vetted by one or more Alpha players). Even if the players describe something happening, the GM has to re-describe it, in their own words, before it "happens." The GM-as-listener then becomes GM-as-translator, where player contributions are only validated and accepted after the GM has listened and regurgitated them. It also points to the critical importance of the "Yes, and/but..." especially the "Yes," part which is a validation of "I listened to what you just said." If there's no "Yes," sometimes that gets translated as "I'm ignoring your input / concerns" = "you are not important to me."

    The ramifications for GM-less play are also interesting. It definitely seems like an important part of GM-less play is making sure the listening role still happens, either that everyone is listening to everyone else (unlikely, in my experience), or that certain people are listening to certain other people and pushing other people's ideas in addition to their own, using their authority to negotiate a situation where everyone has input. Often times this works because certain people may be better at listening to specific players than others. For example, I may find all of Player X's contributions to be grating, but Player Y might be able to tease something out of them that the whole group will accept / appreciate, helping us validate Player X's input.

    Posted By: Levi KornelsenLet us say that I'm designing a game. I want the players to pay extra-close attention to other players when those others are engaged in activity X.

    Is my proper course, then, to give the "audience" the ability to issue a reward or penalty on activity X, to interact in some way through the system, that kind of thing?
    I think that, more generally, making listening be a key element to something that the players are allowed to do with the rules is a good way to encourage them to listen.

    Possibilities:

    (1) When a player judges that another player has done something cool, they can give them fan-mail
    (2) When they judge that another player has picked up on an element that THEY left out, the player may award themselves an "Inspired-You" point
    (3) When player A judges that player B has picked up on an element that Player C left out, that player may flip the snurgle to the second upright position in the third and a half chamber.

    In general, any rule that says "When Player A judges that Player B has ..." is going to be a rule that Player A must pay attention to Player B in order to take advantage of.

    EDIT: That said, there's a difference between "When Player A judges that some player has done X, they can do Y" and "When Player A judges that Player B has done X then they can do Y in regards to Player B." The former recognizes the existence of the contribution, the latter recognizes authorship.

    Posted By: komradebobPosted By: TonyLBPosted By: Paul BI think Tony's post is describing very much a "best practices" approach to GMing, i.e. "good GMs need to be great listeners."
    Well, yes ... but also a Game Design point: "If you intend to give players the ability to validate their own input, you'll need someothermechanism to draw people into listening attentively to each other."

    I don't know quite how to force that, but non-rpgs tend to set up the possibility by taking turns.

    Here. This is probably the most valid design point this discussion has produced.
    Taking turns is a great way of handling shared responsibility.
    The catch is that those turns need a lot clearer beginning and ending conditions than the average RPG provides.
    Nebulous beginnings and endings don't always work. You'd need a much more structured game.

    I also agree with Paul B's cynical analyis of the situation and I think you need to take that one further, and back around the tree, to make this clear.

    1. The first post mentioned "with great power comes great responsibility" - which doesn't mean that the responsibility is handled all that well in all that many cases.
    In fact, I think it's fair to say that people who handled the GM responsibilities poorly may have had something to do with influencing people to come to a site like this.
    Being from a country where we vote to split up responsibility, I'm all for less power at a point and more for a mass approach.

    2. I suspect that a lot more of the people who would be satisfied with being merely players can get their fix a lot more easily with computer RPGs, which have an inherently fair (or, at least, consistently and predictably unfair) approach. These games certainly do the same thing as a bad GM: sidelining a player's agenda to fit the pre-written outline of their stories, artificially limiting the growth of the character, ignoring you except when you're allowed to answer questions, etc.
    That means that there may well be more GMs left out there willing to run games than there are players to participate in them.

    3. The maturity level of a group tends to settle at its lowest common denominator. If you've got very immature players, you will have very immature games.
    If everyone can contribute equally to those game, however, some of the more immature moments will be counterpointed by the more mature players.

    So, if these observations are valid, then, perhaps, the listening skills of the group are, indeed, important to develop. The question then becomes: how do we encourage players to listen to eachother more than the GM?

    \n Arpie:

    I've been running an experiment in my Reign game for a couple months now, allowing players to give each other an XP for "making your evening cooler for any reason." And that's the only guideline I've given. It's a rough Fan Mail ripoff of course (insofar as I've explained that "cooler" can extend beyond in-game events), but nobody in my Reign game knows PTA. It's been a very, very interesting exercise.

    First session, nobody gave anyone else an XP -- despite the fact I had left the requirement so wide open, and despite the fact it could be so easily gamed. I mentioned this over at Cultures of Play and someone suggested there was a stagefright/unfamiliarity element to it. That's probably true. I also feel there are some authority issues going on, even though the Table Daddy has handed down the mandate.

    Second session, I had a guy give someone an XP, and that person immediately gave the other one an XP back. So they're learning, slowly. And the quid-pro-quo element was fairly predictable. To their credit, though, they each came up with a "that was really cool!" bit of verbal validation.

    We're several sessions in and it's still very slow -- like, one or two people will participate and everyone else will just shrug. Despite the easy XP, the spoke-and-hub arrangement is so deeply ingrained that they continue to seem truly oblivious to each other's contribution to the game. I'm going to debrief after our next session and see if there's some element of resentment among the folks who aren't getting XPs, particularly if they're also not giving XPs.

    For those who have participated, this little bit of mechanical inducement is very much working to open their eyes and ears to the fact that there are other people at the table.
