\n Here's an interesting point of data for the Free debate.

Neil Gaiman's latest book, The Graveyard Book, has been on the New York Times Children's Bestseller List for 53 straight weeks. For a large portion of that time, you could watch video of him reading the entire book online.

As I said: an interesting point of data. Now, the book wasn't available as a PDF, but as a streaming video, lacking the portability and grandma-appeal of a print book, so there's definitely some features that the for-sale book has that the videos lack. But still: at least in terms of raw content, releasing it all online didn't seem to hurt the book sales at all, and (it could be argued) releasing the videos for free online may have driven book sales further than they would have been without.

    \n Well, OK.

    In the case of The Graveyard Book, the free thing was significantly different from the thing you pay money for. Although we might argue the "content" was free, it's strange to separate the content from the format. Being able to listen to streaming audio isn't the same as owning a book. I can't listen to streaming audio on the London Underground.

    With PDFs, the thing being offered is pretty close to the thing being sold. If you've got a free PDF, you might print it out rather than buying a book. You can't print out streaming audio.
