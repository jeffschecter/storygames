So, over on The Forge moves into its Winter phase\u2026, there's been some talk of scholarly talk about tabletop games.

\n

I'm very curious about this; I'm a linguist, and I'm beginning to get interested in discourse and conversation analysis, and I'm discovering that, at the table, we do some very strange things with, say, tense and aspect. However, if I'm to investigate this more deeply, I want a broad range of literature related to the topic to read.

\n

So, post authors, journals, articles. I want to know what people have written that you-all like, and not just linguistics, of course, but sociology and such, as well.

    \n In the Nordic countries they talk about it as "ludology," the study of games, but I think that's patting ourselves on the back a bit too early, since this is hardly a real discipline yet or even more than a research tradition. Most of the academic work that's been done -- at least as far as I've seen -- has been preliminary or just not very rigorous. There is certainly a lot of though-provoking discussion and writing out there, but my sense is that a significant portion of it is trying to reinvent the wheel, rather that drawing on rigorous analytical methods already developed in traditional disciplines. Plus, most of the folks working on games come from very, very different backgrounds. For example, I'm a Sinologist (which means I have no disciplinary background, hardly) with interests in intercultural communication, social management, and some passing reading in ritual theory. I doubt I've read any of the same books as Bill and Matthijs. It's going to take a long time and many more publication options for us to share our findings and really build methods of talking about these things.

    If we're listing the mediocre stuff that exists right now, there's also the First Person, Second Person, and Third Person books published by MIT Press, which have some discussion of roleplaying (they include complete versions of Puppetland and Baron Munchausen, for example, plus articles by Paul Czege and Rebecca Sean Borgstrom), but I was still pretty disappointed with them.

    \n Well, yes and no. Experiments with game theoretic aspects of psychology have proven that payoffs are far more complicated than simple incentive structuring will allow you to test. That doesn't mean the mathematics is wrong, it just means that our ability to apply it is crude.

    For example, the famous Nash equilibrium (famous to mathematics undergrads, that is) only happens if people have full knowledge of what the equilibrium strategy of each player is, and full rational understanding of what the payoffs are for everyone. With such exacting mathematical requirements it isn't surprising that experimentation shows less rigorous results. Even one person misunderstanding their situation or an experiment that identifies less-than-accurate payoffs throws the whole thing off, sometimes right over a cliff, as the theory actually predicts.

    However, in logic and computer science, where inputs and payoffs can be concocted in extremely tightly controlled situations, iterated game theory allows for highly effective algorithm design for extremely complicated setups.

    Anyway, Nash isn't the only game theorist out there, von Neumann was actually the first to formalize what had been kicking around mathematics for 200 years.

    \n I asked something similar on Twitter. My anthropologist friend recommended me:

    Laughter: An Essay on the Meaning of the Comic, by Henri Bergson
    Man, Play and Games, by Roger Caillois (Paperback)
    Homo Ludens: 4181, by Johan Huizinga

    James Wallis agreed, saying he'd been recommending those books for years. When I asked him for other recommendations, he suggested:

    A Pattern Language: Towns, Buildings, Construction (Center for Environmental Structure Series), by Christopher Alexander (Hardcover)
    Frame Analysis: An Essay on the Organization of Experience, by Erving Goffman (Paperback)

    Jessica Hammer, who does psychological research involving games, recommended:

    Moves in Mind: The Psychology of Board Games, by Gobet
    A Theory of Fun for Game Design, by Raph Koster

    And specifically for roleplaying games:

    All the Dungeons a Stage: The New Performing Art of Fantasy Role-playing Games, by Daniel Mackay
    Shared Fantasy: Role Playing Games as Social Worlds, by Gary Fine

    \n Since all the classics have already been posted, let me add:
    \n

    \nThe Ambiguity of Play -- Brian Sutton-Smith (I owe a lot to this book, if only because it made me realize I'm not crazy) [Ludology/sociology of play]
