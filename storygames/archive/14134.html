http://jeepen.org/dict/

Let's stick with just the truths, not the cake.

Honestly, I pretty much agree with everything here, but if we all agreed with each other all the time what would we post about?

So I'll nitpick -

#2 - can't go wrong with letting people succeed.

Usually - but sometimes a player will realize the opposition is fake and it ruins something for them.

#9 - There is no rule that says everyone should have equal direct impact on the story or equal screen-time.

But maybe there should be. I know I get bored if I spend too much time in audience mode...it's one of the reasons I prefer GM-less games, because I get more screen-time.

#15 - Don't be afraid of patch-work stories.

I'm not sure if patch-work stories means the same thing here, but I know that if, at the end of the night, I go home saying "What was up with that tangential bit? And that other thing?" I'm less satisfied.

Anybody else have problems with them? Or want to disagree with my problems?

    \n Oh, I can totally argue against the Jeepform Truths.

    Restrictions foster creativity.

    Up to a point. A few restrictions foster creativity. But pile them on and they kill it. You don't make Apocalypse World more creative by insisting everyone speak in verse.

    You should always have a message or premise.

    This is the one that really bugs me. No, you shouldn't. Games with messages, like stories, plays and films with messages, are tedious and preachy.

    Play with themes. Use resonant images and events. But please, please don't shoehorn a message into your game.

    \nYes! This gets at a divide between the philosphies and function of this kind of play (that includes most trad and indie play) vs the kind that includes Jeep (where you often know the story ahead of time, or all is available to everyone as it happens in play). In the first, knowing where you are going when the whole point is to have tension inserted into the experience of play by having forking paths that surprise, upset and delight the players would kill that beautiful tension.

    However, if what you're shooting for is intense play, that everyone is on board for, and the surprise and delight come from the details of what happens. Or even that they way you're adding fiction is by having everyone generally know the overall thrust of the story, or certain benchmarks that will guide play--since you are not having an external input (eg dice) create the tension of play, but the sheer surprise of what a moment in as story will look and feel like, or what decisions the human players make about motivation. Then knowing what will happen can function beautifully.

    The strengths of the "into the unknown" play style is that, conceivably, you can have a much broader variety of stories since you are not married to a single tale. As it plays out though, I challenge you to show me how broad our exploration of tales has been in rpg as a whole. I think we're getting more diversity now.

    A weakness of "known story play" is that not everyone wants to know what will happen. If you can't buy in to that tool, play will break down for you. I expect that there may be prejudice against it that would not be borne out by play, though. Try it, you might like it.

    The most important purpose of a story is to oppose player interaction.
    Be afraid of patch-work stories.

    \n 13.Keeping player number low and keeping players close together, such as in the same room, enables you to do cooler things with the form, helps keeping a unified vision and facilitates telegraphing.

    This, however, is the biggest problem I have with jeepform larp. But I understand that jeepform purposely has different goals than the type of larp I usually participate in, and I accept that.

    In my mind, this makes jeepform "tabletop rp without dice, or a table".

    As I wrote in the 2010 WyrdCon Summit Book (http://www.mortalisrpg.com/forum/downloads/2010SummitBook.pdf) I think that the one thing that larp does better than any other medium of expression is simultaneous action - creating the sense that right now, in this moment, multiple significant things are happening relating to this game and you, as your character, are only participating in some of them, though you know that others are exist.

    You see a group in your peripheral vision having a heated conversation that you think may relate to something you care about, for example, but you are busy completing this trade negotiation with another player and you can't delay it without risking losing the advantage you have at the moment. Plus, you know that in the next room, something else important is happening, cause you saw it on your way over here. But you really need to finish this negotiation right now, even though it means you aren't participating in those other conversations. That sort of thing.

    To me, simultaneous action is the "great divide" between tabletop play and larp play. But small games with a single location and focused action set aside that possiblity.

    Which is fine. They gain things in the bargain. The game becomes more of a shared experience. There is a decreased experience of the fractured multiplicity of experience that you get in a big sprawling game where one player's experience of the event is vastly different from another's. And there are benefits to that.

    But it bugs me a bit. It feels kind of like sitting down in front of a TV or movie screen and seeing a slideshow. The photography may be good, but it's not taking advantage of the medium. Even Ken Burns documentaries, which tend to have a lot of still photographs in them, mix things up with interviews and reinactments and music, to take advantage of the medium he has to work with.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarI don't think that Vi \xe5ker jeep ever called its form Larp.
    \nThe form grew out of Nordic freeform larp. The first big discussions and analyses of it happened through the Knutepunkt conferences.

    It's usually structured as a "living room larp", but purposely de-emphasizes things like props, costuming, and set dressing, preferring instead to focus entirely on characterization and story development.

    It's sort of a hybrid. It uses the general format of larp, meaning that the players speak in character 90+% of the time and mechanics are designed to appear as in-fiction as possible, like the bird-in-ear technique which allows the GM to guide the action by using a simulation of a spoken internal monologue. And they make some use of physical space - players sitting next to each other are characters sitting next to each other. But it also shares a lot with tabletop rp - it's done with a small group and only one thing is happening at a time, allowing all the players to experience all of the action.

    In a lot of ways, jeepform is an attempt to take the best elements of larp and the best elements of tabletop and combine them together in a narative-first hybrid.
