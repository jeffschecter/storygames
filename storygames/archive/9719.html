    \nThe Key of Bring British
    Earn 1 XP whenever you do something quintessentially British. This includes stopping for tea, issuing a tot of rum, maintaining a stiff upper lip, engaging in condescension and/or patriotism, or giving your enemy a fighting chance. Earn two if this behavior results in extreme danger.

    La Cl\xe9 de L'\xeatre Fran\xe7ais (?)
    Earn 1 XP whenever you do something quintessentially French. This includes keeping your magnificent white uniform clean, being disorganized, stopping for wine, appreciating beauty, or running from a fight. Earn two if this behavior results in extreme danger.

    The Key of the Colonial
    Earn 1 XP whenever you do something quintessentially colonial. This includes putting one over on a European, being devilishly practical or pragmatic, enduring great discomfort, or fighting dirty. Earn two if this behavior results in extreme danger.

    \nThe Key of the Stiff Upper Lip

    Earn 1 XP whenever you carry on, dismissing personal injury (bullet wounds, animal bites) as unimportant. Earn two if the wound is slowly killing you: for example, you have lost an arm and are bleeding. Buyoff: Cry like a baby.

    If period detail does matter, remember that this is before Victorian times, when many of these stereotypes were established. That's where the idea of the prim and proper Englishman came from (as I remember). Before that, we were jolly rustic yeomen.

    \n Prior to the 1770s, France was pretty rural, backwoodsy, peasanty, and COMPLICATED. Unlike England, France was a country that was made up of a core that had expanded in fits and starts through conquest and inheritance over a few centuries, so each region had little bits of their own customs and rules (to the point where you could make a killing smuggling salt across the river to a village in a different region, but which you could see from your own). The regional governments were ill-prepared to deal with this, and so fought each other and fought against reform from the centre CONSTANTLY. Oh, and they had no roads to speak of.

    The people? Imagine your archetypal rural peasant in the pre-industrial era, and that's basically it.

    France was about to experience its biggest population explosion of all time, though. Its geography basically set it up to peak early, as far as Early Modern Europe goes. France's population and that of its overseas colonies skyrocketed during the 18th Century, and then kinda crapped out. By the time just before the Revolution, France had hit its pre-industrial population cap, and was then pounded by waves of famine if the weather wasn't just perfect.

    Also, the upper classes were obsessed with what the French called gloire (there's an accent on one of the vowels, but I forget which): basically the last shivering tenets of chivalry and all that came along with them; the only true achievements were those made by combat! Which is why the French found themselves in war after war on the continent and abroad.

    So, short version:
    Upper class: needs to fight to prove itself
    Lower class: too many people on the farm, so I might as well see the world and get fed by the army

    I heartily recommend The Pursuit of Glory by Tim Blanning, which covers the period from Westphalia to Waterloo (1648-1815).
