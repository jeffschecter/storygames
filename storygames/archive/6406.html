\n OK, so I had this idea ... let's say your guy fights mutants but in the process risks getting infected with their mutant blood and becoming more like them, sprouting horrible extra limbs and auxiliary brains. Each time you come in contact with the mutants, you have to choose an exposure level. Low means infection is likely but you have some metagame control over it, high means it is unlikely but you have no control. There are six, six-item mutation lists, so you would roll 2d6 to find your particular new problem. One thing to note is that getting the same thing twice "activates" it, so maybe you get extra arms that just hang there, but if you get extra arms again, they work! So any time you get to choose, that's potentially good. Although you want to keep your mutations subtle so that people don't start hunting you like you are hunting other mutants. Anyway:

Bathed in gore
Infection on any roll but a six.
Upon infection, roll for region and choose any of the six mutations.

Damn, that was messy
Infection on a roll of one through four.
Upon infection, roll twice for region and mutation and choose the result you prefer.

A splash here and there
Infection on a roll of one through three.
Upon infection, roll for region and mutation.

Crap, I got a little on my kilt
Infection on a roll of one or two.
Upon infection, roll twice for region and mutation and let your friends choose the least agreeable result.

I don't think I got any on me
Infection on a roll of one.
Upon infection, roll for region and mutation two times and take both mutations.

My question, Story Games - do you see an optimal path here? Is there a compelling reason to choose the edge cases of high or low probability? Assume you don't really want mutations, but if you have to get them, you want them to be awesome.

    \n Here's some flavor:

    It's a miserable fantasy backwater! We farm, we starve all winter, we die young. There's not much of a feudal thing going on because, well, there are insects. They are big - man-sized, bigger. They are reasonably intelligent, wildly varied in form and utility, and completely unknowable. They keep us on the move.

    The insects move from place to place and, for the most part, we get out of the way because they are fucking dangerous. Sometimes, though, they settle somewhere worth fighting for, and then lunatics with swords get hired to clean the bugs out. When there is no paying work, the lunatics with swords either become vicious warlords or wander into bug-infested areas looking for stuff left behind by the previous human inhabitants. Those poor fools often left in a hurry, with what they could carry and no more. Once the bugs get a notion to settle down and build their dirt cathedrals, there's a mad rush to get out of the way, because they bring disease.

    Their transparent insides-goo is highly infectious and causes your body to grow insect-like parts. Seriously! It's amazing. New brains, even, ones that start fighting with your own for control of your changing body. Eventually they win, and that's why people avoid cutting up the bugs. Unless you're, you know, a lunatic with a sword.

    Acquired mutations are usually dead and weird looking. Sometimes mutant traits get reinforced and become functional and weird looking. There are guys, half gone, with huge rock-crushing insect jaws, swiveling leg joints, and freaky glands that shoot acid. They are hated and feared and usually hunted down with more enthusiasm than the insects they are becoming. But it's best not to mess with a guy who can see in the dark through his segmented fucking eyes and has feathery antennae that can smell colors or something. That's a guy to leave alone. He's got enough problems without you sneaking up on him with a rock.

    \nMaybe a Fool gets to have extra wealth (from selfishly keeping it for himself)
    \nMaybe a Gujin becomes feared so that others back down more often.
    \n
    \nSo each Path can become its own "class" (if you will) that has its own "class feat" that you get better at as you "level up" in that Path. The Villain gets more henchman with each advance on the Villain Path, the Fool gets more Wealth with each advance on the Fool Path, the Gujin gets more intimidating with each advance on the Gujin path, etc.
    \n
    \nThe Path's that are already beneficial of themselves can have "feats" that include obligations as well as benefits. The more villainous Paths are all about the selfish...the personal power that being villainous can get you. The Path's that are more heroic come with catches. Some kind of community entanglement that welds the character more tightly to society.
