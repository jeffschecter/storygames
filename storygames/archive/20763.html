\n The biggest issue I encounter when trying to share this hobby is answering the question of why someone would willingly play 'monstrous' characters for fun (which is why I usually start with safer games). Take the World of Darkness: Vampires are rapists. Werewolves accept bestiality. Changlings have oppression superpowers. Prometheans learn to be human by harming others. Demons utterly destroy someone's soul just to maintain a cover. Wraith literally has you playing someone's Shadow.

Beast, well Beast.

Thing is I don't know what the answer is. I am not these monsters, yet on some level I identify with them. But what does that mean? What does that say about me? What do people get out of engaging their dark side this way?

    \n I think you're taking very specific thematic readings of some of these games, which, while they're not necessarily wrong, really oversimplify the way in which the reader is going to approach them. Sure, they take the idea of playing monsters as their foundation, but several of these games (can't say all, since I haven't really read all of them), thematically, don't really require that you play amoral, horrible people. You frame Changeling as "oppression superpowers," but I feel like its default setting actually allows for some of the most traditionally heroic fiction in the NWOD. The primary motivation of the players in Changeling, at least in my looking through the core rules, seems to assume an interest in protecting the community against extra-dimensional big bads and corruption from within, since the only hope for Changelings is to stand together, or to fall into slavery. Similarly, I've been thinking of running a Promethean game in which queer kinship replaces the quest for humanity (which I always hated about the setting). Even in the default setting, the Created don't hurt people because they WANT to -- for the most part, it's the GM that's going to do most of the hurting. Playing monsters isn't necessarily ALWAYS about playing bad people, it's about creating narrative tension. Some people are going to use it as permission to play jerks (or murderhobos), sure, but I think this gets back to where there's a far wider range of interpretive possibilities for these games than you necessarily assume.

    Seriously though, in what context are we interpreting these things when we ditch the author? Personal experience? Cultural zeitgeist?
    \nYes and yes? Aren't those the contexts by which you'd interpret an author so you - in your model - could interpret their art? I mean, there's a whole bunch of contexts in which to view art: the demands of the author, or their backstory, isn't the principal one.

    Case in point: you pick up a book. It is marketed as a tragedy. There is a missive from the author at the start informing you that this book is meant to make you sad and should be interpreted as such.

    Now, you read the book and it hasn't make you sad. In fact, it's all pretty laughable! So, either you have defied the author's instruction, failed to appreciate their ability, or think the book has failed as a work of art. Whichever way, you are clearly refusing the author's interpretation of their own work.

    Does that make it any less art? Is it actually a comedic classic to be remembered through the ages? Is the author's intent relevant when you are considering your personal opinion, or is it just hearsay?

    You could side-step these questions and claim that the market - the sheer profitability of the thing - defines whether the work has merit, but you would be failing to understand anything about art, beauty or human volition. I could only wish you luck in your cold, robotic life.
    \n

    And if an author can't determine what their stories mean, then how can stories be used to communicate anything at all?
    \nBecause an audience can determine what the stories they hear mean. An author may be improvising a meaningless stream of thought, and yet our beautiful human minds will seek out meaning for ourselves. It's how come we all have different opinions. I can try and persuade you of a thing, but being maximally persuasive isn't the same as telling a perfect story.
