    Posted By: Emily CareIs the player rebellion supposed to happen because they get sick of doing scenes again and again? Or because they want to take things in a different direction? Seems like they have a lot of latitude as the scenes play out.
    \nThe ending conditions were probably not the most thought-out part of the design -- really the only conscious goal was to further emphasize the agency and separate-person-hood of both Shakespeare and the individual actors. It's just one more decision that is intended to be made in the sketchy layer between the people at the table and the 'players' in the game/play.

    (After writing that I realize that this may not be what people are referring to when they say 'player rebellion' -- there's no mechanical support for any sort of specific 'rebellion' thing in the game other than the ability of the actors to arbitrarily walk out on the play; but of course throughout the game the actors are likely to be in various states of tension vis-a-vis Shakespeare, his daughter, etc. But they have to express that within the leeway/latitude of the game, which as-mentioned is considerable.)

    \n I played in both sessions of My Daughter on Sunday. First session, I was an actor/friend. Second session, I played Shakespeare, in order to see both sides of the play possibilities before I continued my eternal gushing about the game.

    All around, this game was beyond-all-expectations fantastic. When it was pitched, I didn't know what it was about, mechanics-wise, but was blown away by the limits and certainties, and how much they inspired my play, rather than constricted it.
    I want to dive into full AP, but I don't have much time at the moment, so:

    Both games were not only challenging (in the best way- I was excited and enthralled each time a new installment of a scene morphed and grew) to play, but evoked highly emotional and moving responses in me. During the second session of the day (yes, 6+ hours of playing this game),as Shakespeare, I was swept away by the emotional impact of watching my friend's reenact my memories, and all the sides of my daughter they revealed- sides I had largely ignored in her, or had never known existed.

    I ended up physically removed from the actors (across the room from the table, with a ream of papers in my hand, sitting tense and intense on the edge of my chair) and knew the moment the play was ended- after two intimate and touching soliloquies and after the 6th installment of our Final Conflict was reenacted- Shakespeare saw, for the first time, that what he had thought were "hidden motivations" behind his daughter's departure were, instead, glaringly obvious to everyone but himself- and he was forced to confront the painful reality of his role in running her out of his life. I found myself, as a player, incredibly close to tears while simultaneously relieved and excited to have discovered this clear moment of emotional closure.

    This is, hands down, the best game I've played in a long, long while (and that was true after two back-to-back sessions) both in how the innovative mechanics worked, and in its staggering emotional power.
    I want to shout praise about it from every internet rooftop, and I can't wait to play it again.

    This is my favorite part, that you are playing an actor who knows Shakespeare. Once I started putting the actors in scenes, it got awesome. You'll hear this suggestion on the recording, but I wanted to know more information about my actors in the beginning, because I see WS as trying to get a particular state, and if I know my actors a little better, then I can try adding them to scenes like ingredients.     Posted By: Ice Cream Emperor
    To some degree this is intentional -- Shakespeare doesn't really know how his friends feel about the whole thing until he sees them playing out his scenes -- but can you think of any particular things you would have liked to know, other than their relationship with the Daughter?
    \nI definitely want to know their names, how old they are, and how I know them; that\u2019s basic information that they currently aren\u2019t required to provide. The answers to the other questions are a little trickier.

    The way I see it, there are two fun parts to the questions: 1) Agendas that are 2) secret.

    The questions promote the actors to have agendas of their own, which is fantastic. It is also fun for them to have secrets, simply because having secrets is fun. Here\u2019s the problem; if the agendas are too secret, then we may never get foreshadowing or clues. That makes the big reveal at the end fall flat. If someone who was never seen or alluded to was the killer at the end of the mystery novel, the reader feels cheated.

    (Side note, we read the answers to our questions off at the end. Does it say to do so? I forget. I thought it was cool, but it might conflict with the current end conditions, which I wouldn't want changed).

    I think you could preserve some secrecy, while giving the players the chance to hint at their agenda from the start, and then you get that pleasant callback/reincorporation thing and foreshadow the reveal.

    The most basic way to do this is to require the players to announce three of the six answers they have. They get to choose, so they can keep the secrets they want, but since many of the questions are linked, we can get a better sense of what might be hiding behind the curtain. Most of our players revealed none of the answers to their questions, which I don\u2019t think is as strong a move as they were thinking. There are actually fewer opportunities to reveal these hints subtly than you might think, and many can be overlooked during the excitement. There are probably more elegant solutions for hints than the one I just suggested, but I do think that the dial needs to be turned in the \u201cmore information\u201d direction somehow.

      \n For this post mega-post, I have created two cheat sheets. Here\u2019s the first:

      Players = real people
      Actors = Shakespeare\u2019s friends.
      Roles = People that Shakespeare said are in a scene. Actors pick Roles to play during a scene.



      So we played again last night and I put forth a few options about table talk:

      1.\tPlayers can talk outside of scenes, but you must always remain in the character of your actor.
      2.\tPlayers can talk out of character and joke around.
      3.\tPlayers must sit silently between scenes. The only speaking that occurs is through the voice of your actor while choosing roles.

      Both of these are different from how we\u2019ve played in the past. In previous games, we all just shut up until Shakespeare gave us a scene, which provides the intensity that Tori alludes to.
      Last night, we chose option one. It quickly became clear that we would need to speak out of character for things like rules clarifications and breaks, so we made an ad hoc rule that you would hold up a pen if you needed to speak OOC. In general, we stayed quiet. We would interject a few comments using our characters\u2019 voices, and a couple of interesting things were said, but we focused on using the scenes to say what needed to be said to Shakespeare and each other. After scenes we would do the "attaboys" in character, like you talk about in your post above, Daniel.

      Posted By: MathalusIs Johnstone's method cheating that fun?
      \nYes.

      Techniques:

      1. Play an actor who is a caricature. Daniel playing an actor isn't that different from Daniel playing an actor playing a character. But when I drop Fat Tony to actually start playing a character in a scene, the difference is marked, which simulates Fat Tony's acting skills to a certain degree.

      2. Play the actor when you do the line readings. I would make comments about the "script" as the actor, like Daniel notes, but without trying to engage Shakespeare in conversation or anything. These asides are similar to when we as players laugh at stuff and point out things we like, and can replace some of those.

      3. Lose more of the actor as you get deeper into the scenes. When you get to the most-developed version of scenes, your actors have probably made all the comments they are going to make, and should be getting down to the business of actually playing the characters. So, essentially, the less of the character in the play you are allowed to play, the more of the actor it seems appropriate to play.
