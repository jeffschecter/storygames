      \n Coming from me, the pedant, this may seem odd, but... I never had much problem imagining what the "Indie RPG Awards" meant in terms of eligibility and creators. No more than the Independent Game Developer Awards at E3 every year. I always saw it as mostly a culture--with a variety of business models--that is at or near the fringe of what is commonly called "mainstream." The point being that such products can enjoy an award opportunity and recognition without having to compete with companies with multimillion dollar budgets, large teams of specialists, significant marketing skewing results (e.g. review copies to every mag and web site and blog, versus can barely afford to get it out the door).

      Maybe that distinction is more aggravated in video games than in RPG texts, given the current opportunities for small (or individual) design companies--we can make books that compare favorably to WotC and WW products, whereas there's about a 0% chance a group of five video game designers can create a full product that compares to the Call of Duty 4 team (obviously, the small, casual, and mobile game markets narrow this gap, making them more like us).

      Posted By: jenskotI was just about to post that. I love Mouse Guard. But it's not an indie rpg from what I understand.
      \nHold on. Let me take this first of all from my side. Mouse Guard was registered and voted on without comment from anyone about its ineligibility. Both Archaia Press and Luke Crane published RPGs in previous years that were no one doubted were indie (i.e. Artesia and Burning Wheel). Mouse Guard was published by the two in collaboration, and I knew from the title page that Luke retained copyright to his writing. Based on this, it did not occur to me to question its indie status.

      Now, that said, it may be that I have already expanded past the definitions of what Andy originally envisioned when the awards were created. I have already let in games that were collaboration between more than one party, such as last year's "Forward, to Adventure...".

      Still, I don't think I'm basing this on an arbitrary question of style. Standard freelancing is that you surrender your copyright to the hiring company. There is an awful lot of this in publishing in general and among RPGs in particular. I think we're losing sight of something if we look at collaboration of two producers of indie RPGs, both retaining their rights -- and call it the same as a larger company completely buying the rights of freelance writer, and doing what they like with the material.

      I think I should update and clarify the guidelines about what qualifies as indie -- and I'm open to debate on this. Personally, I would prefer that it be broader than only one-person-operation self-publishing.
