\n I didn't want want to pollute emily's Solitaire RPG challenge thread, so therefore this discussion where you can inform me. Because I'm not sure I get solitaire RPGs - or rather: how they can possibly be RPGs.

All in good spirits of course - I want to be wiser, not be in the middle of a shouting contest.

I play a lot of boardgames as well, and I also enjoy good solitaire wargames. There are only a few that are really good and worth playing IMO, but I'll name them here, perhaps it might inform you why I think what I think. OK, actually I only know of three good ones: An oldie called Raid on St Nazaire, one called Pizza-Box Football and a brand-spanking new called Labyrinth - The War on Terror. Check them out on Boardgamegeek if you like - main thing they are really different games, but you can get excited playing them even if you're on your own.

Now, story games. I find it really difficult to see how a solitaire game can provide the most important part: shared imagined space. Tell me how it could work - do you talk to yourself fx. Do you record/log what you are saying, and is the final "outcome" - story, whatever - as important, or more important, than play itself?

I don't consider adventure books ("there's a troll. If you attack it go to P34, if you run go to P12) to be roleplaying, so perhaps here's the hiccup already. To me there must be some kind of communication between at least two people.

Or am I totally xmas bonkers?

    \n I would say that the idea is to start from a place where we're thinking about RPG design, and then march away from there into one-player territory.
    That is, rather than starting with a board game idea, or a card game idea, we start with "okay, RPG. Hm." And then we move in the direction OF board games and card games.

    My assumption is that stuff like How to Host a Dungeon is best classified as a solo activity that role-players will enjoy. Its subject matter places it very close to RPG content - heck, you can use the product of play afterwards as the basis for a campaign!

    I agree, though - it is kind of wonky. Design concepts like clouds-and-dice make no sense here: where's the cloud? Since only one human is interacting with the thing, there can be no narrative, no description, beyond what can firmly interlock/interact with the rules. You can extrapolate from, say, Monopoly what's "really" going on, but your ideas therein will do nothing at all to actually affect the mechanical side of play, except insofar as you might make different decisions based on that. But any "fiction" you create around Monopoly is not going to interact with reward systems at all.

    Hypothetical example:
    1. On a random card draw, the player's character finds a photo of a person.
    2. The game asks the player what the person in the photo means to her character. The question(s) is leading, so to develop a strong connection.
    3. At a later juncture the game tells the player that as a result of a conflict, her character must leave one of her objects behind.
    4. Whether the photo is discarded or not (instead of a, say, practically much more useful object, like a swiss army knife) can be a purely fiction-driven decision. It is the player's judgement, and it depends on how strongly the player respects the fiction of course, but it can still instil a "I would never part with the photo of my dead wife." reaction.

    One way to tackle the simpler version, i.e. projecting imaginary content, could be to very literally explain what is occurring "in the fiction" when a given thing happens mechanically.     In a game where we're trying to create a reasonably discernible fictional environment, nailing that stuff down is key. Every procedure, and permutation therein, should probably be rooted in some logical, fictional explanation, unless it's rooted in, say, how stories work.

    \n Marc, accountability is an interesting issue, and one that I hadn't thought of. It reminds me, in many ways, of people forgetting to roll+harm in Apocalypse World, right? There's a counter-incentive to do it (the best that can happen is that you just take the harm, as normal) and it isn't strongly worked into the procedures of play. It forks off to the side, so it's easy to blow right by. Similarly, if I'm playing a solo RPG and fail to get away from the lobsterfrogmen, I might just draw a different card and decide that first draw "didn't count," as long as the procedures make that an obvious way to cheat.

    Like with harm, my initial intuition is that -- if you work the procedures for "failing" more tightly into the procedures for everything else, so it's not a fork off to the side -- then people won't cheat. They may not even be ABLE to cheat that way, because failing (or some other outcome that the player is not initially excited about) literally becomes "what has to happen next." Whereas, with harm, I was suggesting that "you have to roll+harm before you can take harm" and "it tells you what kind of harm to take" as possible ways to tie it more tightly in. In your lobsterfrogmen example, maybe you have to follow through with the procedures to find out what happens next in the game. Or, as in Burning Wheel, failure is also critical to make progress.

    \n This thread has a lot of interesting points in it, but my initial reaction is the same as Per's.

    To me, roleplaying (in our "story game" sense of the word; obviously you can walk down the street imagining yourself as Batman, but most people wouldn't say, "I played a roleplaying game on the way to work today")... anyway, to me roleplaying is the act of communicating something to another person.

    The whole thing is about interaction between people, and the creation of something beyond one person's purview. I engage in roleplaying when I communicate something to another human being in a way that allows me to interact with them.

    If I'm just sitting and not opening my mouth, I'm just an audience to someone else's story.

    If I'm just talking, and not listening and accepting input, then I'm just inventing a story, playing at being an author (is writing a book roleplaying?).

    Here's where it gets interesting, though:

    Imagine a situation like a Skype game, or, even better, a PBEM game. Not being able to see the person in front of me, I have no way of knowing that I'm playing with another live human being. If a "game" could respond to me in a way similar to the way another player does, and I cannot tell the difference, then I am truly roleplaying alone.

    Still, to me, the conscious act of communication aimed at another human being seems inextricably linked to the definition of roleplaying (the definition in my brain, that is; yours may differ).

      \n Such an awesome thread!

      Designing for solo play can be highly subtractive. Most of our excellent RPG theory was designed by examining multi-player play. It contains many assumptions that look like requirements but aren\u2019t. With solo design you can simply ignore elements that were previously indispensible. I think the way to go is to start from the phenomenon of daydreaming, as Jonathan suggested, and then work towards "daydreaming with rules."\n
      Posted By: Zac in DavisOne way to tackle the simpler version, i.e. projecting imaginary content, could be to very literally explain what is occurring "in the fiction" when a given thing happens mechanically. One weird thing about a lot of board games, card games, etc. for this purpose is that there will be these odd little permutations of the rules that don't inherently carry some sort of fictional explanation - what, exactly, is happening when I draw a card while playing Magic: the Gathering, for instance?

      Different cards suggest different answers:
      Is it adding books to my collection? (Library of Leng)
      Is it gaining new powers? (Demonic Pact)
      Is it amassing wealth? (Prosperity)

      In a game where we're trying to create a reasonably discernible fictional environment, nailing that stuff down is key. Every procedure, and permutation therein, should probably be rooted in some logical, fictional explanation, unless it's rooted in, say, how stories work.






      \nI don't want to erase Zac's insight here. Literally defining what it fictionally means to draw a card is a pretty powerful mechanic.

      However, I also want to point out that NOT defining what it means is equally powerful. The human mind craves narrative meaning, and can project it on purely mechanical processes. By not telling you what it means to draw a card, it invites you to create a narrative around it. This is why playing Magic can feel very narrative at times, even though the game largely avoids talking about it. Race for the Galaxy has a similar effect. Android is another interesting case. Each player plays a highly structured narrative, however everyone is so busy playing that narrative, they often have no idea what anyone else's narrative looks like. The narrative aspect is very nearly solo.

      Transmitting and coming to agreement on the fictional content of an RPG is a huge part of what a successful game typically needs to do. In a solo game, this is no longer a requirement because there\u2019s no one I need to transmit that fictional content to, or receive it from. I think Solo rules are much more about helping me to clarify and develop my own fictional picture of the situation in a way that is fun.

      The solo design challenge is super awesome. I can't wait to see what everyone comes up with!

      Oh yeah, and I am totally neutral on whether or not How to Host a Dungeon is an RPG, but it is a story game, because the rules tell you to write down what happens as a historical record.

      \n I made up a solitaire game, once. You see, working security at a casino is less foiling dashing bands of motley criminals played by famous actors and more standing around for hours at a time, during which you will perhaps help three people figure out which garage they parked their car at. So I got bored, and imagined a hypothetical scenario: I'm playing an RPG, but the GM has been, for reasons complicated to get into, put in a giant black box. They can see and hear me and what I do, but only communicate via "yes" and "no". So I've got to drag the situation out of them with yes/no questions, and the reactions of NPCs or the environment, and so forth.

      The "black box" was of course the nearby roulette wheel and its steady stream of black/red and even/odd. The technique... wasn't perfect, largely due to the lag between "answers", but if you really do buy into the scenario and restrict yourself from asking unreasonable questions like "Does gold rain down from the skies allowing me to purchase that fancy mansion I'm after?" it can work surprisingly well.

      One fun aspect of the technique is that, while it works "freeform" with your yes/no questions determining the results of your uncertain actions, it can actually also work well with existing game systems, too. "Okay, I'm going to try to pick the lock. Is this an average lock, obstacle of about 3?" NO. "More difficult than average?" YES. "Ob 4?" NO. "5?" NO. "6?" YES. "Damn, 6! Is it higher difficulty because... maybe I hear some guards coming, and so I have to work quickly?" YES. "Okay, rolling it!"

      What works about it, I think is that most of the time you'll get a yes on the first or second thing you come up with, but every now and again you'll get a string of "no"s that force you think a bit outside the box. And things get pretty interesting if you remember to occasionally ask narrowing-down questions instead of serial-guessing questions, as well.

      Posted By: TeataineShared Imagined Space
      To me this is a term with inherent weaknesses. To put it briefly: I do not believe there is such a thing as a truly shared imagined space. I believe the fictions we create in one single game-session is as multiple, and different, as there are players (including the GM). I believe the sharing is part of the interaction, not the fiction. The interactive praxis is a shared effort.

      This relates to my idea of how a role-playing game goes from
      - the vision of the game-designer
      - through the interaction of the game-group (player-player interaction and player-character interaction)
      - and on to produce the individual, and mainly internally imagined, fiction of each participating player.

      Simply put: vision -> interaction -> fiction.

      You may argue that the fictions produced by one game-group will be related, but not that they are the same. These fictions are only "shared" in a very vague sense of the word.

      The thing is that within this simple theoretical system, any role-playing game, including solo-rpgs, may be easily understood and analyzed as part of the rpg-family. ;-)

      \n I have an interesting anecdote to add to the conversation. Call it a solo RPG AP.

      I am a senior medical student. Roleplaying games are core to medical education. Every doctor who graduates from medical school and plans to practice in the US will have roleplayed to some degree. In order to get a spot in a residency in the US you must pass the US Medical Licencing Exams Step 2 Clinical Skills Test. Big name for an elaborate and expensive LARP. Medical students in their 4th year of medical school will converge on a handfull of cities and "pretend to be doctors treating patients". There are rules that I won't get into here, but the gist is that they set up a faux clinic, and you rotate to 12 different patient rooms. Each room has a paid professional actor that you interact with, diagnose, counsel, and then write orders and a clinic note for. There are certain actions that you just pretend to do because of danger and discomfort, such as rectal exams and female pelvic and breast exams. But it is all pretend. No one is sick. And you pass if you play well enough.

      This is not the only example of roleplay in medical school. Subjects that are difficult to talk about, that make people uncomfortable thinking about, or are just outside of the experiences of most students, we role play them. It sets up a safe place to practice, it allows for an audience and feedback, and it decreases anxiety of students considerably. Subjects such as sex, drugs, drinking, money, homosexuality, religion, cross cultural medicine, and end of life care are some but not all of the areas that medical students role play pretty extensively prior to ever interacting with a patient. They are considered invaluable to medical education, and most people would say that a medical school that doesn't engage in these activities is not worth going to. Your education will suffer.

      To give you an idea about how these role playing sessions are actually games and not just free form role playing, I will relate my most recent session. It was during my palliative care rotation last June. Palliative care is the branch of medicine that deals with helping patients make decisions in situations where there are really no good options. The most difficult aspects of this class are that you have to help patients make decisions about care when there is no option that is curative, and the other options have significant negative side effects. Most patients are going to die. Soon. There is a ton of loss that you have to deal with. The stories and relationships are amazing. It is uncomfortable even for people who are good at it.

      So here is the game. We were lead through a guided experience. We were instructed to create these cards that each had one thing of significant value to us, each within one of 5 spheres: things we possess, relationships, activities we love, objects in nature of significant meaning to us, and goals that we have for our lives. This gave us each a deck of 20 cards with all the most important things to each of us. There was considerable room for interpretation of the meaning of each sphere, and my 4 possessions turned out to be qualities, while my friend's were all objects. We were then told to imagine that we were the character in the following fiction, and the more we immersed ourselves in it the more we would gain from it. The "GM" then read a piece from our perspective that related finding a lump in our armpit, relating it to the doctor, biopsy of the lump, the wait for initial diagnosis, the actual diagnosis of cancer, the treatment, the relapse, the further treatment, the non-response to treatment, the slow decline, and eventual death. Throughout this reading, the "GM" would stop the reading and we were asked to lose a couple of cards at each dramatic moment. Some losses were chosen, some were randomly chosen by a partner. There was one episode of recovery of cards. It was amazingly difficult losing each thing that was important to you. Eventually, you were left, contemplating the very end of your life, left with nothing as you left this world. All 12 of the participants in this exercise were left crying, with a significant sense of loss, and a new understanding. There is no way to really relate the feelings that I got from this exercise, but the exercise reliably created the same general experience and feelings in all participants.

      The understanding gained was that each of us had different things on our cards. Each thing was really important to the owner of the card, but there were some that were more important than others. My experience of taking a friend's cards left me with the thought that I would have liked to be able to ask her what cards she wanted me to take. I devastated her because I took the relationship that was most important to her and the place that meant the most to her. She cried when I did it. To be able to avoid taking those would have been the most humane thing possible at that moment. In real life, we each have imaginary cards, each person has different cards, and through my actions, I will end up taking cards away from my patients. Therapies always have both positive and negative consequences. I will now always ask which cards they value most. My patients deserve that.

      In this exercise, I learned a great deal about my own values, my mortality, but for my practice, I have gained an empathy that it would have taken terrible mistakes to learn without the game. The cost would have been great for my patients, but also for my psyche. I do not need to make those mistakes now.

      To return to the topic at hand, the fact that this was done as a group added some to the experience, but it could have easily been done solo. It was a highly immersive simmulative narrative generator, with input about things of highest value to the participant added to the narrative by the participant, and an interesting game element created with just a very simple mechanic. Even in a group setting, the game took place almost exclusively in the heads of each participant. There was an Imagined Space, but I cannot say that it was really Shared. The narrative related by the "GM" could easily have been read solo. The random selection of a card by a partner could have also been done solo. The effect would not have been identical, and participants would be denied the role of a health care provider in that instance, but the other half of the experience would have been retained. There was a bond created between the participants, but this could have been created if the experience took place in different locations and asynchronously. The value of the experience could have almost completely been reatained by a solo participant who took it just as serious as those of us who experienced it in a group.

      In essence, it had what I consider to be the essentials of an RPG. A narrative that can be interacted with and allows significant contribution by the participant, wrapped in a set of game mechanics that keeps tension in the narrative, and allows the the immersive exploration of role and situation. Each person left with an entirely different and complete narrative, even if they were all highly related. The fact that it could have easily been done as a solo RPG makes me think that it is not only possible, but my experience of it makes me feel like it could be very meaningful to people when it is perfected.

      \n Sorry, but I'd have to say that this "AP" is very far removed from a solo RPG....simply by virtue of the fact that interaction with other participants occurs.

      It's more like a multi-GM multi-player experience, where each actor portraying the symptoms of a specific patient is acting as a quasi-GM for their one-on-one scene/session with a given player.       I thought that what we were aiming for here was an experience that approximates roleplaying, or even embodies the essence of roleplaying, without requiring external participants at all.

      I see where the last part of the post is heading back toward solo play, but the AP at the beginning of the post just doesn't seem to support this play style.

      It's basically like getting the game Clue (or Cluedo) then trying to simply guess who the killer was, what the murder weapon was and where the murder was committed. There's no real mechanisms to stop you from just guessing every possible combination....

      Posted By: Per FischerOK -- when I play boardgames, I tend to "roleplay". When I play Diplomacy I take on the persona of the leader of the power I'm playing. Same thing in Civilization or Empires in Arms. And Junta!. I sometimes talk in character, heck I even make decisions in character even though they are not objectively the best. Am I playing a roleplaying game? Obviously not. Am I roleplaying?
      \nHere's where I think the line between a roleplaying game and a not-roleplaying game is: does the game respond to your role-playing? Is there an obvious, supported feedback circle between your role-appropriate decisions and the other bits of the game?

      So yeah, you're most probably not playing a roleplaying game. Are you roleplaying? I think you are (just not in a fulfilling way, because it's a bit like having a conversation with a wall), but opinions will vary.

      I do that too in boardgames sometimes, but I always find it's more satisfying to not do it. Our group had/has the tendency to roleplay in Twilight Imperium. Our games would drag for 9+ hours because we were doing galactic diplomacy, trade, and technological advancement, "roleplaying" our civilisations. Now we learned to shoot for the Victory Points (which is the actual point of the game) and our play time has halved and games are more satisfying.

      We started playing HeroQuest this year, as a sort of nostalgia trip. When we started, I tried to approach it as D&D, I was miserable. I thought it was the worst game ever. Then I started treating it like a boardgame and play on an abstract level, to win (not trying to play a character in a situation) and the game was suddenly very fun (if still a little wonky mechanically).

      Our group has been playing a lot of Arkham Horor lately, too. I found that we resorted to "roleplaying" ("what's going on, fictionally" and "characters in a situation") to rationalize things when the rules were unclear. We would try to figure out what's going on fictionally at that moment and then make a judgement call. I wouldn't call it roleplaying though, because we weren't making character decisions based on "what's going on" but based on the abstract mechanical stuff. Likewise, the game wouldn't respond, support or change in any way if we actually did roleplay stuff, like if my Private Dick started to hit on the hot Psychologist. No feedback circle.

      In other words, role-playing is playing (pawn, actor or director stance) a character in a situation. A roleplaying game is when that activity is suggested, supported, rewarded by the system - it's meaningful. When we're doing it because or with the mechanics, not in spite of them.

      \n Just to clarify, my previous post had more than one example of RP in medical school. There was the USMLE Step 2 CS roleplaying-within-an-exam type LARP. That was decidedly not a solo RPG, and was used to give context to RPing in med school. The real point of the post was the second example, the end-of-life guided-experience-with-game-mechanics example. This was effectively a solo RPG in the context of a group setting.

      This experience changed the way I see RPGs. I have been obsessed with capturing that sort of experience in an RPG ever since. It effectively was an RPG that took punchy and character-pertinent cues and embedded them in some simple game mechanics (similar to Aspects), combined them with some simple fictional context generating cues, and strung them together and recombined them into a coherent narrative. All the while, the game obeyed all of Vincent Baker's Clouds and Arrows advice about connecting the fiction to the mechanics. It was extremely cool, and was perhapse the most well designed RPG I have seen, and the author didn't even intend to write an RPG.

      I have been thinking about creating gravitas in the fiction of an RPG for some time now. Utilizing things of value to the participants in the real world really made this experience have meaning. The fact that I eventually had to choose between my relationship with my father and playing with my daughter really made the idea of loss of everything come home to me. I have been designing a game about the kidnaping of a child, with participants using a real world relationship with a child to model the in game relationship, to the point of using real world experiences/objects/names/etc as game cues. This was supposed to connect the fiction to the mechanics, and create a very emotion laden game. From this thread, I think I need to check out The Plant. I will check back in here later to see where this thread goes.

      Okay, to me, How to Host a Dungeon is a very GM-centered Solo RPG Activity/Game.

      A lot of GM fun is generally preperatory, or during game, about finding connections between very different things, then building on them. It isn't as much, at least for me, about character building, or even world development exactly.

      It's more about creating a clock-workk situation and setting, then introducing random developments and seeing what happens.

      With a traditional GM set up, for me, that random element is 90% player interaction, and about 10% system function ( generally related directly to player actions).

      Anyway, I guess I'd just focus more on that end when/if, I'm designing a solo game. In the past, when I've made up solo minis gaming rules, that tended to be my focus. Not trying to challenge myself in terms of winning with one side or the other, but rather simply challenging myself by throwing in some kind of random input/random changes of focus to force me to think of how that fit with other stuff at play, including precedents of earlier random stuff.

      In any case, all of that seems to be in a largely different direction from the emphasis I see in this thread, which seems more focussed on how a character-player can get the goodness of gaming that they're used to.
