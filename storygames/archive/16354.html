    \n You're looking at too short moments in rpg history, methinks. I mean, I wouldn't say that stakes setting is "gone again". Many of the most foundational pure-form stakes-utilizing games were published just what, seven years back. That's just about enough time for me to consider them fully matured. The issue is further confused by the insistence of the Internet to take every idle musing so very seriously. A few years back everybody was all about those stakes-setting discussions Jonathan mentions, but as far as I understood at the time, it was just a bunch of discussion about people misunderstanding stakes setting, not any profound sea change. Functional play remains functional play, no matter the Internet trends.

    I wouldn't deny that trends change, but at the sort of level of detail you're looking at there's probably no more profound answer than "Vincent Baker's been investigating alternatives recently". That's your perceived trend: one prominent designer has been doing things in somewhat different ways lately. Besides, I wouldn't even call e.g. Apocalypse World a non-stakes game, it doesn't have any fundamental differences from say TSoY, or going further back, Sorcerer. (This isn't too surprising - most of the stakes-setting discourse in the Internet is extremely superficial, mostly concerning things like who says what instead of the fundamental issues of currency and narration control.)

    \n It's part of the evolution. Stakes setting was a revolutonary idea that got explored in a variety of ways. Is it still? Probably. I think it depends on the game.

    It was popular because it was new and lots of designers wanted to try out this new tool and play with it in new ways. I'm sure part of this reason was inclusion in Dogs and PTA, both big early indie games and influencers - those two were my gateway drugs. Then it lost its shine and it became clear that this wasn't a system meant for every game.

    I haven't played any of the games Eero listed, and the two I have played (AW and Fiasco) I disagree have stake setting. Fiasco you pick to establish or resolve a scene. The resolve isn't set up as a stake. The scene plays out, and you (or the rest of the table) decides if the resolution is mostly positive or mostly negative for your character.

    Anyway, I think we're seeing less stake setting because it's not a good fit for every game. It probably isn't a good fit for most games. I also know a lot of players who have a problem with it and it sometimes proved to be a barrier for me to convert story-focused but traditional familiar players to indie games. It's a very meta concept. Setting stakes requires players to pull back from the fiction a little bit or a lot. A lot of people don't like to play that way.

    Lately I've been seeing games trend towards stake setting as more of an undercurrent of resolution systems. Whether it's Fiasco's "let the scene play out to find the stakes" or Smallville's "let each action be their own stakes within the fiction" or some other style.

    \n Jonathan: Apocalypse World has stakes-setting, it's just incorporated into the game crunch. It's exactly like TSoY in this regard: in both games you can certainly have stakes like "I turn my rival into a frog with my magic", but only if you have the crunch that permits you to request these stakes. In AW it's called "moves", in TSoY it's called "Secrets", but the basic principle is the same. In both cases the most important feature is there: we know in advance what we're rolling for, and how to interpret the roll directly against that goal.

    Of course it's a bit different from raw universal conflict resolution with free stakes-setting, but if we're talking about strictly the latter, then that's just a specific extreme variation of the form. And if we're talking about stakes-setting in general, then I think that the fundamental question vs. older generic skill systems is whether stakes are present at all, not whether there are mechanical constraints or guidelines upon the kinds of stakes you can use. In this regard I consider any game where the rules establish binding pre-roll stakes to be "stakes-setting". The player doesn't have to be able to choose any possible stakes freely for it to be stakes-setting; in fact, I can't name a well-written game that would claim such, they all use different tools in predetermining the stakes.

    As I intimated above, I mostly think that people have just accepted the last round of stakes-setting hulabaloo and moved on. Apparently nobody here is feeling like talking about the actual topic of the last round, which as I understood it was about contrasting conflict resolution with task resolution. Perhaps the new topic could be about whether mechanically constrained stakes (Apocalypse World) are better than freeform stakes (HeroQuest, say) or organic non-stakes conflict resolution systems (Sorcerer, IaWA, D&D)

    Aaand here we are again depicting how these Internet discussions about stakes-setting are slow and weighty affairs. We could probably do one page of defining our terms before we even started talking about whether "stakes" or history or not. And another page about whether opening the safe is a stake or not.

    \nWhy stake setting:
    One notable early game that used stake setting was Luke Crane's Burning Wheel, in which every roll has more or less predefined stakes. For Luke, at least, he explains pretty straightforwardly that the stake definition part of a roll in his game is to fight a toxic gameplay culture of rolls that are unproductive, deceptive, or repetitive -- in other words, to keep illusionism out of the picture. Part of the assumption was that people might come to Burning Wheel from a toxic D&D game in which the GM is expected to "tell the story," and manipulate all roll results to that end; by formalizing the communication process, you can make this harder to do, and (more importantly) signal to the players that you weren't supposed to do that. Let it Ride and "Say yes or roll dice" are part of the same structure here; they make it clear that when the players roll dice, it should be meaningful, and they should be (diagetically) well-informed about the situation -- otherwise they aren't really participating in the game. You can also look at this as the clarification of IIEE as an important part of resolution mechanic design. So in this light, stake setting is a tool to put the focus back on the things the players are aiming to change or accomplish in play.

    Why not stake setting:
    So in the lead-up to Apocalypse World, Vincent talks a lot about the "moment of judgement": a point during gameplay where you have to refer to the fiction to apply the rules. There were a few game designs in the intervening period between Dogs and AW that were essentially finite state story machines: the rules interacted with each other and with the numbers on your sheet, and you made mechanical decisions, and then you were supposed to narrate stuff that was in line with the mechanical results. But, since the mechanics didn't refer to the fiction, it had a tendency to become thin and unsatisfying, because the game still "works" without proper narration. If you look at D&D 4th edition, for example, you see people complaining about there being no roleplaying in the game, and other people saying "well just narrate stuff during combat!" You can do this, of course, and the game even suggests you ought to, but since there's no mechanical reason to, it often falls by the wayside in favor of "I hit him again." (Note that I like D&D 4th a lot, but it's an example of this problem.)

    My impression, which is not scientific, is that it can be difficult to align stake setting (in which the consequences of a roll are generally decided before the roll itself, and you are often encouraged to ask for what you want rather than what you believe might reasonably arise from the existing fiction) with judgement (which relies on looking at both the result of a roll, the character's decisions and the existing narrative to move the narrative forward). As Eero basically says, Vincent wrote AW, and he happened not to use stakes (although there ARE stakes when you write a front, just not directly in conflict resolution). Maybe this gave people the impression that stakes and judgement are incompatible. I suspect they aren't, but, on the other hand, stake setting is a means to an end -- if you have another way to make sure the game focuses on the player's choices, or you're not worried about a "brain damage" culture any more, what's the benefit you're looking for from having stake setting in the first place?

    \n To add to what Liam's saying, one of the other concerns about stake-setting is "play-before-you-play" issues. Games with really effective stakes-setting don't detail possible outcomes any more than they have to, they simply say the thing that's at stake ("Your mother's trust"), not how it will be dealt with after resolution. Otherwise, it can feel like you don't actually get to play out the meatiest part of the game, where the consequences actually get determined. This is related to Liam's point about feeling a disconnectedness with the fiction. Reactions to the potential for disconnect and pre-play have varied. "Fiction first" is definitely one. Emphasizing task resolution over conflict resolution is another (and the recent indie interest in OSR is partially related to that, I think). On the more experimental and freeform side of things, there are games that reject mechanical resolution entirely (like Silver & White), replacing it with some other interesting mechanic that structures play without really resolving tasks or conflicts.

    \n Considering free (that is, players can choose what they are rolling for pretty freely) and explicit (players know in advance what the dice will establish for the fiction) stakes-setting, it's pretty much a feature of universal resolution systems. So you'd want to have it in any system that tries to get by with just one resolution procedure for any situation. In fact, making an universal resolution mechanic meaningful requires explicit stakes-setting; as we know, traditional games get by without explicit stakes-setting in large part because the resolution procedure involves very concrete fictional events and actions that at least give the players an illusion of concrete stakes if nothing else. On the other hand, if your resolution system is just "when you need to resolve, take one die per character sheet trait that's been introduced into the scene and then roll them all hoping for big numbers" (a common universal resolution mechanic), then the procedure itself in no way implies stakes. This is why an explicit discussion of "why are we rolling, again?" is often a necessity in this sort of game.

    The amount of pre-roll negotiation and the ambiguity of stakes-setting may be drawn back by adding focus and mechanical elements that reduce the ambiguity. A resolution system may also be made to resolve first and establish meaning later. Both of these are different from explicit stakes-setting discussion in that nobody needs to ask why we're rolling the dice right now. It seems to me that particularly that first thing is pretty common because extra detail and focus in a game is simply fun. You can publish something like HeroQuest (a grand-daddy of free and explicit stakes-setting) once, but doing that again and again isn't useful in any way. In this way it's not a surprise that vanilla stakes-setting is somewhat less common as the time goes by.\n

    \n Personally, I do not consider Burning Wheel to involve stake setting, but that may be a semantic argument. In Burning Wheel you state your intent and describe how your character goes about fulfilling that intent in the fiction of the game (this constitutes the task). The GM then considers that intent and task, tells you what skill is appropriate to fulfilling it, what the obstacle is (the number of successes required) and the consequence of failure.

    The intent put forward by the player and the consequence put forward by the GM look a lot like stakes. But it is limited. In Burning Wheel, your intent must be closely correlated with the task. In other words, the character, your fictional avatar in the game world, must be capable of taking an action that brings about the result you're seeking (your intent). You can't, for instance, say: "My intent is to make the castle tumble into ruin. My task is stabbing the king during the ceremony." Unless, of course, it has already been established that there is some sort of connection between the state of repair of the castle and the life of the king. Otherwise, the GM would be more than justified in saying: "Nice try. Taking out that castle is going to require an Artillerist test. Hope you brought your siege engineers!"

    On the GM side, the consequence must also follow from the intent and task. If you're trying to seduce a barmaid with a poetry test, the GM can't say: "If you fail this test, your mother catches the plague and dies!" That's just not within the scope of the test.

    To me, and again this may just be my semantic hangup, stake setting implies a disconnection between the action taken in the fiction and the consequences of that action. Taken to an extreme, this means a player can use any of a character's abilities to achieve any result. There is little mechanical incentive not to use a character's best ability to accomplish everything the player wants to accomplish all the time.

    It also tends to involve some level of negotiation between the players: "No, I don't accept that. How about if the consequence is this instead..." At best, IMO, this leads to really watered down fictional events after players negotiate away any outcome seen as too detrimental to their characters. At worst, you spend significant portions of game time negotiating about potential outcomes rather than playing to find out what the outcomes are.

    In the end, setting stakes or intent and task or any mechanisms in that vein are techniques. Games should incorporate the techniques that make the experience of playing them better and exclude techniques (even popular techniques that work well in other games) that don't serve that purpose.

    \n Thor, pretty much no conflict resolution game works by setting up stakes unrelated to character intent and activity. In fact, it would be wise to use some other term for the rare exceptions like Polaris, where conflicts (whether characters get what they want) are actually resolved by a higher-order narration-trading game between the players. I guess they might be termed "narrative resolution", although Polaris is a bit of a poetic halfway thing in that it involves strongly defined player roles (the Heart, Mistaken, etc.) that obligate the players to endorse certain sorts of stories. If you're willing to interpret the Heart as a mythic character separate from the protagonist of the story, then it's totally conflict resolution

    Burning Wheel, Shadow of Yesterday, Dust Devils, Mountain Witch... pretty much any classical conflict resolution game has the same set-up where players declare character intent and the GM shapes this intent into the stakes before the roll. Here's how I wrote the central procedure in my TSoY rewrite:\n
    Posted By: Solar SystemConflict stakes The Story Guide has the specific responsibility of declaring the stakes of the conflict after hearing the goals of the characters. This is a slightly intricate issue, so pay attention. Normally, when a conflict is initiated, it is pretty obvious what the conflict is for: a character wishes to bed another, say, and the conflict is over whether he manages to convince the other character to play along. The goals of the characters are rather unambiguous in this sense: whatever the characters want to do, that\u2019s usually what the conflict is about. In these cases the Story Guide just nods and gives the go-ahead for players to proceed with resolving the conflict. The most typical situation where the Story Guide needs to clarify the stakes is when a player\u2019s declared goal is too shallow. For example, a player might describe that his character is going to shoot another character. All well and good, but that is not actually a goal, it\u2019s just what the character is doing to achieve his goal. Is the character trying to stop the other from hurting him? Is he trying to subdue the other by wounding him? Is he trying to kill the other character? For conflict resolution the Story Guide needs to find out and bring these intentions on the table, as the players will need to know what they are resolving when narrating the conflict sequence. Another situation where the Story Guide needs to modify the stakes from the intents described by the players is when a character wants too much. For example, a character might want to rob the emir of a valuable jewel kept on the bosom of emir\u2019s daughter. Can the player just declare a conflict over this and resolve the exciting situation in one roll? This is a matter of leverage, propriety and scope. Leverage, propriety and scope When a character can try to succeed in something, he has leverage. A character who has wings might try to fly because of those wings, for example. A character who has sneaked into the bedchambers of emir\u2019s daughter might try to steal a valuable jewel. Leverage is being in the fictional situation that allows a character to attempt a given goal in the first place. When a character\u2019s declared goal is kinda sudden and unexpected in the context, it is a matter of propriety. This is not so much about moral judgement most of the time, but rather about the norms of the fun game \u2014 a very typical campaign norm is that named, important characters are not killed just like that, off-hand. So in this kind of campaign the Story Guide might question the propriety of the goal and perhaps clarify that while the character might seem to die, he might also come back later as the result of a miraculous rescue, a common motif of adventure stories. When the group thinks that some matter of fiction should not be skimmed too quickly, it is a question of scope. If the emir and his jewel are not important, the group might think that it is a swell idea to have the daring thievery resolved in one conflict \u2014 just roll Thievery (I) against the emir\u2019s Suspicion (R) and be done with it. If that pace is too quick, however, the group might wish to insist on a smaller conflict scope \u2014 finding out whether the character can even get into emir\u2019s seraglio, or how he outwits the guards, or how he charms the emir\u2019s daughter \u2014 these are all fine and exciting conflicts on their own, well able to branch in unexpected directions. When determining the stakes of a conflict, the Story Guide should keep an eye on all of leverage, propriety and scope: is the character in position to try for this goal, or should he first maneuver into position? Is the goal an appropriate event for the genre? Do we, as a group, want to skim or slow down over these events? Making these decisions is not as difficult as it might sound, and the rest of the group will certainly have their own notions. If in doubt, go with the goal declared by the player \u2014 if the emir\u2019s jewel is just a step in some interesting story, take care of it in one conflict and move on.

    \n So, you've got stake-setting in Dogs and PTA. Inspired by those games (and directly misinterpreted from the PTA text) was a concept that was called stake-setting and later called story-boarding (notably by people who found it dissatisfying).

    People who found story-boarding to be dissatisfying deliberately wrote their new games to avoid the mention of stakes (to prevent people from story-boarding instead) while still retaining the function of stake-setting. Examples include Apocalypse World and the Rustbelt.

    \nYes. That is an example of system-regulated stakes. The entire D&D combat system is basically similar to e.g. Heroquest's complex conflict system. The stakes thing is not significant for it because it's already effective in processing the sorts of stakes it's used for.

    Where conflict resolution (and by extension stakes-setting) becomes important is in the context of general skill systems that do not have conflict-regulation mechanisms like hit points backing them. For example, D&D ability checks and the skill checks in the ordinary traditional games; these don't have firm conflict resolution procedures attached, which means that it's basically up to the playgroup to utilize these systems in ways that actually resolve things. Stakes-setting is one way of quickly turning an arbitrary system such as task resolution into a conflict resolution system: identify conflict, agree within the group that it's gonna be resolved via this task resolution (or whatever) and then resolve. Games like Burning Wheel and TSoY do this, and you can use the same method with a traditional game, too.

    All that's not going to be meaningful for you if e.g. you've always had conflict resolution procedures in your personal play habits, in which case you've been able to utilize the specific task resolution systems of individual games in productive ways. I believe that some gamers have grown up like this, they've learned early on to use the task resolution systems effectively. Other gamers, on the other hand, have had their problems, usually involving illusionism or obfuscation or simple inability to communicate intent among the group. For these gamers an explicit conflict resolution system (with free and explicit stakes-setting or not) is a boon, as it effectively enforces a procedure of play where players focus on identifying conflicts, and the system is used effectively in resolving them. This is why many have experienced explicit conflict resolution as an improvement on the purposefulness of their play.

    \n The real issue is how sub-systems at different scopes are coupled (weakly, strongly, or in extreme cases even not at all).

    For example, Dogs has two low level resolution sub-systems: one mechanical - the bid and see, one social - say yes or roll the dice.

    It has a next higher level resolution sub-system which is strongly coupled with both lower level ones, i.e. the "conflict resolution" subsystem.

    It then has two higher level subsystems: the NPC frames - which naturally couples with the conflict resolution subsystem, and the fictional positioning from scene framing to determining the responses of the NPCs - which is loosely coupled with the conflict resolution system. However it is this last system which most strongly influences how the conflicts of the town are resolved, not actually the conflict resolution subsystem.

    There may be a third subsystem at this level, if you count player moral judgement - but that seems to emerge in some games more than others.

    The traditional complaint about task resolution is that the task and the goal are too loosely coupled through the GM, which under some social circumstances is problematic, see the whole OSR sense of DM impartiality as a social remedy to this problem, rather than the typical indie game mechanical solution.

    Ultimately the scopes* of the different subsystems and how they are coupled is a powerful set of design decisions, being aware and using them right or playing with them consciously is far better than blasting or glorifying a particular strategy such as explicit stake setting or task resolution.

    - Mendel


    *Fun Fact: Scopes don't need to be hierarchical, they can break the fiction down a huge variety of ways.

      \n When people talks about any one of these things (task/conflict resolution, fortune in the middle/at the end, stakes setting, etc.) there is a widespread tendency to take an entire block of things together and call this shambling mound of ripped-off rules "it".
      Then someone ask about another of these things, someone take a entire block of things, and say "this is it". Someone points out that they are looking at the exact same block as before, and confusion and a lot of arguing ensues.

      The widespread tendency is to look at a game, let's say Dogs in the Vineyard for example, and when you hear that that game has "conflict resolution", you start to spread around your newfound knowledge about the way conflict resolutions needs Mormon gunslingers to works.... (the example is ridiculous and exaggerated, but when you have people confuse conflict resolution with explicit stakes-setting or one-roll resolution, it's not the same thing?)

      Ron Edwards use the word "Synecdoche" a lot in his big model essays to describe this process of mistaking the part for the whole (or the other way around).

      The meaning of these words, in Forge Theory, is much simpler: you don't need to take out the entire block of rules from a game to get conflict resolution, for example. Conflict/task is ONLY about "when you roll" (or more generally, when you use your game's equivalent of a roll). Do you roll dice to see if your character is stong/fast/clever enough to pull/climb/notice something? That is task resolution, you are literally "rolling to see if you can do the task". Or, in your game, you roll ONLY when there is a conflict between characters? That is conflict resolution.

      Vincent found a very clever way of describing conflict resolution: "roll the dice or say yes". People in the Internet were able to misunderstand even something so simple, but if you read the entire chapter that describe that rule, its meaning is clear: don't roll for task (climb a tree), roll only when there is conflict: conflict resolution.

      This doesn't requires stakes, and doesn't even require that the roll resolve the conflict of interest between characters (often it doesn't). The combat system in D&D is conflict resolution, because you use it... only when you have a conflict.

      The problem of these definitions is that they are TOO simple. People search for some hidden higher "truth", something mind-blowing that describe an entire game system. But these words are made to be used with each other, simple concept that have to be put together.

      So, ending this rambling: Stefoid, you are wrong. Really, really wrong. You made a pile of a lot things and called the entire pile Conflict Resolution, even if there are really a lot of examples of games that use conflict resolution that violate every single thing you said. Even Dogs in the Vineyard and Primetime Adventures would not be conflict resolution by your definition! Both require A LOT of human judgment after the roll to resolve the situation.(and this other little bit is called Fortune in the Middle: if you don't have that human judgment it's fortune at the end)

      About stakes, many others have already said what happened, but seeing that a lot have said otherways too, I think I will add this post to the first camp: the problem with "stakes" was a similar problem to the one I talked about conflict resolution: corruption of the meaning of the word.

      "Stakes" was always the wrong word to describe what in Trollbabe was called "goal" (but could be called "intent"). I don't know where, in which game, the meaning of these two words were exchanged and "stakes" began to be used to describe the character's objective, but this caused a lot of the problems: calling it "stakes" a lot of people did misunderstand the meaning, did not understand that it was the characters in-fiction objective, and started story workshopping games that weren't made to be played like this (an this is easily shown in the amount of people who complain online about PTA, and when you ask them about it, it come out that they misunderstood exactly that point of the system)

      These days, I see a lot of games with stakes, but (1) they are less explicit about it, and (2) they don't call them stakes anymore.

      The problem was (and is) not the use of "explicit stakes": they work really well, and that is reflected by the way they took off in indie game design in 2004-2006. The problems was and is about making people (and in particular this gaming culture) to understand how to use them. At the end dropping the word was simpler.

      P.S.: The new edition of Trollbabe has almost 50 pages more than the previous one, and has less rules! The added pages contain detailed and precise instruction to help people understand how to use the rules. At this time is, I believe, the best "teaching test" for the kind of explicit stake-setting conflict resolution used by a lot of other games, including PTA.

      \nYour problem here is that you're trying to pin down definitions, Jason. This is difficult when you're not asking "what is your definition?" but rather "what is the official definition I can hit people in the head with?". This latter question is foolish, at least until we finally get the RPG police going. They could probably have a, like, special office for terminology definitions. It would surely be a fine day, for afterwards nobody would need to understand anything of substance beyond the terminology people happen to use, they could just confidently use words with dictionary meanings to chatter at each other.

      D&D combat system is definitely a conflict resolution system for any meaningful way of using that term that I know. It funnels and regulates all fictional activity into terms that are trivial to apply for answering the question of who wins this fight. I've never heard of anybody playing D&D in a way that would have undermined the conflict-resolution power of the combat system; even the most railroady GMs are forced to cheat on the dice rather than ignoring the outcomes of the combat system. Simultaneously the Climb skill in 3rd edition for the most part is utilized only as task resolution, simply because the system in play proffers no strong guarantees about the significance and meaning of the resolution procedure. (That is, there is no guarantee that the task of climbing a wall coincides with a conflict, and even if it does, there is no guarantee that climbing the wall would contribute to resolving that conflict.) Obviously enough, if you play it like conflict resolution, then it is conflict resolution; doing stakes-setting is one way of ensuring that you're not rolling those Climb checks just to feed the GM's perverse need for dice rituals. Say these magic words before you roll, and the check magically turns into conflict resolution: "This wall is in between me and my goal. If I succeed in this check, I want no more shit about this wall - I'll have cleared it fair and clear!"

      Something I usually say about these vapid terminology discourses is that hopeless confusion will persist as long as one insists on viewing "conflict" and "task" as complementary opposites. They're in fact phenomena at entirely different levels. There are resolution systems that are both conflict and task resolving at the same time, and ones that resolve neither. If the only way you can view task resolution is as something that is not as good as conflict resolution and is somehow opposed to it, then you don't understand much about traditional rpg writing. If you feel like this applies to you, dear reader, I recommend my write-up about task resolution The Shadow of Yesterday.       \n This is how "What's at Stake" works in Dogs in the Vineyard:\n
      To launch a conflict, we begin by establishing what\u2019s at stake, setting the stage, and figuring out who\u2019s participating.
      \n
      Establish what\u2019s at stake. Any player can make suggestions, and everybody should feel free to toss it around until you arrive at the right thing.
      What\u2019s at stake is: does your character\u2019s brother shoot the woman?
      \n
      So now you only have the one 1 left. You can\u2019t See my Raise, so I win the stakes. Your character stands there with the wind out of his sails and I have my character push past and on into town, where he murders the shopkeeper\u2019s supposed wife.
      \nYou don't set Stakes like: "If I win, I get this. If you win, you get that." There is no "if X wins, Y happens". Instead you just agree on what specific thing you are fighting over. What is at stake (singular). Who wins get to say what happens. And the actions back and forth in the conflict help inform that final result. It's important not to preplay because we don't know if someone is going to Escalate from talking to shooting till we actually play which will greatly influence how we determine what happens to what is at stake. By establishing too much up front, we make a lot of the actual conflict feel forced or predictable in Dogs.

      I think Stake Setting works great for many games.

      For example, Stake Setting in Mouse Guard (called Conflict Goals) are awesome!\n
      Conflict Goals
      Each team picks a short-term or immediate goal they wish to accomplish as a result of this conflict. Conflict goals are similar to your character\u2019s session Goal. Include a statement about your character, an action and a target.

      Lieam is ambushed by a snake. He knows the snake is going to try to kill him. He sets his goal: \u201cKill this snake so that Kenzie and Saxon are safe.\u201d
      The snake\u2019s goal is simple, \u201cKill this mouse so he cannot find the location of my nest.\u201d
      \n
      Winning a Conflict
      If you reduce your opponent\u2019s disposition to zero, you win the conflict. You achieve your goal. Lieam wins the conflict against the snake. He reduces the GM\u2019s disposition to 0 while his disposition is 1. He accomplishes his goal; he kills the snake. However, if you lost points from your own disposition, you have to compromise with the loser. See the Compromise heading.
      \n
      Losing a Conflict
      If your disposition is reduced to zero while your opponent has points left, you have lost. You do not accomplish your goal as stated. The snake does not kill Lieam or drive him away from its nest.
      \n
      Compromise
      If you lost, but took points off your opponent\u2019s disposition, he must offer you a compromise. Compromises are like twists, but in this case they are the result of the whole conflict, not just a failed roll. And players also get to introduce compromises, not just the GM. Once the conflict has ended, the first thing you do is compromise. The loser makes an offer to the winner, \u201cFine, you win, but\u2026\u201d The degree of the compromise should be appropriate to how much disposition the winner lost.The results of the conflict are set\u2014the winner achieved his goal, the loser did not. Now discuss an appropriate middle ground for compromise.
      \nI don't think Stake Setting is problematic in itself.

      The problem is that Stakes became popular and was then added to games that didn't have or need it... like Dogs.

      It's like any sort of hack. You have to be careful how your additions affect the rest of the game.

      Although Stakes became so popular, people assumed games like Dogs already had them. Most games I've played with experienced Dogs players usually have them try to set 2 sided Stakes with some level of pre-play. I then show them in the rules where that isn't how it works and almost every time they are completely surprised!

      But in Mouse Guard Stakes works great because it works with the design itself. We may pre-play the result slightly but the conflict itself then tell us how much of that result happens so there is a reason to play out the conflict in detail.
