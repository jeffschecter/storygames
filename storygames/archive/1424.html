Spun from Dave Younce's Abulafia thread

So I've scrapped a first draft of the pilgrimage game and plan on hitting the drawing board again soon. Chris Chinn suggested that a lesson-scene be framed not by a student who wants to learn a particular lesson. Instead, it is framed by a student asking a challenging question that test the teacher's wisdom. This is very similar to the plot-oriented "I wonder if..." questions in Meg Baker's 1001 Nights. For example:
"When is it right to use force?"
"Why should you serve others before yourself?"
"Why should you serve yourself before others?"
"How can you show compassion to your enemy?"
"How can you show disapproval to your friend?"
"Why is honor more important than duty?"
"Why is duty more important than honor?"
"To what point should you remain loyal to your family?"
"Why should you be modest in the face of pride?"
Dave's compiling a bunch of different questions for a new Abulafia generator. Since so many of our games are centered around singular questions (frex, Dogs in the Vineyard: "Do you shoot or give up the gun?") perhaps we can collectively come up with some thought-provoking questions for the generator as well?

    Got a link to a draft? Might help with responses.

    Another option is turning those questions into statements that a character believes, then putting that character in a situation that might call it into doubt. So for: "When is it right to use force?", you have a character who believes "it's always right to use force". OK, what about now, in this situation? Burning Wheel obviously uses this with its beliefs, and Covenant uses a similar approach.

    If it's a game about students, there are all sorts of youthful ideals that could be examined in this way... You could frame based on a another character who shares a students ideals... I have no idea if this is relevant or not.
