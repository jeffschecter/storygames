    \n Okay, I spent about an hour with these guys and really got into their products and services, here's a skinny:

    \u2022 The cards look great. Totally professional. You wouldn't think twice if you opened up a commercially available game from toys r us and these were in it. Their killer app right now. This is also the tech they have sunk the most capital into.

    \u2022 The boards for boardgames are essentially very large versions of the above described cards. That is to say, they look great, gorgeous color, nice surface feel, but ultimately flimsy. They want the capability to print on chip board as much as we all do, but that's a cost benefit thing for them. Being a young company, they don't have the money to sink into that arena yet. As more people buy boards from them, the more they will have and the more demand they will see.

    THESE BOARDS ARE PERFECT FOR PROTOTYPING, or for use in beta/gamma playtests. Go ahead, give them a little money. Maybe that kind of board is perfect for your tile-based game!

    \u2022 The pawns and play pieces were really nice, recycled wood plastic stuff. Felt nice in the hand, but they only offer what they've got on the site, not custom stuff. Good if you can use it.

    \u2022 The play money was just BW xeroxed printer paper. Totally the least impressive thing they have.

    Yeah, Chits would be nice, as would the texturing process that fantasy flight or games workshop uses on their boards. I imagine once they get the capability to print harder stock boards they will start making chits. It's a process.

    Also, the guys were very friendly and really knew what they were talking about, go ahead and email them if you have questions. They know games, not just printing, and were very upfront about their capabilities and limitations.

    \n Okay I'll try this again - I got timed out before.
    \n
    \nI looked at the site and can tell you some of what the results will be because I used the techniques myself.
    \n
    \nPrinting: They are using dry ink. The problem is that this will rub off with use unless it is sealed with some kind of varnish. Also dry ink is mat - not shinny.
    \n
    \nBoards: Card stock boards are flimsy but if people are kind to their games this doesn't have to be a fatal flaw. I've got a game from the early 80's made this way that is still fine and playable.
    \n
    \nBoxes: Looks like corrogated cardboard - like a pizza box. This isn't good. The box sells the game and this won't sell. Board game consumers like pretty and shinny and are very demanding this way.
    \n
    \nFixes: If you want to you can paint on a single coat of shellac to seal the dry ink. Shellac is alcohol based so it isn't too toxic. It's work but should fix the cards and boards. I can't think of a fix for the boxes. They are good for mail order but not direct sales.
    \n
    \nDisclaimer: I don't know these guys and am not saying that they are making a bad product. I use very similar techniques myself. Also their prices look good. It is similar to what my games cost out at from my workshop. I get more shinny glitz because I put in the sweat equity. They certainly have a professional web page and I'm certain they can produce what they say. I'd say they are worth give a try to.

      But again I ask, why do I bear that fulfillment burden if I charge a profit when someone who doesn't, doesn't? ASHCAN! heheh), or moving 100 a day at $20 profit per unit.

      Lastly, take the "single token at cost" case to the other extreme: I design a game that need some 300 elements, 200 cards, several boards, etc: a HUGE economy of scale, in terms of their die cutting setup times, job prep, whatever they have to do to make limited number of machines do a variety of jobs. This ginormous game costs me (say) $50 to make, but their opportunity cost (time lost while presses aren't running between setups) is a fraction of what it would be for (say) a $10 cost game.       \n If they are the publisher or not is rather just an issue of semantics.
      \n
      \nLets say they are the publisher. The publisher of what? That game? Exclusively? Not unless you grant them an exclusive production license. Otherwise they are merely the publisher of THAT VERSION of the game. Which is not hard to make an arguement for.
      \n
      \nWe print some RPGs and card games and resell them through our B&M store and online store, doing basically the same deal, production and retail. Its effectively the same exact thing, though they do mail order and we do mostly B&M through our local store. We don't lay any claim towards being the publisher, however. Just the printer, and if we also sell the game through our store, then we are also the retailer. But I want no claim as the publisher...it can bring no real extra value, can introduce additional liabilities, and disenfranchises the designer/publisher from being more involved with us in partnership to help sell the product. Its all basically the same thing, perhaps just looked at from different angles.
      \n
      \nAs for why they might be charging 50% of the profit when they sell the title and just production costs (marked up to include their own profit margin) when selling to you directly, that also makes sense. They are taking a cut for being the retailer. There ARE costs involved for engaging in that extra step, both real and possibily opportunity costs. For our B&M store, its both, as there is a cost to our square footage to have items on display, a labor cost to populate the item into our website for sale and an opportunity cost in that if we market and sell that item, we've put effort into doing that whereas we could have marketed and sold some other item instead. Profit motivates.
