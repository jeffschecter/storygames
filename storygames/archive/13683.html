\n I will be at PAX East this year. The attendees are young, energetic, and hungry for new games. Most games seemed organized on the spot, just grab one of many open tables, scream out what game you are about to play, and go!

I am planning to run Mouse Guard and Dogs in the Vineyard non-stop till I drop. But 4 hours is a tough sell for random pickup games.

How do I make Dogs in the Vineyard work in 2 hours?

Mouse Guard is no problem. I've refined my Dogs games so I can get epic sessions in 4 hours. But 2? Help!

    \n How about even more heresy?

    Make a very small Town, relationship-map style, that places the players at each others' throats. Kind of start in mid-action, perhaps, with, say, a group of townspeople about to execute one Dog's sister under the leadership of a second Dog.

    It'd be tricky to make it work right, especially with strangers, but the benefit would be that the players would be more engaged with gameplay. A lot of Dogs conflicts mean the other players are sitting out, waiting for their scene. By putting Dogs on both sides of conflicts, you have twice as much player involvement in any given scene.

    And definitely skip initiations, unless you can tie them really, really, really tightly into the Town. (E.g. the Dogs aren't traveling together, but arrive in the Town separately. Each "initiation" is the opening scene for that character with a major Town NPC, as they enter the Town. In scene two, they all meet up in the Town square, ready to make a plan of action.) (Oh, and maybe make those initiation scenes action scenes, like a murderer fleeing from the scene of the crime, so the second scene is already imbued with a sense of urgency.)

    \n@jason:

    agreed, if initiation scenes couldn't be integrated in a way that also reveals the town in play, I would consider not using them (although I do love them).

    @mease19 @MatrixGamer @Paul T:

    Based on all your feedback, what if...

    I announce the Town's problem... why you are all here.

    I hand out pre-gens with Names, Backgrounds, and Gear pre-selected.

    Then I put out 2 shared sheets of paper. 1 with a list of Traits that tie into the Town. A second with a list of all the Town's major NPCs and Sins.

    Players choose their Traits and Relationships from the above. Each player must share at least 2 Relationships. I encourage them to leave some Trait and Relationship dice unassigned to define in play.

    Start playing!

    Taking inspiration from Initiation Scenes, every player has an individual solo scene. The idea is you all know each other but traveled from different locations so you each get 1 scene in the Town by yourself. The scene must include an NPC you have a relationship with. And I will push for a conflict to showcase how the basic rules work.

    After every solo scene in town is done, you are all together.

    Additionally, I will really boil down the town to its essence and minimize distractions where possible. Try to get the judging as quickly as possible without losing sight of the empathizing (which the fist solo scenes can help with).

    I have no idea if this will work! But I would give it a try!

    \n John, another idea:

    Run the game exactly as you would for a 4-hour slot.

    After an hour and forty minutes, announce that there's twenty minutes left.

    I suspect your players will protest and you'll wind up with a session that's close to 3 hours, at least.

    If they don't protest, then take a 3-minute pause and work all the remaining issues and plot points into a final 1 or 2 conflicts. Yeah, it'll feel contrived, but at least you had 1:40 of all the awesomeness you already know you can bring, and if the players really are in a hurry, they won't mind some contrivance.

    \n The most time-consuming part of a game of Dogs, in my mind, is the bit where the Dogs are going around town, trying to find out as much information as they can. Of course, as GM you're throwing information at them everywhere they go, and trying to get them in the know as quick as possible, but I find players still like to talk to everyone and get a sense of the different sides in a troubled town. It is important too, for developing sympathy for the townsfolk, making it feel real, and for bringing out some of the more subtle interactions between the Dogs. But it's also time consuming, and less interesting in a convention context, I think. I often find myself flat out saying "all the cards are on the table" to prompt things to move towards resolution.

    I wonder if you could jump-start the game by having a reliable source give the characters the low-down on the town right at the start? Maybe one of the Dogs gets a letter from a sibling telling them all about what's going on? And you can explicitly tell the players "everything in this letter is 100% true, I am not jerking you around".

    You'd lose some subtlety, and I think folks would still want to chat to people a bit, but it might cut down on the long conversations that drag out the game, and move straight to the hard choices and judgement that the game is about. Maybe?

    Posted By: Simon CTo play devil's advocate for a moment, would the choices be as hard without getting to know the townsfolk naturally?
    \nProbably not! But you've gotta sacrifice something to fit a whole town into 2 hours.

    The solution here is exactly what we're discussing:

    Get the players to own the problems in the Town but enlisting them in the NPC/Town creation process. A few simple, leading questions, a strong simple setup (Hate & Murder, that's the ticket), and a few options for the players to choose for the NPCs will get them riled up in no time.

    In my Lady Blackbird hack, I list all the NPCs and ask questions like, "Which one of these is rumoured to be a cannibal? Which one is incredibly good-looking?" It gets them involved in the NPCs right away, imagining stuff about them. For a Dogs town, it could be a list of the main NPCs, and then, "Brother Jebediah, you've heard terrible stories about one of these people's unlawful sexual behaviour. Who is it, and what did you hear?"

    That allows you to use your preset relationship map, just putting a twist on the rumours. (Say the player thinks Sister Josie is sleeping with the Steward. In the first scene, he finds out that, no, she's actually sleeping with the Doctor. Or the Steward is having an affair, etc.) It gives the players something to start on (e.g. accusing Sister Josie, who, remember, is one of the Dogs' sister/mother/daughter/girlfriend) but also still leaves them something to discover.
