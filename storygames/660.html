Player Skills -- Brainstorm

Josh Roby
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Play Advice
So on Musings and Mental Meanderings, Thomas and I were talking about the different "player skills" that people use in various roleplaying games. Much like GM tasks, these can be present or absent or differently-distributed from one game to another. I think it would be interesting to start trying to identify the different skills.

This is a brainstorming thread to do just that. So.

Stakes-Setting
Scene Framing
Investing Characters in the Situation
Attaching Characters to other things (other PCs, NPCs, organizations, etc)
Describing Action
Speaking in Character
Thinking in Character
Creating Characters with Proactive Motivations
Buying Character Abilities (Traits, Powers, Stats, Whatever)
Spending XP
Provoking Other Players (hitting flags)
Spending Character Resources Strategically
Spending Character Resources Unstrategically (because it's in character)

Yes, they will overlap. Some of them will need explanation. I don't care. Throw more on the pile, ask clarification if you need.

    Okay, here's an attempt at unpacking HARDCORE, some of which may overlap with previous skills.

    - Noticing moments of potential conflict or transition in the fiction, even when they're not obvious.
    - Realizing when no one else is taking advantage of the opportunity to be HARDCORE and stepping in. (Conversely, realizing when people have been HARDCORE too often recently and letting the moment pass.)
    - Being unafraid to take narrative risks that alter the state of the fiction in important ways as appropriate to the scope of the game or conflict.
    - Knowing what actions or events will make your notions concrete.
    - Developing those actions or events within the limits of your agency in that particular game.
    - Executing those actions or events in a dramatic fashion.
    - Being able to extrapolate from the current state of the fiction to a future state.
    - Setting up future conflict or difficulty for yourself in that future state.
    - Understanding what the rest of the group will find cool rather than grandstanding.

    This has actually been really useful to articulate, because I never realized how responsive and context-dependent HARDCORE is. Causing Mount Etna to erupt by accident can be HARDCORE. So can getting back together with your ex-girlfriend. (Both are examples from real play that were acclaimed as HARDCORE!) It does tend toward the dramatic-edging-into-melodramatic; calling for HARDCORE is like saying, "Let's go over the top . . . all together!" But HARDCORE is at its best when it's a response to things that are happening in the fiction rather than to previous plans. HARDCORE is a willingness to go wherever the story is going . . . except more so, and in a way that surprises and delights the other players.

    Tony, a manifesto just may be coming.

    --Jess
