\n Here's a catch-all topic talking about finances, taxes, and legal mumbo-jumbo. But until that snowball gets a-rollin', I have some questions:

1) When selling your own games - RPGs, card games, what have you - as an indie designer, are you required to get a license to become a company to do so? "My name is Ryan, but before I can sell anything, I need to apply to the state to officially recognize my company, Cool Dude Games, as a thing, before I can sell stuff under that banner."

2) How do taxes work for you? Do you not count your game profits at all? What can you deduct, such as material costs, travel expenses to and from cons, and advertising?

3) Where do you get your stuff printed? Any cool local places near you? What arrangements and discounts have you made? What tricks have you learned?

4) For those of you who have successfully used Kickstarter to fund things, are you happy you did? Did it impact your taxes at all? What do you wish you had done differently?

    \n From my own XP:

    1) Yup. Typically you need at least a state business license and may need a local (municipal) one as well. For example, to sell stuff here, I'd have to have a Seattle business license. In all honesty, some of the best people to talk to about this are your bankers. Walk in, tell them you want to set up a small business account, and then start asking questions about what else you need to do. If they're good, they'll put you on the right track.

    2) I last did this a while ago, but doing taxes for your small business can be rough. Luckily, it's easier if you run all your expenses and income through a seperate business bank account. As I recall, most things related to your game can be deducted as costs, including going to conventions and buying other games for "research," but it might be worth your money to hire a tax professional to help you out, especially if you end up making significant income.

    3) Cool local printers are great if you can find one and develop a good relationship. Vincent does all his printing locally, for example. Most other folks shop around and compare quotes on the internet, traditionally, or get word-of-mouth recommendations from other indie game publishers. I know several awesome local places but they may not be right for you. There are so many tricks involved in printing; that's a whole thread in and of itself.

    \n You can run yourself as a personal business (not as a company) without filing a thing. Then when you do your taxes you fill out Schedule C (profit or loss from business). Schedule C is basically a subtraction of allowed business expenses from revenues to determine how much of your business income is taxable profit. Or you can run your business as a hobby and declare hobby income instead of doing Schedule C. The downside is that hobby income expenses aren't subtracted from your hobby revenues. They're combined with all your other tax deductions. So you have to itemize your deductions to get credit for them. And your hobby income is combined with your job income. So if your expenses are small they may not exceed the standard deduction and you end up paying taxes on your whole hobby income, rather than a subset of it.

    \n Yeah, and if you use Turbo Tax and pay up for the $100 business version, it will walk you through Schedule C easy peesy...and make sure you're deducting the right amount, not all expenses are 100% deductible.
    \n
    \nWhat you will really want to investigate however are your state standards for sales tax. That is usually a separate filing from your state income tax. Its basically the sales tax you're supposed to be collecting when you sell stuff...but even if you don't collect it...you gotta pay the state what you should have collected. Some states let you get away with not paying sales tax for out of state internet sales, but more and more states want paid for them too. Failure to pay those results in getting your license to do business revoked when they catch up to you.

    \n The IRS doesn't mind at all if you under-report your business expenses. Don't want the hassle of tracking and claiming mileage on your vehicle when you use it to drive to conventions? Then don't bother. Seems like a hassle to expense a home office when you use your main computer and internet connectivity more for video gaming than for your indie RPG design and publishing business? Then don't bother claiming a home office expense. Just claim the easy stuff. Just claim the stuff you can track with a folder of email receipts and an envelope of physical receipts. Postage expenses and mailing supplies. Receipts for meals only when you're at a trade show or convention. Web hosting fees. You still come out ahead by offsetting your sales revenues with just these easy expenses.

    \n Keep in mind also that if you engage in retail sales to the public (as opposed to strictly wholesale to retailers and/or distributors) you will need a Sales Tax lisence for your state. You will also need to register to collect and remit Sales Tax to any other state you do business in (as at a convention). Just about everywhere makes them easy to apply for online and offers a web site for payments.

    For mail order sales, you charge for in-state sales, but customers are responsible for paying Use Tax on inter-state sales.

    It is very much worth paying an Accountant for some consulting time to make sure you're in compliance with your state's various guidelines for small business.

    \n 1. Depends on the state. If you're a sole-proprietor in MN, you can just do a DBA (Doing Business As). You don't need a specific license to be an indie publisher. But, you should check with your state and local government. Sometimes, it's a PITA. For example, one of the local governments here requires a license that requires a full background check if you're not a property owner and you want to rent a place in town to open a business that sells something. If you set up an LLC, which is probably overkill unless you think you're going to get big (then do it at the start), you'll need to jump through a couple more hoops. Setting up a bank account also requires additional paperwork if you haven't done one since 9/11, so if you set up an LLC make sure you have everything that's required.

    2. A great online accounting program that automatically pulls in PayPal: http://outright.com/. You need to claim all your income, but you can deduct all your expenses including mileage on your car (or actual expenses but mileage is much easier) and food. You also need to track starting and ending inventories, etc... If you just do POD with LULU, then it's easier because you have no inventory. Accounting isn't all that hard and something like Outright can get you prepared for tax time. If you're making a ton of money, you also need to pay estimated quarterly taxes or face a penalty. I screwed up last year and owed $2000+ in under payment. I paid a penalty. It sucked.

    4. I haven't done a Kickstarter yet, but I've had three friends fund expeditions or aspects of expeditions with Kickstarter. You need to pay taxes on the money just as you would anything else.

    \n Yes, the Kickstarter money is business revenue. Now if you have expenses to offset that you can avoid actually paying a tax on it, but only if you time your expenses and your revenue for the same tax year. Otherwise you'll be paying the tax in one year and then waiting for a refund until the next which may create a cash flow crunch.
    \n
    \nIf you do a Kickstarter and have any kind of big windfall success at it, I would heartily recommend hiring an accountant, because there are creative business things you can do to help rectify that situation if you know what you're doing. Unfortunately many of those methods are not available to sole proprietors operating on a cash accounting basis, so...if you are going to start making Kickstarter a regular thing and you actually think you might be in a position to pull in real money with it...upgrading to an actual S-Corp or LLC (IIRC Adept Press is an S-Corp) can open up alot of tax accounting options.

    \n I also run a non-profit and I would be reluctant to choose that option for a publishing company. Not against it but reluctant.

    There are many restrictions on what you can sell, how, and how much, lots of paperwork, and setting up and dissolving can be a long process.

    There are also benefits, certain tax exemptions with specific restrictions, and you can get discounts on services (sometimes as much as 10-20%).

    That said, the details vary state by state. If you want to start a non-profit, you will still need to speak to an attorney and/or an accountant for details pertinent to your specific situation and location.

    As a social club, non-profits are amazing and I highly recommend them.

    \n You'd want to consult a tax accountant, but there is a concept in the U.S. Tax Code called the Annual Exclusion Amount which is the amount of money that can be freely gifted from 1 person to another 1 person each year without being considered a taxable event or needing to be tracked against the Lifetime Gift Tax Exclusion.
    \n
    \nI believe the annual amount is $13,000-ish these days (it used to be $10K but has since been indexed) and is often used for parents and grandparents to transfer sizeable funds to their descendants either as "start up money" for the next generation or to transfer wealth to avoid estate tax.
    \n
    \nBut its also the part of the law that means you don't have to declare your birthday presents or every time a friend buys you a drink as taxable income.
    \n
    \nUnless there's an exception that prevents you from using the Annual Exclusion amount to apply to solicited donations I'd say you're probably covered, unless the total donations you received from a single person over the course of a year was REALLY big.
    \n
    \nIt makes me wonder if Kickstarter might be even more effective when used by an Individual rather than a business. I don't think business entities get to use the annual exclusion amount, so I wonder if a tax accountant would characterize those backer donations as "gifts" meaning each individual one could be excluded for no tax due.
    \n
    \n...There's gotta be a catch there somewhere...I'd be surprised if the IRS would leave that open for long.
    \n
    \nSo verdict being...definitely worth investigating with an actual tax expert.
