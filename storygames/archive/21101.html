\nI favor playing to find out what happens the most. The other two are still important and I vacillate back and forth on what it most important to me in the moment.
\nThis, along with my earlier comment about combinatorics and RISS, along with @Paul_T 's very well-reasoned points about the need for a truly unifying principle, made a couple of things just click for me\u2014my own "mirror moment" if you will. :-)

Here's how I see RISS. (To an extent, I've kind of always viewed GNS this way, because of how Ben Lehman originally explained it to me, but it was very heretical there, whereas I suspect @2097 will like this.)

You do have an organizing principle, the true goal of play or dare I say, Creative Agenda. This will be one of the four corners. You then can have some, all, or none of the other corners as well. But because there is always a hierarchy among the corners, you end up using the nPr formula, rather than nCr, so this leads to a whole lot of options:

Single-corner: 4 possibilities, clearly.

Two-corner: 12 possibilities (4P2). RI, RS1, RS2, IR, IS1, IS2, S1R, S1I, S1S2, S2R, S2I, S2S1. Here, the first one listed has primacy, but the game's design includes elements of the second thing as well. In the instance of a clash, the second thing will always "lose." So this is why, e.g., both RI and IR are included.

Three-corner: 24 possibilities.

Four-corner: also 24.

Total: 64. Much, much less constraining than a 3- or 4-fold model. Now, for my money, the 24 four-corner options are basically White Whales that we might chase but really aren't possible to achieve. As Sandra pointed out, Burning Wheel, one of the most technically and creatively ambitious games of all time, really only hits three of the corners. But that still leaves 40 options!

Sandra herself has alluded to this schema, by the way\u2014how do we resolve Hatred Parade clashes? By choosing which corner has primacy in a particular game. (It might not be at the design level, even, but rather for a particular outing the group deciding how to interpret a particular design.)

Now, I don't know that I can find examples for all 40 combinatorics options, but in subsequent posts I'll try to throw up the ones I can easily think of.

    \n Jeepform games are IR. There is a pre-set list of scenes, and we improvise reactions within that structure. If the outcomes of, e.g., the character relationships are pre-determined as well, then you have RI. "The Mothers" is like this: the game only ends once a particular emotional trap has been sprung.

    BW is IS2S1. Fundamentally, what we care about are the Beliefs. (Not the Instincts, by the way: you keep mixing those, Sandra! ) This is the character motivation stuff. But to work on those motivations in a reasonable way, you need good system mastery--S2. Finally, the way obstacles are set, the skill list, the lifepaths, and so on, all emphasize strict adherence to the verisimilitude of the setting.

    By the way, I think it's the fact that BW is a 3-corner design is what makes it so hard to really grok for so many players / groups. It's not that it's mathematically so much more complicated than, say, Exalted. It's that it's thematically complex and requires multi-level engagement.

    \n Interesting! Matt, for what it's worth, I suspect my personal interpretation of GNS/Big Model/Creative Agenda is/was similar to yours (and not entirely canonical).

    How do we distinguish (or do we?) between psychological motivations (the "why" of play) and the techniques of play?

    For instance, something like this...\n
    2097 said:

    That all represents games where In is bossing Rg around but made three different concessions to Rg.

    \n...seems to suggest that it's all about techniques. (In this view, a pre-scripted beginning is a "concession" to a different priority, because it uses a "foreign" technique.)

    Whereas a more Forgean view (where creative agenda is king) sees no contradiction or "concession" happening whatsoever; instead, the "pre-scripted" elements in, say, Montsegur serve to enhance the drama and are necessary for it, far from being concessions to a different or competing agenda.

    In fact, under RISS, Montsegur could even be seen as a purely Rails game (and... probably should be). Otherwise, how do you distinguish it from a railroady campaign game which is all about deciding how your characters reacts to a powerfully resonant prescripted in-play event?
