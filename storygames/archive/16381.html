\n Hi, folks. I am going to be trying a game of Microscope with my regular board gaming group shortly and I'm really excited about it. It fills a niche I've been looking for for some time.
\n
\nThat said, I Know My Group and one thing that makes me very apprehensive is the mechanics for playing out a Scene. Nobody in my group has any familiarity with storygaming at all; there's a couple that I think will take to it like a duck to water, a bunch that have no real experience roleplaying in any form, and a couple that I think will have real problems with the Scene stuff, possibly game-breaking problems. They're a Type: the kind of roleplayers that are aggressively simulationist/gamist, they like the trappings of the setting/milieu but are mostly focused on identifying with their character and Winning with that character and doing whatever they think will best benefit that character.
\n
\nOne of the reasons that we don't play trad RPGs with this group is that reconciling these play styles with these people has proven problematic in the past. "Well, just don't play with those guys" is not an acceptable option; the reason we play board games is that it is a form of gaming everyone can enjoy without these issues coming up. Maintaining a good mood in the group is more important than any particular game.
\n
\nSo having said all that, I'd still kind of like to give Microscope a shot, but without the freeform Scene play. I was tossing around the following possible house rule for Scenes:
\n
\n- The current player States the Question and Sets the Stage as normal. He then begins describing the events of the scene in as much length and detail as he wishes, as long as he does not answer the question. At some point, he must stop and nominate someone else at the table. That person must then continue narrating the scene, and finish it by answering the question.
\n
\nThe rather obvious problem here is that it takes a lot of the interactivity out of scenes - hell, it takes all the roleplaying out of Scenes.
\n
\nI may just be being a big 'fraidy-cat on this issue, as well, but there are some significant divisions in the group as a result of the last time we tried to run a trad RPG, so I'm nervous.
\n
\nAny thoughts? Suggestions?

    \n You could probably do it with dictated scenes, or just go straight to voting:
    State the Question and Set the Stage (and possibly include/exclude characters?)
    Starting to the current player's left, each player dictates an Answer/outcome, ending with the current player.
    Finger vote between the proposed answers.
    Next scene.

    \n How about never doing scenes? That's an option in Microscope as well, right? As I understand the game text, you'd only be doing scenes where the subject matter and tightened sufficiently for a living curiousity to compel a scene: as long as the group is merely abstractly interested, there's no need for a scene. If a scene is happening, then presumably the reason is that there is an overwhelming curiousity towards playing it out. If it's just part of the group that is interested in doing the scene, then perhaps the rest of the group could be left out so they don't mess it up?

    Fundamentally my choice in this kind of situation would be to try it out, then stop once it becomes obvious that the group isn't on the same wavelength. Go back to boardgaming afterwards, and not spend too much energy with basically trying to play a roleplaying game while dodging the core of the game. If you can't, then you can't, and it's sad and all, but not something that can be fixed by changing the rules of the game. Meanwhile it makes sense to try different games now and again in the hopes that the people in question grow towards creative compatibility, or the right game happens along.

    \n I just realized I never followed up with this.

    We did in fact end up going with dictated scenes. This was forced by the limited amount of time we had available as much as anything else. It worked out quite well, we went back to it for two more sessions afterward because people were having so much fun.

    Some things that came up that I think were worth mentioning:

    1) A couple of people had a lot of trouble with being in the hot seat, especially since the game is adamant that you're not allowed to get help. I did the best I could to encourage people to just riff off of the existing stuff, and they got better, but there was a lot of deer-in-the-headlights going on.

    2) One person really didn't engage with the cooperative nature of the game. He clearly had his own idea of what kind of setting and events he wanted to create and carved out a section of the history where he spent all of his turns. Everyone else meddled around with each other's events and had a fine time with it, and then there was B, off writing his own little saga on the other end of the table. The Focus mechanic didn't really help with this.

    3) The resulting history was a gonzo mashup of steampunk pirates, magical 2001 obelisks, theocrats and tyrants, uplifting Garuda-bird aliens, and the overarching theme of religion vs. technology just plopped out right in the middle of it completely unbidden. It was amazing and I could take any one Period of this game and run multiple campaigns in it.

    \nAwesome.

    I think removing role-played scenes makes Microscope a very different game. The direct interaction of role-playing balances the stand-alone (and scary!) authority of history creation. Without that interaction it could be a lot easier for players to feel like they're just making stuff by themselves, like your one player did. Role-playing is also a nice break from the hot seat: you get to relax and play at your own pace, which I think is pretty critical.

    To me role-played scenes are the pay dirt that all the history making sets up. The unchoreographed things that happen in role-play spark players to go in whole new directions when they make their own history.

    Daniel, you mentioned that the Focus didn't keep your one player from making things unrelated to everyone else. I'm guessing these were very broad Focuses like war, space travel, etc? Tight Foci like "the terrorist attack on Pharsus IV" (or even tighter "Jak Dorin, the terrorist behind the bombing on Pharsus IV") are always better, I think, because they make players create stuff that clearly interrelates.
