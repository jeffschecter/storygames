\n I've finally managed to play my first game of IAWA, and had a great time, but some questions came up about the conflict system that I was hoping to get opinions on.
\n
\n1
\n
\nIf you start a conflict to try and prevent someone from doing something (e.g. you lock them into a cupboard to stop them getting out of the house) and they take the default consequence of Injure or Exhaust, do you still stop them from doing whatever they wanted to do? In other words do you get your intended action in addition to the consequence?
\n
\n2
\n
\nIs there a limit to the number of people you can force to answer your roll in a conflict with multiple people? We had someone object when a monster attacked three people at once in a conflict, injuring all three of them. The rules certainly seem like you can ask as many people to answer as you like.
\n
\n3
\n
\nHow should you handle a conflict where someone comes in halfway through, e.g. someone doesn't get involved to start with, but then jumps in on the second round. Do they just participate for the 2 remaining rounds? Does an advantage dice gained in the first round still count against them, even though it was gained against a different person? We assumed it did, an advantage dice was an advantage dice.

    \n I'm just this dude who plays a lot of wicked. Someone else might come along and contradict me and be right.

    1) Winning a conflict doesn't give you the right to prevent anyone from doing anything, so you shouldn't start one with the idea that you're going to. Rather, you can either get the other person to agree to not doing that thing during post-conflict negotiation, or you can exhaust / injure them in order to make the next conflict when you stop them easier.

    Basically, conflict is like hitting someone with a stick. You don't hit someone with a stick and then they instantly stop doing whatever they wanted to do. You hit them with a stick, they go "ow!" and then say "stop what you're doing or I'll give you another one."

    2) As long as you can justify it in the fiction, no. So if my demon of pain goes "I screech with the horror of a thousand undiscovered hells, and all of your heads explode" that's something everyone who can hear it has to answer.

    How did you end up with a monster in Wicked, btw? I thought that all it had was animals, demons, gods, and people?

    3) I don't believe someone can jump into a conflict midway (though this one I'm uncertain about). You're better off just rolling for anyone who might be involved. They can always bail if they don't have anything to do. Something I found useful as a GM was to get everyone at the table into conflict at the same time. Often it would start in neat little pairs, but rapidly people found ways to cross over.

    \n That's something the group has to decide on. Everyone does it differently, and there's no one right way that's best.

    What we do: Cross off the top name and add it to the bottom of the list. Keep doing that if you need to, until you get to the name of a character who is going to be in the next chapter.

    When you cross off a name: You cross of the name of a character you own. What ownership means depends on what your group thinks, but for us, it usually means "a character you have put on the owe list."

    \n Don't forget that winning the initiative is an advantage, especially if you're rolling against two or more other people.

    So we have me and J vs the Lone Wolf, and we're all rolling equal dice.
    There's a 1 in 3 that the Lone Wolf will win initiative, 2 in 3 that one of J or I will.
    If the Lone Wolf wins initiative, her die stands. It's likely to be a high die. J and I have two shots to beat it.
    If one of J or I wins initiative, our die stands. It's likely to be a high die. The Lone Wolf has one shot to beat it.

    Like John says, it never adds up to a sure thing. It's perfectly possible to roll five 2-on-1 fights in a row and have the Lone Wolf win each of them, just by chance. But over the lifetime of the game in publication, J and I will beat the Lone Wolf more often than she'll beat us.
