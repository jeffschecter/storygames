    Every established factof the setting creates certain opportunities for play, but closes off other ones. If we establish that all dwarves are male (a simple biological fact), then we have closed off the opportunity for playing female dwarves, but at the same time raised some other issues of dwarf gender and how they relate to other races. The simple statement "this is a fantasy world" limits a player's ability to introduce science fiction elements into play, or vice versa.

    The setting is there as fuel for ideas and play. Some GMs and players need or like a lot of detail, so they have a lot of potential situations, conflicts and characters. But having a detailed setting is also restricting. Some prefer fewer restrictions and more potential to define things within a loose framework.

    I think these are simply different approaches to gaming, and neither "detailed setting are good" nor "detailed settings are bad" can be taken as objective truth. They're style things that sit with some people better than others.

    First: Wow, and thanks.

    I'm a bit staggered that THE John Kim is taking the time to tell me where I'm wrong -- I rather feel as if I'm about to graduate from something and am now defending my thesis before a senior professor. And fortunately, unlike in an actual thesis defense, I'm free to keep restating my thesis until I make it clear enough that people get it.

    So:

    "Detail = bad?" No. Of course not. And I never said that, although I can see why reasonable people got that impression.

    Likewise, "good setting and character background info...get in the way of fun gaming"? No, and, again, actually, that's not quite what I said: I was talking about "too much" of a good thing -- although "too much" is vague to the point of meaninglessness, so I'll try to make that clearer in this post.

    But "the kind of setting information I ...gives plentiful flavor without significantly limiting ...options" -- there, I think, Jack Aidley's nailed it.

    Specific example time:

    I strongly, strongly recommend that everyone click over to this Forge thread (http://www.indie-rpgs.com/forum/index.php?topic=12424.0) where someone is absolutely struggling with the fact that he and his group want to start a game, because they love -- but they wanted to do things that aren't compatible with canon, and they were stuck. And it's not the crappy "extended universe" stuff that various licensees added to the setting that was the problem, either. It was the movies, the very thing they loved for in the first place ("too much of a good thing," remember). They wanted to play powerful characters who changed the fate of the Galaxy -- but Lucas's characters in the original movies : How could they be the heroes without contradicting canon?

    So they were faced with a dilemma between dialing down their characters to be lesser heroes -- in other words, de-protagonizing before play even -- or setting their adventures in some time or place so far removed from the original films that there was no possibility of interaction -- in other words, abandoning the heart of the setting, the very things they loved about it, and marginalizing themselves.

    What I (and others) suggested: . Take out the parts that get in the way -- the specific identities and actions of established characters -- and put in what you want: make the characters that invent be the heroes who blow up the Death Star(s), have a father / mother / mentor who's gone to the Dark Side, struggle to build the aggressive power to overthrow the Emperor without becoming as corrupted by power and aggression as he is.

    I don't know if that group ever followed this advice or not. But think about it: The very coolness that attracted them to the setting in the first place got in the way of making it their own and creating their coolness.

    Now - extended metaphor time:

    Imagine that, instead of roleplaying, we're building Tinkertoys. (Or Legos, or Erector Sets; whatever, as long as it's got physical structure and can be extended). Imagine, moreover, that while we just build our own structures from stratch out of raw, unassembled components, we also have hundreds and hundreds of other peoples' pre-existing Tinkertoys to play with -- to play with AND MODIFY, clicking in new pieces and taking out existing elements to get the design that's ideal for us personally.

    (Bear with me, people).

    Now, some of these existing structures are huge: They have lots and lots of materials. (Thick setting sourcebooks, complicated character generation, whatever). Some of them are tiny, with only a few pieces. Is either inherently easier to play with? Not -- I mean, the little tiny Tinkertoy might be less intimidating for me to mess with and add my own stuff to than something enormous, but then again I might be more worried about messing up something so little by making the slightest change.

    So SIZE isn't the primary issue. It matters, but other things matter more. Imagine two Tinkertoys that each use the exact same number of pieces -- heck, even the exact same kinds of pieces -- but that are radically different SHAPES: One is a sprawling spiderlike structure with lots of long, thin arms sticking out; and the other is a dense three-dimensional grid, like the framework of an office building.

    NOW: Which is easier to modify for your own creative purposes, either by adding new elements or subtracting old ones -- the closed, dense grid, or the open, spidery structure?

    It's the open structure, obviously. Don't like a particular arm? You can snap it off, easy, and click something else in you do like. Want to add a new piece? Just click it onto one of the existing arms -- there's plenty of places to attach it, and even if what you add on is pretty big, there's plenty of empty space between the arms for it to fit into. What's more, because the structure's got a lot of sprawling arms already, anything sprawling you add on will look like it fits with the rest, rather than as if it's tacked on.

    By contrast, anything you add onto the grid will look weird if it's not a simple extension of the gridwork, or alternatively a little tiny piece that fits into the limited empty area inside the grid. If you want to do something of a radically different shape, you need to take half the grid apart to make room.

    The problem isn't really the AMOUNT of existing material: It's the DENSITY.

    An old-school D&D dungeon module is, literally, a closed, high-density gridwork: There is nowhere the protagonists can do that is not already defined -- mapped out, stocked with specific inhabitants (monsters) and objects (treasure), and described in the "box text." There is you can do creatively to that closed, high-density object without ripping out an existing piece first.

    But once you get out of the dungeon, in the ancient days before "Forgotten Realms" was published, you were in open, low-density, with a few strong struts to latch onto: You're in some sort of fantastical sort-of-medieval kingdom with monsters, magic, elves, and dwarves, and there's a town nearby where you can buy a set list of stuff, and that's IT. Want to invent a character, a city, a kingdom? Sure: Just grab a hex or ten on the blank map and start drawing.

    Now, compare the setting (pre-1983, before the Extended Universe clogged it with crap). Looks pretty wide open, right? Millions of planets, millennia of history,plenty of blank canvas -- except the part we all cared about first, the pivotal events around the fall of the Empire, is filled in, and the rest is damned sketchy. So we've got an incredibly dense core, and then a bunch of struts sticking on that are, frankly, too weak to build much onto as-is.

    Now, compare the REAL WORLD. At first glance, this seems massively dense: There is literally nowhere you can go that doesn't have something there. Question is,

    I looked over John Kim's "Sillicon Valley Vampire Slayer" Buffy campaign, and while it refers to plenty of real places I could find online -- Redwood City, Big Sur, Sand Hill Road in Menlo Park, even a Buddhist temple in Mountain View (actually, there're several) -- it puts businesses and people in those places without bothering to check who actually was at such-and-such an address -- I don't think you all had a Buddhist temple in Mountain View in mind, and if you did, I presume you did not actually discover they were involved in nefarious supernatural activities?

    My reading of John Kim and his group is that they used "the real world" as a vast toolkit of evocative places and institutions, but not as "canon" to abide by without room for contradiction or invention. That means the "Sillicon Valley" of the game, even setting aside the supernatural elements, is as much John & co.'s own invention as Narnia is C.S. Lewis's, or Middle-Earth is Tolkein's -- and before anyone faints, remember how much both those authors loved the real-world landscape of rural England, loathed the wastelands created by modern industry and World War I, and incorporated those elements into their imagined setting.

    I've been talking about setting here, primarily, but the same principles apply to character generation: If I have a GURPS-style sheet filled with dozens of specific skills and attributes, or a freeformer-style character history filled with dozens of specific incidents, I've left myself little room to define what that character is about without either (a) ripping out something I already have or (b) creating something only tangentially connected.

    Whereas if I have a -style character sheet, I know a bunch of important things about my character in really vivid detail -- "I can't shoot worth a darn 2d4," "I have prophetic dreams 1d10" -- but there's plenty of empty space in between and all around to build off into. Same with the setting: There are a few evocative broad strokes -- the long arms of the spidery structure -- and lots of cool Western details about guns, coats, the Faith, what's in a typical town -- the little points of attachment where you click new parts in -- but there's plenty of room all around.

    The problem with lots of details is not having a lot of detail: It's that the more detail you put into your setting, or your character, or your other preparation before play, the harder it is to avoid filling in all the blank places for further creation. You create richly detailed settings or elaborately detailed characters with lots of room for further creativity, but you have to be damned deliberate about the structure as you do it and make sure you keep building struts and points-of-attachment outwards rather than closing in and filling-in. (Remember basic solid geometry: As an object increases in volume, its ratio of surface area to volume -- its potential openness to the outside -- , unless you deliberately change its shape).

    And to end with a manifesto moment:

    Roleplaying is a creative endeavor.

    Unlike reading books and comics, or watching movies and plays, or contemplating the Mona Lisa, it is not primarily about appreciating someone else's creativity: It is about expressing your creativity.

    Unlike writing books or plays, and a bit like making a movie, and very much like improv theater, it is not about expressing your own creativity : It is about expressing your creativity with other people so that your best ideas feed off each other to create something none of you could have imagined on your own.

    Cool settings created by others are great -- as long as the setting, and your own attitude, give you creative space to move from appreciating the creativity of others to expressing your own.

    Cool character creation and other preparation before play is great -- as long as the procedures for prep, and your own attitude, give you creative space to move from expressing your own creativity alone to expressing your creativity in synergy with other people.

    WE ARE ALL CREATIVE. Let's do something with it.

    Semipiternity said: "The *best* settings are those tinkertoy structures we can feel free to add onto to our heart's content *without* rearranging any of the parts that are already in place."

    I'd agree, emphatically. To start with, it's an extension of the classic Ron Edwards "my GM Herbie" efficiency principle from "System Does Matter," which is that while your GM or your group can indeed junk any rules or setting elements that get in the way, it'd be a lot less work if the obstacles weren't there in the first place. So the ideal structure is one that suggests and guides without constraining:
    - "ah, this is a spidery hub-and-spokes thing, so I can make the end of this spoke a new hub and radiate more spokes out of it"
    - "ah, this is a continent surrounded by ocean, so I can make some islands over here"
    - "ah, this is a mythic setting soaked in anthropology and culture clashes, so I can make a new kingdom torn between the new Lunar Way and tribal tradition over here and customize its funky magic thusly." (I've not played any Glorantha games, but I suspect that while it's a massive setting, it's actually not that dense in an obstructive way -- "Your Glorantha May Vary" is a much better mindset for a company to promote than "Buy our Metaplot!").

    Shreyas, I think you and I find the same things exciting or tedious, but "matters" / "doesn't matter" is a side point, really, and mainly a matter of personal taste. What really gets to me is when the predefined thing that matters most to someone, and which they find most exciting, completely stymies their ability to move on to create something of their own -- the problem.

    Likewise, John, I actually think whether or not people like "exploration of setting" is a tangent. At least, it's a tangent until we separate out
    (a) the people who want to "explore setting" by finding out stuff predefined by someone else, and who'd love a "dense, closed gridwork,"
    from
    (b) the people who want to "explore setting" by creating new stuff, either completely on the fly or as a logical extrapolation from some predefined element, who'd love a "low density, open spiderwork."
    from
    (c) the people who don't want to "explore setting" in either sense, but who find any big blank patches in the setting very distracting from their focus on something else, and who feel more comfortable with high-density
    from
    (d) the people who don't want to "explore setting" in either sense, but who find lots of extraneous setting detail very distracting from their focus on something else, and who feel more comfortable with low-density. (This, I think, is Shreyas).

    The bit I quoted above isn't the end-product of worldbuilding - although it could certainly be included in the marginalia, as in Nobilis. But certainly writing short passages from various PoV characters (preferrably outsiders or travellers) gives you the eyes to see the setting not only from a fresh perspective, but from a ground-level perspective. Most worldbuilding is about the god's-eye persepctive - this continent here, populated by these people, with these major national/cultural groups.

    Someone on the ground - actually in the setting, doesn't have that sort of knowledge - they take in the surroundings on the level of the details here: the five senses, custom and cultural cues. By building the world from the details up instead of the top down, I've found that it creates much more emergent behavior - more complexity, and certainly more unpredictability. There was one instance where I was working on a setting and I found out, quite without meaning to, that one of the species (I prefer 'species' over 'race') was going to be a bunch of real fuckers. I didn't start out with that in mind, but it made a lot of sense as I kept shaping the setting and since it was an elf-equivalent in the setting, it really threw off the players.
    If the setting gives me sensible information which I can visualize and understand, then I can remember it and extend from it. So "Greenhaven is a thriving town of 5000 which is in the hilly country at the upper end of the Bright River which runs through the country of Queensland." That makes sense to me. Particularly if there's a map showing the country and river, I can form a picture in my head of it and I can make up my own cool characters and places in the town during play. In contrast, suppose I read "Greenhaven is officially an tertiary district of Dellslandau, whose abnormally high production of copperbrew in recent years has troubled the District Administrator." I'll tend to simply ignore that and it's unlikely to come up in play.
    The difference between those two entries, to me, is that the one is static and the other dynamic. The first passage just ends with 'so fucking what?' Nothing in that tells me why I'd ever want to go there. The second is much more intriguing - first of all, it hints at the cultural context of the place, I know they make copperbrew (but I have no idea what it is, so that makes me curious), and I know something about the situation as well, which is dynamic and intriguing (Why is copperbrew such an issue? And who am I going to side with in the conflict?)

    The first passage is information that I'll need at some point to locate Greehaven, but I have to want to go there before I care to find (or place) it on the map. Of course those exact details may not come into play - but good setting material makes you want to work that stuff in somehow, even as background noise.
