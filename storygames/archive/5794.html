We're doing more right than we're doing wrong

\n
\n
\n
\n\nJack Aidley \n\n\n
\n
\n\nFebruary 2008 \n edited February 2008 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n There's been a whole load of discussion lately about "why the indie publishing model produces unreadable books" and other related things. How we can improve the process and where it's going wrong. And time and again, I find myself shaking my head and going "what are you on?" are the screen. Then I rend my clothes and beat my chest. Oh, yes, I do.

Why? Because Indies games are already typically clearer, better designed, better constructed and better laid out than the majority of commercial games. Recently I picked up Dark Heresy, and WHFRP, and I was reading through Dark Heresy and thinking, hey, this is really well written for a commercial - the rules suck, but it's pretty and they haven't let the layout get in the way of readability too much, but what is up with the typesetting? Then I read WFRP. Ick. What an awful, unreadable choice of font! At least it is ragged right, hey?

Then I put in an order for Primitive, InSpectres, My Life with Master and Dogs in the Vineyard.

Primitive is the worst of the four (in terms of production values and explaining itself) but, you know what, it still lays out the rules of the game in a clearer, more straight forward way than the commercial games. As for the others, they're leagues ahead of the mainstream. InSpectres and My Life with Master are a positive joy. Clear, functional layouts with plenty of wide space and a legible font in a decent size.

And that's just layout, where we're supposed to be at such a disadvantage. When you get into the rules, we're frickin' miles ahead in terms of producing a set of rules that are coherent, playable and actual work together to produce play that is fun.

    \n Jack, it depends what you compare your text and writing process to. I don't want "text that's more understandable and better edited than many mainstream RPGs" to be the standard. I don't care much whether mainstream RPGs are better or worse than indie RPGs. I measure the quality of games against the standards of other media. I'm not saying anyone else should, but I do. I want this:

    - Text that draws me in and provokes me like a brilliant short story
    - Rules presentation that's as clear and structured as the best board games
    - Play structure that's as easy for entry-level players as any video game
    - Editorial work that's as time-consuming and work intensive as writing a novel for a major publisher
