\n A few months back I played in a game of 3:16 and blogged about it. I figure The whole point of "actual play" is to share your experiences, so I figger I'll share them here too. A lot of the experience, for me, was in comparison to a game system I've been working on myself, so the session was both fun and educating.
\n
\n
\n(originally posted on February 6th, 2009):
\n
\nThis past weekend at a local GoPlay! event, I got the chance to play in a one-shot session of a nifty little game called 3:16 Carnage Amongst the Stars, which was written by Gregor Hutton. It was a fun session, and I figured I would get back into the spirit of game session logging by writing some \u201cactual play\u201d thoughts on my experiences with this system. Keep in mind that I don\u2019t own a copy of the rules, and I\u2019m writing this review from my memory and my game notes alone, so I might get some terms and rules mixed up. As such, this isn\u2019t a \u201creview\u201d as much as an introspective play report.
\n
\nAlso, while playing this game I noticed a lot of similarities, play-wise, that it shared with Cannibal Contagion. Apologies in advanced if I make frequent comparisons, but that\u2019s where my mind is, and their similarities really helped me put the game into a few perspectives, which I appreciated.
\n
\nSetup:
\n
\nAll I originally had heard about the system from various folks was that it was \u201cStarship Troopers: The Movie: The Game\u201d - meaning, space marines murdering space bug with full-on bloody space action. In space! I love space, it\u2019s awesome, and so was 3:16. Nick Smith (co-author of Classroom Deathmatch, one of my favorite games ever), assembled this session, with three total additional players (Myself, Joel, and Evan). He laid the basic premise out pretty effectively, and since said premise was simple enough to understand, we easily jumped right into character creation.
\n
\nIn 3:16, character creation is delightfully quick, and this was explained to us as being so because your character will likely die (and quickly), and when that happens you\u2019re supposed to be able to quickly jump right in with a new one. Cannibal Contagion has the exact same mentality, so it was easy for me to grasp, and I was eager to give it a shot.
\n
\nAfter jotting down some quick color basics (name, nickname, reputation), we assigned points to the two core stats. That\u2019s right, only two, and they\u2019re totally appropriate: one for killing things (called FA), the other for doing everything else (called NFA). That seemed easy enough to understand, but later I noted some potential balance issues with these stats, which I will discuss much further below. My character was named James Jimson, aka \u201cDouble Jim,\u201d and his FA and NFA were 3 and 7, respectively. I don\u2019t remember his reputation, but it was something about being the only survivor of a terrible massacre.
\n
\nOnce we wrote our stats, Nick informed us that those stats are now used to determine our initial rank. The higher your NFA, the higher your rank, and my character had the highest, netting him a rank of Sergeant (the highest you can be at creation). This allowed me to give some more commands to the others, but gave me a few more (mostly-narrative) responsibilities. I\u2019m not entirely sure if \u201cnarrative responsibility\u201d is a worthwhile or even tangible mechanic, but it wasn\u2019t a big deal in the end. I also got a cooler assortment of weapons to pick from, as well as an Evacuation pod.
\n
\nWe wrote down our weapons and health levels, and then jumped right in.

    \nActual Play:
    \n
    \nI got the impression that my own recent experiences with more cutthroat-style games of betrayal and factions affected my interpretation of this game, and it seemed that thus was true of the other players as well. We all immediately got into a \u201cshoot your allies\u201d and \u201ckill your superior officers\u201d kind of mentality in one fashion or another. I would like someday to replay this game, and take a more dedicated, less cutthroat approach to the story. That being said, the approach we took was a whole lot of fun, so don\u2019t think for one second that I\u2019m complaining!
    \n
    \nOur group consisted of three characters. Mine was a hard-but-resourceful Sgt, Evan\u2019s was a soldier known for high ratios of friendly fire, and Joel\u2019s was a do-what-it-takes \u201cjunior officer\u201d who had no respect for inept senior officers. We went forth on a series of missions to destroy these gigantic dinosaur-monsters who carried massive building-sized laser guns. I won\u2019t go into too many details on the scenario, but I would like to talk about some of the mechanics.
    \n
    \nI liked the basic ideas behind the core dice mechanic. When you want to kill things, you roll a d10 and try for less-or-equal to your FA score. When you want to do anything that doesn\u2019t involve killing, you roll likewise against you NFA score. For a Spaceship Bughunt kind of game, that\u2019s really all you need! When you succeed in a kill, you subtract a single token from the somewhat ambiguous token pool that represent\u2019s the enemy\u2019s strength. While pretty vague in its implementation, it is extremely similar to Cannibal Contagion\u2019s \u201cThreat Score\u201d mechanic, so it made total sense to me.
    \n
    \nWhen you succeed in removing a token, however, you also roll a number of dice determined by what weapon you are using and how far you are from the enemy. This determines your \u201ckills,\u201d which in fact mechanically only determines how much experience you gained from that action. I don\u2019t remember this being clearly explained to us at the beginning of the session, and don\u2019t recall learning about what exactly \u201ckills\u201d did until the end of our test encounter. I can see the given explanation of Kills and their mechanical importance as needing some initial clarification, at least for the purpose of setting a good frame of mind.
    \n
    \nThe actual dice mechanic is a basic \u201cWin versus Fail\u201d roll. If you achieve success, you get to roll your Kills, scratch off an enemy token, and then go wild describing how you won and what you did. Thing is, as it was explained to us, there are no real bad consequences for your own failure other than having to wait an extra round or two for the enemy to die. Each character can remove at most one single token from any enemy encounter, so failure simply means that one less token can be removed during this round of the battle. While that means that the enemy could of course then be allowed to survive for another round and thus potentially do more damage, it seemed that all it really ended up happening was that we were either Killing, or Waiting. It might have been a bit more suspenseful if the enemy didn\u2019t have to use any mechanics to hurt the characters, thus making its prolonged survival a much more threatening menace.
    \n
    \nThat being said, describing the kills was a blast. We had a good mix of veteran story gamers on board, so we all took to that aspect of the rules with glee and gusto. Sadly, I understand there were a good number of mechanics that we just weren\u2019t able to employ, due to the one-shot nature of the gathering. We didn\u2019t get to use vehicles much at all, and we only briefly and shallowly dipped into the numbers behind the specially-empowering Flashback mechanics (see below for some notes on that). I do particularly enjoy the per-mission advancement mechanics; I find that being able to mechanically invest in your character as soon as the first couple of encounters makes games a whole lot more fun, and makes for an ultimately more rewarding play experience.

    \nSome Nitpicks:
    \n
    \nAwesomeness is not often unmarred by some minor level of boo-ness, though. I\u2019ve got a few tiny gripes about the game and the session we played. First, NFA needs clarification. The NFA (non-fighting ability) stat is too vaguely powerful, at least as far as we were able to interpret. As written in the rules, there is nothing at all preventing you from using the NFA stat to do everything, resulting in lots of kills and lots of discarded bad dude tokens. This was a point of heavy discussion during the game session, when we realized that several players were just using NFA to narrate how they \u201ccleverly\u201d took out groups of bad guys without directly shooting or stabbing them. In my opinion, there needs to be some specific clause in the text that states exactly if/how NFA can be used to affect kills and bad dude tokens. We looked and looked for something like this, but found none.
    \n
    \nI was confused about the Strength Flashback mechanic. It was explained to us that you only get one, ever. Well, this being a one-shot game, I used mine in the very final conflict. I think that this mechanic should be clarified a bit to prevent that, unless no one else really cares. In the earlier incarnations of Cannibal Contagion, there was a mechanic called the \u201cAgsomafa\u201d finishing blow, which was a once-only-ever ability each character had, which allowed them to just win any one single Showdown. Problem was, in every playtest using this mechanic, the players waited until the very last encounter with the Big Bad and just chained them together, taking out the Big Bad with no sweat. That mechanic, needless to say, is no longer present in Cannibal Contagion. I think 3:16\u2019s flashbacks might benefit from a bit of tuning to make them more compatible with convention-style play. Cons seem to be the bread and butter of indie game promotion these days.
    \n
    \nAlso - and this might just be a situational nitpick and not something wrong with the game itself - there should also be some narration limits inherent to the guidelines. I noticed that some players would really go totally overboard not only when declaring their actions, but then again when their declared action succeeded. If you spend five minutes narrating what you are going to do, then spend five more minutes narrating how what you did succeeded, the game is hampered - unless, of course, everyone is doing this and they\u2019re all on the same page. I got the impression that the mechanics were meant to move fast and bloody. Our session wasn\u2019t as fast, but I\u2019m also sure that a lot of that had to do with all of us being new to the game. Perhaps the rules could benefit from a quick guideline, such as \u201cwhen stating your action, just give a quick statement on what you\u2019re trying to accomplish; wait until you succeed to go all-out.\u201d
    \n
    \nFinal Thoughts:
    \n
    \nI had a really good time with this game. I always love playing one-shots of these games at mini-cons like the GoPlay! events, and this was certainly no exception. I think it would work best with a short-run campaign format, though, and should Nick every want to run such a game (HINT HINT), I want in.

    \n As I recall, the rules text is pretty clear about FA and NFA.
    \n
    \nIf the end result of your cleverness is dead bugs...its FA...whether you shot them, rigged up an IED, or used your piss and a fire extinguisher to expand a crack in the cliff face and cause an avalanche. If something is going to die as a result...its FA.
    \nPeriod. No exceptions. Ever. There are specific clauses in the rules that tell you what you can do with NFA. Causing kills ain't one of them.
    \n
    \nYou get more strengths and weaknesses as you level up. Leveling up occurs between missions. Its possible to run 2 or 3 missions in a single session, so its possible to get more flashbacks even in a one shot. But not if you just ran 1 mission. Even at a Convention you can typically run more than one mission.
    \n
    \nAs I recall you don't say what you are going to do until you do it. You say..."Ima gonna use Fightin" or "Ima gonna use Non Fightin". Then when it gets to your turn you say "Ima shootin that damn bug witha my big azz rocket pod o' doom" then you roll and you say "That bug is the ded...BOOM...and so are 6 of his little friends...eat red hot fire balls of death mutha fuckas". That's pretty much what you get to say most of the time...you can say more...but why...

    \n Hey Nathaniel

    Thanks for the comments, I did catch those on your blog in February. However, your thinking and commentary about the game would probably benefit greatly from actually having a copy of the book. I'll hook you up with a copy if you'll write a review of it.

    In another thread I explained what AA does in the combat round (essentially copied and pasted from pages 18 and 19).

    Briefly, from those same pages here's how FA and NFA work (copy/paste ahead...)

    On a success\u2026
    (FA) \u2026remove one Threat Token and cause \u201ckills\u201d, roll for your weapon at the set range and describe what happens,
    (NFA) \u2026achieve the task attempted, e.g. you change range by one step (retreating or charging), and you describe it,

    Nowhere does it say remove a Threat Token for NFA. I don't know how more crystal clear I can make it.

    Now, when I play it I sometimes remove a Threat Token as GM when the players totally bone some Aliens with an NFA thing outside of an encounter (like they poison the food store or water supply of some alien city or somesuch). But that's me drifting the game from written (oh noes!) and I don't have to take the Token out of the pool unless I want to.

    After all they should only be coming out in encounters in one of four ways:
    (1) removed by a successful FA roll that causes Kills
    (2) removed en masse by a Strength
    (3) removed one at a time by a Weakness
    (4) removed by me to power a Special Ability

    Cheers,
    Gregor

    \n Strengths are handy for clearing final encounters and getting medals. There is definitely an incentive to do that. It's not a problem. You only ever get 5 in your lifetime (unless you exhaust them first and then let your character die - a suboptimal trade, though, since you lose all weapon upgrades in the process).

    Game-wise... If you can get levelled up (and survive, of course) without resorting to using them then that's even better. You'll need to use Strengths for promotions, and that's best when you have improved your NFA first. You'll need to keep them for when you really, really need them too and try to get other players to use their Strengths to your advantage.

    If you do have to clear an encounter with a Strength, you're actually best rolling dice first and getting kills through a successful FA roll before shouting "Strength!". Therefore double-dipping your kills in the encounter...

    Strengths allow you to buy into the macho lie that you can beat these aliens. They're fine.

    Weaknesses are actually very nifty in their own way too, but harder for people to appreciate I think -- and most people have a hard time describing themselves actually losing when using them. The group should ride their ass hard if they try to skip on the fictional penalties of using a Weakness.
