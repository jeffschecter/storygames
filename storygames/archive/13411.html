    Gaming with Kids: From D&D to FiascoOriginally I didn\u2019t think the improvisational style of these types of games because they require active listening and creativity in real time, something many adults don\u2019t handle well, let along teenagers. How wrong I was. The students, who range in age between 13 and 19, all really have taken to the game and with some guidance have manged to collectively create some pretty good stories. The really great part of all this is that these types of games are far more pedagogical than traditional \u201creactive\u201d RPGs like D&D since they foster things like creativity, cooperative storytelling, and active listening \u2013 the students can\u2019t simply sit back and react to the situations described to them, or \u201cpush buttons\u201d by choosing preset powers which were elements of our recent 4th edition Dungeons & Dragons game that really rubbed me the wrong way. Rather than spending 5 minutes picking a power and then going back to checking their phone, students are now listening to each other intently, building off what others have introduced, and creating something that in the end is much greater than their individual contributions.
