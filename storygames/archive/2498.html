I've begun running a full-on battlemap-&-miniatures, 5-foot-step-laden, attack-of-opportunity-filled, oh-dear-Christ-it's-a-mindflayer-riding-a-beholder-tastic D&D game using Monte Cook's Ptolus over VoIP, with my friends and I wallowing in the crazy numbers and fruity variant rules of D&D like pigs in shit.

    One thing I've done with my D&D3 is to limit the amount of source material I use from the outset. That way there's never any question about whether this feat or that feat is unbalanced, just whether it's in the materials I've identified to use.

    Another thing is to Xerox the pages from the DMG with the NPCs and their equipment loadout, and use that as my NPC generation tool. Get a stack of index cards and if you think of an NPC, scribble a few notes about them on the card, then chuck it on the pile. Then when you are improvving and need an NPC you already have a 'cast'. The same is true of locations (although Ptolus has so many locations already that you're almost certainly good to go.)

    One last thing: in D&D3, high level characters are not just heroes - they are also celebrities and people love to talk about celebrities.

    I'll post more tips as I remember them.

    Hiya,

    I've been running a D&D game for the past couple of months, and when we started we decided that all of the players would share in the decision-making processes in the game, so that everyone has the chance of getting and/or doing the things that they think would make the game really cool for them.

    Now, this being D&D, a lot of the focus of our games is on using miniatures for combats against various foes. That's fine with us -- we decided going in to the game that we wanted to play a hybrid between a miniatures game and a role-playing game, and D&D fit the bill very well.

    If that's going to be a big part of your game too, then I'd recommend using the "Fantastic Locations" series of accessories published by Wizards. Each booklet has two full-color double-sided poster-sized maps scaled for use with miniatures, and they help you make big beautiful encounter scenes for the game. They have published five so far:

    Fane of the Drow: http://www.wizards.com/default.asp?x=products/dndacc/950037400
    Fields of Ruin: http://www.wizards.com/default.asp?x=products/dndacc/953640000
    Hellspike Prison: http://www.wizards.com/default.asp?x=products/dndacc/950047400
    Dragondown Grotto: http://www.wizards.com/default.asp?x=products/dndacc/953777400
    The Frostfell Rift: http://www.wizards.com/default.asp?x=products/dndacc/957557400

    Each one also has a 16-page scenario booklet, but I usually ignore it and just use the maps for the game. The "Fane of the Drow" has good maps for underground dungeon encounters, and "Fields of Ruin" and "Dragondown Grotto" both have good maps for outdoors & wilderness encounters.
