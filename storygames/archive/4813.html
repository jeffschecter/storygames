\n How important is randomness and uncertainty?

I've got this system where you split up a deck of cards between the players, and you work with what you are dealt. Everybody has the same number of cards and the same number of opportunities to apply their cards. High cards win. In practice the spread evens out for the most part, and you win about as often as you lose, but there is some uncertainty in every outcome. It's also jazzed up with some interesting choices, but at the simplest level, it's not a very engaging system - just an uncertain one on the individual conflict level. I suspect that players will freight conflicts with meaning and use valuable or worthless cards accordingly, but that won't have any impact on the system itself.

Part of me likes this, because it de-emphasizes the conflict in favor of the outcome, but I have nagging doubts. It's not as satisfying as chucking a big pile of dice, you know? And if the outcome is all I care about, why randomize at all?

What do you think, Internet people?

    \n I actually was thinking about this in recent weeks, but probably not in the same terms as you. As a game master I want player characters to fail in dramatic but not game breaking ways, but I ultimately want them to succeed more than they fail, and for their successes to outweigh their failures (so in the end they have gained).
    \n
    \nAs a player I want control over my failure the same way I want control over my success. That is one of the reasons in recent years why I've had problems with a pass/fail style game. I want degrees of success and failure.
    \n
    \nThe reason I bring this up in the context of randomness is, I find games with the most random elements also tend to be pass/fail. How many times have you come up with a master plan only to blow the die roll? I think a lot of people think this simulates reality (there is always a chance of pulling the wrong wire, no matter how trained you are, and if you had no idea what you were doing you still have a chance of pulling the correct wire), but i don't play for the reality, I play for the drama and excitement and escape.
    \n
    \nI'm always torn between having the thrill of randomness in a game versus having control over the story and my character. Why play a game where I never can do the cool things I want to do. A truly random game kinda plays itself, and again doesn't seem that fun.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarPart of me likes this, because it de-emphasizes the conflict in favor of the outcome, but I have nagging doubts. It's not as satisfying as chucking a big pile of dice, you know? And if the outcome is all I care about, why randomize at all?
    \nActually, as I read this thread, I have a different question for you: If you want to de-emphasize conflict, why have any sort of conflict resolution?

    But to actually address the topic of uncertainty & randomness, I think it's one way to create a sense of tension. A failure that could happen at any time seemingly out of your control can build up over time. Another way, which needn't be random, is a "clock ticking down" mechanic, but that has to be balanced right in order to work. Another way is to present overwhelming choice in the face of doom -- I've played many chess games that have been rather tense, because in the endgame I have around a dozen choices that might have been decent moves, but so does my opponent, so I have the mentally maneuver around him before I even lay hand to a piece.

    I think randomness is probably the easiest way to create tension in an average situation, though. Maybe not the most rewarding, but the easiest to work with. Of course, so can the illusion of randomness, if you don't let on that it's all a facade.

    That's not to say that there aren't methods of randomness that aren't about tension, but when it comes to dice and other instantly-generated random artifacts, you have a neat thing happen: you have hope from the player of rolling "high" or "well" (whatever that means for a given system). You have cheering at Rolls of Awesomeness. You have dashed hopes at critical failures. If anything, the dice are less important to the game and more important as an object of emotional impact (though, without the game as a vehicle for that, it doesn't work).

    Maybe that's why your system doesn't have the jazz you want? The emotional impact of your randomness isn't there, because it's done during this preliminary period. What you're left with in a game of unequal resource management in your RPG, rather than a game of chance.

    Naturally, I'm probably talking out of my ass, but even typing this up has helped me sort out some thoughts about my own card-based game design.

    \nI'm a little fuzzy on how this works, and I don't see any clarification in the thread. Here's what I'm assuming:

    a) The deck is split between the players at the beginning of the game.
    b) Players play cards from their personal deck strategically (meaning they look at what they have in their hands and play according to their wishes in the conflict).

    In that case, you're front-loading the randomization in the beginning when the deck is cut, but after that a player who so desires can figure out what cards everyone else has, and keep track during play. Which means that there's not really a random element to the card playing, only the original deal.

    The main consequence of this is someone might wind up with a really bad hand and know it. Their only real choice, then, is to try and win conflicts that other players find insignificant. Ability to frame conflicts is therefore VERY important, which you seem to want to avoid.

    If you want it to be truly random, divide the deck, have each player shuffle before each draw, and play off the top without looking.

    \nActually, you said 'we', which is either the royal 'We' or is meant to include everyone reading the post. But enough about that.

    There are (at least) three uses for randomizers between the fiction and the players: The first is the one you're talking about, where the randomizer determines a course of action in lieu of player choice - ie. players will never choose to fail unless the dice tell them they fail. The next is determining whether the character is capable or competent enough to fulfill the action that the player has chosen. And the third is determining outcome when a player choice is made without full knowledge. In a lot of the games that get talked about here, the players have complete information (PTA) because the only information in the fiction is the information that the players create. However, in order to simulate character action in a world where the players necessarily have incomplete information, there must be an opportunity that the player's choice (and character action) is going to bump up against hidden information.

    In that case, randomizers are a good way to simulate that, and still have players making interesting character decisions.

    \nIn my experience, this is not where the unsatisfying part of high-level DnD is found. I'd actually single out the remaining randomness as one of the most frustrating parts of high-level DnD, since single rolls of a d20 completely determine your fate with great frequency.

    At lower-ish levels, combat rolls tend to have more time to average: sword swings are only rarely instant-kills against players, and failed saves against spells are more likely to apply debuffs or take away turns than cause a player to go play Portal for the next hour. At the lowest and highest levels, the reverse is true, and players have the constant potential to go from being in perfect condition to not playing for the next hour or more (at the lowest levels, coming back from the dead is not practical, while at high levels combat just takes that long to finish).

    Anyway, that's one of the top complaints I hear on DnD forums, and it resonates with my own experience.

    Posted By: Jonathan WaltonEric, is that part of where the incentive to own more books and become more familiar with all the rules comes in? Because it allows you to reduce the threat of some random thing injuring, hampering, or even killing your character / party? In such a case, rules mastery (and leveling) is kinda like an exertion of "player empowerment" in that it gives players greater ability to determine their own fate. Yeah?
    \nIt's definitely true that rules mastery leads to player empowerment in DnD. In my current Eberron game, coming up on session 30, I'm playing an Artificer, whose defining class feature is basically "access to every rule in the game." So, in any given situation, I actually have a huge amount of control over my fate, and how much of the spotlight I want to grab. Sometime I choose to destroy opposition by myself, and other times I hang back and play a supporting role, but it's almost always my choice.

    It's honestly a huge moment for me whenever we're in a tight spot, I think for a minute, then pull out a MacGyver-esque combination of weird spells, items, and rules text that gets us clear. It's my step-on-up moment in that game, and it's completely dependent on my rules mastery. I should note that a "home run" for me here is a plan that has no real dependence on dice whatsoever, either because the dice are never used or because all the rolls are trivial (that's your plan? Okay roll d20+22 vs. DC 20... um, right, moving on...).

    The person playing the druid has much less rules mastery, so, despite playing a very strong class, doesn't have as much of a mechanical voice at the table.

    Leveling has a kind of perverse effect on player empowerment, in my experience, since the rewards are neither linear nor always a net gain. At any one level jump, some players might be seeing massive boosts in power and narrative authority, while others might actually be worse off against expected opposition than they were the previous level. Again, rules mastery is the order of the day.
