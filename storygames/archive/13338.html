    \n I think as a beginning player, playsets that are heavy on crime and rely strongly on genre tropes from Coen Brothers or Elmore Leonard style stories hold up best for me. Some of the playsets have been a bit adventurous, which is probably fun for experienced players, but these have generally fallen a bit flat for me in play. By contrast, low-level crime sets work wonderfully in my groups.

    \n An easy one: be sure to have Objects that will naturally make ideas spark in the player's minds. Either "how I'd use it for mischief" or "this will be so embarassing when it comes out" or "oooh, someone wil *really* want this thing", and so on.

    Furthermore, Locations: pick places that underline the color of your playset, but that also suggest situation ideas. "A seedy, rundown bar" and "The cops pub" are both places to get a drink but hey, very different ideas of what can happen there!

    And, last but not least, insert some recurring detail. Maybe a relationship or two have something to do with a location, or an object *clearly* belongs to some Location, and so on. If these "invisible threads" suggest "possible story ideas" to the players, well, all the better.

    \n One thing that makes the Boomtown playset work for me-- is that players attempting to portray Old West characters-- have used deadpan, deliberate, lack-of-affect speech. Which helps keep the humor dry and creates a juxtaposition between atrocious behavior and calm, flat execution.

    It's the opposite of broad, corny, 'funny voices'. I have been guilty of Bad Acting in Fiasco and other games.

    Overtly zany elements-- like making a character a Trekkie, or an over-the-top Redneck, or objects such as a bag of jumping mice... are hard to maintain without lapsing into slap-happy silliness.

    I'd say that emergent humor is preferable to pre-determined humor:

    The odd dynamic between a step-father and stepson is going to produce both drama and humor, even tho there is nothing 'automatically' funny about the relationship.

    \n Relationships and Needs seem to make or break play and sets.

    the tightrope in Relationships is to be vague enough for the players to find something they identify and enjoy playing; but specific enough that it shapes interaction and calls out separate voices around the table.

    Jason beat me up repeatedly about Needs for the "Transatlantic" play set. The end result was much better than what i had originally stamped out. I think it's the hardest part to write effectively and to try to create beats that frame the play set. Needs are the carrot and the nail.

    Locations and Objects can be generalized if need be, but there's very little reason to not have fun here and to use these fulcrums to point to Theme by providing specific color and detail. I took the notes from my play tests and went back to these and fortified them to steer towards the general story threads that were hit on or hinted at through repeated play.
