\n So, in the past, some of my players have disliked the following resolution system:

Player: I try to stop the bleeding!
GM: Okay, the wound is pretty nasty! Roll!
Player: What do I roll?
GM: This is a Healing skill check; skill checks are 2d4.
Player: Okay. (Shakes dice. Rolls.) Five.
GM: Cool. For you, you do an average job.
Player: So, what happens?
GM: You stop the bleeding.

This is supposed to support (or at least not interfere with) immersion. One friend complained that his immersion was broken by the process of figuring out which dice to roll, shaking them without a target number in mind, reading the "5" without knowing whether that was good enough, and waiting for the GM's response.

So, this weekend at Ubercon, I did it a little differently:

Player: I try to stop the bleeding!
GM: Okay, the wound is pretty nasty! For most people, this would be a 50-50 shot! With your "pro" healing skill, though, it's not too hard. Roll the same 2d6 we roll for everything,
Player: Five.
GM: Just a bit below average. Quite sufficient to stop the bleeding.

It went over fine. Which proves nothing, but is at least one data point. I'm curious if folks here have a significantly different reaction to these two processes. Which seems more compatible with maintaing a character POV?

Further, would it make a difference if you had either of the following charts in front of you?

# - HOW HARD?
3-4 - not too
5-6 - somewhat
7-8 - 50-50
9-10 - extremely
11-12 - near-impossible

# - HOW HARD?
3 - near-automatic
4 - not too
5 - somewhat
6 - significantly
7 - 50-50
8 - very
9 - extremely
10 - incredibly
11 - near-impossible
12 - theoretically possible

I'm curious about how much different tastes and thinking styles will vary on this subject. In Delve, players are supposed to get all the info they want before acting, which is entirely sufficient for me. As long as I don't fail easy-seeming tasks on good rolls, or succeed at difficult tasks with low rolls, then I'm fine. But apparently not everyone thinks like I do on this.

    \n When I play something where rolls simulate the success or failure of my actions (which isn't all that often anymore), I really like knowing what I'm rolling for. Even better, I like having the same targets every time, with difficulty modeled through a modifier to my ROLL (not to the target score). FATE's Fudge Dice system comes to mind ("you're going to need a Superb result to do this"), or even Apocalypse World's roll+ (where you always roll 2d6 and add a value between -3 and +3 to the roll). AW feels especially good because you always have the same sort of target: 10+ is always "great, worked just like you wanted to", 7-9 is always "you succeed BUT" and 6 and below is always a miss (which means that something ELSE interesting happens).

    I would definitely err on the side of less granularity; AW's miss/hit-with-complications/hit feels just right.

    \n The reason I put the disclaimer in my first post (I don't play--can I say simulationist? I'll just do it) simulationist games very often. I still do from time to time. And when I do, I would say it's the search/thought time that goes into it that most affects my immersion.

    The difference is really in how long I spend outside of "I am my character" mode. When I need to do anything more involved with numbers--say when I have to adjust my target number--or when I need to do more than basic interpretation with the result, I spend time in metagame mode. Using Fudge again, let's say:

    "Okay, so my GM told me this needs a +4. I'm using Guns, which I have at +2, okay. I rolled a + + - 0, so +1. That's +3. Now I invoke "Patient Zero Must Die!", so that's a +5. Okay, cool, I hit!"

    This means I just spent x seconds thinking about stuff from a level that's definitely higher than my character immersion level. Whereas:

    GM: "You seize his head by force? Cool, roll+hard."
    Me: *rolls 5 5. +2: 12* "I rolled a 12, so I suffer little harm, inflict terrible harm, and frighten or dismay my enemies!"
    GM: "Cool, this is what happens..."

    In example one, I had to find exactly where my target was, based on my skill level and the difficulty level. Then there were additional considerations. Example 2, I rolled, I knew I was aiming for 7 (or better yet, 10), and that was that. The difference for me is really just in search length. Even though it doesn't seem like a big deal, I think I'd prefer your first table in the long run.

    I keep trying to think if any other aspect arising from these tables would harm my immersion, but I can't think of one. This is really dull, but for me it seems to boil down to how long I'm forced to leave the "I am my character" mindset.

    \n My thoughts listed in the order they occur to me and based on my preferences when my "playing in this mode of play" settings are turned on:

    1) Saying "Ok, roll Healing" is preferred to "Ok, roll", "so what do I roll?" If I'm deep in character headspace (which is a place I don't normally play but sometimes do and am assuming for these comments) remembering skill names and what to roll for what is one of those grain-of-sand-irritants that wind up disproportionately annoying. If your skill list is suitably defined and not obnoxiously duplicative (like say having 16 different skills for persuading someone) the GM should be able to announce on the fly what skill is appropriate without trouble.

    2) Knowing that "pretty nasty" = 50/50 shot for untrained people and "pro" training makes things easier is good to know at the beginning of play for context, but I don't want to know that for each roll. If you're using a 2d6 die roll, any gamer worth their salt already knows what the odds curve looks like.

    3) Just tell me the target number. "its not too hard" "ok I rolled a 5" "that's enough" is another one of those grain-of-sand irritants for me. Just say "Roll healing, you need a 5". My brain can auto process 5 on 2d6 = not too hard way quicker and less intrusively than doing the back and forth thing. An advantage of having and easy to parse dice system that you might as well take advantage of.

    4) Like Lula I'm not a fan of the overly granular version of the table. But better than making the numbers ranges like in the first version of the table, just leave some of the lines blank. In other words, the table goes from 2-12 but only 4 or 5 of the rows are filled in. I can see for myself that 4 is "not to hard" therefore 3 must be a little easier than that and 5 a little harder than that without needing new labels. Also, isn't 6-7 50% on 2d6 6 being 42% and 7 being 58%?

    \n David, I don't think we're that far apart on 2-3 really, just hitting the devil in the details.

    What you want to hear as a player is "not too hard"
    That's what I want to hear as a player too.

    Difference is saying "you need a 5 on 2d6" = saying "not too hard" to me. I don't need or want "58%". That kind of false precision drives me nuts for entirely different reasons. But what I do need/want is to get from "what am I trying to do" through "evaluate how difficult it is" to "make a decision" and onto "what actually happened" as quickly as possible. I'm pretty sure, that's right in line with what you need/want too.

    So for me, the most appealing option is the one that requires the fewest steps, the least back and forth with the GM, and least amount of my brain power needing to process.

    Where I think we may be different is here: given the choice between the experience of imagining details during this process vs. getting through the process as quickly and painlessly as possible so I can get back to imaging the important stuff...I'm going to pick the latter nearly every time. In others words..."doesn't seem that hard but I need to be sure not to screw up" is how I imagine the real world too...but that takes longer to process so my preference is "don't waste my time making me translate real world speak into game speak and then back to real world speak...just give me the damn number. My brain can imagine details far faster than you can describe them anyway.

    And THAT, now that I think about it...is probably the biggest single immersion enhancing thing you can do...speed. The human brain processes an amazing amount of information very quickly. Far more quickly than the spoken word can begin to fill. That's why my attention wanders when GM's read flavor text outloud. Listening to flavor text occupies like...i don't know 1/3 of my brain...the rest is racing on to other stuff cuz there's nothing there to occupy it. If I've got to listen to flavor text I'd much rather it be handed to me in writing so I can read it in about 1/10 the time it would take the DM to read it aloud.

    So every part of your system that slows down the information flowing into my brain, leaves opportunities for my brain to decide to do something else while I'm waiting. Saying "you need to roll a 5" is thus more immersive than "not too hard" for me because the amount of real world game space time is less...thus less time to get distracted by insufficient game input.

    That's the first time I've tried to articulate this point in writing, so hopefully that made sense. I don't know how universal this is, however. I tend to be the guy mentally making circular motions with my hand and thinking "come on, speed this up, get to the point already, I'm bored"

    As an example, if there is going to be a dialog between two characters, I often (not always, but often) find it more immersive to quickly summarize in the 3rd person what the conversation was about then to actually roleplay out word for word the dialogue. Why? Because in my brain, my brain is filling in the word for word dialogue FAR faster than we could actually have our mouths form the words. That's less true with players who are skilled at improv and familiar enough with their characters to have entire conversations in their character's voice without breaking a sweat. But at lesser levels of ability where players are hunting for their character's voice...i'd rather just skip to the end and let my brain fill in the voice...same as I do when I read a novel and the author summarizes a conversation without putting the whole dialog in quotes.

    \nWell, that's sort of going into one of my Stock Rants. I unfortunately never numbered them for easy reference as Holmes did back in the day:-)

    But I've often said that in any given RPG the unshared imaginary space is way way way bigger than the shared imaginary space.

    Take for example a chase scene: "You're running down a dingy street on the fringe of the commercial district. Its early morning so there's not too many people, mostly just vendors getting ready for the day" That's a pretty reasonable phrase for an area the action is just passing through. And it has just enough touchpoints to ensure that we're on the same page on the meaningful stuff. But now...each of us has a visual image of that street in our heads...and there ain't no way its the same, and aside from the stated details might not even be similar. We're all drawing visual imagery from our own experience. Someone who lives/lived in a big city like NYC or Chicago is probably populating that street with references to real streets they've walked down. Others are populating with scenes they've seen on Law and Order. Whatever. Most of the time that doesn't matter, and I'm sure is pretty obvious stuff.

    But what that means is that the movie playing in my head about our game is never ever going to be the same as the movie playing in your head. At best it will be like two movies made by different film schools from the same sketchy script. Similar enough to be recognized, but ultimately never even all that close. Armed with that realization it became clear to me that the Shared Imaginary Space is kind of like the new D&D setting...just a handful of points of light surrounded by wilderness. The lights being the shared part.

    I've come to decide that the killer app of TTRPGing is to treat this as a FEATURE not something to be minimized. Cuz if your goal is to minimize the unshared bits and maximize the shared bits then we'd be better off playing MMORPGS where what that street looks like is absolutely identical for each of us because its drawn in real time in hi res graphics, and there's never any doubt who's positioned where. Since that sort of shared space is always going to be superior on a computer than on the table top, IMO at the table its best to go the other way and embrace the unshared part as not only unavoidable but something to be enjoyed. In that sense I'd much prefer imagining my own street than having to sit through a detailed description of yours. Not because my street is better, but because the processing time to imagine a street is more akin to the processing time to "take in" a real street vs. the much slower processing time to talk about it. Imagining my own is thus more immersive to me.

    Of course, this comes with the corrolary of being perfectly willing to modify on the fly what the video of my street looks like as new elements of it become shared.
