How Do You Play? -- Alternative RPG Group Dynamics

Josh Roby
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Play Advice
Okay, so in a variety of threads we're talking about "how RPGs work" and comparing them to "how sports work" and "how LARPs work" and "how quilting circles work." Repeatedly, we're referring to "how RPGs work" by referencing a traditional/mainstream play structure that is, I highly suspect, composed of 80% assumption and 20% reduction. It's something like this:
Traditional Play
There's a GM who's also often the host. The group gets together on a regular (weekly, biweekly) basis to play an ongoing game that has no planned endpoint. Each week is a new installment, forwarding the story onward. Individual characters may die; they are usually replaced. Play continues week to week until the players get bored and/or real life interrupts the weekly meetings. The underlying assumption seems to be that the game usually ends before the story reaches climax.
I don't think that the above is very realistic, either in terms of how the folks on this forum play or in terms of how folks beyond this forum play. I think it makes a lot of oversimplified and disparaging assumptions about how gamers interact socially. I think it mistakes how gamers present their games with how gamers actually play their games. In short, I think it's bullshit.

What I'd like to hear in this thread is how you currently play. If you'd like to add in how you used to play as well, that's gravy, but I'd caution: (a) I want to hear about your current play structure first, and (b) please be careful to describe how you actually played, not how you might have described it to another potential player at the time.

This is how I play:

LA Games Playtesting Circle
We meet weekly, either on Tuesday or Wednesday depending on individual schedules. There are currently five active members, and on any given week there's three to five of us there. If you can't make it, you can't make it, and it's not a very big deal. We play lots of different games, and with the exception of our very first game, we haven't played any single game in two consecutive sessions. There is no planned 'endpoint' to the circle, but we've had to members drop out to pursue other commitments, and while we miss them, it hasn't messed up anything.

The Summer Campaign
Ever since I headed off to college, and then after I came home and my brothers have taken their turns going to college, we have been doing "the summer campaign." Each summer we play a new game: a short-run, three-to-six session thing. They've been pretty traditional fare, with a GM and a preplanned story arc to fit the available time. We meet sort-of weekly; there are a lot more than six weeks in a summer vacation, and we don't plan to play every single week. We play when we can, and since our planned story-arc doesn't blithely assume that we'll play every week, it's not a problem when we don't. Over time we've gone from incorporating other friends home from college to playing with the wives. We usually make it a dinner-and-game night thing.

Belletrists Online Tabletop
I'm actually not doing this anymore, but was up until recently. In all truth, I've discovered that I live in a giant city with lots of gamers and setting up a face-to-face game is actually easier than setting up an online game. But. When we were playing, we met up once a month on a MUSH server (sort of like souped-up chat). When one player couldn't make it, we just scene-framed her out of the action. When two players couldn't make it, we rescheduled. We played Dogs; the assumed commitment was to complete one town and see if we wanted to continue. We didn't finish the town; nobody was very hung up about this.

Pick-Up Gaming
This last sunday my wife was bored so we invited Jesse over to play/playtest my reversed-engineering game. He showed up, we played a pretty long session (8 hours), and ate curry. Pretty simple. My wife and I pick up and play games occasionally, as well.

I'm going to skip over Con games, even though I'm starting to consider them a large part of my RPG "diet." How do other folks play?

    How I used to play when I lived in Philly, 2 months ago.
    We'd play every week on a Wednesday. A given game would last 6-9 weeks at a stretch and then there'd be a gap of a couple of months before we got the next one together. Everyone would gather at our house at 7 and order Chinese, kibbitz and hang out. Around 8 or 8:30 I'd move people to recapping the previous session and talking about what they liked in it and what they thought was coming up in the current session. Then I'd take a smoke break and plan for 10 minutes, then we'd play. Play would last about 3 - 3 1/2 hours, wrapping up just before midnight. If someone couldn't make a session then we'd try and reschedule for that week. I'd try and pace things so everyone's Kickers wrapped up in the last or second-to-last session.

    How I used to play when I lived in Ithaca, 2 years ago.
    We'd play every week on a Thursday. I was GM'ing a long-running 7th Sea game. My friend Dan was always the host. We'd meet at the ABC cafe, down the block, and eat dinner and hang out together. Then we'd go up to Dan's place and game from around 8pm to 1 or 2 am. We'd kill a couple bottles of wine and a bunch of chips and cookies while we gamed. Around 12:30, Bobwoolfe would lay down on the floor and pass out, but we'd nudge him awake if we needed his input.

    The game was a "long-running" campaign, but I always knew that there would come an ending. By the time I started this campaign I'd already run, and successfully ended, three other campaigns, each with a duration of 2 1/2 - 3 years, so I knew it could be done. Within a couple of weeks of starting, I knew roughly what the final revelations would be. I just didn't know how the players' path from where they were would lead them to those revelations. About 6 months before I had to leave Ithaca I announced to the players that we were entering the Endgame and that
    a) I would not be introducing any new subplots, only revealling existing aspects of stuff as they discovered it, and
    b) I would not be framing up adventures for them. Instead, they should establish their next goals and targets before each session and I would have prepped the material to cover said goals and targets.
    It worked out pretty well, IIRC.

    Oh, this is worth sharing, and I'd meant to include it but forgot. You'll note that the three games I'm involved with are all online (or maybe you didn't, but they are...)

    There's a reason for this, and it plays into footprint stuff. I've got a close group of friends and we share a lot of the same interests. Board gaming, design, similar tastes in fiction and in study, all that jazz. But we're all busy, busy, busy people.

    I'm a fulltime student, and I'm working 24~30 hours/week to pay for that. Same thing with Nikki. And Will works full time, does school part time, and is involved in mentoring high-school students. Oh, and Jared just got married and is teaching 7th graders.

    All this means that social gathering are catch-as-catch can. Getting everyone together in once place happens about once every six weeks or so. Sometimes Nikki and I will be the only ones free. Sometimes me and Will. Sometimes Jared, me and Nikki. You get the idea.

    The result of all this instability is that we don't meet regularly, we just meet when we can. With chances for interaction so few and far between, we want every single instance to be as fun-packed as possible. And it turns out that, for us, a one-shot of PTA just isn't as satisfying as a night of board gaming. This is probably due to the fact that some of us just don't feel like we're done at the end of that one-shot. We want to play the rest of the show. Not in the sense of 'that'd be fun, but if we don't that's okay' (which is the case with our board game play), but in the sense that 'man, part of the fun for this activity was invested in future play, but we probably will never get that effort rewarded. Suck.'

    So, yeah, a lot of the social footprint stuff that's been discussed recently matters to me.

    One that I haven't seen so far here (if I missed it in the list, sorry):

    Semi-competitive GMing

    Back in high school, the group I played with met two nights a week, for about six hours each night. Usually, two sessions would happen each night, for a total of four a week. More than half the people in the group GMed, and several different systems were played.

    At the start of the weekend, there would generally be multiple people willing to GM, sometimes each with multiple ideas for games to run that night. They'd toss out vague descriptions of their ideas, along the lines of "I've got an idea for D&D, characters about eighth level. It's a city adventure." The group would (eventually) come to a consensus about what to play, and the selected GM would start their adventure. It would continue for about three hours, and would either wrap up by then, or we'd stop at a natural stopping point for it to be continued later.

    The "semi-competitive" aspect came in with the fact that GMs wanted to get to run their ideas... and so would try to put on the best "show" they could while they were running games, so the group would want to play in their adventures again.

    This lead to a lot of heavy illusionist GMing in some cases, where the GM would march the group through an adventure while making it seem as if everything was their idea. On the flip side, some of the GMs became extremely good at improvisation, essentially tailoring an adventure as they went along to the players who were there that night and their characters.

    I have two modes of play (three, if you count the boardgames).

    tabletop campaigns or one-shots

    We either play a mini-campaign (when we know about how long the game runs), an "infinite campaign" (in which we don't) or one-shots (one session, or two if it's just too long for one). There are no set playing dates; we try to reserve a playing date a week or few weeks before with a reservation matrix (an email with dates of the next month; players mark the days when they can play, can probably play and cannot play - then we pick a date from those which fits all). We usually play once every two weeks or once a month, depending on free times. Campaign's and mini-campaigns have set players (my current Fading Suns has four), with one-shots, the GM usually sends an invitation email to players of his choosing. I usually play campaigns with a certain group and draw players to one-shots from a pool of about 20-30 players, consisting of folks I've been playing with and know. I usually play on weekday evenings, with session length of 6-7 hours (or more with one-shots).

    I usually play once or twice a month.

    LARP's

    LARP's are usually advertised two or three months prior to the event (some even longer); invitation-only games could have a slightly shorter notice time. I make the decision to participate well before the event, if I have nothing else scedhuled. Participation time ranges from a long evening to a weekend, plus the preparation time.
