\n Something bubbled up from this thread that had me thinking. Fred mentioned that some have complained about SotC for extended play because there is no meaty system for character advancement.

So, do you think character advancement is necessary for campaign-style/extended play? If you want some mutiple choice:

a) Totally unnecessary.
b) Nice, but not essential.
c) Very important, but not essential.
d) Absolutely essential.

For my part, I'm in the "b" camp. And I suspect the only reason I'm not a full-on "a" man is some early hard-wiring as a result of D&D. It reinforces the idea of forward momentum for the campaign, but I think the proper shepherding of a campaign arc minimizes its necessity.

    \n I'm think B too. Part of me wants to say C, but I think that's just the D&D hardwiring getting me too.

    I suspect that what I really need in a long term game is some sort of structure, a mechanical structure, that shows me that I'm making progress of some kind. That need I rate at D. Character advancement is the largest way this has been addressed in RPGs to date, but certainly not the only way that it could be. Burning Empires "Infection" mechanics are an example of something else that scratches that itch.

    I wonder what other ways are out there...

    \nCrossposted with Graham, in which he says what I meant more concisely

    I've seen games with and without it, and I don't really like either. In games that do have advancement, I often find that the rewards don't line up with what is being rewarded. For example, in a D&D game, my rogue gets experience for defeating monsters, which causes him to level up. When he levels up, he gets more skill points, which I can put into Diplomacy. Whaaaaa? I'm rewarded with higher Diplomacy for fighting monsters? IMO, this problems extends to a lot of games in a lot of ways - I chose D&D because it's easy to pick on. On the other hand, games without character advancement often feel static to me, and somehow deny the reality that people change. I recognize that RPGs and real life frequently diverge in a lot of ways, and even so I find it hard to accept a world where people don't - or can't - change. Come to think of it though, that's not really synonymous with advancement. I used to be able to juggle pretty well, but I haven't done it in so long that my skills have atrophied. Having spent time developing other skills, I'm now good at different things than I used to be.

    So I guess my answer is: For me, character advancement is (b) nice, but not necessary. As an addition though, character change is, in my view, (d) absolutely essential.

    \n The more I think about it, and deal with it, the more it seems essential for the possibility of character growth to be available and expressible in a way that is linked meaningfully into the game system. The measure of the character's journey, and just how that measure is shown to have meaning in the game, is dependent on the context of the game in question. It could be traditional as a direct increase in a readily measurable skill such as in Dungeons & Dragons, or it could be something more subtle like reaching (or failing to reach) a character's goals feeds back into a general effectiveness pool.
    \n
    \nWhatever the case, I know for me personally that long-term, campaign style play is going to fall down and go boom if I don't have some system based feedback mechanism for which to capture the journey of my character.

    \n I can think of three extremely successful long term campaigns I've been in that did not have character advancement in the expected sense. In fact, most of my long campaigns that have stopped have stopped because characters have advanced out of the player's area of interest. So now even with an advancement system I start characters where they are fun to play and pretty much leave them there.

    The three systems I'm thinking of are Classic Traveller (a multi-year game with zero advancement), Spirit of the Century (might not qualify as, although there is character continuity it's very episodic), and our hybrid Spirit of the Far Future which shows signs of having serious legs. In both SotC-ish games we have a very constrained advancement rule that can never increase the power of the character yet permits growth: after every session a player may add and remove an aspect (maintaining ten) and may increase a skill by one rank while decreasing one from the new rank to the old -- that is, the skill pyramid is retained (say, move a 3 to a 4 and a 4 to a 3). It's possible for a character to change quite drastically over a handful of sessions, but the change is gradual and the power level does not change.

    \n Depends on the genre, the game and how long it goes.

    James Bond doesn't really get better. Maybe in this new crop of movies, we will see a slight arc... certainly in Casino Royale, he isn't quite a suave and sophisticated ubermensch that we've seen in other films. The Casino Royale book, Bond is a wet-behind-the years neurotic screw up. He screws up bad. Not suave at all.

    But Conan does get better over time. Conan isn't a good horseman or bowman in the earliest of his chronicles (and this highlighted more in the 70s comic), but he is decent with a sword and great at climbing. Not to mention he works his way up to king. For me, Conan, and Elric are more influential on my fantasy gaming than Tolkien... and that path from beginning to high flautin' end is pretty cool to me.

    Certainly most Campbellian Hero Journeys ask for some kind of character advancement. While Frodo and Sam seem to stay pretty constant, Merry and Pippin become warriors and leaders.

    So. I like character advancement.

    Supers is another place, over time, many characters really do become more powerful... or if not more powerful, more well rounded. Kitty Pride starts out as a 13 year old who can phaze through stuff. But she picks up some martial arts, can pilot a sophisticated jet and certainly has paramedic skills in a fairly short span of comics. Her phazing ability gets marginally better (she can do it longer), but that is about it. However, I look at that, and say, yeah, the character gets better over time. There is more she can do, and the more she can participate in.

    Or look at Weapons of the Gods. Here is a place were character advancement is *really* part and parcel of the genre tropes. "His kung fu is unbeatable!" "Yes it would seem so. But not vs. the Seven Sisters Dragon style... go young man, journey forth to the Mountain of Plume White and learn from reclusive Sacred Tenbaku the Seven Sisters Dragon Style!" Good lord, how many times have we seen this in wuxia film or comics?

    RPGs, for me, are about emulation of these tropes and genres. Not perfect fits by any stretch.... I've yet to play a character who is dynamic and "cool" as Conan and feels like the series of stories that REH wrote.. although I've played a couple that come close.

    But is it necessary. No. Change is probable, and that should be reflected by the game system... allies acquired, enemies made, certainly trauma can result in changes both physical and mental. If you got an arm hacked off last session, it should affect the character mechanically. But skills atrophy and new hobbies emerge in all of us.

    Not necessary. But I'm probably not going to invest quite as much into the PC if there wasn't advancement.

      \nSo I guess the question is what definition of campaign play are we using? If a series of games using the same setting, mechanics, and (mostly) the same characters but where previous games have no impact on later games, then sure, nothing has to change. But that's a totally different set up from a series of games where it is expected that earlier sessions have a direct impact on later sessions. In the later style, obviously something changes, yet it need not mean mechanical improvement of characters.
