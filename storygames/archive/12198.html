    \n I played with a difficult person for a while recently. (He wasn't nearly as bad as your guy, Arnold, but he definitely made game night way less fun for me).

    I agree with everyone here that not playing with him is the only real solution. A new game is not going to change or alleviate his basic dickishness.

    Once you move on, and game night is more fun, you'll be all like "Whoa, why did we play with that guy for so long?"

    But ... lots of the people we game with are connected to us socially in some way, so just saying "Fuck off!" or "Get lost!" is really not a viable option. Being rude or dramatic will just turn it into something bigger than it deserves to be. If you've covered this ground with him before, I think you're okay to just set up other games where he is not involved. Change houses or the day of the week you play for a while. If he learns about these games and asks, then you can be honest. ("Playing with you wasn't real fun, like we talked about that one time.")

    \n Arnold,

    Before the next game you run, take him aside, tell him the things you've noticed, tell him how hard it is for you to have fun with these things going on. Pause. Let him either explain himself or get defensive and assholish.

    If he was completely unaware and seems contrite, give him a second chance.

    If he is (as I would expect) a dick about it, give him an ultimatum ("if this behavior at the table when I'm GMing doesn't cease, I will need to ask you to leave" or something similar, be direct).

    In either case, unless he up and leaves or refuses your ultimatum and you boot him, run the game. Call him when he does things you described to him an unacceptable. Keep a mental tally during the game. IF he exceeds your patience, walk out with him at the end of the game and ask him not to return while you are GMing.

    Oh, I'm assuming that this dick player wouldn't break the group if he leaves. If that is the case, then I would talk to the players sans the dick player and advise them that you are at the end of your rope and need to take drastic action. Perhaps a player who knows dick player better offers to talk to him or take care of it. Then offer the group an ultimatum that either dick player shapes up or you will ask him not to return. As long as the group sans dick player doesn't object, then you follow the steps above.

    I hate these situations.

    \n When this does happen, and for me it normally happens at conventions, I usually consider the age of the player. If they are 16 or 17, well, that's hormones and fragile ego of youth for you. If they are older, then I have far less patience for it. At Cons, when I've had enough I usually get up and leave with a short apology (for the other players). Life's too short and con time is too precious to try to correct the behavior of others. Besides, if I'm a stranger they're not going to listen to me anyhow.

    For people that I'm going to play a longer term game with, I also perform the age old "Beer Test", that I heard on a gaming podcast. Sons of Kryos maybe, or Canon Puncture. To those that don't know, the "Beer Test" is where I ask myself if I would want to have a beer with this person at a bar and just chit chat. If I wouldn't, then it's a big wake up call, saying that I probably shouldn't play any games with them. If I would, then I ask them if they want to go for a beer, and do the aforementioned chit chat. This way I learn a bit about the person, and pave the way to making a friend.

    \nDoes anyone out there have experience dealing with a player like this:

    I am a player like this.

    I should know better than to say that, because whenever I visualize bad behavior at the table, or identify with a perpetrator;

    reality intervenes and the actual bad behavior dwarfs my imagination with new frontiers of human failing, far beyond what I see in the mirror.
    And yet...

    -Constantly interrupts other people

    I am prone to manic enthusiasm. And prone to having overthought games so much beforehand, that I'm overprepared and overexplain nuances, implications, ranges of choice available... especially when trying to prompt players who seem hesitant. Can you discern a reason for the interruptions, beyond sheer disregard for others? If so, it still doesn't excuse the behavior. But it might point the way to a solution.

    -Provides a steady stream of insults

    This can be intended as humor, as bonding, as a tough-love teaching method, as an attempt to diffuse tension rather than escalate. If so, the subtext is being lost in your case. But is destructiveness ALL that's being communicated in these insults? Or is there a message buried in there?

    -Boasts that every character he plays is "himself." and mocks anyone who believes they can do otherwise

    I subscribe to this. I hope I'd defend the notion in a civil manner. But if someone who disagreed were too dismissive, refusing to hear me out, I might stoop to cheap boasting and mockery. I'm not proud of that, because two wrongs don't make a right. But, I can see it happening. Have the players on the other side of the issue left enough room for everybody to agree-to-disagree? Or does your problem player feel like there's not enough room for his point of view to co-exist at all?

    -Leaves the table or talks over the scene when his character isn't involved

    Hard to defend. But I gotta ask-- is this willfully inconsiderate, or is this a symptom of an inability to contain themselves? Is it really that they player can't be bothered to behave correctly/ or is it that they can't figure out how-to behave right, even if they wanted to?

    What I'm asking is, has anyone found a silver bullet?

    Yes. Brilliant Gameologists' 74-part podcast series on how to avoid kicking people out of your game. The cure may be worse than the disease:
    http://brilliantgameologists.com/category/sodes/gm-advice-sodes/

    Was it worth it when you tried your solution, or did it lead to more trouble?

    I know this much: there's a lot of zero-tolerance tough talk in the replies so far, and I'd certainly hope that getting rid of me wouldn't be the only solution considered, if it were me being the jerk.

    "You don't have to act out to get attention. We all want to pay attention to each other, but we have to take turns."

    Sounds good. Give it a shot. But mean it... make sure they indeed Don't have to act out, to feel like they're allowed to be themselves at the table. Make sure there really is a reasonable alternative, and that they know it.

    One other thing. This worked on me once: You know how Jack Black or Seth Rogan get to play smart alec dick characters, but they're so devilishly charming that the audience is on their side? Well, that sucks for guys who aspire to be like their cool jerkwad idols; but lack the magic appeal to pull it off.

    One time in particular, a game group just flat-out INDULGED me, bigtime. It would have been reasonable to fear that indulging me would create a monster, by 'rewarding' bad behavior. But for whatever reason-- they played-along. Not begrudingly, but to-the-hilt. It was immensely gratifying to me, and therapeutic-- to be a prize ass and get nothing but kudos for it. I hope the other players were pleasantly surprised at how fun it was to let my bull-in-a-china-shop routine generate adversity to play off. (But I know it had to be unnerving, too.) Just doing it once, I got it out of my system, to a large degree. Is this something you could try? Doing it his way, once, full on?

    \n I find bad behaviour much more easy to deal with when I am GM'ing, because it interferes with my official role as GM, so it's less of a social issue. It irritates me if I am trying to run a game, and people are pissing around or behaving counter-productively. So, I will call them out there and then. Otherwise, it's much more problematic.

    Separately, I do believe that in many cases, a friendly private word really can change things for the best, and I have seen players who are well known for behaving badly transform their behaviour. Make it about their behaviour and not them. Talk about incompatible play styles rather than personal failings. Also, let it come from you, and not the group. Own your criticism.
