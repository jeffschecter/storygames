  • shreyas
    August 2006

    William Burke and I have been talking a lot about his concept of "accepting system" lately. It means, you examine a system's structures of reward and punishment, and choose to act in the form-and-spirit of that structure, confident that it's rewarding you for good things and punishing you for bad things.

    That's playing hard, to me, and I think it's great. You get better at it by getting better at reading systems and seeing what they want out of you, and simultaneously by reading your fellow players and seeing what their readings of the same systems are like. When you play most effectively, you are playing with well-aligned conceptions of the text and deep understandings of it.

    As for playing to win, well, that's what you do if your game has a win condition.

    What Jasper said.

    I think that everyone is going to say that they appreciate effort. But the question is what sort of effort. I'm seeing all sorts of agenda clash here potentially. Put another way, sure, there are agendas where "playing to win" is valid. But there are others that will clash with it horrifically. As long as the group has that agenda across all play, no problem. Simple as that.

    Note that I have a pet peeve with the term min-maxing, in that it's used derogatively. That's an artifact of incoherent designs. That is, according to Von Neumann's Min-Max theorem - which is the genesis of Game Theory, players will always try to maximize their benefits while minimizing costs. This is not some bad behavior, but simply the most sensible thing a player can do at any time. Giving opportunities for a player to min-max, and then expecting the player not to is bad game design. Period. Just calling it bad behavior is simply saying that you don't share the gamism agenda that the game is promoting with it's min-maxing opportunities.

    These sorts of derogatory terms simply come down to players making specific notes of clashing sorts of agendas. The "munchkin," a term that came about from convention reports of relatively young kids come to play D&D in a very straighfrowardly gamism manner, simply means that the player in question is playing D&D in one of the various other ways that the system supports, and doesn't like the way the kid plays. The kid may never have seen another way to play it, so expecting him to just know better, when he's enjoying the way he plays, is non-sensical.

    How do you get better at it? Well, if "it" means gamism, then do you mean at being an effective player? Are you seriously asking how one becomes better at strategy and tactics? Or does the question mean something else?

    Okay, to your questions!

    Question 1: My oppinion is that gamist supporting games are mega fun. They might be considered the most basic form of gaming because everyone has a natural desire to "win" at something. However, I caution you not to focus just on winning. Gamism is more about addressing Challenge than it is about reaching the goal line first. The fun is in the hard work it takes to get the prize, not the prize itself.

    Question 2: Heckyeah there is a place for them. Gamism is just as legitimate a Creative Agenda as Narrativism and Simulationism. You can have fun doing all three. And you dont have to resort to traditional gaming tropes to support that CA.

    Question 3: Heh, well as with anything you need practice. But also I encourage thoughtful analasys of game design. Examine why certain options are presented in the rules the way they are. Consider applications for an option that are unusual or "outside the box." Play multiple games and be mindful of your own play decision. It helps to post in Actual Play forums as well.

    Question 4/5: Min-maxing is a legitmate strategy, but it's not the only one. Pay close attention to currencies and reward cycles in the game. That's where the real system-strategy is. As for munchkinism, I don't know what you mean by that exatly, but it never has good conotations. I tell my friends to leave detrimental behavior at the door. That usualy takes care of it.

    Question 6: Yes it does belong in some games and not others. Polaris would be a very poor game to play with the Gamist agenda. DnD however, rocks for Gamism. And remember, it's not the games themsevles that are Gamist or Narrativist or whatever, it's the players. Certain games can support that play style, but if that style is at odds with what everyone else wants to do, that will kill your fun in a heartbeat.
