    \n Still trying to decide how much to pledge on some of these.
    Usually, I avoid ordering printed books from abroad (I live in the EU) to save on shipping (among other reasons), and print them myself with my (very efficient, full-duplex) laser printer - but decks of cards (much more assembly work required than just printing and stapling) and books where color is essential (it's a black and white printer) throw me a curve one.
    People familiar with Dialect: what's the language deck like and how much of a hassle to you think it would be to print and assemble?
    People familiar with Bluebeard: how integral are the illustrations in the Book of Rooms to the gameplay? What about the Deck of Objects and the Tarot of Servants?
    Also, for both games, just how much language based are these various accessories? Consider I'm disproportionately more likely to play any of these games in Italian than in English, which usually involves my translating on the fly any player-facing texts. In such a situation, if I used custom decks of cards for, say, The Quiet Year or Fifth World, I'd end up referencing a legend all the time anyway, or translating out loud on the fly for everybody, with very little gain over just using a regular deck of playing cards (as I do) - in such a context, would said accessories benefit me at all?

    "Tales from the Loop" looks and sounds very interesting! Has anyone played a beta version or anything like that (e.g. Mutant Year Zero)? How well do the "story" elements of the game hang together? I find it interesting that it presumably has "story game" elements but also prepared scenarios and/or campaigns. How do those work together?
    \nI think that Tales From the Loop will be similar to Mutant: Year Zero in the same way Powered by the Apocalypse games are similar to Apocalypse World. The dice mechanic and other basic ideas stay the same, but are re-imagined and modified to suit the new game. I contributed some minor texts to Mutant, and was part of the development of the game, did playtesting with the main author, and so on. But I don't know what has carried over to the new game.

    Mutant has many story game inspirations: playbooks and picking conflict roll consequences from a list from Apocalypse World, community building over time from The Quiet Year, and also OSR inspirations in the hex crawl world map with random encounters in each hex. Most of the prepared scenarios are plug-and-play scenarios that you can drop into any hex and run from there, but in the main game there's also a main story arc in the background that the players can engage or not. If they do, they get to interact with some other prepared events and locations. The later games in the series adopted a more linear campaign structure, but with the end-goal that each playable race (mutated anthropomorphic animals and robots) will reach the Zone and join the mutated animals from the base game in the sandbox.

    I'm writing one of the stretch goal scenarios for the Tales RPG! I can't give you any input into the rules at the moment, as I haven't seen them. Fria Ligan, the publisher, has promised a beta PDF (for funders or everyone, I don't know) on Dec 25.
