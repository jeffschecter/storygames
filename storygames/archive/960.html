  • D-503

    I think the acceptance of player narrative input is a really good thing for the hobby, it really broadens out what people can do in games and can be a lot of fun.

    That said, it's good because it's a broadening out of the play possibilities available to us, not because it's intrinsically better. Intrinsically better for some people sure, but not per se.

    When I play I prefer not to have narrative input, because part of the joy of gaming for me is the act of discovery and the impression that the game world is a real place that I'm exploring. Narrative control kills that for me, as I can't imagine it's a real place when I made a bit of it up a minute ago.

    Now, I suppose one could argue that I had been damaged in some way, but leaving aside the fact that that argument kills off any dialogue as I can't meaningfully respond to an analysis which categorises me as incapable of working out for myself what I enjoy and why (plus it's incredibly patronising), I also think it's wrong.

    Tastes vary, having a broader range of gaming catering to more tastes is great, but a lack of narrative input may not be because people are straightjacketed from that but because people don't always want it. The sad thing historically, is there always were those I think who would have wanted it, but until quite recently nobody catered to those guys.

    It's a pretty common device. In theory(ish)-speak, it moves an important informational coduit and consensus forum between the players to the SIS. Instead of sitting around and deciding, player-to-player, what to do next with the narrative, you do it through the proxy of your characters in the SIS. It's less efficient and can be heavy-handed (or underhanded) when the GM starts confusing character motivation and the role of this consensus-building tool.

    It's just a different forum for player negotiation.
