\n Has any of you experience with playing a group one PC is a (super)powerful leader/champion and the others are just his sidekicks/friends?

The closest thing I remember in this direction is Buffy (Cinematic Unisystem). If I recall it right, the Slayer was more powerful than the others.
Yet, I think I have never played any RPG where people were not somewhat balanced.

I wonder: does this work practically (at least for a one-shot)? Or are all the sidekick players gonna get discouraged?

What I have in mind:
In a foreign world, one of the players is a prophet, chosen by some deities. The other players are his followers. They either succeed establishing a new religion - or fail and die horribly trying.

    \n FASA's Star Trek RPG was very strict about chain of command, to the point where a lot of players were discouraged by the lack of agency. So you gotta be careful. That said, I think the best way around that is to give everybody a different specialty, maybe not "superpower" but very useful in various types of situations. And then toss in situations where each character gets a chance to use their specialty, maybe even whole little side-adventures, while the Prophet is busy with less worldly things.

    \n In Kingdom, some characters have the authority to make decisions, and the rest simply don't. (There isn't necessarily a chain of command.) For example, one character might be a God-Emperor while the others are his wife, his advisor and his fool.

    This is "balanced" by having the powerful character lack key abilities: foresight and understanding the common man.

    \n In general systems do balance, Cinematic Unisystem both gives the non-Hero characters (white hats) more drama points at a cheaper spend, and kind of mandates that the Slayer character needs help and support from the others and when that happens everyone gets rewarded. As a GM you do tend to end up with encounters weighted so the Slayer has foes they can best while the white hats have easier opponents and if the Slayer chooses badly at the start of the encounter the white hats main job is to stay alive until rescued. Equally it's a system where final death is somewhere between difficult and impossible to attain.

    When going for it in the set up I'd suggest you need to be sure that the players will be comfortable with both the situation, and who is playing what. A sympathetic player as the prophet is going to look to share plot and limelight with the other players and ease the job of the GM, someone who lets the power go to their head is going to be harder work. Be careful of making the other characters too fantastic, 1st ed Victoriana does this, upper class characters get loads of social bonuses in game but to balance it with the points spend lower class characters all end up as near super heroes. It doesn't make sense.

    \n One of the most memorable games I've ever played was a 4th edition D&D campaign featuring an exiled prince returning home to reclaim his throne from bad guys. (I know, I know. Sometimes they're clich\xe9s for a reason)

    The prince was clearly the main character, but the game worked brilliantly nonetheless for a couple of reasons:

    - Everyone was cool with this premise from the beginning. The player of the prince wanted to play a "face" character and everyone else had a solid, enjoyable concept that didn't require them to be front-and-centre in the public eye.

    - Everyone apart from the prince got their own dedicated sub plot, so there was always interesting stuff tied directly to them even if they weren't the driving force of the main plot.

    - Everyone apart from the prince had their own specialist skill that set them apart, so sometimes they were the driving force of the main plot.

    - Mechanically, the prince played a supporting role, so when he excelled he was also directly causing someone else to be awesome as well.

    Witch: the Road to Lindisfarne deals with a single central interestingly, by having her be the one under threat the entire time and giving her different agendas from the rest of the players in framing scenes and describing atmosphere.

    There's also probably much to be learned from various Doctor Who games, but admittedly I haven't actually played any so couldn't give a recommendation.

    There are a lot of ways to interpret a 'powerful leader'.

    Are they numerically superior (higher level, better stats/equipment, etc)? Are the plot central (the exiled prince example from above)? Are they group caller/leader (ie meta)? Are they somehow player superior (parent in a group of kids)? Are they a financial backer or architetect/strategist with little else to offer?

    Each would have different benefits and pitfalls. In the end, of course, it's mostly about the group and what they're interested in playing. In one group I've been with for a long time two of the players preferred to stay quiet and be along for the ride, while the other two took the spotlight - one as a power-gamer beating the rolls and one as a roleplayer/plot driver. Because that's what they wanted to be there was seldom reason to try and force equality.

    Really I've never heard an idea that won't work on a limited basis (right group, one-shot game, etc). Such things don't always equate to a broadly popular ongoing game however.

    \n In the case of your setup, would it work to separate authority and competence?

    For instance, if one character is a prophet, word of god, and all that, maybe his followers are the ones with the actual skills?

    For instance, playing a powerful Prince alongside his bodyguard (who must protect the weak, inexperienced Prince) makes for an interesting dynamic balance, because the Prince has the authority and the power, but the bodyguard has the means to save his life - and, in theory, the power to take things into his own hands. The Prince is the "leader", but he depends on the bodyguard to survive, especially if the bodyguard is the only one who knows the local culture or what life is like on the streets.

    \n Seriously, you want to play Kingdom. The situation you\u2019ve set up is pretty much a one-shot engine of wonder for showing off why the system works. And that\u2019s taking as the premise you want there to be a Chosen One prophet and the establishment of a new religion or failure to do so.

    You\u2019re correct in thinking that the issue is spotlight time, but I think you might be a little too attached to the idea of spotlight time being attached to character-power. I think the development of games in the last decade has pretty effectively proven that the key is to \u201clet everyone play,\u201d and to promote not equal character-power but rough parity in the ability of the decisions of a given player to influence a portion of the time the players spend together at the table.

    Let me quickly throw together a brief Kingdom seed setup in the context of your game as an example:
    \n

    The Prophet

    \nA small group surrounds the Chosen One, disciples and confidants. No matter where or when, the world is seldom kind to the finders of a new Way.

    The Kingdom is the whole group of the proto-religion, beyond the disciples to the rank-and-file.
    \n

    Customize (choose one for each)

    \n
      \n
    • The world is one of [ incredible high fantasy | the 20th century third world | the 1st century Levant | the dark future of the 40th millenia, where there is only war ]
      • \n
    • The general populace of the local area think [ your cult needs to be burned out with fire | you are the best hope in a dying world | very little as they\u2019ve heard nothing of you | you\u2019re all frauds and mountebanks ]
      • \n
    • The presence of doubters in your ranks [ is omnipresent and ever-ready | is unthinkable to folk so pure | is welcome as people who never doubt cannot be taught | is subversive, threatening, and cannot be countenanced ]
      • \n
    \n

    Threats

    \n
      \n
    • There\u2019s an outsider agitating the local populace against the Chosen One.
      • \n
    • A rival cult is horning in on your turf.
      • \n
    • Disbelief is cutting into your ability to recruit among the people.
      • \n
    • The local political authority wants to cozy up to you for the public leverage and offers implicit threat if you don\u2019t go along.
      • \n
    • The iconic artifact imbued with the grace of your Higher Power has been perverted.
      • \n
    \n

    Locations

    \n
      \n
    • The Mount of Transfiguration, where the Chosen One was revealed
      • \n
    • The tomb of the first resurrected disciple
      • \n
    • The town center, where preaching among the people occurs
      • \n
    • The upstairs room, site of the secret meetings of the true insiders
      • \n
    • The Grove of the Walking, a small public space where the aspirants come to worship and learn
      • \n
    \n

    Character Concepts

    \n
      \n
    • The Chosen One
      • \n
    • The Doubter, what it says on the tin
      • \n
    • The Fisherman, best recruiter
      • \n
    • The Rock, both best brawler and most steadfast of the disciples
      • \n
    • The Betrayer, destined to be the death of the Chosen One and both of them know it
      • \n
    \n

    Names

    \nMatthew, Mark, Luke, John, Peter, Andrew, Bart, James, Simon, Davey, Piter, Mickey, Mike

    Abigail, Bathsheba, Helah, Jemima. Lydia, Salome, Zilah, Melanie, Emma, Geri, Victoria
    \n

    Crossroads

    \n
      \n
    • Have the outside agitator murdered in secret?
      • \n
    • Inform the local authority about a non-existent threat against their lives from your rivals?
      • \n
    • Directly oppose the usury of the local moneylenders?
      • \n
    • Attempt to subvert the prophecy of the Chosen One\u2019s oncoming death by lynching the Betrayer?
      • \n
    • Force the community to bend the knee to your god at the point of the sword?
      • \n
    • Pursue an inquisition of the iniquitous and rout out the unbelievers to be burned alive?
      • \n
    • Preserve the physical body of the Chosen One in suspended animation so that they can lead your people for centuries to come?
      • \n
    • Engage in duplicitous means to raise enough money to throw amongst the crowd when the local authority displays the Chosen One after his arrest and asks the public\u2019s will?
      • \n
    \n

    Notes

    \nThe hook in playing this sort of thing in Kingdom is that the Chosen One, while being the focus of prophecy, is still involved in dealing with the implications and ramifications of the nascent religion and the works of his disciples.

    In Kingdom, the Chosen One needn\u2019t have the in-game role of Authority (the player or players that get to make the decisions for the way the Crossroads is resolved. He could just as easily be the Touchstone, who gets to exemplify how the rest of the folks in the Kingdom (in this case, the cult as a whole) feel about what\u2019s going on. Or the Perspective, who gets to predict what will happen as a result of deciding one way or another on the Crossroads. For extra fun, it helps to know that these roles aren\u2019t fixed; another player can challenge you to take your role or you can willingly give it up to take on another. The Chosen One going from Authority to Perspective is a perfectly sensible process that changes the influence over the story and the dynamic without changing his identity.

    Kingdom works just fine for one-shots. Pick one of the really compelling Crossroads and push hard on the threat of Crisis (which is what happens if the direction of the Kingdom itself gets too far out of sync with the peoples\u2019 interests and inevitably ends in a situation that can destroy the Kingdom as a whole), and you\u2019re gone.

    You can see some live play of Kingdom on my YouTube Channel. . I suggest the New Avalon games as they are multi-session, multi-Crossroads play, but there are several one-shots in there, too. And if you want something with bigger scope and less attachment to individual characters, check out Microscope by the same author. Great stuff, across the board.

    @SquidLord: Excellent write-up, but I wonder how it\u2019s played out. After the customization is done, what thoughts goes through your mind in how to set up a scene?

    One of the key understandings is that Kingdom is GM-less, the first phase where you and your fellow players create characters is going to drive a lot. Even in the absence of numeric quantification, there are important things about your character that\u2019ll drive your eventual scene setting. Of particular note is that every player\u2019s character is explicitly defined with where they\u2019re most likely to be found, what their wish or fear about the Kingdom\u2019s future, what personal issue that they have that holds them back, and some kind of relationship to the character played by the person on your left. In particular, the wish/fear is a huge driver of some initial direction, in part because once characters are created, the next step is for the first player to create a Crossroads, an essential question about what the Kingdom is going to do. Making up a good one generally involves paying attention to the other characters\u2019 wishes and fears, and hitting several of them at once.

    Instant hooks.

    Once that first Crossroad is created, it drives the scenes that follow. They should all be about what your character thinks or does about that Crossroads. So, as an example, if the Crossroad is, \u201cDo we hang the Betrayer before he has a chance to give up the Chosen One according to prophecy?\u201d that\u2019s probably going to be a huge hook for anyone interested in or fearful about the future of the religion. The Authority will give orders to the Kingdom about things that do happen, the Perspective will give predictions about things that will happen as a result, and the Touchstone will have things to say about what the folks outside the inner circle think about it and can push everything hard to Crisis if their interests aren\u2019t seen to.

    (Things get even more interesting [or \u201cinteresting\u201d] when there is more than one person with a given Role, which can absolutely happen \u2013 and when no one does. Hint: The latter can go badly, very quickly.)

    So, for myself, when I\u2019m hitting that Scene creation, I\u2019m thinking about what my character wants, which I\u2019ve already thought about and which everyone else knows, and I think about how the Crossroads intersect with that, and then I push hard on it. I pull other peoples\u2019 interests and oppositions into the Scene so that there\u2019ll be tension and conflict. I give other folks a chance to be awesome while definitely pursuing my character\u2019s goals. Kingdom is a game that really supports playing a character that is at odds with other characters (or all the other characters) as long as you\u2019re all interested in the continuance of the Kingdom itself.

    Definitely check out our videos if you have the time. There was a lot of fun being had. I suspect that this coming Saturday we\u2019ll be playing Kingdom again in the 700 Horsepower setting, so there might be more of it to be had.

    \nYou could be thinking about In the Spotlight, my hack of InSpectres which is designed for playing TV series with a lead character, including Doctor Who, Blackadder, House, Sapphire & Steel, etc.

    It's GMless and each player has a supporting character (one of the Doctor's companions, for example) whom they play most of the time, but they can also earn spotlight dice which can be used to take on the role of the lead character: only the lead character has the power to resolve the plot of the episode.
