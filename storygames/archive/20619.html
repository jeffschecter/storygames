\n I am not starting this thread to convince you that trad games are better than "indie" games or that you would enjoy them more. I am not interested in a flamefest. Nor do I claim to speak for all trad gamers out there. What this thread is for, is to help gamers with a strong "indie" preference to understand why some trad gamers continue to stay trad and reject "indie" games even after having some experience of playing them. If you enjoy something about trad games that you find lacking in "indie" games (even if you prefer "indie" games on the whole), and if you think this list is missing anything important, please add to it.

edited (but not yet comprehensively)

Reasons why some people consciously stick with trad games despite having tried "indie" games include:-

(1) campaign play, which is generally supported by trad games more than by "indie" games, lets you follow the life of your character over a sustained period of time, and lets you play them in all sorts of situations and with a variety of different experiences of play - you can get to know them as a person and become interested in how they get on

(2) campaign play, which is generally supported by trad games more than by "indie" games, means that when you turn up for your weekly gaming session, you can be confident that you will enjoy what is, for you personally, a tried-and-tested game, rather than finding out on the night whether it is totally opposite to anything you would enjoy

(3) some people like to only have to learn one ruleset (or a small number of rulesets) and to be able to use those same rules again and again once they are familiar with them, rather than having to relearn new rules for every new game; trad games (more so than "indie" games) generally have infinite replayability and are often campaign-based, meaning that the same ruleset is used over and over again

(4) some people even if they are energetic and passionate about playing their characters simply do not want to have to be distracted from thinking as their characters by having to also contribute in an authorial or directorial way to the wider fiction of the game, even if they are perfectly confident in their ability to do so; other people may lack confidence in their ability to contribute outside the agency of their character, or dislike the pressure of doing so in front of a group, or dislike the responsibility entailed in doing so, or may simply lack the energy or inclination to do so;

(5) for some players both the game-world itself (including all the fictional elements constituting it) and the challenges of the game may seem more concrete and more real to a player who is not personally responsible for designing them;

(6) some people just want to roll dice in anger and face a threat in the hope of triumph and glory, and really do not care about some elaborate story or, for that matter, in-depth characterisation, immersion or even anything you might reasonably call "roleplay"

(7) some people may prefer to explore a game-world that is utterly mysterious to them and may find that it feels less mysterious if they know that they have creative power over it outside the agency of their character, or if there are no GM secrets (or player versus player secrets)

(8) some people may prefer to be able to keep player secrets from other players, either out of fear that other people will metagame and use out-of-character knowledge to their characters' advantage, or out of a preference for being mysterious

(9) people like to game with their friends, so they like games that can accommodate their friends, which for some people gives broad-church games a special appeal - everyone can be found a place within the game, everyone can be accommodated, and the social experience of assembling with friends to game trumps any narrowly game-related goal

(10) some people like games with rules for any sort of situation their character might end up in, whether it's an exciting or poignant or dramatic situation, or a so-so situation, just so that (because the rules are there) they (the players) have the freedom to take their characters into those situations and see what happens - so that (in a sense) it feels like real life (because anything can happen)

No doubt this list is incomplete and imperfect. Please add to it.

    \n "Indie" is a bad term for this purpose, yes. "Coherent" is much better, provided one subscribes to the analysis underlying it. I myself prefer "progressive" as the counterpart to "traditional", and "Forgite" if one actually wants to discuss the cultural impact of Forge-originated games as a whole. The advantage of a term that does not attempt an analytical description ("Forge-originated" or "Forgite" does not actually imply anything about content) is that one does not need to talk simultaneously about design analytics and cultural history.

    (And I do, of course, disagree with Jason about whether the tradition exists. One merely needs to take a list of the 20 or so most popular cornerstone ideas in roleplaying games and trace their evolution through the history of the hobby to ascertain that yes, the mainstream of the rpg design field has always been very traditional, with relatively few exceptions. The reason that people perceive e.g. Forgite and Nordic freeform games as different is exactly because they make a break from the tradition and largely start anew from first precepts. It's no different from art history in any other medium.)

    Aside from quibbling with terminology (pointless when everybody actually understands what he's talking about), I don't think that there's anything wrong with Omni's list. Those are all fine reasons to like games that do those things, whether you think that only trad games do them or not.

    For my own part I practically ignore the vast majority of trad games, but not because of structural issues like these; the usual reason is that, as the characterization "traditional" suggests, these games rarely offer me interesting and fresh experiences. There are definitely exceptions, trad games that I feel are provocative and intellectually relevant; they usually achieve this by either being authoritative treatments on their subject matter, or by having literary merit head and shoulders above any alternatives. Examples of trad games where I have no difficulty whatsoever understanding the appeal are things like Paranoia, Pendragon, Ars Magica, Delta Green, JAGS Wonderland, GURPS. There are others as well, of course, to a greater or lesser degree.

    What I wrote above can be expressed more succinctly by saying that I don't like games that are mediocre. That's not so strange, I think, and holds as well for proge games as trad ones - it's not like there aren't many games outside the mainstream tradition that are unimaginative, repetitive and redundant in the field as a whole. I suspect that many "indie enthusiasts" hold similar positions on this, even if their opinions on which games are or aren't relevant to their interests differ.

    \n I think this is a great list, omnifray! And very thorough. (I can't think of anything offhand to add to it, though I hope others will.)

    In my experience, familiarity and nostalgia are also big drivers of people preferring to play trad games. (And, no, I don't look down on that: both are very reasonable things.)

    There are also types of games and traditions which are BETTER suited by "trad" rulesets and playstyles. For instance, I can think of:

    * OSR-style gaming
    * "Illusionist" or "Participationist" play; where an auteur GM provides an experience for a relatively passive group of players looking forward to experiencing the GM's story

    There are probably others. Some simulationist kinds of play might be relevant.

    Having said that, I agree that some of these are misguided/misleading, and that's where the "familiarity" thing comes in.

    While indie gamers, as a group, do tend to play a lot more short-form and one-shot games than the typical "trad" gamer (as far as I can see, anyway), this isn't in any way mandated by the rules of the games they play. D&D is just as well-suited to a one-shot as Dogs in the Vineyard, and, indeed, the majority of well-received and popular "indie" games are designed for campaign play. I see this as more a matter of the play culture than as a feature of design method.

    Issues like "character immersion" are notoriously hard to pin down. The Pundit says that fear mechanics are immersive... others don't want anyone to interfere with their character's mental process at all. Yet others like to immerse in character but can only do so in freeform roleplay (because consulting rulebooks ruins it for them), while yet another group needs an agreed-upon ruleset they trust to fully "immerse" themselves in play.

    I also agree that "the rules cover everything" is a bit of an illusion, bolstered by "rule zero". D&D says that "you can do anything", but this is patently untrue. Just try to create a character who is an aging banker or a 5-yr-old prodigal telekinetic with no understanding or control over his powers... a lot of "indie" designs claim to be more focused in their texts, but, in reality, can be applied freely and evenly to any genre and any situation much more flexibly than a typical trad game.

    \n For me, there are two things which keep me away from trad games *in general* (as opposed to be uninterested in a specific game, which can happen just as easily with any type of game):

    1. A lack of understanding of the goals and procedures of a given game. I'd like to know what I'm playing and why, and how to go about it. How do I play this game *well*, how do I engage with it fully, and make it fun for my fellow players? A lot of "trad" texts are really unhelpful here, but, generally it has more to do with the people:

    2. The common culture of obfuscation which often exists in "trad" gaming. Illusionism (railroading) is a common example, but it extends to other things, even going so far as to shame people who try to talk about the game at all. Articles like "Make sure your players don't find out!" are prime culprits here, and many gamers internalize such knowledge. "A good GM never lets the players in on the secret." "A good player never reveals his plans." And so on.

    Otherwise, I'm happy to play a "trad" game anytime, in the hypothetical. (I wouldn't want to play Rolemaster because it has too many charts and rules; but I wouldn't want to play Burning Wheel for the same reason. It has nothing to do with "trad"/"indie".)

I remember playing in a couple of games where other players revealed to me, in an out-of-character way, their characters' malevolent schemes directed at my character, so that I knew, but my character didn't. I deeply resented that because it spoilt that aspect of the game for me. To be clear, I was perfectly OK with being the target of these schemes within the fiction. What made the out-of-character revelations upsetting for me was that I would far rather be at liberty to think purely as my character, with confidence that I am not influenced subconsciously by my out-of-character knowledge of the fiction, and without having to strain to mentally separate what I know about the fiction from what my character knows. By spilling the beans, the players I refer to made that impossible for me. As a subsidiary/ancillary problem, they also prevented me from engaging with their schemes as an honest challenge of skill, and they made it so that I could not genuinely figure it out for myself, and even if I had already done so prior to them telling me, I would now have looked like a metagaming cheat if I had relied on what I had previously figured out in my in-character decisions.

    If you have never met anyone who wanted to keep in-character secrets secret, then your horizons are exceptionally narrow. I have played countless roleplaying games (especially live roleplaying games) where keeping secrets from other players is the staple of the game (or at least a significant element in it). I do not give a damn hoot whether the person's reason for wanting to keep those secrets is that that person is worried that other players would metagame, or simply prefers to keep an air of mystery about them as an aesthetic or narcissistic preference, or whether their reason is (as mine often is) that they want to inject an element of mystery into the player-on-player interactions in the game for the enjoyment of all.

    By strongly and clearly implying that anyone who wants to keep in-character secrets secret from other players is a dick, you have managed to insult my favourite playstyle and thousands of perfectly sensible, reasonable, amiable, thoughtful and imaginative players who prefer to play that way. Not that I really mind in all honesty; you've done it out of pure ignorance, not malice. But I would invite you to reconsider your position in light of what I've said here, and certainly to reconsider how you phrase it.

    I don't doubt that you have been the target audience of a million and one fatbeardgasms from people salivating over their character sheets, but it is one thing to tell someone who is not playing in the same game as you all your character's secrets, and entirely another thing to inflict purely out-of-character knowledge of those secrets on your fellow players who are playing in that same game, such as to spoil their enjoyment of that game.

    OF COURSE I know that many gamers, including many trad gamers (and probably most "indie" gamers), expect all information about player characters (and in "indie" games sometimes even all information about the fiction as a whole) to be open knowledge among the players even if their characters don't know. OF COURSE I have heard people say things like "we're all mature enough roleplayers that you can trust us to treat out-of-character and in-character knowledge separately". But I don't give a hoot about that; it totally misses the point. The secret-keeping style of play that I love doesn't just give the potentially apprehensive player reassurance that the game will be played with a straight bat. More importantly, much more importantly, it facilitates an authentic, mysterious feel for the game, of a specific kind that games without secret-keeping cannot ever exactly duplicate. It is irrelevant that you may enjoy how your PbtA games enable you to provide what you describe as mystery for the GM; that is an entirely different kind of experience that you are talking about (I do not mean better or lesser, I simply mean different). Games without secret-keeping are NO USE AT ALL from the point of view of producing the particular kind of authentic mystery vibe that many people enjoy and I personally love best (and that many other people either hate or are completely ignorant of).

    Okay. I'll pretend there is a solid distinction to be made - though it's never been demonstrated by anyone and even a quick, simple glance at the hobby from, say, 1977-1987 should bury the idea that there is a "traditional" roleplaying game structure in a shallow grave.
    \nThe point of my list is to demystify a phenomen for the "true believers" of the "indie" RPG scene who perceive "defects" in many of the RPGs the "masses" flock to and are baffled as to why the "masses" continue to flock to them even in some cases after having been "educated" and "shown the light".

    This endeavour does not depend in any way on having a neatly defined category of "trad" or "indie" games based rigorously on history or design-analysis. It is entirely sufficient that we have a loosely defined and generally understood notion of games tending to one end or the other of a spectrum (or several spectrums that tend to correlate with each other). How to help a person who sees all the pluses and none of the minuses of one end of the spectrum, to understand why other people stick to games at the other end of the spectrum?

    It does not matter that some games might be hard to categorise as trad or "indie" - case in point, maybe, Burning Wheel, or Fate, or even PbtA at least from what I've personally seen of it (just the one one-shot). What matters is that there are games that can be considered a "central case" of trad or "indie" and others that seem to cluster around them sharing similar characteristics to a greater or lesser degree, and that we can talk about reasons why people might tend on the whole to be drawn to one cluster or the other (even if some of those people might also like particular games in the other cluster under particular circumstances).

    I propose that the central cases of a "trad" RPG would include AD&D 1st edition, Warhammer Fantasy Roleplay 2nd edition, Dragon Warriors, possibly White Wolf games (such as V:tM or V:tR) or (from reputation) GURPS. I propose that the central cases of an "indie" RPG would include Dogs in the Vineyard, OctaNe, Fiasco or Montsegur 1244, and probably Duty and Honour (the only one of these that I have not played is Dogs in the Vineyard). Games such as Burning Wheel, Fate and PbtA (of which the only one that I have played is PbtA, but I have played Mouse Guard) are more borderline. As I have mentioned, the one game of PbtA that I played did not feel significantly different to trad games to me, in actual play. Whereas OctaNe, Duty and Honour and up to a certain point (though less so) Montsegur 1244 had quite a different feel.

    Since this is all about typical trends, and clusters of games around central cases, so as to give practical insight into human behaviour, any attempt to analyse it in terms of absolutes is misconceived.
