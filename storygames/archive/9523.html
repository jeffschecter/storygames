\n In the Social rules thread, I'm seeing many rules that just couldn't apply to groups I game with.

For example, Wilhelm's rule, "Play stops whenever anyone leaves the table", wouldn't work for our group: Simon needs to go and make dinner and we've got so little time that we couldn't stop every time someone wanted the loo. Similarly, the idea of bringing snacks for yourself just wouldn't work. So many people bring snacks for the table that it'd be rude not to share what you've got.

What rules in that thread wouldn't work for your group and why?

Please use this as a way of speculating on the fascinating diversity of groups, rather than an excuse to say how you hate people who use laptops and you've got no problem killing their character if they do.

    \n I wish I could say "texts are all right, but no phone calls", as one of my players, through no fault of her own, more or less never has a real day off from work - the store manager calls her all the time regarding projects and things to catch up on. She's a manager, and even on her days off, which she'll refer to as a "day off", she'll still go in and pull a couple of hours. So, this is one of those situations where there's nothing to be done; it's just annoying. It doesn't help that she has wicked ADD and can't concentrate for more than two hours per session, but at least the upshot is that she insists on playing as often as possible, which works for everyone.

    I've been able to say "no texting, no phone calls" with previous groups (I mean, if it's an emergency, okay, fine ^_^), but this time I can't, in fairness, make it stick. One thing that has been an ironclad rule for a long time is: spectators should be seen and not heard. For some reason, I tend to meet people who are intrigued by RPGs, but don't actually want to play (or at least, don't want to use rules in any meaningful way). I had a player's girlfriend once ask/insist that there was a flautist in the scene, in-game, and could she portray him, okay, go - and then she starting pretending to play the flute. Thus, when the president of my cooperative asked if he could sit in on a session, I said sure, provided he not speak. He asked a couple of questions early on, and I told him, "You're cool, man, it's just distracting", and that seemed to work out okay.

    I'm going to check out that "Playing in Public" thread - none of us have big enough rooms at the co-op (at least, ones with enough space) that we can play in privacy, so we end up going to the kitchen or the common room instead, which of course means several walk-through interruptions every session (yeah, even in the span of two hours). While the exchange students (who are legion) tend to stop for a moment to ask questions, the Americans living here seem to know that we're "doing something" and act like we've hung up a Do Not Disturb sign at every entrance. We don't tell anyone to piss off, as it's not just our space, but it's far from ideal being there in the first place.

    \n I have no use for social rules, simply because we don't really go too legalistic around here when it comes to entertainment activities in general. Games have rules, but do we really need social rules as well? When I play with the local teenagers, for example, they defer to my age and experience. When we play with my expert roleplayer friends, we communicate like adults. Setting up rules smacks too much like clumsy pre-teen club activities to me. Children might find that rules help them act better, but adults? I'm not seeing it.

    To demonstrate - if a teenager in "my game" constantly plays with his phone, I tell him to put it away and concentrate. I can do this, not because we have some sort of funky tablet nailed to the wall with a "no cellphones, we're roleplaying dammit" rule on it, but simply because I'm the adult in the room and I'm expected to keep the activity focused. Some calls are important, some are not; some people wouldn't fiddle with the phone if they didn't need to, some would; some games are distracted by the phone, some are not. I have no reason whatsoever to make "no phones while playing" a rule when reason alone guides us in what's appropriate and what's not. The same goes for most of the social rules people have discussed here.

    Might just be that we play in a too relaxed manner. Perhaps a really fast and intense gaming style would benefit from rules. To me making rules for when somebody is speaking in-character sounds like the group has been clenching their buttocks too hard, but surely it must be an useful rule for somebody.

    I think, by and large, what people were referring to as rules in the other thread and here are more along the lines of "this is the kind of behavior we as a group do or do not encourage." They say "no cellphones," meaning that they personally turn off their own phones when they play and say -- or at least WANT to say -- "Could you please put that away and concentrate?" when the kid next to them is more engrossed in his texting than in the activity around the table.     For my part anyway, they are social rules in the way that people not tramping through the flowerbed to knock on the front door is a social rule: there's no security guard standing there to enforce a big "KEEP OFF THE GRASS" sign and put an armlock on anyone who steps off the concrete, you just don't invite over people who'd treat your prized gladiolas like a mosh pit.
