A Different Take on Classes (-or- Does Everyone Need to Use the Same Mechanic?)

Blue
March 2006 edited March 2006 in Story Games
Here's a little idea that struck me while reading through some other threads....

Imagine a game where your character's class/profession/archetype/whatever determined not only what they were best at, but also the mechanic they used to determine success.

A player running a Stage Magician might use cards.
A player running a Game Show Host might use a spinner card (ala board games).
A player running a Gambler might use dice.
etc.

Or perhaps it's all dice, but different classes use slightly different rolls. Most players roll d8s and total, but players playing characters who are Servitors of Chaos roll exploding d6s and add (exploding == if you roll a 6, re-roll and add). [Impact for this example: In general, players rolling d8s get an average of 4.5 per roll with a max of 8. Rolling exploding d6s gives an average of 4.2 but have no upper max....)

Thoughts??

    That said, consider three possible mechanics to be used in conjunction:
    Standard poker deck, Aces=1, Jack, Queen, King=11, 12, 13 respectively. Average result of 7, equal chance of all values 1-13.
    2d6: Average result of 7, no chance of 1 or 13, middle values more likely than extreme values (symmetric).
    2d4 exploding: Average result of about 6.7, no chance of 1, no upper limit to values.

    Are these 'balanced'? How fair beyond this could you go and stay balanced?

    I have an idea that's simmered on the back burner for a while - different 'classes' of worldwalkers - people who can move between/interact with alternate universes.

    Technos (the guys who use technology) use dice. This is the most reliable in terms of probability, but doesn't have much opportunity for 'surprise moments'.

    Arcanos (the guys who use magic) use Tarot cards, less reliable in terms of probability, but the Major Arcana offer chances for big effects.

    Numinos (the guys who use freaky one-off powers) use a mechanic where they have a copy of a book (which symbolizes your character) - you flip the book at random and plunk a finger into it. Whatever the first noun or verb is after your finger determines the score (by letter count - i.e. 'disaster' is 8), but the nature of the word can drastically alter the result ('disaster' being a good example of one such change).

    The main issue that I see with multiple resolution methods is that you have to ensure that the probabilities are at least close, if not perfectly in line. Otherwise you're putting the players at a disadvantage to one another. And that sort of analysis is hard to do.

    The idea of multiple systems in a single game is intriguing, and you can do really cool stuff with it on the design concept level, but I wouldn't go any further. Unless all the mechanics are very simple the buy-in for new players rises exponentially the more of them you have, and the harder it is to "master" the game (in the sense of players coming to understand fully what the system does for them and how to use it to get the most they can out of it).

    Having a storytelling game with with distinctly divided authority over the past/present/future is awesome, though. Add in a rotation schedule for scene framing and you're good to go.

    It's a sweet and intoxicating lie. Like all lies you can use it to a degree, but be careful not to be swallowed in.

    Once you define to yourself "Different aspects of the game use different mechanics(Or Gamist mini-games)", you're very close to the point where you get a new mechanic for each small detail, breaking apart game cohesion and bringing up overhead.

    Your examples are simple and straightforward, each Class/Aspect merely has a different randomizer, little change except balance and mood. If you create whole new sub-systems you'll need to be careful. Extra careful if your design goals give entry to such sub-systems.

    Think on your end result and reverse-engineer from there.
