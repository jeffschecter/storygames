    \n Eclipse Phase: Beautiful setting, traditional ruleset. Freely available online. Read it, then decide whether to buy.

    Burning Wheel: Ruleset that superficially seems traditional, but really isn't: for example, lots of stuff about characters following beliefs. Complex and great.

    Fiasco: Not out of print, as far as I'm aware, and there is information about it on the Bully Pulpit Games site. Superb. GMless. Jason left a toy cockroach in my bed once.

    Man. Chris, you could hardly have picked three less comparable games. If you like trad games or the setting, get Eclipse Phase. If you're buying for the ruleset and want something non-traditional, get Burning Wheel. If you're looking for something rules-light and storytelly, get Fiasco.

    \n The rules are way better than Shadowrun, though they are similar. They are pretty crunchy and traditional, and it's not particularly story-gameish, as you say. There's also a lot of rules, and not all of them really have a point beyond attempting to be granular and realistic.

    Personally, I wouldn't run it, and I wouldn't automatically sign up for a game of it, but with the right folks it seems like it could be a lot of fun.

    If I could figure out a good way to run it with FATE, I would.

    Burning Wheel isn't everyone's cup of tea, but I think it's awesome. Tolkien-inspired character-driven fantasy roleplaying. All the rules are designed to drive the characters to do interesting stuff. A+. Get the Gold Edition or whatever it's called; $25 and all the rules you'll ever need. The Revised books are great; I own them all, but if you're just now getting into it I'd suggest getting the Gold Edition.

    If you don't own Fiasco, you should. It's an extremely smart game. It's a good game for people who aren't really "gamers", too.

    So out of the three, I recommend you hunt down Fiasco. Zero prep, plays out in two or three hours, nonstop fun.

    \n Circles are awesome. Basically as player can bring an NPC into the game by making a circles test. This NPC must be related to a characters previous lifepath (A Knight, would know Knights, Pages, Lady's in Waiting). The GM sets the obstacle based on some factors (time, likely hood, station, etc), the test is then made. NOW the test doesn't say if the NPC exist, but the NPC's disposition to the player. If a player fails a roll, maybe the guy he is looking for actually hates me or is working against him...maybe its a trap, and someone noticed the players looking for a dude. It is fantastic, one of my favorite game mechanics ever (as a GM and a player).

    Relationships are also very cool. Initially they are bought during character generation for a number of points. The cost of relationship is based off the NPCs importance to the setting/world situation, and can be made cheaper by making the relationship an enemy, a forbidden relationship, family, etc. You can also gain new relationships by circling up the same hombre enough times.

    \nThis sounds like a hack of Warhammer or Burning Wheel, for sure. They're both gritty fantasy, 100%.

    Burning Wheel is one of my favorite games OF ALL TIME. It's just excellent if you've never played it before. It is crunchy, but I think on a par with 4E and other fantasy systems. The concepts are easy to get, but the rules can take a few tries to get down cause there's a lot of them. I think Burning Wheel Gold is a must if you're getting it... cause it's basically the newer, cleaner, smoother version.

    Honestly, there's a lot to unpack about Burning Wheel. The reasons why I think it might work for your zombie game idea is when you said "fraught with moral choices, and with an investigation in-between". Burning Wheel hits the sweet spot with player agency, character development, and meaningful choices. Beliefs, instincts and traits help make that happen. So, for a zombie game, where fast paced character development and tests of moral fortitude are at the forefront of the genre, that seems really fun actually! It might not hit the horror spot as well, but that might be something you can add. It certainly can have brutal combat... you get hurt, you're hurt for a long time. And combat is a mini game unto itself.

    The only other thing I would suggest is looking at zombie games that exist, though I can't think of any off the top of my head, I know there's a few floating around that people really like.
