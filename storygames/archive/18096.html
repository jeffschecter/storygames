\n Jason (and everybody else who's played Durance more times than me), what happens when one Notable is bound by an Oath that the events of play later make it impossible to break?
What specifically happened tonight at our game was that the Governor was sworn to never allow any harm to come to Mr. Valentine, the Dimber Damber (who, as we discovered, was actually the His Highness's secret son); but later the Dimber Damber was murdered (by a Bolter for very personal reasons, though with help from the majority of the military officers), making it practically impossible for the Governor to break his oath. In fact, the Governor had - a couple scenes earlier, and only after being confronted by the Judge Advocate about the matter - announced a plan to put Mr. Valentine in confinement to better protect him, but the critical Question about the murder attempt was posed before the Governor's plan had any chance to come to fruition.
We were confused that the Governor had no possibility to break his oath now, thus de-protagonizing the character. In fact, we made the ad hoc ruling to proceed just as he had broken it, so that he could now fade into the background after his player performed some highly consequential action (she altered one colonial survey parameter). But how would you have handled this?

    \n I'm with Praion - just because the Governor didn't harm the Dimber Damber directly doesn't mean the Dimber Damber wasn't harmed. Oath broken. Oaths are framed in an active way "I will never" but the practical emphasis in on never, not I.

    But in a rare case where it becomes impossible to break an oath (I can't think of any but I'm sure it is possible), I'd call that a lucky break for the character. At that point what is most interesting? Are they still compelling to play, even insulated from transgression? If so, awesome. It doesn't really hurt the game. If not, fade them out just like you would had they broken their oath.
