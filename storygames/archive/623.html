    May 2006 edited May 2006
    Yes, I think Emily and Jason are right.

    Your status in society needn't be related to the status you display when you act. So you can have a nervous Dog or an aggressive peasant. Those strange combinations, such as high-social status with low-acting status, can make great characters.

    I think it's important we don't confuse high status with aggressive. You can do high-status aggressively:

    TOWNIE: "I demand you sanctify my stillborn baby!"

    Or quietly:

    TOWNIE: [walks up to Dog. Silently hands baby to him.] "Sanctify him."

    And equally you can be aggressive, and even violent, and low status:

    TOWNIE: "My baby! You've got to sanctify him! I'll...I don't know...I'll kill him if you don't!"

    And - having thought about Emily's post - you can reverent and forgiving and very high status:

    DOG: "Come here. Kneel. [Touches forehead] You are forgiven."

    Huh. I can't do examples today. *

  • ptevis
    May 2006
    I think people are getting hung up on the idea of status as a static quality. Johnstone wants to talk about about it as a dynamic property. This is because he's not as concerned with characters as he is with relationships. So status has nothing to do with who you are but rather with how you relate to people in the scene.

    Personally, I find this focus on the transactional nature of personal interactions useful for portraying NPCs. I agree with with his contention that every statement should either raise or lower status, as this leads to dynamic and interesting exchanges. I could see using this as a guide for Duels of Wits in Burning Wheel for example.
  • Jason_Morningstar
    May 2006
    As far as games that address Johnstone-style status I think that's an empty field - you can choose any point on the continuum of status in any game as far as I can tell. You can be told to dominate someone in The Roach, and do it from a low status position - it isn't the obvious choice, which is what makes it so delicious.

    It's easier to see status as dynamic when you introduce a third (and fourth) character into the scene.

    • Graham
      May 2006 edited May 2006
      Does anyone get the feeling we're using the term "status" without really understanding how it applies to RPGs? (I'm including myself in this).

      High status, for example, could mean the player himself doing high status things ("Give me the dice! Now!"); or a player describing his character playing high status ("I make him kneel at my feet!"); or the system allowing a player to do high status things (such as ignore other people's suggestions), or the system allowing a character to do high status things (such as kill them).

      One major problem is that Impro often describes status in a behavioural way (e.g. holding eye contact). But this doesn't quite translate into a gaming situation: are we talking about the player saying "I hold eye contact" or the player actually acting out the eye contact? It's very different.

      We've taken this theatrical term, status, and we're trying to discuss it, but it works in a very different way from on stage.

      Graham
