\n Reversed Engineer. Hereto referred to as the Rev/eng contest.

So last year I ran this contest and it worked out pretty well (you can view the remains @ http://www.kevinallenjr.com/reverseengineer/reversed.html). I had a good time envisioning the contest and running it. That was a year ago.

Now I\xe2\u20ac\u2122m in a bit of a quandary. I don\xe2\u20ac\u2122t know if should run it again, and if I do, how. There are a lot of variables to consider. Sooooo I\xe2\u20ac\u2122m putting it out in the public forum to see what people think. I\xe2\u20ac\u2122m not taking votes, I\xe2\u20ac\u2122ll be the final arbiter of wether or not I run the contest again, but I want to do something people are going to enjoy. Clearly I can\xe2\u20ac\u2122t please everyone, but I can certainly hear what people have to say.

So here's some questions to think about and respond to-

1. When would be a good time to hold this contest. Right now? November? Later in the year? Not again, thank you?

2. Would you like to retain the same format as last year. People make character sheets, then differant people make games.

3. Would you like to take last years format and twist it a little, but generally retain the same notion (find an interesting starting point for a game, develop material then switch designers). Keeping everything in the realm of "regular" rpg design.

4. Would you like to do something that is the same idea as above, but really (and i mean REALLY) push the bounds and get experimental with it? Perhaps by starting the design process in a very UN-rpg place.

let me know

That way, there's a much, much higher chance that most-or-all of the first round submissions make it into a game, since each submission goes to three participants for round two.

    \n I think that one woman's inspirational sheet is another man's nightmare. The trick is to match up a sheet with someone who is inspired to make a game from it.

    Since this is a contest with points, I think we can use them a few different ways to balance it all out.

    Method 1: Silent auction

    Everyone gets 50 points. Once the sheets are posted, designers can bid on the sheets (maybe on a web page?). Unspent points translate into a bonus to your final score in the contest later *if* you submit a complete game.

    (optional wrinkle: everyone who spends 50 points on a sheet gets to design a game for that sheet, regardless of how many designers that means are designing a game for the same sheet.)

    Method 2: Countdown auction

    Everyone gets 50 points. Once the sheets are posted, a thread is opened. First person to post claiming a sheet gets that sheet. During the first 30 minutes, it costs 50 points to claim a sheet. The next 30 minutes it costs 49 points, etc. Anyone claiming a sheet after 25 hours gets that sheet for free (and 50 points added to their score if they submit a complete game). (If we wanted 2 days instead of 25 hours, we could change the time period to every 60 minutes, say, or give people 100 points.)

    Method 3: Secret auction

    Everyone gets 50 points. You submit a list of sheets, and how much you would spend on each (you can leave as many blank as you wish). Highest bidder for each sheet gets it, with ties broken by a) whoever submitted a sheet gets priority over someone who didn't; b) whoever was outbid on more other sheets gets priority over someone who didn't get outbid as much; c) random draw (or they both get the sheet? Worked OK for Jonathan's sheet last year, but it means possibly more orphans.) In phase two, if you didn't get a sheet, and there are sheets left over, you bid on how many points you *want* for designing a sheet for it. Ties are broken similarly, with lower bidders winning this time.

    Optional wrinkle to the above three methods: People who submit a sheet get 50 points, and people who do not submit a sheet (i.e. the rest of the world) get 25 points (giving submitters 'first dibs' on the sheets).

    One advantage of all these methods is that they serve a dual function as votes for the sheets for the 'Most Inspirational' (highest price) and 'Most Intimidating' (lowest price)

    Method 4: Voting

    Everyone submits an ordered list of 5 sheets they want to design a game for. Sheets are distributed to designers similarly to the 'secret auction' method above.

    ----

    I'm sort of partial to the 'Countdown auction' method as it seems the least amount of work for the organizer. But I'll volunteer to help count votes or sort out scores if people like one of the other methods better.
