\nDammit, Morningstar, I did not need another project!

But for when I have time, here are some thoughts on Helensville, a play-by-blog colonial New Zealand diary game.

From Jason's thread:\n
Posted By: MikeRM

I want a game set in this under-appreciated time:The mid-nineteenth century.

I want a game built around this under-utilized genre:The colonist's diary.

I want a game that capitalizes on this common social circumstance:Helping and/or feuding with one's neighbors.

I want a game that helps players learn about this important topic through play:Agriculture.

I want a game focused on this fascinating but ignored location:New Zealand. (Ahem.)

I want a game that addresses this uniquely human (but sadly neglected) circumstance:Being a stranger in a strange land.

I want a game that evokes this little-used emotional response:Coyness.

I want a game that helps solve this problem at my table:I don't have a table. (That's the problem I want to solve, not a refusal of the question.)
\nSo. Helensville is a town, a real New Zealand town, where my father's ancestors on his mother's side settled in (if I remember correctly) the 1860s. The ancestors themselves were an interesting lot. One of them ran away to sea at the age of 9 and changed his name. We're still not certain what his real name was, because as he lay dying he reportedly said to his children, "You think my name's Channon, don't you? That's all you know."

My mother now lives near Helensville and is volunteering in the town archives (yes, this is also partly inspired by the "If your mom made a game" thread, thank you, Matthijs). She was telling me the other day about some of the old diaries they have there - she's cataloging their collection. She also found a hilarious book of guidance for being the "Perfect Woman" which I've offered to scan because it sounds like it deserves to be on the Net somewhere.

So in the game Helensville, you play a colonist who has settled in New Zealand, the back end of nowhere, in the middle of the 19th century. You keep a diary. In play, this takes the form of a blog, and you blogroll the "diaries" of the other people who are playing in the same game. (Possibly everyone who ever plays the game, since I don't see it becoming anything more than niche.) Anything those other people establish in their diaries as having happened has happened, and you can comment on it or not as you please; you may have a different perspective on it, including that they lied about it in their diaries, but it did happen in some way. As players you know what is in the other players' diaries, but as characters you generally don't unless someone says something like, "I showed Lettie my diary today, and she was most shocked at my description of Mr Bennett". Then whoever's playing Lettie knows, in character, what was in at least that entry.

You have a game book with samples of real 19th-century diary entries, some guidance on being the Perfect Woman (or the Ideal Man if we can find such a volume), the names of some actual families who lived in the town, and other such background material. You have, probably, an event table which gives you things like crop failures or bumper crops, animals giving birth, the river flooding and the like, for which you roll dice when you want to. Or maybe you just pick one.

Remember, if someone says in their diary that the river flooded, the river flooded.

The aim of the game is to play off each other's diary entries and achieve certain goals which you probably declare in your diary using some formula. "I am determined that I shall marry Archie by the end of the Spring", that kind of thing. All of the blog entries are in character at all times; I suppose you might communicate among yourselves by email OOC, but the game shouldn't require it.

You can help or feud with your neighbors, or both. You are strangers in a strange land, thrown together by circumstance. The light is different, the trees are different, the birds are different. You're trying to live as much as possible as if you were in England, but you aren't.

Indirectness, even coyness, is encouraged in the diary entries.

    \n I have huge admiration for my intrepid ancestors. Can you imagine leaving your homeland in Cornwall because you're a devout Quaker and refuse to mine lead for use in war? Can you imagine a six-month sea voyage in a 1m x 1m x 2m box? Can you imagine arriving and finding that the land you'd "bought" in London is in the possession of fierce tribesmen who don't want to part with it, and the "city" you've emigrated to is a hill covered in scrub? Can you imagine being an 11- or 12-year-old girl, alone in the house when a party of Maori warriors turn up and want a meal? (They eat and move on without harming you, but you don't know that ahead of time.) Can you imagine running away to sea at the age of 9?

    All of these things actually happened to (different) ancestors of mine.

    \n That's a reasonable question. I have several responses, the first of which is that I'm not sure that's necessarily a problem. After all, people are people in all times and places.

    The second would be that providing seed events and issues that are typical of the time and place would deal with that to a degree.

    It's also possible that an outline of key events, local and world, might be of benefit, though I'm not sure how important they would be to people living in a small town. I mean, if it was round the period of World War I, obviously, it would be important, but I don't know that there was a lot happening in the 1860s that would have been that important to a relatively isolated farm community. The Waikato wars would need a mention, though.

    If I ever do this project I'll probably discover a completely different answer to your question again in the course of doing the research.
