    \n I'll copy some of the text here. This first bit is lifted pretty straight outta the previous thread:

    Suffering harm
    When a player is faced with injury, we use this move:

    Endure calamity
    When you face immediate and unavoidable injury or distress roll. On a 10+, both; on a 7-9, choose one:
    -It doesn\u2019t hamper you, you can ignore it for now
    -you\u2019ll find out later that things are less dire than it seems; you will recover without complication
    On a 6 or less, you suffer the calamity in full. If it\u2019s a lasting thing, make note of it below:

    An example from play:
    GM: The ogre\u2019s club falls hard on your shoulder, roll to Endure.
    Player: *rolls a 7* I want to maintain my position, so I\u2019m going to choose the first option: it doesn\u2019t hamper me.
    GM: Great, so you can fight through the pain for now, but when things calm down you\u2019ll discover that your collarbone is fractured. Be sure to note down that injury.

    Some players have resources to help them better endure. Warriors for instance get this move:

    Endurance [ ] [ ] [ ]
    When you Endure calamity, you may choose to mark slashes through any number of endurance boxes and add that amount to your roll. Each box may hold up to two slashes (the second slash forming an \u2018X.\u2019)
    When you have a good meal and some time to rest, recover all endurance boxes with a single slash through them. Boxes with an \u2018X\u2019 may only be recovered by extraordinary means.

    Wearing armor or whatever acts the same as above, gives you more bonuses to spend when you Endure.

    \nThe healing or deterioration of injury is handled purely within the fiction. If a dire injury goes without medical attention, it\u2019s sure to worsen (possibly triggering another Endure calamity roll.) This is true for situations up to and including character death. The bottom line is, we follow through with the injury in whatever way the fiction demands.

    An example:
    GM: Your shoulder is now clearly inflamed and broken, if you don\u2019t stop and tend to it, it will get worse. (soft GM move, deliver bad news about their injuries, or something)
    Player: I have to continue on to get out of here by nightfall, I can\u2019t deal with it now.
    GM: In that case, you definitely feel an infection starting to take root, roll to Endure.


    Monsters (shorthand for \u201canything you fight\u201d) have a set of moves that describe the type of harm they might inflict on the players, alongside other things they might do. A monster\u2019s list of moves is the primary place to adjust the danger that they pose to the players. You can add tags to a monster move to affect the way players interact with it.

    Tags:
    Vicious [x]: -x to Endure calamity
    Piercing: ignores armor
    Malignant: the harm done is difficult to heal
    Mortal: life-ending shit if not swiftly dealt with

    An example Monster move-list:

    Wasteland scavenger
    snatch something from their grasp
    stab at their guts (piercing, malignant)
    use a weird gadget

    The GM carefully selects monster moves to control the pacing of a scene, not necessarily to act optimally on the Monster\u2019s behalf.

    \nInflicting harm

    When a player wields a weapon to inflict harm on something, they just describe their attack, within the purview of that weapon. \u201cI bring my sword down on their head,\u201d \u201cI chop at it\u2019s tail with my axe.\u201d Weapons have tags that provide guidelines for the type of things you can do with them:

    Weapon tags
    Vicious: Requires one extra defense box to shrug off
    Stunning: A blow to the head can disorient or strike unconscious
    Quick: Counters quick defenses
    Piercing: Punches right through armor
    Forceful: Counters forceful defenses
    Defensive: When you Cross blades with a defensive weapon, take +1 forward to Endure
    Nimble:Works well for disarming, or other tricky maneuvers; can\u2019t be forceful
    Slow: Can\u2019t strike quickly

    When a player engages someone in armed combat, we use this move:

    Cross Blades
    When you engage a foe in armed combat, roll\u2026
    ...if you\u2019re inflicting permanent damage, -1
    ...if you\u2019re delivering a mortal blow, -2
    success: make an attack with your weapon, and choose one:
    -make your attack forceful, quick or vicious
    -avoid your opponent\u2019s attack
    partial success: You make an attack with your weapon and expose yourself to whatever move your opponent makes.

    \n Monsters may have defenses they can use to protect themselves from certain types of attacks. Defenses are short descriptive phrases like \u201cCovered in armor,\u201d or \u201cForceful blocker.\u201d Each defense has a rating that indicates how many times they can be used. In this way, they act as a sort of specialized HP. If a player swings their sword at a monster that is \u201ccovered in armor,\u201d the GM may reveal that defense and mark off one use to shrug off the attack.

    Some monsters may have more powerful or absolute defenses, indicated by an asterisk. For example, an ancient dragon might have the defense, \u201cthick, hardened scales*\u201d These defenses are absolute and can be used any number of times to avoid harm. To defeat such a monster is to understand and find a way around their defenses.

    Some example defenses for humans:
    Agile dodger
    Quick hands
    Master of (swords, axes, spears, etc.)
    Forceful deflector
    Armored
    Strong bones
    Huge and stalwart
    Resistant to poisons

    Some example defenses for other creatures:
    Thick hide
    Decentralized nervous system
    Iron skin
    Made of stone
    Regenerates flesh
    Made of fire
    Gigantic

    -Armored [ ] [ ]

    \n It strikes me that there are various ways you can handle descriptive damage - one that I found incredibly effective in the past was not to tell people 'you have a broken collar bone' but to tell them what they feel - to describe the pain, the difficulty they are finding when they try and move in particular ways and so on.

    Telling someone "your chest feels wet and tight and its hard to breath, there is a stabbing pain there and you feel dizzy and sick. As you try to get a breath bloody froth spurts from your mouth" is going to have a rather different effect to telling a player - oh you just got a punctured lung. Maybe if they get medical help have the doctor tell them that, but why should you?

    Changing the perspective you narrate from to the pov of the players character can be interesting...

    Having said that, when I did run that sort of system players tended to freak out and panic whenever they got hurt.

    \n I'm glad to see people are thinking about my proposed hack, and finding ways to apply it. Have you used this in play, or is it just brainstorming/design so far?

    You've simplified the harm move (to two options), which I like in some ways and dislike in other ways. (Mostly, the bad is that I suspect the choice will be too obvious in many situations: it won't be all that much of a choice in a lot of typical scenarios, and that could make the move less interesting.)

    One thing your version does not allow is for a chance of death (or, alternatively, it leaves it entirely up to the MC). This could be a bug or a feature, but some more thought about how that works could be helpful. If someone shoots an arrow into my back, how do we decide whether it's deadly or not? Things could get a bit weird if I knew, for example, that the MC wouldn't kill my character on a miss, but *might* if I roll a partial hit and choose to continue fighting.

    I've never got much use out of tags, but if they work for you, that's great. How do you use them in play? (My issue is that tags are purely narrative color, which seems unnecessary to me if I already have narrative color to begin with. If I narrate someone using a huge, heavy mace, what does it add to the game to say that it has the tag "unwieldy" or "messy", for example?)

    The 'cross blades' move is VERY interesting! That's some new technology I haven't seen before, and it's got some very fascinating potential applications to *World moves. I'll definitely be thinking about that again! Once more, have you played with it already, or not? (I'd be tempted to add in a "secure an advantage, take +1forward" into there, so you can position yourself for a killing strike before you actually do it.)

    Finally, I think this version of the descriptive harm could really benefit from a "fallout"-style move. Something you roll at the end, when the smoke clears, to see just how bad the damage is. That could be a good place to handle odds of death, if it's a feature of your system.

    (And that's a solid observation about how the more unclear you make injuries/damage and the more you describe them, the more "scary" it seems, and the more "screen time" it will take up. An important consideration when making any kind of injury/harm system!)

    \nIn this case, I want tags to have more mechanical weight. With the way defenses work, a weapon's tags mean a great deal. For example,

    A master fencer might have the defenses: Quick evasion [ ] [ ] [ ], Armored [ ] [ ]
    If you...
    ...strike at them with a hammer (forceful, stunning), they can avoid the attack by marking off a box of "quick evasion"
    ...slash them with a longsword (vicious), they can avoid the attack, but they must mark off two boxes of an applicable defense, (because of the vicious tag.)
    ...stab them with a rapier (quick, piercing), they can do nothing to avoid the attack, because it's tags make the defenses inapplicable. The quick tag counter's their quick evasion and the piercing tag punches right through the armor. So the attack stands, and the fencer takes a stab wound, the severity determined by the intent stated when triggering "cross blades."\n

    \n Thinking more about this, \n

    If someone shoots an arrow into my back, how do we decide whether it's deadly or not?
    \nI think it works basically the same as when using HP. Whether the GM deals damage using numbers or description, the GM has to follow their principals to decide how deadly something is. A GM can make HP totally meaningless if they're not following their principles in deciding the numbers. A GM following their principles can't just kill you capriciously. We're relying on those principals in the same way as with HP.

    I think the key for managing this descriptively is solid communication about the risks and potential outcomes of injury. If death is on the table, the player needs to know that when making their decisions. A revision of the Endure duress move with that in mind:\n
    When you face immediate and unavoidable injury or distress, ask the GM exactly how serious it is, and roll. On a 10+, both; on a 7-9, choose one:
    It doesn\u2019t hamper you, you can ignore it for now
    you\u2019ll find out later that things are less dire than it \tseems; you will recover without complication

    On a 6 or less, you suffer the calamity in full. If it\u2019s a lasting thing, make note of it below:

    \nSo now between "ask the GM exactly how serious it is" and rolling is where we spend our HP/Armor points. And we know exactly what choice we're making on a 7-9.

    As far as the NPC stuff goes, I think I'm content to can the symmetry and settle for something move abstract on that end. Maybe using a few fixed levels of damage with fixed levels of harm that you check off.

    \n I'm imagining Offer counsel as Gandalf in Theoden's court. It's like a better, but much more limited version of parley.

    You don't need any leverage here, but the trigger is trying to be more restrictive.
    It requires that you "offer honest wisdom" and that your counsel must be delivered in the form of "what would best serve" the person you counsel.

    It's useful when you know more than someone, or are dealing with someone stubborn or foolish.

    An example from our play test:

    The party is aboard a ship and has discovered a treasonous plot. The sage believes that the best course of action is to turn around and return to port, disrupting the traitors' plan.

    The sage tells this to the captain. The captain is quite reluctant to abandon the voyage, not wishing to lose money. Now, wisdom dictates that it's more important to turn around and prevent this coup, but the captain's judgement is compromised by fear or greed.

    The sage rolls to Offer counsel, gets a 7. So the captain reluctantly accepts the sage's wisdom, but will hold the sage responsible for anything that goes wrong. The sage asks me, "how might this counsel lead to ruin?"

    I say, "this plan hinges on whether or not you can bring the treason to light and hold the traitors to task. If their plot succeeds, they'll definitely come after the captain." So now the sage knows what they have to do to make sure their counsel doesn't backfire.
