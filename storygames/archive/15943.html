\n Logistically one of my biggest problems with That's Drama is in order for others to playtest it, I need to get a deck in their hands, which is going to cost me about $20-25, which severely limits the amount of plyatesting I can do and is super-extra frustrating if they never get around to playtesting it (fewer than half of the people I sent decks to actually playtested it)

I really like the idea of card-based RPG's and storytelling games though. There's just something compelling about them. (I even collect them - Everway, Misery Bubblegum, Once Upon A Time, Torg - and just got Saga: Dragonlance the other day)

So, little survey: if I made a storytelling game with fewer cards, who would be willing to print out and cut up ~40 cards? Or take ~40 index cards and write a couple sentences on each? In order to playtest a 1-2 hour introductory improv-storytelling game? "Like Once Upon A Time but 'good' (and setting-agnostic)" is what I'm shooting for.

    \n How about this: Check out the Print-and-Play version of the game YOMI.

    Plastic sleeves for CCGs cost about $5 for 100 on eBay or other online places.
    Most folks have old CCG cards laying around, and if they don't you can get a pile of cheap Lands or whatever on ebay for like a buck for a million.

    So make a PDF version of "That's Drama" that is CCG card-or-less in size (less is fine, see next)

    Folks can then print them out, cut them up and stick them in CCG-card-backed-sleeves. If there's a lot of cards (how many does the original set have?) people can have their friends come over with scissors, spend five minutes cutting/sleeving cards together, then play.

    That method is extremely cheap, most gamers already have the resources needed to do this (or, like me, just need to buy a few packs of sleeves from eBay or http://www.coolstuffinc.com ), win-win. I'd even suggest going this route with the "final product".

    Anyway, if you do that (and I'd be interested in the original set, not necessarily a cut-down version), I'd give it a shot.

    -Andy

    \n I've been playtesting multiple card-based games. Here are things that I've done:

    The Quiet Year
    The Quiet Year uses oracle-like cards to help determine how a year in a post-collapse community develops. It uses 52 cards. For playtesting, you just play with a standard deck of playing cards, and you look up each entry in an oracle list. The game divides the deck into seasons, and for each season(/suit) you have a list of the 13 cards and what they mean.

    Vulture City
    I've created print & play cards. For my own set, I printed them, and put them in sleeves (using junk magic cards in the sleeves, behind the paper slips, for added stiffness). When I send others the playtest copy, it'll be print & play style too.

The other one uses custom cards and that's been a bit more of a challenge. There were a couple of kind souls who actually went to the trouble of printing out and assembling a deck on their end, although I have no idea how they went about it. I actually did something rather like Andy's suggestion when I constructed a deck for playtesting - although local game designer Philip duBarry gave me this handy link some time ago:

    PnP - Making Cards

    I haven't tried it myself yet, but the results are impressive. FYI - I actually did have Gamecrafter whip up a prototype for me of my game and it turned out rather well. I think the cost was around $15 or so.
