\n Hey all,.
Christmas is coming and I was thinking about getting some kind of game for my three year old. He seems like he is ready to handle some simple rules and basic turn-taking. I realize there probably isn't anything out there that is approachable for him that I would really find engaging (except in the way that playing with my son is always engaging, of course), but I'm wondering if there's anything out there besides Candyland and Chutes and Ladders that I should be looking at.
Thanks,

    \nFamily Passtimes makes a number of great family board games targeted at the younger set. My daughter (6) still shows little inclination to follow rules or take turns, so I play much more freeform games with her, basically collaborative storytelling. I'll tell a story, then ask her a leading question to get her to start telling the story for a while.

    My daughter watches me play games, and sometimes makes up her own versions of them, which I play with her. We made up a version of How to Host a Dungeon called How to Host a Princess Party. I made a simple d6 table of possible guests to the Princesses birthday party. She rolls, and then adds that guest to the party. She routinely fudges the rolls to get the guest she wants.

    After watching me play Race for the Galaxy with her godfather, she created a game called "Fortunes and Misfortunes". We take turns drawing from the deck and placing the cards in a row. Each card adds a fortune or misfortune to the story. For example, the Rebel Outpost is a castle that the Princess visits (a fortune). The Alien Scout Ship is a "bad spider" misfortune. Oddly enough, almost all my cards turn out to be misfortunes and hers turn out to be fortunes.

    \n Have you checked BoardGameGeek? There are probably a bunch of GeekLists there of "good games for small children". German game companies still seem to produce a lot of kids' games that aren't just rehashes of old chestnuts, and you can get a lot of them from importers like Funagain.

    I've also read a good essay (can't remember where) on the merits of Candyland \u2014\xa0despite the fact that it is pointless as an actual game, being entirely chance-driven, it has value as a meta-game, teaching little kids the idea of what a game is: taking turns, doing what the cards tell you to do, winning and losing, dealing with someone else winning. It's kind of like the "tutorial level"

    \n Another quick suggestion from ages ago, I think by Mike Holmes:

    IIRC, he'd hacked together some kind of adventure game, and was playing with his little one. He used some kind of die with letters on it. When the little one wanted to have their character do something that was in question, the die was rolled and if the young'un could think of a word that started with that letter, the task succeeded.

    I thought it was pretty nifty, especially for younger kids.

    \n Jadyn (my just-4yo) and I play a very stripped down version of D&D 4e. She picks out a few of my dungeon tiles and set up a map. We each pick one of my minis and put it on the map. We take turns moving until we are adjacent to each other. We take turns hitting each other: I tell her what she needs to hit, and she rolls the die (usually a d8--she likes those). If she hits, she rolls another die for damage (usually a d6--gotta keep it fair). The winner gets to play with the treasure, i.e., my pile of mult-hued glass beads.

    Posted By: ElizabethUno does what Candyland does, only teaches number and symbol recognition as well. Plus, it's cheaper and you can get it in all kinds of wacky themes (Nintendo Uno, Pokemon Uno, whatever).
    \nIn my experience preschool kids have a hard time fanning out and sorting through cards. I tried playing Uno with my daughter (who, I believe, was 5 or 6 at the time) and she quickly got overwhelmed by the number of cards in her hand. She eventually stopped looking through her hand for the card she needed and resorted to drawing more cards, which just accelerated the problem.

    They can manage a game like War or Slamwich where you just lay down your top card each turn, but an Uno hand would get too large for them to handle.
