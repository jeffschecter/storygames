\n Okay, after reading a bunch of games lately (Over the Edge, Marvel Codex, Lady Blackbird, Apocalypse World, Barbarians of Lemuria, Seven Leagues, Poison\u2019d, Little Fears, PDQ, Spellbound Kingdoms) I've come up with a system. Not all the details are worked out, but I think I'm on to something. Really the design is a simple d6 die pool that is a hybrid of 'keep highest/roll and sum'.
\n
\nSo please let me know what you think so far.
\n
\nResolution
\n
\nWhen you try to overcome an obstacle, you roll six-sided dice. If you are ... 'all alone,\u2019 you roll one die; \u2018in consort with,\u2019 you roll two dice; \u2018in league with,\u2019 you roll three dice. Add a die if you have a quality that can help you. If that quality has any tags that apply, add another die for each tag. You can also add additional dice from any other advantages or possessions you might have, keeping in mind the \u2018rule of six\u2019.
\n
\nRoll all the dice you\u2019ve gathered. Keeping the highest die you drop the rest, unless you roll multiples with the highest results which is then doubled. For example, with two dice you roll a 5 and a 2. Keep the 5 and drop the 2. Or with three dice you roll a 4, a 4, and a 1. Add the 4 to the 4 making it an 8 and drop the 1. If you have to roll a penalty die, from having some disadvantage, roll an extra die along with the rest, but drop out the highest one. Remember that you will always throw at least two dice, even if you are \u2018all alone\u2019 and have no bonuses. The second die is a penalty and you drop the highest score, with doubles negated.
\n
\nWith the \u2018rule of six\u2019 you can not throw more than six dice at any one time. Should your bonus dice go above six, you are allowed re-roll any of the dice (save those with penalties). Then follow the regular rules to determine the outcome. For example, with eight dice [6D + 2R] you roll 6, 5, 5, 4, 3, 2, 2, 1. You can re-roll any two dice (except penalty dice). You decide on the lowest two and roll a 6 and a 3. Your results are now 6, 6, 5, 5, 4, 3, 3, 2. You keep the highest doubles which gives you a score of 12.
\n
\nYour score needs to match or exceed the threat number to overcome the obstacle. [more coming...]

    For me, I have to be comfortable with the mechanic first. Then I can move forward to integrate it into the setting. It is just the way I think, if I'm not happy with the resolution there is little chance of wanting to play whatever genre it is.

    As far as design goals, I want something that gives off a narrative vibes, but actually plays fairly traditional (Over the Edge as an example). 2d6 I'm fond of, but also want something that offers a bit more. I like die pools, but not anything above 6 dice at a time--gets too unwieldy for me. I like the idea of exploding dice, but also want to keep it reigned in. Over time, I have come to really dislike +/- modifiers. I also want to stay away from traditional stat/skill trees. This design (I hope) gives it to me. It looks promising.

    \nI'm not either, but something like this might work:

    When you try to overcome an obstacle, you will roll a "pool" of six-sided dice. Each die in this pool will be one of two different colors, for example red or black. Follow these steps:
    1. Assemble dice - you will gather all the dice that may apply to your roll, like so:
      • If you are 'all alone', add one black die to the pool.
      • If you are 'in consort with', add two black dice to the pool.
      • If you are 'in league with', add three black dice to the pool.
      • If you have a quality that can help you, add an additional black die to the pool.
      • If that quality has any tags that apply, add another black die for each tag
      • For any situational advantage you have, add an additional black die.
      • Possessions you have may also allow you to add black dice.
      • For each situational disadvantage you have, add a red die to the pool.
    2. Partition dice - dice are separated into three different groups: roll, reroll and excess. This is done like so:
      • Put all red dice into the roll group.
      • Add black dice to the roll group until the group contains six dice or you run out of black dice.
      • If you have any black dice remaining, add them to the reroll group until it contains as many black dice as the roll group, or you run out of black dice.
      • If you have any black dice remaining, they go into the excess group and can be disgarded.
    3. Initial roll - Roll dice the roll group. The result of each die is considered separately (e.g. 6, 5, 2, 2, 2)
    4. Re-roll - For each die in the re-roll group, you can reroll a black die of your choice from the roll group. For example, if you had two dice in the re-roll group, you could re-roll two of the black dice from your initial roll.
    5. Apply penalties - For each red die that you rolled, eliminate a red or black die from your roll with the highest value. For example, suppose you rolled three black and two red dice. The black dice come up 6, 2, 2 and the red dice come up 5, 2. Because you have two red dice, you must eliminate the two dice (of either color) with the highest values, so you remove the black 6 and the red 5.
    6. Score the result - The highest number remaining in your roll is your score. If you have two or more dice with that same high value, double your score. Continuing the previous example, you have 2, 2, 2 remaining. The highest result is 2, but since you have more than one dice with that result, you double the score to 4.

    \n Two games which you might want to look at for interesting and potentially relevant die pool systems would be Alpha Omega (beautiful economy with low numbers of dice for multiple actions) and Houses of the Blooded (Wagers) which might give you some additional inspiration for this.

    Personally, I find the rule of 6 is a relatively elegant patch for a problem in the system. At this stage, I would try to investigate how the system could be streamlined a touch to eliminate the problem at the source. If I may, here are a few things going on with a ton of information here. If that information is useful somewhere else or you are intending on every die roll to be a MAJOR EVENT OF IMPORTANCE then a system with this complexity might be perfect.

    1) Add dice to your pool if you have positive traits (numbers, qualities, tags)
    2) If you have more then six dice, remove the extras and use them as rerolls.
    3) If you have penalties, remove the highest result after the roll.
    4) Use the highest number rolled and if that is a pair, use double the score.

    Many other games handle this in different ways. One approach is to simply have penalty dice remove from the die pool, which would lessen the need for the rule of 6 and remove step 3. Alternatively, you could have penalties and your extra rerolls work for opposite purposes; penalties reroll your highest dice and the extras reroll your lowest dice. Generally, if you can minimize the dice fiddling a tad, the system would be a little easier to teach from my perspective.

    Just food for thought! If looks good, but this is how I tend to approach these problems.
