\n I want to prepare a one-shot with the German macho RPG Barbaren! (http://story-games.com/forums/discussion/15521/barbaren-the-ultimate-macho-rpg-available-in-english/p1) along with My Little Pony Roleplaying is Magic (season 1 edition: http://www.mlprim.com/).
So, players get to either play a barbarian or a pony. They are on a quest together, i.e. they have to travel to a foreign land. The barbarians want to save their best friend from an evil sorcerer and the ponies need a special potion from this sorcerer to save Ponyville. So, they have to work together to achieve their goals.

I have a system question: What should I do system-wise?
1. Pick either one of the two systems, convert one setting to the other system?
2. Run both systems in parallel within a game, i.e preparing NPC attributes in both systems?
3. Convert both settings to a generic system (i.e. Fate Acclerated, Cortex Plus Leverage, AW engine)?

Currently, I think I'm favoring the third approach. But I would like to hear your opinion on what could work at all (or can't work) or what approach you would suggest.

    \n I haven't played Barbaren, but what is it that attracts you about this idea? Is it the juxtaposition of barbarianism and love-and-tolerance? I feel like all three options above are focused on "how do I map these character sheets" without addressing "these are the principles I want to enforce". For me, the first thing that came to mind was a Trollbabe hack - a one-stat game, with a sliding scale between "barbaric" and "harmony".     \n You might this earlier thread of mine useful reading.

    The basic idea I had, which I would apply here, would be to choose a basic and low-detail conflict resolution engine, like Primetime Adventures or The Pool or Fiasco (or even Archipelago/Archipelago II) and overlay it over the other two systems. (A simple token bidding system could work, too - whoever wants something has to give points to their adversary, who may accept or deny by bidding a higher number themselves.)

    So, when a Barbaren character does something, you respond as a Barbaren GM. When a Little Pony does something, you respond as you would in that game. So far so good - and the schizophrenia of the whole thing is a feature, not a bug, where perhaps due to some quirk of the rules a Barbaren character can't open a door but a Pony can...

    But whenever a situation comes up which can't be handled easily using the rules of either game as written (the most obvious example being a conflict between a Barbaren and a Pony), fall back to the "overlay" system.

    In other words, when the rules fail, or an outcome is not obvious, or when a Barbaren starts arm-wrestling with a Pony, switch to the simpler and more general conflict resolution engine. (Maybe they each bid tokens to win the contest, for example.)

    Perhaps you use Fiasco as your "basic engine" - if so, freeform those things which aren't covered by existing rules and have someone give that character a Black die or a White die if their fate is in question.

    The weird emergent situations (like where a mighty Barbaren warrior can hack down a dozen monsters but can't arm-wrestle a sweet Pony) would be part of the fun.

    \n (In my experience, statting up everything in two different systems isn't a great idea. I mean, it may be great for laughs! But it's not very functional, as anyone trying to "convert" one character into another game system knows: a lot is lost in the process, and there will be contradictions which could potentially be frustrating. "So in half the scenes I'm allowed to use a sword, but in the other half I cannot, but I'm effectively invincible because this system doesn't have a damage system?" That kind of stuff, but also more important character-identity issues.)

    \n Sounds like maybe you'd start by pony-talking and maybe have to escalate to barbaric-fighting with fallout - wait, somebody did that already...

    Let's try again. Everybody plays a barbarian and a pony. You go through every scene twice; first as one, then the other. Same adventure, same world but... askew, slightly off. Think the Mystics and the Skeksis of The Dark Crystal.

    Begin to draw parallels between the characters - your barbarian is hurt, your pony is hurt. Also start introducing cross-skills; maybe after an encounter, your barbarian gains Diplomacy; at some point, your Pony just has to kick some-one. Towards the end, you end up with one character: Ponan the Barbronian who should have all the necessary skills to tackle the final challenges.
