    \n I love how, when reading some of these, the first reaction for many is, "what's so funny about that?" ...and then you realize why it's funny. Like "Move Earth". Awesome.

    Anyway, I tried to look up my favourite D&D artifact, which was something like "X's Magnificent Exercise Machine", where X was something like "Baldorfo the Mad". Or maybe it was "Diabolical". Alas, I couldn't find it: it was a huge contraption which you entered and found yourself on a conveyor belt. Then you were attacked by various deathtraps. To avoid death, you would need to exercise. For example, at one point a blade would fly at your ankles, making you jump, and then some other hazard would make you fall down to the ground, after which a spike coming up from the ground would force you to perform pushups to avoid death, until you got to the next part. It was in one of my favourite supplements--if I remember correctly, it's the only D&D book I still own (though I don't have it here with me).

    Anyway, while searching for it, I found a "preview" of the table of contents for the 4E D&D Player's Handbook. And, let me tell you, from the viewpoint of this thread, the table of contents is... well, very oddly ordered. Consider the following excerpts (all in order, with no omissions or changes by me--just read down the table of contents in order):\n

    Waist Slot Items [immediately followed by]
    Wondrous Items
    \nThese bits, from combat, read like a manual for seduction or something similar: order of operations for going on a date, perhaps? I think it describes planning for and carrying out a date.\n

    Cover and Concealment
    Targeting What You Can't See
    Movement and Position
    Creature Size and Space
    Tactical Movement
    Falling
    Flanking
    Pull, Push, and Slide
    \n...just a few lines further down (perhaps from the other dater's perspective?):\n

    Crawl
    Delay
    Escape
    Grab
    Opportunity Attack
    Ready an Action
    Run
    Second Wind
    Shift
    Squeeze
    Stand Up
    \nAh, how puerile. But lovely.

    It also reminds me that one of my AD&D books misspelled "martial arts" as "marital arts" about a third of the time, to great comic effect ("hard marital arts" vs. "soft marital arts", for instance, or "can take decades of gruelling training and body-breaking punishment to master").

    Sorry if this is off-topic.
