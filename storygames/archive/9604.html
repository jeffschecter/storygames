    \n My impression, after half an hour or so of skimming it over morning coffee, is that this isn't so much a game as it is a long rant about what the author doesn't like about the games he's played (not that there's anything wrong with that!), a very simple basic resolution mechanic (draw a card; higher numbers are better; GM decides the difficulty and interprets the results); and a whole bunch of suggestions about how, if you want, you can make it more game-like by inventing stats and resources and stuff.

    Essentially, it's FUDGE with cards.

    Again, there's nothing wrong with any of this, I'm just not sure who the target audience is. Most people familiar with roleplaying games already know you can make up your own games. Most people who aren't familiar with roleplaying games but who might be interested in playing one are probably going to be put off by the idea of a "game" that tells them to invent their own game before they can play it.

    \n

    Most people familiar with roleplaying games already know you can make up your own games. Most people who aren't familiar with roleplaying games but who might be interested in playing one are probably going to be put off by the idea of a "game" that tells them to invent their own game before they can play it.

    \n
    \n

    I was really excited about FUDGE when it came into existence. I loved the idea of a system that was designed to be flexible and as a tool for players to develop their own games with. The problem is, that's not what it turned out to be.

    \n

    But a "toolkit" game, where you can modify IIEE, have modular resolution, different methods of situation generation... that would be really neat.

    \n

    It's kind of where Vincent's Anthology Engine is going, actually.
