    \n I already covered this ground in my full report, so I'll just paste in the complainy parts here:

    I found that these cards came up so often (EVERY 6 is a Hall of Mirrors, and EVERY 7 a secret passageway) that after awhile it became hard to meaningfully incorporate them into the Dream. it was all like, "OH, another Hall of Mirrors. I, um, guess I'm on another whirlwind journey of delirium where visions dance before my eyes that I cannot touch." It wasn't. . .bad, exactly, it just watered down the narrative. The Merchant King posed another problem--the Mirrors and passageways cycle such that they'll ensnare different cards each time, but the King cycles such that the same cards follow him each time, and each time he adds another card to his wares. As it happens the 3 or 4 cards following him were Face cards, which are near-impossible to acquire since you need to trade an equal-value card. So a particular card that I needed to acquire another card to unlock the way to particular location was basically OFF LIMITS as soon as the King ensnared it.

    . . .

    Another awkward feature (though maybe intentionally so!): it's really hard to hold on to an item long enough to use it for its intended purpose. they're always getting stolen, or else you need/want to discard them for another, or what have you. It was just another factor, like the recurring mirrors and such, which serve to draw the game out, by means of delaying success/resolution. Which is a neat feature to have, but it got repetitive after awhile. Again, the more often something happened, the harder it was to "make sense of" its meaning in the dream.

    It's an interesting design concern, because one of the Smoke Dream's inspirations, the computer text or graphical adventure, does rely on puzzle-solving with repeated stymieing of solutions, and lots of trial and error. And in the context of a dream, everything is ephemeral and all elements can disappear or shift or transform at any time. But a dream also has the quality of the portentous, so the more it mutates, the harder it is to draw meaning or significance out of it.

    I'll also echo that the instructions were a mite confusing, though I'm not sure I agree on all points above. For instance, I'd certainly make sure a big important restriction like DO NOT PEEK AT THE CARD ENTRIES is up front with flashing lights, because if it's buried in, like, step 7, then people might piss it. Er, miss it.

    \nHey Jackson,
    I'll replace "New Crompton" with "that hotel in Seattle during GPNW09", and pretend this was addressed to me.

    I thought I was holding in my hands a book of choose-your-own-poetry creative exploration, which would allow me to play with the symbols and images of a dream, touching them fleetingly or deeply (whichever I thought fitting), finding and creating and changing the meaning of these things as I did so. I expected poetry and dreamspace. (a play experience like Adrift, or Love Nothing.)

    Instead, I got something that felt like it paid homage to old-school text adventures, where I GET ROPE and EXIT N and USE ROPE and YOU CAN'T DO THAT HERE and EXIT E and "There's a pond here, with fish. If only you could catch one..." and "Oh shit, I need the fishing rod. I think that was three moves west and two moves south" and EXIT W EXIT S EXIT W EXIT S EXIT W and GET FISHING ROD and YOU HAVE A FISHING ROD.

    For the player who isn't interested in mental gymnastics (ie, me), there's a lot of cycling through cards without considering the story they create, and only pausing to imagine the dream at certain junctures.

    Like this: Hall of mirrors (count six cards, put on bottom of deck), Axe (don't want it, move on), Heart (pick up, drop), Swarthy Cousin (take as companion), Witch of Brass... "Oh, so moments after I encounter a brave adventurer, the witch sets in on the two of us!"

    In that example, you'll see, I interface with several cards without even considering their impact on "the dream".
    I don't imagine wandering through a hall of mirrors, and then emerging at its exit and nearly stumbling over an axe, kicking it out of my path and continuing on... suddenly hearing the pulse of my heart, hiding in some reeds just over there.

    I just game those cards, and wait till I get to a point where imagining the inherent story (a.) makes sense, and (b.) will produce meaningful results.

    \n So pretty much everything i wanted to say here has been said. So i'm going to just do some bullets that i think might be helpful to you as you move forward:

    \u2022 Treat your reader like they are reasonably intelligent but have somehow never played any game ever. Simple, short sentences. Hand hold, but don't patronize. When Henson was working on Sesame Street i think he said "our audience may be short, be we shouldn't ever play down to them." This is a game that NORMAL PEOPLE WHO DON'T GIVE A SHIT ABOUT D&D COULD PLAY. Embrace that, and cater to it.

    \u2022 One rule per sentence.

    \u2022 Repeat important rules and concepts. Consider telling your reader what you are going to say, then say it, then tell them what you said.

    \u2022 Don't worry about people cheating. Cheating is great. The world would simply stop spinning if folks stopped cheating. Embrace deck stacking and reading ahead as an emergent behavior. People want to look under the hood, you can advise against it, but don't scold them for it.
