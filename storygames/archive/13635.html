\n Plenty of games have moments (long game turns, short game turns, in-fiction alignments of characters) where a given participant is neither GM nor playing their character. This player may make jokes, toss fanmail, watch in rapt attention, or get bored and antsy. They may occasionally get handed temporary duties like minor NPCs, but not much beyond that.

How do you feel when you're in that situation? How do you feel about being put in that situation?

Personally, I dislike it.

Sometimes, I shrug and go, "ehn, just not my taste; this isn't the perfect game for me, but I like enough other parts to still play".

Other times, I put on my engineer goggles and go, "well, perhaps it's a necessary evil to ensure character authorship across multiple players; no use complaining about the cost of doing business."

And then there are the times where I go, "this is fucking stupid; this game is a failure for not giving us ways to stick all the player characters in the same scenes."

    \n I enjoy the audience role if the game itself is interesting enough; if there are new revelations to consider, the roleplay is engaging, or if I have plans to make for my own character. I've yet to a play a really engaging game where being "audience" was a drag. In less eventful games, of course, it's a big issue.

    However, one thing I find is that games which DO place all the characters together all the time suffer from one of two issues:

    First, the quality of the story often suffers. Great fiction is rarely about a group of characters, and the same group of characters, who are together all the time. Private moments between characters are powerful and necessary to deeper characterization; doing everything together often stretches suspension of disbelief.

    [Edit: exactly as Graham said, above!]

    Second, unless the group is very small, I find that in large scenes my character is being quiet/waiting for others to speak/disclaiming authority to others so much that it's even more boring or even less engaging than being an "audience member". For instance, large-scale combat in a D&D game, where a large group of characters faces off against enemies, could mean that I don't get a "turn" more often than once every 10-20 minutes. But I've found the same in situations of pure roleplay: a group of four characters is guaranteed to encounter scenes that are only interesting to two or three (or maybe even just one) of those players. The other one or two is limited to sitting back and letting the others enjoy whatever issues they are facing. Just because "my character is there" doesn't mean my interests in the game are necessarily being engaged.

    I think I often enjoy having a range of quiet moments and active play, where bullets (or emotions) are flying. A good story often seems to demand that, so "sitting out" is a fine price for me to pay.

    In my experience, playing a character in a large scene with many other characters involved often feels just as much like being an uninvolved audience member.

    However, the strong and continuous engagement of being an active player all the time may be part of why I enjoy GMing as much as I do.

    I *do* agree, also, that RPG design can and should strive to do more to engage all the participants as much as possible. I don't think we've moved as far as we can or should just yet. I'm looking forward to other responses in this thread!

    \n You know, this makes me think:

    The reason mechanical turn-based games (such as combat in many systems) can be a drag, is because you have nothing to do until "your turn". So, reactive mechanics and the like, where you take action not at a set time, but when something-or-other happens, or when you expend resources, are one potential solution.

    The reason scenes with all the characters present can feel just as lame or inactive as pass-around-the-camera with separate storylines is that in most RPGs any kind of resolution is dependent on interaction with the GM. For example, in a game of Delve, it doesn't matter if all four characters are investigating one room, because as soon as we've said, "OK, Jim, you try picking the lock while I look through these underwear drawers," I'm effectively "sitting out" until the GM has dealt with Jim lock-picking and is ready to tell me what I find.

    So, mechanics that can be engaged by any subset of players are an interesting potential solution. If could turn to Lisa while Jim and the GM handle the lockpicking, and she could tell me what's in the drawer, there needn't be any "audience time" at all. Oddly enough, in games like that, I often wish I could just spectate, and not miss out on what's happening with others...

    \n Audience time also seems to be a product of the tyranny of the scene. The scene takes on an inside/outside structure locking out anything or anyone that isn't given explicit entry in its defined scope of time, space, and/or theme. And whether these scenes were truly locked before they typically become even more difficult to interact with as they get more rule intensive (like Conflict resolution in Dogs), unless there is an explicit access granted to the "audience" players.

    What frustrates me about the prevalence of structured and mechanized scenes in so many games is that there really are other options in breaking up the narrative - there is no reason that multiple threads of times and places cannot proceed concurrently as we play, except that we need the tools (fictional and system-wise) to handle it. So literally in some cases, as an audience member I find myself frustrated because I realize that these other two audience members could be doing something nifty right now too, except the possibility did not seem to have occurred to the game's designer.

    Certainly there are good reasons to fix and lock all progress on one scene at a time, but like all decisions about narrative flow and mechanics this shouldn't be done lightly, and the consequences should be considered carefully.

    - Mendel

With the more focused char-gen I'm using now, hopefully you would have been more primed to care about the mission -- getting to the core of the village's problem, to thwart it and/or steal its supernatural secrets (rather than helping redeem drunk NPCs or other tangetial crap). With all the PCs on board for that, all you need are multiple plausible paths to follow and multiple plausible approaches to the tasks that arise. It then becomes a collaborative effort to form the best approach to the one objective that everyone shares. No one wants to miss out on having input, and no one wants to absent a good mind from a decision.

      So, the decision to dive is shared. If you really want to, then everyone else knows why, and that's enough to elicit participation and anticipation. If you don't really want to, then someone else comes up with a better idea, and you do that instead. Or, if you're just covering bases, we fast forward and summarize.

      Tasks that don't involve decisions get summarized in seconds. Paul gets the water while Dan gets the log while Matt gets the village elder; done.

      None of the preceding was hypothetical; it's all happened in the vast majority of my playtests.

      Bad scenario design that leaves the players floundering with no idea what they might productively do will of course sabotage all of this. But as long as the players have options and leads and motivation and know they can fast forward, it's all good. (Questions, puzzles, and auguries help on the "leads" front.)

      Separately:
      Good point about (a) intense time pressure + (b) multiple disparate tasks to try + (c) no need for strength in numbers. That is flat terrible; sticking together makes no sense. All I can say is that's the only time that particular thing has ever come up; I think I rushed through a ton of stuff because we abruptly realized players had to leave. Normally, the phases go: (1) there are many things to do and plenty of time to do them in; (2) there's one urgent thing to do. Sometimes urgency arrives before clarity, but then there's usually a survival reason to stick together.

      That's a fair amount of particulars! I'll readily admit, making constant PC interaction fun for the whole group the whole time isn't always a simple design spec! But, y'know, switching from author to actor to audience can get complicated too.

      \n Well, one possible (strict) definition for "audience time" is when:

      a) play is ongoing,
      b) you have no narrational responsibilities in the current scene/moment (e.g. speaking lines for a character, resolving/narrating outcomes, describing scenery), and
      c) you have no ability to affect the game-state mechanically (e.g. rolling dice, dispensing bonuses and penalties, making rulings).

      Parts b) and c), combined, suggest that there would be no effect on gameplay if you were simply not present at the table.

      The strictest definition, would, of course, be:

      You (the player) may not speak until this scene/moment is over.

      You're absolutely right, though, that the examples we're talking about (and real life) blur all these categories. For instance, mechanics like Fan Mail and the customary dispensing of experience points at the end of, say, a Vampire session arguably don't impact the game mechanically in that very instant, but rather over the long term.

The group itself had a mix of male and female players, all non-white, and most new to our events so I wanted to make an impression.

      I\u2019ve run Dogs over 100 times for over 200 different people. I\u2019ve made a few hacks along the way (I love it out of the box) but the changes that are relevant to this thread\u2026

      1. A Short Term Fallout option is that you are Alone and in Trouble. Dogs players have a tendency to stick together which I find very boring and mechanically if you have 4 Dogs in every conflict, Dogs win with minimal fuss unless it is against a mob. I also want to give the NPCs a chance to influence Dogs and build empathy which is difficult with a group and easy with 1-2 people. And if different Dogs empathize with different people, the Dogs themselves conflict and the game is almost always better for it.

      2. You don\u2019t makeup NPCs. Instead I provide a list of all the major NPCs in the town with a picture, name, role, and 3 word description. Players are encouraged to choose at least 3, and 2 must be shared. Extra Relationship dice may be left unassigned as normal.

      I tell the players to go crazy with the relationships. You can decide Nathaniel is your dad, killed your dad, your dad\u2019s boss, your boss, pushed you to sin, you saved him from sin, you tried to kill him but he got away, he saved your life\u2026 almost anything. Then I walk away from the table. I give them 10 minutes alone. I find this makes them feel more free to be creative and don\u2019t keep looking to me for approval. Instead they go nuts, and they almost always do. And since 2 relationships have to be shared, they all discuss these connections together.

      When I come back, every player is 10 times more invested in the game and they haven\u2019t even started \u201cplaying\u201d yet. And because they all are coming at the NPCs from different perspectives\u2026 they all want to know how someone else\u2019s relationships conflicts or confirms theirs in play. Dogs helps with this process because towns are small, isolated, and most people are related somehow. So it is easy to suspend disbelief that it would end up being so soap opera like.

      Then in play, even when players weren\u2019t in the scene, they paid attention to every detail. They wanted to know if their old instructor really did it and what that says about their own relationship that they chose themselves. Everything is connected to reveals or consequences in any scene will come back to you. So you have a reason to care! And because towns are small, it\u2019s trivial to reunite, resplit, and then reunite the PCs again at any moment at any place. Bob just revealed who gave him the alternate edition of the book of life\u2026 everyone wants to be there\u2026 bam\u2026 no problem\u2026 you\u2019re there. It\u2019s the best of both worlds. They can be individuals and then when it is convenient they can be together.

      And meta never came into it. Knowing an NPC just revealed something and wanting to be there to see the consequences is meta, your character doesn\u2019t know that but the people playing don\u2019t know what meta is, don\u2019t think about it, and don\u2019t care. And they were super immersed in their characters. These were people mainly new to RPGs. In character creation, they asked which \u201cBackground\u201d in Dogs in the Vineyard is the \u201cBrick\u201d and which is the \u201cDPS\u201d (damage per second, see World of Warcraft). But in play you would think they had been roleplaying for years.

      Dogs has the problem that you can\u2019t join a conflict once it is started which can be a drag and contradicts much of what I described above. I use some rule variants from Afraid, the never published horror version of Dogs, but the join mid-conflict rules have been clunky. What I do now is let the players help with their traits, relationships or belongings. But they can\u2019t use them again in the next conflict. Simple but it seemed to work really well. And emotionally was satisfying to the players on the outside wanting to join in.

      Next time I\u2019m going to include the Town\u2019s Sins on the list of NPCs at the start of the game and see how that works.

      \n This just seems to be solving a symptom of the problem. Of course big conflict-based scenes run the risk of isolating the less involved players.
