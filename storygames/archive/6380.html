\n So this is an idea I've had rattling around in my head for a few weeks now. I thought I'd throw it out there for comment and feedback to see where it goes. This is nothing more than a, "what do you think?" thread.

I admit I really dislike fannish discussions about "canon" when it comes to fantasy (or any other) fiction. I hate the discussions about how/why this or that works "in the world" or "what if" games. If a fantasy world has a "rule" I'm the first to want to break it. Consider World of Warcraft with its technologically advanced gnomes who can not be Priests or Paladins. The first thing I WANT to be is a Gnome Paladin who has turned to faith forsaking technology, especially in light of the destruction of their home city in the wake of a technological disaster. That goes double for the Undead who have had their connection to life and nature severed. All I want to do is play an Undead Druid who, through shear force of will, has, with great desperation, grasped back that connection.

So, in retaliation for my inability to have this need serviced through other games I imagined a game called "Exceptional Fantasy." (Do you see where I'm going with this?) Basically it's core mechanism works like this: First, there would be some mechanism where by "rules of the world" could be added to a list called The Canon. So you would end up with a list of things like:

Vampires can not walk in sunlight.
Magic requires human sacrifices.
Nobles only ever marry other nobles.

And so on....

Then there would be another set of mechanics that would allow you to BREAK the rules in The Canon. In fact, it would be mandatory that during character creation your character breaks one of the rules. So the whole game would be about establishing "rules" and then exploring situations which break those "rules" because it always seems to me that the interesting choices and situations to explore always lie on those boundary conditions.

Jesse

    Posted By: JDCorleyI'm surprised that the first setting that leaped to mind for you was fantasy, given the centrality of stereotypes ("splats", ugh) to White Wolf stuff. I think this is a great tool for those sorts of settings as well.
    \nHmm, good point. I think a great tradition to fantasy, and modern horror, and many other types of stories - especially as they appear in RPGs.

    D&D novels are especially rife. You have the half-elf who grew a beard, rejecting the culture of his elven foster parents. The dark elf who fights against his people for the forces of good.

    The problem is, these can become exceptional cliches, which destroy any reason to break the canon in the first place.

    Having a mechanic that tells you your job is to break the canon, I think will help keep them unique - especially if each time you make character you have a new set of canon - which doesn't mean you have to have a new setting. "Elves are cultured, refined and enlightened" doesn't have to conflict with "Elves embrace the inherent magic of nature", it only sets a different facet of canon to break. In a sense, you display what is important about the setting by playing with contrasting elements.

    And taking it beyond character, in play, does work like Keys, in that it forces change to occur in the campaign world. Does the priest using magic without his holy symbol now mean there's a paradigm change in magic? Or is he special somehow? Either way you answer it, makes for another interesting story.

    Posted By: Josh Roby
    Jesse, could you unpack this a bit? What is it about canon-rules that makes you want to break them?
    \nThere are so many answers to that question. Some of it is a form of intellectual spite. The failure of fans to realize that "canonical rules" are a function of authorial history and not a place that "is" being "revealed" by the author. Worse, the fact that some publishers and authors basically pander to that mentality and in fact *are* "revealing" a "world" and its "rules" and thus hamstringing themselves as artists. Gamer culture in some circles is particularly entrenched in this. Players who can not conceive of making up their own setting because "world building" is too much effort. I can't tell you how many times in the past I've been told by a GM of a pick-up con game I was in that I played the character wrong because such-and-such *made up thing* "isn't like that."

    More practically I view such rules as character positioning devices. Characters who somehow break the "rules" of their universe usually present an interesting situation to confront. The Undead Druid who fights for his connection to nature... what makes him so driven? Why is it THAT important to him? What do his fellow undead think of him? How do they treat him? Is he a god among his people or an outcast?

    Similarly when such "rules" are broken mid-story it usually serves as a massive thematic highlighter on the real world things at stake. It's a giant metaphorical wake up call to the audience saying: Hey! This story, it's about THIS right here. Pay attention.

    Note: I also think that when characters *conform* to the rules can be equally as thematically telling. Which is why if I were to flesh out this mechanic I think there would be certain benefits from conforming to the The Canon as well. So, yes, setting level Keys basically.

    \n In my mind that's why everyone starts out breaking just ONE rule. They are, by definition, exceptional in their world. Like Edward Elric in Full Metal Alchemist who can do alchemy without a transmutation circle.

    I could see that leading to fun play: you break some important rule ("the dead cannot be brought back to this world") to bring your love back to Earth, hoping that this will be the end of it all. However, if the dice fall the wrong way (or whatever), then you might find yourself facing enemies you had defeated and killed long ago.

    That sounds like a fun mechanic, and an interesting gamble.
