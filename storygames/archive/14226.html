\n The modernize-old-game contest thread got me thinking about this.

I loved TFT, but I may have loved it for the wrong reasons - I loved it because if I was a shitty GM, it devolved to at least being a pretty good boardgame.

(I also loved it because I could practically fit the rules in my head. I also loved it because a character - several characters, even - fit on an index card. My wandering monster table was a stack of index cards.)

(And GURPS lost the je ne sais quois for me, but that's a topic for a different thread.)

I still have the books. I think I still have all the cardboard counters from the solo-adventures. But I'm not sure I want to play it again. The setting was pretty bland. Unfocused, beige fantasy. And it was essentially a board game.

Could it be reclaimed? Could its tactical combat live in harmony with today's cutting edge RPG design thought? How would you do it?

    \n Jmstar ran some for me last year, and I ended up running for some friends after that.

    We basically played it as a run-and-gun tactical game with a little roleplaying and color in-between fight scenes. Think of it as 4th edition D&D (very) light.

    Creating maps on hex paper on the fly with colored sharpies was pretty cool. As was letting players pick out their character from several sheets of cardboard heroes. We were averaging one dead hero per encounter.

    I'm not sure I would make it into anything that it is not. I found it fun simply on it's own.

    \n I love TFT, still play it sometimes, and think it is mostly awesome as is.

    I use some house rules I feel make it even more awesome that the original RAW, but it's too much to provide an accounting here. Many of these were on their way to becoming official in a 2nd edition of TFT that didn't materialize before Metagaming went under.

    None of the additions I include narrativize it, much less make it cutting edge. One thing is I've started to "4E-ize" some of the monsters to make them less vanilla. When I have time, I'll provide some examples here.

    I will say that the speed of TFT combat relative to some modern examples provides some room for "story" that isn't present in some modern tactical role-playing games. Don't know if that makes providing some mechanics for that type of play a good idea.

    \n I am a huge fan of TFT. I agree with these sentiments already expressed:

    1. It is far from vanilla. It has a very distinctive flavor, much of which comes from off-hand remarks sprinkled throughout the text. Steve Jackson had a very focused idea of what the game should be and there are many cool features (like the whole jobs focus) that enforce that vision.

    2. It is what it is. I've never seen a "let's make TFT more awesome" project that made it more awesome.
