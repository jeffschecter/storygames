\n Kind of a do-it-yourself thread, inspired in part by the Show Your Work thread.

I've been wanting to do a map of the Nentir Vale for my 4E game, used as an in-game prop for the players. My basic idea is to redraw the map provided in the Dungeon Master's guide, then allow the players to use it during games, adding their own notes and locations.

Would love to have the map have that crinkly, weathered feel, but I'm not sure of the best way to do this. I've heard about soaking paper in coffee or tea to give it that weathered look, then buring the edges with a lighter or match. Anyone have any suggestions on how to do this? Is there a way I can draw the map, THEN do the weathering, or wil this ruin the ink? Does anyone have an example of something they've done before?

    \n Yes and yes.

    You will need good quality art paper (Arches or something like that)
    Waterproof ink pens ranging in size from 0.2mm to 0.5mm
    Draw your map and let the ink dry

    Soak in coffee or black tea
    Dry in an oven at 170-ish degrees
    Do not lay directing on the oven grill otherwise you get grill marks

    There are other ways of aging as well.

    \n Certain paper takes to water and color better than others. I did a bit of this myself a while back and I'd suggest trying a few different kinds of paper. Some I used curled up terribly and others looked almost untouched after the process (I was just soaking it and letting it dry in the sun). Go to an art store and pick up some different weights of paper and see how they react.

    Also, I'd recommend scanning your map drawing before you try to weather it, just in case something goes wrong and it gets ruined. At the very least you could print it back out really lightly and retrace it, rather than having to redraw the whole thing.

    \n The method I liked in college was to design the maps on a computer and then print them out. This allows you to use some really nice calligraphy fonts and cut and past in intricate wind roses and other symbols. I then mix up a 2x or 3x coffee solution and pour it into a pizza pan. I let the paper soak, drape over Tupperware lid, and microwave dry. I never got the burned edges to look right but I remember that rolling it up and then going at either end added thickness and made the burn slower. Also, burning the edges while the paper was still wet seemed to help too. The nice thing here is that you are using a fairly standardized technique so it is easy to update the map on a computer and repeat the process to include extra locations on the map.

    \n If you want something to look not just old-ish but used, fold it up and keep in in your backpocket (The tighter your pants the better.) for a while, every now and then switch front and back as well as refolding it. Paper that gets used a lot looks very different from Ye Olde Scroll most aging techniques focus on. (And, IMO, much more interesting.)

    Haven't tried combining this with coloring/aging yet, though. I'd imagine this could look really cool, though.
