\n Go with me here. I'm trying to work something out.

Some things seem fair in games. Some things don't. Often, I struggle to articulate the difference between the two.

Could you give me some examples of Fair and Unfair things? I'm particularly thinking of old-school GM actions, although we can widen it later.

And, despite the frivolous nature of the title, I'm genuinely interested, so don't dogpile this with jokes, please.

Fair: "Someone jumps from the shadows and attacks you! I roll to hit!"
Unfair: "Someone jumps from the shadows and attacks you! Roll damage!"

Fair: "There's a pit! Roll under your Dexterity or you fall in!"
Unfair: "There's a pit! Roll a 20 or you fall in!"
Unfair: "There's a pit! You fall in!"

What else?

Graham

    \n It's fair if there's a chance that you might not be smitten by the Great Hand of Injustice. It's unfair if there isn't.

    On the other hand, it's perfectly fair to mete out this kind of punishment to NPCs who just hang around waiting for PCs to gut them.

    It's about deprotagonisation. You can give the player a ludicrously thin chance of escape and they'll think it fair.

    So for example, which do you prefer:
    - 3d6 damage with a 50% chance to parry; or
    - 1d6 damage with no chance to parry?

    I think most people would go for the first. You can probably test how small a chance to parry they are willing to accept. What about 30%? Who would go for that?

    \n Graham, I think it boils down to: anytime that something happens to my character or in an arena where my character could conceivably interfere with it and I cannot affect it, it gets an Unfair flag.

    However, as your roll-20-or-fall-in example shows, that doesn't mean everything else is Fair. There's a very slippery slope of acceptable probabilities, and these in turn are also affected by potential spend-a-drama-die mechanics that allow me to choose to skew those probabilities further. As GB Steve points out, this is definitely a slippery slope, and some folks will balk at 30% while others will only balk when it hits 5% (natural 20); however, it's also contextual, and that context is defined by the play group, as Zak points out. It is also determined by genre and mood, though: I'll accept much less character effectiveness in, say, a Horror game than in a Supers game.

    \n I see 3 broad types of unfairness which players and gm's can both commit.

    Theft of authority - taking onto yourself and/or from another some authority that the system/table assumptions says you cannot take. GM saying you're scared if system/table says Player decides that is a theft of the Player's authority. Includes actual cheating. We can all view some rule systems as inherently unfair in this way (e.g., someone may regard any system that mechanically determines a PC's emotional responses as unfair) and simply avoid those systems for that reason.

    Deceiving others in the exercise of their authority - not telling others information the game/table would assume they would have ~ that's a way of stealing their authority but it's done via information management rather than just "force". Since GM's manage information in most systems, it's a GM failing most often.

    Unconscionable conduct that simply spoils fun. Being a jerk, importing out-of-game arguments into game etc., being a prima donna. Since everyone shows up to have fun, it is unfair to spoil their fun.

    Fair is a matter of having a set of agreed upon rules, and abiding by them.

    \n

    If you've agreed that the GM has complete and utter control of the difficulty of challenges, and that your part is to overcome the challenges as they are put before you, then anything that happens within that context is fair.

    \n

    If part of the agreement includes that all players are treated equitably, and the GM bases his challenges on how much he likes one player or another, then it's unfair.

    \n

    ...but I don't guess that's what you're looking for.

    \n

    Specific examples of rules that I personally thought were unfair...

    \n

    BW Scripting: So, what you're saying is, I blocked an attack that didn't happen? Why the hell would I do that?

    \n

    So, so, wait.. I failed to setup this action.. but unless I expend a later action, I still try to do the action and automatically fail?

    \n

    (This falls in with my earlier point: If you agree to it, it's fair. I've never agreed to it, insisting, as a forceful player, that we drift the rules to suit my preferences; Whether that is unfair of me is mostly for Alex to decide, as he was the one most affected by it)

    \n

    Mildly unfair: Certain weapon choices in 3:16; The powerblade, in specific. It's thematically fucking cool Seriously, a chainsaw sword?.. But it's less effective in all ways than the shotgun. There's not a single gamey reason to choose the powerblade over the shotgun. 3:16 is gamey enough that it's rough to make a choice that hasn't any mechanical advantages, just because it's cool. Which isn't to say that I wouldn't do it, because the difference is fairly small.. But it's undeniable.

    \n

    A specific example of a ruling that might have been unfair, but was fun anyway:

    \n

    The game is Star Wars D6. The ship we're on is about to blow up. One of the players pulls out the "Technical Manual" and asks to make a roll to reroute power from the overloading reactor to the weapon systems. The GM gives him a stupidly high target number. As the result of crazy rolling and the wild die, the player succeeds. The GM says it buys time for more NPCs to be evacuated from the ship, and we give the enemy ship a hell of a broadside blast. He also got Starship Repair as a skill at level 1. But our ship still explodes, propelling the PCs still aboard into the future.

    \n

    Dude succeeded, and the effect was basically color. We still blew up. I thought it was unfair briefly, but the story was interesting enough that no one protested.

    \n

    Same game, different ruling: My character did not go aboard the star destroyer for the meeting because she didn't want to surrender her weapon. So the stormtroopers boarded our ship and demanded her weapon. 6 of them. Oddly, when she ended up in a cell, sans weapon, I was furious at the unfairness of it.. but I wasn't angry at the GM, at all.

    \n Stop trying to force it all into one box! There needn't be one rule that explains everything. There's different sorts of fairness here.

    Rules do seem to play a part. So does the fiction.

    Unfair: "You want to summon something? OK, your chance of success is 1%".
    Fairer: "You want to summon something? OK, your chance of success is 1%, it says here."
    Fairer still: "You want to summon something? OK, your chance of success is 1%, it says here. Yeah, summoning Mythos entities is always really difficult."

    Like Wolfe says, rules can sometimes seem totally unfair.

    Unfair: "OK, you made your sneak roll. You sneak 5 feet. You're exposed in the middle of the room. Roll again".
    Unfair [Apocalypse World]: "You're going straight into the gunfire? Take 1 harm, then."

    Fair: "He attacks you. You've got a 50% chance to parry, otherwise it's 3d6 damage.
    Unfair: "He attacks you. Take 1d6 damage."

    That's related to a psychology thing I can't remember right now. It's to do with risk.

    \nI think "take 2 damage" is unfair as a GM or "opposing Player" declaration without appeal because most Players believe their fun is compromised if they do not have a change to react to changes in environment/information and to meaningfully affect outcomes for their PC. Actual outcomes of any meaning in the game must have permitted input from more than one source - otherwise someone is just telling their story and not playing a game. Just as you need opposition to make accomplishment matter, you need chance to exercise roleplaying (choice/rolls etc.) to make outcomes fair. "I didn't have a chance to affect the story on a point important to me" is unfair - as long as it's clear the system/table agreed that you were allowed to affect story at that level (e.g., you don't get to affect some things because that's beyond the scope of what your authority in game is about period, the sun still comes up tomorrow).

    I think this this then connects to one part of why it's sucky to have to roll stealth 10 times to sneak into castle, with failure = same thing whenever it takes place, just as a fair/fun game must give a chance to influence outcome, exercise choice, "play game" for each 'significant thing', then it's also sucky if it "keeps making you make the same choice, reroll the same roll, when what the significant thing is hasn't changed" because really it's saying "your first choice and roll didn't matter all that much as failing on it and failing on 9th roll are identical in outcome".

    Procedures in game text: Usually fair, but not always. If unfair enough, they drift over to the far right and cease to be part of the "rules" area, which is pretty much what houserules are all about.

    Actually, "arbitrary" means "at personal discretion." Its the kind of ruling you get when the judge or arbiter makes a ruling based on personal opinion rather than upon law. In more common parlance it means a decision based on personal idiosyncrasies or whim, rather than upon structured or underlying logic. An aribter is someone you go to because you trust their personal judgment to be fair.

    No, really. Wikipedia agrees with me and the net is never wrong.

    ....

    However! That doesn't mean that arbitrary = unfair. It just means that arbitrary = as fair as the person passing the judgment happens to be at the time. Which, very often, can be amazingly fair. More fair to the actual situation, in fact, than a judgment based solely upon systemic logic, as the systemic logic may lack proper context in the human situation at the moment. (Thus the reason that arbitrary decisions are allowed in some legal contexts, despite the law being a big systemic logic pile.)

    So there are times where arbitrary decisions can be fair, and times where they can be unfair. But the same can be said of systemic decisions. The big trip up in RPG circles seems to be that some folks feel that one side or the other is either inherently more fair, or at least more fair more often on a regular basis. Thus folks who want systemic logic point to books for "fair" rules, where those who want arbitration point to GMs or the group for "fair" rulings.

    And then each side tells the other side it is wrong, Prima facie.
