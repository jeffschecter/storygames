    \n Ah, Killer 7. It's almost a game I'd rather watch than play, though I did finish it (though I tried to replay it about a year ago and had a terrible time getting back into the groove). My advice is to play with Kaede as your primary, since her sniper scope coupled with the ease of acquiring the weak-point-lock-on power-up is pretty much the game's win button. Sure her health sucks, but hardly anybody should be able to get near her.

    I should also note this is the only game where the official strategy guide has an entire chapter devoted to analyzing the plot.

    I think Killer 7 is perhaps best gamed as grist for Over the Edge; Suda's US-as-viewed-by-Japan and Japan-as-viewed-by-the-US-as-viewed-by-Japan is more like Al Amarja than anywhere that exists in our reality.

    \n I'm struck by the thought that surrealism in role-playing is exceptionally difficult without a referral/contextual superstructure, such as genre conventions, to fall back on - so as the players know how their characters should react to surreal twists. Otherwise the surreality simply becomes the reality of the imagined space.

    The most Burroughsy game I heard about was a Sorcerer campaign set in Algeria... actually, I'm sure the AP is here somewhere. That said, the genius of Killer7 is it's inimitable style; it's pure Suda.

    Recently I've been mulling over a game that might be called something like "Kar\u014dshi." The premise is simple: the players are Japanese salarymen who must empty their inboxes, fufill their quotas and vie for promotion - all whist avoiding deadly stress-induced hallucinations.
    Basic keys-and-pool system but actions by other players 'lock out' the size of your pool and left-over dice go into two Lacuna-style 'static' gauges, one for events ("Suddenly, the shift-manager is all up in your grill") and another for surreality ("Only now do you notice your keyboard is made of viscous marshmallows"). Possibly a 'yes, and' system to help produce good surrealism. Hm - thoughts?

    \n Nathan: I thought of some more advice. Don't get frustrated or bored by the gameplay. Suda51 does this thing where he doesn't design mechanics for classically "fun" gameplay, he designs them for thematics (i.e. fun of a different kind than video gamers are used to). For instance, it's thematically relevant in K7 that your guys are restricted to moving along plotted lines while the bad guys are free to move all over the map. I swear, just about everything in that game is thematically significant.

    (No More Heroes is maybe even stronger in this regard. It requires the pattern memorization, determination, and split-second button twitching for palm-sweating durations of time that hardcore games of the past did, all the while being a BITING satire of hardcore games and the culture that surrounds them.)

    Mike: IA (or M&C&S, whichever) has been on my to-design list for ages. It hasn't got anywhere yet because it's hard to figure out. That said, I'm strongly leaning towards a Spione hack, at least to get started with before I scrap the first system to write the real one (which nearly always happens when I design a game). But what I really dream of is a system that uses Burroughs' repetition-with-a-twist technique (discussed and encouraged in The Rustbelt, but not mechanized) in a mechanically meaningful way, and also might involve cut-ups as a resolution mechanic. Big things that need to happen is ways for the scene (or Routine?) to wildly disjunct, for the story to unfold achronologically, and for nothing to ever happen the same way twice (thus preventing the surreality from becoming just another reality).

    Tulpa: they would accelerate as fast as you wanted them to, but there'd also have to be times when it happened unbidden. Although I'll probably let the sexual stuff be customizeable; I'm more interested in BDSM happening than anal sex, and Burroughs was the other way around.

    Wilmer: It's coming along. I had to scrap a big chunk of GM techniques (ripped off of Cruel Fortunes from Poison'd) to start writing an even better set.

    \n I have kind of grown out of reading Murakami, and I haven't actually read Hear the Wind Sing. His first two books are kind of impossible to find, and from what I've read of Pinball, 1973, a little bit clumsy. He hasn't really found his voice until A Wild Sheep Chase.

    Anyway, recommended starting point: Hard-Boiled Wonderland and the End of the World. Kafka on the Shore is a lot more directly Lynchian and better structured but it felt a little too overtly surreal to me. If you're not afraid of just diving in with both feet, The Wind-Up Bird Chronicle is the only one I would consider exceptionally good.

    As far as short stories, most of the ones in after the quake are good, with my favorite being the very comedic Super-Frog Saves Tokyo.

    \n I love my clumsy first novels. I'll see if I can't rustle up Hear the Wind Sing from somewhere. It's only now that I realize that Murakami has written the introduction for my edition of Akutagawa shorts.

    I've been putting more thought into surrealist play: It occurs that having certain tangible goals (as part of a definite scenario) would focus play a great deal.
    Paranoia is characterized by brutal acts of insensibility, GM fiat and weird slapstick, but having specific parameters in regard to player-character expectations allows the player to react/interact with nonsensical or surreal situations with narrative purpose.

    Mulling my Murakami-style game, I'm now convinced that the characters should (now/also) be low-ranking mobsters balancing a dayjob (and associated traits) with "night-time" criminal personas. Just need to work out a system for 'mission' generation and I'll throw the mess up on Praxis.
