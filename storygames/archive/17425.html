    \n Nick's and Robb's advice are the core techniques, I think: 1) Listen and prompt (rather than guide or plan), and 2) keep the goals and dangers independent. Regarding (1), I think an important corollary is that you listen and prompt for the whole session. There's a tendency to use this technique just to seed the adventure, and then GM more traditionally once you get rolling, but Ghost/Echo stagnates under a plot dictatorship.

    I always start by asking general questions about the setting, making sure everyone gets a say. Just 5 minutes worth, maybe, whatever that gives you. This might be a little frontloaded, but especially if you're dealing with newer players I think it makes the next step less scary.

    After that, the players pick names and come up with character descriptions of the form "I can ____, but unlike others who can, ____." (I think I stole that from somewhere around here.) The first blank tells you something new about the world and about what the character's role is. The second makes the character unique in an Apocalypse World sense. (E.g.: I can build and fix illegal mechs, but unlike others who can, I do it with military-grade tech.) Players get an extra die when they do their special thing. I like to round off chargen with leading questions about how the characters know each other, with the goal being that not everyone is best buds.

    Finally, I keep a word map handy, in case we need to generate some relationships between characters and ideas. (This idea also comes from around here.) I don't give much instruction on how to read such a map, because that's part of the fun.

    One thing I recommend (because it's something I've failed at) is taking frequent breaks. Constantly coming up with dangers and inventing the game world as you go is fatiguing for a lot of players, so I think breaks would help keep the energy up and the ideas flowing.

    \n Well, thanks for the kind words. When you've got a table that good, it's easy to look like you know what you're doing. We ended up going in a very epic console-JRPG direction once it became clear everyone at the table shared that vocabulary and predilection. (Seriously, like, we had a villain named Crane with one black feathered and one stainless steel mechanical wing on either shoulder...) I WILL take credit for that question, which is so great for the super-evocative list of names that come baked into G/E.

    The one place where I feel like we might have flailed a little was in figuring out the way Harm piles up and affects further Danger. We mostly used it as a combination of tag/condition (When Demon's Ghost Scanner got shut down before she unjacked, she had a smoking plug stuck in her head that attracted stray ectoplasmic energy, frex) and an easy way for me to toss another danger on the pile if things were looking cushy. I thought I balanced the threat of incapacitation against going for the jugular too often; my impulse is to not take away anyone's chance to keep playing!

    Anyway, I loved it, and just hope the PS3 version of it drops sometime soon.
