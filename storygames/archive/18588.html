    \n The question that really tripped me up was: "7) How much setting detail do you like your RPGs to contain? On a scale of 1 to 10\u2026"

    This forces respondents to limit their answers. "I like games with a lot of setting detail" is positioned as mutually exclusive with "I like games that let my group define the settings ourselves." If respondents like more than one kind of game (and many of us do), there's no good way to answer that question.

    Question 5 was phrased less problematically because it simply asked, "[How much] do you enjoy...." Question 7 might have avoided this issue by being broken into two questions formatted like that: "How much do you enjoy games with a detailed setting?" and also "How much do you enjoy games with very little setting?"

    Another way to approach this is to try to avoid (or supplement) subjective questions of preference by asking about some other, ostensibly more objective indicator of what players like. Yeah, it's still self-reported, but you might get more useful data that way. Like, in the example above, if you were to ask me, "How much do you enjoy games with X?" and also separately ask "How much do you enjoy games with Y?" I might think, "Well heck, I like both!" Then you can also ask, "How frequently do you play games with X?" or even "When was the last time you bought a game with X?" (and then ask again for Y). Even if I say I like both kinds of games, if it turns out I'm playing and buying one kind a lot more than the other, that tells you something useful from a market feasibility standpoint.

    \n People are notoriously bad at self-reporting what they like and what they don't like. Malcolm Gladwell always says that, "if [he] asked just about anyone what kind of coffee they like, they would always answer: 'a rich, dark roast'. But if we actually put coffee in front of you and had you pick your favourite, you would almost all choose the weak, milky coffee."

    Do your classes address any tools for getting at this information in a more reliable way? JasonT points out a good one, which is to ask people about measurable behaviour ("Have you played an RPG without a GM in the last 30 days?", or, maybe, "Of your last three gaming sessions, how many required you to look up setting details in a game book?").
