\n This is the Compass:


- It's intended to be adjusted for each scene to establish the context for all players.
- Specific fields can be crossed if you want to avoid them in your setting (i.e. cross-out left field for "silly").
- Additional mechanics (i.e. dice probability depending on "chance" etc) are possible, yet not the focus for now.

Do these cover the most important parameters?
Is the description adequate - or do you have better words for it?

Any feedback is appreciated.

    \n If you're looking for words, I'd use "expected" instead of "predictable", perhaps, and "light" instead of "trivial", but I think all of them are just fine as-is, as well. Clarity is sometimes more important than implications, and we clearly all understood what all the terms mean.

    * Unlike Airk and 2097, I *like* the implication that there is no "middle state". It's more helpful to an actor/player/director to be forced to nudge things a little bit one way than to have a vague "neither fast nor slow" directive. You can use that for inspiration when you're a bit stuck.

    * The implications of these "sliding scales" are pretty solid. If I say, "Ok, we're going to have a scene with Judy and Bill, in the drawing room, and it's going to be Slow in pace, as well as Light, Intimate, Slow, Predictable, and Silly, but have Crucial importance", I get a strong sense of what's going to go down.

    * What about the room for surprise, though? The most obvious example being a slow, monotonous scene in a horror movie (Predictable, Intimate, Slow, Trivial, Dark, Serious) which is then interrupted by a gruesome discovery or a monster coming through the door?

    "Surprising" and "Predictable" don't cover the range of going from one to the other, and similarly for all the other categories. (Say, a Silly scene with a sudden Serious one-liner which changes the whole course of the scene.) Isn't that an important part of storytelling?

    \n Just for clarification: You place moveable markers on a field. So, it is 4 fields, indeed. Yes, first I started with 5 fields, yet wanted to force players to take a position. 4 fields have worked well.
    Crossing out is just to reduce some options, i.e. if you want to avoid playing "silly" as option.

    Other alternatives you might also consider:
    - reliable (instead of predictable)
    - casual, mellow (instead of trivial)
    - grave (instead of serious)
    - relaxed, calm (instead of slow)

    As for "surprising": it's the potential of randomness. So if it starts with an illusionary familliarity, it still is an unpredictable scene (i.e. with horror suprise).

    Of course, specific words are debatable. The important things that the COMPASS covers the most useful categories.
