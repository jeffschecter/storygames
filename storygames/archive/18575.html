When we talk about designing a roleplaying game, we talk about designing a game, when we really should talk about designing a session. To design a game, you need rules and rules sets, but to design a session, you need structures.

To make a good roleplaying session, you need a game that have the following:
- Goals for the players. Most of the times it's the same goals as the characters.
- Some sort of tools of interaction, normally in form of a character.
- Each scene framed with a clear intention. The intentions is what the scene should be about.
- Uncertainty. Normally handled by dice.
- Reintroducing previous story elements to create a story that has a substance.

...and some things that aren't handled in roleplaying games.
- A framework to make a solid group, so everyone are on the same level of how the interaction within the group should be handled.
- Structures to free everyone's imagination and make people build on each others ideas. If anyone says "I can't play this because I don't have that kind of imagination", it's the game's fault.

    \nRevised version

    GoalsWhen playing a game, the first thing that you need to know is how to win the game. To play a session in a roleplaying game, the participants needs to have goals. The goals can be a common one or specific for each person, but what's really, and I mean really important is that these goals are known by everybody so that we can create events with the other persons' goals in mind. If one person have as a goal to not reveal that the character as an assassin, the others can create situations where that goal is put in danger. Goals can even be vague, like "To create a story together" or "To explore social constructions".

    In roleplaying games, we often have characters, but the characters' goals doesn't have to be the same as the players. A goal for the player could be to destroy the world. The characters goals could instead be to survive the apocalypse.

    The goals the characters can also be shared or individual. If they are travelling as a group, the group could have a goal and by striving for that goal, obstacles can be in the group's way. This is how traditional roleplaying games works. In collaborative storytelling games, the setup could instead have each character with it's own with individual goal. By making two or more of these goals collide, you reach an uncertain point in the story and things starts to happen.

    Even game master controlled characters (or shared, if collaborative) can have goals, but these only works as obstacles for the player controlled characters. They will either stand in their way or be the reason why the characters have their goal. As an example, if one non-player controlled character's goal could be to destroy the world, the characters' could be to prevent that from happening.

    Without goals, nothing will happen. Only the participants needs goals, but the characters in the story can have their own goals as well. All goals must be known by everybody to make an interesting game.

    \n Actually we all think on goals once we're into one situation, though we usually won't be able to think of one before getting into it. It's part of our adaptative way of thinking as human beings. You don't use goals at all? Think again: Probably after a few minutes into any game you'll have a short term goal; in the worst case it would be "let's understand this game". After maybe 10 sessions in a sandbox campaign your character would have formed some sort of bond with PCs, an NPC, a pet, a place or an activity and go quite far to protect it.

    So goals may not start being proactive, or appear right away, but anybody will eventually develope their own, even if it's a small one and/or doesn't require the character to do too much.

    Now, goals are formed around something the players get to care about, so it's hard to have the players care about anything in the setting without experiencing it themselves first, nor invest straight up front into something of their own (unless they are into creative writing and feel good/safe about the GM and other players toying with their creations).

    Players will go with usual conventions though, so if the GM states their families have been kidnapped, they will react in consequence, making "saving them" their priority goal. Most of times at least, depending of what character they are playing (don't expect a chaotic-evil running to save them)

    Of course, blatantly imposing goals on the PCs feels like totally denying players their freedom of choice and/or giving zero value to their input; waiting for players to come up with everything only works if you've got creatively-extrovert players (a.k.a. GMs) and finding a middle ground, though usual, still takes some time, a few sessions at worst. That's why random tables exist.

    So Paul, no need to put the pressure on the players. Make a list of simple goals related to your setting, have players roll the dice on that list and see what they get. If you want to keep PCs in the same group, make goals interlock (like, to kill this demon you will need to help the party Elf go through the mountains and convince a Druid to give you certain scroll, etc) and if you want to have fun seeing at each other throats, make those goals opposite or exclusive and give them resources to throw at each other. (I tried this last one some time ago and it made us impro an entire Game of Thrones-like session, it was awesome)

    Tools of interaction
    I can say "character" and you will instantly, as a roleplayer, understand what I mean. It's so typical to use a character as a tool of interaction for the story. It's so typical that people don't even realize that they are adding characters without thinking of it. What else can we use to interact with each other during a session?

    We could follow a place by together playing out events that occur on that place from the ice age up to present days. What happened during all those centuries and how did that change the landscape? We could also play out a war and instead of following characters, we follow different countries.

    But this is not what I mean with tools of interaction. What I described above is just different kinds of story elements that we together follow during a roleplaying session. How can we together describe the environment? That's a tool of interaction. How can we decide on a theme for this session? How can we build on each others' ideas? How can we insert plot twists? How can we create a dramatic curve? How can we use each others' imagination to say what we want? How can we understand each other's intentions non-verbally? You can ask all kinds of questions about how we should interact, based on where you want the session to go.

    To think in this way is important if you want to design a session. How are we, as a group, going to interact with each other to create a story? It is as simple as that.

    \n Thanks Rickard!

    Well, I've never played with Archipielago's ritual phrases but the concept seems like an incredible simple fun way to negotiate the fiction among all players. This process has to be something that doesn't allow one player to negate other player's input all the time and rewards building over each other's ideas; I've got a comic publishing company and our brainstroming sessions for argument ideas always work faster and better that way, and get incredibly fun btw; on a good day everyone gets creatively charged with this kind of interaction and ideas snowball into an awesome avalanche.

    I'd also like to try Tenra Bansho's 3 act structure, though from APs it seems that when you add the zero act for all players and character creation time, it usually takes more than a single session to play... one full session. It's not the only game that uses the separate acts structure, what experience have you had with any of these?

    Adding random setting-breaking twists in the middle of the session a la Fiasco also seems to improve the experience too, though it may work with specific genres/games/groups only. In Fiasco's case it works because it's a mandatory process in the game that produces the whole "fiasco" feeling of the story. There are a lot of other ways to make twists in a story, though there aren't many games with procedures for all or at least a wide enough range of options and usually this require an experienced GM/players to be pulled off.

    About scene framing I was thinking of something around the lines of Donjon but handled a bit differently: E.G. roll for perception, on a good roll you get to add specific details to your advantage, on a bad one the GM gets to add details against you. So instead of having the GM prepare all details beforehand and telling you "no, you don't see anything" on a bad roll, you have something interesting happening either way. You can base a whole system on this if you think it well.

    Intentions of scenes
    Creating scenes are something that happens naturally in stories. Whenever you take a leap in either time or space during the story telling, a new scene is created. Each scene should bring something to the story, either by a decision made, a revelation about a element in the story, or an escalation of a conflict. Note that these three can be combined. A revelation may lead to a decision that is the upstart of a conflict (escalation).

    Intentions of scenes can be seen as short term goals for the players and never the characters. They exist so everyone are on the same page in where a scene should go, but not how it ends. "Patricia enslaving Patrick in this scene" is not an intention. That's a statement of what will happen, and it's rather pointless to play out something that doesn't have an uncertainty. Ask instead "why?". That's a revelation, and possibly a decision. Ask how she feels about it. Ask what kind of consequence it will bring. That could bring both a revelation and an escalation of a conflict.

    I talked earlier about goals - about telling the players how to end the game (winning condition) or what the game is about. "You're going to kill your master" or "You're going to convince a ronin to fight for your village" are two goals. The intentions of the scenes can be about something else. They can be about creating a relation with the ronin or making decisions so your loved ones aren't hurt by the master's command.

    The intentions can stay the same or change during the session. In This Is Pulp, all scenes are framed with a danger and the intention is throughout the session to overcome each danger. In InSpectres, you go through four different phases, and each phase brings it's own intention. There is no right or wrong in how you deliver intentions but it's important that you think of this when designing a session because it guides all the participants in how they should play your game.

    Goals of the game
    When playing a game, you need to know what the ending condition is for two reasons. So the ending wont be a surprise and therefore anticlimactic. A game needs its build-up to the ending to make it exciting. The second reason is that the ending condition creates a strive for all the participants. They will know what the game is about and do whatever they can to travel towards that ending. These are the goals of the game.

    The goal must be measured in some way. To have fun or to create something great is to loose to measure, where creating strategies or building a story is easier to measure. The story can be good or bad but at least it's built. Goals can be handled in several ways but what's important to notice is that these are goals for the participants.

    The goals can be for the group or be individual. They can change during the course of the game. But what is common is that all goals must be known by everybody to create a homogeneous experience. One player can't aim for a horror session while another is planning a strategic plan without them knowing about each other's goals. This will tear the group's collaborative experience apart. It may turn out as a successful session anyway, but the chances will decrease. If they know about the individual goals, they can adapt to each other to either hinder or help each other to reach their goals.

    In roleplaying games we often have characters but these doesn't have to be the same goals as the participants. The goal of the game, and what the participants should strive for, could be to unleash the apocalypse while the goal of the characters can be to survive the end of the world (and the end of the game). What's important is that the character goal can be a secret and it can be "terminated", but a participant's goal should never ever be either a secret or terminated. If the character's goal to survive a maze to see it's children again and during the session the character is finding the children killed, the character's goal is terminated. If this is also the participant's goal, the game would end for that person. That's bad if none of the other's goals are achieved. If the goal was a secret, another participant could terminate the goal without knowing about it. That's also bad. The goal could change to find the children's murderers which allows the participant to continue playing, but what this example shows is the danger of melting the participant's and the character's goal together.

    The goal of the game exist so everybody starts at the same level. To show what the game is about. It creates a strive, it contributes to a build-up, it should be known by everybody and it can change during the course of the game. The goal of the game is the first step in designing a session.

    Is your "ending" the end of a session, or the end of a campaign/storyline/module/adventure. Is it, "this is when we know we all can go home?" or "this is when we start a new cycle of storytelling?" It sounds like you're talking about the latter, but the thread is about sessions.

    Through the lens of my AD&D games, circa 1985, we often played late into Friday night / Saturday morning. We'd start at 7 PM or so and we'd finish around 1-2 AM. It took 6-7 hours to get to a satisfying stopping place. Part of that was the 30-60 minutes of dithering before we started, or the 30-60 minutes in the middle when we stopped for pizza. In terms of story arc for a particular session, I presented the players with some kind of challenge or threat, and they responded, and then they got XP for it. The session was complete when they had faced the problem. Sometimes they won, sometimes they lost. Over many of these sessions, they'd earn enough XP to level up. Then the real cycle was complete. That's why they played. (It was totally incoherent in many ways.)

    I feel like this discussion is retreading a lot of ground that was covered on the Forge, in terms of creative agenda (and incoherence) and social contract. That's fine! It's good to start fresh and think about things in new ways. The idea of session design is a cool, new approach. The "session" as a fundamental unit of time for games is obvious but largely ignored in a lot of design thinking.

    I submit that there are smaller units of time that might be interesting here, and they accumulate into sessions: let's call them "beats" (or "scenes," if you prefer). There's been a lot of design effort here. I'd be curious to see what results from working toward session design from both directions--from the scenes, up, and from the full-story-arcs, down.
