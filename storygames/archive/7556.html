\n On Friday, John, James and William play a game.

Next Friday, John and James show up but William has to cancel. However, John brings his girlfriend Sarah. They play, continuing from last time in some sense Not necessarily chronological order - it might be same characters, different characters, different location, something seemingly unrelated but with thematic links, I dunno.

Saturday, John and Sarah are alone together, but are still so buzzed about the game that they want to play more - so they do, by themselves, the two of them - but again in some sense, it's still the same game.

Tuesday, James, William and mutual friend Sam meet up for something unrelated, but they play a bit of the game anyway. Again, it's still somehow linked.

Is this possible? Can one have a discontinuous group and maintain a continuous "game", and if so in what sense? Where can the continuity be located, if no one person is there for all sessions?

I want this, in a real sense, especially for online play. But I wonder where the boundary between separate games and all one game truly lies. What is continuity, anyway? Why do we want it? Is it a vital part of proceedings, or can it be bent and twisted or even disregarded in the service of our social purpose, which is to play the game, here, now, with the people available at the time?

    \n Universalis can play like this. We've had Uni sessions which have added new players or were missing previous players, yet were still part of the same continuity. The hard part is recordkeeping -- whenever a session starts, regardless of who is present to play, the records of the existing continuity have to be available so that players know what is going on. In games with a GM, the 'keeper of the continuity' is of course the GM. In Universalis, it could be anybody, or everybody, depending on the group.

    I've experimented with writing out the events of our Uni game in a fashion similar to a movie script (with a column of initials and coin expenditures down one side) so that it could be read by the players before the next session. That's something that would be easier for a chat room game -- the chat log is the script. Or, it could be set up as a wiki.

    I like Meserach's idea of an oral history, though; the story gets told to people, and then they play the game and add to the tale, and what the previous players remembered and felt was important is what retroactively becomes true. It would be a viral meme, spread by word of mouth.

    Thread drift: (Game as virus -- the game seeks to reproduce itself, 'infecting' new players, who then become the vectors for the spread of the virus to new hosts...)

    The main problem I see with the oral history model is that as the story gets longer and more complex, and has more side-stories that have been explored, and more events and interconnections to remember (and forget), the players will eventually have to become epic storytellers like Homer -- able to recite the entire Odyssey from memory.

    Which is not a bad thing, really...

    JD may well have a different experience from mine. For me, very few people actually did know everything that happened, including the Story-Tellers and assistants. Basically, the more often you were involved with the weekly LARP session, the more likely you were to know most of what was going on.

    Likewise, the more regularly present you were the more likely you were to be tapped to join "the staff".

    This, in turn, meant you were more likely to be able to work events you were interested in into the game as "official" parts of the story, either as a player, or an ST.

    The next closest thing I saw was unofficial Cell-Structure. Usually, one of your RL acquaintances/pals who you gamed with would be really, really into the LARP, even if you weren't. They would act as your contact, keeping you (and other friends) up-to-date on the LARP events. Since they were really into the game, they'd often be "tapped" either to act as staff or to enact ST generated events, or as a source for reporting in-game events to STs.

    Again, my experience with LARPS is limited to on-going casual VampLARPs, inevitably played at bars with a fair amount of drinking involved, and usually a couple dozen regular players and perhaps 2-3 times that casual players. Other styles of LARP do differ significantly.

    \nI probably could do something mostly-exhaustive, but I'm really damn lazy. So I'll just talk about my own LARPs.

    In Paragons, a Vampire game, I kept notes on the event, and on 'the circle'. The circle went like this: At the end of each event, the players formed up into a big circle, and I'd have bits like "On the count of three, point to the player whose character provoked you most to action this game." Then I'd get explanations, and give pointed-at people more XPs. The notes I made on these things, I fleshed out a bit, and printed up each event along with the character sheets (on the back, actually), as a kind of "rumor sheet".

    ...

    In Dustbowl Prophets, I kept a "Rumor Deck", a giant stack of business cards. One of the very very common abilities in the game was the ability to draw seven cards from the deck, read 'em, and then put seven back. However, you didn't have to put back the same seven; you could throw some of the ones you drew away (or all of 'em), make up new ones, and shuffle them in. I started the deck loaded each night with little notes about the previous event, which I deliberately distorted.

    ...

    Sea-changed, which I'm starting in the next month, and have already started group prep on, will use both kinds of continuity, and also has a facebook group and a wiki. It hasn't hit play yet, and the tools are mutating, by my general idea there is that rather than producing the "canon", it's better if I work on ensuring open lines of communication and coordination, and to ensure that those same lines have lots of room (maybe even some rewards) for one-to-one or one-to-many communications about what is and isn't in the group fiction.

    ...

    Every game I've ever played also saw the "informal cells" that Robert mentioned upthread.

    \n I had the same problem as you. The way I fixed it:

    - I adopted the concept of TV series (is there a plural?) episodes. So each session is an episode. You don't expect that everyone would sit through it. So every session has a beginning and an and with its own climax, cliffhanger, etc. And the episodes can be enjoyed separately without knowing the former episodes. (I know it became a trend to have story arcs in American TV since B5 and it's an old tradition of British TV for example in Dr. Who, but most of the time even those series have self-contained episodes. But if it bothers you, think rather about A-team or Knight Rider or Mission Impossible.)

    - The big difference between a TV series and RPG is that you know exactly who "watches". So while a TV series has to have continuity (no, it doesn't, but the audience likes to think that it's a complex world, so the writers try to write continuous episodes) all the time, because there is a possibility that there will be at least one person who sits through the whole series, you know exactly wich episodes can have references to wich former episodes and wich doesn't have to.

    - We can call that half-continuity. Because if you have person A and B playing in one episode and person C and D playing in another, the two episodes can be totally contradictuous. But if there's a third episode with B and C or any mixed possibility, than it has to be continuous regarding to both of the former episodes. (For example if A and B stole a jewel, than C and D can steal the same jewel and B and C can protect the jewel. Episode I and II contradicts each other, but episode III references both.)

    - And if you are arrived here, than you can go further. If the players forget something, you can do as it never happened. Really, that works and it's awesome. For example once I GMed a session where I didn't want an NPC to interfere. The players forgot about that he was there, so I just didn't mention them that he's at hand until the next session.

    Remember, it's a game and what happens won't be set in stone. The joy comes from what happens right now and not what story comes out. The players will even remember different stories than it was in reality. So, continuity really doesn't matter. If the players are somewhat open-minded, than even if they were present and remember, even then it can be refreshing to screw the continuity.

    (If you are interested, I specially use this format: The PCs are agents of an organization and they get missions from them. I tell the players what their mission is and start the adventure in medias res at the first important scene where something happens. I ask them before the session till when the players have time and I use an independent factor based on realtime to press them on. (A mighty enemy closing in or a poison having effect.) I use forced scene framing to make sure that the climax will happen in the current session. I'm having a continuous story arc for my own joy, but make changes depending upon wich players will be present at the next session. And if I have to choose between continuity and what can be made joyfully in the next session, than I rather have the latter.)

    \n The OP mentioned stuff about not necessarily kepping it chronological. Here's my suggestion:

    Keep a piece of paper, a wiki, or similar. After the each session, write down a couple of questions, like "What will happen to X?", "Who was behind the mysterious Y?" and "How did Z get so disillusioned?". Start every session (except the first, of course) by deciding on one or a couple of questions to answer during play. Someone will probably know enough about the background to some questions, and can fill the others in. then finish by writing down some more questions. Cross out the answered questions, or actually write down the answers if you want a written record.

    You can make huge leaps in time ("What civilization left those ruins behind?"), or small ones, and after a number of sessions, you've got a big interconnected story.

    Seems to me there's two critical things to discontinuous stories with contiguous, player-possessed and -controlled characters:
    * Episodic play, containing a complete story arc in a single session (or two, at most) - Many have mentioned this common technique for dealing with all sorts of story or logistical constraints (been done by comics and TV for decades).
    * System does not employ niche roles - To unpack this a bit, I mean that you can't have a system which relies on niche fulfillment by individual characters. A good example is D&D: the cleric misses a night and you either roll in a sock-puppet NPC or you start to bend the game plans to suit lack of healer support. Or the Shadowrun Decker: he's out, and suddenly you have missions to sites with no cyber security, or missions well outside of the techosphere where cyberspace is ubiquitous. And there's the whopper: a GM with critical secrets to apportion (highly GMful games that rely on surprise for much of their fun)--she misses a session, and it's often either "pick up game" time or a-whole-nother arc (even with new characters!) must be born by a substitute GM.

    Everything after those elements is logistics, be it communicative (how do we keep everyone on the same page, story- or event-wise?) or practical (where do we game when our usual host can't participate?). One can resolve each of those using any one (or a combination of) a host of techniques and technologies, many of which were mentioned above.

    \n Some people have tried to deal with this by having a (in-fiction) setup that can explain the come-and-go of characters. Keeping character and player knowledge close to the same makes handling the continuity of information pretty easy, overall (at least in theory).

    For instance, the action takes place on a small island. Helicopters and planes are always coming and going, and the personnel on the island all have duties that require them to travel back and forth on an irregular basis.

    So, if players A, B, and C are there on a given night, then they agree that it must, of course, be the day when character D has to take off to make a delivery and won't be back until the following session. If the next time player D is back, then so is his character, but maybe this time character B has to make a quick trip off the island...

    Ideally, this would be a GM-less game, but that's not a setup I've ever seen friends of mine try.

    My question: Has anyone seen this work really well, or is it always a little... you know, kind of fishy and unbelievable?
