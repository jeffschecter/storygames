\n It's time for the now-apparently-semi-annual Name Game! (Formerly known as the "Title Game," but "Name Game" rhymes so it wins.)

Game designers come up with game titles way more often than they actually design those games. Sadly, many potentially awesomely titled games are lost in this process.

Six months ago, I started a brainstorming exercise: I come up with a title, you come up with a game concept. It can be a board game, rpg, card game, whatever. It can be just a theme, just a few mechanical ideas, but hopefully a little of both. The point is to keep things moving, not produce a perfect, complete game right off the bat.

Then you challenge me with a title of your own and we go back and forth until we can't go on any longer.

A few took up the gauntlet during the last game, but Fred Hicks and I went back and forth for over a month coming up with dozens of game ideas. This eventually led to Dinocalypse, a new dinosaur-themed supplement for Spirit of the Century. See? This thing actually can produce some cool stuff.

I'm hosting a Name Game on my livejournal, where the branching comment structure can allow for multiple forking challenges and counter-challenges, but I'd also like to have an ongoing challenge on story-games with Andy's permission.

Let's follow the F1 Re: Esc rules of etiquette since we don't have branches.

Fred's kicking things off with the title:

It's About Damn Time

To recap: The first poster on this thread will post a game idea based on Fred's title and then propose a title of her own. Someone else will post a game idea based on her title, and so on, until we plotz.

    Surf Party Mars!

    It's about surfers on Mars. All the surfers are young, hip and sexually attractive aliens. They bed-hop. Different races get different bonuses for different surf stunts. The relationship mechanics are intertwined with the surf competition: your tryst with the pink snake-haired groupie affects your ability to ride the waves. Like Contenders, it's set against the background of the surf party, but it's really about relationships between the surfers and the smashing and making of dreams.

    \nJesus Christ What the Hell Is That

    A party game along the lines of the dictionary game or Balderdash. One person takes the role of Jesus, selecting a passage in the bible. He announces the book, chapter and verse. Players then write down fake answers on slips of paper. Jesus reads all of the fake answers and the real one in random order. Players then vote on which one they think is the correct answer. Players get points if people guess their fake answer or if they guess they correct one. Jesus then rotates ... ahem the role of Jesus rotates to the left.

    \nThe Warhol Clock

    Andy Warhol has a time machine! Players take on the roles of drag queens, amphetamine junkies, and other assorted Factory hangers-on who try to win Warhol's favor by manipulating the past in order to create celebrities and everyday objects for the artist to paint. The game alternates between "muck around with the past" scenes and "hang out at the Factory" scenes. Resolution is card-based. Players spend cards to increase their chances of success in manipulating history; the cards that are spent then form a "Warhol Deck" from which players draw during Factory scenes to see how impressed Warhol is with their time traveling antics. Players can take voluntary penalties during the Time Travel scenes in order to stack the deck during the Factory scenes.

    \nBroken Dreamers is the game of crash victims lost in comas in a those lost in the wasteland where dead fantasies go to die. The world is created step by step out of fragments of everway, planescape, and oWoD materials, and the resolution heavily favors co-operation in achieving any action. Tragically, only one dreamer can return to the world of the living. Resolution is also card-based, but uses regular playing cards in what essentially is a layered game of War with the Deck. The game is GMless.

    \nSemicolon: The Fragmenting

    You're a novelist with a deadline and writer's block. As you fall asleep at the keyboard one evening you enter a world ruled by run-on sentences and dangling participles in the guise of fictional characters from various books you've read. Conan to The Yearling to Jane Eyre. You must escape by playing their games and solving their puzzles. The game uses no randomizing method of resolution. Instead players must use phrases based on English grammar to steal narration rights from the other players. When specific conditions are met that player's character can wake up and "win" the game.

    Call Me Bob

    A drinking game for a large group. One player starts, turns to another player and says: "Hi, I'm Bob" while bobbing his head furiously and taking a drink from his beer. The new player turns to another player and says: "Hi, I'm [name]" while doing something representing the name and taking a drink from his beer, adding "Have you met my friend Bob" while bobbing his head furiously and taking a drink from his beer. Play continues with each new player adding their name and recalling the names of all of his "friends" and acting out all of their names. If a player is unable to successfully recount the names of their friends or forgets which friend is which or forgets to act out the name, they lose and chug a full beer. Play starts anew. Some potential names include: Eileen, Dick, Gabby, Mark, Matt, etc.

    \n Ubermenschen Fantasy Fulfilled

    In A World where the fascists have finally and conclusively won (players have their choice of which fascist movement has conquered the earth), things have begun to break down after a mere few weeks. Scientists are warning of the increasing dangers of inbreeding, far-right factions are calling for yet more purges, and the inevitable compromises made by those in power constantly threaten to show through. In this challenging (in many senses) game, at least one player must play a character from each of the lower-class rightists, upper-class leaders, and middle-class researchers. The game's asymmetric systems affect one another in a complex fashion, leading to several evenings of bleak-as-fuck roleplaying.

    \nBleak-Ass Fuck

    The ozone layer dissolved; the carbon dioxide grew; the seas rose; Rome burned. 2,000 of the world's best - the best athlete, the best politician, the best physicist, the best prostitute, you can name the rest - were loaded on the last hope, a multi-nationally-built spacecraft with untested technology, supposedly designed to reach the stars. 229 light-years away, a planet was found that could support life, and the best scientists said that it probably had single-cell organisms. By the time you got there - about 400 years, if the tech worked - it should still be ripe for starting over.

    The tech didn't work. Oh, cryogenesis worked. Worked for 5.5 billion years, the fucker did. Enough time for life to form, plants to grow, lizard-people to evolve, start a gamma-radioactive war, and turn their lush green-ball into a wasteland. No ozone layer, high carbon dioxide, boiling seas, and burning rock. There's 2000 of you, 1 million of them left, and one of you have got to go. Man up or die in a worse hell-hole than you started from.

    With Swords

    \n

    SWORDS. Smith-Wyst Ordered Respritory Dysfunction Syndrome. A role-playing game where the PCs are carriers of the next big thing in catastrophic illness. Those with SWORDS are carriers. They will not die from their disease. However, every non-SWORDS person they come into contact with will contract a sudden and incurable respritory disease. It is 100% communicable and 100% deadly. One SWORDS carrier cannot contract the deadly version, so those with SWORDS can only safely come into social contact with others who are carriers.

    \n

    The stories revolve around the herding, capture, and forcable quarrantine of every known SWORDS carrier. Potential individual storylines can range from dealing with the incarcaration to being asked to help hunt down those that have avoided capture. After all, no regular police force wants to risk contact with a violent SWORDS carrier. Eventual end-game to a campaign style play could involve determining if the world remains occupied with SWORDS or non-SWORDS persons.

    \nThe Florida Keys Spacecrash, a nightmare scenario for the burgeoning United States space program of the early 1960s. As part of their training, all pilots had to go through an improv theater scenario about how they would handle a calamitous systems failure while in space. You are an astronaut-in-training, playing out that scenario.

    This is a jeepform game inspired by Apollo 11, featuring heavy use of props. The goal is to prevent the Florida Keys Spacecrash through the clever use of random objects you have lying around your "space capsule" (ie, whatever room you're playing in.). Players can bring as many strange and unusual objects as they can find before the game, but every object must find a purpose in order for the crash to be prevented.

    Complications arise with each attempt to repair the capsule, but can be diminished or reversed by having personal scenes between astronauts (ie, Dealing with each other's space madness), between the astronauts and Houston (ie, Explaining the various systems malfunctions), or between the astronauts and their loved ones (ie, I love you, Barbara. Take care of the kids).

    \nNecktie Tourniquet

    A party game of zany tongue-twisters for 3-12 players from ages 3 to 93. Players draw cards from a single deck. Each card bears a single word. Players start with a single card in hand. On a player's turn, they can either (1) draw a card from the top of the deck, (2) blindly trade a card with one other player at the table, or (3) issue a challenge. To issue a challenge, the player takes two or more cards from their hand and lays them down in front of another player in a particular order of the player's choosing. That player must then say the words on the cards, in order, five times fast without erring. If the player fails to do so, they are knocked out of the game. If the player succeeds, the challenging player is knocked out. Zaniness ensues.
