    \n Star Wars d6 system. I'm 16\u2026

    The GM says everyone starts at effectively level 1 when you first join the group. Being an inexperienced roleplayer, I didn't understand what that meant. Everyone else was effectively 10th level or higher. Which meant I spent most of the time doing nothing or failing constantly.

    So I bribed the GM to have 1 on 1 sessions where I could catch up with the other players. Then in a game involving time travel, I went back in time and stayed there after the rest of the players traveled back to the present. Everyone was like\u2026 dude, your character is unplayable now.

    So I met with the GM and convinced him to let me use my knowledge of the present to invent the death star, become rich, and kept myself perpetually alive by turning into cyborg. I explained that logic demanded that by the time it was the present again, I should be level 20. The other players protested but the GM caved in! It was ridiculous. I was ridiculous. I was so annoyed that they wouldn't let me start at the same level as the rest of the group that I used time travel to become more powerful than everyone combined.

    By that point I was mad with power and convinced the GM that if there is a Light and Dark side of the force\u2026 there should be a Grey side! And I wanted to be the first Grey Side Jedi! Which of course meant that I could have both Light and Dark side powers and to stay true to the Grey\u2026 I just had to balance every good act with an evil act.

    Granted, I was 16\u2026 but I was ridiculous!

    \n Shadowrun. I'm 19\u2026

    We just spent the last 2 years playing superhero games which was fun. The group decided they wanted to start a game they used to play when they were younger\u2026 Shadowrun. It sounded like we were going to play sociopaths who were willing to kill anyone for money. I was not into that at all. I liked playing good characters who made the world a better place. And it seemed everyone was fetishizing the Cannon Companion sourcebook. The game seemed like an excuse to revel in gun porn. Which I was not into. I should have just not played the game. It was clear everyone wanted what I didn't but I still desired to hang out with my friends.

    So they let me play a hacker. Which meant I got to do nothing for 2 hours when combat started (and combat was sloooow\u2026 2 hours was probably 4 rounds of combat). And when it was my turn to do something cool, because the hacking rules were so confusing the GM ruled that I would resolve all hacking with a single roll. Which meant my spotlight time was 10 minutes while everyone else had fun for hours. So it became clear that violence in this game was the only way to have fun.

    So I made a character that could fight but would only subdue his opponents. Of course that ended up meaning\u2026 I could engage in combat but I sucked. Eventually, someone just said, "dude, just play a normal character\u2026 stop trying to fight what the game is about." I should have stopped playing but instead\u2026

    I embraced the carnage that is Shadowrun. But I went too far. I borrowed all the books I could find and made the most broken min maxed character I could. I figured if combat was why the game was fun, I'd make the most powerful character I could to ensure I would have the most fun. And on top of that, because I couldn't relate to my character, I made them a brainwashed amnesiac killing machine so I didn't have to bother with a character background that would never come into play anyway.

    It was ridiculous. My character was so broken, I always went first, instantly killed almost anything in 1 action, and bullets bounced off of me. I was too effective and made combat unfun.

    \n I can't believe I'm about to write this\u2026

    Captain Drawback!

    I'm 17 and we're playing DC Super Heroes. The GM says, "you can play anything you want\u2026 imagine a character concept for a new superhero or just copy any of your favorite superheroes. Then make them with X build points."

    So I'm like\u2026 I want to play Spiderman, Batman, Superman, Green Lantern, Wolverine, Professor X, or Magneto! Any of these characters would be awesome!

    Then I try to make my character\u2026 and I couldn't do it. Nothing I did with those numbers came even close to making even a C list superhero. But I wasn't the only one. Everyone was having the same problem but the more experienced players were all taking something called\u2026 drawbacks. But they had to take a lot of drawbacks\u2026 a stupid amount\u2026 to make the characters they wanted. It seemed silly to me but I was growing frustrated so I decided to experiment.

    I took almost every drawback in the book. It was ridiculous. I just went down the list and took every single thing I could. I needed an equally ridiculous character concept\u2026

    Captain Drawback!

    I can't remember why I did this\u2026 but I decided I was insane due to an accident during the shooting of a Frosted Flaked commercial. You know those commercials where adults are blacked out so that they could remain anonymous while they professed their love for Tony the Tiger and sugar crusted cornflakes? My character was one of those people except it was during a live benefit show\u2026 except something went wrong\u2026 seriously wrong!

    The silhouette / blackout / anonymous feature didn't work and everyone saw my character's identity\u2026 everyone knew he was obsessed with frosted flakes. He snapped. And Captain Drawback was born!

    Most of his drawbacks were related to tigers or powdered cereal. But strangely I made it work! I was able to buy so many powers that I could effectively negate the danger of the majority of my drawbacks. I lost most of my powers when within a 20 foot range of frosted flakes\u2026 no problem! My unaffected teleport power got me out of danger! And on and on and ridiculously on!

    \n Oh, are we talking about being problem GMs, too? Because I was that.

The player (sorry, Steve!) got to play somebody other than the cool guy he loved for most of the campaign, until four years had passed.

    What is it with me and prison? Hopefully I'm over that.

    One cool example of how system really does include the social interaction of a group: One time, being a dickish GM, I arbitrarily decided that a guy who had been tramping around Africa had contracted river blindness. I thought it would be dramatic and cool. It went like this:

    JASON: Your guy has river blindness. He is slowly going blind.

    PATRICK: No, he isn't.

    JASON: Actually he is. He's got river blindness.

    PATRICK: No, he doesn't.

    And the group just nodded in tacit support of Patrick, and that was that.

    \nThe NYC community went through at least a year of strife after Dogs in the Vineyard and Burning Wheel Revised were released.

    A bunch of us who were not having fun playing D&D 3E played Dogs and were like\u2026 why is this fun every single time we play? I ran it over a 100 times and maybe had 2 bad games. It felt like magic. So we assumed that whatever theory this game was born out of must be some sort of special secret sauce. Granted, we had no idea what that theory was talking about but we knew the results were amazing so there must be something there. We started our own theory forum to try to understand it all which ended up alienating a bunch of people who loved playing D&D or who believed rules don't matter, it is the GM that makes games great. Lots of friction occurred. I knew at least 2 gaming groups that broke apart over arguing what to play, Burning Wheel or D&D. It was especially bad because we knew something we experienced was amazing but didn't have the words to explain it and pointing to forge theory articles and forum posts just confused people and gave them even more ammunition not to play the games we were excited about.

    I remember carrying Dogs in the Vineyard and a prepared town in my backpack for a year every time I visited my old gaming group hoping at some point they would try it. They did. 2 people commented\u2026 "this games actually makes you roleplay your character."

    \n An anecdote of both the DM and I both being dickish was when my friend said he was going to come up with a 2E campaign that was custom-built for our characters. That wasn't really something that we did in that group all too often, so the other players and I were stoked. All of the PCs in the party were Chaotic except for one who was True Neutral, and the first potion of the campaign involved us going to a logging camp where the loggers were striking. When we got there, we were told that we were to meet with management to find a way to force the loggers back to work. Now, this was one of those instances where real world politics got in the way of gameplay, but I'm a staunch lefty and labour supporter and my friend knew it, so when we got to the picket lines I told the DM flatly, "I'm not going to cross."

    "What?"

    "I'm not going to cross the picket lines. I'm going to sit out here in solidarity with the loggers."

    "But you have to go."

    "No, I don't. I'm not helping management."

    Then we stared at each other for a couple of minutes. I'm not sure how we got back on track after that, but we managed to work around that impasse. A session or so later, a company of the Duke's troops (who were Lawful Evil) stopped us on the road to wherever we were going and demanded our loot/equipment. Being a bunch of Chaotic smartmouths who weren't likely to hand over our hard-won treasure simply because we were being shaken down by a random encounter, we told the Lieutenant to go fuck himself. The DM mentioned that there was four of us and about 20 of them.

    "So?" I asked. "I'm not kow-towing to this dickbag."

    Then the PCs started scrapping, were nearly slaughtered, were arrested by the remaining troops and thrown into the Duke's prison. I think we maybe lasted another session with him as the DM. It wasn't a fondly remembered series of adventures.

    \n I've been a 'problem player', but not really because I was a dick per se, but because I pushed with my expectations against what the game was pretty clearly doing.

    Storn Cook, if he sees this, will remember the time I 'broke' a Champions game we were playing in like 15 years ago. The GM was incredibly heavily invested in his intricately designed world, with multiple branching continuities and weekly real-time news updates and stuff, it was actually a beautiful thing that he had meticulously constructed, and the game we were currently involved in was heading towards a big climax that was a nicely traditional superhero team arc, where we were about to break big-time. Without getting into minutia, he presented us with an offer that wasn't meant to be a choice, it was the next chapter of the story. My character turned it down, and talked the other PCs into going along with him. The GM was completely stunned, didn't see it coming, literally said "OK, well, you... broke the game. I'll need a few weeks to figure something out." and we quit in the middle of the session. I played only a few more games before he went 'on hiatus' with the game, eventually restarting it a few months later without letting me know. The argument can be made that I wasn't being a dick, but it was a situation where it was CLAR at the table that I was breaking hard against where the game was heading, and I took a probably needless stand 'on principle' when I should have trusted him to let me have character bits that worked into the new paradigm. When someone gets that upset, if you're empathetic, you don't just shrug and say 'welp, that's rough, but it's what my character'd do...' you try to work out a reasonable compromise, you talk out the issue. We didn't, really, and it wrecked what had been up until then a pretty cool game.

    The other time was recently, when I got involved in a group (and a DM) whose play style was not really entirely compatible with mine. But this was at a time where I was JUST starting to do gaming outside the store after many years of concentrating almost solely on gaming to build the business, so I was bound and determined to stick with it, because I needed to 'get used' to gaming outside my 'comfort zone'. So I stubbornly stuck with it, even as I came home from games doing significantly more complaining than nerd-wanking to my wife afterwards. She was the one who convinced me to bow out, and I think the DM was happy he didn't have to ask me to stop coming because the style was not really a good fit for me.

    Sometimes, it's not an attitude problem or even cluelessness, sometimes it's just the determination to have fun even if it makes you miserable trying.
