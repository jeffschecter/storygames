\n One of the things my girlfriend and I spend a lot of time talking about is where we get inspirations for our stories. What little elements of life inspire ideas, and then what mutations those ideas go through before they can be used.

So, last night I was thinking about using visuals to inspire an entire setting. I'm sure most of you have done this: you dream up one really cool image, either a place, a character, or an event, and then you build an entire setting -- and sometimes a narrative or game or both -- to create a place for that image to fit. And sometimes by the time you're done, the image has changed slightly, but hopefully for the better.

Here's what I've been looking at: in a lot of video games lately, characters have cool dangly props -- scarves, capes, long hair -- that move in interesting ways as the character navigates the environment. This is partly to show off the games' physics, but also it adds a sense of motion an movement, even when the character is static. Similarly, the "dungeonpunk" design aesthetic that many modern fantasy RPG artists have embraced involves lots of little hanging bits, be they amulets, scroll tubes, scarves (again) or other animated accessories. Wayne Reynolds and Steve Prescott spring particularly to my mind.

So, I started thinking "wouldn't it be cool if in my setting, everyone had lots of cool flowing layers and carried little gadgets and scroll tubes on their belts? What could a setting have that would cause that?"

What I came up with was a world where some particularly aggressive tree similar to the walking palm has overrun the world. The trees, possibly with malicious intent, are constantly shifting and crawling across the land, making any large-scale cultivation or civilization basically impossible. Instead, you have city-states that are walled off and protected from the trees, and adventurers that move between them. The adventurers all possess copies of a magical map that shows the few safe points in the world; the maps are artifacts of some past time, so they are a limited commodity. Since they need the maps at a moments notice, and must always carry them, they all have cool scroll tubes on their belts.

To make the setting one notch more hostile, I decided that the weather was also incredibly unpredictable, so you have storms and sudden temperature swings throughout. As anyone who does a lot of outdoor activities will tell you, layering is essential to keep dry and adjust to temperature, so all the heroes also wear multiple capes, cloaks, wraps, and the like. As an added benefit, the layers can be used to break the outline of the human form, and thus add to natural camouflage.

Further refinements to the idea were that the trees, the storms, and other elements of the hostile environment are all gods or spirits in an animistic sense, and are actively hostile to humans for any number of reasons. Humans have found safety by using the bodies of dead gods to build their cities on: major hills and rises, and sometimes lakes, are all remnants of other gods that have died. The maps that the heroes carry, I figured, show the relative distance between different dead gods, and the hostile gods don't mess with the dead gods out of either respect or fear. The gods thing also mean that the characters might carry amulets and charms which may be able to drive away hostile forces.

So, I have no game (if it's a game), I have no story (if it's a narrative form), but I have a setting that serves my visual desire of characters who wear layers of dangly bits.

Anyone else have a similar example to share?

    Adam, here's a thing to consider: what does your setting require? If it's hot, look at cultures that live in hot places. Is it on mountains? Look at cultures that live in the mountains. Are they literate? Look at cultures that are literate.

    \n

    I find the "spiky armor" phenomenon to be deeply irritating. They're artifacts without a culture to support them. In real life, armor wasn't covered with spikes, and that's not because they somehow never thought of it. It's because it didn't make sense to get super-spiky. It's heavy, it catches on stuff. Spiked armor is more a sign of dilettantism that badassery in my eyes.

    \n

    I'd be a lot more scared of this guy...
    \n

    \n

    ... than this guy, who looks like he got his armor at Hot Topic:
    \n

    \n

    ... and that's because the first guy has stuff that looks like it means business. He's not wearing armor to show off, and the stuff that is there to show off carefully doesn't get in the way of the things he really needs. He lives in a cold place (wearing wool and fur), comes from a tradition of craft (sleek metalwork), likes to be recognized in battle (one dangly bit holding on his cape, plus the bright, geometric colors on his shield). His name is probably Alfred or something.

    \n

    The second guy, who I'm naming Bulgor the Eye-Groin, looks like he was designed by his publicist. He doesn't have a way to put away his sword. His spikes look terrifying but are going to get in his own way and that of his allies. (Never mind the weird anatomy the guy must have. Does he have double shoulders or something?) His horns are probably mostly for being grabbed by the guys he's fighting. In order to show off his pirate boots, his shins are unarmored.

    \n

    So, start off bottom up. Figure out how the place has evolved, or why it's been created, or whatever. Build things from the ground up. If you start with the window dressing, you'll forget to keep out the rain.

    I'm big on scarves, cloaks, baubles, and other assorted danglies. In fact, my heroes as envisioned wouldn't wear armour at all, because they are primarily travellers and explorers, and chances are anything that might try to kill them is scary enough that escaping it is much more viable than fighting it. If they have extra protection, it's more like climbing gear than war-harness. Ankle-and-wrist, knee-and-shin. Maybe some light torso protection. Like modern special forces, with a fantasy design, and capes and hoods and magical amulets to keep the moss-spirits away in the night.

    Hino can live in my setting, and so can Anubis, Altair, and perhaps some version of Captain Jack Sparrow.

    The real-world culture this is definitely an issue. The "layered" look I want starts with hot-weather cultures (loose-fitting wraps and robes, mouth scarves, veils) and then adds on temperate climate layers (waterproofing, leather, hooded capes) and then prepares for the worst (fur robes and accents). If the weather can go any which way at any time -- and it actively hates you and wants you dead, so it will -- you need to be prepared for anything.

    \nYeah, that gets the core of what I'm thinking about: can you look at something visual, or imagine something that inspires you, then build the setting around the idiosyncrasies of the visual element and explain why they are, in fact, practical?

    Like Daniel's Lego-men-with-spears.

    So, I guess a more complex objective would be to take something like the Reynolds rogue above (and you're right, those are knives, just in case her enemies run out and need one within easy reach) and explain why, in her world, it's a good idea to have your arsenal in a place that's not easy for you to get to, where it might easily be dropped, and interfere with the movement of your arms. OK, so maybe that's not the best example.

    Like, OK, having just read Shock 1.1 (so cool, btw), it would be like taking the illustration on page 43 and nothing else, and deciding what was going on in the entire setting that made that picture make sense. For an extra twist, include misperceptions, like my belief, upon first viewing, that the reflection showed a much dirtier, more-run-down, and lower-tech version of the scene than the actual vista.

    I think we all do this, to some extent. Actually, I know we do, every time we watch a movie where we are presented with a slice of the world, we subconsciously fill in the gaps. Sometimes we even have debates about how the rules of the world work. Take Star Wars, for example. There may be a canonical reason how their FTL works, but I have no idea what it is. So -- at least implicitly -- I extrapolate the function of the SW hyperdrives based on what we see on the screen, and I extrapolate an inventor (or multiple inventors) of FTL technology, and patent struggles, and government interventions, and all that background stuff.

    You're talking about the Casbah, right?

    \n

    Yeah, we started with the idea that the place is really wet and moldy and hot. Windows are open. The people living at the base of the space elevator are very poor and make their houses out of some sort of adobe-like polymer. Originally, they were all the same, but they've been repaired and modified over time. The windows have these little peaks to keep rain dripping around, rather than into, windows. The roofs are actually domed to let the water run off, but the frame of the building is a cube because that's an easy building module, so they've cut holes in the corners to let the water run out, neither of which were in the original design. There are also abundant gutters in the streetsand they build little Japanese-style foot bridges over them to get into their houses.

    \n

    We'd also started with the idea that the people living there were North African Arabs who'd left in some sort of economic migration. From that came the Arabic symbolism \u2014 the chamsah, the fact that there's a casbah at all, and the two guys drinking tea conspiratorially. It was supposed to be like Algiers, but a wet company town instead of a colonial puppet.

    So, that's about how I do it at my house.

    \n So I arrive at the picture with just the picture and none of your story. I see a North African-style city with a big shiny high-tech tower in the distance. I see a road, slick and shiny, but in the reflection, the tower looks like a mountain. One of the antennae isn't reflected. The buildings too are less-square, more run-down, as if they were built by less-skilled hands, or out of shoddier materials.

    Now my job is to figure out what kind of world makes that possible. Is it a matter of reality/illusion? If so, which is reality? Is it a matter of two parallel cities, somehow meeting at the point of the Silver Road? Is it a representation of the inevitability of some symbols, showing that these people, in this place, will always build that Tower, no matter how many times it is torn down?

    I guess I'm getting at some kind of inversion of what you have here. Actually, a LOT like that contest that was up a few weeks back, now that I think about it, with the cropped Victoriana-style image of the airship with the female pilot, except starting from a bit more of a generality.
