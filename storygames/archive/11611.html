\n So, a while back I played a short game of Sorcerer with Joe and Daniel. Colin (ccreitz) asked me to write it up, and since there was some cool fiction, and I also realized something pretty important about verisimillitude and how we made demands on each others' fictions, here it is. I said there was cool fiction, right? We can start with that.\n
Posted By:Joe on his blog 19 February 2009An excerpt from Johnstone's description of his character's demon, a passer:
When he cuts her open, just under the breast bone and down a few inches, there is a
plant, like peyote, under her skin. It is red inside, like a fig, sour and tangy like lime.
Krauss eats it out of her, and the effect is more potent than LSD or mescaline. Her skin
heals quickly. This is the first in a serious of similar discoveries.

Yes. That's what beauty reads like.

I'm GMing a game of Sorcerer...

It's set in Tangiers and Algeria, in late 1954. Algeria is still under French control, but there is a rebellious spirit building. In mere months, the first of the cafe bombings will occur. Tangiers is a different story - it's burgeoned into an experimental artist's haven. Drugs, sex, the avant-garde.

The characters are creators and seekers, ranging from artist dilettante to espionage agent to mad doctor.
Demons are the producers of mind-altering experiences.
Sorcerous rituals rely on creating, fabricating and investigation. Painting demonic visages with a "special paint" made from the hide of the Faiber Beast, which you acquired from a guy who knew a guy - that's how you contact a demon. That, and you get high.

In other words: Sanity death-spiral! Psychedelia! Mind-altering demons! Strange creatures! Cold war espionage! Avant-garde art! Lies!
In other words: A tribute to Hunter S. Thompson, William S. Burroughs, and Wikipedia's entry on Algiers.

    \n Yeah, so, Algiers and Tangiers, in 1954. Stalin is dead, the Algerian Revolution is about to begin, and Tangiers is still the international village known as Interzone. We are playing covert agents amidst the drug-addled, beatnik artist community.

    Daniel's character is the incompetent son from an Austrian dynasty of industrialists (ie arms dealers). His father has sent him to Tangiers to finish a deal. But he is too afraid to make contact. Instead, he falls in love with a prostitute, and writes poetry. To cover himself, he lies. He writes fake reports back about how well he is doing, and how many contacts he has made, but he has only made one, so far. Through a ritual where he eats paint and cockroachs (I think) he summons a demon to live inside of him. It causes him to secrete a hallucinogen in his sweat. Demons, you see, are also psychedelic drugs.

    His kicker: One of his father's agents shows up, in person, to see how well he has been doing.

    During the game, he finally manages to go and see the guy who he was supposed to go and see. This guy wants a shipment of guns to be smuggled into Algeria. We the players know who the guns are for. Daniel's character does not. He also meets a strange giant caterpillar who gets him to eat its children and gain strange powers, like the ability to pass through dirt as if it were not there. And visions. I don't have any notes and stuff for his character, and my memory is only so sharp, so. Not much to say here.

    \n My character is Christopher Krauss, son of German immigrants to America. While studying journalism at UC Berkley, he was recruited by American intelligence to infiltrate Communist organizations. Then he decided Socialism is better and became a double agent.

    English Name: Christopher Krauss; German Name: Christoph Heinrich Krauss; Literary Name: C.H. Krauss.
    Appearance: Late 30s, shaggy brown hair and beard. Krauss cultivates an air of stylish ambivalence and mysterious knowledge -- the kind of worldly mentor any young, aspiring writer or seeker of knowledge would find easy to hero-worship. A hip dude.

    Stats: Humanity 5, Stamina 2 (Graceful), Will 5 (Mental Training, Manipulator), Lore 3 (Avant-Garde), Cover 5 (Intelligence Agent), Price: People Are Resources (-1 to non-manipulative social interactions).

    After WWII, the spying business became intolerable. The Cold War made everything so very serious all of a sudden, and Krauss wanted a way out. Marxist economics, German occultism, and early psychedelic drug research* led him to a narrow yet ill-defined branch of medicine, and to track down its lone specialist, Dr. Amelia Tizara. And by "track down" I mean he summoned her from another reality by creating a trail of paperwork to justify her existence. She agreed to meet him at a medical conference in Paris, provided he made the arrangements. He agreed.

    * I think we also discussed Soviet psychic research manuals.

    Krauss met Amelia Tizara for the first time in the university's greenhouse. She was floating naked in the green murk of the hydroponic pools. Krauss brought clothes, and her entire history in paperwork that he had constructed in a mescaline-fueled haze. In return, she pulled him from the clutches of both American and Russian spymasters.

    Krauss resurfaced in '49 as a successful author. He had friends, contacts. He knew important people, like always. Important people in Algiers, for instance. Who could blame him for leaving McCarthy's America?

    Kicker: It is 1954. The American ambassador throws a party at the consulate in Algiers. Krauss has a few words with men from the intelligence services, who make overtures, but nothing serious. There is a German at this party, a Soviet agent. He approaches Krauss, gives him a task, makes it clear he works for the Russians again. They want to know things, French things. With no options but to spy again, Krauss goes back to the Americans. They accept his offer gladly, and he is once again a double agent.

    All his life, Krauss has aspired to Mussolini's description of Lenin: "an artist who has worked in men as others have worked in marble or metal" (Bullock 1993, 374). He preferred manipulating fictional people and merely socializing with the real ones, but the old life has come back for him. And no one is more pleased than Amelia Tizara...

    Dr. Amelia Tizara
    Demon Type: Passer
    General Appearance: Attractive and professional, late 20s or early 30s.
    Binding Strength: +5 Christopher Krauss
    Stats: Stamina 2, Will 5, Lore 4, Power 5.
    Desire: Mischief.
    Need: To eat soil and sleep in water. She needs some sunlight, too, so she can't be locked up in a box or anything. Krauss keeps her in a bathtub in his house in Algiers. He has soil shipped in.

    Abilities:
    Perception: she grows plants inside her body that, when consumed, grant visions of people she has touched and marked. And they get you high, too.

    Link: Amelia and Krauss know where each other is at all times. This might be true of others who consume the drugs her plants produce, though most likely it only works one-way with them -- she knows where they are, they know very little.

    Cover: Dr. Amelia Tizara is a specialist in the field of cognitive biology. She studies the physical psychology of altered states and anomalous experiences, concentrating on the effects of plant-based compounds on the human senses and nervous system.

    Confuse: pheromones produced by the plants that grow in her body may cause humans to become confused and susceptible to suggestion. These pheromones are present in those who consume the drugs her plants produce, who do not always realize this.

    \n Okay, so, during the game:

    Krauss' spymaster is an East German who shoots heroin in front of him whenever they meet. We played through the first contact scene. More on that later. This spymaster comes to see Krauss later on, at his home, which was an awesome scene.

    This guy comes in, shoots up at the table, and starts talking to me. I try to palm his drugs, but he rolls better and notices. Then he asks suddenly "Why is there a naked woman standing over there?" And Joe describes Amelia coming into the room, naked and still wet, to see what kind of mischief she can get up to.

    I deadpan him. "What are you talking about? There's nobody here but us." And I ignore Amelia. Daniel jumps in to play Amelia at this point, and talks to both of us. I continue to tell the spymaster (whose name I can't recall) that she is not there. Eventually he gets too creeped out and leaves us. Of course, the way we set up Humanity means this is exactly what you need to make a Humanity loss roll for, but I make it. So, I stay involved in the world, instead of being pushed farther into the psychedelic hallucinatory world of demons.

    I have some shenanigans with the American ambassador. I'm not exactly spying for him, but he's involved. He also wants to try some drugs, so I get him some hash, and spike it with Amelia's drugs. I do this partly because I want Amelia to concentrate on watching my back and so she stays out of my spying-on-the-French operations, because she's a petulant little bitch who likes to stir up shit.

    Unfortunately, she's a petulant little bitch who likes to stir up shit, so while the demon chauffeur of the ambassador's right-hand man tries to beat me up, she's fucked off trying to manipulate some French bureaucrat I was stupid enough to mention to her.

    When I go and try to spy on the French guys, the head dude goes to a brothel, where he likes to have sex on a table fully laid out for a banquet feast. I get into the brothel, bribe the madame, and try to spy. But he gets busy in the kitchen, and the secret peep-hole windows only look into the girls' rooms. So, instead of sneaking outside and peering up through the window, I have to creep into the upstairs room, tie some bedsheets together, and climb out the balcony and hang about five feet off the ground peering through the window. Because I'm psychedelic. Obviously.

    Anyway, after the French realize I'm onto them, the dude decides to skip to Tangiers and get his rocks off there. I decide the best plan is to open a brothel and lure him in. Amelia seems to like this idea, because it will give her control over a whole lot of people. I am usually exasperated with Amelia. She wants to jump back into stirring shit up full bore (she's been on the shelf for most of her 8 years, after all), and I want to creep back into the game slowly.

    I had plans to keep writing books, of course. But with all my time spent spying, I can't write them myself. So I figured I would just steal them from other authors in Interzone, spice them up a bit and call them my own. Krauss is rather a bastard, after all.

    I roleplayed Krauss very much like Johnny Depp portrays Hunter S. Thompson, only I pretend he's the main character of Naked Lunch instead of Fear and Loathing. I used a short pencil or a pen cap for a cigarette, and when I had a small plastic bowl for an ashtray, I managed to figure out how I could hold the cigarette and the ashtray in one hand and still be able to smoke it and tap the ash into the ashtray. All with one hand.

    We didn't end up playing long enough that our two characters met up, though. Krauss never got to Tangiers, and Daniel's character never got to Algiers.

    \n Right, so, lessons learned.

    Verisimilitude. This is when something that is not true or real feels like it is. It feels plausible, you buy into it. It is, oh lord, "realistic." Which really just means the storyteller has adequately justified it to you.

    So the problem we had in this game is that me and Daniel set up a game that was very heavy on espionage. Spying and spycraft and covert ops. Now, I'm not an obsessive expert, or anything, but I feel pretty comfortable in this genre. I've read fiction, non-fiction, memoirs, seen good spy movies, etc. On the other hand, Joe, our GM, does NOT know the spy genre.

    So what me and Daniel ended up doing is shooting down a lot of his stuff that we thought wasn't up to our standards. I might not have even noticed my own behaviour if Daniel wasn't doing it too. So when Joe brings something up, we both shoot it down, and tell him to do it again. Which is wrong, because this isn't really Joe's problem \u2013 it's my problem. If I'm the one who wants a certain level of verisimilitude in the game, then it's my responsibility to provide it.

    I actually talked about this once before, but with fake examples, and using a much smaller scale for contributions.

    In the actual game, we really did say "No, you're running this scene all wrong," and then go on to correct him, and do one scene again. In which case, why aren't I just running the scene myself? (and if you think it would be weird to GM a scene for your own player character, we played Archipelago II pretty much like that).

    So I came to the personal conclusion that it is your responsibility to contribute to somebody else's narration if you want them to reach the level of verisimilitude you need. If you have a lot of knowledge of a subject, then ask the other player what the important details of their narration are, and then fill in the details that make that possible for you to feel right with it. It's not cool to challenge other players to do better when they have different skills and expertise than you do, and you aren't helping them at all.

    As for this game of Sorcerer, I realized what we should have been playing out was the friendships, feuds, and druggery inside the artistic community. Joe could have run us an awesome game of psychedelic artistic passions, plagiarism, petty and not-so-petty rivalries, and hallucinogenic betrayals, and I could have just narrated in all the spy shit myself. But we ended up playing something else instead.

    So: Do you feel like the mechanics ofSorcererwound up being a help or hindrance to improvising this kind of fiction?
    \nI think that the mechanics of Sorcerer could have been a major help, had we harnessed them properly.

    The issue was that as a GM, this was my first "craft a situation and drop it in the hands of the players" kind of game.
    It was my first game with any sort of "GM prep", save a brief exploit running D&D.

    There was a steep learning curve associated with Sorcerer's actual mechanics, the GMing responsibilities that the game assumes, and the implications of the mechanics.
    I was so swamped by the first two, that I never really made it to the third.
    And it was the third that would have proven useful.

    The mechanics imply momentum, more than hard effect.
    Winning a social test means having social momentum, moreso than it means having elicited a particular response. "No stakes."
    This mirrors the batshit psychedelic genre pretty well: it's about not falling apart, rather than about achieving a particular goal.
    In other words, it's about having power, even when basic securities (life, mental well-being, etc) escape your grip.
    It's about going double or nothing, all the time.

    That's what a Sorcerer game can do, if system mastery is achieved, and setting mastery is achieved, and everyone is on the same page with the same view.
    Unfortunately, we never got there.
