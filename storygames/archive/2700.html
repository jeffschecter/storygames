\n Heya,

This is a post seeking interesting discussion on the process that I'm having some trouble expressing.

I read over on RPGnet a complaint about an RPG being not very innovative in mechanics, but neat in setting, whereas another person said it was innovative in mechanic but that the setting was dull. This got me thinking: Beyond the simple desire to create, design, and share.... Is the point of creating a new RPG about unique settings with mechanics to enhance, or about creating unique mechanics with a setting to enhance, or something in between where the two are embedded together? At what point does a game designer say, "I could do my idea in this existing RPG, but it would be better if I made my own"?

Did any of that make sense?

    \n The correct answer is, "When that designer wants to".

    Innovative in mechanics? Innovative in setting? Those are RPG consumer reasons for why they purchase/like the games they do. It's not an objective category for a designer to follow.

    They should design what they want to do, in the way they are doing it, for reasons only clear to themselves. Unless they like freelancing, in which case they should design what they are told to design.

    \n I'm not really interested in innovatve mechanics unless they are rules-light. It took me a while to chew through ORE and there are games (Tri-Stat, d20) where I've actually been forced to give up reading the rules for fear of boredom closing down the creative part of my brain. It's probably a consequence of getting older but I find I don't have time to read complex rules. I just can't be arsed. This is very different to my teens when I devoured MERP, laughed my way through RoleMaster, ran a few combats in Phoenix Command and made my own "gun game". An innovative mechanic - whoosh, it'd have to be easily explained in ....less than a minute to hold my attention. So, in essence I'm unlikely to like a game with a boring extensive original game mechanic that lacks consistency mainly because I won't be bothered to read it. A game would either have to have an established system (one I already have in my head) or something REALLY lightweight.

    Now, innovative settings are where it's at. I love them.

    \n Games just lend themselves to tinkering and inspire creativity. I believe it's a natural facet of the experience of "play."
    \n
    \nHas anyone ever played a RPG without house rules? I think the point where you have a system that you like, but it needs to many house rules, is a point where you might think about making your own game.
    \n
    \nRPGs are also all about setting, if you don't like a published setting but like the system, that is what designing your own campaign is all about.
    \n
    \nIt seems like the most ambitious players might do both and create an entirely new game.
    \n
    \nI am the kind of player that also doesn't get too hung up on rules. I play a lot of GURPS, heck I've even run a few sessions, and I can't say that I've read all the rules. But we do have some guys who know the rules pretty well so they rarely get in the way, and I've learned them over time by playing. In the meantime I just focus on the story and do what I think would come natural to my character.
    \n
    \nI am working on a design for Game Chef that still hasn't quite made it even to first proof prime-time, mainly because I want to make something that is really different. That's not easy to do, but I hope to corner it before the deadline.
