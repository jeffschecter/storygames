\n This post is rumination-heavy. If you feel your heart and stomach is big enough to take whining, read on. Otherwise, please do something healthy instead.

We're playing based on Next playtest text. Previously we had thirty-something 4E Essentials sessions (about 4 hours each). The DM is the same guy who is our host as well.

Essentials went mildly boring and mildly frustrating for me. The weightless, meaningless wanderings between encounters (read: fighty fights) were basically the most interesting things as these were the occassions where the DM could show us his lovingly created world. The encounters were the obligatory roadblocks enabling and empowering me to these further questions about the worlds mysteries. I almost never used an encounter for what my instincts told me to do: for generating interesting situation (failing to heal a fallen PC, fleeing the whole scene--in short, not doing the expected encounter behavior). It was slow as hell both in terms of character advancement and in discovering the DM's fantasies, but still something to latch onto. And occassionally there was some spark of creativity, often as part of the meaningless (in terms of the fight-scene series part of the game) wanderings and more rarely as a futile attempt to make moves in fights that made effin sense in the fiction. When I saw the lost focus in the Next playtest texts, I had some hopes about more room for these creative moments. Maybe the DM will have an easier time noticing these meaningful moments and responding to our cherished ideas.

We're now playing with the same DM & Tegel Manor Gamescience edition & D&D Next playtest. There is also a new player who is fairly new to the hobby as well but he seems engaged--unreasonably so. He is doing a lot like what I described above as the meaningless wandering between encounters. The catch is that in this game is not so futile at all. But he is doing this with such high hopes that it seems futile again. (E. g. here if we have some zero-level helpers, they can actually help digging the trap for the enemy. They are not _just_ one-hit meat shields.) In our former game encounters were designed by the DM only, and we the players only had moment-to-moment tactics so this is good news in terms of not being negated by the system when you start strategizing, thus saying things out of encounter.
But man, this person wants it big time. He and the GM play out (speak "in character") the hardest negotiations in history (like asking NPCs for further support after some of their friends have already died on our side... for free). I am not sure but it seems that he wants to get those helping hands sooo bad, he could spend the whole session negotiating with the same NPC about some unreasonably unfair request from our side. And that's totally understandable from a "play your characters as real people" standpoint, but holy hell, this is D&D. Real people stay in their homes and hope for the best. PCs are the ones who are not always successful at dying but try really really hard.
The other two players--including me--don't see the point in trying most of these things (buying armor is okay, it's a quick administering of your character sheet to increase survival chances, but begging for a free potion of healing seems like 0-10% chance of success and decreasing (furthermore failure means barely anything--hey, this is no Apocalypse World) so it's not okay: we want to do some adventuring instead. ("Adventuring" is very hard to grab, though: my best guess is that we have a taste for higher probabilities of the GM saying new things to respond to.) The possibility of some PC dying seems not so bad if on the other hand more stuff can leave the GMs mouth than the villagers saying "No deal" one more time.
Not to say that dying PCs are not an issue for me. The beloved Random Stupid Death of old-school is still evading me. I just cannot see any other point to it than standing as an evidence that each and every player decision with the goal of increasing the chances of survival is mistaken. But wait: RSD can be evaded by an infinite amount of player input about securing every detail of the plan and action of the PCs. Moving from inch-to-inch, check for traps, look for monsters, poke with the 10' pole, take one step... Like a mantra. Perfect procedure can make your characters tough as hell. They don't fight they way out, they pick fights. They don't pick anything randomly, they choose one of the researched routes. . Designing then reciting the perfect procedure is a total analytic left-brain, mostly routine puzzle-solving excercise I have done a lot in my RPG career (and in Diablo II) but I realized at a point that this is just not what humans are for. (I know more than one people who could write a program which plays D&D3.5 the same way as the former me.) Happily my perfect routine theory is just not true. Random Stupid Death is random. The DM yawns "here, see, these people ambush you..." and your character is dead. No matter how long you worked on your goggles of ambush detection if the DM says they don't work for some reason. So really, using fictional and mechanical positioning with the objective of saving your PCs is just futile. Why don't just go and see what happens--how the PCs die, mostly--without any puzzle solving, hoop jumping and mind reading the DM?
If I bring a lot of filled 1st level character sheets with me to supply the death machine (one character at a time) or I bring a whole warband at the same time and see how the battle of attrition goes, how can this work together with the more cautious approach of other players? Will my behaviour teach this other approach effectively or will they be frustrated about me "not taking the game seriously"?

I know that a lot of the story gamer people had much fun with Moldvay. Can reading (or playing, even) Moldvay D&D make me understand what is one satisfying way of playing under the D&D umbrella or do I seem completely misaligned for this?

Of course I want to make this game worthwile and saving my ass socially this way.

Please respond if you have something to say about any part of this.

    \n Medieval fantasy RPGs are in general about being epic. For any D&D edition you'll find this translated into a core book where I'd say 75% of the material is oriented to some form of combat. But then again, that's not enough to make an "epic" experience, and the rules tell little about it. That's something for the setting books and/or the GM to establish: a world and a situation where combat matters, and getting better at it is a big part for the story (otherwise there wouldn't be much of a need of leveling up rules)

    I'd say that if you're feeling encounters just as roadblocks, then it's your GM's fault. Besides of being roadblocks, these can be chances to prove your character (as our DM used them in last session) actual turning points of the story (which will change future events in the story) or part of optional sidequests taken to further improve the situation for the characters. For example, as a DM I tend to put the players in situations that may be dangerous, but may gain them powerful allies for a future definitive battle, or allow them access to a Mc Guffin that can be used in many ways to change the story.

    And hearing the players isn't such a bad idea: The most dramatic turn I've taken as a DM totally changed my last campaign and greatly improved the epicness. Players were in the middle of a dungeon facing all kinds of traps and encounters... and while watching a mural painting of the legend of an old villain NPC, the suddenly had an epiphany. They decided to start their own cult and make a stand against all the major kingdoms of the world instead of running around to find why they were being pursued by them. Things started to go macro as they gained more and more powerful followers and it all ended in a bigger showdown than I initially planned.

    But again, I'd say: try both. Check the book and ask everybody what's the fun about it, and try to play it like that. And talk to your GM and that guy, hopefully you'll both commit to what would be a totally different experience of what you're used to.

    \n Yeah, I make players describe what their characters do for every attack, even if they fail. I encourage them to fail in ways that are interesting for them, not "I swing; I miss like a fool." 4E has some odd effects (the cleric hits the orc so hard that his friends get more hit points?) that players have to struggle to explain. "As I pin the orc with my mace, I'm shouting a verse from the Book of Sacred Battles, and my friends may find strength in those words (take +1d4 temporary hit points -- if you're a believer)."

    I've played in Wednesday night "D&D Encounters" games at my FLGS where there was the barest of fictional positioning at all. Some characters were there. There was a vague reason we were all there together. We fought a thing. No one described a single attack or action other than to-hit and damage numbers. I had to switch gears mentally to find the fun.

    So, all my pointed (leading?) questions are about that thing that evil people like me call "creative agenda." It's a term of art (read: jargon) that basically describes the synergy that happens when everyone at the table cares about the same thing and pushes for it at the same time. If you don't have that synergy, you can get different people pushing in different directions at different times, resulting in a weird back and forth effect that doesn't build up speed in any particular direction. At worst, in those rare occasions when people push in different directions at the same time, it results in frustration and arguing over game priorities.

    So this CA thing is really just about talking it out and making sure you all want to play the same kind of game. Is anyone at the table really interested in pursuing the "rules mastery" aspect of D&D, which 4E is so good at? Certainly not you. I anyone at the table really interested in pursuing serious issues that make everyone else nod thoughtfully and say, "You know, I really had never thought of it that way" (or even, "wow, dude, that's really messed up!")? I don't get the impression that's a priority for you.

    More likely, you want the group to all revel in "being" adventurers in this particular world of the DM's creation, being surprised at the wondrous things the DM puts in front of you, basking in the possibility of a fantasy world remarkably different than your real world circumstances, and so on.

    CA isn't a personal agenda, exactly. It's a shared, group agenda, or it's nearly worthless. You already want a bunch of stuff and you're not getting it, or you would be writing a wholly different post. You want your fellow players and the DM to want the same things you want. If you all were on the same page, you'd be all pushing in the same direction and your games would be Awesome.

    1. Figure out what personally brings the fun for you. Get specific. List examples.

    2. Talk this over with the group. Maybe they have fun in a totally different way. If so, change how you have fun with this group or get a different group.

      \n 13th Age is an OGL (D20) game by the two developers of 3E and 4E. Chargen feels like a cross between the two editions, but it gives players more authority to make shit up about the game world (custom backgrounds, relationships to the movers&shakers of the setting, and One Unique Thing that sets the character apart from everyone else in the setting).

      The combat system is lean and fast--almost as quick as Basic D&D, but it takes a slight speed hit from the additional complexity that the class powers offer. It plays like a cross between 3E and 4E with a fair bit of OSR flavor. There's a clever Escalation Die mechanic to give the players a to-hit advantage as the rounds progress; this keeps combats from dragging on and on. There's a clever hit point recovery rule that encourages players to "play through" instead of running away when they're beat up; that avoids the "10 minute work day" problem in 3E. The class powers have a 4E feel to them but they're strongly connected to the fiction. No clerical "I hit you so hard that my friend gets a heal spell" weirdness.

      The rest of the game plays like 3E, except that you have backgrounds instead of skills. Backgrounds are generic "cover all" history phrases that function as a group of skills that make sense for that history. The ranger can have a "head of the Assassin's Guild in Tenrook" background, and that stands in for a variety of skills in different situations: sneaky stuff, political stuff, seduction, disguise, and so on.

      \n The ten-minute workday problem is that a wizard (or cleric, or whatever) memorizes a bunch of spells once a day. They go into a dungeon. In the first room or two, they encounter some monsters and other obstacles. They use up all of their spells, or most of their good ones. Now it's tactically foolish to continue on. They either retreat and come back the next day, hoping that the rooms they cleared aren't populated again, or they make camp in the room they cleared and rest a day to get more spells. Elapsed encounter time: ten minutes (more likely, an hour or two). It's not very heroic.

      13th Age handles recoveries differently. There are "daily" spells and powers, but "daily" is not defined in terms of the sun going down. It's defined as "four good combat encounters." So if you have completed three combat encounters, and you're running out of spells and powers, you need to press on and complete the next encounter to reset.

      Sure, you always have the option to back out and rest and recharge, but this is considered a retreat, and the party suffers a "story setback" (GM-defined).

      Additionally, a lot of "daily" spells have a chance to recharge after an encounter. It's usually something like "Recharge on 16+ (on d20)."


      By the way, "Vancian magic" really has two meanings. There's the "memorize, fire and forget" aspect of it, but there's also the "florid names and a million varieties of each spell" aspect of it, too. 13th Age embraces both, as options for wizard characters.
