\n OK, folks, in this thread there's some interest in doing a competition where, much like Game Chef, you write an RPG based on ingredients. Thing is, the ingredients would be a random title (pick from three), a random slogan (pick from three), and a random cover art image (pick from three)... and no two folks would have the same ingredients! Also, given the graphical nature of starting with a cover, the competition would give considerable weight to the overall presentation and utility of the book design, to whit:
* Evocation of title, cover, and slogan - up to 20 points
* Clarity of rules and procedures - up to 20 points
* Presentation and design utility - up to 20 points
* Playability; fun factor - up to 40 points

So you see that the competition is around 50% about how you make it pretty and how you make it playable without being trained or seeking advice on forums.

Other than those tweaks, it would be a typical RPG contest: don't make a monster that the judges won't finish reading, try to playtest, seek feedback on your blog or The Forge, have fun, be polite and be proud... and maybe have a product to take to market, when done!

This thread is simply to determine two things:
1) Tap-in if you'd participate.
2) Tap-in if you'd judge (judges may not participate).

I will watch the thread for tap-ins until April 17th, noon, EST (should be about 4 PM GMT). After that, I'll get your submissions to you (as whispers in this thread) as fast as I can, and you'll have ONE MONTH to design, test, and layout your game. May 18th, noon EST, is the cutoff to provide a link (in this thread) to your final submission.

I recommend using Google Docs to host a PDF, for ultimate control of presentation appearance across machines. Or host your own PDF on your own site. I don't think we'll be setting up the (cool) FTP form and location, like Graham did for the Little Game Chef (but a judge might offer to do so!).
----
TAP: I'll judge and I'll do the initial randomization of elements for those who tap-in.

Who's in?

    \n To reply to whispers and open questions:
    * Thanks for the Wikipedia link, but that almost seems as slow as my random search terms and/or coming up with a list of numbers at random and using them as a count of images hit at a public domain repository. I found another one (PicFindr) that lets me search on free to use, for commercial, with attribution (or not) and with rights to modify. So between a random search term from the random quotes (or Wikipedia) and two random numbers (1-12, for the site; 1-50 for the image in that site), I can fairly quickly get images for folks. Not a problem (though I'd still like to find a PD art site that does "random link").

    *!* Something to be aware of: just because something is public domain and may be used commercially does NOT mean any person in the photo signed a model release, nor does it mean every location or property in the photo is free of copyright. I won't vet images for this; but I will make sure at least one image is "totally" safe, legally, so someone can go fully commercial with it without risk (and those who just want to make free games or practice or build up a portfolio are free to use whatever). Obviously, some folks are going to be "luckier" than others; that's the nature of a randomizer-based setup.

    *?* I have a question for folks tapping in (don't much care for the opinions of non-participants, thanks): Would it be better or worse, for the random-factor of the contest, if I used the first significant term in the quote, for the random search term? Might work well--creating a tenuous link between art and quote--or it might spoil the emergent coordination of the otherwise-disparate elements. Just a simple poll--I don't guarantee that I'll go with the "majority." In fact, I'm already so opposed to the idea of such close coupling that I wish I hadn't asked. But if a SERIOUS majority wants it that way, I could be persuaded.

    \n Glad to see all the interest--but it means it could take a while to judge.

    Sounds like totally random is the favored (good!), so I won't use terms from the quotes to "seed" the random images from a public domain site.

    Next poll, then:
    Can the images be digitally manipulated?
    Pros - A subtle working-over of an image could make it a better cover--cropping or color balancing, in particular, can make an image "pop" (also keeping in mind most images are landscape while most books are portrait).
    Cons - A total Photoshop Filter orgasm could render an image unrecognizable, or put it into a whole different mood and feel. A good graphic artist could make nearly ANY image fit ANY mood or tone, thereby diminishing the difficulty to integrate all three random elements.
    Also - Given that one has three of each from which to choose, that's... uh... like 27(?)... potential combinations (damned math). Does one really need to, then, expand those options to near infinity, with digital manipulation and multiple layers of tweaks and so-forth?

    I suppose that I, as a judge, would treat that similarly to a weak application of an element--reduce the points for the "Evocation of title, cover, and slogan" category. I mean, is it any worse to make a passing nod to an element than it is to distort it out of all recognition, to better-jibe with your other elements?

    But I'm open to further debate or polling--we still have over a week to nail down the nuances of the rules (which I will update above as we establish them). In particular, what does the Boss Judge think? (I am but a script kitty and promotional hack.)

    \n Checking out the other thread, I am noticing some... well, drifting of the "rules", so that prompts another poll:

    By the rules, the last clause of the quote is the slogan, to which one can add some preamble like "An RPG about" or "An RPG for". However, many folks seem to be using any clauses--or several clauses, sometimes in multiple quotes on the cover--to make their slogans. Which method should I employ, to assign quotes:
    A) Only provide the terminal clause.
    Provide the whole quote, but insist that only one clause be used in the slogan (i.e. enforce with points reductions)?
    C) Provide the whole quote and let the designer use any or all of it on the cover, in any way they can imagine, including multiple slogans or quotes or blurbs?

    I, for one, prefer the spirit of the original rules: make that clause WORK, design monkey! But I want the participants to have fun and have some flexibility, so I could be persuaded to permit B. I am adverse to C, as it means some folks with long or "chunky" quotes get more fodder to really tart-up the cover, while folks with shorter or "thin" quotes are stuck making do.

    But I'll let the masses guide my thoughts on the matter. Thoughts?

    \n As far as I'm concerned, anonymity is a bane to doing a "full" competition. OK for a week-long, but not likely to stay anon over the MONTH we're allowing to design. Plus, folks really should call for reviews or playtests or design advice and anything else for which you'd solicit help at The Forge or your blog. This is a full design competition, right down to cover and page layout and readability--you will want to be 90% of the way to a ready-to-sell product, on this one. Or at least 50% (all done but the playtesting and some tweaking and page reflowing).

    So don't bother with anonymity. I am immune to favoritism. That said, you can certainly fake your name and do a new account at The Forge and stay "hidden" if you like (not much I can do about that).

      Random RPG Cover Competition

      \n

      Official Rules

      \n
        \n
      • All participants have received three elements of three types:\n
          \n
        • Three random Wikipedia article titles (excluding proper nouns and ridiculous Latin species names).
          • \n
        • Three random quotes
          • \n
        • Three random public domain images
          • \n
        • \n
      • Choose one element of each type, and use it as follows:\n
          \n
        • Use the chosen Wikipedia article title as your role playing game\u2019s title.
          • \n
        • Use one clause or phrase from the chose quote to construct a cover \u2018blurb\u201d or slogan or subtitle. You may adjust verb tense or add transitional phrases or even add a phrase similar to \u201cAn RPG about\u2026;\u201d but you may not use the whole quote, nor may you use a mere slice of it and surround it with your own embellishments.
          For example, \u201cThe human race has one really effective weapon, and that is laughter. \u2013Mark Twain\u201d is the quote you choose. You could a blurb, slogan, or subtitle like one of these without a points reduction:\n
            \n
          • \u201cRole-Playing Is Laughter\u201d
            • \n
          • \u201cAn RPG about the human race\u201d
            • \n
          • \u201c[Your Title]: One Really Effective Weapon\u201d
            • \n
          \n Any other use of the quote in the body of the game text is up to you, at your own creative risk.
          • \n
        • \n
      • Use the chosen image for your RPG\u2019s cover art. You may crop, scale, mask, frame, and color-adjust the image as needed, but any further manipulation of it will result in points reductions by the judges (ex: covering it with other artwork, breaking it into pieces, running image filters over it\u2014anything which utterly changes the original effect of some part or all of the artwork). You may, however, feel free to overlay the artwork with one or both of the other elements\u2014the idea is to be creative without utterly bypassing the constraints imposed by the chosen image. Also, keep in mind that most print-publication covers have a front and a back!
        • \n
      • Participants have until noon EST, June 19, 2009, to design, playtest, layout, and edit a complete publication, which will be judged by the Judging Panel (see below) according to these criteria:
        • \n
          \n
        • Effective and evocative use of title, cover, and quote segment - up to 20 points
          • \n
        • Clarity of goals for successful play, rules, and procedures - up to 20 points
          • \n
        • Presentation aesthetics (e.g. shelf appeal, professionalism) and layout utility (e.g. reference, teaching) - up to 20 points
          • \n
        • (Re)playability and basic fun factor - up to 40 points
          • \n
        \n
      • Discussions about this competition will be conducted on http://www.totrpg.com. If you are a judge, you must register with your real name visible; all other participants are free to self-identify or be anonymous (change your Account information). If you already had an account there from Little Game Chef, it was migrated to the new forums.
        • \n
      • Before the deadline above, participants will provide print-ready (or screen-ready, if that is the product) PDFs via Google Docs, their own web site, or some easily accessible online venue. No other file format will be judged.
        • \n
      \n

      Judge Roster

      \n
        \n
      • David Artman \u2013 Graphic artist, technical writer, 28-year player of RPGs, and designer of several Icehouse pyramid games and a handful of nearly complete RPGs (and hoping for inspiration to finish them!). Published philosopher and short fiction author.
        • \n
      • Andrew Cooper \u2013 Has been playing RPGs since 1989 and has dabbled in everything from Robotech and RuneQuest to Dogs in the Vineyard and Capes. Participated at The Forge for years and at Story-Games since its inception. Helped run Games on Demand at GenCon a few years and organized the events there one year, even though unable to attend at the last minute (thanks to Mike Holmes for bailing him out!).
        • \n
      • Steve Dempsey \u2013 Original inspiration for this competition at Story-Games.com, editor of Places to Go, People to Be, and founder of Stevecon, which promoted indie gaming in London. Now involved with Indie Press Revolution.
        • \n
      • Andrew Morris \u2013 Bio pending\u2026.
        • \n
      • Joe Murphy \u2013 Runner-up in Game Chef 2006. Contributor, tester, and editor for about a dozen published games. Active in the Irish and Scottish indie and design scenes. Has run two design competitions and has entered many of them.

      \n This is, admittedly, a bit of a bump... but I don't want to make more threads, as we've got our own sandbox at totrpg.com.

      The other Judges and I are wondering if some folks have abandoned the competition out of "bad" random results (in quotes because I don't think any are inherently bad... just some are trickier). If you're still working the comp, please go to totrpg.com, register, and just make a thread with, maybe, your initial ideas, random brainstorming, or spitballing. Ultimately, we want this comp to be far more cooperative and supportive than any kind of anonymous comp, so let it all hang out.

      Stuck for a mechanic? I bet folks can give you fifty, if you can describe the nature of play you want.
      Not sure how to use your art? We got some eagle eyes, when it comes to cropping or reinterpreting (and we're thinking of permitting some re-roll, with a MAJOR caveat).
      Can't figure out how a skink and geopolitics go together? What if it's a world of skinks, lizards, salamanders, and frog, all vying for dominance of the landmasses shrinking due to global warming while trying to get others to reduce their carbon emissions? (Pulled THAT one straight outta my ass!)

      We're here to help. The only thing we (or at least I, as a Judge) won't do is write your cover for you. That's kinda the point of the comp. but that doesn't mean other participants won't have an idea or two to offer!

      And I, for one, am readily available for general advice on tools, best practices for layout and fontography, and PDF production (some folks are going PDF-only, which opens a LOT of options for cool widgets actually IN the book!).

      Come on in, the water's fine!
      David
