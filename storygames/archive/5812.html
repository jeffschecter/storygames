    \nIt's already been done: the variant as taught to me worked something like this:

    Instead of Mafiosi/Werewolves, you have one or more Things.

    Each "Night" phase, the Thing(s) decide on one Scientist to convert. That player is secretly informed, and from now on plays as a Thing.

    During each "Day", the players collectively talk about and vote for who to "test".

    If you test an innocent scientist, nothing happens.

    If you test a Thing, they die and are removed from the game.

    Here's the tricky bit: you are guaranteed two tests each day, but also, if you test successfully, you get another test.

    \n I love this game too. I've been playing it with my family for a number of years now, and it's become something of a family tradition. My wife is terrifyingly good at this game, while my dad has this peculiar "werewolf face" that gives him away every time. It's kind of a mix of gleeful and guilty. Of course, being a chronic tinkerer, I've added a number of different characters to the game.

    The Hunter
    The packaged game has a hunter, but it's lame. We play that the hunter has three (or as many as there are werewolves in the game) "kills". The hunter gets a chance to wake up before the werewolves, and point at one person, killing them instantly. They get tapped, and sit out. The hunter is cool because it adds confusion to the game when the townsfolk don't know if a death is the result of a rogue hunter, or the werewolves. On the other hand, a canny hunter can clean up the town really fast.

    The Sorceress
    Every night, before the werewolves wake up, the Sorceress gets a chance to cast a spell. She points at two people (one of which can be herself). Those two people are tapped. They wake, acknowledge eachother, and go back to sleep. Until the sorceress next wakes, anything that would happen to one of the pair, happens to the other instead. So if the werewolves point at one of them, the moderator taps the other instead. If the townsfolk lynch one of them, the other one dies in their place. The sorceress is cool because, well used, the spells can get your enemies killed and save your friends or yourself. Of course, they frequently backfire and get the sorceress killed.

    The Necromancer
    Once per game, the necromancer gets to wake up, and point to a dead person. The dead person then points to a person who they think is a werewolf. (We play that dead people get to watch the game, but aren't allowed to comment on it - this can be almost as much fun as playing). The necromancer is cool because it gets dead people involved in the game some more, gives more information to the villagers, and is thematically awesome. It's a one-use role though, so it's not so popular.

    The Flautist
    This started as a joke, but it's actually a cool role. We play that the card of dead people isn't revealed until the end of the game, so the townsfolk never know if they've lynched a werewolf or not. The flautist, however, upon dying, utters a final plaintive piping on a flute. This doesn't save the Flautist's life, but it lets the townsfolk know they've lynched a villager. The flautist is cool because it adds some more information to the game, without complicating anything. You can add the flautist even when you've already got a lot of extra roles in the game.

    There should be more RPGs like this one.

    \n Simon, that's a whole thread itself, I think. It's a topic done before, but it might be worth revisiting. It certainly is fascinating.
    \n
    \nI really like the mechanics of these games... in theory. In practice many of them boil down to one skill - lie-detecting. And what's interesting is that, as a species, we're pretty well adapted to lying well. Oh, we're also pretty well adapted to discerning when somebody lies. But the end effect is that often you're just guessing.
    \n
    \nWhich isn't much of a game at all. That is, as a competitor, you need to have multiple opporitunities to show off whatever ability it is that the game highlights. If, in fact, most people are essentially guessing like 80% of the time, the randomness of those results skew any perception of actual ability on the other 20%. And you'll never know who is a better player than another.
    \n
    \nJust which is luckier.
    \n
    \nSo you might as well just flip a coin to see who wins.
    \n
    \nNow that's for the simplest versions. In some of the more complex versions there might be combinations of abilites that skew the odds one way or another, and determining when this is true is a test of people's logic abilities. Yes, I know that folks find this tedious, but for me it's the only way to salvage such a game for serious play.
    \n
    \nFor an icebreaker, the simple versions are fine, and play fast enough for it not to matter if it's a skill game or not. But for anything else, I strongly recommend using as complicated a rules-set as possible. What's interesting is that they're typically not all that much more difficult to understand how to play, as often the complexity simply comes about from the number of extra abilities in the game. Meaning that each player can easily learn their individual role.
    \n
    \nMaking it an "Easy to Learn, difficult to master" sort of game. Which I think is optimum. But which many people dislike.

    \n I'm talking about singly, Justin. Yes, I know that the original game has the scryer position, which is asymetrical with the rest of the positions. Even that bugs me a tiny bit, truth be told. But it's not too bad, because it has an elegance of it being a single exception. Basically the person who gets that position is the lottery winner, and we don't begrudge the lottery winner. From another POV, with that position singled out, the scryer becomes a target, and has more responsibility and pressure, so it could almost be seen as a negative in some ways.
    \n
    \nIf/when you kill that elegance by having many of the positions have powers, then the problem is that the folks who get to be plain-jane werewolves, or plain-jane villagers are going to feel unlucky. I would. Like, "Oh, there are lots of powers out there.. but I don't get one." Worse, there are positions that are just more likely to get you killed out of the game for no benefit to you at all. These folks are really unlucky.
    \n
    \nLike Comatose in your set. Lets see, I don't get to talk with the other players the first day, and then I'll automatically die at some point thereafter. All this does is limit the player's interaction with the game. Yes, I see how it makes for a more complex game overall, and that I applaud. But, while it makes the game better for other players, it makes it worse for the player who gets the position.
    \n
    \nThat's not to say that I'm against these sorts of game effects existing. That is, it is interesting to have a character who can't speak the first day in some ways. But I'd have that fault as part of a position that also gives some offsetting powerful advantage. Perhaps the comatose character, having been attacked by the werewoves, gets to know the identity of one werewolf when he comes out of the coma. Or something like that - that's just off the top of my head.
    \n
    \nThe game will contiune to be asymetrical (and asymetrical is a good thing), even if the powers are balanced, simply because the powers are different from each other. Personally I'd always be designing toward making each role give the player more interaction, not less.
    \n
    \n
    \nThere are some things that your rules doc doesn't seem to cover. Is there a more exensive one (this one is a summary, right)? For instance it's not clear if the players know all of the potential roles. Do they have a list of powers? Is this list one of potential powers, or of the powers that are definitely included in the game?
    \n
    \nI'm fascinated by the idea that you have players give out their roles at the beginning. I think that's a great idea, especially thematically (the idea of a village where nobody knows who the other villagers are, even putatively, is really weird). And you'd think that there would be a demand from the good side that this happen anyhow. But procedurally, how does this occur? What's up with the code? Why is that important? Why can't everybody just send the moderator their role claim, and then he reveals them all (I understand that making one player do it before another is unneccessarily complex and punitive). What am I missing there?
    \n
    \nAlso, while I'm at it, the rule that everything happens simultaneously at night is as broken as the original Magic:the Gathering timing rules. Sorry. That is, looking at your example, scrying a participant who is killed doesn't tell me anything. That is, it supposes that I can't scry a dead character? First, the rules don't indicate that specifically, though some of the language about players killed being out of play might read that way (note that in a lot of versions, being dead doesn't put you out of play at all). Second, even if that's the case, then why is the scry allowed in this case? In fact, the rule before it says "this may result in some powers negating others." You might just miss it - there need to be ways to verify that a player is not cheating in all cases possible. Leaving only the moderator to do detection (and his interest isn't as strong as that of an opponent). Game Theory dictates that, when feasible, you should eliminate the profitability of cheating. I won't damage the game to get these things, but I will go to some inconvenience to prevent cheating, yes.

      \n 50-100 roles will be... difficult. Each role which acts in secret must be awakened in the night and silently queried.       \n I AM, in fact, thinking of something more LARPy. This is Story Games, after all... I'm interested in the game less for the competition than to see what evolves from play. Hence one of my desires for complexity.
      \n
      \nYes, it will take longer to play. No, I don't imagine that you'd ever use all of the cards. Or very rarely.
      \n
      \nEver play Cosmic Encounters? It's a game that claims to have a ton of replayability based solely on the fact that you play up to six of dozens of roles each game. The combinations of positions that can actually occur in play is staggering.
      \n

They should merely alter the flow of play, and the strategies that are employed based on the discovery of which roles are in play.
