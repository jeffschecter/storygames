\n The school year has begun anew, which gives me a new opportunity to connect with my gaming friends. There is a certain campaign I've always wanted to run which is starting to border on white whale territory.

I want to run a game in the vein of Scott Pilgrim, Homestuck, and maybe a bit of Earthbound/Mother (which I have sadly never had the luck of playing - some day, though). A game of blinding 8-bit colors, improbable physics, superpowered badassery, and histrionic interpersonal drama. A game where it's not about saving the world - it's about every single fucking thing it's safety will cost you, and who you end up when the pixels have settled.

I'm willing to do some hack work, but I'd rather have something that works out of the box. I'd prefer something with a strong GM role. Thanks in advance for any suggestions!

    \n To specify more about Anima Pime (free with no art, twenty bucks for a printed version, I think less for a PDF), the games selling point from what I understand is that 90% of the time, you're freeform RPing, although there is a GM invoved. So when you're walking through town, solving puzzles in Insipisphere, or playing a concert, you just describe stuff and it happens. But when there's a SHOWDOWN it goes into a combat game with emphasis on fighting techniques and special moves and the outcome of the fight drives the story forward from that point. The structure reminds me a lot of Scott Pilgrim specifically, since most of the books are Scott going about his life doing things punctuated by EPIC BATTLES that determine the next chunk of his life.

    \n I'll second Wushu, its details = dice system will take as much color as you can throw at it. 'Combat' is either mooks/environment as a threat level (for example, survive the prom with your pride intact) while the nemesis battles are everything you would expect for a boss battle. Characters are described as a set of phrases with a rating that you have to roll equal to or under. For example, "I was born under the star of the Bass Guitar (5)" or "Ginger, soul optional (1)".

    Just to throw it out there, you could hack Lacuna and use it's heart rate mechanic. I actually ran a lone wolf game of it that had a strong Scott Pilgrim vibe and it was a hoot.

    \n I totally run Wushu in the same way Thark describes Anima Prime - Dice and game system are only ever involved in (important) conflicts. I think a lot of games are like that, you just throw away the task resolution. Being a fan of both, I think the biggest difference between them is ultimately that Wushu is color-mandatory, the game doesn't work at all if players aren't pilling on cool descriptions regurlarly, and the system does nothing but ensure that, while Anima Prime is color-optional. It encourages you to do awesome stuff, but doesn't require it, and has more board game elements and tactics that you can engage with instead of the fiction. I think both could work for your purposes, especially the improbable physics and superpowered badassery, but AP is best if you want a bit of crunch and has some video game RPG nostalgia inbuilt in it, while Wushu is better if you want your players to narrate awesome stuf and won't take no for an answer. You should read both, at any rate.

    Neither really intrisically support your more specific premise:
    "A game where it's not about saving the world - it's about every single fucking thing it's safety will cost you, and who you end up when the pixels have settled."
    I guess Bliss Stage does that? As well as Sorcerer and a handful of other more somber indie-games, I think. It could probably be hacked pretty easily into Wushu though, like maybe players can only roll traits rated at 5 by sacrificing their relationships or parts of their own personality or something.
