\n Many years ago, I purchased Character Law/Campaign Law. It was only after I gone home and read through it that I realized I needed other Rolemaster books. Undaunted, I used the combined talents of "not having all the information I needed", "misunderstanding 80% of the information I did have" and "using things for purposes for which they were not intended" I managed to put together a campaign based on Barry Hughart's "The Bridge of Birds". I had fun. My friends had fun.

Have you had any similar experiences of playing "wrong" but having fun nevertheless.

    \n When I was in second grade I was introduced to D&D; by a a by a group of friends who'd been playing long enough to make it to 3-5th level. I got to start as a 1st level M-U which means I got 1 magic missile and 2 hit points.
    \n
    \nThe DM had forgotten all of his stuff...including his dice. Alls we had were character sheets...so he invented an entire scenario on the fly involving an evil druid (yeah...we didn't know that was wrong) who was holed up in a giant fortress (yeah, that's kind of wierd too). The entire scenario was played purely free form with everything adjudicated by DM fiat. In the end the Druid was a bazillion levels and through us in the dungeon where my friends amused themselves by torturing my character to death for kicks. I was hooked.
    \n
    \nNext time we played I thumbed through the books and scribbled down everything I could from the rules...the To-hit tables and the monster stats for Orcs, Skeletons, White Dragons, and Green Dragons. After that I typed up the rules for my own D&D; game to play with other friends where we'd take turns drawing mazes, putting pictures of monsters in them and fighting the monster whenever we ran into the picture...of course all of the monsters were Orcs, Skeletons, White Dragons, and Green Dragons.
    \n
    \nThat was the right way to play D&D;, right?

    \n It was 1979. I was like 10 years old. I had gone to some summer program where my friends got to play in some short D&D game. I heard about it second-hand and it sounded really, really cool. That was the first I ever heard of D&D or role-playing.

    I mentioned this game to a friend, and he said he had the books or something. It turned out that he had B1: In Search of the Unknown, which is a very odd module. He didn't have the rules though. He'd call me on the phone and we'd "play" through that module. Mostly stuff like: "There's a passageway here. Right or left? Right? That takes you to a room full of monsters!" No fighting or anything. It was still kinda fun.

    One of the summer program counselors was a good friend of the family. She bought me the D&D Basic Set for my birthday. We opened it and tried to play it, right then and there, no prep. I think we expected it to be playable with the ease of a board game and in as much time. We managed to create some characters and then we called it quits. My dad somehow had been selected as the DM, but he had no interest in the game after that initial session. I read the rules and tried to understand them, and eventually that counselor, her fiance, my dad, my brother, and I tried to play again (or for the first time). I was the DM. It was a disaster, but I was intrigued.

    Eventually, my brother and I and that B1 friend figured out the rules well enough to get by. We played D&D for many years.
