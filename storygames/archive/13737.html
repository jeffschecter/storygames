\n So, Deliverator Matt has been running an AW for me and 3 other friends. He's itching to play a character, and mine is nicely poised to retire to safety or die trying, so we've decided that I'll MC next.

Matt and I have been having chats for a few years about the character-POV style of immersion that characterizes some of my favorite games, and Matt has argued to me that AW is well designed to support that. So, I'm going to give it my best shot, but some advice would be handy! (Even if the advice is: "Don't try it.")

As a player, of all the times I've ever tried to do a Move, here's my best guess at the breakdown of Matt's MC responses:
1) 95% -- Okay, roll!
2) 4% -- I don't think that would work here, but feel free to try something else.
3) 1% -- I don't think that would work, you're taking forever, the situation escalates, now what do you do?
4) 0% -- Okay, you try it- Stop! Don't roll! It doesn't work.*
5) 0% -- Okay, roll! You got a 10? Well, for some reason, it doesn't work!

There are very good reasons for these percentages. However, the end result is that, as a player, I know I have the ability to rule certain possibilities (and even probabilities) into and out of the fiction. This creates an information mismatch between me and my character.

My character is talking to an NPC. The MC roleplays their disposition as distrustful and taciturn. And yet, I know that all I need to do is announce an attempt to read them, and I have a high chance of finding out what they want. But my character doesn't know this! If I'm trying to imagine the fictional situation from my character's POV, I'm going, "Crap, they're not gonna talk." But as a player, I know they probably are, because there's a rule that says so and all the other players keep using it.

So, back to the 5 responses above. Without other options on the table, #2 and #3 wind up as preludes to #1, which can't help with the player/character information mismatch. #5 seems like a dick move that would break resolution. So, what do you think about #4?

Allowing "you don't know what's possible/probable until you try" into the range of interactions between character and world would better synchronize player and character points of view. But it might also undermine the way Moves are currently guaranteed to push the narrative forward.

All this is just a first guess. "Try adding uncertainty between initiation and execution" is all I've come up with so far. Maybe other options would be better for character POV and/or have less downside. Any advice would be much appreciated!

Ps,
-David

*or, "It works easily."

    \n Hi David,

    I've never had the issue you describe. I'm not saying it doesn't exist, just that it's never happened to me.

    Here's an example:

    My operator, Lafferty, is talking to some scrawny cockroach named Rolfball. Lafferty wants to know where a missing member of his crew is and Rolfball doesn't want to tell him. I look down at Lafferty's sheet and (just making this up on the fly here) he's got Hard+2, Hot+1, Sharp+1, Cool+1, and Weird-1. So Lafferty's a tough-ass dude who's also fairly sexy, perceptive, and slick, but he doesn't traffic with that psychic maelstrom bullshit. If I want Rolfball to spill the beans, there are a bunch of different things that I could try. I could just offer him some jingle (Barter) to bribe him for the info. I could threaten him with a beat-down (Go Aggro). I could offer him sex or try to convince him that he should just tell me (Seduce or Manipulate). I could try to read his body language to see what he's hiding (Read a Person). Normally, I could ask the psychic maelstrom where my crew member is, but Lafferty hates doing that (partially because he's bad at it). Or if I've got other crazy moves, I could use those. Or, perhaps best of all, I can just start doing things fictionally, based on what I think Lafferty would do, until it's clear to either me or the GM that I'm making a move. If you really want to immerse in AW, honestly, I'd suggest the latter approach, letting the moves emerge naturally from the fiction rather than picking the move you want to do and then having the GM ask you: "Okay, how do you do that?"

    So, let's say things work like this:

    JWALT: "Rolfball, if you have any idea where Clarion is, you best tell me quick." I glare at him.
    MC: Rolfball hesitates and looks over one shoulder, as if checking to see if he's been watched. "Told you, don't know nuffin 'bout nuffin."

    At this point, I don't need to Read A Person, because I already know 1) he's lying, and 2) he's worried about the consequences of squealing, probably because someone else might come kill him. That's just from the fiction.

    JWALT: "What are you scared of, Rolfball? Who's got you all spooked."
    MC: "I don't know what you're jabbin' about. Nobody messes with Rolfball." He scampers a few feet away and starts going through the drawers of his cabinet.
    JWALT: I walk over and put a hand on his shoulder. "You don't got to worry about anyone hurting you, Rolfball. I just want to find Clarion. You can come with me if you like and I'll make sure nobody does you no harm for a good deed."
    MC: So... you're manipulating him, by offering him protection?
    JAYWALT: ....Uh, yeah, I guess I am. So I roll+Hot?
    MC: Yup.

    Does that help? Do you see what I'm saying? Am I not understanding your question right?

    \n Thanks, guys. I appreciate your thoughts on this topic. Unfortunately, I've tried most of these already.

    On declaring moves:

    Early in this game, I used to just roleplay the conversation with the NPC, and eventually the MC would say "are you trying to Read a Person?" Well, yeah, I guess I am. Now I know what to roll (+Sharp), but I also know that I can learn how to get what I want from him, even though the fiction hasn't offered that as a possibility.

    Since it didn't make any difference, I eventually adopted the shorthand the other players were all using, and said, "I try to Read him."

    One could just blame my play group for thinking mechanics-first instead of fiction-first, but I don't think that's fair. Everyone looks at their sheet and uses the move they have a +3 in and which lists stuff they want among its success outcomes. Why? Because they want to know their options, and what's the best way to get what they want -- same as in any other game. Not doing this takes a certain level of discipline and clarity of priorities that I think is a tall order.

    On move results in the fiction:

    Learning how to get what you want from someone in the real world who is specifically trying to hide that involves a long, multi-step process, possibly months of building trust, demonstrating value, learning weaknesses, earning favors, gaining leverage, etc. It can be done! And maybe it can be done quicker by a badass! But in one quick conversation? I'd have to see it to believe it. Which I'd love to try! But we haven't been roleplaying out situations where the rules tell the player "Ask the MC this question, which they must answer honestly."

    I guess I could ask Matt to roleplay out his MC answers rather than answering directly, but that seems completely socially unfeasible. I mean, that is asking a lot out of him (to invent and portray the NPC's process of coming around) and the other players (to stay enthused through a long one-on-one, when a single sentence would have gotten the fiction to the same point).

    On character badassery:

    1) Having decent odds to do something nearly-impossible, but also decent odds to fail, doesn't really say "badass" to me. "Living an adventurous life of extremes," perhaps. I still don't know why people should tell me stuff they'd never tell anyone else under similar circumstances.

    2) I don't want to be such a badass that the apocalypse becomes a light-hearted romp. So yeah, I could answer any cognitive dissonance with, "Well, cuz I'm special, mufuckaz!" but I don't want to.

    Before I get a billion responses about playing wrong, let me reiterate that these are from the perspective of someone trying to immerse in character POV. I am also happy to play AW in other ways; I'm just exploring this particular one right now.

    \n John,

    I'll be curious to hear Dave's answer, but I suspect that the answer may be,"Yes."

    I've always liked Vincent's phrase, "made-up journalism". That's an appealing style of gameplay to many.

    So, this whole issue of the AW move:

    In my mind, this is one aspect of task/conflict resolution and clear/reliable rules and currencies and unclear/unreliable rules and currencies that's either obvious or impossible to understand.

    When you're engaging a conflict resolution system (even one which provides lots of surprises), you typically know that you have a certain chance of getting what you want, and (depending on choice of system) you might know the exact odds, too. In the case of Read a Person, you know what your list of questions is, and due to the nature of dice rolls in AW, you know your odds of being able to ask one question. In the "real world", you have no way of knowing that you'll ever be able to find out what a certain person wants. In AW, you know your odds for doing that, exactly.

    In a trad GM fiat/Rule Zero/task resolution game, there is a much stronger element of mystery. Each time you go to roll the dice, or each time you try to do anything in the fiction, you literally have no idea whatsoever how it will end. Maybe you will roll the dice and something terrible will happen to your character. Maybe you won't roll the dice, and instead you'll just get what you want. Maybe you'll really well and still not get what you want (in which case you know you're facing some unusual opponent or some unusual situation).

    The process of playing the game is a mysterious black box, not unlike real life (and especially mysterious, weird, unusual situations in real life).

    Of course, this breaks down for more experienced/less gullible/older gamers when you start to see the social patterns underlying it all. It's not that hard to go from "mysterious fiction" to "OK, I know Dave, and when he arches his eyebrow it means that he knows he should tell me what the NPC's thinking, but he doesn't want to because he thinks it would make the game 'too easy' for me."

    I'm not convinced there's a solution to this whole conundrum.

    My solution at this point is to realize that, yeah, we're playing a game, and suspend my sense of disbelief. If I can imagine that Dave's voice is the husky tone of a femme fatale, I can easily imagine that this die roll here is somehow reflected in the fiction. (An obvious one being that, yeah, obviously my character is so slick that he can just read the crap out of people.)

    I also find, perhaps ironically, that stakes-setting games allow me to do this better, in a way. In AW, you effectively always know what the outcome *can* be. If you're setting stakes, though, you might have dramatically different results from the same "move", coloured in different ways, depending on the situation.

    New idea. Represent degrees of certainty via outcome bell curves via # of dice.

    Let's say there are 3 degress of uncertainty: very, fairly, barely.
    For Very Uncertain tasks, roll 1d6: 1=awful, 2=bad, 3=okay, 4=good, 5=great, 6=amazing
    For Fairly Uncertain tasks, roll 2d6: 2=awful, 4=bad, 6=okay, 8=good, 10=great, 12=amazing
    For Barely Uncertain tasks, roll 3d6: 3=awful, 6=bad, 9=okay, 12=good, 15=great, 18=amazing

    Skew toward expected outcomes with bonus/penalty to roll, multiplying # of steps (from okay-good) by # of dice. So, expected good-great on 1d6 = +1, and expected awful-bad on 3d6 = -6.
