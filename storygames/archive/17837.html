    \n I've had great success with Fiasco at conventions, so I think if you just go in and facilitate the game you should be fine.

    I try to make my character push a plan strongly, and let them fail with as much style as I can. Essentially, trying to lead by example.

    The Fiasco Companion has some advice on convention games, and I have found it to be very good advice. The single most important idea I have taken from there is to take the first turn yourself (again, this lets you lead off the game by example - set your character a scene in which they try to do something crazy and let the table decide if you succeed or fail).

    \n I've had some successes with Fiasco at conventions too.

    I keep it to 4 players with a view to running around 3 hours of game.

    I do a quick talk through of the playsets I have with me and try and whittle them down fast rather than having players obsess over them. I tend to describe them by genre and play style as a way for the other players to talk about the game they want to play while we choose. Naturally I don't bring any playsets I wouldn't be happy to play :-)

    I have the playsets created as double sided laminated print outs and have a second copy of the relationships options as it's the one everyone is going to have to choose from. That way people can be looking over options while others are picking which speeds up the setup phase a bit.

    Having the 'playmat' out with the process of play is valuable, I make sure to take some breaks but talk about the game a bit during them too.

    Don't know if any of that will help.

    \n More than other games, I think Fiasco requires a group of players who are on board with each other. They needn't be experienced - in fact, a bunch of new people works beautifully - but they must be willing to play off each other. When I've had a below-average Fiasco experience, it's because one player has been doing funny things (either throwing stupid stuff in or playing defensively) and everyone else needed to control him.

    Pick an easy playset. The first time I tried to facilitate Fiasco, I let the players pick the playset. They went for the Cthulhu one, which was really hard, and the game didn't really work. (Never trust players, is what I'm saying.)

    \n Hey Lisa, instead of contributing one die and making a statement about your character's future in sequence as per the rules, just have a group discussion where you lay out the days, months and years ahead.

    I also agree with Graham - when you facilitate, strongly model the behavior you want to see in others. Make sure you are clear about the game's play style and your expectations. It absolutely can have all the fun sucked out of it by players who are defensive and want to win, or players who view the lack of a GM as an excuse to try to break it.

    I always choose three playsets and let them pick from among those options, which saves you from analysis paralysis and handily omits more complicated or difficult playsets. Pick ones you like that are easy to relate to and straightforward, not the ones that are set in obscure times and places or have special rules. The Ice is a good choice because it is neutral ground, imaginatively. Any of the more mundane ones (Flyover, Main Street) are good,at least for an American table.

    \n The good playsets are the simple petty crime ones. Try letting them choose from from Main Street (American small town), Gangster London (London gangsters) and Boomtown (Western). All the ones in the main book are good.

    But don't give them Alpha Complex and Unaussprechlichen Klutzen. Players might latch on to them (Paranoia! Cthulhu!), but they're not good for a first outing.

    Here is a thing! In the first round, I suggest that people should simply focus on playing the relationship between them and another character.

    So, if our characters are Alfie (my character), Bob, Charlie and Dan, I begin by playing the relationship with Alfie and Bob. They're father and son, so we play a scene at home. Next comes a scene about Bob and Charlie. They're former convicts, so let's see Bob meeting Charlie when he's released from prison. And so on. There's no pressure to do anything other than play the relationship.*

    This does a few things. Firstly, it gives players who like traditional games some structure. Secondly, it defuses story gamers who want to INTRODUCE BURNING PLOT into the first scene: "Let's go and find your long-lost brother!". Thirdly, it protects against solo scenes, which require a lot of inventiveness and can fizzle easily in the first round.

    The problem with Fiasco is that it's wide open. You can do anything in a scene and that can be paralysing. So it's okay to explicitly explain expectations, whether that's to D&D Players, indie gamers or completely new players.

    For example! In one game, we had a guy who had never played a roleplaying game before. His character was a cat burglar, whom he narrated having Spiderman-style sucker pads and a jetpack. We didn't want to say no. So he became a sort of crazy inventor figure, who genuinely had a jetpack in his van: we were never sure whether he was brilliant or a fantasist. And we explicitly explained what we were doing there: "How about if we make you a sort of crazy inventor and we're not sure whether the jetpack works or not?". He loved that game.

    \n I have lead convention games of Fiasco three times, and all three game went great. But I also saw another game (one I was almost part of) go not-so-well because the players didn't quite have a sense of what they were in for; one of them remained disengaged through Act 1 and then left during the break. So, it's in your own best interest to make sure people know a little bit about what they are in for so that they can determine for themselves if the game is right for them.

    I always work with the assumption (perhaps incorrect!) that potential new players know nothing about story games and it's my job to give them a quick briefing before they decide if they want to sit down and play the game. I might say something like this: "Fiasco is one of my favorite games, and it's something that you'd have no problem convincing me to play virtually any day of the week. But it's very different than a lot of other games, and I realize it may not be everyone's cup of tea. It's much less structured than other RPGs, and there really isn't a "win" condition like you might have in a board or card game. Have you ever seen a comedy improv show? Think of it like that, only more violent and a few dice thrown in. The game lasts between two and three hours with a short break about halfway through. It really works best if everyone participates and takes an active role in the fun. If you don't like talking, it's probably not going to be a game you'll like much. But if you are willing to commit to your character and you enjoy being entertained, particularly by dark humor, I think you will really like this. So, if it's not your thing, no hard feelings -- there's a D&D table right over there, and they'd love to have you."

    If it sounds like that is designed to politely scare people off... well, in a way it is Study their body language and facial expressions to see if they are into the idea or not. If they seem like people who would really enjoy this, for God's sake, don't scare them off! But if they are just joining your table because they happened to stumble upon you and don't seem willing to be active participants, then they (and you) will probably have a more enjoyable con experience at another table.

    And once you've found your right mix of players, I agree with everything said by everyone else, particularly about sticking with a simpler playset.

    Have fun, and please post Actual Play reports!

    * "One and done" (does not require additional sessions to complete your story)
    * Character creation is quick and fun
    * Very newbie-friendly

    Don't let players establish Relationships that don't involve their own PCs\u2014It's an advanced skill to "force characterization" on another without deprotagonizing; and it's an advanced skill to accept such force.

    And I'm surprised no one's said "make an INSTA-Setup that SINGS". Or use the one that comes with 80% of all playsets (and 100% of BPG PotMs), as the author(s) thought it would SING.

    You might find it's a lot easier to slap down an INSTA Setup's index cards and say "pick a seat".
