\n an IO9 article on how to approach writing/updating pulp-Sci-Fi. The issue it wrestles with greatly is how the classic pulp stories are driven predominantly by Imperialism. Whether or not you're a fan of Sci-Fi, I see a lot of issues about culture imperialism here with regard to game writing and perhaps this will give you some different context...

IO9 article on Sci-Fi

"And that's probably the bottom line \u2014 respect for the source material and awareness of its flaws aren't contradictory values. They go hand in hand. What is most cool and distinctive about these classics is also likely to be what's most universal and magical. And what's most weird and upsetting about them is likely to be a kind of tumor on the narrative. In any case, a kind of awareness \u2014 and maybe, like Broadmore says, a willingness to satirize where appropriate \u2014 are indicated."

    \n [D&D with Porn Stars: 3]

    That middle section in episode 7 made me very angry and sad. Right in the middle, Sasha Grey says:

    "That could've worked, um, if you hadn't had said, [sarcastic look] 'Oh we have to roll on how large a blanket it is.' You could've just had a blanket, we would've succeeded. You don't roll the dice to make a decision and then [disbelieving chuckle] roll the dice again to decide how that decision's gonna... [sputters]... I don't even understand what I'm saying!"

    And that is exactly to the point of what's wrong with Zak's handling of the game. And Zak completely misses the point, as evidenced by his lame comment on the blog, "It wasn't the worst plan, but, seriously, no matter what Sasha says, you roll a one and that's that." It never should have got to the point of someone rolling! Such a stupid way to handle the situation.

    John says above, "I'm glad they're having fun anyway." I'm sure Sasha did have fun hanging out with her friends and engaging with them, but did she actually have fun playing the game? I have no idea, but I suspect not much.

    \n On Easter, I had about a dozen friends over to watch the John Woo historical epic "Red Cliff", about the historical battle of Chi Bi which blends historical data recorded from the war, reenactments of parts from the Romance of the Three Kingdoms tale (a fiction crafted around the war, extremely interesting but also unmistakably written by the descendants of the Shu clan/Liu Bei), and all around Hong Kong cinema awesome (I was hoping for a scene where Zhou Yu would leap sideways in slow motion, firing two bows at once, but alas... There were doves, though!).

    To prepare, before I even watched the movie (but pulling on my knowledge of the era, having read quite a bit about the history of that time, plus playing all the epic video games surrounding the Three Kingdoms era), I put together a summary sheet with faces, roles, and summaries of all the major players in the battle (that had relevance to the movie), as well as a relationship map. It was useful as a crash course to follow along for folks unfamiliar with that period, who still wanted a little bit deeper knowledge of who was who going in.

    The PDF guide I made can be downloaded here: http://kitkowski.com/?p=111

    A side note: I'd say this movie is a Must Watch for RPG geeks. You have historical characters "epic'd up" on all levels, a backdrop of a huge war that zooms in from individual sword-and-spear battles to the movers and shakers laying out strategies. It has cinematic kung fu, cinematic strategy, cinematic persuasion/oration/charm events; it's basically got it all. I simply cannot believe it flew so far under the radar for the US release, both in limited theaters and on its DVD/Blu-Ray release. The action scenes with Guan Yu and Zhau Yun, the "Turtle Strategy" scenes were worth the price of NY Theater-price Admission alone.

    Note, we watched the Two-Part original/"International" release on Blu Ray, taking an hour intermission between movies. It was not the American release that had something like 2 hours cut. Although the American "cut" version got high marks across the board, and the Asian/International version probably has a little too much "hunting a tiger they obviously don't have the budget to show" scenes, I'd of course steer viewers to the 2-part original "International" version. It's something like $30 at Amazon Here's the link to the Blu Ray; DVD is 5 bucks less. Totally worth a buy, even if you watch it once or twice and resell it.

    You can see the trailer at the English site: Red Cliff Trailer and Official Site

      \n [3 Red Cliff]

      Hans, there is no sequel. Just two parts of the same movie and Andy's seen both parts.

      Andy, the reason that Red Cliff got such a soft release here is because it came out in China in Summer 2008 and lost its bid for an Oscar nomination two years ago. That kinda killed the momentum and gave the American distributor ammo to tell them to revise it because it "obviously didn't work for American audiences" since the Oscar folks didn't love it. That's also what happened to Hero, notably (most Americans have still never seen the 3+ hour original cut), and is increasingly the trend for big budget period blockbusters from China and Hong Kong. Who heard about The Banquet (aka Black Scorpion whatever) or Curse of the Golden Flower or Seven Swords (well, even Rich and I thought Seven Swords was pretty awful) or The Promise? Honestly, Crouching Tiger, Hero, and House of Flying Daggers are the only ones of these to really get a wide release in the US.

      Heck, Zhang Yimou's remake of Blood Simple was released in December, but we probably won't see it until next year at least.

      \n Joshua A.C. Newman and I need pictures of you as a teenager \u2014 or looking like one \u2014 for Misspent Youth.

      As he began to fill out the graphic design elements, Joshua found we had plenty of punky imagery, and plenty of scenery, but not enough people and science fiction.

      So I'm putting out a request to email Joshua (and CC me) with pictures of you as a teenager, or at least passing for one. These would be used in the art, and likely highly-manipulated to ape the punk rock 'zine style of Maximum Rocknroll.

      In particular, these are the kinds of pictures we're looking for:

      * Subject is you (so you can give permission)
      * You are or look like a teenager
      * In the picture, you are:
      ** Dressed punkily
      ** Dressed weirdly or gothy or anything non-standard
      ** Looking surly, joyous, pissed-off, weird, goofy, or silly
      ** Engaged in physical activity: running, jumping, fighting, at a protest, etc.

      Note that not all of these need to be met to send a picture in. In fact, I'll take something as long as the first two are met. The hope I have is to use all these pictures either in the book, or in the secondary material. I need to do some kind of semi-formal model release but I'm sure it's pretty easy to do, even online.

      You can send your pictures to rob.bohl@gmail.com and joshua@glyphpress.com.

      \n [trick.ly 2]

      It's just an alternate (shortened) link to a URL. If someone has the full URL, they can get to it without having to solve the clue.

      So, you're not keeping something private with trick.ly. You're making it incrementally public.

      The folks who follow you most closely guess the clue first, and start talking about what you've shared. There's some resulting buzz, and your close followers have minor, nice status for a short while. But the answer to the question gets shared around, and probably the full URL as well, and probably you get search indexed, and so ultimately what you've shared is public, but with some extant fan enthusiasm that helps more distant newcomers know what to make of it.
