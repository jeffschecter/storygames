\n Here's a couple of half-baked ideas for running Planescape games.

* Individual sessions could be structured like Dogs in the Vineyard sessions. The PCs find people in conflict, but the root of the conflict is a clash of philosophies rather than an excess of pride and sin. (Although there can be plenty of that, too!) The DitV toolbox can be used here: Reveal the burg through play. Don't pre-judge the ending. Give the NPCs motivations that anyone could understand. Give the PCs the power to decide the conflict authoritatively.

* Write up a big list of philosophies and beliefs drawn from each of the Planes. (And perhaps from specific Layers or Realms). For example, the belief inspired by Mount Celestia could be, "Justice tempered with mercy is the surest path to enlightenment." Pandemonium's philosophy could be, "Sanity is a polite fiction that hides the true self." (The Prime's philosophy: "Kill the monsters and take their stuff!") Have players choose a philosophy for their character from the list. Now the player has some depth to her character beyond faction affiliation, and the DM can spotlight that player's belief when the party is visiting that specific Plane. (Or Layer, or Realm.) Beliefs could give some mechanical benefit, as well; for example, +2 to a die roll once per session when the PC is acting in accordance with their belief.

Anyone else have any half-baked little ideas for running Planescape games?

    \nIt's the same problem you get with any setting that has a lot of background: Forgotten Realms, Warhammer Fantasy, T\xe9kumel, etc. (Not to mention settings based on books, TV shows, and movies.) In my experience, there's usually an enthusiastic GM who knows the material backwards and forwards, and players who are either passingly familiar with the setting or know nothing about it. It's really challenging for a GM to communicate that enthusiasm without burying the players in useless details, but I've seen it done well.

    I really like the Planescape planes. Admittedly, some of that is the satisfaction of "setting mastery". Beyond that, though, I like the creative constraint of having a fixed number of major planes. If there's a community in, say, Arcadia, what's it like? How do they interact with the natives? How do they interact with (or mitigate) the hazards of the plane? How do they manifest, or contrast with, the overall philosophy of the plane? If I want a place that just doesn't fit on any known plane, I have plenty of options as to where I can put it (demiplanes, etc).

    \nPlanescape is the perfect game for DMs who never actually run any games, because it's so much fun to think about.

    For me, the weak spot is in the published adventures. I've been reading through some of the older adventures (especially "Modron March" and "Dead Gods"), and while the overall plots are solid, there's just no motivation for the PCs. Just the standard "I want to hire you to do this thing", and sometimes, not even that. There's some really obvious and terrible railroading, which I guess was par for the course for D&D adventures back in the day. If I ever do get around to running Planescape again, I'll want to use those adventures as inspiration and general framework, but I'll have to completely rethink how the PCs get involved. (Including giving them the option to walk away and do something else, if they're so inclined.)

    \n Well, part of the thing is that you don't need to read every setting supplement that's ever been put out. You could just read, say, the stuff on Gehenna in the Planes of Conflict boxed set, and you're loaded for adventures.The tear-drop palace of the god of thieves, who occasionally manifests as a giant jellyfish! The abomination's lair! The tower of the arcanoloths, and their basement library of 'contracts' carved into the skin of caged petitioners! Nimicri, the moon that's secretly a giant mimic! And that's just one plane.

    And the monstrous compendiums are, of course, full of delicious and juicy plot hooks that D&D does best: the weird-ass monsters you're fighting, their weird-ass abilities, and the occasional gems of the ecology and habitat/society sections. Bet you could get pretty decent mileage out of that alone.
