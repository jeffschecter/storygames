\n A couple of my friends tend to gm in a way that, despite the title of the thread, seems to work for a certain group of players. I certainly hate it, it made me leave their games. But then again, I have to admit it's my opinion, other players and GMs may have their own, and since there are people still playing with them I'm trying to figure what it is that they are doing right. If I get to pinpoint it, it will definitely go to the Techniques thread, but I need to extirpate anything that can turn the technique into dysfunctional play. I gotta beg your pardon if I sound highly opinionated against their GMing style, I've got my reasons. But at the same time I'd really like you to convince me there's something to be saved here. I do feel that there's something worthy, but I can't pinpoint it yet.

What do they do? -allow me to rant a bit- They GM trad games as Vampire and 3.5 D&D in exactly the same way: players are "adventurers" in a place unknown to them, they get to be railroaded into a quest willing, under a threat or fall into it because they don't have anything else to do or go to. They do a lot of prep in advance, in the best case the prep is so much that it's the equivalent of a complex net of railroads, but never gets to be a sandbox. Whenever the GM frames scenes, they of course don't tell everything to the players, let slip here and there some small misunderstandings if that will help them railroad them further; and otherwise tries to trick them into doing something by instilling more doubt in them. When the players roll the dice and fumble, their character gets humilliated at best and impaired or killed at worst. They do try to use the fiction to save the players if this came to happen by bad luck, but not when it's by the player's choices, even if these choices were taken because of their misdirections.

I have tried to talk them both out of this GMing style, didn't had too much success (so you know, it's not a communication problem), then tried to talk to them about why they do it but they can't explain it too well. They sustain that they do this for the players, that doing otherwise would be to make the game too easy for them. Since I can't get too much more from them, now I'm in the territory of inferences.

My best guess is that they are running a game in two levels. One is in the fiction, in which they tell a story for the players to experience in first person (both like to frame great scenes and impressive or really strange characters) though they need/love to have a certain degree of control about what the player's will experience and how they will react, since the right reaction will trigger another scene or twist that they hav prepared in advance. When that reaction doesn't come, or makes them think the game will derrail in the future, true dysfunctional GMing starts in the shape of NPCs that bend and breake rules "because they can, even when you don't know why... yet!", menaces that pop out of nowhere and miracles preserving the statu quo everywhere. And those are their best excuses anyway.

The other level of the game they are running is a meta-intrigue. They misinform, misdirect and try to trick the players into following the plot in a certain way or another depending on what scene or future twist they have in mind. They can't lie, unless a good dice roll allows them to do it. PCs have to believe, unless a good dice roll allows them to doubt. If anyone plays inthe their hands, the GMs have the right to mock, power down or kill their character.

Player's can't meta-game, it's against the social contract and strongly enforced by the group (except when the group is already frustrated with the GM). But suddenly in a couple of sessions all of them are plotting against the GM. They start to min-max, powergame, look for loopholes in the rules, push small timebombs into the fiction that go under the GMs radar to blow their railroads a few scenes later, look for the slightest excuse to kill NPCs or destroy any other device the GM could possibly use to railroad them... and they all have fun with this. Even the GM.

Ok, maybe I'm looking at this in a too negative way. There are probably a lot more good things in this kind of gameplay than I'm not seeing. If you had a similar or better experience with trad games that go along these lines, please add anything that could help me see this GMing and playing style in a new light.

    \n It's classic illusionism play. Lots of people like it. Lots of people think it's the only way to play, really. It puts all responsibility for Fun in the GM's hands. The players are there for a show, and a good GM is a rock star. The GM manipulates the table subtly like a master magician, and the result is spectacular. Everyone is impressed by the adroit storytelling. Player choices matter less than they might believe, because the GM is going to push things the way he wants regardless of what choice a player makes. The GM probably fudges dice and changes stuff on the fly to make his story happen, and mostly the players feel that's fine, because that's what GMs do, because of Rule Zero, and because it makes for a better story.

    Personally, I hate this style of play and even find some forms of it ethically problematic, but to each his own. I used to run lots of games this way, so it's not like I don't know what I'm talking about. I've talked at length about this elsewhere.

    I'll add one thing:

    I often hear people say that this is the way they love to play, and why should it change if it isn't broken? It's tempting to say, "Hey, ok, sure, if you say so." The thing that keeps me coming back, though, is this part you're describing:\n
    [...] when the group is already frustrated with the GM [...] But suddenly in a couple of sessions all of them are plotting against the GM. They start to min-max, powergame, look for loopholes in the rules, push small timebombs into the fiction that go under the GMs radar to blow their railroads a few scenes later, look for the slightest excuse to kill NPCs or destroy any other device the GM could possibly use to railroad them... and they all have fun with this.     \nI suppose I could say: point this out to them, show them this behaviour, and ask if it's the way that they want to play. Is this their idea of an ideal game? Do they really enjoy this behaviour, or do they engage in it because they don't know what to do instead?

    Now, if you accept this playstyle as something you can't or won't or don't want to change:

    For participating in such a game as a player, I think the key is to "get onboard the train" as much as possible. Figure out what the game's about and playing along as much as possible. Is the GM hoping that you'll be naive, idealistic heroes who get screwed over because of their own scruples? Then make that kind of character and play it to the hilt.

    * Think of yourself as an NPC, rather than a PC. Make it a challenge to portray your character as memorably as possible, engage with game-world details (culture, weaponry, magic, tradition, whatever), put on an accent, make your character a foil to the other characters and give them what they need (the funny guy needs a "straight man", the sad loner needs someone who is fascinated by their stories of lost glory, etc, etc.).

    * Talk to the GM; offer to help them with the railroad, and design your character accordingly.

    * Recognize moments when you DO have full freedom (perhaps in trouble-shooting a problem the GM has set out and expects you to overcome, in preparing your character background, falling in love with a NPC who is likely going to betray you, etc), and save your gamer energy for those moments.

    Actually, i am a DM who does a lot of those things listed, but only in our 16 year long 2nd edition d&d campaign. In the last few years I've tended to agree with Warrior monk on alot of this. But I also agree with AsIf too. I think maybe there is room for all of these types of GM styles. And i do use both styles of play. In our long game it is heavy railroad. In the other games i run or am part of, I work as hard as i can to curb that kind of behaviour. I don't use this heavy handed 'technique' when i play Dungeon world or AW or Tremulus or other indie style games, when i'm playing those games I decry this sort of behaviour.

    Whats funny about this is that the players actually like the 2nd edition game the best, i don't know if that says somethign about being railroaded or if it has to do with the nostalgia of playing for so long, but we play once a month and the people come and really want to see what will happen next. I can barely convince them to play anything else.

    How i do it:
    I agree that alot of it is ledgermain, stagecraft, and I am a good storyteller so it works for me (in that particular game); but a lot of it is also a desire to feel special and that the world has a kind of fate behind it where you are destined to do great things. I consider the game to be successful and i do agree that this type of DM style is actually sub standard, but at the same time I do vary my approach so maybe my style isn't as heavy handed as your DM's is. How i do this is to vary the approach to different parts of the story, encounter, episodes, so that its not always railroading that is occurring. I do this by creating different types of challenges with different types of possible solution mechanisms. I never tell them which one i am utilizing, so that they can never truly know what is in their hands and what isn't. Its fraud, but if you like you can steal it and defraud some poor unfortunate story gamers too... here it is:

    Event mix
    Railroad
    Railroad with Grand pivot
    Open ended ("proper way to play")
    Simulationist
    Collaborative.

    Railroad
    In railroad parts, the outcome is more or less certain, the only thing that isn't rigged is the combats, but after two decades of playing the game i know what people can and can't beat, and if im unsure, i play out the battle at home before the game to simulate the situations to a 't', this is where i get to have those beautiful set peice situations that are typcially rare in open ended story games. I am a decent writer (i'm a novelist by hobby too,) so this where i get to use those 'talents'.
    Example: Young woman NPC is destined to turn out to be the kings daughter and win the crown. If she dies in the first adventure we never see that happen, at level 9 the girl takes up her fathers sword in a climactic battle at the city gates, she holds it aloft. People were excited, they loved this scene. Railroaded completely. The key to railroading is to know that secretly people like form and meaning, but not consciously. When your monsters die, get mad or embarrassed or take on a wounded look... in the above moment of glory for the girl, she had to roll very high... do not fudge rolls (and never ask for do overs of scenes, i would walk out of a game like that), if she didn't roll high, weave that into the story (in my case I rolled 20 and max damage.... maybe there are other levels of railroading that i don't know of!)

    Railroad with Grand pivot
    Basically a railroad, but a huge decision is inserted sort of like a choose your own adventure fork. The outcome is usually humungous and will obviously show that the game is not railroaded (teehee)....
    Example: A character had to choose between becoming king and following his religion. Each one had massive consequences but the point of the excercise was to show that he had absolute control over the outcome.

    Open ended
    In the open ended parts, i play it more or less how the you high end GMs do here on story games, more or less how AW supposes it should be done. Typically speaking, just read AW and this would be how its done.
    Example: A character decides that a certain legal clause gives him control over an expedition. This could seriously derail my story, but this was an open ended section, so i let him roll his CHA to see if he could woo the captain into believing his side of events. He made the roll, became leader (against the parties wishes lol)...

    Simulationist
    Simulationist parts are like open ended except there are real life factors that can't be controlled like weather and npc reactions, these are pre generated (and then I stick to them no matter how its going) or randomly rolled.
    Example: A cargo ship carrying seige guns was known to have run aground on a sand bar, near where a battle had taken place. A character decided to lead his men to capture it. I designed the scenario so that everything ran on a time table, even the weather. It was a flat enounter becuase he dilly dallied and the ship was rescued before he could even attempt the operation (the downside of simulation... but still interesting.)

    Collaborative
    Collaborative parts are when i have no freaking clue how the heck they can do this, but I essentially make that whatever they decide to do will work no matter what.
    Example: The party is stuck in a castle that is designed by the great trap maker.... They must navigate a passage that contains some instant death trap. I had no idea how the trap work or how it would be disarmed. I also didn't leave it to a roll. I listened to the players decide on what the trap was and then agreed with them. They disarmed it, and would have been able to no matter what. In a sense then, i let them DM the world. The key here was that they didn't know it was collaborative, they thought they had outwitted me on the trap.

    RINSE AND REPEAT. Muwahahaha....

    Basically alternate between these, the railroads still remain as buoys in the midst of the story, but these other parts strengthen both the illusion and give some power over to the players. I'm open that some parts are railroaded, i just never say which. Its a bizarre thing, but people are having fun and i guess thats why people play these kinds of games. (to have fun i mean, not be ermm railroaded).

    I now open the gates for those who would bash me... lol.. maybe i deserve it. But as i've said above, I play your style too and i like it alot, so don't try to convert me, I have one foot on both sides of the river....

    \n I liked your in-joke, Paul! And I love this thread again. @cupbearer11 that was great.

    As to the restated questions raised by @WarriorMonk, I will say this to get a more theoretical ball rolling: In a sense, you're railroading all the time. The difference is a matter of grainsize. Every time a GM personally decides what the interpretation of a roll or the next event to occur is, they are creating a tiny little section of railroad and running the players down it. Boom, that's a thing that happened. Tiny little section of railroad. The difference is that in classic illusionism these sections are larger than one scene, they often comprise whole acts or adventures, and tangential lines generally lead back toward the central one; while in some styles of play they are smaller than a whole scene, teeny-tiny, and when strung together in sequences they end up someplace nobody really expected (in theory). Railroad sections can be exactly the size of one scene, as well. That's like a Fiasco turn when you Resolve. They can be any grainsize.

    It's even possible to mix grainsizes together, although the level with the longest sections must maintain continuity precedence over the lower ones. My 1991 HyperTheater piece "Ghosts in the Machine" is an example (although today we would call it a "Theatrical LARP"). From the aftershow press release: "The presentation included one "Primary" PlotLine, which could be resolved in only one of two ways. "Secondary" PlotLines were developed around supporting Co-Actor Characters and hourly themes, and each possessed a number of possible resolutions (many possessed no specified resolution at all). "Tertiary" PlotLines were comprised of clues and props given to Players, which drove them to seek out other Players or props, or to enact sequences which did not radically undermine the higher tiers. There were, of course, a number of red herrings thrown in for good measure. No indication was made to Players that these distinctions existed."

    No matter where you decide to stand, even if it's just for a lark, or just for a little while, it takes some energy and artistic effort to produce a campaign. Any kind of campaign. Whether you prefer TV ensemble dramas, serial comic books or murder mystery theater pieces, they all require their artists. Narrativist Gamers shouldn't think Simulationist Gamers are any more "wrong" than they themselves would wish to be considered. Improvisers don't think actors who memorize lines are categorically lesser artists. CYOA writers don't think linear novels are categorically a lesser artform. They are just different artforms, they work on different levels, they have different degrees, angles and distributions of artistic intention, and they are all enjoyed by someone. Surely no open-minded artist would willingly become prejudiced against another artist's work simply due to the fact that they work in different artforms. Right? Right.

    \n As a resident King of Grognards I feel I'd be remiss if I didn't chime in.

    First and foremost, I believe there is very little objective in anything, especially things like game playing. I find it's all just subjective preference. I've met all types of people, and found they're entirely different at a fundamental level. Because of that I never bother to attempt to influence. I merely relate facts, or my own biased preferences and experiences, and then let people do as they will with it.

    While I can't say for certain from your limited description, I think it's likely that I'm exactly the type of GM you're talking about, and that most of the many games I lead or play in are of that style (by conscious choice of the participants btw). When I started gaming (1980ish, I was 8 at the time) I was just learning, and obviously didn't go to the lengths I do now. I used mostly purchased modules, tried to adhere to rules more closely, was far more strictly railroady, and hadn't learned the lessons that adapted my style. I'd say by the time I was 18 I'd settled into where I am today. Very little has changed in the last 24 years, mostly because there hasn't been any need.

    I'll do my best to offer examples & descriptions, and attempt to answer your questions. If I'm going to be DM'ing a game this is my normal method.

    First and foremost I learn the system. This means reading every word that exists for the system, probably several times. I study it. I break down the mechanics to understand the underlying math. I analyze the subsystems. I categorize, and relate it to other games I know better. I attack it, and beat it into utter submission (including house rules and mods where necessary).

    Next up I gather players. This means understanding the people I play with enough to make an educated guess at how they'd relate to the system, and vice versa. I'll ask who is interested, provide a synopsis of the system, and gauge reactions. Then I'll arrange meeting with them, individually or in a group, and ask what they're looking for from the game. I'll also ask if they have initial character concepts they're going to be interested in.

    Third step is writing the adventure. I generally do both an overall campaign synopsis and an outline of a specific adventure within it. That lets me place the specific into the general, but also see how the general needs to be hinted at from within the specific. I also make an effort to customize it around any initial character ideas, and based upon the desires of the players.

    So the campaign is the actual story I'm trying to tell. It would be the overall novel. The adventures, like individual chapters, are a combination of how to relate the story, and what the players want to experience. Success or failure within it all is going to be threefold: chance, player skill, and my balancing of difficulty.

    Next I'll get with the players and actually make the characters with them, offering my suggestions and insights relating to the overall campaign. At this point they'll be expected to provide back story, a roadmap, etc. Then I'll take these specifics back to the adventure and campaign design and integrate all the elements with each other. This should produce a final adventure ready to play.

    That adventure will be an extremely detailed 'mini-sandbox' with numerous plots and events happening simultaneously that relate in some way to the overall campaign and at least one of the characters. I'll have pages of npcs (with backstories and mannerisms), locations, demographics, encounters, tricks/traps/puzzles, and so on. How the players proceed through each adventure is up to them, however the story elements within the campaign are going to happen regardless, so the players can be a part of it to impact the outcomes, or ignore it and let it happen. The further they go afield, the less prepared the area will be, and therefore the less detailed, real (and imo fun) the adventure will be.

    That last is a fundamental truth of the playstyle. People who play this way want details, and generally some degree of simulation. Yes we're telling stories, and roleplaying...having conversations, evolving plots, and so on. However it is the detail items that 'make it' for them in the same way realism elements and details can make or break a book or show.

    The above would probably take about 2-4 weeks, but happens while we're playing other stuff. Once I've established the system and overalls each weeks adventure will usually require 5-15 hours of prep time on my part. This is writing the adventure, working out details, preparing handouts and props, considering likely alternatives, and so on.

    Between adventures players will be asked for further input. They can largely shape the next adventure, or at least the options within it. Over and over again it goes like this. However, within each adventure (and for the overall campaign) certain elements must be fixed both to provide the detail people crave, and to tell a coherent story. Players don't have to participate, but 95% of the time they seem to want to. As I've said in other threads it's the difference between improv, and a movie or novel. Both are fun, but they're not the same, and one can never fully create the experience of the other.

    So what specifically does this style provide (mind you other styles may provide some or all of these in differing ways):

    1. Coherent story/plot with direction and unity of purpose (infinite avenues often results in fracturing or stasis).
    2. Details and fullness (which often result in more options for players, and richer, unexpected turns).
    3. Integrated sub-plots (often cleverly or subtly achieved).
    4. Preparation allowing for rapid adaptation within the prepared scope (through familiarity of system, character, player, campaign, and adventure).
    5. Ease of epic length campaigns (can and have plotted 1st-30th level taking several years across a single evolving story).

    I would point out that this is not limited to old school games, though old school games often expected this style. I run Mouse Guard the same way, with some modification. I've seen AD&D run in near total sandbox focused on narrative. The one doesn't necessitate the other.

    I realize this is somewhat general. If you have specific questions about some element, or need specific examples, just say so.

    I've been wanting to post a bit of an aside in the meantime, and just got around to it now. I hope I won't derail the fantastic discussion which is ongoing. But I feel that this is important - at least to me. Here is the bit that got me thinking:\n
    In a sense, you're railroading all the time. The difference is a matter of grainsize. Every time a GM personally decides what the interpretation of a roll or the next event to occur is, they are creating a tiny little section of railroad and running the players down it.
    \nIt seems a lot of people are agreeing with this statement. I've seen it a lot in RPG discussions: "We're all railroading! When you frame a scene as a GM, you're railroading!" "You're railroading, too. You just have more 'branches' in your railroad!" "It's the same shit! Come on, man! We all do it."

    This has always rung hollow to me. I'm willing to accept that my personal view of "railroading" is different from other peoples' (although I think it's pretty close to the Forge definition for Illusionism, for what it's worth), but this is how I see the issue:

    No. It's not the same. It's not about scope or scale, or how many decision points are built into a game.

    It's about the social interactions between the people, and about how the System handles significant decisions. To me, railroading happens when someone (usually the GM - though not always) doesn't engage with a meaningful (and "game legal") decision or move by another player, and takes action to negate it. (Kind of like "blocking" in improv.)

    It's fundamentally different from doing the stuff that a GM is supposed to do, like framing scenes or presenting adversity. It's about a) not engaging with the people at the table, not honouring their choices, first, and b) not responding to their input into the game (valid "moves"), by making your own moves instead.

    In a GMed RPG, often it is the GM's role to provide challenge and danger: "There's a huge monster guarding the entrance to the tomb! It wants to fight!" That's not railroading. The game simply *wouldn't run* if the GM didn't say anything like this: the GM *has* to provide something for the players to interact with.

    But if the players make a decision - "No, we don't want to risk a fight. Let's go back to the bandits' camp, where we left the Princess" - and the GM chooses to invalidate that decision by saying: "Uh, on the way to the camp, you meet a huge monster! It attacks you!/You're going to have to fight it if you want to get to the camp!"... that's when railroading happens.


    1. The GM is ignoring the player's decision to engage with the game a certain way. The players say, "Screw this dungeon. We want to start a cult in the city, instead!" The GM - the actual, human being at the table - ignores what they said altogether and tries to force them to keep playing the scenario she has in mind ("Uh, there's a huge storm outside, totally unpassable. The only way back to the city... is to pass through the dungeon I designed").

    The players are making choices, and the GM simply *does not* accept those choices. She's not listening to the players; she flat-out ignores what they say. That's one half of the picture.


    2. The other half is not engaging with the System in the right places. The GM is supposed to make certain decisions (like how strong the enemy is). The players are supposed to respond to that, by making decisions ("Ok, this monster is too tough for my 7th-level Monk. I can only hurt it on a critical hit, and even then I only do 2 points of damage. Let's run away!"). That's the players' move in this game.

    In a railroading situation, the GM invalidates those valid game moves, by misapplying his or her powers of authority to change things to the way she wants them to be: "Ok, never mind. Let's say your Monk gets a +10 bonus to hit, just for this fight. Can we get back to it, now? Roll initiative!"

    That's the GM saying, "No, you're wrong. I don't accept your choice to run away. I want you to make the 'keep fighting' move here. You're going to keep fighting this monster. Here, look, I modified things to keep you happy for now. Now get back to rolling those dice! Go!"

    ---

    In essence, to put it in Vincent's Apocalypse World lingo:

    Roleplaying is a conversation. It's like taking turns, except it's not exactly taking turns, you know?

    The players talk and they something, and then they turn to you, and it's your turn to make a move. You respond to them.

    So what's going on here?

    *** Some GMs decide to break the conversation, and they start making their moves when it's not their turn. ***

    They're trying to play on the players' turn, too. (And that's exactly why the players feel unhappy about it. They can feel the conversation isn't going right. They're not getting to talk - or, if they are, no one's listening.)

    ---

    That's how I would describe railroading. Not a matter of scale or scope. It's when the GM a) invalidates the social contract in play (e.g. that the players' strategic decisions should have weight, or ignores their interests in what they choose to engage with in the game altogether), and b) breaks the expected System in play, by making moves (and making decisions) in places where it's the players' turn.

    That's how I see it.

We should be studying her output as much as we study our own (and I can easily think of a dozen more sibling vocations for which the same statement is true). Don't be prejudicial.       \n Lots of interesting observations here.

      I think both JD and David_Berg are onto something in the sense that, yes, there are expectations created by the style of play the GM is going for and the rules in use at the table.

      I'm not sure that changing to a different ruleset would fix the *social* problems here, but there are two solutions being which are worth at least thinking about:

      * JD points out that switching to a looser set of rules can allow the railroading/GM guidance (whatever we want to call it) to be more seamless, easier to handle. Some games are nicely set for Illusionist practices, with freeform roleplaying being a good example - D&D 3 certainly *is* rigid enough that obvious and clumsy manipulations are necessary to force certain outcomes into play, which, combined with player investment in character builds, is bound to create some friction.

      So, a looser system with more room for the GM to adjudicate outcomes might make the railroading easier, more seamless, and reduce some of the social friction we're seeing here.

      * Dave seems to be suggesting the opposite approach: if you enjoy this battle of wits, then make it more explicit. Pick a ruleset and stick with it: something which is robust enough that the players and the GM can really compete with each other, so that there are clearer lines for victory and defeat.

      I could see accomplishing this in D&D3 by modifying your social contract to include the rules being more front-and-center. For example, when an NPC or monster appears and certain rolls are made, the GM reveals AC numbers, skill totals, or other stats, and the players take note of these. So, part of the process of discovery for the players is learning the statistics of their opposition. Then everyone commits to sticking to these, and rolls all dice in the open - in other words, in a future encounter the players can reference the stats they already know to argue the case for an attack landing when the GM might be tempted to fudge and say it didn't.

      This gives the players something objective to pin their attempts to: they know the "target", and can pin it down, so that they can work out a plan, and have a sense of whether it's going to work or not. The GM is forced to show his hand a little more in this style of play, so that the players can have a legitimate attempt at success or failure, and a sense of their chances.

      Once this is recognized as the social contract at work, the group might get used to treating fictional positioning the same way: the GM makes a determination, the players discover it little by little, and everyone sticks their guns to see how the thing actually plays out.

      As an aside (it may be irrelevant), I've had experiences playing with groups like the one described: perhaps a great group of people, or they were fun RPG players, but the GMing was a little stilted for my tastes, something felt dysfunctional. In those groups, over time I'd only play in one-shots (which tended to be put together with more care, on one hand, and less attachment to outcomes and character, on the other hand) or if I was GMing (which everyone enjoyed), and bow out of the "regular" campaigns which I didn't enjoy as much.

      Also, WarriorMonk, I agree with your thoughts on NPCs. Of course players can and do often care a great deal about NPCs, just like characters in a book or a TV show! But, as you point out, that's not something the GM can *force* on them. The players decide whether they care or not, and how much. They might decide that your honourable paladin is actually an arrogant asshole; the GM can't control *how much* they care, or how they see the character.

      I think the best GMing happens when the GM *recognizes* how the players feel about the character and reacts to it to build better scenes or more drama, by exaggerrating or challenging their feelings for the NPC. A GM who *assumes* the players will feel a certain way about his or her NPC and then tries to force them to react a certain way when they don't isn't exactly stellar.

      What's entirely missing, then, is any recognition of a style of play in which the GM uses force specifically to give the players what they want. The "consent" issue overshadows the that of artifice, and makes it sound like something that you reluctantly agree to, as if you have to lie back and think of England, rather than something that you might engage with enthusiastically because it produces good games.

      The virtue of the glossary, both then and now, is that it serves as an external reference point to keep everyone talking about the same thing. What would that new reference point be, if I accepted that the definitions should be shifted? All that would happen, I expect, is that you and I will use the terms idiosyncratically, and everyone who has already been getting their jollies from ostentatiously declaring some forms of play to be morally and ethically inferior to their own will continue to use them in that way.

      \n Well this is after all a tricky discussion, I was actually asking what could be salvaged from certain kind of dysfunctional play, not what could be salvaged from railroading, but it was easy to miss the disctintion because you don't know much about my group of players nor the GMs mentioned. However I'm thankfull for all the insights and reccomendations about railroading, it's been interesting and quite instructive.

      I guess I can resume the technique mentioned into something simpler like:

      Signal Railroading: Whenever you want to comunicate an aesthetic experience to the rest of the players, use this sign to ask them permission to temporarily relinquish agency rights from them. The sign can be a phrase, a change in your tone, a hand signal or it can be mixed with a game mechanic.

      On another note, I had a singular experience yesterday that gave me an insight on why these GMs react like this and where do they learned to do it. We were testing a game in development and we were implementing special abilities for the game. One of the playtesters made an ability that called for us to wing a mechanic on the fly to support it, caught us unprepared and seemed to unbalance the game. It was late, we were all kinda tired and not thinking right. On the morning I realized that it wasn't the mechanic that was unbalanced, but that we had forgotten to play attention to the turn order and how many actions and reactions we were making. The combat seemed unbalanced from to this player's side, but it was because the opposition wasn't taking actions to attack (which had the right to do, mechanics-wise) and was only reacting instead. At first we thought it was his special ability that made the game unbalanced, but later I realized that the player was rushing things in a way that made us lost track of turns.

      The thing that relates this experience to the thread theme is that for a moment I had the same feeling the mentioned GMs should have had before (coincidentally, they both play with this playtester I'm talking about). It felt like losing control of the game, like nothing of whatever I could do would change the outcome and that I could just endure it and prepare better for it the next time. As a GM (and illusionist) I've felt this a few times before, the moment when all your work comes crashing down because players made the only thing you didn't thought about. It's hard not to lose control then and try some desperate move to make things the way they were before. I think that on most of those occasions I managed to build on it and move in the dark for a bit until new options appeared. It's a kind of a sensitive moment for a GM, his emotions might get exposed on that moment and I wouldn't be surprised that a bit of mockery applied by the group to the GM on that moment could trigger a dysfunctional railroading episode.

      Another reflection I had this morning from that moment is that either as a player of GM we're interested in enjoying certain feelings through the game. I want to feel like I'm a huge axe-wielding half-orc that has nothing to fear from this or that kind of enemy in particular. As a GM I want to feel comfortable in the role of a powerful villain (just enough to make a good performance actually, not to rule the setting) and I want to transmit that to the players. The game mechanics and stat blocks give us a way to defend it, to corroborate that it's an acepted truth infront of others. It's natural that once you find this to be true, you try to minmax to protect those feelings, either as aplayer or as a GM. After all, those feelings are the reason you're playing.You're going through all of this to experience something.

      So I talked it with a friend and a couple of minutes ago we came up with a mechanic for our game called "Plot Armor". I'ts basically an instance of the game called by the GM to present a character without fear of having the players kill his feeling inmediatly. For a single turn the GM has the right to be badass, can show off and attack the players without the need of rolling dice, but doesn't do any damage at all nor imposes any statuses on the PCs, except maybe narrative (enemy cuts a thin wound on your cheek, sends you flying but you land on your feet, surprises you with your guard down but stops his fist in front of you, though that doesn't make you flinch a bit, etc.) This way plot armor covers both the NPC and PCs for a single moment to deliver the aesthetic experience the GM wants. After that, combat resumes as usual.
