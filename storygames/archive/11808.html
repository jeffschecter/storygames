    \n In Watership Down, a lot of the tension comes not from mission goals versus personal goals, but mission goals versus personal nature. Rabbits just want to hang out, silflay some carrots, make kittens, tell stories. They need scouts and lookouts, and males do establish a hierarchy, so you've got your Owsla.

    But striking out, journeying across hill and dale, crossing streams, assessing traps, is bewildering to bunnies. They're not great at it, or at working together. Bigwig is aggressive and takes his sweet time coming to accept Hazel's leadership. Fiver is seer-crazy and may scream and run away. Dandelion (maybe? the clever one) may get excited about his discovery and not be able to explain it. Hazel has leadership skills and is always working to keep the rabbits motivated and on target.

    So I'm thinking a game specific to the WD portrayal of rabbits needs to contrast Goals with general Bunny Nature (fear response, not too bright) and, for the PC-ish tier of bunnies whose names I'm remembering, pop those against a Noteworthy Trait that is useful in some circumstances but not others. This sounds like story (and storyteller) structure, but suggests mechanics as well.

    \n Hey Kevin,

    Mouse Guard is a great game, and I think it's a great choice for somebody's first RPG. It is designed to support that well. It is also designed in a very focused way to emulate the comics. It plays like a Mouse Guard story - you have tiny anthropomorphic mouse people with swords and armor, you have beekeepers and musicians, you have outsized threats from predators and weather, you have no people, dogs, or cats, it is a fantasy world.

    The biggest problem may be the Nature raw ability, which is going to be irrelevant. I'm not sure how that impacts other systems completely, but it surely does.

    Resources are irrelevant, unless you get very esoteric.

    The skills port over fine.

    Scale is off. Weather won't work quite right.

    As you noted, all the weapon stuff is out the window. Again, you are voiding the warranty in a huge way, because numerous play balance issues are involved in each of these omissions.

Can someone fill us in?

    But where I was going was a sketch of how it could work mechanically. An extraordinary rabbit like General Woundwort has some personal quality, a drive to protect rabbits from discovery by humans, that allows him to force his will on many, many rabbits, overcoming their natural tendencies: the rabbits of his warren pass droppings underground, put up with overcrowding, and are tightly controlled by the Owsla. So the General has, say, a FATE Aspect that he invokes again and again, or a large dice pool with the biggest dice in the game, that he uses to overcome the rabbits' Nature.

    That special quality should have some kind of synergistic effect with Goals or Motivation. Fiver's vision of the first warren's destruction convinces Hazel and a few others, but along the journey, it's Hazel's Goal to find a safe new warren that keeps the various rabbits in line; his Goal aspect or pool interacts with his leadership quality.

    Or maybe there only has to be two traits, the Talent and Nature, either one of them being used to solve problems as the situation demands, but Nature also resisting un-rabbit-like actions such as controlled breeding!

    Thoughts? I'm more of a house-ruler than any kind of designer.
