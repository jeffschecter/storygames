So we're playing Sorcerer in the world of Elric of Melnibone (an alternate version where Elric didn't survive childbirth). John is the GM.

\n

Humanity is love. Pacting includes Summoning as the cost of admission, so Sorcery is a tiny bit more forgiving (because you only have to make 2 humanity loss rolls instead of 3).

\n

The PCs

\n

I'm Hibbukal, Exalt-Surgeon, inhumanly cerebral and deeply aristocratic.

\n

Pete is Sendric - Hibbukal's brother and patriarch of our House (Khanjar'a)

\n

Mike is Mosun - Sendric's primary human trading partner, ship captain, and secret lover of Hibbukal's daughter.

\n

What we're up to

\n

Mosun had been sort of tagging along for the last two sessions since his personal stressors were mostly resolved, but his Kicker wasn't. We're in this transitional space where we're fighting through this awful jungle but we don't have many opportunities to show our love (since they're not with us). Anyway, Mosun's been sitting in the highly stressful situation of having 1 Humanity. Can't risk a summon, but can't gain the humanity back.

\n

The Unconscious

\n

Anyway, Mike and I had this great conversation before the game about the unconscious, and Mike used that as a launchpad for some really wicked roleplaying. At one point an NPC - a potential source for love and humanity - propositioned him, and he described his hand slapping her before he thought about it. It was perfect, because it was this really believable reaction of someone who's experiencing extremely traumatic stress and feels like someone's trying to play him.

\n

I unfortunately slipped up a bit, and went darker and more physical than I should have with the cerebral, removed Hibbukal. The dark was fine, but the physical was a slip-up. I hope the others will chalk it up to Hibbukal's stress after being badly mauled in the failed summoning. So whereas Mike improved his play by thinking about his character's subconscious, I flubbed my play by forgetting to lock onto the fact that my character is more cerebral than I am.

\n

Anyway, I really thought Mike did an excellent job of bringing his character's pain back into the middle of the game. But now that I'm sitting at 1 humanity too, I think we're really feeling the hurt. Hard to get humanity back in a game about love when you're many miles away from the ones you love.

    \n I'm with James on his first point.

    I think it's important for the Humanity definition to be applied with great elasticity. Not to one person or story or connection, but to whatever is at hand.

    How the three PCs treat each other seems to be where the story is now. You want your Humanity to go up? Make sure to find ways to treat each other with love!

    In my last two long Sorcerer stories, most of the game's Humanity revolved around how the PCs treated each other, though all of them had relationships with NPCs that were of equal importance to each PC.

    In one, the PCs were convicts in a state penitentiary being torn apart in a sorcerous turf war. They didn't know each other at the start of play. But how they bonded against the threat of the penitentiary's soul destroying culture became some of the highlights of the game.

    In the other game, they started as Mercs in the same company with a long history. And the threat to their bond became the fulcrum of play.

    \n Hi Alberto,

    In Sorcerer each Character has a rating for Humanity. This rating might rise or fall during play based on the actions of the Character. In each game, the GM defines what the definition of Humanity will be.

    If you do things that are in alignment with the definition of Humanity, your Humanity might go up.

    If you do things that are are against the definition of Humanity, your Humanity.

    The key point is, what is at stake is what the Characters do... concrete actions. It isn't enough to be affected emotionally or have a reaction. What is at stake is how the Character actually behave within the context of the definition. So, if a man could either get the diamond he's spent years searching for, or save his brother's life.... If he choose to save his brother for Love, then he might gain a point of Humanity if the definition is Love. If he choose to get the diamond and forgoes attempting to save his brother, he might lose a point of Humanity.

    It's a track of the conduct of the characters across the choices they make through play.

    \n Cedric, to answer your question...

    Humanity is the big behavioral regular in Sorcerer. It can go up or down for a character, and as long as it's above 0, your character is still "human."

    But if it drops to 0, it means your character has slipped from the "tribe" of humans, as per the definition of Humanity. If Humanity is "Love" for example, the character has become incapable of love. If your character hits a Humanity of 0, the character is no longer capable of being a PC and the character sheet is handed over to the GM. This, losing the character through inhuman actions, is the threat that hangs over Players/Characters in Sorcerer, not death. What is at stake is how a character behaves and the choices he or she makes in relation to other people.

    Humanity, then, if it drops to 0, can finish a character, and thus that character's story. However, most folks make sure to pump their Humanity back up if it starts dropping low. It's like a Vic Macky on THE SHEILD or Walter White on BREAKING BAD or almost any character on BATTLESTAR GALACTICA... they're all capable of doing horrible things, but then they do this one little, morally strong thing that redeems them enough and keeps them from wholly monstrous.

    Sorcerer stories end when a Kicker is resolved. A Kicker is a story beat written by each Player for his character that demands a choice of some kind. The Kicker is completely open-ended and without a planned resolution of any kind. Finding out how the Kicker is resolved --and who the character is by the time it is resolved -- is the largest unit of narrative in Sorcerer play.
