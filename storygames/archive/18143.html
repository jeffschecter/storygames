\n I've had versions of this discussion before, and they usually veer off into various types of dismissal, where someone thinks my position is more extreme than it is. So let me lead off with what I'm not saying:

I am not saying that every roleplayer must choose between:
(a) getting into their character's head and acting out everything that character would do, regardless of any other concerns (like enjoyment of other players)
(b) using their character to do whatever "works best for the story" with zero sense of any ongoing character identity

I am not saying that.

What I do want to say is that I've felt a tension between two competing desires when I play. These are, roughly, the desire to fully explore an established character, and the desire to responsively engineer resonant and engaging scenes. Much as it's usually possible to do some degree of both at the same time, I find that it's rarely possible to maximize both at once. Thus, tension.

How do you navigate this tension?

    \n Here's an example:

    My Monsterhearts character Amun is attempting to forsake his infernal dark power in favor of the "Lynx Force" that gives power to Sage, the witch he has a crush on. Amun wants to be on the winning team, and, due to getting his ass kicked by Sage's powers, he figures her team is it. Now, Amun's ex-girlfriend Lavender is the key to various factions' schemes -- her continued existence is holding open a portal, which allows the infernal dark power, among others, to exert influence into the mundane world. The only faction who wants this portal closed is Sage and the witches. Amun decides, in the grandiose and bloodthirsty way that embodies him, to kill Lavender, as quickly and thoroughly as possible.

    As it happens, the various player characters with an interest in Lavender (also a PC) converge in a hospital where the portal physically manifests, and Lavender rushes toward it. Previous contact with the portal has demonstrated to the players that it will suck people through to some unknown "elsewhere". Removing Lavender from earth would presumably remove the portal from earth too. Dave knows this for sure. Amun doesn't, and also doesn't particularly care, as he's intent on proving his usefulness to Sage and the Lynx Force with a dramatic action.

    Dave rolls the dice and gets a level of success that allows me to inflict extra Harm, which will kill Lavender. Everyone else at the table wants to see Lavender wounded but not killed, to give all the other present characters a chance to react and develop their own dramas. Will the Chosen act to save a life, even if it means keeping the portal open? Will Sage reveal her own secret intentions to the others by helping finish Lavender off? Will Lavender reveal what she hopes to find on the other side of the portal? Sounds like a great scene!

    On the other hand, I have a very concrete vision for who Amun is. He wants power, and wants it with a desperation that leads him to act impulsively and compromise himself in various ways. Also, his idea of power is to be allied with someone powerful, and to earn their favor. This kid is basically a needy little bastard trying to act like a badass, and his more positive emotions, like affection, are only triggered by those who can fit into that schema. Amun is a specific sort of teenage misery engine, and a large part of my interest in Monsterhearts was in seeing how that would play out, in the context of the other characters. Will the Witch adopt him as a worthy equal? Will the Vampire take him as a subservient thrall? Will the Chosen kill him to end his toxic behavior? So if I don't find out what happens when "Amun kills Lavender because he can", then I'm not getting all of what I came here for.

    The group's social contract offers some support for both decisions. On the one hand, a hearty endorsement of Monsterhearts' "play to find out" ethos, and a general appreciation for rich characterization. On the other hand, a helpful spirit of working to give each other opportunities to have fun and to shine.

    Just to clarify: I am not looking for solutions to this specific situation. It's simply a recent example that I can remember in some detail. I present it only in case it was unclear from my original post what I'm talking about.

    \n Postscript: it seems I just took the opportunity to free-associate about the topic here - it's probably because you opened the thread so widely. The following doesn't really have a central point, I'm just musing randomly. Perhaps there's something interesting there.

    For me this is a game design question. Different games handle this tension in different ways, and I switch games like a wine connosseur looking for variety. In some games it's a valid play to play either way, while in others individual players should always look out for number one, and there are mechanics or other players tasked with dramatic concerns. Of course in many games these issues are left to methodology, in which case we could talk about how a given person or group plays a given game. A game can also be crafted in a way that causes this issue to not apply at all.

    This is mainly a feature of games that rely on players to both advocate for their characters and coordinate drama, without distinguishing between these tasks explicitly. To pick an example very familiar to myself, TSoY is vulnerable to this conflict if the Story Guide plays relatively hands-off and relies on the players to frame scenes. If the Story Guide's technique is more dedicated to carrying the dramatic coordination burden, then the players never need to worry about it, and they know that they can do whatever is right for their character, relying on the SG to spin the consequences. The difference between these modes of play isn't really in the rules (although I chose to write the SS version of the text to clearly advocate for a strongly drama-coordinating Story Guide), but merely in how the group construes the GM/player task division in practice.

    But anyway, yes: this is an issue in roleplaying, a basic question for design. Overall my own favourite solution tends to be walling off these responsibilities clearly: let somebody else worry about the dramatic tension, I worry about depicting my character correctly. This has the not insignificant advantage of resulting in genuine, raw drama where it is almost logically impossible to fail by undermining the integrity of your central characters. Works well for psychological drama, which is almost all modern drama in one way or another.

    (I should clarify that it is, of course, possible to fail in dramatic characterization even if players are explicitly freed from worrying about dramatic conceits. The most common failure in my experience is that the player doesn't know how to construe who his character is, or doesn't know how to show this to the other players so they can appreciate it. This is part of the reason for why I say that character advocacy properly consists of two parts: decide what your character wants, and show it to the other players.)

    This conflict between external (plot-concerned) and internal (character-concerned) construction of drama is a reason I don't like Houses of the Blooded and other similar games: they explicitly suborn character advocacy to dramatic concerns. All players are supposed to be thinking about the story, basically writing it in advance, and then making moves that are "good for the story", so that there is no particular guarantee that anybody would be caring about what the character wants. From my viewpoint this is not desirable, as I want to be playing to see what the story is like, or even whether there is any story. A game that tells me that I should "fake" the events of the game and force them to go against their natural course merely to fulfill a preconceived dramatic need doesn't sit well with me. For me, the game is almost always better if you show clearly who the character is, and let the story be about that, instead of first figuring out what the story is about and then changing the character to fit.

    To apply this to your example Monsterhearts game, the question I would have to ask as a participant would be: how do you know that having Lavender live would be the most interesting outcome? The action might tug the carpet from under other players' feet, the ones who relied on Lavender for their stories, but that's where you need to make your artistic priorities clear: are you in it for the raw, unexpected, fair chaos of creative gaming, or is it important for you to have the game be safe and respectful of the notions of each individual player equally? Is it cool for the events of the game to surprise you, or should the events follow the storyline you chose in advance? For myself it's almost always that first one I want, so I'd go with the character integrity and rely on the creative processes of the game to lead us towards interesting consequences.

    To clarify, there's nothing wrong with playing a game where people respect each other's planned storylines, either. I personally dislike this, that sort of forum game sensibility bores me. It is, however, entirely possible to design games that provide explicit plot protection of various sorts. The crudest are the forum games where you're not allowed to "god-mode" or make changes to NPCs introduced by other players or do anything to another player's character without permission or... the list goes on, because effectively the creative desire is to enable each player to have maximal control over their own sandbox, while allowing them to deside for themselves how their own personal piece of the fiction interact with the rest of it.

    However, leaving aside this extreme, there's plenty of respectable gaming out there that desires players to have dramatic coordination powers and responsibilities. Monsterhearts might be one, I don't know - haven't read or played it. Point-buy systems are usually like this, as is FATE most of the time.

    \n Have a few techniques I use to balance this tension:

    1: First, design characters that allow you to do that. Think ahead. What themes, types of stories and types of scenes is the story likely to engage with and how do I create a character that works with that. Not just works with but further that kind of play. But, you can't see the future so this is mostly about guess work.

    2. I communicate with my fellow players. "Hey. My character is going this direction, which kind of sucks. Could you help me push things in another direction instead?" If the characters internal motivation don't take them in the best direction at the moment, outside help often works like magic. Someone might say. "Oh. I know exactly what to do!" and comes up with a devilish plan to get you on track again.

    3. I see to that my character fails. My character might be very motivated to do X, but they can always fail. I had a very violent character that often wanted to kill other PC. Somehow, when a fight got serious, I always saw to that she ended up losing the fight, or getting hurt, or by mistake let the other PC escape.

    4. I see to what alternative paths I can take. Perhaps the obvious thing for my character to do is X, but that would suck. Without changing my character feelings and motivations I look to a less obvious path to take motivated by the same feelings and emotions. "The obvious thing would be to kill you, but instead I'll make you life hell. Bwahahahah!" I might have to think of a reason why not to do the obvious thing, but often a simple reason can be found, like being afraid to fail.

    5. I wait. This one is surprisingly effective. Just stall a little, and a new situation and solution will arise.

    \n Thank you for that amazing link. Now I'm going to try to focus back more on the OP and example.

    I think the key to understanding the tension of the example is this:\n
    Everyone else at the table wants to see Lavender wounded but not killed, to give all the other present characters a chance to react and develop their own dramas.
    \nI absolutely agree this isn't part of the (normally completely false) conflict of "what the character wants" versus "what makes a good story". This is more part of an (often completely real) conflict between "what I as the player expect my character to do in this situation" and "what the rest of the table wants my character to do in this situation". In other words, it's not your desires that are necessarily at issue - both the fiction and the system are saying "yes, do it, murder her, hahaha yes", it's that you feel the burden of aligning your desires with the outcome the rest of the players want.

    This is not a false burden, among many groups, including my current one, there's palpable tension/disappointment when someone, say, goes too far with violence in a previously lighthearted game, or has not followed a plot point that really motivated or moved everyone else. So you're not wrong about this. But I would identify it as a social issue rather than a game design issue (though as I mentioned with respect to FATE and other games with personality/goal mechanics they may contribute to this if they are not flexible enough). Either this is a group where we invest in each other's individual visions of character fidelity in the situation, and they agree to swallow their disappointment if you passionately charge after something they'd rather you didn't, or it's a group where we invest in some shared concept of how things will go collaboratively and you agree to swallow your disappointment if you have to hold back or concoct some justification for an action you feel is not true to the character/situation.

    (By the way, this is why Primetime Adventures doesn't always succeed at producing great play despite being highly collaborative - because collaboration can and must lead to compromised desires.)
