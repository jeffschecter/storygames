\n Andy K started a thread on 'Why I support, buy and play and love those crappy little single-idea thematic RPGs...' He was throwing out random silly examples of such things, and he said this: \n
The 5-page-long Dungeons and Dragons (ish game) that uses simply playing cards, has a setting of mythical India, and can be busted out and completed in hour-long sessions.
\nThat hooked into me a bit - even though watching the 91 (?) episodes of the Hindi Mahabharat TV series was many years ago now! - and I wasted much of a morning making notes.

So - anyone interested enough in seeing this to encourage me to work those notes up? And if so, would that interest extend to buying the PDF for a couple of dollars or some very modest ransom deal, or just a freebie for curiosity?

I should confess that I've no idea how well it'll work out, and I don't really care enough about D&D to delve past superficialities, and the 1-hour thing is a challenge. But some of it looks like it would fit quite well (like castes = classes, and "kill them and take away their wickedness") or offer nice opportunities for subversion.

    Posted By: Sydney FreedbergHaving read Buck's translation of theRamayanaand theMahabharata, I think these stories are pretty exotic for your average Indian person too. I mean, how often do you find magical ponds, talking deer, 101 babies germinating in clay jars, or a bridge to Sri Lanka made entirely of monkeys anywhere on the subcontinent?
    \nOr burning bushes that talk to you, creatures with the wings of eagles and the voices of lions, or guys being hung on crosses then coming back to life the next day? I don't know a lot of folks that have daily experiences of that, but to many people it isn't all that crazy, nor all that unfamiliar. Though, I have seen Crazy Jesus action figures that leap right out of the grave on sale at some boutique stores downtown, so I guess everyone gets a bit that.

    Of course, I think this could be done right. I mean, there is a lot of really awesome stuff to work with here and a game that brings some understanding (not just compliance, actual understanding) of the themes, motifs, and values of the Hindu religious epics could totally rock. I mean, hell, maces and fights with bows on chariot back are awesome -- but the clash between duty and desire for freedom are even more awesome, and that conflict of moksha and samsara underlies a lot of the works we're talking about.

    One of the best ways I've found to overcome the tendency to exoticize is to not get freaky only about the color/details -- but to put some focus on the real human elements and thematic meaning that goes on behind them. Its easy to make a spectacle of crazy guys who totally charm cobras, and harder to do so with the Irula tribe who have to do dangerous tricks with snakes because their people have been forced out of the forests where they've lived as snake salesmen for 4000 years and now have to make their way in a hostile world.

    Not, mind you, that it even has to be all that deep and serious. The real thing to watch for is are we playing a game about people, or are we playing a game about those zany Hindus and their crazy myths. Cause if it is about people there can still be lots of crazy, and not be falling to the exotic.

    I think Tim and Sydney expressed a bit of confusion over what exotify really means, and maybe are missing a lot of where you are coming from. There's a lot of exotifying of the western world and turning "our" heritage into the Other.

    Not that what I'm saying is that it makes it justified to exploit Indian "cool factor" or whatever. Just that exotifying source material (be it cherished mythologies or movie genres) is a huge part of gaming history. But that's a random tangent, and what I wrote this post originally for is that first paragraph.

    Tim, I do think Shreyas is making good points (albeit bluntly). You're dealing with a foreign culture and, in your game text, I think you should deal with that in some way.

    There's different ways of dealing with it: the minimal way is to say, in the text, "Hey, I know these are Westernised versions of the Hindu gods, and it's just a bit of fun". Or you could research a little and portray things in a mixed way: so you get a flavour of Indian myth, seen from a Western point of view. Nobody's suggesting you need to make it a game about modern India.

    Interestingly, White Wolf are rather good at things like this. They do their research. If you read Demon, say, it's obvious it's heavily influenced by Christian literature such as Milton: but it's still a great game. You could do the same: do a bit of research, be conscious of where you're Westernising things, and you'll end up with a richer game.

    Posted By: Anemone
    Texts can clearly teach people as well, but where does it suggest in the OP that this is about teaching anything?
    \nPeople learn from everything.

    There is a particular way of relating to cultures besides that in which people are brought up in that is getting referred to as "exotifying." Doing this is harmful because it encourages a concept of American/Euro-derived, primarily white, protestant-ethic culture as "normal," while encouraging people to think of other cultures as "Other," capital o: a group with such different goals and cultural ideals and day to day lives that nothing about them can really be understood (or, the subtext is, really as valuable as what the West values.)

    Japan is the best example because there's so many things that exotify it. L5R is a great example -- it takes an outsider's view of "historical Japanese culture" (informed largely by opinion of what's "cool" rather than an actual understanding of said history) and plays up the exotic elements -- color-coded samurai! deviously sexy courtiers! magical things that end with "-o"! -- while eliminating any perspective on the actual subtleties and elements of history experienced by actual people.

    The reason your own cultural context matters here is because it affects how well you can choose to identify exotic elements without failing to recognize mundane elements. The Old West period of American history is exotified to a tremendous degree, for example, but originally by Americans -- which means that, among other things, there is a much better opportunity for others to respond and offer perspective. You get Westerns like Unforgiven because people within the culture are able to step forward and present different takes on it from a position of familiarity. When someone from outside a culture produces something that exotifies it and presents it in this lopsided way, there are fewer people who can react to it with useful perspective -- and eventually you can get a situation like the American geek's typical view of Japan, where an actual perspective on the culture is essentially impossible due to the heavy weight of the exotic elements laid over the top.

    "I automatically distrust non-Chinese scholarship on China, considering it inherently wrongheaded and biased until, upon reading it, I discover that the author is actually pretty knowledgeable.     "...Likewise, I automatically distrust any roleplaying depiction of culture that is not firmly grounded in the cultural background of the author.     "And honestly, I hope other people hold me to the same standards. And, unfortunately, sometimes I don\xe2\u20ac\u2122t. Sometimes I don\xe2\u20ac\u2122t put in the time necessary to be trustworthy. Sometimes I just say stuff or make stuff up.
