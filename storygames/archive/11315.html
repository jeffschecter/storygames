\n So I was (re)watching The Green Berets the other day and it got me thinking about why the movie has received such a critical lashing over the years. Yes, I will admit that the movie has its hokey moments and sometimes characterizations are broad, but it's a solid picture with a very specific, pro-war political stance that makes it somewhat unique.

It's that last that I think dooms The Green Berets to eternal derision, because there's a particular narrative about Vietnam that no one dare deviate from. It was still in its proto-formation in 1968 when the movie came out but it's definitely solidified into something you'll immediately recognize today.

The war is pointless and wastes both American lives and funds. All soldiers are secretly or openly anti-war. Every military effort by the Americans is a failure. All soldiers are addicted to drugs or drink or both. Atrocities are a regular part of a soldier's life in war.

And so on. Basically if it's negative, it's in there. Something like Platoon exemplifies this with its relentlessly downbeat and anti-war message.

This tendency is carried on into the roleplaying field. Probably the first game that comes to mind to the indie-gaming crowd is carry: a game about war, a game that takes its cues from The Things They Carried (natch), but also the Vietnam cinema that supports the above narrative. Certainly there's nothing in the game that specifically forbids the inclusion of positive moments, but you can tell the author's intent would be subverted by such a thing. I also tend to think the game would fall apart if players worked against the anti-war clich\xe9s.

Let me state right here that I have nothing against carry and very much want to play it. Don't write to me saying, "Hey, man, you're dishing on carry!" Because I'm not doing that. I'm only pointing out that it's part of the prevailing narrative of Vietnam, which contains a whole raft of clich\xe9s particular to the anti-war sentiment, just as a relentlessly pro-war stance has its own clich\xe9s.

Anyway, I was looking at games like carry and Shell Shock \u2014 the latter of which, admittedly, is about any war, not just Vietnam \u2014 and wondering just how one would go about telling a story about Vietnam that doesn't fit the anti-war narrative. The last game to take a fairly pro-war stance, or at least not a definitively anti-war stance was Palladium's Recon, originally released in 1986 during the height of the Reagan era. But until one wants to tangle with a decidedly old-school system and try to tease some story out of it, you're out of luck.

How do we go about telling a story like The Green Berets? Where's the story game that allows for a positive approach to America's most unpopular war. If we don't want to wallow in the soul-destructive, corrosive pool that a game like carry encourages, what do we use? What do we do? Does this really call for an entirely new system with mechanisms in place to encourage positive messages? And is it even possible to do so?

I wonder.

    \n I was insufficiently clear. If you're going to take Green Berets as the ur-text of a story-game about Vietnam, you can't ignore the fact that it is widely regarded as wholly divorced from the reality on the ground: a propaganda film, pure and simple. The only way to deal with that from a design perspective is to acknowledge that a Green Beret version of Vietnam would have to be a fantasy.

    One version of this is "reverse PowerKill": play carry, and have the PCs torch hooches and frag cherry LTs but then have a PowerKill move where the GM tells the players how their characters were heroes and patriots fighting to save an unappreciative native populace from the perils of Communism; alternately, play old school D&D and translate it similarly: the dungeon is the jungle, the orcs are the VC, "town" is the Land of the Round Door Knobs.

    It's a false move to argue that, oh, there are these different interpretations of Vietnam and they are totally distinct. Rather, the controversy over the legacy of Vietnam is part of the material that one has to include in any attempt to interpret it, so the decision to ignore that controversy to tell the story of Vietnam as an instance of patriotism pure and simple smacks of puerile jingoism.

    I think any effort to re-tell Vietnam as a feel-good story of wartime heroics will rapidly descend into self-parody.

    \n Sam, I'm not entirely sure what you're looking for:

    1. A game that could be used to tell a positive story about *a* war, and hence could be used specifically to tell an overall positive story about the Vietnam war
    2. A game that somehow is specifically geared to tell a positive story about the Vietnam war
    3. A game (variant on 1 or 2) that not only can tell a story about the war where it is, on balance, a good thing, but where there is no anti-war issue or pressure *at all*?

    2 seems way too specific (but, since, say WWII doesn't have the same social narrative that you're worried about, yet people run games set during WWII, maybe I'm missing something about why you think existing games don't work for Vietnam specifically), but 1 and 3 are different and I'm not sure which one you're asking for. I think 3 might be the wrong thing to go for - since most "stories" in the sense you seem to think D&D or Twilight 2000 are not "story games" are in some way about moral conflict, and "do we have a good reason for killing these folks" is the 800 pound gorilla of moral conflicts in war. If you ignore that issue then, yeah, I think you tread into the realm of being only able to do pro-war *propaganda*, not an honest pro-war stance (think of, say, Red Dawn vs. - if we extend "pro-war" a bit loosely - Army of Shadows). In a nutshell, I think you're likely to fall afoul of Ben's point.

    If all you want is 1, then it doesn't actually seem that hard. Heck, even carry could be read, if you were so inclined, as about the way in which war is a necessary but brutal undertaking and crushes the souls of combatants even as it is the only way to destroy evil (I guess). A game like Unsung could certainly focus on the small-scale moral choices that face soldiers (e.g., do I break my word to this intelligence source that I would protect him? Do I torture these prisoners for crucial information?) in the context of a background assumption that the overall war is just. You might even be able to use something like Dogs in the Vineyard - assume that the US' superior firepower (on the local scale, not the strategic) means that essentially no-one can gainsay your unit's decision about how to deal with a village. Kobayashi's approach sounds pretty solid for finding those lower-scale moral conflicts.

    I haven't seen Green Berets - in my mind, I am substituting Red Dawn - but if you really want the conflict to be *just* about overcoming non-moral obstacles (can we find the sniper, can we rescue our buddy), and that doesn't tackle the propaganda aspects, as PTA might, then I think a game like D&D will probably work fine, for "story" in that sense.

    That said, and putting my personal convictions hat on, my initial reaction to the question, "how can I run a game where the Vietnam War is awesome and was totally a good idea" is nervousness.

    ETA: A parenthetical caveat about dismissing option 2.

    I can't help but think you've painted yourself into a corner here. Also, I think you should *play* carry, if you haven't (my impression is that you haven't, but I could be reading wrong). I'd suggest you play Ram, if you can.

    If you'll indulge me in some professional jargon... just war theorists break war down into *jus ad bellum* - the justice of entering a war - and *jus in bello* - the justice of one's conduct of the war - phases. Traditionally, these are understood to be separable. That is, one can fight for a just cause using unjust means (a common example given is Palestinian terrorism) or one can fight for an unjust cause, but fight justly (a common example given is Erwin Rommel). There's a lot of current debate about whether the categories are really that separable, and of course the examples are deeply contestable, but I hope the distinction is at least roughly intuitive.

    In the case of Vietnam, I can see the two getting conflated because the "dominant narrative" you complain about is that the war was *both* unjust *and* often or even predominantly fought unjustly.

    Now, the first reason to make the distinction is that the main games you've mentioned are really about jus in bello considerations - how individual soldiers act in the conduct of the war. I'm not familiar with Shell Shock, but that's certainly the focus of games like carry or Unsung. That may be some of the going around in circles in this thread - you can perfectly well have a game that focuses on the moral challenges facing individual soldiers while endorsing the overall war, or remaining agnostic about its justification. In fact, I think it would be impossible to have a morally interesting character in a war game *unless* s/he thought the war was justified or was motivated by some other positive value, like honor (the caricature at least of Gen. Lee) or loyalty to comrades. A game about war where all the PCs were what you describe - wastrels who hate killing and think the war is pointless would probably be really depressing and boring (maybe it would be about the way we all do things because authorities tell us to even when we know it's wrong, or something - so at least depressing). Even in a game like 3:16, the moral interest is maintained in large part through dramatic irony - *we* (the players) know that the PCs are part of unthinkable atrocity, and so we wait for their epiphany or comeuppance.

    If you wanted a game about jus ad bellum concerns, that would be very different - you know, it'd be about politicians duking it out with Duels of Wits or some such. It's also why I'm confused about why you think Vietnam poses a *special* problem for this - there's no such "narrative" about WWII (at least it's far from the dominant one), yet your arguments would seem to imply that it'd be impossible to run a "positive" WWII game. That seems like a *reductio*.

    The flip side is that I can't see what you want out of a game that would retain its moral interest, if you want to portray Vietnam as justified overall and also object to characters who may face moral dilemmas as part of the war... where's the story? If you're not allowed to question even the rightness or wrongness of killing *this* person *here*, what is your story about? I mean, you could tell an interesting story that skirts the margins of the war (like much of Catch-22) but I take it that you're not interested in a game that's about Marine supply officers trying to maintain their good cheer amidst office politics.

    If the game is *just* about meeting non-moral challenges, then I don't really see why you've ruled out some games that you ruled out, like D&D. If the question facing players is usually something like, "can we take out that sniper before he gets our buddies," why not use a game that's mostly about tactical simulation of some sort?

    So, in order to make heads or tails of what you want, I'm left with a few questions:

    1. If you think Vietnam was justified, why do you assume that even asking if killing is wrong is a step away from being anti-war? Couldn't the answer plausibly be, no, in this case killing is not wrong?

    2. Why should asking whether killing is wrong in *this* circumstance be scandalously anti-war?

    3. If you're ruling out questions like 2, what sort of moral questions *do* you want to face characters?

    4. If you don't want the game to focus on *moral* questions, why rule out primarily tactical games like D&D and so forth?

    5. How would you run a "story" game about a unit in WWII that wasn't "anti-war," and why can't you do that for Vietnam?

      \nI have to disagree with you there, and while I can't wave my terminal-degree credentials the way Daniel can (and God bless 'im), I will say that there's a substantial minority of those involved in the Vietnam conflict who saw what they were doing as a just thing. There's a difference between the whole "the dominoes must not fall!" mindset of the strategic planners and the point of view of warfighters directly engaged in the nuts-and-bolts of making the war happen. And there's a strong case to be made that something like the Khmer Rouge bloodbath in Cambodia could have been averted if we'd managed to keep South Vietnam independent of the Communist north.

      But my point is that the domino-theory geopolitical calculations are inextricable from the context of any story about Vietnam, because they (and not preventing genocide across the border in Cambodia) were the rationale supplied for the warfighting mission in the first place. The fact that some soldiers believed in the anti-Communist mission and -- what's more -- behaved honorably on the battlefield doesn't change the fundamentally erroneous (and arguably unjust) strategic thinking. In fact, I would argue that a soldier's perception of the "rightness" of his or her mission is completely irrelevant to later historical judgments: we expect soldiers to believe in what they're doing--that's why we indoctrinate them. Nearly twenty years ago, when I was shooting rockets in the desert at Republican Guard units 25 km away, I didn't care whether we were fighting for oil or to free Kuwait or whatever: I just wanted to shoot some goddamn rockets. And everything I've read about the psychology of war convinces me that most soldiers stay in the fight in order not to let their buddies down, not out of any particularly patriotic conviction (though this certainly may play a role in recruitment and retention over the course of a career).

      Now, you used the term "warfighter" which is a phrase that I've come to associate with a particular operational-level professional--what the XO of my old unit would have called an "operator"--who is mainly concerned with the management of warfare: getting Unit A to point X with enough personnel, equipment, supplies, and ammunition to perform its tactical mission (whether that mission is combat, combat support, or something else). But again, we would expect the strategic judgments of even those military professionals to assume rather than argue the "rightness" or "necessity" of their mission. We pay them for their managerial expertise, not their strategic judgments.

      All of this goes to the point that I am trying to make, but which I suspect you simply can't hear. And that is that you can't play a story-game about the Vietnam War specifically that does not acknowledge the fraught socio-political context in which it occurred and expect that story-game to be anything but wishful thinking at best and revanchist propaganda at worst. You can have heroic Green Berets living among the Montagnards and interdicting Commies on the Ho Chi Minh trail in the hopes of creating a better tomorrow, but you can't pretend that the story has a happy ending. The best you can do is lay the blame on the parties you find the most odious.

      An additional point: you note that no one doubted the rightness of WW II, but this is not exactly true. Certainly there are very few people today who will argue against its justice or strategic necessity (but there are indeed some), but during the run-up to World War II, there was a rather strong isolationist sentiment that only Pearl Harbor finally put paid to.

      Thus: today we can tell stories about World War II that ignore pacifist and isolationist sentiment, because there is a general consensus as to the justice of the war. We can't tell the same sorts of stories about Vietnam, because the same sort of consensus doesn't exist.

      \n- The game shall not end up being a story that challenges or deeply questions the validity of the war aims in Vietnam

      \nTo kinda put a capstone on the thread, as the discussion's been played out (didn't even see the thread until a few mins ago), I suggest using a miniatures game, or if you want to address the period with some respect, to actually dig up reports of actual heroics committed at the time (in the lines of "We were all surrounded and cut off, but the young guy stepped up, rallied everyone and we made it to safety" or other such Stars-and-Stripes stories), and either play those out as-is with the system of your choice, or use stories closely based upon those true accounts. Just note: At that point, you're basically playing what is traditionally a WWII story, but with slightly different guns and uniforms and slightly different asians.

      Looking into Vietnam for heroics is... well, it's different. You'll have to therefore go in different directions (or else use Recon, as JD suggested above: I played it in Junior High School with about as much knowledge of the war as any other kid in JHS, it was a solid game I remember). It's like wanting to tell heroic stories about WWII from the Nazi army. "But hey, not stories of people who actually wanted to be in the army to kill gays and jews for Hitler, just conscripted men who believed they were staving off threats to their homes, who performed admirably despite the fucked up situation they were in". If you really want to play Heroic Nazis, asking about how to go about that online will generate bafflement and ire in people, but so be it if that's what you really want (there were some heroic stories that had nothing to do with Hitler in that era, mind, but still...).

      \n A few thoughts on reading this thread:

      Anti-war films sugar up wars so you can feel bad about enjoying the violence. Good ones do, anyway. In other words, I agree with Truffaut in this case.

      There are games that are or could be modified to be value-neutral about war. Ogre Whiteside's game in development, Combat Diaries, is a World War II game about storming Normandy and following the soldiers afterward. Most of the systems are concerned with unit cohesion and friendship and it's definitely a dirty hippy indie game.

      This reminds me of a fascinating critique of the Vietnam (and Iraq II) War; that, lacking an overarching mission they could really cleave to, soldiers fought only for one another. The argument goes on to say that that makes atrocity more likely, because if all you have to fight for is your friend, and your friend dies, there's no reason not to be really vicious with the killers. That would seem to endanger Combat Diaries, if that argument is as true as it feels to me, as a neutral look at war.

      But, finally, war is about killing people. I don't believe anything psychologically interesting or realistic can come out of a game that wants to ignore the damage done to one by the act of killing another human.
