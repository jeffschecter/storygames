\n "The Pentasystem is an open system for playing character- and story-centred roleplaying games or "story-games". In particular, it is designed for playing story-games in which the story is about the main characters' attempts to change the world because of the things they care about, what they are prepared to risk or sacrifice to achieve that, and how the experience changes them."

I've just got my latest draft of the Pentasystem (version 0.8) into a mostly self-consistent state. I think it's baked enough now that I'm ready to show it to others, though it's definitely still work in progress. There may not be much more of this progress for a little while, though, as I'm about to have less time to work on it.

I've uploaded it to Google Docs, chapter by chapter. Some of these contain ideas and techniques that you could easily port into other games (especially the Coolmap and Paths). When it's finished it will be CC-Attribution, so feel free to raid it, with the disclaimer that this is not the final version.

The system is based on rolling between 1 and 5d6 (depending on the character attribute in use), with success numbers determined by the character's rating in the arena in which the conflict takes place (physical, mental etc.). Particular numbers also cause either short-term good consequences and long-term bad consequences or vice versa, and the longer you keep the conflict going, the greater the short-term risk (and long-term reward). I think it's rather clever.

I killed the GM and took his stuff and distributed it among the players. Everyone plays multiple characters, runs sets, frames scenes and owns particular aspects of the game world.

An initial group brainstorm determines what people want to see in the game, and an ingenious budgeting method means that they will get to see that.

Both the group and individual characters have things to strive after and achieve. Player engagement is a major design goal.

Chapter summary in the next post.

    \n Here's what's in each chapter. [BEGIN INFODUMP]

    Chapter 1: Introduction contains:
    * an explanation of what the Pentasystem is,
    * its aims (to model fictionality, not reality),
    * how it works,
    * how roleplaying games in general work (for newbies),
    * how the Pentasystem is different from D&D (for D&D players),
    * influences (Solar System, Dogs, FATE, FLFS, Principia, Verge),
    * acknowledgements,
    * system summary,
    * what you need in order to play, and
    * how narration works.

    Chapter 2: The Coolmap contains:
    * the idea of the coolmap, a diagram of the entities and themes in the setting which might be interesting, and how the players vote for the ones that they actually find interesting and want to see in the game,
    * how this creates a budget for NPCs, sets and so forth so that what people want to see will be what they actually see,
    * how to create a coolmap from scratch,
    * the Coolmap Brainstorm,
    * the modified approval technique (yes, and; yes, but; no, and; no, but, although).

    Chapter 3: Creating Characters contains:
    * the main/supporting/minor character distinction (basically PC/NPC/furniture in trad terms),
    * the five arenas of the Pentasystem (physical, mental, emotional, creative and identity),
    * building a character by allocating points among the arenas (determines the success number on the dice when in conflict in that arena),
    * making a character with a high rating in an arena more interesting through extra dimensions (which give bonuses in some circumstances) - strong, fast or tough for physical, for example,
    * attributes, which are the character's self-identity, and their ratings, which determine the number of dice rolled,
    * playing against type,
    * motivating attributes, which get characters into conflicts and recharge their pools,
    * assigning pools to the three most important arenas for your character and using them for extra oomph in conflict,
    * Influence, which determines how many subordinates a character controls,
    * meshing characters together into an unstoppable story machine and drawing an R-map.

    Chapter 4: Structure of the Story contains:
    * the Freytag five-act structure,
    * how the Exposition scene for each main character establishes their key attributes, pools, arenas and dimensions,
    * how the expository conflict gives them a goal or dilemma to work on,
    * creating an Inciting Incident which means that the characters can't ignore something that happens but must try to change the situation,
    * how to get the characters together in a group, if you want to,
    * the turning point, falling action and resolution and what they look like.

    Chapter 5: Conflicts contains:
    * the conflict process (which is still more complicated than I'd like, and I need to design some really good play aids),
    * choosing and narrating outcomes ("stakes"),
    * how participating in a conflict creates consequences for the characters and what they need to do to deal with those,
    * ways to win an important conflict at the cost of further complications for the character, including putting important things at risk and revealing secrets,
    * how the dice probabilities work.

    Chapter 6: Paths contains:
    * the concept of a path, a structured set of challenges leading to a payoff and further challenges,
    * how to create paths,
    * what classic situations look like as paths.

    Chapter 7: Scenes contains:
    * how to "frame" and structure scenes,
    * the idea of "sequels" in which, after conflict, the characters respond to what's gone down in a way that changes them or sets up for the next conflict.

    Chapter 8: Situational Attributes and Sets contains:
    * how to budget and run a set so that it has interesting challenges for the characters.

    Chapter 9: Special Effects contains:
    * the concept of Special Effects, like stunts or Secrets which enable characters (or sets) to do things outside the core mechanics,
    * some examples.

    [END INFODUMP]
