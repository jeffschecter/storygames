My Incredibly Controversial Definition of RPGs

\n
\n
\n
\n\nLuke_Wheel \n\n\n
\n
\n\nAugust 2009 \n edited August 2009 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n As you may or may not know, I think a lot about roleplaying games -- how they're built and how're they're played. I like to talk about that stuff too. On the internet, I like to write and post about it.
\n
\nRecently, MSM asked me for advice on his game idea that he had posted on the Forge. He posted and contacted me off-list and specifically asked me to respond. Here's the thread. Mike's idea was so broad and non-RPG-like, I felt compelled to present my thoughts on what an RPG is so that we could have a more productive discussion. I wanted to lay out a definition rather than saying, "This isn't an RPG" or "What makes this an RPG?" Clyde taught me that a provocative statement is better way to provoke a thoughtful answer.
\n
\n
My definition (of a boombastic jazz style) wrote:
\n
"[An RPG is] A game in which a player advocates the goals, priorities and survival (or doom) of a persona who, in operation of the game's mechanics, is confronted with one or more ethical choices."
\n
\n
\nI expected the definition to catch flak. I said a naughty word in my definition -- "ethical." Gasp! Shock! It got a predictable kneejerk reaction and I got to curse at Cuntracycle. Cool.
\n
\nBut it got me to thinking. I cannot come up with a reasonable roleplaying game that does not have at least one ethical question hardcoded into the game system/mechanics.
\n
\nMy definition of an ethical question: The player will be asked to choose between right and wrong, good and evil, the good of the individual or the good of the many, self vs group in the course of play (via operation of the game as written), etc. A bit of normative ethics and a smattering of applied ethics.
\n
\nFor examples:
\nD&D;: Good. Evil. Duh.
\n
\nShadowrun: You are Robin Hood sticking it to the man until he sticks you (and then you stick him back). Shadowrun even had racism in the game!
\n
\nRIFTS: Uh, in the core rules you can either be a mecha-Nazi or you can fight the mecha-Nazis. Hmm... And if that's not enough, RIFTS has alignments on page 14 -- principled, scrupulous, to miscreant, abberant and diabolic evil.
\n
\nVampire: You rape and kill innocent and powerless human beings while satisfying your carnal hungers and lust for power.
\n
\nGURPS: On page 10 of GURPS LITE 4e under disadvantages one finds a Bad Temper and a Code of Honor. I wonder if those would lead to ethical decisions in a game.
\n
\nI'm not talking about "narrativist" or "gamist" or "simulationist" games. I don't even know what those terms mean. (My examples are conservative and traditional!) I'm talking about a fundamental quality that, to me, differentiates an RPG from a board game (or card game). You can have ethical boardgames, but for the most part, you're just pushing chits around trying to win, managing resources, staving off/accelerating the end game, etc. In an RPG, you're operating a persona -- an avatar on which we hang quite a bit of emotion. This condition lends itself nicely to associating or sympathizing with the situation of play and bringing our values to play to make a decision.
\n
\nI'm not saying that every decision in every RPG is an ethical one, but I do think that the core of RPGs involves an important decision by the player in which he weighs his value system against his options. And I'm not saying that players can't and don't behave like sociopaths in RPGs. Sociopathic behavior is just one possible answer to the questions presented.
\n
\nSo my question to Story Games isn't really if you agree with me or not. I'm right. If you think I'm wrong, post an RPG and why there are no ethical decisions part of playing that game.
\n
\nAm I overlooking obvious games that fall outside the mode? I know there are edge cases out there, but am I blind to a world of RPGs that involve rote, valueless choices?*
\n
\n-Luke

      Posted By: Tristan
      And I ask you, Brand, to share a more accurate and semantically correct definition of Ethics.
      \nJesus, now I'm all on the spot.

      Okay, I think the best way I've heard someone put it was an old professor of mine who basically said "Ethics is what happens when you try to answer the joint questions: Who are we and how ought we to live?"

      Now, that leaves a lot of grey areas, like the tactics based on moral stances issues that John and Ralph are going on which may or may not be ethical. (I think that many tactical plays have an ethical element to them. The rhetoric of competition, for example, is a very ethical in its character -- whether or not someone is a better person because they can defeat another in equal competition, if the competition was equal, etc.) However, I find it a pretty good functional definition for a broad-scope discussion of the issues.

      So, when you're in a relationship what does it mean about you? Does the person you're with doing something right or wrong reflect on you? How do you chose your partner (if you ever are going to chose one partner) to be best for you? Or should you chose based on what's best for you or what's best for the family? The community? Should you marry for love? For a nice rack? These are all questions about who you want to be and how you want to live.

      Another good definition for this discussion might be Aristotle's "self realization." For old Ari ethics was a matter of coming to know yourself so that you could act in accordance with your true nature. Someone who did that would do good and be happy. Anyone who did not (and most did not) would not be. Thus the ethical inquiry was a search for who you are, how you relate to the world, and what you should do about it. Or, character creation and exploration, conflict and conflict resolution, and the part at the end of a campaign where we all go "Wow, Superman really is a jerk, isn't he?"

      So when I read Luke say "Ethical choices" I read "Make decisions about who the character is and how he should live and how that affects/effects the world he lives in."

      \nPersona is key to the definition.
      \n
      \nWhen I play poker, I can choose to make suboptimal as well as optimal decisions. I can make decisions that affect my strategy across multiple games. I can make decisions that affect other players both strategically and socially. However, in the magic circle of poker, I'm not making any ethical decisions.* I'm playing a game to the best of my ability to win. When I play a hand in poker, I'm not doing so in service of a fiction being created among all of the players. If I make a sub-optimal play to hide my strength for later, I'm not doing it because my persona is a weak king, sacrificing his subject's well being now so that they will have the chance to rise up later.
      \n
      \nDiplomacy is an excellent example of a game tangled in ethical decisions. There are numerous ways to play Diplomacy, and being ethical is only one of them. But Diplomacy play isn't about a narrative. There's no persona (supported by the game in anyway). There's only you the player and your strategy to win.
      \n
      \nThis may seem a subtle or useless difference to some, but to me this is a chasm dividing RPGs from other games. We use personas to create fiction so that we can make (or ignore) these curious ethical decisions in a safe place and then toy with the results in an imaginative sandbox. When the game's over, we can be disturbed by the choices or pleased with them. And we get to leave them at the table.
      \n
      \nVery cool.

      \n-L
      \n
      \n*Poker games that involve cosmic weight on the fate of the game or the winner throwing the game because of some moral obligation are the stuff for movies. They are beyond the immediate experience of playing the game.

      Posted By: Luke Wheel"[An RPG is] A game in which a player advocates the goals, priorities and survival (or doom) of a persona who, in operation of the game's mechanics, is confronted with one or more ethical choices."
      \nAt the risk of entering this thread very, very late...

      This sounds like a good encapsulation of the Burning* RPGs. I think it's probably broad enough to be arguably applied to most games, although it depends on what meaning is inferred by several words.

      "A game in which a player advocates the goals, priorities and survival (or doom) of a persona" could be read as eliminating any game where a player advocates for more than a single persona, by necessity of the article a indicating singular, but seeing as how this would eliminate any game with a GM, such as the Burning games, I cannot believe that it was intended as such.

      The phrase "survival (or doom)" is weak sauce. I think what you're saying here is that the player is advocating for survival unless it runs counter to any goals or priorities the persona puts above its own survival, meaning that survival is just a particular example of a priority. This definition ignores story-telling games where the player might suddenly decide to willfully ignore the goals and priorities of the character in favor of fulfilling an emergent narrative for the delight of the players/audience. If you're comfortable with making that kind of division, then great. If you're not, you need to change the definition.

      "In operation of the game's mechanics, is confronted with one or more ethical choices" hangs on the meaning of "confronted" for me. If the players of the game go around killing indiscriminately without a thought, are they really "confronting" ethical choices? If the lexicon of the game's mechanics do not equally favor "good" choices and "evil" choices within the magic circle of the game, is that tacitly removing ethics from the equation? Put another way, if a game gives you a sword that takes away HP, and confronts you with pinatas made of HP and full of Reward Tokens, is it really a confrontation with an ethical choice, since there are no other mechanics for confronting HP pinatas?

      Even if the magic circle does not mechanically remove the ethical choice, if the players do not recognize the ethical choice at the time, can they be said to have made one? If players see the ethical problem themselves, but willfully play characters who don't see it, is that confronting an ethical problem, or not? I think Gregor Hutton's 3:16 advocates this sort of scenario while players are in the lower ranks. Who, in your definition, is confronting the ethical choice, the player or the character? My guess is that this only refers to the character, because the definition says the ethical choices must be mechanically supported, and only the character's actions are bound by the mechanics of the game. The player is ultimately always aware that the character's world is an imagined fiction, and thus no true consequences result from actions, rendering it ethically void outside the magic circle.

      What this definition says to me is, "RPGs should make players advocate for the goals and priorities a persona, and, by way of the game's mechanics, make ethical choices." This is a great statement, because it raises all sorts of good questions for designers to think about.

      I still maintain that the definition could use some tuning but I agree with it.

      I think once you have a\xa0persona, that implies a Character within a context. That context includes place and time (Setting and Situation). Once you engage the character "in operation of the game's mechanics" (i.e., via System and Color), you have to make choices for ("advocate") that character. You're not making choices in a vacuum or outside a context or even outside an ethical context because of the aforementioned stuff. Those choices necessarily engage ethics, because they're choices about a fictional character's life.

      I had a paragraph here saying that bringing ethics into things just confused matters, but then I wrote the paragraph below about Toon. Thinking about an RPG not as an opportunity for ethical choice but as a persona's context that implies a\xa0fictional ethical system\xa0jives with Luke's definition and offers a new way of looking at game design and play--which is, I think, what Luke was getting at all along.

      Toon holds up as an RPG under this definition. You have a persona in a situation and you're making choices for that persona within that context. An ethical system is necessary for decision-making. What does Bugs do? It depends on Bugs' ethical system.

      Monopoly and Donkey Kong fall down as RPGs because you don't really imagine yourself as the token (as the thimble or car, or as Mario). You're not making decisions as if you were the thimble or Mario. You're playing in pawn stance. There's a "character" but it's not a persona ("mask") so you're not empathizing with the situation of that character.

      World of Warcraft, on the other hand,\xa0can be\xa0a RPG if a player engages her character through a mask (with empathy). If one plays in pawn stance, however, it's not really an RPG, right? But I spent a fair amount of time playing a WoW paladin who refused to kill small, helpless animals or even help other characters who did so. I refused certain quests because "my paladin" (I) found them abhorrent. That's role-playing. (Even on a non-RP server.)

      \n I scanned the thread, but I won't pretend to have read everything everyone's said.

      "Ethics"
      Luke, strictly speaking, I don't think anyone can argue with what you've *said*. HOWEVER, your definition *implies* something about play about which simply isn't true.

      Since RPGs take a narrative format, pretty much by definition play is going to involve ethical situations (it is almost a tautology, as someone said earlier). However, your definition implies that these ethical situations are the *point/focus* of play. That's simply not true across the spectrum of play styles... and you know it.

      Your definition has a Narrativist/Story-Now/thematic-play bias, where ethical/moral/thematic issues ARE the focus of play. But when it comes to Gamist or Simulationist play, ethics aren't the point. Sure, ethical decisions get made, but they are more of a byproduct than something the players are actively engaging.

      "Persona"
      This part I very much agree with, though I would rephrase it. Rather than say "persona"---which implies a character---I would say that players pursue a particular "story/game interest". What I mean is, instead of thinking of Bob playing Barku the Barbarian, Bob is playing the role of the protagonist.

      I feel explaining it that way makes it easier to understand the role of the GM, who obviously has an agenda (antagonism/opposition), but is controlling varying and divergent characters, as well as other narrative elements like the environment. I feel my explanation also explains certain phenomenon, like why players enjoy hosing their own character, which would seem to run counter to your original definition. (Protagonism requires antagonism/opposition to flourish, thus the player actually has an interest in the type and intensity of antagonism/opposition that the character experiences.)

      I admit that "pursuing a particular interest" is just a play technique, and as such, it's possible to design a game that doesn't require it. However, after several years of thinking about all this stuff and playing experimental games, I have come to view this "playing a (story/game) role" thing as a defining element of the "RPG" genre. Like I said, you can a make story game without it, but without it the game will feel like something different.
