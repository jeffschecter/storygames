    \n Whenever I play the Roach with new people, there always seems to be someone who thinks the game is supposed to be played more seriously than it is. So I always quote Jason from page 39:

    "Kill them. Kill them all. Cleanse Pemberton with fire. It makes the game so much better when campus luminaries are being stacked up like cordwood."

    Another good piece of advice is on page 25:

    "Don't fixate on victory, fixate on crafting Scenes that will be outrageous, memorable, and fun to play."

    \n I agree fully with the second statement.

    My problem with the first statement is that, that only works if you build to it. This is actually mostly a problem with *second* time players. What happens is everyone remembers how crazy insane everything got at the end of the last game and then immediately jump to that same level of craziness in the opening moves of the second game and then things kind of die because there's no where to go.

    So while yes, in the long run don't shy away from doom and destruction but also don't burn down Pemberton at the Convocation.

    \n I like Jesse's last post. It's a balance: don't hold yourself back from describing dramatic stuff; but, also, remember that there's fun in the low-key stuff. One of our best conflicts, in the Roach, was between a history professor and a physics professor. The history professor spotted a missing minus sign in the physicist's working.

    The game, pretty much, runs itself. But since it's GM-less, don't hold back from:

    * Entering other people's scenes at a comically bad moment.
    * Providing adversity to other players.
    * Letting your narration go wild. Don't just narrate what your character does: narrate buildings falling down and giant spider machines.

    The Roach does require creativity. We've found it can flag towards the end. Don't be afraid to take a break halfway through, recharge your creative batteries, talk about other things, then go back to it.

    Oh...I...disagree with most of what Jesse says on his blog. For me, the humour is in the characters being unlovable grotesques from the start; and from Roached characters just being extensions of the unRoached personality. After all, they wanted power before. Now they're just prepared to kill for it.

    Graham

    \n I haven't been much on SG lately, so I missed this thread. Hey, M\xe5ns! Here's my advice:

    Make sure that everyone knows that the connections set up in the beginning (you like the one to your left and hate the one to your right ... or was it the other way around?) are temporary. The players are free to have their characters change their minds in the first scene. I've had players fail to get this, so point it out.

    Also, in your first scene, think of a way to really hurt (preferrably professionally or emotionally, rather than physically, this early in the game) the character you hate, and go for it. Once you've framed that conflict, it's a wonderful slippery slope to cutting off body parts and burning down buildings. But it's important to, well, get to the conflicts in this game, I think.

    The Roach is the favourite indie game in my (slightly conservative) play group. I'm sure you'll have a blast.

    \n Yes, you need someone to hate. But you don't really need someone you like.

    The thing that will happen, that will fuck up your game, is that you'll have really weird, abusive relationships. Since the one you like always hates you, he'll be very mean to you. Like, burn down your house and tell the faculty senate you molest the students mean. And if you keep insisting you like him, well, it might be funny, but it's more a round-robin My Life with Master than the roach. For the Roach, you'll want two people really hating each other. In the startup situation, that's not the case. If A hates B, that automatically means B likes A. And you won't get a war on campus, you'll get S/M.

    some people will team up because of what happens in the fiction, but then they both like have uses for each other. Don't worry; people rarely start liking the one they hated from the start, but they will start hating the ones they liked.

And I play these games to have interesting characters and see how they change based on the decisions they make and the experiences they live through.
