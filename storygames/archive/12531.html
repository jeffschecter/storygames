    Posted By: McdaldnoWould you donate some time and materials to crafting simple trophies or medallions or some other trinket for the winner?
    \nPrecious metal prices have rarely been higher than at the moment, and I am unemployed. Also, I'm not that great a jeweller. My best work uses rapid prototyping methods, and they are prohibitively expensive. We're talking about $100 just to get started. I tried making a trophy not long ago in fact, and they wanted about $300 for just the casting. In brass. A more manageable silver medallion could be produced at between $15 and $20 apiece if I hand-carved it in wax, but I barely have that much money.

    In my present conditions, the best I could manage would be a decorative wooden bowl, or an extremely simple medal / medallion or two. I doubt I could run to seven of them.

    [EDIT] It's not that I'm unwilling to give my time, it's just I don't have any money and I wouldn't feel good about offering a sub-standard trophy.

    \n First of all, that part of the FAQ is from over six years ago and I should update it.

    Basically, part of Ken's comments are off-base. Being traditional and/or being a supplement for rulesets you didn't design do not at all disqualify an entry from the awards. For example, Thou Art But a Warrior is also for someone else's ruleset.

    The licensing part is more tricky. Notably, there were some complaints regarding Mouse Guard winning last year. Both Mark Smylie's Archaia Press and Luke Crane had won Indie RPG awards in the past with no complaints, but some people felt that the collaboration meant that the result was no longer independent. As a result, for this year, I left it up to the voters rather than trying to make my own judgments for each entry. I put instructions with the voting that if any voter had an issue with an entry, they should speak up before the results were announced.

    I think I will revise the FAQ to no longer be absolute. The problem is this - Most RPG small press and independent press basically consist of one author helped by their friends and/or family. What I really would not want is to absolutely disqualify collaborations - where one author publishes with the help of another. For example, the RPGirl Zine was also a collaboration of authors, and prospective authors had to make submissions which were judged for inclusion.

    As for The Day After Ragnarok... Ken Hite has self-published before, and has been on the Indie RPG Award voter list since before I took over the awards. Pinnacle as far as I know is the design house of author Shane Lacy Hensley. The question is whether this should be considered a collaboration or a non-independent work-for-hire. I'm not sure how I'll do it in the future, but for this year, we decided by none of the voters objecting to its indie-ness.

    \n Fair enough, Moreno. The FAQ should definitely be updated.

    The thing is that there are many RPG books that are clearly non-independent. An amateur author submits a work on spec, or a professional author is paid up front - and their copyright is then owned by the company they submitted to, who edit and publish it as they see fit.

    Conversely, there are authors who completely make and sell their product on their own.

    Even though there is a huge grey area between these two, I think the distinction is still very important - and gets at the vitally important principle of creator control. The problem is that creator control isn't something that is obvious just from the packaging of a product, and I don't want to have to do investigative work on every entry to the awards to find out the contract they were written under.
