    \n Well, to be obscure and circular "A Braunstein" is a game in the style of Braunstien, which was an early, multiplayer, refereed minis game with players being allowed to go outside of simple shoot-em-up style minis play with their character actions. Dave Arneson played in Braunstein (iirc) and another game set in a Banana Republic run by a guy named Mjr Weseley(sp? and no, I never remember the guy's proper first name).

    Anyway, the original designer/ref ended up being called off for service, and Dave picked up the idea and started running a fantasy version which (again, iirc) became Blackmoor, and eventually evolved into D&D. As I was very young at the time, I wasn't there for any of this, and have picked up the story many persons removed.

    Other folks will have nifty links about th game, and a couple of folks got to play in a replay of the game last year or so at a convention.

    So, basically, I'd like to see what folks around this joint would come up with to do something similar, especially being familiar with all sorts of dirty-hippy mechanics and techniques for gaming.

    \n Off-thread, someone mentioned an aspect that I hadn't realized I hadn't mentioned about B-Steins, so I'll post it here:

    Generally, every player in the game is going to have their own character or faction goals ( probably several).

    The goals are very likely at cross-purposes, making alliances and enemies not-completely-obvious from step one.

    Assuming there is a GM, their role is to basically be a neutral referee, more than anything else once the game kicks off.


    Naturally, any of this could be tweaked in different directions over the course of an individual design, but I'd think of those as baseline assumptions.

    Edit:

    One other quick thought: In these early, multi-player, competitive games, role-playing is a tactic.

    Not sure how you can work that in, but something to consider. Dave Arneson, in the Banana Republic game was supposedly especially good at using role-playing as a tactic leading to his eventual winning of the game.

    \n Here's an initial draft of what i'm looking at for Saturday. I'd like opinions/feedback:
    What the heck is a \u201cBraunstein\u201d?

    Braunstein was a one-off, multi-player, refereed miniatures game designed by Dave (\u201cMajor\u201d) Wesely waaay back in the day (1967). The setting was a fictional town named Braunstein during the Napoleonic Wars. Wesely later went on to create and present similar games, including one set in a fictional Central America country (\u201cThe Banana Republic game\u201d). Significantly, one of the participants in these games was Dave Arneson, the creator of the Blackmoor Campaign and co-creator (with Gary Gygax) of Dungeons & Dragons. In a very real way, Arneson\u2019s experiences with \u201cBraunsteins\u201d (we\u2019ve simply given the style of game the name of the original) led to the creation of D&D and later role-playing games.

    Style elements of Braunsteins

    While I\u2019d hardly call myself a scholar on the subject, a few things strike me as being significant about Braunsteins. Participants in the contest should consider which of these elements they\u2019ll use (or modify) in their own projects.

    Multiple players
    Braunstein was designed as a multi-player game, with each player taking on the role of an individual character or faction leader. Each character or faction would have their own goals, and even apparent allies might end up at cross-purposes, while apparent enemies might find some common ground to work together on.

    Neutral Referee
    The designer of the scenario is also likely to be the referee. Neutrality is important in this regard. Further, the referee is expected to make up rules or methods to cover situations not inherent in the rules, as the players are empowered to attempt any sort of actions their characters or factions should be able to attempt within the context of the scenario. It\u2019s also worth considering exactly what responsibilities and capabilities you wish the referee to have within the game. Off-hand, outside of possibly some pre-planned events, the referee really won\u2019t be engaging in the central creative role, fiction-wise, as a GM in more traditional role-playing games. The referee here is more of a facilitator and mediator than a truly active participant.

    One-Shot
    A Braunstein is generally conceived as a one-shot game, to be played in an environment not unlike a gaming convention or a single evening meet-up at a club or game-shop. To that end, the designer will be working under time constraints, both for total play-time and for teaching time for players. On the other hand, unlike a game meant to be printed up and distributed, the designer can be a good bit looser with the write ups, perhaps getting away with mere notes for himself, and cheat sheets for the players.

    Scenario-based with pre-generated characters and pre-loaded conflicts
    In general, players will interpret the characters they receive, rather than create their own from scratch. Likewise, characters should start within a web of conflicts. By design, this should put the point of the action squarely on the player-characters, rather than outside events or GM characters.

    The table, maps, minis, and other physical stuff
    Braunsteins were originally made to be used with miniatures and/or maps, necessitating the use of a table. At the same time, the nature of the conflicts in a multiplayer game almost inherently suggests the need for players to separate and go conspire throughout the course of play. Designers should consider this and take it into account in their project.
    I would recommend, at minimum, looking at the classic game, Diplomacy, to see how that game works with a similar game-play situation.

    Strictly speaking, the use of minis and maps is likely a cultural artifact due to the original players being minis gamers. Participants in this contest, however, should consider what possible benefits they could derive from having a table set-up, beyond just a nod to tradition. At minimum, it provides a physical central meeting point.

    Likewise, while the original Braunstein was a sort of modified minis wargame, there is no particular reason to restrict conflicts to a merely single physical conflict occurring continuously over a short period of time, the way one finds in minis wargames. Time can be broken up by \u201cscenes\u201d or similar pacing devices, for example, and the draw of the early Braunstein and Banana Republic games seems to have ended up centering on character actions outside of direct battlefield domination.

    Setting
    Someone noted that the original Braunstein was a historical setting, and felt that this was an important element of the design of similar games. I personally tend to feel that this, as with the use of historical minis as a core element, is mostly an indication of the gaming roots of the designers and players. Having said that, I would suggest that an easily -grasped situation and setting are almost a must, given the one-shot nature, the multi-player competition, and time-constraints of a Braunstein.

    Going of the tracks, coloring outside the lines, and role-playing as a tactic
    In these predecessors to RPGs, role-playing appears to have been a tactic to be used in an attempt to win the game. By that, I mean that characterization (as well as attempting character actions not covered by any core rules) was seen as a means of outwitting the other players, or at least getting them to be more compliant with your own schemes. How a participant in this contest works that into their design is up to them. Dave Arneson was apparently quite the character (no pun intended) in the banana republic game, leading to him being declared the winner. A large part of that success was apparently due to going almost entirely outside of the rules-structure to accomplish his goals.

    Winners and losers
    Braunstein and its successors were meant to be played competitively, and that competition was meant to be between the players, not between the referee and the players. Designers should consider how they will decide the winner or winners of their game.

    Goal: each participant will be responsible for presenting an idea for a Braunstein scenario, along with title, and developing the game to the point it is playable. Refinement, in terms of layout, artwork, and presentation is appreciated, but not a primary requirement. After all, Dave Arneson was apparently known for bringing early drafts of the D&D rules to sessions scribbled on whatever scrap paper was near at hand.

    Current working time is estimated at two weeks.

    Team design: While each participant is considered to be the primary author for purposes of determining a winner, participants are allowed to recruit any additional aid from anyone they choose, including other participants working on their own scenarios. Teamwork, and a general workshop approach, is encouraged.

    Judging a winner:
    Winner will be determined by secret vote, held one week after the deadline for presenting projects. Voting will be restricted to participants, including those recruited to aid in design (so put them in the credits) even if they did not head up a design. Designers may not vote for their own design. Participants who did not lead a design effort may not vote for a design they worked on. Voting will last for 48 hours, then tallied and announced.

    Exact deadlines to be announced when the official contest thread is posted.

    What to judge a design on:
    How close does the design stick to general elements of Braunsteins?
    Has the author consciously addressed elements in a new or novel fashion?
    Has the author provided enough information for someone other than themselves to run this game, provided any physically required components were present?

    Provided that enough projects are presented in a relatively complete form, I suggest we follow-up with actual play-tests soon after the end of the contest. I personally would score a design higher if the author and team are able to get a play-test and refinement in before the end of the contest.

    Thoughts? Anything I could refine or clarify? (especially appreciated if you've run a contest or participated in obefore).

    \n So after a few too many drinks last night I tossed this email off to Bob about the Braunstein thing. I've edited it slightly so as not to offend the delicate ears of SG readership:

    My thinking:

    One way to go is to just program out a really complicated "situation" in the style of the original Braunstein. Here's a bunch of characters, here's your backgrounds, here's your to-do items, now go. It appears the players have simply no rules AT ALL, they just start saying what they want to do. The ref then times out arrivals and movement according to what's cool and what puts people in maximum contact for maximum fireworks. I don't see a lot of guidance for the ref -- and why would there be? The ref knows "how" to run his own game! This one seems tough, insofar as you're basically setting up an unresolved opening to a novel, or perhaps just running a CENSORED Jeepform game at that point. CENSOR that noise. CENSORing Jeeps. :-(

    Another way to go is to totally deconstruct and reconstruct the Braunstein experience, using modern design thinking to hand a lot of mechanical control over to the players while maintaining the cool shit that comes from being the ref (a creative director holding everything together, impartial judgement). I think you could build some mechanically interesting ways to either mandate impartiality, or to give the GM a set of motives that are perpendicular to the players' efforts. That's something I was thinking about today: The players have their to-do list and a list of assets and liabilities (belong to X faction, friends to Y, enemies of Z) and they're earning VPs, maybe, for working their way through their hit list. But then the GM is also earning VPs, but for totally different stuff -- each time a player uses an asset, each time a player achieves a goal, and so on. So the GM's motives for ruling one way or another aren't related to the players' motives. This might also introduce some replayability, in that people could "compete" for the most reffing VPs.

    I'm not feeling a strong sense of how to profitably leverage the natural social tension between players who all want what they want, and the effort of the ref to remain truly impartial. Like in sports, the ref needs to remain above the players' agenda. I'm not sure it's fun or healthy to try and dick with that; it turns into D&D Diplomacy at that point, right?

    A third way I was thinking about, which I hinted at earlier, was a meta-Braunstein engine. Like...you'd have Oracle-like lists of place, factions, and goals. The players would get a budget with which to buy those things, with a minimum of one each (so you could have more places, or goals, or whatever). Goals earn you VPs but places and factions earn you in-game benefits. And maybe everyone blind-draws a name from a hat, and that name (another player) is now "your enemy." So you get some benefit when working against them. I dunno. Just kind of riffing here.

    I was noodling a bit on ref-less Braunstein and I just don't know how to get there while maintaining the cool "ref handles all the details" vibe I'm getting from the stuff I'm reading.

    And that's as far as the beers + shot is going to let me think before sending me off into bullshit-land.

      Fast'n'loose map use
      My impression of CE's map use is that he pretty much let's the players move their pieces ( characters, troops, whatever) anywhere on the map they wish, unless there is some sort of in-fiction reason they shouldn't be able to easily do so.

      I personally would even go a bit more story-game-y with that idea, and say that a player could move their stuff around freely unless there was both an in-fiction reason and another player ( including perhaps the ref) who was willing to contest the thing with some sort of dice roll-off that involved an alternative outcome/stakes. (Right now, the thing I'm working on has dice as a depleting resource when used in a roll-off, so it might matter even more in that design).

      So, for example in Jon's game, there might be merchants moving into an area locked down by government troops. It's pretty easy to see how there could be an obstacle to movement. Still, it would require some other player to be willing to actually make an issue out of it and go to a roll-off for that to impede such movement, and the roll-off wouldn't necessarily be a simple block. There would need to be some sort of alternate outcome involved, with some sort of confrontation ( go to minis combat?) or other event described by an opposing player to bother.

      I'd still encourage a bit of role-playing/free description in the case, however. Merchants sneaking in in disguise might suggest something other as an outcome than merchants fast-talking their way past a checkpoint. Either adds to the fiction, but later complications might be different.

      Combat and Role-playing:
      Jon, my impression is that the role-playing in the original B-steins was at least partly a reaction to general lack of rules and partly due to the deadliness of combat. So, role-playing in that context ( especially with a roughly sketched pregen character) is only partly about development of the character in a "deep" sense. It may well grate on some sensibilities, but it strikes me that "playing one's character" in a way that engages the other players positively is also a survival tactic in-game.

      So, if I play up a rotten, villainous character, but the other players are "into it", they may well choose to let the character live, at least for a while, because of the amusement of the actual, real people involved.

      The other thing that might encourage role-playing (how ever it's defined) is the shortness of a single event.

      Without the freedom of an open-ended campaign to allow, slow, thoughtful character development, players are more likely (IMO) to grab onto something recognizable, maybe even stereotypical/archetypical, and run with that for characterization. They may well also go in a direction they wouldn't in a campaign set-up, just because they can explore options a bit, knowing that they have no commitment to the character or situation beyond this single game session.

      I got a bit more done on Maladicion.
      Los Ochos Diablos Rojos de Juarez y La Maladicion de Drakula core mechanics

      Free-Play and rules you won\u2019t find in this game
      This game is based around the concept of free-play; Players simply describe what is happening, speak as their character, and move pieces around as appropriate, based upon the info you\u2019ve been given and any in-game precedent that develops during play. When you disagree with another player, you can call for a contest. This means you won\u2019t find character stats, initiative rules, combat results, movement rates, or, frankly, lots of stuff you\u2019re probably used to seeing in games.

      Contests

      A contest occurs whenever two players disagree on described events, and simple negotiation does not resolve the difference. A player does not need to have a character directly involved in a turn to initiate a contest. To initiate a contest a player simply announces their wish to do so.

      The call for a contest must be made in a reasonable amount of time. The referee is authorized to deny a call for a contest if a player is too slow in acting. Generally, the contest should be initiated immediately or at most a few seconds, after events are described.

      The player must describe an alternate outcome. Simple blocking of an event is not acceptable grounds for a contest.

      The player who has had their description challenged may relent, and modify their description of events using parts of the alternative outcome.

      If the player does not relent, or the contesting player does not find the modified events acceptable, a contest occurs. The referee must step in to act as a facilitator, and clarify the terms of the contest. The referee may announce either player\u2019s outcome disproportionate, and demand a different and more equitable outcome.

      A contest only ever involves two players. The referee is given full discretion to break down multi-player disagreements into two-player contests.

      Each player rolls one their dice.

      The high roll wins, and their outcome is added successfully to the fictional events. The winner turns their die over to the referee, while loser retains their die.

      The special quality of d4s
      D4s are the only dice that can be added to the result of another die. During a contest, either player may roll a single d4 and add the result to their previous roll. A d4 may be rolled and added after the results of the initial contest roll are seen. If a player adds a d4 roll and wins the contest, they must surrender both dice to the referee.

      Other Contests
      He same mechanics described above can be used for essentially any other purpose during the game when two players disagree.

      \nDice in the Game
      Dice in the game are a purely meta-game mechanic. They represent nothing more than the individual player\u2019s interest in having their preferred outcome occur during contests. They are also used to determine the winner of the game after the final turn has been played.

      Dice are a scarce and depleting resource. This is an intentional part of the game design. Each player begins the game with 12 dice (4 each d6s, d10s, and d12s), while the referee has 24 (8 each d4s, d8s, and d20s) in his bowl. There are 24 turns to a game, and players can individually each roll up to four of their remaining dice during the determination of the Game Winner after the last turn of the game. Needless to say, putting some thought into the dice economy is key element of play.

      Losing Dice
      Whenever a player rolls a die in a contest and wins, they must surrender that die to the referee\u2019s bowl of dice. Players keep the die if they lose the contest.

      If the referee ever has to roll a die for any reason, that die is immediately and permanently removed from the game, once its result is determined.

      Gaining Dice
      Players may gain more dice by achieving objectives, by meeting any special Gain Dice conditions on their faction sheet, or by role-playing villager(s) in a turn where none of their own characters are present. Some objectives can only be determined after the final turn of the game has been played. Players may always attempt to gain an objective die, although the referee has the final say if there is any question regarding whether the objective was attained. Players may hold off claiming an objective die until later in the game if they wish.

      When a player gains a die, they get to choose any die from the referee\u2019s bowl.

      There are no rules governing players loaning any dice between one another, and players are never required to reveal the remaining number or types of dice in their cup. The referee\u2019s bowl is left openly on the table and can be examined by any player at any time.

      If a player has no dice
      If a player is ever completely out of dice and can prove this to the referee, the referee may loan a die to the player from the referee bowl. The referee chooses the die based on whatever criteria they choose, keeping in mind their neutral role in the game. The die is immediately removed from the game after the roll, just as if the referee had rolled it himself. The referee may choose to deny a request for a die.

      Turns
      The game takes place over three days: Friday, Saturday, and Sunday. Each day consists of 8 turns. After 8 turns, the day is over and the story picks up again at dawn of the following day. At the beginning of each day, the referee shuffles and deals out turn cards. Each player receives two turn cards, face down. The turn cards are marked 1-8. Red cards are turns that take place during daylight, black cards at night. Starting from the ace, the player holding the appropriate card flips the card up and starts their turn. Players may not skip a turn.

      There are no rules governing trading turn cards, nor are there rules governing players voluntarily revealing turn cards that they have been dealt.

      No turn can last more than ten minutes of real time. The referee is required keep watch on time, and gives an appropriate warning if the limit is close to being reached. After ten minutes of play time, the referee is required to end the turn.


      Playing a turn
      Each turn is roughly like a key scene in a film or book. When a player has a turn they should provide enough information to get the turn started. That info will include:

      Location
      Simply drawing an imaginary line around the boundaries of an area of the board with your index finger is adequate. Key locations tend to be \u201cset\u201d, but space between key locations is \u201cmutable\u201d. This means a player can pick and use spare terrain bits to create further locations as they wish. The space covered can include the entire board.

      Characters
      The player must always include at least one of their own characters, and they may freely include characters belonging to other players or villager characters.

      Timing and Continuity
      If it takes place at the same time as the previous turn, none of the characters present in that turn may participate in the new turn. The turn may be a direct extension of the previous turn.

      Situation
      The player should announce both general events taking place and give some sort of active goal for at least one of their own characters. They may describe the goals of characters not their own, but this may result in a contest if the owning player disagrees.


      When the player is satisfied with the set up, they announce that the turn has started. This initiates a period of free-play. Play continues until the player who started the turn decides to end the turn, or after ten minutes have elapsed. Any contests that occur during the turn also count against the time limit.

      \nWinning the game
      There are two categories of winners in the game: The game winner and the Players\u2019 Choice Award.

      The game winner is determined as follows:
      Each player in the game with any surviving characters on the table picks any four dice from their cup, rolls them openly and totals the results. The player with the highest total is declared the winner of the game.

      The Players\u2019 Choice Award
      Each player picks one other participant and votes for them as the player who added the most enjoyment to the game. They should be judged on role-playing, sportsmanship, competition, and creativity. The vote is secret, and the ref tallies the votes.

      If either contest results in a tie, the referee acts as an instant tie-breaker.

      General hints on winning the game
      Dice Control
      Dice are a depleting resource in this game. Using higher value dice or adding d4s to rolls will allow you to achieve your individual objectives more easily, and thus regain dice.

      Pacing Control
      Controlling the pacing of the turns will encourage faster or slower use of dice. It may be to your advantage to end one of your turns quickly or suggest controversial events, depending on whether you wish to retain your dice or deplete those of the referee or other players. If you set up short, easily resolved turns, you will also set yourself up for the ability to gain dice by role-playing villagers. If you set up longer turns, you are more likely to draw out dice from the other players or the referee.

      Outcome stakes
      You may choose to relent or even take losses as a result of your particular faction qualities. The other players will not know those details, so use it to best effect.

      The subjective win
      The Players\u2019 Choice Award is entirely subjective, so play up characterization and reasonableness of outcomes in contests. Don\u2019t be afraid to \u201cgo off the rails\u201d. A real coup is pulling off a win in both categories, especially with a referee tie-breaker. Remember that players vote for the Players\u2019 Choice Award prior to rolling for game winner.

      Get big dice
      The more large dice you have, the better your score is likely to be at the end of the game. Make sure to balance your dice in cup versus your need to win individual contests.

      The Game is not balanced
      Some factions get more dice in-game, some get more after the game ends. Play to your faction.

      Manipulate the referee and other players
      It\u2019s dirty and nasty, but a good chunk of this game is about how the other real world human players react to your play-style. There are tons of places where the reaction of the other players will matter, and not just in the Players\u2019 Choice Award. So go on and do it!

      Be a trouble-maker
      Feel free to off-handedly mention any number of difficulty inducing possibilities, whether you have characters involved or not. Other players love material to work with, and so does the referee.

      Pull them in
      Whether it is your turn or not, pull in any of the other players or the referee if it is to your benefit. If they have a character involved in events, they\u2019re less likely to be conspiring with another player against you away from the table.

      Know when to take the lead in the fiction
      People tend to accept precedent and let that affect their further judgments. There is very little defined in the game, so feel free to embroider whole cloth on ideas you have, recognizing that it is harder to undo something than to establish it. This game is a cross between horror movies, westerns, and low-budget Mexican b-flicks, so run with it.

      You\u2019re only playing one time for four hours!
      Don\u2019t do a slow burn, Jump straight into things and do it big. Ham up the acting and accents. Make the events described big!

      Jon: I'm looking at an add-on "bullet-time" sub-system for Maladicion. I'm not sure if it can be any help to you, but my idea was to encourage fun, but short, combat periods ( which does fit with the heavily armed and monstrous characters involved with mine).

      Basically, during a turn, any player can call for events to bullet time (as per David Berg's pacing arc). This shift-down can be contested like anything else by other players.

      Assuming events go into bullet-time, each involved player can roll one die from their cup of any type. Players are not required to roll a die, and all dice to be rolled should be placed out under each player's hand before any are rolled. In other words, players are NOT allowed to pick their die or change their die due to seeing the results obtained by other players.

      This is the number of d6s they will recieve for the turn's bullet time. The referee will have a special set of d6s for this purpose, which are distinct from any others used in the game.

      The referee watches as each player rolls in turn, and gives the player the rolled number of bullet-time d6s.

      The turn action then goes into a slow-motion combat mode. It is still a free-play style of action. The player with the greatest number of bullet-time d6s has the initiative to start.

      They begin describing actions for one character at a time. These actions should be small- a quick move, a draw and fire, ducking behind nearby cover, and so on.

      Parallel to the main rules, thes actions can be interrupted/contested by another player. Contests occur as usual, with outcome setting and the referee insuring equitable outcome stakes on each side.

      During the bullet-time, players may use other dice from their cups and add d4s to their rolls as normal, in addition to or instead of their bullet-time d6s.

      The referee can use their dice in their bowl as usual, if necessary to decide order of events.

      If a player has one of their described actions contested and it goes to a contest roll-off, the winning player gains or maintains initiative, but loses their die. A losing player retains their die. On a tie, reroll results.

      At the end of the turn, or at the end of bullet-time, all bullet-time d6s return to the special pile of bullet-time dice. Players may NOT retain them for other use.

      I think this will help with the dice impoverished situation, while still allowing for action movie style combat to occur.
