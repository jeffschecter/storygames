    \n I'll go ahead and say that I disagree with Jonathan and Bret, for my game and any in its mode.

    GLASS is a highly modular LARP system, and it's for live combat, and so I can't just "go on The Forge and make it for next to nothing." I did all that five years (and more) ago and got as far as one can with thought experiments and spreadsheet math. And that's where the similarity of tabletop design and LARP design ends.

    For "initial playtesting," I've had small get-togethers where very experienced LARPers played with the various components and tried short battles with some Abilities; and those meetings resulted in new rules or tighter rules. That's all well and good; but it's also like saying one's "tested Magic; The Gathering" by pitting a couple of decks against each other. In actuality, there's a VAST gulf between those sorts of thought experiments/quick testing and full "regression testing" with scores of folks all pushing at their own agendas and trying to maximize the system (or best-model their character concept with the system elements). Even tapping local resources and friends, I can't hope to fully test GLASS's intricacies even with scores of such short sessions.

    And "blind testing" or "strangers with APs?" Forget it. VERY few LARP organizations with whom I could get in touch would be willing to run such a "full test" for me, in lieu of their monthly campaigns. So I also lack the sort of blind testing that a tabletop RPG can enjoy just by being available for download as a PDF and having the relative small buy-in of one session away from one's "normal, weekly game." SO I will probably always have to make do with guided testing (though I hope that one of my later runs won't require it--I'd like to see various helpers and volunteers interpret the rules, at least once).

    So anyhow... no, I won't be buying software with the money. No, I won't be paying one or two or ten folks for artwork. No, I won't be buying a short-run printing, to maximize my profits. I'll be paying the bulk to the state, as fees for State Park campsite rental; the rest will go to promotion, printing (tags, character sheets), and maybe even sets and props. The beneficiaries will be the very playtesters that help to make the system "perfect." And LARPing in general, if I can attract folks to the game that would normally eschew playing the typical high fantasy stuff that's all one can do in my area. And worst case scenario: Umstead State Park has some extra funds for maintenance and preservation.

    (I wasn't going to evangelize, here or on the submission web site... but all the chat here has me wanting to point out that key difference between my game and any others I've seen posted there. All that $1000 will "get for me" is more work to do and, hopefully, some tired-but-happy campers. )

    \n Some points!

    Lester is completely freaking wrong. There's no way Blowback would have gotten made without the DivNull Lark. Well, okay, maybe eventually somehow, but Shreyas and I had crazy life changes and financial upheavals and other freakouts, not to mention a wedding two weeks before the release date. Blowback would have been back-burnered at best. It was the money, the luxury of knowing it was set apart and I didn't have to feel guilty for spending it on my game. And it was knowing that I had to get this shit done and it had to be something awesome, because Lester mentioned that if it went well he might do another $1000 the next year. I felt like I owed it to the people who submitted and didn't get the cash, and the people who had an idea but were worried about the cash it'd take to make it a reality, to do the best game I could and hold up my end of the deal. Me not wanting to be the reason Lester stopped doing the contest was a HUGE motivator.

    That said.

    I really don't know why everyone thinks good automatically means more expensive. There's nothing I can do in InDesign that I couldn't do with additional time in Pages. If you're looking for something to invest in that will make your layout better, don't spend $700 on InDesign, spend $40 on a really good graphic design primer. Subscribe to graphic design and layout resource blogs, read the articles, download the free fonts that are professional-quality and GOOD.

    As far as art goes, if you're married to an idea and you won't sacrifice your artistic integrity\u2014 if it means giving up on a game because you can't afford the art of your dreams\u2014 well, that's your decision. Mist-Robed Gate spent $15 on stock art for the cover. Two thirds of the interior art was from a royalty-free clip art book of Japanese kimono patterns we got at Barnes and Noble for $18. Shreyas could have thrown a hissy over using Japanese kimono patterns when he spent so much time on the authenticity of the Mandarin and the recipes\u2014 and frankly, I expected him to. But he rolled with it.

    Blowback's art is lavishly, horrifically expensive to me. When I decided to change the cover background to the suburbs instead of an oil pipeline in the desert, I thought to myself "Oh God, do I really want to spend another $15 on this cover? I already spent $35!" The total for art was $211. It's easy to say "Oh, stock photography doesn't work for every game," and I'd agree with you wholeheartedly. But if you never saw Blowback, would you think that a game about badass spies would really lend itself to stock photos? It's not like it's a game about multi-ethnic businessmen shaking hands or white women going to the spa.

    Oh, and It's Complicated Revised. I've gotten nothing but compliments on the sickeningly cute anime art. That's all stock, too\u2014 there are only two full-page illustrations in the game, and some talking heads for examples and a flower motif. It cost me less than the cover for Blowback. I suck as an artist\u2014 though I'm an okay graphic designer. I can certainly manipulate artwork though, and it was just a matter of creating multiple expressions for the little talking heads to bring them to life. When I was first working on the revision, I wanted WAY more art than that\u2014 I wanted a full-page illo every few pages, like in the original version. I added thousands of words, and because I took out so much of the art, the page count's identical. No one's asked me why I put so little art in a game that is ostensibly anime-driven.

    I've said this stuff before, and I've been dismissed as a rare case, because I've been successful\u2014 so clearly my initial success with publishing for nearly no money is based on my now-existent fame and popularity, even though half the people I met when I was selling It's Complicated thought I was Ben's girlfriend Alexis, and I still get called Emily Shoemaker (even by my neighbor, JULIA BOND ELLINGBOE, on TELEVISION). I don't think it matters who you are. If you've got a good product, and you believe in your product enough to be flexible with its presentation, you can make it happen.

    And if you need help figuring out how, let me know. I'd be happy to help you figure out lower-cost art alternatives that offer the same feeling, or introduce you to people who might want to help you collaborate, or whatever. Just please don't say money's why you can't make something you believe in.
