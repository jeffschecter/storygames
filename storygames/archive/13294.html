    \n I'm (not) writing a game called Bukowksi in Love. It's a love story about a beautiful girl and an emotionally-tortured drunk trying to make a fresh start of it. Mechanically and thematically, it's sort of a hybrid of Breaking the Ice and Fiasco.

    In the first act, you can pour yourself shots of whiskey in order to add dice to your roll. During the act break, you pound all the shots you've lined up for yourself. And then in the second act, you try not to ruin your burgeoning relationship.

    There's poetry writing mechanics as well.


    Okay, why is it that I write my best material when I'm attempting to write my worst? This sounds like an amazing game. I'm actually going to design this for real.

    \n I'm not writing my homage to monster movies of all stripes, Tell That to an Angry Mob (points for getting the title reference). Play boils down to crazy relationship brinksmanship between the villagers and the monster(s), and I... jeez, for the life of me, maybe I can't explain it, but it involves kicking off the conflict by one side handing a chit to the other as a "we owe you one" that must either be repaid, or paid in spite.

    Kind of like, uh, "we saved your baby. (the humans give the monsters a token)" The token can be used to demand/request something of the humans (in this example), anything from tribute to a favor in kind to friendship, and if the request is denied, the monsters can escalate the situation and take what they want by force. Escalation, though, will give the humans an excuse to escalate right back, unless a player-character intervenes. The token is handed back if the humans freely-ish give what's asked of them.
    The humans can then offer the token to the monsters in exchange for the creatures doing something good for the village. Basically, you can do a good deed for the other side to get the token, and then you can hold that token over their heads to demand something of them.
