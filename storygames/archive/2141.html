Roleplaying Is (rant)

Josh Roby
January 2007 edited January 2007 in Story Games
So this is something that I'm seeing more and more of, which may be that it's showing up more often or I'm just noticing it more.

I see folks ask questions, and then other people answer with this syllogism-thing that goes "Since Roleplaying is X, therefore Y." A lot of times, the question is about a game under development, so the designer is asking things like, "How should I structure the skill list for Hippo Ninjas on Mars?" and he gets responses like "Well, since roleplaying is making the characters confront difficult moral challenges, the skill list should be organized along the kinds of moral challenges they'll be facing." There's the alternate version that goes, "Well, since roleplaying is an exercise in player creativity loosely based on the material provided, the skill list should be open and flexible so that the players can do anything they want with it." What I don't see a lot of is basing the answer on the design intents of the game-in-question, or when that isn't available, the asking of clarifying questions like, "Well, what do the Hippo Ninjas do on Mars?"

I also see a sort of related phenomenon where a design is criticized as not supporting what roleplaying "is." This goes something like, "Design Y is no good because roleplaying is X and the design doesn't do that." The reverse, "Design Y does not do X and therefore the game is supposed to be something other than X" doesn't seem to be considered, since the axiom Roleplaying=X doesn't seem to be questioned.

Over here in my little world, every game is played differently, has different emphases, inclusions, and exclusions, and each game produces a different experience. A game is a roleplaying game not because the experience it produces is similar to other roleplaying games, but because the tools and techniques that it uses to create that experience is similar to other roleplaying games' techniques and tools. If a new game produces the same exact results as another game I already have, why do I want that new game? And yet it seems a great deal of discussion seems to orbit around making different games produce experiences that fall into some sort of canonical and approved box.

A lot of this stems from identity politics, which is like our community's foot fungus: annoying and recurrent. We seem to be constantly stuck on defining who we as a group are for reasons that are murky at best: out of some fear of exclusion or something, I'm guessing (see all the "geeky" threads in the last month). In the quest to assure ourselves we're all in the group, we're trampling on the idiosyncracies of our individual experiences. I enjoyed my game because I got to really get into my character, and you enjoyed your game so it must have been because you really got into your character, because we're all doing the same thing, right?

It's slightly ridiculous, but it takes no small amount of courage to say, "This game that I'm excited about is not all things to all people." I don't know why it's so hard, but it is. It's especially ridiculous because if you're all excited about it, you're presumably excited about it for specific reasons. But so often that it's rather disturbing, the specific reasons and specific effects that get us so fired up about a game get glossed over when we try and share our enthusiasm. Instead we try to show how the game has some sort of broad-based mass appeal, which often has little to do with the specific things we liked. At best, we're a little disingenuous; at worst we're misrepresenting ourselves: lying, in simpler terms.

Why can't games be idiosyncratic and unique? Why can't different designs produce different effects, and be appreciated for their different effects? Why can't we say, "That doesn't sound like the thing that I find personally gratifying, but it's fascinating nonetheless." Why do we trample on specifics in favor of shoehorning everything into one box or another?

I'd really like to hear what you liked in your recent game experience or book purchase or IM chat or whatever. I really don't want you to pitch it to me; I really don't want you to explain how the individual experience or individual artifact is a member of a larger group of experiences or artifacts. I don't care. If you tell me what is actually engaging and entertaining you, you're telling me way more than if you try to make your play sound as awesome as it felt. In fact, that sort of personal account is probably more likely to sell me on that game than any sort of pitch would.

However, I'm just one poster here, so maybe I'm going contrary to the desired flow around here and other folks really want to connect experience A and experience B to contextualize them and draw some sort of conclusions. Maybe I'm totally off my rocker and missing something essential to the discussions that are taking place. Maybe there's some value in "what you describe does not fit my personal definition of roleplaying which I am not interested in questioning at the moment." So tell me, Story Games: am I missing something? Or can we appreciate our games without stripping them of their individuality? Can we appreciate games and designs as things unto themselves, rather than insisting that they fit in?

My intent (as much as authorial intent matters) is to guide players to create a new and fundamentally different experience So if you get together a bunch of players to play FLFS, I would hope that you'd give the game as written a try since that's why I published the book. If you want to go all JDCorley on the game and change up how things work, that's certainly your perogative, and thank you for your thirty bucks. I certainly can't stop you and I wouldn't want to. In my humble opinion, though, you're missing out on an opportunity to try something different, something that you might enjoy more than your usual fare. Or maybe it'll be something that's a nice change of pace. Maybe you think it sucks. The important thing for me, though, is that it's something new and different, and if you strip out the stuff that's foreign to your usual play style, it won't be new and different, it will be the Weekly Game with some steampunk paint splashed on top of it.

    I talk about "rack shots" and "smash cuts" in PTA and not in other games, too, for the same reasons. PTA is the game of making your own television show. Other games aren't. So when playing PTA I talk the teevee talk. When playing Dogs I talk the fallout talk. And so on.

    Judson, I'm skeptical of the existence of generalisms for successful roleplaying, and I think this gets back to how we group roleplaying games, which I touched on earlier.

    As far as I can see, the set of things we call roleplaying games are games which share similar techniques and produce rather dissimilar results. The core shared technique is playing a role: making decisions for a fictional character and assuming that your real-world decisions direct the actions of the fictional character. Other common techniques include rating character competency, rolling dice, and investing greater power in one player at the table. It's the tools that are used that make an activity a roleplaying game.

    When we start talking about "rules of thumb and shorthand guideposts towards successful play" we're bridging the gap between the techniques used and the results produced. That's the "successful play" bit. The thing is, in order to articulate those rules of thumb, we have to define successful play. For general rules of thumb, we need generally defined successful play. When we create some generalized definition of successful play, we kill the appreciation of individual experiences: they're either successful or unsuccessful, based on some outside criteria.

    To continue the carpentry metaphor, there are no general rules of thumb for successful carpentry; there are, however, general rules of thumb for successful carpentry of a particular school or style. Do you expose the dovetails or hide them? Depends on whether you're doing baroque or craftsman carpentry. So too I see value in general rules of thumb for particular styles of play. Maybe you could use creative agendas here and talk about general rules of thumb for narrativist play, but I find the CAs a little too reductive. I prefer to consider each game as a different style of play, a different experience to be had.

    Curly, I agree with you in large part. The only "problem" with importing techniques from one game into another is that it's very easy to replace elements of the game rather than supplement them. If you imported fallout into PTA, you'd end up with tons of traits on your Protagonist sheet, and activating them all would foul up the resolution system. Get what I'm saying? By no means am I saying it's impossible or verboten, but I am saying that doing so introduces some risk of trampling the original game's intended design and operation.
