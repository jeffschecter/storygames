\n Memory 1:
In the movie "Finding Bobby Fisher," Ben Kingsley plays the mentor of the chess wunderkind main protagonist. Ban has been awarding certificates of accomplishment to the kid for the kid learning and striving and accomplishing. Then there is a scene where Ben intentionally ruins this reward cycle by flinging certificates at the kid, saying something like "Here, have as many as you want. Does that make you feel good?"

I had a game-design take-away from that scene:
1) Ben just ruined the game he had set up. The certificates had weight before, because they were gated by a seemingly-solid procedure. Earning a certificate was an accomplishment. But then Ben exploded the game by making it look like it was all a sham.
2) "Real" accomplishment and illusionary accomplishment are difficult to disentangle. All that kid needed was a solid-seeming procedure to gate the certificates, and earning them seemed like an accomplishment.

Memory 2:
I'm in high-school, and a friend of mine is regaling me with the awesome adventures of some fantasy book hero. The guy does all this bad-ass stuff, and accomplishes mighty things. And I can't help but think the whole time, "So what? You do realize that the author can write anything he wants down, right? So it's really not impressive at all that the dude scaled the wall with his knives." Similarly, while I enjoyed the first book in Feist's Riftwar Saga, and first book of Dune, later books in both series left me totally cold. In the first books, I could believe that the author was "playing fair" with me, putting people into hard situations and then narrating what would come of it in a way I could buy into. Later books gave the heroes superpowers that let them get out of anything arbitrarily. So who cares anymore.

Now, to RPGs:
I think I am particularly sensitive to this "weightiness"/"real accomplishment" issue. RPGs that allow us to tell cool stories without enough constraints/struggle/difficulty leave me cold. And yet RPGs that conduct themselves much like boardgames (D&D 4e, played straight) also leave me cold, because I'd rather play a good boardgame.

Is anyone else like this? What is going on with my brain?

    What you're describing is what I call "Legitimate Tension" or "Legitimate Adversity." By those phrases I mean that the opposition is real, played honestly, and might actually succeed even if the players do everything "right."

    So, yes, I totally get where you're coming from.

    \n I like your idea of "Rubber Banding". Over the years I've come to realize that things like Drama Dice in 7th Sea and other kinds of "Fudge Results" mechanics act as a hidden "Rubber Band."

    The opposition remains fixed through out play. However, at the beginning the game the players have few resources, so he seems powerful and tough. But as the game goes on the players accumulate more "Fudge Results" bennies so that even though the opposition remains fixed through out the players are given the illusion of finally beating the opposition in the end.

    It's a clever way to encode a "story arc" into the mechanics. The characters get kicked around at the beginning but ultimately triumph in the end.

    \n Well, yes and no. Millions upon millions thrill to read thrillers in which there is never any real doubt about the outcome. Justice prevails in paperback thrillers, although it may be punched in the face several times before the ending. I think the phrase "suspension of disbelief" is a bit over/mis-used, so hey, let me steal the concept here and over/mis-use it further. To enjoy these sorts of properties (thrillers, mysteries, comic books, episodic television, medical television, legal thriller television, action movies, horror movies, romances...), you have to be able to pretend that the ending (or the outcome of other tensions) is in doubt. A skillful author/creator will aid the reader/consumer by having characters who are in doubt about the ending, adversaries who push hard against the ending, and narration that makes it seem like several different endings could happen. But if someone interrupts you when you are on the first page of (say) the latest Lenny Bliss mystery by Bob Sloan (a series of mysteries you really should be reading, they're very good) and says "how does it come out, does Bliss catch the guy?", you're really not going to be giving anything away to yourself or them if you say "Yep."

    Jesse calls it a safety net, but that's not really right. That implies that a creator who doesn't use the safety net might reach the same sort of ending but it would be "better" because, in their heads, they thought it might come out another way. I don't think that's true, because the experience of the story for the reader/audience/consumer is indistinguishable. Of course in gaming all the roles are intertwingled - it might feel better to be a creator who doesn't accept a particular limitation, but that's just one person's feeling. We know limitations aid creativity, speaking generally, so it shouldn't be a big surprise or counterintuitive that a (for example) "catch the bad guy" limitation on mystery stories might help many mystery story creators and make the process of creation more fun for them.

    \n Jesse,

    While the dynamic I described is "traditionally" produced by the design of a GM, it can also be the result of a properly design RPG or something a player puts into a GM-less game with the right options. And of course it all comes down to the emerging, evolving social assumptions of play. No One Gets Hurt and I Will Not Abandon You can help identify some of those structures before hand, but as usual the problem with talking about assumptions is that there is no method to ensure that you've voiced them all.


    Mark,

    It seems to me that you desire the experience of an ecology of risk, where the character difficulties of the game exist not only in relation to your character but to each other. Blatantly trashing that ecology by raising it uniformly, or by introducing something that shocks it massively seems to be a source of massive problems - and no wonder that would screw with the very real sense of accomplishment that you've taken in understanding and adapting to that ecology.

    Now what I'm arguing is entirely different, I'm saying that you need your ecology of hazards in a strata of character activity, say direct obstacles to running your businesses of questionable legality, but not in every other concievable risk area. So you might get shot or be getting married, and those will interact with your business problems, but you don't need to have an entire ecology of romantic problems to deal with or an entire ecology of back room surgery complications.

    And there are other elements that we can eliminate the risk of because we just aren't that interested in them. Sure some of the characters don't eat too well, but does that mean this game needs to track their medical status, and face the possibility that one or more of them will die of a heart attack or maybe a prolonged bout with cancer. And of course all that changes if one of the players wants to have her character have one of these - but do you really feel cheated that there isn't an ecology of difficulties here, that we don't properly reflect that some heart attacks and cancers are more lethal or more insidious than others, or is are you good with that player to dialing the disease she wants?

    - Mendel

    \n Level scaling isn't about challenge or difficulty, it's about pacing and rollout of new content. Of course it fails to satisfy if your emotional payoff is in beating challenges.

    If, on the other hand, you're most interested in exploration or in narrative (defined as story arc, not as some ineffable story-now kind of thing), it's vital.

    When I play console games, I like scaled challenges because I am a tourist. I like to see all the "stuff" in the game, and I have neither the time nor the desire to develop skill at something so ephemeral as which buttons to press in response to what cues.

    When I play (certain) RPGs, I want to get a new power, fight different monsters, visit different fictional environments. The purpose of leveling is to pace that.

    \nI think this is moving from one extreme to the other. You've reduced any sense of accomplishment and legitimate challenge down to a simulated sensation.

    There must be an aspect of causal reality (or in-fiction verisimilitude) for accomplishment to be legitimate.

    For example: If I was to return to the starting town at level 20 and fought the dire-rats that challenged me at level 1 and found them still a challenge, I would know my accomplishment was redundant. If, however, I could kill them with ease, then I know my 'level' has meaning - I have legitimately progressed. Similarly, if I rushed ahead to the dragons lair at level 1 and managed to kill it, my understanding of in-game challenges would be destroyed.
    Simply because game-masters are proficient at balancing challenges to match character levels doesn't mean we should become complacent about our interactions with the game-world. This is why I advocate throwing over-powered and under-powered monsters (correspondent to the character-level) at players; it enforces the 'reality' of the situation.
