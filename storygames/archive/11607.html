\n[Edited to make the title less easy to misinterpret, for the sake of any new readers. I hope that's OK with you, Andy!]

Last night I met up with some gamers--all strangers to me--to play In a Wicked Age..., as a one-shot game. I'm reporting because of a couple of interesting things that came up, and a question it raised for me about the way many gamers seem to approach this game.

And, boy, was it an interesting game of IaWA. They organized the game via an online Meetup.com event, which meant guests RSVP and then show up. Six people were supposed to be there. A big group, to my eyes, but, hey, why not? Sounds like a fun challenge.

For whatever reason, shortly after people started arriving, the number grew to nine. I was wondering, "how is this going to go?" I can't remember the last time I played a game with nine people. Yikes! And we only had about two or two and a half hours.

Most of the players were self-avowed In a Wicked Age... fans, and said it was their number-one game, which they played regularly. Despite this, most claimed to be fairly unclear on the rules (as it turned out, this was true). There were also a few newbies ("I've only played D&D, and one game of Werewolf..."). Most of the people knew each other, but there were a few people, like myself, who were new to the group.

Two of the players decided to co-GM the game. That left seven players.

The two GMs began to introduce the game, making a big point of how this was a "narrative-based game", and not your typical RPG. "There are some dice involved, but we very rarely roll them. You only roll if you want to roll, as you'll see." "We brainstorm characters and create the plot together before we play." I found this disclaimer interesting.

Next, another unusual procedure: the GMs had everyone close their eyes, and then announced different movie ratings: "PG-13!" "NC-17!" etc. We were supposed to raise our hand if the rating being named was the highest level we wanted to handle explicitly in the game. Someone chose "PG-13", so that was our cutoff, in terms of game content. (For the record, all the players were adults)

This seemed like a pretty good idea on a number of levels, but felt very strange to me in the context of a game like In a Wicked Age... For instance, it meant we just threw out the "Blood & Sex" Oracle altogether, since it was too likely that something un-PG would come up. We then voted on which Oracle to use, and ended up drawing two cards from The Unquiet Past, and two from Nest of Vipers. One of the cards was deemed "uninteresting", and another drawn. Then characters were brainstormed, and (I believe) eight were identified.

"We need more characters," said the GMs. "We need at least half as many NPCs as there are PCs, so we need to draw another card." (Need more NPCs? With seven PCs in the game and less than two hours to play? Hmmmm.) We ended up drawing two more, from Nest of Vipers.

Nothing too unusual here, but I got the impression that mixing Oracles and drawing whatever number of cards they liked was the standard procedure.

Then there was a brief "demo" of a conflict, with dice being rolled, but no narration taking place. ("He didn't double me, so we roll again...") Again, close to right, but something felt a little off to me here. I'm pretty sure the few newbies didn't get much out of the demo, except to see that certain dice would be rolled and that the highest number mattered. Also, the GM who was facilitating had the impression that we "roll initiative" for the first round of an action sequence, but not subsequent rounds. The "initiative" was handled correctly, though, despite several of the experienced IaWA players thinking that being doubled in the initial roll meant everything was over.

We spent a good deal of time banging out Best Interests (with a fair bit of success), using a "conflict map". People seemed to find a lot of glee in the messy picture that resulted, even though we didn't really ever refer to that page after we began playing. Then players were instructed to take some time to write a physical description of their character on their sheets (this gave several players pause, and took a while), even though we were never prompted in any way to describe our characters in play.

Finally, we assigned dice to forms and skipped Particular Strengths (which seemed a wise thing to do, since time was running out quick; probably half the session was up by this point).

To begin the game, one GM intoned "In a Wicked Age..." and described three of the characters meeting out on the street outside a mansion (which was one of the elements drawn). The first difficulty that arose was that many players were asking, "Do I know so-and-so? How long have I been doing X?" The GMs responded that you can just decide whatever you like, and not worry about background details. "You can narrate anything you want. Only, if you disagree with something someone else says, you can roll dice against them." The GMs explained that you could roll dice against another player if you disagreed about something. Note: not characters disagreeing, but players disagreeing.

Example from play: "Have we signed the deed yet?" "I thought we had." "No, I think it's better if we're still waiting to sign it." "But the card we drew said, 'deed, signed and sealed'!" "Do you guys want to roll dice over this?"

Fortunately, in actual play only conflicts between characters led to dice-rolling, with backstory stuff being quickly negotiated by the players. This was good.

The players with characters in the scene began to posture and talk to each other, doing funny voices and being entertaining, but without doing anything to move the story along. In response to inactivity, the GMs started to introduce the other characters into the scene one by one, but without introducing conflict. e.g. "Oh, yeah, while you're talking, you see there's a red-haired woman across the street who can see you."

This reminded me of my one failed attempt to play IaWA in the past, where I was GMing, and a very similar lack of anything *happening* took place.

Things continued much in the same vein, with players entertaining each other with dialogue and, it seems, waiting for something to happen. An NPC (demons possessing the mansion) caused conflicts via one of the GMs narrating bizarre stuff into play, like "for some reason, you can't climb the stairs, like your legs won't respond to your commands." Some dice were rolled over this sort of thing, with minimal narration. Here, something interesting happened:

When a person would win a conflict, the GMs explained: "Now, you, loser, have to narrate how the other person wins. And if you don't make it interesting enough, they can take away your dice [i.e. damage two forms]." Comical descriptions of failure were quickly delivered and commended: "Great job! You certainly understand this game. I won't take away any of your dice."

In future conflicts, much the same took place. The loser would narrate their failure in some drastic fashion ("I fall down the stairs, and my cloak falls off, and I land on the ground facefirst, then skid out the front door, like this: Ouch!"). When I won a conflict and said, "OK, great, but can't I negotiate now for some actual consequences, in exchange for not damaging the dice?", there were some very confused looks around the table, and people seemed uncomfortable.

I was informed that, anyway, damaging dice wasn't all that important, because the all "refreshed at the start of the next session, anyway".

The game progressed as a comedy of errors, mainly involving slapstick humour. Which was, mind you, played up to good effect, and many players were both entertained and entertaining. My character was a hapless, luckless murderer, who begged his murder victim to help him plead with his wife. An unlucky cultist tried to climb the stairs several times, and each time ended up sliding out the door on his ass. Someone fell out of the window. Shingles from the house fell down on buyers whenever the seller was trying to convince them of its value, leading to more laughs.

But it seemed like little was ever at stake in the game. For instance, one of the characters was a magical bird, whose Best Interests were to steal some hair from the red-haired woman. The entirety of her involvement with the game was to swoop in at random moments, pluck out a hair, and then fly off (out of the scene). But everyone seemed to find it terribly funny. And it was, from time to time. Getting in-character and goofing around can be a lot of fun.

However, it reminded me of gaming in past years, where I seem to remember feeling that this was what roleplaying was all about. In other words, the GM provides plot, and the players provide Color and laughs by portraying their characters. Roleplaying = talking in funny voices, Rollplaying = rolling dice to fight things. Everything else ("story") is the territory of the GM. It seems to me that in this game, without the GMs injecting story, everyone was a little lost, and my own failed game a year ago was a fumble for the same reason.

My question is:

Am I remembering correctly? Are a lot of gamers out there kind of conditioned to expect and enjoy contributing to a game purely by character-portrayal? And become paralyzed or confused when the burden of moving along the plot falls on them? Or is my memory playing tricks on me?

I also seem to remember that those gamers who were considered "great roleplayers" were sometimes people who learned to break up this tedium by throwing their characters into wacky, unexpected situations, creating thereby surprise and conflict. But, in IaWA, it seems to me that everyone has to do this. To enter any scene with a clear direction for the character. And to push for their desires. But it seemed that in this game, people really were just so used to merely acting out dialogue or non-essential actions ("I take a piece of the bush and braid it into a hat for my pet donkey!") that this was not a comfortable thing for them.

I'm curious to hear what you think.

Finally, no long post is complete without a personal attack: I must demonstrate what I mean by the above, for otherwise my writing here would be mere character-portrayal, and not conflict-inducing. Therefore I must say that all these people proudly say that they have learned to play In a Wicked Age... from Ryan (Ry). Ha! Time to roll the dice, Ryan!

    \n A couple of things:

    First of all, one of the things that wasn't clear in the IAWA "how to pick best interests" chapter is that something very bad has to happen if you don't pursue them. In other words, if I make a character who is not pursuing their best interests hard, what happens then that is compelling? If (as someone put it), Jimmy McNulty's best interest is to stop drinking and listen to Lieutenant Daniels, and he does nothing of the kind, that can certainly be a compelling story, but only because McNulty comes stumbling into the room and fucks an alligator or something. Players who aren't used to or don't really like PvP are not going to go charging after best interests in a roomful of strangers, it just isn't going to happen. I just met this person and I have no clue how they are going to react at all when I obliterate them. So even if they write a good, pointed, at-your-guy best interests, they're going to be as reasonable as they can about it, and will often give the farm away just because they were asked to, and if there are no consequences to failure to pursue the best interests, the game will grind to a halt, as you saw.

    Second, "conditioning" is the wrong word - it is entirely negative and discounts the idea that player preferences are aesthetic choices based on taste. Plenty of players like giving input into the game world strictly through their character. It is what they want to do, it is what attracts them to the hobby. And other than negotiating after losing or winning a conflict, they can do just that very easily in IAWA. It is not a bad game for them where they have to narrate one billion things.

    Third, "loser describes how winner wins" is just not IAWA at all. Like, that's not the game. It doesn't even much make sense. I'm all for house rules if people like them, and these folks seemed to, but I'm not sure I would go to IAWA for that. The only two things unique about IAWA are the owe list and negotiating having an actual gameplay framework (the stick - the threat of just bludgeoning the other person instead.) Someone going to an IAWA game and not finding any real negotiations-with-sticks is going to be in a very similar position to someone going to a D&D4 game and finding "well, we don't use 1-square shift rules". The natural reaction is "whawhu huh?" even if everyone seems to enjoy it.

    Fourth, IAWA can be used with NPCs to charge after the PCs if they're lagging behind a bit. They can especially be used to pick off PCs who have been hit hard. I don't think a version in which the NPCs provide the bulk of the conflict is dysfunctional in any way, it's just VERY hard on the GM and not nearly as competitive. Actually that might be a good idea for a group of strangers who are a little shy about punching each other in the face.

    Finally, strangers would far rather do comedy together than drama. That will never change, ever. Even if it had been played absolutely according to the book, I guarantee you would consider it a comedy.

    By the way, I LOVE the "close your eyes and raise your hand to identify the rating" bit of the game. That is a great way to get people to state that they might be squicked out by something without feeling like they have to stand in front of everyone and say "hello I am a wimp."

    \n You know, the game wasn't as bad as all that. That's the weird thing about it: I'm pretty sure I remember (if I think way back) thinking that all one-shots are kind of like that--goofy, inconclusive, just an excuse to get together and talk at each other in funny voices and make each other laugh. I was probably one of those people that said, "I only campaign play", because in my experience, that was the only way to get a meaningful story and any character development happening.

    Now, I was only active in about 20 minutes of gameplay, since my character wasn't one of the people immediately in the first (and only) scene. I think one of the GMs framed me in as, "oh, yeah, there's also a sketchy guy watching all this from the trees." I eventually picked a moment where a player whose play I was enjoying was separated from the others (coincidentally, he was the character it was in my Best Interests to kill), and just asked if I could be present in the same room as him. We had a very enjoyable interaction where I threatened to kill him, then realized he was much stronger than me (this was established in the fiction; no dice rolled here) and started begging him to help me plead with his wife for my children's lives. I had a blast for those 20 minutes or so; it just wasn't meaningful story play. I rolled dice twice (and got onto the Owe List both times, despite winning!), once to scramble out the window (in which I was comically stuck) and then to "accidentally" bump into one of the other characters as I fell out of the window. You often see some kind of clear signal from the GM that this is the moment where you can do something significant, and then a pointed look at the player in question.

I've played with quite a few story gamers/indie gamers who use game systems to crank out story and impact each other, and have next to zero interest in the particulars of description or portrayal.     2. To introduce the idea that there are games that don't run on rails. That players can and should be able to have a stake in the game and the story and more than that can actually fully direct it.

    With those broad goals and knowing that I am for the most part dealing with an audience that will ask me "what do I need to roll to fire uphill in a rainstorm" I generally try to praise and encourage the following behaviour on the assumption that these are the basics of developing narrative gaming comfort level and if these start to emerge then narrative play can deepen in future sessions.

    1. Describe your actions, don't just roll the dice and look to me to tell you what happened based on a number.

    2. Become comfortable narrating consequences, the dice tell you that you have not lost this conflict but that the other player's character has the upper hand, what does that look like?

    Realistically in a one-shot I'm happy if those things are achieved, I may be setting my expectations low, but I'm not sure how to raise them to the next steps without making it a game that is not friendly to newbies, which is the purpose of a one-shot (well for me at least).

    3. Learn how to advocate for your character and your characters interests, don't sit back and wait for the GM to make something 'happen' to your character.

    4. Challenge others into counter point positions so that plot must move forward, create scenes and situations where other characters are forced to 'deal' with your character.

    5. Raise the level of narration by increasing description of scene, setting and action and how they interrelate.

    So this is the membership from where I find players and these are the goals I have for introducing indie play, any suggestions on how they could could be altered for higher success?

      Plenty of people run IAWA as one-chapter games, which to me is like playing Monopoly but omitting buying and selling houses. You could play the game with just jail, "Go" money and railroads but why play Monopoly at that point? Anyway, room to disagree there.       Anyway, we continue to roll and describe what we're doing, back and forth. No one sets stakes, we just say what we're doing and keep on keepin' on until someone gets wiped or gives in.

      When that happens, the winner makes an offer to the loser, if they wish: "Hey, I can exhaust you, OR I can blow up your meth lab."

      If the loser's meth lab is important to their best interests, they may very well say, "Screw you, I'm keeping my meth lab." and the winner says, "Fine, you're exhausted...." and on their next turn starts with, "So I'm driving a pickup truck through the front wall of your skanky ass trailer park meth house." and now the loser has to fight off an attack while exhausted.

      \n Hey Sara, you don't have anything to apologize for. IAWA has a history of thrashing GMs the first few times they take it out for a spin. It's kicked my ass a few times.

      Jason did a pretty good job discussing the resolution system, but I thought I would break it down a little bit.

      Firstly, the dice do not resolve anything except who can injure or exhaust the other person. It's not a resolution system; it's a coercion system. If our characters are fighting over the magic sword, the dice will not tell us who gets the sword. All, the dice will tell us is who has the advantage, and finally which one of us can injure or exhaust the other. That is the stick.

      So, first we roll. If I get the higher roll, my dice stand and you have to answer. You reroll. Let's say my dice are still better (but I don't double you). I have the advantage. We narrate what that looks like, maybe I've got a firmer grip on the sword.

      The next question is does someone go on the Owe List. For simplicity, let's assume we're rolling the same sized dice. So, no one is on the list.

      The next question is a big one. Are we going to keep rolling, or do we want to negotiate. More pointedly, are you scared enough to give me something I might want more than hurting you? Maybe I say, give me the sword and this is all over, but you're all "No way!"

      I've got the advantage. But that advantage die is fickle. We roll again and I have the higher roll, but when you pick up your dice to answer, you beat me (but don't double me). Now you have the advantage. Again we can negotiate. This time you have the stick. If I don't want to get injured or exhausted, perhaps I better offer you something that you want instead.

      Maybe getting the sword isn't all that important to you anymore. Now I'm all "Take the sword! It's yours!" And you're all "nope, looks like you're exhausted from the struggle. Lose dice!"

      As for all the confusion about ordering and picking sides and stuff, it's actually much simpler than you're making it out to be, I think. The IaWA rules are pretty simple:

      * When a "Oh, no, you don't!" situation comes up, everyone involved rolls dice.
      * Then everyone gets to say something about what their character is doing, in order of die rolls.
      * When someone says something that your character opposes, or something that could hurt your character, you reroll your dice and say how it turned out based on that die roll.
      * Once you've had to "answer" someone (which means "narrating the outcome of their action against your character"), though, you don't get your own action any more--you've been busy struggling with whoever is doing something to your character.

      There's no need to worry about who's potentially going to end up on the Owe List or who is on which side until the dice are rolled and narrations exchanged. It's fun and flexible, because I might start out wanting one thing, but, based on how things have changed since the dice were first rolled, I might decide on a different course of action by the time my turn to narrate an action has come around.

      There might be a game that's more well-suited to what you're trying to do than IaWA (perhaps a game that doesn't force players to go at each others' throats in order to be successful, for instance--that's something that can be difficult for strangers to do), but that's a whole other discussion. If you're happy with what the game is doing for you, there's no need to go looking elsewhere!

      I believe firmly that roleplaying games, more than their players, create a certain experience at the table.
