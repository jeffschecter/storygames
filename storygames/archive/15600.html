\n I have the opportunity to introduce role-playing games as a means of academic enrichment at the middle school where I teach, but the game needs to accommodate a larger group of students - say ten to fifteen. Do you folks have any suggestions as to indie or general commercial systems I might use? I have considered three groups of five students with a game like Microscope. The system would need to be such that I could supervise the three groups at once. Of course, there may be a system for specific indie game that would handle ten to fifteen students, of which I am unaware. Thank you ahead for any attention you may give this topic.

    \n L\xe6relyst was specifically designed for classroom use. Each group controls one character - so a class of 25 might have five or six characters, for instance. It was also based on the idea that working well in the group would give you XP - so spending the necessary time, listening to others, focusing on the task etc would help your character level up.

    It's in Norwegian, and is out of print, so it's fairly obscure But if you want more details, let me know.

    \n It might seem odd to mention my own games, but I've used 1001 Nights with up to 8. And I've run Psi*Run for many, many kids in the age range you're looking at, in groups of 4 to 8. I agree with Matthijs; having teams of 4 kids run a single character can work well.

    Be prepared for some kids to really get it and get into it, and others to not really follow or want to actually play; in the year-long in-school playtest I did of Storming the Wizard's Tower, I had a handful of kids who really wanted to get into it and a middle group that was fine but not really enthusiastic, and a handful that just didn't really care, so long as they didn't have to do work. We made maps, talked about trade and cultures, outlined how the cities worked, made characters and played. We all had fun, and some of the kids came away from it interested in role-playing outside of school.
