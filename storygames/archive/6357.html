    Posted By: Ryan Stoughton3.) What do the players (including the GM if there is one) do?
    \nEach player creates a character focused on a particular role: offense, defense, or, support. Ideally, each player's character covers a different ability niche so that the group of adventurers formed by their characters is able to overcome a wide variety of challenges. The GM's role, then, is to provide a wide variety of challenges against which the players can pit their adventuring party.

    Challenges consist of each side (the players on one and the GM on the other) managing a pool of resources that are brought to bear against the other side. In the players' case, these resources include their characters' special abilities (magical items, spells, combat prowess, skills) and survival ability (hit points). In the GM's case, these resources are opponents (typically fantastical monsters) with resources similar to the players' characters, as well as the setting environment itself, e.g., weather hazards, terrain, visibility, society, etc. In addition, each side's expertise with the game rules themselves is also a primary resource.

    The typical form this challenge process takes is the "scenario." This is typically a mission given to the characters via an in-game situation provided by the GM, e.g., the characters are hired by a patron to retrieve a hidden treasure. The players then try to complete the mission via their characters, and the GM tries to thwart the mission via their opponents and the environment.

    If the characters survive the mission, the players are rewarded with an increase in their character's power\u2014more magical items, spells, combat prowess, skills, and survival ability (hit points). The GM then presents a new mission with an increased level of difficulty. This sequence continues as long as the characters survive.

    If the characters do not survive the mission, the players create new characters, and a new mission is presented by the GM.

    Posted By: Ryan Stoughton4.) How does your setting (or lack thereof) reinforce what your game is about?
    \nThe setting template implied by the rules assumes that the characters' world is filled with: a) a great deal of forgotten treasure hidden in dangerous locales, b) a great deal of hostile creatures who frequent said locales (and thus interfere with the characters accessing the treasure), c) divine and arcane power sources the characters may draw upon, d) a moral universe with clear distinctions between Good and Evil (so enemies are easily identified, and exploration of moral premise does not interfere with adventuring), e) societies populated by those generally less powerful than the PCs, and therefore who rely on them to undertake the most challenging missions, and f) multitudinous diverse cultures that provide 1) ample opportunity for strife, i.e., conflict that leads to missions the characters can undertake, and 2) a wide array of options for building PCs.

    Posted By: Ryan Stoughton5.) How does the Character Creation of your game reinforce what your game is about?
    \nPlayers choose from a finite list of ability packages, called "races" and "classes," that define their niche. Each race/class focuses on different aspects of in-game ability (melee, magic, skill use, etc). Each race/class requires the support of other classes to cover the areas in which they are lacking in order to face the challenges the GM will throw at them. This reinforces the "adventuring party" concept, as no single PC will ever have all the resources they need to complete a mission.

    All characters typically start at the lowest rung of power, and the players are presented with a progression of abilities (and future resources, like magic items) for their character that can only be acquired by sending it on missions. The more difficult the missions, the greater the rewards are that are earned, and thus the faster the character can move up the progression path. This reinforces the central conceit of mission-based play (since it's the fastest path to advancement), the ever-increasing rate of challenge (advancement will slow if the difficulty does not keep pace) and the overall goal of accumulation of power (since characters start at the lowest rung).

    Posted By: Ryan Stoughton6.) What types of behaviors/styles of play does your game reward (and punish if necessary)?
    \nIt rewards tactical thinking and rules mastery, i.e., being a proficient, effective player of the game. While it does not overtly punish casual, inefficient play, such players will find completing missions more difficult, as they will not be best utilizing their character's resources.\n
    Posted By: Ryan Stoughton7.) How are behaviors and styles of play rewarded or punished in your game?
    \nAll reward hinges on mission completion. Play styles that do not prioritize this goal will not complete missions without greater loss of character resources, potentially inclusive of all their characters' hit points, i.e., their PCs death is far more likely. Since all player input is done via their character, PC death ends the player's ability to contribute to, i.e., participate in, the game.

    Also, a primary path to mission completion is rules mastery. The rules contain a number of optimal strategies, as well as a number of suboptimal ones. The better a player is able to distinguish between the two, the better they are able to complete missions with minimized resource loss.

    Posted By: Ryan Stoughton8.) How are the responsibilities of narration and credibility divided in your game?
    \n(I'm answering here without 100% certainty about what "credibility" means in this context.)

    Among the players at the table, one is singled out as the GM, or "Dungeon Master" or "DM" in this particular game. The DM, and only the DM, has the ability to create setting; frame, start, and stop scenes; control the actions of NPCs; and control the effects of the in-game environment.

    The narration of the other players at the table is limited to the actions of their own PCs in reaction to the settings, situations, and NPCs presented by the DM. The sole exceptions to this are setting elements and NPCs specifically created via character resources controlled by the player. E.g., a player can use a magical power to summon a monster that fights on their behalf, use a magical power to bring into being landscape elements such as magical walls of fire, or establish the existence of an NPC cohort/companion for their PC that is under their control.

    However, the DM is given authority to override player narration, but is advised to reserve such veto power for exceptional situations. These are typically situations where the player's narration is somehow disruptive to the play experience.
