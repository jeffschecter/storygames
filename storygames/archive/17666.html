    \n I typically start a session off by telling new players that once we get past the Tilt, no one's character is safe. That way you usually avoid someone killing another PC off in the 2nd scene. But you still have a good chance of someone's brains getting blown out towards the end of the game, or during epilogues.

    I've never had PC death be an issue in a Fiasco game, except when playing w a trad gamer who studiously avoided their character dying until the "perfect moment". But even then it wasn't a big deal.

    Someone dies...you talk about them and refer to them and play scenes that revolve around them. Or flashback.

    \n Paul, that's a good question.

    In the Fiasco game that I played last Tuesday, my character killed another protagonist in the first scene. It was practically the first thing that happened in the game.

    It worked out, we did flashbacks easily to flesh out the life of the dead character, but when the aftermath happened they managed to roll a whopping 14 white.

    How the heck are you supposed to have that kind of awesome ending? The player suggested that maybe they weren't dead after-all... but the group shut that down as being outside of the scope of suspension of disbelief.

    We ended up going with him being remembered as a hero by the community, having a school named after him, and his mom marrying the president.

    But it was weird, and felt awkward.

    \n I've played characters that died off in the very first scene. Depending on the genre of game, we've either used flashbacks (a lot) or we introduced time travel. As well, if you're playing with supernatural themes, death can often be just the beginning. The non-flashback ideas are fun, but they definitely lend themselves to a gonzo style of play. Thing can go over the top very quickly when time travel is introduced.
