\n
\n
\n
\n\nWolfe \n\n\n

\n\nJuly 2009 \n edited July 2009 in Game Design Help
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n This is essentially an opinion poll. I'm not discouraging discussion, but what I'm looking for is an opinion one way or the other.
\n
\nBackground: I have a system that involves a single die roll, roll under a given target number, so low numbers are better than high numbers. Sometimes it's pass/fail, but other times, a degree of success or failure is important. In these cases, I have one of two ways to express the result.
\n
\nMethod 1: Margin of Success or Margin of Failure. If your target number is a 10, and you roll a 6, your Margin of Success (MoS) is 4. If you roll a 17, your Margin of Failure (MoF) is 7. It is possible to succeed with a MoS of 0. It is impossible to fail with an MoF of 0. This can complicate comparisons. Obviously Success beats Failure, but what about MoS:MoS/MoF:MoF? MoS 6 is better than MoS 4, but MoF 2 is better than MoF 7. Whether higher Margin is better switches based on whether or not you're talking about successes or failures.
\n
\nMethod 2: Positive and Negative Margin. Instead of expressing it as MoS 4 or MoF 7, it's simply expressed as Margin 4 and Margin -7. This simplifies comparisons, I think. 4 is definitely higher than -7. Two successes, Margin 6 is better than Margin 4. If it's two failures, Margin -3 is better than Margin -4. The higher margin wins.
\n
\nSo the Question: Which method is easier, more intuitive, better, in your humble, or not-so-humble opinion?

    \n Ralph,
    \n
    \nThere are three types of task resolution in the game (and then the conflict rules, which basically means combat or debate).
    \n
    \nIn some cases, the margin is used only as a color indicator. A bare success would be described differently than a sweeping success. These are the pass fail situations, and usually fall under the single-roll simple tasks. Rarely will margin matter in a simple task, but I'm pretty sure there may be instances I'm not thinking of.
    \n
    \nIn the case of extended tasks, a negative margin moves you away from success, where a positive margin moves you forward. If the extended task is contested, when you move yourself toward success, you move the opponent toward failure, and vice versa.
    \n
    \nIn complex tasks, it's a similar deal, except there's no success point. You're simply trying to get the highest cumulative result you can on a fixed number of rolls. Positive margin increases the result, negative margin reduces the result. Rarely will complex tasks be directly contested; Usually, it will be two parallel complex tasks, with the results compared.
    \n
    \nNegative margin (which I've been using as basically synonymous with Margin of Failure) has a slightly different effect in combat. If your opponent attacks you and succeeds with a higher margin than you, the difference in your margins is used to buy combat effects to use against you. At the moment, the current rules do not allow for the defender being able to buy combat effects, something I think needs to be rectified.
    \n
    \nThe target number is determined by the character's abilities. I'm aware that there's nothing particularly innovative about this as a core mechanic, but it works as a fairly solid base upon which more innovative things can be built. Obviously this makes the idea of describing it as a die roll, or actually having it be a die roll untenable. I'd have to take the entire system back to formula to rework an already fairly solid base mechanic.

    \n Sounds like Spread in Passages (a d20 hack system). Justin was dealing with a similar issue about negatives and such, and his text was straining to work with a +/- system. In the end, I suggested Spread (I might not have come up with the actual term) while editing it, and it essentially did the "absolute value" bit of the math by default.

    So, in short, I think MOS/MOF is the way to go, and it's really intuitive that "Failed by 5" is worse than "Failed by 3" and the additive "Succeed by 4 is 11 better than Failed by 7" process is pretty clear, too (you just add the values, if it's S v F, and you subtract low from high, for comparative degree of S or F).
