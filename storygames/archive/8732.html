\n So, by now we've had a gazillion discussions about how to publish, why to publish, experiences with publishing etc. Mostly very practical, sometimes ideological.

But what does it feel like to publish your game? If you've published a game, what emotions are associated with it - before publishing, at the moment of publication, after publishing? (Do you/did you feel elated, frustrated, happy, angry, sad, empty, edgy, excited...? And at what time?)

    \n It's weird. I've written about half a million words in well over 30 books for one trad RPG publisher alone, but you know what? Not one of those books game me the same sense of achievement that getting MSGtm from MS through to printing through to shipping to IPR has. That is all.

    \n It's an excited feeling for me every time i begin to form my ideas into a book. Each section that gets completed is another step toward a final goal and i have tons of fun developing them. Sure, there are rough patches, like when one change affects 60% of the rest of the work you've already done (had that happen before and it sucks), but it is an enjoyable experience for most of the process.
    \n
    \nAfter publishing, it's actually even better. I like to sell the books so that i have enough to make another of course, but i love the feedback that i receive from readers more than anything else. When i just released my second book for the Apocalypse Prevention, Inc. line (API Worldwide: Canada), it was an awesome feeling that there were people that couldn't wait for it to be released and bought it the first day. Talk about an ego boost.
    \n
    \nWould i do it again? Hells yeah! I hope to make this a part of the rest of my life.

    \n\nFebruary 2009 \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \nBefore: Stomach-wrenching guilt and frustration with myself. Because I'm constantly working on something, there's hardly a time where I don't feel like this in regards to some project.

    During: Even when you have something done, dealing with all the little steps of printing and distribution are a royal pain. Even when I ran off those 50 copies of Geiger Beta and overnighted them to GenCon, that was a lot of stress and scrambling. Also, you inevitably find mistakes in your game after you print it, which is upsetting.

    After: Worrying about whether I should be promoting my products more; worrying about whether I'm even making money of sales of Push 1 to game stores through IPR; worrying about keeping track of sales so I can pay contributors their cut of Push 1; worrying about the next project or how to revise Geiger Beta for the Gamma version. Feeling the responsibility of running the game a lot at conventions, instead of enjoying playing other things.

    Summary: Almost everything about self-publishing sucks for me, which is why I keep trying to get away from it. In contrast, working on Hard Boiled Cultures with Fred was liberatingly easy and invigorating, since we went from concept to finished product in a couple weeks. I'm just not emotionally prepared to do the autuer indie designer thing where you spend a couple years toiling over a 100+ page game. For a while, I felt like there was something deeply wrong with me for not being able to do that, but I'm increasingly trying to turn instead to design and publishing models that work much better for me, instead of feeling required to jump through the hoops I've constructed for myself in my head. I think contest games, roleplaying poems, and stuff like Mexican Standoff, XXXTreme Street Luge, and Ghost/Echo are great pointers in that direction.

    \n I approach publishing with a very craftsman-like ethos, and I am most happy when the finished product is well put together and looks good \u2014 the same sort of feeling that you'd have when you just made, for instance, a good-looking and sturdy chair. However, that's all the design and production side of things; the distribution and sales bit? I really could skip entirely and not feel like I'd lost anything. In fact, annoyance with "getting the word out" and making sales is pushing me in Jonathan's direction, where I'm looking at how I can keep designing and publishing without having to deal with actually selling the damned things.

    \n\nFebruary 2009 \n

    \n
    \n I love it!

    I love the initial puzzle of trying to turn a spark of an idea into a game. I love the rush when I realize that this idea is *good*. I enjoy writing. There's a bad moment when I can't stop thinking about an idea that's not working. I get grumpy.

    I like it when other people spark to my game and want to talk about it and play it. It makes my day shine. When it doesn't happen, the game dies on the vine, no matter how good the idea was. That's sad, but there are always more ideas. There's fear here too. Nobody wants to see their idea wither.

    The logistics of publishing are a meaningful challenge. They aren't fun in their own right, but I enjoy figuring out how to do something I've never done before. When publishing happens at the same time as life, I get stressed. I'm not that much fun to be around.

    Seeing the book in print is weird and beguiling, like finding a baby flower fairy in a basket on your doorstep in the morning.

    Marketing is another puzzle. I find marketing to be a weirdly satisfying process. I claim it's because I like to see my game get to the right people, but I suspect it's actually rooted in vanity. I am a vain person.

    Fulfillment is a minor annoyance, but I do get to communicate with each person that buys my game, no matter how briefly. That takes the sting out of fulfillment and makes it worthwhile.

    Going to Gen Con and actively demo-ing my game to people was deeply fun. It was the most fun part of making How to Host a Dungeon. I don't know if that feeling will stay or not. I think I enjoyed Gen Con a lot more for having a meaningful job to do than I would have if I'd just gone for fun.

    Getting money is nice. It makes me just a little less fearful about the future to know that I'm making some money, no matter how little, at something that's not my primary career.

    All in all, the good massively outweights the bad.
