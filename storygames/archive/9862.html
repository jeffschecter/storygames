    \n Christopher,

    I got it from DriveThru RPG, but, as James noted, WotC went batshit and pulled it. This is a shame because stuff like OSRIC and Swords & Wizardry, despite purporting to be re-imaginings of that same ruleset, flatly aren't. The original D&D is neat little game of negotiating a hostile environment for maximum gain while managing resources (which all boil down to one primary resource: time), while OSRIC and Swords & Wizardry are all about "having an adventure" or something, and import a bunch of unnecessary rules from later D&D editions and supplements that dilute the dynamics of the original game.

    (Don't listen to the yahoos -- there's nothing unplayable about it. There's parts of it that have no actual use, like all that crap for raising armies and building castles, but it contains a complete, playable game if you keep to the dungeons.)

    \n Yeah, I'd just like to add another voice to dispell the "unplayability" myth surrounding the LBBs. They are certainly poorly written and badly organized, but if you sit down and read them through, you will find a completely playable and totally complete game. Yes, there are points of ambiguity and gaps here and there you have to paper over, but they actually aren't that large.

    That said, the attraction of S&W (whether core or whitebox) is that they've done much of the cleaning up and organizing for you. However, there is stuff in the LBBs that *isn't* in S&W, so it's still an interesting exercise to engage with them. Stuff like explicit procedures for dungeoneering and wilderness adventures. The latest version of S&W core did add some of this stuff in, I'm not sure if S&W whitebox has it.

    Posted By: Marshall Burnswhile OSRIC and Swords & Wizardry are all about "having an adventure" or something, and import a bunch of unnecessary rules from later D&D editions and supplements that dilute the dynamics of the original game.
    \nUm, that's a weird characterization.

    First of all OSRIC is a straight repackaging of AD&D1e: so not OD&D at all.

    S&W Whitebox is a clean up of *just* the LBB rules. If it commits any sin, it is the sin of omission. As I mention above the LBB contain pretty explicit procedures for adventuring, much of which aren't included.

    S&W Core includes a few very judicious additions from Greyhawk: variable weapon damage, fast hit point progression, and more spells (hey, magic missle!). In the latest revision, they also added back a big chunk of those missing dungeoneering procedures (although not the wilderness adventure stuff).

    So I would argue that S&W gets you pretty dang close (I dunno what all this "having an adventure" stuff is about) to what Marshall is talking about. The LBB do have a little more though.

    \n In defense of the "unplayability" of OD&D, I can see where folks are coming from in denying that harsh label, since obviously several folks (myself included) are playing it just fine. But in the context of its original appearance, its hard to deny that out-of-the-box, rules-as-written, and without any existing experience with RPG's (or wargaming) the three little books were a ludicrously arcane text. A general shape of play can be gathered from them, but much of the details are left up to freeform interpretation of the details. Indeed, this freeform aspect is a large appeal of playing OD&D.

But in the context of its original appearance, its hard to deny that out-of-the-box, rules-as-written, and without any existing experience with RPG's (or wargaming) the three little books were a ludicrously arcane text. A general shape of play can be gathered from them, but much of the details are left up to freeform interpretation of the details. Indeed, this freeform aspect is a large appeal of playing OD&D.
    Of course, they were published by people known for their minatures wargames, and it part was a tie in to an existing product (Chainmail). So arguably, they were reasonably playable for their target audience. Certainly the group of wargamers that I was hanging out with at the MITSGS in 1974 had no real problem figuring out how to play the game in several different ways. (Interestingly enough, one of the early forms of play that didn't last was wandering around in the wilderness with large numbers of men-at-arms until you saved up enough gold to build a keep.)

    \n Peter, I had an old Apple II game called, I think, simply enough "Wilderness Adventures".
    \n
    \nIt was pretty much a D&D; inspired, wander around killing monsters game, but its thing was very much managing a troop of men at arms to do your killing for you.
    \n
    \nThe fighting was pretty simple, what was far more involved was the outfitting of the troop with various armor and weapon combos (each of which had strengths and weaknesses vs. different monster types), organizing them into different formations, and figuring out how to keep them fed and paid. Also you had to figure out how to transport all the loot.
    \n
    \nThings like Long bows being super powerful, but hiring men who knew how to use them was expensive. If you managed to take control of a castle you'd get that castle's knights in your warband. They wouldn't cost anything but if they died they couldn't be replaced.
    \n
    \nI mention this because I think that game was rooted in the same spirit as all of those Henchman and Hirelings rules in the old games. I totally found that fun.

    Posted By: Peter AronsonOf course, they were published by people known for their minatures wargames, and it part was a tie in to an existing product (Chainmail). So arguably, they were reasonably playable for their target audience.
    \nFair enough, but part of OD&D's eccentric charm is how it exploded past the limited target audience it was originally intended for, to large numbers of people who had never heard of Chainmail or even wargaming and thus had to guess at all the system's unstated assumptions. Its kind of the same thing now, as new retro-gamers with no connection to 1970's wargaming try to make sense of the text; and I think that's the context in which James was warning of OD&D's unplayability. It really is a play experience best picked up by playing with an existing group, not by a cold reading of the text.
