\n is the rubbish title for a fantasy project I'm undertaking.

The premise: you are a Warden, a mystical guardian of the land. You are imbued with unique abilities, divided into archetypes (known as roots), and summoned to protect the land and its traditions where threatened.

The vibe: robin hood/fairytale land ruled by an oppressive royalty and plagued by monsters. Darkness is robbing the land of its seasons. When called upon by a seer Wardens are bound by oath to resolve an issue, be it protecting a village from the wildhunt, rescuing one of the last unicorns from a Royal hunt, or mediating between the living and the dead.

Wardens are drawn from ordinary folk by the Seer community (the descendants of the old magic, outlawed by the Royals - like the BBC tv series Merlin). Such folk are given a root, a plant that will grow inside them. This transforms them into something alien, a force imbued with the power of the Hearth, the land's ancient heart. The type of root given will determine their 'class': Raven (spirits/mystic), Wolf (ranger), Oak (defence/support), Sparrow (interaction/influence) or Stag (warrior).

My question: i had the notion that each adventure would begin with a ritual. This is the Seer summoning the Wardens from their 'sleep' (whatever it is they do when there are no problems to solve - downtime, essentially) and binding them, by oath, to help. This process, represented with dice rolls or whatever, generates a pool of points. THis is used by the Wardens to purchase starting resources including what i call 'Branches', connections to the mortal world they can draw on to help. The rest becomes Life (hp essentially), but shared communally. Wardens survive until all points are spent at which point all is lost.

So what I would like to know is how alien can I make these beings? Should they remain fundamentally mortal and able to interact while not 'in costume', like Batman or something? Or should they be more like the characters you'd get in a game like Whispering Vault, where you remain fundamentally alien and the adventures are almost always based around the ritual and the resolution?

If that makes sense, thanks.

    \n Yeah, make that one of the variables played with in the game. Maybe younger Wardens are more human, and they tend to become more inhuman over time. At first you're just a superhero with a secret civilian identity, and at the end, after burning away everything from you human life, you're nothing more than a magical spirit.

    It could be as simple as determining say three "stages" of development (human, fey and alien) and giving them all some appropriate character crunch (I get the impression that this is one of those games). Then say that all characters start human, but the player can choose to mature them one stage whenever for a one-time power boost (and the changes that come with the next stage, of course). So players can keep hold of the inherent humanity of their characters, or they can throw that away, or they can only give it up under the gravest duress.

    Maybe every time you die you mature one "stage", on top of the above.

    \n So, I have some system ideas. Some of these I've posted before in a less coherent form elsewhere on the internets. That is: I may have posted them here but I can't remember!

    1. Encounters

    I'm inspired for this in part by PAX's Thornwatch boardgame. In particular the modular grpahic novel style board elements. These look really cool. Also someone pointed me toward the 'Index Card RPG' which seems to use similar ideas. I happen to think these are cool. I also happen to like using boardgamey elements for rpg design these days (not that I'm any professional nor expert). This is because I like the idea of codifying how games play as much as possible. Similar to what Blades in the Dark does: it sets out how it plays and how things are done in specific terms, rather than just here's the resolution system off you go, and don't forget rule zero (which I've alwasy found a huge copout).

    So I posit designing adventures on the basis of a series of encounters using boards/cards to represent things in a very graphic way, as text free as possible.

    Each encounter has a Threat value and a Key. The latter determines what actions are required to beat it, the Threat is how many successes or 'damage'. from those actions, from the players, is required. This is deliberately nebulous: the Key could be a boss enemy type, or it could be an actual giant locked door guarded by enemies where defeating them doesn't affect the Threat. Such monsters are considered ancillary.

    Monsters don't track hit points. Instead they are either represented by Threat, or they are Ancillary. The latter are defeated on any successful attack, barring unique abilities in their necessarily simple writeups. For example, an Orc would be a basic hit-and-done foe, while a Walking Skeleton might have an ability to, if defeated as an Ancillary foe, gets to come back immediately one time.

    The GM will have a resource pool, Darkness, more on this later. One of its uses is to grant any Ancillary foe this ability.

    Ancillary monsters are a distraction, they are a challenge, but not THE challenge. They can create interesting conflict for the players. Isn't that the point of enemies anyway? They can make it harder for them to target the Threat value.

    Example Encounter Off the Top of My Head: The Goatsblood Shrine.

    So the GM lays down an encounter board, a gorgeously illustrated dark and foreboding ancient copse on top of a lonely hill, like something Shub Niggurath would draw.

    The GM informs the players this is the Goatsblood Shrine and it is surrounded by dancing cultists, summoning a demon. The Threat represents the spell of summoning and the cultists are merely ancillary foes to stop the players disrupting that ritual. The key: casting spells to disrupt the darkling sorcery at work here. One player, having already acquired the Book of the Goat, must read from it and incant a spell using his Sorcery skill. Doing so deals damage to the Threat. The cultists will want to stop him, and the players must stop them doing that.

    This is a timed Threat (so perhaps not a good example): if the players cannot deal enough damage in X turns then the ritual completes successfully. Perhaps another way to do this would be for the GM to add 1 Threat each turn until the THreat builds up to X at which point the ritual triggers, though this seems unfair to the player who will find his efforts just being undone by the GM adding Threat. But it's only an example.

    This leads to Advantage:

    In the game certain actions can confer Advantage to the players, or leave them Disadvantaged if they screw up or fail to defend etc.

    While Advantaged relevant actions do increased outcomes - eg Threat damage.
    While Disadvantaged relevant actions are made at 1 less die. That is, they are harder to pull off, but doing so doesn't cheat the player on the outcome he's thusly earned.

    \n I think that this sounds a lot like a superhero thing, so perhaps you should think of it like that - sort of like WoD, but instead of gloomy monster-angst you go "isn't it glorious to be a success in life".

    Then again, if you don't want to do the Peter Parker thing where the superheroic goals and concerns are complicated by human affairs, it might be better to put the supposed humanity of the player characters entirely out of the picture. Just focus on how to be the best mystical troubleshooter you can be.

    A thought: some rare games treat the player character not as a protagonist of their own story, but rather as a witness or background character of somebody else's story. (Examples: Drifter's Escape, Spione, Dirty Secrets, Acts of Evil, Valravnar for Asagrimmr, arguably D&D 4th edition...) If it were the case for you that you wanted these Wardens to not have personal human drama, something like this could be the thing for you. The Wardens do the fighting and struggling, but they do it for the sake of some NPCs, different ones for each adventure.
