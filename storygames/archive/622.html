La Ludo Lernanta Lingvo (LLLL)

Josh Roby
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games
This is a "Serious Game" whose intention is to teach the players Esperanto. One player takes on the role of the Ludmastro or game master; all others take on the role of Ludisto, or player.

The Ludmastro starts with a pretty large vocabulary printed on a sheet in front of him. He presents the ludistoj with objectives to acheive and challenges that they must overcome.

Ludistoj begin with a character sheet that has only a handful of words on it. They must use these words to overcome the Ludmastro's challenges. Players can speak their native language all they want, but what they say in that language never has any impact on the fictional world. However, anything that the players say in Esperanto immediately happens in the fiction -- even errors.

Play goes around the table. Everyone must start their Esperanto statements with kaj (and) or sed (but). The Ludmastro can translate what the other players say or he can remain enigmatic to let them discover what error's they've made.

Example of Play:

Ludmastro: All right. Your objective is: get out! And we start: Alis et Bob estas en salono.
Alice: Um, okay, that sounded like our names, and (consulting her sheet) 'estas' means 'is' and 'salono' is 'room', so we must be in a room. Okay, um. Oh, here's 'porto', which means 'door.' Here goes. Kaj salono estas porto. Did I get that right?
Ludmastro: That's interesting. And the room is a door? Okay. You must be in an antechamber to somewhere.
Alice: Darn! I wanted there to be a door!
Bob: Okay, my turn. It looks like 'staras' is 'stand' so, um... Sed porto staras en salono.
Ludmastro: (Nods.) Kaj la porto estas ferma.
Alis: Okay, that's a new word, that's... (consults sheet) ... oh, closed. The door is closed. So obviously, since we want to get outside, I'll want to open it, and that's... 'malfermas'... Kaj Alis malfermas la porto.
Ludmastro: La porto estas malferma!
Bob: Malferma is... open, so... ooo! Kaj Bob marshas tra porto!
Ludmastro: Congratulations, Bob, you make it out of the room. You get first pick of the next set of words.

Whenever the players acheive an objective, the Ludmastro gives them a new set of words to add to their sheets. Whoever made the "winning" statement gets to pick one word out of the new set that they get and the others do not; she writes that down on her sheet. She can use it freely, but she cannot use its translation in her native language. The other players can try and pick up on what the secret word is, and the player can even try and explain it to them in Esperanto.

The end result should be players creating sentences both correctly and incorrectly, with their mistakes making their ongoing story more interesting. If it works the way I'd like it to, there'd be lots of madcap developments and the players would build a rather collaborative puzzle-solving mood.

    To teach written Chinese, I would probably start with a few radicals, which are the ideographical units that combine to form characters. The most famous incarnation of this is probably:

    Mu (Tree)
    http://www.zhongwen.com/d/164/x236.htm

    Lin (Forest)
    http://www.zhongwen.com/d/170/x76.htm

    Sen (Overwhelming Foliage, Jungle)
    http://www.zhongwen.com/d/180/x203.htm

    Teach a few radicals and then players get to try to combine them to form meaningful characters. And you could teach fun stuff like proper stoke order along the way. Teaching spoken Chinese would be a somewhat different game, with learning Pinyin (the phoneticization system), learning tones, etc. The fun part of the design would be trying to teach all of this at once in a way that didn't overwhelm new language learners.
