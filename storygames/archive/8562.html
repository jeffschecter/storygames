\n Rather than derail another thread, I'll raise this question here. What sorts of setting details inspire you and how do they do it?

I mentioned being inspired by npcs, trying to find the conceptual threads that tie them together, then extrapolating from that into a larger world. What I didn't mention is that I find that the most minor details, the ones that have to do with everyday life, that inspire the most interesting stories. For example, the NPCs in the adventure Deep Trouble for Over the Edge have dissasociative personality disorder (multiple personalities). Individual personalities may be tied to antagonistic, competing conspiracies, but it is how they greet each other that really inspires me. Because they may switch personalities several times over a night, a gathering may have one person greet everyone multiple times per meeting. This fits with the setting's focus on subcultures that often seem confusing to outsiders but perfectly sensible to members. That theme can not only be carried beyond the Deeps, but it can bring about subtle conflicts and adventures that intrigue me much more than the struggle of conspiracies that dominates the setting.

So, what about you? What do you find inspiring and how? Maps? Shopping lists? Timelines? Something else?

    \n I found it amusing to understand after a couple of similar discussions that most of the people like Big Ideas, while I am definitely a fan of Little Ideas.

    By Big Ideas I mean something that really defines the setting, like "this is a post-apocalyptic world with winged dwarves everywhere and universities fighting one another by hiring pirate ninjas", or, I don't know, "in this town everyone has a religion of his own, is either a deity or a high priest of their tiny shrine they keep in the backyard". While this kind of statements seem to set the tone of the game quite well, for me they are nothing more that a classification necessity of marginal interest. (And my own setting does not have that One Sentence That Defines It All, which makes it extremely hard sometimes to explain it to new players, by the way).

    Little Ideas are something insignificant on a large scale but something non-trivial, something that exemplifies those Big Ideas to prove that they are indeed cool. And I do mean Little Ideas with emphasis on "ideas", they gotta be interesting and inspiring. Not necessarily unique, but inspiring. Fluff does it better usually, but it's not a strict requirement, as some crunchy bits can also be exciting. Give me an example of a pirate ninja being hired by a professor to meddle in his rival's research, and I'm sold.

    Illustrations do it unsurprisingly well for me. If a picture is good, it's almost certain that I'll have the feeling that I want to use it in my game, and the variety of ways how I can use it. (yet again, illustrations are drawn Little Ideas) For instance, that's how I found use for D&D4 in my campaign

    \n I approach a setting as both a player and as a GM. In each role, I want different things.

    As a player, I want to know where I fit in. The setting should inspire me by drawing me into that world. Who am I here? Who can I be? And who might I become? I like a hint of mystery, and I enjoy colorful details that give me the sense that the world is bigger than I am, even when I realize that those details will likely elude the light of play time. I like mechanical bits that let me tint my "normal" character so that it fits into this world. I like options that let me do something different than usual by virtue of playing in this setting.

    As a GM, I want to know how to use the setting. I want neither to be too constrained nor too free. A setting must give me structure so that I know I am going down the right path, but it also needs to give me a toolkit to customize the setting to suit my preferences. A setting should inspire my GMing muse. Give me ideas that send my brain spinning down a creative path. Don't spell it all out for me, but do take care of the unpleasant chores. For example, a D&D setting might give me ten plot ideas for using a certain monster, but it needs to give me well-considered stat blocks for those monsters. Also, show me how to use the setting to engage players.

    \n This thread reminded me of something. When I was a kid and used to color, I'd color in all the people or animals (or talking trains or whatever) in a picture, and never color the backgrounds. My teachers all thought I was a freak.

    I guess part of that still carries over, because good NPCs sell me on setting the way few other things do. Not that I'll always even use the NPCs in game, mind you, and mostly when I'm a player meeting named NPCs is dull.

    But NPCs as exemplars of the setting, NPCs that give details on how people live by showing those things being lived, NPCs that give a sense of the scope and reach of the world in their position and reaction, NPCs that tell me what the game is about and what the PCs should be doing... these things are magic mojo.

    \n I have a theory about settings and Myers-Briggs personality types.

    So you have four main personality type groups: Rationals (NT), Artisans (SP), Idealists (NF), and Guardians (SJ).


    Rationals (NT = abstract utilitarians) want stuff they can use, but they focus on the big concepts. Too many details can be overwhelming. They want the details to be stuff used at a specific time and they don't want to sift through long prose for important high-level facts.

    Artisans (SP = concrete utilitarians) love details, but only the useful details. There are probably a lot of "gearheads" in this group, so they like crunchy mechanical stuff in their setting.

    Idealists (NF = abstract cooperators) want story and characters. They want to understand first and foremost the social connections, but at a high level. I'm thinking factions and important people, roles and races. They don't like to nail too much down. They prefer ideas to specifics.

    Guardians (SJ = concrete cooperators) want story and characters, too, but they like the specifics. Who did what to whom -- when, and why, and how? They'll read a timeline and find it valuable, where an abstract player might skim it.

    Of course, all personality-type stuff is a gross generalization, but I think the most important lesson to take from M-B analysis is that people think in different ways. A writer can focus heavily on writing for one or two types (their own, most likely), or try to write for all types by saying things in different ways and by organizing things in multiple ways.

    P.S. If you insist that you can't be pigeon-holed into a Myers-Briggs type (even by one's own analysis--forget the tests), then I'd put down 2:1 odds that you're some kind of NF, especially an INFP. Ha.

    \n Graham,
    \n RE: Your OP.
    \n
    \n To me its facets that bring the setting alive. This usually means two things to me:
    \n1) Groups are real, the various cultures have multiple factions. The idea of a homogeneous culture that all believes and behaves the same way is sort of absurd and even if it is realistic, its definitely boring.
    \n2) Some indication of what daily life is like. Is this an agrarian society? How does trade, communication, transmission of news or other setting details work?
    \n
    \n Also, I like plot hooks or unexplained mysteries as well.
    \n
    \n For me, stuff that doesn't matter:
    \n1) Numbers - I don't care if it has 1 culture or 100
    \n2) Maps - Maps can be helpful, but its not a deciding factor
    \n3) History/Timeline - Unless it adds to the tension between factions or informs why the daily life in this setting is different from life today it is just minutiae I am not interested in.
    \n4) Signature characters - I hate sig characters from Elrond to Mask of Winters, these signature characters can have a rel negative impact on the players ability to interact with and/or impact the setting.
    \n5) Implied setting - Implied setting is great if its a genre with well established genre conventions. But I think it breaks down when it is a genre that can be re-interpreted (like steampunk can imply magic or no magic, gun powder or no gun powder or victorian or not Victorian, etc.).
    \n6) In setting fiction - In fact, I never read these.
    \n
    \n I hope this helps.
    \nDave M
