    Shreyas,

    Discovering your pricing structure is a real trial and error process. I have, just this year, found numbers and methods I am happy with and it took 3 years or so to get here.

    First, here's a question you have to ask yourself. What is your objective? Is it to make a living while creating art? Is it to get a little closer to making a living creating art? Or is it to just have fun creating art? This is key and it determines how flexible you are. I'm paying the bills with my art, so I may be more demanding than other artists in the business doing it for a hobby. Everything is relative.

    Very important. I have discovered that you HAVE to time your art to really determine anything. I have a free project timing program that I downloaded. I time everything from initial research to the final and I keep records of the results. This tells me alot. It allows me to more accurately schedule and price. Time your work and, if your objective is to support yourself, you know how much you NEED to make for your art. (Regardless of Industry Standards.)

    Industry standards are $100 for full-page black & white, $50 for half-page black & white and $25 for quarter-page black & white. Color generally doubles a price. I do not keep to these standards myself for lots of reasons, but it's good to keep this in mind when pricing. This is generally what publishers expect to pay.

    I don't post my rates in public, but I will e-mail you my current 8.5x11 rates breakdown if you like. (Though the page breakdown is only a guide. Each project is unique.)

    I hope this has been helpful. Please feel free to ask questions if anything is unclear.

    Pricing by the size seems to be standard, but if you're going in the direction of working with people you consider something akin to peers rather than big companies, I'd price using a scale of time and effort instead. Be up front with your client, tell them why you're charging what you are and they'll probably be more satisfied with what they end up getting. Pricing by size seems so arbitrary and I think it usually means one party, the artist or the client, is going to get ripped off, whether they realise it or not (unless the client is easily impressed or the artist is hard up on cash).

    Some of the elements to consider that'll add to the time and effort spent on a piece of art:
    - Perspective (complex cityscapes, no matter how small the illustration is, are going to slooow you down unless you're some kind of savant)
    - Colour (hand-painting/inking can be very time-intensive while colouring in something like Photoshop might be fairly simple)
    - Complexity of the composition (Squeezing a set number of objects into a certain space can be tricky)
    - Level of client input (In other words, the more picky the client intends to be, the more you should charge. Of course some degree of editing is to be expected, but micro-managing will sap the will of any artist and deserves a surcharge)

    Again, these are guidelines for more casual stuff, where negotiation is an option. The big guys probably aren't going to give a shit one way or the other, since it's more of a buyer's market out there, what with the number of desperate artists out there.

    And I'm speaking hypothetically here, I should say. I've done some freelance illustration, but always for charity, more or less. The money is gravy and I do it either for fun, because I want to help the client or because the exposure is better than the money would be.

    I agree with Keith, price your work on a case by case basis.

    Pricing based on size and complexity doesn't make sense anyway, because when you need to draw something complex like a town scene on a quarter page, it's often much easier to draw it bigger and ajust the size in Photoshop.
