\n In the past several years, there has developed a strand of roleplaying game design that breaks with a lot of the traditions of older game design. Roughly speaking, I'm talking about the design and play traditions that fed into The Forge, were created on it, or came out of it by one or more generations' distance from it (in other words, games influenced by games influenced by (etc.) games developed on The Forge).

What features do these games tend to have? What kinds of things make you look at a game and go, "Yep, it's one of those?"

I realize that this may be a difficult-to-parse question, so maybe my personal examples will help illuminate it:

* Emphasis on what's interesting rather than what's physically possible
* De-emphasized combat, as compared to most games in RPG history
* All players have roughly the same level of power at the table (this doesn't mean identical power, it just means roughly equal)
* Structured play, especially structures around scenes, stories, and plots
* Short-run, lower-session-count games, as compared to....
* Design that focuses on what the game is about
* Exclusive design rather than all-encompassing design; that is to say design where the mechanics touch the things that the game cares about, rather than attempting to encompass everything that might happen during a game
* Shorter pre-game prep time
* Lack of a GM
* De-emphasis or lack of pre-planning of a session's material (e.g., "GM plot")
* De-emphasis or lack of secrets between players of the game

(There are many more I think.)

A few disclaimers/notes/thread mod thingies:

* Identity politics are not welcome here. I'm not talking about a litmus test of what is and what is not ideologically pure. I'm looking for a description of common elements, trends, themes that you see in these games.
* I don't want to argue about whether this design area exists. I think it does, and it seems like an entirely uncontroversial statement to me.
* These things don't need to be present in every game.
* I'm not looking for a grand unified theory, the common denominator, I'm looking for recurrent features.
* This is descriptive, not proscriptive.
* I don't want to argue about what the games should be called. Forge-derived? Indie? Hippy? Swine? Fligleflorp? I don't care and if you want to argue about that, as Swamp Thing said, "Do not bring your evil here."
* If someone says "this makes it one of those" don't argue with them. I want their opinions, not Truth.
* That said, if someone offers something and you don't understand it, asking for clarification is totally in-bounds

    \n Things that stand out for me as "indie" or "Forgey" or whatfuckingever in RPGs:

    * Greater interest in arriving at thematic truths, rather than achieving likely/realistic physics-oriented outcomes
    * Direct manipulation of player psychology, typically through game economics
    * A high level of abstraction in how conflicts are fought and resolved
    * Greater expectation of player authorship, and the responsibility that comes with that
    * Lots of short-form play (1 to 3 sessions, done in 1-4 hour increments) with little setup time
    * A general rejection of pre-play GM preparation, particularly plotlines or hooks
    * A generally high level of self-awareness ("meta" awareness) baked into the play process
    * A requirement to come at the mechanics via the fiction, rather than via tactical play (which, done poorly, sometimes creates a conflict of interest with bullet #1)
    * Subversion or outright rejection of empowerment fantasy
    * Frequent attempts to approach more "serious" subject matter
    * Poorly playtested and stress-tested rules texts, as compared to the development regimen you find at more established publishers
    * Designer Not Included
    * A requirement that you play the rules in their proper spirit, and with that a general disregard for trying to mechanically balance the rules against players who break that spirit

    ( I SWEAR TO GOD I'm not trying to start a fight or be provocative on those last three items. But my tolerance for rules fuzziness -- and my expectation that I have to come at the rules with a firm understanding of what the designer's intent was behind them -- is much, much higher when that IPR envelope shows up at my doorstep. And, I have to admit, I'm getting fonder of the final bullet as time goes on.)

    \nI'll try and jot down some aspects, as promised. These games...
    \n
    \n1) ...are actually designed to work as written.
    \n
    \n2) ...don't demand the role of game designer to the GM (corollary to 1). This is the same as "they don't have the 'the GM will figure it out' rule"
    \n
    \n3) ...are the product of an actual game design process (instead of relying on assumed 'default standard practices')
    \n
    \n4) ...have rules that explicitly tell the players how to play that game
    \n
    \n5) ...are the product of a post-"supplement treadmill" culture: if they even have supplements those are never 'required'
    \n
    \n6) ...have dropped the social role of the GM as leader. If they have one, the GM can have any kind of different powers in the game rules, but never power on the social level, above the rules.
    \n
    \n7) ...don't demand that one player at the table assume the role of the entertainer for the evening.
    \n
    \n8) (corollary and consequence of 1,2,6,7) Are really fun to GM: if you GM you are still playing, not fulfilling a role that is "work". Also, they are easier and less scary to GM: people who would have never GMed try, and sometimes discover they are very good GMs!

    \n In my opinion the analytical basis for all of these differences is solitary and comprehensive: if you take the concept of "traditional" literally and realize that there is, in fact, a tradition of mainstream roleplaying game design, then the difference becomes obvious in that Forge-style games all share a willingness to look behind that tradition and discard its assumptions. In my experience this is the lowest common denominator for Forge games vs. traditional design, the one from which all other differences accrue. I sometimes call these new games "progressive" to emphasize this difference in fundamental design method.

    (Of course, this does not mean that all non-traditional games are Forgean. Lately we've even had Forgean games that have not been created from the first principles, but rather by emulating the games that originally were. So from that viewpoint I could say that we're looking at a new tradition.)

    More on topic, Robert had:
    * All players have roughly the same level of power at the table [...]

    More generally, I'd say this: There is a continuum from games with no differentiation of player role (do any actually exist?), to those with small, temporary differentiation (Roach, Fiasco, Penny, "Happy Birthday, Robot!", etc), to those with permanent but still relatively democratic differentiation (InSpectres, Houses of the Blooded), to those with marked differentiation but narration rights still in everyone's hands (Dogs, maybe Mouse Guard), to those with marked differentiation with a GM ultimately responsible for maintaining the fiction (Vampire, D&D, etc).

    "Forge games" tend to come from the left end of that continuum, and especially, they tend to stake out its leftmost extreme. Democratic contribution to the fiction is a hallmark of the style. Rules that say "winner narrates" are very Forgey.

    I'd also like to re-postulate my thesis that "Forge games" are postmodern, or react to postmodernism.

    \n JD, you are forgetting the big elephant in the middle of the room: RULE ZERO. Or "the golden rule": : "the GM is above the rules". This is true in almost every "traditional" rpg (with very few exceptions like the James Bond RPG). And it's NOT true in every Forge game I know. This is the BIG difference, that you are willfully ignoring.

    The GM in dogs is a "normal" GM? Really?
    Tell me, in "traditional" rpgs, the GM is FORCED, by RULES, to "say yes or roll the dice"? Has a LIMITED pool of resources for conflicts? Has RULES about NPC creation that he has to follow? has RULES about scenario (town) creation?
    Do you know that, in Italian forums, where they were not "prepared" by the previous Forge rpgs to accept these things, the initial general reaction was "this is NOT a RPG", and "it's impossible to GM a game in this way", and "who wrote this game really hate GMs"?

    Let's call some rules for this discussion.

    1) "what is a game system?". I consider a game's system the one given by the lumpley principle: the procedure really utilized to change the SIS. No matter what the game manual says.

    2) with very few exceptions, the procedure utilized by EVERY "traditional" rpg is ALWAYS THE SAME: "ask the GM, and the GM will tell you". We can call this single game "mother may I". The so-called "rules" inside the game manual aren't real rules at all, they are "suggestions": the proof is that the player can't use them without the GM' permission.

    Talking about systems, and single rules, we have not the "rule zero", but the "system zero", the system of Mother-May-I

    This system has the following characteristic:
    1) the world is always descripted by the GM, even to the littlest scenographic elements: the player can't say "I grab a horse outside the saloon", he has to ask the GM before: "GM, there is a horse outside the saloon?". The player can't add nothing to the game world, he has to ask the GM first.
    2) The results from the rolls (or the resolution system in general) are always narrated by the GM
    3) The players can use the resolution system to do something, but not to achieve something or get a specific result. It's always at the GM's whim and discretion what happen as a result or achievement after the player do (or fail to do) his action (task resolution)
    4) It's always the GM that say when the player can roll the dice (or use the game specific resolution system), or the action is impossible, and it's the GM who say WHEN you roll, TO DO WHAT and say to you if you CAN use your traits or what you have on the character sheet.
    5) The GM can change any rule, anytime, anywhere, at whim, without having to tell anybody about it.
    6) The resolution system is at least in part "secret", allowing the GM to ignore the real results and choose the results that he want instead. This is NOT considered "cheating" in system-zero, but it's considered "good GM-ing" even if the players are not informed about this GM's "power", and even if they would not agree and they would really be enraged if they will know. (the adherence to an abstract "good way to GMing" is considered better than reaching an agreement among the players about the rules, in "system-zero". It's not only allowed, but even told as an advice, in "traditional" rpg books, to lie to players about the real rules used)

    Now, the real problem in determining the common characteristics of Forge games, is that it's "traditional" rpgs that have this COMMON system, not Forge games. How can you define "every other game system apart from this single one?"

    Like that: Forge games are not system zero. Almost every single "traditional" rpg in commerce IS. They are, almost all, exactly always the same exact game system (system zero), with the same experience of play ("Mother-may-I")

    This has a problem: give little credit to the few games that, even when everybody else jumped on the bandwagon, refused to go to system-zero-land, as James Bond RPGs, or even, for clear historical reasons, OD&D (but AD&D 1st was already system-zero, the DMG is really clear on this. Vampire really only went a little further on Gygax's path). And to games are not "traditional" but not "forge" either.

    The first ones can be considered a more limited subset of system-zero, they usually only have the first 4 characteristics, and a more limited form of the 5th that allow the GM to make on-the-spot rulings, but not in secret. They could be called "old school" and we could leave it at that.

    About new games that are not tied to the forge... it's becoming difficult to say exactly what it's forge-influenced these days or not. once upon a time, if you were forge-influenced, you were on the forge, period. Now, someone could not even know that the forge exist, but could have played some game of Dogs in the Vinweyard, and be inspired to write a game of his own, with conflict resolution, fortune in the middle, shared narrational authority, limited reasources for the GM, and the game could be at the end a coherent narrativist design... what we could call this game? It's a forge game? Even if the designer don't even know that the forge exist? It's not? Even if is a compilation of second-hand forge techniques and design philosophies?

    Everybody draw the line differently, I suppose. For example, I consider Don't Rest Your Head a Forge game, and Spirit of the Century not, but the line is becoming more and more blurred with time.

    Robert, I think that this thread could go one way or the other: if you want to define what forge games ARE, I think we can't do it in any meaningful way without considering what they AREN'T (system-zero), and this could drown this thread in flames. Or we could consider the evolution of forge games, historically, to see how all this happened, even if this will lead to consider games like D&D4. Tell us what you prefer.

      Posted By: Robert BohlDe-emphasized combat, as compared to most games in RPG history
      \nI don't find this to be true at all. Instead, the rules emphasize what the game is about. Combat is still a big part of many (Dogs, In A Wicked Age, Capes, Burning Wheel, 3:16). However, the rules for combat rarely overshadow rules for other types of conflict, or personal or dramatic changes, or what have you. At least they usually are given precisely equal weight - usually because the mechanic is the same. But in some games, like Burning Wheel and 3:16: combat still plays a pretty big role.

      And D&D 4E essentially has killed any huge division between "indie" and "mainstream". Because D&D is the most mainstream game there is. It's also hugely indie/forge/whatever in its design. Each rule is very clear and focused. The game as a whole has a main drive of gaining power though adventure conflict, and everything else helps build towards.

      That's because there's one thing that made Forge games unique for the better part of the decade. The game designers were actually looking at the goals of the game and making rules to support those goals. Before that, all our RPGs were looking at other games and making tweaks and changes. More detailed combat here, more detailed character creation there, more variety in character actions/skills/abilities here. Brownie points for making jokes. Removal of death for cartoons. And so forth.

      Then the Forge style sweeps through and for the first time, really starts culling these assumptions. We get new games that get rid of separate combat rules because it's not important. Games that get rid of the GM because that's not important. Gets rid of weapon charts or power lists because those aren't important. And of course, all the while, new stuff is added in like always.

      And now we've started to come full circle, 4E defined a central goal for the game and then dumped a huge amount of accumulation from D&D that did not contribute to that central goal. And then completely new systems were added back in to help support that central goal better. It looks like Warhammer Fantasy Roleplay may be following suit (though perhaps leanring more from board games than indie games, we'll see). There's still a market for generalist design, but specific design has grown out of its indie roots. Much like rap in the 80's, we're no longer just playing on the streets my homies, and while there's still room for other styles of play, our influence will be felt and will permanently change the landscape of gaming.

      - integrate design and content
      - are relatively inexpensive

      - have indifferent art
      - aren't edited to any great degree
      - require good knowledge of indie games to be run
      - work only if played a certain way, typically the gaming style of the designers group

      Posted By: ccreitzIsn't it quintessentially post-modern to say "Let's make a story using this theoretical framework!"? That is, isn't the game text an artifact best engaged within that tradition?
      ...not in my head. That's quintessentially structuralist, especially when you add in shades of one-true-wayism and begin to think that "this theoretical framework" is the only way to make a "real" story (or a story of artistic merit, however you choose to privilege your praxis). Compound that with the fact that storygamers don't really play exclusively from the book, but from the book and a generous helping of their Forge-derived collection of techniques from other games, and you get a pretty potent canon that is followed rather strictly.

      At best, an ever-evolving pastiche of play techniques drawn from many different sources might be creeping towards something resembling postmodern play. Now, most Forge-derived players are probably leaping up to claim that they do that right now, but... no, not really. A perspective that encompasses a handful of perspectives (rather than just one perspective like those filthy trad players) isn't really that broad at all \u2014 not when there are literally millions of perspectives you can draw from the rest of the world's history, art, science, and current events.

      And just to be clear: I'm counting myself among the beggars with a handful of perspectives. I don't think I've ever contemplated what pomo play would look like, let alone attempted it. It would be... dizzying, I think.\n
      Up one more level: what about games whose texts are written in the light of self-awareness? Is there a better place than postmodernism to stand and take in KPfS?
      Irony only gets you one pomo point, dude. Consider: both kpfs and Sorcerer are responses to and challenges against "modern" game design as exemplified by Vampire. However, whereas Sorcerer is in many respects a reactionary response ("this is how you do a real story, as told by this playwright from the 40s"), you are right that kpfs is an ironic recontextualization of Vampire's setting and system. Now, I have not played, but from what AP reports I have read, play still tends to progress as a linear and straightforward character drama, challenging nothing especially. The book is built out of irony, but I do not get the sense that play can claim the same.

      Calling kpfs postmodern is like claiming To the Lighthouse is postmodern. Certainly it is founded on a conscious distrust of what has come before, but that's a theme common to both modern and postmodern thought. To get to postmodernism, you really need to break out of the structures of before by exploding assumptions, not just calling them out and giggling at them.

      \n From a very cursory reading of the wikipedia article on literary structuralism, I daresay that role playing games are in fact highly structuralist, as is the Forge approach of applying the Big Model to play.

      For mine and others' benefit:

      In literary theory, structuralism is an approach to analyzing the narrative material by examining the underlying invariant structure, which is based on the linguistic sign system of Ferdinand de Saussure. The structuralists claim that there must be a structure in every text, which explains why it is easier for experienced readers than for non-experienced readers to interpret a text. Hence, they say that everything that is written seems to be governed by specific rules, a "grammar of literature"[4], that one learns in educational institutions and that are to be unmasked. A potential problem of structuralist interpretation is that it can be highly reductive, as scholar Catherine Belsey puts it, "the structuralist danger of collapsing all difference"[5]. An example of such a reading might be if a student concludes the authors of West Side Story did not write anything "really" new, because their work has the same structure as Shakespeare's Romeo and Juliet. In both texts a girl and a boy fall in love (a "formula" with a symbolic operator between them would be "Boy + Girl") despite the fact that they belong to two groups that hate each other ("Boy's Group - Girl's Group" or "Opposing forces") and conflict is resolved by their death. Structuralist readings best focus on how the structures of the single text resolve inherent narrative tensions. If a structuralist reading focuses on multiple texts, there must be some way in which those texts unify themselves into a coherent system. The versatility of structuralism is such that a literary critic could make the same claim about a story of two friendly families ("Boy's Family + Girl's Family") that arrange a marriage between their children despite the fact that the children hate each other ("Boy - Girl") and then the children commit suicide to escape the arranged marriage; the justification is that the second story's structure is an 'inversion' of the first story's structure: the relationship between the values of love and the two pairs of parties involved have been reversed.

Irony is one possible manifestation, maybe the least subtle, along with fourth-wall games. The work will play with those expectations, and acknowledge that that's what it's doing. It will show you the fuzzy boundary, the tangled hierarchy. It's postmodern.
