Heya,

This goes out to all the designers out there. I was looking over all the responses to Vincent's RPG play history and a question came to me. How much are your designs influenced by games you played before you published your first game? For me, my early games were Rolemaster, ADnD and Call of Cthullu. In my first game, Ember Twilight, they were all heavily influential. But now, I feel like my designs are 180 degree departure from them. What's your experience?

    I often say stuff like "there are two kinds of roleplayers in the world: those who started with D&D, and those who started with GURPS." Which is reductive, sure. Right now in this slice of history, though, the vast majority of active gamers fall into these two categories, and you can really see the differences in how they approach games. There are also some smaller sets: the players who cut their teeth on Call of Cthulhu, those who started with Amber, the World of Darkness spawn. Sure, they play other games, too, but I find that the first game you really got into has a pretty profound effect on how you see the hobby.

    Shevaun, am I right in assuming you haven't played anything like GURPS or Champions, with a strong point-spend character-n-world construction set feel to it? My D&D/GURPS dichotomy is mostly about working within a system (D&D; gain levels, choose options) and constructing a system as you go (GURPS; continual point-buy). You're right in that the WoD games emphasized (at least in advice text) a continual development of personal crises; that's the large-grain structure of many of those games. The large-grain structure of CoC is more character-as-puppet to explore a mysterious situation; the large-grain structure of Amber is a continually developing set of relationships between characters. You see these emphases cropping up over and over again, and they are often traceable to the players first game experiences (Jason being an exception).

    There's... quite a lot out there besides D&D/not-D&D.

    I would have said "Those that played D&D and those that played Traveller." GURPS came out a decade later beside Hero (in a relative sense).

    In a survey I had my con attendees fill out I seperated gamer histories by Pre-AD&D, Pre-vampire:the Msquerade, and D&D 3e. I think those time periods actually have meaning in how you approach games.

    I can't say that those foundation games have much influence on me (other than how not to write games). The first few RPGs I developed were a means to emulate solid gaminess that I saw in much later games like Masterbook and DC Heroes - the numbers having concrete meaning and a logarithmic-esqe scale.

    Some were a means to try other resolution systems for LARPs.

    Now, the games I buy certainly influence my ideas on game design. Scene framing, characters developed through narrative, currency and the like is a significant divergence. I always included story/theme/premise into my mechanics and means to pull out my narrativist ways but the design has always been steeped in task resolution. Now I far prefer conflict resolution and playing with player decision making that drive premise. I also used to be a consumate system fiddler. I'd hack systems to meet my tastes on a regular basis. Now I far prefer to have systems do what I want on the outset.

    What have I become?!!!

    The first RPG-as-such that I played was Marvel Super Heroes (after owning and not understanding Red Box D&D as an 8-year-old). We did not enjoy it. We played a fair amount of Car Wars with some RP drift as well as Renegade Legion (which I liked about 2% of) and a homebrew Battletech. Cyberpunk followed shortly thereafter and was the biggest influence on me for the longest time. That led to homebrews both by me and others (some designed on a D&D basis, some on a Cyberpunk basis). Eventually, in my college years (mid-late 90s) I worked out a system called Hero Factor that was a touch like Cyberpunk, only with a "protagonometer". Eventually, I wound up doing a huge FUGE hack called "Warhamster" (after the fictional game played by the characters in Dork Tower), which I was told really looked like GURPS, which I tried using for a while until I grew tired of buying all the books and being consistently disappointed by the rules while loving the source material. I got Sex and Sorcerer to help me figure out where to take my games, got tired of a system that was entirely shoehorns, and went back to writing my own systems.

    So... the first big design influence was Cyberpunk. Roll+skill+stat:arbitrary difficulty.

    ... and look at Under the Bed and Shock:

    I see in Shock: a realization of what I missed in Cyberpunk combined with what I loved. Under the Bed was an experiment in a system captured in the wild. I had some stuff I wanted to do, I tried to figure out how to do it, that system's what came out.

    So... nope. D&D had little impact and the GURPS influence was deliberately abandoned. Cyberpunk's implied theme (I say "implied" because the rules don't say it) of humanity thriving like Norwegian rats in a radically crashing system influenced me a lot.

    I'm going to recast this one slightly: I think we're very much a product of the games we've played.

    The products we've played are certainly important, and Josh's general assesment always strikes me as a valid one. (Though you could replace GURPS with HERO and D&D with any of its numerous clones.)

    However, for me the people I played with and the actual shape of our games was just as important (in good ways and bad) as the product we were playing. I know I was early and long in D&D -- but I have a different focus than a lot of other D&D starters that I know because of the way my early groups focused on D&D.

    But did those first years of gaming totally focus how I would see gaming for decades? Oh hell yes. There are still ways which even now, as an adult 25 years later, I'm still struggling to unlearn lessons learned in those early games.

    When I was introduced to rpgs, they were only sold at the non-kid-friendly hobby shop on campus.
    (They sold balsa wood, tiny jars of paint, shrubbery for model train terrain. Not 'toys'.)

    I lived in graduate student housing. I was in 5th grade.

    A couple years later the mall toystore chain carried boxed TSR products.
    Art was slick color airbrushed muscles.
    'Blockbuster sequel' accessibility pandered to middlebrow tastes.
    I lived in a suburban house. I was in 7th grade.

    I still find myself hearing echoes of this cultural divide in the hobby.
