    \n "Parlor narration" is what happens when the in-game fiction and the game mechanics don't feed into each other--when what happens in the fiction has little connection to what happens on the tabletop. The simplest form is we roll off to decide who gets to say what happens, but that roll-off isn't based on character attributes, situational features, or anything else; it's just us as players winning the roll, or pulling the high card, or what have you. It can be problematic if you are trying for a game in which a player's choices are tightly bound to the character's situation.

    \n To quote from the thread: \n
    PARLOR NARRATION

    These games raise an interesting issue. They are essentially "I get to say what the dice tell me" procedures, organized into specific scenes and what-to-roll rituals. Unfortunately, this is not an SIS-generating procedure, any more than putting on a funny hat and voice when playing Monopoly is role-playing. These games are entirely too structured in terms of what a character "is," imaginatively speaking, and more generally, what "can happen" during play. In contrast with fairly ritualized games like My Life with Master and Polaris, I think these are marked by a complete inability for characters actually to do stuff outside the immediate instructions of the rules, up to and including making crucial choices about relationships with other characters.
    \nSo, it looks like the term was meant to talk about a disconnect between mechanics and fiction, but one that cuts out free narration. Where the rules say what happens in the fiction so directly that there what is narrated by the players has no effect (as in the Monopoly aside) or cannot vary from what the mechanics strictly dictate. It seems like fictional board games like Tales of the Arabian Nights would fall under this.

    \nBill: Nice deciphering! Part of what this shows, I think, is that disconnects between narration and mechanics can happen in a number of different ways, but often with a tendency for the player to feel like what they say or do doesn't really matter, that it's "merely color" tacked on top of whatever the actual mechanics are. This is especially obvious in games with unlimited narration rights (the ones we first thought of), because there are often no other mechanics aside from saying who gets to talk, so the game becomes "color + your turn" and that's it.

    Overall, it's kinda like some of the older Forge conversations about getting "deprotagonized," but you're being deprotagonized by the way the game is structured, not by what the GM or another player is doing (i.e. preventing you from making choices that matter). "Deprotagonization by design," one might say

    \n There's a flaw in play and games that comes up a bit in RPG discussion: when what I say doesn't matter. "Okay Dave, nice portrayal, but now we're just going to do exactly what we would have done if you'd never spoken. The fictional situation and mechanical options/incentives haven't meaningfully changed."

    We need a term for that flaw. To date, "Parlor Narration" has been it. "Inert Narration" might be better.

    The litmus test for this flaw is not whether a given narration impacts mechanics, it's whether it impacts subsequent play.

    As a game design critique, the question is how my narration impacts play.
    • "Mechanics use derives from the fiction, so any adjustment to the fiction matters mechanically" is a traditional (or should I say '80s-'90s?) answer, and I think it's quite sufficient, though dependent on group coherence re: who cares about what fictional content.
    • Freeform takes that dependency on group coherence and chops out the influence of mechanical incentives. "Inert Narration", rather than being stuff that's irrelevant for mechanical reasons, can only be stuff that's irrelevant for "quality of fiction" reasons. Aesthetic standards, basically -- color, vividness, genre-fidelity, plot- or premise-relevance, etc.
    • There's a weird place where mechanics lead the way, and though you're supposed to justify in-fiction how you got those trait-based bonus dice, some groups kinda hand-wave it because the narration is difficult and unsatisfying. So what's written in the rules as Active Narration erodes into Inert Narration by social dynamics.

    \nEmily: Taking into account what came before is not always enough to save parlor narration (or whatever we want to call it) from being lame. Take, for example, Once Upon a Time, which seems like a great idea for a game but I've always had it be unsatisfying in practice. The problem in OUAT is that the fiction -- and maintaining a quality or interesting narrative -- isn't really the point of the game. The point of the game is to win by getting rid of all of your cards and the fiction creation is only incidental to that (or has become so in most of the games I've played). Even if you do your best to connect your narration to what previous players have done, you still often get a contrived, unsatisfying story driven by the desire to play all your cards and win, even in situations where playing your cards will introduce elements that make the story objectively worse or overly complicated or contrived.

    EDIT (crossposted with Todd): Yeah, I would argue that OUAT and Wushu are related, in that they require you to narrate certain things in order to win, but don't care what those things are. That really hits this problem right on the head. It's very much "Say something, but what you say doesn't really matter!"

    An additional problem can be introduced introduced by the open-ended nature of rights to "free narration," unrestricted by guidelines, intent, characters, traits, subject matter, or anything else. Even if you are instructed to "make it work with what's come before," as you often are, that's more easily said than done, without any tools for you to track what's already been done and then build upon it. Definitely, there are many play styles, especially freeform styles, that simply rely on the players to keep all the important facts and fictional bits in their heads, ready to throw down and continue the story when called upon, but I would argue that -- in the best freeform play -- there is some degree of breaking up responsibilities, where players aren't responsible tracking, pacing, and figuring out ways to incorperate EVERYTHING, but rather just the things within their purview. Otherwise, players can be overwhelmed and choose their own points of interest or focus, creating a "too many cooks" phenomemon if people are interested in different things and there is not sufficient overlap to produce a core sense of "aboutness" (i.e. "what are we doing here? what is this game about?").

    \n I reckon its like Chinese whispers. I describe something that is vivid in my imagination, you interpret that into something more or less vivid in your imagination. More vivid if I have been descriptive enough to engage your imagination (to fill in the blanks that the spoken description doesn't mention), less so if my description leaves you 'meh, whatever' and you aren't interested enough to engage with what I'm saying.

    Its like when you read a book. You and the author will have different versions of what the author is describing. When an author is described as having 'vivid' prose, I don't think its necessarily that their words are especially effective at 'description'. They are probably just effective at drawing attention, then your own imagination kicks in to provide the vividness.
