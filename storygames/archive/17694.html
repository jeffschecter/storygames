\n The scenario: You have one shot to hook a person on story gaming. This is an adult person, ages 18-35, male or female. They have no experience with gaming, outside of video and common board games. What is the game you sit down with them to play that you believe will have the highest likelihood of keeping them wanting more? (Really just a hypothetical). Interested in your argument about the game you choose and what aspects you believe appeal to someone "outside the bubble".

    \n Really depends a lot on their tastes in boardgames, videogames, movies, etc. Mostly, I'd stick with something with really low setup where you can launch right into play quickly and easily finish up in a few hours. Do they like Coen Brothers or Wes Anderson films? Then go for Fiasco. Do they like spy thrillers? Then maybe Hollowpoint (I haven't played it so that's a guess) or Lacuna can go that route too especially if they have a thing for the movie Inception. Do they like horror? Then definitely Dread.

    \n I intentionally kept from getting too detailed. But, I would agree about Fiasco. I think there's enough variety of playsets that no matter the person you can find something that matches their interest. I introduced my sister to Fiasco using Regina's Wedding (rather than something more overtly crime oriented) and she loved it. For my brother, we played Manna Hotel (he's a David Lynch fan), and for my 4th grade students we played through a kid friendly Dungeon Delve playset. Something for everyone.

    \n I would totally agree with Fiasco if they've the right film mind. Dread is fun and tactile if they like horror, and if they do common board games they might feel pretty comfortable with it.
    If they're fantasy/sci-fi fans I'd add that Lady Blackbird sets up super-fast and is light and easy to understand, though you need a decent number of people to fill out the characters. It's a little longer than the others here, but doesn't need to be too long - one session can still do it just fine.

    \n I've heard it said that School Daze is a great game for first-timers. Yes, it's my game, but the words belong to those who are not me. The theme of high school, the light-hearted nature of the game, and the free-form way it operates gives new players an empowered feeling. You can run it like anything from a John Huges movie to to Buffy, to Harry Potter.

    I also love Fiasco for this, as I think will be a recurring theme. Fiasco has a lot of the same qualities that I listed above, albeit the themes are much darker by default.

    \n Yet another vote for Fiasco. Play a round of Cards Against Humanity and/or pour a few stiff drinks beforehand to prime the pump a little and you're guaranteed to have a great night of fun.

    I'd also suggest something like The Quiet Year. Because no one has to play a single character and you get to take turns, it's strangely board game like and I've had some success getting none-gamers on board playing it. It also definitely hammers home the "story" aspect.

    Also... I can't recommend this quite as wholeheartedly - the other games mentioned are much safer choices - but I've been genuinely surprised at how quickly people can latch onto Burning Wheel. For a rather large group of people I know, that game has been the gateway drug into the story game world, or even RPG's in general. I grant you, it's not nearly as simple as Fiasco and it tends to demand a little more from players (and a lot more from you if you're gonna be teaching n00bs). But if you're patient, well-versed with the basic rules, are using con scenarios with pre-gens, and are wise enough to keep the scripting sheets off the table as much as possible... it's a good intro for people who you think don't mind starting out somewhere between the deep and shallow end. People who dig intense, serial TV shows like The Wire or Battlestar Galactica tend to really dig Belief-driven drama. And some people like a little challenge, you know?

    I keep seeing Burning Wheel come up in discussions and have never read it, just about it. Is this an essential if I'm to be a true story gamer? I think all the great stuff I hear about it has me feeling intimidated.
    \nComing from a true Burning Wheel lover, I can enthusiastically say no - I don't think any game is essential to be a "true story gamer." If you care about making great stories with your friends, that's the only qualification for that you need in my book. Part of the reason you hear a lot about it is simply because it's been around a while, at least in terms of the story game revolution. And it is quite good. It's not everyone's cup of tea - it's a little crunchy - but it's well-designed and does what it sets out to do very well. Next time you're at a con, try it out and see if it's for you.

    Yet another vote for Fiasco. Play a round of Cards Against Humanity and/or pour a few stiff drinks beforehand to prime the pump a little and you're guaranteed to have a great night of fun.
    \nYES! Warm-up games are great! For my recent game of Fiasco we played a game of Fluffy Bunny Tea Party which worked grand. However any board/card game which encourages a bit of roleplaying, storytelling, or at least lots of talk is good for this. Other examples are Gloom which works best when you expand upon the cards with a bit of story, Dixit, and there are others that I am forgetting about at the moment. Just steer away from any heavy strategic game where everyone is silent concentrating on their next move as you want everyone in a talky mood.

    \n I would choose Once Upon a Time, because the game has a clear structure, takes under an hour to play, uses tropes, the crunch part is very small, and the game has a clear goal. It also takes creativity to play, and any of the participants can play it again without me being around. I've been playing the game on Halloween parties and after parties, so I know it works with non-gamers.

    The only roleplaying game that I felt really confident to play with non-gamers is This Is Pulp, for the same reasons as Once Upon a Time. Perhaps half of the playtesters of my game (of 21 playtests \u0e23\u0e01 2-4 players each) has been non-gamers, and it worked swell.

    \n You talk to them a bit, and find out what they like. Then you pick some games from your shelf that you like, and that might fit their tastes. Then you pick one of them together after talking about them a bit.

    If you turn the question around so it's about something else, it becomes easier to see, I think. Say you were going to show a movie to someone who'd never seen movies before! You wouldn't say "this is the perfect movie for recruiting beginners" - you'd say "Well, I know she likes SF books and fantasy comics, and I like the first Star Wars movies and Alien and The Hobbit, so I'll tell her about those to find one that suits her".
