\n So, I'm currently writing up the final draft of A Taste For Murder, my murder mystery game.

At the end, there are traditional English recipes. So, for example, here's part of the recipe for Yorkshire pudding, with the ingredients in bold:\n
Now for the Yorkshire putting: sift a quarter of a pound of plain flour, together with a pinch of salt. Mix a quarter of a pint of milk with a quarter of a pint of water. Make a well in the centre of the flour, break an egg into it, and pour half the milk and water mixture into it. With a whisk, begin to work the egg, milk and water into the flour, starting at the edge of the well. As you go, pour the rest of the milk and water mixture, and beat until you have a batter. Place in another preheated dish, on a high shelf, for thirty minutes.
\nNow, I realised that, since it's a murder mystery, these recipes should really have some poison in them. For example:\n
Now for the Yorkshire putting: sift a quarter of a pound of plain flour, together with a pinch of salt. Mix a quarter of a pint of milk with a teaspoon of potassium cyanide and a quarter of a pint of water. Make a well in the centre of the flour, break an egg into it, and pour half the milk and water mixture into it. With a whisk, begin to work the egg, milk and water into the flour, starting at the edge of the well. As you go, pour the rest of the milk and water mixture, and beat until you have a batter. Place in another preheated dish, on a high shelf, for thirty minutes.
\nThe problem is, I know little about poisons. Could you recommend me some fun poisons, together with how much you'd need to poison someone, and what the effects would be?

Graham

    \n Oh, dear.

    Clostridium botulinum, of course, because of the plausible deniability. Everybody makes mistakes in home canning. Miniscule amounts.

    Arsenic, like the Bradford sweets mass poisoning of 1858. Gotta have arsenic! 9 grains, around 5.8 grams, enough to kill two people.

    Domoic acid poisoning, leading to the delightfully-named Amnesic Shellfish Poisoning. Miniscule amounts.

    Common Monkshood root is an aggressive neurotoxin and can easily by mistaken for horseradish. There's a related aconite used as a deadly poison in Nepal. In Alaska, the Athabsacans used it as a paralytic to coat their beluga harpoons. It's a beautiful flower. Several roots, ground.

    Mix a healthy dose of benzodiazepine anticonvulsants with alcohol and shazam! 500mg, maybe. To be sure.

    \n Datura stramonium or Datura inoxia both are really pretty night blooming plants.

    Flower, seeds, and leaves contain very powerful alkaloids. Typically 1-3 flowers can kill or a handful of seeds. Both can be dried and powdered. From there they can be put into drinks, food, mouth, or eyes. Just touching the flower then rubbing the eyes can be enough to do some people in.

    Effects: Complete delirium (as in you can't tell reality from fantasy, not the same as hallucination) followed by spasms, sleepiness, and death

    I've got a whole host of ethnobotany books with plants like this if you need more information.

    ara

    EDIT: 1930's England a lot of these could easily come from world travelers who find exotic cultures.

    \n You need at least 60mg of nicotine to be deadly to an adult human, and it clears from the system very quickly (because it's water-soluble). A little more for regular smokers.

    The nicotine content of cigarettes has increased since the 1930s so I'm not sure how many cigarettes that would have been; Wikipedia says 5 for contemporary cigarettes (or half a cigar). A pack of 10 should do it easily.

    But I also seem to remember that nicotine was used to kill wasps back then (it is, after all, the tobacco plant's natural insecticide), so it could sometimes be found in garden sheds along with arsenic.
