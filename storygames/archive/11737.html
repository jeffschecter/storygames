\nThis week we had to take another break from our Burning Empires game, due to a player absence. Laura, Jesse, Patrick, and Will got together and played a game of Montsegur 1244. We talk about playing within the constraints of real historical events, playing with a set cast of characters and their built in relationships and situation, finally we cover our standard topic for GMless games; who does the game fill the GM's role systematically.

    \n Something I found bizarre is the comment about witchcraft. Now I don't remember how the card presents it in Montsegur but... witchcraft would not be fantasy at all, in medieval france.

    I mean, if you have demons show up visibly, or a witch that shoots fireballs it's clearly fantasy. But for someone living in medieval europe witchcraft was something real: it's prejudice, superstition, general nastyness and ostracism against, generally, women that showed too much independent thought and behaviour. Men (and their wives) tended to get very scared by that

    But enough rambling... witchcraft can definitely be used in a game that stays very grounded in the mundane day by day life of the castle. Just like the Grail: ok, it exists... but its significance is more in the faith that people have in it than in any magical power. Just like, say, the Shroud of Turin.

    I guess it's something akin to the supernatural dial in Dogs: *everything* (from the grail to the voice of god) can be explained even without resorting to the supernatural, and its significance to the people that strongly believe in those things will not be diminished in any measureable way.

    Anyway, good episode

    \n Hey,

    In the discussion I may have over emphasized the potential for fantasy with the Witchcraft card. Obviously the degree to which the group wants the elements to be fantastical in nature is customizable.

    My MAIN point was that however you interpret the card, things like the Witchcraft card and the Holy Grail are tone changers. Even if you go with the totally mundane it's all just superstition and fear interpretation as soon as someone starts muttering about Witchcraft, cows start dying of plague, etc the game is going to go in TOTALLY different direction from that point forward. Those things draw attention and can not be easily ignored and are laced with all kinds of emotional content. Holding a card like and the choice whether to play it or not and when is a BIG DEAL.

    Jesse

    \n Yeah, absolutely (I say just from hearing the podcast, and not knowing much about the game outside that description).

    Actually, I forgot to mention that I really liked this episode. The descriptions I'd read of M1244 elsewhere didn't really grab me, but listening to the way it actually played out made me very keen. I suspect I'll pick it up once my self-imposed ban on buying new RPGs is over. I've long been enamoured of the idea of pregen characters who have their conflicts ready-to-boil, and I also like games in which characters can't die in play, forcing the conflict in other directions.

    I guess what I'm getting at is that I like the way your podcast "reviews without reviewing", in that you just discuss the game, and the way its bits interact, without just setting out to say "good/bad".

    Keep it up!
