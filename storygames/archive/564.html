  • Old Scratch
    April 2006
    Rolemaster and Spacemaster.

    Very complicated process in creating characters, especially high-level characters.

    Roll ten stats. Only five of them give you development points. Use those development points to buy skills.

    Ranks 1-20 offer +5, those over that +2.

    Add your Stat bonus. Add your Equipment bonus. Add your racial mods. Add your cybernetic mods or magic or whatever. Total them up.

    It ended up being a lot of bean counting. Experience points were even worse, with experience points for six dozen things (in Rolemaster, including the destruction of gems??!?!) and then apply a whole series of modifiers.

    For combat, split your bonus, adding part of it to your attack roll and part to your defense roll, roll an open-ended d100, add your all your bonuses, subtract their defensive bonuses and consult a chart. Make a note of all the damage you do and criticals, because you'll get experience points for that.

    If using SpaceMaster, there's a very complex system for building a ship as well.

    Not that I'm exactly complaining: when I didn't have a game going on, I'd spend hours making up characters and get totally lost in the numbers. Now, though, when I think back to the folder stuffed full of these "characters", I get a bit of a chilll down my spine.

    I don't think this was nearly as bad as the Leading Edge Phoenix Command stuff, but it was still pretty bad, and I find it easier to understand a 1040A then I did the Rolemaster/Spacemaster system.

    I just finished a game with a nightmare amount of paperwork. Players had to keep track of their characters finances (when each was as wealthy as your average multimillionaire), all the staff they employed, the amount of time devoted to various activities in an average week (in a game with multiple worlds with different time rates), numbers and types of troops, property portfolios, time devoted to relationships with important NPCs, a diary of their activities (both future and past), calendars, and so on. One player even had a list of all her PCs staff, including birthdays and the location of their quarters within her house. Oh, and of course, she had detailed plans of the layout of her PC's house. I did less, but still ended up with 20+ spreadsheets.

    I found it stupid and tedious. Another player really enjoyed it and did it for the fun. But if other people didn't do the same work, she'd get all 'oh you can't prove your character has enough money to pay for that influence buying act, if you do it it will break the game for me and ruin my fun!' while refusing to refrain from doing that sort of influence buying thing herself. So everyone ended up tracking all this crazy stuff because you never knew when she was going to demand proof that it was plausible for a character to do something. Then the game died, which was probably a good thing in hindsight.

    Oh, and this was an Amber DRPG game, where technically all the information on a character sheet should be able to fit on a stamp.

    As a result, I'm currently keen on games with low paperwork demands in practice as well as in theory.

    So, Internal, I've got some questions about your game.

    I'm still trying to figure out what the premise is. I mean, the character sheet, even the "EZ" version, ultimately focuses around this "Tax Owed" stat (which I'm still having to get over the paradigm clash of not being BW style tax...) But as far as I can tell, "Tax Owed" doesn't have any purpose in the game.

    Now, a lot of the stats that lead up to that are evocative. I really like "number of dependants" with the sort of subversive suggestion that dependants really are little sub-characters, since they don't fill in their own sheet, AFIACT. While related characters have been used frequently, actually treating the character's family, household and spouse so directly really pushes for PCs that are married with children, which is fairly unusual in RPGs in general (Pendragon excepted). And, assuming that less Tax Owed is better, I love the fact that you encourage more dependants and a spouse.



    But you don't spend any time on actually fleshing out these "dependants" Instead I just multiply by 3200 and subtract from my Income. Speaking of: AFAICT, Income and Taxes Paid both come from these "Form W-2" things, but I can't find any anywhere in the game materials. Did I miss something? Is there a suppliment out there I have to wait for before I can make a character? Dude, HoL already did that, and it was funnier.

    Finally, there are a bunch of little auxilliary stats that I can't figure out what they're good for. Biggest example: this little checkbox about "Contribute a dollar to the presidential election fund." Huh? It doesn't change anything on the sheet, and I can't see how it effects gameplay either. Why would I check or not check that box?

    Oh, and the biggest issue - dude, the tax lookup table? It goes on for pages! Maybe when you started putting in dividing headers at each thousand dollars of income, you might have asked yourself "is this table too complex?" I mean, I guess you really only use it for chargen, but still, since so many of my decisions in making my character revolve around the relationship between my marital status, Taxable Income and Tax Owed, it might be nicer if this was a simple formula or something. It looks pretty linear.

    Still, I think there are some neat ideas in this game, but I really think you need to decide what your premise is and address it directly, rather in this weird tangential "Tax" thing.
