\n I'm appealing once again to everyone's collective wisdom.

What are different possible design profiles for conflicts involving more than two sides?

I'd like to put together a little catalogue of the different possibilities, possibly find new ones that haven't yet been tried. If this thread gets going, I'll type it all up and present it as a free little offering of thanks.

For instance, I'll start with two I'm familiar with:

The Classic (D&D) Model

* Participants roll to determine order of actions.
* Each participant, on their turn, can roll to cause damage to one or more targets of their choice.
* You lose if you take more damage than your limit (i.e. hit points)
* You win if you are still standing at the end, when no one remains to oppose you.

The In a Wicked Age... Model

* Participants roll to determine initiative.
* Highest initiative makes a challenge against one or more targets, targets roll to defend themselves.
* If your turn comes around before you've had to defend yourself, you get to make your own challenge. If you've had to defend yourself, you don't.
* If you rolled better than an opponent, and the conflict isn't over, you get a bonus in the next round.
* You lose if you're beaten really badly, either by a challenger or a defender, or if you lose in the third round.
* You win if all your opponents lose.

(And, yes, negotiation is a big part of the IaWA model, but it's more about consequences than the actual flow of conflict, so I'm not going to include it in the "profile". After all, you could negotiate mid-fight in D&D as well. I see it as a perpendicular issue: IaWA conflict could be perfectly functional without the option to negotiate, for example.

    \n The Sorcerer Model

    * Everyone declares actions in any order.
    * Everyone rolls appropriate stat.
    * Actions occur in highest single die to lowest single die order.
    * The target of an action who has not himself acted either aborts his action and defends with full dice or defends with just 1 die and keeps his action.
    * Any one who has already acted defends with full dice against any action which targets them.

    \n Hey, wait! An important (very important) part is: how do you know how gets what they want and who doesn't? Please add that in, folks!

    What I'm trying to get at with this is not only how to order actions but how to decide who gets what they want when, and how it affects you if two different people are ganging up on you, etc.

    Here's one more:

    The Shadow of Yesterday/Solar System Model (BDTP/Extended conflict)

    * Everyone declares intent (you need a special action to change your intent later in the fight)
    * Everyone declares actions; you may try to damage one or more opponents, or roll to gain an advantage in the next round.
    * Players negotiate to figure out which actions (if any) oppose others.
    * Everyone rolls simultaneously, actions happen simultaneously (although the narration may be ordered logically, game-wise this has no effect)
    * If you take more damage than the threshold, you're out and you do not get your intent. You've lost.
    * If everyone opposing your intent is out, you've won: you get your intent.

    Any corrections are welcome, by the way, if I get something wrong.

    \n The Primetime Adventures Model

    * All involved players name their desired outcomes. All desired outcomes are parallel intents--none can prevent another from occurring.
    * Each player's draw is compared to the Producer's draw.
    * If your draw was better than the Producer's, you win (and achieve your desired outcome).
    * If your draw was worse than the Producer's, you lose (and do not achieve your desired outcome).

    Is this one right? I'm not sure about it.
