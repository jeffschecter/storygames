    \n The idea to break up spellcasting into categories is a good one. Supernatural stuff tends to need clear/understandable limits. They also usually need specific fictional elements, though. These moves feel like an entirely procedural ruleset. Even the Mage has a bunch of foci or whatever that start you off with something concrete to envision. Especially Utility Magic, which is like saying "eh, you know, you can do a bunch of, like, magic stuff." If I have to make everything up anyway, why do I need a new class? This is maybe why people say they're not fiction first enough. Have you seen Jacob Randolph's new Mage series (dragon mage, winter mage, clock mage)? Those are exciting because they're based on specifics, and you play them for the stuff you probably wouldn't have thought of on your own, which is inspiring. (Also, have you posted at the Something Awful DW thread? Only some of them are active on the g+ DW group).

    Also, some of this stuff seems a bit fiddly? Like, ask yourself what you gain by writing out all of Grey Magic as opposed to just: When you defend with your magic, add INT to your roll instead of CON. Or, why roll to see how many times you can mimic a monster move? Right now, the player has to roll even if they don't ever intend to mimic it. You could set it to a stat modifier's amount, or just twice, with an advanced move that gives you three times instead (or four even). Or allow the player to choose to "forget" it on a 7-9, just like a normal spell. All of those options would be quicker and probably give the player more confidence in that power.

    \n Generic isn't always bad. Those moves just seem a bit like they are basic moves for a wizard-centric game, where each character is magic and they are differentiated by what kind of magic they can do. So, like one character is all about the Night and Romance and the Stars, so she can defend with a cloud of stardust, cover herself with a cloak of night, and inspire passionate feelings in people (or whatever other stuff she can shoehorn into those three concepts). And another character is all Fire and Ice and Battle. No cloak of shadows for that dude! The same as when you try to hack and slash, it matters whether you are using a two-handed battle axe or a knife, right? Sure, the results could be "deal your damage" and nothing more, but then why play DW? Likewise, the Frost Witch fighting a White Dragon shouldn't be able to just switch to fireballs. The player should have to be a little more creative and find something within their wheelhouse.

    So yeah, letting players choose that during play is fine. You might end up limiting them too much with a phrase they have to always use afterward, though. Probably a collection of motifs, concepts, or elements would work best. And if you want the player to be able to change things up over the course of the game, you could model that after the blue magic format in your last post.

    Grey Magic:
    Okay, so you've got a list of differences, now you need to beat on the move until you find the best way to do each one. It sounds like part of the trigger should be setting up a defensive spell, since you want to be able to spend that hold to defend anyone in range of your magic. I would say go with a die roll for damage though. It may be worth making two base damages for your class -- one for weapons, another for magic.

    Blue Magic:
    I feel like I've read a previous discussion of this kind of blue magic somewhere before, and maybe even commented on it, but I can't remember right now. Maybe it was your stuff on g+? Someone else's? Blue magic is a popular idea. I dunno. Some thoughts (and some variations to consider):
    1. I'd maybe go with three slots to start with? I'd at least playtest it with three, although that might be more appropriate for a mage that starts with ONLY blue magic and not two styles.
    2. Mimicking moves that get used against you is also quite a limit--you have to get pummelled a bunch before you can do any yourself. Might be better to mimick monster moves you have witnessed or have been used in your presence.
    3. "Half as effective" often tends to mean "doesn't work at all." It's okay for stuff like damage, but I think it's better to go the opposite direction, especially with magic, and have the spell work plus unwanted side-effects. Like you end up casting two spells and the GM picks the other one.

    Also: I don't really like Arcane Learning. The GM is already giving you something useful and interesting on a 10+. If you want the class to get more info on magic stuffs, something like Bardic Lore might work better -- you see a magic item, you ask a question. Or you see magic in use you ask a question. etc.

    \n Absorbing only is probably fine if the class has some other starting moves. The witness-mimic mage should probably have that as their main deal. Keep in mind that I don't play video games at all and know absolutely nothing about Final Fantasy, so all I have to go on is thinking what I'd want to do while playing at the table. And I'm just throwing out ideas here, you still gotta make 'em work.

    Verb and noun reminds me of Ars Magica. Seasons sounds like kind of a cool idea, actually. You'll probably have to sit around thinking up different types of mages until you figure out a system that works for you. But even if you just have a few words for inspiration, like they are there to give the player a loose idea of what their magic is like, is a good idea.

    Go ahead and steal from the Bard! Who cares? Nobody's gonna sue you.

    Oh, edit: Cast a protective spell probably works fine, actually, although you want to make sure there is room there for the actual intent of the spell to succeed. Like, if you cast Protection from Cold so the party can travel through the ice storm, the real point is that they don't freeze and neither does their equipment. Which could be modeled with damage reduction, or with you spending hold to "overcome an obstacle" or something similar. So you cast Protection from Traps on the Fighter so she can run into the throne room and seize the golden idol and it doesn't turn into "okay, you take 7 damage from the poison darts, oh you spend a hold to reduce that to 3?" and more like "okay, there's molten lava, poison snakes, and an evil curse before you grab the idol. Oh, that's your three hold? Good luck getting back with it!"
