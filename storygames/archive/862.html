Design Elements for a Gateway Game

Vaxalon
June 2006 edited June 2006 in Play Advice
Let's say you're going to design a game, with the intention that it becomes the next "gateway" game, a game that people would find first and be drawn into the roleplaying hobby.

What elements would it have?

Here's some things that come to mind for me:

1> Numerous hooks into popular culture.
2> Does not call itself a "roleplaying game" anywhere.
3> Rules that can be grasped as quickly and easily as Scrabble's.
4> Cards rather than dice.
5> Can be played by any number of people, from two up to six, preferably eight.
6> Uses a minimum of jargon, and what game terms it does use, are well defined.
7> Can be played easily online.
8> Can be bought in stores, and online, and comes to you in a box.
9> Can be bought online, and downloaded.

I'm willing to discuss the whys and wherefores of MY list, but before you can comment on mine, I want to see YOURS.

    One of my big requirements for a gateway game would be that it have depth and replay value. That's what gives me problems with a lot of "learn the rules in 5 minutes" games: there's no depth to them, and no sense of either strategy or challenge. You can't give someone pure fluff and expect it to drag them into gaming; that's like trying to get someone into cards using War or Go Fish. It'll work for little kids, but I think most adults and teenagers would be unimpressed.

    (Edit: obviously, getting them into cards using Bridge or high-stakes Texas Hold-em is probably a bad idea as well; I think that makes a good analogy for our *current* problem with role-playing games.)

    June 2006 edited June 2006
    Okay, so we've got almost twenty elements. Let me compile:

    1. Numerous hooks into popular culture.
    2. Does not call itself a "roleplaying game" anywhere.
    3. Rules that can be grasped as quickly and easily as Scrabble's.
    4. When bought in a box, all that is needed for play is included.
    (If downloaded, the only thing you need to add is a deck of cards.)
    5. Can be played by any number of people, from two up to six, possibly eight.
    6. Uses a minimum of jargon, and what game terms it does use, are well defined.
    7. Can be played easily online.
    8. Can be bought in stores, and online, and comes to you in a box.
    9. Can be bought online, and downloaded.
    10. Fun begins when the box is opened and does not generally let up.
    11. Extensible in interesting and fun ways, by the buyer or through add-on sales, but not crippleware.
    12. Can provide a satisfying play experience in short chunks of time.
    13. Can get up and running into play in 5-10 minutes
    14. Doesn't require one person to act as rules referee and player of every character not played by the other players and creator of situation and color and setting authority.
    15. Can be played and enjoyed in one sitting. The game can be continued through further play, but doesn't have to be.
    16. The game has been extensively playtested, by both gamer and non-gamer alike.
    17. Whatever story is used follows a well-known and easy-to-follow genre format, such as a quest, a murder mystery, or television procedural.
    18. There's no 'character sheet'. All character attributes can be represented with the physical tools that come with the game.

    In response to the criticisms of downloadables as a "gateway game" design element, which I agree with, I am going to deprecate the internet-related elements. Those aren't design elements of the initial game; they may be something to figure out later.

    I'm also collapsing related design elements, and removing elements unrelated to the design of the game itself.

    So we're left with:

    1. Numerous hooks into popular culture. Whatever story is used follows a well-known and easy-to-follow genre format, such as a quest, a murder mystery, or television procedural.
    2. Does not call itself a "roleplaying game" anywhere, but it is one anyways.
    3. Has rules that can be grasped as quickly and easily as Scrabble's rules, which fit on the inside of the box. The game uses a minimum of jargon, and what game terms it does use, are well defined.
    4. The game is bought in a box, and all that is needed for play is included.
    5. Can be played by any number of people, from two up to six, possibly eight.
    6. Fun begins when the box is opened and does not generally let up. Can get up and running into play in 5-10 minutes
    7. Extensible in interesting and fun ways, by the buyer or through add-on sales, but not crippleware.
    8. Can provide a satisfying play experience in short chunks of time. Can be played and enjoyed in one sitting of two hours or less. The game can be continued through further play, but doesn't have to be.
    9. Doesn't require one person to act as rules referee and player of every character not played by the other players and creator of situation and color and setting authority.
    10. There's no 'character sheet'. All character attributes can be represented with the physical tools that come with the game.

    Does that summarize well, our work so far?

      I've never played Magic, but I did play the Rage CCG ( http://www.boardgamegeek.com/game/2133 ) -- and that could definitely be a gateway to playing other World of Darkness games.

      I think many games can be gateways to other games -- sometimes RPGs and sometimes not. Checkers is a gateway to Chess. Risk is a gateway to Axis & Allies and more detailed Wargames. Monopoly doesn't have a very strong story component, and the real-estate theme isn't well supported by most RPGs, so it's not a good gateway game to something like D&D. Monopoly is a good gateway game to other boardgames though, like Settlers of Catan ( http://www.boardgamegeek.com/game/17419 ).

      Battletech, a tabletop wargame, was definitely a gateway game to the Mechwarrior RPG.

      Talisman, Heroquest, the D&D Boardgame ( http://www.boardgamegeek.com/game/6366 ), and more recently Runebound ( http://www.boardgamegeek.com/game/9829 ) and Descent ( http://www.boardgamegeek.com/game/17226 ) are all examples of boardgames that are gateway games to RPGs -- specifically Fantasy RPGs like D&D.

      The Arkham Horror boardgame ( http://www.boardgamegeek.com/game/15987 ), and the Call of Cthulhu CCG ( http://www.boardgamegeek.com/game/8817 ) are both good gateways for the Call of Cthulhu RPG.

      Of course a lightweight RPG can also be a gateway to more detailed RPGs.
