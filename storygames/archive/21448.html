\n I wrote last month a bit about a farce I wrote for Tales of Entropy. As I mentioned then, there's a bunch of scenarios I've written for the game in Finnish that are waiting in the translation queue for me or somebody else to bother to put into English. I recently translated my Hellraiser scenario that I'm happy enough to bring up for consideration here. Mainly I'm motivated by showing off my writing in a snappy little package, of course, but there are a few points of interest here on top of that, too.

First, to get it out of the way: I just think that Hellraiser is a really cool horror property. I am particularly fond of the early '90s anthology comics which I think capture what is best in Hellraiser as a franchise: provocative, contradictory world-building, storytelling that is static in terms of plot while being thematically meaningful. It's all over the place, being written and drawn by whomever, but that just adds to the charm. The authors have no clue whatsoever what to do with Clive Barker's central ideas, and they're being asked to stretch those few concepts out into dozens of stories. To top it off, apparently the suits didn't really care about what the comic book did, considering how horrorful it managed to be. For me personally Hellraiser was a first experience of horror that had intricate mythological world-building like Dracula, but that was also angry, counter-cultural and violent.

(The movie's not such an emotional thing for me. I might not be a movie person in general, but mainly I just think that it's average. The moralistic yet ambiguous storyline is good, the phantasmagoric ideas are great and the monsters are fine, but for me it's nowhere near the top of the horror movie canon. It's the perfect Sorcerer movie, though. Important as a case study, I might say, while being only average to watch.)

That aside, Hellraiser makes a good case study for something that I've been talking about when it comes to a certain topic that crops up a lot particularly in more traditional RPG circles. That topic is often termed such:

"How to adventure in the world of a pre-made story?"

I have literally witnessed this topic of discussion for 20 years, and it was probably going on even before then. It's an immediate question when it comes to playing something like the Middle-Earth Roleplaying Game or other such license games based on novels or movies or such: given that the Big Story that the setting is built around is already in existence, canonical, cut and dried, what is it that you actually do with all this material?

My personal breakthrough on the matter occurred in the mid-'00s, in the hot Forge years. I was playing a lot of My Life with Master, Dust Devils and similar drama games at the time. One particularly boring day the crew wanted to play something so I just decided that heck, we might as well throw The Lord of the Rings into Dust Devils and see what comes out. Not like I hadn't played the game in all sorts of settings before, and like many fantasy hobbyists, I could quote LotR in my sleep anyway, so it'd be a good opportunity to share my love of the books with the youngsters I was gaming with at the time.

My big breakthrough in how to go about this sort of "novel adaptation" was to be entirely committed to the creative inspiration of the source work while simultaneously being entirely unconcerned about enforcing canonical story direction or setting detail. When you already have a game that precisely determines who gets to say what when, why would you need an additional busybody hovering over everything with the canon hammer?

This being the insight, my LotR scenario became a great success: I told the players that we would be starting from what the lore knows as the Council of Elrond, and you could make your own character if you wanted to, but I would love it if you chose to play one of the people from the book, instead. No pressure about portraying them correctly: we would go through their background and identity quickly, and after that it'd be 100% your character, nobody else gets to tell you "what Frodo would do". The plot would be entirely up for grabs, too: you tell us what your character thinks would be the best course forward here, when the story is on the brink of destiny, and if somebody disagrees they can very well go to conflict over it like a man, or keep their peace, compadre, like you're supposed to in Dust Devils.

The story we got out of this approach was great because it managed to simultaneously be very LotR-colored without being at all like it in terms of plot. It was the first time I used the Middle-Earth at a roleplaying game table in an emotionally authentic way, as something other than a background for weak ersatz-D&D. Dust Devils obviously made things very tense, very sudden, very bloody in its own inimitable style.

So that's my "secret recipe" for how to grab an arbitrary movie or novel or whatever and "play it": you take the setting, the characters, the themes, and you input them into a game that gives you dramaturgical constraint. Hilarity ensues as the players bring their interest in the original material, while the game brings its own ideas of how, exactly, you craft your story. You get a very real sense of the setting and characters, and who cares if the plot will be different? That's just about the best part, a new story.

Entropy works well for this purpose, whence my Hellraiser case study. The scenario text is intended to support both Hellraiser-knowledgeable and green groups of players, obviously. You just grab the central facts of the property (as laid out in the scenario text) and go, make your own story. It doesn't have to be the same as the original.

    \n Heh, sure. And yeah, Paul, I think you grok this - I'm not presenting anything revolutionary here, just trying to be concise about a neat trick that I don't see people doing nearly as much they should. I mean, Dragonlance is just about the only big-name RPG product that outright advocates "playing the novels", and even there the rpg stuff was first, or nearly so. It works much better than one would assume just by looking at how rarely people just go "I'll play Frodo, I'll bet I understand him better than Tolkien ever did".

    Or, in the case of Hellraiser: who has the chops to do it without the simplification or outright ignorance of the philosophical ideas that making a movie involves, or the geek cruft that comics accumulate?

    But let's see what I meant earlier:\n
    Hopeless_Wanderer said:

    "[C]ommitted to the creative inspiration of the source work while simultaneously being unconcerned about enforcing canonical story direction". Let's start here.

    \nWhat I mean by this is simply that narrative art consists of layers, structurally speaking, yes? Not everybody conceives of their creative process structurally, but the structure nevertheless exists in how we allocate meaning. One possible way to perceive this structure is in terms of themes in potential (what we used to call a "premise" at the Forge, as per Lajos Egri and Ron Edwards) that become thematic meaning in the actual story. That is, we can distinguish between the message of a story, the building-blocks of the story, and the actual story, even if the only thing we have at the end is the actual story. Something like this:

    Abstract: We exist as cognitive beings who exchange ideas.
    ->
    Theme I want to discuss as an artist: Clive Barker in Hellraiser wants to help you confront your own ideas about BDSM. J.R.R. Tolkien in The Lord of the Rings wants to talk about true humility and nobility.
    ->
    A narrative world pertaining to the theme is created: Clive Barker invents phantasmagoric concepts like the Cenobites and the Lament Configuration. He puts a lot of work in visceral visual conceptualization of the Cenobites as inhumanly extreme body-modifying BDSM hedonists. J.R.R. Tolkien goes crazy working out languages and mythologies that express the mythic worldview of his themes.
    ->
    An actual story is constructed out of the legos: Clive Barker populates his fantasy with a few hedonistic-yet-normal people whose actions will activate the engine of his hedonism-turned-into-fantasy-imagery. A few "normal people" victims too, for the ensuing carnage, and to provide contrast. J.R.R. Tolkien likewise sets up some hobbits and warrior princes to provide an extreme contrast of humility between the two.
    ->
    Concrete: Here's a book, maybe read it sometime?

    So when I said that I want "commitment to the creative inspiration of the source work", I meant that you can't just take the narrative world out of the above process and apply your own superficial preconceptions to it. That's how you get something like the Middle-Earth Roleplaying Game: a gamer geek's understanding of The Lord of the Rings as a backdrop for D&D adventure.

    Rather, you need to take the entirety of the thematic stage-setting seriously, from the beginning of that creative process up to the stage of world creation. You don't only take the name of "Lothlorien" and the image of elves living in a forest; you also need to take the literally inhuman philosophical significance that Tolkien gives his elves. This requires understanding and appreciating the original work, of course - if all you see in Hellraiser is actors in funny monster suits, and all you see in LotR is long-winded tourist gazetteer, then of course your own "take" on the material is going to be just as shallow.

    If everything goes well, what you do in your exegesis of the original work is that you successfully create a "lego set" of places, people and events that are infused with their original meaning. You might drop some stuff that's in excess of what you're doing with the stuff. You will drop the actual plot, because the point of playing with that lego set is to experience the material through your own story creation, which may or may not lead to essentially the same story. (Hellraiser, for example, in Tales of Entropy: will you really make a story that won't essentially be "somebody calls the Cenobites, and their mysterious fantasy reality completely redefines everybody's dramatic priorities"?)

    \n So we understand the legos, yes? The Lament Configuration, Pinhead, even Frank Cotton are all legos in the sense we can detach them from their original story and just use them for our re-telling. As per the rules of Entropy we add some legos of our own during character creation, too. Lords of the Rings has much, much more legos than Hellraiser due to the nature of the work, but the principle is the same - more legos just means that you need to create less yourself, and you have more options for incorporating them in different ways.

    I said earlier "while simultaneously being unconcerned about enforcing canonical story direction" as the other thing you need to handle in addition to creating your legos correctly. This simply means that you can't get emotionally or intellectually attached to that most superficial layer of a narrative work, the actual plot. When you play the Council of Elrond (it's a really good starting point for giving this treatment to LotR, I recommend it highly) you can't think in terms of what happened in the original story; you must think about what your character wants and why, and what they're willing to do to get their way. You need to use the rules, and if those rules say that Boromir punches Elrond and browbeats Gandalf, then off we go to Gondor to see what the One Ring might do for the war effort. Use the hobbits a cobblestones or something, we're setting mythical humility aside (and regretting that greatly later on, obviously, when the internal logic of the thematic story universe catches up with our choices).

    When writing that Hellraiser scenario I had to think pretty hard on who I'd choose for my pivot character, and what situation I would start the scenario in. I didn't even particularly need to go with the actual characters and situations from the movie; the archetypal content of Hellraiser is literal demon-summoning, it would be just as much "the Hellraiser experience" if I made up my own demon-summoning situation. I'm happy with Frank from the movie, though: he presents the fundamental existential motivation for summoning demons, and the most superficial misunderstanding of the Cenobites possible. Obviously the moment I chose for the game's "kicker" (the dramatic moment to start the game from) was a little before Frank actually manages to solve the Lament Configuration; the scenario is simple, there isn't any real choice in the matter. Frank without the Lament Configuration is just a sad and superficial asshole, and Frank after he opens the gate - well, those who know the story know that the event limit his dramaturgical options rather severely.

    But making that choice for when to start the story, I have to accept that Frank may choose to not solve the Lament Configuration. Or most hilariously, perhaps he fails? (I would totally ask for a success in conflict to open the LeMarchand puzzle box; it's a constant conceit in the Hellraiser stories that the puzzle is far from trivial, on many different levels.) What happens then? It depends on what other characters we have in the game, maybe the players chose to create their own cast (probably more colorful than the original, considering the tastes of gamers - demon-hunters and such, perhaps). It will certainly be far different from the original story.

    I wouldn't otherwise harp so much on abandoning the canon, except the rpg culture is historically so very biased on this single point that I feel that it requires extra emphasis. It's a simple idea, obvious really, that if you give players some characters to play and expect them to play them as per the game's rules, then of course they're going to make their own choices, and you can't be second-guessing them all the time. Except obviously when we look at how license RPGs get played, the second-guessing is rife: "no, an elf would never do that", "no, the Star Fleet is going to prevent you from doing that", "no, that character is not available because as per the chronology he is currently in King's Landing". You need to let go of all that and just treat everything as lego pieces, and play by the rules of the game you're playing and not by some internal auteur-GM headcanon.

    \n Excellent reply, Eero. I like your take on this!

    I agree that Luke is absolutely easy to "drop" from the story without undue difficulties. However, I disagree that this is somehow unusual or strange.

    Isn't that true of the formula for most epic fantasy fiction? (I'm consider Star Wars fantasy for purposes of looking at the plot; hopefully that doesn't offend anyone.)

    It seems similarly easy to drop Frodo from Lord of the Rings (assume he died bringing the Ring to Rivendell after all, after instance, instead of recovering in their sickbay), although the presence of Leia as ALSO being a Skywalker makes it even starker in Star Wars.

    But doesn't most fantasy depend on an "everyman" character like Luke or Frodo or Bilbo (or even Neo in the Matrix), who is clueless of the larger plot and its concerns, and gets thrown into it despite that?

    I've always thought that the purpose of that viewpoint character as someone who doesn't understand what's going on is engineered specifically so that we, as the audience, can relate to them. In a foreign, fantastic environment, it helps to have a gawking farmboy who is shocked by everything he sees and needs to have it explained to him. That character is a stand-in for us, the audience.

    Of course such a character could be easily removed from the actual plot in most cases, I would think.

    Usually, in such stories, there is a "prophecy" (Neo, Luke) or Maguffin (Frodo, Excalibur, etc) which forces them into the spotlight, despite being entirely unsuited to being a major player in the Larger Plot otherwise. I am going to tentatively say that this is the very PURPOSE of that literary conceit - to justify making a relatable Everyman character the central character in a world-shaking story.

    (This isn't a particularly considered theory of mine, so I'm happy to hear counterarguments. It seems to me that considering it is still somewhat relevant to our topic of designing scenarios based on fictional "properties" and settings.)

    \n Can't drop Frodo from LotR, no - he carries the central theme of humility. Without Frodo and the other hobbits you lose what is arguably the single most important idea in the novel: that it is more important to be virtuous than to be strong. Also, the secondary idea: the best way to be virtuous is to be a humble country squire, rather than a world-wise warlord or such. Arguably the only thing that the dramatic events of LotR are actually about is a deconstruction of paganic notions of virtue from a Christian perspective: Saruman falls despite his knowledge, Boromir falls despite his bravery, even the hobbit falls, yet is delivered by his humility and and faith in human nature.

    Neo in Matrix is very similar to Luke Skywalker, yes. The thematic idea of a boddhisatva delivering the world from suffering is much stronger in Matrix, which makes a "generic" protagonist more useful, though. You could swap him for somebody who actually stands for something, but then you're just adding secondary thematic ideas - it's basically a "what if Buddha, instead of being a completely generic person - meaning a young white man insofar as America is concerned - was a woman or a socialist or a dog" alternative take. Fine for what it is, but the more exceptional and interesting your protagonist, the more the focus of the story will be on that instead of the general human yearning for enlightenment. Arguably, if you want to speak about enlightenment to America, you're going to have to do it with a 30-ish good-looking white male actor; somebody generic.

    \n Aragorn being Aragorn and not Frodo does give LotR its characteristic social conservative vibe - fascist vibe, I would say in informal conversation, but watch and see me use that term in the Internet to refer to any beloved author, particularly one I like myself [grin]. If Aragorn was Frodo, then we would lack the class society setup that gives structure to the kind of world Tolkien imagines: Aragorn should not be forced to make humble choices at all, for he is a true hero and a true king. This is actually sort of what the ruling class does in LotR: they ask the humble subject to take on the difficult tasks so they don't have to. "I am too powerful, I would fail in this task" they say sincerely. It is not a good or bad thing that Aragorn is unable to be truly humble; it simply is the way it is with kings. Tolkien sort of criticizes rulership, but not really; what he is really after is the idea that you shouldn't wish to be more than you are. True greatness is in accepting your station, like Samwais Gamgi does, and as does every good character in the novel.

Make them brothers with Boromir in fact and develop a sort of buddy dynamic between the two, contrasting them. It'll be really tragic when Boromir-the-prideful-brother betrays his kin.

    \n You know, the Gandalf scenario wouldn't have to start from the Council of Elrond at all. It tends to be my go-to example for this because of my Dust Devils experience, but for specifically Entropy, and specifically Gandalf, there could be other interesting starting points...

    For instance, Tolkien makes much hay at multiple times from a sort of causal knowledge thriller: what does a character know at a given point in time, and how does this justify their actions. Gandalf particularly ends up often explaining about how he "only suspected that it might be the One Ring" or "did not know that Sauron knew" or whatever. This isn't just in LotR, it's also in the various appendices and unfinished tales and so on. For instance, Gandalf did a commando dive to Dol Guldur at one point simply to discover the identity of the "Necromancer", and found the lost dwarven king Thrain II in its prisons. I find it notable how much these Gandalf's jaunts are presented to matter in an otherwise epic storyline: for the want of a shoe the kingdom is lost, the story seems to say. Or rather not shoe, but some petty piece of information - the ring-wraiths would totally have captured Frodo if they'd been faster on the trigger, of he'd have tarried even a day longer before escaping the Shire. That's a very modern element in Tolkien's otherwise old-fashioned storytelling sensibility, I think. An agent thriller kind of thing.

    (The ancient author would rather choose to swim in the ironic lack of knowledge on the parts of the characters, instead of justifying last-minute saves and sudden plot twists by acquired information. An old-fashioned story would be all "oh how tragic that you would marry your own mother for not knowing who she was", while Tolkien is like "ha ha, the reason for why Gandalf could only stop you from that marriage at the last minute is that he had to go to the libraries of Denethor to figure out that she's your mother.")

    Given the above, how about starting the scenario really early? Like, right after Bilbo's adventure. The scenario text needs to outline very carefully what Gandalf does and does not know. He still thinks that Sauron is dead (he thinks that the Necromancer is one of the ring-wraiths), he still thinks that Saruman is an ally, he merely suspects the One Ring yet is unable to prove it one way or another without some heavy library diving. Perhaps most importantly: Gandalf does not yet know that the Third Age is coming to an end imminently, within the next generation. He knows his mission (to protect the world from the preternatural evil left over from the Silmaril War), but there is yet much about its specifics and scope that escape him, even at this late date. His only reason to suspect that hobbits might prove key to Fate's design is his theology (Gandalf's not omniscient, remember, and he doesn't remember everything about his previous life over the sea, and he does not have a straight line to Eru or anything like that) and personal gut instinct about what the world is like. All this, and he's still just about the only Istari who has not thoroughly forgotten and abandoned his assigned task, even if he goes about it in some pretty abstract way, hanging out with hobbits and such.

    \nSpeaking strictly for Tales of Entropy, how this plays out depends on the current roles of the players. Basically two scenarios:

    Somebody else is Narrator: The Narrator player describes how the Ring is calling to you, and asks for conflict to sway you to the dark side. You presumably choose to resist (maybe not very hard, though). There's a conflict, Frodo vs. the Ring. The outcome determines whether Frodo succumbs to the Ring. If Gollum is a NPC and present, you would presumably call on him to "help", describing how him attacking Frodo here and now helps him remember himself. Or, the Narrator calls on Gollum to spark Frodo's feeling of ownership; the so-called "Grains player" (who has no character in the scene) makes the call on whom Gollum helps (in the abstract mechanical sense; in the fiction he's trying to help himself, of course).

    You're the current Narrator yourself: You decide whether the Ring is tempting Frodo right now or not. If it's not, or you just want to talk about it without putting any teeth in it, then there's no conflict and you can have Frodo do whatever you like. (You could declare conflict against your own character if you wanted to, too.) If you declare that Frodo decides to claim the Ring (essentially go insane; it's a really bad move rationally speaking at this point, speaking of how the Ring works), perhaps you'll narrate how he becomes invisible and disappears from his companions in the mountainous landscape of Mount Doom. End scene. Afterwards, the other players probably vote you a 2-point increase in Frodo's Shadow score, representing him giving in to the ultimate temptation.

    I would consider it a totally weak-sauce game for playing LotR if you couldn't do internal character conflicts like that. There's no Lord of the Rings, and few other modern stories, without internal character conflict. The fact that Entropy conflicts don't need to be extremely serious in consequence should make it better than Dust Devils for playing LotR in this regard.
