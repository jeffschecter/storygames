\n So, having recently read the rules for the two short-form games in Push, I've been noodling around with the idea of making a short-form game/ RP poem stemming from the idea that RPG's evolved from something other than wargames.

Now, I'm not entirely sure I even want to make a game, but all this noodling has gotten me thinking about character creation. I want to think about what makes character creation fun. I want to think about what makes character creation a mini-game. I want to think about how to bring techniques and methods from outside the roleplaying tradition into character creation. What I mean is, I want help thinking about these things.

My shining gem in this regard is Breaking the Ice. I still marvel at just how damned fun, creative, and brilliant the CC in this game is. Maybe it just hits my sweet spot for creativity within a framework. I don't think that I just like it for its simplicity, either. I enjoy CC in Burning Wheel, but I don't think it's as good as CC in BtI, in the CC-as-game sense. It works very well for making a good character for Burning Wheel. But BtI works well for making a good character for that game and for having a great amount of fun and a creative experience in character creation.

From the perspective of the roleplaying tradition, the BtI word-association-cloud mechanic for CC came out of left field. It feels like something I did in middle school/high school English classes. What are other places we can mine for CC?

I feel like Emily has set an incredibly high benchmark for CC that I want to come close to reaching or not try to make a game at all. With the idea of making a game in mind, doing CC that retreads old ground just feels boring to me, and saps the life out of my interest in designing a game.

    Posted By: MelinglorWow, then ALL games have fun character creation!
    \nOnly games that people play.

    I have the most fun in character generation when I feel like I am:

    * Creating an optimal outcome, in a situation where failure is possible. This is most easily understood in terms of mechanical efficiency (minmaxing) but also applies to narrative mechanics -- doing the best possible job of building a character to achieve my particular narrative goals, or choosing the best possible set of characteristics/traits to represent my vision of the character.

    * Coming up with surprising/unexpected/counter-intuitive combinations of traits, whether they be preset mechanical abilities, story elements or other personal characteristics. Brainstorming/free-association exercises like in Breaking the Ice are a lot of help for this, but so is a very genre-heavy world with an enormous canonical setting, which provides opportunities to be unusual and surprising in relation to the existing game world (I'm a half-orc thief, yeah!)

    * Exploring a complex system with many emergent properties. Again this is easiest to see in a crunchy game like Burning Wheel with the interaction of lifepaths & traits & such, but consider also Shock: or In A Wicked Age, where the initial setup creates a huge complex of interactions & possibilities.

    There's probably more but I think those are the big ones, for me. I do also like rolling stats but that kind of ties in to the first category in a frustrating/fun way.

    \n Everyone's idea of fun character creation will be different (and I agree; while I realized over time that SotC's not really my cuppa, it did have, flat-out, the most fun character creation exercise of any game I can recall playing).

    I can roll and have fun with most character creation methods. I like ones that involve the other players or setting somehow (mention to Dogs in the Vineyard, Polaris, Unknown Armies, and others).

    The only thing that makes chargen un-fun for me, and this is my own issue, is when a character has skills or 'feat-like' entities with dependencies. That is to say:

    1) You have choices in abilities and the like to take.
    2) Abilities lead to further abilities. An Ability Tree, where higher abilities require that the character first take lower abilities.
    3) But you're encouraged to plan your character well, to be the most effective.
    4) So, you have to plan out your "character's future lifepath/career" that he will eventually reach in the game, while you are making your character at first level. ("OK, I want to eventually get Feat X. The lowest level I can get it at is 12. This feat requires me to have those feats, at levels Y and Z. Which means that if I want to take Feat X when I eventually get to 12th Level, I must take Y and 9th, Z at 4th, and that means I need to take only A, B, and C at first level).

    I call this "career planning". I hate to do it in real life, so I don't want it in my game-time.

    Admittedly, the only system off the top of my head that does this is D&D 3e. It doesn't happen in 4e, which was one of the reasons I took a liking to the new edition so quickly. Also, Ryan S's "E6 Hack" basically hamstringed and gutted this effect, which is why the most fun I had in D&D 3E as a player was when I played using the E6 hack. Less Focus on My character's Future, and more focus on playing him in the moment.

    So yeah, if any game comes out where you can get awesome abilities, but to get the awesome ones you need to first take lesser abilities, and to maximize the effect you should do it in a special streamlined order, then I'd probably get the hives from trying to make a character. I don't mind that so much in my console RPGs, because the downtime between chargen and getting those super-powerful-abilities can be like 2-3 days. But in an RPG it can be several months.

    -Andy

    \n I want my character creation experience to be two things:

    Provocative. I want my CC effort to really get me fired up and moving. \n
      \n
    • IAWA does this with the fiercely evocative Oracles. (though I agree with Ogre, the dice assigning thing feels a bit arbitrary to me too.)
    • Beast Hunters does this with its multitude of freeform traits.
    • Crazy random games like Reign or V&V or Warhammer FRP challenge me to transform this weird collection of stats and powers and fluff into a special little snowflake and I enjoy that most of the time.
    • 1001 Nights does this by getting me to freely associate on the five senses (which feels odd but brilliant, like someone asking me to build a bridge by playing the piano.)
    • D&D 4E does this purely with fluff, and it doesn't always succeed. The Avenger and the Druid and the Sorcerer -- none of those classes ever caught my eye before, but the PHB2 fluff was bitchin' cool (especially the druid!) and provided a great foundation for riffage into special snowflake land.
    • Mouse Guard asks just the right questions, in just the right amounts, and at just the right times (your Goals reset every session, which is neat.)
    • Hell, even GURPS does this, although the implied focus is on efficient horsetrading of game efficiencies, which is something that doesn't get me too fired up. But GURPS also sets out exactly how (for example) a lecher with a vow of chastity is mentally tormented, which gets me going.
    • Old school race / class / profession games like D&D or Star Frontiers or whatever are the worst in this regard; there's a limited menu of choices and none of the fluff around them particularly rocks.
    \nEfficient. I want to get the most bang for the least effort. \n
      \n
    • IAWA is simple, but not efficient -- it can take a lot of false starts and mental hemming and hawing to get an IAWA character out of me.
    • Beast Hunters feels like it goes a trait or six too far -- coming up with sixteen evocative traits is a lot of work and can jumble the character a bit. (I can't bring myself to write down 'sword' or 'horse' or 'strong' as a BH trait, but then I'd feel like I'm cheating myself out of some goodness.)
    • To me, GURPS is purest evil in this regard; I don't think I get a lot out of fiddly points distribution. I mean, if I put a single point into Shoveling, and the GM isn't interested in Shovelling, then what's the point? Or, if I put points into Shoveling, and the GM is on the ball enough to notice that, is the GM also going to play to the 1000 other flags on my sheet? if not, why specify them? The resolution is too high given the time scale of an RPG campaign.
    • D&D3E was a paralyzing menu of preplay choices, with evil prerequisites meaning you had to have your career planned out like a Prussian offensive.
    • 1001 Nights gets efficiency perfectly right, IME -- there's just enough there to springboard me into play, but not enough that it's onerous to generate.
    • WFRP is roll, roll, roll, make a couple choices, read some provocative fluff, you're done.
    • Reign is very good too. One roll, copy some numbers, and then start explaining.

    \n I just read a review of the board game Smallworld, an update of Vinci, that had this little gem of an idea:\n
    Players will decide which race they will initially guide by a mechanism that, to my knowledge, was pioneered in Vinci.

    The race plaques are mixed, with five being revealed in a row. Likewise, ability plaques are also mixed, and one placed beside each of the revealed race plaques.

    When a player selects a race, he may take the bottom race and corresponding ability plaque for free.

    If he desires one further along in the row, however, he must place one victory point token onto each race / ability combination he passes over. Thus, if he desires a particular race / ability combination that is further along in the row, it will cost him victory points to obtain it.

    If an opponent subsequently opts to take a race that has victory point tokens on it, he also secures those victory points.

    This is a VERY clever mechanism that gives players much to consider and eventually makes less attractive races more appealing.
    \nThere's gotta be some juice there for a character creation system.
